Abstract
In this study, we explored the impact of distractor relevance on attention allocation and
cognitive performance in a sample of N = 21 participants (normal hearing) within a chal­
lenging, multidirectional auditory environment. The experiment utilized three loudspeak­
ers to project a target signal and distractors, designed as name­number pairs selected
from the most common Danish names and a range of non­decade numbers, embedded in
multitalker babble noise. Our findings demonstrated that the relevance of distractors sig­
nificantly influenced secondary task performance, with participants showing higher accu­
racy when distractions were relevant versus irrelevant. While pupillometry measurements
indicated heightened cognitive engagement and listening effort in response to relevant
distractions, EEG analysis revealed no significant difference in alpha power modulation
between the two types of distractions.

Introduction
Processing of distractions is a crucial area of study as it sheds light on the mechanisms
underlying attentional processing. Attention is the process of choosing a portion of the
sensory input and disregarding the remainder [1]. To understand how we navigate the
complicated and constant changing of the environment around us, it is important to under­
stand how we use our limited attentional resources to filter out the relevant from irrelevant
information.The ability to effectively filter out irrelevant distractions is critical for optimal
performance and decision­making in various contexts. For instance, in an office environ­
ment, an individual needs to be able to focus on their work while ignoring background
noises like the tapping of the keyboard. Similarly, when driving, it is important to ignore
irrelevant distractions such as billboards to maintain focus on the road.
Our capacity to manage the sensory environment around us is mostly dependent on at­
tention, a fascinating and complex cognitive process. By removing distractions and unim­
portant inputs, it enables us to selectively concentrate on pertinent information. However,
not all distraction are irrelevant. For instance, we would like to be able to hear a car honk­
ing when we cross the street. The attentional system can be broadly divided into two
categories: top­down attention and bottom­up attention [2, 3, 1, 4]. Top­down attention
is also known as voluntary attention and is driven by our goals, expectations, and prior
knowledge [5, 6]. Top­down or goal oriented attention is the capacity to actively focus
our attention in accordance with our objectives, expectations, and background informa­
tion. When reading a book, for instance, you employ top­down attention to concentrate on
the words, sentences, and paragraphs, directing your attention in accordance with your
comprehension of the context and your particular reading objectives.
Bottom­up attention, on the other hand, is motivated by the properties of the stimuli them­
selves and acts in a more automatic and instinctive manner. Based on stimulus char­
acteristics like saliency, novelty, and intensity, it grabs our attention [7]. For example,
when sitting in a quiet library and our attention is captured by the sudden sound of a book
dropping or an unexpected cellphone ring. Our attention is unintentionally drawn to the

Our perceptions are shaped by a combination of bottom­up and top­down attentional pro­
cesses. Top­down attention enables us to deliberately direct our cognitive energies toward
stimuli that support our objectives and intentions. It enables us to concentrate on relevant
information when situated in a complicated and dynamic environment. Contrarily, bottom­
up attention acts as an alerting mechanism, drawing our focus to potentially significant or
dangerous inputs that may need quick processing. The neural mechanisms underlying
top­down and bottom­up attention are still not fully understood. However, studies using
neuroimaging techniques such as EEG have shed some light on how the brain processes
attention. For example, studies have shown that top­down attention is associated with
increased alpha and gamma band activity in the brain, while bottom­up attention is asso­
ciated with increased theta and beta band activity [8]. This may occur when performing a
cognitive task that calls for top­down attention, such as solving a challenging problem, or
when responding to an unexpected environmental stimulus, such as a sudden loud noise,
that calls for bottom­up attention. Brain areas like the prefrontal cortex and parietal cor­
tex are usually involved in top­down attention whereas sensory regions and the posterior
parietal cortex are frequently used for bottom­up attention.
Understanding the neural mechanisms that control attentional sound processing is crucial
to the creation and advancement of hearing aids and other assistive technology for people
with hearing loss. Speech comprehension has significantly improved thanks to conven­
tional hearing aids’ emphasis on noise reduction, especially in settings with high noise
levels. These devices excel at supporting top­down attention through the use of spatial
filtering since they predominantly amplify speech coming from the direction the listener
is facing. However conventional hearing aids with excessive noise reduction may acci­
dentally suppress vital auditory cues, especially in loud contexts where important cues
pertaining to the environment may be unwittingly repressed, striking the right balance is
critical. This introduces a paradox wherein the very function aimed at improving the user’s
auditory experience might result in important auditory cues being suppressed. Increas­
ing our knowledge of how the human brain distinguishes between relevant and irrelevant

1.1
Frequency bands in the Electroencephalogram:
Significance of Delta, Theta and Alpha waves
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non­invasive technique commonly used in neurosci­
entific research and clinical practice to measure the electrical activity of the brain. The
EEG characteristic is similar to pink noise in that its power decreases as its frequency
rises. Distinctive spectral peaks that represent periodic brain activity—the so­called brain
oscillations—are superimposed on this noise­like pattern. These oscillations, or brain­
waves, can be analyzed in terms of their frequency, amplitude, phase, and location on
the scalp.
Oscillations are fundamental to the functioning of the brain. They represent the summa­
tion of synchronized activity of neuronal populations and are thought to play a critical role
in various cognitive processes such as attention, memory, and consciousness [10]. Os­
cillations can be categorized into different bands, each associated with different cognitive
and physiological states.
Delta (0.5­4 Hz): These are the slowest waves typically associated with deep sleep and
unconscious processes. Delta waves are often seen in infants and young children, and
their prevalence decreases with age [11]
Theta (4­8 Hz): Theta oscillations are often linked with drowsiness, early stages of sleep,
and relaxation. On a cognitive level, they have been associated with memory, learning,
and spatial navigation [12].
Alpha (8­12 Hz): Alpha waves are prominent during states of relaxation and quiet wake­

fulness. They were initially detected in the occipital lobe when subjects had their eyes
closed, but can be seen in other cortical areas as well [3]. It is believed to regulate the
information flow in the brain by inhibiting areas that are irrelevant or distracting to the cur­
rent task, effectively controlling the degree of ’gating’ or ’filtering’ of sensory information
[13, 14].
The capacity of alpha oscillations to desynchronize and synchronize in response to cog­
nitive activity is one of their key characteristics. Often referred to as event­related desyn­
chronization (ERD), alpha desynchronization is characterized by a decline in alpha power,
which denotes higher neural activity and heightened cognitive processing. Alpha syn­
chronization, also known as event­related synchronization (ERS), is characterized by an
increase in alpha power, which reflects a decrease in neuronal activity or an inhibitory
process [15]. For instance, during visual perception and attention activities, task­related
alpha ERD has been observed over the parieto­occipital regions [16].
On the other hand, ERS is characterized by a rise in alpha power, which indicates an
inhibitory mechanism or decreased cortical activity. Typically, alpha ERS takes place in
areas not directly involved in performing the task, operating as a ”gating” mechanism to
prevent the processing of irrelevant or distracting stimuli [17, 18]. It has been proposed
that this desynchronization and synchronization of alpha power is essential for attentional
gating, enabling the brain to concentrate on important information while blocking out dis­
tractions. Consequently, the regulation of alpha power is a method for selectively regu­
lating the information flow inside the brain [13].
In conjunction with these dynamics, alpha lateralization, an asymmetrical distribution of
alpha power across the cerebral hemispheres, plays a significant role in various cogni­
tive processes. This is particularly evident in tasks requiring visuospatial attention, which
result in lateralization of alpha power. During such tasks, the hemisphere ipsilateral to
the attended visual field exhibits a decrease in alpha power, while the contralateral hemi­
sphere demonstrates an increase [19]. This hemispheric asymmetry of alpha power, also
referred to as alpha lateralization, is thought to reflect the active suppression of task­
irrelevant areas and the facilitation of task­relevant areas [8]. The active suppression of
task­irrelevant areas and the facilitation of task­relevant areas are thought to be reflected

In the auditory realm, the function of alpha power lateralization is considerably substantial.
Extensive research, such as the work by Malte Wöstmann et al. in their paper ”Adverse

weight to this assertion. They have found that under challenging listening conditions and

Alpha power has been found to rise with memory load in the context of working memory,
indicating that alpha activity is involved in the storage and manipulation of information.
Additionally, it is believed that the lateralization of alpha power during memory retention
indicates that the contralateral hemisphere is actively involved in memory processing [21].

memory because of their dynamic desynchronization and synchronization in response to
cognitive activity, as well as the differential lateralization they display. These systems pro­
vide a means of selectively controlling the flow of information within the brain, assisting
in the concentration of crucial information while removing distractions [15]. Alpha oscil­
lations may offer important insights into the neural bases of cognition and function as
possible cognitive state biomarkers as our knowledge of these dynamics expands.

Role of Pupil Dilation in Attentional Processes
The mental effort needed to complete a task is referred to as ”cognitive load.” The easiest
tasks involve low cognitive load, whereas complicated tasks requiring intensive process­
ing or multitasking involve significant cognitive pressure. A study showed that pupillary
dilation can be used to measure cognitive strain. They discovered that during a mental
multiplication work, pupil grew larger as the difficulty of the assignment rose and shrank
after the task was completed [22].

These early conclusions have been further supported by contemporary studies. According
to a study , greater pupil dilation was associated with higher cognitive load as measured
by a speech comprehension task [5]. Similar to this, a 1995 study found that reading a
text in a second language, which naturally requires more cognitive effort than reading in
one’s native tongue, causes bigger pupil dilation [6].
Understanding the neuronal mechanisms behind the pupillary response to cognitive load
has drawn increasing attention in recent years. The locus coeruleus­norepinephrine (LC­
NE) system is thought to be crucial in this occurrence [7,8]. The brainstem’s LC­NE sys­
tem, which controls attention and arousal, is located there. The LC­NE system reacts to
an increase in cognitive load by producing more norepinephrine, a neurotransmitter im­
plicated in the body’s stress response. The pupil enlarges as a result of this stimulating
the iris’s dilator muscle [7].
Although there is strong evidence linking pupillary response to cognitive load, the connec­
tion between the pupil and attentional processes is almost as convincing. There are many
different aspects to the idea of attention, which includes functions including attentiveness,
selective focus, and executive control. The brain bases of each of these features have
been investigated by researchers using pupillometry.
Pupils enlarge in response to stimuli that demand rapid attention. Surprisingly, this re­
sponse incorporates both internal attentional shifts and external signals, not just external
cues. Pupil size rose before cognitive tasks, according to Daniels et al. (2012), demon­
strating a change in attention and alertness on the inside [9].
Pupil dilation is another example of selective attention, which is the capacity to concentrate
on a specific stimulus while ignoring unimportant distractions. A study was conducted in
which subjects saw a computer display of several moving dots. The participants were told
to focus on a certain group of dots while ignoring the others. The researchers discovered
that attention to the dots caused the largest pupil dilatation, indicating a more active level
of selective attention [10].
Pupillometry research offers a sophisticated, objective, and non­invasive way to investi­
gate human cognition, especially the complex facets of cognitive load and attention. This
method provides a potent means to delve into the complex nature of attention because it

is based on the well­established function of the LC­NE system in attention and arousal.
We can quantitatively infer information on changes in cognitive load, the use of atten­

executive control by evaluating changes in pupil size.
Given its possible effects on cognitive load, listening effort—generally defined as the men­
tal work needed to absorb speech—has been the subject of numerous studies. A trust­
worthy physiological indication of this effort has evolved in the field of pupillometry, which
measures changes in pupil diameter. The pupillary response is a reasonable indicator
of listening effort because it reflects autonomic nervous system activity and is impacted
by cognitive load. According to research by Wendt et al. (2018), pupillary responses
can serve as a sensitive indicator of listening effort during speech processing. A study in
2018 noted that the pupil dilation is not only a simple, linear measure of effort but rather
a subtle combination of responses from our autonomic nervous system (ANS) [23]. The
cognitive­induced changes in pupil size show a non­linear pattern [24], meaning that the
pupil dilation is minor for tasks that are either too easy or too hard. The latter may be be­
cause the effort might be withheld if the task is perceived as too challenging to succeed.
Therefore, pupil dilation seems to reflect a person’s readiness to invest more cognitive
effort, implying it’s worthwhile to deploy more mental resources to attain a goal [25]. Sim­
ilarly, When someone is overly exhausted, there is a greater likelihood that they will exert
less cognitive effort since they are less engaged and a decrease in effort exerted. This
in­turn gets reflected in the pupil dilation as a decrease in pupil size [26].
The larger the pupil dilation, the higher the cognitive load. This shows that more cognitive
resources are allocated as the complexity or difficulty of the speech increases, leading to
bigger pupillary reactions if effort is not withheld. Another study in 2017 looked into the
connection between pupil dilation and cognitive spare capacity, or the leftover cognitive
resources accessible throughout a task. They discovered a correlation, indicating that the
amount of cognitive resources still available after a task is finished influences the pupillary
response, with bigger dilations occurring when there are fewer resources available [27].

1.3
Research Question
Considering these gaps in the literature, this study aims to investigate the neurophys­
iological responses to relevant and irrelevant distractors using EEG and pupillometry.
We hypothesize that relevant distractors will elicit a larger neural response, indicated by
changes in alpha power, and greater pupil dilation, compared to irrelevant distractors.
This implies that relevant distractors increase cognitive load and demand more attentional
resources compared to irrelevant ones. To validate our hypothesis, we will examine the
spectral power of the EEG signal using spectral analysis and measure pupil dilation, en­
abling us to determine the neural mechanisms underlying attentional processing of these
distractors.
In the context of alpha power, when individuals are presented with relevant distractors,
there will be a significant decrease in alpha power, indicating alpha desynchronization or
Event­Related Desynchronization (ERD). This decrease in alpha power reflects increased
neural activity and heightened cognitive processing in response to the relevant distractors
. On the other hand, when individuals encounter irrelevant distractors, it is expected that
alpha power will remain relatively stable or exhibit minimal changes.

2.3
Stimuli
2.3.1
Target Signal
In this experiment, the target signal employed was a captivating recording of a biography
centered around ’Simon Spies’. To ensure consistency and seamless integration within
the trials, the continuous speech was divided into 46 equal parts. Each part, lasting ap­
proximately one minute on average, was equalized to maintain a consistent root mean
square (RMS) level across all parts.
The target signal featured a single voice, and was designed to ensure coherence through­
out the experiment. This was achieved by seamlessly connecting the end of one trial to
the beginning of the next, forming a continuous narrative flow. As a result, participants
experienced an uninterrupted and immersive storytelling experience as they progressed
through the trials.
2.3.2
Distractors
The primary objective of incorporating distractors into the experiment was to effectively
divert the participant’s attention away from the target signal. To achieve this, we designed
set of distractors, refereed to as ’distractor pairs’, was employed. These distractor pairs
consisted of names followed by brief second of silence and then numbers. We utilized
the top 126 names in Denmark. This comprehensive selection included 63 male names
and 63 female names.

Furthermore, the numbers chosen for the distractor pairs ranged from 21 to 99, intention­
ally excluding the decade numbers such as 10, 20, and 30. This exclusion was deliberate,
ensuring that each number presented in the distractor pairs contained a similar number
of syllables. Consequently, this approach facilitated a balanced length across the dis­
tractors, contributing to a more standardized length of the distractor. The numbers were
presented after the names, with a consistent duration of 2 second from onset of name to
the onset of the numbers.
In order to further enhance the effectiveness of the distractors, they were embedded within
babble noise, which was normalized just like the target signal. Babble noise, also known
as multi­talker babble, is a type of background noise that simulates a crowded and noisy
environment, resembling the sound of multiple people speaking simultaneously.
The purpose of incorporating babble noise in this experiment was to create a realistic
auditory setting. By introducing this type of noise, it aimed to replicate challenging listening
scenarios that individuals may encounter in real­world situations, such as trying to focus
on a specific speech signal in a noisy social gathering or a bustling sound environment.
In this experiment, meticulous attention was given to the normalization of the babble noise.
By ensuring that the overall intensity and amplitude remained subliminal compared to the
target signal.The core principle of the experiment was to interweave the distractors into
the babble noise, making sure they share similar normalization attributes with the target
signal.
The presentation levels of the signals were determined so that the target signal was set at
0 dB, forming the reference point. The distractors were rendered at a slightly attenuated
level of ­3 dB, creating a subtle but crucial acoustic distinction. Meanwhile, the babble
noise was calibrated even lower at ­6 dB relative to the target signal.
It is worth mentioning that both the name and number distractors underwent a conversion
process from text to speech. This conversion was conducted using an online software,
ensuring that the names and numbers were pronounced with an authentic Danish accent,
providing culturally authentic experience for the participants.

Preprocessing of EEG
For the processing and analysis of the collected EEG data, we utilized the FieldTrip tool­
box [28] , a versatile open­source software suite developed at the Donders Institute for
Brain, Cognition and Behaviour in the Netherlands.
The first step in the pipeline is the extraction of markers and triggers from the unprocessed
EEG data, which are used to precisely segment the continuous data into trials. Then, to
lessen the computational strain for upcoming processing, the data is resampled at 200
Hz. A bandpass filter is used to keep track of the frequencies of interest (1 ­ 80 Hz) , and
a bandstop filter is used to eliminate noise created by electrical power around 50 Hz. The
data is separated into individual trials after preprocessing, resulting in clean, segmented,
and trial­based data prepared for advanced analysis.
The next step in the pipeline was removing the noisy or bad channels. Initially, a neighbor­
hood structure for channel interpolation is established using a specific layout, ’Biosemi64
Mastoids.’ For each subject, the script prepares a clean working structure by removing
bad channels. The bad channels are identified through a visual identification for each
subject. Identified bad channels are subsequently interpolated, replaced based on the
average of neighboring channels. The script finishes by retaining only the relevant trials
and saving the preprocessed EEG data for further analysis.
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is a robust and essential approach for the removal
of artifacts from EEG data as well as source separation. Artifacts frequently taint the
intricate details and nuanced brain signals recorded in EEG data. The EEG signals can
be severely distorted by these non­neuronal signals, which can come from sources like
eye movements, muscle contractions, or even technical interferences, making it difficult
to make sense of them. The accuracy and dependability of the EEG data are improved
by the effective method that ICA offers for separating these artifacts from the true brain
signals. Furthermore, the application of ICA goes beyond artifact elimination.

Spectral Power Analysis of EEG
The clean EEG signal undergoes transformation into a time­frequency representation us­
ing the ’morlet’ wavelet, enabling detailed analysis of frequency dynamics over time. This
transformation covers all channels. It utilizes a wavelet width of 7 and generates power
spectrum output with frequencies of interest range from 2 to 25 Hz in 0.5 Hz steps, with the
analysis time spanning from 0 to 65 seconds at 0.05 second intervals. The padding is set
to optimize the Fourier Transform speed, with a sampling rate of 200 Hz. This conversion
offers a high­resolution perspective of signal characteristics across individual trials
Following the conversion, the EEG signal is time­locked to the onset of distractors and
stored in separate variables based on the different experimental conditions. This step
is essential for capturing the specific EEG response elicited by the onset of each event,
enabling subsequent analysis and comparison across conditions.
To further analyze the data, a baseline correction employing a ’relative change’ approach
is implemented from ­1 s to 0 s to the onset of every distractor event ,to establish a con­

for all subjects, consolidating the information and providing a representative response for
each condition. This averaging process allows for a clearer understanding of the aver­
age EEG response to each event. Finally, the grand mean power spectrum is computed,
which aggregates the power spectral information across all conditions and subjects.

Cluster Test
The script begins by preprocessing the data for each subject. Following preprocessing,

2.7
Procedure and Task
2.7.1
Experimental Procedure
The experiment involved two simultaneous tasks. In the primary task, participants were
required to concentrate on a narrative titled ”Simon Spies,” played from a central speaker.
Concurrently, participants also had to manage pairs of distractor signals presented on the
left and right side speakers. Only one of these sides, however, was designated as the
’relevant side.’ At the end of each trial, participants were asked ’Yes’ or ’No’ questions
about the narrative. Participants used a keyboard with dedicated ’Yes’ and ’No’ keys
to register their responses. These answers provided a measure of their attention and
comprehension of the narrative.
The secondary task was layered onto this primary task. It required participants to mem­
orize numbers associated with names that were presented from the relevant side, as
indicated visually on a monitor in front of them before the start of each trial. If the monitor
prompted ”Listen to the left side,” participants had to maintain focus on the central narra­
tive, memorize numbers associated with the left­side distractor pairs, and ignore distractor
pairs on the right side.
After the completion of the primary task, a 3x3 grid was displayed (see figure 2.7). This
grid contained three numbers from the relevant side, three numbers from the irrelevant
side, and three numbers not presented during the trial. Participants were then tasked with
identifying the three numbers that they had heard from the relevant side. This process
assessed their memory retention during the multitasking situation.

4
Discussion
4.1
Behavioral Results
Our findings offer thorough explanations of how relevance of distractions affects both task
performance and attention allocation. The concept of ”distractor relevance” refers to how
much a task’s environment or other factors may make a stimulus that is unrelated to it
remain significant or demanding of attention. The amount of attention we give to such
stimuli can be considerably impacted by the relevance of the distraction, which can then
have an impact on how well we perform on both primary and secondary tasks. The high
accuracy rate in this study’s primary task performed by participant is evidence of subject’s
capacity to prioritize and concentrate on the primary task (’Yes or ’No’ questions) at hand
despite the presence of distractors. In essence, individuals were able to appropriately
respond to the ”Yes” or ”No” questions by filtering out distractor inputs, both relevant and
irrelevant.
This is consistent with Lavie’s Load Theory of Attention [29], which postulates that our
attentional resources are limited and that we can only analyze distractions if we still have
attentional resources available after processing task­relevant stimuli. In this case the task
relevant stimuli was the ’Yes or ’No’ questions as well as remembering the numbers from
the relevant side. The good performance on the primary task in the setting of our study
shows that participants were capable of effectively allocating their cognitive resources to
the primary task, possibly at the expense of processing the distractors.
With regard to the secondary task, participants’ accuracy of (73.2 percent) in identifying
and choosing the numbers connected to relevant distractors offers compelling proof of the
substantial impact of distractor relevance on both the allocation of attention and memory
processes. When we compare the recall of relevant versus irrelevant number hits, the
p value was significant which clearly supports the outcome’s statistical significance. It
shows that, assuming no meaningful influence, the probability of observing such a high
degree of accuracy of choosing relevant numbers occurred by chance alone is very low.

4.2
Pupillometery Results
The results of our investigation support our hypothesis that a relevant distractor will cause
a greater increase in pupil size.
The difference is statistically substantial, which pro­
vides strong evidence that distractor relevance has a considerable impact on pupillary
responses, which are frequently utilized as physiological markers of cognitive processing
and engagement.
The results of our investigation provide empirical support for the idea of ”inattentional
blindness,” especially when it comes to the lack of pupillary response to unimportant
distractions. This phrase, which was initially used by Mack and Rock (1998), refers to
the cognitive phenomena in which people ignore completely obvious but irrelevant inputs
when their attention is focused elsewhere. This is a byproduct of our cognitive system’s
selective attention mechanism rather than a result of any deficiencies in visual perception
or cognitive abilities. This idea’s core premise is that our cognitive system has a finite
amount of processing power. Given the variety of stimuli that constantly saturate our
senses, processing every single piece of information with equal attention is neither cogni­
tively efficient nor even possible. As a result, in order to ensure effective processing, the
human cognitive system evolved to give priority to processing stimuli that are pertinent to

==PAGE:47==
the job at hand or those that have meaning for the individual, while ignoring or paying little
attention to the irrelevant stimuli.
To put things in perspective, the process is comparable to a spotlight that only illuminates
a small portion of a large landscape; the brightened area represents stimuli that are the
focus of our attention (relevant distractors in our study), while the remainder that remains
in the shadow represents stimuli that we are aware of but choose not to pay attention
to (irrelevant distractors in our study). This spotlight is a representation of the restricted
capacity of our brain system because it can only illuminate a specific area at once.
In our experiment, the absence of pupillary response to irrelevant distractions serves as
a representation of this ”shadow area.” When exposed to the irrelevant distractors, the
pupillary response, a physiological indicator of cognitive processing and attention, did not
exhibit any appreciable dilation. This shows that these stimuli were probably perceived by
the cognitive system, but because they were deemed unimportant to the task at hand, they
were not given major cognitive resources, leading to a condition known as ”inattentional
blindness.” In cases where the cognitive system is subject to an information overload,
selective processing is essential for preserving cognitive efficiency.
Our results demonstrated that the participants’ pupils responded significantly to the rele­
vant distractors. Notably, the relevant distractors’ names and numbers elicited differing
degrees of pupillary response, with the numbers evoking a stronger response than the
names. This difference in the intensity of pupillary responses could be attributed to the in­
herent task­related significance of the numbers in the experiment. The participants were
explicitly instructed to remember the numbers for a subsequent recall task, hence attach­
ing a higher level of importance to them. This could have resulted in an elevated level
of attentional engagement and cognitive processing effort when the numbers were pre­
sented, manifested as a stronger pupillary response. This is in line with previous studies
showing that items directly related to a task often receive a higher level of attentional
priority [1].
An intriguing result of our investigation is the extended pupillary dilatation seen after the
presentation of the relevant distractor, even lasting longer than 8 seconds. A persis­
tent pupillary dilatation reflects continuous cognitive processes and is frequently linked

to increasing cognitive load [31]. It appears that participants in this instance continue to
engage cognitive effort long after the pertinent distractor has been delivered.
The function of memory allocation in task performance may provide an explanation for this
phenomena. Participants had to keep the number in their working memory for a future
recall task once the relevant distractor was presented. The cognitively taxing function
of working memory maintenance has been linked to sustained pupillary dilatation [32].
This is due to the fact that recall preparation, interference avoidance, and maintaining the
number in active memory all demand cognitive effort which is reflected in the sustained
pupil dilation [33] .
4.2.1
Correlation Between Relevant Distractor Evoked Pupil Responses
and Secondary Task Performance
A crucial component of cognitive processing may be seen in the positive association be­
tween relevant distractor­evoked pupil responses and the percentage of relevant numbers
that were correctly remembered. This relationship postulates that more involvement, as
implied by higher pupillary responses during the presentation of pertinent distractions,
encourages more effective encoding and later retrieval of the material.
It has long been known from prior studies that higher levels of cognitive involvement lead to
better task performance. According to studies, paying closer attention during the encoding
stage of memory processing makes memory traces stronger and makes recall easier [34,
35].
This connection supports the idea that pupil dilation can be useful as a concrete indica­
tor of mental effort and involvement. Although our results are statistically significant, it
should be highlighted that the correlation strength is only moderate. This suggests that
successful recollection is probably influenced by a number of factors in addition to cogni­
tive attention during distractor presentation. To gain a deeper knowledge of the memory
process, future studies may dive into these additional potential factors such as cognitive
abilities, motivation and interest.
The average MPD generated by irrelevant distractors and the proportion of irrelevant hits
were observed to have a weak connection and non­significant p­value, which points to a
relatively small effect of cognitive engagement with irrelevant stimuli on task performance.

4.3
EEG Power Results
Our study investigated the impact of relevant and irrelevant distractors on brain oscillatory
activity. Initial expectations focused on alterations in alpha power, a notion supported by
prior research, suggesting that alpha power reflects cognitive and attentional processes
[8, 16]. The theories of Klimesch (2012) and the ”gating by inhibition” principle suggest that
alpha oscillations regulate information processing and a rise in alpha power signifies an
active inhibitory process for irrelevant information [8, 13]. However, our findings presented
a more complex picture. Instead of a specific shift in alpha power, we observed a general
increase in power across all frequency bands, with more pronounced changes in the lower
delta range. This finding suggests that both relevant and irrelevant distractions stimulate
broad cognitive exertion, as indicated by increased power across the frequency spectrum,
particularly in the delta band. As such, our results invite a reinterpretation of our initial
hypothesis, implying that attentional processes and distractions affect a wider range of
neural oscillations than previously thought.
Drawing comparisons between these investigations and our own, it appears plausible that
the introduction of any distractor—relevant or irrelevant—necessitates some level of cog­
nitive processing from the participants. This notion stands in contrast to our pupillometry
study’s conclusions. Pupil dilation is widely recognized as an indicator of arousal and cog­
nitive load [36], while EEG power, especially within particular frequency bands, mirrors a
range of cognitive and sensory processes [37]. The observed discrepancy between our
pupillometry and EEG results suggests that the cognitive load induced by relevant and
irrelevant distractors may differ, which may not translate into the type or extent of neural
processing they involve.
To ascertain the relevancy of distractions, the participants had to at least partially com­
prehend them, a mental activity potentially reflected in changes in alpha power. The
lack of substantial difference in alpha power modulations between relevant and irrelevant
distractions in our study suggests a possible limit to the gating or inhibitory role of alpha
oscillations. Despite the variability in the relevancy of distractors, they might have exerted
comparable cognitive demands, leading to similar fluctuations in alpha activity.
The design of distractors could have also played a significant role in the results observed.

The dual­component nature of the distractor pairs (names and numbers), coupled with the
task requirement to selectively attend to only the numbers from the relevant side, adds
a layer of complexity to the cognitive processing involved. Participants had to not only
segregate the auditory input based on spatial location (left vs. right), but also based on
the type of information (name vs. number). This introduces two levels of ’relevance’: the
side of presentation and the type of distractor component. Essentially, participants had
to ignore both the irrelevant­side distractors and the names from the relevant side, while
focusing on the numbers from the relevant side.
Therefore, this task likely demanded flexible cognitive control and selective attention, as
participants had to dynamically shift their attention based on both the spatial location and
the content of the auditory input. This level of attentional control and cognitive flexibility is
often associated with changes in alpha power [8]. However,it’s possible that this complex
task requirement led to a general increase in alpha power as participants attempted to
inhibit irrelevant information (both the irrelevant­side distractors and the names from the
relevant side). This could have resulted in no significant difference in alpha power be­
tween relevant and irrelevant distractors, as the alpha power increases associated with
each type of ’irrelevant’ information (irrelevant­side distractors and names from the rele­
vant side) may have overlapped or intermingled.
Our study’s findings, characterized by an absence of a significant difference in alpha
power between relevant and irrelevant distractors, could be due to the taxing nature of the
experimental design itself. Previous research has shown that there is a lack of increase
in alpha power when the task is either incomprehensible or exceedingly difficult and only
an increase when the task was at a medium cognitive load [38, 39]. Even­though there is
the contrary evidence that the task in this experiment was too challenging , as depicted
by the behavioral results, the dynamics of alpha power in response to task demands may
assume a U­shaped function. It might be that alpha power is low when tasks are relatively
easy or exceedingly demanding, and surges during tasks of moderate difficulty. This per­
spective is illuminated by prior work that identified amplified effects of working memory
load, possibly suggesting that the complexity of the tasks in those studies did not reach
the level necessary to observe a decrease in alpha power [40, 41].

Kousaie et al.’s description of sentence­level processing in a stronger and a weaker lan­
guage bears resemblance to our study’s cognitive demands. Participants needed to pro­
cess both relevant (”stronger”) and irrelevant (”weaker”) distractors, which might have
been sufficiently taxing to exhaust available cognitive resources. Consequently, a failure
to observe increased alpha power under these conditions could be anticipated. There­
fore, the parallels between the alpha power patterns observed in both our study and that
of Kousaie et al.’s offer an alternative interpretation of the results, potentially indicative of
a state of resource exhaustion as opposed to differentiated cognitive engagement. How­
ever, this interpretation requires further investigation for validation.
Further, the utilization of babble noise in our study, present in both the relevant and
irrelevant distractors, may offer another possible explanation. Prior research has indi­
cated a general enhancement of alpha power when deciphering speech amidst noise as
compared to a quiet environment, implying the necessity of supplementary attentional re­
sources for processing speech in noisy conditions [42]. In the context of our study, given
that distractors were presented amidst babble noise, the availability of these additional
cognitive resources may have been compromised. This, in turn, could potentially explain
the absence of a discernible difference in alpha power between relevant and irrelevant
distractors.
Our studies included a spatial component as well, dividing the distractor types according
to whether they were presented from the left or right side. We did not discover any ap­
preciable changes in alpha power in response to distractions delivered from either side,
despite this segregation. This result challenges past research that suggested lateralized
attentional processes in the alpha band (Worden, Foxe, Wang, Simpson, 2000) and im­
plies that the spatial location of the distractor may not significantly alter the alpha power.
However, the lack of such an effect in our study may have been caused by the overall
high cognitive load brought on by the task and the aural environment, which masked any
minute variations in spatial attention.
In conclusion, while our findings add to the body of knowledge about EEG alpha oscil­
lations and attention, they also highlight how complex these processes are. The lack of
distinction between relevant and irrelevant distractions in terms of alpha power suggests

analysis tools.

Conclusion
In conclusion, our study revealed that the relevance of distractions significantly affects
task performance, attention allocation, and cognitive processing. Participants demon­
strated the ability to prioritize the primary task and selectively process relevant distractors
while filtering out irrelevant ones. Pupillary responses indicated heightened engagement
with relevant distractors, while EEG alpha power showed similar modulations for both rel­
evant and irrelevant distractors, possibly due to the complexity of the task and demands
for cognitive control. Further research could explore the temporal dynamics of pupillary
responses and alpha oscillations, investigate the role of cognitive load and task difficulty,
examine individual differences, and integrate multiple neuroimaging techniques to gain a
more comprehensive understanding of attentional processes and cognitive control in the
presence of distractions.
