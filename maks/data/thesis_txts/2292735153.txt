ABSTRACT
A stated goal of biotechnology is the creation of biosustainable and cost-effective
cell factories. For many individual compounds this goal have been reached, but
the development of novel cell factories remain an outstanding challenge. To
achieve an efficient translation of idea to cell factory, new methods must be de-
veloped and our understanding of the mechanisms of life must be deepened.
One important new genome editing method is called multiplex automated
genome engineering (MAGE), which has the capability to make multiple simul-
taneous edits directly to the bacterial genome. MAGE is a powerful tool in strain
engineering, but bacterial genomes are compact and complex, and the intricacies
of native gene regulation are improperly understood.
To help the scientific community towards a better understanding of the mecha-
nisms governing gene regulation and to develop tools to harness the full power
of MAGE, the EMOPEC library and tool was developed. The basis of EMOPEC
is a comprehensive assessment of the Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence. Using a
method termed FACS-seq, a library of green fluorescent protein (GFP) with a ran-
domised SD sequence was sorted using FACS and the resulting bins sequenced
using next-generation sequencing. From this data the EMOPEC library was cre-
ated, and to make the library easier to use for the community at large, an online
EMOPEC tool was created, which helps users choose the right SD sequences from
the library (available at http://emopec.biosustain.dtu.dk).
To validate the portability and efficacy of the EMOPEC tool, we created 110 new
strains based on EMOPEC recommendations to modulate 11 different native pro-
teins in native context to 10 different expression levels. The expression level of
the target proteins of 33 of these strains were experimentally determined to test
whether the predicted values correspond to the measured protein expression. The
strains designed with EMOPEC were found to correspond to the predicted values
to a satisfying degree with R2 values ranging from 0.50 to 0.72, and found to be
as accurate as other state-of-the art solutions.
To asses EMOPEC in a real-world scenario, a library of MAGE oligos were created,
targeting 163 different E. coli transcription factors. The library was created using
Microarray-Oligonucleotide (MO)-MAGE. The oligonucleotide library was cre-
ated and processed. Creating a strain library from the oligo library and assessing
the properties of the mutations is still a work in progress.

1 INTRODUCTION
Metabolic engineering and synthetic biology are scientific disciplines where the
intricate and specialized natural machinery of life is understood and adapted into
useful applications. Comprehending and utilizing the features of living organisms
is key to developing a sustainable future for mankind.
This thesis concerns itself with the EMOPEC (empirical model and oligos for pro-
tein expression changes) program, and the validation of the underlying model.
EMOPEC was developed by the author of this thesis along with Mads T. Bonde,
Margit Pedersen, Alex T. Nielsen, Markus J. Herrgård and Morten O. A. Sommer
on the Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Biosustainability, Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark in late 2014 and early 2015.
EMOPEC is a tool for making specific alterations to the translation process in
bacteria. It is based on a nearly comprehensive database of the so-called Shine-
Dalgarno sequence1. Shine-Dalgarno sequences are a genetic sequence with a
majority of the responsibility for recruiting the ribosome – the cellular machinery
turning genetic information into proteins.
1.1 Project Overview
To validate whether EMOPEC is at all useful, the project is divided into two par-
allel subprojects. Part one (fig. 1.1A and B) is constructing a series of individual
controls, which will be independently measured and used to directly evaluate the
performance of the mutations suggested by the EMOPEC tool.
Part one is further divided into two batches, where the method of isolating the
individual clones differ. In part 1A (figure 1.1), a classical allele specific PCR
approach is used. In part 1B, a faster and more modern approach utilizing next-
generation sequencing technologies is used.
Meanwhile, a diverse library of mutant Escherichia coli will be created (fig. 1.1C).
The library mutations are constructed using EMOPEC to alter the expression of
163 transcription factor targets. The library will be evaluated by applying selec-
tive pressure with selected antibiotics. The goal of this exercise is to locate tran-
scription factors conferring resistance to the given antibiotics, thereby testing
EMOPEC in a real-world application which could potentially be used to elicit
other fitness enhancing mutations.

Using EMOPEC to generate targeted diversity before applying selective pressure

from random noise. Secondly, the mutations will not have to arise by natural –
or induced – events which means the effects of particular mutations will become
apparent after fewer generations of bacterial growth.
By targeting E. coli transcription factors, it is hypothesized that a greater pheno-
typic space is explored by altering the expression of a relatively small amount of
target genes. 163 annotated E. coli transcription factors has been targeted and is
expected to influence a significant majority of the more than 4000 annotated E.
coli genes.
Thesis Statement
Can we validate the EMOPEC claims of targeted and rational translational ex-
pression modulation with real-world use cases?

2.1 Microbial Cell Factories
Biology as a technology has been in use for thousands of years – even before any-
one understood the concept of a cell or microorganism. Several ancient cultures
have been using microorganisms to ferment various substrates. In fact, human
have unknowingly domesticated a series of microorganisms which have adapted
specifically for the nutrient-rich conditions in controlled fermentation2, and no
longer posses the ability to survive in the wild.
The advent of modern biology and industrial fermentation processes have paved
the road for a new industry which uses biological substrates to synthesise chem-
ical compounds with the help of microbes. Using the natural machinery of liv-
ing organisms comes with a series of advantages which include the ability to
metabolise a wide array of complex substrates and the ability to catabolise ex-
tremely advanced compounds. A few examples of different kinds of products
made by cell factories is shown in fig. 2.1.

2.2 The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology
The central dogma of molecular biology is a model of information flow in biolog-
ical systems formulated by Francis Crick in 19586. It states that within the three
polymers which are central to life as we know it, information can only flow in cer-
tain directions and will under most circumstances only flow from DNA →DNA,
DNA →RNA, and RNA →Protein. The information flow is illustrated in fig. 2.2.
A - DNA replication
B - RNA transcription
C - Protein translation
Figure 2.2 – The 3 general transfers are shown in this illustration. A – DNA is
replicated in all dividing cells. Once every cell generation the whole genome
of the cell is duplicated. B – In a living cell, DNA is translated to RNA, which
C – is translated to proteins. Not shown are the 3 “special transfers”, which are
RNA →RNA, RNA →DNA, and DNA →Protein. The 2 first special transfers
occur mostly in relation to vira, while the third can occur in vitro but has never
been demonstrated in vivo7.
The central dogma is used to illustrate where biological systems are regulated.
Anytime information flows from one polymer to another energy is expended and
it is safe to assume that some kind of regulation is in effect.

2.3 Transcription and Transcriptional Regulation
The first most common information flow in living organisms is the transcription
of DNA to RNA. RNA serves many roles and our understanding of its importance
as an independent effector in biological systems has been increasing in recent time
following numerous discoveries of the purpose of RNA8,9.
Transcription of DNA to RNA is mediated mainly by the RNA polymerase com-
plex. In prokaryotes, RNA polymerase binds to a sigma factor which in turn bind
to promoters on the DNA molecule. Promoters are special DNA sequences which
the sigma factors will specifically bind to and initiate RNA polymerase transcrip-
tion.
Different types of sigma factors recognize different promoter sequences. This cre-
ates one mode of regulation in bacteria. For instance, in E. coli, the σ70 sigma
factor is active most of the time and makes sure the house-keeping genes are ex-
pressed while σ38 is responsible for expressing genes useful in conditions which
stresses the cells10–12.
A different class of proteins which regulate the transcription process is called
transcription factors and consists of DNA binding proteins which can either acti-
vate or repress transcription initiation. Activators and repressors have their own
recognition sequences where they bind to DNA specifically. Transcription factors
is another example of a mode of regulation, and most transcription factors can
alter their binding strength based on the environment and are thus a quick and
effective manner of regulation. A simple overview of two modes of regulation
with transcription factors are shown in fig. 2.3.
2.4 Translation
RNA is most widely known for its role as an information carrier of coding se-
quences (CDSs) for proteins. RNAs transcribed for the purpose of being a tem-
plate for protein synthesis are called messenger RNAs (mRNAs), and the protein
synthesis process is called translation.
The main actor in protein synthesis is the ribosome. The ribosome is a large com-
plex consisting of both proteins and ribozymes (catalytic RNA molecules). The
ribosome recognises a small sequence and the initiation codon, the combination
of which is called the ribosome binding site (RBS). Inside the ribosome binding
site is the Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence1 which is more or less complementary
to the 3’ end of the 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) – a key component from the
ribosome complex.

A typical mRNA can be divided into 2 types of regions: The untranslated regions
(UTR) and coding sequences (CDS). The UTR immediately upstream of a CDS
carries the ribosome binding site, located upstream of the initiation codon with a
small spacer. The spacer is usually 3-7 nucleotides but can be op to 17 nucleotides
long13. The first UTR in front of the first coding sequence is commonly called the
5’-UTR.
The ribosomal binding to mRNA and the initiation of translation is a complex
affair that depends on a multitude of factors. mRNA transcripts have been shown
to be non-modular, as a particular 5’-UTR have been shown to give a 530-fold
difference in expression when placed in front of two different CDSs14.

Predicting Translation Expression
Predicting the efficiency of translation is a big step in the rational engineering of
bacterial organisms. Salis et al. (2009)14 have developed a thermodynamic model
which includes most of the important steps in the translation initiation process
and is named the RBS Calculator.
The model is based heavily on the in silico prediction of RNA folding. There are
several algorithms and software packages available, which do quite well at pre-
dicting simple structures18. Problems and limitations of the algorithms is their
inability to predict pseudoknots (non-nested base pair interactions) accurately19.
9
==PAGE:16==
They are also quite limited by the assumption that RNA folds in the same way un-
der in vivo and in vitro conditions, which have been shown to not be the case20.
The translation initiation rate is calculated as a function of the difference in Gibbs
energy between the initial condition which is the free mRNA molecule folded in
on itself (∆Ginitial) and the ribosome bound to the mRNA (∆G f inal). The Gibbs
energy difference is termed ∆Gtotal and the equation is:
∆Gtotal = ∆Gfinal −∆Ginitial
∆Gfinal comprises several different terms:
∆Gfinal = ∆G16S + ∆Gstart + ∆Gspacing −∆Gstandby
Where the terms in the equations are shown in fig. 2.5. ∆G16S is the Gibbs en-
ergy of the hybridisation between the 16S rRNA and the mRNA transcript. In a
similar fashion, ∆Gstart is the hybridisation of the initiation codon to the formyl-
methionine transfer RNA. ∆Gspacing is a penalty for suboptimal spacing where
the ribosome has to “compress” or “stretch” the mRNA region between the SD
sequence and the initiation codon.
∆Gstandby is more involved, but it is itself a small model of the Gibbs energy needed
for the ribosome to bind unspecifically to the mRNA transcript. mRNA has been
shown to fold and unfold extremely rapidly, and it is unlikely that the ribosome
has time to attach itself directly to the ribosome binding site21. A way to explain
how transcription is still possible under these conditions is to assume the ribo-
some binds unspecifically to mRNA and then slides along the structure until it
can form a stable initiation complex with the mRNA.
The ∆Gstandby model as described in Espah Borujeni et al. (2014)22 is – briefly
stated – a penalty that approximates the size of the binding surface available for
unspecific binding. Binding surface is defined as unpaired nucleotides anywhere
upstream of the ribosome binding site. The model also encompasses the energy

2.5 Characterisation of the Shine-Dalgarno Sequence
In an attempt to dig deeper into the mechanisms of prokaryotic translation,
EMOPEC (Empirical Model and Oligos for Protein Expression Changes) was
born24. EMOPEC is an empirical model based on a nearly comprehensive char-
acterization of the 6 bp Shine-Dalgarno sequence. At the heart of EMOPEC is
a library of 3087 SD sequences associated with an experimentally quantified ex-
pression value.
The EMOPEC library is created using a novel method named FACS-seq which
is illustrated in fig. 2.7. Green fluorescent protein (GFP) was heterologously in-
tegrated into the genome of Escherichia coli creating a strain fluorescing a green
color under UV lighting. GFP expression was suppressed by mutating the Shine-
Dalgarno sequence in front of GFP to an anti-SD sequence.
A comprehensive library of E. coli strains expressing GFP in different levels was
created by introducing a randomised SD sequence in place of the anti-SD se-
quence (fig. 2.7A). The cell library was then sorted using a fluorescence-activated
cell sorting (FACS) machine capable of sorting single cells based on their fluo-
rescence activity which is proportional to the amount of GFP expressed in the
individual cells (fig. 2.7B).
The cell library was sorted into 17 bins which were each sequenced and the abun-
dance of the sequence in each well was counted. Figure 2.7C shows a small subset
of SD sequences with their relative abundances in each well depicted as a his-
togram.
4066 of the possible 4096 (46) combinations were found in the sequencing dataset.
Out of those, 3087 sequences had more than 50 reads and an accurate estimate of
expression related to SD sequence could be achieved. Due to the stochastic nature
of gene expression, a gaussian curve was fitted over the distribution of abundance
in each well.
As previously described, the context of the mRNA transcript is extremely impor-
tant to expression. Alteration of the Shine-Dalgarno sequence in the context of
the GFP coding sequence and the particular 5’-UTR chosen in the experiment
may not mean a similar alteration in a different context. However, one could ar-
gue that alterations to the SD core sequence is a small change of 6 bp compared to
the thousands of basepairs in a typical mRNA transcript or the 50-100 basepairs
in the 5’ UTR region of a typical E. coli transcript25.
To test this hypothesis and to create a useful tool for genetic engineering,
EMOPEC was created. EMOPEC does two very simple things. It predicts the
location of the SD sequence in front of a CDS by finding the subsequence with

2.6 Recombineering & MAGE
Recombineering (recombination-mediated genetic engineering) enables re-
searchers to engineer genomes based on homologous recombination. In wild
bacteria, homologous recombination is a rare event, but it is possible to use sys-
tems from bacteriophages to enable efficient recombination28,29.
The λ Red system is a recombineering tool based on the λ Red bacteriophage,
which enables double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) recombineering in E. coli with the
Gam, Exo and Beta proteins. Gam will prevent the native RecBCD nuclease from
degrading DNA introduced in the cells, Exo is a 5’→3’ exonuclease which will de-
grade a single strand of DNA and turn a dsDNA molecule into a single-stranded
DNA (ssDNA) molecule. Beta (β) will bind ssDNA and facilitate genomic inte-
gration30.
To simultaneously increase efficiency and simplify the system, it is possible to
remove the Gam and Exo proteins from the equation by using single-stranded
DNA as a starting point31. The current theory on how integration takes place is
that it happens during replication and the ssDNA is inserted in the same way an
Okazaki fragment is normally inserted32. Okazaki fragments are how organisms
deal with the fact that the DNA polymerase only synthesises new DNA in a 5’ →3’.
It is even possible to use several different fragments of DNA to target multi-
ple sites in a genome. MAGE33 (multiplex automated genome engineering) is
a method for rapid and efficient targeted insertion of specific mutations using
multiple single-stranded DNA recombineering. Several short ssDNA oligos are
transformed into E. coli cells, and integrated into the genome mediated by the β
protein.
The integration efficiency of MAGE is an important consideration. After running
a MAGE experiment, the resulting culture will be a mixture of the background
strain as well as combinations of the inserted mutations. The exact efficiency of
MAGE is a function of the extent of the designed modifications and the target
site. Single base pair substitutions or insertions can achieve efficiencies around
15-20 %, while a 6 base pair substitution/insertion is around 5-10
The simple nature of MAGE allows for cyclic operation as an easy way to increase
the integration efficiency. The MAGE cycle is shown in fig. 2.9.
The pMA7 System
An important factor to consider when doing small substitutions is the methyl-
directed mismatch repair (MMR) system in E. coli. When E. coli replicates its
own genome, the DNA polymerase makes mistakes34. E. coli DNA polymerases

for certain mismatch pairs such as C:C37,38.

which recognises nucleotide mismatches39. This has been shown to work quite
well32, but it has the caveat that the MMR is no longer functional and therefore
natural off-target mutations accumulate in the cells32,40, since mismatches now
happen at a 100-fold higher frequency41. Another solution is to use modified
bases which acts as the natural nucleotide bases but are not recognised by the
MMR system42. However, DNA oligo synthesis with non-standard DNA bases

2.7 Oligonucleotide Synthesis for MAGE
Even considering the steady decrease in costs of synthesising artificial DNA se-
quences, traditional synthesis of synthetic DNA remain costly when one consid-
ers targeting hundreds of loci with thousands of different oligos. To have one
thousand 90 bp nucleotide oligos synthesised individually via traditional means
by the industry leader comes at a cost of approx. US$ 17,500 at time of writing
(Integrated DNA Technologies).
The advent of microchips as a synthesis platform can dramatically reduce this
cost, albeit with a few restrictions. Microchip DNA arrays are traditionally used
as a means to quantitatively measure DNA molecules of a specific sequence in a
complicated soup of thousand or maybe millions of different DNA molecules.
18
==PAGE:25==
DNA molecules of known sequence are bonded to known spots on the microar-
ray. By hybridising the spots with a soup of fluorescently tagged DNA or RNA
molecules with unknown sequence, it is possible to get a measure of which DNA
sequences are present by measuring the intensity of the fluorescent spots on the
microarray. This technology is quite useful for quickly and simply measuring the
abundance of particular transcripts in a particular condition.
The evolution of this technology has seen several innovations in chip design, to a
point where it is now possible to synthesise a completely customisable chip con-
sisting of up to 100,000 unique oligos (http://customarrayinc.com). A pop-
ular way of manufacturing chips comprises a similar strategy to the traditional
DNA synthesis, but does so with inkjet printer technology where the synthesis
takes places in micro-droplets on a chip instead of a traditional reaction tube.
In comparison to traditional synthesis, the cost of one thousand oligos is
US$ 2,000 (CustomArray, Inc.). The price does not scale with the number of
oligos – only with the size of the chip. One oligo costs the exact same as a thou-
sand oligos which costs the same as 12,472 oligos, as they are all made on the same
chip. This means that a full chip of 12,472 is still US$ 2,000 while the equivalent
price with individually synthesised oligos is now over US$ 200,000.
Using MAGE with thousands of target have been shown feasible in Bonde
et al. (2015)49, and the method has been coined MO-MAGE (Microarray-
Oligonucleotide MAGE).
Chip oligo synthesis does come with a few caveats compared to traditional indi-
vidual synthesis: The final product amount is in picomolar range and the final
product is delivered in a single mixed tube, since it is so far not possible to auto-
matically extract desired sequences when cleaving oligos from the chip.

To turn oligos from a chip into oligos useful for MAGE, the first step is to amplify
the primers. This is done over two rounds of PCR, where the first step is quanti-
tative PCR (qPCR) which allows real-time supervision of the PCR process and to
stop the reaction as it leaves the exponential phase.
This done to avoid a bias in which oligos are amplified50. Suppose, for example,
that the designed oligo pool contains more GC than AT pairs, but a small fraction
of the oligos are rich in AT pairs. The GC nucleotides would be used up before
the AT nucleotides, and the AT-rich oligos would now be amplified at a higher
rate than the rest of the subpool.
The qPCR round is followed by a round of normal PCR. This round uses modi-
fied primers to generate DNA fragments that can be turned into the final ssDNA
oligo facing the right direction (see next section on oligo optimisation). In the
forward primers, the first two nucleotides from the 5’ end is phosphorothioated
to prevent degradation by the λ exonuclease enzyme. The 3’ end of the forward
primer contains a deoxy-uracil base which is targeted by the USER enzyme. The
reverse primer is 5’-phosphorylated which has been shown to be favored by λ
exonuclease51.
The modified fragments are then digested with λ exonuclease to turn the dsDNA
fragments into single-stranded DNA fragments. The previously mentioned mod-
ifications to the primers help guide the λ exonuclease to preferentially digest the
undesired strand.
An additional step with USER, DpnII and guide primer excise the barcodes from
the ssDNA oligo fragments and leaves only the core oligo ready for MAGE. DpnII
only cuts double stranded DNA, and a guide primer is therefore used to anneal
to the reverse primer region of the barcoded oligo to create a complete binding
and excision site for DpnII.

2.9 Optimal MAGE Oligos with MODEST
There are a number of parameters that are quite important when designing the
actual oligos for use with MAGE. The most important factors in a host with a
deactivated MMR system according to Wang et al. (2009)33 are:
• Oligo size: Optimal is 70-90 bp
• Strand: Since the oligo imitates an Okazaki fragment, the oligo should be
complementary to lagging strand
• Secondary (2°) structure: The formation of stable hairpin structures in the
oligo can prevent genomic integration. This is avoided by maximising the
minimum free energy (MFE) of the predicted secondary structure.
Creating the oligos manually for MAGE is a fairly simple process: The loci of in-
terest is located, the genetic sequence is extracted, the desired mutation is intro-
duced and which strand is the lagging strand is calculated based on the location
of the oligo, oriC and ter. Less important – but still a good idea – is to predict the
2°structure of the oligo using a DNA/RNA folding software (e.g. ViennaRNA52)
and the oligo is adjusted if the returned MFE is too low.
Nevertheless, creating several thousand oligos in this manner is not feasible. To
automate this process, we created MODEST53 as a tool to quickly create large
numbers of oligos quickly. MODEST can accept custom mutations, but also has
the ability to automatically generate commonly used mutations, such as knock-
outs, residue mutations, and translational expression changes using a custom im-
plementation of the RBS Calculator54. An illustration of the optimisations per-
formed by MODEST can be seen in fig. 2.12.

3.2 Allele Speciﬁc PCR
After MAGE cycling, cells were allowed 1 hour for recovery and then plated on
agar plates for incubation overnight. For each library, single colonies were trans-
ferred to a 96-well plate with milliQ water and were then transferred from the
96-well plate to a square agar plate using a 96-channel pipette. The process flow
is illustrated in fig. 3.2.
Primers were designed with a maximum number of mismatches between the
primer specific to mutants and the sequence of the background strain. 2 primers
had 1 mismatch, 8 primers had 2 mismatches, 24 had 3-4 mismatches and 15 had
5-6 mismatches. Primer sequences are available in table B.2 in appendix.

3.3 Next-generation Sequencing
The next batch of control strains was isolated using a different approach which
utilises next-generation sequencing. The process is outlined in fig. 3.5. Multi-
plexing was done by pooling all the oligos targeting the same locus in one MAGE
experiment.
MAGE products were allowed to recover overnight to make sure MAGE inte-
gration had finished and cells were homozygous before plating (fig. 3.5B). 94
Single colonies were inoculated in 96-well plates with LB broth along with the
MAGE product and background strain as a negative control. Plates were incu-
bated overnight, and then stored at 4 °C (fig. 3.5C).
Six sets of primer sets were designed. One set for each locus, designed to amplify
a 100-120 bp fragment centered on the mutation site. Colony PCR was used to
amplify the target loci individually (fig. 3.5D), and the plates were then pooled
in a new plate, purified (fig. 3.5E), indexed, and sequenced (fig. 3.5F). 96 indexes
were used corresponding to single wells, and the samples were sequenced with
75 bp paired-end reads.
Reads were scanned for mutations with a simple search algorithm and categorised
as background, mutant or ambiguous/error. Out of 558 sample wells (6×93, as
one sample well failed during the sequencing) that made it through sequencing,
419 (75.1 %) were classified as background, 125 (22.5 %) were classified as a muta-
tion and 14 (2.5 %) were classified as ambiguous/error. Of the possible 60 mutants,
52 were found in a well one or more times.

4 CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
EMOPEC was previously developed as a tool to create mutations modulating the
expression level of a native Escherichia coli protein by making changes to the
Shine-Dalgarno sequence in front of the gene, while disturbing the native con-
text as little as possible. To validate the EMOPEC approach, 110 strains were
designed, targeting 11 different native genes in different genomic contexts with
10 evenly spaced expression levels.
98 strains were created in total using MAGE, of which 46 strains were isolated
using a classic allele specific PCR approach, and the remaining 52 were isolated
using a next-generation sequencing approach. The expression levels of proteins in
the mutant strains were measured using proteomics with liquid chromatography
and mass spectrometry.
The performance of EMOPEC was found to be similar to what is considered state
of the art54,57,58 in translation initiation rate prediction.
EMOPEC is a tool for a niche, and can be seen as a subset of the other current tools.
While the current tools predict the context of gene expression such as mRNA fold-
ing and ribosome standby sites, EMOPEC only considers the Shine-Dalgarno se-
quence in the ribosome binding site. EMOPEC is primarily an empirical model,
while the alternative models are primarily biophysical models with some empiri-
cal equations.
Additionally – as a real world test for EMOPEC – a library was designed to mod-
ulate the expression levels of 163 E. coli transcription factors. An attempt was
made to introduce the library into an E. coli strain, but the resulting sequence
data showed no mutations and further work needs to be carried out to create the
strain library.
Once the library is completed, it can be exposed to stressful conditions. Monitor-
ing the change in allele frequency over different conditions could provide insights
into the function of the transcription factors showing the greatest change over
conditions. Specific strains can then be isolated or mutations re-introduced into
the background strain and analysed. The impact of the modifications can then be
examined on a genome scale using an integrative omics approach (see e.g. Cho
et al. (2008)62 and Cho et al. (2014)12).

5 METHODS
5.1 Strains and Media
All experiments were performed using the EcoMA701 as a background strain,
which is an Escherichia coli str. K-12 substr. MG1655 containing the pMA7
plasmid. pMA7 contains ColE1 ori, araC, PARA::beta::dam and bla. λ Red beta
and dam genes are inducible by addition of arabinose to the culture (via araC),
while the cells are ampicillin resistant due to the constitutively expressed bla gene.
NCBI Reference Sequence NC_000913.3 was used as a source for gene annota-
tions and genomic sequence. EcoMA701 was provided by Margith Pedersen.
In all experiments LB (LB-Lennox) media was used containing 10 g/L tryptone,
5 g/L yeast extract and 5 g/L NaCl in demineralised water. LB+amp refers to LB-
Lennox containing 100 mg/L ampicillin.
5.2 Oligo Design
All oligos designed in this study was created using a local version of MODEST53
with a local EMOPEC plugin resulting in a unified pipeline which does the follow-
ing: Find the target gene and extract the CDS (coding sequence) and the 5’-UTR
(untranslated region). If the gene is located on the negative strand, the sequence
is reverse complemented to make sure all sequences are on the same strand and
in the same direction as the target gene.

5.5 Isolation of Individual Controls with asc-PCR
Isolation of Individual Colonies
After finished MAGE cycling, MAGE products were allowed to recover for 1 hour.
Then 100 µL of MAGE product dilutions to 10−1, 10−2, 10−3 were plated on LB+amp
agar plates and incubated at 37 °C overnight. Single colonies were picked from
the agar plate and transferred into a 96-well V-bottom plate containing 150 µL
milliQ water. 5 µL of colony in milliQ were immediately transferred to a square
LB agar plate using a Liquidator 96 Manual Pipetting System (Mettler Toledo).
Primer Design
To separate the mutants from the background strains, allele specific PCR (asc-
PCR) was used. Primers for asc-PCR were designed using a custom script based
on a simple mismatch finding scheme where the goal was to create primers with
maximum mismatches in the 3’ end of the primer were polymerase extension
starts32.
The mutated sequence is aligned (primitive alignment without gaps) to the back-
ground sequence, and mismatches are tallied up. The primer is then generated
by starting at the most 3’ mismatch and extending the primer towards the 5’ end
until the calculated melting temperature reaches 55 °C calculated with the Biopy-
thon63 MeltingTemp module with the thermodynamic values from SantaLucia
and Hicks (2004)64 and the salt correction (50 mM Na+, 1.8 mM Mg2+) from
Owczarzy et al. (2004)65.
asc-PCR
All asc-PCR reactions were carried out under identical conditions except anneal-
ing temperature and extension time. 25 µL reactions were mixed using 12.5 µL
OneTaq Quick-Load Taq 2× Master Mix (New England Biolabs) was mixed with
forward and reverse primer to 0.2 mM each, 5 µL culture directly from stored 96-
well culture plates, and nuclease-free water (IDT) was added in twice the amount
of OneTaq master mix. If the reaction was multiplexed, 0.2 mM of each primer
of each primer set was used32. Extension time was calculated based on longest
product, 1 minute per kilobase.
To get the annealing temperature for a primer, two-step PCR (see appendix A.1)
was used with a melting temperature set to a gradient. Two columns of a PCR
plate was used, with wells prepared with the conditions described above. The first
51
==PAGE:58==
column of wells was using MAGE product to simulate a mutant and the second
column was using the background strain as a template.
Temperature was varied over the rows by setting the thermocycler to heat the
block in a gradient. Initially, the annealing temperature was set to 56-62 °C. If all
the lanes were negative the temperature was lowered and if all lanes were positive
the annealing temperature was increased.
After an accurate melting temperature range capable of distinguishing between
background and mutant was found, asc-PCR reactions were carried out by mixing
OneTaq master mix with primers in a PCR plate and using a 96-channel pipette
to transfer 5 µL template from cells in milliQ water storage plates.
On all asc-PCR plates, MAGE product was used as a positive control which
should always give a signal when probing for mutations, and an EcoMA701 cul-
ture was used a negative control which is expected not to show bands when prob-
ing for mutants.

Finding Mutants from Sequencing Data
Assessing mutants was carried out using a custom Python script which performed
a basic text-search in the reads to classify them according to locus and mutant
status. To speed up processing, a search expression was generated (using regu-
lar expressions) for each target locus. The search expression consisted of 5 bp
upstream up the predicted Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence, 6 nucleotides of any
type and then 5 bp downstream of the SD sequence.
Each read in each well were searched twice, once for the given sequence, and once
for the reverse complemented sequence. If the search expression returned a hit,
the SD sequence would be extracted and classified as background, mutant 1-10,
or other for the locus which returned a hit. No special consideration was given
to the paired-end nature of the reads, each read was processed individually.
After tallying the hits up, wells which counted the mutated or background se-
quence in more than 98 % of the classified reads were considered pure colonies.
If no sequence held more than 98 % of all reads the well was considered erroneous.

Determination of Mutation Frequencies from MiSeq Data
First, a method was created, using Python, which was intended to make use of
the paired-end data. Reads were aligned to E. coli genome (NCBI NC_000913.3)
using bowtie 267, and sorted and indexed using samtools68. PCR fragments were
“predicted” using the designed primers and the E. coli genome. PCR fragments
was then “mutated” in silico.
pysam (https://github.com/pysam-developers/pysam) was used to iterate
the reads by reading each predicted PCR fragment and querying the aligned reads
BAM file for reads in the fragment position. Reads are then categorized as be-
longing to the background strain, a mutation strain or unknown. In overlapping
regions, a read pair is counted as a mutation or background) if either read match
the mutation or background.
Another more naive and slow method was developed as well. Here, a search ex-
pression was created for each mutation by looking for 5 bp of flanking nucleotides
with anything in between. Each read was searched twice (once in normal direc-
tion, once reverse complemented) using the search term and classified as back-
ground, mutation or other.

A GENERIC PROTOCOLS
The following appendix contains laboratory protocols used several times in the
course of this work.
A.1 Two-step PCR Protocol
1. 94 °C at 180 minutes
2. 94 °C for 30 seconds
3. [melting temp] for 30 seconds
4. 68 °C for [elongation time]
5. Go to step 2, 29 additional times
6. 68 °C for 5 minutes
A.2 Touchdown PCR Protocol
1. 94 °C at 5 minutes
2. 94 °C for 30 seconds
3. 64 °C for 30 seconds, decrease temperature by 0.5 °C per round
4. 68 °C for 30 seconds
5. Go to step 2, 9 additional times
6. 94 °C for 30 seconds
7. 54 °C for 30 seconds
8. 68 °C for 30 seconds
9. Go to step 6, 24 additional times
10. 68 °C for 5 minutes
