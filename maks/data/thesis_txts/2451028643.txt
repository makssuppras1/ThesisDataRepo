Summary
The digitization of text, and especially the rapid growth of the amount of text
transmitted through diﬀerent social media platforms, have increased the desire
of multiple researchers and companies to understand human emotions. This has
led to great eagerness for developing several sentiment and emotion methods
to quantify human emotion from text posted in social media. Most of these
sentiment analysis algorithms need labelled datasets to be able to approximate
emotions. Thus, third-party human readers have been widely used to create
them. The aim of this study was to investigate if third-party human readers
are able to reliably quantify the emotions felt by an author when writing a spe-
ciﬁc text. To explore this, we ﬁrst collected an emotion’s labelled dataset of
tweets using a crowdsourcing platform. Secondly, we calculated the discrepancy
between the emotions felt by the author of a tweet and the emotions trans-
mitted to third-party readers. Thirdly, ten relevant sentiment algorithms were
implemented to explore their performance in relation to the emotions felt by the
authors of the text. The results obtained showed evidence about the discrepancy
between the two parties of text, both author and reader, in two diﬀerent scales
of emotions (mDES and polarization). Finally, the performance analysis of the
ten sentiment analysis methods was tested in quantifying the emotions felt by
the author of the text and the emotions transmitted to the third-party readers.
The results obtained arise diﬀerences in the accuracy of results obtained by the
various algorithms in a simple polarization scale of emotion.
Keywords: Emotion analysis, sentiment analysis methods, scales of emotions,
social media, survey crowdsourcing platforms

Chapter 1
Introduction
The digitization of text, and especially the rapid growth of the amount of text
transmitted through diﬀerent social media platforms, have increased the desire
of multiple researchers and companies to understand human emotions. This has
led to great eagerness for developing several sentiment and emotion methods
to quantify human emotion from text posted in social media. Most of these
sentiment analysis algorithms need labelled datasets to be able to approximate
emotions. Thus, third-party human readers have been widely used to create
them using crowdsourcing platforms. The application of this approach to label
emotion-related datasets assumes that third-party human readers are able to
reliably quantify the emotions felt by an author when writing a speciﬁc text.
Thus, the motivation and interest of this research is to verify the truthfulness
of this assumption.
The main goal of this study is to investigate if third-party human readers are
able to reliably quantify the emotions felt by an author at the exact moment of
writing a speciﬁc text. If proven wrong, it would mean that the currently used
sentiment analysis algorithms are designed to predict the emotions transmitted
though the text but not the emotions felt by the authors themselves.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
The starting point of our research was a collected dataset composed by a list
of tweets rated by their authors. To explore our main goal of the research, we
divided our study into three main parts. First, we collected the emotions trans-
mitted to third-party readers of a subset of the tweets of the initial dataset in
the same emotional scale. A crowdsourcing platform was used for the collection
of the data. Secondly, we calculated the discrepancy between the emotions felt
by the author of a tweet and the emotions transmitted to third-party readers.
Diﬀerent scales of emotions, such as mDES and polarization scales, were used
to further analyze this discrepancy between both parties of text. Thirdly, ten of
the most relevant sentiment algorithms were implemented to test and compare
their performance in relation to interpreting the author’s emotions. Diﬀerent
sentiment analysis algorithms, from both lexicon and learning-based approaches
were included in our analysis. Their performance was only analyzed using the
polarization scale thus making it possible to include all ten methods in the
comparison.
The current report exposes all work done during the research. Chapter 2 con-
tains a summary of all the diﬀerent concepts, methods and research related
to sentiment analysis of text. In chapter 3 basic information about the initial
dataset of the project is provided. In chapter 4 the whole process of gathering
the ﬁnal dataset needed to evaluate the hypotesis of the research is explained. In
addition, all data treatment is also included in this chapter. The main structure
of this collected dataset is exposed in chapter 5. In chapter 6 the analysis of the
discrepancy between the emotions felt by the author of a tweet and the emotions
transmitted to the third-party human readers in diﬀerent scales of emotions is
presented. In chapter 7 the analysis of performance of ten relevant sentiment
analysis methods is analyzed in terms of their ability to predict the emotions
felt by the authors of the tweets. Finally, the conclusions, ﬁndings and future
work are summarized in chapter 8.

2.1
Emotions
Emotions are considered the primary motivational system of human beings,
playing an essential role in their everyday life [94]. Humans have an awareness
and some understanding of their own emotions which seems to be more intu-
itive than rational or logical. Understanding human emotions means acquiring
knowledge related to people’s thoughts, perceptions, actions and relationship
with others [47].
Throughout the years vast research related to understanding diﬀerent areas of
emotions has been published. Therefore, emotions are considered a complex
topic[45].
Focusing the research of emotions into linguistics, studies in vari-
ous areas have been developed to explain how humans use language to convey
emotions [88]. For example, studies of aﬀect [19], subjectivity and point of view
[17, 95, 96], evidentiality [28, 15], attitudinal stance [20, 21], modality [25, 69, 74]
and appraisal [57] have been produced. It is documented that people can feel
and express emotions through computer-mediated communication, even if it is
asynchronous and text-based [90].
The way emotions are transmitted through text diﬀer from the way they are
conveyed through face-to-face conversations [104], because body expressions and
voice prosody inﬂuence the recognition of emotions [97, 18].
In these prior
studies, third-party humans were directly asked to quantify the emotions felt
by ﬁrst-party humans. The results of the study were obtained by quantifying
the discrepancy between both parties.
On the other hand, in studies based
on measuring the emotions transmitted through text, third-party humans were

asked about how a piece of text, which could be either a word or a sentence,
makes them feel. They were not asked about the emotions that the author of
the piece of text was feeling or was trying to transmit while writing it. Thus, the
results were obtained from third-party humans, and they were compared to the
rated emotions extracted from reading a word or text, and not from the emotions
that the author was feeling or trying to convey. This ambiguity is widely found
on emotion analysis methods of text between the objective of the tasks and the
methodology executed to obtain the great truth of the diﬀerent methods. That
is why our motivation of the research was to study the discrepancy between the
emotions felt by the authors of the text and the emotions transmitted to the
third-party listeners.
Since contributions to the subject of emotions come from so many diﬀerent
disciplines and due to the complexity of emotion’s quantiﬁcation, it has been
diﬃcult to unify the representation and quantiﬁcation of emotions into a unique
scale. Scientists, as well as non-specialists, agree that there are both positive
and negative emotions [45]. But in psychology research, it is argued that "whilst
positive and negative sentiment are important dimensions, there are many dif-
ferent widely socially-recognized types of emotion, and the strength of emotions
can vary" [93]. Moreover, it has been demonstrated that other forms of more
concrete emotions exist - such as anger, fear, sadness, enjoyment, disgust and
surprise [33, 101].
There are also diﬀerent dimensional models of emotion -
such as valence, arousal and dimension [58], or positive and negative sentiment
can coexist [38] as they are relatively independent in any context. As it can
be realized, diﬀerent scales of emotions and emotions’ strength have been used,
depending on the domain and the conclusion that one wants to extract from the
result. In this project, we have focused on the emotion analysis of the text, so
more details about the diﬀerent scales used to measure emotions into the just
mentioned ﬁeld are given on section 2.4, after introducing the concepts related
to emotion analysis of text.
Later in section 2.4, it is shown that various sentiment analysis methods have
focused their research on sentences between being objective and subjective. Sub-
jectivity is the linguistic expression of showing thoughts, perceptions and inner
states through text [104].
The contrast of subjectivity is objectivity, which
presents events or describes the state of the world without any emotion related.
In [104], it is stated that special emphasis needs to be put on third-personal ﬁc-
tional narrative text to present and transmit characters’ thoughts, perceptions,
and inner states. So, the broadcasting of these emotions through text is not a
trivial problem because many sentences are not explicitly written to transmit
emotions and emotions can vary depending on the context in which the sentence
appears.

2.2
Social media and Twitter
During the last decade and a half, the burgeoning popularity of many social
media platforms, such as Twitter, Facebook or LinkedIn, has been evident. This
boom raised with the proliferation and aﬀordability of Internet-enabled devices
such as personal computers and smart phones [12]. A social media platform is
considered to be any website or application which provides social experience in
the form of user-interactions and allows humans to express themselves quickly
and freely in the context of a wide range of subjects [13].
"Traditionally, consumers used the Internet to simply expend content - they read
it, they watched it, and they used it to buy products and services. Increasingly,
however, consumers are utilizing platforms, such as content sharing sites, blogs,
social networking, and wikis, — to create, modify, share, and discuss Internet
content. This represents the social media phenomenon, which can now signiﬁ-
cantly impact a ﬁrm’s reputation, sales, and even survival." [50]
A rich and diverse list of social media sites, varying in terms of their scope and
functionality exists. Some of them are focused on the general masses, others on
professional networks, media sharing sites, blogs, social news, bookmarking or
conversation sites [50]. What all of them have in common is that they transfer
and contain a tremendous amount of data which can be leveraged for analysis.
Part of these data often convey information about the author’s emotional state,
judgment or evaluation of a speciﬁc person or topic, or the intended emotional
communication [86].
"In threaded discussions, people not only express their opinions on a topic but
also interact with each other. However, the discussions could be highly emotional
and heated with many emotional statements between participants." [55]
In our project, we have chosen Twitter [9] as the social media side of study
as it contains enormous amounts of data. These data are related to diﬀerent

2.3.1
Sentiment analysis algorithms
As we have already introduced, many diﬀerent methods for analyzing sentiment
analysis have been developed. Despite this vast work, it is still unclear which one
works better for identifying the sentiment of a text [88, 13]. Moreover, one of the
latest surveys published [77] related to the task - which performed a benchmark
comparison of twenty-four state-of-the-practice methods using eighteen labelled
datasets - aﬃrmed that:
a) There is no single method that always achieves the best prediction perfor-
mance for all diﬀerent datasets.
b) Existing methods vary widely regarding their agreement, even across sim-
ilar datasets. This aﬃrmation suggested that the same content could be
interpreted very diﬀerently depending on the choice of a sentiment method.
c) Existing methods are more accurate in correctly classifying positive than
negative text, suggesting that current approaches tend to be biased in
their analysis towards positivity.
Furthermore, in [91], it is stated that an important characteristic of sentiment
analysis methods, seen as both an advantage and a drawback, is that they can
be highly topic-dependent. This means that algorithms that predict sentiment
accurately on texts from one speciﬁc domain may be much less accurate on an-
other domain. In the same paper it is also stated that the creation of topic-based
sentiment algorithms can lead to best performance if the negative sentiment is
expressed with sarcasm and irony through personal or topic-related names [91].
Finally, a general agreement in the classiﬁcation of the multiple sentiment anal-
ysis methods into lexicon-based and learning-based approaches exists.
The
lexicon-based methods use a deﬁned gold-standard sentiment dictionary to cal-
culate the sentiment on a feature-level of text and then generalize the sentiment
on sentence or document-level [44]. On the other hand, learning-based algo-
rithms use machine learning techniques, usually supervised classiﬁers, to learn
the sentiment-relevant features of text [88]. Both approaches are explained more
in detail in the following subsections.
2.3.1.1
Lexicon-based algorithms
The fundation of the lexicon-based algorithms is the sentiment lexicon. A senti-
ment lexicon is deﬁned as a list of lexical features, usually words, with a known
==PAGE:26==
10
Sentiment analysis and text: An overview
speciﬁc related sentiment label [88]. It is used as a gold-standard sentiment dic-
tionary and it has been developed for calculating sentiment in diﬀerent scales
[44]. Some of the most well-known lexicons - LIWC [73], GI [87], VADER [44]
- contain words categorized into sentiment polarization.
In others - ANEW
[23], SentiWordNet [34] and SenticNet [26] -, the words have been labeled with
average happiness scores for sentiment intensity.
According to [44], a comprehensive, high-quality lexicon is essential for obtaining
a fast and accurate sentiment analysis on social media text. But the creation
of such sentiment lexicons is time and resource consuming. That is probably
why not many of them exist and much of new research relies heavily on using
preexisting manually constructed lexicons [44] most of which are domain-based
[91]. Each sentiment lexicon has been created with the use of diﬀerent strategies.
While some have been created manually from a development corpus, others have
been built semi-automatically, by applying heuristics, such as the identiﬁcation
of synonyms, to extend the list of words that they contain [91].
Some of these lexicon-based algorithms have also introduced heuristics [44, 6]
in conjunction with the sentiment lexicons, to improve sentiment analysis per-
formance. These heuristics are usually syntax- or grammar-related rules that
help to understand more in-depth the lexical properties, thus including, more
context awareness on sentences [44].
To sum up, the methods listed above start with a collection of terms with known
sentiment associations and then apply a set of rules to predict the sentiment of
texts based on the occurrence of the words contained in the dictionary [91].
According to [44], the three general shortcomings of lexicon-based sentiment
analysis algorithms are:
1. They have trouble with coverage as most of them do not include new
lexical features arisen from social media text. Examples of this new lexical
features include the usage of emojis, abbreviations or repetition of letters.
2. Some lexicon-based methods do not take into account semantically fea-
tures of sentences, which can modify the sentiment extracted from a spe-
ciﬁc word. For example, the usage of a word in conjunction of other nega-
tion or modiﬁers ones can substantially vary or even reverse the sentiment
of one sentence.
3. The acquisition or extension of a sentiment lexicon can be very laborious
and time-consuming.

2.3.1.2
Learning-based algorithms
Learning-based algorithms use machine learning techniques, usually supervised
classiﬁers, to learn the sentiment relevant features of text. These methods use
supervised learning to create a classiﬁer which is built thanks to labelled datasets
[88]. During the training process, the classiﬁer learns diﬀerent characteristics
that allow to distinguish the polarization or sentiment score of text.
An extensive list of existing supervised learning methods can be applied as a
classiﬁer to sentiment [55]. Within the most relevant ones, there are typical ma-
chine learning classiﬁers - such as naive Bayesian classiﬁcation [72] or support
vector machines [72], and more complex deep learning approaches, such as mod-
els of Long Short-Term Memory [35] or Recursive Neural Tensor Network [85].
These methods usually start with a collection of texts that have been annotated
by human coders for sentiment. Then, they learn text features that associate
with positive or negative sentiment. The text features learnt are then used to
predict the sentiment of new texts [91].
The creation of such an initial collection of text, which ends up being labelled
datasets, is also time and resource expensive. That is why some methods have
opted to use distant supervision for the creation of training datasets. According
to [89], by using distant supervision, "the methods can obtain massive noisy-
labelled tweets selected by positive and negative emotions as training set and
build sentiment classiﬁers directly". Some forms used as distant supervision in
sentiment analysis of social media data include emoticons [89, 35] and speciﬁc
hashtags [61].
The performance of learning-based algorithms is heavily dependent on the choice
of feature representation of text [89]. How to learn representations of text is an
open area of research [75]. There is a tendency to focus on designing eﬀective
features to obtain better classiﬁcation performance [72, 51]. In learning-based
sentiment classiﬁcation methods, this tendency is based on learning dense, low
dimensional and real-valued vectors to represent a word - also known as word
embedding [89, 59, 68, 53]. The main disadvantage of this approach is that it
can extract non-sentiment features that associate with sentiment because they
are frequently used in sentences of a special polarity. As an example, it can
happen that neutral sentences change its polarity just because the name of a
politician appears on it [91].

According to [44], there are three shortcomings [44] of learning-based sentiment
analysis algorithms:
1. Labelled training data is not often easy to acquire and a large amount is
needed to obtain reliable results.
2. Finding features in short text is a diﬃcult task.
3. They are usually more computationally expensive in terms of CPU pro-

2.4
Dimensions of emotions
As we already introduced in section 2.1, there are diﬀerent dimensional models
in which emotions can be represented and quantiﬁed. In this section, we are
going to list the most representative ones used for diﬀerent sentiment analysis
methods.
1. Binary Subjectivity classiﬁcation
[ Objective / Subjective ]
Initially, several algorithms opted to ﬁrst detect the subjectivity and ob-
jectivity of the portion of text [78, 103, 106]. Subjectivity is the linguistic
expression of showing thoughts, perceptions and inner states through text
[104]. The contrast of subjectivity is objectivity, which presents events or
describes the state of the world without any emotion related.
2. Binary polarization
[ Positive / Negative ]
Once the subjectivity sentences are detected, the sentiment for the subjec-
tivity sentences can be classiﬁed into positive or negative. This approach
was hardly replaced for trinary classiﬁcation of emotions [108].
3.
Ternary polarization
[ Positive / Negative / Neutral ]
It is the most general classiﬁcation of emotions and probably also the most
used one, especially for classiﬁcation of opinions. Generally, the neutral
dimension reﬂects the absence of subjectivity.

4. Five-level classiﬁcation
[ Very Negative / Negative / Neutral / Positive / Very Positive]
The ternary polarization is extended. In this new scale, both positive and
negative dimensions are subdivided into two diﬀerent sub-levels. Thus,
the sentiment is classiﬁed into ﬁve diﬀerent levels.
5. Normalized Sentiment Strength
[ -1 , 1 ]
Many applications would beneﬁt from being able to determine not just the
binary polarity, but also the strength of the sentiment expressed in text
[44].
The strength is represented with a unique continuous score normalized
between -1 and 1, with 0 as the neutral state [44].
6. Dual Scale
[ Positive score + Negative score ]
It is known that both positive and negative sentiment can co-occur in
several extreme situations [37]. So, the text can also contain a mix of
positive and negative sentiment and for some applications, it is necessary
to detect them both simultaneously. In these solutions, the strength of the
sentiment in both dimensions is expressed [93]. There are diﬀerent scales
in which this strength of the sentiment is represented.
(a) Five-level scale
[ 1 / 2 / 3 / 4 / 5 ]
The strength of both positive and negative sentiment in texts is si-
multaneously calculated on a 5-level scale. From 1 (no [dimension]
sentiment) to 5 (strong [dimension] sentiment).
(b) Normalized Sentiment Score
[ 0 , 10 ]
The strength of both positive and negative sentiment in texts is si-
multaneously calculated on a normalized score between 0 and 10.
7. Semantic Diﬀerential Scale
[ Valence , Arousal , Dominance ]
The emotion is quantiﬁed in a 9-point scale in terms of valence, arousal
and dominance. The neutral dimension is represented by the midpoint
(5).
• Valence : from unpleasant (1) to pleasant (9)
• Arousal : from calm (1) to excited (9)

The process of obtaining emotion-rated datasets of text is known to be time and
resource expensive. According to [44], manually annotating a comprehensive

are related to personality or past experiences for each person [13]. The more
concrete the emotions need to be labelled, the more complicated it is. As a
consequence, not many annotated datasets on a 5-point scale of emotions exist.
For our data collection, thoroughly explained in chapter 4, we need to obtain
the emotions perceived by a third-party human from a list of tweets. In other
words, we need to acquire a dataset of tweets with their emotions rated by
external raters. To be able to choose the best method for executing this task,
we have studied how others have done similar tasks before.

2.7.1.1
Design pattern
Django’s main goal was to make it easy to make changes to one particular
piece of the application without aﬀecting the other parts, as the components are
loosely coupled. That is why it was designed following the Model-View-Template
(MVT) pattern to achieve strict separation between pieces of an application.
MVT is a modiﬁed version of the Model-View-Controller (MVC) pattern of
software architecture [43]. In this initial pattern, Model referred to the data
access layer, View to the part of the system that selects what to display and
how to display it, and Controller to the part of the system that regulated the
two previous elements by deciding which view to use, depending on user input,
accessing the model as needed. In the case of Django, most of the Controller
functions are already handled themselved by the framework. That is why the
new pattern has been deﬁned.
In the MVT pattern,
• The Model refers to the data access layer. This layer contains everything
about the data: how to access it, how to validate it, which behaviors it
has, and the relationships between the data.
• The Template refers to the presentation layer. This layer contains presentation-
related decisions: how something should be displayed on a web page.
• The View refers to the business logic layer. This layer contains the logic
that accesses the model and defers to the appropriate template(s). You
can think of it as the bridge between models and templates.
To be able to use the Django framework, all the logic created ad-hoc for our
website has been developed on three Python ﬁles and diﬀerent HTML templates,
CSS and Javascript ﬁles:

contains deﬁnition and description of the database tables.
Thanks to each representation, we have been able to create, retrieve, up-
date and delete records in the database using Python rather than writing
repetitive SQL statements.
• views.py contains all the logic for the diﬀerent pages of the website. Each
view deﬁned receives information related to the request on the website and
return the layout to render based on the request and the logic developed
for each view.

Chapter 4
Data collection process
As explained in the introduction, our ﬁrst goal of the research is to quantify the
discrepancy between the emotions felt by the author of a text and the emotions
transmitted to a third-party reader.
As shown in the previous section, the
data related to the emotions felt by the authors when writing tweets is already
collected. To be able to fully analyze the discrepancy between both parties of
text, we now need to acquire the third-party emotions for each tweet already
rated by its author. It means that we need to collect emotion-related data from
several readers of tweets already rated for the authors, to study the emotion
transmitted on a written text related to the original text. This way, we will be
able to deeply study the bias between the emotions felt between the ﬁrst and
the third parties of a tweet.
In this chapter, we explain the whole process that we needed to develop in order
to collect the emotions perceived by the third-party raters from a list of tweets.
We have divided the chapter into four diﬀerent sections. The ﬁrst one explains
the general view of the schema created to obtain the mentioned emotions for
each tweet. Furthermore, the three other sections show the main blocks imple-
mented to achieve it: Tweets’ preprocessing, Website implementation, Amazon
Mechanical Turk implementation.

4.1
Emotion rating gathering schema
After analyzing the diﬀerent methods and processes developed on other re-
searches to acquire data related to sentiment from text, the main point that
we had clear in mind was to follow a procedure and scale as similar as possible
to the one used to acquire the already-collected emotions felt by the author of
each tweet. For this porpuse, we had to discard all applied ordering classiﬁca-
tion algorithms as annotation schemes - such as MaxDiﬀor Best–Worst Scaling,
even though they are a widely utilized method for classifying the intensity of
the tweets. Thus, our annotation scheme should be a sequential schema of all
20 emotional dimensions annotated on a 5-point scale each.
Having the annotation scheme decided, we needed to determine the procedure
in which we would obtain the ratings.
As we already stated in section 2.5,
it is proved that in sentiment analysis environment, Crowdsourcing platforms
can achieve similar quality-results to experts. Therefore, for time and resources
limitations, we decided to use one of these Crowdsourcing platforms to obtain
our desired information. Then, we decided to utilize the Amazon Mechanical
Turk platform for collecting the rating due to simplicity and previous experience
of the collaborators of the project using it. Instead, we put special attention on
how to achieve the quality of the responses of the diﬀerent AMT workers. That
is why we thought about the possibility to use various quality control tests or
psychological tests just to let qualiﬁed workers rate our tweets. Unfortunately,
all examples of diﬀerent control or psychological tests were related to accept or
discard workers in relation to how similar they thought about an already set
topic. With this solution, we detected the possibility of obtaining biased results
to a speciﬁc way of thinking or feeling.
Finally, because emotion is quite a
subjective ﬁeld, we discarded the option to use these quality control tests, as we
did not want to end up having biased results skipping and/or accepting workers
based on their speciﬁc way of thinking.
To summarize, we use Amazon Mechanical Turk to collect the emotional rat-
ings of the list of tweets from third-party raters. In order to follow the same
annotation schema as the one used for gathering the emotional information of
the authors of the tweet, we have used an external website to show the infor-
mation of the diﬀerent tweets, allowing us to rate each tweet in the 20 diﬀerent
emotional dimensions. Furthermore, a previous and additional task has also
been implemented - following the same schema - to obtain tweets that are fully
understandable in English. Figure 4.1 shows the general schema that we have
implemented to collect the desired data. More details about the tasks and the

In order to reduce costs on the Language task designed, diﬀerent concepts of

We have created the function mostly_english to check if a tweet is mainly writ-
ten in English. The function needs a tokenized list of words and a dictionary
of English words to deﬁne each tweet. A dictionary of 109584 diﬀerent English
words has been used. It contained contractions, which are widely used on social
media text and all words were written in the under-case form.
The developed mostly_english function follows the next algorithm to classify
the tweet as English, Non-English or doubt.
1. Check if the English dictionary is a list of words and it is not empty
2. Iterate through the given list of tokenized words of a tweet to check if each
word matches any pattern
a) The word is an emoji
b) The word is ascii
c) The word is formed by less than two letters
d) The word is a punctuation word
e) The word is a special token
f) The word is a Twitter user or a hashtag
g) The word is a link
h) Otherwise, the word is considered a ’valid’ word
i) The word is also an English word within the given English dictionary,
then also count it as an English word
3. Classify the tweet according to its words
a) If more than the 50% of the tweet is written with non-ascii words
(skipping emojis), it is considered as a Non-English tweet.
b) If all the words of the tweets are English words, it is considered as
an English tweet.
c) If the tweet is formed with more than 5 ’valid’ words and more than
70% of those are in English, it is considered as an English tweet.

d) The rest of the tweets are considered as doubt.
Within this group, there are tweets formed just by links, links with
emojis, just emojis, retweets, tweets with non-valid words and tweets
with valid words but that cannot be determined as English for the
given dictionary.
Multiple alteration of this algorithm have been made to ﬁnally came up to the
most accurate solution, in terms of classiﬁcation and reduction of the number

6.1
Consolidation of the annotations
To be able to continue our analysis, we ﬁrst need to consolidate the three ratings
of the diﬀerent dimensions of emotions of the various AMT workers. To be able
to do it, we initially implemented two diﬀerent approaches to notice the main
diﬀerences between them in order to decide which one works better for our data.
Approach 1:
During the creation of the dataset used on SemEval 2016
[64], they used a two-step procedure to consolidate the annotations given
by 5 diﬀerent workers also on a ﬁve-point rating scale.
This two-step
procedure consisted in:
1. If three out of the ﬁve annotators agreed on a label, they accepted
the label.
2. Otherwise they mapped the categorical labels to integer values [-2,-
1,0,1,2]. Then, they calculated the average and ﬁnally they mapped
that average to the closest integer value.
Approach 2:
During the creation of the gold emotion standard of the
SentiStrength algorithm [92], they collected the sentiment of comments on
a ﬁve-point rating scale from diﬀerent coders. To consolidate the results
given for the diﬀerent coders, they calculated the average value of the
coders’ results for each comment and rounded it to the closest integer.
After calculating the consolidated value for both approaches as explained, we
started by calculating the Spearman’s correlation [42] between the data obtained
in both approaches. The results can be found on Appendix A.4.2. With the
results retrieved, none of the dimensions have a coeﬃcient lower than 0.65 but
they are not very close to 1 neither. This means that the approach chosen will
have an impact to the ﬁnal results.
Furthermore, we show the distributions of both solutions for each of the 20
emotion dimensions to see how diﬀerent the diﬀerent approaches behave. The
results can be found on Appendix A.5.1. The main diﬀerence that we can see
with the distributions are related to the annotations of lower intensity [1, 2] of
the dimensions of the emotions. We can realise with the plots of distributions
that Approach 1 tends to reduce the intensity of the emotions, setting more
times to 1 the intensity of the diﬀerent dimensions of emotion. While approach
2, as it does not discard values, tends to send part of this low intensity values
to 2 instead of 1. However, no much diﬀerence can be appreciated with the
annotations of neutral or higher intensity [3, 4, 5].

7.1.1.1
Aﬀective Norms for English Words (ANEW)
The Aﬀective Norms for English Words (ANEW) [23] is a lexicon dictionary
with large number of words in the English language. The main goal was to
provide available standardized materials to study emotion and attention across
diﬀerent investigations.
Following Osgood’s view [67], the emotion of each word was deﬁned as a coin-
cidence of values on three major dimensional views; valence, arousal and domi-
nance. To asses this three dimensions, diﬀerent groups of raters, all Introductory
Psychology class students, were asked to complete the Self-Assessment Manikin

were asked to rate how they felt while reading each word.
SAM is an aﬀective rating system that use graphic ﬁgures to comprise bipolar
scales to represent the diﬀerent values along the emotional dimensions. And
the Semantic Diﬀerential Scale is a 9-point scale. In other words, the emotion
of each word within the ANEW’s dictionary is quantiﬁed in a 9-point scale in
terms of valence, arousal and dominance. To acquire the ratings of the ANEW’s
dictionary, 5 emotion’s strength (1,3,5,7,9) of the scale were represented with
ﬁgures. The following scales with ﬁgures were used:
• Valence: From pleasant to unpleasant represented from a smiling, happy
ﬁgure to a frowning, unhappy ﬁgure.

7.1.1.4
Valence Aware Dictionary for Sentiment Reasoning (VADER)
The Valence Aware Dictionary for Sentiment Reasoning (VADER) [44] is a sen-
timent analysis engine based on a gold standard sentiment lexicon, which allows
to express both polarity and intensity of sentiments expressed in social media
text. VADER also includes ﬁve general rules, that processing syntactical and
grammatical conventions, help to optimize the sentiment intensity in text.
The VADER engine is based on a gold standard sentiment dictionary which
contains over 7500 lexical words with their related valence score. This valence
score is deﬁned in a scale from -4 (Extremely Negative) to 4 (Extremely Pos-
itive). This scale allows to show for each word the sentiment polarity (posi-
tive/negative) and the sentiment intensity on a scale from -4 to 4.
The creation of the dictionary of words was started from a list of words based on
other well-established sentiment dictionaries of words, such as LIWC, ANEW
and GI [87].
In addition, other common lexical features common in social-
media text were also added to the list. This lexical features were created by
emoticons, sentiment-related acronyms and slangs.
The sentiment scores of
each of the elements of the dictionary were obtained by asking humans to rate
the sentiment valence of the words in the Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT)
platform.
The rule-based model include the following heuristics to the engine to optimize
the sentiment intensity of the text based on grammatical and syntactical cues.

7.1.1.5
SentiStrength
SentiStrength [93, 92] is a sentiment classiﬁer that uses additional linguistic
information to simultaneously calculate positive and negative sentiment strength
in short informal text.
For each text, it extracts a positive and a negative
sentiment strength on a 5 point scale. SentiStrength was created and evaluated
through diﬀerent sets of MySpace comments and other types of text. It was
specially developed to decrease the scarcity of other sentiment methods with
the new characteristics provided by informal text (e.g. abbreviations, emoticons
and truncated sentences).
The core of the method is a dictionary of sentiment words with associated
strength measures. The dictionary includes 2310 terms classiﬁed for either pos-
itive or negative sentiment strength with a value from 2 to 5. The word list
contain stems, standard English words and non-standard but common words in
social media.
The initial version of the dictionary was created using human judgements and
it was ﬁne-tuned later to optimize the sentiment strength of words in MySpace

labels for 215.154 sliced phrases in the parse trees of 11.855 sentences from
movie reviews collected by Pang and Lee (2005) [70]. The sliced sentences were
labeled into 25 diﬀerent levels of sentiment (from very negative to very positive)
using Amazon Mechanical Turk platform. The picture below these lines show

representations of words. Then, SSWE can be used as features in a supervised
learning framework for Twitter sentiment classiﬁcation.
For obtaining the continuous representations of words, three diﬀerent neural
networks are developed to eﬀectively represent the sentiment of text [89]. We
will just focus on one of this neural networks developed (SSWEu), as it is

SSWEu was developed to capture, both the syntactic context of words as well
as the sentiment information of sentences.

7.1.2.3
OpenAI Sentiment Neuron
The OpenAI sentiment Neuron [75] was discovered while training a model to
predict the next character in the text of Amazon reviews. The authors state
that [75], "While training the linear model with L1 regularization, we noticed it
used surprisingly few of the learned units. Digging in, we realized there actually
existed a single “sentiment neuron” that’s highly predictive of the sentiment
value."
The model is based on a single layer multiplicative LSTM (mLSTM) with 4,096
units.
These 4,096 units, that are just a vector of ﬂoats, can be considered
feature vectors representing the string read by the model. After training the
mLSTM, the model was turned into a sentiment classiﬁer by taking a linear
combination of these units. The output of the model is then a value between -1
and 1, which represents the normalized sentiment strength of the input text.

7.1.2.4
DeepMoji
DeepMoji is a model that uses a variant of the Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM) model that has extended the distant supervision to a more diverse
set of emotional labels. The main structure of the model is showed below in
Figure 7.6.
Figure 7.6: Structure of the deepMoji model. Source: [35]
The DeepMoji model projects each input word into a vector space using an
embedding layer. A hyperbolic tangent activation function is used to enforce
each embedding dimension to be within [1, 1]. Then, two bidirectional LSTM
layers with 1024 hidden units in each are used to capture the context of each
word. Moreover, an attention layer that take all of these layers as input using
skip-connections is used. This last attention layer help the model to deﬁne the
importance of each word for the prediction task and generates a representation
of the text. This representation vector obtained from the attention layer is a
high-level encoding of the entire text, which is used as input to the ﬁnal Softmax
layer for classiﬁcation.

7.4.1
Uniﬁcation of the dimensions
Various dimensions of emotion are used in the 10 sentiment methods used for
our analysis. In this section we deﬁne the transformations applied on the various
scales so that we can correctly compare the sentiments predicted for the methods
and the gold standard sentiment values given by the authors of each tweet.
The simplest representation form of the emotion used on the distinct methods is
the ternary polarization. For this reason, we have transformed the other scales
into a ternary polarization as follows:
• Semantic Diﬀerential Scale: As explained in the Hedonometrics implemen-
tation [31], the valence score represents the dimension from unpleasant to
pleasant or, in other words, from sad to happy. Then, following the same
approach that was used, we consider that,
– If the average valence score of the tweet is lower than 5, then the
tweet is considered to have a negative polarization.
– If the average valence score of the tweet is higher than 5, then the
tweet is considered to have a positive polarization.
– If the average valence score of the tweet is exactly 5, then the tweet
is considered to have a neutral polarization.
Distinct values of thresholds to the value 5 for classifying neutral tweets
have been added, but none of them have improved the performance of
the results of the methods. So that, we have left the exact score of 5 to
consider a tweet as neutral.
• Dual Scale: In all diﬀerent types of dual scales, we have followed the
approach used in [93] to transform the dual scale into the ternary one.
For converting it, the subtract of the negative score to the positive score
has been used. Then, the ﬁnal polarization has been decided as follows,
– If the subtracted calculated value is positive, then the tweet is con-
sidered to have a positive polarization.
==PAGE:111==
7.4 Results of sentiment detection
95
– If the subtracted calculated value is negative, then the tweet is con-
sidered to have a negative polarization.
– If the subtracted calculated value is exactly 0, then the tweet is con-
sidered to have a neutral polarization.
• Normalized Sentiment Strength: According to [44], the sentiment polarity
is presented with a numeric value on a continuous scale ranging from -1
to 1. Then, the polarization can be represented also as,
– If the continuous score is positive, then the tweet is considered to
have a positive polarization.
– If the continuous score is negative, then the tweet is considered to
have a negative polarization.
– If the continuous score is exactly 0, then the tweet is considered to
have a neutral polarization.
• Five-level classiﬁcation: As the names of the diﬀerent levels indicate, the
ﬁnal polarization of the tweets can be calculated as,
– If the sentiment level is ’very positive’ or ’positive’, then the tweet is
considered to have a positive polarization.
– If the sentiment level is ’very negative’ or ’negative’, then the tweet
is considered to have a negative polarization.
– If the sentiment level is ’neutral’, then the tweet is considered to have
a neutral polarization.
• Modiﬁed Diﬀerential Emotions Scale (mDES): the procedure followed for
collapsing the diﬀerent dimensions of the mDES scale into a polarization
classiﬁcation, is already explained in section 6.3.

Discussion of sentiment detection
In this chapter, we have exposed the results obtained from the implementation of
the most relevant sentiment analysis methods. We have used those to investigate
the ability of the diﬀerent algorithms to quantify the emotions felt by the authors
of the tweets.
As already explained in section 7.4.1, we have ﬁrst uniﬁed all the results obtained
from the distinct methods to the same unique scale of emotion. This uniﬁcation
has been necessary to properly compare the results of all sentiment analysis
methods. In our case, we have started with the simplest polarization scale, as it
has been the only one that allowed us to compare all 10 implemented sentiment
analysis methods.
The general performance of the sentiment analysis algorithms in predicting the
emotions analysis felt by the authors of the tweets is quite low.
Afterwards, we have calculated how well all 10 sentiment analysis methods quan-
tify the emotions felt by the authors of in the polarization scale. The results
shown in the table 7.8 indicate that DeepMoji is the top-performance method

OpenAI and LabMT. Even though the results indicate that these three are the
best algorithms for calculating the emotions felt by the authors of the tweets,
we can realize that the performance obtained is not really high. Just DeepMoji
achieves a performance higher than 75%. On the other hand, the worst ones in

By analyzing the confusion matrices of the algorithms, it can be seen that those

The accuracy is higher for all the methods when they predict the emotions trans-

Furthermore, we have then compared the results explained above with how well
sentiment methods are able to quantify the emotions felt by the authors of the
tweets. The results of this comparison are shown in 7.9. By analyzing the re-
sults, we see that the values of accuracy are higher for all the methods when
they predict the emotions transmitted to the third-party raters. This diﬀerence
of accuracy retrieved for the two approaches can reﬂect the discrepancy quan-
tiﬁed in chapter 6.2. In [13], it has been explained that most of the diﬀerent
methods were initially created from detecting the opinions of the text. This
could be the reason why various sentiment analysis methods perform better in
quantifying the emotions transmitted through the text instead of the emotions
felt by the authors of the text. But, ﬁnally, they are either reused, or created
with the same approach as the old algorithms for detecting the sentiment felt
by the author.
Further work needs to be done to demonstrate if the discrepancy found would
have any eﬀect on the results of sentiment analysis methods using other scales
of emotions.
In this case, we have compared the results in the polarization scale. During
the previous chapter 6.2, we have demonstrated that discrepancy between the
emotions felt by the author of a tweet and the emotions transmitted to a third-
party human reader exist. More concretely, this discrepancy calculated depends
on the emotion scale that is used. For the polarization scale, this discrepancy
exists but it is not very high. The results summarized in table 7.9 can be the
ﬁrst evidence that the bigger the discrepancy between the emotions felt and
transmitted between the two parties is, the worse the current methods devel-
oped for sentiment analysis will predict the emotions felt by the authors of a
tweet. Consequently, it could be believed that the quantiﬁcation of this dis-
crepancy has to be considered more when developing the sentiment algorithms

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The current report describes all research and work performed to investigate if
third-party human readers are able to reliably quantify the emotions felt by an
author at the exact moment of writing a speciﬁc text. Our research has been
particularly focused in analysing it in short text, such as tweets.
The main ﬁnding of our research is that a discrepancy between the emotions
felt by the author of a text and the emotions transmitted to a third-party hu-
man reader exists. This discrepancy is specially signiﬁcant for concrete negative
emotions with diﬀerent intensities. In addition, it has also been discovered that
the majority of the most relevant sentiment analysis methods perform better
in quantifying the polarization of emotions transmitted to third-party human
readers than the emotions felt by the author of the tweets. These ﬁndings sup-
port the fact that current sentiment analysis methods are better at predicting
the emotions transmitted from the text in a polarization scale than the emo-
tions felt by the author of the tweet. This is probably a heritage of the initial
functionality of abstracting the opinion from the text. Our research has thus
proven that sentiment analysis methods need some reﬁnement to improve their
ability of quantifying the emotions felt by the authors of the tweets. In fact,
this reﬁnement might be related to the methods used for labeling gold standard
datasets.

Conclusion
To be able to succeed in the procedure of verifying our hypothesis, it was essen-
tial to dedicate a long period of time to the design of the data collection. Indeed,
big part of the research was concentrated in developing all the steps needed to
obtain the labelled data for our analysis. For this reason, it was experienced
that the creation of labeled data related to emotion is a time and resource ex-
pensive task. A lot of work was put in consolidating the initial given data to
be able to create concrete and eﬀective tasks in the crowdsourcing platform.
Furthermore, some discussion was introduced to deﬁne the correct questions for
gathering the exactly information needed for our analysis. The main constraint
of our data collection development was to depend on external data to render
the information of the tweets on the website. This factor restricted the total
number of ratings to be gathered within a period of time and considerably pro-
longed the time invested in obtaining short amounts of tweets. Regarding the
ﬁnal data collected, it was veritable surprising to observe how in many cases
three diﬀerent third-party human raters reported considerably diﬀerent ratings
for the emotions of the same tweet.
In relation to the quantiﬁcation of the discrepancy between the emotions felt
by the author of a text and the emotions transmitted to a third-party human
reader, it was interesting to realize that this discrepancy variates when diﬀerent
scales of emotion are used. The results obtained exposed that it was specially
challenging for the third-party raters to quantify exactly the same strength of an
emotion dimension as the author of the tweets. That is why the more concrete
the dimensions of emotions of the tweet, the larger the discrepancy between the
emotions felt by the authors and the transmitted ones to the third-party readers
are.
Finally, the most challenging part of the implementation of the research was to
understand and develop all 10 most relevant algorithms due to their diﬀerences
of approach. Each of them use variate and concrete concepts related to sentiment
analysis natural language processing, machine learning and deep learning. In
addition, some research was also needed for the uniﬁcation of the dimensions of
emotion.
The research left space for future improvements and work to be developed. First
of all, the results should be veriﬁed with a larger sample of tweets. Secondly, the
results gathered though the data collection were considered acceptable due to the
quality constraints already included in the crowdsourcing platform. Despite the
fact that no analysis of the detection of inconsistent ratings was included in the
report, it is believed that further work in detecting anomalous ratings could be
performed in the future. Thirdly, more detailed analysis can be developed using
non-supervised learning techniques, such as clustering, to detect patterns or
groups of tweets that tend to be well- or miss-classiﬁed for the third-party human
readers.
Lastly, other scales of emotions using subsets of sentiment analysis
==PAGE:119==
103
methods should be used to further inspect the bias of these methods with regards
to the quantiﬁcation of the emotions felt by the users of tweets’s users. The
research also opens possibilities to go further in the following aspects - the
development of a new sentiment analysis algorithm which is particularly focused
on retrieving the emotions felt by the author of the text; or applying some
reﬁnement to the current sentiment analysis algorithms, with a main objective
of calculating the emotions felt by the authors of the tweet and not to detect
the opinions of the text.
