Abstract
Developments in the market of bridge building have made great strides in terms of
efficiency, since numerical calculations of structures have become ever prevalent. This
efficiency enables large-scale bridges to cross even greater distances, often at the expense
of bridge deck weight and size. Increasingly narrow and light bridges carry more risk
in terms of wind exposure, making it necessary to provide external damping, to guard
against both extreme cases, as well as long term wear and tear caused by minor structural
movements.
When exposed to wind the actual hazards lie not in the force of the wind itself, but
rather its ability to trigger modes in the bridge, making it oscillate forcefully up to the
point of total structural failure. Of special note is the phenomenon ’flutter’, which is a
combination of heaving- and rotational mode shapes, due to its proven history of danger,
as seen in the Tacoma Narrows incident. Since live scale testing is somewhat costly, and
collapsed bridges constitute a PR nightmare, extensive numerical testing is not only
applied in construction but also in damage prevention.
Current prominent methods of providing damping include viscous- and tuned mass
dampers (TMD), both having been successfully utilized in several projects. However,
more recently a lot of work has been done on the development of electromagnetic res-
onant shunt dampers (ERSD), which functions in a similar manner to the TMD, but
instead of adding extra mass, it simulates it through the interaction between a magnet
and coil connected to a shunt.
The aim of this thesis is then to create a model capable of simulating the effects of
wind loading as well as external damping in three steps.
First, a dynamic model is made using the finite element method (FEM), recreating
the entirety of the bridge in individual 1D elements to represent a 3D model.
Second, aeroelastic theory is applied in order to assess the degree to which the bridge
is in danger of experiencing hazardous modes.
Third, several dampers are described and implemented into the model, creating and
examining the dampening effect, which is necessary in the case of flutter.
Lastly, this is a very complex model, with many moving parts. In order to increase
computational efficiency significantly system reduction is applied to the model. This is
done using quasi-static condensation.

Preface
This master thesis titled ”Damping of Large-scale Bridge Vibrations” was prepared at
the department of Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The
thesis meets the requirements for acquiring a Master of Science in Engineering degree
in Civil engineering. Supervisor on the project was Jan Becker Høgsberg.
The scope of this thesis is the design and numerical analysis of damping in large-scale
bridges, using well tested systems, in the form of viscous damping and tuned mass damp-
ing, as well as the newer electromagnetic resonant shunt damper, in order to compare
their effects and feasibilities.

This thesis is aimed at readers with a functional knowledge of the Finite Element Method
(FEM) of numerical calculation for large systems, along with a basic level of knowledge
in the field of structural dynamics.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis aims to examine the different types of damping on a large scale structure,
with a special interest in comparing the resulting effect of the different types of dampers.
The construction in question is a slender, long span suspension bridge with a narrow
deck. These types of constructions a especially susceptible to wind and as such, there
will be a need to improve the aerodynamic stability, especially in relation to flutter, as
this is a known hazard.
The current longest completed suspension bridge in the world is Akashi Kaikyō
Bridge in Japan with a main span length of 1,991m. But with each successive record
breaking span, attention fixates on the next unconquered strait or uncrossed water, and
as such we should be ready to face the challenges they entail. The aerodynamic stability
of these giant structures is paramount to the safety of their use, but luckily the solutions
are already known, they just need implementation.
1.1
Thesis overview
In Chapter 2 all theory related to the construction is presented. First the bridge construc-
tion is described and the process of transforming it to a finite element model, capturing
the structural motions, is described in detail. From this several system matrices will be
constructed.
In Chapter 3 the theory behind aeroelastic effects are laid out and the implementation
is discussed. Of special interest is the continuous relationship between the aerodynamic
memory forces and its effect on the bridge. In order to calculate the modes that result
from this, a state-space formulation is made.
The chapter concludes with a flutter
analysis trying to ascertain the critical external wind speed that are able to trigger
flutter effects.
To greatly save computational time the system of equations is reduced. This process
is described in details in Chapter 4.
The reduction that is employed is quasi-static
condensation, which applies master-slave relations forming quasi-static relations between
active and passive parts of the bridge.
In Chapter 5 all theory related to the applied dampers is presented. The damper
implementation procedure and their effects are studied and discussed in Chapter 6. Fi-
nally the most important finding of the project is summed up in the concluding chapter
7.

This project considers only the center span of the suspension bridge, i.e. the part
between the two pylons (see Figure 2.1). This bridge section should be the most vul-
nerable section towards flutter failure, especially when this span is dimensioned longer
and longer. The main part consist of four pillars (two for each pylon), two main cables
connecting the pillars, periodically vertical stringers from the main cable to the bridge
deck and the bridge deck itself (see Figure 2.1). The construction model is made in
3D to capture flutter-effects, and will be modeled as a finite element model using beam
and bar elements (see Section 2.1). The model data are chosen to resemble a bridge,
potentially built in the future. The dimensions of the cables (main and stringers) are
based on already existing suspension bridges, and the bridge deck data are from the
project Storstrøms-broen expected to be finished in 2023 [10]. The main span is set to

2.1
Finite Element Model
In order to recreate the actual conditions that the bridge is subject to, a finite element
(FE) model is created, to properly conduct the numerical analysis. The full bridge model
used for all studies in this project can be seen in Figure 2.2.
The first task is to choose the type elements that correctly represent the function of
the different parts of the structure, of which two have been identified. First, considering
the bridge structure in Figure 2.2, the bridge deck is represented as beam elements.
Beam elements have the advantage of being able to resist multi-axial loading, twisting
and bending while only discretizing the domain in 1D along a single axis, in this case
the x-axis along the bridge deck. Second, the cables are in this case designed as bar
elements since they are only exposed to loading along their longitudinal axis. In both
cases the cross-sections of the elements are unchanged along its entirety and can be fairly
assumed to remain unchanged, even during motion.
Figure 2.2: The full Finite Element bridge model
The two types of elements (beams and bars) must be implemented properly to simu-
late realistic conditions. First the theory behind beam and bar finite element formulation
is laid out and afterwards the implementation is discussed of each of the element types.

The effect of the wind on the bridge is capable of triggering oscillation. This chapter

System Reduction
The complexity of the model and resulting number of calculations, inevitably leads to
extended calculation time. In order to ease the calculations, and limit time consumption,
especially on larger models, several methods have been developed to condense the system
matrices.
Since different notations for system matrices have been used in the former sections
and will again in this chapter, it is prudent to note that all system matrices from here
on out are for the total system, of both structural and aerodynamic contributions.
4.1

from the dynamic set, which can greatly reduce the size of the system matrices. Since

The choice of dynamic DOFs is also important, for the representation of the system
to be one that includes large amounts of modal mass and displacement. For further use,
the original FE model of the bridge, shown previously, is segmented as shown in figure

Figure 4.1: Dynamic nodes; nodes with dynamic DOFs are filled
Each highlighted segment contains a total of 12 dynamic DOFs, with the node at
the deck level having all of its DOFs and the upper two nodes only maintaining the
positional DOFs and not the rotational ones. While there are several ways of selecting
which DOFs to use, this proved the most useful method for several reasons.
First, the deck is fully defined by its own DOFs, since both the movement of the
bridge, and the rotation between and around the nodes play a significant part in the
flutter analysis. This facilitates a need for the movement of these nodes to be applied
to the system, as without them, it would not be possible to obtain correct modes.
Second, the upper nodes, which forms the main cable and connect to the stringers,
need to have their directional DOFs, since flutter will cause the main cables to move,
but rotation does not factor in greatly, as the stringers do not transfer rotational forces.
They are therefore left out in favor of lowering computation time.
Third, the lower stringer nodes provide none of their DOFs to the quasi-static con-
densation vectors. This is due to the use of the rigid links, described in section 2.1.3.3.
The implementation of the condensation needs to be done in concert with the implemen-
tation of the rigid links to ensure that no DOFs become the subject of several systems of
determination. As both of these methods actively utilize certain DOFs to control other,
some overlap is possible. Since the rigid link ensures the correct relative movement be-
tween the deck and stringers, by also using the master/slave principal between the nodes
along the deck and the lower stringer nodes, these can then not also be determined by
the quasi-static condensation.
On the other hand, a DOF can be used as master to several systems, as is the case
here for the deck nodes. Employing both methods actually also cause some DOFs to
be both master and slave, in different systems, since not all nodes are dynamic in the
condensation, but all are masters in the rigid links. However, the division is made, so
that the rigid links are enforcing the movement of the lower stringer nodes, removing
them entirely from the quasi-static condensation.

the structure, with the spring ensuring a suitable resonance for the mass, to extract the
most amount of energy possible. An illustration is shown in figure 5.1.
The effect of a correctly tuned mass and spring should be as illustrated in the figure,
where the primary mass moves, causing the spring to elongate or compress. This in turn
causes the secondary mass to move in a delayed fashion, making sure the kinetic energy
in the two are opposite in direction.
For the damper to have this desired effect, a few things can be determined about the
parameters:
• The secondary mass must be large enough to have an impact
• A noticeable reduction in dynamic amplification must happen at resonance
• c must be tuned for maximum energy extraction

ness and damping coefficient.

Implementation of
Damping Systems
In order to identify relevant placements of damping systems, the movement of the bridge
is evaluated. A few points need to be considered, when discussing optimal placement:
• Potential energy dissipation
• Feasibility of design
The potential for energy displacement increases with modal displacement; damping the
biggest displacement removes the most energy from the system. In the case of the bridge
deck, this happens at the middle of the span, for both displacement and rotation. While
this is not surprising, it does present the problem of also being the worst placement of a
damper for the static structure, leading to serious questions about the feasibility of the
design.
6.1

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The model of the bridge produces the appropriate mode shapes, when solving the eigen-
value problem. The elements and their matrix contributions were examined, and their
effect later confirmed by the use of [1]. While a few shortcuts were taken, the system
was in the end solved, using the appropriate tools, instead of applying e.g. the penalty
approach of avoiding cable modes.
The aerodynamic contributions were applied and examined with mixed results. While
the memory force was present through the application of a state-space representation
and the eigenvalue analysis functionally producing both mode shapes and values, the
flutter analysis was inconclusive. The net damping should be able to fall to zero solely
by the effects that the wind has on the structure, this was not achieved.
The system reduction functioned well in reducing the calculation time of the eigen-
value problem, as well as when used to produce the results in the implementation chapter
6, which was appreciated, since they turned out to be wrong.
As such, only a little time was wasted, rather than a lot. Overall there were a lot
of pains in implementing the condensation, as most other parts of the system required
tailor made solutions, but the saved time and efficiency in being able to constantly being
able to run the program with new solutions more than made up for it.
The dampers were described and largely implemented, although later scrapped, due
to unfortunate circumstances. While it would have been nice to have some results to
demonstrate it, the viscous damper already showed signs of being both difficult to im-
plement and ineffective at the chosen position.
The implementation looked promising, but took a left turn at the very end. While
certainly regrettable, the confidence in the few results, which could be produced was
very low.
While it would be prudent to discuss feasibility of designs at this point, that would
not be a responsible cause of action. Since the dampers were not able to be implemented
in the way they were intended, a lot of uncertainty is involved in the final product. As
such, no real opinion can be formed on the matter.
A concluding remark may be that the author should have taken the advise of the
supervisor and done this project with a partner.

CHAPTER 8
Further Work
The flutter analysis was a bit of a let down, and the situation lends itself to further
examination. The bridge is setup to be quite an extreme case, and as such it seems
highly unlikely that it should not be able to be forced into motion by any wind speed.
This should definitely be investigated, so as to determine is the flaw is within the
implementation or if this deck, though very unlikely, is simply not susceptible to flutter.
More work could certainly be done on proper utilization of the special properties of
the ERSD damper. Since it functions by the voltage of the electrical current restricting
the movement, it is possible to create a system which can smoothly transition between
different values of m, k and d, enabling it to dampen several modes.
If this can be implemented, the need for a system akin to the ones presented in
chapter 6 could potentially become redundant.
The viscous dampers implemented in chapter 6 was basically useless, as the angle
at which it needed to reduce the movement of the deck, was much too narrow, creating
several problems.
First, the tuning of the damper is difficult. Since it requires the damper to lock in
the point of impact, and this is done by an approach akin to the penalty approach, by
simply adding a large amount of stiffness. This stiffness needs to be large enough to
restrict movement, but making it too large cause computational issues, compromising
accuracy. This became very evident, as the value used was not able to fully constrain
movement, before the damping parameters were affected.
Second the effect of a viscous damper is not made for work at an angle and would
simply benefit greatly from working and an angle which is at or close to 90°.
An obvious solution would be to go back and choose at better solution, which takes
the angle into account, from the beginning, but that seems difficult at this point. In-
stead, moving the connection upwards above the deck, to a point on the pillars above
the deck, would be a serviceable solution. This creates a much steeper angle, makes the
vertical locking much effective, provides an angle closer to 90°and even moves the weight
of the damper (though small) to the pillar.
Finally, the lack of quality results in the final implementation is of course a sore
point in the report. While time became an issue at the end this is not an excuse, and
the code should have been able to handle these calculations at this point. As the thesis
set out to try and apply the ERSD to a large scale system, it is highly disappointing to
