Executive summary  
 
During the 1980s and 1990s, most organizations were resource-based view where they focused on 
improving internal efficiency and business processes inside organizations. Nonetheless, the rapid 
changing structure of competitive environment coupled with IT lower costs and improved power 
and capabilities have led companies to look beyond their boundaries into the realm of digital 
supply networks, DSN. Surviving the competitive dynamics of today digital age made adoption of 
DSC imperative and not a trend anymore. Therefore, in order for organizations not to be left behind 
it is crucial for them to embrace successfully digital opportunities and transformations in their SCs.  
Addressing the research gap and Assisting Practitioners 
Despite a lot of hype that exists today about DSC, there are also studies that show a high degree 
of dissatisfaction of the companies that claim they have already digitized supply chain. While most 
of the current research emphasizes how vital is for companies to go digital, there is a limitation in 
the research that explains the underlying causes of such high rates of dissatisfaction. As successful 
adoption of DSN requires great investments and resources, this thesis aims to study if these 
changes lead to any performance surpluses over the cyberthreats to which companies are exposed 
when goint digital. Additionally, the thesis presents a model that facilitates organizations with 
concerning issues about DSC/DSN. 
Exploratory Survey Research and Literature Review 
The abovementioned gaps have been addressed with a systematic and extensive literature review 
of around 70 publications and an exploratory survey study of 24 multinational companies that are 
the leaders in their industries and around 20 of them are manufacturing companies. Additionally 
to selecting leading companies in order have empirical results of high quality; also have been 
targeted VP SCM and senior SCM with strong profiles. 
The Digital Supply Chain Framework and its implication 
The Digital Supply Chain framework was devised based on review of other similar or somehow 
related framework models and then reinforced with explorative data in the implications section. 
The framework groups the main successful factors, that are needed for implementing a digital 
supply chain, into four main groups. The following groups are described as follows: manage 
people, manage technology, increase integration of internal and external systems along with 
increase in connectivity, and how to manage cyber-risks and resilience. The model helps firms 
with an execution roadmap on how to boost cross-functional collaboration within a company as 
well as horizontal collaboration among partners in the extended value chain. Moreover, the model 
outlines the importance of standardization and modularization of supply chain elements so that are 
possible the integration of interface systems that ultimately permits a real digitalized supply 
network with many partner.

Preface 
 
Almost a year ago when I was doing the internship at SKF Airasca, Italy,  where they produce hub 
bearing units for automobiles, I was surprised by their efficiency in manufacturing as well as in 
warehouse management. After I had visited their highly automated warehouse with RFID and 
other technologies that make human intervention almost unnecessary in order to load a truck, I 
realized digital technologies would influence greatly the whole supply chain. Whether it is called 
an intelligent supply chain or an efficient one or agile supply chain, etc. it is possible only due to 
innovations in technologies. Therefore, I have decided to do my dissertation in this direction and 
contribute towards this field.  
At the beginning, I was asking myself what is the current state of supply chains in terms of digital 
transformations. Living in a dynamic and globalized world, digital transformations of supply 
chains occurs gradually and there are no well-defined boundaries for a linear traditional supply 
chain, for a hybrid supply chains, and for a fully digitalized supply chain. Because there is never 
an ending point for firms to rest.  
As I have learned, technology adoption for companies is not the main factor that unleashes the full 
potential of a digital supply network, but collaboration coupled with changing people mindset and 
integration of supply chain systems are the main factors that govern the reengineering process of 
a linear supply chain into a digital one.

1 Introduction 
 
Digital technology disrupts the traditional business operations and sets a new supply chain 
paradigm. Firms that re-invent their supply chains and pass from the paper-based or hybrid supply 
chain to the digital one will create more value and appropriate higher profits (ATKearney, 2015). 
Today most firms operate on hybrid supply chains that combine paper-based and ICT-supported 
processes. Business units are usually characterized as silos that do not share information openly 
and so leading to sub-optimal performance (Capgemini Consulting, 2011). There is an increasing 
number of companies that invest heavily in ICT to digitalize their business models including their 
supply chain management. One of the leader in automotive industry, BMW is investing in 
accomplishing a fully digitalized factory with an agile and demand-driven supply chain. Stefan 
Rummel, the head organizer of the 15th International Trade Fair for Logistics, Mobility, IT, and 
Supply Chain Management in Munich, said: “Where logistics people meet, they almost always 
talk about digitalization”.  
One of the most relevant challenges for companies from different industries is to increase the 
supply chain visibility and agility (Farahani, 2015). The visibility helps to eliminate waste along 
the supply chain and to increase the efficiency by knowing in real-time how many products are 
being stocked, where are being stocked, and where are being sold so that to reduce logistics and 
operating costs (Christopher, 2000). Enhancing the supply chain visibility allows for a better 
collaboration between firms with suppliers and customers and this leads to a higher competitive 
advantage. Nowadays, when markets are becoming less predictable and demand volatility is high, 
flexibility is an important aspect to be taken into account when manufacturers choose their 
suppliers. Once the supply chain is fully digitalized it becomes more agile which allows 
manufacturers to respond more quickly to demand changes in terms of volume and variety (K Nath 
and Salehi-Sangari, 2008).  
There are digital technological innovations that drive digital trends and permit the digitalization of 
supply chain. The most important digital enabler is Big Data and as defined by IBM (Zikopoulos, 
2015) has four vital attributes which are high-volume, high-velocity, high-variety, and high-
veracity. As data and hyperconnectivity play an important role in digitalization only 42% of 
companies state they know how to extract meaningful insights from the data available to them 
(Oxford Economics, Workforce2020). Although 96% of marketers value the importance of making 
data-informed decision; yet only 13% of marketers are taking action to address disruptions and 
achieving measurable impact (McKinsey & Company, Marketing disruptions 2014). Big data 
analytics is showing to have promising results and great influence on companies operating and 
financial performances. The data has to be stored and managed in such a way to be analyzed 
==PAGE:9==
 
10 
 
 
 
 
quickly (openSAP, 2016). Smart Data instead is data from which signals and patterns have been 
derived by intelligent algorithms (Gartner IT Glossary, 2014d).  
3D printing, also called additive manufacturing, leads to a global connected supply chain and to 
new production locations, new production materials, and new supplier configurations. Predictive 
analytics provides firms with estimation about possible future outcomes and this is done by using 
different techniques such as machine learning, data modelling and game theory. By integration of 
data, predictive analysis contributes to more accurate decision-making and helps to identify any 
risks or opportunities in the future, and with the help of prescriptive analysis best solutions can be 
proposed (van Rijmenam, 2014). Another digital enabler is the cloud computing paradigm defined 
as a method of computing where scalable and elastic IT-enabled capabilities are delivered as a 
service using Internet technologies (Gartner IT Glossary, 2014a). Cloud computing leads to the 
rise of global real-time data consistency, fast distribution of analytics and synchronized IT systems. 
The last although not the least is the Internet of Things (IoT) which refers to the ever-growing 
network of physical objects that contain embedded technology to communicate and interact with 
the external environment, all via the Internet (Gartner IT Glossay, 2014c). 
Supply chain managers who want to transform digitally their supply chains should also consider 
the digitalization of the entire firm with its products, and the way customers and suppliers interact 
with their firms. Digitalization of supply chain can contribute to digitalization of the business 
models within four areas which are connected products, embedded services, shared products, and 
omni-channel distribution (ATKearney, 2015). Once demand forecasting becomes more accurate 
with digitalization of SCM, firms expect to significant reduction in outbound inventory and 
optimization of batch sizes. 
 
1.1 Scope and research question 
 
This thesis aims to examine how digital technologies redefine the Supply Chain and to give a more 
clear insight of what is meant by the DSC. It seeks to explain what digital transformations took 
place so far in supply chain and also trying to find out what other digital transformations in supply 
chain will occur into the future. Digital transformation of supply chain is no longer a trend but it 
is imperative and is going to affect each industry (Newman, 2016). Along the advantages that 
digital transformations bring, this paper aims to find also the drawbacks or risks that firms expose 
to when going digital. It is important to analyze what damages or what is the potential financial 
loss that these risks can bring to companies. A global research study conducted by Capgemini 
Consulting and GT Nexus (2016) shows  that 75% of respondents answered that digitazing the 
==PAGE:10==
 
11 
 
 
 
 
supply chain is “important or very important” whereas only 5% of respondents said that are “very 
satisfied” with the progress so far. Another research done by AMR Research, now part of Gartner 
Inc., reports that about 65% of large firms have adopted DSC, but less than a fifth of them asserted 
that they a satisfied with the result (Ranganathan, S.H. Teo and Dhaliwal, 2011). The key findings 
of these studies that reflect a high degree of dissatisfaction suggest that digitization should take 
place in the whole value chain of business ecosystem that includes all players. Businesses can no 
longer be competitive on their own; lower computing costs, cheaper storage, and less costly 
bandwidth have encouraged firms to outsource more processes and to stretch their supply chains 
around the globe (openSAP: Mack, 2016). Today every business is more dependent on the external 
partner than ever before and many relationships among partners are still one to one relationships. 
The greater the alliance with suppliers, the greater will be the extent of digitizing the SC, and 
ultimately the greater will be the benefits realized from it (Ranganathan, S.H. Teo and Dhaliwal, 
2011). Most of research so far has been focused on the digital transformations of internal supply 
chain management within an organization and digitalization of business operations between 
organization with its suppliers and customers. Less research has been done on the digitization of 
the extended supply chain and on other digital transformations among organizations and partners 
along the value chain (GT Nexus, 2016; Thalbauer, 2016). As successful adoption of digital 
transformations in SC requires considerable investments in terms of time, money and effort, it is 
relevant to study if these conversions lead to any performance improvements, and examine the key 
factors that influence these transitions. 
Since creating more value by going digital does not depend only on the firm itself but increasingly 
on how firms can effectively operate in a global digital ecosystem of supply chain partners. This 
research paper is intended to detect any knowledge gaps in the research done so far and trying not 
to duplicate the existing research (Alvesson and Sandberg, 2011). This study takes a holistic 
approach by analyzing the impacts of digital technologies on supply chain as well as which benefits 
and risks come along the DSC.   
 
This thesis seeks to give a complete answer to the following research question: 
RQ: Which are the benefits and risks of Digital Supply Chain and do the benefits outweigh the 
risks? 
The need of common, clear, and unifying terms are vital to any academic research in order to avoid 
any confusion. Below are given by the author the benefit and risk definitions used in this thesis.  
Benefit: A factor or advantage that contributes to an increase in firm profit margin. 
==PAGE:11==
 
12 
 
 
 
 
Risk: A situation or possibility that exposures a system to break down and cause financial losses 
for firm. 
Moreover, according to Stock and Boyer (2009), Benefit in SCM is defined as a theme composed 
of three sub-theme: value creation, customer satisfaction, creation of efficiencies. 
 
1.2 Structure of the thesis 
 
This study paper is organized as follows:  
Section 1 contains the introductory part that outline the overall research topic and provides the 
rationale for the research, motivating why it is important to conduct the study. Section 2 builds on 
the selected research methods, namely systematic literature review and survey execution with 
sample selection. Section 3 presents the findings from the systematic literature review and 
identification of current gaps, which gives grounds for a new definition and framework. Section 4 
introduces the new definition of DSC and highlights the conceptual framework that encapsulates 
two dimensions. Section 5, which is the empirical research part, reveals the current practice and 
shortfalls by presenting the the results and findings of survey; section 6 discusses the findings of

theory and practice; section 8 presents the conclusions, limitations, and instructions for future 
research.

2 Methodology 
 
Due to the explorative nature of the research question,  a survey research approach was found 
appropriate because it provides real-world observations and it is better at telling how often 
something has happened (Yin, 1994; Kelly, Clark, Brown, and Sitzia 2003). The first step in 
seeking an answer to the research question was to carry out a systematic and thorough literature 
review whose sources of information are explained below (Section 2.1). Based on findings from 
literature review, a survey questionnaire was created and executed (Section 2.2). The sample 
selection and study is described in the last part of this chapter (Section 2.3). 
 
2.1 Literature review 
 
The purpose of the systematic literature review is to get a comprehensive view of the current state 
of digital transformations in SC and of the advantages and disadvantages that these transformations 
bring. The literature review, which is also the main source of secondary data used in this 
dissertation, has been executed according to the guidelines provided by Rowley and Slack (2009). 
The procedure is divided mainly into two parts; 1) literature search and 2) actual review. 
The literature search started with the search of different materials that may convey any valuable 
information to the thesis in some ways. These materials collected include research papers, journal 
articles, consulting reports, books, dissertations, and webinars about DSC/SCM from leading 
organizations. The sources used to find materials are mainly the library databases from DTU Findit 
and Polytechnic University of Turin. The publishing date for the written materials was considered 
starting with the year 2002 since the broader usage of Internet. Once the initial search, conducted 
for all terms related to DSC, provided xx preliminary papers there was done the first screening 
process that identifies which articles can be safely excluded after an abstract review. Lastly, the 
remaining papers were thoroughly reviewed and some of them were excluded for the final review 
and full analysis. During the actual review of final articles, the citation pearl growing was used to 
add new sources from the references of relevant articles.

3 Literature review 
 
This chapter provides any findings from the literature review related to the subject of study. The 
first section provides a description of the technological innovations that enable digital 
transformation in supply chains and within organizations overall. The second section describes 
how the DSC differs from the traditional or hybrid SC from the managerial perspective and depicts 
the main benefits that come along with DSC. The last section discusses the cybersecurity issues 
and some approaches to address cyber-threats when going digital. 
 
 3.1 Technological innovation enablers 
 
There is a set of innovative technologies that are also called game changers such as Big Data 
Analytics, IoT, mobiles, etc. that  drive digital trends and without them, the digitization of supply 
chain would not be feasible (DSCi, 2016; ATKearney, 2015; Farahani, 2015). Below are described 
the digital technologies that current literature mostly supports for digital supply chain adoption 
and their potential impacts on SC. 
 
3.1.1 Big Data  
 
Although Big Data has no unique definition adopted yet there are some authors like Ward and 
Barker (2013) who suggested a data framework that explained a boom in data based on the ‘3 Vs’. 
Firstly, the volume of Big Data datasets is a relevant factor as it is beyond the capacity of classical 
database management. For instance, Intel considers only organizations creating roughly 300 
terabytes of data per week as Big Data generators. Secondly, data is now generated at a higher 
velocity than ever and according to IBM, ‘every day 2.5 quintillion bytes of data are created and 
approximately 90% of the data in the world today has been created in the last two years alone’. 
Finally, the variety of Big Data can exist in different formats. So far, most organizations used to 
store structured data but now semi-structured data like XML and most of unstructured data in any 
type may include digital information (Rozados and Tjahjono, 2015).  
Correlations enables us to make use of Big Data by identifying useful patterns or proxies for it. In 
other words, correlations help us capture the present and predict the future (Mayer-Schönberger & 
Cukier, p.53). By most of authors, this is called advanced analytics that is mainly the scientific 
==PAGE:20==
 
21 
 
 
 
 
process of transforming data into insight for making better decision. According to Lustig et al. 
(2010), advanced analytics is considered to contain three main sub-categories: descriptive 
analytics, predictive analytics, and prescriptive analytics. Descriptive analytics examines what has 
happened in the past as a way to have better approach for the future. Predictive analytics explores 
real time and historical data to make better predictions about future events based on probabilities, 
in other words they are algorithmic-based techniques based on machine learning and data mining 
(Siegel, 2013). In addition, prescriptive analytics use databased predictions to suggest any actions 
that can help to harness or to avoid on a particular outcome and they rely on stochastic 
computational programming of random variables (e.g. Monte Carlo). 
 
3.1.2 Cloud Computing  
 
Cloud computing offers important benefits regarding flexibility especially for the decentralized 
and loosely coupled nature of global supply chains (Jede and Teuteberg, 2015). Most of the 
literature categorizes CC services into three models (Hoberg et al.,2012): ‘Infrastructure as a 
Service (IaaS)’, ‘Platform as a Service (PaaS)’, and ‘Software as a Service (SaaS)’. It is expected 
SaaS to be the foremost common model and currently it is dominating the literature. This is 
because SaaS generally serves as a “front-end” for the final user (Huang et al., 2013). 
Consequently, other authors characterize CC in SCM as a style of computing in which scalable 
and flexible IT-enabled capabilities are brought as a service using Internet technologies (Gartner 
IT Glossary, 2014a). From the provider persective, SAP offers already eight cloud services for 
SCM processes like B2B trading, information interchange, and procurement. Another example is 
GT Nexus, which has the biggest CC-based global SC network that is accessed by more than 
25,000 firms and organizations including renowned firms like Procter & Gamble, Hewlett & 
Packard, and DHL. 
 
3.1.3 IoT 
 
The Internet of Things (IoT) is the network of physical objects that comprises embedded 
technology to communicate and sense or interact with their internal states or the external 
environment (Gartner IT, Glossary, 2014c). In July 2016, Netherlands became the first country 
with a nationwide network dedicated to the Internet of Things (Networked Readiness Index, 2016). 
According to McKinsey & Co, by year 2025, the IoT will have an economic impact of roughly 
==PAGE:21==
 
22 
 
 
 
 
from $3.9 trillion to $11.1 trillion a year from which business-to-business (B2B) applications will 
probably comprehend around 70% of the value. The building blocks of IoT are described below. 
Smart sensors that are embedded on the production floor allow for transparency and traceability 
in supply chain (Burton, 2016). These smart sensors include sensing nodes/sensors, embedded 
processing nodes/sensors, wired and wireless communication nodes (Machado, 2015). Sensing 
nodes vary vastly in their usage of IoT but some prominent examples include those measuring the 
temperature, humidity, water or gas flow meters for smart energy, radar vision when active safety 
is needed, also sensors for filling status, packaging quality, product quality. These sensors/nodes 
will carry a unique ID and will be controlled separately via a remote command and control 
topology. Sensors/nodes that are more complexed are the embedded processing ones whose local 
processing capacity is furnished by microprocessors or microcontrollers. The wired and wireless 
communication sensors/nodes have the role to transfer the information collected by the sensing 
nodes and processed by local embedded processing nodes. Once the data is processed and new 
commands are created, the communication sensor brings back the new commands to the local 
embedded processing sensors to carry out a task (Shah and Machado, 2015). 
Another key constituent of IoT is RFID technology (Wu and Yue, 2015). In recent years, the topic 
of RFID has been greatly researched in the literature (e.g. Zhu et al, 2012). RFID is a generic term 
that applies to three technologies: active RFID, passive RFID, and near field communication 
(NFC). The main advantage of RFID tags is that they can be read simultaneously more than one 
by the RFID reader (Wand and Pettit, p.273). The active RFID requires a power source in order to 
transfer its signal and it comprises at least an individual identity and a transmitter. The passive 
RFID instead does not require a power source because the only power they need comes from the 
special reader, which is a transceiver that both transmits a radio signal and receives the reply as a 
radio signal from the tag (Wand and Pettit, p.274). NFC is still new and very specific form of 
passive RFID and is specifically designed for very short-range communication among devices. It 
comes in two forms at present, the first one is an NFC chip carried within a card assembly similar 
to a credit card. Whereas the second one is an NFC chip embedded in mobile phones and/or these 
phones are capable of reading NFC chips which permits phone to behave in the same way as a 
card (Wand and Pettit, p.276). RFID technologies have been in usage for the last 15-20 years 
especially in the retail industry. 
Machine to machine (M2M) communication, also called by other authors manufacturing to 
manufacturing communication, happens when sensors of an industrial machine permits it to 
communicate with other equipment or machines. This M2M communication can be between two 
machines from the same plant or even between a machine and another machine from the supplier’s 
manufacturing plant. This leads to an informed manufacturing process that can be described as a 
==PAGE:22==
 
23 
 
 
 
 
system of networks which is a more proactive paradigm and where problems are identified more 
rapidly and even fixed without human intervention (Shah and Machado, 2015). As Elon Musk said 
at the Tesla Gigafactory Grand Opening (2016): “People should start imagine and design the 
factory as a product or a giant machine that builds machines”. 
 
3.1.4 Mobiles 
 
The industry of the future will require data acquisition and sharing throughout the supply chain 
(Brettel and Friederichsen, 2014). This vision for interconnected business services and processes 
is feasible due to technological developments, especially mobile (Bharadwaj et al., 2013). There 
are some sectors of supply chain where smartphones are increasingly used. Two most prominent 
examples include scanning bar codes and exchanging product photos (Szymczak, 2013). Although 
there is gaining more ground for adopting smartphones for proof-of-delivery signature, access to 
distinct reports and dashboards, and for control machinery in industrial settings (Mathew et al., 
2006). Other authors like Li et al. (2010) focus on the principal benefits provided by mobiles such 
as mobile data collection, scheduling, location-based services, voice calls, and information 
dissemination. In addition, mobility permits consumers and workforce to collaborate with, and 
contribute to sources of information, content creation, knowledge, and entertainment – anywhere 
and anytime (Jacobson, 2011). The workforce is increasingly expected to conduct business 
operations from their smartphones, including those who are immersed in supply chain 
management. It is foreseen an inflow of data such as mobile use, searches, and mobile orders. 
According to the Center for Global Enterprise, the global monthly mobile data traffic will expand 
from 3.7 exabytes in 2015 to 30.6 exabytes by 2020. Nevertheless, there are some challenges 
facing when using these mobile devices and according to a study performed by Muller et al. (2012) 
the current use of tablets is for personal purposes. This study suggests that: (1) should be improved 
the application design for these devices, (2) to advocate an integrated experience across all types 
of devices, and (3) to understand the social impact that have these devices. 
 
3.1.5 Additive manufacturing or 3D printing 
 
This is a process of making three dimensional solid objects from a digital file and it is 
accomplished by laying down successive layers of material powder with an electron beam or laser 
untill the entire project is created (3D Printing, 2015). A disruptive technology that alters already 
==PAGE:23==
 
24 
 
 
 
 
the supply chain in many industries. Companies that use 3D printing increase the time for product 
development, time-to-market, order fulfillment, and manage to obtain a cost reduction. The advent 
of additive manufacturing changes the way products are being manufactured and sold. In a 
traditional model, a manufacturer produces a product and sells it in its solid physical form but with 
the 3D printing, the manufacturer may create only the digital design of a product and sell this 
design to costumer and ultimately the consumer, after having acquired a 3D printer at home, may 
print this digital file and obtain the product in its physical form (Crowe, 2016). It is still inefficient 
for manufacturing firms to produce their goods in one part of the world and then sell them in other 
parts of the world. Moreover, after quick delivery services such as from Amazon and Alibaba, 
customers are making their buying decisions based on how fast the products will be delivered to 
them. For example, UPS is investing heavily in additive manufacturing centers all around the US 
so that can produce and deliver the products faster after receiving a demand from the local area 
(Hall, 2016).

 Digital Transformation inside a single company 
 Digital Transformation between organizations and theirs customer

3.2.1 Benefits 
 
Below are described the most encountered benefits in comprehensive literature review about DSC. 
 End-to-end visibility: With traditional ERP is problematic to achieve real-time visibility 
into which goods are in the warehouse, which goods and in which trucks are on the road, 
and which machines are running (Huang, 2016; BCG: Industry 4.0, 2015).  By moving to 
an outside-in model, one that is powered by an ecosystem of trading partners, vendors, 
service providers, and customers , firms can have real-time knowledge and information 
about the movement of a product in the entire network (CSC, 2016). Having installed 
smart sensors on the production floor gives transparency and traceability of products along 
the extended supply chain (Burton, 2016). Tagging technologies such as RFID, NFC, and 
GPS provide real-time data feed for physical movements (Capgemini, 2011).  Beside to 
these track-and-trace capabilities, cross-functional teams collocated in supply chain 
control towers monitor and direct activities across the entire chain by taking advantage of 
real-time, transactional data obtained by the above described track-and-trace capabilities. 
End-to-end visibility is powered also by mobility as insights about some changes needs to 
reach quickly all players (CSC, 2016). This benefit, end-to-end visibility, translates 
consequently into other benefits like better decision-making and capability to foresee 
product bottlenecks and shortages on the factory floor. 
Increasing visibility is like turning the light on when you are operating in the dark or like 
putting all the information in an analytics engine. Improving visibility in the extended 
supply chains is considered to be the most relevant challenge across all industries because 
high tech firms are mainly knowledge-driven, and cooperation to gain and share insights 
with partners is a basis to maintain a competitive advantage (Farahani and Meier and 
Wilke, 2015). Furthermore, a study conducted by GT Nexus in 2016 showed that 94% of 
respondents are making investments to increase supply chain visibility and also that 
increasing transparency is their first priority.  
 
 Agility: In a study of 101 respondents carried out by the founder and CEO of Supply Chain 
Insights LLC, 40% of them defined supply chain agility as the ability to adapt the supply

chain to variations in demand and supply. There is still a gap for many supply chain leaders 
about agility and few companies like Inditex are mature in supply chain agility. Firms 
improve supply chain agility through the redefinition of manufacturing processes, which 
have to be also digitized in order to improve the agility. While many organizations think 
of digital manufacturing and automatically think of 3D printing, there is much more like 
cloud, mobile, machine learning, predictive analytics, M2M communication, and IoT 
(Cecere, 2014; Jede and Teuteberg, 215). For example, RFID gates installed in stores give 
the opportunity to track and manage in-store replenishment and so radically increasing on-
shelf products availability (BCG: Industry 4.0, 2014). A BCG study shows that the leaders 
in DSC management enjoy increases in product availability of up to 10% and more than 
25% faster response time to market changes. Agility ultimately translates into other 
benefits like for example, winning bids, which requires speed and agility to define, select, 
cost out and deploy right-sized and skilled team (Reddy, 2016). 
 
 Process Automation: To obtain a complete execution of an end-to-end process without 
the need of manual intervention has been set as the final automated process scope 
(Capgemini, 2011). Some authors assert that a feature of the DSC is smart warehousing 
which includes robotics like dual-arm robots from ABB. Also smart warehousing 
comprises the usage of wearables and augmented reality systems like smart glasses 
containing software from Ubimax that help employees to navigate through the warehouse 
across optimized routes via the glasses’ graphics display (PwC: Industry 4.0, 2016). Smart 
warehousing, digital twining, and logistics that might use drones or autonomous trucks for 
last-mile delivery are boosting further the process automation in the DSC.

and innovative for companies to undertake and the focus we place on it at Schenider 
Electric allows us to improve supply chain efficiency and enhance our customers’ 
experience”. Another firm executive who states that digital transformations in SC has an 
impact on efficiency gains is Zahid Hai, SVP service operations deployment and planning, 
Sodexo (France).  Due to transparency in supply chain, forecasting and real time

3.3 IP and Cyber-Risk 
 
As mentioned above, the DSC is a customer-centric platform business models and in order to 
optimize the agility and performance of these platform business models it is needed to exist trust 
among all players. In an environment where the threats of hacking, IP theft including confidential 
information, trade secrets, and counterfeiting are at a high level trust cannot be easily built and 
maintained and here the new management challenges come (Howells and Ravenfeld, 2016). 
Although, cyber-risk is the main threat that companies expose to when digitizing their supply chain 
operations, there is still a lack of literature about cyber threats and security of DSC. 
The term “cyber” refers to electronic communication networks and virtual reality. The virtual 
reality describes the intangible nature and thus the difficulties in estimating losses while networks 
are referred to cyberspace (Swiss Re, 2014; CRO Forum, 2014; Lloyds, 2015; Willis, 2013a). The 
Institute for Risk Management defines cyber-risk as “any risk of financial loss, disruption or 
damage to the reputation of an organization from some sort of failure of its information technology 
systems” (IRM, 2015). Other authors like Cebula and Young (2010) use broader definition for 
cyber-risk: “operational risks to information and technology assets that have consequences 
affecting the confidentiality, availability, or integrity of information or information systems”. The 
National Association of Insurance Commissioners (2013) includes in cyber risk: identity theft, 
disclosure of sensitive information, and business interruption. Similarly, other authors like Bohme 
and Kataria (2006) concentrate on only one type of cyber risk such as data braches. In recent years, 
cyber-attacks have affected financial systems, smart grids, nuclear facilities, and the retail industry 
(Richards, 2014; Reuters, 2014a). There are different figures about the global costs caused by 
cyber risk. While Symantec (2013; estimating 113 billion USD) takes only direct losses (e.g. theft 
of money) into account, McAfee (2014; estimating 445 billion USD) also includes indirect losses 
such as loss of trust, reputational costs for hacked company that affects companies’ stock prices, 
and defence costs (e.g., antivirus software, insurance). Different papers have distinct taxonomies 
for cyber-attacks methods. Some authors like Warren and Hutchinson (2006) characterize the most 
common attack methods currently available into four categories: 
==PAGE:31==
 
32 
 
 
 
 
1. Password sniffing/cracking software is one of the simplest methods attacks. Using software 
packages like Brute or Passfinder to access a system through a file transfer protocol (FTP) 
port and attempt to find password file. Alternatively, using software that systematically 
uses a combination of password until one is found successfully. This type of attack could 
harm a company’s DSC in different ways. For example, hackers could delete or modify 
information relating to orders, pricing, or product description. In addition, individuals with 
unauthorized access could copy data for fraudulent purposes or competitors use. 
2. Spoofing attack is a malicious party impersonates another device or user. Web spoofing 
happens when an attacker creates a fake website and users do not note that they are using 
a bogus website and give their credit card details. 
3. Denial of service attacks happens when access to a computer or network is deliberately 
hindered or degraded because of malicious action taken by another user. This type of 
attacks do not necessarily damage information directly or permanently but just compromise 
their availability. Such attacks may be very effective in disrupting the DSC processes by 
crashing the system, which means that suppliers cannot access the on-line service. 
4. Direct attack may happen under several forms and one type of direct attack is when hacking 
into a computer system and rewriting or stealing information. Another form is when 
attacking computer files and destroying, modifying or extracting data. This type of attack 
may be less evident to companies as they may not realize they have been hacked. Authors 
like Khan and Sepuldeva Estay (2015) state that cyber-attacks do not always “come 
through the front door” and that this is the key difficulty for supply chain executives to 
cope successfully with cyber threats. Therefore, companies need to develop cyber-
resilience to protect their DSC, especially the weakest link in supply chains that are most 
vulnerable to attacks.  
DSCs are more vulnerable to cyber-attack than traditional ones and the point of entry for hackers 
is often the weakest link in the chain. Exacerbated by increased complexity and the aim to be 
visible, DSC shares relevant data every second, thus becoming an easy and profitable target for 
hackers (Khan and Estay, 2015). The number of cybersecurity breaches is growing by almost 40% 
each year. A survey carried out in 2016 by the Digital Supply Chain initiative (DSCi) team found 
that 95% of respondents agree that the digitization and sharing of firm information with third 
parties boosts the importance of cybersecurity measures. According to the results of another survey 
conducted in 2016 by the SurfWatch Labs, 89 third-party vendors access a typical company’s 
network each week and 75% of respondents saw growth in third-party connections, yet only 16% 
of companies evaluate third-part security annually while 25% of companies never evaluate third-
part security. Theft or erroneous information and data in SC, because of a cyber-attack, can cause 
adverse consequences like intellectual property breaches, interrupted operations, counterfeit, and 
==PAGE:32==
 
33 
 
 
 
 
sensitive data custody. IoT nodes are particularly notorious soft spots for hackers (TheONEbrief, 
2016). Intellectual property (IP) is at the core of success for most companies and they increasingly 
spend vast resources to develop it. Yet, IP protection is often considered a responsibility or task of 
legal or IT department and not integrated into risk program of supply chain. Increased 
collaboration with trading partners such as suppliers and customers requires an intensive IP 
protection that goes beyond contracts and IT security. Today, most firms do not consider how to 
manage effectively IP-related risks in their extended digitized supply chain (Maguire, 2016).  
High-tech organizations such as aerospace or pharma companies, where IP costs are considerably 
high, may be more reserved into adopting digital transformations in their supply chains. Therefore, 
a more comprehensive way of looking at cyber-risks in SC is required. Because the security level 
of a firm depends on security measures of other trading partners in the SC, most companies invest 
less than the economically optimal amounts (Biener, Eling, and Wirfs, 2015).   
Two aspects that should be taken into account when organizations look to reduce vulnerabilities 
are security and resilience. Security is a preventive action or technique that contributes to the 
reduction of the possibility of an intrusion, whereas resilience is the organizational capability of 
returning to normal operating conditions after a disruption (Sheffi, 2005). Every firm that depends 
on a DSC needs to build in a cyber-resilience framework. A more concise and accurate definition 
of cyber-resilience is given by Estay & Khan (2015) who define it as “the capability of a supply 
chain to maintain its operational performance when faced with cyber-risk”.  
Traditional risk management has been based on a process of risk identification (FMEA), risk 
impact evaluation, risk prioritization, risk monitoring, and various actions towards avoiding, 
mitigating or transferring risk (Eling and Schnell, 2016). However, with more digital 
transformations in SC and with increasing SC complexity, some of abovementioned techniques 
are becoming obsolete when coping with cyber-threat issues. More papers are still supporting the 
supply chain resiliency assessment and management (SCRAM) framework that have been devised 
by Pettit and Fiskel (Pettit et al., 2010). Through empirical research, Pettit identifies seven 
“vulnerability factors” and fourteen “capability factors”.  Moreover, the SCRAM framework 
establishes an economic tradeoff between investment (capabilities) and risk (vulnerabilities). 
Another SC resilience framework that considers cybersecurity more thoroughly is the initiative 
“Partnering for Cyber-Resilience” created in 2012 by the World Economic Forum and led by Elena 
Kvochko. With more than 100 organizations involved in this initiative, Kvochko published a 
framework for the measurement of cyber-threats by calculating “cyber-value at risk” and by 
combining eight factors grouped in three categories: assets, vulnerability, and profile of the 
attacker (WEF, 2015). In the literature review was not found a more accurate SC cyber-resilience 
framework.

New ways of managing cyber and IP risk are the standardizing technology and operational 
practices along the compliance with increasing regulations and industry standards (DSCi, 2016; 
TheONEbrief, 2016). Companies are increasingly liable for the actions of third parties and author 
Steven J.J. Weisman (2016) asserts that to mitigate the third-party risk organizations should use 
encryption, security software, anti-spear phishing software, dual factor authentication, use of 
virtual private networks.

4 A new definition and framework 
 
Since the literature review provides different definitions for a DSC, this section bears a new and 
more comprehensive definition. In addition, below is presented the framework that based on two 
dimensions helps to identify a trade-off about how much resources should be invested in 
safeguards for cyber-risks. The need to conceive a framework is because different industries have 
various structure of cyber risks and threats for firms and the framework aims to provide an 
overview.  
 
4.1 Definition of ‘Digital Supply Chain’ 
 
Formal conceptual definition is critical to any academic research that leads to good empirical 
theory-building (Wacker, 2004). As discussed in section 4, the current literature displays different 
definitions for DSCs. Some definitions put more weight on harnessing information flow and 
analytics while other definitions support more the outside-in approach or sharing economy where 
firms build more value together with the trading partners. Therefore, after observing mainly these 
two sub-groups of definitions, a new definition is given below in order to bridge the gap of the 
sub-groups.  
The definition of digital supply chain: 
A digital supply chain is a supply network with nodes that represent trading partners and the flow 
of real-time information on every path provides network end-to-end transparency for firms to 
optimize performance and minimize risk. 
Unlike the definitions found in current literature, the abovementioned definition emphasizes the 
synergy of Tier 1/Tier 2 vendors and customers that are the nodes in digital ecosystem. The greater 
the synergy with trading partners, the greater will be the extent of transparency in digital supply 
chain. Put simply, more nodes integrated in supply network means more sources of instant data, 
which ultimately contributes to the visibility map. Therefore, for organizations to build an agile 
and yet still efficient supply chain is not necessarily a conflict in terms anymore. 
The definition, as it asserts the synergy of nodes in digital supply chain, also supports the 
standardization of nodes interconnection and so forging supply networks into plug and play supply 
chains (Plate and Winkelhaus, 2016; DHL, 2016).

4.2 A Framework for Digitizing the Supply Chain. 
 
Since digitalizing the supply chain is no longer a trend but it is imperative, the question is not 
anymore ‘whether’ to innovate but is ‘how’ to adapt digital transformation in supply chain. The 
framework presented below, called DSC framework, builds upon two models of  CGE (2016) and 
Windelberg (2015) and it aims to aid companies in transforming their supply chains. As much 
advance as companies make in incorporating DSC elements into their traditional supply chains, 
the reality is that this is a never ending game and no firm will ever reach a point where it can stop 
(DSCi, 2016). While there is no a unique path of becoming a champion of DSC/DSN, each firm 
must find the most appropriate path to follow. For most firms the first step towards a DSC is to 
determine what urges the need to differentiate and to immerse in innovation. Companies must start 
with one or two small transformations and act fast because most of them think if they wait then 
they can do everything before doing anything (Talking Logistics, 2017). The small success after 
starting small and moving quickly can make organizations more inclined to make investments that 
are more significant. The DSC framework lists four categories of factors that affect successfully 
the transition to a digital supply network.

The four framework helps to explain how to manage these four groups of factors around a digital 
headquarters that is created and possessed by digital champion or leader. The elements of the DSC 
framework are described below: 
 Increase integration and connectivity: Integration of supply chain operations is one of 
the first steps to embrace successfully a DSN. In order to make feasible the integration of 
SC activities into the DSC, processes must be standardized and in compliance with 
regulations. Standardizations starts by giving all products and raw materials a clear ID, 
because connecting products and digitizing the extended supply chain can be done based 
on unique identification. The collaboration interface among companies, suppliers, 
subcontractors, and customers must be standardized as well. If a procurement director from 
a large company works with thousands of various vendors and each deliver invoices 
differently, then it is a grueling process for the procurement director to put the right 
purchase orders. While electronic data interchange (EDI) will not vanish soon, companies 
through standardization must create multi-directional insights and exchanges of invoices 
with application programming interface (API). Since companies in a DSN create more 
value with an outside-in approach than an inside-out approach, and also based on the fact 
that organizations may have more than one DSC, they must be capable to implement 
services like order fulfillment quickly. Therefore, to achieve complete integration of DSC, 
companies must build standardized modules and more important to standardize the 
interfaces through which are connected ((Barkawi, 2016). Once a DSC is standardized and 
fully integrated, it also becomes a connected ecosystem that allows partners to share 
directly information and that ultimately creates a full visible supply network in real-time.  
 
Technology: This category of factors presents all the available technologies that firms can 
harness for optimizing performance and reducing risks. As mentioned in the section of 
technology enablers, without the appropriate use of innovative technologies digitalization 
of supply chains would not be feasible. As volumes of real-time data are increasingly 
almost exponentially firms must prioritize the investments in new data sources and in their 
acquisition. Therefore, adequate amounts of investments should be made in technology in 
order to collect real-time big data that later can be leveraged. Because without analytical 
tools to process the new data, and with the lack of decision-making tools, all other 
technology is useless and visibility in the DSN in not provided. Then, organizations must 
combine more extensively the information technology (IT) and operations technology (OT) 
such as Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) systems in order to build an 
infrastructure for success at scale. This implies that many companies should include cloud-
based services, additive manufacturing, predictive maintenance, digital twine, etc. so that 
==PAGE:37==
 
38 
 
 
 
 
the agility of production system is increased and firms can respond quicker to demand 
volatility. Investments in machine-to-machine (M2M) should also be considered because 
collaboration among machines is as important as collaboration between people when 
digitalizing the SC. Additionally, through mobile usage of both customers and employees 
firms can better stimulate demand and keep engaged with customers. Currently, sales 
department forecasts the demand partially based on available data and some intuition, but 
very soon future artificial intelligence will more effectively match demand and supply. 
Finally, organizations especially distribution partners should consider to invest in new 
delivery technologies such as self-driving vehicles and drones. When prioritizing in which 
technologies to invest companies should make some assessments a priori and evaluate the 
payoff of these investments. 
 
 Cybersecurity and Resilience: This group of factors concerns the cyber threats and aims 
to address the cyber risk sources and impacts of cyber-attacks. As mention in the literature 
review section, DSC minimizes risks such as compliance risk, supplier risk or the risk of 
not satisfying a surge in demand over a short time (Deloitte, 2016). However, once these 
supply chain risks are minimized with digital transformations, there are cyber risks that 
should get more attention. Thus, the likelihood of losing confidential and trade secrets will 
continue to rise and it is further enhanced by extensive flow of information in DSN. Firms, 
that are usually reluctant to share data outside their boundaries, should take measures with 
other partners and establish regulatory and legal policies that all partners must comply. 
Companies with high R&D intensity such as high tech or pharmaceutical firms must 
definitely adopt such regulatory policies. Additionally, to mitigate cyber-risks 
organizations should look for weak linkages into the supply network and try to monitor 
them closer in cooperation with other partners. As some SC cyber-attacks are unavoidable, 
businesses must also be capable to maintain operational performance after a cyber-attack. 
Therefore, businesses must find an economic tradeoff between investments in capabilities 
and risk vulnerabilities. 
 
 People: Insufficient qualification of employees and lack of awareness represent a major 
gap towards digital transformation in SC. Therefore, it is important to evaluate the skills 
of the current employees in SCM and check if their skills are enough for a successful 
migration to DSN. Otherwise, firms must search for new professional workers with new 
skills and data analysts and data mining workers are the most in-demand roles. Once 
predictive algorithms can take over on small, and medium levels of decision-making 
processes, SCM departments are expected to shrink due to digitalization and that stresses 
even more the importance of having the adequate people with the right skills. Increasing 
==PAGE:38==
 
39 
 
 
 
 
decentralization while centralizing the right activities to accomplish economies of scale, 
and internal cross-functional collaboration will be the baselines for creating successfully a 
DSN. Not only the internal collaboration among departments must be improved but also 
the horizontal collaboration between the company and its suppliers like tier 2 or 3 suppliers. 
Since DSC implies extended flows of information among all nodes that are the partners, it 
is relevant that trust builds among them so that a firm can have access to the ERP system 
of another partner and vice versa. Finally yet importantly, changing the culture and the 
people mindset while managing them is a key success factor towards DSC. Because how 
people are operating and using the supply chain must change otherwise firms would not be 
able adopt digital innovations since all organizational change is derived from people 
willing and capable of moving to the future. Even if executives might think that have 
significantly changed the people mindset, they should not overlook the organizational 
inertia that might slow down the SC digitalization process.  
 
Digital hub: This is an attribute of DSC leaders. In a digital hub, also called a digital 
headquarter, administrative process are optimized and automated. Companies that try to 
build the most digitalized supply chains should build such digital headquarters. A digital 
hub comprises a control tower in which employees from cross-functional teams monitor 
activities across value chain by taking advantage of real-time big data. Firms building a 
digital hub must also include a trust broker, which is liable for end-to-end auditing of 
information flow in the DSN, and so monitoring the safety of data flows.

6 Discussion 
 
The findings on DSC benefits corroborates the theory in terms of competitive advantages that firm 
gain after adopting a DSN. Even the relevance of benefits from findings is in accordance with the 
importance of benefits from theory. The study also corroborates that agility is increased in 
organizations with DSC, as supported in theory. The findings refine the theory regarding 
companies’ satisfaction with digitally enabled SC. Although, the explorative results completely 
sustain the low number of firms that are very satisfied with DSC, the findings do not neither 
provide proofs for a high level of dissatisfaction. This is in contradiction with the results of four 
different studies done by Bacheldor (2003), Rossi (2003), Supply Chain Advisor (2003), and GT 
Nexus (2016), which all display a high level of dissatisfaction of companies after digitizing the 
supply chain. Despite the importance given to cyber-attacks and cyber security in theory, the 
findings show that most firms are have not been a victim to a cyber-attack, although this does not 
undermine the importance of cyber threats that companies are facing when going digital with their 
supply chain. As the findings show, the small number of companies actually being exposed to 
cyber-attacks is explained by increased investments done by firms in cyber security. 
In the sharing economy of digital age, organizations that follow an outside-in approach, in which 
digitizing SC implies synergistic and complementary capabilities with trading partners, create 
more value than cannot be achieved independently (Ranganathan, Teo, & Dhaliwal, 2011). The 
survey findings do not fully support this part of theory because companies with DSC appear to 
collaborate less with tier one & tier two suppliers than firms with traditional or hybrid supply 
chains do. Supply chain transparency, that provides real-time awareness of operation process, is 
achieved by the integration of all trading partners in a supply network. Since the findings do not 
show a strong correlation between SC digitalization and collaboration with suppliers or other 
subcontractors raises the question of whether digitalizing the supply chain necessarily means 
integrating the tier one, two, and three suppliers in firm’s ERP system.  
The results of many studies such as Cecere (2014) and GT Nexus (2016) show that supply chain 
managers have high expectations about investments done by companies in the direction of supply 
chain digitalization. The findings of this study corroborate the results found in the current literature 
and these inflated expectations of supply chain managers about DSC could mean that the current 
state of digitalization is on the rising curve on the hype cycle. Lastly, the findings of study refines 
the theory in terms of technology enablers. Theory gives high importance of additive 
manufacturing on SC digitalization or of augmented reality to automate warehouse management 
(PwC, 2016). The findings do not fully support this; instead, they show that there are some other 
technology innovations such as team foundation server (TFS).

7 Implications 
 
This section describes the implications of this dissertation on theory and practice. Sub-section 7.1 
presents how this thesis is useful for the academia and how it has helped to bridge the research 
gaps and flaws. Sub-section 7.2, which is an execution roadmap of DSC framework, provides 
suggestions and practical guidelines to managers, taking into measure how the findings reflect on 
the introduced DSC framework.  
 
7.1 Implications on theory 
 
From a theory building perspective, previous studies have examined and cited the positive impacts 
of DSC such as end-to-end visibility, increase in agility and efficiency, and reduction of costs. 
However, there is lack of proof at what ‘costs’ these benefits are achieved in terms of cyber threats 
to which firms expose to when digitalizing the supply chain.  Therefore, the work of this thesis 
covers this flaw by keying-in the malicious acts of cyber-attacks and by uncovering the importance 
of their consequences. As, current literature does not offer thorough insights about cyber supply 
chain risk management (CSCRM), this dissertations adds value to this aspect by highlighting the 
adoptions of agreed upon standards and protocols for information flow. There is even a larger gap 
in the theory, which mentions possible methods of securing data sharing and the work of this thesis 
emphasizes the use of digital encryption for data shared in DSN. Once these cyber SC threats are 
reduced, the level of satisfaction that companies have with DSC is increased and the delay with 
digital transformations in SC is reduced too. Thus, the work of this thesis comes to explain why a 
small number of companies are satisfied with the results of DSC, as stated by many studies from 
theory.  
While the literature outlines the importance of collaboration and synergy with partners in SC, there 
is lack of available information in how companies can create the compatibility required for 
horizontal collaboration among suppliers, customers, etc. along the extended supply chain. In other 
words, all theory mention the benefits and importance of adopting a DSC, but theory misses 
information on how to redesign the current SC into a digital one and this thesis underlines a 
systematic approach towards modularization and integration of companies’ interfaces and 
interoperability. As the interviewed companies have said that one of the two largest barriers toward 
digital transformations in SC is the lack of what it really mean to do and not because DSN is not 
part of a business strategy.

7.2 Implications on practice 
 
Digitalization of supply chain is not near an easy journey to embark on and therefore executives 
and senior supply chain managers will definitely need guidelines on how to proceed successfully 
with digital transformations.  
All changes in an organization start with the people who are working in that company and with 
other stakeholders such as customers. Therefore, first designate a professional who can be a supply 
chain manager or senior executive working already within the company or someone from outside 
of organization. Additionally to the fact that this person should have leadership skills, it is 
important that the assigned person has the experience and knowledge necessary to influence others 
to make changes. Help him/her to recruit a team of people with required skills because as found in 
answers of interviewed companies one of the major barriers to SC digitalization is lack of skills. 
The most relevant skills that team members should have are the data mining and data analysis. 
Additionally to the knowledge of the field, the team must be composed of people who are willing 
to changes and who do not show a resistance towards maintaining the status quo. Then, make a 
clear decision-making policy for the team and assign more authority to the person leading the team. 
After having a recruited team with a leader, it is equally important to train and manage rightly 
other people from cross-functional teams because implementation of DSC without people who are 
able to work effectively and cross-functionally is almost unreal. After investments done in human 
resources, organizations must prioritize the technology investments. Executives of a firm must 
prioritize technology investments according to their impacts on SC and costs. The survey finding 
that says real-time transparency in SC is the vital attribute of DSC has a key implication for 
practitioners, which is to invest firstly in technologies that can allow this transparency outside 
organizations. Based on findings about BDA, the CEO and other executives must secondly invest 
in technologies that permit to capture, structure, and use outside data effectively.  
After all investment in IT and OT technologies are achieved and the firm has a created a 
infrastructure at scale from technology point of view, firms must focus on outside interoperability 
and connectivity along with other partners in the DSN. Managers must have meetings with 
managers from tier 2, tier 3 suppliers and with other subcontractors so that they agree upon 
common standards about their companies interfaces within the DSC. Then, the next step is to allow 
the supply chain to be forged on standardized modules because standard modules and the way they 
are interconnected must be defined in order to permit a quick setup and firms’ collaboration. This 
means that companies may prefer API instead of EDI for specific data exchanges such as invoices.  
==PAGE:57==
 
58 
 
 
 
 
Managers must not necessarily wait until the abovementioned steps are completed in order to 
enhance the cyber-risk management capability. The findings about cyber-risk mitigation strategy, 
in which almost less of respondents do not have such a strategy yet, have a strong implication  on 
managers about DSC risk management. Companies need to develop and implement a 
comprehensive cyber-risk mitigation strategy, which must be clearly communicated and 
constantly measured. Such a strategy must identify the sources of potential cyber-attacks and 
address them efficiently. SC managers must seek for the weakest linkages in DSC and try to reduce 
their vulnerabilities. To secure information sharing in a digital supply chain is done largely by 
building trust among partners and setting common secure rules and policies for information sharing 
along the supply network. Firms when assessing the SC cybersecurity investments must take into 
account also the impact of such attacks on their company. The CEO or other executives of firms 
with high IP value must be more precautious about incorporating elements of DSC. Finally yet 
importantly, managers must also develop a resilient strategy for managing successfully the impacts 
of unpredictable cyber-attacks.  This contingent plan should include authenticity of products in 
order to avoid their counterfeiting, safety, and quality. Finally, not to be inflicted by digital 
disruptions of SC, senior managers should determine which risks could be avoided or mitigated in 
order to continue with the digital transformations of SC.

8 Conclusion 
 
In recent years, digital technologies have increasingly affected every aspect of how organizations 
do business, including global supply chain networks. This is also driven by price reduction of 
lower computing costs, cheaper storage, and costly bandwidth. Therefore, supply chain leaders 
must take advantage of possibilities that digital technologies offers and combine information 
technology (IT) and operations technology (OT) 
This study investigates the dynamics underlying the digitally enabled supply chains management 
activities, examined the key factors that govern the initiatives for implementing a DSC and factors 
that make companies reluctant in going digital with their SC. Following a systematic and extensive 
literature review, it clears up how the surge of information inflow from all sensors whether they 
are on production floor, on products already sold such as wind turbine or jet engine, or on raw 
parts that are in warehouse or in a truck on a road.  All these data sources provides real-time big 
data that create a virtual constantly changing map that offers real-time transparency for firms in 
DSC. In addition, this study examined how firms can harness BDA in order to sense and match 
demand and supply, so that the risk of not satisfying a surge in demand to be reduced while to 
reduce also the bullwhip effect. Put simply, this paper analyzed how being efficient and agile at 
the same time is no longer a conflict with the help mainly of digital innovations. It discussed how 
cloud-based applications allow supply chain managers to access an ERP system all around the 
world and how this increases flexibility. Additionally to reinforcing the importance of 
cybersecurity and resilience, this study discussed how firms could address these issues in relation 
with possible losses of cyber-attacks for firms. A large pharmaceutical company, that has high IP 
value and that may have colossal financial losses if compromised drugs reach the patient, must 
allocate more resource for SC cybersecurity than a mining or textile company.  
The study described how the structure or the design of supply chains is reengineered once digital 
transformations are adopted. From a traditional linear supply chain firms shift toward a hybrid one 
and ultimately they create or become part of digitally supply ecosystems that is characterized with 
horizontal collaboration along the value chain, increased connectivity and responsiveness. Also 
this paper introduced a framework that aims to aid practitioners with the execution of digital supply 
chains. The DSC framework groups the successful factor of adopting a DSC into four groups and 
explains how the factors should be managed in order to become a leader with a digital hub in SC.  
It is worthwhile to note some of the findings: First, there is increased agility of companies that 
have built a DSC. Second, a large barrier to SC digitalization is the lack of internal system 
integration to support digitization and mentioned in implications on theory section, this study 
aimed to explain how integration of systems can be created by standardization and modularization. 
The second finding may explain the third interesting finding, which showed that companies with 
==PAGE:59==
 
60 
 
 
 
 
DSC do not necessarily share their planning and scheduling information with tier 1, tier 2, or tier 
3 suppliers.  
 
8.1 Limitations and notes for further research 
 
Despite captivating findings and implications, this study has some limitations that are worth to 
mention. First, the measures used in the survey for assessing the performance impacts of DSC 
were perceptual and future researchers could attempt to use more objective measures for grasping 
the DSC benefits in financial terms. Second, the questionnaire answers from each analyzed firm 
came from a single respondent, who was a supply chain manager, and therefore it raises the 
question of common method bias. Future researchers may address this problem by conducting 
Harmon’s one-factor test for common method bias (Ranganathan, Teo, & Dhaliwal, 2011). Or 
other researchers may interview a collectively a small group of supply chain managers from large 
companies. Third, the small number of respondents, 24, and the need to keep the survey simple in 
order not to compromise more the respondent rate, do not allow any statistical analysis of results. 
Therefore, future researchers may attempt to interview more firms and/or use questions with more 
descriptive answer options.
