Abstract
This thesis concerns the concept and design of a multi-slice Σ∆-modulator for audio
applications. The multi-slice Σ∆-modulator uses multiple Σ∆-modulators in parallel to
increase the effective resolution of the analog-to-digital converter (ADC).
A Python model is used to compare two different Σ∆-modulator structures with two
different loop filter orders and oversampling ratios (OSR). This comparison lead to
a decision on an oversampling ratio of 64. The cascade of integrators with weighted
feedforward summation (CIFF) Σ∆-modulator structure is chosen because it shows less
sensitivity to non-idealities in the Python model. A 4th-order loop filter of the CIFF Σ∆-
modulator is chosen over a 3rd-order based on a noise budget analysis. The noise budget
analysis shows that the total power consumption of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator with
a dynamic range higher than 96 dB is lower when using a 4th-order CIFF Σ∆-modulator
than the 3rd-order. The digital hardware complexity of the decimation filter for the
multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is estimated from the number of adders required to imple-
ment the decimation filters. The discrete-time Σ∆-modulator is designed using cascode
inverter-based amplifiers for the switched-capacitor integrators. In the amplifiers the
benefits of the FD-SOI process node is used to achieve lower threshold voltages of the
transistors.
The expected performance of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator has been simulated with tran-
sient noise and across global and local process variations. The multi-slice Σ∆-modulator
achieves a dynamic range of 97.7 dB while consuming 127 µW when using 16 slices from
a 800 mV voltage supply. The multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is sensitive to supply voltage
variations with a worst case performance degradation of 12 dB of signal-to-noise-and-
distortion ratio (SNDR). A proposal using adaptive body-biasing in combination with
a redesign is suggested to solve this issue.
The simulated performance of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is compared to the state-of-
the-art in analog-to-digital converters achieving a Schreier dynamic range figure-of-merit
of 179.7 dB. Finally, suggestions for future work related to the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator
are presented.

Resumé
Denne opgave handler om konceptet og designet af en parallel-koblet Σ∆-modulator til
audio applikationer. Den parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulator bruger flere Σ∆-modulatorer
i parallel for at øge den effektive opløsning af en analog-til-digital konverter.
En Python model er brugt til at sammenligne to forskellige Σ∆-modulator strukturer
med to forskellige filterordener og oversamplingsforhold. Denne sammenligning leder
til en beslutning af et oversamplingsforhold på 64. En kaskade af integratorer med en
vægtet fremkoblingssummation Σ∆-modulator struktur er valgt på grund af, at den ud-
viser mindre følsomhed for uidealiteter i Python modellen. Et 4.ordens filter er valgt for
Σ∆-modulatoren over et 3.ordens filter baseret på en støjbudgetanalyse. Denne støjbud-
getanalyse viser at det totale effektforbrug af den parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulator med en
dynamisk rækkevidde på over 96 dB er mindre for et 4.ordens filter end for et 3.ordens fil-
ter. Kompleksiteten af det digitale kredsløb i decimationsfilteret for den parallel-koblede
Σ∆-modulator er estimeret ud fra det nødvendige antal additionskredsløb, som der be-
høves for at realisere decimationsfiltrene. Den diskret-tids Σ∆-modulator er designet
med kaskode inverterbaserede forstærkere i skifte-kondensator integratorerne. Fordelene
ved en FD-SOI procesnode er brugt i forstærkerne til at sænke tærskelspændingerne for
transistorerne.
Den forventede ydeevne af den parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulator er simuleret med transient
støj og over globale og lokale procesvariationer. Den parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulator
opnår en dynamisk rækkevidde på 97.7 dB med et effektforbrug på 127µW ved brug
af 16 parallel-koblinger fra en 800 mV spændingsforsyning. Den parallel-koblede Σ∆-
modulator er følsom for forsyningsspændingsvariationer med en værste tilfælde ydeevnened-
sættelse på 12 dB af signal-til-støj-og-forvræningsforhold. Brug af adaptiv body-biasing
i kombination med et redesign are foreslået for at løse dette problem.
Den simulerede ydeevne af den parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulator er sammenlignet med
state-of-the-art for analog-til-digital konverter og opnår en Schreier dynamisk række-
vidde figure-of-merit på 179.7 dB. Til slut er forslag til fremtidigt arbejde inden for
parallel-koblede Σ∆-modulatorer præsenteret.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the 1960s the discrete-time Σ∆-modulation was introduced in analog-to-digital con-
verters (ADCs), which enabled trading increased digital circuitry and speed for higher-
accuracy and conversion rate.
The interest in Σ∆-modulators increased due to the
reduction in power consumption and area for digital circuits because of the process
node scaling during the last 40 years, which was famously predicted by Gordon Moore.
The Σ∆-modulator’s achievable switching speed started becoming a limitation for these
ADCs in the early 1990s, when digital video and radio required high-speed electronics.
This lead to further development of the implementation of the Σ∆-modulator. The chal-
lenge in speed requirements was addressed by the introduction of the continuous-time
Σ∆-modulator, which relaxes the speed requirement for the analog circuitry [1].
Previous to the continuous-time Σ∆-modulator, other solutions to lower the high-speed
requirements were proposed. A specific concept of using several lower-speed parallel
Σ∆-modulators all converting the same analog input and combining their digital output
bit-streams to increase the effective accuracy was proposed. This reduced the need for
running the Σ∆-modulator at high-speeds. Two classes were proposed by Ian Galton in
[2]. The ΠΣ∆-modulator [3], [4], where the individual ADCs ran at a minimum sampling
frequency of twice the bandwidth (Nyquist frequency) and another time-interleaved Σ∆-
ADCs where several Σ∆-modulators processes a part of the high-speed input signal at
a lower speed [5], [6]. The drawback of these parallel solutions was an increase in both
analog and digital circuit complexity. With the introduction of continuous-time Σ∆-
modulators a better solution to the problem was proposed and the motivation for these
concepts were gone. There has not been much research in parallel Σ∆-modulators since
the introduction of continuous-time Σ∆-modulators.
In this thesis, the parallel approach is used not to reduce the speed requirements of
the Σ∆-modulator, but to achieve variable resolution of the ADC. The resolution is
controlled by having multiple parallel Σ∆-modulator ”slices” and turning these individ-
ual slices on and off. The word ”slice” is used for the individual Σ∆-modulators to
distinguish it from the similar used concept ”channel”. Multi-channel ADCs are usually
used in CCD-cameras, ultrasound scanners and various biosensors, where the individual
channel convert a small part of the total desired signal. In multi-slice ADCs, all the
slices convert the same signal and the digital outputs are combined to increase resolu-
tion. Variable resolution could be used to keep a constant desired resolution for variable
input by digitally tracking the input signal. This could lead to a reduction in power
consumption. In this thesis the application is audio for wearable consumer electron-

consumption are critical and the input signal strength is rapidly varying making it an
adequate application for the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator. The signal bandwidth of inter-

Audio ADCs do not require high peak effective resolution in the form of a high sig-

[7]. The reason is that audio signals are usually a linear combination multiple frequen-

these frequencies [7]. Instead, the dynamic range (DR) of the multi-slice ADC is the
performance parameter in this thesis. The dynamic range is the range of input signal
level that the ADC can detect.

Summary
This chapter presents the relevant data converter theory for understanding Σ∆-modulators.
It starts by presenting quantization errors and the gaussian noise model for quantization
noise. Then it introduces the effect of oversampling and noise shaping this quantization
noise. The equations showed in section 2.3 are all based on a linearized model of the
Σ∆-modulator. In practice the Σ∆-modulator is highly non-linear due to quantization
being a non-linear operation. This non-linearity means that the Σ∆-modulator is im-
practical to analyze mathematically. Instead the linearized model is used for designing
the Σ∆-modulator in this thesis and the actual performance is then simulated.

This leads to a decision in both oversampling ratio and Σ∆-modulator structure for the

of an FIR sinc filter and an IIR polyphase half-band filter is also presented and an in-
vestigation in how to choose the structure of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator with these
decimation filters is described.
Section 3.1 describes the modelling of two Σ∆-modulator structures and presents the
modelled performance when used in a multi-slice Σ∆-modulator. The Σ∆-modulators
are compared for different Σ∆-modulator structures, oversampling ratios and loop filter
orders and an oversampling ratio. Based on this comparison an oversampling ratio is
chosen.
Section 3.2 describes the modelling of non-idealities in Σ∆-modulators and compares
the two Σ∆-modulator structures’ sensitivity to these non-idealities. This leads to a
decision in the Σ∆-modulator structure.
Section 3.3 describes the modelling of the two filters used for the decimation in this
thesis. The first being an FIR sinc filter for a decimation in frequency by 32 and the
second being a polyphase half-band filter for the final decimation by 2. The design of
these filters are described and their complexity evaluated.
Section 3.4 presents the investigation of how best to combine the multiple Σ∆-modulators
and their decimation filters. Here a figure-of-merit is presented and three different struc-
tures are investigated and compared to each other.

the slope of degradation for the CIFB is steeper than that of the CIFF. This means that
in case of output clipping, the CIFF does not degrade as fast as the CIFB.

3.3.1

of being simple to implement, coefficient and multiplierless and that the comb part of
the filter runs at the output frequency, which saves power.

4 System level design
4.1
The cascode inverter-based amplifier in
switched-capacitor integrators
To lower the power consumption of the Σ∆-modulator a cascode inverter-based amplifier
is used instead of a traditional OTA. The work in [18] presents the benefits of using a
class-C inverter-based amplifier for switched-capacitor integrators in Σ∆-modulators in
regards to both power consumption and noise. The downside of these amplifiers are that
they don’t have any biasing. This means that their performance variation across process
corners is greater than their traditional OTA counterparts. Another downside is the
single input. The inverter-based amplifier is always amplifying any input signal against
its natural voltage trip-point, which is set by the physical properties of the transistors
and not by another voltage source. This trip-point also varies with different process
corners and from local mismatch variations in fabrication. The difference between a
desired voltage reference point of a designed inverter-based amplifier and the actual can
be thought of as an input-referred offset of the amplifier. The input-referred offset of the
cascode inverter-based amplifier can easily be more than 20 mV across process corners
and temperature. The inverter-based amplifier also does not achieve great low-frequency
gain [18]. In order to achieve higher gain cascode transistors are added to the inverter.
The cascodes are biased by the supply rails. With cascodes the inverter-based amplifier
can achieve more than 70 dB DC gain for a low power consumption [18].
To circumvent the issues with a varying trip-point, a DC cancelling technique is used

4.2
Noise budget
With the relevant theory presented for the cascode inverter-based amplifier and espe-
cially the calculated noise and cascode inverter-based amplifier method using simulation
results, a noise budget for the 3rd and 4th-order Σ∆-modulator is made. This noise
budget also generates the required input sampling capacitance for each filter order and
estimates the final SNR of the Σ∆-modulator. These results then leads to a decision in
which filter-order is required to achieve the desired specification of 96 dB dynamic range
with a reasonable amount of Σ∆-modulator slices.
For the noise budget not all the noise sources in the Σ∆-modulator are considered.
Instead, the quantization noise and the noise from the first integrator is calculated and
considered. The quantization noise can be found from the Python modelling and results
shown in Table 3.1. The procedure becomes more complicated since a supply voltage is
not given by specification but instead decided upon by looking at the cascode inverter-
based amplifier design plot for different transistor lengths and supply voltages. These
amplifier design plots are dependent on the capacitive load of the amplifier, therefore
anytime the capacitive load changes, due to a change in the supply voltage, which leaves
room for a larger input signal amplitude, a new contour plot must be made. The process
is therefore iterative and explained as a flow chart in Fig. 4.3. It should also be noted
that a differential structure is assumed for the noise budget calculations, since this is
==PAGE:68==
50
4 System level design
what will be implemented. The choice of a differential structure is due to its increase in
signal-to-noise ratio compared to a single-ended structure, the increase in power-supply
rejection ratio and the lowering of even order harmonics [9].
The flow chart presented in Fig. 4.3 starts with creating an initial guess of the noise bud-
get. Usually the quantization noise should not be the largest part in the noise budget,
since the uncorrelation between the input signal and this quantization noise is only an
approximation. Therefore, the quantization noise is always set lower than the noise from
the first integrator. The input sampling capacitor size is calculated based on the noise
calculations for the first integrator. The input sampling capacitor is then evaluated to
ensure that its size feasible to implement. The limits of the capacitor size are dependent
on the process node and design. In this thesis a minimum of 50 fF and maximum of 1 pF
is used. If the input capacitor is not within the limits the allocation of the noise bud-
get is changed. When a reasonable guess for the input capacitor is found the resulting
load capacitance is calculated, by using the dynamic range scaling coefficient found as
in section 3.1.3 and equation (4.4). This load capacitance is then used for generating
the cascode inverter-based amplifier design plot. Here the supply voltage and transistor
length is chosen based on the minimum power design that can achieve an oversampling
ratio of at least 128. This over-design is decided to make room for variations in process
corners and temperature. If the minimum power design changes the supply voltage more
than 50 mV then a new noise budget is created and the design process starts over. The
limit of ±50 mV is set to ensure that using the design method shown in the flow chart
in Fig. 4.3 terminates.
The design process is repeated for both the 3rd and 4th-order Σ∆-modulator. All the
iterations are not presented here instead only the final noise budget, estimated SNR and
input sampling capacitor are shown.

settled properly. The quantizer is therefore made to make a decision on the falling edge
of ϕ1 giving this node half a switching period to settle. The quantizer needs to make
this decision fast, since it only has the non-overlapping time to make the decision before
the output of the quantizer is needed for the integration of X1 and X3.
From Fig. 4.9 it can be seen that the Σ∆-modulator is pseudo-differential. This re-
quires common-mode feedback to ensure high common-mode rejection. The common-
mode feedback circuit used in this work was presented in [21]. It is realized in Fig. 4.9
by the Cm capacitors.

5.1.1
Switch design
For the Σ∆-modulator designed in this work all the switches are of same size and are
implemented as transmission gates. The on-resistance of the switch is dependent on the
input voltage of the switch. Reducing the on-resistance can be done by increasing the
width of the transistors. This increased area increases the gate capacitance, which leads
to increased charge-injection. The charge injected from turning off the switch can be
estimated by [9]:
Qch = WnLnCox(VGS −Vtn) + WpLpCox(|VGS| −|Vtp|)
(5.1)
where Wi and Li is the width and length of the NMOS and PMOS transistor, VGS is the
gate-source voltage, Vtn and Vtp is the threshold voltage for the NMOS and PMOS tran-
sistor respectively and Cox is the oxide-capacitance. In a switched-capacitor integrator
the largest charge injection from the input switch connected to the sampling capacitor
is when the VGS is equal to the supply voltage VDD, this then leads to a voltage change
by:
∆V = Qch/2
Cs
(5.2)
where Cs is the sampling capacitor for the switched-capacitor integrator. For increased
areas this charge injection can cause errors at the output of the integrator. Therefore,
there is a trade-off between low on-resistance of the switches and the charge injection.
In this thesis the switches were designed by varying the widths of the PMOS and NMOS
in a testbench of the first integrator to find a switch design that ensured that the output
of the integrator settles within half a switching frequency.

From the simulated results of the single-slice and multi-slice Σ∆-modulator it can be
seen that the results match with both the Python modelling and the behavioural Ver-
ilogA implementation of the design. The main focus has been on the design of a low
power single-slice Σ∆-modulator and here most of the focus has been on the design of
the integrators. This includes proper power reduction scaling between the integrators
and taking into account both small and large-signal behaviour.
The single-slice Σ∆-modulator shows great results in the typical process corner case
and for global process corners, temperature and local process variations. The compari-
son of the power spectral density of the 4th-order Σ∆-modulator showed that the noise
transfer function in the final transistor-level design fits with the noise transfer function
of the Python model.
The use of an intermediate step of using behavioural VerilogA models was proven useful
in showing the influence that non-idealities has on the noise transfer function, especially
that the quantization noise within the signal bandwidth of interest is much higher than
for the linearized Python model of the Σ∆-modulator due to the finite gain of the am-
plifiers. It can be seen from the power spectral density that going from the behavioural
VerilogA model to a transistor-level implementation a lot of distortion is added. This
is also shown for the investigation of the output swing of the first integrator, since the
small-signal parameters of the VerilogA model are not affected by large-signal changes
as is the case of the transistor-level implementation of the amplifier.
Comparing the noise budget made in section 4.2.2 and the expected SNR of the single-
slice Σ∆-modulator with the simulated result shows a good correlation. The expected
SNR was 85 dB and the simulated peak SNR of the single-slice Σ∆-modulator is 83.7 dB.
Since the noise budget did not take into account non-idealities and thereby not the higher
quantization noise within the signal bandwidth the difference of 1.3 dB is within a reason-
able range to be explained by these non-idealities. This means that the design flow used
for designing the integrators has been helpful in obtaining a low power Σ∆-modulator
design.

transistors in the amplifiers. Another option is the introduction of body-biasing of the
input transistors of the amplifiers. Body-biasing is especially interesting in a FD-SOI
process, since there is no risk of accidentally forward biasing any parasitic diodes from
source to drain of the transistors. Having a adaptive body-biasing (or a trimmable con-
stant solution) can change the threshold voltage of the transistors and thereby reduce
some of the effects of a lower or higher supply voltage. The foundry used for this thesis
(ST Microelectronics) have participated in research regarding integrated temperature
and process variation monitors for 28 nm FD-SOI in [22] and other work has presented
the use of these monitors for adaptive body-biasing in [23], [24]. A combination of re-
design, adaptive body-biasing and a very low voltage variation low-dropout regulator as
a supply for the Σ∆-modulator could prove a possible solution to the issue.
The results of the simulations of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator show that it is possi-
ble to increase the performance of the Σ∆-modulator by increasing the number of slices.
From the power spectral density, it can be seen how the increase in slices lowers the
noise floor but not the correlated harmonic distortion. It also shown that the slope of
the SNR as function of number of slices follows the 3 dB increase per doubling in slices.
The dynamic range is also shown to follow the expected benefit of increasing the number
of slices. Here the linear regression of the dynamic range actually shows that the slope
is slightly higher than the expected 3 dB. This is most likely due to the limited input
level amplitude points simulated for the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator.
A decimation filter is not implemented and synthesized for transistor-level simulations
in this thesis. Instead, the simulation data for the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is processed
through the Python models of the decimation filter using the decided parallel structure.
This showed that the low-pass filters have enough attenuation so that no noticeable
aliasing occurs. This is a first step for the actual digital implementation of these filters.
Another decimation filter design iteration could be made to investigate how lowering
the performance of these filters affects the overall ADC performance, so as to minimize
the size of especially the first decimation filter, since this filter is a part of the parallel
path.

6.2
Future work
During the project there have been topics and design issues that could be interesting to
investigate further, before a potential tape out of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator. Some
of these topics and issues are described below.
• The single-slice Σ∆-modulators sensitivity to supply voltage variations should be
solved using either adaptive body-biasing, a redesign of the cascode inverter-based
amplifiers or a combination of both.
• The switches designed for the Σ∆-modulator are the same for all integrators. This
is most likely not required and designing smaller switches for the nodes where a
small effective capacitance is seen, could mean an area reduction of the single-slice
Σ∆-modulator.
• The decimation filters should be designed in Verilog/SystemVerilog to investigate
both the area and power consumption of these filters when used in the multi-slice
Σ∆-modulator structure.
• From the state-of-the-art table it can be seen that continuous-time Σ∆-modulator
achieves the highest figure-of-merit.
It would be interesting to investigate the
multi-slice Σ∆-modulator using continuous-time Σ∆-modulators.
• Layout should be made of the single-slice Σ∆-modulator, so that a parasitic ex-
traction can be made. This will show the influence of parasitics to the performance
of the Σ∆-modulator.

• The concept of combining multiple Σ∆-modulators can also be investigated further.
In this thesis the slices are combined by being summed. Another way could be

• A study in how to use a variable resolution Σ∆-modulator for audio applications

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis presents the design of a multi-slice Σ∆-modulator, where combining multi-
ple Σ∆-modulators is used to achieve an increased analog-to-digital converter (ADC)
resolution.
The concept and original motivation of combining multiple oversampling data convert-
ers is presented in the introduction. The scope of the work presented in this thesis is
specified and the learning objectives are presented.
To be able to understand and design the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator the relevant the-
ory behind Σ∆-modulators is presented and the theory of the benefits and limitations
of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator are shown. This includes
The multi-slice Σ∆-modulator has been modelled in Python for two different struc-
tures (cascade of integrators with weighted feedforward summation (CIFF), cascade of
integrators with distributed feedback (CIFB)), two different oversampling ratios (32, 64)
and two loop filter orders (3, 4). To ensure the choice of the correct structure and over-
sampling ratio the performance of the modelled single-slice Σ∆-modulators has been
compared and based on this comparison and an analysis of the structures’ sensitivity to
non-idealities the CIFF Σ∆-modulator with an oversampling ratio of 64 is chosen.
To estimate the complexity of the decimation filters of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator
a multi-stage decimation filter consisting of a decimation by 32 finite impulse response
(FIR) sinc-filter and a half-band polyphase filter are modelled in Python and presented.
The design of the decimation filters include reducing the hardware complexity. Three
scenarios of multi-slice Σ∆-modulator structure are presented and their total digital
hardware complexity is estimated based on the number of adders required to implement
the decimation filters. This lead to a structure where the FIR sinc filters are in the
parallel path but the half-band polyphase filter is not.
To decide on the required loop filter order for the Σ∆-modulator a noise budget is
made for the 3rd and 4th-order CIFF Σ∆-modulator respectively. These noise budgets
sets the specifications for the cascode inverter-based amplifiers used in the integrators
and the input sampling capacitors. From the noise budget an estimated SNR for both
loop filter orders are calculated and the estimated number of slices required to reach the
dynamic range specification is presented. Based on the number of slices required and
the total required capacitor area for both loop filter orders, the 4th-order Σ∆-modulator

96

is chosen as the single-slice Σ∆-modulator.
The schematic of the pseudo-differential 4th-order Σ∆-modulator is then presented to-
gether with a timing diagram. To verify that the schematic corresponds to the desired
Python model a behavioural implementation using VerilogA models for the amplifiers
and quantizers is presented and compared to the ideal Python model. This behavioural
implementation showed that the schematic of the 4th-order Σ∆-modulator implements
the desired noise transfer function.
To implement a transistor-level implementation of the 4th-order Σ∆-modulator, the
design of the switched-capacitor integrators using cascode inverter-based amplifiers is
presented. The design of the 1-bit quantizer used is described and compared to the Ver-
ilogA quantizer used for the behavioural implementation to ensure proper functionality.
The design performance of the transistor-level, single-slice 4th-order Σ∆-modulator is
then presented. The single-slice Σ∆-modulator achieves a dynamic range of 84.2 dB
at a power consumption of 7.9 µW in the typical process corner. The single-slice Σ∆-
modulator is also simulated across global variations including process corners, tempera-
ture and supply voltage. Here the performance of the design is shown to be sensitive to
supply voltage variations. A Monte Carlo analysis is made to investigate local mismatch,
where the designed Σ∆-modulator shows 3σ SNDR variation of 2.16 dB.
The expected performance of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is simulated and evaluated.
Here it shown that 16 slices are required to achieve a dynamic range of 97.7 dB, consum-
ing 127 µW. The multi-slice Σ∆-modulator has also been evaluated with the Python
models of the decimation filters. This evaluation showed that the decimation filters did
not alter the performance of the multi-slice Σ∆-modulator.
The designed multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is compared to the state of the art ADCs. Here
it shows a great performance within audio ADCs with a Schreier dynamic range figure-
of-merit of 179.7 dB. Finally, a list of possible known issues and research topics within
the designed multi-slice Σ∆-modulator is presented.

APPENDIX C
Auto-evaluation of project
process
A brief auto-evaluation is required according to the DTU official guidelines for writing
a Masters thesis. This is to include the original project plan and learning objectives for
the project and a revision of the same. A ”time-guess” was submitted during the first
month of the project and can be seen in Fig. C.1.
The time-guess is divided up on 4 phases (separated by the grey horizontal rows) and
include a 3 week literature study phase, a 7 week Python modelling phase, a 3 week
VerilogA modelling phase, a 8 week transistor-level design week and finally a 5 week
report writing phase. Each phase is concludes by a larger review supervision meeting
where the work made during the phase was presented for the supervisors and additional
actions were given to complete the phase. These milestone weeks are marked by the
vertical ”milestone” columns.
The actual progress of the project was tracked and can be seen in Fig.
C.2.
Here
it can be seen that especially the first 3 phases were followed quite well with the original
time guess. More writing was done during the project than expected, which proved
useful towards the end of the project. During the project mostly figures, equations and
tables were generated down. The only things that were not accomplished was the Ver-
ilog/SystemVerilog modelling of the decimation filters. This is not required to fulfill the
learning objectives for the project, but could have been interesting. Only the first DTU
Electronics Group student seminar was attended in the project, due to prioritizing the
milestone deadlines instead and writing a paper on ultra low-power, low voltage Σ∆-
modulators for the NORCAS 2019 conference in Helsinki and attending the conference
during one of the student seminars. The final design of the Σ∆-modulator was com-
pleted in study week 22, but generating all the simulation results were not completed
until study week 24.
Overall has the project process been good and all the intended work has been com-
pleted.
