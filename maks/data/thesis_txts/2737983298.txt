Abstract
As the importance of transportation continues to rise in today’s increasingly connected
world, accurate sensing of the environment becomes critical in the pursuit of fully au-
tonomous cars. Radar is one of the key enabling technologies in this regard, because of
its ability to perform even in severe weather conditions. The demand for increasing mea-
surement accuracy in these scenarios implies the range, velocity and angle estimates of
a single radar are no longer sufficient: advancements toward distributed radar networks
have become a necessity. This work investigates the optimal way of fusing the data
from multiple Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) Phase Modulated Continuous Wave
(PMCW) radars, and the influence of the parameters of the network on the sensor fusion.
This is done in the presence of uncorrelated phase noise and for for static, isotropic point
targets, where only a one-dimensional angular estimation was considered.
The recently developed phase noise compensation technique for Single-Input Single-
Output (SISO) PMCW radar networks is extended to the MIMO PMCW radar network.
A first network processing method used in this work is the Spatial Matched Filter (SMF).
A mathematical model is proposed to show the difference between this approach on a
monostatic path and bistatic path. The monostatic path implies the received reflection at
a radar comes from its own emitted signal, whereas this reflection comes from the signal
emitted by another radar for the bistatic path. The coherent combination of the SMF on
different paths, considering phase offsets between the radars, is investigated. It is found
to produce too many false targets to be usable in practical scenarios, and coherently fus-
ing the data is not advised. A mathematical model is proposed to investigate the cause
of these false targets appearing, and different methods are considered to reduce them.
Backed up by simulations, it is found that the false targets cannot be sufficiently reduced
in automotive scenarios. The non-coherent combination of the SMF from different paths
is investigated but found unable to increase the network resolution compared to a single
radar. A method based on bilateration is investigated, where the inherent angular capa-
bilities of MIMO radars are used to distinguish real from false targets. In this case, it is
found the network can improve the resolution compared to a single radar, under certain
conditions: low number of available antennas, high range resolution, targets close to the
radars. In search of a solution with a better resolution than the SMF, the Iterative Adap-
tive Approach algorithm is investigated, and a processing chain to fuse the data from the
different sensors using this algorithm is proposed. In this case, the network can increase
detection accuracy and reliability compared to a single radar, but not the resolution. How-
ever, the resolution for single paths using this method is found to be far superior to the
previous methods. This comes at the cost of much more expensive computations, as this
algorithm is iterative and needs a big matrix inversion in every iteration.

1
Introduction
1.1
(PMCW) Radar for automotive applications
In today’s globalized and interconnected world, cars have become indispensable. The
ever-evolving automotive industry has, in the last decades, turned its attention to Ad-
vanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) and the dream of realising fully autonomous
cars. Designing such systems is not only desirable from the profit perspective of au-
tomotive companies, however: they are relevant in a broader societal setting as well.
Advancements in autonomous driving would provide the possibility to reduce road acci-
dents and provide mobility options for disadvantaged populations. These advancements
can thus be related to the different sustainable development goals defined by the United
Nations [1], such as ‘9: Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure’, as well as ’10: reduced
inequalities’.
In developing ADAS and autonomously driving vehicles, radar plays a crucial role [2],
[3]. By emitting electromagnetic waves and looking at their reflections, radars can de-
termine the range (through the delay with which the signal returns) and velocity (through
the Doppler effect) in the radial direction, that is, towards or away from the radar [4]. In
the case of a radar with multiple transmit and multiple receive antennas, also deemed
a Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) radar, the angle of different targets with respect
to the radar can also be estimated (through the delay at which the signal arrives at the
different antennas of the radar [4], [5]). These features are key to enabling several ADAS
capabilities, such as adaptive cruise control, lane change assistance, collision avoidance,
emergency braking, blind spot detection, and so on [2]. Compared to other technologies
enabling range, velocity, and angle measurements, the key advantage radar has is its
resistance to bad weather conditions [6]. A list of advantages and disadvantages of dif-
ferent sensors is given in [2]. Figure 1.1 shows an illustration of the ADAS concept and
the associated sensors.

In the context of automotive radar, many different waveforms can be used. Because low
peak power is desired, continuous wave (CW) radars, that continuously transmit a wave-
form, are often preferred to pulsed radar, which sends out pulses with periods of silence
in between. Some popular automotive waveforms include Phase Modulated Continuous
Wave (PMCW), and Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW). The reason this
thesis uses PMCW is the impact of phase noise (PN) on the performance of the radar.
Phase noise is characterized by the short-term deviations from the desired phase that
occur in a signal. The impact of phase noise on a single radar is investigated in [8].
The conclusion made there is that having a large signal bandwidth reduces the impact
of phase noise, because the low-frequency components of the PN occupy only a small
fraction of the bandwidth, and the PN power spectral density is highest at low-frequency
offsets. As PMCW has a higher receiver baseband bandwidth when compared to FMCW,
it is the waveform that is used in this thesis. The tradeoff with PMCW is that the high
signal bandwidth places more stringent requirements on the analog-to-digital converter
(ADC).
Another important parameter is the operating frequency of the radar, as the use of the
electromagnetic spectrum is heavily regulated. These days, newly developed radars op-
erate in the 76-81 GHz band [9], which is why in this thesis, an operating frequency of 79
GHz is used.
1.2
From single radar to radar networks
Although a single radar can estimate range, angle, and radial velocity, it has some lim-
itations. Firstly, the angular resolution of a single radar is fundamentally limited by the
number of antennas [10]. Adding more antennas to a single radar chip increases the
complexity, the size of the module, and the issues associated with coupling between the
antennas. From a cost perspective, it is thus desirable to have as few antennas per
radar as possible. Secondly, the strength with which a target reflects the electromagnetic
waves, characterized by its Radar Cross Section (RCS), will depend on the angle at which
the waves arrive. Having a network of distributed radars introduces spatial diversity [11],
which helps to alleviate this effect, and thus improves the chances of a target being de-
tected. Another fundamental limitation of the single radar is that only the velocity in the
direction of the radar can be measured. Having multiple radars that each give an esti-
mate of the radial velocity with respect to that particular radar then allows to estimate the
velocity vector [12]. Lastly, the complex real-life environments in which automotive radar
is used can introduce so-called ’ghost targets’. These are false targets detected by the
radar that occur because of multipath phenomena: the signal might have different paths
with a varying number of reflections from the radar to the target and back. A single radar
will struggle to make a distinction between real and ghost targets. These four reasons
motivate the use of distributed radar networks, which form the main topic of this thesis.
Though there are plenty of benefits to a radar network, there are also some serious chal-
lenges associated with this concept. The biggest challenge in networked radar is the
synchronization between the radars. After all, to extract the maximum amount of infor-
mation from a radar network, all different propagation paths should be processed: from
all radars to the targets to all other radars. Furthermore, each radar will introduce some
level of phase noise, and depending on the way they are synchronized, the phase noise
generated by different radars can result in a higher level than for the single radar case.
Achieving different degrees of synchronization in time and/or phase between the radars
has been the subject of plenty of research in the past years [12]. Section 3.2 will explore
the synchronization issue in more depth, once the necessary terminology has been intro-

1.3
Problem statement
1.3.1
High-level description
As explained in the previous section, the synchronization challenge in PMCW radar net-
works can be considered to be solved. This opens the opportunity to start investigating
what the optimal way is of combining the information of the different radars, which leads
to the fundamental problem that is investigated in this thesis:
What is the optimal way of combining the information from different radars in a
radar network, to achieve the best resolution for target localization?
To examine this, the thesis will look into different ways of doing the range-angle processing
of the network. An important aspect of this project is that each radar ’sees’ the world
in its own range-angle coordinates. When attempting to combine these, the different
coordinate systems will have to be taken into account and combined into a single, global
coordinate system. Another important aspect arises when a radar processes the reflection
of the emitted signal of another radar. This is called the bistatic propagation path. It is
fundamentally different from the monostatic path, which is the propagation path from a
radar to the target and back to this same radar (section 3.1 provides a visual illustration
of the different propagation paths). The important aspect is that for the bistatic path, the
angle of arrival and angle of departure are different for close targets. This will impact
the angular processing. Lastly, note that the target velocity was not yet mentioned in
this subsection: this is because, to limit the scope of the project, only static targets are
considered, and the velocity thus does not play a role.
To investigate such methods, a MATLAB simulator framework is used that was developed
at the imec company, where the thesis work was carried out. This simulator framework
is explained more deeply in section 3.5. The simulator was already fully implemented for

1.3.2
Brief literature overview
A high-level overview of the different ways the network processing is done in the literature
is given here, to frame the problem. In chapters 4, 5 and 6, which deal with this range-
angle processing, a more in-depth literature review will be provided at the beginning of
the chapter, when the necessary terminology has been introduced.
From the available literature, many papers achieve promising results with some form of
’matched filter’. The idea is to compare, for all possible target locations, the actual re-
ceived signal with the signal that would be received if there were a target there. This can
be implemented in different ways.
The first class of methods is the coherent processing techniques. By coherent processing,
it is meant that the relations between the phases of the different radars are known, and
taken into account. Such methods have only recently been investigated in the literature,
as the required phase synchronization between the different radars had to be investigated
first. [12], [14], and [15] are examples of such methods.
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
3
==PAGE:15==
Though they achieve great resolution, they come with the issue of many spurious peaks
in locations where there are no targets. Chapter 4 will investigate such methods, to see
whether the issue of these spurious peaks can be dealt with by adjusting the radar network
setup, or if it is inherent to the problem. Section 4.1 discusses these papers, in detail, once
this thesis has introduced the necessary background material.
A second class of methods is the non-coherent processing techniques. This means that
the phase relationships between different radars are not taken into account. Within this
class, there are two main ways of doing the sensor fusion. The first option is to non-
coherently combine matched filters for the different propagation paths, as suggested in
[14]. They claim it has reduced resolution compared to coherent fusion, but also reduced
complexity and less spurious peaks. The second option consists of doing detection first
on each propagation path and then combining these detections. [16], for example, per-
forms detection using only the range information, and then uses bilateration to localize
the targets. It suggests using the angular capabilities of individual radars to distinguish
between actual targets and the ghost targets that this method introduces. Chapter 5 inves-
tigates both types of non-coherent processing methods, in an attempt to find out whether
non-coherently combining information from the different radars can result in a better res-
olution for target localization when compared to what a single radar can achieve. Section
5.1 conducts a more in-depth literature review of these methods.
The existing literature on distributed radar networks can predominantly be divided into
these two categories. When looking toward the realm of angular processing methods that
can be used on a single radar, but have not been investigated yet in the context of radar
networks, many other opportunities become available. Such methods will be discussed in
section 6.1, the literature review of chapter 6. As motivated there, one particular algorithm,
the ’Iterative Adaptive Approach’ [17] will be investigated, to see how this algorithm can
be used to combine the information from the different radars in the network.
1.3.3
Scenario and assumptions
To limit the scope of this thesis, this subsection narrows the scenario that will be investi-
gated and explains the assumptions made.
Firstly, regarding the radar network. This thesis considers networks of two or three radars
(with a brief theoretical extension to four radars in chapter 4, which will be clearly indi-
cated), that are distributed along a one-dimensional baseline. The idea is that this rep-
resents the front of a car: the distances between the radars will be chosen to be realistic
in such a scenario. The precise amount of radars and the spacing between them will be
indicated for every simulation, and the impact of the spacing will also be investigated.
Regarding the considered angles: in reality, the position of a target in three-dimensional
space with respect to the radar can be described by the range and two different angles, us-
ing the spherical coordinate system [18]. For simplicity, this thesis only considers radars
with one-dimensional antenna arrays. This can be imagined, for example, to represent
the localization of targets in a plane in front of the car. The antennas on every radar will be
considered isotropic, which means the radiated power is equal in every direction, and no
antenna pattern is taken into account. Section 3.2 will explain the assumptions regarding
the synchronization of the network and the impact this has.
Secondly, regarding the targets, which are assumed to be static in this thesis. Moving
targets will have some non-negligible effects on the angular processing [19], which need
to be compensated. That falls out of the scope of this project. Furthermore, the targets
are assumed to be isotropic point scatterers, meaning their RCS does not depend on the
angle of the incoming electromagnetic waves, and no extended targets are considered.

2.1.1
SISO overview
A high-level overview of a PMCW radar is given in figure 2.1. Ignoring the top and bottom
branches for now, as they will be introduced in section 2.2, the following operating steps
can be distinguished. First, the PMCW radar generates a sequence of symbols or chips,
that come from a discrete set of values. This happens in the ’pseudo-random noise (PRN)
generator’. This sequence is then modulated, depending on the type of code sequence
used, at time instants that are multiples of Tc, the chip time. After upconverting the signal
to the carrier frequency, the waves are then amplified in the power amplifier (PA) and
transmitted on the antenna. This process is repeated continuously.
6
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
==PAGE:18==
After traveling outward and being reflected off the targets, the electromagnetic waves
are then collected by the receive antenna, and amplified by the corresponding low-noise
amplifier. Downconversion is now done by use of the mixer in the receiver branch and
the same local oscillator that was used in the transmitter branch. After passing through
a low-pass filter to reduce interference, the signal is then converted to the digital domain
using an ADC. One known issue with PMCW radar is the spillover: the strong transmitted
signal will also be picked up directly by the receiver. A solution to mitigate this is proposed
in [20], and it will thus not be taken into consideration in this thesis.
The signal then undergoes the digital processing steps required to extract the range and
radial velocity information. A distinction must be made between the different timescales
present. There is the time between different symbols within one code sequence, also
called fast-time. One code sequence S, consisting of Lc fast-time steps, is shown in
figure 2.2, where the different symbols are denoted s1 to sLc. The value of each symbol
is used to modulate the phase of the carrier signal.
Figure 2.2: Structure of one PMCW radar code sequence
There is also the time in between the same emitted symbols in different repetitions of the
entire code sequence, called slow-time. This is illustrated in figure 2.3. The fast time will
contain information about the range, while the slow time will contain information about the
radial velocity through the Doppler effect.
Figure 2.3: PMCW frame structure

After digitization, the received signal is first circularly correlated with the emitted code se-
quence. The correlation is circular because the same sequence is repeated continuously,
as the CW in PMCW stands for a continuous wave radar. This PRN sequence is designed
to provide a circular autocorrelation that is as high as possible at one lag and as low as
possible at all other lags. Subsection 2.1.4 further explains the sequence properties and
why this matters. The result of the autocorrelation then allows to esimate the time delay
that the received sequence has undergone, which is directly proportional to the distance
traveled. The final processing step in the SISO case is then to perform a Fast Fourier
Transform (FFT) to extract the radial velocity, as moving targets will cause a frequency
shift due to the Doppler effect.
More details on the different processing steps can be found in [21].

2.2.2
MIMO modes
To estimate range, velocity, and angle in a MIMO radar, it is necessary to separate the
signals corresponding to different Tx antennas. Many different ways of separating the
waveforms are possible [26], [27]. A few examples, including the mode that is used in the
remainder of the thesis, are listed below:
• Time domain MIMO
• Doppler domain MIMO
• Outer code MIMO
• Range domain MIMO
Time domain MIMO consists of each transmitter emitting their waveform alternatively,
without any overlap in the time domain. This is illustrated in figure 2.5a. Though this
technique is simple and easy to understand, its main drawback is the power loss compared
to other methods: only one power amplifier is active at a given time. Per time window,
there is thus less emitted power when compared to the other MIMO methods, where all
transmitters use their own power amplifier at the same time. Another disadvantage is the
reduction in the unambiguous velocity from (2.7) by a factor of NTx.
With ’Doppler domain MIMO’, different implementations can be meant. In this thesis, it
implies that a linear phase progression is applied amongst the different repetitions of the
code in slow time. An example is shown in figure 2.5b. The different received waveforms
can then be separated in the Doppler domain on the range-Doppler map, as they now map
into different frequency regions of the FFT from (2.3). A disadvantage with this technique
is the reduction in the unambiguous velocity from (2.7) by a factor of NTx, just like in time
domain MIMO, as the Doppler domain is now effectively partitioned between the different
Tx antennas.

3.2
Synchronisation
3.2.1
The synchronisation problem
To correctly process the different mono-and bistatic paths in the network, the radars need
to have some form of time and/or phase synchronisation. This is necessary to separate
the information from different paths, but also impacts the problem of uncorrelated phase
noise: there is no range correlation effect anymore. For any type of CW radar, the range
correlation effect [8] is an effect that attenuates the phase noise for close targets. It orig-
inates from the fact that the received signal is simply a time-delayed version of the trans-
mitted signal, meaning the phase noise on both signals is related. As explained in section
2.1, the radar works by using a reference signal, at the carrier frequency. This reference
carrier signal is used both to up-convert the transmitted signal, and to down-convert the
received signal. For short ranges, and thus short propagation delays, the phase noise
on the reference signal when receiving is still correlated with the phase noise that was
on the reference signal when transmitting, which is this range correlation effect. When
making the extension to a radar network, however, this effect might no longer be present.
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
21
==PAGE:33==
If each radar generates its own waveform independently from the other radars using its
own reference signal, there is no longer a range correlation effect, as explained in [13].
This leads to the term ’uncorrelated phase noise’, which is a major problem for FMCW
radar networks. Consequentially, it will become harder to detect different targets due to
an increased noise floor.
For PMCW radar, however, this is less of an issue. The reason for this is given in [8].
It is explained there that the large receiver baseband bandwidth of PMCW radar means
the low-frequency components of the PN occupy only a small fraction of the bandwidth,
and the PN power spectral density is highest at low frequency offsets. In the range do-
main, the PN appears as white Gaussian noise for the APAS codes due to lack of any
correlation between the binary phase code and the complex exponential representing the
phase noise. For the P3 codes, the PN energy is concentrated in a few range bins around
the target, and quickly becomes negligible further away. For both P3 and APAS, the high
receiver sampling rate implies that the range correlation effect only appears for very short
ranges. For typical PMCW radar parameters and typical PN power spectral densities, and
for P3 and APAS codes, the PN is thus not an issue. The absence of range correlation
effects in the radar network does not matter, since these effects only play a role for very
close targets in the monostatic case anyway.
3.2.2
Current solutions
Different approaches currently exist to do the synchronisation between the different radars
and mitigate the effects of uncorrelated phase noise. One approach is to share the same
high-frequency reference signal, that thus has the same phase noise, between all the
radars, using e.g. optical fiber cables [14] or polymer microwave fiber [32]. This will intro-
duce correlated phase noise, but the problem with such methods is that these cables are
very sensitive and might be unfit for an automotive environment, with a lot of mechanical
vibrations. Another approach is the use of radar repeater networks [15], where there is
one radar that generates the waveforms, and the repeater elements simply emit (repeat)
the received signal. Though this helps in the sense that these repeater elements do not
introduce their own phase noise, this method can result in ghost targets appearing.
Yet another approach is to share a low-frequency reference signal (in the MHz range), as
these signals are easier to transmit through cables. This will also ensure that the centre
frequency is the same for each radar, and there will not be any carrier frequency offset
(CFO). Each radar then separately has its own phase locked loop (PLL) to multiply the
frequency locally, and the resulting phase noise might be correlated or uncorrelated: this
depends on whether the dominant source of the phase noise is the shared low-frequency
oscillator signal or the PLL [33].
3.2.3
Approach used in this thesis
The approach used for synchronisation in this thesis is the one where a low-frequency
reference signal is shared between the radars, such that there is no CFO. Additionally, it
will be considered that there is no timing offset between the radars. In practice, the direct
path can be used to calibrate this, as we know the distance between the two radars and
thus the time that this signal travels from one radar to the other. As for the phase noise: the
worst-case scenario is considered, where the dominant contribution to the PN comes from
the PLL, resulting in uncorrelated phase noise. Furthermore, a compensation method for
the uncorrelated PN in PMCW radar networks has recently been proposed [34]. This
compensation method will be explained in section 3.4, alongside an investigation of the
impact of PN.

3.4
Uncorrelated phase noise
3.4.1
Impact and compensation technique
As mentioned in section 3.2, this thesis considers the worst case scenario from a phase
noise perspective: the case where the PLL has the dominant contribution to phase noise,
which results in uncorrelated phase noise in the network. This will form a ridge in the
range-Doppler map along the Doppler dimension [13], in the range bins corresponding to
the bistatic and direct signals. This ridge can obscure the weaker targets with a different
velocity at the same bistatic range, which is why it needs to be compensated.

profile for a radar network. The used Power Spectral Density (PSD) in the simulations

was used. This figure shows the range profile of radar 1, for a simulation setup shown
in figure 3.6, and each radar has 2 Tx and 2 Rx antennas. As shown in figure 3.1, the
convention used here is to number the radars increasing along the y-axis: radar 1 is

the phase noise and range correlation effect, [13] proposes a method to compensate
these ridges, in the SISO radar network case. The idea is to extract the slow-time phase
vector from the range bin of the direct path. This happens after range processing, but
before Doppler processing. The vector then contains the phase noise, and this phase is
subtracted from the bistatic range-slow-time matrix. This results in the introduction of a
new correlation effect, that is not limited by the monostatic range, but by the difference in
propagation delay of the bistatic and direct path. This thesis will now verify whether this
method is applicable to MIMO radar networks as well.

3.4.2
Extension to MIMO radar networks
As mentioned in the previous subsection, a method has has been proposed in [13] to limit
the impact of uncorrelated phase in the context distributed PMCW radars. This work has,
however, only been done for a network of SISO radars. As the core of this MSc thesis is
to investigate a network MIMO PMCW radars, the work from [13] has been extended to
MIMO radars.
To extend this method, it is applied as follows. First, the data is split into the mono-
and bistatic parts. At that point, there are NRx different range-slow-time matrices. Every
bistatic range-slow-time map now contains NTx different direct paths. These different
direct paths all contain, in principle, the same phase noise. This follows from the archi-
tecture from figure 3.3. The phase on the different direct paths will, however, also have
a phase offset from the different distance travelled. This can be seen from e.g. (2.18),
with θ = ±90 deg, which is the case for the direct paths as the radars are all on the same
baseline.
Care should therefore be taken to not compensate every separate Tx-Rx bistatic range-
slow-time map with its own direct path. This would result in applying the phase offset
from the direct path to the bistatic range-slow-time bin, introducing the ±90 deg steering
vector into the bistatic bin, which should only contain the steering vector from the target.
To correctly extend [13] to the MIMO radar network case, therefore, the phase extracted
from a single direct path bin per transmit radar should be used to compensate all the
different bistatic Tx-Rx paths coming from that transmit radar.

3.5.2
Extension to MIMO PMCW radar networks
This thesis extended the simulator framework by adding functionality to support a network
of an arbitrary amount of MIMO radars. To extend this, a loop was programmed over
all of the different radars. First, the transmitter baseband and RF front-end blocks are
used for each radar. The propagation was then modified to be separated into a forward
and backward propagation phase. It is first carried out for each forward path: from the
radars to the targets. Afterwards, the backward propagation phase is simulated: from
the targets to all radars. This models the mono-and bistatic paths. The direct path is
simulated separately after this. Once this has been done, the signals are summed at the
receiver of every radar. The receiver RF and baseband front-end blocks are then used in
the same way as described in the previous subsection.

3.6.2
Method
General idea
The concept of a matched filter is widely used for many different applications, since it is the
optimal linear filter, in the sense that maximises the SNR when there is additive stochastic
noise [36]. The core idea is to correlate a known signal with an unknown signal, to check
whether the known signal is present or not in the unknown signal [37]. One example
of this is, in fact, the range processing of a PMCW radar, where the received signal is
correlated with a delayed version of itself, to find which delay ’matches’ the received signal
best, which in turn leads to the range estimation. Another example of this is the Fourier
transform used for Doppler processing: (2.3) can be interpreted as a correlation with the
signal if the Doppler frequency corresponds to one of the Fourier transform frequencies.
The idea of the SMF, which is also referred to as the radar ambiguity function [11], is
to correlate, for every possible target location, the actual received signal with what the
received signal would theoretically be if there was a target at that location.
Interpolation
To carry out this SMF, a coordinate system must be defined. In the single radar monostatic
case, a natural choice would be the range-angle coordinate system with regards to the
radar. After all, the common way of processing a MIMO radar is to do range-Doppler
processing, detect the range-Doppler cells in which a target is present, and then perform
angular processing on these range-Doppler cells along the different antennas [30]. This
results in an estimate of targets with a certain range, radial velocity, and angle with respect
to a certain radar.
To merge the output of different radar sensors, however, one common coordinate system
is necessary. In this thesis, a global Cartesian coordinate system consisting of an x-and
y-axis, as depicted in figure 3.1. This is the method proposed in [14]. In that paper,

3.7
Summary
This chapter introduced a model for a distributed radar network. After introducing the
naming convention in figure 3.1, the monostatic path (from radar i to target and back to
radar i), bistatic path (from radar i to target to radar j) and the direct path (radar i to radar
j) were defined in figure 3.2. Subsequently, the synchronisation problem was tackled.
This thesis uses a low-frequency shared reference signal, which every radar individually
up-converts using its own PLL. The worst-case scenario of a dominant PLL for PN is
considered, leading to uncorrelated PN in the network. The impact on the radar network
when using PMCW radar and using the range domain to separate the different radars and
antennas was then shown to be limited. The recently introduced compensation method
in SISO PMCW radar networks to compensate the ridge along the Doppler dimension
due to uncorrelated PN was then extended to the MIMO PMCW network case. The next
section illustrated the simulator framework used in this thesis. The final section then
introduced the concept of the Spatial Matched Filter on a single mono-or bistatic path in
the network. After extending the signal model to take near-field effects of the network
into account, it was explained that this thesis carries out the SMF after range processing,
similar to the way backprojection works in SAR. The interpolation from the different local
range-angle coordinate grids to the global Cartesian coordinate system using a rectangle
function was explained, and a mathematical model was proposed for the SMF method.
This model was then validated using simulations, where it was found that this method
results in a sinc-shape alongside a circle of constant range for a monostatic path, and a
more irregular product of sincs alongside an ellipse for the bistatic path in the near-field.

4
Coherent Processing of the Network
This chapter introduces a first approach to fuse the different mono-and bistatic paths in
the radar network. It starts with a literature review of current coherent processing meth-
ods in section 4.1. The most basic implementation of a coherent SMF from [14] is then
adapted to the radar network in this thesis, and given in section 4.2, together with simula-
tion results. Following this, section 4.3 investigates different modifications to the network
setup, in an attempt to reduce the issues that come with this method, which will turn out
to be grating lobes. It checks whether the methods that change the distance between the
radars and the number of radars, as proposed in [15], adequately reduce these issues.
It also quantifies the effect of using two different carrier frequencies, as proposed in [14].
Furthermore, it checks the impact of the number of antennas, the impact of windowing,
and methods where the mono-and bistatic paths are coherently processed in pairs and
combined afterwards. Lastly, section 4.4 summarises this chapters findings.
The contributions this chapter adds to the existing literature are as follows. It provides a
mathematical model of the grating lobes for the spatial matched filter approach, specif-
ically for a coherent distributed radar network. Among the mitigation methods for these
grating lobes, the pairwise comparison of individual paths in the network was not found
in the literature. Another contribution is the combination of different mitigation methods,
and the discussion on why the author considers this approach can not work in realistic
automotive scenarios.
4.1
Literature overview
In the literature, there is a lot of focus on how to achieve different degrees of coherency in
a radar network. Information on what can be achieved once this full phase coherency is
reached is, however, more scarce. With coherent processing of the network, it is meant
here that the angular processing takes into account the phase differences between all dif-
ferent possible antenna paths in the radar network. The reason for coherently processing
the radar network is that all possible information is used, even the phase offsets between
the different radars, which should lead to a higher resolution. This is the objective of
all approaches listed below, which all suffer from the same problem: a large amount of
significant sidelobes.
[12] approaches the problem from a far-field virtual array point of view. They utilize a setup
of widely (when compared to the wavelength) separated MIMO radars, just as in this the-
sis. They claim that using all propagation paths for angular processing would result in
a very fine interference pattern with high sidelobes because fully coherent processing of
the network comes down to the processing of one big sparse antenna array. They there-
fore proceed by processing the different monostatic paths separately. They do process
the bistatic paths coherently, though, and are able to gain an improvement in resolution,
compared to the single paths. The reason for this is their use of mirrored antenna arrays.
In the far-field, when the virtual array concept holds, the virtual array antenna positions
can be calculated by the sum of the coordinates of the physical antenna positions, as
explained in section 2.3.2. By mirroring the antenna arrays from one radar compared to
the other, the virtual array positions of the different bistatic paths no longer overlap. This
way, they are able to create a virtual array with twice the aperture. Eventually, this leads
to an improvement in the resolution of a factor two, by only considering the bistatic paths.
The mirroring of the antenna layout is not considered further here, for the following reason.
34
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
==PAGE:46==
The virtual array they create with double the aperture is located in between the two radars,
while in this thesis, scenarios with 3 radars are considered as well. Furthermore, this the-
sis aims to combine all different mono-and bistatic paths, and not just the bistatic paths.
[14] utilizes a setup of single antenna Tx or Rx radar elements, that are widely separated.
This means they only have bistatic responses, which they then process simultaneously
by compensating for the phase difference of every path. To this end, the paper introduces
the formula for the coherent SMF approach that forms the basis for the investigations in
this chapter. The paper also states the issue of having numerous high sidelobes around
the main peak and characterises this level by stating simulation and measurement num-
bers for the peak-to-maximum sidelobe ratio (PMSR) and peak-to-average sidelobe ratio.
Furthermore, it includes simulation and measurement results for a dual frequency band
operation mode as well, which slightly reduces these sidelobes. In the single band case,
their PMSR amounts to 4 dB, and in the dual band case, it becomes 5 dB. [40] uses the
same approach, though not for an automotive scenario, and achieves great resolution,
when the figures are shown in linear scale. Resolution for radar systems should be inves-
tigated in dB scale however, because of the large dynamic range in response strength.
In dB scale, it seems as if they too have the issue of high sidelobes.
Yet another approach to fully coherent processing is given in [15]. There, a network is set
up by using a single MIMO radar and multichannel repeaters. Similar issues were found
there: numerous high sidelobes. The paper suggests some design recommendations:
using at least three subarrays, using sparse arrays for the subarrays, and modifying the
distance between the subarrays. Though these recommendations allow them to achieve
a PMSR of 6.8 dB, they then resolve to use sparse array processing methods to reduce
the sidelobes further. Such sparse array techniques are another approach to the angular
processing of a radar network. [41], for example, introduces such techniques for a two-
radar system.
What is missing in the literature is an evaluation of whether coherently processing the
network can work in a realistic automotive scenario, even with the described issues, and
whether the potential benefits of such a system are worth the effort required to establish a
fully coherent system. This chapter will start from an SMF over the full network, based on
the proposed method from [14], in an attempt to gain insight into the problems described
above and investigate different methods of negating these issues.

Section 4.2 found that the simple coherent SMF does not improve the resolution compared
to the single path SMF, because of the grating lobes, which are nearly as high as the main
peak. An attempt is now made to see if these grating lobes can be reduced by changing
the network setup, using different methods. The methods were only attempted in a far-
field situation. This was done as a starting point, because the equations developed in the
previous section can be used to intuitively understand the different attempted methods.
Furthermore, it will be shown that none of the methods work adequately well in far-field,
and thus the more complex near-field scenario was not tested.
It was verified that (4.2) is a correct approximation for the region around targets in the
far-field of the network, by comparing it with results from the the simulation flow of figure
3.9. As this is indeed the case, the different methods in this section can be tested by
simply applying them to (4.2): this allows for faster simulation and testing, while still pro-
viding valid results for the region around a far-field target where the monostatic circles and
bistatic ellipses from the SMF overlap. No additive noise or phase noise was simulated
in this section. As will be seen, none of the methods work adequately well even in the
absence of noise, which is why its impact was not characterized. The simulations are run
for a single target in the far-field at an angle of θ = 10 degrees from the radars.
4.3.1
Pairwise combination of the individual paths
Method
To the best of the author’s knowledge, this method has not yet been attempted when
coherently processing a radar network. A pairwise combination of the individual paths
allows us to utilise (4.6) to predict where the grating lobes will be. As can be seen in
that equation, the grating lobes will occur in different positions for different combinations
of paths, as Dik + Dhl will be different. For the final spectrum, one can at each location
take the minimum of the spectra of these coherent pairs. After all, at the target location,
all different spectra have the same peak, and taking the minimum is the same as taking
the value of any of the spectra. At other locations, however, taking the minimum should
ensure maximal grating lobe reduction. The grating lobes in the resulting spectrum should
be reduced since the grating lobes of the individual spectra are in different locations.

4.3.7
Discussion
The conclusion here is that it is not recommended to put in the extra effort of constructing
a fully coherent network. Coherent processing results in too many grating lobes that
cannot adequately be suppressed. If detection is taken into account, they can therefore
not improve the resolution. As mentioned before, this conclusion contradicts the literature
[14]. The main reason for this is detection is not considered there, and they do not consider
targets that would not be equally strong reflectors. The different suppression methods can
reduce the grating lobes by up to 12.9 dB. A one-to-one comparison with the literature is
not possible, because the level to which they can be suppressed depends on the specific
network setup used. It can be seen, however, that these methods are in the same range as
the PMSR of [14], of 4 dB, and the PMSR of 6.8 dB from [15]. The achieved suppression
in this thesis is higher than these numbers because of the higher amount of radars and
antennas, and because of the combination of different suppression methods. Chapter 5
will now investigate whether a network setup can provide an improvement in resolution
when combining the different paths in a non-coherent way.

4.4
Summary
This chapter investigated the sensor fusion of a fully coherent MIMO radar network. First,
a coherent SMF over the full network was attempted that compensates for the phase of
every Tx-Rx antenna path, within and between radars. It was found that this method intro-
duces many spurious peaks, which were found to be grating lobes, rendering it unusable
in practical scenarios, when a peak-based detection algorithm is used. A mathematical
derivation of these grating lobes in a far-field scenario was given. Different variations of
this method were then tested: as suggested in [15], changing the distance between the
radars, and the number of radars, as suggested in [14], changing the carrier frequency.
Furthermore, changing the number of antennas, and computing the spectrum by taking
the minimum value of different coherent pairs of mono-and bistatic paths was also inves-
tigated. While these methods are able to suppress the grating lobes, their performance
was found to be insufficient in realistic scenarios. They would thus not be able to provide
an improvement in resolution compared to the single path. With resolution, the separa-
bility of different targets, where one might produce a weaker reflection than the other, is
meant. It was found that it is not possible to sufficiently suppress the grating lobes, to
avoid many potential false alarms when performing detection. The grating lobes could be
suppressed up to 12.9 dB, which is smaller than the potential 30 dB difference in reflectiv-
ity that targets may have in automotive scenarios. This chapter therefore concluded that
making the effort to establish a fully coherent radar network is not worth the benefits from
a resolution standpoint.

Non-Coherent Processing of the

Based on the findings from chapter 4, this chapter investigates whether non-coherent pro-

starts with a literature overview in section 5.1, after which the method of non-coherently
combining the matched filter outputs from [14] is introduced and evaluated in section 5.2.
Afterwards, the bilateration method from [16] is introduced in section 5.3. The equations
from [16] are extended to a more general case, and this section investigates when bilat-
eration can provide an improved resolution. Lastly, section 5.4 summarises the findings
from this chapter, and motivates the possibility of moving on to different methods in chap-
ter 6.
The contributions this chapter adds to the existing literature are as follows. It applies the
non-coherent combination method from [14] to the radar network in this thesis, modifying
it to become a partially coherent spatial matched filter, and compares the results with the
existing literature in section 5.2. Furthermore, the idea from [16], to use the inherent an-
gular capabilities to select the correct targets after bilateration is validated by an example.
The equivalent resolution from this paper is extended, as the equations are derived for a
more general scenario in section 5.3.
5.1
Literature overview
Different non-coherent processing methods are investigated in the literature. These can
broadly be divided into two categories. The first one is to do a non-coherent combination
of the data before doing detection, and the second is to do the non-coherent combining
after doing detection on the individual paths.
In the first class of methods, [46] introduces a non-coherent ambiguity function on a MIMO
radar with widely separated antennas, and [14] applies this equation to a photonics-based
radar network, simulating and measuring the responses. The findings from [14] are that
the non-coherent combination provides a worse resolution than the coherent processing,
especially in the cross-range dimension. They suggest to use the non-coherent ambigu-
ity function as a way to continuously provide target detection on the coverage volume,
and then use coherent processing around the detected targets for higher resolution. The
benefits of this approach are that it is a computationally efficient way to combine the in-
formation from different radars. The downside is that they report poor resolution. Their
radar network consists of widely separated single antennas. In the case of this thesis, the
radar network consists of separate radar units, that each have a set of Tx and Rx anten-
nas. Therefore, a partially coherent combination becomes possible, where the individual
mono-and bistatic radar paths are processed coherently as in subsection 3.6.2, but then
the paths are incoherently summed together. This should help improve the cross-range
resolution compared to [14], as there will now be an angular profile along every monos-
tatic circle or bistatic ellipse, as explained in subsection 3.6.2. Section 5.2 will investigate
this, as well as whether non-coherently combining the different path SMF’s can improve
the resolution, when compared to a single path SMF.
48

In the second class of methods, one approach is given in [47], where the authors propose
a minimum mean squared error estimator based on multilateration to estimate target posi-
tions. They incorporate both monostatic and bistatic distance measures in this estimator,
and start from the assumption that it is known beforehand which mono-and bistatic ranges
belong to one target. For this, they refer to [48], where the authors are able to assign mea-
surements to different targets by using a multihypothesis algorithm, assigning probabilities
to the different hypotheses based on the accuracy of the measurements and the tracking
of different targets using Kalman filters. This approach was made for radar networks of
widely distributed SISO radar sensors. Therefore, this method would not fully utilise the
inherent angular capabilities of the different MIMO radars in this thesis. [16], on the other
hand, suggests a different approach to multilateration. They use a setup with 3 radars on
a baseline, and 2 targets at symmetric azimuth angles ±θ from the middle radar. They
then compare the separability of these 2 targets when using the middle radar, assumed to
be a MIMO radar, with the separability of these 2 targets by using the range information
of the 2 outer radars. These 2 outer radars will have monostatic iso-range circles: by
looking at the intersection points of these circles, the targets can then be located using
bilateration. The authors suggest that if these 2 radars have inherent angular process-
ing capabilities as well, the intersection points of the circles where no actual targets are
present can be filtered out. Compared to [47], they therefore use bilateration instead of a
minimum mean squared error estimator, and assume it is possible to use individual MIMO
radars to remove ghost targets, instead of using a multihypothesis algorithm.
In this chapter, section 5.3 will verify whether bilateration can improve the resolution when
compared to a partially coherent matched filter. The approach from [16] is used, since [47]
does not utilise the inherent angular capabilities of the different MIMO radars. Further-
more, the equations from [16] will be generalised to investigate the separability of targets
at non-symmetric azimuth angles, which will allow to assess the potential resolution im-
provement of this method.

5.3
Bilateration
5.3.1
Method
As discussed in 5.1, the multilateration method investigated in this thesis is the one in-
troduced in [16]: the authors use bilateration on a network of SISO radars, only look at
the monostatic ranges, and suggest that ghost targets could be removed if the radars are
not SISO but MIMO. The concept is illustrated in figure 5.7: by doing detection on the
range-doppler map, the ranges at which targets are present can be found. These ranges
can then be mapped into the monostatic circles or the bistatic ellipses. The intersection
points can then be calculated, and these represent the possible targets.
Figure 5.7: Illustration of the bilateration principle
In principle, both monostatic and bistatic ranges could be used for the bilateration. This
thesis, however, only uses the monostatic ranges. The reason for this is the following.
Consider there is a target at a certain range r1 from radar 1. Now consider we move
a point across an isorange circle from radar 1, away from this target. The monostatic
range from radar 2 is given by r2, and the bistatic range, as explained before, is given
by r1+r2
2
. As we move across the isorange cicle, r1 will be constant. Therefore, the
monostatic range from radar 2 will change twice as fast as the bistatic range. We will
have to move a larger distance before reaching a point that will fall in a different bistatic
range bin, when compared to a point that will fall in a different monostatic range bin for
radar 2. The resolution using bilateration on an isorange circle from one radar will thus
be better when we use the monostatic range to the other radar to distinguish between the
targets, compared to when we use the bistatic range.
This thesis only uses a two radar setup to investigate the bilateration. The reason is similar
to why bilateration is used instead of multilateration, as explained in section 5.1: if more
than two radars would be used, the isorange circles will not perfectly cross at one point,
because they are inaccurate, as the range resolution is not infinitely small. Combining
more than two radars simulateneously therefore requires, just like combining more than
2 mono-and bistatic paths, a multilateration algorithm such as the one proposed in [47].

Equivalent resolution
Subsection 5.3.2 showed that, in one specific scenario, the bilateration method could
provide an improved resolution when compared to the matched filter method. This sec-
tion investigates if it is possible to estimate in what other scenarios it could provide an
improvement.
Investigating the resolution in all possible directions is a complex problem. From the
viewpoint of one radar, an angular resolution, tangential to circles of constant range, can
be defined, as well as a range resolution in the radial direction. These tangential and radial
directions will be different for the different radars, when seen from the global coordinate
system.
What this section will investigate, is when the bilateration method described before can
improve the resolution of a single radar. That is, the resolution of a single radar in the radial
direction will remain limited by the range resolution, but the resolution in the tangential
direction can be improved by bilateration. This will indicate in what scenarios having
a radar network and using bilateration will improve the resolution, when compared to a
single radar case. It does not provide the possibility of making general statements about
the resolution in all directions when considering the network.

Summary
This chapter started off with a literature overview, in which different methods of non-
coherently fusing the data from the different radars were discussed. Afterwards, the par-
tially coherent matched filter, based on [14], was investigated. The next section discussed
the bilateration method suggested in [16]. It was demonstrated with an example that this
method can provide an improved resolution compared to a single radar. The equations
for the equivalent resolution using bilateration from [16] were extended to a more general
case, which allowed to compare the bilateration resolution with the single radar resolution.
These two methods of non-coherent angular processing both have their own issues. The
partially coherent SMF cannot provide an improvement in resolution in far-field or near-
field, when compared to a single radar SMF. This is because the overlap between all of
the monostatic circles and bistatic ellipses will create a large ambiguous zone, wherein it
is no longer possible to know which peaks belong to targets and which peaks are artifacts.
The bilateration method, on the other hand, can only provide an improvement in angular
resolution if a very high range resolution is achievable, and even then, only in scenarios
where a low number of antennas is available. It is most effective in the near-field, at short
distances, in the region in between the radars. In this thesis, the bilateration only com-
bines the monostatic information from 2 radars. Approaches to combine more radars and
bistatic information using multilateration exists as well. These require a multihypothesis
algorithm, however, to estimate which monostatic and bistatic range bins belong to the
same target. Since no multihypothesis algorithm was found that can take advantage of the
inherent angular capabilities of the MIMO radars considered in this thesis, this approach
was not investigated.
Concluding this chapter, the non-coherent resolution methods that were investigated probed
to be inadequate to (significantly) improve the resolution of the network compared to a sin-
gle radar. Chapter 4 already concluded that the coherent SMF combination comes with
its own insurmountable challenges for distributed radar networks, and can therefore not
be used either. Based on these findings, chapter 6 will investigate a different method from
the SMF, to see if another avenue of angular processing can provide more fruitful results.

Iterative Adaptive Approach
In the previous chapters, the methods were based on different ways of using a spatial
matched filter on the different mono-and bistatic paths. In chapter 4, the different paths
were combined coherently. Chapter 5 investigated non-coherently summing them, or
using bilateration, where the partially coherent SMF was used to eliminate ghost targets.
These chapters started from the SMF method, because of the promising results from the
literature when using this method. This chapter investigates approaches other than the
spatial matched filter to do the single path processing, starting from the angular processing
of a single radar, and extending this to a radar network. It starts with a literature overview in
section 6.1, where different methods are explored, and the choice to use the IAA algorithm
is motivated. Section 6.2 then explains the workings of this algorithm for a monostatic
path, and explains an approach that can be used to utilise the algorithm on a bistatic path
as well. Furthermore, it introduces different methods to combine the information from the
different paths, as well as a detection algorithm that can be used on a single path. It
finished with the full proposed processing chain for the radar network. Section 6.3 then
introduces the results: after discussing the simulation method, it compares the results for
the different ways of combining the paths. Some considerations regarding resolution are
then explained, after which the different types of possible errors are introduced. The last
two subsections are dedicated for error characterisation on the single path and on the
network respectively. The chapter then summarises the findings in section 6.4.
The contributions from this chapter to the existing literature are as follows. A method is
proposed to carry out the IAA algorithm in the near-field on bistatic paths, and different
ways of combining the IAA outputs of the different paths are investigated. Furthermore,
the effects of the radar network parameters on the IAA performance are characterised.
6.1
Literature overview
In the literature, the topic of estimating the direction of arrival (DOA) from an array model
like (2.21) has received a lot of attention over the past decades. This generic problem is
not specific to radar, but also has applications in areas like ultrasound beam formation, au-
dio noise suppression, and many more. Popular methods can be divided into two classes:
parametric and non-parametric. An overview of different angular estimation methods for
a single MIMO radar is given in [5].
Some popular parametric methods for DOA estimation are the MUSIC algorithm [50] and
the ESPRIT algorithm [51]. These methods can provide much better resolution for the
same number of antennas when compared to the matched filter approach. The problem
with such algorithms is that they are based on the signal covariance matrix. This matrix will
be rank-deficient in case of coherent signals, which is the case with a radar. The reason
for this is that the reflected signals from different sources come from the same transmitted
signal. This has to be dealt with, using spatial smoothing [52]. The spatial smoothing tries
to improve the rank of the signal covariance matrix by combining the covariance matrices
using only a subset of the antennas. Since only a subset of the antennas is used, the
effective aperture is reduced, which reduces the amount of identifiable targets [53]. This
is problematic for this thesis, because the goal in automotive radar networks is to achieve
good resolution using a network of radars that have as few antennas as possible while
achieving good resolution. Furthermore, these methods often require a lot of different
snapshots for optimal performance, which are not available in a fast-changing automotive
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
63
==PAGE:75==
environment. This thesis therefore turns its attention towards the class of non-parametric
methods. Some popular methods include the conventional beamforming, which is equiva-
lent to the spatial matched filter without the interpolation: it is a matched filter across angle
in one particular range bin. Other methods include the compressed sensing approach.
[41] is such an example where, similar to this thesis, an attempt is made to combine the
data in a distributed radar network, but using compressed sensing algorithms. The ben-
efits found there are good single snapshot performance, and an algorithm that is able to
combine the different mono-and bistatic paths, called fusion FOCUSS. The drawback with
compressed sensing is that the performance is sensitive to targets appearing off the grid
[5], which is why it is not further considered in this thesis.
The remainder of the chapter will instead focus on another non-parametric method: the
iterative adaptive approach (IAA) algorithm [17]. The method will be explained in section
6.2. Its advantages are that it is a nonparametric and hyperparameter free approach,
which shows good performance even with a single snapshot, and works for coherent
sources. Its drawback is the high computational cost: it is an iterative algorithm, that
includes a large matrix inversion in every step, as will be explained in section 6.2. This
IAA algorithm has been used in the literature for DOA estimation [17], and for MIMO
radar range-angle Doppler imaging of a single radar [54]. Another application where it has
successfully been used is in the joint DOA and direction of departure (DOD) estimation, to
eliminate ghost targets due to a multipath environment [55]. The remainder of this thesis
will now start from the IAA algorithm as proposed in [17], and investigate how to apply this
to a radar network. Furthermore, it will characterise its performance in a radar network
setting.

6.2.2
Combining the paths
The previous subsection explained how the IAA is carried out for a single path. This results
in range-angle profiles in the local coordinate systems: along a circle for the monostatic
paths, or an ellipse for the bistatic paths. These different range-angle profiles must now
somehow be combined, in order to fuse the data from different radars.
A first way of doing that is to sum all different range-angle profiles in the global coordinate
system, similar to the non-coherent sum from chapter 5. For the bistatic paths, these
coordinates are calculated in the process of determining the steering vector, as seen in
(6.9) and (6.7). For the monostatic paths, the coordinates in the global system can easily
be calculated since we know the range and angle to the radar, as well as the radar’s
location. As will be shown, in section 6.3, this way of combining the paths is not optimal.
Another option is to do detection on the individual range-angle profiles, and then map
only these detection points to the global coordinate system. Clustering can then be used
to merge the different detections into a combined, better estimate of the target location.
This thesis explores the use of two different clustering algorithms: the K-means clustering
algorithm [58] and the DBscan algorithm [59].
6.2.3
Detection
As mentioned in the previous subsection, one way of combining the different mono-and
bistatic paths is to do detection on the separate paths first, and then cluster the detec-
tions in the global coordinate system. To do this detection, this thesis uses the Bayesian
Information Criterion (BIC) algorithm proposed in [17].
The basic idea of this algorithm is to keep including more peaks from the IAA spectrum,
to better fit the data, until the benefits of adding another peak are sufficiently offset by a
penalty term, to avoid overfitting. A good introduction to model order selection is provided
in [60]. This complex topic, however, could be a thesis on its own, and the details fall
outside of the scope of this thesis. Suffice to say that for the detection, two other possibil-
ities were considered: the generalised likelihood ratio test proposed in [61], and a simple
selection of all peaks that are above a fixed threshold below the largest peak in the range-
angle profile. It was empirically determined that the BIC provides the best performance
out of the three in this context, consistent with the findings from [54].

them. It becomes clear from this figure that this is not the optimal way of doing so. Figure

map, as there are peaks in different locations due to the overlapping profiles that do not

The problem is that the IAA is carried out along a single, angular dimension, in range
bins where a target has been detected, resulting in different range-angle profiles in the
local coordinate systems. This in contrary to the SMF method from section 3.6, where
the SMF was performed for every grid point in the global coordinates, and rectangular
interpolation was used to solve the mismatch with the local coordinate systems. This
is not possible for the IAA method, as it needs to be calculated along a grid of angles,
for the entire range-angle cut at once. These range-angle cuts can be converted to a
global coordinate grid, but this conversion will be inaccurate, due to the range resolution.
This means they will not overlap at the correct locations. Furthermore, the precision with
which they are converted to the global coordinate grid severely impacts the results. If a
very fine conversion is utilised, the shape of the range-angle profiles might be preserved,
but they will not overlap at the correct locations, and if the conversion is very precise, the
adjacent range-angle points will not even be in adjacent pixels in the global grid. If a very
coarse resolution is used, the peaks of the range-angle profiles might overlap, but the
shapes will be distorted, as some range-angle points might be mapped onto the same
global coordinate point. These are the reasons why this way of combining the different
IAA results was not further considered, and instead, attention was turned towards doing
the detection on individual paths, and then clustering the detections.

6.3.3
Achievable resolution and impact of grid size
The example in the previous subsection raises the question of the kind of resolution that is
achievable using the IAA method. Let us first look at the achievable resolution of the IAA
on a single path: as the spectrum is evaluated on a discrete angular grid, this indicates
that the grid size will have an impact on the achievable resolution. However, decreasing
the space between the grid points amounts to increasing the amount of columns in the
matrix A and ˆ
P from algorithm 1, which in turn leads to a larger matrix R to invert, severely
increasing the computational cost.
It was empirically determined that the grid must be oversampled compared to the de-
sired resolution. For example: if we want to be able to distinguish two targets that are
2° apart, the grid sampling size should be a few times smaller than 2°. In this thesis,
the grid size was therefore set to 0.25°.The reason is that a resolution better than 2 de-
grees is not necessary for an automotive setting, where it is most important to be able to
distinguish between different objects in front of the car, but this 2° resolution would be a
significant improvement compared to the methods from the previous chapters. In practi-
cal scenarios, however, the grid size should be chosen application-dependant: what kind
of computational power and memory storage is available, and what kind of resolution is
desired? Furthermore, another approach could be to use a higher resolution for detected
ranges that are further away, as the resolution in angle would have a bigger impact on
the absolute resolution in meters.

be distinguished overlap, the resolution is limited to the resolution of the best path. Fusing
the data from the different paths is therefore not expected to increase the resolution. It
can, however, increase the accuracy and detectability. To explain this, the errors made
by a single path must first be characterised.
6.3.4
Types of errors for single path IAA + detection
Type I: IAA error
The first type of error involves accuracy. This accuracy is impacted by two factors: first
off, the monostatic circle or bistatic ellipse along which the IAA is carried out does not
exactly pass through the target, because the target range is only accurate up to a range
bin. Secondly, the IAA algorithm itself is not fully accurate: the peaks in the angular
spectrum can be off by a few angle bins. Factors that impact the accuracy are thus the
number of iterations in the IAA and the IAA grid resolution, as explained before, and the
range resolution, number of antennas and SNR. The fact that the peak is not exactly at
the target location will be deemed a type I error in the remainder of this chapter.

Type II: BIC error
The BIC detection algorithm is not perfect: it can detect false targets. This is impacted
by the SNR and number of antennas. At low SNR (0-5dB), it is also prone to missing the
correct target.
6.3.5
Single path IAA performance
This subsection evaluates the IAA performance for the single paths. For this, a distinction
must be made between the different types of errors discussed in the previous subsection.
Simulation outcomes are classified into two categories: if only one target is detected, it
is counted in the type I error category. If no target is detected, it is counted as a type II
error. If more than one target is detected, the detected target closest to the actual target
is counted as the type I error, and the other targets are counted as type II errors.

The first simulated scenario is one where there is a single radar located at the origin, and
a single target, at 100m and 0 degrees from the radar. When looking at the type I errors,
it was found that they seem to represent samples from a normal distribution, discretized
at the grid points of the IAA. To verify this, figure 6.10 shows the scenario described,

proportion of results falling within one, two and three standard deviations of the mean
were calculated, and found to be 67.5%, 94.6% and 99.1%. For a normal distribution,
these should be 68.3%, 95.5% and 99.7%. The numbers are not perfectly the same due
to the small sample size (N = 5000) and the discretization effect. They indicate, however,
that the normal distribution is a good approximation for the type I error distribution after
the IAA+BIC algorithm.

6.4
Summary
The chapter started off with a literature overview, in which different angular estimation al-
gorithms for a single MIMO radar were investigated. The parametric methods like MUSIC
and DOA reduce the available antenna aperture due to the need for spatial smoothing
algorithms, as the signals are coherent in this thesis. From the non-parametric methods,
the IAA is picked for further investigation due to its good single snapshot performance,
and ability to work with coherent signals. The IAA method from the literature [17] was
then introduced to process monostatic paths, and a method to process the bistatic paths
by sweeping a single angle across the bistatic ellipse was proposed. With the single path
IAA established, the chapter then proposed a processing chain to fuse the different paths.
One method is to combine the IAA range-angle maps before detection, but this was found
to be suboptimal. This is because when converting the IAA results from the local range-
angle coordinates to the global Cartesian coordinates, they do not necessarily overlap at
the right location due to the range not being exact. Another method, performing detection
of the individual IAA maps using the BIC algorithm from [17] and afterwards clustering
these detections in the global coordinate system was therefore proposed. Section 6.3
then described the results. After the simulation method was explained, the problems as-
sociated with merging the IAA maps were illustrated, and an example showed the working
of the clustering algorithm. A next subsection then discussed the achievable resolution
with this method on a single path, and why the fusing of different paths can improve accu-
racy and detectability, but not resolution. The different types of errors with this processig
method were then demonstrated, and several simulations showed the effect of the IAA
grid size, SNR and number of antennas on the IAA performance of a single path. Finally,
a simulation was done for the IAA method with BIC and clustering on the full network,
demonstrating its possibility for increased accuracy. It was found there that the DBscan
is perferred over the Kmeans clustering algorithm because of its outlier rejection capabil-
ities.

7
Conclusion and future work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis revolved around the optimal way of fusing the data from multiple radar sen-
sors in an automotive setting, distributed along a baseline that represents the front of a
car. It was investigated for a MIMO PMCW radar network in the presence of uncorrelated
phase noise for static, isotropic point targets, and only a one-dimensional angular esti-
mation was considered. Looking at the literature, it was found that there has been a lot
of focus in recent years on solving the problem of uncorrelated phase noise to achieve
a usable radar network. For a SISO PMCW radar network, [21] found that the impact is
manageable when using the range domain to separate the signals from different radars.
It does cause a ridge along the Doppler dimension in the range bins of the direct and
bistatic paths, but a compensation method was recently introduced for this [13]. After in-
troducing basic concepts of a MIMO PMCW radar in chapter 2, the subsequent chapter
3 verified the impact of phase noise in the MIMO PMCW radar network, and extended
the compensation method to this case. This chapter also introduced the SMF method
from the literature, to convert the received data for a single mono-or bistatic path to a re-
sponse in the global xy-coordinate system for target localisation purposes. This method
was adapted for the PMCW MIMO radar network and modified to do the matched filtering
after range processing, equivalent to the backprojection known from SAR. It was found
there that the monostatic response of this SMF amounts to a sinc shape on a circle of
constant range with respect to the radar. It was shown mathematically and through simu-
lations that the SMF on the bistatic path amounts to an elliptical cut of a map consisting of
the product of two sincs, one oscillating faster than the other, which implied the resolution
on the SMF of a bistatic path is inherently worse than for a monostatic path in the near
field. In the far field, the SMF on the bistatic path becomes equivalent to the monostatic
path.
Chapter 4 investigated a first fusion method from the literature [14]: a coherent SMF
across the entire network. Known issues with grating lobes from the literature were de-
scribed mathematically, and different mitigation methods were proposed: varying the
number of radars, antennas, the distance between the radars, and combining the different
mono-and bistatic paths pairwise, taking the minimum of the resulting spectra. Though
it was found these methods reduce the grating lobes, they could not be reduced to such
a level that they would not cause a number of false alarms in a realistic scenario, when
detection algorithms are taken into consideration.
This is why chapter 5 moved on to incoherent fusion methods. It was shown through sim-
ulations that the incoherent summation of the coherent single path SMF’s cannot increase
the resolution. The bilateration method from [16] was implemented, and demonstrated to
be able to increase the resolution in one scenario. The equations from [16] were then
generalised to assess when this increase in resolution was present, and it was found that
this is only the case for closeby targets, a high range resolution, and a low number of
antennas.
The final chapter 6 then proposed a processing chain to fuse the different paths through
detection on the individual paths and then clustering the resulting detections in the global
coordinate system. The individual paths were processed using the IAA [17], where the
bistatic paths were processed by sweeping an angle across the ellipse of constant bistatic
82
Sensor Fusion Algorithms for Distributed MIMO PMCW Radar
==PAGE:94==
range. Detection was done using BIC [17]. It was demonstrated by simulations that pro-
cessing the network in this way can improve accuracy and detectability, but not resolution.
It was also shown with simulations that increasing the number of antennas and the SNR,
and refining the grid size increases the single path IAA+BIC accuracy.
Tying these individual conclusions together, the following can be said about the sensor
fusion of a MIMO PMCW radar network. Coherently processing the full network at once
produces too many false targets to be usable in a realistic scenario. Incoherently combin-
ing the different mono-and bistatic paths does not increase the resolution with the SMF
method. Bilateration can provide a better resolution in scenarios of closeby targets, low
number of available antennas, and high available range resolution. If good resolution is re-
quired, the IAA algorithm is a good choice: though computationally expensive, it achieves
a good resolution for the single paths. When combining these paths through clustering
after detection, the accuracy and detectability can be further improved.
7.2
Future work
Based on the work in this thesis, some recommendations for future work are included in
the list below.
• The methods in this thesis should be experimentally verified, to support the claims
made by mathematical modelling and simulations.
• The results from chapter 4 suggest that the coherent fusion of the different radars
provides inadequate performance.
In [12], however, only the bistatic paths are
utilised, and because of an asymmetric antenna setup, combined with the fact that
the virtual array positions are the sum of the locations of transmit and antenna ar-
rays, this concept could be extended to also include a monostatic path. Given a
setup of one radar in the centre, of which the monostatic path is utilised, and two
radars on the sides, with the right offset between the Tx and Rx array, it should be
possible to create an even bigger virtual array located at the centre of the setup.
• The results from chapter 5 suggest the following further research. It can be investi-
gated if the proposed method of bilateration, where the inherent angular capabilities
of the radars are used to eliminate ghost targets, can be generalised to a multilater-
ation approach as in [47]. Research needs to be carried out to indicate whether the
inherent angular capabilities might be used to generate a better performing multihy-
pothesis algorithm, taking into account this extra information to define more accu-
rately which range circles and ellipses belong to which targets.
• The results from chapter 6 open up the following research avenues. Firstly, it should
be mapped out for practical systems what the available computational power is,
and what the optimal grid size of the IAA algorithm therefore is. Multiple algorithms
to speed up the IAA exist in the literature [62], [63], and should be tested for this
scenario.
• In general, the resolution cells using either IAA or the SMF can be approximated
by circle segments, as explained in chapter 6. It should be investigated whether
these resolution cells can be approximated mathematically, to give an expression
for the achievable resolution in Cartesian coordinates. For example, for the SMF
method, a technique such as the one suggested in [64] can be used to estimate the
resolution cells.
