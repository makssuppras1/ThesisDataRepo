Abstract
This thesis is a proof-of-concept on how simulation modelling can improve production
scheduling to address the observed risk of a manual scheduling approach and provide
companies with a competitive advantage through an increased operational performance.
The investigation is performed through a developed discrete-event based simulation model
that mirrors a ﬁve-step production system with analysed characteristics from process engi-
neering industries including discrete production orders, bills of materials and worker shifts.
To perform the experimental study for the utilised general simulation methodology, an in-
put data set of a brewery was developed and the common priority rules of Earliest Due
Date, Shortest and Longest Processing Time and Critical Ration were implemented in
the simulation software FlexSim, before multiple experimental scenarios were conducted
in FlexSim and its connected optimisation engine OptQuest.
The results illustrated performance improvements across all scenarios with a maximum
expected improvement in service level of 1% and 25% respectively from two diﬀerent
baselines indicating that using FlexSim and OptQuest to combine, customise and build
priority rules can lead to improvements. Additionally, the study exempliﬁed OptQuest’s
inability to automate multi-objective decision-making, and a generic nine step scheduling
framework is therefore introduced for companies to beneﬁt from the discovered abilities
of simulation modelling. Included herein is a preparation to ensure relevance of the used
simulation model, priority rules and decision variables and ﬁve weekly operational steps to
use FlexSim and OptQuest to facilitate scheduling. Included is also the use of a business
intelligence tool to automate output analysis of a simulation and thereby further facilitate
the scheduling decision and communicate the decided schedules.
The proof-of-concept shows a potential of simulation to facilitate scheduling.
For the
generic conclusions, future practical use cases are recommended to further support the
investigation into the scheduling framework and simulation modelling’s abilities.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The global manufacturing industries have over the past decades experienced an increasing
demand, which has created unseen challenges for the industries to quickly accommodate
customers. To succeed today, companies are therefore required to be both eﬃcient in the
manner that they address demand but also competitive as a respond to the increasing
global competition that exists across industries (Heizer et al., 2017). When discussing
competitiveness, it can be measured through parameters such as delivery time, price and
quality (Heizer et al., 2017). Therefore to perform and thrive in today’s market, companies
must seek to optimise their operations by investing in technologies and people to keep
up with the fast development in operational techniques.
With the increased demand
and challenges caused by the more complex processes from the growing development in
technology, it is more than ever important for operation managers to maintain an overview
of, and even more crucial to identify, the areas performing below optimal. Consequently,
leaders today seek techniques to support their decision-making to manage the experienced
challenges and optimise the eﬀectiveness and productiveness of their operations.
This same search for decision-making techniques is also present within the process engi-
neering industry, where companies compete through the process of converting raw mate-
rials to ﬁnished goods (FG) by designing, optimising and scheduling their operations to
best ﬁt an experienced demand on any given day (Heizer et al., 2017). Using Operations
Management (OM) techniques and best practices is therefore key, because it can provide
guidelines for key activities and resources (e.g. equipment, materials, production ﬂow and
people) required.
A still ongoing issue for companies to succeed is deﬁning their production schedules in an
eﬀective and optimised manner. When discussing production planning and scheduling, it
is the process of considering and planning the required raw materials, equipment, labour,
production times etc. and then selecting a schedule that best matches these considerations
to the demand and/or shipment deadlines exposed on the production (Stadtler et al.,
2015).
This important and complex process has historically been and often still is a
manual work process conducted by only a few specialists in an organisation as is the case
in Novo Nordisk’s Pilot Facilities for the production of the active pharmaceutical ingredient

The introduction of the well-known Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system was ini-

An opportunity for companies to gain a competitive advantage therefore exists and a way
to pursue this opportunity is through the use of simulation, because it can manage, handle
and optimise resource utilisation and therefore potentially production scheduling. Simu-
lation mimics a real system over time by applying mathematical models and production
logic to the simulation model making it into a realistic mirroring of a production system
(Rossetti, 2016). Simulation is already a widely used tool in OM across diﬀerent indus-
tries, because simulation is an useful method to design, test and verify changes in a speciﬁc
environment (Larsen, 2020b) and as well as incorporate a system’s complexities and all
its interrelationships (Rossetti, 2016). Simulation is already used for design and veriﬁca-
tion purposes, but the question is how eﬀective a discrete-event based simulation (DES)
model can also be used to solve the scheduling problems within manufacturing processes,
where the objective is to reorganise and allocate resources to improve the operational
performance of the entire production system.
With this challenge and desire in mind, this thesis will in collaboration with the danish
consultancy company NIRAS study the possibility to use DES for production scheduling
to address the risk of manual scheduling, to ﬁnd an optimised schedule for a production
system and thereby gain a competitive advantage.

3.1
Simulation
In the process engineering industry, many elements aﬀect the overall operational perfor-
mance of the related production systems due to their complexity and size. To be able
to identify areas causing a decrease in performance in such systems is, as introduced in
Chapter 1, beneﬁcial for companies, because the operational insights to e.g.
resource
utilisation and bottlenecks can allow for a quantitative and data-based decision-making.
A technique suitable to create such operational insights is simulation, which is a power-
ful and beneﬁcial descriptive and prescriptive decision-facilitating tool (Rossetti, 2016).
When using simulation to analyse an operational system, a constructed simulation model
of a system in question, ﬁrst and foremost, allows for a descriptive analysis of the system’s
current behaviour through a mirroring of the system’s ﬂow and logic in the simulation
model (Rossetti, 2016). But the descriptive analysis is not the only possibility with sim-
ulation, because according to Rossetti (2016) ”a key use of simulation is to convey the
required behaviours or properties of a proposed system”. This means that simulation can
also support a prescriptive analysis, where simulation is used to not only describe but also
recommend how a system should be utilised. This is exactly what the objective of this
thesis is when trying to deﬁne and recommend how production orders in a given system
are to be scheduled to address the existing bottlenecks and constraints.
As simulation is based on knowledge and data regarding a certain system, it is necessary
to have access to suﬃcient data and relevant information regarding the system to depict
13
==PAGE:27==

the system in the best possible manner. Insuﬃcient knowledge or data will lead to a non-
representative model that is not able to provide valid insights for decisions (Larsen, 2020a).
A suﬃcient understanding and related data about a system is therefore a prerequisite for
simulation together with a veriﬁcation of the built simulation model to ensure validity and
conﬁdence in the gained insights. If this is in place, simulation can become a very useful
tool for operational analysis. Another relevant aspect to introduce when already discussing
validity and conﬁdence in a simulation’s conclusions is the ability to include randomness
and uncertainty into a simulation model (Rossetti, 2016)(Larsen, 2020b), because through
simulation modelling the unpredictable behaviour of operational systems such as machine
breakdowns or yield considerations can be included and analysed.
This is a strength
of simulation modelling, because it allows for the analyse of the modern and complex
operational environments that exist across industries (Larsen, 2020b).
3.1.1
Diﬀerent types of simulation
After introducing some of the beneﬁts and requirements for the use of simulation, it
can further be said that modern process simulation distinguishes between three types of
simulation methods (Grigoryev, 2021), which are introduced in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Description of the three simulation methods and their application areas
Contrary to DES and System Dynamics (SD), Agent-Based Simulation (ABS) diﬀers these
methods by solely focusing on the individual agent’s interaction and behaviour in the
system (AnyLogic, n.d.a). As Grigoryev (2021) states, ABS can be widely used as it is
suitable from the operational to the strategic level, with other words illustrating companies
to governments. The agents in the system are deﬁned through behaviour and therefore,
the relation between the individual agents is provided by a set of rules and the given
environment in the system (AnyLogic, n.d.a).

SD is an abstract technique that neglects smaller details in the system contrary to ABS
and DES and therefore not focuses on the individual object in the system, but instead
mimics a complex system (AnyLogic, n.d.b). Consequently, SD is appropriate for long-
term strategic decision-making.
An essential characteristic for the SD method is the
feedback loops, where the feedback loops can e.g. be a company that invest in stocks, the
higher turnover, the more the company can use for stocks (AnyLogic, n.d.b).
As Grigoryev (2021) claims, DES is concerning the medium to low abstraction level,
which is also where manufacturing projects reside.
Thus, the thesis will examine the
DES technique for the simulation and therefore no further elaboration of the remaining
simulation techniques will be made.
3.1.2
Software for discrete-event based simulation
To carry out a simulation project through the described methodology, it is essential to
carefully choose a proper simulation software to build the simulation model. Given the
fact that there exist numerous simulation software tools and therefore can be troublesome
to pinpoint one software, Dias et al. (2016) have performed a study to rank the top 19 most
relevant DES tools based on diﬀerent categories. The study illustrates, that according to
their criteria, the top three tools were Arena, ProModel and FlexSim. The ﬁnal ranking
based on their analysis and the applied categories can be seen in Appendix E.

For phase three and four of the later introduced simulation methodology, the OptQuest
add-in to FlexSim will be utilised in the search for an optimised production prioritisation.
OptQuest, oﬀered by OptTek, is a leading simulation optimisation engine that can supple-
ment the scenario experiments when evaluating the performance of an operational system
(FlexSim, n.d.e).
When using OptQuest, the optimisation engine will iterative deﬁne scenarios on your
behalf, analyse the scenario and based on the analysis continue with a new and opti-
mised scenario with the aim to optimise speciﬁc KPIs deﬁned by the experimental design.
The optimisation engine will therefore not deﬁne a mathematically optimal solution, but
instead continuously test new solutions in search of an optimisation.
In order for the
optimisation engine to continuously ﬁnd better scenarios for a given system, OptQuest
is built as a metaheuristic that combines diﬀerent optimisation techniques such as Tabu
search, Scatter search and other search algorithms (OptTek, n.d.a). The optimisation en-
gine is as stated by OptTek (n.d.a) ”designed to be a “black-box,” general purpose, global
optimization algorithm” indicating that the speciﬁc design of the metaheuristic is not pub-
licly available. The fact that OptQuest’s speciﬁc search practices cannot be deﬁned limits
the reliability of its generated solutions. To counter this issue, an approach utilised will
therefore be to use the OptQuest engine to search for feasible and optimised scenarios
in the cases, where the number of possible solutions becomes unmanageable for manual
scenario experiments. By utilising this approach, the suggested solutions by OptQuest
can be manually tested in an experiment to ensure the suﬃcient conﬁdence in their KPI
performance. This use of OptQuest will allow the thesis to consider more scenarios than
otherwise and the fact that all three of the earlier described simulation tools (Section
3.1.2) together with multiple other tools utilise OptQuest’s optimisation engine (OptTek,
n.d.b) supports the validity and usefulness of OptQuest for optimisation purposes within
simulation.

General simulation methodology
In a simulation project, similar to other analytical tasks, it is crucial to be consistent and
structured in your work to ensure that the analyses conducted are in fact relevant for the
task at hand and that the task is approached from appropriate directions. This can be
achieved by selecting and following a methodology when developing a simulation model
and project, because it can facilitate that all necessary preparations as well as analyses are
completed and not neglected. To not neglect one or more parts of a simulation project is
especially essential for the initiating phase, because of a simulation project’s direct reliance
on a suﬃcient understanding of the given operational system’s logic, data and boundaries.
One methodology that can ensure consistency in an analytical and problem-solving project
is the DEGREE methodology (Rossetti, 2016) consisting of the following steps:
1. Deﬁne the problem
2. Establish measures of performance for evaluation
3. Generate alternative solutions
4. Rank alternative solutions
5. Evaluate and iterate during process
6. Execute and evaluate the solution
This six-step methodology is suggested for general problem-solving, but as Rossetti (2016)
states ”... the general DEGREE approach needs to be modiﬁed to explicitly consider how
simulation will interact with the overall problem-solving process”.
Therefore, Rossetti
(2016) suggests an adjusted general methodology consisting of six phases for use when

3.3.1
Production scheduling
To establish an understanding of production planning, and scheduling in particular, Stadtler
et al. (2015) state that the overall aim of production planning is to ”... derive detailed
plans for the diﬀerent plants and production units”. Through these plans, the aim for a
given production unit is to understand the schedule to be conducted over a short time
period and hence their deﬁnition of production scheduling is that ”a production schedule
indicates for each order to be executed within the planning interval its start and completion
times on the resources required for processing” (Stadtler et al., 2015). Therefore, it can
be said that a production schedule through the deﬁnition of start and completion times
also states the sequence in which orders are to be executed.
This deﬁnition of a schedule is well aligned with the machine scheduling deﬁnition stated
by Eiselt and Sandblom (2012), because for them scheduling includes a sequencing part
to determine the task order and a scheduling part that deﬁnes the start and end times
of a task on a machine. Furthermore, to describe production scheduling, it is relevant to
introduce Silver et al. (2017), who argue that ”short-range scheduling applies at the level of
the individual work center, where operators and supervisors need to know which of a set of
jobs in queue, or soon to be in queue, to process next”. These three well-aligned statements
about production scheduling therefore illustrate that scheduling is about deﬁning how to
prioritise tasks at each process step and through such prioritisation establish an order
in which the required jobs are to be executed in the diﬀerent production steps.
This
understanding of production scheduling is the deﬁnition utilised for the thesis and can
additionally lead to the overall objective of production scheduling that according to Silver
et al. (2017) simply is ”... to provide a schedule for each job and each machine”.

3.3.3
Academic studies in optimisation of production scheduling
Given the introduction to production scheduling and how it is utilised in practice, it
is relevant to explore the academic perspective by investigating diﬀerent methods. Silver
et al. (2017) state that production scheduling is about ﬁnding an order for the jobs to each
machine. A way to accommodate this is by using priority rules according to Hvam and
supported by ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al. (2021) who claim that ”Priority rules are popular because
they are characterized by the simplicity of implementation, satisfactory performance, and
a substantially reduced computational requirement”.
A research conducted by Pitzer et al. (2011) explores the utilisation of genetic program-
ming to establish priority rules, but do also addresses standard priority rules such as:
Longest Processing Time (LPT), First In First Out (FIFO) and Shortest Processing Time
(SPT). To execute the study, a DES model was built imitating a production site with
purpose to optimise e.g. resources utilisation and cost. The used method is a combi-
nation of genetic algorithms and optimisation strategies obtained from simulation. The
DES model included typical elements from a production system e.g orders, BoM, shift
plans and human and physical resources. The conclusion for the study illustrated that the
generated rules based on the genetic algorithms performed better and are more beneﬁcial
than standard priority rules (Pitzer et al., 2011).
In 2016, Xanthopoulos et al. (2016) investigated the sequencing problem with stochastic
processing times and deadlines as a part of the system.
To fulﬁl the study, four per-
formance metrics were established to compare how diﬀerent dispatching rules performed.
Standard priority rules e.g. SPT and EDD were used together with setup-oriented rules
(Xanthopoulos et al., 2016). The analysis was conducted based on a DES model and ex-
periments were ran. A parameterised priority rule was generated based on the experiments
and statistical tests were performed to analyse the output. The best performing priority
rule was SPSU (a rule that prioritise the job with the smallest sum of processing and
setup time (Xanthopoulos et al., 2016)). The study has conﬁrmed that the standard rules
are utilised, because they are common used for deadline oriented KPIs. Furthermore, it
is seen that Critical Ratio (CR) and SPT are beneﬁcial regarding due date and ﬂow time
oriented KPIs (Xanthopoulos et al., 2016).
ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al. (2021) carried out an examination regarding optimisation in operations
scheduling. The study applies a DES model, where the eﬀect of following priority rules were
explored: FIFO, EDD and Slack Time Remaining (STR). A multi-criteria optimisation
consisting of Weighted Product Method and Weighted Sum Method scaling techniques
was used (ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al., 2021). An experimental study was divided into two: A
single-objective optimisation to identify baseline and the multi-criteria optimisation to

==PAGE:40==

obtain the best-case result. The single-objective experiments illustrated that STR and
EDD both performed the best and worst results, while in the multi-criteria experiment,
the best result was EDD when considering Weighted Sum Method (ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al.,
2021).
Another study has been carried out to investigate the interplay between scheduling rules
and deadline assignment methods (Vinod and Sridharan, 2011), where a DES model has
been built to support the examination. The study investigates seven priority rules among
others FIFO and SPT and four methods to assign due-dates e.g.
dynamic processing
plus waiting time (Vinod and Sridharan, 2011). The simulation leads to an experimental

time into account. Another scheduling rule, SPT, did also score well in tardiness and was
the rule performing best of all when considering due date methods (Vinod and Sridharan,

Finally, a completely diﬀerent approach is to implement a large planning tool such as
the SAP Modules utilised by Stadtler et al. (2015) in their pharmaceutical use case. For
scheduling, the SAP APO PP/DS module is in the use case utilised, because it allows
for the consideration of production constraints such as labour availability, capacities and
holding times. Based on the constraints and given objectives, the module can generate a
production plan respecting the constraints. However, the actual scheduling must still be
an interaction between the manual planner and the system, because the SAP module is not
able to include all parameters for an optimal sequence according to Stadtler et al. (2015).
However, it is a semi-automatic scheduling, because the planner can select sub-sets of
orders and then utilise an incorporated optimiser and sorting algorithms from the module
or simply rely on visual Gantt Charts (Stadtler et al., 2015).
This use case therefore
illustrates the opportunities of having access to sorting algorithms and an optimiser in
the large planning tools, but also the complexity and challenges that can arise when
implementing an oﬀ-the-shelf solution like SAP.

A ﬁnal point to emphasise is the division of the deﬁned system into a push and a pull part.
It is, because the system is comparable to the push-pull logic introduced by Silver et al.
(2017) ”that is, components are pushed to an inventory stocking location according to a
master schedule based on forecasts, and then ﬁnal products are customized to order (pulled)
after that point using the component inventory”. By relating the described system to this
description from Silver et al. (2017), it can be said that the system’s push-pull point is
the intermediate storage, because aggregated production orders are pushed to the storage
from where shipment orders pull the required material. The use of such push-pull system
is possible, because of the aggregation of shipment orders into daily production orders
for each input material type. These aggregations equal forecasts of the daily required
material amount needed, and thereby ensure that a shipment order is not solely dependent
on unique production orders but can pull the required material from storage. Having a
push-pull point in the system allows for the opportunity to use diﬀerent priority rules in
the brewing process and packaging process, which potentially in the experimental study
(Chapter 6) can yield optimised schedules to improve operational performance.

4.3
Establishing performance metrics
After deﬁning the system, the next step is to setup up performance measures that can allow
for a comparison and evaluation of diﬀerent production schedules’ performance (Silver
et al., 2017).
When deﬁning the performance measures utilised, ”Managers should be
very concerned with the trade-oﬀs implied in the choice of which performance measures to
use” according to Silver et al. (2017), because diﬀerent measures have diﬀerent focuses
and therefore highly will aﬀect the comparison of schedules by establishing the framework
for comparison. When looking at performance measures for short-term scheduling, one
can consider elements such as WIP inventory, because ”high WIP levels mean that more
money is invested in inventory, and therefore is not available for other purposes” (Silver
et al., 2017), operational performance in relation to each job’s due-date as well as measures
focusing on the utilisation of the limited resources within a system. In addition to the
general elements by Silver et al. (2017), more focus points can be taken such as e.g.
looking into waste levels in the Lean perspective. Therefore to allow the PoC model a
broad perspective, the following four KPIs are selected:
• Overall service level
• Overall average material stay time in intermediate storage
• Total quantity wasted due to expiry
• Total sum of overtime work
As mentioned, the KPIs are chosen to allow for a comparison of diﬀerent schedules per-
formance and thereby of directly inﬂuence on the simulation’s objective. Therefore to
ensure suﬃcient understanding of the KPIs each of them will be brieﬂy described and de-
ﬁned in relation to the SMART-technique that ensures a KPI is Speciﬁc, Measureable,
Attainable, Relevant and Time-bound (Larsen, 2020d).
Firstly, the overall service level of a simulated replication, with a given priority rule that
will create a speciﬁc schedule, measures the eﬀectiveness of the system in completing
shipment orders on time. The service level is therefore deﬁned as the number of shipment
orders delivered before or at their deadline time divided with the total number of orders in
the period. The calculation of service level will be performed each time an order has been
delivered and is therefore a continuous measure where the KPI’s value at the replication’s
termination will be reported.
Secondly, the average material stay time within the intermediate storage is selected, be-
cause this KPI can be related to the WIP-levels of the system. It can, because the longer
prepared input material stays in the storage, the longer resources are tied-up and not
utilised.
Furthermore, this KPI also has a relevance for the imaginary brewery’s cus-
tomers, because the shorter stay time of input material, the longer shelf-life of the FGs.
To measure the KPI, the stay time of each material batch is calculated from the material

is moved into the intermediate storage and until all material in the tank is pulled by the
packaging process or removed due to expiry.
Additionally, the average quantity wasted due to expiry is chosen, because its ability
to illustrate the relation between the selected brewing schedule and packaging schedule.
In the simulation, if a certain material reaches expiry time, the content will be omitted
and not available for the packaging process. This will therefore be able to visualise if

The last KPI illustrates the sum of overtime (in minutes) across all processes with worker

the process can begin despite the result of overtime for the ﬁnalisation of that production
order. In a production, overtime work is often costly and the KPI is therefore very relevant
to use together with the service level KPI, because with some schedules a high service level
might be reached but with the trade-oﬀof a high overtime cost.

In the FlexSim model, diﬀerent types of agents ﬂow in each of the diﬀerent main ﬂows.
In the initial order ﬂow, the agent is a production order entity, which is evaluated, placed
in the production list and after production removed from the system. The same is also
the case for the shipment ﬂow, where the shipment orders are created, in turn allowed
to check for and extract material from storage, create a packaging request and thereafter
leave the system when handled. In both the order and shipment ﬂow, an entity therefore
enters and leave the system during the simulation.
This is, on the contrary, not the case for the initial brew, manual and packaging ﬂow. It is
not, because the available resources are the ﬂowing agents in these ﬂows. Here, the agents
therefore continuously interact with a related order list to handle the respective task of
each resource. When a task is handled, an agent does not leave the system but instead
returns to the initial starting point of the given ﬂow to handle the next task at hand. The
resources in the simulation model are therefore created at simulation start and maintained
throughout the entire simulation. Lastly, the batch ﬂow is also a ﬂow where entities in
the form of batched input material enter the ﬂow and leave the system after transport to
the storage. However, diﬀerent from the order and shipment ﬂow, the batch entities are
here not created at simulation start but are instead created by either the order ﬂow as
premade material or by the initial brew ﬂow after brewing has completed.
The reason for implementing the system’s logic through ﬂows with diﬀerent types of
agents is two-folded. Firstly, the diﬀerent agent types allow for the implementation of
the push/pull logic introduced in Section 4.2. It does, because by both having a pro-
duction order entity and a shipment order entity there is no direct connection between a
shipment order and the input material utilised for its fulﬁlment. If instead a shipment or-
der would enter the system and then move through production of required input material,
fermentation, transport and then packaging, the possibility of aggregating the production
orders would not exist and a speciﬁc shipment order could only be fulﬁlled through its own
speciﬁc production orders. Secondly, the usage of diﬀerent agents allows for the sharing of
resources across ﬂows, which is needed for the fermentation and storage tanks. One can
45

argue that this would not have been necessary if a shipment order would be the ﬂowing
agent through the entire model. But with the implemented push/pull logic there is a need
to seize a storage tank in the batch ﬂow and keep this storage tank in the system despite
the ﬁnalisation of the related batch ﬂow. This is possible with the usage of diﬀerent agent
types, because the fermentation tanks and storage tanks themselves are also created as
agents in each of their supportive ﬂows and then utilised by the other ﬂows through the
use of lists.

A second important model element visualised in Figure 5.3 is the creation of resource

Section 3.3.1, is about deﬁning how to prioritise tasks at each process step. Thus, prior-
ity rules will be introduced, and because ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al. (2021) also state that ”...the
choice of appropriate dispatching rules is not a trivial task and depends on the relevant key
performance indicators” diﬀerent rules will be examined, because diﬀerent KPIs have been
established (Section 4.3) and will presumably perform diﬀerently with diﬀerent priorities.
For the testing of FlexSim and OptQuest for scheduling, four priority rules are therefore
selected: EDD, SPT, LPT and CR.
As the main KPI is service level, it was preferred that at least one priority rule with the
ability to maximise service level was chosen. Thus, the priority rule EDD is selected,
because ”this coordinate mechanism is mainly useful when the goal is to reduce job late-
ness, minimize ﬂow time and it also guarantees minimization of maximum and variance
tardiness” (ˇCerveˇnansk´a et al., 2021), where the ability to reduce job lateness is directly
related to service level. Also related to service level, the CR and SPT rules are selected,
because ”the CR and SPT are known to be eﬀective for ﬂow time and due date related
metrics, respectively” (Xanthopoulos et al., 2016), where the performance of CR in rela-
tion to due date metrics is further supported by Silver et al. (2017). Furthermore for SPT,
Silver et al. (2017) explain that SPT performs well in relation to the WIP-level, which is
highly relevant in regard to the expiry and material stay time KPIs that are both focused
on the intermediate storage’s ﬂow. Lastly, LPT is selected in relation to the overtime KPI,
because SPT can cause orders with a longer processing time to experience delays (Silver
et al., 2017) and hence might result in overtime work as larger jobs are moved to the end
of a worker shift.
Overall, the four are selected, because neither are expected to perform best in all KPI’s
and due to their ability to be implemented and understood by a large audience. Many
other priority rules are introduced in literature and could have been selected, which would
have aﬀected this experimental study. However, as this is a PoC, it was decided that well-
known rules should be prioritised to ensure as wide relevance and familiarity as possible,
which is also aligned with the recommendations discovered in the literature study (Section
3.3.3). The selected priority rules and their properties are presented in Figure 6.3.

Proof of OptQuest’s capability with new
input data
With the ﬁrst eight scenarios presented, the results illustrated that priority rules are useful
and beneﬁcial as well as the ability of OptQuest to partly improve performance. With only
minor improvements in performance seen in the previous scenarios, this additional scenario
intends to better illustrate the potential of OptQuest by establishing a new data set and
baseline that is evidently poor, and not practical, to allow OptQuest to ﬁnd solutions
performing signiﬁcantly better than baseline. Before presenting this ability, the new input
data will be presented together with the model changes necessary.
New input data
To better visualise improvements through OptQuest, the new input data structure is
reorganised compared to the original. The restructured input data and baseline have the
purpose to be obviously inferior for the KPIs compared to the other established priority
rules. The ﬁrst and main change in the input data has been to convert the production
orders from being based on daily aggregates for each input material to instead be individual
for a given shipment order.
Hence, an individual production order with the required
amount is now created for all input material needed for each individual shipment order.
This change has the following impact on the production system simulated:
• Smaller production sizes in general across the production orders
• More individual production orders to be completed
• A larger total amount to brew, because each individual production order is increased
to match the required batch size instead of only daily aggregates being increased
• Increased pressure on resources, because the production orders are smaller but can
still not be mixed in tanks or other resources
With above impacts, the eﬀectiveness will decrease for the production system, because
it will carry an extra pressure. A second major change to the original input data is to
the structure of shipment orders being originally organised in an EDD chronological order
meaning that order ID 1 had a deadline either before or at the same time as order ID 2
etc. In the new input data, the shipment orders are instead assigned a random order ID
meaning that the order with the earliest due date no longer must be order ID 1, but can
be the order with the highest ID. The principle of this is to use the priority rule FIFO
and clearly demonstrate that this performs inferior, because handling an order that is not

date related KPIs, but is exactly the principle of a FIFO priority rule.
Changes to the FlexSim model
The new input data requires simultaneous changes to the model logic in FlexSim. First to
utilise the priority rule FIFO, a new logical decision-statement is implemented in the model
to symbolise this prioritisation. These decision-statements for the brew and shipment side
are as following and are naturally only focused on the randomly assigned shipment order

Brew: ”WHERE BrewCutOﬀ<= Puller.PullTimeLimit ORDER BY OrderID ASC”

The two implemented logic statements will be used similarly to the ones presented in

the subset of orders for prioritisation.
This time frame is in this scenario changed to
include all three weeks of input data to fully visualise the FIFO principle, meaning that
when prioritising, all production and shipment orders are available for selection each time
a resource is available. Another aspect to mention is that with the production orders
being directly connected to individual shipment orders, the production system now only
includes a pure pull, because the shipment orders now pull the material through the entire
system. This pull aﬀects the model’s tank logic, because storage tanks will now be directly
reserved to speciﬁc shipment orders and cannot, if not empty, be used for other orders. A
model logic is therefore implemented such that all tanks reserved to a shipment order will
be drained and released after this shipment order is either handled or cancelled regardless
if the tanks are empty or not. If tanks are emptied due to a shipment order has already
been completed, the content will not count as expired. A last comment regarding the
changes is that the model logic of premade material (Section 4.4) is kept, which therefore
still aﬀects the orders with a BrewCutOﬀbefore simulation start despite the randomly
assigned order ID of their related shipment orders.

With the completion of the experimental study in Chapter 6 and the outline of each sce-

7.1
The potential of FlexSim and OptQuest for production
scheduling
The nine experimental scenarios all contributed with insights into the match between
production scheduling and simulation modelling. Thus, this section will elaborate on and
combine the brief conclusions for each scenario.
The most important observation is that the experimental study managed to illustrate that
simulation modelling can lead to performance improvements compared to a given base-
line. An expected improvement was visualised in scenario six and nine when combining
priority rules for the production system’s push and pull side and further in scenario seven,
where additional adjustments to the system’s characteristics provided further improve-
ments. This is important, because without performance improvements, the relevance of
deﬁning a scheduling framework based on simulation modelling would be very limited.
In relation hereof is the conclusion that through experimental scenarios, the ability to
compare expected performance of diﬀerent schedules also exists. It does, because through

the priority rules, diﬀerent schedules are tested in a veriﬁed mirroring of the production
system, which leads to a quantitative foundation for the decision. A quantitative founda-
tion is what planners in many cases try to achieve through Excel as e.g. seen by Vorm
(Appendix B), or similar tools, and simulation can support this aim while adding a level of
conﬁdence in the data by using suﬃcient replications to address the production system’s
randomness.
When mentioning the priority rules’ ability to provide diﬀerent schedules, it is relevant
to mention that the rules must be predeﬁned and implemented in the simulation model
before experimental optimisation. It was seen in scenario eight that new priority rules
can be built by OptQuest, but this required relevant order characteristics available to
the model. Hence, in both cases, a preparation and selection of relevant characteristics
are needed.
This suggests that simulation modelling is not an oﬀ-the-shelf solution if
used for scheduling, but must include a prior preparation and veriﬁcation of the model.
Furthermore, in relation to OptQuest building the prioritisation queries, it was illustrated
how unexpected combinations could lead to a higher performance. This discovery supports
the use of simulation modelling, because similar to e.g. OR tools such as mixed integer
programming or heuristics it can explore solutions not directly obvious.
Talking about OR, the inability of any of the conducted scenarios to automatically suggest
one solution that performed best across all KPIs must be mentioned. The manner in which
the OptQuest optimisation was deﬁned included each KPI as an objective and therefore
showed that OptQuest cannot automate such multi-objective decision but only facilitate
it. This was directly visualised with the existence of multiple optimal solutions in each
optimisation. If only one KPI had been selected as objective or if the KPIs were combined
into one single objective, with e.g. diﬀerent weights and directions for each (Lusby, 2020b),
the simulation model would have been able to automate the decision-making as only one
solution could be deﬁned as optimal. However, the development of such single objective
when multiple KPIs exist, as is often the case, will also require a study into how diﬀerent
designs of the single-objective can lead to an optimised performance.
Another important aspect of simulation modelling when considering production schedul-
ing is related to the use of multiple replications for both the OptQuest optimisations and
related experiments. Multiple replications are used to ensure conﬁdence in the reported
KPI values, but come with the consequence that the randomness aﬀecting each individual
replication is diﬀerent. This aﬀects the production schedules reported for each replica-
tion that therefore are individually diﬀerent and will include longer processing times than
expected, and scheduled, for some orders. This ability to address the randomness and
provide expected KPI values for the scheduling decision, therefore creates the need to con-
duct a deterministic planning replication with the selected solution to obtain production
schedules that are expected to provide the solution’s performance. How and when the
randomness will then aﬀect the schedules when in operation will be unknown, but the
multiple replications have shown what the expected performance will be.

On the basis of the previous insights, the input received from stakeholder interviews and

modelling, represented by OptQuest and FlexSim, can facilitate the scheduling decision

ensure that the foundation for scheduling of a speciﬁc production system exists. Diﬀerent
from the two is, however, that OptQuest related considerations must be made.

Furthermore, this step is important, because in both the literature study and the experi-

Select, or deﬁne, available priority rules
Closely related to the KPIs are the priority rules that OptQuest can select among when

Prepare/adjust simulation model
Without a veriﬁed simulation model neither an OptQuest optimisation or any simulation
experiments are possible. Thus, a simulation model mirroring the production system must
be established and veriﬁed before any scheduling decision can be made. When establishing
the model, the system boundaries of the simulation should also be considered to address
that the solution space of a simulation can become very broad, because uncertainty can
aﬀect many aspects of a given production system according to Schow (Appendix K). In this
thesis, a simulation model for the imaginary brewery was built from scratch, but another
option is to reuse already established models. This option is as an example seen with
the case company NIRAS’ already established client models for e.g. utilisation analysis or
production plan validation (Appendix D). With a model already established, the resources
required to adjust the model are limited compared to the establishment of a new.

Lastly, as preparation, it is required that the planner deﬁnes the available decision variables
for the OptQuest optimisations. Earlier, scenario seven (Section 6.5) indicated that the
more decision variables available the bigger improvement could be gained. Thus, a decision
must be made to decide how many settings within the production system that can be
changed when optimising. For this decision, the trade-oﬀbetween room for optimisation
and a too large solution space for suﬃcient exploration by the optimisation engine exists.
Furthermore, a decision is also to be made whether the optimisation will focus on selection
of predeﬁned priority rules or the generation of rules based on order characteristics. Both
options showed expected improvements, but will require diﬀerent implementations.

Practical relevance of scheduling framework
To conclude on the developed scheduling framework that is based on the PoC project’s
conclusions, the framework’s relevance is considered.
Initially for the scheduling framework to be of relevance for process engineering companies,
it is important to remember that it is based on simulation modelling.
Therefore, its
outcome will be directly dependent on the quality of the simulation model utilised. Hence,
the quality of scheduling decisions made will never be better than the quality of the
implemented priority rules, demand data and master data for the production system.
This can be a limitation for the developed framework according to Lund (Appendix S),
because her observations when interacting with clients show that available master data is
often not of a suﬃcient quality. With this in mind, it is important to state that without

suﬃcient master and demand data available, the scheduling framework is not the ﬁrst
implementation recommended to process engineering companies.
The issue of data quality is, however, not only aﬀecting the thesis’ developed scheduling
framework, but would also be an issue if other solutions were preferred such as the SAP
scheduling module (SAP, n.d.) or the ROB-EX scheduler (ROB-EX, n.d.) introduced
during the thesis. These are also dependent on the the available input data to improve
performance. Further comparing the framework to the other solutions, an investigation

alternatives are focused on stochastic processing times and this is therefore the strength of
the developed framework. But it must be mentioned that alternatives exist and the rele-
vance of the scheduling framework therefore is dependent on the importance of addressing
existing uncertainty, which according to Ahn-Louise Larsen can vary between companies
(Appendix J), and a given company’s resources to understand and implement a validated
simulation model.
Another comment when discussing uncertainty is the required ability to establish accu-
rate estimates of the uncertainty as stated by Schow (Appendix K). It is, because if the
stochastic characteristics implemented into the scheduling framework are not representa-
tive, an oﬀ-the-shelf solution like ROB-EX is probably a better choice. However, if the
estimates are representative, an ability to schedule based on expected performance exists.
With this in mind, a higher relevance of the framework for production systems used for
market production is indicated due to the diﬃculty of establishing the uncertainty of R&D
processes, which was stated by Vorm (Appendix B).
Moving away from the issue of the establishment of the required simulation model, an-
other aspect is that a judgement-based decision is still to be made despite the developed
framework. Here, it is argued that the decision can now be made on a more in-depth
data foundation that addresses uncertainty. However, this does not change the fact that
a judgement-based decision, similar to what planners do today, must still be made. With
that said, a better foundation for decision-making is a beneﬁt of the framework together
with the ability to provide improvements to a baseline performance that was seen across all
experimental scenarios. Especially in scenario nine (Section 6.7), a large improvement was
seen from an purposely inferior baseline. In this regard, it should be mentioned that the
potential improvement is highly dependent on a company’s existing baseline. If multiple
earlier initiatives within a company have already been executed to improve scheduling, it
will require more resources in the preparation phase of the framework to improve perfor-
mance due to the need of customised priority rules and well-established decision variables.
On the contrary, for companies not already focused on scheduling, the framework can al-
ready through the standard priority rules provide an improvement in performance. That

both possibilities exist supports the framework, but obviously it must be noted that the
more resources invested in the preparation of the operational process, the higher potential
for improvements.
A last point to bring forward is the use of the deterministic replication to deﬁne the
actual production schedules.
When randomness exists, it is expected that scheduling
changes must be made during a production week. In this case, a new walk-through of

applied. Such new walk-through is also seen in step seven of the general procedure for

and can improve performance for process engineering companies if implemented. For it to
become a success, it is however required that the companies ensure a suﬃcient data quality
of their input, master and demand data as well as are able to provide veriﬁed estimates of

framework has the ability to create performance improvements, when decision-making is
made on the basis of the generated in-depth data foundation.

Chapter 8
Thesis discussion and outlook
In order to conclude the study into the possibility of using simulation modelling for pro-
duction scheduling, this chapter will take a step back and discuss the thesis’ choice of
methods and the usefulness of the study’s conclusions. Through this discussion, the aim
is to clarify the choices made and evaluate their relevance and impact. Furthermore, this
chapter will conclude the collaboration with the case company by suggesting next steps
for the industry as well as NIRAS to beneﬁt from the thesis and further investigate the
potential of simulation modelling for production scheduling.
8.1
Choice of method
When discussing the method choices made, the ﬁrst point is the use of simulation. The
thesis’ aim is to illustrate that simulation modelling can be used to optimise scheduling,
which according to the experimental study (Chapter 6) it can. However, other methods
exist and can be considered when a production company seeks to optimise scheduling.
According to Hvam (Appendix I), one will always have to consider an OM and OR aspect
of production scheduling. This is supported by Wolsey (1998), in the book used for DTU
course 42114 Integer Programming, which also indicates that OR can facilitate scheduling.
Here, Wolsey (1998) refers to a publication by Graves et al. (1993), which introduces
OR heuristics and algorithms for scheduling both single machine, Flow Shop and other
types of production systems. For Flow Shop scheduling, that according to Graves et al.
(1993) is similar to the production system studied, it is discovered that a two-machine
system easily can be scheduled through OR, while three-machine systems become NP-
hard and other methods such as branch and bound heuristics must be developed. Similar
conclusions were introduced by Lusby (2020a). The opportunity to use OR for scheduling
is therefore an alternative and can, most likely, also facilitate the scheduling decision. OR
was not preferred for this thesis due to the thesis’ clear focus on simulation modelling.
However, if the required capabilities within OR were present, a similar study could have
been conducted to investigate a potentially diﬀerent scheduling framework. In this regard,
86
==PAGE:100==
8.1. CHOICE OF METHOD
it is argued that simulation allows for a more intuitive introduction to new technologies
than the advanced mathematical planning methods from OR, because of the ability, e.g.
in FlexSim, to combine the 3D and 2D simulation environment. This is beneﬁcial for the
need to ensure acceptance of new scheduling methods when bridging the gap between the
new technologies and what is used today as earlier introduced. A similar tendency is seen
in regards to the stochastic processing times. Graves et al. (1993) argue that ”Surprisingly,
there are many cases where a simple rule which is merely a heuristic for the deterministic
model has a stochastic reformulation which solves the stochastic model to optimality”,
which shows that OR methods might also be able to handle the randomness aﬀecting
scheduling. However, the strength of simulation modelling is the ability to easily compare
multiple replications, as illustrated with the developed dashboards in Power BI (Appendix
T), which deviates the simulation approach from the advanced characteristics of OR and
e.g. allows production workers a continued understanding of how a decision is expected
to aﬀect performance. Therefore, it can be said that OR solutions are a clear alternative
to simulation modelling and can, if designed to the speciﬁc case, facilitate scheduling, but
based on the need to balance new technologies and the traditional planning tools still used
today, simulation modelling has some adjustable and beneﬁcial characteristics.
The second discussion point for the chosen method is the used simulation technique. Even
though, the project scope (Section 1.2) stated that the built simulation model is based
on DES, it is relevant to reﬂect and discuss the utilised technique, but certainly also,
how the remaining methods, SD and ABS, might have aﬀected the project. According to
Silver et al. (2017), the strategic level, which SD is categorised as, is most likely used for
long-term planning projects, because the essence of SD is using feedback loops causing
agents to circulate in the system with a long-term strategic purpose (Grigoryev, 2021).
With SD, minor details can be neglected in the system (AnyLogic, n.d.b), which would
have aﬀected the level of detail for a built simulation model. Therefore, it was concluded
that SD is inappropriate for the thesis’ type of simulation project, because it would sig-
niﬁcantly change the production system’s structure and logic. To support the deselection
of SD, Grigoryev (2021) further conﬁrms that SD is used for the strategic level and not
the operational and tactical level, which this thesis explores. Contrary to SD, the adapt-
ability of ABS is higher, because according to Grigoryev (2021) the application spectre
extends from operational to strategic level.
The core concept for ABS is the interac-
tion between agents in the system and therefore the simulation technique is well-suited
for social-, transport- and economic systems or the interaction between departments in
an organisation or hospitals (Davidsson et al., 2007). The possibility for an ABS model
for this study seems improbable, because the designed system is one production system,
which does not interact with other. However, the argumentation for neither ABS or SD
being suitable for the project does not complete the discussion point. The deselection of
ABS and SD is not a condition to automatically select DES. Rossetti (2016) describes a
discrete system to be: ”Just as discrete systems change at discrete points in time, (...)
observations are gathered at selected points in time when certain changes take place in the
system”. This description matches the core concept for the built simulation model, since it
includes entities that enter and exit the system and that events take place at certain times.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
To ﬁnalise the investigation of using simulation modelling for production scheduling, a
conclusion to the thesis and thereby an answer to the research question can be made.
Based on discussions with the case company and external stakeholders, it is discovered
that an opportunity to gain a competitive advantage exists for companies, if simulation’s
ability of a quantitative foundation for improvements can optimise production schedul-
ing. To investigate this and address the risk of a manual scheduling approach, a PoC
study based on the general simulation methodology has been conducted. To increase the
study’s relevance for a broad range of process engineering companies, a ﬁve-step imaginary
brewery was established through an analysis of input data and characteristics of earlier
industry projects. This production system included generic characteristics such as discrete
production orders, BoMs, worker shifts and a push-pull point.
To investigate the potential of simulation modelling, the production system has been im-
plemented in FlexSim through six main ﬂows each with a diﬀerent agent type. The model
included lists to control tank availability, while randomness was implemented through a
triangular distribution for the stochastic fermentation time and an exponential random
variable to illustrate a representative packaging OEE. Furthermore, input data with dis-
crete shipment orders and aggregated production orders was developed to allow for a
simulation of two weeks operation. Through the model and input data, a performance
baseline across the service level, average stay time, expiry and overtime KPIs was estab-
lished, with the EDD priority rule, before a step-wise experimental study of individually
using, combining, customising and building standard priority rules was performed. For
this, OptQuest was used to explore multiple solutions with each KPI implemented as an
objective and a single constraint of no cancelled orders used to guide the optimisation.
With the experimental use of FlexSim and OptQuest, it was possible to illustrate per-
formance improvements through optimised scheduling. An improvement was illustrated
across all scenarios and the highest service level performance of 92%, compared to a base-
line of 91%, was seen when customising priority rules through multiple decision variables
in OptQuest. Based on the experimental study, it was therefore concluded that simulation
93
==PAGE:107==
F. F. Carlsson and L. Werngreen-Nielsen
modelling can in fact create a quantitative foundation that can lead to improved schedul-
ing. Here, scenario nine showed that the improvement potential is highly dependent on
the baseline. However, because the ability to automate the multi-objective scheduling
decision was not shown, the experimental insights were translated into a suggested nine-
step scheduling framework. This framework included a preparation phase to ensure the
existence of suﬃcient input data, a validated simulation model and a selection of relevant
KPIs and priority rules for a company’s unique production system.
After preparation, a weekly ﬁve-step operational process was suggested to continuously al-
low a steering group to make the scheduling decision based on expected performance across
various available solutions. The strength of providing a data foundation that addresses
the uncertainty of a production system was determined to be a deviation from available
alternatives. To further facilitate the decision, the possibility of an automated visuali-
sation and communication tool was illustrated through three Power BI dashboards that
aimed to bridge the gap between organisational functions and between new technologies
and scheduling tools used today. Through the framework, it was eventually argued that
companies have an opportunity to continuously optimise their scheduling performance, if
the prerequisites of available input data with a suﬃcient quality and the capabilities to
accept and introduce the new technology exist within the company. Lastly, the possibil-
ity to further illustrate the potential of simulation modelling was suggested to the case
company, and industry, if the discovered conclusions were applied in practise while further
academic support can be explored through studies into the use of customised priority rules
or the use of OptQuest with an aggregated single-objective function.

are to run for the research projects. Here, they explain that they will need x grams of
API for a speciﬁc phase, which then have to be produced by the pilots. The ﬁrst thing
the project manager then hands over is a process description of the diﬀerent production
steps, which the project’s production campaign must go through.
In broad terms the
production steps will include cultivation (opdyrkning), fermentation (gæring), recovery
(grov rens), puriﬁcation (ﬁn rens), some chemistry surrounding side chains (if necessary),
spray drying and ﬁnally some storage and release activities. In each of the process steps
after handover of requirements from the project managers, Vorm looks into the expected
yield and expected percentage of active ingredient in each process step (because yield and
active percentage is determining how much useful material is actually passing through the
production processes). When he has this information, he inputs everything into his own
homemade Excel sheet, which combines the project information with his master data about
the pilot facilities. Such master data includes tank sizes, how long time a fermentation
can live in each given tank, which resources have priority for which projects, maintenance
information and etc. Included in this Excel sheet is then also the overall demand of active
ingredient that the project demands.
With an end demand for a given project (the unit is grams of active ingredient), Vorm can
calculate through the entire process backwards and deﬁne (based on his expected yield
and active percentage in each process step) how much input and output (in grams and
material units) there have to be in each process step. Based on these input and output
data, he can then ﬁnally say how long time it will required in each step and by that
knowledge deﬁne, which pilot facilities should perform the diﬀerent process steps and in
which time interval each project as a minimum has to be performed.
• Additional information about potential break-downs in speciﬁc processes, which can
result in the loss of a entire production campaign, is also included. To deﬁne these
information, Vorm has an expected success rate include in the recovery phase and
puriﬁcation phase (e.g. 80% chance of a successful production and 20% chance of a
required remake).

Optimisation example as introduction to OptQuest
To introduce how OptQuest can be used in FlexSim, a small simulation model has been

customer enters the bank and ends when the customer ”releases” the resource used for

characteristics apply:
• The service process is the only process within the system.
The processing time
depends on the resource available and is simulated as the mean service time of
the resource plus an additional individual customer handling time simulated as a
exponential distribution with a mean of 30 seconds
• Two service resources are available
– An ATM can support customers without any break in-between services with
a mean service time of 120 seconds
– A Cashier can support customers face-to-face with a mean service time of 210
seconds. When a customer has been serviced, a mean in-between time of 30
seconds occurs when the cashier resets his/her desk. This in-between time is
also simulated as an experimental distribution
• The inter-arrival time of customers is on average 40 seconds and is in the system
simulated through a exponential distribution with a mean value of 40 seconds
The system is implemented as a process ﬂow model in FlexSim (Introduced in Section

Step 3 - Finding the required number of fermentation tanks based on the
amount of premade material
Through step 2, it was seen that all the required premade material was not able to be
simulated as premade due to a lack of available fermentation tanks. The next step in
the determination process is therefore to ﬁnd the required number of fermentation tanks,
because step 1 and 2 have shown that in the long run 12 fermentation tanks can be
enough, hence the only aspect requiring this number to increase more is the need for
premade material. To ﬁnd the required number, the input data (Section 5.1) can be used,
because it is known that all production orders with a brew cutoﬀearlier than the start time
of the ﬁrst worker shift for the brewing line(s) must be premade. Through aggregation in
the input data, this amount aggregates to 280m3 coming from 12 production orders (ID
1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 and 14). Of the 12 orders, four has an amount required
higher than the capacity of one fermentation tank (25m3) and hence require two or more
tanks. Of these four, one order has an amount required of 51.8m3, hence requires three
tanks. By adding these insights together, it is seen that no fewer than 17 fermentation
tanks can be used to ensure the premade material in fact is premade.
This knowledge together with the acceptable utilisation of storage tanks in step two (Figure
21, it is decided that the resource level of the baseline should be 17 fermentation tanks
and 15 storage tanks.

Interview guide - Camilla Thuge Lund, Deloitte
Method
Given the fact that Camilla Thuge Lund is working as a consultant for diﬀerent produc-
tion companies to facilitate their planning, a qualitative interview is ideal, because Lund
potentially can provide insight into multiple production companies at once and the chal-
lenges and characteristics that they are experiencing. If each of the companies should have
been reached quantitatively, the same level of detail in the information provided would
not have been possible.
Source to support qualitative interview: (Department of Sociology, n.d.)
Interview details
Interviewee: Camilla Thuge Lund
Date: 12 January 2022
Title: Consultant
Company: Deloitte
Contact information:
Interview Objectives
The interview’s aim is to engage in a interaction with Camilla Thuge Lund to learn about
her experiences with production planning and scheduling across some of her earlier clients.
Optimally, the aim is to learn about former clients’ production system, their scheduling
approach and their challenges. Furthermore, the aim is also to receive some input from
Lund in regard to the thesis’ simulation approach for scheduling and how Lund sees a
usage of this.
Questions and interview structure
*All marked with blue is only for the interviewer to guide the ﬂow of the interview
Start by introducing the aim of the interview, explain that all inputs are valid and will
beneﬁt the project and outline the expected duration of the interview. Then to set the
scene, the ﬁrst question is regarding Lund’s experiences with production scheduling at
former clients
Can you bring forward some examples of previous projects / client interactions where
production planning and scheduling have been involved?

– Can you brieﬂy elaborate on the given production system and which overall

– How was the client beforehand scheduling their production?

Notes from answers:
The interview went into a free ﬂow conversation surrounding the above questions after an
initial walk-through of the interview’s aim and questions and the purpose of the overall
master thesis. Below is the notes taken during the interview:
To initiate the interview, Camilla Thuge Lund elaborated on her earlier experiences which
span across multiple industries where she has worked as planning manager and supply
chain responsible as well as many years within the consulting. Today, Lund is working
to unify SOP and ﬁnancial forecasting. Lund is therefore primarily focused on holistic
planning and high-level planning.
The ﬁrst example that Lund brought forward was her work at UniBake in England where
they developed a production-wheel, which was a running four-week plan that described
in which order the products should be handled on the production lines to optimise OEE
and ensure that expiry considerations were not violated in the storage. In this case, the
scheduling considerations were focused on the productions batch sizes which should match
155
==PAGE:169==
Appendix
the customers’ need, because they based on the decided production wheel now knew when
the individual slow- and fast-moving products should be handled. In these cases, for when
a product was included in the plan, it was the longest time frame of the demand that
decided when it was added to the production wheel and in which frequency it should be
performed. When working with frequencies and with worker shifts, as they did at Unibake,
Lund outlines that it is always important to have some popular products (some always
can be sold) that you can produce if the last order in a schedule ﬁnishes ahead of the
worker shift such that the worker shift is not idle in e.g. the last hour. Therefore these
types of products were often schedule last on a given day such that no setup was needed
if the production should produce more than scheduled due to extra available time.
In Unibake’s production system the production processes included: Measuring of material,
mixing of material and then a conveyor production line that ﬁrst divided the dough into
equal portions, added the portions to a tray that then moves through an oven before a
cooling process is conducted prior to ﬁnal packaging. When talking about the challenges
in this planning setup, it was focused on expiry. Not expiry in the sense that the product
would go bad but in the sense that the customers demanded that at least e.g. nine months
of shelf life was available at delivery. If these demands were not met, the products were
often sold with discounts, which is why that the overall planning for Unibake was to
optimise in relation to proﬁt margins (based on the shelf life), customer satisfaction and
OEE of the production facility.
The logic in their planning and scheduling has been through their ERP system, SAP, where
they e.g. have used Wilson’s formula (EOQ) to deﬁne production quantities where after
the ERP has calculated required amounts of materials and etc. From their the detailed
scheduling has often been made through known patterns such that e.g. a ﬁve-inch burger
bun was always produced right before a four-inch burger bun. Lund knows that other
methods and advanced algorithms exist for this but she has not worked with these. In
relation to detailed scheduling (or sequencing), Lund has not worked that much in this
area, but her experience tells here that it is often an Excel exercise to deﬁne one optimal
schedule as a baseline. Then, it is often the aim to perform this schedule in a cyclic manner
to try and reach/achieve the just calculated optimal schedule from Excel.
Talking about simulation for scheduling:
Simulation is a big part of Inventory and Production Planning where simulation is used for
scenario planning. An example is from 2003 where Lund was a part of Carlsberg that had
just implemented an SAP Optimizer to deﬁne the overall production plan for Carlsberg.
However, this was a signiﬁcant change for the production employees and they thereby
wasted a lot of time on trying to determine why the SAP Optimizer was suggesting wrong
plans, because they quickly noticed that it did no correctly divide productions between the
western and eastern production facilities. Therefore, an issue exists in the capability and
importance to understand the technology used to ensure trust in the data and solutions
obtained.
Planning today:
156
==PAGE:170==
F. F. Carlsson and L. Werngreen-Nielsen
What Lund today sees in regard to planning is that people (specialist) are often promoted
from e.g. the production ﬂoor to the logistic department and thereafter get the responsi-
bility for planning processes. Such employees, originally production workers, do not have
the best likelihood of fully understanding the new available technology and its opportu-
nities. This is an important point for Lund to mention, because when using simulation
or other technologies a big focus point should always be to be knowledgeable about what
is the critical aspect of your simulation as well as how you can convince the production
about the value and validity of your technology and results.
Lund, today, knows about a few simulation tools that they recommend and utilise, which
include Kinaxis, OO, SAP IBP, etc. All these tools are scenario-based and have incorpo-
rated resource planning. These tools typical work across supply chain networks and with
constraints focused on capacity and shelf-life in diﬀerent kinds. So, the question that they
in Deloitte are trying to help customers answer is which product should be produced in
which week and in which quantity. From there it is often a planning unit that based on
this demand deﬁnes a schedule for the production in the up-coming week. According to
Lund, it is in the majority of the cases for Consumer-Packaged-Goods companies on a
weekly basis that they deﬁne and “lock” such production schedules. This means that it is
often on a Thursday that they deﬁne the schedule for the upcoming week together with
a rough plan for week two and three. Then they do everything again the week after and
by that plans the manning capacity one-week at a time. When ever ad-hoc changes are
required, it is most likely manual changes that are being made. These changes will often
be based on experience and be a discussion between customer service, a planning group or
maybe even the customer if it is a large and frequent customer. Otherwise, it is a planning
group that takes a judgement decision.
Opportunities of thesis:
Lund argues directly that there is always a need and desire for help with scheduling
and this need has existed for a long time and remains today. Therefore, Lund sees an
opportunity in the thesis project. Furthermore, this opportunity will only increase if one
can manage to bridge the gap between how scheduling is done today and the logic and tools
that production workers use today and the new facilitating tool. Meaning that if you can
visualise and communicate your approach, conclusions and decision in an understandable
manner there is most likely a future for such tool.
Challenges in scheduling today:
According to Lund, it is the same challenges that exist in regard to scheduling across
many diﬀerent industries. The challenge is always to ﬁnd the right balance in regard to
shelf-life and timing. An example of the similarity can been seen with shelf-life, because
for bread manufacturers shelf-life is important to avoid the production going bad while
it for electronic manufacturers is important to ensure that the product is still in demand
when intended for the market, because demand tendencies are highly volatile in today’s
market.
Another challenge for companies is that today a lot of technology is presence in the com-
