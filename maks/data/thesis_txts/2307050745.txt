Resumé
Med stigende produktion fra vedvarende energikilder vil de konventionelle produktions
-teknologier, heriblandt atom-, kul- og gaskraft, have færre produktionstimer og til lavere
priser, når de producerer. Herved bliver deres indtægter fra salg af energi reduceret
betragteligt, hvorved deres faste produktionsomkostninger ikke længere kan dækkes.
Dette er det såkaldte missing money problem. Med mindre, at foranstaltninger til at
supplere indtægterne fra salg af energi bliver indført, foventes det, at kraftværkerne bliver
gradvist nedlukket. Kraftværkerne er dog fortsat krævet i perioder, hvor produktionen
fra vedvarende energikilder er lav. Estimering af kraftværkers missing money kræver
kendskab til deres omkostningsstruktur, samt deres indtægter fra salg af energi.
I
dette projekt bliver der foretaget en vurdering af kraftværkers omkostningninger, samt
opstillet en markedsmodel, til vurdering af missing money problemet under øget produk
-tion fra vedvarende energikilder. Analysen bliver gennemført på en mindre test model
samt en model af det samlede Europæiske elsystem. Analyser af energimarkedet på begge
modeller viser, at under øget produktion fra vedvarende energikilder vil kraft -værkerne
ikke være i stand til at få dækket deres faste omkostninger. Specielt de forhenværende
grundlastværker, atom- og kulkraft, vil have store udfordringer ved at få dækket deres
faste omkostninger. For at undgå lukninger af kraftværker, hvis fortsatte drift er krævet
for at opretholde en høj forsyningssikkerhed, bliver tre forskellige kapacitets -mekanismer
undersøgt. Disse inkluderer en strategisk reserve, et kapacitetsmarked samt en tilskuds
-betaling til udvalgte kraftværkstyper per produceret energienhed.
Analysen af kapacitetsmekismerne viste følgende.
En strategisk reserve kan med
fordel anvendes ved stigende vindpenetrationsniveauer, ved at øge aktiveringsprisen samt
størrelsen på den strategisk reserve. Ved at afstemme mængden af aktiveringer samt
aktiveringsprisen med de estimerede faste omkostninger for kraftværker der opererer på
markedsvilkår, kan mængden af missing money reduceres i hele systemet. Kapacitets
-markedet kan løse missing money problemet fuldstændigt, ved at kompensere den
ønskede mængde af reserver. Under øget produktion fra vedvarende energi vil kapacitets
-betalingerne fra kapacitetsmarkedet stige. Ved en vindpenetration omkring 55 % og over,
vil betalingerne for kapacitet være højere end energi. Grundlastværkerne vil grundet
deres høje faste produktionsomkostninger blive prissættere i kapacitetsmarkedet, når
vindpenetrationen øges. Ved indførelse af et kapacitetsmarked i energisystemer med høj
produktion fra vedvarende energikilder kan det forventes, at kul- og atomkraft vil blive
erstattet af gasteknologier med lavere faste produktionsomkostninger. Tilskudsbetalingen
per energienhed til udvælgte kraftværker til dækning af missing money viste, at det ikke
begrænser missing money i systemet. Ved øget vindpenetration kan de modtagende
kraftværker underbyde vindenergi, og derved øge de totale forbrugeromkostninger.

Summary
With increasing production from variable renewable energy sources (VRES), the conven
-tional generation technologies, including nuclear-, coal- and gas-plants, will experience
fewer operational hours and at lower prices, when they do produce. Thus, their revenue
from sales of energy will be reduced substantially, at which their fixed operational costs
no longer can be covered. This is the missing money problem. Unless actions are taken
to supplement the revenue from sales of energy, it is expected that the power plants
will be gradually shut down. However, the power plants are still needed for periods of
low production from VRES. The estimation of the power plants missing money requires
knowledge on their cost structure, as well as their revenue from sales of energy.
In
this project an assessment of generators operational costs is undertaken. Furthermore, a
market model is setup to evaluate the missing money problem under increased production
from VRES. The analysis is conducted on a small test-scale model, as well as a model
of the European electric system. The analysis of the energy market simulation on both
models shows that, with increased VRES production, the conventional generators will
not be able to cover all their fixed costs. Especially, the former base load plants, nuclear
and coal, will experience greater challenges to cover their fixed costs. To avoid shut
down of power plants, still needed to maintain a high security of supply margin, three
different capacity support mechanisms are investigated.
These include a peak load
reserve, a capacity market and a premium payment to selected power plant types per
produced unit of energy.
The analysis of the capacity support mechanisms showed the following. A strategic
reserve can advantageously be used at increasing wind penetration levels, by increasing
the activation price and the size of the strategic reserve.
By aligning the number
of activations and its activation prize, with estimates on fixed costs of power plants
operating on market terms, the amount of missing money in the entire system can be
reduced. The capacity market can resolve the missing money problem completely, by
compensating the required amount of reserves. With increased production from VRES,
the capacity payments will increase. At a wind penetration level of around 55 % and
above, the payments for capacity will be higher than energy. The base load plants will
due to their high fixed costs become price setters in the capacity market at increase
VRES penetration. Introducing a capacity market in highly renewable energy systems,
it can be expected, that nuclear and coal plants will be replaced by gas plants with lower
fixed operational costs. The premium payment to selected power plants covering their
missing money showed that, the missing money in the system will not be reduced. At
high wind penetration levels, the premium receiving power plants can undercut wind
energy, leading to higher total consumer payments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the light of present and forthcoming periods, with the European electricity sector
experiencing an unprecedented transition towards liberalized and decarbonized systems,
relying on variable renewable energy sources, questions are raised regarding the ability of
energy markets to provide conventional generators with adequate revenue to cover both
fixed operating cost and capital cost. The liberalization of electricity markets in several
western European member states up through the 1990’s, and the ongoing formation of
the European internal market for energy, challenges to sustain and attract adequate
capacity is expected. The former vertically integrated systems had to a large extent
overcapacity installed, so naturally the amount of reserves was high at the beginning of
the liberalization. However, with the liberalization implying a transition from central
planning of decommissions and investments, towards decentralized decision-making by
suppliers of energy, new measures to ensure adequate capacity is required (EU 2016).
The missing money problem exist in liberalized electricity markets, due to prices
being too low to cover conventional generators fixed and capital costs. In theory, the
energy only market will ensure adequate revenue and spark new investment, if prices
were allowed to rise to very high levels in scarcity periods (Stoft 2002). However, imposed
price-caps, high national standards for reserves, and bilateral must run contracts with
specific power plants, most specifically nuclear, all adds to the missing money problem.
The new perspective is the increasing production from renewable energy sources, which
lead to the statement of this thesis. The missing money problem will worsen as the
production from renewable energy sources is increased.
Conventional generators will
operate less, and at lower prices, when they do produce, reducing their needed revenue.
However, at times of low renewable production, the conventional generators are still
needed to supply consumption.
As a result, several European countries have already implemented capacity support
mechanisms, with others taking measures for soon implementation. The capacity support
mechanisms have direct impact on consumer payments and market operation.
The
optimal policy design to keep a high security of supply, by ensuring adequate revenue
to conventional generators, while still minimizing consumer payments, is of key interest,
when designing electricity markets.
In this report, the missing money problem in electricity systems is quantified, under
increased renewable penetration. Different capacity support mechanisms is evaluated,
in terms of their ability, to ensure adequate revenue to conventional generators, while
at the same time, reducing the increase in consumer payments.

CHAPTER 2
Missing Money Problem
In this chapter, the price formation in an energy only market is described, which
supports the description and evaluation of both the revenue adequacy problem and
the capacity adequacy problem. Open access data of day-ahead electricity prices from
the western part of Denmark is used to quantify the missing money problem in operating
electricity markets.
As mentioned in the introduction, the missing money problem
is expected to increase for conventional generators, as the share of production from
variable renewable energy sources (VRES) increases. The conventional generators will
find themselves pushed out of the energy market for two main reasons. First, since
VRES have low-to-zero marginal cost, they are willing to produce at any price, and will
get scheduled before conventional generators. Second, the increase in supply lowers the
electricity price. Thus, conventional generators produce less, and with lower revenue
when they do produce, cutting into their profit margin, needed to cover their fixed
operational costs. This may lead the plants to be shut down. However, as mentioned
earlier, the plant are still needed for times of low VRES production. Nonetheless, the
missing money problem is not a new problem, emerged from increasing VRES. Joskow
(2006) defines the problem in the following way.
Spot wholesale electricity prices for energy and operating reserves will not be high
enough to cover both operating costs and capital investment costs required to attract
new investment in long-lived generating capacity to support a least cost generating
supply portfolio
A distinction between the missing money problem, revenue adequacy problem and
capacity adequacy problem is used throughout this report. The missing money problem
refers to conventional generators inability, to get all their operational costs covered from
sales of energy, from the power plant owners perspective. On the other hand, the revenue
adequacy problem refers to the inability of energy markets to provide adequate revenue
to conventional generators, from the system perspective. The capacity adequacy problem
refers to the inability of energy markets to attract investments in new generation. Also
from the system perspective.

The Energy Only Market
The design and function of the energy only market is described by Stoft (2002b). The
principle being that each supplier and consumer bids their marginal cost of production
and consumption into a market pool. Suppliers are willing to bid their marginal cost
into the market, as they will see their fixed costs as sunk. Any profit made serves to
cover their fixed costs. The supply curve, also called the aggregate supply curve, is
found by summing horizontally all individual generators supply curves. Likewise, the
demand curve is found by summing horizontally all consumers willingness to pay for
electricity. The intersection between the supply and demand curve sets the market price
for electricity. The principle of the electricity market clearing is illustrated on Figure
2.1. As opposed to a theoretical model, the real-time demand for electricity is almost
perfectly inelastic. With most consumers sealed from real-time prices, due to long-term
fixed price contracts with electricity distributors, they are not incentivized to change
consumption due to real-time price variations.

2.2
Revenue Adequacy Problem
The revenue adequacy problem is related to the ability of energy markets to provide
adequate revenue to generators, covering both the operating costs and the capital
investment costs. As mentioned in the previous section, the optimal mix of generation
technologies and capacity is self-regulated, if regulatory intervention is prevented in the
energy only market. In this context, the regulatory intervention refers to the presence
of system reliability constraints and price reducing measures by system operators, like
the use of strategic reserves, bilateral reliability-must-run contracts, and an imposed
price-cap set too low.
As I will also discuss later, several factors causes the revenue adequacy problem,
which in turn leads to the capacity adequacy problem. These includes imperfections
on the demand-side, out-of-market actions by the system operators and an imposed
reliability criteria. In this section, the focus is on describing why energy prices do not
rise high enough during scarcity hours, which prevents generators from having all costs
covered and thereby also attract new generation. In the former vertically integrated
systems a reserve margin was easily imposed, as the state-owned company could invest
in needed back-up capacity, without the intention to profit on investment. The reserve
requirements, often by political choice, was enforced the system based on statements like:
”1 hour of blackout in 10 years”, ”electricity delivered 99.996 % of the time during a year”
and so forth. However, the same reliability standards remains in force, even as electricity
markets are liberalized. As noted by Hogan (2013), the reliability planning standards
and the standards that would follow from conventional cost-benefit calculations are very
different. Nonetheless, the reliability standards themselves would not pose a challenge,
if better scarcity pricing was implemented. This would reduce the need for a politically
induced reserve margin. As argued by Cramton (2006), the actual problem is found
in price-caps, which has been set too low, in order to control the utilization of market
power. The price-caps are often regulated to be based on an estimate of the value of
lost load, or simply set to reduce high price-spikes. Bushnell (2005) states that most
surveys sets the value of lost load between 1775 e/MWh and 44.500 e/MWh. However,
using the annualized capital cost estimates of a new peaking plant on 40.000 e/MW-year
(Energistyrelsen 2012) and previous reliability statement ”1 hour of blackout in 10 years”,
the value of lost load, keeping the probability of blackouts very low, can be calculated

2.3 Capacity Adequacy Problem

2.3
Capacity Adequacy Problem
The incentive for investment in new generation capacity in the energy only market is
limited by a number of factors stated in the following.
First, Stoft (2002b) mentions, the limited demand-side participation in energy markets.
If there is no short-term price formation in the market, it can force the market operator
to impose a price-cap on electricity prices. Unless the price-cap is set at the correct
value of lost load (VOLL), reflecting consumers willingness to pay for electricity (or
reliability), the market outcome can be distorted and lead to wrong investment signals.
A price-cap set too high, can potentially lead to consumers paying more for electricity
than the value given to them. On the other hand, a price-cap set too low will reduce the
revenue for producers cutting into their needed profit margin. The estimate of VOLL is
a compromise across all consumers, as consumers value of electricity varies. For example,
hospitals value of electricity is high enough to allow them for installing an emergency
generator. Furthermore, the lack of real-time control of power flow to specific costumers
prevents physical enforcement of bilateral contracts and therefore discourages costumers
from buying long-term contracts.
Second, A.
Botterud (2008), mentions, the financial risk of investment in power
plants is high due to volatile electricity prices. Peak load plants are highly exposed due to
their low capacity factor and dependency on uncertain price spikes. Also, the lumpiness
of large base-load plants can cause reduced prices and profitability, which potentially
limits investments. The long lifetime and long pay-back time of new investments poses a
challenge towards regulatory risks. The framework conditions, at which investment was
undertaken, may change due to environmental regulations or electricity market design
changes. Unless a long-term market with permanent framework conditions is created,
where investors can hedge their financial risks, the uncertainties may significantly reduce
new investments in generation capacity.
Thirdly, the exercise of market power from large established companies is of concern,
as they may choose to postpone investments in new generation capacity to drive up
prices and increase profits from existing assets. Unless the barriers of entry are low
for new investors, this potentially causes electricity prices to be inefficiently high (A.
Botterud 2008).
Fourth, the use of peak load reserves by the system operator can potentially distort
energy prices and thereby investment incentives.
If system operators are willing to
reduce the use of peak load reserves in peak load situations, the price for energy can
increase further. The maximum price paid to operating reserves effectively caps the
price in the day-ahead market reducing revenue from needed price spikes and thereby
reduces investment incentives. The peak load reserves will only be deployed in the energy
market, when dispatched capacity is unable to meet demand. Instead of a price-spike,
the activated peak load reserve caps the price at its activation price (A. Botterud 2008).

Fifth, as mentioned in section 2.2, a regulatory imposed reserve margin above the

3.1 Peak Load Reserve

3.1
Peak Load Reserve
Peak Load Reserves1 are physical reserves inserted into the day-ahead market, when
supply cannot meet demand, usually consisting of demand responsive resources and
older power plants in danger of being decommissioned (Baritaud 2012). In that sense,
the reserves works as a physical protection against involuntarily load curtailment. The
Transmission System Operator (TSO) takes full control over the operation of the peak
load reserve, by paying the plant owner all costs of operation plus a smaller payment for
profit. In the Nordic countries, Sweden and Finland, the peak load reserve is activated
during peak-load in winter periods. Whereas, in Germany, the peak load reserve is used
to ensure system security in network constrained areas due to internal bottlenecks.
The peak load reserves are typically deployed at a price just above the highest bid
in the day-ahead market. Thus, the activation price will effectively be the day-ahead
market price-cap. P. Fritz et. al. (2011) argues that the activation price of peak load
reserves must equal the average price of VOLL, to not reduce investment incentives from
price spikes in the day-ahead market. As noted by Vries (2004), the activation price
must be adjusted based on security of supply criteria, amount of peak load reserves and
fixed cost estimates on the most expensive generator in day-ahead market not to reduce
investment incentives. As opposed to the activation price in Sweden and Finland just
above highest bid, he suggest to fix the activation price. The more generators that is
operated as peak load reserves, the lower activation price is required due to increasing
number of activations. As will be seen later, in Section 6.4, the activation price may
also be adjusted in relation to increasing renewable penetration.
As noted, price formation in electricity markets is influenced by the use of peak load
reserves. The electricity price will most the time be set by the marginal plant operating
on competitive conditions. In periods where supply cannot meet demand, the peak load
reserve is activated and sets the market price - usually just above the most expensive bid
in the day-ahead market. In case supply cannot meet demand, with the peak load reserve
activated, the electricity price can increase to the price-cap in the market. However, as
most electricity markets still have substantial amounts of overcapacity installed, the
peak load reserve will effectively act as the price-cap in the market. In relation, the
Swedish peak load reserve has been activated 9 times in the period from 2009-2013. All
activations occurred in peak-load periods during winter with outages on large nuclear
and hydro power-plants. Of important notice is that, at none of the activations has the
electricity price increased to the price-cap in the market (Kraftnät 2013).

System operators will often enter into rapidly implementable bilateral contracts, with
specific power plants or demand response resources to solve network constraints and
maintain network stability. A bilateral contract between the TSO and plant owner of
the peak load reserve typically specifies an annual capacity payment independent of
the output of the resource.
The payment of energy depends of the contract design.
Energinet.dk (2012) describes two main options for the contract design of peak load
reserves (PLR):
• Model A: TSO have disposal rights of both capacity and energy. The
TSO buys energy directly from the PLR at contractually defined prices and sells
the energy onwards to the day-ahead market or regulating power market. The
energy payment can be either the PLR´s marginal variable costs or the spot price.
• Model B: TSO have disposal rights of capacity but not energy. The PLR
is obliged to bid its capacity into either the day-ahead market or regulating power
market at activation. Thereby, the PLR will solely obtain payment for energy
through the day-ahead or regulating power market.
Sweden and Finland make use Model A to operate the peak load reserve, where the
TSO will pay the owner its variable cost of energy agreed upon in the contract, when
the peak load reserve is activated. The peak load reserve is activated at 0.1 e above
highest submitted bid by the TSO. Contrary to generating units the demand responsive
resources are operated using Model B, which allows the resources to bid into spot- and
regulating power markets at all time – not only during shortage periods.
Nonetheless, the use of peak load reserves is by default inefficient. S. Stoft (2013)
argues that peak load reserves are inefficient for two main reasons. First, as the peak load
reserve is not allowed to submit bids in the spot market, the dispatch will be inefficient.
Even though spot prices rises above marginal cost of the peak load reserve, it is still
only activated when supply cannot meet demand. Second, if the activation price of the
peak load reserve approximates VOLL, the capacity adequacy problem is the same as for
an energy-only market, in which the attraction of new capacity ultimately depends on
market regulators estimate of VOLL. Consequently, he argues that the Swedish peak load
reserve model is inefficient, with activation price slightly above highest submitted bid in
the spot market. This removes scarcity rents, suppresses new investment, and distorts
prices from efficient spot market pricing with occasional price spikes. The Swedish peak
load reserve model will cause total system capacity to be reduced.

3 Capacity Remuneration Mechanisms
3.2
Capacity Payment
Capacity payments is a regulatory mechanism that establishes a payment to generators,
in addition to the income from the spot market. The capacity payments are intended to
encourage investments by increasing and stabilizing volatile income from sales of energy.
Capacity payments uses the price as management tool to achieve the desired level of
capacity and are regularly updated reflecting the need for new generation capacity in
the system, with the challenge being to set the level of capacity payment correct. Set too
low, the mechanism will not attract the needed capacity. Set too high, the mechanism
will not be cost-effective and too much capacity will be attracted. Thus, the issue is
analogous to considerations regarding feed-in tariffs for sustainable energy producers (A.
Botterud 2008).
Three different capacity payment schemes is examined. First, a scheme with fixed
capacity payments currently used in Spain. Second, a scheme using dynamic capacity
payments in England and Wales from 1990 – 2001, and thirdly, a scheme using a
combination of fixed and dynamic capacity payments currently used in Ireland.
3.2.1
Fixed Capacity Payments in Spain
Since 2007, the Spanish TSO Comisión Nacional de Energía (CNE) have provided
capacity payments to conventional power plants including hydro, coal, gas and fuel-oil.
The scheme is separated into two parts, which has the purpose of partly to ensure
available capacity on the long-term, and partly to ensure available capacity on the
short-term (CREG 2012).

Dynamic Capacity Payments used in England and

A different kind of capacity payments were used in the former Electricity Pool of England
and Wales.
The capacity payments were made to each generator (or generator set)
declared available for dispatch in the day-ahead market, and were equal to the Loss
of Load Probability (LOLP) multiplied with the excess of the Value of Lost Load
(VOLL) over the generators bid price (if not dispatched) or the system marginal price (if
dispatched). The capacity payment for any cleared time-period was calculated ex-post
based on the following principle (CREG 2012):
Capacity Payment = LOLP · (VOLL −max[Spot Price ; Bid Price])
(3.2)
A link between the payment for energy and capacity was created. When the spot price
was high during peak load periods, the corresponding capacity payment was also high.
An additional incentive, besides the high spot price, for generating units to be available
in peak load periods was established.
However, F.
Roques (2005) emphasizes that
the mechanism was widely criticized for being prone to manipulation through capacity
withholding. Established operators would hold back capacity from the market to increase
the capacity payments to newly installed capacity. The reserve margin increased to 25.3
% in 2000/01, which was way above the target of 20 %. Even as the capacity margin
was increasing, so was the capacity payments, contrary to the expectations
Furthermore, the calculation of VOLL and LOLP is complex and can possibly send
wrong price signals if set wrong. An overestimated LOLP can partly be compensated
by an underestimated VOLL. Albeit, the relative weight of LOLP and VOLL influences
the riskiness of investing in peaking plants. An increase of VOLL and corresponding
reduction of LOLP, so that their product remains unchanged, can increase the magnitude
of capacity payments but are to be paid less often. Peaking plants may operate less often
but can earn the same profit on average, as capacity payments will be correspondingly
higher.
By calibrating LOLP and VOLL so there is convergence between very high
peaking prices and capacity payments, the mechanism will not reduce the risk for
investors in peaking plants (Newbery 1997)

3.3.1
Short-term Capacity Market
Short-term capacity markets are designed to provide a supplementary payment to existing
generation covering some of the fixed costs. The markets allows for participation from
generation and demand response resources to provide solutions meeting the target for
resource adequacy.
Short-term capacity markets have been operated by PJM4 from
1999 - 2006 and NY-ISO5 since 2000.
The two markets differ in the way that the
capacity market in NY-ISO make use of geographically differentiated auctions and an
administrative determined sloped demand curve.
3.3.1.1
Capacity market in PJM 1999 - 2006
The PJM area was restructured in 1999 leading to increased competition into the
electricity industry, coinciding with the imposition of a short-term capacity market
cleared few months before delivery of energy. The product traded in the capacity market
is Unforced Capacity (UCAP) in the form of capacity certificates, accounting for the
difference between installed capacity and available capacity. The system responsible
estimates each generators UCAP based on historical availability and a test to determine
maximum power delivery. A 100 MW unit might only deliver 90 MW of max power in a
test. The average availability of the unit in the last five years might be 95 %, qualifying
the unit to deliver 85.5 MW UCAP. Moreover, penalties are imposed on generators not
delivering the sold capacity in day-ahead markets and during real-time operation.
High initial capacity market clearing prices led to an investment boom in especially
gas-fired generators, accounting for 90% of capacity additions. On Figure 3.7, the annual
capacity additions, retirements, estimated total revenue for a gas-fired combustion turbine
(CT) and capacity payments for a CT is displayed.
The capacity payments would
gradually decrease as the amount of capacity additions came online. The amount of
capacity additions experienced between 2002 and 2003 increased the reserve margin to
23.4 percent, as compared to the target reserve margin for reliability of 17 percent in
2003. The excessive amount of capacity and lower payments for capacity lead to an
increase of retirements and a decline in new generation coming online from 2004 to
2006. The time delay of capacity additions compared to capacity market prices is the
challenge. New installed capacity causes capacity payments to decrease, damaging its
own projected revenue stream. (Wilson 2008).

3.3.2
Forward Capacity Market
Forward capacity markets are designed to promote projected plants to contribute in the
capacity auctions on equal terms with existing plants. This opens up the opportunities
for generation, demand, energy efficiency and transmission network (in USA) to provide
solutions to meet the target for resource adequacy. Forward capacity markets has been
operated by PJM since 2007 and ISO-NE7 since 2006. PJM operates an ICAP (Installed
Capacity) market whereas ISO-NE operates a capacity market with call-options - also
referred to as a market with reliability options. The design elements and experiences
from the two capacity markets is examined in the following.
3.3.2.1
Capacity market in PJM
In 2007, PJM replaced their short-term capacity market (see section 3.3.1.1) with
a long-term capacity market called the Reliability Pricing Model (RPM). The basic
elements of the RPM capacity market design includes a locational market definition,
a sloped demand curve, a three year forward procurement period, new market power
mitigation rules and increased incentives to perform in actual shortage periods. A base
residual auction (BRA) is held 3 years in advance with subsequent incremental auctions
(IA) leading up to the delivery year.
The three year forward period is intended to
provide incentive for the participation of new entry. Also, additional time is provided
for decisions on unit retirement and allows for more competition from long-lived assets
that needs modification to comply with environmental and technical constraints.
A key for a well-functioning capacity market is the link between the capacity and
energy market - acquired capacity must perform in actual scarcity situations.
Due
to under-performance during extreme peak periods in the former short-term capacity
market, PJM had to make changes in their market design to provide additional incentives

to perform during scarcity periods. The new scheme relies on penalty charges and bonus
payments for availability during scarcity periods (S. Newell 2015). In the RPM design,
demand side resources (DSR) and energy efficiency products (EEP) are offered as supply,
competing with offers from generating units directly in the capacity auction. The idea
is that DSR and EEP are imposed the same technical constraints as conventional power

in real time markets, but care must be taken as the clearing price in the capacity

the conventional generators.
Greater focus is also given measures to mitigate market power. PJM will take control
on supplier bids, in case a supplier owns more than 20 % of total required capacity
within a zone or three suppliers in unity can exercise market power to influence prices
by supplying the marginal capacity. Buyer side market power is mitigated by a rule
called Minimum Offer Price Rule. Buyer side market power could be the intervention
of individual states or utilities signing long-term bilateral contracts with guaranteed
payments covering fixed costs, which requires new capacity to be offered into the capacity
market at zero price. To avoid demand side market power PJM screens every bid for
being submitted on competitive basis. In case of depressed auction clearing prices, PJM
will take control of bids from all new capacity covered by bilateral contracts (PJM
2015a). However, concerns on the capacity market administrative complexity, especially
in terms of market surveillance, is raised by Bowring (2013).
He remarks that, the
current capacity market structure with large measures to mitigate market power have
the potential to distort the market if not treated correctly. The increased control of
PJM on bids from market participants potentially removes free market incentives.
3.3.2.2
Capacity Market in ISO-NE using Call-Options
In 2008, ISO-NE introduced a long-term forward capacity market, with a capacity
auction held three years in advance of delivery year. The traded product is call-options
and not capacity certificates as seen in the capacity markets in PJM and NY-ISO. This
type of capacity market is also referred to as reliability options. Still, the load serving
entities are imposed to buy call-options corresponding their estimated peak load plus a
reserve margin. The ISO-NE capacity market is similar to the PJM forward capacity
market in the way that it uses locational capacity auctions, allows for participation from
existing and new capacity as well as demand responsive resources and energy efficiency,
make use of a sloped demand curve and uses several subsequent incremental auctions to
adjust for imbalances in procured capacity.

3.4
Capacity Subscription
A market using capacity subscriptions is suggested by Doorman (2003), at which both the
quantity and price of capacity are determined from a capacity market. The suggestion is
only described in literature and has not been implemented in any markets. The concept
implies that consumers buys an amount of reliable capacity from suppliers (generators)
to be available during peak demand periods. A type of fuse is installed at each consumers
connection point allowing the system operator to limit consumption during peak load.
The fuse or Load Limiting Device (LLD) is activated at different points depending on
each consumers preference for reliability. Consumers with high reliability preferences for
uninterrupted load buys a more expensive LLD, reflecting the need for more capacity in
the system. Thus, consumer preference for reliability is directly reflected in the volume
of available capacity.
A system using capacity subscriptions works as follows (Vries 2004): An electronic
fuse (LLD) is installed at each consumer, which is activated, when the demand for
electricity approaches available generation capacity. The electricity use from each consumer
is then limited to the capacity of his fuse. The price of the fuse depends on the cost
of generation needed to guarantee a peak supply of electricity equal to the size of that
fuse. The total payment for the fuses represents the costs of keeping an equivalent of
generation capacity available. Consumers with high reliability needs will buy a more
expensive fuse than consumers who do not mind shifting consumption to another time.
Therefore, load reduction only occurs on a voluntary basis, reflecting each consumers
willingness to pay for the needed capacity.
Capacity subscriptions are distinguished from other capacity remuneration mechanisms,
as there is no centrally determined volume of optimal generation capacity.
Instead,
each consumer will decide on how much reliable generation capacity he wants available
and how much generation can be interrupted during peak load periods. Consider for
instance an industrial consumer with a 24-hour production line that consumes 10 MW
and a second process that only needs to be operated periodically and consumes 4 MW.
The company may opt to purchase only 10 MW worth of capacity subscriptions, which
means it may need to interrupt the second process when the fuses are activated.
Generating companies are only allowed to sell a volume of capacity subscriptions
equal to their available capacity. A generating company with four units of 600 MW
could choose to sell only 1800 MW worth of capacity subscriptions. This would allow
one unit to be off-line for maintenance during peak load periods. However, there is a
possibility that two or even more units are unavailable simultaneously. As in the case of
capacity markets, the generating company needs to weigh this probability and the cost
of the penalties against the revenues from the sale of capacity subscriptions.

3.5
Premium
The use of a premium as a capacity remuneration mechanisms is not suggested in any
literature. The premium, intended to cover missing money for selected generators, has
similar structure, as feed-in tariffs using premiums for renewable energy producers. The
feed-in tariff has two main design structures.
First, the use of a fixed price, where
payments are independent of the market price, offering a guaranteed payment. Second,
the use of a premium price, which offers a premium (extra payment) on top of the market
price, linking the premium payment to the market price level. Typically, a strike-price
is defined, at which the premium is not paid above. At high prices premium payments
are low, and at low prices the premium payments are high. To the authors knowledge, a
premium is not used as a capacity remuneration mechanism in any liberalized electricity
markets. However, the premium is comparable to tax reductions on selected power plant
types. In the Swedish energy agreement from 2016, it is stated that the taxation level on
nuclear and hydro power plants will be reduced. The tax was formerly paid per energy
unit produced (Regeringen 2016).
Reducing a tax or paying a premium to selected
generators with similar magnitude, do in principle have the same effect.

Test Systems
Two test systems are used to undertake simulations. First, the small IEEE RTS 24-bus

IEEE RTS 24-bus System
The 24-Bus system used in this project is based on the three-zone IEEE Reliability Test

transmission lines. The system is modified to the needs of this project. The generators
operational constraints and marginal costs are updated with data based on C. De Jonghe

data from western part of Denmark for a summer and winter period. The wind and solar
data is not included in the original data set. A weekly simulation time is chosen to better
reflect the fluctuations in renewable production, but still keeping the computational time
low. The system consists of one hydro plant, three coal generators, two combined cycle
gas turbines, two single cycle gas turbines, two oil fired combustion turbines and two
nuclear power plants.

The binary values in the unit commitment formulation is needed to represent physically
-based non-convexities such as minimum power output, start-up costs and minimum up-
and down-time.
However, the presence of binary variables in MILP (Mixed Integer
Linear Program) models prevents obtaining marginal prices as dual variables of the
balance constraint (A. Conejo 2012). The method used in this project, to solve the
unit commitment problem and find the dual prices on the balance constraint, follows
the approach suggested by B. Hobbs (2004). First, the mixed-integer unit commitment
program is solved to find the optimal allocation. Second, the integrality constraints
are removed, and instead equality constraints (cuts) are inserted, enforcing the integer
variables to assume their optimal values in the resulting linear program. Third, the linear
program is solved to find the dual prices on the balancing constraint, with the added
equality constraints. Thus, the output from the unit commitment program, determining
production profiles on each generator, is used as input to solve the economic dispatch,
and then determine the market prices from the dual of the balancing constraint.
4.5
Exogenous Activation, Deactivation and
Investment Analysis
The simulations of the capacity remuneration mechanisms (CRM) is modelled in a
iterative process allowing for changes in the portfolio of power plants. The starting point
for changes is from a system perspective. The activation, deactivation and investment
in power plants is always approved at central level. For example, with use of peak load
reserves, generators operating on market terms may still have missing money and thereby
a reason to leave the market.
However, from a system perspective these generators
are still needed for system security, so their deactivation is denied.
In reality, an
extra payment to these generators bypassing the electricity market could be granted
for them to stay active, but is not included in the simulations undertaken in this project.
Instead, when simulating each CRM, a steady state is assumed when no generators are
added or subtracted the system. A reserve margin of 115 % of peak load is used for all
simulations. In the following, a detailed description of how the activation of mothballed
power plants, deactivation (mothballing) of power plants and investment in new power
plants is undertaken. The analysis is is undertaken in an exogenous manner, as it is
based on market prices the from previous simulation.

4.5.1
Activation of Mothballed Power Plants
From a system perspective, the activation of generators is only needed in situations where
generators have been mothballed from previous market clearings. The zonal threshold
(see Table 4.5), depending on capacity remuneration mechanism, still forms the demand
for capacity within each zone. In case, the active amount of capacity within a zone goes
below the zonal threshold, mothballed generators can be activated. Two simple rules
for activation are used. First, only one generator within each zone can be activated at
each iteration. Second, the generator with capacity closest to the required within the
zone is activated. The required amount of capacity is the zonal threshold subtracted
the capacity from active generators within the respective zone. Nonetheless, mothballed
generators are not expected to be activated for two reasons. First, most power systems
initially have overcapacity and deactivation below the zonal threshold is not allowed.
Second, no generators are initially mothballed.
4.5.2
Deactivation of Power Plants
Presumably, most electricity systems initially have substantial amounts of overcapacity
compared to the reserve margin. From a system perspective, the deactivation of power
plants should only be allowed, if security of supply is not decreased. In this project,
the reserve margin is used as reference for the minimum amount of active capacity in
the system. The deactivation of generators is determined based on the input showed on
Figure 4.4. The generators with highest missing money will be deactivated first, since the
missing money in this context indicates the willingness to stay in the market. However,
two constraints are applied to the deactivation. First, a maximum of two generators
per zone is allowed to be deactivated. Second, if the deactivation of a generator makes
the total amount of active capacity within a zone go below the zonal threshold, the
generator is not allowed to be deactivated. The zonal threshold depends on type of

4.5.3
Investment in New Power Plants
For each time a market optimization is run, an investment analysis is undertaken based
on an exogenous approach - independent of whether existing plants have been activated
or deactivated. The revenue that could have been made in the market for the specific
power plant serves as the basis for the investment analysis.
It is assumed that the
generator will not influence future market prices.
A standard evaluation criteria for investments in power plants is the use of spreads,
which consists of the annual average electricity price deducted variable costs expressing
the potential profit for a power plant. Under normal circumstances, a projection of
future electricity prices is used as basis for evaluating an investment. However, future
electricity prices are not calculated in the simulations. Therefore, electricity prices from
the prior simulation works as the investment basis.
It is assumed that whenever the electricity price goes above or equals the marginal-price,
the specific power plant will produce. If the revenue from sales of energy is enough to
cover all cost including capital cost, an investment in the given plant-type is profitable.

The expected revenue from power-plant operation also depends on the type of capacity
remuneration mechanism (CRM) that is used in the market (see Table 4.6). With the
use of peak load reserves, the expected revenue for a power-plant operating on market
terms is only from sales of energy. With capacity markets, a separate revenue from
the sale of capacity can be expected. It is assumed that new plants will receive the
latest capacity market clearing price times its capacity. Again, the assumption being
that the power plant is scheduled and does not influence future prices in the capacity
market.
Furthermore, the generator must be scheduled in the day-ahead market to
receive revenue from the capacity market. Otherwise, a small peak load generator, with
high marginal cost and low fixed- and capital-cost, could potentially earn enough revenue
from the capacity market to cover all cost, without making sales of energy. The peak
load generator would in that sense become a purely financial product.
If a premium is used, the expected revenue, for a power-plant operating on market
terms, is from sales of energy and a possible premium for the selected generator types.
In the energy only market, the only expected revenue is from sales of energy.

Second, it is intended to investigate
the price composition of generators, at which investments are profitable. The increased
renewable production will expectantly benefit investments in generators with low capital
expenditures, if capacity remuneration mechanisms are introduced.
Investments in
power plants with large capital expenditures is not expected, without high average energy
prices and a high capacity support level.
Similar to the activation and deactivation processes, the investment process is con
-strained. Investments are only allowed for one power plant type within each zone during
each simulation. As example, investments can be undertaken in a coal- and CCGT-plant
within the same zone, but not two CCGT-plants within the same zone during the same
simulation. However, investment in the same plant-type can be undertaken over several
optimizations. This is implemented to minimize a boom-bust circle, where investments
are undertaken in several power plants of the same type, which will later be deactivated,
as they influences the electricity price, to their own disadvantage by decreasing their
required revenue.

Modelling of Peak Load Reserve
In this project, two main approaches are used to undertake the modelling of the peak load
reserve. First, the approach used in Sweden and Finland with a fixed size of peak load
reserves is modelled. This approach does not seek to solve the missing money problem,
but can be seen as a transitional model for shorter periods. The costs are controllable
and is not expected to increase with increasing production from renewable energy sources.
However, plants not included as peak load reserves are expected not to have all their
fixed costs covered, and could eventually be mothballed or decommissioned. Second,
an approach attempting to increase revenues for conventional generators operating on
market terms by increasing the size and activation price of peak load reserves is modelled.
The reasoning being that, unless a generator gets is fixed costs covered, by sales of energy,
it will usually be decommissioned, unless it is included as a peak load reserve. With
increasing production from renewable energy sources, the size and activation price of
peak load reserves is expected to increase, as more generators are expected not to get

remaining supply cannot meet demand, with a marginal price set higher than the most
expensive plant in the day-ahead market clearing. Even if the most expensive plant is
used as a peak load reserve, the activation price will always be higher than its marginal
cost. To ensure that the cheapest plant among peak load reserves is activated first,
the activation price is varied slightly depending on marginal cost. In that sense, a coal
fired power plant is activated before a oil fired power plant due to lower marginal cost,
without regard for start-up cost and technical constraints. The entire process is updated
continuously until a steady-state is found. If mothballed plants are activated, operating
plants are deactivated or investment in new plants is undertaken, the process is run
again. In case no plants are added or subtracted the portfolio of power plants a steady
state is assumed to be found.

6.3
Simulation of Capacity Market
In this section, simulations are undertaken using the capacity market model described in
Section 5.1. At each wind penetration level, the activation, deactivation and investment
loop is run (see Section 4.5). The principle is visualized on Figure 6.7, where the amount
of small gas plants is increased until the reserve margin is met. Naturally, the investment
is only undertaken, if it is profitable. The costs displayed subsequently in this section
is after the activation, deactivation and investment loop is run. There are two general
constraints for the investment analysis. First, if total active capacity in the system is
unable to meet the required reserve margin, investment can be undertaken in any type
of plant, without expected energy production. Second, if total active capacity in the
system is able to meet the required reserve margin, investment can only be undertaken
in plants, with expected energy production.
The first constraint ensures that investment is allowed in plants needed to meet the
reserve margin. Investment in small gas-fired peak load plants is profitable, when total
installed capacity cannot meet the reserve margin. At this point, the capacity market
price is at least the price-cap, set to the annualized investment costs of a new peak
load gas plant. The second constraint limits a boom-bust cycle. For example, at high
wind penetration, coal and nuclear plants will have high amounts of missing money.
The capacity market prices increases and investment in gas plants is profitable, only
supported by capacity payments. However, a complete transition to gas is not intended.

In the simulations, it was assumed that all suppliers will bid their true missing money
and that no suppliers attempt to manipulate the market prices. Also, as previously
described, full control over activation, deactivation and investment in old and new plants
was assumed. In reality, the capacity market is intended to leave the decision making
on new investments and decommissioning of power plants to the market participants.
Furthermore, the possibilities of trading is also present in a capacity market.
The
product traded, capacity, becomes a financial product. Traders may sell capacity not at
their disposal, with the hope that they can trade capacity at lower prices later, or earn
more on sales of capacity than penalties from non-performance in the energy market.
Another approach of selling capacity cheap and bidding high in the energy market can
potentially lead to a market distortion, where capacity acquired in the capacity market
has no intention to be available in the energy market. To avoid market manipulation,
the capacity market operated by PJM, has very large penalties for non-performance
in the energy market (see Section 3.3.2.1). PJM also performs tests on all generators
available capacity, and can at the event of nontransparent bids require full information
on the generators financial data. This removes the free market incentives, but reduces
the utilization of market power and avoids distortion of capacity and energy prices.
Nonetheless, the market monitoring is pervasive and can potentially lead to higher
consumer costs. This is not included in the capacity market simulations. Therefore,
it is expected that the cost of operating a capacity market will be more expensive than
calculated in the simulations.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion of the results, modelling assumptions and how capacity
support mechanisms can operate in smart energy systems.
8.1
Smart Energy Systems and Flexible Prices
Besides the actions taken to create the single European electricity market and increase
the production from renewable energy sources, measures are also taken to create smarter
energy systems, by enforcing more demand-side participation. As presented in chapter 2,
the limited demand-side participation is one the main reasons, why missing money exist.
A smart grid implies enhanced price-signals to consumers, by exposing them to real-time
prices, and thereby increase demand-response from the use of centrally controlled heat
pump operation and electric vehicle charging.
A more flexible demand-side would among others reduce the need for back-up generation
capacity, when the renewable production is low. However, questions remain on how
flexible the demand will become. Presently, demand-side participation is only observed,
when large profit can be made, by energy heavy industrial facilities. Nonetheless, the
increased demand-side participation will not reduce the missing money problem for the
conventional generators. Most likely, it will simply reduce the necessary price-spikes.
This is comparable with the triads used in UK to charge consumers a high payment,
based on their consumption in the three half-hours of highest demand. Large consumers
will reduce demand in peak load hours, to avoid high triad payments, limiting the
needed price-spikes, which reduces the revenue of conventional generators. Until the
demand-side is fully flexible, a secondary revenue stream to cover fixed operational costs
for conventional generators is needed.
The capacity support mechanisms can also be used to impose higher demand-side
participation. The capacity market can include demand-side resources on equal terms
with generation capacity, and the peak load reserve may partly consist of demand-side
resources. Implementing capacity support mechanisms is not a barrier towards more
demand-side participation, and nor is a capacity support mechanism a barrier towards
a smart energy system.

8 Discussion
8.2
Modelling Limitations
The simulation model used in this report is a deterministic model.
The modelling
inputs of wind production, solar production and load profiles is taken as fixed inputs.
Especially, the uncertainty of production from renewable energy sources is not covered.
Furthermore, the forced and planned outage on conventional power plants is not considered.
These aspects influences the occurrence of price-spikes in the energy and balancing
markets. With higher renewable penetration in the system, conventional generators will
increasingly rely on hours with very high prices. The occurrence of these price-spikes is
set to occur in hours with outages on large power plants, high consumption and a very
low renewable production. This composition of events is indeed random.
Therefore, the prices calculated in the model may be too low, as the random occurring
price-spikes are not modelled. However, the behaviour of individual generators is not
modelled as well. Considering that a generator preferably have higher availability in
peak load hours with high prices, than in hours with low prices, is not included in
the modelling. Contrary, this potentially limits the occurrence of price-spikes, as more
generators may be available in peak load periods.
Secondary sources of revenue; intra-day markets, regulating power markets, ancillary
services and the use of operating reserves is not modelled. Revenue from these markets
may reduce the missing money problem, but it is not evaluated that it has the potential
to solve it. The quantity and prices exchanged is simply too low to compensate all
generators.
However, more sources of revenue, than sales of energy, is available in
operating markets. Also, the operation of combined heat and power (CHP) plants is not
modelled. This allows generators to make up losses from sales of electricity, by sales of
heat. The missing money problem may solve itself, by coupling the two markets. Still,
keeping in mind that most conventional generators operating in Europe is not CHP, the
only revenue is made from sales of electricity.
Another cornerstone in the modelling is the cost estimates on conventional generators.
This is used as input to evaluate the selected capacity support mechanisms ability to
reduce or solve the missing money problem. Again, depending on the ability of each
generator to maximize profit, bilateral reliability contracts agreed with system operators
and its actual cost of operation, each generator in operation will have different missing
money.
Across Europe, the market framework conditions are various and influences
the cost structure of the generators. The results of the missing money problem across
Europe may prove different, if actual fixed operational costs were known.

8.3 Results

8.3
Results
The calculated total consumer payments on the energy only market, capacity market,
peak load reserve and premium all showed different results under increased renewable
penetration.
The capacity market can be used to resolve the missing money problem, but will
lead to higher consumer payments. These calculated consumer payments are around 20
% higher than the total consumer payments and remaining missing money in an energy
only market. However, the cost of the capacity market may be higher in reality. It
is assumed that all suppliers bid their true missing money. Unless strong surveillance
to mitigate utilization of market power, the capacity market is potentially the victim
of strategic behaviour to drive up prices. Manipulating the capacity market is easier
than energy markets, due to a fixed demand and close reserve margin. Withholding
capacity can potentially cause a large price increase. Therefore, the calculated costs of
the capacity market will most likely be higher, if implemented in a functioning market.
Regarding the use of the peak load reserve (PLR) as a capacity support mechanism,
important design features must be taken into consideration. The size and activation
price of the PLR influences the missing money for generators operating on market
terms.
It was showed that increasing the PLR amount and activation price can be
used to reduce the missing money. Especially, at high wind penetration levels, the PLR
reduced the missing money with a comparable increase in consumer payments. However,
operating larger peak load reserves is not in line with current European standards. These
specifically want to exclude the use of state-aid and reduce political interference to create
a single European energy only market. Holding a PLR of 15 % or 20 % of peak load may
be too excessive. The larger amount of PLR´s, the lower activation price, to provide
conventional generators operating on market terms with adequate revenue. Operating
a smaller PLR, similar to the Swedish on 5% of peak load, its activation price must be
much higher. The cost of holding a PLR is based on estimates of fixed operating costs.
In reality, the costs may be higher, but are not set too increase consumer payments
noticeably. The PLR is not set to be more exposed to strategic behaviour, than seen
in operating energy only markets. Although, operating a large PLR, its activation can
more easily be caused in peak load periods, by withholding capacity from the energy
market.
The use of a premium to selected generators gave very useful results on a capacity
support mechanism, which must be avoided. Obviously, the premium implemented was
excessively high, as it was set too cover the missing money completely for the selected
generators. However, the results can be related to reality. As mentioned in Section 3.5,
the Swedish government has recently removed a tax per produced unit of energy on
hydro and nuclear generation. Referring this to the results calculated in this report, the
reduced tax will only help to reduce electricity prices, potentially spark investment in
hydro and nuclear, and thereby removing the profit for hydro and nuclear. Providing a
premium to selected generator types, or reducing a tax, will not solve the missing money
problem.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In this project, the main concern was to investigate the influence of increased production
from variable renewable energy sources on conventional generators revenue, and how
different capacity support mechanisms can be used to provide additional revenue, covering
their missing money from fixed operation costs.
The optimization models used to
undertake the simulations were implemented in the Python programming language.
Six different capacity support mechanisms in operation and suggested in the literature
was described. These include the peak load reserve, capacity payments, capacity markets,
reliability options, capacity subscriptions and the use of a premium payment. Out of the
six capacity support mechanisms, the peak load reserve, capacity market and premium
payment was selected for further investigation in the simulations.
The energy only
market was also simulated. Two market clearing models, the economic dispatch (ED)
and unit commitment (UC), were setup for later use in the simulations. Cost estimates
on conventional generators variable, fixed and capital cost was compiled.
The cost
estimates were used to quantify the missing money for individual generators and in the
system.
Approaches to model the peak load reserve, capacity market and premium
payments were also developed. The simulations were undertaken using the IEEE 24-bus
test system and a European test system. All simulations were undertaken assuming
investment costs in renewable generation as sunk.
The simulations of the energy only market showed that, conventional generators are
left with more missing money from fixed operational costs, when the wind penetration
in the system increases. The increased wind penetration will result in a greater need
for support, if the same amount of reserves is to be retained. The two market clearing
models (ED and UC) were compared in regards of their influence on the missing money
problem, under increased wind penetration. The UC model will cause lower missing
money in the system stemming from variable costs, which includes start-up cost and
variable operation and maintenance costs. However, when the fixed operational costs
are included, the choice of market clearing model do not have any significant influence
on reducing the missing money in the system.
The capacity market simulations showed that increasing the renewable penetration
will only lead to reduced consumer payments, compared to a system without any renewable
production, when the capacity market is implemented.
The capacity payments will
increase, as the renewable penetration is increased. At renewable penetration levels of
around 60 % and above, the capacity payments will be higher than payments for energy.
Also, the generator type with highest missing money, price-setter in the capacity market,
changes from peak load plants to base load plants, when the renewable penetration
is increased.
Nuclear and coal fired power plants may see themselves be replaced
==PAGE:113==
94
9 Conclusion
by gas-fired peak load plants with low fixed operational costs and low investment
costs. Performing the same simulations on the European system showed similar results.
Although, the wind penetration level at which capacity payments will equal payments
for energy is around 55 %. However, this level is expected to increase, when the amount
of reserves in the European system is decreased, leading to higher payments for energy.
The simulations also revealed that large differences in capacity market clearing prices
across Europe can be expected.
The capacity market prices are higher for systems
relying purely on nuclear energy (France), than systems with a variety of generation
technologies.
The peak load reserve (PLR) simulations showed two important results. First, the
Swedish PLR model, with a size of 5% of peak load, does not reduce the missing money
in the system. Second, increasing the size and activation price of PLR’s can serve to
reduce the missing money problem. Increasing the size of the peak load reserve, with an
activation price just above highest bid, showed to be unable to reduce the missing money
problem significantly, when the wind penetration was increased. Therefore, PLR´s with
a size of 15 % and 20 % of the peak load and with increased activation price was
investigated. The PLR with a size of 15 % of peak load and a activation price of 1000
e/MWh showed the best results, in terms of reducing the missing money in the system,
with a corresponding increase in consumer payments. In fact, at wind penetration levels
above 40-50 %, this PLR configuration showed the best results of all the simulated
capacity support mechanisms, in terms of reducing the missing money in the system,
while the corresponding increase in consumer payments is as low as possible. Still, at
wind penetration levels above 60 %, the activation price needs to be even higher to
reduce the missing money in the system towards zero.
The simulations of the premium payment provided very clear results. Providing a
premium per produced unit of energy, to cover the missing money for selected generator
types, will not reduce the missing money in the system. Instead, it allows premium
receiving generators to produce at lower prices. At a certain wind penetration level,
the required premium is high enough for the premium receiving generators to undercut
wind and cause total consumer payments to increase. The breaking point, at a certain
wind penetration level, where wind energy is undercut by premium receiving generators,
reduces as generators with higher marginal cost are selected to receive the premium.
The premium may be excessively high, but proves the point that a premium or a tax
reduction per produced unit of energy will not resolve the missing money problem.
Overall, the simulations showed that increasing the size and activation price of a
peak load reserve is better than implementing a capacity market, at high renewable
penetration levels, in terms of, reducing the remaining missing money in the system,
with the smallest corresponding increase in consumer payments.
The implementation, simulation and analysis of the investigated market models has
provided important knowledge on energy system modelling using the python programming
language. Improved knowledge on the market mechanisms in terms of the missing money
problem, the use of capacity support mechanisms, and cost structures of conventional
generators in highly renewable energy systems has been acquired in this master project.

9.1 Further Work
95
9.1
Further Work
The conclusion and discussion highlighted the results and some of the assumptions taken
in this project. Suggestions on how to improve the model and improve the results from
this report is listed in the following.
• Stochastic input parameters. The input parameters to the model were modelled
as deterministic.
These included wind production, solar production and load
profiles.
Furthermore, the forced and unforced outages on conventional power
plants was not included in the model.
The price-spikes needed to cover most
thermal generators fixed operational costs is occurring randomly. Coincidence of
outages on large power plants, low renewable production and high demand can lead
to price spikes. The uncertainties should be considered, as the mismatch between
demand and supply affects the electricity price. The importance of undertaking
stochastic modelling in highly renewable energy systems is also mentioned by M.
Morales (2014).
• Aspects of capacity support mechanisms. Depending on aspect, the imple
-mentation of a capacity support mechanism will be met with different views. The
consumers are interested in the cheapest electricity and the suppliers are interested
in getting the largest profit. These aspects are covered in this report, but the
possibilities of trading are not covered.
For example, implementing a capacity
market will open up the possibilities for trading with a new commodity. Therefore,
investigating how to trade capacity is an important aspect for future investigation.
This would also include an implementation of how to model strategic behaviour in
both the energy and capacity market. The gaming aspects that characterize the
energy and capacity markets can provide different results from the simulations.
• Improve the peak load reserve simulation on the European model. The
simulations of the peak load reserve on the European model showed different results
than on the 24-bus model. This was due to the inclusion of hydro as peak load
reserve. To undertake further analysis on the European test system, countries with
high dependency on hydro generation should not operate a peak load reserve. This
approach should show improved results.
• Political aspects. Most countries have clear defined political determined pathways
towards a renewable energy system. Therefore, it would be very interesting to
investigate the influence of the capacity support mechanisms in a future energy
system, where all fossil fueled power plants are shutdown. The increased demand
response from electric vehicles and heat pumps, as well as the interaction between
electric, heat and gas markets, in a completely renewable energy system, can
change the results in this report. The use of a capacity support mechanism may
not be required.

A.2 Appendix B: Call Option Example

A.2
Appendix B: Call Option Example
The following example is based on S. Stoft (2006). The load purchases a call-option
with a strike price of e1000/MWh from a supplier for a price of e40.000/MW. The call
option gives the load the right to buy power from the supplier at a price of e1000/MWh
at any time. Load exercises the call-option exactly when the market price is greater
than e1000/MWh. It is assumed that the spot price goes to e5000/MWh in scarcity
periods.
Because the call-option is a financial agreement, the load will purchase its
power from the market operator for e5000/MWh and the supplier will sell its power
to the market operator for e5000/MWh. However, the supplier then have to pay the
load e4000/MWh under the call-option, so that in effect, the load has bought power
from the supplier at a net cost of e1000/MWh. This means, that if a generator supplies
load in scarcity periods it earns a net revenue of e1000/MWh, and if the supplier fails
to perform during shortage hours, it makes nothing and loses e4000/MWh paid to the
load to cover the high price.
In Table A.8, an example of call-options is presented.
In the energy market without call-options the spot price is capped at e5000/MWh.
The spot-price rises up to e5000/MWh ten hours during a year. The consumer has
bought a call-option for e40,000/MW from the supplier, set at the ”missing money”
from expected peak-price revenues. A good performing generator present in all shortage
hours will make exactly the same revenue from the spot market as with the call-option.
As seen, the payments from consumers is exactly the same with a call-option as without
a call-option. Good performers are still rewarded and bad performers are penalized.
The call-option removes market-risk of uncertain price-spikes by assuring generators a
fixed annual revenue and prevents the exercise of market power, as suppliers would be
penalized whenever withholding capacity from the spot market. The call-option also
hedges the consumer against high spot prices, while at the same time assuring adequate
revenue for well-performing generators. Still, keeping in mind that the call-options do
not directly resolve the missing money problem, it only provides the income a generator
could have expected in the spot market.
