Abstract
DTU Roadrunners is a team of engineering students at the Technical University of
Denmark that has been participating on the Shell Eco-Marathon for more than ten
years. Their aim is to design and build a prototype in order to make it the most fuel
eﬃcient vehicle. Due to new regulations in the competition, their car, already highly
eﬃcient, is not able to compete more; therefore a completely new body shell needs to
be designed.
One of the most important factors that determine fuel eﬃciency of a vehicle is its aerody-
namic drag. Thus, in the present project, the aerodynamic performance of the prototype
is analyzed and improved by means of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) using the
software STAR CCM+. A validation against the Ahmed model is made. This reveals
that a steady and transient state Reynolds Average Navier Stokes (RANS) simulation
in combination with SST k −ω turbulence model gives enough accurate results for the
current study.
An iterative process is performed over a signiﬁcant number of diﬀerent geometries. These
shapes are generated using NACA airfoils to design the body shell, they are also based
on previous aerodynamic analysis and taking into account design constraints such as
race regulation or ergonomics.
After using the conventional approach (N designs = N simulations) to generate the
shell, the drag of the already low drag vehicle is reduced even further using a newly
introduced methodology in the automotive sector; the adjoint method. Some limitations
are discovered on the tool, mostly related with the mesh quality and the diﬃculty to
achieve a smooth surface. Although some adjustments are required during the procedure,
is managed to reduce the drag of the shell even more than before using the adjoint
method.
As a result of the new rules constraints, the new prototype has a 13% larger frontal area
than the old vehicle, however, reductions of 24.5% of the overall drag and 11.3% wetted
area are achieved. Also the CD · A is reduced by 24.6%. Therefore, a signiﬁcant aero-
dynamic improvement of the prototype’s shell has been achieved in the present project.
Keywords: fuel eﬃcient vehicle, Shell Eco-Marathon, aerodynamics, streamlined body,
CFD, optimization, adjoint solver

Chapter 1
Introduction
Aerodynamics plays a main role in the engineering of almost all vehicles. Race cars
are designed to generate large down-force for traction and to minimize lap times, while
keeping the associated drag penalty in check. Production automobiles are designed for
low aerodynamics drag in order to increase fuel economy, while still producing a pleasant
aesthetic shape for the users. The ones in which this project is focused, the fuel eﬃcient
vehicles (FEV), are designed to have the lowest aerodynamic drag possible to push the
boundaries in ultra-low energy consumption competitions, while following a set of design
restrictions.
There are many approaches to reduce fuel consumption, however one of the most eﬀective
is to reduce the driving resistance of the vehicle. In the fuel eﬃcient vehicles the driving
speed is relatively constant and the race tracks are ﬂat and leveled, the forces required
to accelerate the vehicle and to go uphill are small. Therefore, the propulsion is used
almost entirely to overcome the sum of the aerodynamic drag and the rolling resistance.
Figure 1.1 plots the required overcome forces for fuel eﬃcient vehicles driving at constant
velocity as a function of vehicle speed. Even though these vehicles drive at a relatively
low speed, the aerodynamic drag still has a great inﬂuence in the overall driving resis-
tance. By the time the vehicle is driving at 30 km/h, about 42% of the total power
required to overcome the driving resistance is used with aerodynamic drag.
In that way the aerodynamics becomes one of the most important factors aﬀecting the
overall power eﬃciency of a fuel eﬃcient vehicle. By reducing the aerodynamic drag,
less power is needed and fuel consumption can be reduced, the main focus in the type
of vehicles in this report.

1.1
Project Background
The Shell Eco-marathon is an annual student competition organized by the global group
Shell where students of around the world are challenged to design, build and drive the
most energy-eﬃcient one-person vehicle. The Roadrunners team of Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) has always been an active participant in that competition with
diﬀerent vehicles.
As a result of a change in the Shell Eco-Marathon oﬃcial rules, one of their vehicles,
commonly called Innovator and already quite successful on aerodynamics, is not longer
allowed to compete. The new rule do not allow to drive vehicles with rear wheel steering,
only front wheel steering is permitted. Since Innovator 6.0 not fulﬁll the requirements,
the team took this opportunity to design a completely new vehicle with front wheel
steering and carefully studying its aerodynamic performance, the Innovator 7.0.
The major challenge is to keep or even improve the same aerodynamic eﬃciency as the
old Innovator since these new steering system will lead to a new vehicle with a larger
frontal area. However, it has many engineering problems, the ”best” body for the new
Innovator, is not necessarily the body with the absolute lowest drag. The vehicles system,
including the driver, chassis and energy/drive system, must work in concert to produce
the maximum output. That is why a new fuel eﬃcient vehicle with a good aerodynamic
performance will be designed, but always keeping in mind that aerodynamics is not the
only factor aﬀecting it.

1.2
Aims and objectives
The main aim of this project is to reduce the aerodynamic drag force of a fuel eﬃcient
vehicle by optimizing and modifying its shape. Additionally, the challenge is to obtain
a better understanding of the aerodynamics involved in designing a streamlined land
vehicle.
In order to achieve the main aim, the speciﬁc objectives for the project are:
• Determining the ﬂow behavior on a streamlined land vehicle by simulating the
body of it in a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software using a simulation
tool for validate land vehicles aerodynamics.
• To validate the ﬂow behavior on the prototypes proposed by DTU Roadrunners
and based on the results and the aerodynamic theory involved in a streamlined
body propose an optimized.
• Reduce the drag of the optimized proposal by means of a CFD optimization
method.
1.3
Methodology
This project is carried out in conjunction with another thesis where the transmission,
the steering system and suspension of the new fuel eﬃcient vehicle has been designed
[22]. Both projects are connected and any change in one of them needs to be informed
to the other.
The thesis starts with an overview about the Shell Eco-marathon competition, after-
wards, the four diﬀerent vehicle geometries proposed by DTU Roadrunners are pre-
sented. Chapter 4, introduces the aerodynamics fundamentals relevant to the vehicle
body design and the CFD techniques employed in this project.
Next, the process followed by means of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to obtain
the ﬁnal prototype shape is divided in three phases: validation, analysis and optimiza-
tion. This process is schematically described in Figure 1.2.
Validation phase
The main goal of the validation phase is to come up with a simulation setup that
provides reliable results that are applicable to the fuel eﬃcient vehicle. In Chapter 5 an
initial setup based on literature is created with the Ahmed model [15]. After solving the

Analysis phase
In this phase of the project, two geometries of the vehicles proposed by DTU Roadrun-
ners are analyzed in Chapter 6 using the setup of the validation phase (altered with the
new geometries). This part is conducted in order to acquire an understanding of the
ﬂow ﬁeld around the diﬀerent geometries and take some decisions on the design of the
ﬁnal shape.
Optimization phase
Finally in the optimization phase provided by Chapter 7, diﬀerent CAD-models geome-
tries of the vehicle are generated regarding the results extracted from the analysis phase.
These geometries are tested, compared and evaluated for choose the most eﬃcient. After
that the Adjoint method is applied, depending on the results, the geometry is automat-
ically reshaped, this operation is repeated several times.
To sum up, the resulting conclusions and recommendations for further investigation are
given in Chapter 8.

2.1
Philosophy behind the Shell Eco-Marathon competi-

The Shell Eco-Marathon is a competition that challenges students around the world to
see who can design and produce the vehicle that travels furthest using less energy. This
competition is held in Europe, America and Asia.
2.1.1
Basic principles
The basic principle of the competition is to travel the furthest on the equivalent of one
liter of fuel, being in that way the most fuel eﬃcient vehicle.
There are two categories of vehicles, the UrbanConcept class, that focuses on more con-
ventional concept designs to meet the needs of today’s drivers, and the Prototype class,
that focuses on maximizing eﬃciency and where the teams can design more futuristic
streamlined vehicles. This last category is the one in which the report will be focused.
Each category is also divided by the following energy types:
• Internal combustion engine fuels that includes gasoline, diesel, ethanol and liquid
fuel made from natural gas (CNG)
• Electric mobility, where the vehicles are powered by hydrogen fuel cells or lithium-
based batteries

Chapter 4
Background Theory
This chapter provides a basic understanding of the aerodynamics involved in the body
design of a fuel eﬃcient vehicle and the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) tech-
niques employed to simulate and optimize it.
4.1
Principles of aerodynamics for low-drag vehicles
In order to design the shape of a competitive low-drag vehicle, it is necessary to have
in mind and understand the fundamentals of aerodynamic drag and its causes. Low
aerodynamic drag vehicles have been under development for long time, as reducing
aerodynamic drag is vital for improving fuel economy.
In ﬂuid mechanics terms, cars are classiﬁed as bluﬀbodies and aircraft as streamlined
bodies. Bluﬀbodies exhibit large ﬂow separation, resulting in a thick trailing wake. On
the other hand, the ﬂow on a streamlined body is in great part attached. Bodies with
separation exhibit much larger drag forces than streamlined bodies without separation.
Figure 4.1 reﬂects this diﬀerence and by comparing these two shapes it is seen that the
airfoil is the one that shows minimum drag, because of its streamline shape. In addition,
it shows less or almost no turbulence at the end. It is important to notice that on the
streamlined bodies the drag is primarily viscous drag and on the bluﬀbodies the drag
force is primarily pressure drag.
Aircraft aerodynamics theory can not be applied directly onto land vehicles, various
characteristics make the ﬂow ﬁeld diﬀerent around the vehicles. To design a streamlined
land vehicle some important and speciﬁc aerodynamic concepts and parameters must
be kept in mind.

4.1.1
A short history of streamlined land vehicles
For some decades the world’s automotive industry has been under pressure to develop
vehicles which are less demanding on fossil fuels and to be less harmful to the environ-
ment. Alternative fuels and the usage of diﬀerent technologies (e.g. electrical vehicles,
hybrids, ...) are some of the most important research areas, but they are not the only
approaches to improve vehicle’s eﬃciency.
In the 1970’s, car aerodynamics were not a major concern, but with the emergence of the
oil crisis it was discovered that the amount of the aerodynamic drag has a great inﬂuence
in fuel consumption. Since then, decreasing the drag force acting on road vehicles and
thus their fuel consumption, became a major concern for car manufacturers.
The ﬁrst known streamlined land vehicle was developed by Jaray and Klemperer in 1920
[37], they discovered that an axisymmetric teardrop body (i.e., torpedo) had the lowest
drag in free air but when the body was brought close to the ground the drag increased
drastically. To preserve the low drag characteristics, Jaray and Klemperer cambered the
body, ﬂattened the belly and/or arched higher the topside of the body. A version of
the 1920 Jaray-Klemperer body was constructed in the 1950s and it was found to have
a drag coeﬃcient of only 0.15, around a third of today’s passenger cars. Later, more
bodies were designed and constructed achieving even better results (Figure 4.2).
Nowadays aerodynamics play a major role in the engineering world of almost all vehicles.
Race cars are designed to generate large down-force while keeping the associated drag
penalty down. Road automobiles are designed for low aerodynamic drag in order to
decrease the fuel consumption but still implying an aesthetically pleasant shape for
the occupants and luggage. Finally, the engineering discipline under examination in
this report is fuel-economy cars, in which reducing aerodynamic drag is crucial. This
discipline involves vehicles where the vehicle speed is relatively constant and thus the
inertial eﬀects (accelerating mass) are small. Therefore, the propulsion is used almost

4.1.2
Aerodynamic forces
There are always two basic categories of aerodynamic forces acting on each point of
the vehicle surface, pressure and shear. The pressure force is normal to the surface of
the body, due to the relative velocity between the two. This force is responsible for a
vehicle’s lift and part of the drag. On the other hand, the shear force (e.g. friction) is
tangent to the surface of the body due to the viscosity of the air and contributes only
to drag (Figure 4.3) .
The resultant forces can be divided into various components and the most common
directions are shown in Figure 4.4. Based on this coordinate system, three forces can be
deﬁned:
• Lift (L) is the force that acts upward (z direction), lifting the car oﬀthe road
surface. For passenger or race cars it is preferable a negative lift, commonly known
as downforce, in order to push the car to the surface. In contrast, as explained
later, in the case of a low drag vehicle both forces (lift and downforce) have to be
avoided.

• Side force (Y ) is positive into the y direction. The side force is important in

4.1.3

As previously mentioned, aerodynamic drag is the force working in opposite direction
to the vehicle’s velocity, hence it has to be overcome by the engine in order to accelerate
or drive steady.

the total drag of the vehicle is inﬂuenced by other factors than only aerodynamics

reduction in the total drag value of a fuel eﬃcient vehicle. It must not be forgotten that
the rolling resistance also has a great inﬂuence on the overall drag, for that reason it is
important to be careful since an improvement to reduce aerodynamic drag can worsen
the rolling resistance.

The viscous friction drag consists of two components, the skin friction, Dskin, and the
boundary layer pressure drag, Dpres BL. They are grouped as viscous friction because
both are a direct result of the boundary layer.
The boundary layer is the small layer of air ﬂowing over the body between the vehicle
surface and the free-stream ﬂow. Because of the ﬂuid viscosity, the velocity of the air

4.1.4
Basic vehicle body concepts
As shown in section 2.1.3 a fuel economy vehicle can have many forms, but in order to
reduce the aerodynamic drag and design a better low-drag prototype some factors in
relation to aerodynamics need to be considered:
• Body shape
• Stagnation point
• Ground clearance
• Side wind behavior
• Wheel fairings
4.1.4.1
Body shape
The body shell required a shape that will achieve some degree of laminar ﬂow with
minimized separation. The logical choice for the side view and top view of the vehicle
is a standard airfoil, leaving in that way a teardrop shape.
As can be seen in Figure 4.8 the same body performs diﬀerently depending if it is in
free air or close to the ground.
On a symmetric airfoil in free air, the average pressure on the top is equal to the bottom
and therefore there is no lift, on the other hand when this symmetric airfoil is near the
ground, the area between the body and the ground behaves like a Venturi tube, the
velocity increases and therefore the pressure decreases producing in that way downforce.
As mentioned before in this type of vehicles, the lift and the downforce should be avoided,
therefore a solution to avoid this downforce can be to camber the body such that its
belly is ﬂattened. When an airﬂow close to the ground is cambered, the airﬂow pattern
changes, the velocity on the belly is decreased, and if the right amount of camber is
designed, this velocity can be matched to the top of the body and lift eliminated.
Another design factor that can reduce the vehicle’s drag is to make it as narrow as
possible, since a narrow car will have smaller nose area and the laminar ﬂow at the nose
has high shear compared to turbulent ﬂow downstream [40].

4.1.4.2
Stagnation point
The stagnation point of the vehicle is the point at which the air splits to pass above or

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a branch of ﬂuid mechanics that uses numerical
analysis and algorithms to analyze systems involving ﬂuid ﬂow. This chapter introduces
the governing equations that are used in this study. The CFD software used in that
project is the STAR-CCM+ ver. 10.02, developed by CD-adapco.

Three basic approaches to modeling turbulence are available in STAR-CCM+; models
that provide closure of the Reynolds Average Navier Stokes (RANS) equations, Large
Eddy Simulation (LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation (DES). Most simulations will
rely on the ﬁrst approach (RANS) due to it is the one that uses lower computational
resources while still providing accurate results, LES is skipped as an option due to its
high computational costs. DES is an hybrid modeling approach that combines the best
features of RANS and LES together, therefore this technique is also applied in order to
see the eﬀects of the model.
4.2.1
Governing equations
Fluid dynamics is the study of ﬂuids in motion and is based on the three laws of con-
servation for physical systems:
• Conservation of mass
• Conservation of linear momentum (Newton’s second law of motion)
• Conservation of energy (ﬁrst law of thermodynamics)
These physical principles are developed into the fundamental equations of ﬂuid dynamics:
the continuity, momentum and energy equations. The form of governing equations that
is generally used in CFD is known as the Navier-Stokes equations that include the eﬀect
of the viscosity on the ﬂuid ﬂow.

such as body and gravity forces are absent.

4.2.3
Detached Eddy Simulation (DES)
As mentioned before, DES is a hybrid modeling approach that combines the best aspects
of RANS and LES methodologies in a single solution strategy. The model attempts to
treat near-wall regions with RANS, and treat the rest of the ﬂow with LES. STAR-
CCM+ recommends to take care with that model as is not the solution to all turbulence
modeling problems, it needs to be sure that the mesh resolution requirements can be
met and the high computational costs involved are justiﬁed.
4.2.4
Turbulence models
The turbulence models are responsible for providing closure of the governing equations in
turbulent ﬂows. This is typically modeled by adding one or two extra equations. Two of
the most popular models for RANS simulations are the k−ϵ and k−ω turbulence models,
on the other hand, when a DES simulation is performed often the Spalart Allamaras
turbulence model is applied.
4.2.4.1
k −ϵ turbulence model
The k −ϵ turbulence model is a two-equation model in which transport equations are
solved for the turbulent kinetic energy k and its dissipation rate ϵ. This model provides
a good compromise between robustness, computational cost and accuracy and is one
of the most used for industrial applications. The main disadvantage of Standard k −ϵ
model is that it does not perform very well with large adverse pressure gradients, for this
reason the Realizable variant of the k −ϵ model is most often applied since it performs
better. For the current study this last model is applied.
4.2.4.2
k −ω turbulence model
As the k−ϵ model, the k−ω is a two-equation model where the transport equations that
are solved are for the turbulent kinetic energy k, but instead of using the ϵ rate a speciﬁc
dissipation rate ω, is used. A signiﬁcative advantage of the k−ω model over the k−ϵ is its
improved performance close to the wall and under adverse pressure gradients. However,
on the other hand, the k −ω model is really sensitive to inlet boundary conditions for
internal ﬂows.
==PAGE:55==
Chapter 4. Background theory
33
On the Shear Stress Transport (SST) variant of the k−ω model, the sensitivity problem
is addressed blending a k −ϵ model in the far-ﬁeld with a k −ω model near the wall.
The SST model has been greatly applied in the aerospace industry and also is the one
used in the current study.
4.2.4.3
Spalart Allmaras turbulence model
The Spalart-Allmaras turbulence model solves a single transport equation that deter-
mines the turbulent viscosity. Spalart-Allarmas model is recommended for applications
in which the boundary layers are largely attached, like aerospace external-ﬂow applica-
tions, or in this case ultra streamlined land vehicles.
4.2.5
Finite Volume Method
To solve the complex governing non-linear equations described previously, they need to
be discretised. This is done by dividing the computational domain into a ﬁnite number
of small control volumes (cells) where the variable of interest is located at the centroid of
the control volumes. The transport equations are discretised and applied to each control
volume, since all the quantities are only known in the cell centroids. Interpolation proﬁles
are used in order to estimate the variation of the concern variable between cell centroids.
Four individual terms are discretised from the governing equation: source, convection,
diﬀusion and transient.
For the current study steady and transient simulations are executed. In a steady state
the transient term is not used and thus the concept of physical time-step is meaningless.
4.2.6

coupled with CFD, has lead to the appearance of diﬀerent optimization methodologies.
The adjoint method is one of them, an eﬃcient tool that predicts the inﬂuence of many
design parameters and physical inputs on some engineering quantity of interest, known
as cost function. In other words, in this study the method can tell how changing the
shape of the vehicle’s body will aﬀect on the overall drag.
The adjoint method provides direct guidance on the shape changes, in that case to
improve aerodynamic performance, with only one solution of the adjoint equation, which
is independent of the number of design variables. Since the vehicle optimization involves
many design variables and the traditional optimization method (N conﬁguration = N

To ensure quality and estimate accuracy of CFD simulations, veriﬁcation and validation
must be performed. The veriﬁcation checks that the equations are solved correctly and
validation is the comparison with experimental data.
Since no wind tunnel experiments are carried out to validate the results obtained for
the Eco-marathon prototypes, other validation methods are employed. To validate the
simulation model, the ﬂow around a simpliﬁed ground vehicle geometry, the Ahmed

suﬃciently accurate results when is applied on the fuel eﬃcient vehicle.
Ahmed et al. developed the Ahmed model standard automobile shape in 1984 for a
study into time-averaged ﬂow features occurring in vehicle wakes [15]. The model is a
bluﬀbody with a curved front, straight center section and an angled back-light (angle
which is variable) (Figure 5.1).

body shape, it allows to perform an experiment with regard to only one aerodynamic

the eﬀect of the mesh density is also studied. These simulations are part of the veriﬁ-
cation process. The medium mesh density is used as a basis and from here rougher and
ﬁner meshes are studied. And ﬁnally, as mentioned before, the distance between the
inlet boundary and the front edge of the Ahmed body is varied to see the eﬀect on the
results.
Many simulations have been run, but only the most relevant and with useful results of
the Ahmed model are described on Table 5.1.
All the simulations ran suﬃciently long to reach the convergence, plots of the residuals
from the governing equations, and the total drag force are used to ensure it.

5.8
Conclusions
The above analysis has clearly demonstrated that the steady state and transient sim-
ulations with the SST k −ω turbulence model are the ones that matched better with
the experimental data from Meile et. al. [36] and Lienhart et. al. [34]. This means
that from now on, only steady state and transient RANS simulations with SST k −ω
turbulence model will be run.
On the other hand, it is also observed that 4 m is an adequate distance between the
inlet boundary and the leading edge. Increasing this distance has almost no eﬀect on
the CD and it only increases the size of the computational domain. A detailed study of
the inlet distance inﬂuence can be found in Appendix A
Finally, the medium mesh density seems to be acceptable for these simulations, since
with the ﬁne mesh the results are not changing. In order to be sure that the solution
is independent of the mesh resolution, a mesh independence study is carried. The mesh

Application onto the Shell Eco

After the validation study in Chapter 5 with the Ahmed model, the two prototypes

This chapter, ﬁrstly describes the changes on the Ahmed simulation S8 and presents
the set-up that is used from now on. After that, both prototypes are simulated and
analyzed in order to see which one, or which features of each have a better aerodynamic
performance.

Vehicles geometries
The geometries of the vehicles have been provided by the DTU Roadrunners team and

simulation a free-stream velocity of 8 m/s is used, instead of the 40 m/s used on the
Ahmed model. This leads to a decreased Reynolds number, but as the dimensions of the
prototypes are larger than the Ahmed body, the diﬀerence is small. It decreases from
2.78 ·106 to 1.87 ·106, then it can be assumed that both models are still in the same ﬂow
regime.

6.6.5
Summary
In conclusion, as expected, a similar shape to an airfoil performs much better in order
to maintain the ﬂow attached on the body surface for longer. Therefore, the side view
and top view of the vehicle it should resemble to an airfoil. As mentioned in Section
4.1.4.1, a symmetric airfoil in ground proximity leads to higher pressure drag as well as
downforce, in order to counter this, the side view airfoil needs to be cambered. The top
view of the wheel fairings can also be shaped as an airfoil to minimize separation.
On the other hand, all kind of surface irregularities must be avoided wherever possible.
The surface needs to be smooth and the sharp edges curved. One of the most important
edge might be the front nose (A’ and a’), this has to be curved to increase the propensity
of laminar ﬂow in the high shear start of the boundary layer. Design smoother transitions
between the main body and the wheel fairings also helps to avoid ﬂow separation.

Race regulations
As already stated in Section 2.2, the prototype must fulﬁll all the race regulations in
order to pass the technical inspection in the competition. These rules are also used to
specify certain dimensions of the vehicle.
Track width
The track width has a big inﬂuence on the extent of the frontal area of the vehicle
and consequently to the aerodynamic drag. So, from an aerodynamic perspective, it
has to be as small as possible. On the other hand, from a safety perspective, a very
small track width could lead to a low rollover resistant vehicle. As one of the goals of
the competition rules is to maintain safety, they already specify a minimum authorized
==PAGE:96==

74
distance between wheels, which is 50 cm. This is the distance considered for the new
prototype.
Wheel base
As the vehicle has a front steering wheel system, the larger the wheel base, the larger
the frontal area must be, since the maximum steering angle of the front wheels had to be
increased. The competitions rules only specify that must be at least 100 cm, however, it
is important to take into account that even that a short wheel base will reduce the size
of the frontal wheel fairings, it also will be much more diﬃcult for the driver to control
the direction of the vehicle. On the other hand, how the longer the vehicle, the heavier
it will be.
Then a trade oﬀbetween the length of the vehicle and the maximum steering angles
needs to be found. In agreement with the other project [22] it is decided that 1400 mm
is a good value for the wheel base because it gives enough space for the driver and the
maximum steering angles are small while still allow the driver to have a good control of
the vehicle.

Weight
A maximum total weight of 28 kg for the whole vehicle (assuming a pilot’s weight of 45
kg) is imposed at the beginning of the project as an own requirement. It is estimated
that the shell needs to weigh around 12 and 15 kg to fulﬁll the requirement, therefore,
the longer the vehicle, the heavier it will be.

Like the current DTU’s urban concept, Dynamo 11.0 (Figure 7.7), the two rear-view
mirrors of the new prototype will be integrated inside the vehicle body.

Shape 2
The frontal area of Shape 2 is similar to the previous one, but the overall drag is reduced

resulting in a more rounded curvature. Since the vehicle is 3.5 m long (maximum length
allowed by the competition rules), this poor behavior when subjected to side winds also
results from the large area seen by the incoming wind which the vehicle has to overcome.
Shape 3
In this shape, the exact curvature of the NACA 4313 airfoil is used on the sideview
of the vehicle. This yield to a shorter height on the place where the head of the pilot
should be. In order to make more room there, the vehicle ground clearance is reduced.
Since the total length of the vehicle depends on the ground clearance rate h/l (Figure

fairings frontal area and therefore a smaller overall frontal area.
With all these improvements there is a signiﬁcant reduction in the wetted area of the
vehicle, and therefore a consequently reduction in the friction drag.
Finally, the top and the bottom parts are connected smoothly and following a curve, this
together with the decrease area on the side view, lead a shape with a good behaviour
with side wind.
Shape 3 is still generating downforce at 0◦yaw angle, and according to the theory
mentioned before, there is no advantage in creating lift or downforce in a FEV, therefore
next step is reduce it as much as possible.

needs to stop, the Shape 4 is the ﬁnal model that will be further optimized by means of

between the body and the fairings are shaped to avoid separation.

7.3
Adjoint method
The adjoint solver utilizes the primal ﬂow simulation data in order to compute the surface
sensitivities, as stated before in Section 4.2.6, not all the models are compatible with
the solver. The simulations setup used until now is transient state using the segregated
ﬂow solver but in order to ensure compatibility the settings must be replaced in steady
state using the coupled ﬂow solver.
To ensure that the adjoint solver converges in a robust manner, it is very important
that the primal solution is well converged. Several attempts with the new set-up are
==PAGE:109==
Chapter 7. Optimization
87
run using diﬀerent measures that STAR CCM+ recommends to enhance the coupled
ﬂow solver convergence behavior. Despite these eﬀorts, it is not possible to obtain the
needed level of convergence in the primal solution. Although for a standard ﬂow analysis
the level of convergence is enough, if the adjoint solver is run, after few iterations the
simulation stays at high residual values.
One problem that might be not allowing the required level of convergence of the sim-
ulation is the intersections between the wheels and the virtual tunnel box, there is a
very small angle between them and it seems that even with a very low scale factor and
reﬁnement boxes, the mesh quality is not good enough. In these intersections, also the
velocity of the wheels and the moving ground are faced, therefore are critic zones. As
the wheels are the only part of the vehicle that cannot be modiﬁed by the morpher, a
more simple simulation setup with only the body of the vehicle is performed. This new
simulation converged enough to start the adjoint solver. As seen on the residuals plot in

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to obtain a shell for the new fuel eﬃcient vehicle of DTU
Roadrunners team, that provides low drag but also meets the oﬃcial standards of the
competition.
The main conclusion of the present work is that at the end of that process a more eﬃcient
vehicle in comparison with the previous one is achieved. The new prototype reduces the
aerodynamic drag by 24.5% while having a 13% larger frontal area and enough room for
the frontal steering system.
The process to obtain the ﬁnal shell has been carried out in three diﬀerent phases:
validation, analysis and optimization. The speciﬁc conclusions achieved in each of these
phase are described below:
The validation phase:
1. Multiple simulations on the Ahmed body have shown that employing steady and
transient state RANS simulations in combination with the SST k −ω turbulence
model produce accurate results. The validation shows a good correlation on the
ﬂow behavior and the CD values between experimental and numerical data.
2. On the other hand, the Transient Realizable k −ϵ turbulence model yields less
accurate results.
3. DES simulation with Spalart-Allmaras model was not able to predict the behavior
of the ﬂow behind the Ahmed body. The DES model requires signiﬁcantly more
run-time and a ﬁner spatial discretization to obtain satisfactory results than the
RANS method, it was decided to discard it.
97
==PAGE:120==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
98
4. No information was found about the inlet conditions used in the experiments. In
this study it was found that four Ahmed body’s lengths showed close agreement.
5. A simulation setup able to represent the ﬂow behavior on the prototypes is achieved
using this validation.
The analysis phase:
1. The CFD analysis of the two prototypes provided by DTU Roadrunners, enabled
to obtain a better understanding of the ﬂow ﬁeld around a prototype vehicle. The
use of NACA airfoils to form the body shell, signiﬁcantly improves the aerodynamic
performance.
2. On the other hand, all kind of surface irregularities must be avoided, since friction
forces play a main role in this type of vehicles.
3. In regard to the wetted area, it is observed that it should be as low as possible,
thus the friction force increases almost proportionally to it.
4. From the results of the diﬀerent conﬁgurations for the openings of the three wheels,
it could be concluded that covering them decreases the drag whereas the downforce
increases.
The optimization phase:
Two diﬀerent methods were used in order to optimize the initial CAD-model geometry
created with the outcome of the analysis phase; the simulation-iteration and the adjoint
method.
Simulation iteration
1. A shell with low drag has been obtained through the iterative process. The drag
of the initial CAD-model was reduced by 56.3 %. However, since the vehicle has
a large number of design parameters associated with the 3D model, the iteration
or traditional optimization requires a large number of simulations and the need of
high CAD skills.
Adjoint method
==PAGE:121==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
99
2. Using the adjoint method, the cost function of reducing the drag, is successfully
achieved in the geometry decreasing the development time. Using this method
a drag reduction of 2.7% is achieved from the shape obtained in the iterative
process. However, some limitations that is necessary to take into account, have
been found using this tool. The method is sensitive to the convergence of the
primal solution, therefore, some adjustments are required during the procedure in
order to obtain the required convergence. In addition, the mesh is sensitive to the
displacement. When the displacement is small enough, the deformation process
is slow but smooth, with 17 cycles the vehicle is deformed 0.024%. On the other
hand if the displacement of the surface is too large, the mesh might contain highly
skewed cells and negative volumes, stopping in that way the optimization.
3. In short, STAR CCM+ adjoint solver in combination with the morpher can provide
valuable engineering insights decreasing the design development time. However, it
is diﬃcult to retain a valid computational mesh every cycle and obtain a smooth
surface. As in external aerodynamics, the smoothness of the surface is important,
the method will need some improvement to be completely useful.
8.1
Recommendations for further research
Although a more eﬃcient vehicle has been designed in this project reducing aerodynamic
drag and accomplishing the proposed objective, further research could be conducted in
a future in order to improve it even more and validate it in real conditions. In brief the
future studies that could be proposed are the following ones:
• The delayed detached eddy simulation (DDES) could be tested with the Ahmed
body, as this model was designed to avoid the transition from RANS to LES in
the shear layer.
• New cycles could be conducted with Adjoint method in order to reduce aerody-
namic drag even more and therefore improve the ﬁnal prototype.
• The inﬂuence of a ﬁner mesh could be tested with the Adjoint method to see if it
is possible to enhance the obtained results and obtain a more smooth shape.
• Finally, it would be wise to validate the optimized ﬁnal prototype under real
conditions in a wind tunnel.
