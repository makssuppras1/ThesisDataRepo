1. Abstract  
Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a neuropsychiatric disease characterized by obsessive repetitive 
behavior, recurrent distressing thoughts, and pathological self-doubt. Only 40-60% of people with OCD respond 
to treatment. Therefore, other treatment strategies should be investigated in order to improve the treatment of 
OCD. The cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuit has been reported to be hyperactive within people with 
OCD. Specifically, has the cortical brain regions been brought to attention: orbito-frontal cortex (OFC), medial 
prefrontal cortex (mPFC), and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC). We aimed to investigate the relation of excessive 
firing of glutamatergic pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway in relation to OCD. We inserted chemogenetics into 
neurons in the cortico-striatal pathway, projecting between ACC and dorsal striatum (DS), and observed behavior 
in an open field for 20 min in 5 continuous days. Though, other unintended cells in the striatum also were 
transfected, no OCD-like behavior was observed. Surprisingly, an increase in rearing was observed, supposedly 
showing an unexpected effect of activation of ACC. This behavior was reversed after five days without treatment. 
The increase in rearing was interpreted as increased spatial information gathering, possibly reflecting re-
evaluation of spacial information. However, the experiment is needed to be repeated where only the 
glutamatergic cortico-striatal is activated during observations, in order to confirm the origin of the rearing.

3. Background  
Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a neuropsychiatric disease characterized by obsessive repetitive 
behavior, recurrent distressing thoughts, and pathological self-doubt. OCD symptoms gradually increase over 
time and have an average age of onset at 19.5 years (Fenske and Petersen, 2015). OCD is diagnosed in 2.3% of 
the population, whereas 60-70% of these suffer from chronic OCD (Mancebo et al., 2006; Ruscio et al., 2010). 
However, many people with OCD are expected to stay undiagnosed, since mainly patients with moderate to 
severe symptoms seek help from the doctor. Untreated OCD causes significant functional impairments 
diminishing the quality of life (Jacoby et al., 2014).  
OCD is pharmacologically treated with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRI) (Fenske and Petersen, 2015). 
However, only 40-60% of people with OCD respond to the treatment with a symptom decrease of 20-40% 
(Mancebo et al., 2006). This suggests that SSRI treats one aspect of the OCD pathogenesis or merely diminish the 
symptoms of OCD. Therefore, other treatment strategies should be investigated in order to improve the 
treatment of OCD.  
3.1  Brain activity in people with OCD 
The brain activity patterns have been investigated in people with OCD using electroencephalogram (EEG), 
functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), and positron-emission tomography (PET). Dysfunctions in activity 
have been found in several studies, however, the results are ambiguous, as participants and study conditions 
varies. For example, whether the participants were treated varies between studies (table 1). Generally, an 
increased brain activity was found in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), the orbito-frontal cortex (OFC), medial 
prefrontal cortex (mPFC), thalamus, and basal ganglia (especially in caudate nucleus, a part of the striatum) 
(figure appendix 1 (A1) show an illustration of brain regions in rat and human).

Interestingly the affected brain regions are all connected within the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) 
circuit, suggesting that OCD pathophysiology originate from dysfunction within the CSTC circuit (figure 1) (Saxena 
et al., 1998). Furthermore, people with OCD, who respond to SSRI treatment, has decreased brain activity within 
the ACC (Perani et al., 1995), OFC, and caudate nucleus (Saxena et al., 1999) after treatment. Likewise, treatment 
with high frequency deep transcranial magnetic stimulation of the mPFC and ACC, significantly improved the 
YBOCS score (test used to rate severity of OCD) of treatment-resistant participants. This indicated a direct 
responsibility of the mPFC and ACC in treatment-resistant OCD (Carmi et al., 2018).  
Hyperactivity within the CSTC circuit is broadly accepted as a major part of the pathogenesis of OCD. Although a 
lot of brain regions are hyperactive within people with OCD, treatment-orientated studies indicate a causative 
role of cortical regions, specifically of the OFC, ACC, and mPFC. Further studying hyperactivity of the cortical 
regions within the CSTC circuit seems essential to understand, mimic, and treat the pathophysiology of OCD.  
3.2  CSTC circuit  
The CSTC circuit projects from cortex to striatum by glutamatergic neurons, on to thalamus mainly by GABAergic 
neurons, further projecting back to the cortex by glutamatergic neurons. The CSTC circuit either projects through 
the direct - or indirect pathway (figure 1).  
 
Figure 1. The direct- and indirect pathway of CSTC. Dorsal striatum (DS), Globus pallidus externa (GPe), Globus pallidus internal (GPi), 
Subthalamic nucleus (STN), Substantia nigra pars reticulate (SNr), Thalamus (THAL), Ventral striatum (VS). 
The direct pathway is excitatory and signals through inhibitory GABAergic medial spinal neurons (MSN, figure 
1A). GABAergic neurons from striatum project to globus pallidus internal (GPi) and substantia nigra pars 
reticulate (SNr), where it inhibits GABAergic neurons in these regions. The inhibition of GABAergic neurons in GPi 
and SNr disinhibits glutamatergic neurons in the thalamus, allowing projecting back to cortex (Grillner, 2018).  
The indirect pathway is inhibitory, and regulates additional pathways, in order to regulate the circuit (figure 1B). 
GABAergic MSN neurons within the striatum project to the globus pallidus external (GPe). The projections inhibit 
GABAergic neurons within the GPe, leading to disinhibition of glutamatergic neurons in the subthalamic nucleus 
(STN). Within the STN, glutamatergic neurons are allowed to project and stimulate cells within the GPi and SNr. 
This stimulates GABAergic neuron firing from GPi, leading to inhibition of glutamatergic neurons in the thalamus. 
This inhibit glutamatergic neurons in the thalamus, and prevent projection back to cortex (Grillner, 2018).  
Though, this circuit is commonly accepted, the striatum is heavily modulated by other regions (Hunnicutt et al., 
2016) (figure 2A), especially by the nigrostriatal dopaminergic projections.

The direct- and indirect pathways determine if a stimulus returns to cortex. These pathways are highly regulated 
by several neurons projecting to striatum (figure 2A). Glutamatergic neurons project from cortex (Hunnicutt et 
al., 2016), hippocampus (R. Yang and J. Mogenson, 1984), thalamus (Cowan and Powell, 1956), and amygdala 
(DeFrance et al., 1980) to the striatum, supposedly to activate either the direct- or indirect pathway. 
Dopaminergic neurons projects from substantia nigra (SN) and ventral tegmental area (VTA), and has been shown 
to stimulate the direct pathway and inhibit the indirect pathway (Tecuapetla et al., 2016). Serotonergic neurons 
project from raphe nuclei (RN) (Hassanzadeh, 2007), and is also expected to influence the direct- and indirect 
pathway. GABAergic neurons project from at least some cortical regions to striatum (Melzer et al., 2017), and 
could work complementary to the glutamatergic projections. The tight regulation of the direct- and indirect 
pathway makes it likely that even small changes in the firing rate of the pathways influence the resulting cognitive 
function.  
3.2.2  Glutamatergic pathways  
Glutamatergic neurons are characterized by release of glutamate into the synaptic cleft, generally leading to 
excitation of the postsynaptic cell. There are at least five different glutamatergic pathways projecting from cortex 
(figure 2B) (Bourne et al., 2012): 
- 
The cortical-brainstem pathway 
o From cortex to brainstem, specifically SN, VTA, and RN 
- 
The cortico-striatal pathway 
o From cortex to dorsal striatum (DS)  
- 
The cortico-accumbens pathway   
o From cortex to nucleus accumbens, also known as ventral striatum (VS) in rodents 
- 
The cortico-thalamic pathway  
o From cortex to thalamus  
- 
Intracortical pathway (or cortico-cortical pathway)  
o From cortex to other cortical regions

3.2.3 Pyramidal neurons  
Pyramidal neurons can be found within the cerebral cortex, hippocampus, and amygdala, and have been 
associated with advanced cognitive functions. Within cortex, pyramidal neurons are found within layer 2/3 and 
5 (figure 3A) in both humans (Spruston, 2008) and rats (Song, Moyer and Jr., 2018). Pyramidal neurons are called 
so, because of their angled cellular shape. Several types of pyramidal neurons have been identified, all with a 
general structure; many branching dendrites expanding from soma, few to some dendrites expanding from the 
axon, and several branches forming near the end of the axon. This extensive network allows pyramidal neurons 
to both receive and send input from/to several neurons along its projection area. Pyramidal neurons mainly use 
glutamate as their neurotransmitter, and therefore primarily work as excitatory neurons. However, not all 
glutamatergic neurons are pyramidal cells (Spruston, 2008).

3.2.4 Glutamatergic neuronal signalling  
Glutamate is the main excitatory neurotransmitter (figure 3B). Glutamate is a negatively charged amino acid, 
synthesized from 𝛼-ketoglutarate when receiving an amino group. Vesicular glutamate transporter (VGLUT) 
packs glutamate into vesicles, ready for release. Three subtypes of VGLUT have been identified (VGLUT1-3). 
VGLUT1 is mainly expressed in glutamatergic neurons within the cerebral - and cerebellar cortex, hippocampus, 
and thalamus, and VGLUT2 within subcortical glutamatergic neurons projecting from thalamus to the spinal cord 
(Herzog et al., 2006). VGLUT3 has also been observed within in the striatum, hippocampus, and RH, in other 
types of neurons, such as GABAergic neurons (Herzog et al., 2004). 
Once an action potential reach the cell terminals, glutamate is released into the synaptic clefts, where it interacts 
with mGluR (metabotropic glutamate receptors), AMPA (𝛼-amono-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolepropainoic 
acid), and NMDA (N-methyl-D-Apartic acid) receptors. Excitatory amino acid transporters (EAATs) actively 
eradicate glutamate from the synapse (Fairman et al., 1995) and transfer glutamate into adjacent glial cells 
(Schousboe, Svenneby and Hertz, 1977). After entering the glial cells, glutamine synthase converts glutamate 
==PAGE:9==
Page 6 of 41 
 
into glutamine by adding another amino group (Norenberg and Martinez-Hernandez, 1979; Yu, Schousboec and 
Hertz, 1982). Glutamine transporters (GLNT) transport glutamine out of the glial cells and back into glutamatergic 
neurons by electrophysiological forces. In the neurons, glutaminase reconverts glutamine into glutamate, which 
is packed into vesicles, ready to be released again (Mitchell and Silver, 2000).  
3.3  Animal models for OCD  
Within the last two centuries, several animal models resembling different aspects of the OCD pathogenesis have 
been established. The models range over several types, where both genetic-, lesion-, and pharmacological 
models have shown to resemble a part of the OCD pathogenesis (Alonso et al., 2015).  
In 1998, Szechtman, Sulis, and Eliman proposed continuous treatment with the D2/D3 dopamine agonist, 
quinpirole (QNP), as an animal model for compulsive checking (a phenome observed in some people with OCD). 
As OCD is diagnosed by using different rating instruments relaying on self-reporting and introspection (e.g. 
YBOCS), they decided to focus on one OCD symptom: compulsive checking. They injected 0.5 mg/kg QNP twice 
a week for five weeks and analysed the rats in an open-field setting with four open glass cubes fixed at different 
locations. They found a significant return pattern within two of the cubes. They proposed this to be a model for 
at least one type of OCD (referred to as OCD checkers), but not for other types such as OCD washers (Szechtman, 
Sulis and Eilam, 1998).  
The cortico-striatal pathway project from the cortex to the striatum and is a pathway within the CSTC circuit. At 
least 15 subcortical regions have been shown to project through the cortico-striatal pathway to either the DS or 
VS in mice, including ACC, mPFC, and OFC (Hunnicutt et al., 2016). In 2013, Ahmari et al. modelled OCD in mice. 
They induced hyperactivity in the glutamatergic cortico-striatal pathway originating in OFC using optogenetic. 
They repeatedly stimulated the pathway for five minutes per day in six days. This hyperactivity increased 
grooming after just 2 days of stimulation, and specifically increased grooming 1 hour post stimulation between 
day 1 and day 6. Interestingly, two weeks post-stimulation, the stimulated mice still showed increased grooming 
time compared to the control group, suggesting continuous hyperactivity within the OFC leads to persistent OCD-
related behavior within mice (Ahmari et al., 2013).   
OCD pathogenesis seems to be tightly linked to dysfunction within CSTC circuit. Cortical brain regions seem to 
be important both when modelling and treating OCD pathogenesis. Animal models have been successful in 
mimicking some aspects of the pathogenesis, leaving other aspects of the disease un-modelled. In this study, I 
aim to induce OCD-like pathogenesis in rats by increasing glutamatergic neurons activation of the cortico-striatal 
pathway originating in ACC, mPFC, and/or OFC.  
4. Aim 
This project aim to investigate the relation of excessive firing of glutamatergic cortico-striatal pathway in relation 
to OCD. The cortical brain regions, ACC, OFC, and mPFC, have been associated with OCD. Activation of the 
glutamatergic cortico-striatal pathway projecting from any of these regions could potentially to lead to OCD-
related behavior. I aim to transfect the pyramidal neurons projecting within the cortico-striatal pathway from 
one of these cortical regions in vivo, and explore continuous activation of the pathways effect on OCD-related 
behavior.

4.1 Hypothesis   
Viral gene vectors are used for transfection of neurons in vivo. However, several viral vectors are available, and 
transfect different cells with various efficiency, spread, and specificity. Further, pathway limited transfection 
requires additional approaches, complicating the transfection.  
Hypothesis 1: Specific transfection of the pyramidal neurons projecting within the cortico-striatal 
pathway is possible. 
Increased grooming in animals is supposedly associated with obsessive behavior corresponding to cleanliness 
and lack of control of urges/feelings observed in OCD patients.  
Hypothesis 2: Stimulation of the pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway leads to increased grooming in rats. 
OCD and similar disorders are also characterized by safety concern and self-doubt. Decreased rearing has been 
associated with anxiety in rodents (Lever, Burton and O’Keefe, 2006).  
Hypothesis 3: Stimulation of the pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway leads to decreased rearing in rats 
Further, increased border time would be expected as a sign of insecurity and malaise.   
Hypothesis 4: Stimulation of the pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway leads to increased border time in rats. 
If activity in the cortico-striatal pathway leads to activation of the same neurons as QNP treatment, similar 
behavior pattern is expected.  
Hypothesis 5: Stimulation of the pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway leads to excessive and repetitive 
checking of selected items. 
5. Method theory  
To investigate the cortico-striatal pathway, I wanted to stimulate axon firing within glutamatergic pyramidal 
neurons. A novel way to selectively target specific part of neuronal circuits is chemogenetic and optogenetic 
techniques. In the following sections, the theory behind chemogenetics and viral transfection are explained. 
5.1  Chemogenetics  
Chemogenetics is the use of bioengineered receptors, which are activated by otherwise inert ligands (also 
referred to as designer drugs). Several receptors have been engineered to interact with designer drugs (e.g. 
kinases, ion channels, and G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)) in order to establish biological responses. The 
most used bioengineered receptor is Designer Receptors Exclusively Activated by Designer Drugs (DREADDs) 
(Roth, 2016). Within neuroscience, DREADDs are used to either stimulate or inhibit action potentials of the 
neurons they are expressed in. 
In 2007, Armbruster et al. designed modified human muscarinic receptor (hMR) (a type of acetylcholine 
receptors), in order to prevent interaction with acetylcholine and instead promote activation upon interacting 
with the designer drug, Clozapine-N-oxide (CNO) (Armbruster et al., 2007). The primary activation by designer 
drugs, classify modified hMR as DREADDs.  
==PAGE:11==
Page 8 of 41 
 
Designer drugs are small molecules with the ability to enter the site of interest, but with minimized or no 
biological activity. Interaction between the designer drug (CNO) and the receptor (DREADD) activates the 
receptors, inducing a cellular response within the cell. This method provides a simple tool to influence neuron 
firing of certain neurons in vivo. However, care must be taken to optimize the dose of designer drug to avoid 
unwanted side effect as several studies have found that when a high dose of CNO  it leads to behavioral 
(MacLaren et al., 2016) and neurochemical effects (Bærentzen et al., 2019). Other and more selective drugs are 
currently being produced (Bonaventura et al., 2018).  
Different subtypes of hMR binds different G proteins driving either a stimulating or inhibiting response within 
the neuron. hMR has been found in five variants, which all have been modified into DREADDs; hM1D (hMR 
serotype 1 DREADD) , hM2D, hM3D, hM4D, and hM5D (Huang and Thathiah, 2015; Roth, 2016). 
Upon stimulation, DREADDs activates G-proteins in a similar way to other GPCRs, initiating an intracellular 
cascade of reactions leading to a cellular response. Different types of DREADDs are coupled to different 
intracellular G proteins; Gq, Gs, or Gi leading to either excitation (Gq and Gs) or inhibition (Gi) of neuronal action 
potentials (figure 3) (Roth, 2016).

Gq protein activation leads to phosphorylation of PLC by cleaving GTP (figure 4A) (Takano et al., 1996). PLC 
phosphorylates inositol diphosphate (IP2) making inositol triphosphate (IP3), which binds and activates IP3 
receptors. Active IP3 receptors release calcium from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and the extracellular space 
into the cytosol. The increase of calcium within the cell depolarize the neuron, increasing the likelihood of an 
action potential. 
Gs protein activation leads to activation of the Gs signalling pathway (figure 4B). Active Gs proteins stimulate and 
activate adenyl cyclase (AC), which convert ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP) (Jaggupilli et al., 2018). cAMP is a co-factor 
for protein kinase A (PKA), and the cAMP:PKA complex phosphorylates DARPP-32 (dopamine and cAMP-
regulated phosphoprotein, 32 kDa). Phospho-DARPP-32 inhibit protein phosphatase 1 (PP-1), preventing 
dephosphorization and thereby exchange of sodium (Na+) and potassium (K+) trough Na+/K+ ATPase. As PKA/C 
phosphorylates and thereby inactivates Na+/K+ ATPase, a general decrease in Na+/K+ ATPase activity arises. This 
keeps potassium inside the cell and slowly depolarize the cell as more potassium diffuse inside the cell toward 
the negative charged chlorine (Cl-). When the membrane potential has decreased enough to reach the threshold, 
the neuron starts firing.  
Gi-protein influence the same signalling pathway as Gs protein (figure 4B), though Gi protein inhibit AC activity, 
decreasing activation based on Gs protein stimulation (Wong et al., 1991). Gi proteins stimulate flux of potassium 
(K+) out of the cell, leading the membrane potential toward hyperpolarization (Riven, Iwanir and Reuveny, 2006). 
Further, Gi proteins increase calcium influx, leading to activation of calcineurin (Moises, Rusin and Macdonald, 
1994). Calcineurin works opposite the cAMP:PKA complex, and dephosphorylates phosphor-DARPP-32. This 
leaves PP-1 active, resulting in active Na+/K+ ATPase. This leads to a steady membrane potential.  
GPCR is assumable expressed within all neurons as an important part of the depolarizing/polarizing mechanisms 
involved within neuronal signalling. Therefore, DREADDs should be able to function within any neuron, as they 
already express the downstream proteins needed within the intracellular mechanisms.  
5.2  Transfection  
A good suggestion to a perfect virus vector, would be non-toxic, non-immunogenic, and non-pathological while 
showing high transfection rate, high titter, high target specificity, high gene expression, and strictly transfect 
retro- or anterograde (figure 5). Several viruses have been modified into vectors because of superior 
characteristics within one of these points. For instance AAV appears to be safe and non-toxic (Hermonat and 
Muzyczka, 1984), lentivirus transfect neurons effectively and stable (Naldini et al., 1996), and adenovirus vectors 
can be made highly concentrated (Morsy et al., 1998). Transfection with viral vectors is an important tool for 
gene modulation in vivo and has a great potential within human gene therapy (figure 5).

5.2.1 Adenovirus  
Adenovirus is a common virus in mammals causing respiratory tract disease (Cornwell et al., 1982). Adenovirus 
transfect bi-directionally (Salinas et al., 2009) all CAR (coxsackievirus and adenovirus receptor) expressing cells, 
though prefer neuron transfection. This makes it good for cerebral transfection, as neither glial or 
oligodendrocytes express CAR (Soudais et al., 2001). Traditionally, adenovirus serotype 2 and adenovirus 
serotype 5 have been studied for transfection in humans. In 1997, Klonjkowski et al. made an E1-deleted canine 
adenovirus serotype 2 (CAV-2) which was able to infect human cells, but not spread once in a cell (Klonjkowski 
et al., 1997). This made CAV-2 gene therapy safer and more precise, making it preferable to human adenovirus 
2. E1-deletion has later been used in other viruses too.  
Life cycle – CAV2 
There are several serotypes of adenovirus, though many of their life cycles remain mostly unknown. However, 
the CAV2 life cycle is fairly understood (figure 5A). CAV2 transfect neurons retrogradely (enter near nerve 
terminals and travel to soma). When CAV2 is in contact with mammalian cells, it binds to CAR with high affinity 
(Bergelson et al., 1997; Soudais et al., 2001) and triggers encapsulation into the cell. CAV2 is transported within 
the endosome by microtubule motors to the nucleus (Salinas et al., 2014). How CAV2 escapes the encapsulation 
is uncertain, but it is expected to bind nucleus pores and inject its viral DNA into the nucleus, like other 
adenoviruses (Kremer and Nemerow, 2015). This process is very efficient, and successful transfection can be 
observed within days.  
==PAGE:14==
Page 11 of 41 
 
5.2.2 Adeno-associated virus 
On its own, adeno-associated virus (AAV) only enter the lysogenic stage of infection (insertion of viral genome 
into the host genome), and therefore is considered non-pathogenic. Several AAV serotypes have been used as 
vectors, as they both vary in their retro-/anterograde properties and spread (Aschauer, Kreuz and Rumpel, 2013).  
Life cycle – AAV2 
Axonal transport of AAV is serotype-dependent. AAV2 has shown to transfect anterogradely (transfection near 
soma) (Ciesielska et al., 2011) and AAV6 to be retrograde transported (from nerve terminals to soma) (San 
Sebastian et al., 2013). AAV serotypes use different glycan receptors and co-receptors to enter cells. The life 
cycle of most of the AAV serotypes remain unknown. However, the AAV2 serotype life cycle is well described 
(figure 5B). 
AAV2 attach to the cell by interaction with glycan receptors (Summerford and Samulski, 1998) and is internalized 
by endocytosis after interaction with one or more co-receptors. Capsid proteins lead AAV2 towards the nucleus 
and transport it into the nucleus (Johnson, 2010; Satkunanathan, Thorpe and Zhao, 2017). Within the nucleus, 
the AVV2 capsid is expected, at least partially, to degrade, thereby releasing viral single stranded DNA (ssDNA) 
and proteins (Ros et al., 2006). Inverted terminal repeats bind in a hairpin structure, and allow DNA polymerase 
to bind and make double stranded DNA (Ward, 1994). In rodents, AAV DNA is expected to be stochastic inserted 
into the genome from where it is expressed (Rizzuto et al., 1999).  
5.2.3 Viral expression 
When viral genomes are inserted into the chromosomes of mammals, their expression is regulated in the same 
way as all the other host genes. This mean that the inserted genes need to be proceeded by a promoter active 
in that specific cell, in order to get transcribed. However, this also provides a tool for specification of the cells 
within the inserted genes can be expressed. For instance, the promotor for synapsin (a vesicle bound protein) 
can be used to limit gene expression within neurons, as only neurons express synapsin. Further, gene expression 
can be increased by following the genes with sequences for enhanced posttranscriptional expression such as 
WPRE (Woodchuck hepatitis virus posttranscriptional regulatory element) (Lee et al., 2005). 
The viral DNA content is limited, making multiple gene transfections more complicated. When expressing two or 
more genes, it is possible to either fuse the genes, thereby binding the proteins together, or separating the genes 
by a sequence which ensure expression of both proteins though, separates the proteins. Different sequences can 
be used for this. In this report, the following two types are used: 2A sequence and Kozak sequence.  
The 2A sequence is a self-cleaving sequence, which has been suggested to alter the ribosomal function to cleave 
the peptide sequence during translation. This leaves the upstream gene with a tail of 5 amino acids, and the 
downstream with one amino acid (proline) attached to the C-terminus (Donnelly et al., 2001). The Kozak 
sequence is a short sequence which facilitate the binding of ribosomes to the mRNA (Kozak, 1986). Opposite 2A, 
it does not add any amino acids to any of the translated proteins, leaving the proteins unchanged.  
5.2.4 Cre Recombinase 
Cre recombinase, often shortened cre, is a protein that recognize and bind two DNA recognition sites, loxP (locus 
of crossing over on bacteriophage P1). LoxP is 34 bp long and has a core sequence of 8 bp centered in the middle 
of the region, which determines the direction of the region. The region is surrounded by two 13 bp long inverted 
palindromic repeats which cre recombinase binds. Dependent on the orientation of the loxP sites, cre 
==PAGE:15==
Page 12 of 41 
 
recombinase does different things. Within neuroscience, cre recombinase is often used to invert gene sequences 
of non-coding DNA to coding DNA, as a way to limit and specify gene expression even further. Gene inversion 
occur when cre interact with two loxP sites in opposite directions (Van Duyne, 2015). Within neuroscience, cre 
recombinase can be used to target specific neurons projecting between two different brain regions.  
6. Method 
6.1  Animals  
Wild type Long-Evans female rats were purchased from Janvier, weighing between 160 and 200g. The rats were 
housed as pairs together in a cage, within a climate-controlled facility with lighting schedule on from 6 am to 6 
pm. Both food and water were available ad libitum. Animals were acclimatized at least five days in the facilities 
before participating in procedures. All procedures were in accordance with FELASA guidelines and approved by 
Danish Animal Experiments Inspectorate (2016-15-0201-01031).  
6.2  Surgery  
Surgery was performed on rats weighing between 170-250g. Pain was relieved by subcutaneous (S.C.) injection 
of 5mg carprofen/kg 30 minutes prior to the procedure. Animals were kept sedated with continuous isoflurane 
on a heating pad during the procedure, while reflexes, body temperature, and animal breath continuously was 
checked.  
The surgery area was shaved and the area cleaned with iodine and 70% ethanol. Local analgesia was applied on 
the skin (~4 mg lidocaine) and the incision was made. The position of bregma (an anatomical point on the scull 
used as reference) was identified in three orientations: anterior toward posterior (AP), medial toward lateral 
(ML), and dorsal toward ventral (DV), and used to determine the brain localization. Holes were drilled in the skull 
relative to bregma and a nanofil needle slowly lowered into the brain. 150 nl/min was injected. To prevent re-
absorption into the needle due to surface tension, the needle remained within the brain for 7 minutes after each 
injection. The skin was sutured and the rats monitored for 2 days in a recovery cage. After surgery, animals were 
kept in an unshared cage. 5mg carprofen/kg was administered the two following days post-surgery. Behavioral 
studies start after at least 2 weeks to ensure recovery and viral expression. The used viruses and the injection 
sites relative to bregma are laid out in table A1. 
6.3  Behavioral analysis  
Behavioral behavioral studies were performed on rats weighting between 200-250g, all in unshared cages.

6.4  Perfusion-fixation  
Rats were injected intraperitoneally (I.P) with 100mg pentobarbital/kg BW. While the heart was still beating, the 
heart was exposed and a blunt perfusion needle placed in the left lower ventricle. A minor cut was made in the 
upper right ventricle right before the blood system was washed with 150 ml phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) 
followed by fixation with 100ml of 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA). The brain was collected and stored in 4% PFA in 
24h at 4°C and then in 30% sucrose.  
6.5  In vitro experiments  
The perfusion-fixated brains were cut coronal on a microtome and stained Cre (abcam # ab190177) with ABC-P 
(avidin-biotin-complex peroxidase) immunostaining. This was executed as previously described1. For IHC 
targeting cre recombinase an antigen retrieval step was applied. This was done prior to the remaining protocol, 
and includes washing and incubation in initially boiling Tris/EDTA pH 9 for 40 min. The cre staining was 
unsuccessful.  
6.6  Microscopy  
Fluorescent images were captured on a LSM 700 confocal microscope from Zeiss. Fluorescent images of the cell 
bodies were acquired either singular or sequentially with 1-3 tracks for 1-3 colours with a 20x objective. Likewise, 
axons were captured with 3 tracks for 3 colours with a 60x objective. A laser with wavelength of 555nm was used 
to detect mCherry and mRuby, and a laser with wavelength 405nm used for enhanced blue fluorescent protein 
(EBFP).  
Whole-brain images were captured with an Axio-observer 7 with stage with a 20x objective. HXP 120V was used 
as light source, in combination with filters allowing passing of wavelengths between 359 – 371 EBFP and 533 – 
558 for mCherry. 
6.7  Statistics  
All statistical analysis were performed in GraphPad Prism 8.1.2. A matched one-way multiple comparisons 
ANOVA was used to evaluate difference between groups on day 3 (baseline), and a matched two-way multiple 
comparisons ANOVA was used to evaluate differences between baseline day 3 and the remaining 6 days. P values 
< 0.05 was considered significant. All P values from both one-way and two-way multiple comparisons are 
displayed in table A2-5.

7. Results and discussion  
Based on human studies, cortico-striatal pathways projecting from OFC, mPFC, and ACC are very likely to be 
involved in OCD pathogenesis. In the following sections, the cortico-striatal pathway projecting from mPFC and 
ACC were visualized in rats by viral transfection. 
7.1  Mapping the cortico-striatal pathway 
Initially, successful transfection of a pathway needs to be mapped, in order to determine the precise area of the 
two regions which the pathway connects. Previously, glutamatergic neurons from all cortical regions has been 
shown to project to the striatum (Hunnicutt et al., 2016).  
7.1.1 Retrograde transfection of DS inputs 
Injection of a retrograde virus (virus entering through axon terminals) into the striatum should transfect neurons 
projecting to the striatum. The dorsal striatum was injected with a wide spreading, non-specific, retrograde CAV2 
in order to transfect the pathways projecting from cortex to striatum (figure A2). We expected CAV2 to spread 
throughout the DS and transfect glutamatergic neurons projecting from cortex. Further, other pathways such as 
the dopaminergic neurons originating in SN was also expected to be transfected. We used CAV2-CMV-Cre (figure 
A2A) which encoded cre recombinase under a CMV promotor (cytomegalovirus promotor, unspecific). 
Unfortunately, the immunohistochemistry with anti-cre antibody was problematic and we were unable to 
visualize the transfection within the available time.  
Cre recombinase is a nuclear protein, and extra extraction steps is often needed in order to detect nuclear 
proteins. This has previously been done successfully within neurons expressing cre recombinase with DAB IHC 
(Lemberger et al., 2007), though we were unable to repeat this.  
Injections of CAV2-RSV-GFP (Rous sarcoma virus promotor, unspecific) into the striatum has previously been 
observed after 2 weeks, and shown to provide a long and stable retrograde transfection of cells in striatum and 
SN (Soudais, Skander and Kremer, 2004). Therefore, it was likely that the CAV2-CMV-Cre virus used here would 
also have transfected at least these regions. The transfection with CAV2-CMV-Cre virus could have been 
visualized in vivo by injecting another retrograde virus with a cre-dependent fluorescent marker into the 
striatum. This would have allowed detection of cells which expressed both the CAV2-CMV-Cre and the second 
virus, visulizing the brain regions projecting to striatum, thereby providing the same information as would have 
been obtained with cre recombinase staining.  
As no cortico-striatal pathway was detected when transfecting striatum with retrograde CAV2, we tried another 
approach. Instead of mapping all cortico-striatal pathways by injecting in striatum, we chose to map one of the 
three cortical regions projecting to the striatum (figure 7).

7.1.3 Transport of fluorescent marker to terminals by synaptophysin fusion. 
After confirming transfection with the AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre virus, we wanted to enhance the visualization of the 
pathway connecting the cortical regions and striatum. To do that, we used an anterograde AAV8 coding for EGFP, 
synaptophysin, and mRuby (a fluorescent protein) (figure 8A-B). 2A separates EGFP and synaptophysin, but 
synaptophysin and mRuby would be fused together. Synaptophysin is a vesicular protein, and was expected to 
transport mRuby to the axon terminals. The available virus was cre-dependent (LoxP sites surrounds the inverted 
gene sequences for EGFP-2A-synaptophysin-mRuby). Therefore, cre recombinase was needed within cells in 
order to express genes encoded by AAV8-EGFP-2A-synaptophysin-mRuby. AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre was used to 
provide the cells with cre recombinase. It was expected that all cells transfected by both AAV2 and AAV8 would 
express synaptophysin-mRuby and EGFP. Previously, synaptophysin-GFP conjugation has been shown to clearly 
mark soma and detect axonal projections in the striatum (Broussard et al., 2016).  
We chose to mix AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre and AAV8-EGFP-2A-synaptophysin-mRuby (1 part AAV2, 1 part AAV8), and 
inject them together into the mPFC of the left hemisphere and into the ACC of the right hemisphere (figure 8C). 
This was done to increase the information obtained while limiting the animal resources. Though, this saves 
animal lives, projections cannot be expected to be strictly ipsilateral (projections within one hemisphere of the 
brain), making an uncertainty of the origin of observed axon terminals.

7.2 DREADD insertion with AAV2 and AAV6 
In order to be able to activate the transfected neurons, we used a retrograde cre-dependent AAV6 carrying 
inverted gene sequence for mCherry-hM3Dq in the striatum. hM3Dq bind intracellular Gq proteins, and should 
be able to activate them when stimulated. This was expected to lead to increased neuron activity within cells 
expressing mCherry and hM3Dq (Gq). Retrograde cre-dependent AAV6 coding for mCherry was used as negative 
control (Veh) to avoid observing behavioral changes to be caused by the operational procedure, viral 
transfection, mCherry expression, or unspecific binding of CNO. 
To limit transfection to the cortico-striatal pathway, we injected anterograde AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre in the ACC, and 
AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq into the striatum (figure 9A-C). Again, only in cells where both AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre and 
AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq were expressed would the gene sequence be reverted, allowing expression of mCherry/ 
and hM3Dq. The mCherry and hM3Dq genes were connected by the Kozak sequence, which separates the genes, 
though, allow expression of hM3Dq. We expected that cells connecting the ACC and DS would be transfected 
with both AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre and AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq.

The double transfection within the striatum was unexpected. AVV2-EBFP-2A-Cre seemed to spread from cortex 
into the ventricle and corpus callosum. From here it seemed to travel to ventral layers of the cortex, dorsal 
septum, and striatum. The spread of the AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre was similar to the spread of AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq 
in the striatum. However, the diffusion into the striatum would be expected to be gradual as observed within 
the septum and ventral cortex. This could suggest that AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre diffuse through the hole made by the 
injections into striatum, and transfects the striatum from the same areas as AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq.  
AAV2 has been reported to recover from the injection site in cultured cells after 72h, though not after 7 days 
(Reuter et al., 2012). Though, the virus survival near the injection site and within the brain, is unlikely to be the 
same, this suggest AAV2 could be present for at least 72h after injection. Therefore, to avoid this problem, we 
would suggest to inject AAV2-EBFP-2A-Cre 7 days before injecting AAV6-mCherry/-hM3Dq into striatum, to avoid 
translocation of AAV2 into the striatum. However, we have been unable to find others who have used this 
approach.  
In the previous experiments, AVV2-EBFP-2A-Cre did not spread into the ventricles and transfect the ventral 
cortex, septum or striatum (figure 7-8, data not shown). As both of these injections were performed with at least 
50% lover virus concentration (table A1), this suggested a lower virus concentration would prevent some of the 
spreading.  
These studies should be repeated with these changes in order to confirm which or if any of the observed behavior 
were connected to the cortico-striatal pathway. Further improvements could be to use a promotor specific for 
glutamatergic or pyramidal neurons. Further staining of the transfected cells would be necessary to confirm 
transfection within the glutamatergic pyramidal neurons. Several proteins have been proposed as glutamatergic 
markers, and could be used both as promotors and for staining. For example, VGLUT1/2 together has been 
reported to represent all glutamatergic neurons within the brain. Their restricted, but widespread expressions, 
make them good markers of glutamatergic neurons (Herzog et al., 2006). Another example is Emx1 which has 
been proposed as a pyramidal neuron marker within cortex (Chan et al., 2001).  
The transfection within striatum makes it uncertain whether the behavioral changes was a result from activation 
of the cortico-striatal pathway, as planned, or from interneurons or MSN neurons. However, some neurons in 
the ACC was double transfected, suggesting transfection of neurons projecting from the ACC to striatum. We 
therefore accept hypothesis 1.

7.3  Behavior 
The behavioral analysis was inspired by the open-field setting used by Szechtman, Sulis, and Eliman, who 
observed that QNP administration altered rat’s behavior regarding fixed items (Szechtman, Sulis and Eilam, 
1998). Our open-field setup differs from theirs, as the arena and items are smaller and the arena has non-
transparent walls. We used QNP treated rats as a positive control group (QNP), to be able to compare behavioral 
changes in that setup to an established OCD model (figure 10+A5). All P-values are outlined in appendix 11.1 
Two-way and 11.2 One-way ANOVA (table A2-A5).

7.3.2 Increase of locomotion in Gq group  
A significant increase in locomotion between day 3 and 4-8 on at least 50% ±11% of the Gq group was observed 
(highest Pday7 = 0.005, figure 10A). Locomotion was restored on day 13, suggesting the observed behavior was 
treatment-dependent. Opposite the Gq group, a decreasing locomotion tendency within the QNP group was 
observed (lowest Pday7 = 0.0988), which previous studies also have reported (Horvitz, Williams and Joy, 2001). 
This suggest different, possibly opposite influences of the pathways involved within the Gq and QNP group. 
Generally, a significant difference between groups was observed (F(2,5) = 14.01, P = 0.0089), though no 
difference was observed on day 3 between groups or within the Veh group.  
Increased locomotion has not been associated with OCD-related behavior, but was important to recognize, as 
this could influence other measures. Further, the increase of locomotion suggests functional expression of the 
hM3Dq gene. Further, hM3Dq expressing cells could possibly influence on the direct or indirect pathway, as 
activation of these has been associated with movement (Tecuapetla et al., 2016). 
7.3.3 Grooming might be unaffected  
We hypothesized grooming to be increased within the Gq group (hypothesis 2, figure 10B + A5A). However, no 
increase in grooming was observed within the Gq group. A decrease in grooming time could be argued, though, 
it does not have any statistical validation.  
An initial significant decrease of 85% ± 14% of grooming time (highest Pday5 = 0.049, table A4) was observed on 
day 4-5 in the QNP group, but not the following 3 days during treatment. The same pattern was seen for 
grooming frequency of the QNP group (highest Pday5 = 0.0253, table A4, figur 10B + A5A). Decreased grooming 
has previously been observed within QNP treated animals (Horvitz, Williams and Joy, 2001). The tendencies of 
grooming pattern could be alike between the Gq and QNP group, though more animals was needed in order to 
confirm this.  
No difference in grooming between groups was observed (F(2,5) = 1.566, P = 0.2964), and no difference on day 
3 was observed between groups. The available data reject hypothesis 2, suggesting the transfected cells does 
not increase grooming.  
7.3.4 Cell activation lead to increased rearing  
We hypothesized rats within the Gq group would have decreased rearing. However, a significant increase of 
rearing time compared to baseline (day 3) on 44% ± 45% was observed within the Gq group of day 4 - 7 (highest 
Pday7 = 0.0495, figure 10C). Further, a significant increase on 33% ± 13% of rearing frequency within the Gq group 
was significantly increased on day 4 and 5 (figure 10D, Pday4 = 0.0381, Pday5 = 0.0303). This suggests the increased 
rearing time was not only a result of increased rearing frequency, but also that the average rearing period was 
extended.  
Opposite the Gq group, the QNP group significantly decreased rearing time with at least 57% ± 13% on day 4 – 
8 (highest Pday5 = 0.0017, figure 10C) and decreased rearing frequency (highest Pday5 = 0.0051, figure 10D). This 
behavior was in accordance with reports from other studies (Horvitz, Williams and Joy, 2001). Again, an opposite 
behavior between the Gq and QNP group were observed, suggesting the treatments influence cells resulting in 
==PAGE:27==
Page 24 of 41 
 
opposite behavior. No difference was observed within the Veh group. There was a significant difference between 
the three groups (F(2,5) = 10.18 P = 0.0179), though, no difference between groups on day 3.  
Rearing is a natural occurring behavior, where four legged animals stand on their hind legs. Increase in rearing 
has been associated with environmental novelty and spatial information gathering, and decrease in rearing with 
anxiety (Lever, Burton and O’Keefe, 2006). As these animals had been exposed to the arena and items several 
times, this was interpreted as increased spatial information gathering. This observation rejects hypothesis 3, and 
suggest no relation between the activated cells and OCD pathogenesis.  
7.3.5 No difference in border time 
We hypothesized border time to be increased within the Gq group (hypothesis 4). No difference was observed 
within the time spend within the border (lowest Pday7 = 0.3355, figure 10E-F), indicating no preference for the 
border or center. This suggests that the rats in the Gq group were neither more nor less anxious than the Veh 
group.  
Within the QNP group a significant decrease in border time was observed on day 4-6 (highest significant P = 
0.0362, figure 10F). The behavior was gradually restored and appeared similar to baseline on day 7. This suggests 
decreased anxiety within the animals of the QNP group.  
No difference was observed between baseline measurements on day 3 between groups, and generally between 
groups (F(2,5) = 3.146, P = 0.1305). This observation rejects hypothesis 4, and suggest no influence of the 
activated cells on anxiety. Generally, within the basal behavior an increase of both locomotion and rearing were 
observed within the Gq group, suggesting an activation and effect of transfected cells on behavior.

The movement around the center item appear to differ between the Gq and Veh groups (figure 11B+C). The Veh 
group increase movement around the center item on day 8 and 13 (figure 11B), while the Gq group was observed 
to move on top of the center item on from day 4 (figure 11C, manual observation from recordings). This could

7.4.2 Increasing tendency in time spend at the black item 
A significant difference of time spend near the black item on day 5 was observed within the Gq group (Pday5 = 
0.0334, figure 11H), though, no clear trend was observed. At least 10 animals in each group would be necessary 
in order to determine a difference within the least significant day (Pday7 = 0.1867). ANOVA analysis showed no 
difference between the groups (F(2,5) = 0.7126 P = 0.5342), and no difference was observed between day 3 of 
the groups. No difference was observed within the Veh or QNP group.  
No difference in time spend near the corner item or number of visits was observed within the Gq group (figure 
A6C-D). This indicates the slight increase in black item time was not caused by an increased preference for 
corners. However, it could be preferred over the corner item, as it was closer to the center item and had different 
properties (color and placement). Again, this does not indicate any compulsive behavior toward the black item. 
Therefore, we reject hypothesis 3, as nothing indicates compulsive checking occurred within the Gq group.  
7.4.3 QNP model 
The QNP group was observed to spend increased amount of time within the wire item zone (figure A6A, highest 
Pday5 = 0.0049), though, no difference was observed in wire item frequency (figure A6B). This was not in 
accordance with previous observations, where returning time (here measured as returning frequency) was 
decreased, indicating more returns to the item (Szechtman, Sulis and Eilam, 1998). Further, no difference was 
observed within time spend within the corner item (figure A6C-D), which also previously has been observed. 
However, these observations were done after several weeks of QNP treatment, suggesting time could be 
essential to this behavior. The QNP group had increased interest for the wire item as previously observed, 
though, obsessive-checking was not observed within the 5-days-time-frame. The QNP group behaved partially in 
accordance with previous observations, indicating some comparability between this open field setup and other 
studies.  
7.4.4 No lasting effect of 5 days treatment 
None of the increased or decreased behavior in the Gq or QNP group was preserved for day 13 as previously 
observed during cortico-striatal pathway stimulation (Ahmari et al., 2013) and QNP treatment (Szechtman, Sulis 
and Eilam, 1998). This indicates five days of treatment was insufficient to induce a long-term change of behavior. 
However, persistent grooming had been observed by Ahmari et al. two weeks after stimulation, indicating the 
activated cells within the Gq group was not associated with persistent behavior.  
7.4.5 Experimental setup  
Ahmari and her colleagues found increased behavior before and after 1 hour after stimulating the glutamatergic 
neurons projecting from OFC to VS with optogenetics (Ahmari et al., 2013). They stimulated for five minutes, and 
==PAGE:30==
Page 27 of 41 
 
found a significant difference before stimulation and 1 hour after. However, measuring after cell activation with 
CNO is not as simple. After injection of CNO, CNO is metabolized into the active compound clozapine, which has 
been observed to cross the blood-brain barrier and specifically bind hMD receptors after 40 minutes (Gomez et 
al., 2017). Clozapine has been detected in the blood up to 6 hours after I.P. injection (MacLaren et al., 2016), 
suggesting a continous long-term activation of the hMD receptors. Therefore, measurements of the effect after 
stimulation, is not as simple. The impact of time of measurement was unclear, but was expected to lead to a long 
term, rather than a short-term, activation of the transfected cells.   
7.5  Transfected regions and behavior  
Both increased rearing time and frequency was observed within the Gq group, presumable resembling increased 
special information gathering. In the following section, we assume the ACC was responsible for this behavior.  
The ACC has previously been associated with cognitive behavior, including error processing (Amiez, Joseph and 
Procyk, 2005) and attentional control (Newman, Creer and McGaughy, 2015). As the ACC was reported to be 
activated as a response to error, it is possible continuous activation of ACC lead to re-evaluation of previous 
gathered information’s, reflected in this study by increased rearing. Though, we have been unable to find other 
studies which look at behavior after activating the ACC. However, this is pure speculation, but could be tested by 
measuring the accuracy and response time during a task with congruent and incongruent trials. If increased 
rearing is an expression of increased error processing, we would expect a higher response time and increased 
accuracy over time.  
8. Conclusion  
Brain regions in the CSTC circuit have been reported to be hyperactive within people with OCD. Especially, 
including three cortical regions; OFC, PFC, and ACC. We used chemogenetics to activate the pathway projecting 
within the glutamatergic pyramidal cortico-striatal pathway, in order to induce OCD related behavior. Though, 
other unintended cells in the striatum also were transfected, no OCD-like behavior was observed. Surprisingly, 
an increase in rearing was observed, supposedly showing an unexpected effect of activation of ACC. The behavior 
was reversed after five days without treatment. The increase in rearing was interpreted as increased spatial 
information gathering, possibly reflecting re-evaluation of spacial information. However, the experiment needs 
to be repeated where only the glutamatergic cortico-striatal is activated during observations, in order to confirm 
the origin of the rearing.
