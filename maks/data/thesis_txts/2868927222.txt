Abstract
Tangential flow microfiltration (TFM) is a critical step in the downstream processing of biophar-
maceuticals, yet its optimization remains challenging due to the complex interplay of process
parameters. This thesis addresses this challenge by developing a novel hybrid modeling framework
that integrates mechanistic and data-driven approaches to optimize TFM for the recovery of
Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP). Given the limited research on hybrid models for TFM, this
work fills a critical gap by demonstrating how mechanistic and data-driven models can work
together effectively.
The framework consists of two parts: mechanistic model development and data-driven optimiza-
tion. The mechanistic model, built based on the Global Model is used to generate large synthetic
datasets under varying conditions, including changes in the Reynolds number, concentration
factor, pH, and ionic strength. These datasets are then used to train a Partial Least Squares
Regression (PLS-R) model, chosen for its computational efficiency and ability to handle large
datasets.
Feature engineering and transformations, including time normalization and square-root scaling,
are applied to improve the PLS-R model’s performance. Despite its limitations in capturing non-
linearities, the model demonstrates good predictive performance based on evaluation metrics such
as root mean squared error (RMSE) and R². Bayesian Optimization (BO) is subsequently used
to identify optimal process parameters, revealing that operating near the pressure-independent
flux at high cross-flow velocities is advantageous for separation efficiency. The optimization also
indicates that the effects of pH and ionic strength on the process remain minimal, as their feature
importance is negligible.
This study shows that integrating mechanistic and data-driven models can effectively optimize
complex bioprocesses like GFP recovery using TFM. The robust mechanistic model can be
extended to different membrane geometries, while the data-driven model accelerates optimization
and possibly enables scaling to industrial applications.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
This work focuses on one of the primary recovery steps in bioprocesses: the microfiltration stage.
Tangential flow microfiltration (TFM) is a commonly used method in downstream processing,
where the target protein passes through the membrane while larger solutes like cells, cell debris,
and other impurities are retained. This results in a more concentrated protein in the permeate,
which can continue to subsequent purification steps. TFM is widely employed in industrial-scale
bioprocesses, with a critical role in protein recovery, encompassing sectors like food production,
biopharmaceuticals, and biotechnology in general. Efficient protein recovery ensures product
quality, maximizes yield, and minimizes waste, making it indispensable in these industries.
However, the efficiency of this process can be significantly hindered by the challenges posed by
highly polydisperse solutions, a common feature of high-density cell cultures. Such solutions cause
issues like membrane fouling and concentration polarization, which reduce flux rate, increase
energy consumption, and requires frequent cleaning, leading to higher operating costs. Addressing
these challenges is essential for improving the performance and economic feasibility of the process.
1.2
Aim
To optimize the microfiltration step, this work proposes an approach inspired by hybrid modeling,
combining a mechanistic and data-driven model. The mechanistic part of the model will allow
for in silico experiments, simulating various process parameters and conditions in order to better
understand the underlying dynamics. The data-driven part focuses on optimizing the results
to achieve the most optimal process parameters. By optimizing these factors, the model can
identify the ideal conditions to achieve a high yield of the target protein, ensuring an (near) ideal
balance between the targeted protein and other components in the mixture. This integrated
strategy can potentially reduce time and costs, offering a more efficient and cost-effective route
for the recovery and purification of proteins.
The protein of interest, Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP), was expressed using Escherichia coli
bacteria. The results are then validated by experimental data obtained from the literature.

Background
2.1
Studied protein
2.1.1
Green Fluorescent Protein
Green fluorescent protein (GFP) was first discovered by Shinomura et al. [1] from Aequorea
victoria jellyfish.
It is now widely used as a marker due to its ability to emit an obvious
fluorescence when exposed to blue in the ultraviolet range of light, which allows a deeper
understanding of the protein structure. GFP is currently used as a marker of gene expression or
molecular interactions.
The main advantages of GFP are its high sensitivity and noninvasiveness. It is highly stable and
does not need a cofactor to form a chromophore. There is no change to the function or location
of the protein [2]. GFP contains a chromophore in its structure that enables fluorescence, with an
excitation wavelength range of 395 to 489 nm and an emission range of 504 to 511 nm, depending
on the specific GFP variant [3].
Figure 2.1: Structure of the Aequorea victoria green fluorescent protein [4].
The primary market of GFP is in assays that require high-purity levels. The cost of GFP is
relatively high, the price of 1 mg in September 2024 is US$ 2930 [4]. This limits the widespread
use of GFP in industrial processes, and it is only possible to use GFP in research or small-scale
developments. Fortunately, the usage of GFP is economically feasible because only minuscule
amounts of the protein have to be used for it to be present.
To reduce the costs many studies have been conducted, focusing either on the upstream or
downstream processes, as of now it is produced by bacteria and requires a complex purification
process. Avenues that have been investigated in the upstream processes are utilizing recombinant
DNA technology to enhance the yield by modifying Escherichia coli (E. coli), [3] or producing
GFP from transgenic plant cell cultures [5]. The recovery process varies but usually requires
many steps like centrifugation, homogenization, filtration, and chromatography. Most studies

2.2
Downstream processing in bioprocesses
Downstream processing involves a sequence of carefully designed unit operations tailored to the
characteristics of the target product and impurities. The first step, harvesting, separates the
biomass from the culture broth, often using centrifugation or filtration methods. If the product is
intracellular, such as GFP, cell disruption techniques like homogenization or enzymatic treatment
are employed to release it. This step is bypassed for extracellular products, and the focus shifts
to capture and purification.
The capture stage aims to isolate the product from the bulk liquid and removes major con-
taminants. Techniques like ultrafiltration, precipitation, or chromatography may be employed,
depending on the product’s size, charge, and hydrophobicity.
Purification steps refine the
product further, using advanced chromatographic techniques such as affinity, ion exchange, or
size-exclusion chromatography to achieve the desired purity levels.
Concentration and polishing are essential to meet final product specifications. Methods like
ultrafiltration, diafiltration, or lyophilization are used to increase product concentration and
remove trace impurities. Polishing steps also ensure the removal of endotoxins, viruses, or
aggregates, which is critical for pharmaceutical products.
Beyond product recovery, downstream processing addresses sustainability by incorporating waste
treatment and recycling steps. For example, valuable by-products like spent biomass or residual
proteins may be recovered for secondary applications, while wastewater is treated to minimize
environmental impact. The efficiency and scalability of downstream processing are key deter-
minants of the overall process’ economic feasibility, particularly for high-value products like
biopharmaceuticals.

2.2.1
Tangential flow microfiltration
Tangential flow filtration (TFF) also called crossflow filtration (CF) is a critical unit operation,
particularly during the harvesting and clarification stages.
Microfiltration separates larger
molecules like colloids and bacteria and functions at lower transmembrane pressure (TMP) [9].
It is usually operated in continuous mode with a closed system, meaning that the retentate
is recirculated back into the feed stream. In TFF, the feed flow travels tangentially to the
membrane, which lowers the accumulation of particles on the surface of the membrane and slows

down cake formation. This process differs from the typical ”dead-end” filtration, where the feed
flows directly through the membrane. The two are compared in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Visualization of tangential and dead-end flow filtration.
Due to this difference the cake layer does not grow continuously, because of high shear rate created
by the suspension flowing tangentially to the membrane surface, the particles are transported
back to the suspension.
The tangential flow microfiltration (TFM) process employs porous membranes with pore sizes
typically in the 0.1 to 0.5 µm range, making it suitable for separating bacterial cells, yeast, or
other particulates. It is especially useful in processes involving fragile cells or shear-sensitive
products, as the tangential flow reduces mechanical stress. For instance, in the biopharmaceutical
industry, TFM is often employed to harvest cells or recover extracellular products like monoclonal
antibodies or enzymes from fermentation broths.
Another advantage of TFM is its scalability and adaptability. It can be easily scaled up by
increasing the membrane area or operating multiple modules in parallel. Moreover, advances
in membrane technology, such as high-performance polyethersulfone (PES) or polyvinylidene
fluoride (PVDF) membranes, have enhanced TFM efficiency, enabling higher flux rates and
greater selectivity [10]. These improvements have made TFM a preferred choice for initial biomass
removal and subsequent clarification steps.
TFM is also a pre-concentration step before ultrafiltration or other downstream purification
processes. With its operational flexibility and high-throughput capability, TFM plays a pivotal
role in ensuring the efficiency and robustness of downstream processing workflows.
One of the challenges of the TFM is the decline in flux caused by membrane fouling. The membrane
fouling is caused by factors like cake build-up and pore-clogging. The former introduces an extra
plane of resistance to the feed flow, and the latter boosts the inherent membrane resistance
[11]. Additional influences are particle deposition within pores, which can lead to either partial
blocking or complete blocking. All these factors impact the membrane flux, which must be
controlled to efficiently operate the process.

It has been demonstrated that tangential flow microfiltration (TFM) has a critical flux below
which the process operates without causing membrane fouling. This is distinct from the limiting
flux, representing the maximum permeate flux achievable; beyond this point, increasing pressure
no longer results in higher flux.[15, 16]. It is different to limiting flux which refers to the maximum
permeate flux that can be achieved, past that point increasing the pressure does not lead to
higher flux.

a concentration-polarization layer where particles diffuse away. At steady state, the convection

If the Brownian diffusion mechanism controls the back transport it should increase with shear
rate and decrease with growing particle size. But for larger sized particles, the Brownian diffusion
mechanism underpredicts the fluxes. It is referred as the ”flux paradox” [17]. As an alternative,
shear-induced hydrodynamic diffusivity and inertial lift were introduced to describe the behaviour
of particle suspensions [18].
Shear-induced diffusivity and inertial lift are mechanisms that contribute to the movement of
particles in suspension under shear flow. Shear-induced diffusivity arises from the shear forces
acting on the particles as they move through the fluid, causing them to spread out more rapidly
than would be predicted by Brownian diffusion alone. This effect becomes more pronounced at
higher shear rates as the shear forces enhance the random motion of particles. Inertial lift, on the
other hand, refers to the force exerted on particles due to their inertia as they move through the
fluid, which causes them to migrate away from regions of high shear or the walls of the filtration
system. This effect is particularly significant for larger particles and at higher flow rates, as
inertial forces become more dominant, leading to particle migration toward the center of the
flow. Both shear-induced diffusivity and inertial lift contribute to better predictions of particle
flux in systems with higher shear rates or larger particles, where Brownian diffusion alone fails
to explain the observed behaviour [19, 13].
Several studies have explored the application of tangential flow microfiltration (TFF) for the
recovery of GFP, emphasizing the role of operating conditions and membrane properties in
determining recovery efficiency and minimizing fouling. Aspelund et al. [20] investigated the
effectiveness of TFM for recovery from germ extracts using GFP and type I human collagen as
model proteins. They compared it with common dead-end filtration, where filtration cake had a
much higher build-up. Microfiltration proved to be a viable and effective method for clarifying
corn protein extracts, except for scenarios involving significant internal membrane fouling, which
led to increased rejection of high molecular weight proteins. Zuponcic et al. [21] conducted TFM

2.2.2
Process Configurations
Total Recycle: The total recycle configuration is a closed-loop system where the retentate and
permeate streams are returned to the feed reservoir, maintaining a constant feed composition
throughout the process. This configuration is commonly used for experimental purposes to
optimize operating conditions, such as transmembrane pressure (TMP), crossflow velocity, and
temperature, without altering the feed concentration. Aspelund et al. [20] performed their
experiments in total recycle configuration to be able to vary three types of fluxes and TMP.
Recycling both streams enabled the measurement of key parameters like permeate flux and
solute rejection under controlled conditions, making it ideal for preliminary studies and process
development. Parnham et al. [22] used the total recycle mode to study the effects of shear rate,
feed concentration, and TMP.
Diafiltration: Diafiltration is a process configuration designed for washing particles or removing
solutes from the retentate by replacing the removed permeate with an equal amount of fresh
solvent. This approach maintains a constant feed volume while effectively diluting and removing
unwanted solutes from the system, such as salts, acids, or small molecules. For that purpose,
Venkiteshwaran et al.[23] used a diafiltration layout to wash out host-cell proteins to recover
inclusion bodies of the target protein.
In comparison, Baruah and Belfort [24] employed
diafiltration to enhance the recovery of the target protein, which, being smaller, was present in
the permeate. The layout was used to concentrate and enhance the recovery of the target protein.
It can also be used for buffer exchange or achieving specific solute concentrations in downstream
bioprocessing. The technique ensures minimal change in particle concentration while maximizing
the removal of target solutes.
Batch Concentration: In batch concentration, the retentate is recycled back into the feed
reservoir while the permeate is continuously removed without replacement, leading to a gradual
increase in solute or particle concentration within the retentate. Van Eijndhoven et al. [25]
employed the batch filtration setup to study the performance of the membrane at various protein
concentrations. This configuration is widely used to concentrate biomolecules, such as proteins
or enzymes, in preparation for further purification or processing. However, as the concentration
increases, osmotic pressure and fouling can become more significant, requiring careful monitoring
and adjustment of operating conditions to maintain process efficiency.
Feed and Bleed: The feed-and-bleed configuration is designed for steady-state operation, where
the permeate is continuously removed, and a portion of the retentate, called the bleed, is taken
out while the rest is recycled to the feed reservoir [26]. This setup maintains a high tangential
velocity in the membrane module, reducing the risk of fouling and ensuring consistent filtration
performance. Feed-and-bleed is ideal for large-scale and continuous operations, such as protein
recovery or fermentation broth clarification.

2.2.3
Membrane Modules
Hollow Fiber: Hollow fiber modules consist of a bundle of narrow-bore fibers, each with a
dense skin layer that provides structural integrity and ensures filtration performance. The fibers
are typically arranged in parallel, with a flow path through the hollow core, allowing the feed
solution to pass along the exterior while permeate flows through the fiber walls. These modules
are often used in processes requiring high surface area and efficient filtration, offering advantages
in compact design and ease of scale-up. Hollow fiber modules were employed by Zuponcic et al.
[21], who utilized a single hollow fiber module for their experiments, as well as by Baruah and
Belfort [24] in their studies.
Tubular: Tubular membrane modules feature a series of cylindrical tubes made from semi-
permeable material. In these modules, feed solution flows through the interior of the tubes, while
permeate is filtered through the tube walls. Tubular systems are known for their robust design,
making them suitable for handling viscous or particulate-laden fluids. They also provide the
advantage of easy cleaning and maintenance. Tubular membrane modules were utilized by Field
et al. [28], where they compared its performance also with a flat-sheet membrane and introduced
the concept of critical flux.

Flat Plate: Flat plate modules consist of a series of flat, parallel membrane sheets, typically
arranged in a stack with spacer elements in between. Feed solution flows between the sheets,
and permeate is drawn through the membrane surfaces. These modules offer high surface area
and are often used in laboratory-scale operations or small-scale industrial applications, where
space constraints and high filtration capacity are important. A flat plate membrane was used by
Parnham et al. [22] to compare them with a rotary membrane.
Spiral Wound: Spiral wound modules are designed with a set of membrane sheets wound
around a central permeate collection tube, with spacer filaments separating each membrane sheet.
The feed solution flows through the channels between the sheets, while permeate passes through
the membrane layers to be collected in the central tube. This design maximizes surface area in a
compact structure, making spiral wound modules widely used in reverse osmosis, nanofiltration,
and other filtration processes where high efficiency and space-saving designs are critical [27].

2.3
Modelling
The complex structure of membrane filtration makes it challenging to predict its progress and
completion. Numerous attempts have been made to describe the process using semi-physical
models. However, not all aspects are fully understood, preventing their complete representation in
mechanistic models, also known as parametric or white-box models. Concentration polarization
and fouling remain critical challenges in membrane separation processes that require accurate
prediction [29]. To address this, data-driven models have been developed, also called non-
parametric or black-box models. These models can depict process behaviour without requiring
an understanding of the underlying physical phenomena. However, they fail to capture fouling
mechanisms and their dynamics [27].
Understanding particle dynamics for concentrated suspensions to predict the flow and movement
of particles with the help of intermolecular forces is needed [27]. Predicting transport phenomena
through pores requires a detailed understanding of pore structure and interconnectivity. Despite
progress in characterizing pore sizes and surface properties, an accurate 3D representation of
porous channels is still essential but lacking.
A combination of both these tactics is called hybrid modelling. The two approaches complement
each other as mechanistic models can offer insights into the inner workings of the process, and
data-driven models can fill in the gaps by identifying patterns and relationships in the data,
enabling a more comprehensive and accurate representation of the system. Together, they provide
a balanced approach that leverages the strengths of both methodologies [30].
The development of hybrid models is still in its infancy since combining the two is no easy
task. The models are joined in two ways, either serial or in parallel. The choice of the layout

2.3.1
Mechanistic modelling of TFM
As previously mentioned, mechanistic models are built based on prior understanding of the
process and its fundamentals to depict the system and functions using mathematical equations.
They have been used for a very long time to understand singular aspects of the process and
obtain more knowledge for system design and operation control.
Concentration Polarization Model
The concept of ”flux paradox” was first introduced by Zydney and Clark (1986) [18]. They
presented a model based on concentration polarization and shear-enhanced diffusivity to explain
flux decline and particle transport. It assumed the formation of a concentration boundary layer
near the membrane, where particle concentration increases, limiting further filtration. The
authors derived a flux equation that predicted the maximum pressure-independent flux seen in
experiments.
The model assumed a constant particle concentration at the membrane surface, which does not
hold true in all cases. It also neglected axial variations in shear rate and particle concentration,
simplifying the boundary layer to have a uniform shear rate. The assumption of a thin cake layer
is generally valid for a wide range of suspensions containing very small, compressible particles or
particles with a size or shape distribution that allows them to pack tightly in the cake layer
Romero and Davis (1990) [33] went in a different avenue and developed a time-dependent model
to predict how the permeate flux decreases over time with the deposition of particles on the
membrane surface. It was a continuation of their previous work where they worked on a steady-
state model [34]. It predicted how long it takes to reach this steady state, considering factors
like suspension concentration, particle size, and shear rate. Assuming a two-layer formation near

the surface, a stagnant and a flowing layer, their properties change over time due to particle
deposition.
A key aspect was shear-induced hydrodynamic diffusion, which competed with the convective
transport of particles towards the membrane, controlling the rate of layer build-up. It gave
insights into the transient behaviour and offered guidance to the process, mainly in determining
the optimal conditions to delay the onset of significant flux decline and planning maintenance
procedures like backflushing.
In their review, Tien et al. (2013) [35] assessed various filtration models, including those proposed
by Romero and Davis (1988, 1990) [34, 33]. Regarding the Romero and Davis model, they
highlighted specific issues, such as the assumption that cake firmness equals the maximum
packing density and the proposed mechanisms of particle transport, which were found to be
inconsistent with available experimental data.
Song (1998) [11] delved into the process of membrane fouling in cross-flow filtration of ultrafiltra-
tion and microfiltration, seeing as mainly dead-end filtration had been studied. The model was
based on two mechanisms - pore blocking and cake formation, the former being quicker than the
latter since cake formation requires accumulation on the membrane surface. Both depend on
particle deposition on the membrane surface. A mathematical model was established, treating
membrane fouling as a transition from a non-equilibrium state to an equilibrium state. It also
considered time-dependent flux and the period needed to reach a steady state.
The paper expanded on their previous work [36], which focused on concentration polarization.
The filtration dynamics and the transition from concentration polarization to cake formation
introduce a filtration number (Nf) for more detailed predictions.
Krompkamp et al. (2005) [37] introduced a suspension flow model for hydrodynamics and
concentration polarization. It incorporated the shear-induced migration and used an Euler-Euler
approach with the Lattice Boltzmann method (LBM). The model estimated the cake layer
profile, particularly in complex geometries. Navier-Stokes equations were used to calculate the
hydrodynamics.
The model treated shear-induced diffusivity as a function of both the shear rate and local particle
concentration. It accounted for variations in local shear rates and flow velocities rather than
assuming a fully developed, uniform Poiseuille flow. Assuming a constant cake resistance derived
from the Carman-Kozeny relation may not be accurate for compressible cake layers. It did not
consider changes in cake porosity over time, which could influence long-term flux.
While powerful, the model’s reliance on LBM and resolving hydrodynamics and particle transport
required significant computational resources. Large or complex simulations, particularly in 3D,
demanded high-performance computing. Though it is extendable to 3D, the simulations presented
in the paper are primarily two-dimensional (2D), which may oversimplify the actual filtration
process in complex, industrial-scale geometries.

Interfacial Energy Models
Membrane fouling mechanisms, driven by interactions between foulants and membranes, are not
fully understood. Bridging this knowledge gap is vital for linking experimental observations to
theoretical models and improving the predictive accuracy of fouling behaviour. In this context,
interfacial energy models play a significant role by providing theoretical frameworks to describe
and predict the behaviour of particles, droplets, or molecules at interfaces between different
phases. By accounting for interfacial energy—arising from the imbalance of molecular forces at
the interface—these models help elucidate critical processes like adhesion, wetting, and phase
separation, which are central to understanding and mitigating membrane fouling. [27].
Baruah and Belfort developed a predictive model called Aggregate transport model[38, 39,
24, 40] for cross-flow filtration processes. In the first paper, they developed a model where
they considered all three forces influencing the back-transport mechanism - Brownian diffusion,
shear-induced diffusion, and inertial lift, which the previously discussed papers did not. The
focus was polydisperse suspensions and predicting their product concentration. It offered a
quantitative basis for maintaining constant filtration flux conditions and examined the concept
of the critical flux-to-shear ratio. A few important aspects were omitted for the simplicity of
the model, like the adhesion of particles to the surface of the membrane and charge interactions
between particles and the membrane.
The model was then later tested and validated [39] with experiments on transgenic goat milk,
where the model predictions agreed with the experimental data. Experiments were conducted
under various operating conditions. Following that, the insights learned from the model were used
for optimizing the recovery process to maximize the product yield [24]. Using the understanding
of the model, the product concentration was increased from less than 1% to over 95%.
Afterward, the authors established a global model [40] for cross-flow microfiltration and ultrafiltra-
tion. Some of the aspects of the first version of the model that were ignored were included, such
as electrostatics and membrane properties. The model was validated using various experimental
data from different polydisperse suspensions and then various in silico simulations to showcase
different scenarios. The model assumed 100% sieving for salts, laminar flow, no counter osmotic
flow, and negligible membrane charge.
Fouling Models
Classical fouling models describe how particles interact with membranes during filtration, leading
to a decline in performance. In pore blockage, particles completely obstruct membrane pores,
a phenomenon typically seen when particle sizes are equal to or larger than the pore size.
Intermediate blockage occurs when particles attach to the pore walls, partially obstructing the
flow while leaving some pathways open. These mechanisms are common in the early stages of
filtration and depend heavily on particle size, shape, and concentration relative to the membrane’s
pore structure [27].
Over time, smaller particles may penetrate the pores, causing pore constriction by narrowing

the pore diameter and further reducing permeability. Additionally, particles that cannot enter
the pores often accumulate on the membrane surface, forming a layer referred to as a cake
layer. This cake layer acts as a secondary filtration barrier, increasing resistance to flow and
significantly impacting the filtration process. Together, these fouling mechanisms provide a
framework for understanding how particle deposition affects membrane performance, informing
fouling mitigation and cleaning strategies.
Ho and Zydney (2000) [41] developed a model that integrated pore blockage with cake filtration.
The approach, part of the three-mechanism model, provided an accurate yet simplified analytical
solution. It assumed uniform foulant resistance across the membrane surface, reflecting physical
phenomena like ”self-levelling” during cake formation.
Initially, foulants block open membrane pores with deposits that are at least partially permeable.
As fouling progresses, more material accumulates, forming a cake layer. This layer grows while
simultaneously covering the remaining open areas of the membrane. The total flow is modelled as
the sum of contributions from blocked and open areas, capturing the dynamics of pore blockage,
pore constriction, and cake formation in a unified framework.
Tien and Ramarao (2017) [42] focused on the flux decay caused by cake formation but used a
different approach based on particle adhesion theory. It extended the classical cake filtration
theory, typically applied to dead-end filtration, to TFF. It was modified to account for the dynamic
nature of TFF, where the shear forces and particle adhesion influenced the cake formation and
filtrate flux. The adhesion model was based on the author’s previous work [43], where the
probability of the particle adhering to the surface of the cake or the membrane depended on the
roughness.
It also provided an example of testing the operating conditions and illustrating the application
of the model. The authors tried to resolve the phenomena of flux paradox, where the observed
filtration rate is much higher than predicted by simple models. Still, adhesion was found not
to be the only factor influencing the issue. Other factors could be particle back transport or
particle-particle interactions. However, when incorporating them into the model, they only
showed a modicum of influence and did not have enough significance.
Resistance-In-Series Model
The resistance-in-series model describes the filtration process, mainly when multiple resistance
elements contribute to the overall flux decline in a filtration system. The model assumes that the
total resistance to permeate flow in the filtration system can be treated as the sum of individual
resistances from different sources.
Zhang et al. (2021) [44] used an asymmetric membrane to overcome the flux decline caused by
membrane fouling. In an asymmetric membrane, the more open surface faces the feed stream,
and the more closed surface faces the permeate side. With this setup at the start of the filtration,
the membrane would act like a depth filter. The morphology of the membrane would stabilize
the internal cake.

Data-driven modelling of TFM
Different from mechanistic modelling, as previously mentioned, data-driven models do not need
previous process knowledge or understanding of the physical and mathematical aspects of the
system. Experimental data is used to train these systems and to look for critical quality attributes
[32].
Artificial Neural Networks
Artificial neural networks (ANNs), inspired by the human brain, consist of interconnected units
called neurons that can learn from data. The multilayer perceptron (MLP) is the most commonly
used, which includes multiple layers of neurons. Each neuron performs calculations in two steps:
first, it computes a weighted sum of its inputs, and then applies a nonlinear activation function
to produce its output. The MLP is organized into layers, with an input layer receiving the data,
hidden layers processing the inputs, and an output layer producing the final result [45].
The neural network is usually trained using a back-propagation algorithm, which adjusts the
weights to minimize the error between the predicted and the actual outpu s. The training process
involved two datasets: a learning base used to train the network and a test base used to validate
its performance.

Two types of experiments were conducted, one with TMP and crossflow velocity constant and one

Fuzzy Logic Model
Fuzzy logic is an approach that handles reasoning that is approximate rather than fixed and
exact. Unlike classical logic, which operates with precise true or false (0 or 1) values, fuzzy logic
allows for degrees of truth. These degrees are represented on a continuum between 0 and 1.
It involves four key components: fuzzification, fuzzy rules, fuzzy inference system (FIS), and
defuzzification. During fuzzification, crisp input values are converted into fuzzy inputs using
membership functions (MF). These membership functions are also used in defuzzification, where
fuzzy output values are translated into numerical results. Membership functions are essential for
converting non-fuzzy inputs into fuzzy terms, and vice versa [45].

Hybrid modelling of TFM
Literature on hybrid modelling of TFM systems was challenging find.
To the best of my
knowledge, Piron et al. (1997) [54] is the only study on hybrid modelling of TFM. In their work,
two approaches were used: the black-box model and the semi-physical model, where the neural
networks were incorporated to estimate the unknown parameters. Experiments to validate and
train the models were performed with yeast using two different concentrations and changing
various operating parameters.
The paper describes using feed-forward and recurrent neural networks to model the microfiltration
process. The resistance in the series model assumed that several additive resistances affected
filtrate flux. Two physical phenomena were supposed to be behind the cake filtration: the
force balance models, how the particle remains on the surface, and particle back transport. As
discussed in section 2.2.1, classical Brownian diffusion is insufficient to be the only contributing
force; inertial lift and shear-induced diffusion are also part of it. Due to such different influences,
it was found to be difficult to represent all of them in a mechanistic model. Fouling was presumed
to be caused solely by cake formation, and no inner fouling occurred.
A recurrent two-layer feed-forward neural network was applied to approximate the system
dynamics. The input layer had four variables: tangential flow velocity, TMP, feed solution
concentration, and current hydraulic resistance. The system was sampled every five minutes
for four hours to reduce noise from the raw data. Due to the limited amount of data, only
training and test sets were used. The third independent data set (a generalization set) to
study the performance of the resulting network was not prepared. A quasi-Newton method was
implemented to optimize the neural network’s weights. This algorithm is more efficient than
simpler methods like steepest descent but doesn’t always find the best solution. In addition,
two static networks were used to calculate the unknown physical parameters; one was used to
compute the properties related to the diffusion layer, and the other provided the characteristics
of a filtration cake. Outputs of the networks were then scaled appropriately to ensure they
aligned with real-world physical magnitudes, and the networks were carefully initialized using
experimental data to improve accuracy.
The hybrid model outperformed the standard network in terms of prediction accuracy. The
overall error was significantly lower for the hybrid model, which better accounted for the
underlying physical processes. However, it also had limitations, particularly related to assumptions
about constant operating conditions and the specific resistance of the filtration process. These
assumptions influenced its parameters, leading to some discrepancies in the results, particularly
regarding the specific resistance, which was expected to change over time but was treated as
constant in the model.
The hybrid model benefited from incorporating physical knowledge, making it more accurate for
certain tasks and providing an understanding of the process. However, it was also constrained
by the assumptions of the physical model, which can limit its applicability in more complex
situations. In contrast, while less transparent, the standard neural network was more flexible. It
could adapt to changes in operating conditions, making it more suitable for control applications
where such conditions might vary.

Research Gaps and Thesis Objectives
This thesis addresses the challenges in tangential flow microfiltration (TFM) for GFP recovery,
such as membrane fouling, concentration polarization, and polydisperse feed streams, which hinder

• Aims to enhance predictive accuracy of permeation and fouling behavior in TFM systems.
• Develop a framework that accelerates parameter optimization for GFP recovery.
• Reduce dependency on resource-intensive experimentation.
• Improve the scalability and resource efficiency of TFM for GFP recovery through an

Model development method
The basis of the mechanistic model is taken from [38, 40], as it was found to be the most suitable
due to its focus on the filtration of polydisperse suspensions. The model was adapted to align
with the objectives of this study and subsequently validated against experimental data. Following
successful validation, data generation was initiated by systematically varying process parameters
to examine their impact on the process. The data-driven model using the synthetic data is then
employed to optimize the process parameters to reach the most optimal process configuration.
3.1
Mechanistic Model
3.1.1
Global Model
The Global Model is a predictive and optimization framework designed to handle the dynamic
nature of processes in membrane filtration based on the Aggregate Transport Model (ATM).
It models both pressure-independent and pressure-dependent regimes, considering factors such
as particle size distribution, membrane properties, hydrodynamics, electrostatics, and cake
composition. Solving coupled differential equations predicts permeation flux, sieving coefficients,
and solute yield while optimizing objectives like purity, selectivity, and processing time. Unlike
empirical approaches, the Global Model uses iterative simulations, making it ideal for industrial
applications involving polydisperse suspensions and system changes.
ATM provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the behaviour of poly-disperse suspen-
sions during microfiltration. It can only predict the pressure-independent permeation flux and
does not consider pressure-dependent effects, transient states, or dynamic changes in cake layer
composition and particle interactions under varying operational conditions. The ATM focuses on
idealized conditions, often relying on assumptions such as constant particle properties and static
cake composition.

The described framework presents the methodology to predict the limiting pressure-independent
permeation flux and solute sieving coefficients for polydisperse suspensions at any given flux. The
entire spectrum of microfiltration and ultrafiltration operations can be simulated for a specific pH
and wall shear rate by varying the permeation flux from very low values up to the limiting flux.
Each reservoir in the process is governed by a set of n coupled differential equations, representing
the mass balance of n solutes. These equations are interconnected through packing constraints,
system connectivity, and suspension viscosity.
Solving these equations analytically is impractical for complex systems with multiple membrane
stages and polydisperse suspensions involving many solutes and particles. To address this, the
system can be approximated as being in a quasi-equilibrium state over short time intervals.
After each time step, all relevant computations, including suspension details, hydrodynamics,
cake composition, and solute transport, are updated and repeated until the process objective
is achieved.

calculations. Any process in the laminar flow regime can be simulated and optimized. The

Data-driven Model
For the development of the data-driven part of the model, the partial least squares regression
(PLS-R) method is used. PLS-R is a robust statistical method for predicting continuous variables,
particularly when there is multicollinearity among independent variables or when the number of
features exceeds the number of observations. PLS is divided into two types: PLS1, where the
features or the targets are one-dimensional, and PLS2, where both are multidimensional [61].
The method works by extracting latent variables (components) that maximize the covariance
between the features and the target(s), effectively modeling the relationship while addressing
collinearity issues.
The usual PLS algorithms like NIPALS operate the best when there is linear relationship between
features and targets [62]. If the system is nonlinear the model’s performance is lowered [63]. To
address nonlinearity in the model, feature transformation can be used to handle the nonlinear
relationship between features and targets. Generally, feature transformations like log, square
root, and polynomial can be used. They can also reduce the skewness of the dataset, as PLS
operates better when there is a Gaussian distribution. [64].
The type used in this work is PLS2, which allows the model to capture the relationship between

Optimization process
The trained data-driven model is used as a predictive tool to assess the objective function
during Bayesian Optimization (BO) to find the most optimal process parameters. BO employs a
probabilistic surrogate model, most commonly Gaussian Processes (GP), to optimise black-box
functions [66]. It is focused on solving the problem
max
x∈A f(x)
(3.33)
where, the objective function f is continuous and a ”black-box,” defined on a compact domain
A ⊂Rd. The function f has no known closed form or derivatives, but its evaluations f(x) can
be accessed at any point x ∈A. For further details on the BO refer to the work of Frazier [67].
It is commonly used to tune the hyper-parameters in machine learning algorithms, but in this
work, it is used to optimize the design space. BO proposes sets of input parameters to maximize
a predefined objective function, such as the yield ratio of 5 nm to 40 nm particles. The objective
function is evaluated by a Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression model, which predicts system
dynamics based on the proposed parameters. BO then uses these predictions and its Gaussian
Process (GP) surrogate model to iteratively refine its search for the optimal parameter set. The
BO is based BayesianOptimization from the bayes opt Python library.
The main variables of interest are listed in Table 3.4. The sieving coefficients are taken as the
mean values computed from the dataset for the remaining features. The pressure-independent
flux is estimated through interpolation, using the relationship between the Reynolds number
and the concentration factor (Multiplier of concentration) derived from the dataset. The actual
permeation flux is then calculated by scaling to ensure that it remains lower than the pressure-
independent flux, reflecting realistic operational constraints. Each simulation is set to run over
200 normalized time steps, where the time normalization ensures consistent comparison across
simulations of different durations.
Results from the BO should provide the most effective set of process parameters to achieve
the desired objective, such as maximizing the yield ratio within the defined constraints and
operational limits.

Conclusions
The primary goal of this project was to create a system combining mechanistic and data-driven
approaches, leveraging their respective strengths while compensating for their limitations. Due
to time constraints and the complexity of integrating the two models seamlessly, the hybrid
framework adopted a loosely coupled approach. This allowed for an efficient exploration of
process parameters and system optimization while maintaining interpretability and computational
feasibility. Given the limited research on hybrid modelling frameworks applied specifically to
TFM, this work provides an important contribution by demonstrating how such models can be
designed and implemented effectively.
The mechanistic model was based on the Global Model and was used to simulate various operating
conditions, incorporating back-transport equations to account for key transport mechanisms such
as Brownian diffusion and shear-induced diffusion. This ensured that the model could accurately
predict pressure-independent permeation flux, a critical aspect in understanding the dynamics of
polydisperse suspensions during filtration. The model was validated against experimental data in
total recycle mode, demonstrating its ability to replicate key performance indicators, such as flux
decline and solute retention. This validation provided the foundation for generating synthetic
data, which were then used to train a data-driven model.
The data generation process systematically varied key parameters, including Reynolds number,
concentration factor, pH, and ionic strength. These variables were selected based on their known
or potential influence on flux behaviour and sieving coefficients. The resulting dataset captured
a wide range of operating conditions, offering a rich basis for training the PLS-R model. The
selection of PLS-R as the data-driven model was driven by its computational efficiency, robustness
against multicollinearity, and ability to handle large datasets. Feature engineering steps, such as
time normalization and square-root transformations, were applied to improve model performance
and reduce noise.
Initial analysis of the PLS-R model highlighted its capacity to capture essential relationships within
the dataset despite its limitations in handling complex nonlinearities. The evaluation metrics,
including root mean squared error (RMSE) and R², indicated good predictive performance.
Improvements were made by refining the feature space and applying selective transformations to
enhance accuracy. Although the PLS-R model is not inherently designed for nonlinear interactions,
it proved effective within the scope of this work, providing reliable predictions for the optimization
phase. Bayesian Optimization was employed to identify optimal process parameters. The optimal
parameters suggested that operating near the pressure-independent flux, particularly at high cross-
flow velocities, maximizes separation efficiency while mitigating fouling risks. The influence of
pH and ionic strength remains undefined, as previous analysis indicated their feature importance
to be negligible. While these variables showed limited potential interactions with performance,
their exact effects remain ambiguous and warrant further investigation.
The hybrid modelling framework developed in this thesis offers several advantages for future work.
With its robust foundation, the mechanistic model can be adapted to various geometries and
membrane configurations, making it suitable for both lab-scale and industrial-scale applications.
Development of an in silico hybrid model for tangential flow microfiltration in the downstream processing of GFP
51
==PAGE:58==
Although this work focused on lab-scale experiments, the framework’s scalability provides a
pathway for transitioning to industrial settings with further development. The data-driven
component, while initially limited by the synthetic nature of the dataset, demonstrated the
potential for rapid optimization and real-time decision-making.
In summary, this thesis provides a comprehensive outline for combining mechanistic and data-
driven models in a loosely coupled hybrid framework. The proposed approach bridges the gap
between physical understanding and data-driven insights and offers a practical solution for
optimizing complex bioprocesses like GFP recovery while remaining computationally inexpensive.
Future work should focus on refining the hybrid model to better capture nonlinear effects,
validating its predictions with experimental data from industrial-scale processes, and exploring its
application in different bioproduct recovery scenarios. One potential improvement could be the
integration of kernel-based methods, such as Kernel PLS, combined with algorithms optimized
for handling large and high-dimensional datasets as the complexity grows. For the mechanistic
model, future developments could include adding pore-blocking mechanisms and refining the
representation of electrostatic effects to better understand their influence on permeation and
fouling dynamics.
Additionally, a closer integration of the two models can be achieved by seamlessly linking them,
for example, through a loop. In this setup, after completing the optimization process, the
identified process parameters would be fed into the mechanistic model to verify whether they
indeed yield the optimal results. If the parameters are found to be suboptimal, the feedback
from the mechanistic model could be used to refine the data-driven model iteratively, thereby
enhancing its predictive accuracy and overall performance. Alternatively, a sequential framework
can be employed, where experimental data is first used to independently train the data-driven
model. This model can then be integrated with the mechanistic model to enhance its predictive
accuracy by reducing residual errors. By leveraging the data-driven model’s strength in capturing
complex patterns, this approach can help address discrepancies within the mechanistic model
and improve overall model performance. The hybrid framework has the potential to significantly
reduce development time and costs, paving the way for more efficient and scalable downstream
processing solutions.
