Abstract
Monopiles are widely used as a foundation method for offshore wind turbines. A possible way of
making wind energy more economically viable is by improving the design and cost efﬁciency of the
foundations.
The current design codes for piles under lateral loading are based on slender ﬂexible piles in opposi-
tion to the rigid large diameter piles which are commonly used for monopile foundations. Because of
this, it is suspected that the design codes underestimate the performance of the monopiles and studies
have shown indications of this.
This project will investigate this issue by carrying out small scale tests with laterally loaded monopile
foundations in the geotechnical centrifuge at the Technical University of Denmark.
The effect of varying pile and soil stiffness on the pile-soil interaction have been investigated by
creating experimental p-y curves based on the test results.
Test have been carried out with two different piles: A steel pile and an aluminium pile. They are
equipped with 15 strain gauges each which measures the moment. Two types of soil have been used
for the test: soft clay and sand. The clay samples vary in their level of preconsolidation stress and the
sand samples vary in relative density. Finally the effect of varying embedment depth have also been
investigated and in total nine tests have been carried out.
The quality of the prepared soil samples have been surmised by observing the axial force during the
installation of the pile. The clay samples generally showed signs of inhomogeneities and the sand
samples were denser than expected.
Based on the moment distribution in the pile experimental p-y curves have been produced.
It was found that the design recommendations generally underestimate the ultimate soil resistance.
When the normalised response of the aluminium and steel piles were compared it was seen that the
ultimate lateral resistance was lower when the test was done with the aluminium pile, however a
signiﬁcant difference was not expected as both pile should behave rigidly.

1
Introduction
The construction of off shore wind turbines is still largely reliant on government subsidies, for off-
shore wind energy to be an economically viable source of energy. This means that it is still important
to ﬁnd ways to reduce the cost. An area where a potential for cost reduction is present, is the foun-
dation of the wind turbines. Currently, monopiles are the preferred type of foundation for offshore
wind turbines, however the recommended design methods with regards to lateral loading are based
on empirical data from tests with with ﬂexible piles. Monopiles used for offshore wind turbines be-
have rigidly and it is suspected that the ultimate lateral resistance is underestimated with the current
recommendations. If the current recommendations could be updated it could both decrease the con-
struction and material cost of the foundations and make monopiles a viable foundation type at even
greater water depths.
The goal of the project is to investigate the behaviour of laterally loaded monopiles through small
scale tests in a geotechnical centrifuge. The geotechnical centrifuge offers a unique way of getting
realistic results through model test and in that way get a better understanding of the nature of the
problem. .
Earlier studies have already shown indications of room for improvement in the design codes. Several
different aspects such as stress level, load type and load eccentricity are important to gain conﬁdence
in understanding of the soil-pile interaction. This project will primarily focus on the effect of varying
soil and pile stiffness in an attempt to add a piece to the puzzle and ultimately contribute to the
improvement of the design codes. The current codes do not consider the stiffness of the pile when
it comes to the ultimate soil resistance and hence it could be interesting to investigate if this has a
signiﬁcant effect.
In the centrifuge the test will be carried out at an acceleration ﬁeld of up to 70 g. Tests will be done
with piles with a diameter of 40 mm with different stiffnesses: A rigid steel pile and a slightly less
rigid aluminium pile, which better reﬂects the stiffness of a full scale monopile in the ﬁeld. Tests will
be done in sand and in clay with different degrees of consolidation to also consider varying degrees
of soil stiffness and consolidation. Through strain gauges on the pile the moment distribution is
obtained and from this the pressure on the and displacement of the pile is found to create p-y curves.
The results will be compared to results from similar centrifuge tests and existing p-y curves.

In this section the relevant theory relating to this project is presented. This includes the basic theory
behind geotechnical centrifuge modelling and the the theory used to analyse laterally and axially

2.1
Geotechnical centrifuge modelling
The purpose of centrifuge modelling in geotechnical engineering is to be able to make accurate small
scale models of real world prototypes. In a down scaled model the stresses will be lower than in the
prototype. The centrifuge can create an enhanced gravity ﬁeld in which the stresses in the model will
correspond to the stresses in the prototype.
When making small scale geotechnical tests it is important to make sure that the stresses in the
prototype and in the model are identical due to the very non-linear stress dependant behaviour of
soils.

Errors in centrifuge modelling
Apart from the non-linear acceleration ﬁeld described in the previous section there are several other
things that can inﬂuence the results.
One of these is the effect of particle size. In the centrifuge the particle size is not scaled down. This is
mainly because the physical properties of the soil are dependant of the particle size. If sand particles
are scaled down signiﬁcantly they might attain more clay like behaviour, which would produce wrong
results given the difference between sand and clay. Therefore it is better to use the same soil in the
model and prototype to get the correct stress-strain response.
The error that might occur, if the particles are too large compared to the structure, is that the assump-
tion of the soil being a continuum will not be valid. In other words the soil around the structure must
be "meshed" at a certain ﬁneness. When testing piles a ratio of 15 between the pile diameter and
particle diameter is generally to be sufﬁcient to avoid particle size effects. As this project concerns
large diameter piles as used for offshore wind turbines the error from particle size effects is assumed
to be negligible.
2.2
Laterally loaded piles
For a long time laterally loaded piles have been described as beams supported by an inﬁnite series of
springs, however a solution has only been developed for springs with a linear relationship between
the pile deﬂection and reaction. This is not sufﬁcient to describe the behaviour of soil properly and

hence a non-linear description of the spring, such as the p-y curve is needed.
Fundamentally p-y curves is a set of curves describing the deﬂection of the pile (y) versus the re-
sistance of the surrounding soil (p). An illustration of the p-y curve concept can be seen in Figure
2.3.
Lead predominantly by the oil and gas industry attempts have been made to produce expressions
usable for predicting p-y curves under different conditions. To research this, different methods have
been applied for example full scale ﬁeld tests, numerical modelling and also centrifuge modelling.

2.5
Laterally loaded piles in centrifuge modelling
As a part of better understanding the behaviour large diameter monopiles used for offshore wind
turbines under lateral loading, centrifuge modelling has been widely used.
At DTU this has also earlier been a research topic.
Monopiles in soft clay have been researched by Klinkvort et al. (2016) [10] based on the experimental
work of Poder (2015) [15] investigating the inﬂuence of stress level and preconsolidation stress. The
study found that the ultimate load resistance in design codes is underestimated at shallow depths, but
overestimated at greater depth.
Monopiles in sand have been researched among others by (Klinkvort and Hededal (2014) [11] with
focus on change in stress levels and load eccentricity. The study proposed an updated model to
describe the behaviour of the soil-pile interaction.
Of other researchers who have dealt with monopiles is Jeanjean (2009) who studied monopiles with
a combination of centrifuge modelling and FEM [9]. He proposed a new expression for generating
p-y curves, however this has shown to overestimate the soil resistance.
To fully understand the behaviour of laterally loaded monopiles a lot of different parameters need to
be considered. For example this could be: Stress level, type of loading, load eccentricity and pile
ﬂexibility.
Scaling down a monopile has certain physical limitations, which means the model tends to be more
rigid than the full scale prototype. To investigate the effect of this, tests will be carried out with a
13
==PAGE:19==
pile made of aluminium which has a lower modulus of elasticity than steel, and hence has a relative
ﬂexibility that closer resembles that of a full scale pile.

3.1
DTU geotechnical centrifuge
The geotechnical centrifuge at DTU is a beam centrifuge built in 1976 [6] and has a capacity of
approximately 80 gton, meaning a load of 1 ton can be accelerated to 80 g. A picture of the centrifuge
at rest can be seen in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Geotechnical centrifuge at DTU.

of the arm the counterweight is placed.
Centrifuge instrumentation
The centrifuge is equipped with two actuators. One capable of moving the frame horizontally and
one capable of moving the pile vertically.
The horizontal movement is monitored by two LVDTs (Linear Variable Differential Transducers)
and the vertical movement is monitored by a single LVDT. The vertical force during installation is
recorded by a load cell. The lateral loading is deformation controlled and therefore the horizontal
load is not measured. The horizontal load is instead back calculated from the moment obtained from
the strain gauges.
Two laser LVDT are measuring the displacement at the top of the pile 20 mm apart during the lateral
loading. These measurements are used to calculate the rotation of the pile to be used as a boundary
condition for determining the rotation and displacement of the whole pile.

Clay sample preparation
Mixing
The clay used for the experiments is Speswhite kaolin clay. The clay powder is mixed with water
so it ends up with water content of approximately 100 %. The exception to this was the ﬁrst clay
sample which was mixed with a water content of 120 %, however after the consolidation was ended
the sample height was too low to achieve the desired embedment depth of the pile and thus the water
content was lowered to approximately 100 %. It is recommended to mix the clay at atleast 120 %
of the liquid limit [13]. The kaolin clay has a liquid limit if 56 % so mixing at 100 % water content
is sufﬁcient. Furthermore the clay gets easier to handle and remove from the mixer at higher water
contents.
The clay is mixed in the clay mixer shown in Figure 3.4(a) for around 8 hours. A vacuum pump
(Figure 3.4(b)) is connected to the mixer to lower the air pressure during mixing to minimise the air
content in the clay. Due to limitations of the vacuum pump and leakages in the mixer the mixing is
done at an air pressure around 30 kPa. After the mixing is done the clay is removed from the mixer
through the opening in the bottom of the mixer and moved from the mixer to the barrel in a bucket.

3.5
Pile installation
Installation in clay
Before the installation of the pile, the clay sample consolidates in the centrifuge at the desired accel-
eration, to allow excess pore pressure to dissipate. The time of this consolidation is roughly equal to
the time the clay sample has been unloaded when moving the sample from the press to the centrifuge;
this is usually 3.5 to 4 hours.
The pile is installed at a rate of 1.0 mm/s at the desired acceleration. During the installation the
installation force and vertical displacement is measured and recorded.
After the installation of the pile the sample is left in the centrifuge for a half to one hour to let the
pore pressure generated from the pile installation dissipate.
Installation in sand
The pile is installed at a rate of 1.0 mm/s. The sand samples was installed at 62.5 g until the capacity
of the actuator was reached, which is approximately 25 kN. When this occurred either the g level was
reduced and the rest of the pile was installed.
3.6
Lateral load test
The lateral loading of the pile is deformation controlled. The load is applied by the tower moving
horizontally with a velocity of 0.133 mm/s. The frame is moved 30 mm, corresponding to 10-15 mm
displacement at the soil surface. The lateral movement should be enough to ensure that the ultimate
capacity of the soil is reached.

the lateral loading was stopped early as the pile yielded.
6.2
p-y curves: Clay
To compare the p-y curves obtained from the clay test they have been normalised. The displacement
has been normalised with the pile diameter D and the pressure has been normalised with (cu · D).
Due to the uncertainties in the shear strength proﬁle, and to estimate the consequence of these, two
versions of the normalised p-y curves have been made for each test; one where the p-y curves are

of the Np exceeds the value recommended in DNV, but as the rotation depth is approached the value
of Np drops below the DNV recommendation. This is consistent with the ﬁndings in [10]. In Figure
6.7(a) the tests with the aluminium pile (5 & 6) is seen to generally have lower values of Np compared
to the tests with the steel pile (2 & 3), however the reason for this is uncertain.

7
Conclusion
In total nine tests with laterally loaded monopiles have been carried out at the geotechnical centrifuge
at DTU. Six tests were done in soft kaolin clay and three tests were done in dense sand. The effects
of embedment depth, pile stiffness and preconsolidation stress were investigated. From the tests the
following can be concluded.
Both the steel pile and the aluminium pile showed rigid behaviour when tested in clay. In the sand
tests the steel pile show a less rigid behaviour than in the clay and the aluminium pile showed ﬂex-
ible behaviour as the yield moment in the pile was exceeded before the ultimate soil resistance was
reached.
Comparing the effect of the pile stiffness it was seen that the normalised soil resistance in general
was lower for the aluminium pile even though no signiﬁcant difference was expected when the pile
was tested in clay. In sand no usable results were obtained, but it can concluded the aluminium pile
was too weak to be tested in dense sand at the given stress level.
No difference in normalised ultimate soil resistance was expected for the samples with varying pre-
consolidation stresses, however a tendency to higher soil resistance with higher preconsolidation
stress was seen. For the tests that had a lower embedment depth, in general a lower soil resistance
was seen.
Between the tests in sand there was a difference in embedment depth of 1D and a small difference
between the relative densities, however no signiﬁcant differences in the behaviour was noticed.
The normalised p-y curves from the tests were compared to the recommended p-y curves in the design
code DNV-OS-J101. In general it was observed that the DNV recommendations were conservative
compared to the test results and probably even more so than what was expected based on other studies.
Only one test in clay showed reasonably agreement with the design code. The underestimation of the
soil resistance by the code was seen in both clay and sand, however is was more pronounced in sand.
To further investigate this behaviour, the ultimate resistance coefﬁcient Np observed in the tests was
compared to the DNV recommended Np down to the depth of the rotation point. It was seen that the
ultimate resistance factor was underestimated until a depth of around half of the rotation and below
this depth it was overestimated. This is consistent with earlier studies. Furthermore it was noticed
that the Np values were lower in the tests with the aluminium pile.
Finally, judging from the installation forces the clay samples showed signs of inhomogeneities, and
the cu proﬁles back calculated from the installation forces did not match the cu proﬁles predicted by
the SHANSEP calibration which previously have shown good results for kaolin clay. The interpre-
tation of the test results has to some degree been impacted by the uncertainties in the shear strength
proﬁle.
