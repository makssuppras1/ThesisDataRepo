Abstract 
In this Project, an autonomous fryer model has been developed, using two Universal Robots 
UR3 collaborative robots and RG2 grippers from OnRobot. The motivation behind this 
project starts from common inefficiencies observed in fast-food restaurants, particularly 
during peak hours when service is slower than expected. Additionally, employees often 
report fatigue and burnout from performing repetitive tasks. Collaborative robots (Cobots) 
offers a solution, as they are compact, capable of working alongside humans, and offer an 
economically viable alternative for automation. 
Through a detailed analysis of the repetitive tasks in fast-food restaurant kitchens and 
interviewing industry professionals, frying was identified as the most suitable task for 
automation. Market research indicates that the largest sector for this technology is burgers 
and sandwiches, where fried food is consistently involved. The developed system allows 
the robot to receive orders and begin cooking immediately: it picks up a basket, dispenses 
food from the fridge, places the basket in the oil, and begins the cooking process. Once the 
food is cooked, the robot drains it and places it on the slider, where the food is kept warm 
under heat lamps until served. 
This system is scalable, thanks to the flexibility of collaborative robots, making it suitable 
for multiple establishments. The solution is also economically viable, with the return on 
investment (ROI) depending on the volume of work the robot performs. ROI ranges from one 
to five years, depending on the extent of task automation. The last goal was to determine if 
this business can be viable to do a startup and the result is that profitability is achieved after 
the sixth year, as demonstrated by the financial simulation conducted as part of the 
business plan.

1. Introduction 
Automation and robotics have revolutionized heavy industry, ushering in the era of Industry 
4.0 through digital transformation, smart machines, and interconnected systems [1]. Yet in 
fast-food and quick-service restaurant (QSR) kitchens—environments defined by rapid, 
repetitive tasks and tight footprints—automation remains remarkably underutilized. Many 
repetitive tasks in the kitchen can be completed by autonomous system guaranteeing 
constant quality.  
Collaborative robots (Cobots) offer a compelling solution: lightweight, flexible systems 
designed to work safely alongside humans, enhancing consistency, speed, and ergonomics 
[6]. However, integrating Cobots into QSR settings presents unique challenges: limited 
kitchen space, the need for smooth human–robot interactions, technical reliability 
concerns, and questions of economic viability for restaurant operators. 
In the food sector, automation has primarily been applied to processes such as quality 
control and packaging. While automation is used in the initial stages of the food production 
chain, it has yet to be effectively implemented in the final stages of food preparation. This 
gap in the industry presents a unique opportunity, which is the aim of this project, 
developing automation solutions in the last phase of the food chain.  
The final phase of the food production chain refers to the kitchen environment. Currently, 
the only forms of automation present in most kitchens are timers for the cooking process 
and automatic screens displaying new orders. At this stage, the food is generally near 
completion, such as potatoes being pre-cut and meat almost fully cooked. 
The key characteristics to success in the Fast food and quick service restaurants (QSR) are 
the following [7]: 
1. Speed and Efficiency: The ability to serve customers quickly, especially during peak 
hours, is the most important factor. Fast-food customers prioritize minimal waiting 
times [8].  
2. Consistency: Customers always expect the same quality, taste and experience [9].  
3. Cost and price: Competitive prices attract the target clients and retain price-
sensitive customers, particularly in a market with high competition [10]. 
4. Simplicity and innovation: Well-structured menus and offers with the integration of 
digital innovations, such as self-service kiosks, are becoming important.  
5. Brand and customer experience: A brand identity, clean and welcoming 
environment, can influence customer loyalty [11].  
6. Location: Visibility and accessibility are strategically crucial [12].  
Customers who go to a fast food are looking for speed, convenience, affordability and 
predictability rather than a memorable dining experience [13]. The most important feature 
in QSR establishments are the speed and efficiency. Improving these can directly improves 
customer satisfaction. This thesis focuses on optimizing efficiency and speed through 
automation. 
The role of the Cobot in this industry is one of the fields of study in this project. There are 
numerous possibilities, as Cobots are still not widely used in the market. However, this 
thesis will explore and identify the most profitable application for automation in the food 
industry.

The food industry is one of the sectors that consumes the most resources and generates 
significant waste. However, automation through collaborative robots (Cobots) can play a 
key role in significantly reducing food waste, improving energy efficiency, and optimizing 
kitchen processes. This approach aligns with the UN Sustainable Development Goals 
(Figure 1), particularly SDG 9: Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure, by promoting 
automation in a sector that is currently under-automated. Additionally, it supports SDG 12: 
Responsible Consumption and Production, as it emphasizes the use of existing equipment 
wherever possible and contributes to the reduction of food waste. Furthermore, it also 
supports SDG 13: Climate Action, by helping to reduce the carbon footprint of restaurants 
through more efficient processes.

1.1 
Problem and motivation 
How many times have you been in a restaurant and the food was overcooked or worst, the 
undercooked and not ready to serve? This is not the only problem that it can be solved.  How 
often have you waited too long for your meal to arrive? These delays and inconsistencies 
are often due to human errors, like forgetting to cook something, burning food, or 
mismanaging tasks during peak hours.  
The motivation behind the project is to reduce and fix these human errors through 
automation [14]. By integrating collaborative robots (Cobots) into kitchen environments to 
improve consistency, reduce workload, and optimize efficiency in food preparation.  
Working in a kitchen is difficult physically and mentally talking. Many roles involve long 
hours standing on foot and often doing the same few movements over and over again. As a 
result, employee retention is challenging. The turnover rate is high, with new staff constantly 
joining and leaving the team each month. Added to that, hiring is difficult, as shifts range 
from 3 to 8 hours of standing and performing the same task, or tasks with minimal variation 
[15]. For instance, in many restaurants, there are two cooks dedicated just frying and 
packing fries. This issue extends beyond fries, affecting other tasks such as grilling, tray 
preparation, and frying other items. The repetitive work leads to high employee turnover, 
burnout, and difficulty in hiring and retaining staff.  
Automating specific tasks with Cobots offers a variety of benefits. Focusing on the fast-food 
and quick-service restaurant (QSR) sector, these can be:  
1. Efficiency: Robots don’t have distractions. They can begin tasks the moment an 
order is received [8]. 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
2. Time savings: Orders are executed immediately after the payment, without the need 
to communicate or check a screen. 
3. Cost reduction: Higher productivity and efficiency reduce labor and food waste, 
improving profitability. 
4. Hygiene: Reducing human contact minimizes the risk of contamination [16]. 
5. Consistency: Cobots perform tasks with the same precision every time, without 
variation. No breaks are needed.  
6. Quality: Thanks to the consistency the quality is always the same high [2].  
7. Flexibility: Cobots are designed to work with humans, can be easily reprogramed for 
new tasks or improve the current workflow.  
8. Employee satisfaction: Robots can do tedious tasks, allowing workers to focus on 
more important or engaging tasks.  
Restaurant owners are experts in food or business, not in engineering or robotics, this 
should remain the same. For that reason, a company specifically dedicated to offering 
tailored automatizations could have many clients. Regardless, engineering companies face 
several challenges they need to overcome.  
One of the principal challenges in this niche is the integration with existing workflows to 
have the advantages of operators in shared space human-robot collaboration (HRC) [17]. 
Kitchen spaces are usually compact, designed to maximize dining areas. Another challenge 
is human-robot interaction. The curve of working with a robot is relatively easy but, every 
worker needs time to adapt to work in a safe way [18]. There’s also the issue of technical 
reliability [19], technology doesn’t always work as expected. Maintenance and support 
become essential [20], especially when one product depends fully on automation. 
Bearing in mind all these challenges to face, another consideration is the cost. Robots are 
expensive, and customizing an automation project takes time and resources. That’s why it 
is challenging to evaluate if the investment is economically viable, and if it can scale across 
different types of restaurants and kitchen layouts. 
1.2 
Problem statement 
The fast-food industry nowadays is heavily dependent on human labor for repetitive and 
physically demanding kitchen tasks such as frying, grilling, and tray preparation. These 
tasks contribute to high employee turnover, burnout, and operational inefficiencies, 
particularly in an industry already facing labor shortages and rising salary pressures. While 
collaborative robots (Cobots) offer a promising solution by combining precision, 
consistency, and safety in shared human-robot workspaces, their integration into fast-food 
kitchens remains rare due to technical, spatial, and economic constraints. The fast-food 
sector remains largely unexplored in terms of Cobots and intelligent automation, with 
limited academic research and very few companies currently operating in this niche. This 
gap presents a significant opportunity for innovation. 
This thesis addresses the problem of how to identify, evaluate, and prioritize the most viable 
tasks in fast food kitchens for automation via Cobots, from both a technical feasibility and 
business viability perspective. The principal goal is to determine whether a focused 
automation strategy can form the foundation for a scalable and profitable startup offering 
robotic kitchen automation solutions. The study further explores how such a company can 
structure its value proposition, market entry, and service model to align with the operational 
==PAGE:12==

realities of restaurant businesses. After that, the second goal is to design a prototype model 
as a first automation for the company.

2. Analysis 
In this section is going to be analysed the current state of robotic industry, along the market 
context, to finally identify the most appropriate automatization solution. The goal of this 
analysis it to determine the best product for the start-up.  
2.1 
Robotic technology context 
An initial review of contemporary robot types and automation solutions was conducted to 
provide the necessary technology context for the selection of the most suitable approach 
for the project. In evaluating potential automation strategies, three main options were 
considered: traditional industrial robots, collaborative robots (Cobots) and single-purpose 
automated systems. A summary of the main characteristics, advantages, and limitations of 
each option is presented in Table 2 to support the analysis. 
When most people think of a robotic arm, they typically imagine an industrial robot. These 
are usually large machines designed to operate at high speeds and with great precision, 
often handling heavy objects or working across large distances, as seen in automotive 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
manufacturing plants. Industrial robots are typically fixed installations within a workspace, 
performing repetitive tasks with consistent quality [24].   
However, the size and range of industrial robots also present certain challenges. While they 
are highly effective for tasks involving heavy lifting and high-speed operations, they are less 
suitable for applications requiring sensitive force control [25]. Their rigidity and power make 
them less capable of adapting to tasks where varying degrees of sensitivity are essential. 
Therefore, while industrial robots excel in speed, payload capacity, and range, they are not 
the ideal choice for delicate or precision-force tasks. 
In addition, extensive safety measures are required when deploying industrial robots in 
environments where humans are present. To ensure safe operation, robots are typically 
enclosed within safety cages or barriers, separating them from human workers [26]. The 
design, manufacturing, and integration of these safety systems significantly increase the 
complexity, time, and overall cost of implementing industrial robotic solutions. Finally, their 
purchase cost is relatively high, meaning the return on investment (ROI) is often realized 
only after several years of operation[27].  
Given these inherent limitations of industrial robots, collaborative robots, or Cobots, have 
emerged as a solution that emphasizes safety, flexibility, and ease of integration alongside 
human workers. Cobots are typically lighter, less powerful and designed with an emphasis 
on precision and sensitivity [28]. Their outstanding characteristic is their ability to work 
safely alongside human operators, often with minimal or no additional safety barriers. This 
is made possible through integrated force control and advanced sensor technologies, 
which enable Cobots to automatically slow down, stop, or adjust their movements when 
encountering an obstruction or detecting human presence nearby [29]. As a result, these 
characteristics expand the potential use cases of Cobots, making them suitable for a wider 
variety of applications that demand close human-robot interaction and adaptability. 
Finally, Cobots also offer greater simplicity and flexibility in many characteristics. 
Programming for example is much easier, the robot can be programmed while the user is 
physically guiding the robot through desired positions and saving the movements, without 
the need for traditional coding. These features make Cobots highly adaptable and 
accessible, particularly in dynamic production settings [25]. 
The concept of automation is one action that is completed automatically when some input 
conditions are achieved. However, within the scope of this project, the term "automation" 
refers specifically to a single automatic task [30]. A typical example is an automatic door: 
when a sensor detects a nearby presence, the system triggers the door to open. In this case, 
a sensor input is processed, and a predefined action is executed. This is how the basic 
structure of an automated process, which typically consists of a simple input-action 
sequence. 
Single-purpose automated systems operate on the same principle but are often more 
complex and industrialized. They are designed to perform one specific task with high speed, 
consistency, and reliability [31]. Examples include a bottling line that caps bottles, a 
conveyor that moves parts between stations, or a machine that welds a single type of joint 
[32]. These systems are optimized for efficiency but lack flexibility. When is built, they 
cannot easily adapt to new tasks without significant redesign. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
In contrast to robots, which offer programmable flexibility and a wide range of potential 
actions using the same mechanical structure, single-purpose automated systems are 
constrained by their fixed functionality. While they excel in repetitive, high-volume 
production settings, their inability to handle variability or task changes limits their 
applicability in dynamic environments. 
 
 
 
 
Figure 3. Cobot VS Industrial arm VS single automatization 
For a specific application under study, the use of collaborative robots emerged as the most 
appropriate solution. The tasks involved in food industry and restaurants require frequent 
adjustments, varying product types, and close human interaction, making the flexibility and 
safety features of Cobots essential. Unlike traditional industrial robots, Cobots can be 
integrated into existing workflows without major redesigns of the workspace and without 
extensive additional safety barriers. Additionally, compared to single-purpose automated 
systems, Cobots offer significantly greater adaptability. Considering these factors, Cobots 
offer a strategic advantage for the high productivity while maintaining a high level of 
adaptability and worker safety.

2.2 
Market analysis 
Starting a market analysis at the early stages of this project is essential to identify the most 
suitable sector for the application of collaborative robots (Cobots). A clear understanding 
of market size, growth trends, consumer behaviour, and operational needs helps align 
technological solutions with real business opportunities. By analysing the current 
landscape of the fast-food industry in Europe, we can strategically position the proposed 
automation solution where it can deliver maximum value, respond to industry demands, 
and ensure commercial viability. 
The fast-food industry in Europe represents a substantial and growing market opportunity. 
In 2021, the European fast-food market generated a revenue of approximately USD 144.6 
billion [33]. According to market forecasts, it is expected to grow at a Compound Annual 
Growth Rate (CAGR) of 4.7% from 2021 to 2028 [34] . As illustrated in Figure 4, the market 
shows a steady upward trajectory, with revenues projected to reach nearly USD 198.9 billion 
by 2028. This consistent growth indicates a strong and expanding market that offers an 
attractive entry point for innovative automation solutions. 
Several factors are driving the sustained growth of the fast-food industry in Europe [33]: 
1. Demand for Convenience: With a rising number of working professionals and 
increasingly busy lifestyles, the need for quick, ready-to-eat meals continues to 
expand. 
2. Menu Diversification: Fast-food chains are broadening their offerings to serve the 
different tastes and demands of costumers, including healthier, plant-based, and 
ethnic food options.  
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
3. Growth of Delivery Services: The popularity of food delivery platforms has surged, 
accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic. Quick Service Restaurants are increasingly

Despite the operational advantages of automating food preparation, such as reduced 
labour costs and improved consistency, there is considerable public resistance to this 
transition. Media and recent studies have documented a general discomfort regarding the 
idea of robot-prepared food [41]. This phenomenon is not unprecedented. Historically, the 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
introduction of profitable emerging technologies has often been met with societal 
resistance, as observed during the Second Industrial Revolution. 
In this context, a major societal concern arises regarding the future of workers displaced by 
automation. Additionally, beyond employment concerns, consumer attitudes are 
influenced by emotional factors. Research indicates that replacing human cooks with 
robots may increase feelings of discomfort among consumers, driven by the perception that 
automated food preparation diminishes the symbolic sense of love and human connection 
traditionally associated with food. 
In the referenced experiment, a group of 527 participants (51.6% male, with an average age 
of 38.5 years) was randomly assigned to one of three groups: the control group, the human-
prepared group, or the machine-prepared group. Participants in the control group were not 
informed about who had prepared the food, while participants in the other two groups were 
explicitly told whether a human or a robot had prepared their meal [14]. 
As expected, significantly fewer participants in the machine-prepared group believed the 
food had been made by humans (14%), compared to 97% in the human-prepared group. In 
the control group, 95% assumed the food had been prepared by humans, with no significant 
difference between the control and the human-prepared groups unless participants were 
explicitly informed. This indicates that when no information is provided, people naturally 
assume that food is made by humans. 
More importantly, the study found that the expected taste was significantly worse in the 
machine-prepared group compared to the human-prepared and control groups [42]. This 
suggests that negative feelings towards robot-cooked food could harm a company's 
reputation, even if the actual quality is similar or better.  
These results are consistent with the feedback collected during the interviews conducted 
for this project 
 Two professionals currently working in fast-food kitchens supported the use of robotic 
assistance, noting that it could help manage the high workload. However, they emphasized 
that robots should remain behind the scenes. Robots tend to draw customer attention, and 
employees might feel uncomfortable being constantly watched while working alongside 
them. 
Therefore, it is recommended that robots be visible only when the goal is to create a 
spectacle or enhance the customer experience, such as in tray preparation or drink 
dispensing. Otherwise, keeping the robots out of customers' sight, working inside the 
kitchen, may lead to better acceptance from both employees and customers.

SWOT Analysis 
A SWOT analysis is a strategic planning tool used to assess a company’s Strengths, 
Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats in relation to its external environment. This 
analysis helps organizations identify key factors that influence their performance, guiding 
decision-making and strategic planning by understanding both internal capabilities and 
external challenges. 
• 
Strengths: Cobot proposes an automatized solution for repetitive, mechanical, and 
standardized kitchen-related activities, that makes easier client procedures for a 
relative bigger productivity independently from the number of employees. This 
facilitates the preparation chain and enhances the business growth, for higher 
production rate and an easier scaling pathway. 
 
• 
Opportunities: The ready-to-eat fast food business are in are at the peak of their 
growth, both in number and variety. Occasionally this food chains don’t need 
trained staff for some timed and unidirectional protocols, what enwidens the Cobot 
market gap. 
 
==PAGE:36==

• 
Weaknesses: The start-up budget of the company, although highly profitable, can 
be high due to lack of market presence and therefore lack of confidence on the part 
of businesses. There could be a barrier due to lack of marketing and networking and 
due to the lack of existing market presence and therefore lack of trust from 
businesses. 
 
• 
Threats: The only threats in the market are direct competitors, but they can also 
come from the customers themselves, who may reject automated processes and 
lack of personal attention.

Competitors and differences 
Nowadays, numerous projects are focused on automating food preparation. However, this 
trend is not limited to food, several robotic systems specialize in the preparation of drinks, 
such as cocktails and coffee. Among these, coffee preparation robots represent the most 
widespread form of automation. A significant takeaway from the existence of competition 
in this sector is the confirmation of market demand. It is not necessary to be the market 
leader. Rather, it is sufficient to secure a market share that aligns with the company's 
objectives and scale. 
CafeX [54] for example, is a robotic arm that makes coffee like a human, as shown in Figure 
28 Another case is TrueBird [55], it is another coffee machine where the automated part is 
the dispensing process. It uses a magnetic slider to serve the cup once the coffee is ready. 
 
Figure 28. CafeX robotic arm [54] 
Some of the most innovative automation projects include, for example, a Paella cooking 
robot using a UR robot and a gripper from On Robot [56], developed at a school in Spain.  In 
addition, there are noodle making robots in China, as well as cooking chicken for poke 
bowls. Moreover, there are robots for burger assembly and flipping [37], as shown in Figure 
28.

The world is huge, and introducing innovation in a specific area can be challenging. In this 
context, there is a frying machine equipped with a robotic arm known as the "Flippy Fry 
Station." This technology, developed by Miso Robotics, is already in use by companies such 
as CaliBurger. This is the example shown in Figure 29, bottom right image. 
The promotional material used by Miso Robotics to market its automation technology 
highlights several key features. The new compact design uses up to 50% less space 
compared to the previous model. The system is capable of processing 100 baskets per hour, 
nearly double the productivity of a human worker. Additionally, it contributes to the 
reduction of food waste by enhancing speed and consistency relative to manual operations. 
A vision system is integrated to monitor the cooking process in real time. Furthermore, the 
company's website features a "Gross Monthly Value Per Location" calculator, designed to 
estimate potential savings and return on investment (ROI) [57].  
In Figure 30, The Miso Robotics arm can be observed frying food, in this case potatoes. The 
image illustrates the number of baskets, the design of the gripper, and the positioning of the 
robotic arm. The arm is mounted to the ceiling and incorporates a sliding mechanism to 
provide a wider range of movement.

One potential improvement to differentiate this design from existing competitors is to 
modify the mounting position of the robotic arm. The proposed design involves attaching 
the arm to the wall in front of the fryer, on the side opposite the human cook. This 
configuration facilitates collaboration, as the robot never obstructs the cook's access to the 
fryer baskets. 
Another observation is that the endpoint of the cooking line remains consistent. In other 
applications involving similar robotic systems, the robot typically places the basket aside 
rather than delivering the final product directly, like in the Figure 31 This configuration 
implies that the cooked food is always placed in a single fixed position. A potential 
improvement would involve implementing multiple output locations at different heights, 
each equipped with warming lights to maintain the food at the appropriate temperature. 
 
 
Figure 31. Flippy fry station basket endpoint 
The company behind the project specializes in automations rather than in manufacturing 
fryers or cooking equipment. Therefore, what would like to promote is re-use of existing 
kitchen infrastructure and equipment. This strategy reduces costs and also avoids the need 
to discard functional equipment. 
 As a collaborative environment, both the robot and the human must be able to handle the 
baskets easy. To achieve this objective, the baskets will be designed with two different grips:

one for the robot and other for the human. Moreover, a camera system will be implemented 
to detect the location of the baskets. This allows the robot to identify if a basked has been 
moved by a human or if its missing in the expected position. The vision system will also be 
used for process monitoring and tasks control.  
In the proposed automation design, the use of Teach Pendant is not required. Orders are 
directly linked to the robot, allowing the robotic arm to track every moment the status of 
each order in real time. This reduces both, time and tasks for the cooks.  
While the MISO automation uses a single arm with a slider to increase its operational range, 
the proposed design includes two smaller robotic arms. This allows to do more 
simultaneous tasks increasing the efficiency. Furthermore, using two little arms require less 
space due to their compact size.  
The last technical innovation is that the robot will be able to manage the oil changes. The 
robot will be capable to empty the used oil and refill with new one. This system will be 
integrated in the existing fryer and connected to the robot, which will monitor the usage time 
and trigger when the oil must be replaced.

4.2 
Custom design 
Throughout the report, some aspects of the robot’s design have been commented, 
highlighting the differences between the current design and those of competitors. In this 
section, an overview of the design and the reasoning between each decision will be 
explained.  
The principal component of the design is the fryer, which has been designed using the 
Frymaster LOV Electric [58]. This model is used in a lot of establishments and companies 
like McDonalds, demonstrating its reliability. The current configuration includes two fryer 
units, with a total of six frying racks. However, the configuration can be modified with less 
racks or a single, larger one depending on specific need. As many establishments already 
operate with multiple fryers, compatibility with these configurations is important for a start-
up aiming to adapt to QSR setups.  
The two fryers are used together with two UR3 collaborative robots from Universal Robots 
[59]. The specific model was chosen for several reasons: it is lightweight, compact, and 
highly reliable since it has been used in a wide range of applications. The UR3 is compatible 
with different grippers, available worldwide, and offers a long-term value for the company. 
Since it is one of the most used and established models on the market, its value is likely to 
decrease more slowly in the future. Added to that, Universal Robots is one of the most 
established manufacturers in the Cobots market, with extensive documentation, tutorials, 
community support, and an intuitive programming interface, both with the teach pendant 
or by typing code directly.  
To simulate a human hand and fingers, the RG2 gripper model from On Robots [60] was 
used as the termination of the robot. This model is fully compatible with Universal Robots, 
including UR3, and integrates plug-and-play through the URCap system, so no special 
adapters or wiring are needed. Keeping the robot clean and easy to maintain is essential, so 
avoiding wires around is an advantage. The RG2’s wide grip range allows it to handle objects 
of different sizes and shapes, while its adaptative force helps to be more flexible in the 
kitchen and future tasks. The RG2 is also integrated with the UR's programming environment 
and can be programmed easily with drag-and-drop programming to set positions, grip 
forces, and logic or with coding.  
Moreover, the vision system is also developed by Universal Robots. It was chosen because 
it is natively integrated with the UR robot controller using the URCap platform. This means 
there is no need for external drivers, extra wires, or complex setups. The combination of this 
components is shown in Figure 32. 
 
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
 
Figure 32. UR3 with RG2 and vision system mounted 
The advantages of using the camera in the arm (eye-in-hand system) give the robot a high 
degree of flexibility and coverage. The camera moves with the arm, so it can "look" from 
different angles and positions. Another important advantage is that with arm-mounted 
vision, the camera and the robot always share the same frame of reference, which simplifies 
setup and reduces errors, since an external camera requires precise calibration. And in 
terms of scalability for other customers, simply replicating the same robot arm vision unit 
is more than enough, rather than having to design and calibrate a fixed camera for each 
customer. The vision system is important because in a collaborative environment a human 
can change something and the robot needs to know. Also, the kitchen is not as precise as a 
factory, the baskets in the oil can move and the robot must always be able to pick them up. 
It can also detect wrong functions or strange things and make a notification to the staff to 
check the status of the process. The grippers and arms are covered with clothing to make 
the robot easier to clean, just change the clothing and put on a clean one. These have a hole 
for the camera is easy to change for the staff.  
The design has two UR3 robots, and this is for the high flexibility of the robot, one arm can 
help the other with some tasks. The main reason for using two is to increase efficiency, when 
one robot is filling one basket the other can empty another or do independent tasks. Also, 
the range is really wide with six racks is impossible to cover with one robot, there are other 
larger ones to come or add a sliding rail to gain more range. The sliding rail makes the 
process slower, with a longer arm the disadvantage is that it is less compact and costs 
almost the same as two small ones. The final design with two arms looks like human arms 
in the wall, doing tasks similar to what staff do in the kitchen.  
On one side of the fryers is the refrigerator, where the various fried products are stored until 
they are ready for cooking. This is divided into two different parts, the freezer and the 
refrigerator. The freezer is at the bottom and contains the products in a longer therm. It is 
close to the fridge for easy replenishment. The fridge, with three different sloping interior 
compartments, stores the food after the cooking process. The arm can interact with the 
fridge to sincronise the moment to automatically refill the basket and continue cooking the 
next round. When the fridge is empty, the robot will make a suggestion to the staff to refill it, 
which is really quick because it is to take the food from the freezer in the bottom and put it 
in the freezer in the top. This can be done from the side of two elements. 
 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
On the other side of the frying stations is the last place in the line, where the cooked food 
waits before being served. There are three different levels where the robot can empty the 
basket depending on the food that has been cooked. There is a warming system with warm 
lights to keep the food warm. This is important because nobody wants cold food. When the 
staff are finished with the tray, they go to the last part of the process and take the basket 
with the food in it, fill the order with the required amount of product and put the basket back 
in the warm zone.  
The basket design can be used by both the robot and the cook. It has two different handles, 
one for each side. The part where the baskets are and also the end zone is separated from 
the rest of the kitchen with a glass door, because with this increase the hygiene in the 
kitchen, less smoke from this part and re risk of damage to the staff reduces. At the bottom 
of the baskets, also at the bottom of the fryer, there are cupboards where the used and new 
oil is stored, ready to be used or recycled.  
The workflow of the automation starts when a new order is paid, then a cobot takes a basket 
and fills it with one of the products, while that robot turns on the power of the oil to start 
boiling. After that the cobot puts this basket in the boiling oil, after a while the same arm 
moves the basket to avoid the food to stick. When this time is up, the cobot takes the food 
out and if the robot arrives at the end point, it turns the food in the slider, but if the robot 
doesn't arrive, it passes the basket to the next arm to do it. 
This is how a standard automation works, but can have small changes depending on the 
customer's requirements.

4.3 
Safety 
Cobots are designed to work in close proximity to humans, but there are still a number of 
safety measures that need to be taken to ensure the safety of personnel and prevent 
damage to property. These robots are safer than industrial robots, but they are still 
machines and can cause damage if something goes wrong. Therefore, if a process goes 
wrong, the system must be equipped with hardware and software safety mechanisms to 
stop in unexpected situations, interruptions or failures.  
The robot has a large red emergency stop button to stop the cobot immediately, stopping 
the robot's movements but also shutting down the fryer. To restart after an emergency stop, 
the button must be pulled out and then the restart button must be pressed. The robot will 
start again from the home position, any unfinished task will be discarded as the robot will 
not retain its previous state, and the cooks will have to empty the baskets in progress. This 
should only happen if there is a major problem.  
Another important control button is the pause button, a smaller button that just stops the 
arm for a while. For example, if there is less food in a basket than expected, or the cooks 
want to adjust something manually, the robot can be paused without shutting down the 
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
whole system. To resume normal work, the operator simply presses the resume button and 
the workflow continues. 
The robot has a defined virtual limit that cannot be crossed to simulate walls. The program 
is adapted to the place where it is installed so as not to break anything. The good thing about 
the robot is that if it touches something, it stops immediately and a member of staff has to 
press the restart button. This function is called collision detection. Also related to this, the 
UR3 model has a force and torque limiter, which helps if, for example, the basket cannot be 
picked up for some reason. 
Another safety feature is the glass doors that separate the robot's workplace from the rest 
of the kitchen. The doors can be opened, if someone from the staff does it, the speed of 
both robots is reduced by 30%, so as not to cause any damage to the worker and to avoid 
stopping the process because the cobot has touched the cook. 
 
4.4 
Test Plan 
 
Whenever a design has to be tested, the design may have problems that can only be seen 
in the final product. The aim of the test plan is to validate the technical functionality, 
reliability, safety and integration of the collaborative robot (UR3 + RG2 gripper + vision 
system).  
Firstly, the basic things that the robot can do: picking up the basket, refilling the basket and 
throwing the food into the chute. After that, the fryer should be tested to see how long it 
takes to fry different foods. After these two, combining both, it can be tested the 
performance to know what is the real maximum capacity of the automation, how many 
baskets can the cobots do. After that, it should be tried with the maximum capacity for 
basket, the limit is the volume for each product but also the weight. In this test is not only 
for how many baskets, is also to know how much height of each food can be produced in an 
hour at maximum capacity. With this also measure the data to know the maximum capacity 
for a rack and basket, with that can be used for the robot to adapt depending on the usual 
volume of product.  
Another thing to test is the oil splash and how easy it is to clean, also whether the robot 
really needs clothing to be clean, or just something like a glove for just the gripper instead 
of the whole arm. This can be checked while the robot is doing the performance tests, so 
you can see how often the robot needs to be cleaned or clothes changed. 
The vision system needs to be tested and validated, the things to check are the correct 
basket detection and the anti-collision system. Another thing to check is the collaborative 
functionality, if a cook takes a basket out, the robot has to take that into account and stop

5. Conclusions  
The integration of collaborative robots (Cobots) into the fast-food industry has shown 
significant potential. This thesis explored the viability of Cobots in fast-food kitchens, an 
area where automation is currently underutilized. 
The project analysed various tasks and automation opportunities that can be executed by a 
Cobot in a kitchen of a fast food restaurant. After evaluating the options, the fryer station 
was selected as the optimal starting point for automation. Following this, the business 
model was developed to support this automation. The first goal, identifying the best 
automation task, was successfully completed with a well-defined process and using tools 
like the Market opportunity navigator. This step was essential for ensuring that the project 
addressed the right needs in the kitchen. 
The second goal was the design of the prototype. After analysis of competitors and 
considering the company’s motivations, the design was improved, leading to the decision 
to use a dual UR3 robot setup. This approach allows the two robots to assist each other, 
improving speed and overall efficiency. This solution enhances the process and ensures 
better functionality for the kitchen. 
With the proposed autonomous system, the kitchen staff no longer needs to focus on 
repetitive tasks, except in cases where an error is notified by the robots. This achievement 
allows the staff to engage in more valuable and less monotonous tasks, improving overall 
job satisfaction. Customer benefits also arise from this shift, as reducing repetitive work for 
the staff leads to less burnout, enhancing the quality of service and reducing turnover. 
While the design has not fully been simulated and tested, early projections suggest 
promising results. The system is expected to significantly improve kitchen workflow, 
ensuring better efficiency and eliminating delays between order payment and cooking 
initiation. Moreover, the system can operate full-time, offering continuous service and 
maximizing restaurant uptime. 
Regarding the financial aspect, the automation system is economically viable for the 
startup after the sixth year. For the customer, profitability will depend on factors such as the 
volume of work and the number of tasks the robot can perform. The return on investment 
(ROI) for the customer can range from one to five years, depending on how much labour the 
robot can collaborate.

6. Next Steps 
 
The Enhancement of Renderings and Simulations is one of the first areas for improvement. 
The current design requires significant computational resources, making it challenging to 
simulate effectively on standard equipment. The next step will be to optimize the design for 
better performance, reducing the processing power required for simulations. This will 
enable more efficient testing and validation during the design phase, ensuring the system 
can be refined and improved with less strain on resources. 
The Sales Strategy and Product Phases will be executed in two stages to appeal to a broader 
range of potential customers. Initially, a premium version of the product will be launched, 
featuring a high-end design with dual robotic arms and multiple trays. This approach mirrors 
strategies used by leading companies like Apple, where an advanced version of the product 
highlights its capabilities. Once the premium model gains traction in the market, a more 
affordable version with a single robotic arm and simplified features will be introduced. This 
version will be aimed at smaller establishments or those with more limited budgets, 
ensuring wider market adoption. 
One critical area of focus is Improving the Ergonomics of the Fridge and Dispensing System. 
Modifications will be made to enhance the ease of operation, enabling kitchen staff to 
interact with the system more efficiently and comfortably. This improvement will help 
streamline kitchen workflows, making the system integrate more seamlessly into daily 
restaurant operations and increasing overall user satisfaction. 
Another significant change involves Optimizing the Fryer Design. To reduce the system's 
overall footprint, the fryer will be re-engineered to be wider rather than elongated, especially 
if the design is done in-house. This adjustment will optimize space usage in the kitchen, 
making the system more adaptable to various kitchen layouts, thus increasing its 
practicality and versatility. 
In addition, the Enhancement of Fryer Capacity is a key improvement. Making the fryer 
deeper will allow for the simultaneous use of two baskets, increasing the cooking capacity 
of the system. This change will improve efficiency and throughput, which is especially 
valuable for high-volume operations in fast-food restaurants that require fast service and 
large quantities of food to be prepared quickly. 
Finally, the design of the Food Placement System will be optimized for better space 
utilization. By reorganizing the food storage and serving areas, the system will be able to 
accommodate more food in a more compact space, improving overall kitchen efficiency. 
This will also reduce potential delays in food preparation and ensure that the system 
operates at its full capacity without bottlenecks.

Appendix 1. Interviewing people in the sector 
As mentioned in section 1.4, Eugeni Beorlegui, owner of an automation company 
specialising in solutions for the bakery industry, was the first person interviewed, we had an 
online meeting and I presented my idea and we talked about many things. Firstly, he loved 
the idea, he is a big supporter of Cobots and automation, if a task is repetitive it can be 
automated. He really believes that this is the future and he supports his thoughts with some 
arguments.  
If you talk to people who don't have a business, they're surprised that he supports this idea. 
If you are in industry and you have to find people to work, you will see that it is really difficult 
to find someone to work. Many jobs are boring or repetitive but really necessary, they 
happen all the time in your life. But what happens when there are no people to work? From 
his point of view, people don't want to work. What happens in his field if he, or worse, his 
clients, can't find workers, we won't have bread to eat. Later he told me about a friend of his 
who owns some restaurants and in one of them he has to close the restaurant during the 
week because he can't find people willing to cook or serve during the week. So it's the other 
way round, the question is, people who don't have a job, are they looking for one?  
On the other hand, some companies are reluctant to use robots, but from his point of view 
they have clear advantages. A robot doesn't have a bad day, every day is the same, the 
quality doesn't change. This is very difficult for a human, everyone has a bad day or 
something to think about outside of work, or maybe a cold in the winter. Also, robots are 
tireless and can work all 3 rounds in a factory, it doesn't matter. The only cost you can have 
is the maintenance and maybe you have to doit some time before than when the robot 
works less. He explained me in what consist of this maintenance and we talked about the 
cost, and is about 3.000€ every year. Otherwise he explained that it is a good idea to have 
some robots as a backup. It is strange to have some problems in the robot, but not 
impossible, and he had some. Repairing a Cobot is a complex task due to the complexity of 
the mechanisms and enclosures. Instead, replacing the arm is really easy, you just have to 
change the Cobot and upload the programme from the previous robot and you can start 
working. An alternative to using a Cobot is a single automation, which is really useful and 
more economical, but there are two main disadvantages. These are, the complexity and the 
duration of the project than doing it with a robot.  
He started to end the meeting by telling me about the cost of a robot, a full automation, 
taking into account the programming, the installation, the materials and the Cobot, can 
cost around 30,000€. But he argued that this was the same or less cost to the company than 
a worker in a year. When you consider that this robot can work for decades, the ROI (return 
on investment) is really high. But wait, this doesn't mean that a worker will lose his job today. 
It means that the person doing a repetitive task can do other things that are more 
interesting, more complex or more important. 
In the last minutes of the interview he talked about the birth rate. He said that in Spain it is 
decreasing and this means that in the future there will be less people to work, but we will 
still have work to do.

Expert Talk with Juan Jesús García - ABB Robotics Engineer 
As a part of the research for this thesis, I had the pleasure to talk with Mr. Juan Jesús García, 
an engineer at ABB robotics. There, he programs collaborative robots for different projects. 
Upon presenting the idea of adding a collaborative robot in the kitchen of a fast-food 
restaurant, he expressed enthusiasm and interest on the idea. He pointed out that even ABB 
is involved in various advanced robotics projects (such as challenging projects for sewing 
and welding), none of them currently is related to food.  
During the discussion, he remarked that most of the time is a difficult challenge the use of 
the tools in connection with humans. If the tool is conventional for humans, then it is 
difficult for robots, and the same happens the other way around. The best solution is a tool 
for the robot like a robotic gripper, that can handle objects with polygonal geometries, which 
are easier to grasp than cylindrical/round shapes. He introduced me to the “Plug and work” 
technology from Match Zimmer, a new tool that enables the robot to change the tool 
automatically and by itself.  
 
Figure 36. Zimmer Tools [63] 
While highly relevant, we concluded that for the scope of this thesis a single gripper would 
be sufficient as this would help to make the tool switching an optional improvement rather 
than a necessity. Moreover, we discuss about adding a mobile base in the Cobot to achieve 
more working area and we were in the same side of the scale. Despite the interest of this, 
the complexity of this change would increase a lot for the entire project. If the robot can 
move the reference points that are different, then the coordinate system is more complex 
and changes if the robot position changes. Finally, we agreed that is better two arms than a 
mobile base for one arm, being the disadvantages the complexity for the coding and the 
mechanical design of the structure for the base. In the opposite way, the advantages of 
using two arms are the work capacity increases, the interaction between two arms are 
easier, and finally if one has some problem, the other is still working.  
==PAGE:69==
69 
 
 
Figure 37. IRB 910INV ABB [64] 
Juan also contemplates the possibility of using a simpler robot like the SCARA. This would 
be faster, but the disadvantage is the mobility as it only has three degrees of freedom 
instead of the six that the regular robotic arm has. For some functionalities it could be good, 
but it is not enough flexible for our proposes.  
At the end of our conversation, we discussed about the safety. As it is a collaborative robot 
you do not need a lot of safety, but you always shall have some. The first and the most 
important is the emergency stop button. This is very important, because if something goes 
wrong you shall be able to stop immediately the robot. And to complement this then it is 
important to have a restart button, play button, home button and could also be nice to have 
a pause button, because the difference between stop and pause is that before the pause 
the robot can continue working in the same routine, something that cannot be done with 
the stop button (after pressing it, the robot would have to start from the home position).  
For more security the robot shall be close with a key, when the restaurant is close the 
responsible of that turn shall remove the key to turn off the robot and the power system. 
Additionally, he proposed the use of a LiDAR sensor to define a virtual safety perimeter. If 
breached, the system can automatically reduce the robot’s speed and trigger an acoustic 
signal of alarm. However, the implementation of this feature would depend on the activity 
of the robot, the tool or other circumstances.  
Finaly, Juan Jesús García shared his view about the feasibility of the idea. Is available to do, 
even if it is challenging, he thinks that the compensation can be higher at the end, under the 
idea that the long-term benefits can justify the complexity. Also, he is of the idea that this 
tool is an incredible an improvement for the society. He also shared his view about whether 
the robots should be visible in public spaces, stating that it would depend on the consumer 
sector and other practical implications. While open kitchens may pose a challenge for this 
due to odors and smoke, from an engineering perspective he finds the idea of showcasing 
a robot in a restaurant very appealing.

Marta Sibecas 
Marta has several years of experience working in fast food restaurants. She spent two years 
at McDonald’s and has now been working for a year at Goiko, a popular burger chain with a 
slightly different approach to food service. Her experience across both types of restaurants 
gave her a useful perspective on how kitchen workflows operate and where automation 
could potentially be introduced. 
The conversation began with a breakdown of how roles are organized at McDonald’s, where 
the kitchen is clearly divided from the dining area. Inside the kitchen, staff are assigned to 
very specific roles: 
• 
Fried potatoes section – Responsible for all types of fries, such as regular and 
deluxe. 
• 
Flat-top grill section – Handles grilling of meats. 
• 
General fried foods section – Cooks items like chicken nuggets and wings, excluding 
potatoes. 
In addition to these core roles, there are several others involved in the assembly and 
preparation process. One person, often referred to as the pass person, monitors the screen 
for new orders and gets the correct packaging ready (e.g., burger boxes or wrappers). The 
next team member prepares the bread and adds toppings like lettuce or onions. After that, 
another person adds the grilled meat or chicken, and a final person wraps the burger or 
places it in a box. From there, the order moves to the tray assembly section, where two staff 
members complete the tray and hand it to the customer. 
The assembly line setup at McDonald’s allows for quick service because many ingredients, 
such as patties, are pre-cooked and kept warm. The grill team is responsible for keeping the 
warming trays stocked. This system allows orders to be completed quickly, but it also 
creates waste; if too much food is prepared in advance and not used, it must be thrown 
away. 
At Goiko, the workflow is different. While the restaurant also serves burgers, it operates 
more like a casual dining restaurant than a traditional fast food place. Orders are prepared 
fresh, and there is a waitstaff team to serve customers at the table. Still, there are 
similarities in the cooking process, particularly when it comes to burger preparation. 
She pointed out that working in this kind of environment isn't for everyone. It requires a 
genuine interest in hospitality and being comfortable working with people face to face. 
Compared to McDonald’s, Goiko has lower staff turnover, likely because the work is less 
repetitive. At McDonald’s, by contrast, the tasks can become monotonous—standing in one 
spot for hours, repeating the same motions—which often leads to burnout. 
In her view, this is exactly where automation could be helpful. Repetitive tasks, especially 
in high-volume fast food settings, could be handled by machines to reduce pressure on 
staff. This wouldn’t only improve working conditions but could also help restaurants 
address staffing shortages and optimize workflows to reduce waste and speed up service. 
She concluded the conversation by expressing a personal opinion: automation in 
restaurants that want to avoid the fast-food image should be handled carefully. Many 
customers prefer to feel that their food was prepared by a person who cares about the 
quality and the dining experience. For these restaurants, hidden automation might be a 
==PAGE:71==
71 
 
better solution, for example, a robot fryer that handles one specific task in the background. 
However, in fast food environments, where the pace is intense and employees are often 
overworked, automation makes a lot more sense. It could allow staff to shift toward more 
engaging roles, such as interacting with customers and improving service, rather than being 
stuck doing repetitive tasks all day.

Carlota Lara  
The team leader of the Viena restaurant in Sabadell, an experienced professional in the food 
service industry, provided valuable insight into the potential of automation in fast food 
kitchens. When presented with the project concept, she responded with strong interest and 
expressed that the idea was both innovative and highly relevant to the current challenges 
faced in the sector. 
She explained that during peak hours, kitchen operations can become overwhelming, and 
any form of additional support, especially through automation, could significantly improve 
efficiency. Some restaurants, she noted, have already begun integrating small automated 
systems. For instance, flat-top grills with built-in timers allow cooks to manage multiple 
tasks at once while ensuring food is not overcooked. However, the idea of more advanced 
automation was seen as a promising next step. 
One proposed concept involved using a robotic arm to place meat on the grill, optimize the 
use of available space, and control the cooking time for each piece individually. This level 
of automation could reduce the workload, improve food consistency, and free up staff for 
other critical tasks. 
Another practical application discussed was the automation of tray assembly. In the current 
system, once a sandwich is completed, it is placed on a tray labeled with its type. A staff 
member then organizes trays according to the customer's order. This process could be 
handled by a robotic arm, combined with a screen and speaker system that signals when a 
tray is ready. Such a setup could streamline workflow and reduce human error. 
The discussion also included the possibility of automating part of the sandwich preparation 
process. In this scenario, a robotic arm would place ingredients between the bread slices, 
allowing a human cook to finish the process by grilling or wrapping. However, it was 
emphasized that integration must be smooth and supportive of existing kitchen routines. In 
environments where everything needs to go in a fast pace, any disruption to established 
workflows could create delays or operational issues. 
A key point raised was the importance of connectivity. A robotic system connected to the 
restaurant's local network could receive order data in real time and begin tasks 
automatically as new orders appear on the kitchen screen. This would allow the robotic 
assistant, or "Cobot", to act in coordination with the rest of the team. 
Another idea considered was the use of robots for clearing and cleaning tables. However, 
while potentially helpful, this would require advanced mobility, which means that it is a 
more complex challenge compared to other tasks mentioned before like tray display or 
sandwich preparation. 
In conclusion, Carlota expressed a positive view of automation in fast food service. She saw 
it as a beneficial tool for both workers and customers, improving speed and accuracy while 
reducing pressure on staff. However, she also stressed the continued importance of human 
presence, particularly in roles where the staff needs to physically face the customer, 
because human interaction remains a vital part of the dining experience, helping customers 
feel more welcome and satisfied.

Albert Masip  
This conversation took place with a PhD researcher from the Universitat Politècnica de 
Catalunya (UPC), who shared thoughts on how realistic and practical it is to bring robotics 
into a restaurant kitchen. The discussion started with an overview of the project idea and 
gradually moved into the different possibilities and challenges involved. 
Two possible scenarios were considered. On one side, the most advanced option would 
involve a collaborative robotic arm that can move freely around the kitchen, using a vision 
system to recognize objects and key areas to carry out different tasks alongside the kitchen 
staff. This idea was described as highly complex, possibly unrealistic, unless backed by a 
large budget to cover the cost of development and components. 
On the opposite end, a much simpler and more achievable approach would be to automate 
a single, repetitive task, such as flipping burgers or placing fries into the fryer. This type of 
focused automation is more likely to be workable. 
One important technical issue mentioned was the kitchen environment itself. The 
combination of smoke, heat, fast movement, and noise makes it a difficult place for robots 
to work reliably. All of these factors can interfere with sensors, reduce accuracy, and 
complicate programming. From his point of view, this makes robotic integration extremely 
difficult, though handling one specific task might still be realistic. 
Another point that came up was restaurant design. In general, owners want to keep the 
kitchen as small as possible in order to maximize dining space. If adding a robot means 
needing more space in the kitchen, it might not be worth the trade-off, especially if it 
reduces the number of tables available for customers. 
He also brought up examples of how robots are already being used in some restaurants, for 
instance, as servers that bring food to the tables. This is especially common in buffet-style 
or “all-you-can-eat” restaurants where people order repeatedly. In these cases, a robot that 
prepares trays could work well. Still, as mentioned in earlier conversations, this kind of 
setup would need supervision. Some customers are skeptical of automation and might 
blame the robot for mistakes, even when the process is working correctly. 
Finally, visibility was another important consideration. The researcher suggested that 
unless it’s intentional and part of the customer experience, the robot should stay behind the 
scenes. Most people don’t like seeing machines prepare their food, especially if it looks too 
mechanical or clinical. For that reason, robots should be used in a way that doesn’t make 
customers feel uncomfortable, unless the restaurant is designed to make the technology a 
visible feature.
