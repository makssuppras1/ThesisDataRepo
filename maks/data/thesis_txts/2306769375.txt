Summary (English)
A ﬂap actuation system, the Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF), for distributed load
control on a wind turbine blade had been developed in the period from 2006 to 2013 at DTU. The
purpose of the project is to optimize the structural design of the ﬂexible part of the CRTEF based on a
realistic blade section geometry in order to meet the objectives and the constraints of the project. The
objectives include the deﬂection requirements and the energy eﬃciency, while the constraints include
the bending stiﬀness of the structure, the local shape deformations, critical material strength, and
manufacturing limitations.
With the inspiration from the literature review, an arch model with arches forming concave on the
ﬂap surface and enclosing the voids to be pressurized is supposed to result in the bending movement of
the ﬂap when pressure is applied on the voids to straighten the arches. The model was designed using
SolidWorks for the parameterization of the design and ANSYS Workbench for the static structural
Finite Element Analysis (FEA) simulations. The built-in parametric optimizer of ANSYS Workbench,
Direct Optimization of Design Exploration, was used to optimize the design with the parameters of
the geometry. The surface pressure loads during operation of the turbine with the ﬂap installed were
evaluated with XFOIL and included in the simulations.
The model was developed ﬁrst by qualitative analyses to have a reasonable preliminary design, and
then by parametric optimization to have the ﬁnal design. The parameterization of the design was
improved on the way of optimizations, in order to expand the design space to solve the problem of
stress concentration, so that it covers the design with the acceptable material safety factor.
With the consideration of surface pressure loads during operation of the turbine, the optimum design
fulﬁlls the requirements for ﬂap angle of 15◦and -15◦with the actuation pressure of 0.428 MPa and
0.386 MPa, and the material safety factor of 1.58 and 1.71, respectively. The design also meets the
objective for energy eﬃciency by the lower actuation pressure than in the previous project and by the
small volume of the voids. Besides, the constraint of the bending stiﬀness is fulﬁlled with the deﬂection
of less than the ﬂap angle of ±5◦when the turbine is operating without the actuation pressure, and
the constraints of the local shape deformations and manufacturing limitations are also fulﬁlled. With
the results of the thesis, the model could be manufactured and tested ﬁrst in the laboratory and then
on the wind turbine.
In conclusion, the results of the thesis contribute a robust design in the area of smart rotor with
signiﬁcantly better eﬀect and eﬃciency.

Summary (Danish)
Flapsystemet, “the Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF)”, for distribueret lastkontrol på
en vindmøllevinge, blev I perioden 2006 til 2013 udviklet på DTU. Formålet med det aktuelle projekt
er at optimere det strukturelle design af den ﬂeksible del af CRTEF bagkantesﬂappen for en realistisk
geometri af en vingesektion og derigennem opfylde mål og begrænsninger for designopgaven. Målene
omfatter maximum udbøjning og energieﬀektivitet medens designrammerne er på minimum bøjnings-
stivhed, lokale overﬂadedeformationer, kritisk materialestyrke og mulighed for fremstilling. Baseret på
inspiration fra en litteraturgennemgang forventes det, at en model med konkav overﬂade og med et
tilsvarende konkavt hulrum under overﬂaden, vil give en bevægelse af ﬂappen, når det konkave hul-
rum tryksættes og derved retter sig ud. En sådan model er designet i SolidWorks og parameteriseret.
ANSYS Workbench anvendes til statiske Finite Element Analysis (FEA) strukturberegninger. Den
indbyggede ”optimizer” i Ansys Workbench, “Direct Optimization of Design Exploration”, er benyttet
til at optimere designet på basis af de valgte geometriparametre. Overﬂadebelastningen på ﬂappen
under drift af vindmøllen er beregnet med XFOIL og inkluderet i simuleringerne. Et foreløbigt design
blev først udviklet på basis af kvalitative analyser og derefter med optimering af designvariablene
for at nå til det endelige design. Parameteriseringen blev forbedret under optimeringsprocessen for
at kunne udvide designrummet, således at problemer med belastningskoncentrationer blev løst, og
materialebelastningen blev holdt under et acceptabelt niveau inkluderet en acceptabel materialesik-
kerhedsfaktor. Med hensyn til belastning fra overﬂadetrykket under drift opfylder designet kravet om
en ﬂapudbøjningsvinkel på +15, -15 deg. med et aktueringstryk på henholdsvis 0.428 MPa og 0.386
MPa og en materialesikkerhedsfaktor på henholdsvis 1.58 og 1.71. Designet opfylder også målet for
energieﬀektivitet ved at aktueringstrykket er mindre end trykket anvendt i et design i et tidligere
projekt og ved at volumen på hulrummene er mindre. Udover at opfylde kravet om, at udbøjningen af
ﬂappen skal være mindre end +- 5 deg. når møllen er i drift, og der ikke er aktueringstryk, er kravene
til minimum deformation af overﬂaden på ﬂappen også opfyldt og ligeledes fremstillingskravene. Med
resultatet fra denne thesis kan modellen fremstilles, testes i laboratoriet og senere på en vindmølle.
Endelig kan det konkluderes at resultaterne fra denne thesis udgør et betydeligt fremskridt indenfor
SMART rotorteknologien med et design, der er mere robust og eﬀektivt end tidligere designs.

Preface
As a thesis of European Wind Energy Master program, track of Rotor Design, the thesis was mainly
prepared at DTU Wind Energy in fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring an M.Sc. at DTU Wind
Energy and an M.Sc. at TU Delft Aerospace Engineering. The work of the thesis has been supervised
by Dr. Helge Aagaard Madsen, Dr. Athanasios Barlas, Dr. Mathias Stolpe at DTU Wind Energy
and Dr. Mostafa Abdalla t Delft Aerospace Engineering.
The thesis deals with the optimization of the structural design of a robust, eﬀective and eﬃcient
controllable rubber trailing edge ﬂap actuation mechanism. It deals with the literature review of the
relative area and the implementation of parametric optimization using ANSYS 16.0 Workbench as the
optimizer and the static structural ﬁnite element analysis tool for a component with hyperelastic rubber
as the material, coupled with SolidWorks as the design tool. The thesis consists of the determination
of the load cases during operation of the turbine installed with the ﬂap, the qualitative analysis of the
parameters of the design, the parametric optimization of the design, and the solution to the problem
of stress concentration of the design.
Risø, 21-June-2016

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The size of wind turbines has increased steadily in the past decade. With a bigger rotor, the loads
carried by all the components of a wind turbine have become an issue and consequently cost. Since the
lower cost of energy per kWh is desired, reducing the loads on the blades is certainly one of the main
focus of the research on wind turbines. One of the possible ways to reduce the loads without increasing
the cost too much is to build blades with distributed local control surfaces and this concept is usually
called ’smart rotor control’. Flaps, as one kind of the trailing edge aerodynamic devices, can serve
as the control surface to reduce the loads on the blades [1]. A controllable ﬂap can be actuated by
diﬀerent mechanisms. However, the strongest speciﬁcations for a robust control mechanism required by
the wind turbine industry indicate no mechanical, metal and electrical parts in the blades. Therefore,
the Controllable Rubber Trailing Edge Flap (CRTEF) has been designed, manufactured, and tested
at Risø DTU in order to meet the requirements and realize the application in the industry of wind
turbines [2]. The CRTEF controls the ﬂap by applying ﬂuid or air pressure on the voids in the rubber
ﬂap, and the geometry change of the voids results in the eﬀect of the ﬂap. Therefore, the geometry
and distribution of the voids play important roles in the eﬀect and eﬃciency of the ﬂap mechanism.
The project is therefore focused on the optimization of the geometry and the distribution of the voids
so that the desired ﬂap angle can be met under several constraints.
1.2
Relevance of the project
As mentioned in Section 1.1, the locally distributed load control systems of wind turbines could
eﬀectively reduce the loads on the wind turbines [1]. This concept is usually referred as ’smart rotor
control’. Variable trailing edge, also know as ﬂap control, as one of the ways to realize the concept has
been considered as a promising idea, and the potential is shown [3]. Buhl et al. showed that "when
the airfoil with a deﬂectable trailing edge experienced a wind step from 10 to 12 m/s, the standard
deviation of the normal force could be reduced by up to 85% −95%" depending on the input signal of
the control system. Buhl et al. also showed that "when the airfoil experienced a turbulent wind ﬁeld,
the standard deviation of the normal force could be reduced by 75% −81%" depending on the input
signal [3]. Besides, a design and testing of a ﬂap control system was carried out, showing that the
ﬂap from −10◦to +10◦changed the blade section lift coeﬃcient by 1.0 in non-stalled conditions [4].
However, this kind of actuation system might not be robust enough for wind turbines, since it does
not meet the robust requirements of: 1) no mechanical parts; 2) no metal parts and 3) no electronics
in the blade [2]. By comparison, the actuation system of CRTEF would be more ideal since it meets
the robust requirements. The performance of the concept of CRTEF was evaluated with a rotating
outdoor rig test, and it could be shortly summarized to give the change in lift equivalent to 1◦pitch
by having 3◦of ﬂap angle [5].

has to be optimized. Instead of basing on a symmetrical airfoil, the project is aimed at designing for
the DTU 10MW reference turbine, and therefore FFA-W3-241 is the airfoil evaluated [6]. Due to the
feature of the extrusion process which is expected to be used in manufacturing, the project is focused
on analyzing the 2D geometry of the ﬂap with a span-wise constant cross section. The size of the chord
to be analyzed is deﬁned by the chord length of the DTU 10MW reference turbine at 80% rotor radius
from the rotor center based on the work of Andersen et al. [7], which states that the ﬂap being placed
at around this span-wise location gives the optimum reduction in the blade root moment fatigue load.
The ratio of the chord to be replaced with the rubber ﬂap is determined to be 10% so that the ﬂap
is able to change the lift suﬃciently to attenuate a big portion of the expected load ﬂuctuation with
insigniﬁcant drag penalty [8]. Based on all the knowledge, the outcome of the project is expected to
contribute a more eﬀective, eﬃcient and robust model to the development of the smart rotor area.

Literature review
The literature review section is composed of four sections. The literature relevant to structural op-

for both high and low bending stiﬀness of the ﬂap, the mechanism of pressure-actuated cellular struc-

summarized in Section 2.4.
2.1
Conﬂicting structural stiﬀness requirements
There is a lot of literature covering diﬀerent concepts of actuators for ﬂap control. The biggest common
challenge of the material and the structure in this application is the conﬂict between the requirement
for low bending stiﬀness of the ﬂap which leads to low actuation force and the requirement for high
bending stiﬀness of the ﬂap which is relevant to resisting the surface pressure loads during operation
[9].
There are two concepts to resolve this issue. One is to actuate the ﬂap by changing the length of
the skins.
In this way, the bending stiﬀness of the ﬂap is a combination of the in-plane stiﬀness
of the skins which corresponds to the the actuation force and the out-of-plane stiﬀness of the skins
which corresponds to the stiﬀness to resist the surface loads, and the problem might be solved by
using anisotropic materials for the skins. One example of this method is using honeycomb for the
core, carbon ﬁber reinforced plastic for the upper skin and silicone for the lower skin as presented by
Daynes and Weaver [4], but this requires complex actuators embedded in blades which is not robust
enough for the strict speciﬁcations of the industry which mean no mechanical, metal and electrical
parts in the blades.
The other concept to resolve the conﬂict is simply trying to balance the two requirements. In order
to have low actuation force, elastomers are considered to be a suitable material. However, elastomers
are indicated to be not stiﬀenough [9, 10, 11].
Although it is suggested that a combination of
woven materials and rubbers could possibly solve the problem [11], using this kind of ﬁber reinforced
elastomers is not an option in this project due to the designated manufacturing process. Nevertheless,
the structure can be reinforced by simply putting a composite laminate along the chord as shown in
Figure 2.1 which is the previous work of CRTEF [2].
Inspired by the two concepts mentioned, if the balance between the two conﬂicting requirements cannot
be achieved with only rubber as the material, a possible solution for the project might be implementing
an anisotropic composite laminate, instead of along the chord line, as either the upper or the lower
skin.

2.2
Mechanism of pressure-actuated cellular structures
In order to investigate more concepts of ﬂap control and pneumatic actuators, the related literature in
aerospace area is also reviewed. Since the ﬂap actuators to be installed on aircraft are more accessible
for maintenance, they do not need to be as robust as the ones to be installed on wind turbines, and
therefore most of the concepts of the ﬂap actuators studied in aerospace area [12, 13, 14, 15] do not
meet the requirements of wind turbine industry: no mechanical, metal and electrical parts. These
concepts do not give much relevant information for the project due to the big diﬀerence between the
types of actuators.
However, there are still some concepts related to pneumatic actuators can be found. A novel concept
inspired by the nastic movements of plants for morphing structures was proposed by Pagitz et al. [16].
In the work of Pagitz et al., prismatic cells with tailored pentagonal and/or hexagonal cross sections
are connected such that a target shape of the structure can be formed with the corresponding cell
pressure. This concept is somewhat similar to the concept of CRTEF, but there are a few diﬀerent
points making it not directly applicable to CRTEF. First, instead of making voids in a solid as
CRTEF, the structure is built by connecting a big amount of small cells with thin walls, and this is
not preferred in the manufacturing process of CRTEF. Second, in the model, the walls are assumed
to be rigid and connected by hinges, while the material to be used in CRTEF is rubber, and no hinge
could be manufactured in the extrusion process. Also, there is no stress concentration at the cell
corners due to the hinges, but this might be an issue in CRTEF. Although the concept is not directly
applicable to CRTEF, it still oﬀers some inspirations. As shown in Figure 2.2, it is noticed that when
the downward deﬂection is desired, the pressure is applied such that the the upper axial length is
longer; while the upward deﬂection is desired, the pressure is applied to the cells such that the lower
axial length is longer. This is a clear illustration on the mechanism of pneumatic actuators concerning
how the expansions of the cells or voids are translated into the bending movement of the structure.
Using the work of Pagitz et al. [16] as an initial point, a single cell and a single row cantilever made of
the concept of pressure actuated cellular structures (PACS) was dimensioned, realized, experimental
tested [17], and the holistic design and implementation were also studied [18]. In this work, it is
shown that in order to meet the assumption of rigid cell walls as much as possible, the cell walls are
built with glass ﬁber reinforced plastics and thickness of the wall tailored for low bending stiﬀness at
hinges. Although the diﬃculties in building low bending stiﬀness at hinges are possible to be overcome
as demonstrated by Gramüller et al., the material with enough stiﬀness for the cell walls can not be
substituted with the rubber used in CRTEF. Therefore, the concept of PACS is not directly applicable
to CRTEF. However, the work by Gramüller et al. illuminates a part of the mechanism of pressure

2.4
Summary of literature review
Although the manufacturing process restricts most of the direct applications of the literature reviewed,
a certain amount of inspirations and help in understanding the mechanism were still found.
The
summary consists of three parts which are consistent with the three previous subsections.
First,
the conﬂict of the requirements for both the low and high bending stiﬀness was shown to be an
important issue. Considering the goal to fulﬁll the industrial requirements for robust structures and
the designated manufacturing process of the project, the solution by implementing composite laminates
as skins is determined to be a possible solution to the problem. Second, even though some diﬀerences
between cellular structures and CRTEF make it impractical to directly apply the results, some common
mechanisms which give a better understanding in CRTEF mechanism were discovered: the uneven
elongation of axial length is the essence of the pressure-actuated ﬂap bending movement, and the void
would deform towards the geometry with maximum volume when applied with pressure. Finally, the
topology optimization [22] serves as a good starting point of the project. The shapes of the voids are
suggested to have arches enclosing the pressured voids on the sides of the voids close to the surface
of the ﬂap, thereby elongates the ﬂap surface when applied with pressure, resulting in the bending
movement of the ﬂap. This concept resembles one of the ideas in the work of Madsen et al. [24], as
shown in Figure 2.4b. It is also suggested by Madsen et al. that an elastic cover wrapping the ﬂap can
be used to smoothen the cavities on the surface. Combining these two concepts, the baseline model
of the project is designed and optimized in Chapter 6.

3.1
Terminology
3.1.1
Tip
Unlike most literature on wind turbines where ’tip’ refers to the tip of a blade, in this project, ’tip’
refers to the trailing edge tip of a ﬂap if not speciﬁed.
3.1.2
Tip deﬂection
Note that in the project, the term ’tip deﬂection’ does not refer to the deﬂection of the tip of a blade
on a wind turbine. Instead, the term ’tip deﬂection’ referring to the Y-component of the deﬂection at
the trailing edge tip will be used as a measurement of the ﬂap angle and stiﬀness evaluation.
3.1.3
Stiﬀness
’Stiﬀness’ in the project refers to the ability of the design to withstand the surface pressure load during
the operation of the wind turbine. Although there are several ways to measure the stiﬀness of a design,
tip deﬂection is the most direct and clear index and is therefore used in the project.
3.1.4
Eﬀect
The eﬀect of the project is apparently the eﬀect of a ﬂap angle. Therefore, if not speciﬁed, the ’eﬀect’
refers to the tip deﬂection of the design when the voids are pressurized.

3.1.5
Eﬃciency
The eﬃciency of the design is composed of the minimizing the volume of the voids and the pressure
required to actuate the mechanism. Since the volume is not a direct independent variable, ’eﬃciency’
in the project refers to the pressure required to actuate the mechanism if not speciﬁed.
3.1.6
Void pressure
The void pressure means the actuation pressure which is applied on the inside walls of the voids. The
upper void pressure means the void pressure for the upper void, while the lower void pressure means
the void pressure for the lower void.
3.2
Objectives and constraints
Initially, the objectives of the project include: 1. maximizing the eﬀect of the ﬂap angle, 2. maximizing
the energy eﬃciency of the ﬂap control system by minimizing the actuation pressure and the volume of
the voids, 3. maximizing the bending stiﬀness of the structure. The constraints include: 1. local shape
deformations, 2. the strength of the material, and 3. the manufacturing limitations. Although the
overall objectives and the constraints are provided, the diﬀerent objectives are actually contradicting.
As a result, based on the priority and the extents to which the objectives are desired, some of the
objectives and the constraints are quantiﬁed.
3.2.1
Eﬀect of the ﬂap angle
The ﬂap angle was measured to around ±8◦with ±3 bar of void pressure in the test of last project [2].
As discussed in the work of Daynes and Weaver [4], in order to reach the same Cl, a blade section with
5◦ﬂap needs greater angle of attack than a blade section with 10◦ﬂap. This diﬀerence in angle of
attack can be up to 4◦, depending on the Cl of interest. Therefore, greater ﬂap angle can be valuable
to the purpose of the ﬂap control. Besides, due to the expected diﬀerences between the simulations
and reality as discussed in Section 7.8, the target ﬂap angle is determined to be somewhat higher, in
order to be conservative. As a result, in this project, the ideal goal of the ﬂap angle is determined to
be ±15◦, but if this goal cannot be achieved, ±10◦will be the goal instead. In order to evaluate the
eﬀect of ﬂap angle more directly in the analyses, the equivalent tip deﬂection in Y-direction of the ﬂap
angle will be used as the measurement of the ﬂap angle.

3.2.2
Energy eﬃciency
The energy eﬃciency of the actuator can be evaluated with the volume of the voids and the void

Bending stiﬀness
In the beginning of the project, it was not clear how much the bending stiﬀness of the structure should
be, and the structure could be stiﬀened by applying pressure on the voids. However, in order to have a
safe design which does not deﬂect too much when the actuator is not working, a ﬂap angle within ±5◦
during normal operation of the wind turbine without void pressure is set as the objective. Because
this objective is at the second highest priority, after making sure this stiﬀness is achievable, instead of
using it as an objective, it is set as an constraint during the optimization. It is notable that the exact

angle, the equivalent tip deﬂection of ±25.8 mm for ±5◦ﬂap angle, calculated based on Equation 3.1,
will be used.
3.2.4
Local shape deformations
Initially, concerning the aerodynamic performance of the ﬂap, the surface of the ﬂap should be kept
smooth during the actuation process. However, in order to implement the concept with arches as
discussed in Section 2.3, the surface of the ﬂap will not be smooth even before the actuation. As
a result, an elastic cover wrapping around the ﬂap is expected to be used to keep the ﬂap surface
smooth. In order to keep the simulations simple, the elastic cover is not included in the simulations
of this project, but should be used for experiments to investigate the eﬀects and if the ﬂap is installed
on a blade.
Even with the elastic cover, the problem of local shape deformation is not completely solved. During
the actuation process, the arches deform, and could become bumps on the surface. Therefore, in the
optimization process, the location of the arches when the void is pressurised will be constrained in a
certain range in which the formed bumps are still acceptable. Because the values of the range depend
on the location and the shape of the voids, they are determined qualitatively on the preliminary design
of each optimization.

Chapter 4
Models, methods, and software
In the project, several analysis tools were used. XFOIL was used to estimate the static pressure on
the surface of the ﬂap, so that the loads during operation of the turbine can also be considered in the
design process. SolidWorks was used to design the geometry of the ﬂap with the the built-in feature
of parametric design. Static Structural analysis system in ANSYS 16.0 workbench was then used to
perform the simulations using the ﬁnite element method. With the results of the simulations, the
performance of the designs was qualitatively analyzed, in order to have a better preliminary design.
Finally, the Direct Optimization system, a built-in Design Exploration system in ANSYS 16.0, was
used in combination with the parametric design in SolidWorks to conduct parametric optimizations.
The brief theory and settings of the tools will be covered in this chapter.
4.1
XFOIL
4.1.1
Theory
XFOIL is a program for design and analysis of subsonic isolated airfoils developed at Massachusetts
Institute of Technology (MIT). The program uses an inviscid linear-vorticity panel method with a
Karman-Tsien compressibility correction [26]. Modeling of viscous layer inﬂuence on the potential
ﬂow is permitted by source distributions superimposed on the airfoil and wake. The viscous layers are
represented by using a two-equation lagged dissipation integral method. More details of the method-
ology of the program are discussed by Drela [26]. Information on the program can be found on the
website of XFOIL [27]. Although the results from XFOIL could be diﬀerent from other Computational
Fluid Dynamic (CFD) analyses and in reality, it is computationally cheap and the results are robust
[28]. Therefore, it was decided to be used for the estimation of surface pressure loads.

4.2
SolidWorks
SolidWorks is a 3-dimension (3D) computer-aided design (CAD) software. However, in the project,
since the ﬂap was determined to be designed with a constant cross section along the span due to the
limitations of manufacturing as discussed in Section 1.2, a 2-dimension (2D) analysis with the plane
strain behavior should be as valid as a 3D analysis, and a 2D analysis is computationally cheaper.
Therefore, the project will base on 2D analyses and the designed component in SolidWorks will be
a 2D surface. SolidWorks was chosen as the CAD software to be used because of its friendly user
interface, handy sketch tools, and most importantly, its feature of parametric design which can easily
be coupled with the parametric analysis in ANSYS 16.0.
4.3
Simulation settings in ANSYS
Since this project is a design-oriented project, it is way much time-consuming to manufacture every
single model. Thanks to the development of the theories and tools for numerical simulations, the
designs can be simply tested using numerical simulations in the project. The ﬁnite element analysis
(FEA) software to be used is ANSYS 16.0, which is powerful in parametric analysis. Static Structural
analysis system in ANSYS 16.0 workbench is the system used. The simulation model is set up with

settings of the two separate simulations are similar, but they are set up diﬀerently for the void to
be concerned. The detail of the simulation settings will be discussed in the subsections. When not
speciﬁed in the following discussion, the settings are applied to all types of the simulations.

Before using the optimizer to perform parametric optimization, the preliminary design has to be

4.5
Parametric optimization theory and set-up
In order to perform parametric optimization, the built-in optimizer in ANSYS 16.0 is chosen, because
the optimizer can be coupled with SolidWorks to change the parameters of the geometry and perform
simulations automatically. There are two options to perform the optimization: direct optimization
and response surface optimization. Although response surface optimization could possibly save com-
putational cost by generating the response surface with fewer design points, the quality of the response
surface has to be high enough to give valuable results and the quality of the response surface depends
on the number of design points used to generate the surface. The relationships between the input
parameters and the output parameters in the void eﬀect simulations are so non-linear that response
surfaces with good enough quality would possibly require more design points than direct optimization.
Also, in order to create the response surface, all the design points have to be solved, but since the non-
linearity, some extreme designs could not be solved in the simulations, making it diﬃcult to determine
the ranges of the input variables to generate the response surface. Therefore, direct optimization is
chosen for the project. In the direct optimization, there are several methods could be used. In order to
get the global optimum instead of local optimum, ’Multiple-Objective Genetic Algorithm (MOGA)’
is decided to start the optimization with. There are three other global search methods: Adaptive
Single-Objective, Adaptive Multiple-Objective and Screening. Adaptive Single-Objective and Adap-
tive Multiple-Objective rely on response surface and the Screening is useful only with an impractically
big amount of design points in this case. Therefore, these three other methods are not considered.
Although MOGA is capable of optimizing for multiple objectives, the compromise made between the
objectives are not clearly deﬁned. Therefore, only the most suitable objective is deﬁned in the opti-
mization while the rests are deﬁned as constraints. In the MOGA method, crossover and mutation
are performed to generate a new population in every iteration, making it a more reﬁned method than
Screening. With the global optimum from MOGA as the starting point, the gradient-based approach
’Nonlinear Programming by Quadratic Lagrangian (NLPQL)’, which is based on quasi-Newton meth-
ods is used to ﬁnd the even more reﬁned local optimum. The detailed theories of MOGA and NLPQL
can be found in ANSYS help viewer [29] and the detailed settings of the optimizations will be discussed
in the sections where the optimizations are discussed.

Design of load cases
During the normal operation of the wind turbine installed with the ﬂap, two load scenarios should be
considered: 1. When the ﬂap is not actuated, i.e. no void pressure. 2. When the ﬂap is actuated with
the desired eﬀects. Four load cases were designed using XFOIL and the equivalent tip forces were
estimated for these two load scenarios in Section 5.1 and 5.3. The equivalent tip forces were used in
the simulations in Section 6.3. Afterwards, the equivalent tip force was evaluated again in Section 5.4
with two more design load cases included, the model used in the simulations changed, and the method
of the estimation changed. The re-evaluated equivalent tip forces were used in the evaluation of the
result of preliminary design in Subsection 6.3.3 and the design process from Section 6.4 to Section 6.8,
until being estimated again in Section 6.9.
5.1
Deﬁnition of load cases
In the ﬁrst scenario, the ﬂap is still carrying the surface loads during operation, and the stiﬀness of the
ﬂap without void pressure should be designed based on this scenario. In order to cover the operational
range of the turbine, the two most extreme cases of the scenario should be considered.
1. The stiﬀness up load case: In this load case, the ﬂap is deﬂected to -5◦under the maximum surface
pressure load which corresponds to the AOA with the maximum Cl.
2. The stiﬀness down load case. In this load case, the ﬂap is deﬂected to 5◦under the maximum
surface pressure load which corresponds to the AOA with the minimum Cl.
The values of the AOA for these two load cases were then found with XFOIL to be 16◦for the ﬁrst
case and -20.4◦for the second case. These two cases were then deﬁned as the ﬁrst and second design
load cases.
In the second scenario, as mentioned in Subsection 3.2.1, the desired ﬂap angles are ±15◦. When the
ﬂap is actuated to such eﬀects, it should reach the desired ﬂap angles while carrying the surface loads.
Therefore, two more extreme load cases were then deﬁned.
1. The upper void eﬀect load case: In this load case, the ﬂap angle is actuated to 15◦, and the actuated
structure is carrying the surface pressure load which corresponds to the AOA with the maximum Cl.
2. The lower void eﬀect load case: In this load case, the ﬂap angle is actuated to -15◦, and the actuated
structure is carrying the surface pressure load which corresponds to the AOA with the minimum Cl
within the operational range (AOA within ±15◦).
The values of the AOA for these two load cases were then found with XFOIL to be 14.9◦for the ﬁrst
case and -15◦for the second case.

The model used to estimate the equivalent tip force in Section 5.3 was a complete ﬂap without any

5.4.3
New method
The method used to estimate equivalent tip forces in Section 5.3 is to ﬁnd the tip force which causes
about the same tip deﬂection as the surface pressure loads. However, there are some shortcomings of
this method. First, since the pressure loads applied on the surface were set to be perpendicular to the
surface and the direction of the surface changes during operation, meaning that the directions of the
pressure loads also change. On the other hand, the tip force was applied only in the Y-direction which
does not change. Therefore, the equivalent tip force could be diﬀerent depending on the amount of
deﬂection of the ﬂap. Second, the deﬂection of the ﬂap depends on the stiﬀness of the ﬂap, therefore,
when the stiﬀness of the ﬂap changes in the design process, the equivalent tip force would be diﬀerent.
In order to improve these two shortcomings, the equivalent tip forces were estimated with a new
method. First, there was a tip force deﬂecting the ﬂap to the ﬂap angle of interest as demonstrated
in Figure 5.3a. This ﬂap angle could be 0, ±5, ±15◦depending on the load case to be estimated.
Second, the surface pressure was applied despite the pressure loads on the arch parts were missing
as demonstrated in Figure 5.3b. Finally, a tip force was applied to approximately cancel out the
deﬂection caused by the pressure loads, making the structure have the equilibrium at the ﬂap angel
of interest as demonstrated in Figure 5.3c. The opposite number of this counteracting tip force was
then multiplied with a safety factor of 1.1 as discussed in Subsection 5.4.2 and used as the equivalent
tip force. The tip deﬂections of every load case when the ﬂap was deﬂected with only the tip force to
the ﬂap angle of interest and when the pressure loads and the counteracting tip force were also acted
on the ﬂap, can also be found in Table 5.2.

Development of the design
As discussed in the summary of literature review, Section 2.4, an arch model with arches forming
concave on the ﬂap surface and enclosing the voids to be pressurized is supposed to result in the
bending movement of the ﬂap when pressure is applied on the voids to straighten the arches. In the
design process, the concept of arch model was ﬁrst tested in a simulation in Section 6.1 to see how
the performance of such a concept is. Since the simulation was performed with non-linear analyses, a
linear simulation was also performed to check the validity of the non-linear analyses by examining the
consistency of the two simulations. Depending on the design, the arch might be snapping through from
the concave shape to convex shape. A simulation was therefore performed to check if the model returns
to the original concave shape when the voids are unloaded. With the previous examinations, the arch
model was regarded as a feasible solution to the project, and was then designed with parameters in
Section 6.2 to facilitate the optimization later on. In order to make the simulations closer to actual
behaviors, in Chapter 5, the inﬂuence of the pressure loads on the surface of the ﬂap during operation,
either when the actuator is working or not, were estimated to be included in the simulations. Although
all the initially designed parameters aﬀect the performance of every load case, the parameters can still
be divided into three groups: 1. the upper void geometries, 2. the lower void geometries, and 3. the
locations of the voids. The ﬁrst group was considered to be more relevant to the eﬀect of the upper
void, and also to the stiﬀness. The second group was considered to be more relevant to the eﬀect of
the lower void, and also to the stiﬀness. The third group was considered to be more relevant to the
stiﬀness. The three groups are, however, connected by the stiﬀness of the structure, and the structure
with lower stiﬀness makes the actuator more eﬃcient. Since this inﬂuence between the groups was
considered superposable, the four following stages of the design process were devised:
1. In Section 6.3, the qualitative research was performed with all the parameters to have a pre-
liminary design for the next stage. In the qualitative analysis, the location of the voids were
designed so that the structure was stiﬀer than required by the constraint. This space between
the required stiﬀness and the stiﬀness of the structure was then used in the optimizations. The
locations of the voids were then kept constant for the next two stages.
2. In Section 6.4, 6.5 and 6.6, the upper void was optimized using some space of the stiﬀness and
the parameters of the upper void geometry.
3. In Section 6.7, the lower void was optimized using some space of the stiﬀness and the parameters
of the lower void geometry.
4. In Section 6.8, the locations were used to make the stiﬀness almost as low as the constraint,
and since lower stiﬀness of the structure makes the actuator more eﬃcient, the eﬃciency can be
improved.

bending movement of the ﬂap when pressure is applied on the voids to straighten the arches. The
arch model was then roughly designed using SolidWorks as shown in Figure 6.1a.
Although the
model comes with concavities on the surface of the ﬂap which could have undesired eﬀects on the
aerodynamic performance, an elastic cover wrapping the ﬂap can possibly solve this problem. In the
simulations, in order to keep simple, the elastic cover will not be included, but in actual test and
operation, the elastic cover should be used. In the simulations in this section, the material properties
used are for ±15% strain range, but the properties will be changed to for ±50% strain range in the
simulations in Section 6.3. Detailed settings of the material properties in the simulations are discussed

6.1.1
Capability of deformation
In order to check the deformability of the model, the inner sides of the upper void were applied with
a pressure load of 0.09 MPa as shown in Figure 6.3a. It turned out that even a sketchy design can
result in a tip deﬂection of −23.6 mm as shown in Figure 6.3b. Therefore, the concept was thought
to be a possible concept in terms of deformation capability.
6.1.2
Validation of non-linear model
Although the material and the deformation of the model are nonlinear, the validity of the nonlinear
simulation should be checked with a linear simulation. A simulation was performed with linear material
properties (Young’s modulus of 28.4 MPa) and excluding the stiﬀness changed resulting from changes
in element shape and orientation due to large deﬂection, large rotation, and large strain. Although
the pressure required to achieve a tip deﬂection of −23.2 mm is 0.11 MPa which is higher than the
pressure for the nonlinear model, the behavior is similar to the non-linear simulation. The pressure
required for the linear model is higher because the nonlinearity of the material properties makes it
less stiﬀwhen the strain is higher and the linear elastic modulus is calculated by regression analysis
from the tension data in the linear portion. Therefore, the linear material properties make the model
stiﬀer. Nevertheless, the similar behaviors of the nonlinear model and the linear model showed that
the nonlinear analysis is valid.

maximum number of iterations, so an optimization with higher maximum number of iterations should
be performed.

6.6.1
New design
In order to solve the stress concentration problem, the parts with stress concentration should be
trimmed with higher curvature. First, the diameter of the half-circle caps at the slot ends was increased.
Second, the upper side of the arch was trimmed with a spline with lower curvature. Besides, the spline
which constructs the arch was made perpendicular to the surface in order to have a symmetric stress
distribution.
6.6.1.1
Expansion of the slot caps
As shown in Figure 6.19, there is severe stress concentration at the caps of the slot ends, due to large
deformation and high curvature. In order to keep the volume small for eﬃciency and stiﬀness and
increase the curvature of the cap at the same time, the slot width was ﬁrst modiﬁed to 2 mm which is
the minimum limit of the manufacturing method. The diameter of the half-circle caps at the slot ends,
US1_End_Dia, was then increased, as shown in Figure 6.20, and used as an input parameter in the
later optimizations. Also, a transition factor, US1_End_TF, determining the length of the transition
region from the cap to the slot was also included as an input parameter for the optimizations. The
caps were expanded only towards the opposite direction of the arch in order to exclude the inﬂuence
of this expansion on the thickness of the arch.

6.6.1.2
Trim on the arch
In order to solve the stress concentration problem on the arch, however, cannot be done without af-
fecting the thickness of the arch. First, a spline was drawn by putting a tangent point at the center
of the arch, making the tangent direction at the point parallel to a construction line connecting the
center points of the two construction lines of the spline used to make the arch. In other words, it
was made perpendicular to the original spline in order to make it symmetric. The tangent weighting
parameter at the point which is used to determine the curvature at the point was included as an input
parameter for the optimizations. The spline used to trim the arch was then created by ﬁtting this
spline and the curves of the corner ﬁllets as shown in Figure 6.21.

Since the arch would be more slender after being trimmed, the thickness of the arch should be increased
to keep the similar performance as the previous design. Therefore, when deciding the range of the
input variables in Subsection 6.6.2, the lower boundary of the thickness of the arch was increased.
6.6.1.3
Adjustment to the symmetry of the arch
It can also be seen on Figure 6.21 that the maximum curvature of this new spline used to trim the arch
falls on the sides instead of in the middle. Therefore, it is important that the spline is symmetric, so
that the maximum curvature on the two sides are the same. If the spline is asymmetric, the curvature
of one side increases, while the curvature on the other side decreases, making the safety factor lower.
As a result, instead of having the half width of the arch as a function of the width of the design domain
as designed in Subsection 6.3.2, the constraint was changed to having the middle line of the spline
perpendicular to the surface of the ﬂap.

6.6.2.3
Optimization for void pressure
Since an acceptable safety factor of 1.31 was achieved, the next task would be to optimize for minimum
void pressure. However, since the built-in optimizer with NLPQL does not allow using only input
variables as objectives, the optimization for void pressure cannot be performed automatically. Instead,
two optimizations using ﬁxed void pressure were performed, and the objective was still set to maximize
the safety factor. It turned out that the ﬁrst optimization using the same starting point as the local
optimization in Table 6.7 and the void pressure ﬁxed at 0.44 MPa gives a safety factor of 1.09, while
the second optimization using the optimum of the ﬁrst optimization as the starting point and the void
pressure ﬁxed at 0.4 MPa gives a safety factor of 1.09 as well. It was then concluded that in order to
improve the eﬃciency by lowering the void pressure, the safety factor has to be compromised. Even
though the optimum safety factor of 1.31 was acceptable, it was still too low to be compromised. As a
result, the results of the optimization for lower void pressure was not taken, and the optimum point in
Table 6.7 was thought to be the optimum design for the upper void. Most of the input parameters were
rounded to two decimal digits, except for US1_End_TF and the void pressure which were rounded
to three decimal digits in order to have three signiﬁcant digits.

6.8
Utilization of the allowable stiﬀness range by moving the
voids
As discussed in Subsection 4.3.1, because the maximum strain of this design is around 5% when the
ﬂap is having a 26 mm tip deﬂection under a tip force loading, the material parameters for ±15% strain
range is still too conservative. Therefore, the material parameters were changed to the parameters
for ±10% strain range for the stiﬀness check simulations in this section and afterwards, in order to
better simulate the real performance. This change made the ﬂap stiﬀer, but still conservative when
compared with the measured material properties. After the material parameters were changed, the
stiﬀness tip deﬂection was lowered from 22.98 mm to 19.57 mm, making a diﬀerence of about 6 mm
between the stiﬀness tip deﬂection of the optimum design and the stiﬀness tip deﬂection of the strict
constraint of the project, 25.8 mm. This diﬀerence was then used to further improve the void pressure
and the eﬃciency.
6.8.1
Utilization of the stiﬀness
As discussed in the beginning of the chapter, when the stiﬀness is lower, it is also in favor of the
eﬃciency of the voids. Therefore, the idea of this section is to lower the stiﬀness to a stiﬀness tip
deﬂection of 25 mm by moving the voids closer. Because the purpose of the project is to have a ﬂap for
10% chord, moving the voids to the trailing edge would make less portion of the ﬂap being deﬂected
when the voids are actuated. Therefore, the voids should be put close to the leading edge side of
the ﬂap. However, if the voids are put too close to the side, the strain on the side when the voids
are actuated would have bad impact on the fatigue performance at the connection between the ﬂap
and the blade as discussed by Løgstrup Andersen et al. [2]. Therefore, it was decided that the upper
void should be kept at its original location where the stress on the trailing edge side is not too much

6.9.2
Inﬂuence on the design
The increased equivalent tip force for the lower void made the eﬀect drop from 81.01 mm to 54.41
mm when the same amount of void pressure is applied, even though the safety factor was increased
from 1.64 to 1.88. The easiest way to solve this problem was to simply apply a higher void pressure,
so that the desired eﬀect can be met. Therefore, the pressure for the lower void was increased from
0.383 MPa to 0.402 MPa, increasing the eﬀect from 54.41 mm to 80.43 mm, but decreasing the safety
factor from 1.88 to 1.47. The lower void was still carrying lower surface loads than the upper void,
but the safety factor of the lower void became lower than the safety factor of the upper void which
was 1.60. This was thought to be unacceptable and was therefore improved by performing an iterated
optimization for the lower void in Section 6.10.

Evaluation of the optimum design
The ﬁnal optimum design which fulﬁlls the objectives of the project of having a ﬂap eﬀect of ±15◦with
voids of small volume and achieving the stiﬀness requirement was found in Chapter 6. The optimum
design was then evaluated in terms of the ability to return to the original shape when unloaded,
the validity of the equivalent tip forces used in the simulations, the sensitivity of the parameters to
the performance within the manufacturing tolerance range, the convergence of the mesh, the overall
performance, the performance for ±10◦eﬀect, and the performance without surface loads. Besides,
the diﬀerences to be expected between the simulations and the reality and the simulation noises and
signiﬁcant ﬁgures are discussed.
7.1
Snap-through
Although in the earlier designs, there could be a snap-though when the void pressure is applied, this
is not the case for the this optimum design as shown in Figure 7.4 and 7.5. In order to make sure
this is indeed not a problem for the design, simulations with the void pressure and surface loads
unloaded after loading were carried out. The model returned to the original shape after unloaded in
the simulations. Therefore, it can be concluded that this is not an issue for this optimum design.
7.2
Conservativeness of the equivalent tip force
Although the equivalent tip forces could be diﬀerent because the geometries were changed after the
last estimation of equivalent tip forces, the changes were not too much, and even the last method of
estimation is not a perfect method to estimate the equivalent tip forces. Therefore, instead of iterate
for the equivalent tip forces again, the surface pressure loads were applied to check if the equivalent
tip forces used were still conservative enough. As shown in Table 7.1, when the surface loads are
changed from equivalent tip forces to the pressure directly applied on the model, the performance of
the model is even better in terms of tip deﬂection. However, one should notice that the surface pressure
loads are applied on the model without the arch part as explained in Section 5.4, and therefore the
conservativeness for the equivalent tip forces is necessary.
7.3

7.4
Mesh convergence test
Mesh convergence test is important to evaluate if the mesh of the model is valid. The mesh convergence
test had been performed from time to time since the beginning of the project in order to keep the mesh
ﬁne enough while computationally friendly. For the ﬁnal optimum design, the mesh convergence was
performed to check if the mesh is ﬁne enough. The mesh of the model is especially important at the
ends of the slots and the stretching side of the arches due to the high curvature and large deformation
of the regions. The mesh for the slot ends in the optimum design was reﬁned by specifying the number
of division of the cap and the transition curve to 50 for each curve as shown in Figure 7.1a. The ﬁrst
mesh convergence test was performed by using this number as a variable, and results are shown in
Table 7.5. The results show that when the number is increased from 50 to 150, the safety factor for
the upper void eﬀect is decreased from 1.73 to 1.71 and the safety factor for the lower void eﬀect is
decreased from 1.63 to 1.58. Although this decrease is insigniﬁcant, the number was changed to 150
for the ﬁnal model. The stretching side of the arch, was also reﬁned in the mesh of the optimization
simulations. As shown in Figure 7.1b, the curve was reﬁned by specifying the number of division of
the curve to 100, including a bias factor of 2.5, in order to make the middle of the arch ﬁner than the
end of the arch. The second mesh convergence test was performed by using this number of division
as a variable and the results are shown in Table 7.6. The results show that the value, 100, which had
been used was making the mesh ﬁne enough. Therefore, this value was kept in the ﬁnal optimum
model. The ﬁnal decision of the mesh is shown in Figure 7.2.

7.8
Expected unreality of the simulations
Although the optimum design shows satisfying results in the simulations, there are some assumptions
in the settings of the simulations and some estimations which would make the simulation results dif-
ferent from the performance in reality.
First, the material properties used in the simulations are the approximated properties by curve ﬁtting,
instead of measured material properties. The detailed diﬀerence is discussed in Subsection 4.3.1, and
based on the diﬀerence, the performance of the design in reality is expected to be stiﬀer at the regions
with the strain values falling on the part of curve where the stress is underestimated by the curve
ﬁtting method, but less stiﬀat the regions with the strain values falling on the part of the curve where
the stress is overestimated by the curve ﬁtting method. For example, for the void eﬀect simulations,
the stress-strain curve of the material parameters used in the simulations is shown in Figure 4.2a.
Therefore, for the regions with strain values less than around 35% are expected to be stiﬀer in reality
than in the simulations, and for the regions with strain values higher than around 35% are expected
to be less stiﬀin reality. Also, the material parameters are only for strain range within ±50%, but in
the void eﬀect simulations, the maximum principal strain can be up to 82%. As a result, the behavior
of the design in reality should be expected to be diﬀerent, and this is expected to be the dominant
reason for the diﬀerence.
Second, the surface pressure loads during operation were estimated using XFOIL and some assump-
tions. Some diﬀerences between reality and the calculations using XFOIL can be expected due to
the diﬀerence between the laminar-turbulent transition point calculated in XFOIL and in reality, the
assumption of the Reynold’s number, and the quality of the proﬁle of the airfoils. A comparison of
the Cp distributions calculated with XFOIL and tested in the wind tunnel with Reynold’s number of
1.6 · 106 and an angle of attack of 16.4◦shows that the Cp values at the trailing edge are higher in
reality than calculated [28], although the diﬀerence between the upper surface and the lower surface
is rather similar in both cases. Besides, the fact that the pressure loads are applied on the models
without the arch parts as shown in Figure 5.2 also makes the simulations diﬀerent from reality.
Third, in reality, the ﬂap is expected to be wrapped with an elastic cover, in order to avoid the con-
cave on the surface and keep the aerodynamic performance. This elastic cover was not included in the
simulations in order to keep the simulation simple. Therefore, the behavior of the design is expected
to be diﬀerent in reality due to the elastic cover.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this chapter, the achievement and the disadvantages of the design are summarized, followed by the
self-reﬂection on the steps taken in the project and the possible future work.
8.1
Achievements of the design
The design had been developed by using the qualitative analysis and parametric optimization, with
the strategy of utilizing the allowable stiﬀness of the structure, while the desired deﬂection is fulﬁlled,
to optimize for the material safety factor and the energy eﬃciency as discussed in the beginning of
Chapter 6. As discussed in Chapter 7, the optimum design point fulﬁlls the main objective of the
project, which is having ±15◦ﬂap angle under surface loads during operation of the turbine. Com-
pared with the results of the previous project [2], in which the ﬂap angle was measured to around
±8◦with void pressure of ±0.3 MPa, the optimum design of this project also improves the energy
eﬃciency by having a ±10◦ﬂap angle with void pressure of 0.215 MPa and 0.205 MPa when there
are no surface loads, and by having much smaller volume of voids. It also meets the bending stiﬀness
constraint of deﬂecting within ±5◦during operation of the turbine without actuation pressure, the
local deformation constraint of having acceptable deformed shape locally and the material strength
constraints by having a minimum safety factor of 1.58. In terms of the manufacturing limitations, the
minimum void thickness is 2 mm as the slot width and the minimum arch thickness is 3.04 for the
upper arch and 2.71 for the lower arch, which fulﬁll the constraint of having minimum thickness of the
geometry ≥2 mm. Another manufacturing limitation of a process precision of 0.1 mm is also covered
by having acceptable performance when the parameters are changed within this tolerance range.
8.2
Disadvantages of the design
Despite the achievements of the design, there are some disadvantages of this design. First, the design
comes with a concave on the surface of the ﬂap, and therefore an elastic cover is needed to keep
the aerodynamic performance. Second, the deformed shapes of the design still cause bumps on the
ﬂap surface, which might aﬀect the aerodynamic performance and has to be evaluated. Third, when
there are surface loads involved, the relation between the actuation pressure and the ﬂap angle, which
was evaluated by the relation between the actuation pressure and the tip deﬂection, was shown to
be non-linear. This could possibly cause some problems of controlling the ﬂap behavior, but due to
the diﬀerence between the simulation and reality and the fact that the relation was not studied in
a sophisticated way, the issue has to be further evaluated. Finally, since this actuator only uses less
than one ﬁfth of the ﬂap ( 52.22 mm
296.08 mm), instead of having a morphing deﬂection, it acts more similar to
a rotation of the ﬂap. However, this can also be an advantage, since the rest part of the ﬂap can be
used for other purpose, e.g. using stiﬀmaterial to further improve the bending stiﬀness of the ﬂap.

Conclusion
8.3
Reﬂection on the steps taken in the project
If it was possible to start again with the project, there are some steps should have been taken in
other ways to make the project complete more smoothly. First, the equivalent tip forces should have
been estimated using the method as discussed in Section 6.9. Although the model changed during the
design process would still aﬀect the equivalent tip forces, the consistent method of estimation and the
surface pressure loads could have made the change of the equivalent tip forces less in every iteration.
Second, the simulation noises discussed in Section 7.9 should have been taken into account for the
optimizer settings, in order to have more consistent optimization results.
8.4
Future work
Based on the achievements and ﬁndings of the project, there is some possible work to be done in
the future. This includes the improvement of the simulations, other simulations, the experiments to
compare with the simulation, and other possible concepts to be tested. Besides, the optimum point is
considered as a potential design of INDUFLAP2 project [32] and will be 3D printed and tested.
8.4.1
Simulation
In order to improve the quality of the simulations and optimizations and to have more results to be
compared with experiments, some work could be done before the experiments. First, the simulations
and optimizations could be performed again if more accurate material properties are available. Second,
the aerodynamic performance inﬂuenced by the deformed shape of the model with the bumps on the
ﬂap surface should be evaluated. Third, the relation between the void pressure and the ﬂap angle
could be studied in a more sophisticated way. This could be done by estimating the surface pressure
loads of diﬀerent ﬂap angles within the designed range and evaluating the void pressure required at
each ﬂap angle with the corresponding surface loads. Also, the elastic cover to be wrapped around
the ﬂap could included in the simulations.
Moreover, the simulations covering the fatigue analysis and the stability analysis of the structure could
be performed.
8.4.2
Experiment
Before testing the ﬂap on a turbine, some experiments on the performance of the ﬂap should be carried
out in the laboratory. First, the relation between the void pressure and the ﬂap angle without surface
pressure loads should be investigated and compared with the simulation results. Second, the elastic
cover should be included to see the inﬂuence on the ﬂap. Third, the aerodynamic performance with
the the bumps on the ﬂap surface when the voids are pressurized should be investigated.
8.4.3
Other possible concepts
Besides the optimum design of this project, other concepts could also be investigated.
First, in
the optimum design, since there is only less than one ﬁfth of the ﬂap at the leading edge used as
the actuation mechanism as discussed in Section 8.2, the rest of the ﬂap could be replaced with a
component made of stiﬀer material to further improve the stiﬀness of the ﬂap. Besides, if a morphing
deformation is desired, one could consider implementing more than one pair of the voids.
