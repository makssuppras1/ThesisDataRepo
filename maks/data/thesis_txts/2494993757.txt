ABSTRACT
Abstract
Artiﬁcial reefs serve as an habitat for marine life, and deploying these along the coastline is becoming
more common. When artiﬁcial reefs are placed on the seabed, scour will initiate around them causing
them to settle and sometimes topple. To investigate this problem a series of experiments have been
executed, testing a series of cuboid blocks in steady current, to investigate how their aspect ratio (length-
to-height, L/H) and density will aﬀect their settlement process. The inﬂuence of the ﬂow velocity and
the interface friction angle between the sand and the block was also tested. The results showed that if
the block had an aspect ratio of L/H ≥1 it would not topple, and based on these results a criteria for
when to expect toppling has been deduced. Over the range of conditions tested, the settlement depth of
the blocks which did not topple, was found to depend mostly on the aspect ratio of the blocks rather than
any other parameter. With larger aspect ratio followed lower settlement. The settlement depth of the
blocks was also found to be smaller than the scour depth of an embedded rectangle of same aspect ratio,
while the time scale for the settlement depth was in the order of 10 times greater than the time scale for
scour. These observations, ﬁrst of all, makes it possible to predict when an artiﬁcial reef is expected to
topple, but also the behaviour of the settlement of a non-toppling reef along with when to expect the reef
to have reached its equilibrium settlement in a given ﬂow condition.

Introduction
Artiﬁcial reefs are placed at multiple locations along the coastline of Western Australia to provide a
base physical habitat for the local marine life. The reefs are placed on the seabed without any kind of
additional support or foundation (see Figure 1.1).
(a) Abitat Module
[Subcon Blue Solutions, 2020a]
(b) Reef Pyramid
[Queensland Government, 2020]
(c) Apollo Module
[Subcon Blue Solutions, 2020b]
Figure 1.1: Examples of artiﬁcal reefs
When a structure is placed on the seabed, erosion of the seabed in the vicinity of the object will commence,
assuming the seabed is in fact erodible, due to the pressure gradients the structure has created in the
previously undisturbed ﬂow. The erosion of the seabed that occurs because a structure has been placed
there, is called scour.
Local scour has been studied thoroughly for a selection of marine structures. These are structures like
pipelines, slender and non-slender piles, and bridge piers. Common for all of these are that a failure of one
of these structures can have great human, environmental, and/or economic consequences. For instance
the failure of a bridge pier is a great risk to human safety, but the funding needed to repair or rebuild the
bridge is also great. Because local scour can result in failure of these structures it has been of importance
to ﬁrst study the scour processes around these structures, to determine why scour could result in failure,
and thereafter ﬁnd methods to mitigate the scour or to make the structure able to withstand the scour.
Very few studies have been done about local scour of subsea structures. As oppose to the more traditional
structures, subsea structures have a ﬁnite height. Subsea structures can both be placed on the seabed,
but also be ﬁxed to the seabed. Even for the ﬁxed subsea structures the research of local scour is so scarce
that it is common to predict scour with formulas derived for vertical slender piles [Zhao et al., 2012].
For shallowly embedded subsea structures simply placed on the seabed, scour can result in undermining
of the foundation and settlement of the structure (see Figure 1.2). For artiﬁcial reefs this settlement
could be more or less detrimental to the purpose of the structure, depending on how the structure settles.
If the structure settles into one of the scour holes formed on either side of it, while still maintaining a
fairly upright position, one might argue that this is less detrimental to the purpose of the structure than,
if it topples onto its side when settling into the scour hole.
Figure 1.2
The aims for this thesis are to investigate the development of local scour around an artiﬁcial reef placed

freely on the seabed, to understand how this scour will make the reef settle, and when this settlement
will lead to the reef toppling onto its side. This will be done through experimental modelling, where the
artiﬁcial reef will be represented by simpliﬁed cuboid structures. This simpliﬁcation is done because no
work has before been undertaken concerning this particular problem.
Some ideal outcomes from achieving the aims of this research will be to ﬁnd a relation between the
properties of the reefs and their settlement behaviour, which can then be used to predict the stability of
a given reef. Along with this, it will also give an understanding of how a structure, that is not embedded
in the seabed, behaves when scour occurs. If this is possible to obtain, it may provide guidance for the
design of structures that will be placed on the seabed.

Theory and Literature review
As mentioned above, the amount of studies done on the local scour of a sea structure highly depends on
the type of sea structure in question. The more classical sea structures like vertical piles and pipelines
have been thoroughly studied, while comparatively few studies have been done on more untraditional
structures. These are structures like spherical bodies, square bodies with ﬁnite height, and of course
artiﬁcial reefs, which can have many diﬀerent shapes and sizes.
2.1
Traditional sea structures
The traditional sea structures includes vertical piles, both slender and non-slender, pipelines, and also
bridge piers. Local scour processes could lead to a failure of the structure, if it has not been taken into
account, when designing and placing the structures.
Piles
The ﬂow mechanisms occurring around a vertical pile in a marine environment, depends very much on the
dimensions of the pile, relative to the sea state. The ﬂow mechanisms around slender piles is very much
dominated by the horseshoe vortex and the lee-wake vortices created in its vicinity. For non slender piles
diﬀraction becomes more important. For slender piles it is in [Sumer and Fredsøe, 2002] stated how the
size of the horseshoe vortex depends on the height and shape of the pile. The size of the horseshoe vortex
would increase with increasing pile height, until a certain point. Also a square pile with a 0° rotation
to the ﬂow direction, will cause the separation of the ﬂow the occur earlier than for a circular pile. The
book also shows how the bed shear stress increases tremendously on the upstream side of a pile, which
will lead scour and probably the creation of a scour hole around the pile. Along with this the scour depth
increases with increasing velocity in the clear water regime, but is not signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by it in the
live-bed regime.
Pipelines
Scour around a pipeline resting on an erodible seabed initiates due to piping and tunnel scour underneath
the pipe. This could possibly leave sections of the pipe without any support and thereby putting more
force on the span shoulders. This scour process can also lead to sagging of sections of the pipeline, which
might aﬀect the function of the pipe.
In [Chiew, 1990] the initiation of scour processes around a pipe is investigated, along with the local scour
around the pipeline when the pipe is embedded 1/16D and 1/2D. The paper shows that piping and
tunnel scour can be avoided, if the pipeline is embedded 1/2D. The pressure gradient across the pipe,
which leads to piping, was also investigated, and how the pressure gradient can be reduced by placing an
impermeable plate on the upstream side of the pipe.
Pipelines can settle in two diﬀerent ways due to scour, which is described in [Sumer and Fredsøe, 2002].
The ﬁrst one occurs when a scour hole is created under a long section of the pipeline in between the span
shoulders, into which the pipeline will sag. The second when the pipeline sinks into the supporting span
shoulders as the sand underneath fails. This is more likely too occur when the shoulders are spaced more
closely together, than when the pipeline sags into the scour hole in between the span shoulders.
2.2
Subsea structures
For subsea structures, cases of local scour and self-burial have been studied. Along with ﬂow behaviour
around these structure after self-burial has occurred.
The self-burial process has been investigated both by ﬁeld observations and experiments.
Structures ﬁxed to the seabed
Spherical bodies ﬁxed to the seabed have been studied for several diﬀerent reason. Both to study the
ﬂow mechanisms developed around them in a ﬂow, but also their self-burial, as they will roll into the
scour hole created around them and thereby slowly bury themselves. The self-burial can be correlated to
the settlement of a diﬀerent shaped structures.
[Truelsen et al., 2005] studies the scour depth and self-burial around a sphere. The scour depth was found
to be /D = O(0.3) and the self-burial depth e/D = O(0.5), both in steady current. Both parameters
showed to be smaller in waves. The results also showed that scour would stop occurring when e/D > 0.8,

The local scour around a rectangular embedded block, also known as a caisson, has been studied

investigating how their aspect ratio and rotation relative to the ﬂow, aﬀects the local scour process around

and be deepest at the two upstream corners of the caisson, and scour did not start at the centre of the
caisson, before the scour holes from the corners had extended to the centre of the block. Furthermore,
for blocks with aspect ratio smaller than L/H = 1 the velocity ampliﬁcation around the corners of the
block, showed to be more important for the scour process, than the horseshoe vortex. For the blocks
which had the same aspect ratio and orientation to the ﬂow, as the block tested for this report, they
showed to have a relative scour depth S0/D = 0.741 −0.761 and a non-dimensional time scale for the
scour depth of T ∗= 0.254 −0.280, which is very similar despite of one block having double the length of
the other.
Structures placed on the seabed
For structures simply placed on the seabed, ﬁeld studies of the scour and burial of bottom mines were
done in [Inman and Jenkins, 2002]. These studies show how the bottom mines would scour, be buried,
and re-exposed again. Showing how the burial depth of a structure can change after it has settled. The
cylinders would also rotate on the seabed, depended on the direction of the wave crest.
Experimental studies concerning artiﬁcial reefs have been executed, but these studies show how the
gap distance between two reefs inﬂuence the scour depth between the reefs, rather than the scour and
settlement of the reefs themselves. The reefs tested had a hexagonal shape and were placed together in a
group with varying gap distance. The studies showed the scour depth to be greatest when the reefs were
placed with a 1D gap distance. The scour would occur in between the reefs and scour depth would be
almost double as great as when the reefs were placed with no gap distance. Therefore if artiﬁcial reefs
are placed close to each other, this also needs to also be taken into account when considering scour.

3
Experiments
As mentioned in the Introduction, experimental modelling has been chosen to investigate the scour process
around an artiﬁcial reef and how this process will result in settlement of the structure. Experimental
modelling has been chosen over numerical modelling as the scour and settlement involves many processes
which are hard to model numerically.
For simplicity the artiﬁcial reef will be represented by a cuboid with length, L, and height, H, and width,
W = H, placed with its length parallel to the ﬂow direction. This simpliﬁcation has been done as very
little work has been undertaken on undermining and scour induced settlement, making it useful to keep
the geometry simple. Actual artiﬁcial reefs are of course hollow and hace holes, which allows water to
pass through. Whilst future work could allow for this geometric porosity the present work is likely to
serve as a conservative representation of a reef.
The focus of this thesis will ﬁrst be to investigate the criteria for when a structure topples onto its side
into the scour hole, as oppose to settling into the scour hole in a fairly upright manner. Secondly, it
will be to study and understand the behaviour of the non-toppling structures when they settle and the
mechanisms that contribute to their settlement.
3.1
Dimensional reasoning
Before any settlement of a structure can occur, the scouring process must ﬁrst begin. The shape of the
scour hole and the mechanism with which the reef will settle into the scour hole, must in principle depend
on its geometric and material properties. When speaking about the geometric properties, this can be
quantiﬁed to some extent by the height of the reef, H, and the length of the reef, L, on the side parallel to
the ﬂow direction. The aspect ratio, H/L, of the reef will inﬂuence its settlement behaviour. The exact
relation will be tested in this thesis. The material properties of the reef is the density of the material
which the reef is made of. Again, this relation will be tested in the thesis.
In the section above, two parameters aﬀecting the settlement of a structure have already been mentioned.
This is the aspect ratio and density of the structure itself. The settlement may also be aﬀected by the
velocity of the ﬂow, U, as the velocity has an inﬂuence on the scouring process, but also the hydrodynamic
force on the structure. In addition to this the ﬂow is also aﬀected by the kinematic viscosity of the water,
ν.
The water depth and in this case thereby the boundary layer height, δ, will aﬀect the ﬂow, and
thereby the settlement. The density of the water, ρ, in addition to the density of the structure(block),
ρb, determines the submerged weight of the structure, and therefore also inﬂuences the relation between
the submerged weight and the hydrodynamic force. The density of the sediment, ρs, and the grain size,
d50, will inﬂuence the scouring process and thereby the creation of the scour hole. And at last the entire
setup will be aﬀected by gravity, g.
The settlement of the structure, inﬂuenced by all the parameters mentioned above, is deﬁned as translation
in the x- and y- direction along with rotation of the structure. In the expression below these are deﬁned
as Xe, Ye, and θ1e which represents the settlement values when the structure has stopped settling and
reached its equilibrium:

3.2
Parameter space covered in experiments
From the dimensional analysis above it is given which dimensionless parameters potentially could inﬂuence
the settlement of a block.
• The aspect ratio, H/L, of the block is expected to have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the settlement as
a longer block will be more stable and less prone to move than a shorter block.
• The water height is kept constant throughout all experiments, as deep water conditions are assumed,
and the behaviour of the block is therefore not aﬀected by the surface boundary conditions.
• The relative density of the block is varied by varying the density of the block itself, as it is very
relevant to investigate how heavy a material is needed when creating artiﬁcial reefs.
• The settlement is assumed not to be sensitive to the Reynold’s number, and this has therefore not
deliberately been varied although it will vary slightly with the change in ﬂow velocity.
• The Froude number, on the other hand, was kept small, as this is related to the ratio between the
hydrodynamic force and submerged weight. If this ratio becomes to great the block is no longer
stable in the ﬂow, which will aﬀect the behaviour of the block to the point where it is no longer
possible to observe the settlement.
• As the ﬂow velocity has showed to inﬂuence the scour depth (in clear water regime), the relative
ﬂow velocity has been varied to determine if it has inﬂuence on the settlement process.
• The relative grain size is very small and is therefore taken to not have any inﬂuence on the settle-
ment.
• From the geotechnical side of the problem the interface friction angle, φi, between the block and
the sand is varied by using diﬀerent materials, and by gluing sand to the bottom of the steel blocks.
• The internal friction angle is not varied. The stresses introduced into the soil by the blocks will be
relatively low compared to a full scale reef, which could lead to a larger internal friction angle. The
sand has been packed loosely before the blocks were placed on the seabed, making it more likely to
give a better representation of the internal friction angle for a full scale model.

3.3
Test setup
The inﬂuence of the parameters will be investigated by placing the blocks on a sandy seabed and exposing
them to a steady current. The tests will run until the blocks are no longer moving, meaning that they
have reached their equilibrium depth. The settlement, and in some cases the development of the scour
hole, will be recorded with cameras. The setup can be seen in Figure 3.1.
The experiments are executed in a ﬂume. The inside cross section for the ﬂume is 405mm wide and
580mm high, except for the middle section of the ﬂume, which has a height of 880mm, making it possible
to ﬁll in sand to create a sandy seabed. The blocks were each placed at approximately the same spot in
this sandy section. Far enough from the beginning of the sand section for the ﬂow to adjust to the new
bed surface.
Figure 3.1: Side view - middle section of ﬂume
For these experiments the middle section is ﬁlled with 16/30 sand and the ﬂume will be ﬁlled with water
to a SWL at 250mm.
The ﬂow velocity in the ﬂume was chosen through testing. The sandy seabed was evened out, as if a
block was to be placed there but without doing so. The ﬂow in the ﬂume was started and while closely
observing the sand particles, the velocity was slowly increased. The ﬁrst signiﬁcant particle movement
was observed at U1 = 0.311m/s, which was a greater velocity than the critical velocity, meaning that
particle movement did occur before U1 was reached. Nevertheless it was chosen to increase the ﬂow
velocity, in order to shorten the testing time. The velocity was therefore increased until the velocity
reached U2 = 0.351m/s, as more particles moved at this velocity, but no suspended bed-load was created.
This velocity would be the velocity at which the scour of all the blocks would be tested at, and also the
velocity for which the velocity proﬁle would be measured.
The ﬂow in the ﬂume was started by manual controlling, and through this the velocity of the ﬂow was
also controlled. It therefore took time for the ﬂow to reach the desired velocity, and as the velocity was
increased manually, the time it took also varied. For the velocity U2 = 0.351 the time it took for the

Internal and interface friction angle
The interface friction angle between the 16/30 sand and the blocks, is deﬁned herein as the angle at which
a non erodible inﬁnitely long slope will need to have, before sliding of the blocks are initiated. This angle
is estimated by ﬁrst gluing sand onto a ﬂat plank. A block is then placed on the plank and one end of the
plank is slowly lifted, until the block starts moving. The angle the plank had with horizontal when the
block started moving is the interface friction angle. One thing to note is that in reality the sand particles
are able to move in between each other, where as on the plank they are ﬁxed in place by the glue. This
might give a slightly diﬀerent angle, but the diﬀerence is assumed to be so small so that the angle found
with this method is taken to be the actual interface friction angle for this given scenario.
The internal friction angle is a measure of how much shear stress the sand will be able to withstand.
This angle is found by also gluing sand on the bottom of one of the blocks, and then follow the same
procedure as described above. This was done with the steel blocks as experiments with sand glued to
the bottom of these was executed. Again this method prevents the sand grains from moving in between
each other, but the angle found is still taken to be the internal friction angle which the 16/30 sand will
have during these experiments.
All three materials, glass, steel and lead, were tested, by using the blocks with aspect ratio L/H = 2,
as their bottom surface is greatest of all the blocks, which gives a more even contact between the two
surfaces, along with minimising the likelihood of the blocks toppling down the plank instead of sliding.

Hydrodynamic force and submerged weight
The hydrodynamic force acting on the blocks and the submerged weight of the blocks was also determined,
as the ratio between these two variables will tell whether or not the blocks are stable at the given velocity.
These calculations were undertaken to make sure that the velocities chosen were not so large, that they
would carry the blocks downstream rather than initiate scour and settlement.
The hydrodynamic force are determined by the following formula:
Fhydrodynamic = 1
2AρCd ¯U 2
(3.6)
Where A is the area of the block in the cross-ﬂow direction, ρ is the density of water at 20°C, Cd is the
drag coeﬃcient, and ¯U is the velocity of the water ﬂow at the height of the blocks.
As all the blocks have the same area in the cross-ﬂow direction, the hydrodynamic force will be the similar
for all the blocks tested, at the beginning of the experiment. The drag coeﬃcient, Cd, is assumed to be
1.
The submerged weight of the blocks is found by subtracting their unit weight with the unit weight of the
water displaced by them. This means that none of the blocks will have the same submerged weight.
Wsubmerged = (ρb −ρwater)V g
(3.7)
V is the volume of the block and g is gravity.
The hydrodynamic force is divided by the submerged weight. If the ratio is ≪1, the block is hydro-
dynamically stable. As the ratio gets closer to 1 the more unstable the block is, and more likely to be
carried downstream.
The Froude number is also related to the ratio between the hydrodynamic force and the submerged
weight, as the Froude number will have the same ratio if multiplied with the relative density and a drag
coeﬃcient.

From the ﬁgure it can be seen that as the aspect ratio of the blocks decreases, the equilibrium settlement

block length will aﬀect how deep the block will settle. When the longer blocks settled (L/H = 1.6 and
L/H = 2), they would hit the opposite slope of the scour hole, instead of reaching the bottom of the
scour hole. The shorter blocks (L/H = 1), on the other hand, ﬁtted into the scour hole, which meant
that they could settle to a greater depth.
Furthermore, as the longer blocks settle into the scour hole, they will have a more streamlined shape than
the shorter block. Their length will almost create a ramp, that will lead the water ﬂow over the block,
and not into the scour hole. The blocks with smaller aspect ratio will settle and have rotated to a steeper
angle, which means that they will be a greater obstacle to the water ﬂow, than if they had settled at a
more shallow angle, as the longer blocks. This will lead more of the water ﬂow into the scour hole, and
thereby lead to more scour.
No trends can be found when looking at the density of the blocks. The ﬁgure shows that the steel blocks
have the greatest settlement depth, lead tends to have the smallest and glass being in between. This
means that as long as the blocks are heavy enough to not be carried downstream by the ﬂow, their density
appears to have limited inﬂuence on the settlement depth.

equilibrium settlement depth is smaller than equilibrium scour depth for equal aspect ratios.
As mentioned above, the blocks tested for this thesis, are simply placed on the seabed and will settle

the ones with small aspect ratio, than embedded rectangular blocks, which will not move despite of

the force is ≪1, for the blocks. The greater velocity, U3 = 0.359m/s, is not expected to increase the
hydrodynamic force enough to have an inﬂuence on the settlement behaviour of the blocks. This is for
the simple reason, that U3 is not signiﬁcantly greater than U2.

4.5
Bearing area and initial time before settlement
As scour is initiated around the upstream side of a block, this will start around the upstream corners
before extending to undermining of the block. When the undermining starts, the bearing area of the
block is reduced. As this undermining continues, the block reaches a point where it is no longer stable
and will start to rotate and translate into the scour hole. The time it takes from the initiation of the
scour and until the block starts moving, is called the initial time. The onset of movement is deﬁned to
be when the block has moved 1mm, in either the x- or y-direction, from its initial position.
The glass blocks were ﬁlmed from above which made it possible to measure the reduction in bearing
area from the onset of scour, until the blocks started moving. The undermining started at the upstream
corners and spread towards the centre of the block in the shape of two triangles. When calculating the
area of these, it is assumed that these are of equal size and that they will meet at the centre line of the
block, and then move backwards away from the front edge of the block.
Figure 4.13 shows a picture of one of the glass blocks from above, just before it initiated settlement.
The dashed lines indicate the extend of the scour hole and undermining of the block, which means that
the part of the block being within the area deﬁned by the dashed lines, does not touch the sand and
is therefore not directly supported by it. In the picture the undermining has extended beyond the two
corners, connected at the centre of the upstream edge of the block and started moving backwards from
the edge.

4.7
Three phases in settlement
From all the investigations made above, it is possible to make some general statements for the settlement
behaviour of the blocks. More speciﬁcally the settlement can be separated into three phases, where cer-

to the next phase.
First phase
The ﬁrst phase is deﬁned when the undermining of the block has begun, but the block has not yet
started to move. This means that both the block rotation angle, θ1, the horizontal displacement,
x, and the settlement depth, y, must be equal to 0 in this phase.

of undermining is reached this block might never settle or topple into the scour hole, but remain
stable above the scour hole.

5
Discussion
This thesis has investigated the behaviour of the blocks, representing artiﬁcial reefs, when these have
been placed on a sandy seabed in steady current and a scour hole formed on the upstream side of the
blocks. It was observed when the blocks would topple, but also how they would settle, when they didn’t
topple. Among several things, their translation and rotation was recorded, along with the time it took
for them to reach an equilibrium when settling.
Whether an artiﬁcial reef topples or not due to scour is essential for the purpose of the reef, as a reef
that has toppled might not be able to provide the same habitat for the marine life as a reef which hasn’t
toppled.

to settle rather than topple due to scour. It is possible to compare this result with some theoretical
arguments. For example, on a stiﬀslope (Figure 5.1) a block will topple when:

For blocks that don’t topple the vertical settlement depth is likely to be a key parameter. The depth
to which the blocks would settle, depended on the aspect ratio of the block rather than the density.
This trend is in general seen through almost all the results in this report. The way in which the blocks
would settle, varied with the density of the block and the interface friction angle between the block
surface and the sand. The glass block tended to slide a longer distance than the steel and lead, once
movement had been initiated, but in between these sudden movements it would be at rest for a longer
time than both the steel and lead. These results did not change when the velocity was changed. This
could both be because the diﬀerence between the three chosen velocities was not signiﬁcantly great, or
because all of the velocities were greater than the critical velocity. Results from the literature have shown
that the scour depth does not change signiﬁcantly once the velocity has surpassed the critical velocity,
but increases signiﬁcantly from the onset of scour until the critical velocity is reached. The results from
these experiments could imply that the settlement depth follows the same tendencies as scour depth.
That being said, the settlement depth of all the blocks did show to be smaller than the scour depth of
embedded rectangular caissons of same aspect ratio[Zhao et al., 2012].
The average equilibrium settlement depth between the three materials for aspect ratio L/H = 1 was
Ye/H = 0.58. For the same aspect ratio the equilibrium scour depth of an embedded rectangle was
S0/D = 0.761[Zhao et al., 2012]. For aspect ratio L/H = 2 the average equilibrium settlement depth
was Ye/H = 0.41, while the equilibrium scour depth was S0/D = 0.741. For both cases the settlement
depth is signiﬁcantly smaller than the scour depth. Which has been explained in the results as the blocks
becoming more streamlined when they settle due to scour, which the caissons cannot, and will remain as
the same obstacle to the ﬂow. Furthermore the change of settlement depth with aspect ratio, is not seen
for scour depth, as the scour depth is almost the same for the two aspect ratios. The longer block where
too long to settle to the bottom of the scour hole, and would hit the opposite slope when settling, where
the shorter blocks did not, and could therefore settle deeper into the scour hole. Again the embedded
caissons do not settle, and the two aspect ratios also have the same cross-ﬂow area. One could therefore
argue that they obstruct the ﬂow in very similar ways, even though one caisson is twice as long as the
other.
The settlement depth of the blocks with aspect ratio L/H = 1 can also be compared to the scour and
self-burial depth of a sphere, as this is the aspect ratio closest to a spherical shape. One thing to note
is that the sphere was ﬁxed against any horizontal displacement for these experiments. The scour depth
of a spherical body was S/D = O(0.3) and the burial depth was e/D = O(0.5). The scour depth of the
sphere is much smaller than the settlement depth of the L/H = 1 blocks and the scour depth of the
caisson, which might be explained by the sphere being more streamlined than square blocks along with
the scour depth being measured underneath the sphere and not in front of it. The burial depth of the
sphere is only slightly smaller than the settlement depth of the L/H = 1 blocks. This might support the
point of the blocks becoming more streamlined when they settle, otherwise one would expect a square
block to have settled much deeper than a sphere.
When a block starts settling into a scour hole this obviously aﬀects the scouring process, and in this case
it is clearly mitigating the scour process. For artiﬁcial reefs this ﬁrst of all mean that they will not settle
as deep as a scour hole created in front of an embedded structure. Furthermore this could also mean that
once the critical velocity has been surpassed the settlement depth will not increase, even though the ﬂow
velocity might increase.
The time scale indicates how fast the settlement of the blocks occur. This is relevant for the reefs

indication of when to expect the reef to settle.
As seen for the majority of the results in this report, the time scale showed to depend on the aspect
ratio, as the time scale increased with increasing aspect ratio. The blocks with greater aspect ratio would
take longer to reach equilibrium settlement depth, even though the shorter blocks would have a greater
settlement depth.
As the time scale for the settlement depth and horizontal displacement are linked, so is their time scales,
which is seen through them being very similar for each block. The time scale for the block rotation angle
showed to be much shorter than the time scale for both the settlement depth and horizontal displacement.
This can also be seen through the development of the block rotation angle over time. The rotation reaches
an equilibrium value much faster, and while it is still settling in the x- and y-direction.
When the settlement depth was compared with the scour depth of an embedded rectangular caisson it
was seen that even though the scour depth was greater than the settlement depth, the time scale for the
settlement was more than 10 times greater than the time scale for scour. This is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence,
but one needs to take into account that for the caisson the sand is able to freely scour out of the scour
hole, while when the blocks settle into the hole, this will aﬀect the scouring process. Apparently to such
a degree that the time scale is increased with a factor of at least 10 for the same aspect ratios.
Therefore when placing an artiﬁcial reef on the seabed, the time its gonna take for the reef to reach its
settlement depth will be longer than if just a scour hole was formed on the upstream side of it. This also
means that more time will need to pass, before the full scale of the settlement can be seen.
By looking at the bearing area of a structure it is possible to determine the amount of undermining
of this bearing area needed, before the structure initiates settlement. This will also make it possible to
determine if a structure will even start settling, or if it will remain stable where it is.
The reduction of bearing area over time for the glass blocks was recorded and showed it needed to be
reduced to 60 −70% before settlement was initiated, regardless of aspect ratio and ﬂow velocity.
It was also seen how the undermining of the blocks would slow down over time and tend towards an
equilibrium value. This implies that the undermining will only continue to a certain point, not depending
on whether or not the block initiates settlement. This means that if the block is still stable when the
undermining has reached equilibrium, this block could remain stable and never settle, even though a
scour hole has formed on the upstream side of it. This is only valid for steady current with the same
direction, as for instance a wave from the downstream side would very likely push the block into the
scour hole as it has no support on the side where the scour hole has appeared.
If the ramp-up time for the ﬂow velocity had been the same across all experiments, it would also have
been possible to give an estimate of the percentage of undermining needed for the steel and lead blocks.
As the steel and lead blocks are heavier than the glass, and therefore need more support, one would
expect these to initiate settlement at an earlier stage than the glass blocks. How early is not possible to
say.
Through the settlement process, the bearing area of the blocks would increase each time the blocks
moved. As the scour would continue, so would the bearing area again begin to reduce. Whether or not
the bearing area again would need to reduce to 60 −70% as when it was at its initial position, would be
interesting to investigate, as the new position of the block could possibly have an inﬂuence.

6
Conclusion
For this thesis the settlement behaviour of a number of blocks was studied, when these were placed in
steady current and scour thereby initiated. The blocks were used as simple representations of artiﬁcial
reefs, as the settlement behaviour of an artiﬁcial reef has a vital inﬂuence on its function as a maritime
habitat.
A variety of blocks with diﬀerent densities and aspect ratios were tested. The results showed that blocks
with aspect ratio L/H = 1, or above, would settle due to scour, while blocks with aspect ratio L/H = 0.5,
or less, would topple. This was regardless of block density and ﬂow velocity.
For the blocks which did not topple, trends could be found in their settlement behaviour, which made
it possible to part the settlement into three phases.
The ﬁrst phase was deﬁned as when scour and
undermining has begun, but the block had yet to initiate settlement. The second phase, when the block
had initiated movement, but its rotation angle, θ1, was yet to reach its equilibrium. The third phase,
when the block was settling with its equilibrium block rotation angle.
The transition from the ﬁrst to the second phase in the settlement can be linked to the undermining of
the bearing area. The glass blocks showed to initiate settlement when the bearing area had been reduced
with 30 −40%.
The settlement depth of the non-toppling blocks decreased, with increasing aspect ratio. The normalised
settlement depth varied from Ye/H = 0.58 for the aspect ratio L/H = 1 to Ye/H = 0.41 for the aspect
ratio L/H = 2, as an average across all densities. When sand was glued to the bottom of the steel blocks,
this showed to reduce the settlement depth relative to the steel blocks without sand glued to the bottom.
As with almost all previous results the time scale for the settlement depended on the aspect ratio of the
blocks. All the time scales would increase with increasing aspect ratio. The time scale for the settlement
depth and the horizontal displacement were very similar, while the time scale for the block rotation angle
was much shorter. Sand was glued to the bottom of the steel blocks would increase the time scale even
though the settlement distance had decreased.
The time scale for the settlement depth showed to be in the order of 10 times greater than the scour
depth.
The block rotation angle, θ1, had an upper limit, which was deﬁned by the internal friction angle, φint,
but no visible lower limit, as the blocks initiated settlement at angles lower than their interface friction
angle.
This new understanding of the settlement, makes it possible to estimate the expected behaviours of an
artiﬁcial reef that does not topple. It is possible to design the reefs so that after these expected behaviour
(e.i. with appropriate aspect ratio) they can still serve their purpose despite of settlement.
For the blocks which did topple, L/H ≤0.5, they would do so within a very short time period.
Through observations for the non-toppling blocks, it was possible to deduce a criteria for when a block,
or reef, would be expected to topple.

6.1
Future work
These experiments have been the ﬁrst ones undertaken concerning this particular problem, and have
therefore been simpliﬁed compared to the real life conditions. It would therefore be relevant to ﬁrst
execute experiments with more complicated conditions. One of the ﬁrst things to do could be to test the
blocks used for these experiments in wave conditions, instead of steady current, to see how this would af-
fect their settlement process. Furthermore it could be relevant to test more complicated structures which
resembles a more typical artiﬁcial reef. By more complicated meaning a structure with non-uniform
density and made of a porous material.
As all the blocks were tested in live-bed conditions, where a change in velocity doesn’t have signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the settlement and scour process, it could be relevant to test these under clear water con-
ditions, where the literature has shown the scour process is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by a change in ﬂow
velocity.
The experiments indicated the blocks settling in two diﬀerent ways, and it could relevant to observe
and thereby understand these mechanisms better. This can be done by creating a setup where it will
be possible to observe the soil mechanisms, and thereby also determine if it in fact are the mechanisms
stated in this report. The knowledge from coastal and geotechnical engineering will thereby be combined,
and not only take into account one aspect of the problem, while ignoring the other one.
