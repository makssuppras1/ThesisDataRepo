Comparison of Heat Pump Design and Performance for Modern Refrigerants
by Stefan Nielsen
Due to an increased awareness of global warming, the types of refrigerants used in heat
pumps are changing worldwide. HFCs (High GWP) are still commonly used refrigerants
in countries such as the United States, where there are fewer regulations for refrigerants.
If stricter regulations are to be implemented, there can be a huge shift in demand for dif-
ferent refrigerants to be used in heat pumps. With possible future regulations for HFCs,
there may also be changes to the size of heat pumps due to limitations on the size of the
charge.
The charge plays a signiﬁcant role in determining the capacity of a heat pump, therefore
it is crucial to understand the distribution of the charge. This master’s thesis investigates
the ratio between the capacity and charge for natural refrigerants, HFCs, and HFOs for
a one-stage cycle. The model is validated through the thermodynamic and physical pa-
rameters which can inﬂuence the charge, the distribution of the charge, and the economic
aspects in terms of cost of heat, for each refrigerant.
The results shows that R717 is the most optimal refrigerant, due to its low charge and
cost of heat. When comparing R717 and a commonly used refrigerant used in the U.S.,
R134a, R134a exhibits a 84% higher charge and a 14% higher cost of heat than R717.
Furthermore, most of the charge is located in the heat sink, where it contains 39 %-58%
of the charge. However, R717 has its limitations due to its toxicity and ﬂammability,
suggesting that each refrigerant’s advantages also come with disadvantages. Since it
is evident that different properties for each refrigerant will have different implications,
adapting to possible future regulations will not be simple.

1 Introduction
With an increasing global population and thus an increased demand for energy, many
opportunities exist for energy optimization. Studies have shown that in Europe, a saving
potential of up to 42 % can be achieved in existing buildings through space heating [1].
Furthermore, a saving potential of 65 % can be obtained for water heating. In the United
States, space heating consumes up to 41 % of total energy consumption, whereas water
heating consumes 18 % [2]. The results also show that large cities such as New York gets
57 % of its space heating from natural gas plants and 29 % from fuel oil. These values
indicate that there is a large saving potential by improving the heating supply.
Heat pumps can be a potential solution for replacing usage of the natural gas and fuel
oil. Heat pumps have the beneﬁt of being able to produce heat out of electricity from
eg. renewable energy sources. Furthermore, heat pumps are able to provide a steady
efﬁcient heat production, but is highly dependent on the available heat source.
The heat pump’s efﬁciency is dependent on the properties of the working ﬂuid or refrig-
erant running inside the pump. In addition, the quality of a refrigerant depends on many
factors: the thermophysical properties, toxicity, ﬂammability, compatibility with materi-
als, price and impact on the environment, legal restrictions etc.
Recently, legal restrictions on certain refrigerants have been introduced in order to curb
their negative environmental impacts. Today HCFC refrigerants are almost phased out
and future regulations for HFC refrigerants are being al (GWP). For example, the emis-
sion of (only) 1 kintroduced, due to their high global warming potentig of a HFC refrig-
erant can cause the same greenhouse warming effect as a car driving between 10.000 km
and 30.000 km 1 [3].
Negative environmental impacts from the usage of speciﬁc refrigerants are also resulting
in a worldwide change on the refrigerants used in heat pumps. For example, Denmark
is a leading country in using environmental friendly refrigerants and introduced a legal
limit of 10 kilogram charge for HFC [4] in 2007. The result of these legal restrictions was
a 67 % increase in usage of natural refrigerants from 1993 to 2012 [5]. In contrast, coun-
tries with large markets such as the U.S. do not have the same regulations, as the HFC
refrigerants are still commonly used. If future regulations are to be implemented, there
can be substantial impacts on the way heat pumps are designed, which can change the
entire heat pump market.
Due to regulations in many parts of the world, natural refrigerants have achieved al-
ready parity with HFC refrigerants. Furthermore, new refrigerant substitutes such as
HFO are being introduced and new refrigerants are constantly in development. Because
of these regulations, the impact that the heat pump can have on the environment be-
comes extremely important. This impact can be estimated by knowing the exact amount

2.1
Cycle
By nature’s laws, heat energy can only be transferred from a high temperature region to
a lower temperature region. If heat energy is to be transferred from a lower temperature
region to a higher temperature region, external energy must be added. An example of a
system that adheres to this principle is a heat pump. A heat pump works by absorbing
heat energy from a cold region (heat source) and releasing it to a warmer region (heat
sink).
A heat pump or refrigeration system utilizes the principle that "external energy is required
for a ﬂuid to increase its internal energy". The internal energy is dependent on the pressure,
volume, and temperature (i.e. pressure increases with increasing temperature assum-
ing constant volume). Therefore, the phase transition can be determined by changing the
pressure to match the external conditions. When the working substance changes from gas
to liquid it releases energy, and when it changes back from liquid to gas it absorbs energy.
Operating in the supercritical region, the substance will not condensate and will there-
fore not change phases. Rather, it will merely cool the supercritical substance, thereby
changing the internal energy. A heat pump consists of four main parts: a compressor, a
condenser/gas cooler, a throttle valve, and an evaporator. In addition to the main compo-
nents, it also consists of the refrigerant piping, valves, control and regulating equipment
and safety equipment. A principle sketch of the vapor compression system (heat pump)
is presented in ﬁgure 2.1 and illustrates the nomenclature used in this project.

Working ﬂuid
The ﬂuid that runs through heat pump is called the working ﬂuid/refrigerant. In general,
all ﬂuids can be used as a working ﬂuid, but a number of thermodynamic and physical
criteria have to be satisﬁed before a ﬂuid can be considered a working ﬂuid [8]. Besides
having the proper thermodynamic and physical properties, the ideal working ﬂuid has to
be safe (non-explosive, non-toxic etc.), available at a low price, and thermally/chemically
stable. The choice of refrigerant used in a heat pump is also important due to poten-
tial environmental impacts, particularly in the case of possible leakages/spills to its sur-
roundings. As a focus on environmental impact became more important in society, ODP
(Ozone Depletion Potential) and GWP (Global Warming Potential) was introduced to
provide measurable impact indications on the ozone layer and greenhouse effect. Today
HCFC (hydrochloro-ﬂuorocarbon) refrigerants are almost phased out and future regula-
tions for HFC (hydro-ﬂuorocarbon) refrigerants are being introduced due to their high
GWP. Because of these regulations, natural refrigerants have achieved parity with HFC
refrigerants. Furthermore, new refrigerant substitutes such as HFOs are being introduced
to the market and new refrigerants are constantly in development.
HFC refrigerants are still being widely used in the world due to their high variety of
components. The most commonly used refrigerants are R134a and R410a, for industrial
purposes [9]. HFCs such as the two named above are beginning to be out phased in
some countries, such as Denmark, due to their high GWP [4]. Thus, natural refrigerants
in these countries are frequently used as working ﬂuids for industrial heat pumps. Com-
monly used natural refrigerants are R717, R290 and R744. Because of different thermo-
dynamic and physical properties, an HFC refrigerant cannot be retroﬁtted with a natural
refrigerant. This issue led to the development of HFOs, which generally have the same
thermodynamic properties as HFCs. A common replacement for R134a is R1234yf [10].

2.3
Compressors and Operating Conditions
With increasing demand for different applications to meet different needs, there are now
more types of compressors available. Currently there are two primary groups of com-
pressors: the dynamic and the positive displacement type.
• Positive displacement compressors works by ﬁrst trapping the gas in a chamber.
Afterwards, it will reduce the volume which will increase the pressure and temper-
ature. A valve will open when the pressure overcomes the resistance from the high
pressure side.
• Dynamic displacement compressors work by speeding up the gas to a high veloc-
ity and then reducing the velocity by restricting the gas, causing the pressure to
increase.
The selection of a compressor type depends on its intended application. The dynamic
displacement compressor types are generally used for base load requirements, while pos-
itive displacement compressors are preferred for variable load. For this project, the recip-
rocating piston compressor will be used, since this project is aiming for industrial usage
with high efﬁciency, variety load demands, and high pressure ratio [11].

same pressure limitations apply to the transcritical process, where the temperature and
pressure are not directly dependent on each other. The exit temperature from the com-

3 Methodology
This section will focus on the methods used for construction of the heat pump simulation.
The heat pump is assumed one stage and is presented in ﬁgure 2.1. Plate heat exchangers
will be used in this simulation, where pinch point analysis will be used to ensure that
thermodynamic conﬂicts do not occur. Using correlations from the literature, a more
detailed analysis will be used to ﬁnd the surface area. Furthermore, exergoeconomics
will be used validate the economic aspect of the analysis.
3.1
Cycle
A simulation for subcritical and transcritical cycles are produced. In both simulations a
one stage steady state is assumed. The cycles are simulated as follows:
• Compressor - The compressor will lift the low pressure level to a high pressure
level. The high pressure level is determined from the desired condensing temper-
ature or gas cooling pressure. The compressor is not considered adiabatic or re-
versible, thus an isentropic efﬁciency and heat loss factor are given. The isentropic
efﬁciency compensates for friction loss and other losses, whereas the heat loss factor
compensates for the heat loss to the surroundings.
• Condenser (subcritical) - The superheated gas from the compressor will now enter
the condenser. The heat is now released to the heat sink through an isobar process,
which is further investigated in section 3.3. The ﬂuid will undergo three phases:
superheated gas, followed by phase transition, and then liquid phase. Knowing
the desired heat transfer rate and inlet/outlet enthalpy, the mass ﬂow can be deter-
mined from equation 3.1. The mass ﬂow is then used for calculating the heat rate
from the evaporator as well as the amount of mechanical work.
• Gascooler (transcritical) - The gascooler has the same purpose as the condenser,
which is to release heat to the heat sink through an isobar process. The difference
is that the gascooler does not undergo same phase changes as the condenser when
heat is released to the heat sink. As previously mentioned, the temperature and
pressure are not dependent on each other when working in the supercritical region.
Thus the high pressure level must be calculated through a pinch point analysis and
an optimal pressure level must be found.
• Throttle Valve - The throttle valve will expand the liquid (if it’s the condenser)
or the cooled gas (if it’s the gascooler) to the desired evaporation pressure. It is
assumed that no mechanical work is supplied, no heat is exchanged with the envi-
ronment, and there is no change in the kinetic energy, thus assuming an isenthalpic
process.
• Evaporator - The purpose of the evaporator is to absorb heat from a heat source.
The ﬂuid will undergo two steps: evaporation and superheating. The process is
assumed isobar (neglecting pressure loss), thus constant temperature will occur
when the ﬂuid evaporates.

4 Assumptions
When creating a thermodynamic model, assumptions play an important role in ensur-
ing that the model is realistic. Assumptions can vary depending on where the compo-

assumption will be described using products from company websites and values from

4.1
Plate Heat Exchanger
A plate for the plate heat exchanger is a complex geometry as a result of companies’ con-
stant endeavors to create highly efﬁcient plate heat exchangers. In this thermodynamic
model a simple plate geometry will be used, due to correlation limitations and available
values from companies. As showed in ﬁgure 3.5 there are several unknown parameters
which need to be established. These parameters can inﬂuence the surface area, which
then inﬂuences the amount of charge needed.
4.1.1
Plate pattern
The inside pattern of a plate heat exchanger can vary depending on its purpose and the
refrigerant used. Manufacturers do not provide information about the pattern of their
individual plates, meaning that the inside geometry must be decided from the literature
and guesses based on the literature.
The literature uses a simple symmetric plate geometry. The symmetric plate pattern is
a universal pattern which can be used for many different substances, such as oil, milk,
air etc. Different patterns have been developed for refrigerant purposes, ex. SWEP has
a plate pattern called AsyMatrix for refrigerant systems, see ﬁgure 4.1. AsyMatrix was
developed to compensate for the different properties of the refrigerants, which results in
a high heat transfer with minimal pressure drop. It also reduces the necessary volume
and charge.

5 Overall heat transfer coefﬁcient
For a one-stage cycle, three components can contribute to the total charge: the condenser/
gascooler, evaporator, and compressor (if assumed safety components such as separa-
tor, pipes etc. are neglected). The compressor charge is determined by the volumetric
efﬁciency, whereas as the charge for the condenser/gascooler and evaporator are deter-
mined by the surface area, channel gap, and density (see equation 3.36). The surface area
depends on the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient. Thus, a refrigerant’s ability at transfer-
ring heat becomes important when the determining the necessary charge.
This section will compare the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient for the six different refrig-
erants presented in table 2.1. Furthermore, the inﬂuence the different parameters have
on the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient will be investigated.

6 Parameters impact on the charge
As previously described, the surface area is an important parameter to take into account
when determining the charge. The previous chapter found that the overall heat transfer
is at its highest for both phase transitions and is heavily inﬂuenced by the equivalent
mass ﬂux. In this chapter, the key parameters that inﬂuence the charge and the cost of
heat are analyzed.
6.1
Pinch Point Variation
The pinch point determines the driven temperature difference of the surface area in the
plate heat exchanger. An increase in the pinch point will result in a decrease in COP and
an increase of the thermodynamic irreversibility. However, there will be a reduction of
the necessary surface area. The gascooler is not included in this calculation due to differ-
ent optimization approaches, where it is calculated by the maximum COP and therefore
not relying on the pinch point. This can also be seen in ﬁgure 7.7 where the pinch point
does not affect the charge.
FIGURE 6.1: Pinch point inﬂuence of the surface area and COP
As the above ﬁgure shows, the surface area will decrease signiﬁcantly with an increase
of the pinch point, which is caused by increased condensing and evaporation tempera-
tures. To validate the inﬂuence of the pinch point, the cost of head is now included. The
economic aspects are evaluated using the capital investment described in Appendix A.
Purchased equipment cost (PEC) is calculated using the surface area and volume ﬂow
[30] [31]. Figure 6.2 presents an example of the pinch point effect of the charge and cost
of heat using R134a.

mic temperature increases and the surface area decreases. With a decrease in surface area,
the charge will decrease as well.

culating the charge. The ﬁgures indicate that the temperature is the driving force for the
charge. It also indicates that the rate at which the density increases (due to higher tem-
perature) is slower than the rate at which the surface area decreases. Therefore, a higher
temperature difference (higher ∆Tpp) will be preferred if the charge should be minimized.

10 Discussion
This report focuses on a one-stage system which is the simplest system that can be cre-
ated. Furthermore, the model uses reciprocal compressors and plate heat exchangers,
which ensures equal conditions for each refrigerant. However, this will not apply to real
life applications. The main difference will be seen for R717 which normally will be built
with a ﬂooded evaporator. R744 will also contain more components, due to its high pres-
sure and varied pressure levels in the gascooler. For example, a medium pressure vessel
would be added to ensure constant pressure to the throttle valves, which is needed to
make the throttle valve operate correctly. Furthermore, R290 may have an internal heat
exchanger which superheats the gas from the evaporator even further. This is due to pos-
sible liquid hammering in the compressor and can be illustrated in a log(p), h- diagram,
which shows that the compression may crossover the saturation line.
A one-stage system only consists of 3 components: evaporator, condenser/gascooler, and
compressor. This suggests that the plate heat exchangers will have a signiﬁcant role when
the charge is found. The surface area is found by the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient,
which is determined by correlations. Using today’s literature, the correlations are calcu-
lated using a certain type of plate heat exchanger with a certain refrigerant. As ﬁgure 5.1
and 5.2 indicates, there is a signiﬁcant difference in overall heat transfer coefﬁcient when
different correlations are used. This suggests that there is uncertainty when calculating
the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient. Furthermore, the correlation used for R744 subcriti-
cal phase is a theoretical approach which was made for single phase ﬂuids, and therefore
may not be an entirely correct approach for a subcritical ﬂuid.
For each correlation, the plate geometries must be determined. The geometries are di-
vided into outside and inside. The outside geometries are easily found through technical
drawings from manufacturers, but are not optimized for the different temperature vari-
ations and refrigerants. To compensate for this problem the model will calculate the
outside geometries through a common pressure loss in the secondary side. This will op-
timize the height, so that surface area will achieve its minimum. But as ﬁgure 7.4 and
ﬁgure 7.5 shows, the pressure loss is highly sensitive to parameter variations. This sug-
gests that this may not be the most sufﬁcient approach to ﬁnding the minimal surface
area.
The dimensions of the inside geometries cannot be found due to manufacturer policies,
thus the inside geometries are then assumed using today’s literature. Using the geome-
tries from the literature has its drawbacks because the literature often uses small plate
heat exchangers, hence the geometry assumptions used in this model should vary. In
this model the inside geometries are held ﬁxed to simplify the simulation. This creates
an issue when the capacity changes, since the pressure loss will increase substantially
from the channel gap being too low.
The economic assumptions made in this report may result in some uncertainties. The cost
investment for the the entire plant is calculated based on a ratio between the main com-
ponents. This ratio is based on assumptions from the literature, thus validating that this
==PAGE:96==
76
Chapter 10. Discussion
ratio requires further knowledge. Additionally, the purchased equipment cost is based
on a single supplier, where the price is based on a speciﬁc type of component. A more
thorough investigation should be conducted for future work.
Using EES as a solver program results in a comprehensive analysis of parameter varia-
tions. Though, some assumptions must be simpliﬁed to achieve results in the appropriate
project timeframe. For example, the pressure and heat loss from the plate heat exchanger
are neglected. If a more detailed study should be conducted with more variables, a supe-
rior programming software should be used.
Based on the information gathered, it is recommended that if HFCs should be used, the
condenser should be optimized to decrease the charge. This can be done by increasing
the temperature difference between the refrigerant and secondary ﬂuid or by changing
the physical geometries in the plate heat exchanger. There are various physical geome-
tries that can minimize the charge, such as chevron angle, the number of plates, and the
channel gap. The channel gap is the most inﬂuential parameter and should be decreased
if the charge should be minimized. However, minimizing the channel gap results in more
channels to compensate for the pressure loss. By increasing the number of channels the
maldistribution in the plate heat exchanger becomes important. In this model the ﬂow
is assumed to be uniformly distributed. However, in reality the ﬂow maldistribution is
non-uniformly distributed and ultimately no ﬂow will arrive in the last channel. This can
inﬂuence the thermal and physical performance of the heat exchanger if the number of
channels is increased substantially. This was also investigated in [33] where it was con-
cluded that it is questionable to assume uniform distribution.
Sometimes the components cannot be changed due to their effect on other parameters.
Another way to minimize the charge is to increase the pinch point, though an increase
in the cost of heat will occur. This suggests that with the possibility of more regulation
on HFCs, the cost of heat will also increase if the charge has to be minimized. Since
further regulation on HFCs will be inevitable, implementation of environmental refrig-
erants will have to occur. If HFCs should be replaced with more environmental friendly
refrigerants, R717 will be the ideal refrigerant to use due to its low cost of heat and small
charge. This is also shown in article Ommen et al. [34] where R717 proved to be the
superior refrigerant compared to other natural refrigerants and R134a. The article inves-
tigated technical and economic working domains of heat pumps, where they considered
1000 kW industrial heat pump. However, R717 has limitations due to its toxicity and
ﬂammability, which is indicated in the ASHRAE safety group. This can be compensated
by placing the heat pump away from the heating object and using a secondary working
ﬂuid. R1234yf could also be used as a replacement for HFCs. However, this refrigerant
increases the cost of heat.

11 Conclusion
This report investigates the charge in a one-stage heat pump cycle for commonly used re-
frigerants in Denmark and the United States. Furthermore, the distribution of the charge
and the impact of parameter variation have been investigated. The commonly used re-
frigerants in the U.S. (R134a and R410a) has negative impacts on the environment, al-
though they are safe to work with. By setting a limitation on the amount of refrigerant
that can be used, the components must optimized so it can minimize the charge.
Pipes are neglected in this calculation, but have a signiﬁcant contribution to the charge.
However, the pipe length often depends on the building environment and increases lin-
ear with the length. For example, if 1 meter of pipes is implemented, the total charge for
R134a would increase by 40 %.
From changing the driven temperatures, it was discovered that the subcritical cycle be-
haves identically for all refrigerants and follows the saturation lines in a log(p), h- dia-
gram. This change is due to the amount of latent heat and density change. For R744, the
charge depends on where the optimal pressure is located which ultimately depends on
the speciﬁc volume lines in the subcritical region.
By investigating the distribution of the charge in a heat pump, it was concluded that the
condenser/gascooler contains most of the refrigerant, with 39 - 58 % of the charge. The
condenser/gascooler charge depends on the surface area and density. The surface area
is dependent on the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient and the temperature different. The
calculations indicate that changing the temperature difference between the secondary
ﬂuid and the working ﬂuid decreases the surface area. Although, doing this increases
the exergy destruction and thereby increases the cost of heat. The overall heat transfer
coefﬁcient can also be altered by changing the physical geometries. Through sensitivity
analysis and parameter variations, it is concluded that the parameters which determine
the mass ﬂux between the channel gap was the main contributor to the overall heat trans-
fer coefﬁcient. Furthermore, the chevron angle can increase the overall heat transfer coef-
ﬁcient by increasing the turbulent ﬂow. The channel gap can also have a signiﬁcant role,
though the maldistribution can become a problem.
Results shows that R134a and R410a have a 84 % and 90 % larger charge than R717,
respectively. Furthermore, the cost of heat for these two refrigerants are 14 % and 11 %
higher than R717. R717 is the most feasible refrigerant due to its small charge and low
cost of heat. R717 has a 65% smaller charge than R290, which has the second smallest
charge. The replacement refrigerant for R134a, R1234yf, had a 10 % smaller charge com-
pared to R134a, though the cost of heat was higher by 10.7 %. An alternative refrigerant
could be R744 which does not have any impact on the environment and uses a transcriti-
cal cycle. R744 is highly dependent on the temperature lift from the heat sink, where the
COP can increase by 47.2 % by changing the temperature lift, ∆Tsink, from 20 K to 40 K.

main model includes whereas the charge is calculated. Following functions/model have

and R1234yf)
• Transcritical heat pump: 1 stage cycle using a gascooler. (R744)
