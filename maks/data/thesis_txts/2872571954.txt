Abstract
Researchers have been using eye tracking since the nineteenth century to uncover dis-
tinct reading patterns. Some readers take their time, while others skim or focus on key-
words.“Reading the Reader” is a research project at the Technical University of Denmark
in collaboration with the Royal Danish Academy of Architecture, Design, and Conserva-
tion.Its goal is to create a reading application with an adaptive user interface that can
automatically assess the effect of typography settings and apply changes to the inter-
face to provide an optimal reading experience in real time.Building on the “Reading the
Reader” project led by Professor Per Bækgaard at the Technical University of Denmark,
this research investigates how eye-tracking data can reveal individual reading styles and
model them.
I have utilized the publicly available MECO eye tracking corpus to extract key gaze fea-
tures, such as fixation and saccade measurements.By applying an unsupervised machine
learning approach, I grouped readers into three distinct clusters based on their character-
istic gaze patterns.This clustering method aims to differentiate reading styles solely from
basic eye movement data.
The findings from this research show that publicly available datasets like MECO can help
us group readers based on how they naturally read, using clustering methods. This could
lead to adaptive read ing tools that fit each reader’s style. It might also help spot unhelpful
reading habits, reading patterns that could be avoided to reduce eye strain and protect
vision in the long run.

Introduction
Reading behavior varies across individuals, yet our understanding of it has often been
generalized. In recent years, advances in eye tracking and machine learning have en-
abled a new era of personalization, one where reading patterns can be modeled, classi-
fied, and even predicted at the individual level. This transition marks a critical shift from
population-level averages to individualized reader profiles, making it possible to tailor ed-
ucational, diagnostic, and technological tools to specific reading behaviors [1, 2].
Eye movements provide valuable insights into cognitive and psychological processes,
reflecting attributes such as identity, mood, and mental health. [3, 4]. Visual perception is
our primary interface with the world, especially in social contexts: the human brain recruits
over 30 regions to process social stimuli visually [5, 6]. These neural mechanisms not only
help us recognize facial expressions and gestures but also underpin complex behaviors
like reading.
Reading, from a neurocognitive and eye tracking standpoint, is not a uniform process. It
involves orchestrated movements fixations, saccades, and regressions that vary between
individuals and contexts. Eye tracking makes these variations measurable, offering in-
sights into how readers navigate text, manage cognitive load, and comprehend meaning
[7, 8].
This individuality in reading has long intrigued researchers. Early work by Kanás et al.
[9] showed that even basic gaze position data could distinguish quick from slow readers.
Liao et al. [10] went further, identifying five nuanced styles speed reading, slow reading,
in-depth reading, skim and skip, and keyword spotting based on eye movement metrics.
More recently, Davalos et al. [11] captured fine-grained reading behaviors in real world
classroom settings, revealing the dynamic nature of reading strategies. Studies like [12,
13] further emphasize how variations in fixation and parafoveal processing reflect differ-
ences in reading intent and efficiency. Building on this foundation, the present study con-
tributes to the emerging field of individualized reading analytics. Specifically, I investigate
whether consistent reader types can be identified from the MECO-L2 eye tracking dataset
using unsupervised clustering. I then compare these empirically derived groups with es-
tablished theoretical models, such as those from Hyönä & Nurminen (2006), considering
both their strengths and possible limitations. [14], to explore how empirical reading pat-
terns align with known cognitive profiles.
This research is also inspired by the ongoing Reading the Reader project at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU), in collaboration with the Royal Danish Academy of Archi-
tecture, Design, and Conservation. The project aims to make reading more accessible
especially for individuals with visual impairments through the use of adaptive systems.1
This chapter introduces the motivations for exploring individual reading patterns, defines
the research objectives, highlights the study’s key contributions, and concludes with an
overview of the thesis structure.

1.1
Motivation
Humans are inherently individualistic each of us possessing a unique combination of pref-
erences, strengths, and learning styles much like the distinct patterns found in our fin-
gerprints [15].This diversity indicates that a ”one size fits all” approach to education or
content delivery often falls short, as it overlooks the deeply personal nature of our cog-
nitive processes. In both learning and technology contexts, recognizing and supporting
individual variation is essential.Adaptive systems that respond to a learner’s moment to
moment needs have been shown to enhance engagement and improve comprehension
outcomes. [16, 17].
Reading behavior is a powerful example of this individuality in action. Even when two
readers engage with the same text, they may do so very differently. Some skim quickly
for keywords, while others read every word carefully, and many switch strategies based
on their purpose.[18, 10].Eye-tracking research has shown that vocabulary knowledge,
working memory, and task goals all influence how long we fixate on words and when
we make regressions. [1].Cognitive models, such as E-Z Reader, further demonstrate
that readers differ significantly in their lexical processing speeds, leading to distinct eye
movement patterns. [19].
This natural variation suggests that reading tools should not treat all users the same.
Personalized reading environments ones that adapt presentation, pacing, or in text as-
sistance based on behavioral cues can better support readers at every skill level [20, 21].
Such personalization aligns with inclusive design principles and holds potential for improv-
ing reading accessibility, especially for individuals with cognitive or learning challenges.
Over the last two decades, researchers have made progress in identifying recurring read-
ing patterns. For example, Hyönä and Nurminen (2006) introduced a model distinguish-
ing reader types based on regressions and rereading behaviors.Large scale datasets like
MECO [22] have expanded this research across multiple languages, offering a rich basis
for cross linguistic analysis of reading patterns. However, most prior work stops at cate-
gorization and has not fully explored whether naturally emerging reader clusters identified
through unsupervised methods align with theoretical models.
This project aims to bridge that gap.Using the MECO-L2 Wave 1 dataset, I will extract core
gaze features, apply clustering to identify natural reading profiles, and evaluate those clus-
ters against Hyönä and Nurminen’s theoretical reader types. In doing so, I hope to move
one step closer to personalized, reader aware systems that adapt to how each individual
processes text a foundation for more inclusive and effective reading technologies.
1.2
Initial Problem Statement
Reading is a cognitively demanding activity, and individuals approach it in diverse ways.
These differences appear in eye movement patterns, such as fixation duration, regres-
sions, and skipping behavior. While previous studies highlight the value of such metrics,
many rely on controlled settings or unavailable annotations, limiting their applicability to
open datasets.
This thesis examines whether distinct reading styles can be uncovered from the MECO-
L2 Wave 1 dataset, using only available gaze features. Prior classification efforts often
depend on region-of-interest mappings or comprehension scores, which are absent here.
Instead, this work develops a reproducible clustering approach tailored to the dataset’s
constraints.
It includes data from 543 participants across 12 L1 backgrounds, making it ideal for multi-

1.2.1
Research Objective
The objective of this study is to investigate whether individual reading behaviors can be
identified using unsupervised clustering techniques. The focus is on grouping participants
based on their eye movement features such as fixation duration, regressions, and skipping
behavior recorded during natural reading. To evaluate whether the resulting clusters are
meaningful, they are compared against established models of reading styles from prior
literature. The aim is to determine whether consistent, interpretable groupings emerge
without relying on predefined labels.
1.2.2
Scope of the Project
This project uses the MECO-L2 Wave 1 dataset, which includes sentence-level eye-
tracking data from 543 bilingual participants across 12 native language groups. The anal-
ysis is limited to features available in the dataset and does not involve comprehension
scores or region-of-interest annotations. Instead, it applies unsupervised clustering to a
standardized set of gaze-based features. The scope includes developing a feature-based
analysis pipeline, extracting cluster patterns, and comparing these patterns to theoretical
reading groups. The study does not aim to predict comprehension or test intervention
outcomes, but rather to examine variability in reading strategies at the participant level.
1.3
Structure of the Thesis
This thesis is structured into six main chapters, followed by a bibliography and appen-
dices. Each chapter builds upon the previous, moving from theoretical foundations to
implementation, evaluation, and interpretation of clustering-based reading behavior anal-
ysis.
• Chapter 1 – Introduction: Introduces the motivation, research objectives, and
overall scope of the study. It also outlines the structure and key contributions of
the thesis.
• Chapter 2 – Background and Related Work: Reviews relevant literature on eye
movements during reading, cognitive models of reading styles (including Hyönä &
Nurminen, 2006), and prior gaze-based profiling approaches. This chapter also
formulates the final research questions.
• Chapter 3 – Methods and Materials: Describes the MECO-L2 Wave 1 dataset, pre-
processing steps, and the methodology for gaze feature extraction. It explains the
rationale for feature selection and standardization, details the clustering approach
(k-means), and outlines the evaluation strategy, including both internal metrics and
comparison with theoretical reader groups.
• Chapter 4 – Results: Presents the outcomes of clustering using different feature
configurations (3-feature, 8-feature, and 3+1 models). Evaluation includes PCA vi-
sualizations, silhouette scores, confusion matrices, and heatmaps comparing clus-
ter profiles to theoretical G-groups. SHAP-based feature interpretation is also in-
cluded to assess which gaze features influenced the cluster assignments.
• Chapter 5 – Discussion: Interprets the results in light of theoretical reading models
and prior empirical findings. It discusses the coherence and cognitive plausibility of
the discovered clusters, the validity of the evaluation approach, and methodological
limitations.

Prerequisites
To fully understand the concepts and analyses presented in this thesis, readers are ex-
pected to have familiarity with the following areas:
• Descriptive and inferential statistics: Including means, standard deviations, dis-
tributions, z-scoring, and hypothesis testing.
• Unsupervised learning: Especially clustering techniques such as k-means and
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), and associated evaluation metrics like silhouette
score, ARI, and NMI.
• Experimental design in behavioral studies: With an emphasis on within-subjects
designs commonly used in reading and cognitive research.
• Eye-tracking methodology: Understanding of key concepts such as fixations, sac-
cades, regressions, skipping, and common eye-movement measures used in read-
ing research.
• Dimensionality reduction and distance metrics: Basic understanding of PCA,
Mahalanobis distance, and cosine similarity, which are used to interpret high-
dimensional clustering results and align with theoretical profiles.

Background and Related Work
Reading is not a uniform process; individuals exhibit varied cognitive strategies and be-
havioral patterns when engaging with text.These differences ranging from fluent and effi-
cient reading to more effortful and analytical processing are often reflected in distinct eye
movement signatures.exploiting such patterns, recent studies have demonstrated that
readers can be meaningfully clustered into behavioral profiles using gaze derived fea-
tures [23, 24].
This thesis builds on that line of inquiry, using the MECO-L2 dataset a multilingual, large
scale eye tracking corpus to identify individual reading styles through unsupervised clus-
tering.Rather than comparing predefined demographic or linguistic groups, the focus here
is on revealing naturally occurring behavioral structures embedded in the eye movement
data. To lay the groundwork, the following sections introduce the principles of eye track-
ing, the core components of eye movement behavior, and their cognitive interpretations.
2.1
Eye Movements During Reading
During reading, our eyes don’t glide smoothly along the lines of text. Instead, they alter-
nate between rapid movements known as saccades and brief stationary periods called
fixations (Rayner, 1998)[1]. Fixations are when the eye pauses to extract visual and lin-
guistic information, while saccades allow the eye to move to the next target word or region.
These fundamental units of eye movement offer a real time window into the reader’s cog-
nitive processing.
Several eye tracking features have proven essential in understanding how readers pro-
cess written language.These include fixation durations, saccade lengths, skipping be-
havior, and regression rates.For Eaxmple,longer fixation times are often associated with
increased cognitive load or lexical difficulty (Rayner, 2009) [25],and regressions typically
indicate comprehension problems or reanalysis (Frazier & Rayner, 1982; Liversedge &
Findlay, 2000) [26] [27]
In this thesis, we draw on these established metrics to characterize individual reading
patterns. Our data source the MECO L2 Wave 1 corpus offers rich fixation based infor-
mation across a multilingual population but does not include certain features like region
of interest (ROI) mappings or parafoveal preview measures that are common in more fine
grained experimental studies.However, it does provide sufficient metrics such as first fixa-
tion duration, total fixation duration, skipping rate, and regression in frequency, which are
widely accepted as indicators of reading strategies ( Hyönä & Nurminen, 2006). [28] [29]
By systematically analyzing these features, this project aims to uncover latent reading
styles using unsupervised clustering and compare them to theoretical groups described
in prior literature.
2.2
Eye-Tracking and Reading
Eye tracking is a technology that records the movement of the eyes relative to a visual
stimulus, typically using infrared cameras and real time image processing.In reading re-
search, this technique captures both where a person looks (spatial position) and how long
their gaze remains fixed (temporal duration), allowing researchers to reconstruct the read-
ing experience at a granular level.

One of the main strengths of eye tracking is its ecological validity: it records natural, spon-
taneous reading behavior without interrupting the reader. Unlike comprehension tests or
think aloud protocols, eye tracking captures online processing as it unfolds, making it ideal
for investigating real time decision making and attention allocation[30].
This makes eye tracking particularly valuable for identifying individual differences in read-
ing.While traditional studies often report group averages, recent work emphasizes the
need to model variability across individuals especially in educational or multilingual set-
tings where learners exhibit diverse backgrounds and proficiencies [31, 32].Eye tracking
opens a window into these differences by making it possible to quantify and compare
reading strategies at the level of individual participants.
In this thesis, eye tracking data from MECO-L2 is used not just to observe reading, but
to infer individualized patterns from fixation and saccade metrics.These behavioral sig-
natures are then used as the basis for unsupervised clustering, offering a data driven
alternative to traditional classification approaches.
2.2.1
Eye Movement Components and Cognitive Interpretation
Reading involves a sequence of distinct eye movements as fixations, saccades, regres-
sions, and refixations that correspond to different stages of visual and linguistic processing
[33]
• Fixations are the brief pauses during which the eye remains relatively still, allow-
ing the brain to process the visual information. Average fixation durations during
reading range from 200 to 250 milliseconds, though they may increase with lexi-
cal complexity or processing difficulty [1, 34].Longer fixations often signal unfamiliar
vocabulary, syntactic ambiguity,or semantic integration effort.
• Saccades are the rapid movements between fixations. During saccades, visual
input is suppressed, but the distance and direction of the saccade reflect reading
strategies.Short, local saccades often indicate detailed word level reading, while
longer saccades may signal scanning or efficient lexical access [35, 36].
• Regressions are backward saccades when the eye returns to previously read ma-
terial.These movements typically occur in response to comprehension problems or
structural ambiguity and are thus seen as a marker of comprehension monitoring or
error correction [37, 38].
• Refixations refer to multiple fixations on the same word or region. They often signal
uncertainty or the need for additional processing, especially when readers struggle
to integrate lexical or syntactic information [39, 23].
Together, these components form the basis of most eye tracking based cognitive models
of reading. For instance, the E-Z Reader model suggests that fixation duration is directly
linked to lexical access speed, while regressions provide clues about the reader’s real
time monitoring and repair mechanisms [8].

2.3
Theoretical Models of Reading
Over the past decades, a substantial body of eye tracking research has revealed that read-
ers exhibit systematic differences in their visual behavior differences often linked to read-
ing skill, cognitive strategy, or demographic background.Foundational work by Rayner
(1998) [1] demonstrated that skilled readers are generally characterized by shorter fixa-
tion durations, longer saccades, and fewer regressions, whereas less proficient readers
exhibit more frequent fixations and backward eye movements. These findings laid the
groundwork for later models that sought to categorize readers into distinct types based
on measurable eye movement patterns.Among the most influential frameworks is that of
Hyönä and Nurminen (2006) [14], who proposed three theoretical reader groups derived
through clustering analysis of L1 eye tracking data those groups based on progressive,
rereading, and look back fixation durations.These distinctions were expanded by Hyönä
(2002)[40] to include Fast Linear Readers, Slow Linear Readers, Non selective Review-
ers, and Topic Structure Processors using sentence-level gaze metrics. Parallel develop-
ments followed in multilingual and educational contexts. Cop et al. (2017) [41]identified
Adaptive and Struggling L2 readers using clustering on skipping and fixation features,
while Liu et al. (2021) used a Gaussian mixture model on gaze data to differentiate read-
ers based on task comprehension. Ashby et al. (2005)[42] distinguished Phonological vs.
Visual decoders among adults, and Berzak et al. (2017) [43] used syntactic gaze metrics
to reveal native language reading biases. Rivas et al. (2021) [44] introduced Skim Skip,
Goal Oriented, and In Depth reader types through task based clustering, while Liao (2017)
[45] identified Speed Readers, Slow Readers, and Keyword Spotters among college stu-
dents. More recent work by Zhou and Chan (2023) [46] applied unsupervised learning to
categorize attention dynamics in silent reading, reaffirming the need for multidimensional
modeling. Despite these advances, existing models often rely on small samples, narrow
age bands, or task specific constraints.Our study builds on this foundation by synthe-
sizing Hyönä’s fixation-based taxonomy and applying them to the large-scale MECO-L2
corpus.This allows for the discovery and validation of new L2-specific reader profiles, in-
formed by both theoretical alignment and empirical clustering.
2.3.1
Theoretical Reader Groups Based on Fixation Behavior
Hyönä and Nurminen (2006) introduced three prototypical reader groups based on differ-
ences in fixation-based reading strategies, as revealed through eye-tracking data. These
groups were derived from cluster analyses using first-pass fixation durations, rereading
behavior, and look-back fixation times. The reader types are defined as follows:
• G1 (Fast linear readers): Characterized by short progressive fixations and minimal
regressions. These readers process text in a streamlined, left-to-right manner with
little need for rereading.
• G2 (Slow linear readers): These readers exhibit longer fixation durations than G1
but still process text in a mostly linear manner. They may allocate more time to
individual words without frequent regressions.
• G3 (Topic Structured): Defined by frequent regressions and extended rereading
and look-back fixations, suggesting a more integrative or cautious reading strategy
involving backward saccades to re-evaluate prior content.
These theoretically grounded profiles serve as a reference framework in this thesis for
evaluating whether empirically derived clusters (via K-means or GMM) reflect meaningful
cognitive reading styles.

2.3.2
Gaze-Based Reading Styles
Reading behavior can be categorized into distinct styles based on eye movement pat-
terns that reflect cognitive strategy, engagement, and processing efficiency. Classical tax-
onomies often differentiate between reading, skimming, and scanning behaviors—each
linked to characteristic gaze patterns [1, 37, 47]. Reading involves sequential fixations
and moderate regressions; skimming is marked by longer saccades and fewer fixations;
scanning is characterized by brief, erratic fixations and large saccades, often for informa-
tion retrieval.
More recent eye-tracking research has extended these categories by identifying empiri-
cally grounded reader profiles based on fixation durations, saccade lengths, and regres-
sion patterns. For example, [48, 49] describe readers with fast, linear eye movements
and strong comprehension—analogous to the “Fast Linear” readers in Hyönä and Nurmi-
nen’s framework. Conversely, readers with long fixations, short saccades, and frequent
regressions—linked to slower, effortful processing—resemble “Slow Linear” or “Careful”
readers [50, 51].
Other studies report erratic gaze patterns with short fixations and low comprehension,
indicative of disengaged or “Shallow Processing” styles [23, 7]. Additionally, [52, 40] de-
scribe “Strategic Readers,” who flexibly adapt their gaze behavior depending on task or
text complexity. In contrast, “Surface Readers” show minimal engagement, short fixa-
tions, frequent skipping, and often poor comprehension outcomes [53, 54].
These profiles offer a cognitive framework for interpreting the reader groups discovered
via clustering in this thesis. Although these categories are not mutually exclusive, they
help contextualize how eye movement features can reflect processing depth, monitoring,
and reading intent.

3.1
Dataset Description
This study uses the Multilingual Eye-Movement Corpus (MECO), an open-access re-
source designed to support multilingual and second-language reading research. Initiated
by Victor Kuperman and Noam Siegelman around 2021, MECO was developed under
the Open Science Framework (OSF) to address the lack of standardized, comparable
eye-tracking data across languages and populations.
MECO includes several waves of data.The MECO-L1 dataset covers eye tracking and
background data from 580 participants reading in their native languages across 13 lan-
guages.The MECO-L2 dataset focuses on second language English reading, with data
from 543 participants who speak 12 first languages (L1s).All participants read identical
English passages while their eye movements were recorded under uniform experimental
protocols.
This thesis uses MECO-L2 Wave 1 (version 2.0), which includes word and sentence level
gaze metrics from 543 adult L2 readers.Its consistent design and multilingual scope make
MECO-L2 ideal for analyzing variation in reading behaviors across individuals and lan-
guage groups.
Other publicly available corpora also support eye-tracking research in reading. GECO
(Ghent Eye-Tracking Corpus)[61] features bilingual Dutch English novel reading in a nat-
uralistic setting.The Provo Corpus [62] contains sentence level English reading data with
rich syntactic annotations.CELER [63] includes data from 365 native and non native En-
glish readers for multilingual comprehension research.
While these datasets offer valuable insights into specific areas like natural reading in
GECO, predictability in Provo, multilingualism in CELER, MECO stands out for its method-
ological consistency, high participant count per language, and its sentence-based, cross-
linguistic design. These strengths make it particularly suitable for clustering-based iden-
tification of individual reading patterns.The dataset was previously used in studies such
as Siegelman et al. (2022) [31] and forms a strong empirical base for analyzing individual
and group-level reading behaviors.

3.3
Clustering Methodology and Evaluation
This section describes the clustering techniques and evaluation procedures used to iden-
tify reading behavior profiles among participants based on eye-tracking features. The
methods applied here were designed to uncover latent groupings in the data without rely-
ing on predefined labels. Special care was taken to ensure interpretability, reproducibility,
and scientific alignment with the literature.
Figure 3.5 presents a schematic overview of the full analysis pipeline used in this study.
Beginning with the raw MECO-L2 eye-tracking data, the process includes preprocessing,
feature extraction and validation, z-score normalization, unsupervised clustering (3- or 8-
feature), and cluster alignment with Hyönä and Nurminen’s theoretical G-group profiles.
The pipeline concludes with interpretation and result evaluation.
3.3.1
Clustering Methodology
To uncover meaningful reading patterns from the extracted gaze features, a set of un-
supervised clustering techniques were applied. These methods aimed to group partici-
pants with similar eye-movement profiles without requiring predefined labels. Following
the structure proposed by Hyönä and Nurminen [66], clustering was initially performed on
three standardized fixation-related features (progressive, rereading, and look-back dura-
tions), and then extended to include additional behavioral measures.
Clustering Algorithms
Two unsupervised methods were used in this study:

• K-means Clustering: This partitioning algorithm assigns each participant to one of

K-means assumes spherical clusters and is suitable for identifying compact, well-

and 3+1 feature models.

the data as a mixture of multivariate Gaussian distributions, allowing soft proba-
bilistic assignments. This is advantageous when cluster boundaries are not clearly
separable. GMM was used to validate results from k-means on selected configura-
tions ( 3F vs. 8F).
Both algorithms were implemented in R using the kmeans() and mclust::Mclust() func-
tions, with a fixed number of clusters k = 3 to allow comparison against the theoretically
grounded G-groups.

three clusters offer a good trade-off between complexity and explained variance. This
supports our decision to use k = 3 consistently across both the 3-feature and 8-feature
models.

SHAP Model Setup
To interpret feature importance in the clustering process, we employed SHAP (SHapley
Additive exPlanations) values [67], which quantify each feature’s contribution to model
output. Because SHAP is inherently designed for supervised models, we trained a surro-
gate XGBoost classifier to approximate the unsupervised K-means cluster assignments.
This approach is widely adopted in model interpretability research, where surrogate mod-
els are used to provide post-hoc explanations for black-box systems [68, 69]. We chose
XGBoost due to its strong performance on tabular data, robustness to feature collinear-
ity, and compatibility with tree-based SHAP explanations. The classifier was trained to
predict cluster labels from the same features used in the clustering process.
Cluster Interpretation via SHAP Analysis
To improve the interpretability of the cluster solutions and examine which gaze features
most strongly influenced participant grouping, we applied Shapley Additive Explanations
(SHAP) using a surrogate modeling approach. Although SHAP is primarily used in super-
vised learning, prior studies [67] have shown it can be adapted for unsupervised clustering
by treating cluster assignments as pseudo-labels in a classification task.
In our analysis, we trained an XGBoost classifier to approximate the k-means clustering
decision boundaries. XGBoost was selected due to its strong performance on tabular
data, robustness to multicollinearity, and compatibility with SHAP-based interpretation.
The classifier was trained to predict cluster labels from the same features used in each
clustering model.
We applied this surrogate modeling strategy to the following configurations:
• the 3-feature baseline model (prog_dur, reread_dur, lookback_dur),
• the full 8-feature model including behavioral metrics ( rate, skip, refix,
comprehension),
• five 3+1 feature variants, where one behavioral feature was added to the core fixation-
based trio.

3.3.4
Evaluation Metrics
To assess the quality and validity of the clustering outcomes, three complementary eval-
uation strategies were employed:
• Silhouette Score: This metric evaluates the cohesion and separation of clusters by
comparing intra-cluster distance to the nearest inter-cluster distance. Scores range
from -1 to 1, with higher values indicating more distinct and compact clusters. This
was applied to both 3-feature and 8-feature models to assess internal consistency.

external metrics compare the unsupervised cluster labels with the Hyönä-style G1–

by prog_z, reread_z, and lookback_z.

This chapter presents the empirical findings of the clustering analyses conducted on the

feature set to predict each participant’s cluster label.
SHAP values quantify the marginal contribution of each feature to the model’s predictions,
offering a consistent and model-agnostic framework for interpretability. In our case, they
reveal which behavioral and fixation-based features most influenced the assignment of
participants to their respective clusters.

features such as comprehension score and skipping behavior showed negligible contri-
butions in this configuration, suggesting that cluster separability may be driven primarily
by reading speed.

Interpretation as Transfer of Reading Profiles.

compared against the G-group prototypes from Hyönä and Nurminen (2006) using cosine
and Mahalanobis distances. This comparison allows for the cognitive reading profiles from
the original study to be transferred into the MECO-L2 domain, serving as cognitive an-

signature transfer method within transfer learning.
Evaluation Metrics
• Silhouette Score: Measures intra-cluster cohesion vs. inter-cluster separation. Val-
ues close to 1 indicate well-separated, dense clusters; values near 0 suggest over-
lapping groups.
• Adjusted Rand Index (ARI): Quantifies similarity between clustering and ground truth
labels, adjusted for chance. Ranges from -1 to 1; higher is better.
• Normalized Mutual Information (NMI): Measures the amount of shared information
between cluster labels and true group labels. Ranges from 0 to 1.
• Cosine Distance: Values close to 0 indicate better alignment (directional similarity);
values approaching 2 reflect complete dissimilarity due to inverse directionality in
z-scored space.
• Mahalanobis Distance: Accounts for the distribution and correlation structure of fea-
tures, providing a multivariate distance to G-group centroids. Lower is better.

Discussion
The central objective of this thesis was to investigate whether individual differences in
eye-tracking behavior during reading could be used to uncover meaningful reader group-
ings, particularly those that do not rely on conventional population-level averages.. The
final problem statement focused on identifying naturally occurring reader profiles using
eye-movement features, and evaluating their correspondence with the cognitive reading
groups GG1–GG3, as defined by Hyönä and Nurminen (2006).
The findings presented in Chapter 4 confirm that unsupervised learning on fixation-based
features can reveal interpretable reader clusters. Clustering with three standardized fix-
ation metrics (progressive, rereading, and look-back durations) revealed three distinct
groups with partial alignment to the theoretical G-groups. Including additional behavioral
features—such as skipping, regression-in, refixation, reading rate, and comprehension—
improved cluster differentiation and alignment. In particular, the 8-feature model showed
the strongest similarity to GG1 and GG3, while the 3+refix model offered a good balance
between simplicity and alignment quality.
Interpretation of Cluster Profiles
Based on their proximity to the theoretical G-group centroids, the clusters derived from
the MECO-L2 data can be interpreted as follows:
• Cluster 1 (GG3-aligned): Exhibited elevated rereading and look-back durations.
This pattern reflects cognitively effortful reading and repeated integration, consistent
with the GG3 profile observed in thorough or verifying readers.
• Cluster 2 (GG2-aligned): Showed intermediate fixation values across all features,
suggesting a flexible reading strategy. These participants may adjust their engage-
ment level based on sentence complexity or comprehension goals.
• Cluster 3 (GG1-aligned): Demonstrated the lowest progressive, rereading, and
look-back fixation durations. This matches the GG1 profile of fast, linear readers
who proceed with minimal regressions or rereading.
All cluster interpretations are grounded in numerical similarity to GG1–GG3 centroids and
are not treated as definitive cognitive labels.
Interpretation of Clustering Outcomes
The clustering analyses particularly those based on three fixation features and on the
extended eight feature set revealed consistent and interpretable patterns in reading be-
havior across participants. Each model captured distinct gaze-based reading strategies
that reflect underlying cognitive processes.
In the 3-feature model, clusters were derived using standardized progressive, rereading,
and look-back fixation durations, which are closely tied to early, integrative, and revi-
sional stages of reading, respectively. Cluster 1 in this configuration was characterized
by uniformly low values across all three dimensions, suggesting a group of readers who
progress through text with minimal rereading or regressions. This pattern is consistent
with fluent and efficient reading. Cluster 2 exhibited higher rereading and look-back du-
rations while maintaining moderate progressive fixation times, reflecting a more careful

The 8-feature clustering model provided a richer behavioral landscape by incorporating

Contribution Beyond Existing Work
This thesis advances gaze-based reading research by applying unsupervised clustering
to identify reader profiles without relying on predefined group labels. While prior studies
using MECO-L2 have focused on classification tasks ( L1 prediction [41, 31]), this work
enables group discovery grounded in behavior, not annotation.
Key contributions include:
• Integration of fixation-based metrics progressive, rereading, and look-back dura-
tions with higher-order behavioral features skipping, refixation, and a derived com-
prehension score to enrich the clustering framework and capture diverse dimensions
of reading behavior.
• The development of a composite comprehension metric that connects gaze dynam-

beyond surface-level behavior.
• Application of quantitative distance metrics (cosine and Mahalanobis) to evaluate
the alignment between emergent clusters and theoretically established reader pro-
files from Hyönä and Nurminen (2006), offering a principled method for external
validation of the unsupervised grouping.
Although this study does not implement a full transfer learning pipeline, it incorporates
cluster signature transfer [70], where G-group centroids act as anchor profiles and are
compared with MECO-L2 clusters using cosine and Mahalanobis distances. This sup-
ports the transfer of theoretical knowledge across datasets and demonstrates how domain
knowledge can guide unsupervised discovery in behavioral data.
Alignment with Theoretical G-Groups
The primary objective was to evaluate whether the unsupervised reader clusters derived
from MECO-L2 Wave 1 gaze data showcase meaningful alignment with the theoretical G-
groups (GG1–GG3) proposed by Hyönä and Nurminen (2006), which characterize read-
ers based on standardized fixation patterns specifically progressive, rereading, and look-
back durations. GG1 denotes fluent readers with minimal regressions, GG2 corresponds
to more deliberate, rereading-oriented readers, and GG3 includes those relying heavily
on look-backs, indicating integrative or verification-driven strategies.
To evaluate alignment, we compared the z-scored centroids of each cluster to the theo-
retical group profiles using cosine and Mahalanobis distances. Cosine distance captured
pattern similarity, while Mahalanobis distance incorporated feature variances, providing a
robust measure of alignment.
The 8-feature model showed the strongest correspondence with the theoretical frame-
work. Cluster 3 aligned closely with GG1 (fast linear readers), showing high skipping
rates and low fixation durations. Cluster 1 resembled GG3, with elevated rereading and
look-back metrics and lower comprehension scores. Cluster 2 aligned moderately with
GG2, reflecting a more strategic or cautious reading approach.
The 3-feature model also recovered key distinctions between GG1 and GG3 but was less

features such as regressions and comprehension in clarifying nuanced reader differences.
Across models, consistent matches were observed between Cluster 3 and GG1, and

Limitations
This thesis, while methodologically grounded, is subject to several limitations that inform
the scope of interpretation.
First, the analysis was based on the MECO-L2 Wave 1 dataset (English only), filtered to
438 participants based on accuracy, skipping, and coverage criteria. While this filtering
ensured data quality, it may have excluded typical yet valid reading behaviors.
Second, only eight of eleven extracted features were used in clustering. The standard-
ized fixation metrics (prog_z, reread_z, lookback_z) were excluded to preserve indepen-
dence from the theoretical G-group mapping, possibly at the cost of sensitivity to fixation-
driven distinctions.
The behavioral measures used such as skipping rate, regression-in frequency, and com-
prehension score—offer summary-level indicators of cognitive processes but may not
capture subtler phenomena like syntactic reanalysis or discourse-level processing. For
instance, the look-back feature reflects broad regression patterns, not fine-grained verifi-
cation processes.
Interpretation of clusters remains inherently post hoc. While supported by distance met-
rics and theoretical profiles, cluster labels rely on relative centroid behavior and are subject
to ambiguity, especially in cases of overlapping or hybrid patterns.
Finally, z-score standardization—while necessary for comparability and clustering stability—
removes absolute timing information. Distance metrics, therefore, should be interpreted
as relative indicators of profile similarity rather than exact cognitive equivalence.
Future Work
Future studies could extend this work along multiple dimensions. Incorporating Wave 2 of
MECO-L2 would enable longitudinal validation of whether reading profiles remain stable
across texts and tasks. This would strengthen claims about the consistency of gaze-based
reader traits.
Feature-wise, future work could add word and sentence-level linguistic metrics for exam-
ple, cohesion, complexity) or fine-grained eye-movement events. Such features could
help clarify the cognitive triggers of rereading, skipping, or regressions.
Alternative clustering methods such as DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering may capture
more complex or nested reader subgroups.
Dimensionality reduction techniques like
UMAP or t-SNE could support improved visualization of higher-dimensional gaze data.
A promising avenue involves semi-supervised clustering, where theoretical group profiles
( GG1–GG3) guide initialization or regularization of cluster structures. This could blend
cognitive theory with data-driven discovery more explicitly.
Further validation could link clusters to independent cognitive traits such as L1 back-
ground, working memory, or reading scores to test whether behavioral clusters map onto

known reader characteristics.
A long-term application is the development of adaptive reading systems. If reader profiles
can be robustly detected using eye-tracking, such systems could personalize feedback
or instructional content based on real-time gaze data.
Finally, the current approach can be expanded into a transfer learning (TL) framework.
Strategies include:
• Domain Adaptation: Initialize clustering on MECO using statistical profiles (means,
variances) from Hyönä’s data to enforce cross-dataset alignment.
• Feature Alignment: Normalize shared features across datasets and project both into
a common latent space ( via PCA or UMAP).
• Cross-Dataset Evaluation: Train a classifier on Hyönä-style groupings and apply it
to MECO to test generalization of cognitive group structures.
• Cluster Signature Transfer: Continue using theoretical G-group centroids as refer-
ence signatures for interpreting new data clusters.
These strategies could help establish whether cognitive reading styles are stable across
populations and corpora, and support future generalization of gaze-based personalization
models.
This study shows that distinct and meaningful reader groups can be uncovered from eye-
tracking data using unsupervised clustering techniques. By applying clustering algorithms
to fixation-based and behavioral gaze features extracted from the MECO-L2 Wave 1
dataset, we identified consistent patterns of reading behavior that align with theoretical
models of reader cognition.
In summary, the findings provide strong initial evidence that clustering gaze-based fea-
tures can yield psychologically interpretable reader groupings. While limitations remain,
this work lays a foundation for more personalized and theoretically grounded models of
reading behavior, with the potential to inform both research and application in education
and cognitive science.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the potential of uncovering meaningful reader groupings from
eye-tracking data using unsupervised clustering techniques, with the MECO-L2 Wave 1
dataset as a case study. Eleven gaze-based features were extracted to characterize indi-
vidual reading behavior, including fixation durations (progressive, rereading, look-back),
skipping and regression rates, refixation frequency, reading speed, and comprehension
accuracy. Of these, eight features were used for clustering, while three standardized
fixation metrics were retained for post-hoc alignment with theoretical reading profiles.
Clustering was performed using both a minimal (3-feature) and an extended (8-feature)
feature set. The 3-feature model, based solely on standardized fixation durations, already
revealed separable gaze strategies. The 8-feature model further enhanced interpretability
and delivered stronger quantitative performance, including improved silhouette scores
and Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), indicating better within-cluster cohesion and alignment
with external references.
A central component of the evaluation involved comparing data-driven clusters against
the theoretically established G-group profiles (GG1–GG3) defined by Hyönä and Nurmi-
nen (2006). Distance-based alignment using cosine and Mahalanobis metrics confirmed
that several discovered clusters—particularly from the 8-feature and selected 3+1 mod-
els ( 3+refix) closely mirrored the behavioral patterns of GG1 and GG3. These results
support the central claim of this thesis: that unsupervised methods can reveal cognitively
meaningful reading profiles from eye-tracking data alone.
Unlike prior work focused on classification or population-level analyses, this study prior-
itized the modeling of individual differences through a theory-aware, data-driven frame-
work. By allowing natural gaze patterns to emerge without prior label constraints, and
subsequently comparing them to psycholinguistic profiles, the study offers a robust bridge
between empirical clustering and cognitive reading theory.
In conclusion, this thesis provides empirical support for the use of unsupervised clustering
in identifying psychologically interpretable reader groupings from eye-movement data.
The results underscore the potential of this approach for scalable, language-independent
profiling in multilingual reading contexts, with implications for adaptive reading systems,
educational tools, and cognitive assessment platforms. Future work could build on this
framework by incorporating temporal gaze sequences, validating across additional MECO
waves or L1 backgrounds, and linking cluster membership to downstream outcomes such
as comprehension scores or syntactic processing difficulty.

Feature Diagnostics Summary
The following statistics summarize key characteristics and diagnostic checks performed
on the final feature dataset (N = 437 participants):
• Complete Cases: 68.9% of participants had full data for all selected features.
• Look-back Availability: 68.9% had valid look-back measurements, indicating suc-

in early lexical processing times.

dimensions and supports downstream clustering analysis.
