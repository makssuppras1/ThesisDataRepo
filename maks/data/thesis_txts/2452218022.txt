Te Impact of Global Sulphur Cap on Maritime Transport
Abstract
On 1st January of 2020, the upper limit of sulphur outside Emission Control Areas (ECAs) is going to
be lowered from 3,5% to 0.5%. Compliance can be achieved using either low-sulphur-fuels or abatement
technologies, with other solutions being still in a pilot stage. Although the signiﬁcant improvements in
humans’ well being the side eﬀects of the upcoming sulphur cap, seem to be controversial to International
Maritime Organisation (IMO) targets for reduction in CO2 emissions. Compliant solutions increase green-
house gas (GHG) emissions which can be worse if the fuel prices increase. Te increase in fuel prices
can lead to an increase in freight rates;as a consequence, modal shifs or speed alterations are likely to
happen and could have an negative impact on CO2 emissions. At the same time, the reduction of sulphur
in the atmosphere enhances the radiative cooling. In addition, oil reﬁneries emit more CO2 for the pro-
duction of low sulphur fuels in comparison with the heavy fuels due to desulphurisation processes. Te
main focus of the study is to investigate and assess the impacts of Sulphur Cap on CO2 emissions as they
are described through the literature. Te compliance choices are tested on three Mediterranean routes in
both container and Ro-Ro vessels, and a Cost-Beneﬁt Analysis is conducted to assess the ﬁnancial and
environmental impacts of the regulation. However, as long as the shipowners are not being taxed for the
environmental impacts of their choices, the decision- making process for compliance is based solely on
proﬁtability criteria. In that sense, the results of this work could be useful for the design of new strategies
and improvements to avert the negative impact of the sulphur cap in GHG emissions.

Introduction
Te environmental urgency due to climate change created a shif towards ecological sustainability
among societies, companies and policymakers. Te transport sector, as the fastest contributor to climate
emissions, has to deal with a series of green measures. Reduction of marine pollution is considered as
one of the main objectives of the IMO, which under the MARPOL convention aims at the elimination or
minimization of marine pollution provoked by routine operation of ships or marine casualties and involves
either oil or other hazardous chemicals.
Being part of MARPOL Annex VI, the global sulphur cap will enter into force in 2020 tighten the limits
of sulphur emissions both close to the coast and in high seas. From the 1st January of 2020 the limit in
the sulphur content of the fuel used for vessels propulsion is changed from 3.5 % m/m 1 to 0.5% m/m
and continues stable to 0.1% m/m in Emission Control Areas2 (ECAs). Until 2050, IMO targets also at the
reduction of GHG emissions at least 50% in comparison with 2008 level.
Despite the short period lef for the application of the new regulation, the route to compliance remains
challenging. Shipowners have to deal with both the considerable investment required for the compliance
methods (compliant fuel, exhaust gas cleaning systems and retroﬁts in case of older ships) and the uncer-
tainty concerning the availability, the cost and the quality of compliant fuels. Shipowners not only need
to consider the cost-eﬀectiveness of the chosen solution but also evaluate the ﬁnancial sustainability and
proﬁtability of their choice in the long-run and under stricter limits.
All of the above mentioned, in conjunction with the unpredictable changes in the regulatory landscape
and the doubts whether the imposed ﬁnes are adequate to force compliance to all the shipping operators
can cause unfair competition and market distortion. Te unfair competition and the high-cost of new
technologies leave no option to shipowners but to increase the freight rates and pass the additional costs
to the cargo owners. Te shipping sector is going through crises, and the weakest shipping companies are
in danger to disappear if they don’t build their resilience and try to create win solutions.
Focusing on the reduction of air pollution, Sulphur Cap can prove to be one of the signiﬁcant contrib-
utors in GHG emissions which is contrary to the green targets of a low-carbon shipping industry till 2050.
Te reduction of SOx emissions via scrubbers increases fuel consumption and hence the CO2 emissions.
Besides that, the intensive energy processes in the reﬁnery level due to desulphurization of fuels is an extra

2.1
Regulatory framework of 2020 sulphur emissions
Te Global Sulphur Cap is part of the MARPOL Annex VI convention for the prevention of air pollution
caused by ships, as it was introduced in 1997 by IMO. Te regulations shaped through ANNEX VI concern
the control of ozone-depleting (ODS), nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulphur oxides (SOx) and volatile organic
compounds (VOC) and their contribution to local and global air pollution and the further reduction of
these pollutants in designated ECAs [1] . Annex VI put into force on 19th May 2005, and from 2010 the
requirements concerning the air pollution were stricter.
According to MARPOL Annex VI, from the 1st January of 2020, the content of sulphur in fuel oils used
onboard is dropping from 3.5% m/m to 0.5% m/m for commercial ship trading outside ECAs, and remains
stable to 0.1% m/m inside ECAs. Considering the oil fuel availability in 2020, presented by CE Delf [3] ,
IMO strictly decided not to provide extra transition period afer the 1 st January of 2020 for the burning of
non-compliance fuels, and prohibit the carriage of non-compliance fuels on board till 1st March of 2020.

IMO approves a range of options for compliance with the current regulation. Compliant low sulphur
fuels (LSFO) are either distillate or hybrid oils. A variety of new ulta low sulphur fuel oil (ULSFO) has also
recently start developed in the market. Except that, IMO permits the use of abatement systems also known
as scrubbers, as an alternative solution for shipping companies to ”clean” vessel’s emissions before they
released in the atmosphere. Liqueﬁed natural gas (LNG) is also proposed as an available solution mostly
for new ships. However, the high cost involved with these solutions and the uncertainty coming from the
diﬀerent types of fuels that they are concerned, seem to make the implementation of the regulation far
more complex.
Te only regular exception from the carriage of non-compliant fuels afer the 1st March of 2020 con-
cerns the equivalent compliance mechanisms. According to Annex VI, Regulation 4, ships with compliance
mechanisms allowed to bunker and burn high sulphur fuels, with the excess sulphur ’scrubbed’ out of the
uptake exhaust gas [5]. Heavy fuel oil transported carriage permited only as cargo by tanker ships, and

Annex VI, Regulation 18 also states requirements for the quality of oil fuel used onboard. According
to this, fuel oils must not contain any substance or chemical waste which can put at risk the performance
and the safety of the ship [5].
Topali and Psarafis (2019) [2], analysed the enforcement policies and the imposition of ﬁnes, of the
global sulphur cap. According to the authors, the enforcement policies are the same as the ones used for the
control in ECA zones. According to them, the enforcement of regulations is dependent on state port and
port inspection when the ship is at berth. Port and coastal states are obliged to verify the compliance of the
vessel. A bunker delivery note (BDN) should be carried by ships indicating the content of the supplied fuel
oil with the possibility of sample’s veriﬁcation. An International Air Pollution Prevention (IAPP), issued
by ship’s Flag State, should be carried certifying that the ship uses low sulphur fuel. Te inspectors could
also check ship logbooks (oil record book, the record of navigational activities and internal transfer of
fuels, engine speciﬁcations, tank sounding recordings, and fuel oil change records). IMO does not impose
some of the upcoming surveillance methods such as air monitoring for the assessment of smoke plumes,
surveillance of ships close to the coast and in high seas, in situ monitoring systems and port monitoring
sensors but they.
In case of non-compliance, the ﬁnes are imposed and decided by the state which detects it. Te owners
being caught had except the penalty to prove all the actions trying to do for compliance.
Te new MARPOL amendment in Regulation 13, adopted by Resolution MEPC.286(71), implied TIER III
certiﬁcation for new and replacement engines more than 130kw, from the 1st January of 2019. According
to the amendment in Regulation 18, for ships more than 400 GT, the information recorded in BDN and the
implementation plan must follow speciﬁc guidelines.
IMO proposed the carriage of an implementation plan indicating the steps followed for the compliance
to the regulation and released on January 2019 a ”Guidance on the shipping companies and operators for

the use of particulate ﬁlters can remove the particles eﬃciently. Additionally, either Selective Catalytic
(SCR) or Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR) can also be used for NOx cleaning [8].

Environmental Impacts of Sulphur Cap Regulation
Te sulphur cap regulation will result in a signiﬁcant reduction in sulphur concentrations in the at-
mosphere. Tis reduction is directly connected to the improvement in air quality and thereby on human
health.
However, the upcoming sulphur cap is controversial to the target of minimization of CO2 emissions
and therefore, the decrease of the Global Warming phenomenon. Te reduction of sulphur emissions is
directly connected with the reduction of cooling eﬀect, enhancing thus the risk of global warming. Except
for this, the upcoming regulation has a substantial risk of CO2 increase connected to the stakeholders’
choices for compliance. Te increase in the compliant fuel prices can lead to speed alterations and modal
shifs and thus more CO2 emissions. Moreover, an additional increase in CO2 emissions is also related to
oil reﬁneries due to desulphurization of fuels.
At the same time, to assess the costs and beneﬁts of the regulation for the whole society, the envi-
ronmental impacts of the compliance choices should also be take into consideration. Te beneﬁts and
costs of ship-operators should be weighted with the environmental beneﬁts and costs in a whole lifecycle
perspective.
Tis chapter analyzes how and under which conditions each of the factors, as mentioned above, can
enhance the global warming eﬀect. Moreover, it gives an overall analysis in the internalization of envi-
ronmental impacts.
4.1

Sulphur Dioxide (SO2) is the dominant type of SOx in the lower atmospheric level. It is reported that
around 30% of SO2 converted into an acid aerosol which through the wet or dry deposition removed from
the atmosphere [52]. Te reaction of atmospheric SO2 and NOx with water, chemical and oxygen in the
atmosphere creates the acid rain. A part of these emissions is coming from natural resources while the
majority of them is coming from the burning of fossil fuel, the manufacturing and oil industry and the
vehicles.
Acid rain is responsible for forest dieback while SO2 is related to a lot of health problems such as lung
dysfunction, problems in the respiratory system, diﬀerent types of irritations and premature death [52].
SO2 from shipping is connected mostly with health issues. Te released from shipping SO2 reacts with
other pollutants such as ammonia. Te result of this reaction is secondary particles hazardous for health
[9]. It is reported that sulphur from ships is responsible for 212,000 and 595,000 premature deaths per

year[53].

Speed Alterations
Two scenarios concerning the speed follow Sulphur Cap: 1) a slow steaming scenario due to high fuel
prices 2)the increase of speed out of SECAS from ships with scrubbers that carry cheaper HFO.
Te uncertainty in the fuel prices in post - 2020 market increases the need for speed optimization and
eﬃciency in the shipping routes. With various scenarios supporting a signiﬁcant increase in prices of
compliant fuels, the probability of slow steaming is becoming higher.
Psarafis (2019) [59] describes slow-steaming as a solution being practised during periods of depressed
markets. Te author enhances this argument explaining that in periods like that it was noticed the absorb-

can drive to slow-steaming in two diﬀerent levels. For the current vessels, slow steaming constitutes a lo-

According to Cariou (2011) [60], slow steaming is connected with the reduction of CO2 emissions

advantage and increase their speed in open seas. As a result, ship-operators can reach their optimal speed
with lower cost creating unfair competition. From an environmental perspective, the increase in speed
increases CO2 emissions.
Psarafis and Kontovas (2014) [64], examining the speed optimization case, they reported that the non-
linear relationship of fuel consumption and speed leads to a disproportional increase in CO2 emissions
when the same ship is going faster. Based on this, the more expensive cargoes can emit signiﬁcantly more
CO2 emissions as the optimal vessel speed is higher compared with other cases. Moreover, the authors
outline that speed decision making is based on 1) fuel prices 2) market condition and freight rates 3)the
cargo inventory cost and 4)the dependency of fuel consumption on the payload.
Terefore, the choice of speed is a multi-parametric decision. Under the sulphur cap market conditions,
ship-operators will decide the most proﬁtable way to balance the regulatory cost. However, it is unknown
in which percentage their choices will increase the CO2 level.

Methodology
Tis section describes the methodology followed for the estimation of emissions and the costs - beneﬁts
in each case. Taking into consideration the pre-2020 period, a scrubber scenario and an MGO scenario are
comparing with a basis HFO scenario, in both ﬁnancial and environmental terms through a Cost-Beneﬁt
Analysis. For the post-2020 implementation, an economic analysis is conducted examining the proﬁtability
of a scrubber comparing to an MGO investment for diﬀerent fuel price scenarios. Te various assumptions
and the parameters chosen for the cases are describing.
Te fuel consumption is based on average data as they provided from the companies operating the
vessels. A ”botom-up” approach is used for the calculation of fuel consumption for one of the cases to
examine the accuracy of the model in comparison with the real data.
Te calculation of emissions and the cost-beneﬁt analysis are conducted separately for each case. All
the emissions are converted into monetary terms. All the prices are expressed in Euro while all the masses
are calculated in tonnes (ton) per year.
For each of the cases, the Cost-Beneﬁt Analysis conducted in three circles. First, including only ﬁ-
nancial values. Secondly, taking into consideration also the environmental impacts of shipping activities.
Lastly, including the whole life cycle.
5.1
Calculation of Fuel Consumption
5.1.1
Evaluation methods of ﬂeet emissions
According to Psarafis (2015) [34], there are two methods for the estimation of fuel consumption and
thus, emissions. Firstly, the ”top-down” or ”fuel-based” method which provides an estimate based on fuel
sales. Secondly, the ”botom-up” or ”activity-based” method which provides an estimation based on vessel
characteristics and movements. Te diﬀerence between the two approaches lies in the data availability for
each case.
Te ”top-down” method can provide accurate estimations only if the provided sales data are reliable.
Possible inaccuracies can occur due to the lack of precise fuel consumption data. Tis is mostly in regions
where the registration of these data is not obligatory.
Te ”botom-up” method can provide accurate estimations with data relevant to vessel activity. Te
method is taking into consideration information about the movement of the vessel, the technical vessel
speciﬁcations (vessel size and age, engine type, fuel type, etc.), the relevant fuel consumption ﬁgures and
the emission factors.

For the current study, it is chosen the use of the ”botom-up” method due to the accurate and realistic
results that provide in the emission estimation.

manoeuvring and on the port berth.

FCtrip = FCfreesailing + FCmanoeuvring + FCberth

(5.2)
FCmanoeuvring = FCauxiliary + FCboiler

5.1.3
Fuel Consumption in Free Sailing Phase
Te fuel consumption FC (kg) for the main engine (m), for the sailing trip (s) of the vessel (k) is described
as follows:
FCm,s,k = 10−3 · SFOCm,s,k · ELm,s,k · EPk · tc
(5.5)
where SFOCm,s,k (g/KW-h) is the Speciﬁc Fuel Oil Consumption of the main ship engine when it is

(hr) is the time of the free sailing of the ship.
Te three parameters SFOCm,s,k, ELm,s,k and tc depend on the vessel sailing speed Vs,k and as a result

Conclusions- Limitations
More than 70.000 ships will be aﬀected by the Sulphur Cap Regulation in 2020. Under a turbulent fuel
market, the shipowners have to comply and meet the sulphur limits. However, the critical environmental
challenge is not only compliance with the local pollutants but also the long term improvement in GHG
emissions.
For the 2020 implementation low-sulphur, fuel and abatement technology are consist the most dom-
inant solutions. At the same time, LNG expected to be in force afer 2025 while cleaner fuels seem to be
still in a trial phase. However, the sustainability of these solutions, in the long run, is questionable.
Trough the three case studies, the project elaborates the impact of the compliant options in CO2
emissions. Te results enclosed in the previous section they indicate the importance of sulphur cap for the
improvement of human health. However, the results indicate a negative impact of both MGO and scrubber
in CO2 emissions.
From a ﬁnancial perspective, the choice of a scrubber ranks beter comparing to an MGO choice, due
to the price diﬀerence between MGO and HFO. However, and under diﬀerent price scenarios, the scrubber
choice remains sustainable only for big vessels. It is noticed that small container-ships cannot outweigh
the capital cost of a scrubber investment and are more sensitive in diﬀerent fuel price scenarios.
From an environmental perspective, scrubber ranks beter than MGO in the assessment of overall
emissions. In terms of CO2 emissions, MGO contributes more than the scrubber. Besides, the amount of
CO2 is increasing more if the reﬁnery emissions are considered. It is estimated that for the production of
MGO, the reﬁneries emit 13% more CO2 than for the production of HFO. However, this amount cannot
be comparable in size with the signiﬁcant amount of CO2 released by the shipping activity. Overall, this
study calculates a 3% increase in CO2 emissions with the use of MGO comparing to HFO case for each of
the examined routes.
Moreover, further contributors to GHG emissions are also analyzed, and a qualitative risk assessment
is conducted. Te enhance of the radiative cooling phenomenon is characterized by a high likelihood of
occurrence, but it is recommended to be accepted due to the beneﬁts of the regulation in humans’ health.
Speed alterations are considered of a medium impact as it remains unknown under which conditions will
occur. As far as the oil reﬁneries are concerned, the desulphurization of fuels has a signiﬁcant contribution
to CO2 emissions, which could be severe in the future if diﬀerent blends require more energy - demanding
processes. However, possible improvements in these processes could alleviate this risk. Finally, the modal
shif could have the highest impact on CO2 emissions. Tough the poor road infrastructure, the unstable

geopolitical conditions and the unavailability of road connections in some routes are limiting factors.
It should be noted that the estimations of this study are based on assumptions listed in chapter 5. Due
to the lack of data, berth and manoeuvring phase are not including in the estimation of emissions. Te

could be higher with diﬀerent blend types. In addition, the internalization of the environmental impacts
is based on the average values presented through literature. In reality, the cost of externalities could be
charged to the shipowners through a levy in the fuel prices. Moreover, the the transportation cost of
the fuel does not internalized for this study and it is suggested to be considered due to the signiﬁcant
contribution of road transport in CO2 emissions.
In order to meet the objective of reducing the GHG emissions, all the possible choices should be eval-
uated from a whole-lifecycle perspective. Every possible compliance option should be evaluated ﬁrstly
from a practical applicability perspective, and then in the lifecycle.
Te situation cannot change as long as the compliance choice is driven only by proﬁt maximization
criteria. Te imposition of emission taxes, together with speciﬁc limits on GHG emissions, could work
as incentives for ship owners to comply with low carbon choices. Moreover, subsidies could encourage
shipowners to pursue environmental friendly solutions without heavily aﬀecting their freight rates. As a
result shippers can keep transporting their cargoes by ships.
Overall, sulphur cap will bring signiﬁcant health improvements. However, the compliance options
should be evaluated also in the long run from a lifecycle perspective.
