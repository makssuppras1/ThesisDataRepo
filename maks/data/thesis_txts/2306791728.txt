Summary (English)
The laparoscopic surgery technique involves complex bimanual motor coordina-
tion; a proﬁciency only some surgeons learn to master. In the ﬁrst part of the
thesis, task-related functional magnetic resonance imaging (tr-fMRI) data of 50
medical students performing in-scanner bimanual motor tasks is analysed with
multivariate pattern techniques. The purpose is to ﬁnd areas of pre-existing
neural activity with predictive value for the outcome of a 5-day oﬄine train-
ing on a laparoscopy simulator. From the oﬄine behavioural data, a parallel
factor analysis (PARAFAC) is used to extract a dependent variable capturing
performance improvement as input to the state-of-the-art support vector ma-
chine (SVM) applied for a regression prediction. For the utility of the full set
of behavioural parameters, a partial least square (PLS) analysis is applied to
ﬁnd correlation between these and the brain activity. However, neither of the
models could ﬁnd a neurological basis signiﬁcant for the individual performance
improvement on the simulator.
In the second part of the thesis, a novel modiﬁcation of the relevance voxel
machine (RVoxM) is suggested with the aim of including structural connectiv-
ity information in the model to improve prediction and increase interpretabil-
ity. The novel model referred to as relevance connectivity machine (RConM)
is tested in a classiﬁcation of the two in-scanner tasks (transfer and pointing),
and the input of structural connectivity is based on subject speciﬁc diﬀusion
tensor imaging (DTI) data. The model is shown to have a prediction accuracy
statistically equal to the RVoxM, and there are indications that connectivity
information is exploited to some extend. Thus, the RConM may prove to be a
useful analysis technique, however extent investigations are necessary.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The human brain is a complex organism consisting of a dense network of neu-
rons responsible for all communication within the brain and via the central
nervous system to the body. The network is under constant change, and even
though most neurogenesis is complete by ﬁve month age, neurons are formed
and altered thoruhgout life. Research has shown that the developement of the
brain is not only guided by genetics, but that the brain possesses a capacity for
both structural and functional plasticity in response to environmental factors,
including sensory and motor experiences. This kind of plasticity is referred to
as experience-dependent and is forming the foundation of both injury recovery
and motor skill learning, i.e. gaining new abilities, [1], [2], [3].
The brain plasticity can be analysed and inferred at many levels ranging from
molecular changes to global measures of brain activity related to speciﬁc be-
haviours, [1], [2]. The later has become possible with the emergence of the neu-
roimaging modality, functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI). The tech-
nique allows to investigate activity patterns by indirectly visualising metabolic
processes in response to neural stimuli, [4]. Activity and connectivity changes
in response to skill learning can thus be investigated non-invasively to e.g. in-
crease our knowledge of the individual diﬀerences in functional reorganizations
associated with learning outcome, [5].
To match the complex pattern of neural recruitment across the brain associated
with learning a task, the processing of fMRI data requires complex techniques
that goes beyond conventional univariate voxel analysis, [4]. One of the newer
approaches is to use machine learning techniques to ﬁnd multivariate response

During the latest years it has become of interest to investigate and reveal the
neuro-psychological basis for the acquisition of technical abilities associated with
laparoscopy, [7], [8]. The surgical technique involves complex bimanual coordi-
nation, and intensive training is necessary. However, despite of training some
individuals are unable to achieve laparoscopic proﬁciency due to lack of prereq-
uisite psycho-motor abilities. Earlier research has tried to ﬁnd links between
laparascopic learning skills and personality traits or behaviour, [9] [7]. With the
relatively new MR-compatible surgery training box, it has become feasible to
use fMRI to explore and reveal neural activity related or crucial to this complex
motor learning, [8]. It is desired to ﬁnd brain activity with predictive value
for the ﬁnal performance, and thereby make a foundation of evidence-based
training-strategies that goes beyond "simple" behavioural measures.
1.1
Problem statement
This project aims to investigate whether individual diﬀerences in pre-existing
neural activity patterns of the sensory-motor system can predict training success
in a de novo acquisition of complex bimanual motor skills. The experimental
foundation of the project consists of a set of task-naive medical students which
complete a 5-day laparascopic training on a surgery simulator. All participants
are scanned with both structural and functional magnetic resonance imaging
before training (baseline), after the ﬁrst training, and at the end. The project
applies supervised multivariate pattern analysis to try to predict the training
outcome from the functional baseline scans and reveal associated spatial pat-
terns. Additionally, it is tested whether the prediction by current machine learn-
ing models can be improved by including information about structural brain
connectivity. In the following the two raised hypotheses within the project can
be seen:
• The project group hypothesizes that machine learning techniques will be
able to predict the ﬁnal performance/training outcome by neural patterns
at baseline. We expect that the predictive spatial pattern will be in corre-
spondence with ﬁndings of earlier research ([5]) and a prior investigation
of the neural correlates of training over the three scanning sessions (using
a General Linear Model).

1.2
The process
To specify the data of interest, it consists of task-related functional Magnetic
Resonance Imaging (tr-fMRI) data and diﬀusion weighted data (DWI) collected
at the Danish Research Centre for Magnetic Resonance (DRCMR) as part of a
larger research study called "LapSurg", which has laparoscopic skill acquisition
as main focus. The 50 students have performed two bimanual motor tasks inside
an MR-compatible training box during the tr-fMRI acquisition which falls on
day 1, day 2 and day 6 of the experimental period. Half of the participants
received oﬄine laparoscopic training on a simulator from day 2 to day 6. This
includes nine diﬀerent exercises which all result in a set of behavioural measures
reﬂecting the students performance. The fMRI and DWI data are preprocessed
and analysed by standard methods before thrown into the pattern recognition
models.
In the process of predicting training outcome from brain activity, the machine
learning method initially taken into use, is the state-of-the-art Support Vector
Machine (SVM); a sparse solution to regression and classiﬁcation problems of
high-dimensional data.
In order to compile a large set of behavioural scor-
ing measures describing the students motor skills, a parallel factor analysis
(PARAFAC) is used to extract a single component, explaining the overall per-
formance, as the dependent variable of the SVM model. As no strong correlation
between the PARAFAC component and the baseline fMRI activity was found,
an alternative method, the Partial Least Square (PLS) regression model, is tried
out. This model relies on the optimization of the covariance between two sets
of data: e.g. the brain activity and behavioural data. All or several of the raw
performance measures can thus be used and there is no need of decomposition.
At last, an alternative way of trying to increase information of the cortical net-
works of relevance during motor tasks is suggested. This includes modiﬁcations
of the Relevance Voxel Machine (RVoxM), an already extended version of the
Relevance Vector Machine (RVM) that utilizes information of the surround-
ing neighbourhood to a brain voxel during prediction. To investigate whether
remote structural connectivity information could improve prediction outcome,
the neighbouring connections of the RVoxM are replaced by a connectome from
processed DWI data, whereby a novel model emerges. To make proof of con-
cept, a classiﬁcation of the two tasks included in the tr-fMRI paradigm is used

Brain Structure and Functionality
The brain can be divided into the cerebrum, cerebellum, brain stem, and di-
encephalon, and constitutes the central nervous system (CNS) together with
the spinal cord. Concentrating on the cerebrum (representing 90 % of the brain
mass), it consists of an outer layer of cerebral cortex, underlying regions of white

glia and olfactory bulb.
The cortex and deep structures are regions of gray
matter (GM). The cerebral cortex is divided into a right and left hemisphere
by a deep central ﬁssure (the Sylvian Fissure) and can be further classiﬁed into
lobes named after their location: the frontal, the temporal, the parietal, and
the occipital lobe (and sometimes a limbic and insula lobe are also included in
the classiﬁcation), [10]. Figure 2.1 shows the brain divided into lobes.

At a ﬁner scale, the cortex is structured by multiple gyri ("ridge/top") and sulci
("furrow") creating the folded appearance also showed in Figure 2.1. As can

associated with processing and cognition and involves all motor control, sensory
perception, memory, etc. [10].
At a molecular level, the GM mostly consists of neuronal cell bodies. The neu-
ron, also known as a nerve cell, is the functional unit of the CNS, and processes
and transmits information through electrical and chemical signals. The neuron
typically consists of a cell body with small branches called dendrites, and a sin-
gle long projection called the axon. Myelinated axons are called nerve ﬁbres,
and on contrary to the cell bodies found in the GM, these constitute the WM

tissue of the brain. The axon ﬁbres are responsible for transmitting signals from
one part of the brain to another and thereby coordinate essential communica-

brain, [11]. Another form of connectivity is the functional connectivity, which
is the temporal correlation between activity events in spatially remote neural

2.2
Motor Skill Learning
Motor skill learning can be deﬁned as the process of executing movements more
accurately and quickly with practice, [5]. The process can be described both
at a behavioural level, i.e. changes in the actual performance during training,
and at a brain level, i.e. the functional and structural plasticity following from
learning/adapting to a task, [2].
With a focus on the behavioural level, one way of dividing the learning process
is into a fast learning stage and a slow learning stage. The fast motor skill learn-
ing comprises the (initial) acquisition and can be characterized by a signiﬁcant
improvement over relatively short time (steep learning curve) - typically within
a single training session. Of course the learning time scale is highly task depen-
dent. Slow motor skill learning is the further gains and retention of skills after
several periods of practise. It is thus characterized by a slow/more ﬂat learn-
ing curve. Figure 2.2 depicts the principle. The performance improvement, in
general, happens both during training sessions ("online") and between training
sessions ("oﬄine"). The skill stabilization, seen as a highly "oﬄine" process,
reﬂect motor memory consolidation - the intermediate stage between fast and
slow learning [5].
Figure 2.2: A rough graphical representation of the improvement during training divided
into the two motor skill learning stages: fast and slow.

Concentrating on the brain level, research demonstrates that both structural
and functional plasticity are observed during both the fast and slow stage of
motor skill learning [5]. As mentioned in the introduction, this plasticity can be
thought of as an experience-dependent plasticity and is a neuronal adaptation
to achieve new abilities. Large scale structural changes are mostly associated
with slow learning, and an example is GM volume in speciﬁc brain areas, which
has been found diﬀerent in experts versus non-experts. Additionally, diﬀerences
in white matter structure, i.e. structural connectivity, seem to reﬂect or relate
to variation in learning.
More rapid (early) structural changes are seen on
a molecular level.
Changes as remodelling of dendrite branches and axonal
terminals have been observed during motor training, [5], [1].
The functional plasticity includes a reorganization of cortical and cortico-cerebellar
connectivity circuits, which evidently takes place during learning, [3]. Models
beyond conventional univariate analysis have showed that this coupling of dif-
ferent brain areas changes both during the fast stage and between fast and slow
stage. E.g. the activity in the M1 is seen to decrease as the fast stage of mo-
tor skill learning progresses, but increases again during the slow stage - as a
reﬂection of recruiting additional M1 neurons. Other neural substrates of im-
portance during the fast learning stage (which mainly is of concern within this
project) are the premotor cortex (PM), the posterior parietal cortex (PPC),
the supplementary motor area (SMA), the dorsomedial striatum (DMS) (part
of basal ganglia), and posterior cerebellum (PCB), where an increased activa-
tion is expected. A decreased activation, reﬂecting that fewer neurons are able
to carry out the task, is expected in pre-supplementary motor area (preSMA),
the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPC), and as mentioned in M1, [5]. The
approximate location of some of the mentioned areas can be seen from Figure
2.1.
With these general areas outlined, it should be mentioned that as with the
structural plasticity, the functional plasticity following from skill learning is also
individual - and so is the ﬁnal performance or outcome from a training period,
[7].
These diﬀerences can be understood to rest on the concept of "talent",
i.e. that some achieve excellent skills at a task, e.g. playing music, and others
do not. There is thus indications that we each have individual functional or
structural bases in the brain, which priorly settle the skill outcome. As with
this project, there is an on-going investigation of whether neural signal patterns
or early functional plasticity can reﬂect and predict the motor learning outcome,
[14], [15], [5] .

2.3
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a radiation-free medical imaging tech-
nique/modality emerged in the 70s and currently used in radiology as an im-
portant diagnostic and clinical tool. The technique utilizes the high content
of hydrogen in the human body. With its odd number of nucleons (a single
proton), Hydrogen (1H) possesses the ability of "spin": rotation around its own
axes resulting in a small magnetic moment. In the presence of a strong external
magnetic ﬁeld (B0), these otherwise random orientated spins tend to align giving
rise to a net macroscopic magnetic moment (dipole), as illustrated in Figure 2.3.
The net magnetization vector is denoted M and is naturally pointing along the
direction of B0 also called the z-direction. Additionally, the nuclei/spins exhibit
resonance and thus causes M to precess about B0 with a frequency called the
Larmor frequency (ω), [16].

exactly in the x-y plane, and the magnetization has become fully transversal.
Turning B1 oﬀ, a precession of M back to equilibrium begins. This is called
relaxation, and covers a decay of the transversal component of M (Mxy), hap-
pening on a time-scale T2, and a return of the longitudinal magnetization (Mz),
happening on a time scale T1. During the relaxation the rotating magnetization
induces an electromotive force in a receiver coil within the scanner oriented to
detect changes of the transversal magnetization (in the x-y plane). The phe-
nomenon is described through Faraday’s law of induction, and the generated
time signal is called a free induction decay (FID). Several FIDs are recorded
during an MRI scan to reconstruct an MR image; a spatial map of signal am-
plitude depending on the local tissue properties.
The relaxation times, T1 and T2, are tissue speciﬁc and depend on the mobility
of protons and the strength of B0. On a microscopic level, the T2 relaxation
process arises from interactions between the spins leading to a spread in their
resonance frequencies. This entails a loss of phase coherence of the transversal
component and results in a transverse signal decay, [16]. Thus, the experienced
decay is dependent on the given tissue’s T2 value, and similar is the reestablish-
ment of the longitudinal magnetisation on the tissue’s T1. Hence, diﬀerences in
T2 and T1, e.g. between water and fat, create the contrast in an MR image so
that structures appear.
When ﬁeld inhomogeneities are present, e.g. from a nonuniform B0 or chemical
properties of the tissue, it can cause an extra broadening of the frequency dis-
tribution and thus result in a more pronounced dephasing and amplitude decay
than the intrinsic T2. The T2 decay is then referred to as T∗
2 decay, [16]. Often
such inhomogeneities lead to image distortions and are tried to be avoided, how-
ever in functional MRI the T∗
2 decay is utilized. This will be further described
in the next subsection.
In order to spatially map the induced RF-signals into an MR image, linear gradi-
ent ﬁelds are applied to provide a spatial variation in the observed ﬁeld strength
and thus in the resonance frequency of the spins. A gradient along the main ﬁeld
is typically called a slice-selective gradient as only the slice of spins within the
bandwidth of resonance frequencies matching the B1 pulse will be exited and
FID contributing. Now, a phase-encoding (Gy), and frequency-encoding (Gx)
gradient in the y and x direction respectively will make the excited spins precess
with a small variation in both frequency and phase, making the information in
the FID signal spatially dependent and thus the amplitude distribution, m(x,y)
possible to locate. The application of Gy can be seen as a weighting of the mag-
netization m(x,y) by a complex exponential factor dependent on the magnitude
of Gy. Then while applying Gx, also called the readout gradient, the signal is
recorded, containing information of a horizontal line in the frequency domain
of m(x,y). This 2D Fourier space is also called the K-space, and by varying the

reconstruct m(x,y) and obtain the object, an inverse 2D Fourier transform is

sequences used for functional MRI, further described in the following subsection.
2.3.1
Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Since the 1990’s functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) has been the
chosen tool for mapping and visualizing neural activity in the human brain. It
is thus an important tool in the investigation of brain functions and neurode-
generative disorders, [4]
The signal contrast within fMRI relies on the increased cerebral blood ﬂow
(CBF) that follows the increased brain activity induced by some stimulus. The
most common contrast technique uses the venous blood oxygen level-dependent
(BOLD) eﬀect which arises when a neuron is activated.
The cell consumes
more energy and oxygen due to an activation, and this initiates a compensatory
increase of blood ﬂow to the brain by the vascular system. However, the system
overcompensates leading to an increase of the oxygenated haemoglobin of the
cerebral blood during activation. Deoxygenated blood or haemoglobin (dHB) is
a paramagnetic molecule, changing the local magnetic ﬁeld a small amount and
thereby causing ﬁeld inhomogeneity. An increase in oxygenated haemoglobin
suppresses this eﬀect and the ﬁeld shifts towards a more homogeneous. This is
characterized by an increase of the T2* parameter, elongating the transversal
relaxation and creating a slightly stronger signal, i.e. the BOLD signal, [17].
Figure 2.4(a) shows the physiological changes leading to the BOLD fMRI signal.
As the haemodynamic response occurs with a delay to the stimulus, the BOLD
signal can be seen as a "sluggish/delayed representation of the neural signal
[4]. Figure 2.4(b) shows the so-called haemodynamic response function (HRF)
describing the noiseless BOLD response arising after a neural stimulus at t=0.
An initial undershoot can be present due to the immediate metabolic demand
of the activated cells, and the response peak is followed by a late undershoot
before baseline is reached. The undershoot is thought of both a biophysilogical
and metabolic eﬀect, [17].

2.3.1.1
Experimental Design
In task-related fMRI (tr-fMRI), the neural activity evoked with a speciﬁc stimuli
or task is of interest. The duration and number of stimuli-events can be var-
ied and designed according to the study of interest. If the detection of a HRF
response is of interest, a block design is appropriate having continuous periods
of stimuli/task. If on contrary, the shape estimate of the HRF is of interest, an
event-related design is better having time-separated (discrete) stimuli,[18] [4]. A
design will always include stimuli-free periods of rest to have a baseline/reference
signal needed in the statistical analysis of the fMRI signal. Resting periods are
thereby an example of a low-level baseline condition and can either be explicitly
modelled or left as part of the non-modelled leftover signal to compare condi-
tions with. Some study designs may include multiple types of stimulus, which
can be combined in both random or ﬁxed order. In such case one of the tasks
may function as baseline, which then can be referred to as a high-level baseline
condition. Controlling a signal with a high-level condition may remove corre-
lated activation which is not of primary interest, [17]. The chosen experimental
design is described and modelled by the so-called paradigm function, which is
used as part of the statistical modelling of the signal; a process described in
details in a section of Chapter 4.
2.3.2

Diﬀusion Weighted Imaging (DWI) is another MRI based tool, developed in
1980’s. It is a mapping of the diﬀusion process of water molecules and relies
on the general assumption of Brownian motion described by Albert Einstein.
Diﬀusion patterns are altered by the surrounding limiting structures and can
thereby reveal tissue architecture at a microscopic level. In the ﬁeld of neuro-
science, this is used both in the management of pathological states, e.g. acute
stroke, and in the mapping of white matter trajectories and neural connections.
The later is enabled by the extended DWI: the Diﬀusion Tensor Imaging (DTI),
[11]. Figure 2.6 shows the principle of how neuronal structures as bundles of
axons conﬁne the direction of water molecules’ diﬀusion and make it anisotropic.

part into the MR sequence. The refocusing gradient will have its full eﬀect on

focusing of molecules moving in the direction of the gradient will be incomplete
leading to an attenuated signal, [11]. The attenuation of the signal is described
by Equation 2.2 and can be seen to exponentially relate to the apparent diﬀu-

4.1.1
Preprocessing of Functional Data
A set of preprocessing operations of the fMRI data is a necessary step before
making the actual data analysis.
The purpose of the operations is both to
capture and reduce artefactual sources of variability and to ensure assumptions
arising under the later statistical modelling.
The following sections contain
descriptions of the operations, all being standard methods within SPM used on
the tr-fMRI data of this project.
4.1.1.1
Slice Timing Correction
The EPI sequence used for fMRI acquisition is a 2D sequence acquiring a single
slice at a time.
This means that the slices within the volume are acquired
across a time interval corresponding to TR. As the statistical modelling expects
all data within the volume to be recorded simultaneously, a slice time correction
is necessary. The correction consists of a "shift" of the signal in time to match
the timing of a chosen reference slice, e.g. the slice recorded at a half TR. A
sinc-interpolation is used for the lagging of the time series data on each slice,
generating the "missing" time points, as if the slice was acquired at the same
time as the reference slice. [4] [22]. The center slice is used as reference slice in
the slice timing of the tr-fMRI data.
4.1.1.2
Motion Correction
Although eﬀorts are made to maximize subject compliance, motion is unavoid-
able e.g. from swallowing and breathing or larger task-related head movements.
Motions induce two large eﬀects. The ﬁrst and most obvious is the bulk motion
which is a spatial mismatch between the subsequent images in the time series.
This will especially aﬀect the voxel intensity near the brain edge, as e.g., a voxel
outside the brain is suddenly placed inside. Thus, the eﬀect is often reﬂected as
a large signal along edges within the statistical map. The second eﬀect is the
spin history eﬀect, arising when protons move from one slice into the neighbour-
ing. These protons will experience a diﬀerent excitation scheme than expected
==PAGE:47==
4.1 Statistical Parametric Mapping
25
and change the intensity of a slice or a set of slices.
Primarily, a standard motion correction is used to reduce the bulk motion eﬀect.
This correction consists of a realignment of all images to a reference image using
a rigid body aﬃne transformation as registration, meaning that the head is
assumed to only change position and not shape. From the process, six motion
parameters are estimated to describe the movement: three translational and
three rotational. They are estimated by using a cost function sensitive to the
correlation between the reference image and the target image. Here, SPM uses
a least square approach, and a prior smoothing of data can be added to reduce
noise, [22]. A following reslicing of the images is performed to complete the
realignment and in that context a higher order interpolation is typically used
for the best result. In this project, the mean of all images is used as registration
reference, a 5 mm kernel is used for smoothing, and a 4th degree B-Spline
interpolation is chosen during reslicing.
However, as other tools are needed for the spin history eﬀect and additional
movement artefacts to be reduced, an approach introduced by Friston et al [23]
is followed. Without going into detail, this method derives a set of 24 motion
regressors by using a Volterra expansion to take into account the history of
position and thus the history of excitation. The set of regressors is: [R, R2,
Rt−1, R2
t−1], where R and Rt−1 are the six standard motion regressors for the
current and last preceding time point respectively, [23] [24].
The reduction of motion-related signal is of high importance in cases where the
head motion is correlated with the task, and the signal coming from movements
(noise) and from task-related neural activation (BOLD contrast) is diﬃcult to
distinguish. In such case, the experienced signal might be artefactual leading
to misinterpretations. However, it can be diﬃcult to remove the motion-related
signal without reducing the task-related signal as well, and the method should
be chosen with care, [4].

4.1.1.3
Spatial Smoothing
A spatial high-pass ﬁltering is typically applied to the fMRI data to remove high
frequency content and thereby smooth the signal distribution. This smoothing
will reduce the localization mismatch across subjects and increase the signal-to-
noise ratio in cases where activation extends across several voxels. The common
method (also used by SPM) is to convolve the data with a 3D-Gaussian kernel
==PAGE:49==
4.1 Statistical Parametric Mapping
27
ﬁlter, or more precisely a separable isotropic Gaussian ﬁlter. The full-width half-
maximum (FWHM) of the Gaussian speciﬁes the amount of smoothing/blurring,
and a FWHM of twice the voxel size is a standard choice, [4].
The chosen
smoothing for the tr-fMRI data is 6 mm in all three directions (x, y, z). This
is to match the ﬁlter size to the expected size of activation - i.e. following the
matched ﬁlter theorem.
4.1.1.4
Spatial Normalization
For an intersubject comparison to be meaningful, a transformation of the indi-
vidual brains into a common space is necessary. Diﬀerent atlas or templates can
be used for such registration, however a common choice is the templates devel-
oped at Montreal Neurological Institute. These contain the average brain of a
large number of individuals and is referred to as MNI templates. As a prior or
initial step to the normalization, a bias ﬁeld correction and a tissue segmentation
should be performed. A bias ﬁeld correction is included to remove artefactual
intensity modulations arising from the scanner physics/excitation inhomogene-
ity. This "bias" is reﬂected by a broad variation of intensity across the image
and can thus be removed by a high-pass ﬁlter. However, another method of reg-
ularisation is included in the SPM software, taking local information of tissue
type and ﬁeld distribution into account. The inclusion of a tissue segmentation
into compartments (e.g WM and GM) helps the regularisation model in saving
the intensity variations coming from the change in tissue type. Additionally, the
tissue segments - or tissue probability atlases - are used under the estimation of
the deformation ﬁelds ("warps") needed for the transformation of the individual
subject images.
The process of registration can be done with or without a coregistration to the
subject’s anatomical image. The direct method aligns an EPI template to the
MNI space while estimating the mentioned (nonlinear) deformation ﬁeld, and
then normalizes all fMRI images using the same deformation ﬁeld. For the tr-
fMRI data the direct method of registration is used and the mean image from the
realignment is used as template. In the modelling of the bias ﬁeld a Gaussian 60
mm FWHM cutoﬀis chosen. The standard tissue probability maps (TPM) as
well as the standard ICBM/MNI (European brain) space included in the SPM
software are used. For the actual normalization of all the images the original
voxel size is approximately preserved, and a 4th degree B-Spline interpolation
is used.
To sum up the fMRI preprocessing, the raw data has been slice time corrected
using center slice as reference, realigned by a rigid-body transformation for
movement correction, subjected to a spatial smoothing of 6 mm3, and normal-
ized to an MNI registered EPI template, making it ready for the statistical

4.1.3.1

analysis uses β or contrast values from the 1st-level analysis and combines them
for all subjects to make similar testing (e.g. t-test) as in the 1st-level analysis,

the controls. A contrast matrix for an F-test with that purpose, and at the
same time orthogonalized on the mean, can be given by: C = [-1 1 3; -1 -1 -1].

4.2
Processing of Diﬀusion Data
As with the fMRI data, preprocessing the DWI data is a necessary prepara-
tion to reduce unwanted variance components from the time series of diﬀusion
weighted images. A short description is made of the steps involved in the arte-
fact corrections - all of which are performed by the use of the FSL toolbox. FSL
stands for FMRIB software library and is developed by Stephen Smith and his
group from Oxford University. FSL provides tools both for statistical modelling
of fMRI data, independent component analysis, and for DWI/DTI processing,
[4].
4.2.1
Preprocessing
DWI is prone to susceptibility induced artefacts, which are ﬁeld inhomogeneities
arising around interfaces between tissues having diﬀerent magnetic susceptibil-
ities. The EPI sequence is especially vulnerable in that sense since every line
in the phase-encoding direction is sampled with a diﬀerent amount of phase
accumulation. The result, if not corrected, is geometric distortions and signal
modulations. The correction used in this project is the reversed gradient tech-
nique. This means acquiring two images where the directions of the gradients
are reversed such that the K-space is sampled from top to bottom in one im-
age and from bottom to top in the other. The result is two images with equal
distortions, but in opposite directions. From these it is possible to calculate a
susceptibility-correcting transformation matrix.
During the DWI scantime movement artefacts are unavoidable. Similar to the
motion correction step in the fMRI preprocessing, an alignment of every image
to a reference is made through an aﬃne (rigid-body) transformation. The mean
of the acquired non-diﬀusion weighted images is used as reference, but for the
==PAGE:54==
32
Methods
realignment, a Mututal Information measure is used instead of a least squared
error approach due to the diﬀerence in contrast in the DWI images.
The last main artefact corrected for is Eddy currents induced by the fast switch-
ing gradients. These generate currents in the electrical components of the scan-
ner resulting in an extra local magnetic ﬁeld gradient which causes geometrical
distortions (as compression or stretching of voxels). The artefact is corrected for
already by the design of the DSE sequence, which includes a reversed gradient
scheme being robust to Eddy currents,[26], [19].
4.2.2
Tractography and Connectivity Map
In this project the DWI data is used to create a connectivity map of each subject
brain. The ﬁnal connectome is used to make a novel extension of the Relevance
Voxel Machine model described in a later section. As mentioned in Chapter 2,
a connectivity map is generated by drawing trajectories from each voxel. The
processing will not be described in detail, but an overview of the pipeline for
white mater tractography and connectome generation is given in the following.
The MRtrix tools are the chosen software for the process. The software uses
a spherical deconvolution in estimation of ﬁbre orientation distribution instead
of just the diﬀusion tensor matrix, and a probabilistic streamline algorithm for
white matter tracking, [27].
From the preprocessed diﬀusion weighted images with b-value = 2000 the diﬀu-
sion tensor, D, is estimated. Then an FA map is calculated based on Equation
2.5 and a following eigenvector (EV) map is made. The FA map is thresholded
at FA=0.7 to generate a single-ﬁbre (SF) voxel map. The diﬀusion weighted sig-
nal within the voxels expected to contain a single ﬁbre (without crossing ﬁbres)
is used to estimate a response function necessary for a constrained spherical de-
convolution. The result of the spherical deconvolution is used for a whole-brain
tractography with 106 streamlines. At last, a connectome is obtained by making
a ﬁltration of the tractogram to extract streamlines between predeﬁned parcels.
A parcel could be chosen to contain a single voxel giving a very ﬁnely gridded
connectome, however taking dimensionality and computational considerations
into account a node assignment scheme of 300 parcels is used, [27],[19].

4.3.1
The Parallel Factor Analysis
The parallel factor analysis (PARAFAC) is a multi-way method for analysing
N-dimensional data arrays and decompose them into few factors/components
describing the data in a more condensed form. This is an important tool when
dealing with large data sets containing several variables measured in a crossed
fashion.
The behavioural data of this project contains a set of parameters
measured for several subjects on several days - leading to three-way data ar-
ranged in a cube.
As the principal component analysis (PCA) tries to con-
vert 2-dimensional data into a set of linearly uncorrelated principal components
capturing the variance within the data, PARAFAC does the same, but for a
higher-dimensional space and in a more constrained way. This means that the
PARAFAC model can reveal the internal structure of the data while being less
sensitive to noise, than e.g. the PCA or Tucker3, which is another multi-linear
decomposition method [30].

4.4.1
Prediction and Inference
In general, making a classiﬁcation model consists of learning a decision function
f, which is capable of assigning the objects to their predeﬁned class label. The
input data characterized by (x,y) consist of a set of features in x describing the
given observation, and the corresponding class label in y. In the case where y
takes on continuous values, the classiﬁcation problem becomes a regression one,
and the goal is to predict/estimate the dependent value from the relation to the
independent ones (the features). The term supervised refers to the fact that y,
whether being continues or discrete, is known. In the case of fMRI studies, the
experimental condition or behaviour analysed will always be known, [33], [34].
In supervised learning problems (both classiﬁcation and regression) the data
set is divided into a training and test set. The former is used in training the
model, while the latter is used to test the performance and generalization of
it. In this process it is assumed that both the training and test sets are drawn
independently from the same (source) distribution, [33].
Cross Validation
Having a limited amount of instances in the data, in this
case only 25 (oﬄine training) subjects, it is desired to use as much of the data as
possible in the model training, thus a cross-validation (CV) procedure is used.
CV consist in leaving out k samples, train on the remaining, and test/predict
on the left-out samples. This is repeated until all samples have been part of the
test-set. Even though computational expensive, k is in every case set to 1 in this
project, making it a leave-one-out CV, [33]. In a classiﬁcation problem, each of
the CV classiﬁers will produce a prediction accuracy calculated as the fraction
of correct predictions out of number of test-samples. The overall accuracy then
becomes the mean accuracy of all CV rounds.
In a regression problem, the
squared error ∥ytrue −ypred ∥2 is used as performance measure. Meaning or
summing these for all CV rounds can describe the overall performance. However,
the raw squared error can be diﬃcult to interpret and should be seen in reference
to e.g. a random model. This can be done with bootstrap or permutation tests.
Permutation Inference
A permutation test is a non-parametric statistical
signiﬁcance test. It consists in calculating all possible values in the test statis-
tics by making randomized rearrangement/permutation of the labels associated
with the observed data and then running the model. By making e.g. 500 per-
mutations a null-distribution is obtained for the model, and it can be tested
whether the true model with the "correct" labels performs signiﬁcantly better
than random. This kind of testing is comprised of a random sampling with-
out replacement, [34]. Permutation testing will be used in every regressional

Bootstrapping
Bootstrapping is a similar test, but relies on random sam-
pling with replacement. Thus, within the bootstrap sample of Nobs "drawn"
observations, the same observation can appear several times, and some will not
appear at all. On average,
63 % of the observations will be included in one
training sample, and the remaining 37 % automatically becomes the test set,
[34].
From the distribution of e.g.
500 bootstrap samples conﬁdence inter-
vals and bootstrap ratios can be calculated for signiﬁcance testing of the given
variable of interest. The bootstrap ratio, calculated as the distribution mean
divided by the variance, is similar to a Students t statistics, provided that the
distribution is approximately normal. A ratio equal to or larger than 2 will
indicate a reliability of the variable on a α ≈5% level, [35]. Bootstrap testing
is used to test feature weights/loadings in the Partial Least Square regression
to investigate which voxels’ responses show reliable predictive contribution.
Nested Cross Validation
Some machine learning models will include one or
several free parameters, which have to be chosen. The nested CV procedure is a
method for ﬁnding the best parameter values within some interval. It includes
a double - inner and outer -CV looping. In the inner loop a range of parameters
is tested, and the performance measure for each parameter is calculated. The
best model is then tested in the outer loop on a test-sample left out during the
inner-loop training, [36]. Thus, an unbiased performance measure is obtained.
The method is used during Support Vector Machine modelling, as the model
includes one or two free parameters.
Multiple Comparisons
When testing whether a model prediction is signiﬁ-
cant, e.g. by using a permutation test or by testing an accuracy against a bino-
mial distribution, one should be aware of the multiple comparison problem also
shortly mentioned in Section 4.1. When several tests are made simultaneously,
the chance of one signiﬁcant result given some α-level is increased regardless of
the presence of an eﬀect. Multiple tests arise when no strong a priori informa-
tion or hypothesis about the data exist and a wide exploration is made. E.g.
all voxels within a brain is tested as no a priori information reduces the region
of interest to a speciﬁc area (in univariate methods). In multivariate analysis it
might be of interest to make several tests with a varied input of information or
of model speciﬁc parameters. This will often be the case throughout the MVPA
modelling in this project.
Several methods are developed to address the problem of multiple comparisons

make a FWE correction, as mentioned in the section on statistical inference, and

increases, so will the FWE increase, and the methods try to correct for that.
A simple but very stringent method is called the Bonferroni correction intro-

"family" of tests to a corresponding α-level for each individual test by dividing
with the number of tests (m): αcorr = αuncorr/m, [37]. This stringent correction
assumes no correlation between the tests, i.e. that they are independent.
A method controlling the FDR was introduced by Genovese et al [38] and sug-
gested as appropriate for neuroimaging data. It is a statistical process which
ﬁnds the P-value fulﬁlling a desired tolerance of FDR, e.g. 5 %. The calculation
is based on the following equation:

the method during the Partial Least Squares modelling.

4.4.2
Support Vector Machine
The Support Vector Machine (SVM) is considered a state-of-the-art MVPA
technique within neuroimaging, [40]. The method has its roots in the framework
of VC theory, developed by Vapnik and Chervonenkis over the last three decades.
The VC theory seeks to characterize the properties of learning machines from
a statistical point of view - enabling them to generalize to unseen data. The
method provides a discriminative model whose task is to learn the relationship
xi →yi to enable a qualiﬁed mapping of new, unseen objects. Thus, SVM
can be deﬁned as an supervised machine learning technique, where the learning
task can be either a classiﬁcation or a regression problem, [41],[42]. First, the
mathematical principles for a SVM classiﬁer (SVC) are presented after which
extensions to SVM regression (SVR) are reviewed.
At last, a paragraph is
dedicated to the practical implementation and application of the model within
this project.

Project implementation and application
For the implementation of the
SVM, this project utilizes a toolbox by Chih-Chung Chang and Chih-Jen Lin
from National Taiwan University: the LIBSVM software for MATLAB, [?]. It
includes both SVM classiﬁcation and regression, and the possibility of using
linear as well as non-linear kernels.
The input of X to the model is brain activity data in terms of fMRI 1st-level
contrast maps concatenated to a vector. Hence, each row in X represents a
set of voxels for a given observation/subject.
Preliminary to the modelling,
X is scaled to have each voxel (feature) distributed between [-1 1] to avoid
domination from features in a greater numeric range, [44]. At ﬁrst, all voxels
within the brain are used as input to the model, but additional models are made
based on a conﬁned ROI to test whether this screens out noise and improves
the prediction. Depending on the prediction under investigation, the input of
y to the model is either a simple label vector (of -1 and +1) or a performance
score extracted from the PARAFAC model. Following the procedure suggested
by Chih-Wei Hsu et al in [44], the ﬁrst testing involves deciding on a kernel
function and a tuning of the free parameters, C and γ.
Dealing with large
dimensional data, a necessity of non-linear mapping is not expected, however it
is still worth testing
A leave-one-out CV procedure is used in every prediction test to produce ei-
ther a prediction accuracy or a mean squared prediction error (MSE). For the
classiﬁcation tests, the problem statement will be balanced in all cases, hence
the CV accuracy is considered a good measure. The accuracy is tested against
a binomial distribution to test whether the model predicts signiﬁcantly better
than random. The MSE is tested against a non-parametric null distribution
from permutation testing. α = 0.05 is generally used as signiﬁcance level, and
both uncorrected and corrected P-values are considered. In case of a signiﬁcant
prediction a further investigation of the spatial distribution of voxel weights
is presented - including anatomical overlays. For that purpose, the methods
described in the following section, 4.4.3, are used.

4.4.3
Spatial Information from MVPA
Using the SVM within the ﬁeld of neuroscience, it is typically the purpose to
capture a possible relationship between the spatial patterns of neural activity
observed in the fMRI and an experimental or behavioural condition, [32], [4].
The vector of features, x, consists of brain voxels, and their respective weight
parameter will tell something about their relevance to the prediction. Thus,
it would make sense to look at the spatial distribution of the weights for the
extraction of neural information.
But the interpretation of these should be
made with care as the SVM falls into the category of back-wards models, [45].
A large weight does not necessarily mean that the activity observed in the voxel
is strongly related to the condition under investigation. This problem and a
direct solution in a linear case is addressed in the following subsection. Another
way to get a localisation of important brain areas is to look at the prediction
accuracy directly using smaller areas of the brain at a time. This is done in a
method called Searchlight also addressed in the following.
4.4.3.1
Weight Map Interpretation
The interpretability of a model depends on the direction of its functional rela-
tionship. A forward model tries to express the observed data as a function of
underlying variables, X = f(Y). The resulting model parameters are directly
interpretable and can be perceived as an activation pattern, A. In contrast, the
model weights, W, from a backward model can be perceived as an extraction
ﬁlter that decodes latent factors from the data, Y = f(X). The ﬁlter becomes
a function of the signal and noise components found in the data, and it is not
safe to make conclusions about the features on the basis of W in every case.
The SVM, and discriminative classiﬁers in general, are (supervised) backward
models, and a transformation from backward model to the corresponding for-
ward model is optimal before interpreting the outcome. In the case of linear
classiﬁers, the relationship between W and A has been found to be rather sim-
ple. Equation 4.38 shows how to obtain a solution of A, given a backward model
ﬁlter W. For derivation, the reader is referred to [45].

4.4.3.2
Searchlight
Searchlight (SL) is an approach introduced by Nikolaus Kriegeskorte in analysing
functional neuroimaging data with the purpose of targeting diﬀerences in ﬁne-
scale activity patterns and thereby locate brain regions distinguishing the ex-
perimental condition, [46]. The standard parametric mapping of statistical sig-
niﬁcant activity in fMRI processing can be referred to as activation-based anal-
ysis. As mentioned before, the analysis is a mass-univariate approach prone
to noise and lack to combine information from the signal found locally to the
voxel of interest. On the contrary, the SL analysis combine the information
in small spherical areas (subsets) around a voxel and thus produces so-called
information-based maps. The map-value for each voxel is derived from informa-
tion found in the "searchlight area" being centred on this voxel, [47], [32]. This
is illustrated in Figure 4.6.

feature vector for each of the N instances. A classiﬁer is trained based on these features and
the resulting performance, e.g. a CV accuracy, is mapped into the center voxel of the SL. The
SL moves to the next voxel and continues until all voxels have been covered.
The method does not need any a priori knowledge of brain regions, as it can
be a whole-brain approach putting the SL on every corner of the brain. The
information measure used in the analysis can be varied, however it has become
popular to use the generalization accuracy of a classiﬁer - e.g. of the SVM.

4.4.4

As the project concerns the case of analysing a relationship between brain activ-

4.4.5
Sparse Bayesian Learning
The Relevance Vector Machine (RVM) is introduced to the project with the
main purpose of developing a model possessing the ability to supply structural
connectivity information to a multivariate pattern analysis of neuroimaging data
holding functional connectivity. The RVM shares many of the characteristics
of the SVM, but is based on a Sparse Bayesian framework having posterior
probabilities as output rather than just decisions, one of the limitations within
the SVM. The RVM optimises a marginal likelihood function for the target
variable vector and avoids any prior estimation/tuning of e.g.
a complexity
parameter as C, [54]. The Bayesian foundation of the model makes is easy to
modify - simply by adding extra information to the prior distribution of the
model parameters. This has already been done once in order to increase the
intuitive interpretability of the prediction result leading to the Relevance Voxel
Machine (RVoxM). The model assumes that clusters of physically connected
(neighbouring) voxels have similar relevance for predicting the experimental
condition, [55].
In order to take it a step further, it is proposed to include
long-scale white matter ﬁbre connections instead of neighbouring connections.
In the following, the mathematical foundation of the sparse Bayesian regression
model is examined.
Then adjustments for a classiﬁcation problem is added,
and afterwards the extension of RVM to the RVoxM is presented. From here
the desired development into a connectivity-based machine becomes straight
forward.

In both the RVM classiﬁcation and regression, the prior of the weights (seen in
Equation 4.48) is assumed to be Gaussian with α as the precision vector. This
is now extended to include the inﬂuence of a Laplacian graph matrix, K, such
that the precision can be described by the matrix P:

(4.61)
for the prior distribution p(w|α, λ) = N(w|0,P−1). The symmetric graph ma-
trix represents a connectivity between voxels/features either locally or across the
brain. In the ﬁrst case a neighbouring connectivity is considered, and the RVM
turns into the RVoxM proposed by Sabuncu et al [55], who wished to encourage
a spatial clustering of voxels. K can then be deﬁned from the inner product of
the NEdg×M incidence matrix Γ, containing information of the incoming and
outgoing nodes of an edge. An edge connects physically neighbouring locations,
e.g. a central voxel in a volume will have six ingoing/outcoming edges in total.
The structure of K becomes an M×M symmetric matrix with (-1) where two
nodes/voxels are neighbours. The diagonal elements carry the sum of connec-
tions. Together with λ, K imposes a local smoothing over voxel weights, and
the λ parameter determines how similar the weights in a local area should be,
thus the degree of smoothness. As Equation 4.61 reveals, if λ = 0, the model
reduces to the RVM, [57] [55].
In the second case the connectivity is expanded to include remote areas, and the
K is created from a brain connectome. This is a novel approach proposed by this
project to investigate whether structural connectivity obtained from diﬀusion
MRI will be relevant information to the model and increase both prediction
performance and interpretability. The model will be referred to as the Relevance
Connectivity Machine (RConM). Section 4.2.2 describes the procedure of going
from raw diﬀusional data to a connectome to feed into the model. λ is still
a parameter explaining to which degree the connected areas should have an
inﬂuence.

as the standard RVM, but with the inclusion of an extra hyperparameter λ, for
which reason only the re-estimation equation for λ is given here. A standard

4.4.6
The Optimization Process
To summarize, the standard as well as the modiﬁed model relies on the optimiza-
tion of α, and the ﬁnal {αi} values distinguish which voxels (or basis-functions)
are of relevance. A ﬁxed-point optimization scheme including the update equa-
tions in 4.52-4.53 is the original method proposed by Tipping et al for the RVM
and reused by Sabuncu et al for the RVoxM. The process, however, is computa-
tionally expensive, does not guarantee a decrease of the cost-function in every
step, and additionally consists of a greedy forward feature selection (i.e. voxels
cannot be re-introduced to the model after removal), [57]. Thus, alternative
optimization processes have been proposed. First, Tipping et al [56] developed
the FastRVM and later Ganz et al [57] extended this for the RVoxM resulting in
the Improved RVoxM (IRVoxM). The alternative method includes the ability to
analytically judge if a feature should be in the model and allows to re-introduce
"turned-oﬀ" features to the model during optimization. For more details see
[57].

the question of being included in the model or not is the one of interest.
Additionally, a quantitative measure is suggested for measuring the correspon-
dence between a model and the actual structural connectivity. The measure is
derived by ﬁnding the shortest paths between the model nodes using the edges
of the graph matrix deﬁned on the basis of the connectome. The path is thus
an integer representing the number of edges to pass for one node to connect to
another. The inverse path is used as measure such that a value of 1 indicates
that two nodes are directly connected within the graph and 0 indicates that one
or both of the nodes are not part of the graph at all. To take into account that
each node can be weighted very diﬀerently in the model, the inverse paths are
weighted by the average weight of the two nodes in play. As the weights from
the three models can be in diﬀerent scales, they are in each case normalized with
their respective Euclidean norm in advance ( ˆwi =
wi

path for every combination of two nodes in the model is calculated and summed,
and in the end normalized with the number of possible combinations to produce
the ﬁnal measure. It should be mentioned that the negatively weighted set of
nodes and the positively weighted set of nodes are considered separately. Thus,
the measure is calculated twice and summed. Figure 4.8 illustrates the method

Interim discussion
Overall, the SVC model was able to make a good predic-
tion of the two tasks, conﬁrming its utility in MVPA of fMRI data. It is slightly
unexpected that the CSF has such a high prediction value, and the reason could
be headmovement or that the ROI mask simply not extracts the CSF alone
but e.g. part of basal ganglia as well - i.e. that a partial volume eﬀect exists.
It is diﬃcult to prevent that movement (or other leftover noise sources) may
contribute substantially. Additionally, areas irrelevant for the condition might
not be useless to the model as it can assist the model in suppressing noise, [45].
This could explain why the model based on the whole brain predicts better than
the GM and 2nd-level contrast ROI.
The noise and edge eﬀect observed in the spatial distribution of model activa-
tion (Figure 5.7(a)) also indicates that movement has an inﬂuence. It is the
aim to predict from brain activation rather than from movement patterns, and
the role of head movement is thus further investigated in the next subsection.
Apart from the edge eﬀect, activity is also seen in areas where we have a pri-
ori expectation, [59], of an involvement during a motor task, e.g.
M1, PM,
primary somatosensory cortex, cerebellum, and brodmanns area 7 also called
somatosensory association cortex in the parietal lobe, [60]. The last mentioned
is typically involved with visio-motor coordination, making the strong activa-
tion sensible. The sign of activation is related to the sign of the task labels.
E.g. a positive activation is for the task labelled ’+1’ i.e. transfer. However in
this case it is mostly interesting to see the magnitude of activation for the given
discrimination.
Contrary to the activation map, the SL map is not dominated by edges, and
generally the two maps posses diﬀerent features even though having the same
purpose. The SL approach looks at very small areas at a time, and thus cannot
utilize a long-scale functional connectivity across the brain in the prediction.
The full-brain model can capture simultaneous activity in remote areas, but
also becomes more prone to noise as seen in the maps. With that being said,
both methods roughly seem to extract Brodmann area 7 located in the parietal
lobe. Intuitively it seems reasonable that the transfer condition, which demands
high precision in a visual-constrained setup, relies on a somatosensory brain
activation to a higher degree than the pointing condition.
For the further modelling, a linear model is considered appropriate (both in the
case of SVM and of RVM). This is based both on the results of this section, on
the large feature dimension of X, as well as on numerous papers [32] pointing
out that linear classiﬁcation perform well on fMRI data. Additionally, C=1 is
used as initial setting in the rest of the SVM classiﬁcations.

5.5
Discussion Summary
A prediction of motor skill learning, i.e. performance improvement, based on
baseline neural activity (baseline plasticity) was not possible in this dataset -
this was shown both by the SVM and PLS analysis. It is possible that individual
neural diﬀerences to reﬂect talent and skills either are non-existing priorly or
consist of networks too hidden or too ﬁne-scaled to be revealed from the methods
in use.
Turning focus to the experimental setup, there are several factors which are
limiting for the prediction we investigate. First of all, the sample size is rela-
tively small and consists of a homogeneous group of relatively high-performing
medical students. A larger group of e.g. 100 students with diﬀerent educational
background may result in a larger diﬀerentiation among participants from where
e.g. the 20 % worst and 20 % best performing subjects could be extracted for
prediction.
Secondly, the in-scanner and oﬄine tasks do not correlate well.
This has been indicated by the results of this project, but has also been investi-
gated in the larger surgery project, where two in-scanner performance measures,
"drops" and "time" collected from scan-footage, were tested against the simu-
lator performance with no correlation found. This may be due to the diﬀerence
in task diﬃculty, which never goes beyond transfer for the in-scanner exercise,
or to the diﬀerence in body posture and eﬀect of scanner environment. In any
case, it means that a subject handling the tweezers in the scanner poorly can
be well scored in the exercises on the surgery simulator, and this decreases the
predictability. Though it would be advantageous to have the same routine for
both activity recording and training, this is not practically possible with the
limitations of fMRI. A possibility could be to look at the electroencephalogram
(EEG) during oﬄine training sequences and search for predictors within the
EEG instead. Earlier research, [15], seems to have found positive results going
that direction. Another suggestion is to let the subjects get familiar with the
MRI scanner environment and posture before making the ﬁrst actual recording.

The newly developed MOCK scanner, [61], could e.g. be utilized for sham trials.
A test in a MOCK scanner could also be a way of reducing movement artefacts.
We tested the eﬀect of movement in the prediction, and though it was found
reasonably low, the temporal ﬁltering did show a clear presence of movement in
the time-series data - and a reduction is preferable.
Even though only weakly signiﬁcant, I think it is worth mentioning the result of
PLS regression indicating some kind of connection between the baseline brain
activity and the oﬄine ﬁnal performance. It could be interesting to see if exper-
imental replications on a new cohort would demonstrate the same reliance on
diﬀerences in behavioural moving parameters from the simulator, or if it is more
or less a coincidence that these were stressed out here. In the same manner,
it could be interesting to continue an exploration of the spatial distribution of
model loadings across the brain. In this trial, basically no structures were found
with a reliable eﬀect, but a small area of the parietal lobe (Brodmann 7) and
cerebellum were highlighted by thresholding. A replication would show if those
areas could be reproduced, or if the signal simply is to weak and distributed.
Overall, our results produce new hypotheses to pass on to new experiments.
A prediction of certainty within this project was the transfer-pointing classiﬁca-
tion, which both the RVM on a subject level and the SVM on a group level made
convincingly. The spatial maps of model parameters or prediction accuracy (SL)
showed a certain correspondence with what to expect during motor task per-
formance, e.g inclusion of M1, cerebellum and the somatosensory cortex of the
parietal lobe. However in general for MVPA, the model-based maps should be
evaluated with a certain criticism. The forward-backward model transformation
is one way to avoid making interpretations from noise, but there do not seem to
be a clear consensus within the ﬁeld on how to most correctly interpret model
parameters. The SL technique has also been under criticism and literature [62]
suggest certain tools to ensure that results are not distorted. I.e. this method
cannot be trusted blindly either.
Regarding the novel model, we consider the implementation of the RConM suc-
cesful. It delivered results on the level of the original RVoxM, and there was even
small signs of improvement. The weighted inverse path measure indicated that
the classiﬁcation model uses the information of the diﬀusion connectivity - at
least to some extend. Thus, even though the prediction was not improved signif-
icantly, the model’s interpretability might increase and the results obtain better
anatomical correspondence. But more validation measures should be investi-
gated in that sense, e.g. a Mutual Information measure could be considered. In
this ﬁrst trial of the suggested model, several free parameters were set from what
seemed reasonable or computational advantageous. This includes the number
of parcels (300), the region of interest (from an fMRI based mask), number of
streamlines to use in the connectome generation (106), and the threshold of the

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this project, the predictive value of neural patterns at baseline for a training
outcome has been investigated on the basis of functional and behaviroual data
of 50 medical students. Contrasts for the two experimental conditions was ex-
tracted from the functional MRI data by standard preprocessing and analysis.
An extra eﬀort was made in the process of removing movement-artefacts, and
though relatively high framewise displacements was observed, the additional
ﬁltering of data was not applied, as the gained eﬀect was considered to small
relative to the loss of signal power. A PARAFAC model was used to extract
a component of the behavioural data reﬂecting performance improvement. A
component of relevance was possible to ﬁnd, however despite of small model
modiﬁcations, it was not possible to remove the high correlation to baseline
performance.
The state-of-the-art SVM could make a successful discrimina-
tion between the two motor conditions, but a prediction of the subject speciﬁc
PARAFAC score based on the baseline of the high-level controlled complex bi-
manual condition was not possible to make.
In the continued search for an
activity-behaviour correlation, a PLS algorithm was applied directly to a set of
the raw performance measures. In this case no prediction of the ﬁve-day perfor-
mance improvement could be made, but vague indications suggest a correlation
between the neural baseline activity and the ﬁnal behavioural performance, how-
ever experimental replications are needed for veriﬁcation. So bottom line is that
so far, baseline fMRI brain activity do not have predictive value for the learning
performance and cannot be the starting point for an evidence based strategy in
selection of laparoscopic surgeons. This also means that neural areas expected
to be speciﬁc for skill learning could not be conﬁrmed by this experiment. Your
behavioural starting point is thus still one of the best predictors for your end

In a second part of this project, a novel model involving structural connectiv-
ity has been proposed in order to improve model interpretability and predic-
tion. In that process, a standard preprocessing and streamline tracking was
applied to subject speciﬁc DTI data, after which connectivity was found within
a parcellation structure generated by an fMRI data driven approach. Inspired
by the RVoxM, a Laplacian graph holding the strongest connections found in
the individual connectome was used as additional information in the prior of
model weights within a sparse Bayesian prediction approach. The novel model,
RConM, managed to obtain a good classiﬁcation of the two experimental condi-
tions on a subject level and showed a prediction performance on a level with the
RVM and RVoxM. A proposed measure of the models’ connectivity correspon-
dence ﬁnds the RConM of having the highest. Overall, prediction has not yet
been proven to be improved by inclusion of structural connectivity, but there
are indications of a model having higher interpretability while keeping a good
prediction performance. Thus, there are reasons to continue an investigation of
the utility and application of the RConM.
6.1
Outlook
If the investigations of this project do not get one to accept that brain activity at
the resolution level possible to detect today cannot reveal learning abilities, there
are ways of changing the experimental setup for a new investigation of motor skill
learning. Several were mentioned in the discussion, and one speciﬁc could be to
screen a large group of 100-200 individuals with a short ﬁve-minutes test session
to pick out the "extremes" and thereby have a stronger basis for diﬀerentiation
among subjects. In this project, laparoscopic skills were of interest, however a
neural basis of learning ability could also be searched for using other complex
motor tasks in a similar manner. In every case, it would be a good idea to
consider how movement artefacts could be kept low and how the eﬀect of baseline
behaviour more eﬀectively could be extracted from data.
Considering the novel RConM, it has already been mentioned that it could be
interesting to validate its performance further by applying it to more relevant
data. Additionally, it could be interesting to extend the model to be applicable
on a group level. I.e. modify the relevance machine to take a subject speciﬁc
connectivity graph as input while being trained on the whole group. In a future
work, it could also be interesting to include the improved optimization scheme
proposed by Tipping and Ganz. This implies making the IRVoxM algorithm
work more properly without numerical limitations, so that the improved selec-

Appendix C
Appendix C
By thresholding the y-variable used as input to the SVM regression, it is turned
into a classiﬁcation problem which might be easier for the model to predict. The
threshold is varied, and the results for the trials are listed in the following page.
At the top of each plot, the data used is speciﬁed. The left column of plots is the
one of most interest, having the X data from day 1 (baseline) and the y-variable
as the PARAFAC factor 2 - just as in the regression case. The trials in the right
column is for exploration. As the data becomes unbalanced for the diﬀerent
thresholds, both the accuracy, the AUC (area under ROC-curve), and the MCC
(Matthews correlation coeﬃcient) are plotted. None of the results show a good
prediction. For a 2nd level contrast basically extracting the premotor cortex,
an AUC of ∼0.7 and MCC of ∼0.4 for one of the thresholds is obtained as the
best observed result. But with the many tests made, such result was more or
less bound to happen at some point, and it is not considered relevant.
