Abstract (English)
The goal of the thesis is to model how the weather impacts behavior on social
media. We use large-scale data from both the meteorological and social media
data realm. The overall methodology is to use meteorological data as the inde-
pendent variable when ﬁtting a linear model which accounts for multiple group
ﬁxed eﬀects. In terms of behaviors on social media, as a dependent variable
we use both 1) the volume of tweets and 2) Twitter sentiment estimated using
a state-of-the-art model. We aggregate the Twitter data by geography (using
both a grid based, and county level aggregation), investigating the eﬀect of the
urban/rural divide based on population density. The thesis ﬁrst examines the
quality of the raw data sources and cleans the dataset. Based on the cleaned
data explore the connection between weather and behavior using our ﬁxed ef-
fect model. Our main ﬁnding is that temperature and precipitation predict the
volume of social media posts in both rural and urban areas, while other me-
teorological factors do not yield signiﬁcant correlations. In terms of sentiment
analysis, we use a novel 7-dimensional sentiment scoring. We ﬁnd that weather
factors show a correlation with ‘Joy’ and ‘Anger’, but a more ambiguous re-
lationship to ‘Sadness’, ‘Disgust’, ‘Fear’, ‘Shame’ and ‘Guilt’ as expressed on
Twitter.
Our work establishes a baseline of how to integrate meteorological
with social media data; we hope that this methodology can be expanded to
analyze anthropogenic climate change and extreme weather conditions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The changes of daily weather have been proved to signiﬁcantly inﬂuence human
moods and behaviours not only as individual but also the trends of entire soci-
ety in many perspectives. Many meteorological variables have been linked to a
variety of moods [Howarth and Hoﬀman, 1984]. For example, in Howarth and
Hoﬀman’s research, it is shown that high levels of humidity lowered scores on
concentration while increasing reports of sleepiness. Rising temperatures low-
ered anxiety and scepticism mood scores. Another study conducted by [Keller
et al., 2005] suggests that pleasant weather improves mood and broadens cogni-
tion in the spring because people have been deprived of such weather during the
winter. As for some social behaviours, weather has been modelled and proved to
have a strong relationship with active transportation travel behaviour [Saneine-
jad et al., 2012] and consumer spending [Murray et al., 2010]. It is obvious
that weather conditions play an important role in deﬁning human activities and
habits after analysis of a large amount of historic cross-sectional panel data. On
the other hand, during the evolution of social media in the past decade, there
has been a shift of consumption from traditional(e.g. Newspaper) to social me-
dia(e.g. Blog). Rather than a single journalist or scientist telling the public
exactly what to think, social media oﬀers a mechanism for many people of di-
verse backgrounds to communicate and form their own opinions [Cody et al.,
2015]. Social media has become the primary choice for people to express their
sentiments and opinions in daily life.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
The impact of weather changes on human behaviours and the increment of
human activities in cyber world have raised attention on whether the weather
variables are correlated with social media factors produced by the public. The
question educes the hypothesis of the paper that, the social media factors derived
from raw data can be used as a sensor for the weather changes.
Many researches have focused on utilizing Twitter data and employed many
techniques such as nature language processing and sentiment analysis [Sarlan
et al., 2014] to investigate diﬀerent potential linkages of two phenomena in ﬁelds
such as crime prediction [Chen et al., 2015], health allergy surveillance [Lee et al.,
2015] and monitoring natural disaster [Maldonado et al., 2016]. The paper aims
at investigating the relationship between standard meteorological factors such
as temperature, precipitation etc. and social media factors from Twitter, tweet
volume and sentiment scores. This paper uses a diﬀerent approach, Multi-group
Fixed Eﬀect (MGFE), which derives from concepts in econometrics. The tech-
nique has been used into the relationship between meteorological conditions
and the sentiment of human expressions from Twitter [Baylis et al., 2018] where
weather variables are aggregated and used as independent variables to predict
the expressed sentiment at city and user level. This paper turns [Baylis et al.,
2018] research into baseline, partially reproduces the same analysis for contigu-
ous US with diﬀerent granularity, enriches the categorization of data points with
rural-urban scheme and expands the analysis with tweet volume as dependent
variables.
The paper is structured as follows: the relevant literature of current ﬁndings and
research phases in this subject area is going to be discussed in chapter 2. Multiple
data sets are presented in chapter 3 including the spatial scope and temporal
length and some analysis of the data issues. Additionally, the formulation of
the learning process is also included in chapter 3, as well as multiple empirical
model architectures. All observations and results are in chapter 4, a number of
visualization is going to be appended in the chapter to give intuitive impression
of the results. Discussion are concluded in chapter 5, some open questions and
potential extensions to the research are generally illustrated in chapter 6.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Weather data is usually managed by relevant departments or academic orga-
nizations and given interfaces for open usage. Normally, in terms of accuracy,
compatibility and universality for all ﬁelds of study, weather data is saved in
a subtle, ﬁne-grained and easy-consumed way. For the research, the only un-
certainty comes from how to perform conglomeration on diﬀerent data sources
and integrated together afterwards. In contrast, the diﬃculty issues from so-
cial media data, which can be literally translated into how to obtain two met-
rics, user-generated content and sentiment. First of all, data collection phase
is more trivial than weather data as most of social media data is unstructured
or semi-structured streaming data without a determined ﬁxed schema. Then,
in modelling phase, what features of social media data should be used as rep-
resentative to model social media output, what tools within natural language
processing or statistical modelling techniques should be used to quantify the
sentiment of texts and what empirical model should be used to explore the re-
lationship between weather and social media features etc. result in a series of
questions which require a thorough review of existing studies in the ﬁrst place
so that a deliberate solution can be introduced to the project.

2.1
Meteorological Data Resolution and Conglom-
eration
There are generally two types of meteorological data collection, high quality
Measured Weather Data (MWD), normally retrieved from weather stations,
and Gridded Weather Data (GWD), derived from satellite images and interpo-
lation from sophisticated empirical model with certain spatial resolution. MWD
is known for the accuracy and robustness of measurement but the drawback
is also undisguised. In case of US, the uneven spatial distribution of weather
stations results in inevitable troubles to predict and forecast eﬀect of weather
on many industries [Mourtzinis et al., 2017]. The paper has argued the current
trend is to use GWD as basis of weather measurement for scientiﬁc researches
but also questioned the robustness and potential biases in GWD [Mourtzinis
et al., 2017]. It appears that the resolution of regional grid signiﬁcantly varies
the bias of the model and subsequent predictions. For instance, in regional air
quality predictions of organic aerosol in Canada, the 15-km model is able to
yield similar result as 2.5-km one on domain-wide average but for speciﬁc urban
and rural area, 2.5-km model is outperformed [Stroud et al., 2011]. Similar phe-
nomenon also takes place in modelling of heavy rainfall event in Sicily, Italy. The
improvement of spatial grid resolution from 10km to 0.8km is the key element to
prediction accuracy [Caccamo et al., 2017]. Both researches suggest that higher
resolution of grid usually comes up with promising result but it depends on
many other factors such as terrain, land use etc.
The intention of this project towards data resolution is to ﬁrstly select the
most authorized sources in respect to each distinct weather variable and then
look for the ones with highest resolution among these selected datasets. The
approach guarantees the validity and veracity of the dataset and feasibility of
the subsequent modelling process.
As for data conglomeration level, the county map of US is an administrative
divisions to consolidate data points to. The area of county varies from 229,720
km2(North Slope Borough) to 5.2 km2(City of Falls Church, Virginia). Even
though they are in irregular shape and range in land coverage, it still has been
widely used by researchers as fundamental unit for modelling weather data [Elias
et al., 2017] and county-level conglomeration also shows strength of integration
with institutional data ﬁles. Therefore, county-level conglomeration becomes
the prime choice for this project. On the other hand, to validate the result
from county-level data modelling, a grid-based consolidation solution is also re-
quired to act as robustness checks of the project. Standard Numeric Weather
Prediction (NWP) usually have a wide range of variations in grid point tween
distance. For example, some popular National Oceanic and Atmospheric Ad-

2.2
Screening of User-generated Content
In social media, User-generated Content (UGC) refers to any form of visual
media(e.g. text, video, image) that is created by users and become public on
online system. However, it is indeterminate to deﬁne the scope of the content
and quantify afterwards. In many cases, UGC in social media is intimately re-
lated to many common metrics such as comment, post and blog. The researches
on UGC usually fall in media sociology, journalism research, media content re-
search, and reception and eﬀects research [Naab and Sehl, 2016]. As a major
perspective in media content research, sentiment classiﬁcation has been rapidly
developed and studied [Vosoughi et al., 2015]. This research from MIT me-
dia Lab constructs a tweet-based sentiment classiﬁer by combining a Bayesian
model of tweet metadata(e.g. geo-location, timestamp) with standard linguistic
features(e.g. n-grams). The enrichment of Bayesian model of metadata makes
the model outperform the plain linguistic classiﬁer. Using machine learning
models, for instance, support vector machine [Ahmad et al., 2017] and naive
Bayesian [Wang and Manning, 2012] has achieved high performance on classi-
ﬁcation accuracy. As [Muhammad et al., 2016] pointed out, machine learning
sentiment classiﬁers tend to be highly domain/genre speciﬁc, performing well
on the domain/genre of training but poorly on a diﬀerent domain/genre. This is
important insight for this project since we intent to work with social media site
Twitter using English-only tweets sent from contiguous US, it requires a dedi-
cated tweet-trained high-performance classiﬁer. Few candidates will be present
in chapter 3.
Besides sentiment, an interesting discussion falls in understanding of simple
tweet volume in a time series, for example, relationship between user engage-
ment on Twitter and Real-World events [Hu et al., 2015]. As one of the core
measurement of engagement, total number of tweets in a temporal span is always
a signiﬁcant indicator of correlation between human behaviour and activities in

2.3
Modelling Grid Weather Data and Screened
User-generated Content
In psychological literature, the correlation between weather and people mood
has been conducted in many researches with diﬀerent models and the result is
surprisingly diverse. [Li et al., 2014] presents a solution to use general mixture
model to capture the non-linear relationship between mood and climate factors,
while [Baylis et al., 2018] introduces a linear regression to model same relation-
ship. Even though the model varies, these studies have shown common results
that the meteorological factors, temperature, temperature range, precipitation,
humidity etc. are highly correlated with mood on social media. In contrast,
there are no studies found for correlation between weather factors and simple
tweet volume without sentiment evaluations so the model on the volume would
produce many insights to this subject.
The linear modelling process from [Baylis et al., 2018] has also employed by
Patrick Baylis in an earlier paper [Baylis, 2015]. The core concept of both pa-
pers references the FEM massively used in econometrics and biostatistics while
the weather factors act as time-variant independent variables and the measures
of hedonic state becomes the dependent variable. As a type of regression model,
the most important beneﬁt of FEM is it takes into account of unobserved het-
erogeneous variables(e.g. ‘geo-location’). All these variables are likely to cor-
relate to weather factors and dependent variable(e.g. sentiment score, tweet
volume). These unobserved variables for plain ordinary least square modelling
may lead to omitted variable bias.
It is obvious that both meteorological and social media data of contiguous US
are cross sectional data while each data points have a number of heterogeneous
features. Meanwhile, the FEM has also been widely used in analysis weather
impacts on US corn production [Cai et al., 2012], soybean yields [Deschênes
and Greenstone, 2007] and cotton yields [Schlenker and Roberts, 2009]. Baylis’s
approach gives a clear clue of how to utilize same setup of FEM and control these
subject-speciﬁc factors in analysis weather impacts on social media domain. The
process is going to be elaborated in chapter 3 with dedicated changes to ﬁt in
this research.

Despite of the rendered color palettes and tile for visual intelligibility of the plots,
PRISM provides 621x 1405 raster data with higher resolution and constant cell
size(projection of 4 km2 in reality) and yielded from a more composite and so-
phisticated meteorological model which takes into account of, for instance, ter-
rain, elevation and other local weather patterns. In contrast, NOAA’s GHCND
has a very unstable, inconsistent and unevenly-distributed network. Most of
the stations locate at the urban area with relatively higher population density
and barely any resides in the rural. Besides, a certain proportion of the sta-
tions does not remain service through years. it means that the decommission of
certain stations can lead to unstable measurements of weather normal at some
given locations.
Most importantly, many synoptic stations in GHCND network are well outside
and their distances exceed 50 kilometers from the urban area. The diﬀerences of
meteorological natures between stations within tens of kilometers will generally
be smoothed out over time. In consequence, the lack of local meteorological data
does not present a problem in the case of long-term averages.
The demerit of PRISM is that it does not have grid coverage over non-terrestrial
areas such as the Great Lakes. There are very few observations with geo-tags
retrieved from the Great Lakes. It is likely that the tweets are sent out from ves-
sels or anonymous islets where PRISM grid points are completely absent. Since
the ﬁx eﬀect model requires meteorological factors to present as covariates, such
small fraction of data points with missing value is discarded.
Unlike PRISM, NCEP has the global Gaussian grid covering all terrains but
the resolution is much lower than PRISM. Despite all this, the dataset is re-
viewed constantly by Physical Sciences Division for quality, relevance, and per-
formance. Therefore, NCEP is nonetheless used for wind, humidity and cloud
cover inspections.

3.1.3.2
Sentiment Analysis Data
To measure expression of sentiment, we use DeepMoji project [Felbo et al.,
2017], which utilizes millions of texts on Twitter containing emojis for under-
standing many nuances of how language is used to express emotion. The model
has also provided interfaces to perform transfer learning with additional ref-
erences. Besides binary emotional polarity such as positive/negative scheme,
the reference can have multiple emotion classes for reﬁned categorization. An-
other validated dictionary-based sentiment classiﬁcation tool is Linguistic In-
quiry Word Count(LIWC) [Tausczik and Pennebaker, 2009]. It has been widely
used in social media text analysis but there are certain limitations to use LIWC
in my research. LIWC has been made to use realistic texts to accommodate
sentiment analysis from diﬀerent sources while DeepMoji is a dedicated model
constructed exclusively by 1.2 billion(ﬁltered from 55 billion) of tweets contain-
ing 64 well-deﬁned emojis. An emoji has a strong linkage and understanding of
the emotional contents of a sentence. By employing this extra feature, the model
can capture more polyseme and sarcasm. The knowledge of emojis can be easily
transferred to a speciﬁc target task by introducing additional layer on top of the
model. Thus, DeepMoji is chosen with assistance of “PsychExp” [Wallbott and
Scherer, 1986] dataset including 7 emotions(joy, fear, anger, sadness, disgust,
shame and guilt) for ﬁne-tunning the pretrained model. As a result, while the
ﬁne-tunned model is used to estimate tweets, each tweet will be mapped to a
7-dimension vector with probabilities corresponding to 7 emotions.

3.2.1
Data Preprocessing
3.2.1.1
Cleaning
To frame the weather features into contiguous US, a bounding box technique
is used to set intervals for longitude and latitude. Any points do not fall in
the rectangle box are discarded. However, an arbitrary bounding box on US
extreme points always ends up into two typical errors. First of all, a massive
amount of lands including some major cities of neighbor countries are cropped in
the bounding box. As a result, many tweets are captured from these areas which
are in fact not tweets from US. Secondly, there are also tweets recorded from
non-land areas such as ocean or lakes. These observations do not necessarily have
weather features associated for instance PRISM does not provide grid coverage
to any non-land areas. Thus, PRISM, as a model exclusively covering continental
US, is an ideal ﬁlter to cut oﬀdata points that are either overseas or oﬀshore.
To investigate whether the relationship between any weather and twitter fac-
tors changes while weather anomalies occur, the historic weather normals are
==PAGE:30==
14
Materials and Methods
also computed. The weather normal refers to 3-decade average of climatological
variables including temperature and precipitation [Arguez et al., 2012]. In this
research, 1980 - 2010 weather normals is directly summarized from PRISM grid.
Tweet features cleaning includes two steps. Firstly, since the research relies on
geo-location to pinpoint the source of a tweet, any tweet with no “geo-tag” is
removed. Secondly, regular expression checks are also applied to ensure suﬃcient
information can be retrieved from each tweet. Thus, we remove URLs, hashtags
and any one-word or one-character tweet. Additionally, the latitude/longitude
precision given by Twitter data reaches 6 decimal places which is worth up to
0.11 meter. Generally speaking, each “latitude/longitude/timestamp” identiﬁer
corresponds to only one user tweet at a time. Even though it is very rare to have
many diﬀerent users producing tweet within 0.11 meters at same day, it is still
likely to have multiple tweets sent from same “user” at exact same location in
a day. In many cases, it is a service “user”(a non-human machine or stationary
reporting service) constantly producing tweets for speciﬁc purpose or industry
during a regular day. To reduce the noise, we preserve 99.7% of data so any
observation with count greater than 3 standard deviation from the mean are
discarded.
3.2.1.2
Aggregation
Before aggregation, each joint observation is categorized into “urban” and “rural”
class by using population density criterion from [Organization for Economic Co-
operation and Development, 2012]. Then, each joint observation is mapped into
either a 50-km,a 25-km grid cell or a county unit. In each grid cell, the weather
features are averaged in both urban and rural class and counts are summed
respectively in each class. Some cells located at population-densed regions may
not have any rural values while others located at Midwestern US may have no
urban data. In any case, the weather and sentiment features of the absent class
are set to “NaN” and count is set to 0. What’s more, there are some extra sea-
sonal eﬀects on the tweet volume. For example, most of people have vacation
period during summer and Christmas-New Year leave during winter. These con-
ventional activities can bias the total amount of tweet of the month. Therefore,
to control the eﬀect, monthly average is removed from all observations. We made
two plots for the chosen resolutions in ﬁgure 3.4 and 3.5. As for county mapping,
the shapeﬁle of all counties is used to determine where joint observations fall
in. Similarly, in ﬁgure 3.6, the ﬂuorescent green clusters on the background Map-
box tile6 indicates the metropolitan areas of US while the aggregated painted
counties summarized from our data covers many metropolitan areas such as New

3.2 Data Modelling

The intention of these heatmaps is to project the data onto a lower dimen-
sion(2D) so that we can intuitively gain insights about the general distribution
of the data and potential relationships to built.
Generally speaking, we need a model which is able to eliminate the eﬀects of
all individual-speciﬁc features and variation for each individual. Thus, FEM is
estimated and employed in the research.
3.2.2
Multi-group Fixed Eﬀect Modelling
3.2.2.1
Background Theory
The fundamental problem for ordinary least square solution is that The general
formula of FEM is shown as:
Yit = Xitβ + αi + ϵit
(3.1)
Yit is the dependent variable of individual i at time t. Xit is a series of time-
variant independent variables and β is their corresponding parameters. αi is
unobserved time-invariant of individual i. ϵit is the idiosyncratic error describing
error from panel data that both changes over time and across individuals. As it
is explained in chapter 2, the control of αi is the key of FEM. Since all these
unobserved individual factors are assumed to be ﬁxed over time, then αi = αi
should hold. Thus, they can be eliminated by subtracting the mean from formula
3.1.
In a Multi-group Fixed Eﬀect (MGFE), beyond the basic equation, to control
the eﬀects from each independent variable, each variable can be mapped to a
series of groups and for each group, it is qualitatively mapped to a indicator
variable. Then each indicator variable behaves in the way as it equals 1 when
regression takes place within the group and equals 0 otherwise. For example, if
we have two independent quantitative variables i, t and each has been mapped
to 3 qualitative groups 1,2 and 3. then the equation can be written as:
Yit = β0 + α2i2 + α3i3 + φ2t2 + φ3t3 + ϵit
(3.2)
There are a set of group i1, t1 omitted in the equation in order to avoid perfect
==PAGE:38==
22

collinearity11. The intercept β0, which gives the average performance given zero
values for all of the explanatory variables model, which, in other words, corre-
sponds to the scenario when i1 and t1 equal 1. The parameters, αm, φn of each
predictor in 3.2 represents the average diﬀerence relative to the omitted group.
So far, the MGFE model has demonstrated great strength to control the unob-
served variables which are mostly likely to be correlated but overlooked by naive
regression model. For intuitive visualization in 2D, many factors are compelled
and ﬁxed arbitrarily by us, but in high dimension, it is important to have a
mechanism to control all factors constantly.
3.2.2.2
Implementation
In this section, we use county-level resolution and analysis of tweet count to illus-
trate the implementation and how to empirically build the model of MGFE. The
equation is shown as:
Yijtm = f(TMAXijtm) + g(PRCPijtm) + Zη + αi + µt + νjm + ϵijtm
(3.3)
In this time-series cross-sectional equation 3.3, i indexes counties, j indexes
states, t indexes calendar days, and m indexes calendar months. Our depen-
dent variable Yijtm corresponds to the log number of tweets in county i in state
j on calendar day t within calendar month m. Our independent variables of
interest are maximum daily temperature, TMAXijtm, and daily precipitation,
PRCPijtm. We also control for temperature range(TRANG), percentage cloud
cover(PCC), relative humidity(RH), and average wind speed(WS), represented
via Zη, as failure to do so may bias our estimates of the eﬀect of maximum
temperatures and precipitation on the log number of tweets. We include αi rep-
resenting county indicator variables and µt, νjs, representing calendar date and
county-by-calendar month indicator variables, respectively to control for other
confounds. The αi controls the unobserved diﬀerence in geographic factors and
µt is for unobserved temporal factors. Additionally, νjs is responsible for con-
trolling the county-speciﬁc trends such as impacts of development of economies
or populations on tweet features. The ϵijtm represents idiosyncratic error that
varies for each individual ij and time tm.
Additionally, we also bin weather explanatory variables and use indicator vari-
ables respectively for each 5◦C TMAX, 1cm PRCP, 5◦C TRANG, 20% PCC,

We choose most comfortable weather conditions with common senses and ac-

Following the climate econometric literature [Hsiang, 2016],[Hsiang et al., 2017],

county-day.

4.1.2
Anomalies
We subtract the historic weather normal from each explanatory variable so that
the relative magnitude is used for MGFE. We omitted [−2.5, 2.5] degree inter-
val for temperature plots as it can be seen as stable as historic mean. Likewise,
[−0.5, 0.5] centimeter for precipitation. Generally, we expect the same relation-
ships in previous section should be ampliﬁed and the results between TMAX
and tweet count are shown in ﬁgure 4.3 and PRCP in ﬁgure 4.4.
The 25km (4.3a, 4.3d and 4.3g) and 50km(4.3b, 4.3e and 4.3h) conglomeration
levels show consistent trend and distribution of anomalies. It appears that de-
spite urban, rural or integrated case, when the temperature of the day is lower
than usual, people intent to produce more tweets but when the temperature
of the day is getting higher than usual, the increment of tweet production is
not signiﬁcant. This phenomena can also be seen in statistical summary of the
model in appendix B.2. When temperature is higher, their Pr(t) also get higher,
meaning they become insigniﬁcant to the tweet count.
The county-level series, ﬁgure 4.3c, 4.3f and 4.3i, shows more signiﬁcant ﬂuctu-
ation and uncertainty. It is mainly because of the irregular geometric shape of
counties. Some counties is too large in land coverage to get their meteorological
features regularized to one center point. For example, Inyo County, California1
covers a wide range of latitude and diﬀerent landform including death valley
national park. The change of temperature is dramatic both spatially and tem-
porally(even within a day) as well as population distribution. Thus, the temper-
ature of an inhabitant area is likely to be very diﬀerent to the mean value of the
county. As a result, without a well-deﬁned ﬁxed geometric grid, the distribution
of anomaly ends up to be more spread and uncertainty of the curve is more
signiﬁcant than 25km and 50km grid.
Besides the distribution and uncertainty shadow in the plots, the rural-only plot
ﬁgure 4.3i shows a slightly diﬀerent monotonous feature than others. The plot
indicates that when the temperature of the day is lower than usual, people intent
to produce fewer other than more tweets. However, due to the uncertainty and

Chapter 5
Discussion
The ﬁndings from the two experiments are exhilarated as they produce similar
results as [Baylis et al., 2018] and the additional move, enrichment of data points
categorization with rural/urban scheme as well as the sentiment scoring, also
present pattern of human behavior on social media and answer the relationship
between weather features and tweet volume and sentiment ﬂow. Our research
began with a hypothesis that the derived social media factors can be used as a
sensor to weather changes and the results from experiments have been able to
support it with a series of answers. Generally, the MGFE presents a stronger
relationship for the tweet count experiment and the sentiment one appears to
be ﬂawed, but we are going to elaborate on both cases and give a general com-
mentary of some key ﬁndings rather than a simple regurgitation of results in
the chapter.
People use less social media when the temperature is within the com-
fortable range. Regardless of urban and rural scheme, all curves in ﬁgure 4.1
mould similar U-shape relationship. The relative tweet volume of each curve
bounces up while the temperature drifts apart from the comfortable range. As
a signature of UGC, tweet volume in the ﬁgure perfectly represents the amount
of user engagement with social media. Thus, it proves that we can sense the
temperature changes by looking at the general ﬂuctuation of the amount of
UGC.
==PAGE:60==

Discussion
People use more social media when precipitation is higher. Similarly,
the monotonous increasing curves also reveal the impact of precipitation on the
social media engagement. The indoor-outdoor activity conjecture mentioned
in section 4.1.1 and 4.1.2 can be the main reason behind the phenomena but
to verify the assumption, we need to be more speciﬁc of where the tweet is
from, what amenity the tweet is in and what overall percentage of tweets are
sent from indoor areas. We believe by introducing a ﬁne-grained mapping and
geocoding package such as Openstreetmap1 in the future, the data points can
then be categorized into subtler classes and we will be able to explain such
conjecture. Nevertheless, the impact of precipitation on UGC can not be ignored
and we can clearly sense the increment of rainfall from the volume of UGC.
Unlike the abnormal hot days, an unusually cold day makes people use
more social media. It is an important ﬁnding from ﬁgure 4.3. Social media
engagement remains constant while the daily temperature is higher than usual
but when the temperature plummets, people begin to produce more tweets. The
exception happens for county level agglomeration results. This is an expected
ﬂuctuation as county is usually in irregular shape and has huge variation in
area. As a result, it is diﬃcult to regularize a single historic mean weather
factor for these irregular polygons. For example, a granulated data point might
be averaged by all urban areas which are only located closely to the bounder
of a county. However, the centroid weather features of the same county are
most likely be inﬂuenced exclusively by very local factors which are contributed
by surrounding rural areas. Therefore, The robustness check groups, 25km and
50km grid, managed to reveal a clear relationship but the county level failed
with noises.
The surplus precipitation than usual has positive eﬀect on amount
of social media usage.
As another fact of weather anomaly, people’s social
media production has a monotonous increasing relationship with magnitude of
surplus precipitation 4.4. Similar to the second ﬁnding, when the precipitation
of a day is constant as usual, the social media engagement of the day is relatively
less than the case when there is surplus precipitation. It is sensible to believe that
people use more digital device indoor than outdoor and surplus precipitation
gives more opportunities to people to remain indoors. The veriﬁcation shall be
carried out in the same way as the second ﬁnding.
However, it is worth mentioning that our analysis is based on a country with
very regional and predominate meteorological features. To highlight the reac-
tion of social media on weather anomaly eﬀects, it is necessary to repeat same
experiment in countries with more extreme weather conditions as the result may
be signiﬁcantly magniﬁed or completely adverse.

The inﬂuence of temperature and precipitation on other emotions are
undetermined. We were not capable to capture meaningful results on how to
use the change of ‘Sadness’, ‘Disgust’, ‘Fear’, ‘Shame’ and ‘Guilt’ senses of the
public to reﬂect weather changes. The inexplicable modelling results of these
emotions also indicate other shortcomings of the sentiment experiments. We
ﬁgure out that the 7-dimension sentiment classiﬁer has a major weakness in de-
termining the exact emotions. Especially when the text or sentence is extremely
short and neutral, the model tries to give even probability to all 7 dimensions
and ﬁt it to the spike dimension with low conﬁdence. Many neutral statements
are likely to be misplaced into wrong emotion classes. To overcome the issue,
we should ﬁrst of all reduce the dimension of interests. To compare with LIWC,
we can introduce 3-dimensional ‘Positive’, ‘Neutral’ and ‘Negative’ scheme for
transfer learning and secondly set a threshold to the probability so that only
the ones that distinctly express either positive or negative sentiment will be
classiﬁed accordingly and the rest would be grouped into neutral statements.

6.1
Implication and Limitation
Even though we have mentioned many achievements and drawback of the result
in the previous chapter, rooted in the notions of social science, the project has
indeed shown a great potential of revealing the eﬀects of natural activity beyond
human activity in reality but in cyberworld by employing FEM. A few conclu-
sions can be drawn from the study regard to implication and limitation. Ex-
isting researches have presented multiple approaches to investigate the eﬀects
of regular and extreme weather conditions on human daily mood and the do-
main has been properly expanded into sentiment on social media. Our research
has turned the head down to the more quantiﬁed perspective of user-generated
content, namely the volume and examine the validity of employed approach
upon the mature ﬁeld of sentiment research. The observations clearly show that
the volume of daily generated tweet volume can be deﬁnitely seen as an indi-
cator of daily temperature and precipitation changes even in case of weather
anomaly. The result of comparative sentiment research generally complies to
the previous study which also in turn justiﬁes the performance of DeepMoji
classiﬁer. Both branched researches prove the availability and validity of FEM
in the paradigm. On one hand, in a scientiﬁc perspective, with suﬃcient data
support as a foundation, the approach is worth trying to be used for study on
other countries or expanded stage onto global phenomena. On the other hand,
==PAGE:64==
48

in data modelling perspective, it is common to use regression technique on panel
data and the project has provided a reﬁned approach to control the biases in
regression modelling.
We have discussed many demerits emerged during the execution of the project
in chapter 5. It can be concluded as two types of issue, data and robustness
issue of sentiment classiﬁer. There is no doubt that even though we have tried
to collect as many data as we could, the overall set is still a fraction piece from
the whole society. Besides, comparing with data quantity issue, data retrieval
issue for both Twitter and weather data is a more critical one. For example, if
we were capable to have access to all tweet record of US, include non-geotagged
tweet records and manage a more stratiﬁed sample subset, DeepMoji classiﬁer
should be capable to produce more precise results of sentiment analysis. On
the right hand side of the equation, measurement error may also exist between
observed realistic weather and the gridded weather. The error can be especially
noticeable with respect to measures of cloud cover, humidity and wind speed as
they are yielded from NCEP, a model constructed from reanalysis data rather
than physical stations.
6.2
Future Research
We have discussed the possibility to include indoor-outdoor scheme and employ-
ment of LIWC in chapter 5, but there are still more gaps and opening questions
which would beneﬁt from further research.
• Focusing on global phenomena and paying more attention to climate changes
would be beneﬁcial. Many studies have used data of extreme weather con-
ditions such as earthquakes of paciﬁc rim, ﬂooding of Europe and hurri-
canes of North America in order to investigate the inﬂuence on human
behaviors. Our approach can be transplanted to study such phenomena in
longer-term studies. It should be able to show how the change of public
opinion toward climate change on social media.
• Enriching the dataset for both left and right side of the equation could be
helpful. As for Twitter, we are constantly looking for a chance to collabo-
rate with GNIP team under the protocol of academic research. The team
has been granted access to all Twitter data which are composed of more
sophisticated meta and structure. As for meteorological data, substitu-
tion of NCEP is needed. A few candidates have been tested out including
NOAA national weather services and weather underground.
