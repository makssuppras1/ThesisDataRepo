Summary (English)
This thesis investigates the application of neural operators for solving partial
differential equations (PDEs), with a focus on predicting the hydrodynamic
movement of moored floating offshore structures. One neural network architec-
tures is explored: Deep Operator Networks (DeepONets), with a brief foray into
Generalized Residual Neural Networks (GResNets) as well.
The study begins with an analysis of three test cases of increasing complexity,
anti-derivative, gravity pendulum, and harmonic oscillators. The test cases are
analyzed to evaluate the performance, stability, and optimization strategies for
both architectures. The DeepONet architecture shows great promise in provid-
ing a highly flexible, powerful model that allows for generating predictions on
unseen cases. However for DeepONets, training stability emerges as a critical
factor, requiring techniques such as gradient clipping, learning rate scheduling,
and weight decay to navigate highly erratic loss landscapes. GResNets demon-
strate remarkable efficiency for short-term predictions but struggle with error
accumulation over extended time horizons.
Building on these insights, an extended multiple-input multiple-output Deep-
ONet is implemented to predict the movement of floating offshore platforms
in response to incident waves. The model is trained on data from numerical
simulations (mono-chromatic wave data) and validated against experimental
measurements from a scaled down model and numerical simulation data. Both
of which are multi-chromatic. Results demonstrate that the neural operator can
successfully learn the complex mapping between wave elevations and structural
responses, particularly for previously observed wave patterns.
==PAGE:4==
ii
While the model effectively identifies important characteristics such as peak
wave interactions, it exhibits limitations in generalizing to unseen data, espe-
cially in accurately predicting post-peak behavior and properly scaling move-
ment amplitudes for waves with different energy levels. These challenges high-
light the need for more diverse training datasets and enhanced architectural
variants.
This work advances the understanding of neural operators as computationally
efficient alternatives to traditional numerical solvers for complex hydrodynamic
problems, while identifying key challenges and directions for future research in
this promising field.

Chapter 1
Introduction
There is no shortage of engineering and scientific problems that require fast and
cost-effective solutions. In real-world applications, such as real-time decision-
making, control systems, and optimization tasks, the ability to quickly evaluate
solutions is crucial. However, developing methods that are both accurate and
computationally efficient is a challenge.
Traditionally, numerical methods have been the primary approach for solving
complex problems, particularly those involving partial differential equations
(PDEs). These methods, such as finite element analysis and finite difference
schemes, provide reliable and accurate results. However, their implementation
can be intricate, often requiring deep domain expertise, and their computational
cost can be prohibitive, especially when rapid solutions are required.
Machine learning, particularly neural networks, has emerged as a powerful tool
for approximating solutions to PDEs.
Standard deep learning models have
shown some promise in capturing complex patterns and reducing computational
time compared to classical numerical approaches. However, traditional neural
networks often struggle with either accuracy or generalization. Many models
fail to generalize well across varying problem parameters or require extensive
retraining to handle new scenarios, limiting their practical use in dynamic en-
vironments.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
Neural operators have recently been proposed as a promising alternative for
solving PDEs. Unlike standard neural networks, which learn mappings between
fixed input-output pairs, neural operators learn mappings between function
spaces. A seemingly small detail at first, yet when applied to partial differ-
ential equations this fundamental difference is crucial. It recasts the problem
as a classical analysis problem, of mapping functions e.g forcing functions, ini-
tial conditions, to a solution of the underlying differential equation. Not only
enabling the network to generalizes significantly better across a wide variety
of problem settings.
But moreover connecting the field of machine learning
with the more mature fields of scientific computing, and mathematical analysis.
Which allows leveraging the extensive theory of the latter field.
In this thesis the theory and implementation of both neural operators, and
generalised residual neural networks (GResNets) will be covered. A specific kind
of neural operator known as the DeepONet will be considered. Once familiar
with the theoretical background, the methods will be used to solve a few simple
test problems to evaluate their effectiveness and implementation. After which,
an attempt will be made to tackle the real world problem of predicting the
movement and tension of moored, floating off-shore platforms.
The need for predicting the movement of floating off-shore platforms arises natu-
rally as humans expand deep-sea operations. Irregular incident waves can cause
significant strain on such structures. By being able to make accurate real time
predictions certain countermeasures could be taken to lessen the damage. By
training a DeepONet on simulated incoming wave functions we aim to create a
network capable of generalizing for unseen incoming wave forms, both numerical
and experimental.

and scientific laws. Much time and effort have been dedicated to the solution
and analysis of such problems.

2.2
Neural networks
In recent years the powerful mathematical model known as neural networks have
been gaining ground. It has been made possible due to large improvements in
the computer chip industry, as well as certain new algorithms. Initially inspired
by neurological networks in animals brains, NNs uses a system of propagation
of information through "neurons" to map input to a desired output. The con-
nection of input to desired output is created by an iterative learning phase
which updates the neurons role in propagating information. Neural networks
have shown great results when used in everything from image classification to
language generation. It is only natural that they would be applied to solving
PDE’s. The field related to this approach if commonly referred to as scientific
machine learning.

function will compare the difference between the model prediction and known,
correct output data.
To minimize the loss function, one uses automatic dif-

2.3.2
Automatic differentiation
Automatic differentiation is arguably one of the most important algorithms for
the success of machine learning. It allows for computing and evaluating n order
derivatives of functions defined in a computer program. This is crucial in the
field of machine learning since it allows for calculating the gradient of the loss
function with respect to the weights and biases. In turn allowing for increment
changes of the weights to minimize the loss.
It functions on the premise that all functions defined on a computer are broken
down into elementary functions such as addition, multiplication, trigonometric
functions and exponents etc. All of which have well defined and known deriva-
tives. The automatic differentiation algorithm repeatedly applies the chain rule
on these operations.
Which means that the calculated derivative is in fact
"exact" to working precision of the underlying system. It is not a numerical
approximation such as finite difference methods, nor is it a symbolic represen-
tation. Thus the algorithm is both extremely accurate and yet computationally
feasible.
There are two approaches, or modes, in automatic differentiation. Namely for-
ward mode and backward mode. Forward mode works by applying the chain
rule from the "start" i.e the input to the output. While backward mode works
by applying the chain rule going backwards. It is advised to chose the back-
wards mode when there many more inputs than outputs and vice versa. When
used in machine learning the goal is often to calculate the gradient of the loss
function w.r.t the weights and biases.
In such a case the number of inputs
are obviously greater, thus backward mode is used. To conceptualize, imag-
ine a function y = f(x), where one could break the function down into several
composite functions .

2.3.3
Adam optimizer
The architecture used in this paper will make use of the Adam optimizer. Which
is a popular optimization algorithm for finding the minima of a loss function
and updating the parameters of the model.
It can be seen as an extension to stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The exten-
sion is made by keeping an exponentially decaying moving average of the first
and second moments of the loss gradient. These moments affect the updating
of the parameters. In essence, the first moving average (the mean or first mo-
ment) is responsible for estimating the direction of the gradient and providing
adaptive step sizes. Since the sign of the gradient is preserved in this quantity,
successive steps in similar directions will add "momentum" to the step. The
second moment (the variance or second moment) is responsible for capturing
the scale or spread of the gradients. [KB17]

2.4
Neural Operators
Neural operators are a specific class of neural network architectures that learn
operator mappings between function spaces. It is conceptually different from
traditional neural networks in the sense that it does not concern itself with how
a finite set of data points are mapped to another set. But instead it looks at how
an operator "molds" or transform a function to another. As such it is better
equipped to generate high quality prediction on unseen scenarios compared to
classical neural networks, and does not need to be retrained to adapt to changed
circumstances. Which can be useful in the context of solving partial differential
equations (PDE). Since PDE’s can be considered an operator mapping between
an input function and a solution function, one can use neural operators to learn
said mapping.
The mapping is "learned" in the sense that the neural operators can take an
input function, initial conditions etc evaluated on an arbitrary discretization
and "understand" how said input is transformed to a solution function. It does
not provide any immediate insight into how the mapping behaves, however it is
able to evaluate the solution function at given points.

2.4.2
Multiple input and output functions
Certain cases might require several different kinds of input functions. Such as
initial conditions, forcing functions etc. In a similar vein, a system might have
multiple output. To deal with multiple input and/or outputs one has to extend
the architecture proposed above. Let’s start by considering the case of multiple
inputs.
It is possible to simply stack input functions and keep the original design of the
DeepONet architecture. Consider the two different input functions υ(x), u(x).
Figure 2.3: Stacked DeepONet [TC22]
As tempting as it might be, simply stacking the input functions as a column
vector is ill-advised. From a purely pragmatical point of view the dimension
might not cooperate. You could have for example have a time dependent forc-
ing functions, and a two dimensional initial condition. However, even if the
dimensions should work in unison, simply stacking function does not yield the
best results.
Another approach is the so called EDeepOnet proposed by Lesley Tan and
Liang Chen in [TC22]. Where each input function is fed into seperate branch
networks, and eventually joined together. The propose two kinds, a flat version
and a hierarchical version. The flat one simply considers all branch networks in
the final inner product, while the hierarchical first joins all the branch networks

It is not strictly necessary to divide both trunk and branch network equally.
In fact one can for example split the branch network into k parts, yet keep-

trunk/branch networks for different outputs.
What is important to keep in mind, is that the quality of the solution is depen-
dent on the size of the vector in the final inner product. This follows from the
fact that the inner product is a fusion of basis vectors and coefficients.

Residual networks is another form of neural network architecture, which pi-

To better understand, let us
consider a ResNet with one "residual block". Note that in this example the
ResNet is equivalent to the residual block. In general a ResNet can consist of
several stacked residual blocks.
yout = G(yin, θ) + yin

A residual block consists of G, which is a classic feed forward neural network
with weights and parameters θ, and the identity operator I. The residual block
performs two operations on the input yin ∈Rd, and adds them together to gen-
erate the output yout ∈Rd. The input is multiplied with the identity operator
I, and in parallel it is inserted into the feed forward neural network G(·, θ).
ResNet(G(·, θ)) = I + G(·, θ)
(2.14)
yout = (I + G(·, θ)) ◦yin
(2.15)
The seemingly small addition of allowing the input to also "skip" past the neural
network has beneficial implications.
It stabilizes training by mitigating the
vanishing gradient problem. Secondly, it allows the initial input signal to pass
forward in the network unaltered. Which allows deeper layers to gain access to
input features as they were. This prohibits the network from "forgetting" the
input and encourages feature reuse.
2.5.1
State-to-state mappings
Parts of this paper will be dedicated to analyzing how well certain neural net-
work architectures approximate the solution to time-dependent differential equa-
tions. The approximated solution could be formulated as discrete "snapshots"

Generalized residual neural networks (GResNets)
As seen above, a ResNet block can be trained to learn the residual between
states. GResNets extends the idea as follows. Consider a operator L : Rn →Rn
such that L(xk) ≈xk+1. The GResNet, G can then be defined as follows:
G(·, θ) = L + G(·, θ)
(2.21)
Now the choice of operator L is of obvious importance. The goal is to choose
or find L such that it provides a way of advancing the underlying system in
time. It can be chosen as a numerical integrator such as forward Euler and
Runge-Kutta. In such a case the neural network G essentially functions as a
model correction to L. It could also be chosen as a linearised approximation to
the underlying system if it is known. If the system is completely unknown it is
possible to first create an approximation for L using methods such as dynamic
model decomposition.
[CX21].
This is of course only viable if finding it the

2.6
Hydrodynamic movement of off-shore float-

Part of the focus of this thesis will be the analysis of the movement of off-shore,
moored, floating bodies. This analysis is critical for the design and operation
of offshore wind turbines, oil rigs, and other marine structures. At sea, these
structures are subject to translational and rotational movement as waves in-
teract with the body. To describe their motion, we use a classical Euclidean
coordinate system for the translational component, and roll, pitch, yaw for the
rotation around the x,y,z axes respectively.
2.6.1
Challenges in Predicting Structural Responses
Predicting the response of floating structures to incident waves presents signifi-
cant challenges due to several complex characteristics. The interaction between
waves and structures involves inherent nonlinearities, particularly in harsh sea
conditions, which traditional linear models struggle to capture accurately. Off-
shore structures are also subject to excitations across a broad frequency spec-
trum, making response prediction computationally intensive. Additionally, the
responses contain multiple timescales, for instance, the surge motion of a semi-
submersible platform consists of both wave frequency and low-frequency com-

2.6 Hydrodynamic movement of off-shore floating structures
17
ponents, each requiring different modeling approaches. These complexities are
further compounded by the coupling effects between the six degrees of freedom
through hydrodynamic and structural interactions.
Traditional numerical methods for computing these responses, while accurate,
are computationally expensive and time-consuming, making them unsuitable for
real-time applications such as control systems and health monitoring.
2.6.2

structure moves and the damping that follows from energy leaving the structure
in the form of additional waves.
The structural operator is the equation of motion for the floating structure. It
maps the given hydrodynamic coefficients and wave forces to the actual struc-
tural response of the floating body:

As mentioned above, another potential cause for the erratic gradient norms

learning rate by γ = 0.5 every 1500 iterations.
Such a scheduled reduction would in theory decrease the size of the taken steps
in the latter part of the training. Aiding the network to converge to a local
minima. Maintaining the previously learning rate could harm convergence by

3.4.6
A brief mention of Fourier Neural Operators (FNO)
Since gravity pendulums with external forcing exhibit oscillatory solutions char-
acterized by periodic and spectral-dominant behaviors. Fourier Neural Oper-
ators (FNOs), which operate directly in the frequency domain, are uniquely
suited for capturing such dynamics. By leveraging the Fourier transform and a
learnable kernel, FNOs learn to prioritize dominant frequencies and efficiently
represent global oscillatory patterns. This capability makes them particularly
effective for modeling the rich behaviors of forced pendulum systems, includ-
ing harmonic oscillations, resonance, and chaotic dynamics. The spectral-based
approach of FNOs enables accurate and computationally efficient solutions for
these complex systems, positioning them as a valuable alternative to traditional
solvers and other neural operator frameworks.

3.6.2
Training
Training is performed by randomly selecting initial states from the dataset and
using the model to predict k-step trajectories. The reason for picking random
initial states is to lessen overfitting and reduce computational load. When the
model recursively applies its own predictions as inputs for subsequent steps, it
accumulates the error for each . The total trajectory error is then computed and
used to update the network weights via backpropagation. This approach encour-
ages the model to learn stable long-term predictions by correcting compounding
errors, improving robustness in iterative state propagation.

3.7.1
Anti-derivative Case
The anti-derivative test served as a foundational assessment of the DeepONet
architecture. Initial experiments with default hyperparameters achieved rea-

norm spikes at approximately 1000 iterations. These instabilities were addressed
through two primary modifications:
• Learning rate scheduling: Implementing a step-based learning rate
reduction (γ = 0.5 every 1500 iterations) eliminated high-frequency oscil-
latory components in gradient norms, although spikes remained present.
==PAGE:59==

• Weight decay: Adding L2 regularization (λ = 0.001) further smoothed
the loss landscape, reducing both the frequency and magnitude of gradient
spikes.
The combined approach yielded testing errors of approximately 6×10−5, demon-
strating that careful regularization and learning rate management can signifi-
cantly improve training stability for neural operators.
3.7.2
Gravity Pendulum Case
The gravity pendulum case extended the analysis to coupled variables, time
dependency, and non-linearity. Using an expanded trunk network formulation
to handle interdependency between variables, several key findings emerged:
• Default hyperparameters produced decent models (MSE errors ≈7×10−5)
but may have lacked sufficient expressivity for capturing highly oscillatory
behaviors.
• Reducing the order of external forcing from Chebyshev polynomials of
order 4 to linear forcing confirmed that network struggles were related to
oscillatory complexity rather than operator learning capability.
• Increasing network depth from 4 to 6 hidden layers improved expressivity
for non-linear, oscillatory solutions.
• A mixture of activation functions was used in an effort to improve the
expressivity of the network, but in the end the results showcased only a
trivial improvement under certain circumstances, and that proper use of
stabilization methods was more important.
Comparative analysis across different model configurations showed that the
deeper network (T6) achieved the best performance with training and testing
MSEs of (6.89×10−5 ±6.36×10−5) and (6.60×10−5 ±7.39×10−5) respectively.

(FNOs) for this task would be of interest. Due to FNO’s ability to capture

3.8
Conclusions
This comparative study between DeepONet and GResNet architectures across
three test cases of increasing complexity revealed several important insights:
1. Training stability is crucial for neural operators, with gradient clipping,
learning rate scheduling, and weight decay providing effective remedies for
instabilities.
2. Network depth and expressivity has an impact on the ability to cap-
ture complex, oscillatory behaviors, with deeper networks generally per-
forming better on non-linear problems.
3. Physics-informed approaches like the forward Euler method in GRes-
Net demonstrate advantages for longer-term predictions where error accu-
mulation becomes significant.
4. Model architecture selection should consider the specific characteris-
tics of the problem, with DeepONet excelling at learning direct mappings
between function spaces, while GResNet provides advantages in terms of
lower requirements of data, quick training and high accuracy on small step
predictions.
The research demonstrates that neural operators can effectively learn to solve
various PDEs, but their performance depends critically on appropriate hyper-
parameter selection, training stability management, and architecture design
aligned with the specific characteristics of the target problem domain.
A key finding of this study is that DeepONet architectures exhibit highly er-
ratic loss landscapes that require careful stabilization strategies. The gradient
spikes observed across multiple test cases indicate that this architecture class is
==PAGE:62==
52
Test cases
particularly sensitive to hyperparameter selection. Implementing combinations
of stabilization techniques—including learning rate scheduling, weight decay,
and gradient clipping—proved essential for achieving convergence. This sensi-
tivity suggests that preliminary hyperparameter tuning should be considered
a mandatory step when deploying DeepONets in practical applications, rather
than an optional optimization.
DeepONets demonstrated impressive versatility across all test cases, showing
strong generalization capabilities on unseen samples from various PDE types.
Their ability to learn direct mappings between function spaces makes them
particularly well-suited for problems requiring end-to-end solution prediction.
While effective with properly tuned hyperparameters, the architecture showed
limitations when handling highly oscillatory functions. Future research could
explore spectral approaches or frequency-domain modifications to enhance per-
formance on oscillatory problems—possibly through Fourier Neural Operators
(FNOs) or similar spectral-based architectures that explicitly leverage frequency
decomposition.
An important advantage of DeepONets is their robustness in long-term predic-
tions. Unlike iterative methods that accumulate errors with each step, Deep-
ONets directly map from input functions to solutions across the entire domain,
circumventing error propagation issues. This makes them particularly valuable
for applications requiring accurate predictions over extended temporal or spatial
domains without intermediate state evaluations.
GResNets demonstrated remarkable training efficiency, converging to high ac-
curacy for short-term predictions within just 100 iterations—significantly faster
than DeepONet training. This efficiency makes them excellent candidates for ap-
plications requiring rapid deployment or frequent retraining. While they can be
extended to handle longer-term predictions through recurrent step training, this
approach increases training time and still yields performance that generally lags
behind DeepONets for complete trajectory prediction. The choice between ar-
chitectures ultimately depends on application-specific requirements: GResNets
offer superior efficiency and accuracy for short-term or iterative predictions,
while DeepONets provide better overall performance for complex, long-term
predictions despite their higher computational and tuning requirements.

4.0.9
Conclusion
This section investigated the application of DeepONet-based neural operators
for predicting the movement of moored floating offshore bodies in response to
wave elevations. The extended multiple-input and multiple-output (MI-MO)
DeepONet architecture was implemented and evaluated across various datasets,
including mono-chromatic numerical data, multi-chromatic numerical data, and
experimental measurements. Our results demonstrate that the DeepONet ar-
chitecture possesses the fundamental capability to learn the complex mapping
between wave elevations and the resulting floating body movements. The model
performed particularly well on training data, achieving significant reductions in
relative L2 norm errors, especially for the translational movements in the x and
z directions. For previously encountered wave patterns, the model could repro-
duce the corresponding body movements with impressive accuracy. However,
the model’s generalization capabilities to unseen data revealed some limitations.
While the model correctly identified important characteristics such as crescendo
points in multi-chromatic waves, it struggled with accurately predicting the af-
termath of these events and properly scaling the amplitude of movements when
encountering waves with different energy levels than those in the training set.
These challenges suggest that the current implementation may be overfitting to
the limited training data available, placing excessive importance on certain fea-
tures of the few available training samples. A significant challenge encountered
throughout this work was the highly erratic nature of the loss landscape dur-
ing training. Despite implementing gradient clipping, learning rate scheduling,
and weight decay, the optimization process exhibited instability at times. The
gradient norms frequently reached the imposed limit of 100 after approximately
104 iterations, indicating that either more permissive gradient constraints or
extended training periods might be beneficial.
The computational intensity
of these models made comprehensive hyperparameter tuning prohibitively ex-
pensive, leaving room for potential performance improvements through more
exhaustive optimization. Another substantial practical challenge was the ex-
tensive preprocessing required to adapt the data to the fixed-dimensional input
requirements of the standard DeepONet architecture. The uniform downsam-
pling approach, while effective, was time-consuming and potentially introduced
aliasing issues that could affect the model’s performance. This preprocessing
step represents a significant bottleneck in the practical application of these
models to real-world engineering problems where data dimensions may vary
significantly. For future research, alternative neural operator architectures that
can accommodate variable-sized inputs without extensive preprocessing would
be valuable. In particular, encoder-based DeepONet variants or Wavelet-based
==PAGE:83==
73
DeepONets could offer promising solutions to the dimensional constraints en-
countered in this work. Encoder-based approaches could learn optimal feature
representations directly from the raw data, while Wavelet-based methods could
leverage multi-resolution analysis to capture both local and global wave patterns
more efficiently. Additionally, expanding the training dataset to include a more
diverse range of wave conditions and structural responses would likely improve
the model’s generalization capabilities. This could involve generating additional
numerical simulations across a broader spectrum of wave parameters or collect-
ing more experimental data under varied conditions. In conclusion, while the
extended MI-MO DeepONet shows promise for predicting the movement of float-
ing offshore bodies, its practical application currently faces challenges related
to data preprocessing, computational requirements, and generalization capabil-
ities. Addressing these limitations through architectural innovations and more
comprehensive datasets represents an important direction for future research in
this field.
