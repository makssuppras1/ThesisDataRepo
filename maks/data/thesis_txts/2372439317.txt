Abstract
The recognition of Major Histocompatibility Complexes presenting linear peptides by
T-Cell receptors is a key event in adaptive immunity. These interactions are MHC re-
stricted and some studies suggest that are inherent to TCR sequences. However, which
amino acids or consensus sequences control this event are still unclear.
In this project, machine learning methods such as Random Forest, Feed Forward Net-
works and Convolutional Neural Networks were trained and tested to classify TCR α
and β chains according to their MHC restriction to class I or II. Furthermore, predictions
of these classifiers were decomposed in terms of input sequences using novel methods
like Deep Taylor decomposition to understand which features are important for classi-
fication. The results showed that supervised learning approaches can be used for TCR
classification based on their interaction with MHC class I or II giving relevance mainly
to amino acids in the complementarity-determining regions and some others residues
not directly involved in TCR-pMHC contact.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The immune system, as the defence of an organism against diseases, is a complex network
of cells, molecules and processes that search for pathogens to neutralise them. Composed
by early responses and late responses, these can be defined as innate or adaptive immu-
nity respectively. The innate immune system has limited specificity, it is conserved in
evolution and it focuses on fast early responses. In contrast, the adaptive immune sys-
tem is only present in jawed vertebrates and it provides specific and long-term protection
against antigens. In particular, the adaptive immunity can be classified into humoral or
cell-mediated immunity. For the last category, T lymphocytes have an important role
in recognising antigens presented by cells in the context of Major Histocompatibility
Complexes (MHC). This interaction mediated by the T-cell receptor has been studied
over the years in order to determine important amino acids (Attaf, Holland, Bartok, and
Dyson, 2016 , Rossjohn et al., 2015) or kinetics (Gálvez, Gálvez, and García-Peñarrubia,
2016) of TCR - pMHC complexes. As a result, the concept of MHC restriction was
developed to explain the bias of certain TCRs to interact with MHC class I or II with
unclear conclusions about the mechanism behind it.
In this project, an analysis of TCR sequences using Machine learning approaches was
conducted in order to train and test classifiers of TCR α and β chains based on MHC
restriction. Following the hypothesis of inherent signals in TCR sequences that control
interactions between MHC class I or II (Krovi and Gapin, 2016), supervised learning
models should be able to detect and classify sequences according to such patterns. Pre-
vious studies have been able to classify TCR β CDR3 sequences (Li et al., 2016) without
providing further understanding about CDR1 and CDR2 or TCR α role. To investigate
and learn from the models, new algorithms have been developed to open the black box
of Neural Networks and interpret how they operate. The application of these methods
in the field of sequence analysis and bioinformatics could increase the current knowledge
about protein interactions and recognition of sequence patterns.
To shed light on the previous hypotheses, a dataset of unique TCR α and β se-
quences was created with the aim of training Random Forests, Feed Forward networks
and Convolutional Neural networks under different input encodings. Moreover, the fea-
ture importance of Random Forests and Deep Taylor decomposition of Neural Networks
was calculated revealing important regions involved in MHC restriction.

2.1
Adaptive immunity
Adaptive immune responses provide specific and long-term protection against pathogens.
B-cells and T-cells play active roles in these processes. Whereas B-cells intervene in
humoral responses, producing antibodies that are secreted to neutralize pathogens, T-
cells are involved in cellullar responses by detecting, killing or signaling other cells. In
particular, T-Cells can be classified in two major groups: CD4+ (or helper) and CD8+
(or cytotoxic). As seen in Figure 2.1 both cell types have different roles but both depend
on the TCR - pMHC interaction to recognise other cells.

2.1.1
The Major Histocompatibilty Complex
In order for the immune system to detect foreign peptides presented by cells, antigens
are bound, transported and exposed in the extracellular surface in the context of Major
histocompatibilty complex (MHC). MHCs are glycoproteins that can be of class I or II
and particular differences can be noticed between the two. On one hand, MHC class I,
recognised by CD8+ T cells, it is composed of two polypeptide chains, the polymorphic
α chain and the β-microglobulin subunit. It is expressed in all cells and its main function
is to present intracellular peptides such as tumour and viral antigens (Abbas, Lichtman,
and Pillai, 2012) of length 8-11 residues. On the other hand, MHC class II, recognised
by CD4+ T cells, is only expressed in antigen presenting cells (APC) such as dendrites.
It is structured in two polymorphic polypeptides α and β . Because of the role of APC
cells in processing extracellular microbes and proteins, they present longer epitopes of
10-30 amino acids.
Figure 2.2: Scheme and 3D structure of MHC class I (left) and MHC class II (right).
Extracted from Abbas, Lichtman, and Pillai, 2012.
Both MHC class I and MHC class II have a high diversity. This diversity is explained
by their role in binding the vast number of possible antigens an organism can encounter.
The gene loci of these proteins are one of the most polymorphic in the genome (Abbas
et al., 2012) and because of this, the immune cells must recognise the innate variability
of MHCs to trigger immune responses.

2.1.2
T-cell receptors
To recognise antigens presented by the MHC molecules, T cells have specific receptors
that are genetically and structurally similar to immunoglobulins. T-cell receptors are
located on the surface of T cells and formed by two polypeptide chains. The majority of
these chains are of type α : β accounting for most of the TCR-pMCH interactions but
also γ : δ complexes can be found which functions are not completely defined. TCRs
apart from pMHC recognition are also able to transmit intracellular signals in complex
with other co-receptors such as CD3, CD8 or CD4.
Figure 2.3: T-cell receptor scheme (left) and 3D structure (right). Disulfide bonds
depicted in dashed lines. Adapted from Abbas, Lichtman, and Pillai, 2012.
One of the main features of TCR receptors is their high diversity given by recombi-
nations of the germinal genes and rearrangement of their products. In particular, TCR
α is encoded by the TCRα locus which contains variable (V), joining (J) and constant
(C) regions. The TCRβ locus is similar to the TCR α locus but it also includes diversity
(D) regions. Single modifications and rearrangements of these regions lead to a diverse
T-Cell receptor repertoire. As shown in Figure 2.4, the resulting chains present areas of
high variability called complementary-determining regions (CDR).CDRs located in the
loops V domains of TCR face the pMHC molecule. While CDR1 and CDR2 have been
reported to interact with the MHC, CDR3 recognises the peptide bound to the MHC
molecule.

2.1.3
MHC restriction
Evidence of TCRs binding to the same MHC class during development and after selection
of T-cells suggested that TCRs may have a bias towards particular MHC classes. This
phenomenon was called MHC restriction and since it was discovered researchers have
continued performing studies to explain its origin. Two hypotheses are considered. The
fist hypothesis is that TCRs contain inherent information in their sequence that bias
their interaction with MHC hence boosting their selection.
In contrast, it has been
debated if the process of maturation forces TCRs to interact specifically with a certain
MHC class (Krovi and Gapin, 2016).
Despite the fact that no agreement has been
achieved, the hypothesis of germline-encoded amino acids has gained the support of
functional and structural results (Christopher Garcia, Adams, Feng, and Ely, 2009 ,
Adams et al., 2016). The lack of consensus arises from the challenge of measuring TCR-
pMHC because of the low affinity of the complex before and after selection. In order
to produce immune activation, TCR-pMHC interactions are stabilised by CD4 or CD8
being these molecules (Figure 2.5) the main contributors to affinity. Nevertheless, an
optimal interaction is required to not bind self-antigens and strongly detect foreign ones
indicating that certain amino acids must contribute to the TCR bias and specificity.

2.2
Supervised learning
Machine learning methods can be implemented in different manners depending on the
data and the task we want to perform. Supervised learning in contrast to unsupervised
learning, uses labelled data to train algorithms that perform predictions based on what
they learn from the data. In this project, three machine learning models were trained
and tested for binary classification tasks.
2.2.1
Random Forest
A Random forest is an ensemble method based on decision trees. Decision trees classify
data based on their features to split them according to discrete decisions or thresholds.
Every time a split is made, the purity gain of the split (e.g. Gini index) is calculated in
order to find the best combination of splits that will fit our data into desirable targets.
Random forests take into account the predictions of multiple trees to reduce variance
by averaging the predictions in case of regression or casting the majority vote in case of
classification. The multiple trees in a Random Forest are generated by bootstrapping
==PAGE:20==
8
2 Theoretical background
samples from the data and training individual decision trees from those samples (Hastie,
Tibshirani, and Friedman, 2009).
Figure 2.6: Scheme of a Random Forest. Extracted from Verikas, Vaiciukynas, Gelzi-
nis, Parker, and Olsson, 2016.
In addition, Random Forest provides information about which features are more
important for classification. Every time a split is made, the value for the chosen criteria
to measure purity can be stored and assigned to the feature considered for that split.
In this way, the features that are more relevant for predictions would provide the most
purity gain.

2.2.3
Convolutional Neural Networks
As an evolution of Feedforward Neural Networks, a new architecture appeared with rel-
evant applications in Image recognition and classification called Convolutional Neural
Networks. While it keeps features as non-linear functions for activation of neurons and
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Theoretical background
connectivity between them, it reduces the number of parameters by using filters that
focus on parts of the input. These filters, based on the biological concept of receptive
fields, act like the visual system of animals. The filters, which can overlap each other,
scan the input. The combination of multiple filters provides a reduced representation of
the input based on local information. This last characteristic is important in order to
classify images since their sizes defined by height, width and the 3 RGB channels could
lead to a big number of weights in fully-connected architectures. One of the first imple-
mentations of these networks was published in the late 1990s was the LeNet architecture
(Lecun, Bottou, Bengio, and Haffner, 1998). Over the years CNNs architectures have
been improving and are considered the state-of-art in image recognition.

Pooling layers
The main purpose of these layers is to reduce the dimensions of the feature maps and
avoid overfitting of the models. This is done by calculating the maximum, average or
sum of regions of the features map in a similar way as the filters of convolutional layers.
The result of the combination of convolutional and pooling layers is usually connected
to a dense layer. Finally, the output vector stores the probability or support for the
possible classes considered during training.

2.2.4
Deep Taylor decomposition
Nowadays, due to the big amount of new applications of Neural Networks in the field
of Deep Learning and Artificial Intelligence, understanding how these models work has
become crucial. For many years they were considered a useful black box. Because of
their complexity, they could perform difficult tasks but this also make them more difficult
to be understood. More and more these models are used in processes that require high
security and reliability such as self-driving cars or medical procedures. This fact makes
the challenge of learn how Neural Networks proceed more important than ever.
Diverse approaches have been developed in order to solve this problem. Some focused
on global explanations use autoencoders to let the model reproduce the input or modify
it in order to see how predictions change. Other approaches for complex deep learn-
ing architectures were based on filters deconvolution for the case of CNN or attention
mechanisms (Sønderby, Sønderby, Nielsen, and Winther, 2015).
A recent study showed a novel method called Deep Taylor decomposition (Montavon,
Lapuschkin, Binder, Samek, and Müller, 2017) which explains predicted values of a
neural network in terms of the input pixels (Figure 2.10). Initially developed for the
field of image recognition, this method can be applicable to other fields like, in the case
this project, bioinformatics. The advantages of this approach are that it can be fast
computed for individual inputs using previously trained neural networks and it provides
a unique and consistent solution. It differs from sensitivity explanations in the fact that a
decomposition shows positions that directly explain the prediction instead of differences
that the model focuses on during classification.

Lapuschkin, Binder, Samek, and Müller, 2017.

CHAPTER 3
Materials and Methods
3.1
Dataset
Raw data
The Protein Data Bank (PDB) and ImMunoGeneTics (Lefranc et al., 2015) databases
were considered in this project to build an annotated dataset of TCR Vα and Vβ se-
quences with known MHC class interaction. All PDB sequences were extracted in Fasta
format (391579 sequences). Queried GeneBank entries from IMGT/LIGM-DB of TRA
and TRB genes, Homo sapiens, Mus spp. and Rattus spp. including V-Region entries
were selected (11452 entries). TCR α and β chains were detected in the non-processed
data using Hidden Markov Model (HMM) profiles from LYRA (Klausen, Anderson, Jes-
persen, Nielsen, and Marcatili, 2015). Entries with an E-value < 1017 were removed
corresponding to sequences with more than 30% of gaps in their alignment. Unique en-
tries based on the aligned results from HMM search were finally considered for training
models.
MHC class and germline annotation
To assign the interacting MHC class for each TCR chain two approaches were employed:
keyword search of MHC class related terms and MHC sequences recognition by HMM
profiles. For the first case, text mining from GenBank entries and original article ab-
stracts of MHC class I/II, CD8/CD4 and alleles keywords was performed. Abstracts
were accessed by available PubMed IDs in the initial database records using Biopython
(Cock et al., 2009). For the second approach, HMM profiles for MHC I (PF00129), MHC
II alpha/beta (PF00993/PF00969) were downloaded from the protein families database
(PFAM) (Finn et al., 2016) and used to find linked sequences for MHC. Ambiguous
results were manually resolved. A final set of 376 sequences of non-duplicated and an-
notated TCR α and β chains was considered for analysis. IgBlast (Ye, Ma, Madden,
and Ostell, 2013) was used to detect V genes from the dataset for human and mice
sequences by assigning the top match form IgBlast default parameters. CDR3 length
was calculated from the defined CDR3 positions in the HMM profiles and divided by
the maximum CDR3 length.

3.2
Data encoding
Machine learning classifiers require numerical input vectors in which amino acid se-
quences are represented considering a set of features that represent their molecular char-
acteristics or common vector space. Different approaches of input encoding were tested
for models trained in this project.
Sparse encoding
1-of-K encoding was used for the 20 amino acids and gaps.
In this manner, binary
vectors of length 21 represented each position in the peptide or alignment sequence.A
value of 1 is given for the corresponding amino acid or gap coordinate and a value of 0
for the rest of the vector.
BLOSUM encoding
Each position was represented as their BLOcks SUbstitution Matrix (BLOSUM) values
normalised in a range between 0 and 1. Normalisation was conducted by scaling the
matrices by a factor of 0.1.
In particular, the BLOSUM62 matrix was selected for
encoding the data. Moreover, another version of the BLOSUM62 matrix was tested in
which substitution scores were processed by multidimensional scaling to represent each
amino acid (Méndez, Falcón, Hernández, and Lorenzo, 2007) by a single value. This
dimensionality reduction also keeps a relative distance between similar amino acids in a
one-dimensional space.
Molecular features encoding
Measures of polarity, hydrophobicity and volume for each amino acid were used as a
representation of the molecular properties of the sequence residues (Bigelow, 1967, Sweet
and Eisenberg, 1983, Radzicka and Wolfenden, 1988).
3.3
Cross Validation and performance
assessment
5-fold cross validation was used for Random Forests and 5-fold nested cross-validation
(CV) for Artificial Neural Networks (ANN). Clusters with sequences greater or equal
than 90% identity were randomly split into 5 partitions of similar size. In this way, for a
5-fold CV, one partition was used for testing and the other four for training the model,
giving a total of 5 different models trained. In the 5-fold nested CV, as in the previous
method, one partition is also kept for testing but from the other 4 remaining folds, one
is used to choose the best parameters (e.g. best epoch on early-stop training) and the
==PAGE:28==
16

last 3 partitions for training. In an iterative manner, the inner loop generates 4 models
for each test set which sums to a total of 20 models. The mean of the four predictions
for each test was computed to obtain a single predicted value. This predicted value was
later used to measure the ensemble of models performance.
Performance of the implemented Machine Learning methods was measured in terms
of Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC), Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) (3.3)
and classification accuracy per class (3.4).

3.4
Supervised Machine Learning
Various algorithms for binary classification were trained. Random forests were imple-
mented in Python using Sklearn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) RandomForestClassifier and
Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) using the Lasagne library (Dieleman et al., 2015).
Seeds for pseudo-random number generators were set taking the test fold number as
value. The use of seeds allowed reproducibility and comparison of classifiers. The best
models in terms of AUC score on the test set were reported and their particular param-
eters are stated in Appendix A
Input vectors
As important as a proper data encoding, how the data is managed by an algorithm is
also critical. Due to the differences between TCR α and β chains, two approaches were
proposed and tested (Figure 3.2). In the first case, sequences or alignments were used
in the same positions of the input vectors disregarding their type of TCR chain (a). In
the second case, the input vectors were twice as long as the maximum length for a single
input (c). In this framework, TCR α sequences always occupied the first half of the
input and TCR β the second half. In a simplified version of this second case, the type
of TCR chain was encoded as a value of 1 or 2 and encoded as an extra feature in the
single chain input vectors (b). When pairs of TCR α and β chains extracted from PDB
were available in the same training or test set both were provided in the same input
vector.

For the majority of models, training sets were balanced according to their target
classes. For Random Forests, balancing was performed by downsampling the majority
class members to the number of sequences from the minority class within the training
folds selected. In the case of ANN, balancing was applied by removing randomly selected
sequences from the majority class per epoch.
This method of downsizing allows to
eventually use all the data from the majority class after iterations of training.
Random Forests
Random Forests for TCR chain classification were trained taking into account optimal
parameters for such task. Default parameters as stated in the Sklearn library were set.
Gini entropy was used as the criterion for split quality considering a maximum number
of features equals to the square root of the total features. A minimum of 2 samples
were required for splitting a node and one at least in each leaf node. The number of
estimators tested were 5, 10, 25, 50, 75, 100, 250, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000 for data encoded
in blosum, sparse and molecular encodings.
Feed Forward Networks
Feed forward networks were structured in 3 layers: Input, dense middle and dense
output layer. Weights were initialized following a Normal distribution with a standard
deviation of 0.01, mean 0 and updated using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with
a learning rate of 0.01. The activation function for the middle dense layer was set to a
sigmoid function and a different number of hidden neurons were analysed as described
in Table 3.1. The output layer was composed of 2 neurons, each representing one of the
two possible class support values. Activation of the output layer was ruled by a Softmax
function and the predicted class was assigned by selecting the maximum value of the
output vector. Sparse and blosum encoded alignments were selected as inputs.

The architecture of CNNs for this project was defined as one input layer followed by five
1D convolutional layers of filter size 1, 3, 5, 9, 15 with their correspondent pooling layers.

3.5
Hyperparameter optimization
In all the previous models the best hyper-parameters were found by grid-search of prede-
fined values. In an attempt to improve performance of FFN models trained on TCR α
or β sequences only and explain their predictions the Python library Hyperopt was used
(J. Bergstra, Yamins, and Cox, 2013). This library has an easy to use implementation of
Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) algorithm which models the p(x|y) being x our
hyper-parameter values and y the performance (J. S. Bergstra, Bardenet, Bengio, and
Kégl, 2011). To do so, an initial set of random search runs of hyper-parameters under
user-defined values or distributions (Table 3.3) from which the sampling is conducted.
The results from these initial random iterations are then used to generate non-parametric
densities from the initial distributions and constrain the parameter search space. A total
of 50 iterations of hyper-parameters were analysed for each model.

3.6
Deep Taylor decomposition
Weights and biases of pre-trained models were used to perform a forward pass for each
input taking care of selecting the models in which a particular input was considered for
testing. The output vector with the predicted support for each class was then multiplied
by the correct target. In this manner, only relevance for the correct class was decomposed
in terms of the input using the Deep Taylor relevance decomposition described previously
in Section 2.2.4. Implementation of this method was done in Python following an online
tutorial made by the authors. Vectors of decomposed relevances for the same input were
then summed and normalised by the highest value in the sum.

matrices and PSSM logos generated using the web server. For difference logos of aligned
TCR chain sequences, Two Sample Logos were computed (Vacic, Iakoucheva, and Radi-
vojac, 2006).

Protein structures
Protein structures of TCR chains interacting with MHC class I or II were downloaded
from PDB. Decomposed relevances were summed and normalised by position, then added
to PDB files as B-factor values using Biopython (Cock et al., 2009). 3D visualisation
and rendering of structures were performed using PyMOL Schrödinger, LLC, 2015.

4.2.1
Classifiers based on single TCR chain inputs
To begin with, classifiers were trained on single TCR chain sequences or alignments
of both types. In this manner, the TCR chain type was not encoded and no further
information more than the particular amino acid differences between TCR α and β
chains was provided in the input vectors. The same rules for classification would be
applied for each sequence no matter their TCR chain type. Classifiers should learn the
differences between both. The performances of these models were very similar in terms
of AUC value on the test set (Figure 4.4), around 0.65 AUC. Balancing the training sets
helped to increase the accuracy of predictions for the minority class, mainly for Random
Forests, but with the cost of losing accuracy on the MHC class I predictions due to
downsampling. On the other hand, the accuracy of the majority class predictions in
Artificial Neural Networks was not reduced since balancing was performed by randomly
downsampling per epoch. However, the gain in accuracy for the minority class was not as
high for Neural Networks as it was for Random Forest. In any case, overall performance
and accuracy per class were better than random prediction and MCC seemed to be a
more sensible value in order to discriminate between real or random performance.

each class (Figure 4.16) which shared similarities with logos based of enrichment per
position. It is worth to mention that Artificial Neural Networks are able to detect cor-
relations in the data at different positions of the input whereas statistical comparison of
aligned sequences only reflects differences at individual positions. This may suggest that
logos generated from Deep Taylor decomposition could unveil more complex patterns
that rely on correlated amino acids at different positions in the peptides. This fact could
explain the differences in the logos in Figure 4.16 and Figure 4.14.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this project, classifiers of T-Cell Receptor α and β chains for MHC restriction were im-
plemented and their predictions were decomposed in terms of input sequences. To do so,
a non-redundant dataset of annotated TCR chain sequences was generated from public
databases and used to train Random Forests, Feed Forward Networks and Convolutional
Neural Networks.
Supervised learning methods could classify TCR chains based on their interaction
with MHC class I or II. RF and FFN trained on aligned sequences performed similarly to
CNN trained on full sequences showing that these late models can account for positional
information in non-aligned inputs. Moreover, models trained on TCR α or β only showed
a slight increase in performance compared to models of mixed TCR α and β suggesting
that specific rules may apply to the different TCR types.
In order to understand important residues or patterns for MHC-restriction, feature
importance of RF and Deep Taylor decomposition of FFN were computed. Both agreed
that Complementarity-determining regions of TCRs were important for classification.
In particular, Deep Taylor decomposition of the predicted values could specify which
amino acids were important for predicting MHC class I or II.
The accuracy of both classification and prediction relevance in terms of input rely
on precise models. To improve the performance of the classifiers, sequencing data could
be considered in the future to create a bigger dataset. Also, new approaches to process
Deep Taylor decompositions are needed in order to extract the most information of what
classifiers have learned such as consensus sequences. Knowledge about the models could
be also used to select important features and improve performance.
Finally, as shown in this thesis, methods to understand Machine Learning predictions,
like Deep Taylor decomposition, could help scientists to not only use Machine Learning
algorithms as simple tools but also learn from them inherent patterns in big data sets
that individual examples can not show.
