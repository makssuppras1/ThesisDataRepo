ABSTRACT 
In the last decades, there has been increased global concern about the state of current industrial 
systems and process and their effects on the environment, particularly with current practices used 
and rates of the extraction and consumption of raw materials and disposal of waste of products. As 
response, a number of concepts were introduced within the industrial sphere to help industries 
move on to on sustainable paths of production. One of such concepts is Industrial symbiosis, which 
is aimed at creating opportunities for industries to form synergistic networks, where resources like 
water, energy and residuals are shared. The integral thought is that one company’s waste can be 
used by another as a resource. Industrial symbiosis is analogous to symbiosis in natural ecosystems. 
The aim of this study is to search for opportunities to enhance or increase the environmental 
performance of industries in an industrial area in Ghana, specifically the Tema Industrial Area, using 
industrial symbiosis. As part of the study, a literature review was performed and a field trip was 
made to Ghana to gain better insight into the concept of industrial symbiosis, how it could be 
applied in the industrial area and gather data as well. MFA was performed to assess the flow of 
material goods in the industrial systems of three companies, namely; VALCO, Ferro Fabrik Limited 
and Pioneer Food Cannery; to help in the identification of potential opportunities for industrial 
symbiosis. A broader assessment was performed to match the various industrial sectors based on 
their typical waste products. The opportunities identified for industrial symbiosis were the use of 
Spent Potlining produced by VALCO in cement and steel-making industries, the use of slag for 
construction activities and in the production of cement. Pioneer food Cannery had the most use of 
its fish waste, turning it into fish meal and other valuable products such as omega-3 oils and fish oil. 
The benefits of these opportunities were estimated some of which are; a decrease in waste 
generation amounts, decrease in the amount of landfilled waste by about 4,320mt/yr, resource 
conservation and efficiency, CO2 and greenhouse gas savings 5,000 tCO2eq. New business 
opportunities proposed for the industrial area include a waste-to-energy plant, an alumina refinery 
and mushroom cultivation. The main conclusion drawn was the success of the method in the 
identification of opportunities which could help improve the environmental performance of the 
Tema Industrial area. A recommendation is also made on how to get more industries involved in 
industrial symbiosis. This document can be used as a source of literature for further research on 
industrial symbiosis in developing countries.

1. Introduction 
This section gives an introduction to the research topic by giving a background of the problem and 
the state of the industrial sector in Ghana. Justification, objectives and an introduction to the 
methodology are also included in this chapter. 
1.1 Background 
Industrialisation and technology has been the major driving force for the development of most parts 
of the world. Though beneficial, this drive for industrialisation has had severe impact on the 
environment, with some being very disastrous. Inputs needed for production are sourced directly from 
the environment and the subsequent by/waste products from production and used products are 
disposed of into the environment. There is global concern whether our environment and the Earth for 
that matter, will be able to handle these rising levels of extraction, consumption and disposal. There 
is the issue of the availability of resources, which has been termed as ‘resource scarcity’, which is a 
major challenge for industries especially acquiring the desired quantity of raw materials or resources 
to meet production goals (Graedel & Et.Al., 2011). The sourcing of these materials requires some 
sort of extraction or mining activity, or, manipulation of the resource (e.g. land) to produce desired 
yields or products in the case of agriculture. On the part of society, the concerns have been whether 
these resources available now, will be enough to meet the needs of future generations. Aside the 
sourcing of raw materials, the management of the waste which arises out of these industrial processes 
is also a global dilemma, notably the emission of greenhouse gases, solid waste and other liquid 
emissions into the environment. Currently, landfilling remains the common and cheapest option for 
industries for the disposal of industrial waste.  There are a number of environmental issues associated 
with landfilling, such as the leaching of toxic chemicals into groundwater sources, emission of 
greenhouse gases and the availability and siting of land especially in the capital where large sections 
of land which is usually required for landfills is quite scarce and expensive as well.  
Industrial Symbiosis (IS) is a concept of Industrial Ecology (IE) which promotes the sharing of 
material, water, energy and residual resources among traditionally separate companies in close 
proximity usually for economic, environmental and social benefits (Chertow, 2007). It is analogous 
to the biologic symbiotic relationships that exist among organisms in the natural ecosystem, where 
exchanges between organism are usually for mutual benefits (Chertow, 2000). Integral to this concept 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
 
is the thought that waste from one company can be used as a resource for another. An Eco-Industrial 
Park (EIP), is basically a collection of industries with an area or estate where these companies work 
together to achieve an economically, socially and environmentally sustainable industrial ecosystem 
(Lowe, 2001). Like IS, the motivation is usually for the establishment of EIPs are the mutual benefits 
derived, but the difference between the two concepts is that, IS could be implemented in areas or 
settings that are not necessarily parks. The benefits derived by companies who form these synergies 
include; waste reduction, resource efficiency, a decrease in cost of production and the conservation 
of natural resources (Chertow, 2007). 
1.2 Industrial sector in Ghana: Tema Industrial Area 
Ghana is located in West Africa. It covers an area of 238,533 sq.km and is bordered on the north with 
Burkina Faso, Togo on the east, the Ivory Coast on the west and the Gulf of Guinea (an embayment 
of the Atlantic Ocean) on the south. The population of Ghana is estimated to be about 26 million and 
GDP per capita income is $3,638.47 (UNDP, 2017). The nation of Ghana is endowed with numerous 
resources including rich forests, minerals and a wide range of agricultural crops. Export commodities 
include; cocoa, gold, bauxite, manganese and other agricultural products. These export commodities 
are a major source of foreign exchange for the country. Ghana is the world’s second largest producer 
of cocoa, producing an average of 800,000 metric tonnes per year (Statista, 2016), as well the second 
highest miner of gold in Africa. The industrial sector is the second largest sector contributing to the 
gross domestic product (GDP) of Ghana with a share of 26.9% (Ghana Statistical Service, 2015) and 
comprises of agribusinesses, manufacturing, construction, mining and quarrying, electricity water 
and sewage (Ackah, Adjasi, & Turkson, 2014) (Sutton & Kpentey, 2012). The industrial sector 
transforms most of these natural resources into finished and semi-finished products for both the local 
and international markets. 
The city of Tema is home to the largest industrial area in Ghana. The Tema Industrial Area described 
of as the hub of industry in Ghana, with an area an approximated area of 40km2 and over 200 
companies operating in the area. Some industrial sectors located in the industrial area include; food 
processing, aluminium manufacturing, steel manufacturing and metal fabrication, an oil refinery, 
cement production, agro-processing and plastic manufacturing industries. The largest port in Ghana, 
the Tema Harbour, also serves the various industries and the country as well, by providing import 
and export services not just for Ghana but other landlocked countries within the sub-region as well, 
hence the name ‘Harbour City’.

1.3 Justification 
The graph shown in figure 1 was made by the (Global Footprint Network, 2017) and gives an overall 
picture of consumption in Ghana over the last 50 years. Biocapacity is explained as ‘the ecosystem’s 
capacity to produce biological materials used by people and to absorb waste material generated by 
humans, under current management schemes and extraction technologies’ (Global Footprint 
Network, 2017) .Ecological footprint, is basically the amount of resource consumed in an ecosystem 
over a period of time. The difference between biocapacity and ecological footprint is termed as an 
ecological reserve if it is positive or ecological deficit for a negative difference (Global Footprint 
Network, 2017). It is observed that between the periods of 1961 to about 1996, there was a large 
ecological reserve but this has reduced significantly over the past decades and this mainly due to the 
sharp rise in the ecological footprint of the country. In other words, consumption of ecological 
resources in Ghana has become quite unsustainable and if not curbed will result in the shortage or 
complete depletion of some much needed resources. The industrial sector has the highest demand for 
natural or ecological resources (environment) and as such is a major contributor to the current 
ecological deficit. Some companies are currently being affected by this deficit as their sources of raw 
materials and amount extracted have declined, thus affecting productivity and leading to losses as 
well. 
Again, despite its status as the industrial hub, most of the companies in the Tema Industrial Area are 
faced with the similar environmental challenges as stated in the previous section. Most of the solid 
and hazardous waste is landfilled and liquid emissions are either disposed of into receiving 
waterbodies after minimum or no treatment. Again, other issues like irregular power supply and the 
high price of raw materials needed for production has affected the productivity of these industries. 
As a result, some companies are operating below capacity or in extreme cases closed out of business. 
It is therefore necessary that the industrial sector adopts and implements methods and practices in 
their production processes which are sustainable such that, they have less impacts on the environment 
while maintaining or maximizing profits and stimulating development in the various societies in the 
long run.

2. Industrial Symbiosis and Eco-Industrial Parks 
2.1 Industrial symbiosis: Definition 
Industrial symbiosis is defined as involving “traditionally separate industries in a collective approach 
to competitive advantage involving physical exchange of materials, energy, water, and by-products. 
The keys to industrial symbiosis are collaboration and the synergistic possibilities offered by 
geographic proximity” (Chertow, 2000). The concepts of industrial symbiosis and industrial ecology 
are both based on the premise of a developing self-sustaining industrial ecosystem which has 
synergetic networks and cycles with the exchange of materials, water and energy; just as occurs in 
natural ecosystems (Chertow, 2007). In this regard, the traditional linear system of production which 
had little or no interaction among companies is transformed into a cyclical system of production with 
active interactions and synergies among companies (Scheel, 2016). The main motivation for pursuing 
IS is for business reasons. In addition, IS promotes sustainable production in the industrial sector. 
Sustainable in the sense that, the industry maximises or maintains profits, has less impacts on the 
environment and remains socially acceptable.  
The concept of IS was first discovered in Kalundborg, Denmark, when some companies came 
together to share water resources, including wastewater. Gradually, more industries moved to the area 
and in the 1980’s it was discovered that these industries had formed networks which allowed them to 
share material resources, water and heat just like a natural ecosystem. The term ‘symbiosis’ was used 
to describe these exchanges (Chertow, 2000, 2007; Jacobsen, 2006). 
In (Chertow, 2007), a “3-2 heuristic” is considered as the minimum basis identification of IS, meaning 
that, at least 3 entities or companies must share a minimum of 2 resources in order to be considered 
as a type of IS. Simply, the IS network should consist of a primary producer who produces the residual 
material or good, a primary consumer who consumes the residual material to manufacture a product, 
and a secondary consumer who consumes this final product. An example given by (Chertow, 2007) 
is a wastewater treatment plant providing cooling water for a power station and the power station, in 
turn, supplying steam to an industrial user. The words, ‘Collaboration’ and ‘Geographical proximity’, 
are considered as key in the definition of IS (Lombardi & Laybourn, 2012). For IS to take place, the 
various companies must be willing to collaborate to exchange their by-products or waste. Any 
unwillingness or disagreement on the part of a company is likely to halt the synthesis of IS. Again, 
the participating companies need to be in close proximity, preferably in the same area as exists in 
EIPs. The closer the companies are to each other, the more likely there are to form a symbiotic 
relationship. Companies in close proximity within an area also allows for direct exchanges in the 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
 
network (Lombardi & Laybourn, 2012). The words ‘kernel’ and ‘precursor’ are used to describe 
relationships that have the potential for expansion but do not meet the criteria for a 3-2 relationship 
(M. R. Chertow, 2007). In the Tema Industrial Area, the various industries are located in close 
proximity but it is yet not known if kernels for symbiosis exist or whether they have been identified 
if they do exist. The assessment of the industrial area is set to reveal this. 
(M. Chertow & Ehrenfeld, 2012) describes the growth of IS networks as starting from a sprouting 
stage where exchanges occur without being noticed, then to the uncovering stage where conscious 
efforts are made to identify these network exchanges and finally to the embedded stage where these 
identified networks are fully matured with active exchanges and new entrants. Most IS networks are 
usually uncovered as in the case of Kalundborg. For those identified, they are nurtured to the 
embedded stage, but most of the IS networks remain hidden in the sprouting stage (M. Chertow & 
Ehrenfeld, 2012). 
2.2 Eco-Industrial Parks 
The idea and concept of establishing Eco-Industrial Parks (EIPs) is not a new one. Clusters of 
industries are created with the idea of sharing and utilising waste materials, energy and water to for 
economic, social and environmental benefits (Kastner, Lau, & Kraft, 2015). They are usually 
established well-defined areas or parks with a  management body or central authority (Lowe, 2001). 
EIPs employ ‘green’ concepts like industrial symbiosis, industrial ecology, cleaner production and 
circular economy (Lowe, 2001). According to (Chertow, 2007), the establishment of EIPs with the 
concept of  IS is to (1) revitalize urban and rural sites, including brown-field redevelopment, (2) 
promote job growth and retention, and (3) encourage more sustainable development.  
2.3 Existing Industrial Symbiosis Networks 
There are a number of renowned EIPs around the world which were established with the concept of 
IS. Some of these IS networks have been active for decades including the very popular Kalundborg 
Synbiosis. In this section a number of EIPs around the world with a high levels of embeddedness are 
reviewed. The various resource exchanges within the IS network are also shown in diagrams.  
2.3.1 Kalundborg – Denmark 
Kalundborg Symbiosis is the world’s first identified and working Industrial Symbiosis network. The 
symbiosis network was uncovered in 1989 among a cluster of companies and apparently had been 
going on for a long time (M. R. Chertow, 2007). It started in 1961 when an oil refinery, Statoil needed 
water for its processes. The facility was sited near Lake Tissø in Kalundborg, as such conduits pipes 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
 
were laid to transport water to the refinery. In 1972, Gyproc located its facility close to Statoil, which 
supplied gas for their operations. In the following year, Anaes Power Station also set up in the area 
to use the water supply pipes from Statoil (Kalundborg Symbiosis Center, 2017).  What started as a 
collaboration between three industries evolved to include more industries; an oil refinery (Statoil 
Refinery), a power plant (Asnaes Power Station), gypsum board facility (Gyproc), pharmaceutical 
plant (Novo Nordisk), and the Municipality of Kalundborg. These industries share ground water, 
surface water and waste water, steam and electricity, and also exchange a variety of residues that 
become feedstocks in other processes  (M. R. Chertow, 2000; Jacobsen, 2006). Since then, the 
Kalundborg has become the model of IS all over the world. 
In the IS system, wastewater and cooling water from the Statoil Refinery are reused by the Asnaes 
Power Station: the wastewater for secondary purposes, the cooling water as feeder water for the 
boilers producing steam and electricity, and also as input water for the desulfurization process. 
Industrial gypsum produces used as a result of desulfurization process in turn used for the production 
of plasterboard at the Gyproc factory, thereby partly replacing the use of natural gypsum. The Asnaes 
Power Station, a cogenerating power plant, also produces heat for the town of Kalundborg and steam 
for the Novo Nordisk facility and the Statoil refinery. Statoil also generates some steam from its 
operations and heated cooling water is channelled to a nearby fish farm, thus aiding in fish production. 
Finally, solid by-products such as fly ash from coal combustion, sludge from public wastewater 
treatment, and biomass from biogenetic fermentation at the Novo facilities are recycled in various 
ways, such as fertilizer production and biogas, both locally and non-locally (Jacobsen, 2006). Some 
reported results from the symbiosis network include water savings or about 3 million m3, biogas 
production from the yeast slurry generated from insulin production by Novo Nordisk and recycling 
of about 150,000 tons of gypsum from desulphurization of flue gas (SO2) from Dong Energy, which 
replaces import of natural gypsum (CaSO4). The Kalundborg Symbiosis Centre oversees IS network 
fosters IS among industries.

2.3.2 Kwinana and Gladstone- Australia 
Kwinana and Gladstone are both heavy industrial areas in Australia with recognised IS with a diverse 
synergistic network of industries and a high level of embeddedness (Beers, Bossilkov, Corder, & 
Berkel, 2008). Both industrial areas are home to a wide range of mineral industries and are known 
for the intensive mineral extraction and processing activities. 
2.3.2.1 Kwinana Industrial Area 
Kwinana Industrial Area is located 40km south of Perth, along the coast of Cockburn Sound in 
Western Australia. This region is endowed with natural mineral resources including, iron ore, bauxite, 
gold, nickel, mineral sands, diamonds, natural gas, oil, and coal. The industrial area was established 
in 1950 and covers an area of 120km2, making it the largest and most complex industrial area in 
Australia (Beers et al., 2008). The annual turnover of the industrial area is over 4 billion Australian 
Dollars, this makes the area very vital to the economy and local community of Australia. The 
industries located here include an alumina refinery (Alcoa), a nickel refinery (Kwinana Nickel 
Refinery), titanium dioxide pigment plant (Tiwest), lime and cement kilns (Cockburn Cement), a 
135,000-barrel/day oil refinery (BP), and a pig iron plant (HIsmelt). These are complemented by a 
variety of chemical producers, which produce agricultural and water treatment chemicals. Also, there 
are important utility operations, including two power stations both owned by Verve Energy, two 
cogeneration plants, two air separation plants (Air Liquide and BOC Gases), a grain handling and 
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
 
export terminal (CBH), port facilities (Fremantle Port Authority), and water and wastewater treatment 
plants (Water Corporation) (Beers et al., 2008; MacLachlan, 2013). 
The realisation of the IS networks began when the Kwinana Industrial Council was set up to monitor 
air and water quality within the industrial area in response to government regulation. A regional 
economic impact studies was instigated at the request of the industries and this involved analysis of 
material and energy flows as well as the level of integration. The findings of the study revealed that 
between 1990 and 2000, the number of core process industries in Kwinana had increased from 13 to 
21, and the number of existing interactions increased from 27 to 106. Each interaction represents 
either transfer of product(s), transfer of by-products, or commercial cooperation. The Kwinana 
Industrial Synergies Project was initiated to oversee and enhance these IS networks along with other 
bodies (Beers et al., 2008).  
A more recent study by (Beers et al., 2008) identified forty-seven ongoing synergy projects, including 
32 by- product synergies and 15 utility synergies. Figure 5 shows the by-product exchanges in the IS 
network. One synergy in the network is the exchange of gypsum between the CSBP chemical works 
and the Alcoa alumina refinery. CSBP has a stockpile of some 1.3 million tonnes of gypsum since 
switching to new production method. Among other uses the company found that the material could 
be utilized by the Alcoa alumina refinery to assist in plant growth and soil stability in its residue areas. 
Alcoa approximately 10,000 tonnes each year for this purpose. Both companies benefit from this 
partnership which has lower gypsum cost for Alcoa and a reduction of liabilities associated with the 
gypsum stockpile for CSBP (Beers et al., 2008).

2.3.2.2 Gladstone Industrial Area 
The Gladstone Industrial Area is located 550 km north of Brisbane, the capital of Queensland 
(Australia’s second largest state). Production in this industrial area accounts for about 10% of 
Australia’s exports. It was established in 1993 and covers an area of 21,000 hectares (Beers et al., 
2008). The industrial network is known as the Gladstone Area Industry Network (GAIN) and 
comprises of industries including, two an alumina refinery (Queensland Alumina and Comalco 
Alumina Refinery), a coal- fired power station (NRG), a cement clinker plant (Cement Australia), an 
aluminum smelter (Boyne Smelters), an ammonium nitrate, sodium cyanide and chlorine plant 
(Orica), the largest Queensland multi-cargo port, the Awoonga Dam (Gladstone Area Water Board), 
and a shale oil mine and processing plant (Queensland Energy Resources). The major value-chain 
integration is the supply of alumina from Queensland Alumina to Boyne Aluminium Smelters (Beers 
et al., 2008; Golev, Corder, & Giurco, 2014). Figure 6 shows the existing IS networks which include; 
 Alternative fuels for cement production: Waste and by-products with high calorific value 
are used as alternative fuels in the kilns during cement production. The fuel sources are 
domestic tires, solvent-based fuels prepared from hazardous and combustible wastes and spent 
cell linings from the Boyne Aluminium Smelter.

 Water reuse: Queensland Alumina uses secondary treated water from a wastewater treatment 
plant for its mud-washing process 
 Waste separation and reuse: Useful parts of the waste are recovered and recycled by the

2.3.3 Other Industrial Symbiosis Networks around the world 
There are other IS network around the world with high levels of complexity and embeddedness. An 
example is Guayama in Puerto Rico which has a well-established IS network with exchanges of 
==PAGE:22==

wastewater, energy and materials. The typical industries in this industrial area are a coal-fired power 
plant, chemical refining and pharmaceutical industries. The resources exchanged include wastewater, 
condensate, steam and ash (M. Chertow & Ehrenfeld, 2012; M. R. Chertow, Ashton, & Espinosa, 
2008). Another EIP located in Styria, Austria has a large number of industries including; Sawmills, 
mining, textiles, chemicals, power plant, board industry, plastics production, ceramic industry, 
cement plant, material dealers, iron manufacturing, agriculture associations. Approximately 1 million 
tonnes of by-products is produced within the network. About 780,000 tonnes per year which 
represents 77.5% were recycled through direct intercompany waste exchanges, whereas about 
200,000 tonnes (20%)were landfilled or incinerated, and only about 25,000 tonnes (2.5%) were 
handed over to professional waste management companies for recycling (Posch, 2010). Materials 
exchanged in the IS network include; ash, plastics, sludge, iron scrap, wood and paper, heat, petrol 
coke, slag, dust and waste oil (M. Chertow & Ehrenfeld, 2012; Posch, 2010). Another EIP the 
Rotterdam Harbour in Netherlands with industries like cement, chemicals, oil refinery and 
incinerator. The IS network here is mainly focused on exchanges of heat and energy (M. Chertow & 
Ehrenfeld, 2012).  
Most of these EIPs discussed happen to be located in developed countries. There is little literature on 
IS in developing countries. (Bain, Shenoy, Ashton, & Chertow, 2010) conducted a study in India with 
the aim of thoroughly quantifying material flows to identify existing symbiotic connections in an 
industrial area. This study was the first of its kind in India. The results showed that the industrial area 
generates 897,210 metric tons of waste residuals annually, and 99.5% of this waste was recovered, 
81% with reused by the companies that generated them, with one company, a sugar refinery, 
processing most of this amount. The literature search did not turn up any existing literature on IS 
projects within the sub-Saharan Africa region. 
2.4 Environmental Benefits of Industrial Symbiosis based on EIPs 
Some environmental benefits derived from the IS networks are presented in this section. This includes 
benefits made in terms of water and CO2 savings, as well as the re-use of material resources. 
Kalundborg, Denmark: Three water exchange networks helped in the substitution of about 2.8 
gigaliters per year of groundwater with surface water and also helped in the conservation of 0.5 
gigaliters per year of surface water. Also, the supply of waste heat and cooling water for use by the 
industries saved 1.3 million gigajoules per year, reduced seawater intake by 23 gigaliters per year and 
reduced emissions of CO2 and NOx, but increased emissions of SO2 (van Berkel, 2010). 
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
 
Kwinana, Australia: The partnership between CSBP and Alcoa can be considered as 
environmentally beneficial since the large stockpile of waste gypsum is utilized in environmental 
remediation. The introduction of the world’s first commercial 800,000 tonne/yr direct reduction iron-
making plant (HIsmelt), a new technology, in the network also led to a 20% reduction of carbon 
dioxide (CO2), 40% reduction of nitrogen oxides (NOx), and 90% decrease of sulphur oxides (SOx) 
compared to blast furnace steel production (Beers et al., 2008). 
Gladstone, Australia: The installation of a pipe by Queensland Alumina, which delivers secondary 
treated water from the Calliope River Sewage Treatment plant some 8.5km helped the company to 
make water savings of about 6.5 mega litres/day. This also ended the release of treated wastewater 
into surrounding waterbodies by the Calliope River Sewage Treatment plant (Beers et al., 2008). 
 Guayama, Puerto Rico: The substitution of oil-fired on-site steam generation at an industry with 
steam import from a coal-fired power station decreased emissions to about 99.5% SO2, 84% NOx, 
and 95% particulate matter (PM10) but increased emissions of CO and CO2. Furthermore, the use of 
treated waste water as cooling water by a power station avoided effluent discharges of 161 mega 
litres/day (van Berkel, 2010).  
Kawasaki, Japan: Waste products like mixed plastic, organic wastes, mixed sludges and blast 
furnace slag  were used as alternative fuels and raw materials in various applications across the IS 
network. Collectively these diverted over 565,000 tonnes/year of waste into useful applications (van 
Berkel, 2010) . 
2.5 Tools for Quantifying Environmental Benefits Of Industrial Symbiosis 
It is necessary to assess whether expected ‘triple bottom line’ benefits from the established EIP are 
being realised, to find out where there short falls and future enhancements and possibilities that could 
arise from the system. These benefits are usually quantified in economic and environmental terms. 
Social benefits which are related to development on the other hand are sometimes difficult to quantify 
and require a lot of indicators. There is a lot of literature on various tools and methodology which 
have been developed over the years to quantify the economic, social and environmental benefits of 
EIPs. Some of these tools are specific to a type of exchange, i.e. whether its material, heat and energy 
or water systems (Kastner et al., 2015).  
(Kastner et al., 2015) performed a literature review on existing methods and tools used to identify 
and cultivate IS in industrial parks. For heat and energy systems the primary method of assessment is 
the pinch analysis, which involves the use of graphical representations to set ideal targets for a set of 
==PAGE:24==
15 
 
 
process streams, the network is then built based on the best approach to achieve the targets. They then 
mention the work of (Ahmad & Hui, 1991) who studied HENs in terms of an Industrial Park. They 
introduced the idea of heat recovery from parts of the process plant they described areas of integrity 
directly and through centralised systems (Ahmad & Hui, 1991). 
For water exchange systems, the paper mentions tools such as the superstructure model developed by 
(Takama, Kuriyama, Shiroko, & Umeda, 1980), as being one of the earliest in terms of water analysis 
tools. Their method was aimed at solving the planning problem of water allocation in a petroleum 
refinery. Another optimization model tool developed by (Geng, Côté, & Tsuyoshi, 2007) involved 
calculating water savings by using data from direct water reuse among users; second, water reuse 
among users by blending with freshwater; third, water reuse between users and a wastewater 
treatment plant; and fourth, groundwater recharge by reclaimed wastewater or other feasible 
applications. This method was quite comprehensive and more suited for an Industrial Park. (Lim & 
Park, 2008), also developed a mathematical model which was aimed at reducing the carbon footprint 
of industrial plants by comparing the savings made in water network systems in individual industrial 
plants to that of a cooperation of industries. 
Some methodology and tools for quantifying benefits for a material exchange network discussed in 
the paper include one developed by (Kincaid & Overcash, 2001) for a US EPA project, which was to 
locate opportunities for by-products exchange among industries across six metropolitan areas in 
North Carolina, USA. The methodology involves performing a voluntary survey among the various 
industries and mapping potential exchanges using GIS. Material Flow Analysis (MFA) is a widely 
used tool for the analysis of materials and has been applied in a lot of areas, including IE. MFA is 
based on the principle of the Law on conservation of matter, which states that matter is nether created 
nor destroyed but is transformed from form to another. Performing an MFA is basically accounting 
for the flows of material inputs and outputs in a defined system. It can be applied to water and energy 
as well. MFA (Sendra, Gabarrell, & Vicent, 2007) also developed a methodology which uses 
indicators from MFA to analyse the efficiency and ranks of materialization in an Industrial Park.  
For EIPs with multiple exchanges, some developed methodology and tools include models for an 
optimized water and power system, and material and energy and utility networks. However, there are 
still ongoing studies performed on these kinds of systems. Water and power systems are also known 
as ‘interplant water-allocation and heat exchange networks’ (IWAHENs). One method used in 
quantifying such systems was developed by (Zhou, Li, Dong, & Grossmann, 2012), which is a 
==PAGE:25==
16 
 
 
multiscale state−space superstructure is developed to capture all possible network configurations for 
the fixed flow rate (FF) IWAHEN designs with both direct and indirect integration schemes. This 
was otherwise done performed in a single plant or individually.  
For material and energy networks, there are the methods developed by (Karlsson & Wolf, 2008) and 
(Gu, Leveneur, Estel, & Yassine, 2013). The first is Method for analysis of INDustrial energy systems 
(MIND) for the quantification of economic benefits in an IS network and the latter is a mathematical 
model which focuses on maximizing exchanges between industries in an IS network as well as the  
total economic gains or profits derived while minimizing environmental impacts and waste treatment 
costs. Methods for Utility systems which are usually a mix of steam, water and electricity exchange 
systems include works by (Kim, Yoon, Chae, & Park, 2010) and (Zhang, Luo, Chen, & Chen, 2013), 
who both developed methodologies for the optimisation of such systems based on case studies.

3. Methodology 
Two methodologies were used for data collection and analysis of the results. The first was developed 
by (Kincaid & Overcash, 2001) for the US EPA to identify potential by-products partnerships within 
six metropolitan areas in North Carolina. In their methodology, the first step was to prepare an initial 
target list of local industries and other facilities likely to participate in and benefit from the project. 
Next, they promoted the idea of project participation through initial telephone calls. Data was 
gathered through a survey booklet containing a list of potentially reusable items and in-plant 
interviews to review and clarify survey answers. Geographic information system (GIS) maps with an 
associated project database was then used to identify potential partners. Afterwards, research was 
carried out potential partnerships by convening discussions with multiple potential suppliers and 
users, conducting brainstorming sessions with local manufacturing experts, and searching existing 
pollution prevention and waste reduction literature. The final step was facilitating negotiations 
between potential partners (Kincaid & Overcash, 2001). 
The second methodology involved using Material Flow Analysis (MFA) to analyse the flow of 
materials, energy and water. The guidelines for MFA are outlined in a document by the EUROSTAT 
and the whole procedure is based on the material balance principle (Eurostat, 2001). Other similar 
MFA methodologies have been developed based on this. MFA is based on the Law of Conservation 
of Matter which states that mass is neither created nor destroyed. The principle of mass balance is 
used to control an MFA in terms of all inputs, outputs and stocks (Brunner & Rechberger, 2017). 
MFA was chosen because of its wide use in investigating the flows and stocks of any material-based 
system. MFA gives insight into the behaviour of a material system, and can be combined with other 
tools and methods such as life cycle assessment, energy flow analysis, economic analysis, and 
consumer-oriented analysis, to aid in the understanding and control of an anthropogenic system 
(Brunner & Rechberger, 2017).  
In the area of industrial ecology, MFA is useful in controlling pathways for material use and industrial 
processes. It can also be used in the creation of loop-closing industrial practices, dematerializing 
industrial output, systematizing patterns of energy use and balancing industrial input and output to 
natural ecosystem capacity. Other advantages of MFA include easy visualization of the system and 
transparency that is associated with ensuring that the principle of mass balance is adhered to. Also, 
MFA can be combined with other approaches such as LCA and risk assessment. Furthermore, it is 
==PAGE:27==
18 
 
 
also very useful policy-making processes especially in the areas of resource, environment and waste 
management mainly because of the principle of mass balance (Brunner & Rechberger, 2017).

3.2 Data collection 
The selected companies were approached with letters of invitation and a project summary as a 
supporting document. These letters were personally delivered to the offices of the various industries. 
In some cases, e-mails were sent to the companies. The main request was for companies to participate 
by sharing information regarding the various material, energy and water inputs used in their industrial 
system as well as their sources and quantities; the quantities of products produced as well as 
associated by-products and types of waste products and how and where they are treated or disposed 
of. The companies were also asked to provide information on their history, operation processes and 
waste management practices as well. Aside this, the views and perceptions of the various companies 
on IS were also taken.  
Follow ups were made to the selected companies, either through phone calls or personal visits. Some 
companies wanted further clarification of the project, as such on-site interviews were scheduled. 
These interview sessions offered the opportunity to also gain further insights into the operations of 
the company, their views on sustainable environmental practices and Industrial Symbiosis.  
Of the companies contacted, three were willing to participate in the project, namely; Volta Aluminium 
Company, Ferro Fabrik Limited and Pioneer Food Cannery. Two other companies were interested in 
the project, however the data they provided did not meet the criteria for the assessment. The rest of 
the companies either expressed disinterest or declined to participate due to the confidential nature of 
their data and operations. The EPA and Ghana National Cleaner Production Centre were willing to 
share a report of a preliminary assessment they had done on Industrial Symbiosis for the whole of 
Ghana.  
3.3 Inventory 
An inventory of the processes of each company was created in Excel using the data extracted from 
the annual environmental reports provided. The data needed for the assessment was mainly the input 
materials – their sources and quantities, final products and their quantities, various by-products from 
the production process and how they are handled in terms of waste management practices. Also, 
detailed process flows for the various companies were also obtained from the reports. There were 
some data gaps in the reports provided by the companies. To overcome this, assumptions were made 
based on literature to ensure that the models were robust and reflected what actually happens in a 
typical industry.

3.4 Modelling 
Modelling of the material flows in the companies was done using the STAN MFA software. The 
model was focused on the material flows in the production systems of the industries. The first step in 
the modelling process was the setting of the system boundaries which were restricted to the processes 
and materials used within the industry. The manufacturing system was then structured into processes, 
flows (inputs, outputs and emissions) and stocks using the data collated in the inventory. The unit for 
the mass flows was tons per annum. The final good was the quantity of product produced in a year. 
Validation of the model was done by calculating and correcting errors in the model to ensure that the 
mass flows were balanced. 
3.5 Analysis 
Analysis of opportunities for industrial symbiosis was done by carrying out literature search and 
reviewing literature on the production processes of each of the industries, the typical types of waste 
generated and their possible uses. The results of the literature search was used to propose new 
symbiosis opportunities which could be explored by the companies.

4.1 VALCO 
Volta Aluminium Company Limited (VALCO) is wholly owned by the Government and people of 
Ghana. VALCO is a major producer of primary aluminium for the world market.  Located in the 
Heavy Industrial Area of Tema on Ghana’s coast, VALCO was established as a private limited 
liability company incorporated in the Republic of Ghana in 1960 (VALCO, 2017). It employs 557 
people and has an annual turnover of about US$88 million (Sutton & Kpentey, 2012). 
VALCO has a production capacity of 200,000mt of primary aluminium annually, with five Portlines 
and each having a hundred reduction cells. VALCO has operated at 20% of full capacity since its 
reactivation in 2011, this is mainly due to the shortfall and high cost of power. Half of that production 
is supplied to Aluworks (an aluminium casting and rolling company) and Western Rod & Wire 
Company Limited, another local company that uses foundry ingots and molten aluminium for the 
production of rods for high-tension cables. VALCO has signed offtake agreements with Aluworks 
and Western Rod and Wire to purchase 1,500mt of ingots between them (1,000mt to Aluworks and 
500mt to Western Rod and Wire); the remaining 1,500mt of ingots is exported to Europe. 
VALCO’s main raw materials and energy requirements are alumina, petroleum coke and power

of bauxite from which alumina can be produced, there are no local supplies of alumina due to the 
absence of an alumina refinery in the country. The company imports petroleum coke from a number 
of countries in Europe as well as from China. Electricity is supplied locally by GRIDCO Limited (the 
distributor for the Volta River Authority).  
It currently produces an estimated amount of about 3,000mt of foundry ingots per month for the local 
and international markets. The final products include; Low Profile Sows, Extrusion Billets, 22Kg 
Foundry Ingots, T-Ingots, Rolling slabs, Processed/Unprocessed Ingots and Scrap Material. Major 
by-products/waste from the aluminium smelting process include particulate dust, spent carbon 
cathode blocks, spent cell linings, aluminium dross and wastewater.

Figure 7 shows an MFA of VALCO. The detailed process flow was extracted from the Annual

Dock Operations: The first stage of production is the dock operations, where these imported raw 
materials are received, transported and stored in silos for use by the company. The raw materials are 
imported in the following quantities; 69,194mt/yr of Alumina is imported from Brazil and Suriname, 
12,260mt/yr of PET Coke and 3,207mt/yr of coal tar pitch (Pitch coke) both imported from China,

from Switzerland. From storage, the materials are transported by a conveyor belt into bins which are 
then transported to the production site. The major waste product at this stage is the dust but this is 
usually reclaimed and used depending on its quality.  
Anode Production: The next stage of production is the anode production.  Anodes are needed for 
the electrolysis process at the reduction stage. The material inputs for this process are mainly 63% of 
petroleum coke, 13% of coal tar pitch and 20% spent anode butts (parts of the old anode). The coke 
and spent anode butts are crushed and mixed with the pitch coke which acts as a binder and heated 
with steam. The mixture is then transferred to a mould and baked to produce what is known as ‘green 
anode’. The green anodes are connected to copper conductor rods and transferred to the reduction 
cells. The main environmental concern at this stage is the emission of Volatile Organic Compounds 
(VOCs). In VALCO, 12,260mt of PET Coke, 3,207mt of Pitch Coke and 3867mt of spent anode butts 
are used in the production of 19,334mt of green anodes per year.

known as bath, are placed in the cavity of the cells. 66723mt/yr of alumina, 429mt/yr of cryolite and

6. Discussion 
This chapter presents a discussion on the results obtained from the MFA assessment. The 
methodology and approach used for the assessment is being discussed in this section. The final section 
of this chapter focuses on how IS could be implemented in the considered industrial area. 
6.1 Identified  IS opportunities 
In an effort to search for existing and new IS opportunities to help improve the environmental 
performance in the Tema Industrial area, and MFA was performed for three industries namely; 
VALCO, Ferro Fabrik Limited and Pioneer Food Cannery; with focus on the material flows in their 
production systems and the kinds of by-products/waste generated. For each company, one or more IS 
opportunities were identified and are discussed in detail in this section. For the whole industrial area, 
an attempt was made to match the various industrial sectors based on the typical waste generated 
within that sector. In this section, the IS opportunities identified for each company and corresponding 
environmental benefits are elucidated and instances of these IS opportunities in existing EIPs are also 
mentioned. 
6.1.1 VALCO: Spent Potlining 
 Spent potlining (SPL) is a major waste produced from the cleaning of the reduction cells in the 
aluminium smelting process. Reduction cells are where electrolysis of alumina into aluminium takes 
place. These cells are made of steel and lined with carbon cathode block and refractory material 
(bricks). Over time this lining becomes ineffective due to the accumulation of fluorides and small 
amounts of cyanide from cryolite, as such, this old lining (SPL) is cleared and a new lining is installed 
(Pong, Adrien, Besida, O’Donnell, & Wood, 2000). SPL typically contains about 55% carbon (spent 
cathode) and 45% refractory materials. The carbonaceous part is also known as ‘first cut’ and the 
refractory, ‘second cut’ (Miksa, Homsak, & Samec, 2003). Unlike the spent anode butts which are 
recycled in the production of new ‘green anodes’, SPL is classified as a hazardous and toxic material 
and hitherto new treatment technologies, it was mainly disposed by landfilling (Gao, Mostaghel, Ray, 
& Chattopadyay, 2016; Miksa et al., 2003). 
Issues have been raised within the Aluminium production sector about the management and disposal 
of SPL and its effects on the environment. One reason is the leaching of harmful chemicals such as 
fluorides and cyanides from the landfilled SPL into groundwater sources and run-offs. Another issue 
is the requirements of constructing landfills for hazardous waste, predominantly the availability of 
land and the costs involved in construction. Furthermore, the taxes on landfill always seemed to 
==PAGE:47==
38 
 
 
increase, especially in countries trying to phase out landfilling. This prompted the development of 
treatment technologies to handle this special kind of waste. In recent times new uses have been found 
for SPL especially in the cement, pig iron/steel making, power generation, and construction industries 
with some requiring little or no prior treatment (Gao et al., 2016; Pong et al., 2000).  
(Miksa et al., 2003) studied the use of the ‘first cut’ of SPL in the cement and thermal power 
generation as a source of fuel. The calorific value of bulk SPL is reported to be about 5 MJ/kg, while 
that of the ‘first cut’ is about 16 MJ/kg thus making it a good source of fuel (Pawlek, 2012). In the 
cement industry, the ‘first cut’ can be added as a supplement to natural gas and green petroleum coke 
in the production of clinker. It is also reported that cyanide was decomposed at high temperatures 
while fluoride emissions are very low (Miksa et al., 2003). Fluoride from the SPL released into the 
clinker mix also said to act as a catalyst in the formation of clinker, thus at reduced temperatures, 
reactions in the rotary kiln can still proceed. The ‘second cut’ can also be added in clinker production 
as an additive or ‘clinker aid’. In thermal plants, the first cut can be mixed with coal at a rate of 4.5-
8%, for energy recovery. This reduces the amount of coal or fossil fuel used in energy production. In 
the US and Canada, over 70% of SPL generated by aluminium smelting companies is utilized by the 
cement industry (Rahman, Rasul, Khan, & Sharma, 2015).   
The main concern regarding the use of SPL is the release of greenhouse gases like sulphur dioxide, 
especially if the coal has a high sulphur content. This can be mitigated by installing air scrubbers with 
limestone, the end product is gypsum which is also a useful product for cement production. The 
‘second cut’ which is mostly made up of refractory material was also used to produce red bricks 
successfully. The concern with producing red bricks with the second cut is mostly the emission of 
hydrogen fluoride, but this can be handled with the installation of an air scrubber with lime stone 
(Miksa et al., 2003). In the pig iron and steel making industries, SPL can be added in amounts of 
between 5-25 kg/t pig iron as a fuel, and the SPL refractory part and bath material dissolves in the 
slag. For special steels, the ‘first cut is recommended because of its high carbon content. (Pawlek, 
2012).  
In VALCO, the practice was to landfill the SPL generated at a landfill site located off the industrial 
area. But this practice was proving unsustainable for the company due to the same reasons stated 
above, therefore, the practice now is to store the SPL on their facilities and sell them to approved 
EPA scrap dealers. The end-use by these scrap dealers however is not exactly known by the company, 
therefore it is difficult to know if the SPL is being used in a sustainable manner. In the matter of IS, 
==PAGE:48==
39 
 
 
there are a number of cement industries located within the Tema Industrial Area which can utilize the 
SPL as fuel for their kilns as a supplement for coke. More directly, it may be possible for Ferro Fabrik 
to use the SPL in their production process also as a fuel supplement to coke. Also, the construction 
industry can also use the second cut for the production of bricks. Nevertheless, the use of SPL in these 
industries will require some prior preparation and treatment before use. 
An example of such symbiosis is in the Gladstone EIP, where approximately 12kt of SPL from Boyne 
Smelters, an aluminium smelting company, is used by Cement Australia as a source of fuel for the 
production of cement clinker. Boyne smelters used to cover the costs for SPL pre-processing and 
transportation to Cement Australia, and also pays an extra fee for its reuse in clinker kiln, but then 
this synergy ceased because of it was economically not favourable for the company. (Beers et al., 
2008; Golev et al., 2014). Considering the high costs of treatment, it could be recommended that 
VALCO and the interested company could share the costs between both companies. Also, an SPL 
treatment facility could be set up within the industrial area to handle the treatment of the SPL before 
use by the other industries.  
6.1.2 Ferro Fabrik: Slag 
Slag is a major waste material produced in steel making. Slag is basically made up of oxidised 
impurities and forms as a non-metallic residue on the surface of the molten steel. It is categorised into 
different categories based on the steel production method used. In the case of Ferro Fabrik Limited, 
which has ferrous scrap metal as its main input material, the EAF furnace is used for the 
manufacturing of steel hence slag from this method is referred to as EAF slag or steel slag. This class 
of slags contains a range of chemicals including recycled steel scrap, coke, lime and some metal 
oxides (Hosseini, Soltani, Fennell, Thomas, & Aroua, 2016). EAF slag produced by Ferro Fabrik 
Limited is mostly landfilled or used by members of the local community without any prior treatment. 
However, the major environmental concern about the utilization or disposal of slag is the leaching of 
heavy metals (Reuter, Xiao, & Boin, 2004). Also, like the case of VALCO, landfill costs and 
availability of land is also a challenge for the company.  
Alternative uses have been found for slag in the cement production and construction industries. Slag 
can be used in cement industry as an additive in the production of clinker in the ground state because 
of its cementitious properties (Hosseini et al., 2016; Yi et al., 2012). Some reported benefits of adding 
slag in the cement mixture include; the development of mechanical strengths, low solubility of the 
hydrates and porosity, decreased heat of hydration, excellent durability and stronger aggregate–
==PAGE:49==
40 
 
 
matrix inter- face (Hosseini et al., 2016). Also, slag can be used as a substitute for natural aggregates 
in concrete this helps in the conservation of natural resources and also reduces the environmental 
impacts associated with the extraction of these natural aggregates. Again, slag can be used in road 
construction, particularly in the construction of asphalt-surfaced roads. Some advantages include 
good friction, resistance to cracking and a good resistance against water permeability (Hosseini et al., 
2016).  
Some other applications of slag are; use as a raw material in fertilizer production in the agriculture 
sector because of the phosphorus and lime content, and as a low cost solution for the removal of heavy 
metals and phosphorus in WWTP by absorbing the phosphorus, heavy metals and other impurities in 
the wastewater. A major concern with the use of EAF or steel slag is the high content of free lime 
which sometimes hinders its use in the aforementioned industries, however there are a number of 
treatment technologies which can remove this free lime and make it suitable for use. Further treatment 
can also be done for metal recovery (Hosseini et al., 2016). 
In terms of IS in the Tema Industrial Area, there is an opportunity for the slag produced by Ferro 
Fabrik Limited to be directly used by the various cement industries in the industrial area as an additive 
in clinker production. The slag can also be used by the construction companies for road and other 
construction processes like the production of pavement blocks. Also, there is an opportunity for 
industries within the industrial area to use the slag from Ferro Fabrik Limited in their WWTP. 
Furthermore, since Ferro Fabrik Limited is a metal recycling company in itself, they can form IS 
partnerships with the various industries in the industrial area for direct access to the scrap metal from 
waste machinery and equipment.. 
6.1.3 Pioneer Food Cannery: Fish waste 
Fish waste is a major waste product from the tuna processing however this waste has been identified 
as a valuable resource useful for IS. Fish waste comprises of mainly the head, tails, skin, gut, fins and 
frames of the fish. Fish waste can be utilized in the production of other useful products such as fish 
meal, omega 3 oils, collagen and crude fish oil. In previous times, PFC sold their fish waste to the 
local contractors who then produce fish meal out of the waste. But then they realised that the waste 
was not been handled in an environmentally-safe manner was causing further problems in the local 
community. Also, it was becoming difficult to comply with the environmental standards set by the 
EPA, because of some of these issues. PFC recognised these products from fish waste as opportunities 
to improve their production process, generate revenue and reduce the amount of waste generated 
==PAGE:50==
41 
 
 
while improving their environmental performance. Therefore aside their main products, they have set 
up facilities which treat the fish waste into these useful products. The concept of IS was introduced 
to the company by the Ghana National Cleaner Production Centre and with their help, they have 
identified a number opportunities for IS and have already started symbiosis relationships with some 
industries including some pharmaceutical industries and industries in the agricultural sector. A brief 
summary of the identified products and the IS opportunities they present are discussed below. 
6.1.4 Omega-3 Oil extraction 
A plant has been installed at the facility to extract and process Omega-3 oils from the eyes of the 
waste fish head and sold to the pharmaceutical industries and food industries to produce baby food. 
Hitherto, the fish head was cooked together with the body and disposed of with the other fish waste 
at the cleaning stage, but since realising the opportunity this fish head presents as a resource, it is cut 
off at the start of the production before fish moves onto the other stages of production.  
6.1.5 Fish meal  
Fish meal is a major product produced from fish waste and used is in the agricultural sector as animal 
feed, because of its high protein content. The major steps of production are heating, pressing, 
separation, evaporation, drying, grinding and palletisation. The final product is a dry powder or pellets 
and this is sold to feed mill companies in the agricultural production sector. The benefits realised here 
are; 
 Increased profits from the production and sale of fish meal(about 32% of yield from raw fish 
waste 
 Harvesting of about 1% black oil, a by-product of fish meal production, for boiler operations  
 
6.1.6 Fish oil 
The processing of fish waste into fish meal also produces fish oil and water as by-products. Heating 
of the fish waste ruptures the fat deposits and this is released as oil along with water at the pressing 
stage. The water and oil are separated using a decanter. For PFC, this separated oil is collected in 
barrels and exported outside the country. Locally, this oil could be used by the pharmaceutical 
industries in the production of fish oil supplements and food processing industries as well.  
To produce biofuel, the extracted oil is mixed with methanol and caustic soda in order to separate the 
glycerine from the biodiesel. Manganese is then added to purify the biodiesel to make it fit for 
==PAGE:51==
42 
 
 
engines. The by-product, glycerine can be sold to the cosmetic industry for the production of cosmetic 
products (FAO, 2017). PFC uses part of this oil as a fuel for their boilers. There is also an opportunity 
to sell this oil to other industries as a substitute to fossil fuels. This will help reduce GHG emissions 
in the industrial area.  
The remaining water, known as stick water is condensed by evaporation to a thick syrup. This syrup 
is high in proteins and can be used sold to the agricultural sector as fish solubles. It can also be dried 
and added to the fish meal to produce whole meal. This reduces the BOD of the wastewater and 
allows further recovery of oil.  
6.1.7 Collagen 
Collagen is a form of protein extracted from the skin, bones and fins of fish waste, and are used in 
the cosmetics, food and biomedical industries. The company has also identified this as an opportunity 
for IS but is yet to start extraction. 
6.1.8 Sludge 
Sludge from the company is disposed of by a private waste management company. This sludge can 
be treated and sold for use by the agricultural sector as fertilizer. The sludge can also be used for the 
production of biogas which can serve as substitute for fossil fuels. The benefits here include; a low 
cost sustainable source of energy and the reduction in GHG emissions. 
6.2 Environmental Benefits 
In this section details the environmental benefits that could be realised from the proposed IS 
opportunities. 
6.2.1 Less landfilling 
The environmental problems associated with landfilling as stated earlier, especially with the waste 
from these industries, are the leaching of toxic chemicals, emission of greenhouse gases and 
availability of space (land). An IS partnership between VALCO and the proposed industrial sectors 
will divert 1250mt/yr of SPL away from landfills. For Ferro Fabrik, utilization of the EAF slag by 
other industries will divert some 2,100mt/yr of this slag away from landfills. PFC landfills 970mt/yr 
of damaged canned tuna. The environmental concern here is the decomposition of the tuna in landfill 
and the subsequent release of greenhouse gases over time. Instead, the damaged tuna could be added 
to the fish waste and recycled into fish meal. Collectively, the pursuing these IS opportunities within 
the Tema Industrial Area can save some 4,320mt/yr of waste from being landfilled. Also, from Figure 
==PAGE:52==
43 
 
 
10, the cement industry seems to be the most connected of the industries mainly utilizing these 
products as alternative sources of fuel. Aside the case study companies, the cement industry can 
utilize waste from the other sectors like the cocoa industry and even municipal solid waste, thereby 
diverting these wastes from the landfills. 
6.2.2 Savings in raw material 
The use of SPL in the cement industry is used as an example in this case. SO3 content is one of the 
parameters used in determining the quality of cement. This parameter usually has to be in the range 
of 3%. The use of coal with high sulphur content increases the SO3 in the clinker. Gypsum is used in 
such cases to reduce the SO3. (Gomes et al., 2005) in a study on a cement company noted that on 
average 5% gypsum was added in relation to the cement with high SO3 content. However, they found 
out that the addition of gypsum reduced to 3.5% percent after the addition of SPL. Again, fluorides 
from SPL lower the melting point in the clinkering process and this is marked by a decrease in coke 
consumption. (Gomes et al., 2005) again in their study observed a 10kcal/kg decrease in the 
temperature of the kiln after the addition of SPL. Considering that 280 ton/day of coke was used, this 
change resulted in savings of approximately 3.7 ton/day.  
6.2.3 CO2 and Greenhouse gas emissions 
CO2 and greenhouse gas savings can be made by using these residual materials as alternative sources 
of fuel and raw materials in other industries. The savings made in the amount of coal used in cement 
when SPL is added also culminates in the reduction of CO2 and greenhouse gases. (Black & Cooper, 
2016) performed an assessment LCA on the systems of SPL processing, transport and consumption 
in a cement industry. Their results showed that for 1 tonne of SPL used the net benefits gained in 
terms of GHG emissions was 4.0 tCO2e, with most of the savings coming from the consumption 
stage. Therefore for the Tema Industrial Area, approximately 5,000 tCO2e of GHG emissions could 
be saved through the utilization of SPL. (Hashimoto, Fujita, Geng, & Nagasawa, 2010) also in their 
study on IS in Kawasaki Eco-town, Japan, they found out that a cement producer reduced its CO2 
emissions by 43,000 t per year using recycled materials rather than virgin materials. 
Carbon savings can also made from the use of biodiesel produced from PFC’s fish oil. 
(Arvanitoyannis & Kassaveti, 2008) reports the higher heating value of biodiesel made from fish oil 
as 10,700 kcal/kg (44.78MJ/kg) and its lower flash and pour points as 37 and -16 °C. Comparing the 
biodiesel with commercial diesel fuel, they observed no production of sulphur oxides, lowered or no 
soot, polyaromatic and carbon dioxide emissions. With this higher heating value, the amount of 
energy which will be produced from the combustion of the biodiesel produced at PFC was calculated 
==PAGE:53==
44 
 
 
as approximately 113,538 GJ/yr which is significantly high. Currently, PFC uses 1% of this biodiesel 
in its boilers hence it should be possible for other industries to use it in their boilers. This will lead to 
a significant reduction in the use of fossil fuels and the associated greenhouse gas emissions in the 
Tema Industrial Area. 
6.2.4 Resource efficiency and conservation 
One of the aims of industrial symbiosis is the judicious use of resources prior to disposal. From the 
MFA of PFC, it can be said that the company’s current industrial system with IS ensures high resource 
efficiency. From 43,836mt/yr of tuna, the company produces 19,643mt/yr of canned tuna and about 
12,1222mt/yr of secondary products from fish waste. This means that about 72% of the resource is 
used in the industrial system. The remaining 21% of the fish is lost as water. Likewise in VALCO 
and Ferro Fabrik, the reuse of the SPL and slag by other industries is likely increase resource 
efficiency among industries in the industrial area.  
Again, the use of EAF slag in applications like road construction and cement production helps in the 
conservation of natural resources, since the slag substitutes the use of some natural raw materials like 
rocks, lime stone and sand. Aside Ferro Fabrik, there are about 10 other steel manufacturing 
companies in the industrial area. Collectively, these companies produce over 20,000mt/yr of slag. 
Use of the slag generated across these steel industries for various apllications will save the equivalent 
in raw materials. Economically, it is a cheap raw material and in the cement production and 
construction industries, it helps to reduce production costs to an extent. 
6.2.5 Environmental Regulations 
Complying with environmental standards could prove challenging for industries especially where the 
regulations require some extra form of treatment of the waste generated before disposal. For example, 
PFC reports that prior to the implementation of IS in the company, it was a challenge for them to 
meet the EPA’s standards on waste particularly with the wastewater and fish waste released into the 
environment. However since the implementation of IS, the company has been able to meet the set 
standards of the EPA, especially since the installation of the fish meal plant which converts most of 
its fish waste into useful products. Again, the diversion of waste from landfills reduces or eliminates 
the pressure associated with the constant monitoring of leachate and other pollutants from landfills. 
6.2.6 Environmental and Social Amenity 
In the case of PFC, instances and complaints of unpleasant odour and unsightly scenes which were 
associated with the disposal of fish waste in the community ceased after taking steps to implement 
==PAGE:54==
45 
 
 
IS. For the society, they could enjoy the amenities like clean surroundings devoid of waste and clean 
fresh air as well. Therefore implementation of IS in the industrial area can help in the maintenance of 
a clean environment while providing social benefits like less exposure to harmful substances and an 
aesthetically pleasing environment as well. 
6.3 New business opportunities 
The matching of the various industrial sectors also led to the discovery of new business opportunities 
which will either use the waste materials from the industries as a resource or provide direct service to 
other companies. This opportunities are elaborated in the sections below. 
6.3.1 Waste-to-energy plant 
Erratic power supply and high tariffs over the last decade has had a strong impact on the Ghanaian 
economy, with industries bearing the brunt of the situation. This situation was mainly as a result of a 
deficit in power generation and supply. The nation then had to go through a period of power rationing 
(also known as load shedding or ‘dumsor’ in the local parlance), where a part of the country gets 
electricity for a period in the day whiles another is without electricity for that same period and vice 
versa. The industrial sector is the highest consumer of power generated in the country. Most of the 
industries had to reduce their production capacities in order to cope with situation. For instance, as 
stated in the earlier section, VALCO had to cut down on its production to run at 20% capacity. This 
had adverse effects on the industrial sector with the major ones being; revenue losses, staff layoffs 
and in extreme cases, complete shut downs and closure. The energy mix of the country is 
predominantly hydroelectric power plant and thermal power generation plants and this accounts for 
about 99% of the total energy share (Eshun & Amoako-Tuffour, 2016). The remaining 1% is energy 
generated from alternative sources which includes solar, biomass and biogas. An increase in the share 
of these alternative energy sources by including renewable and sustainable energy, especially within 
the various industrial areas, will help the industries to meet their energy requirements and ease the 
energy burden of the nation.  
One of such alternative sources proposed for the Tema Industrial Area is the establishment of a waste-
to-energy plant which will generate electricity for the industrial area. Waste-to-energy plants are built 
to recovery energy from waste products. A typical waste-to-energy plant converts municipal and 
industrial waste materials with high calorific values into electricity and heat by combustion at high 
temperatures. From the assessment done for the whole industrial area, waste materials such as; cocoa 
shells from the cocoa industries (HHV 18Kj/kg), sawdust (HHV 13kJ/kg) from the wood industry 
(Spilacek et al., 2016), palm kernel and shea nut shells from the oil and fats processing industry; were 
==PAGE:55==
46 
 
 
identified as suitable feedstock for the plant. Also, sludge from the Tema Oil Refinery which is 
classified as hazardous waste but high in calorific value because of the residual oil can be burned in 
this facility instead of disposal at the landfill. Electricity generated from the plant will augment the 
current power deficit in the industrial area. The heat energy can also be used by other industries for 
their operations. Environmentally, a waste-to-energy plant will lead to a significant reduction in the 
amount of waste sent to the landfill. Another benefit is the CO2 savings that could be made from 
replacing fossil fuels with biomass. A range of flue gas treatment systems are available and can be 
installed to clean the air emissions from the facility. Furthermore, the waste-to-energy facility can be 
used by the industrial area and the municipality as well. An example of such symbiosis is found in 
India, where most agro-processing industries with high calorific waste products, practice energy 
recovery right in their facilities. In other cases, it is purchased by other energy suppliers who produce 
electricity for their national grid (Bain et al., 2010). The practice is to add some amounts coal in the 
boiler to reduce instances of incomplete combustion, but in this case, SPL from VALCO could be 
used as that supplement. 
6.3.2 Alumina Refinery 
Another proposed business opportunity is the establishment of an Alumina Refinery in the Tema 
Industrial Area, which will refine the bauxite mined in Ghana into alumina, a major raw material for 
VALCO. The mining of bauxite in Ghana dates back to the 1940’s and is a source of foreign revenue 
for the country. The custom has always been to export the mined bauxite to other countries in its raw 
state for the production of alumina. On the other hand, alumina which is the base raw material for 
VALCO is imported from other countries thus increasing the cost of production. Establishing an 
Alumina refinery in the industrial area will be expedient for the country because of revenue generation 
and the creation of jobs, and most especially for VALCO because of the direct access to alumina. 
Examples of IS networks with alumina refineries are Kwinana and Gladstone, which are both EIPs 
in Australia, with synergies between alumina refineries and aluminium smelting companies. In 
Gladstone, caustic soda from the treatment of SPL at Boyne Aluminium Smelters is used by 
Queensland Alumina Refinery (Beers et al., 2008). For the VALCO, this presents an opportunity for 
the use waste recovered from the treatment of SPL. 
6.3.3 Mushroom cultivation 
Another business venture that could be introduced in the industrial area is the cultivation of 
mushrooms using the waste sawdust from the wood industry and waste cotton fluffs from the textiles 
industry as substrate. This could be started as a small-scale business. Growing mushrooms does not 
==PAGE:56==
47 
 
 
require much capital to start and does not need chemicals like fertilizers or pesticides in order to grow. 
It could be described as a sustainable source of protein. Aside being a good source of protein, some 
work has also been done on the use of mushrooms for the remediation of polluted soils, 
mycoremediation, with success. This is done through biodegradation and biosorption which involve 
the break down and uptake of pollutants respectively. As such, it could also be used in treating parts 
of the industrial area which have been contaminated or polluted with harmful chemicals. 
6.4 Methodology 
The methodology used in the research project proved effective in the identification of IS opportunities 
for the selected companies to share material resources. The first part of the methodology was to 
introduce the project to the selected companies and get them to share data on their operations which 
was needed for the analysis. The three companies which were used as case studies for the project, 
namely; VALCO, Ferro Fabrik Limited and Pioneer Food Cannery; expressed keen interest in the 
outcome of the project and as such willingly shared their data in the form of environmental reports. 
They also granted interviews where they explained their operations and gave more insight into the 
data they provided. Furthermore, the procurement and environmental managers from these companies 
made themselves available for further enquiries. The EPA and GNCPC also expressed interest in the 
project since they were also embarking on a nation-wide IS project under the Switch Africa Green 
Project (a UN implemented programme with support from the EU). They also shared a preliminary 
report on the identified IS opportunities across the various industrial sectors in Ghana. This proved 
very useful in the matching of the industrial sectors in the Tema Industrial Area. 
The drawback at this stage of the project was the high number of companies which declined the 
invitation to participate in the research despite the detailed explanation of the project and the 
assurance of data confidentiality. For some companies, even though they expressed interest in 
participation, the data they provided was insufficient to be used for an effective analysis. In terms of 
data quality, a recommendation here would be the use of questionnaires which details clearly the 
information and data required from the company to ensure that the data collected is concise and well 
organised. The environmental reports can be used as supplementary information.  
The MFA was successfully carried out using the data provided by the company. This made it possible 
to characterize and quantify all the waste products generated in the company. Furthermore, all current 
waste management practices were and helped in the identification of new IS opportunities. It is worth 
mentioning here that modelling the processes quantitatively proved quite challenging especially for 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
 
the waste/by-products generated at the various stages of production. This was because there was little 
or no data for the quantities of waste generated in some of the reports provided by the company, as 
such assumptions on those figures were made based on literature. The matching of the various 
industrial sectors for the whole industrial were qualitative. A quantitative model could not be done in 
this case because of the lack of data. It is essential that the quantities of waste generated are known 
and recorded, this will help in assessing whether the IS partnerships will be viable venture to pursue. 
A recommendation here would also be for all industries to maintain an accurate database on the 
inputs, outputs as well as the quantities of waste/by-products produced from their operations.  
6.4 Implementation of Industrial Symbiosis in Ghana 
There a barriers which impede the implementation process of IS networks. (M. R. Chertow, 2007) 
identifies classifies these barriers as technical barriers, informational barriers, economic barriers, 
regulatory barriers and motivational barriers. A possible technical barrier is the incompatibility of the 
companies based on the kind of waste generated. Informational barriers has to do with low 
information on the potential uses of the waste products. This makes it difficult to identify potential 
markets and suppliers. Economic barriers such as taxes, even the high cost of treatment of the waste 
for the target market and unreliable markets are likely to inhibit the incentive use the waste as a 
resource. Regulatory barriers such as laws on hazardous waste may also prevent industries from 
participating in IS. Lastly, motivational barriers on the part of firms, public sector agencies and other 
relevant stakeholders must be willing to co-operate and commit themselves to the process. 
From the results of the assessment, implementation of the IS in the Tema Industrial Area can be done 
successfully if these barriers are removed. The major requirements for IS are the identification and 
quantification of the material resources and the various industries that could utilize these resources. 
The next step us to get the companies to cooperate and form partnerships for the purpose of sharing 
waste products. Education of the companies on the benefits of IS is one way to get them to cooperate. 
As a form of motivation, policies could be formulated to provide incentives to companies which 
willingly participate in IS.  
The informal sector is a very important stakeholder in the industrial sector, particularly in the area of 
recycling. In the current industrial system, the informal sector which includes local scrap dealers and 
waste collectors, act as ‘middle men’ in the exchange of waste materials between companies. The 
handling of the waste resource by these middle men maybe unsustainable and this may lead to the 
loss of the quality of the waste product during the transport or exchange. Nonetheless, considering 
==PAGE:58==
49 
 
 
their role in recycling activities across the industrial area, they could be formally engaged so they also 
understand this concept of IS so they also get involved. There is also the need to regulate the activities 
of this group to ensure that environmental standards are met and that the network exchanges are still 
viable and reliable. On the other hand, IS offers the opportunity for the companies to exchange their 
waste directly so the role of this informal sector may be diminished going forward. Direct exchange 
of waste products between companies may lead to the reduction of the cost of waste collection and 
also ensure that the quality of the materials being exchanged in the network meets the requirements 
of the receiving company.  
For successful IS implementation, it is usually beneficial to set up a directing or coordinating body 
or authority to really monitor the IS network and make sure that these networks are creating the 
needed socioeconomic and environmental benefits (Lowe, 2001). Again, the body will be in charge 
of identifying new opportunities for symbiosis within the industrial area, and the introduction and 
integration of new companies into the IS network. An example is the Kalundborg Symbiosis Centre 
for Kalundborg and the Kwinana Industrial Synergies Project for the Kwinana EIP (Beers et al., 2008; 
Kalundborg Symbiosis Center, 2017), which are bodies set up to ensure a successful IS network.  The 
setting up of the GNCPC (Cleaner Production Centre) is therefore timely and essential for the 
implementation of IS in Tema and other industrial areas. Among other functions, they provide the 
various industries with information and tips on cleaner production and resource efficiency. The 
GNCPC also has direct oversight on the current IS programme being implemented in Ghana, and they 
are making conscious efforts to introduce the various industries to the concept. As part of the 
programme, they are working to create a platform where interested companies will have the 
opportunity to trade their waste with other interested parties.  
Furthermore, as part of its plan to revamp the industrial sector in Ghana and create jobs, the current 
government has introduced a policy titled ‘one-district-one-factory’, where an industry will be set up 
in each of the 216 districts of the country. The idea is to establish industries which will harness the 
natural resources in each of these districts and process them into finished products. A number of 
concerns have been raised about the sustainability of such a project, with the view that most industries 
which were set up in the past few years have ceased operation. The concept of IS could be applied to 
this idea, where the waste products from the factory in one district is used as a resource by a factory 
in the next district or even within the factory to ensure that these resources are judiciously used with 
minimum impacts on the environment. Nonetheless, this will require further research before 
implementation. 
==PAGE:59==

6.5. Future Prospects 
As the concept of IS is still relatively new to developing countries, specifically Ghana, this research 
can be used as a basis for further studies. Going forward, there is the need to create a database with 
all the identified and quantified waste products from the companies in the industrial area. Life Cycle 
Assessment can be performed to study the environmental impacts of all exchange types that have 
been implemented in industrial parks as well as the assessment of the benefits gained. (Kastner et al., 
2015). Furthermore, research focused on the economic benefits of IS in the Tema Industrial Area can 
also be carried to determine which IS networks are profitable to pursue and how to enhance the 
benefits from those with low profits. This could be used to motivate industries which are more 
economically inclined to participate in IS.

7. Conclusion 
 
In conclusion, the goal of the project, which was searching opportunities to enhance the 
environmental performance of the Tema Industrial Area in Ghana with IS, was achieved. Three 
companies located in the industrial area, namely; VALCO (an aluminium smelting company), Ferro 
Fabrik Limited (ferrous steel production) and Pioneer Food Cannery; were used as case studies in the 
project. Application of MFA on the production systems of these industries, proved useful in the 
characterization and quantification of the various types of waste generated, and also in the 
identification of disposal methods and IS opportunities. An attempt was also made to match the 
various industrial sectors in based on their typical waste products. 
For VALCO, spent potlining (SPL) from the aluminium industry was identified as a waste material 
which could be used in the cement and steel industries as a source of fuel. Slag from Ferro Fabrik 
Limited can be used as a resource for clinker production in cement industries and used as aggregates 
in the construction sector as well. Pioneer Food Cannery had already identified some IS opportunities 
with help from the Ghana National Cleaner Production Centre, some of which are; fish meal 
production for agricultural industries, omega-3 oil and collagen extraction for pharmaceutical and 
food industries, crude fish oil for biofuel and export and the application of sludge on agricultural 
lands.  
The environmental benefits to be gained from pursuing these IS opportunities include; a reduction in 
the amount of waste generated at the various facilities, a significant reduction in waste sent to 
landfills, protection of ground water and surface water resources, resource efficiency and 
conservation, CO2 savings from the use of alternative fuels and less pollution. Economically, 
companies will have the opportunity to generate revenue through the sale of the waste product and 
make savings from the reduced costs of waste management. 
The matching of the various industrial sectors also led to the discovery of some new business 
opportunities which could be started in the industrial area. The first is a waste-to-energy plant which 
will recover energy from waste products with high calorific values. This recovered energy in the form 
of electricity and heat can augment the shortfall of power in the industrial area and also reduce the 
use of fossil fuels for energy generation. The setting up of an alumina refinery in the industrial area 
to convert the bauxite mined in Ghana into alumina for direct use by VALCO. Lastly, a mushroom 
growing venture which utilizes saw dust from the wood industry as substrate. 
==PAGE:61==
52 
 
 
The success of IS hinges largely on the willingness of the companies to participate. More work needs 
to be done in this regard through awareness creation and education of the industries on the importance 
and benefits of IS. For the purposes of IS, it is advisable that the industries identify and quantify all 
the waste materials or substances from their operations. This will inform whether an IS synergy can 
be pursued successfully or not. The successes achieved by Pioneer Food Cannery used as an example 
and also serve as a motivation for companies to pursue IS.

List of Assumptions 
VALCO 
In the calculation of the production of the green anode, it was assumed that PET coke, pitch and the 
spent anode were mixed in the ratios of 63:17:20 respectively. 
Ferro Fabrik Limited 
In the calculation of the metal fractions in the scrap metal, the assumptions made for the metal 
fractions were 25% for ferrous metals, the remaining 75% was discarded as non-ferrous scrap 
metals.  
It was assumed that a tonne of liquid steel produced an average amount of 132 kg of CO2 as 
emissions (Yellishetty, Mudd, Ranjith, & Tharumarajah, 2011). 
I was also assumed that the steel slag output was approximately 20% by mass of the steel output. 
Pioneer Food Cannery 
The data was modelled for the production of canned pre-cooked tuna.  
The mass of the head of the fish was assumed to be 20% of the total mass of the fish. 
It was also assumed that the yield of oil from the omega-3 extraction process was 35% of the mass 
of the fish head. It was also assumed that the waste from the extraction would be added to the fish 
waste from the cleaning stage for the production of fish meal 
It was assumed that for the filling and seaming stages, about 5% of the canned tuna was discarded 
as damaged products. 
For fish meal plant, the assumptions were as follows, fish meal produced was 18% of the total fish 
waste, fish oil 12%, evaporated water 12% and stick water 58%. Sludge was assumed to be 15% of 
the stick water.
