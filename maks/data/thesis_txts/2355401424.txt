Summary (English)
This thesis takes a Bayesian approach to solving the linear inverse problem of
electroencephalography (EEG) source localization. The approach focuses on an
eﬃcient solution capable of real-time performance on a consumer smartphone.
The proposed model is developed in three steps: The ﬁrst step is a simple model
using Bayesian inference and assuming Gaussian distributions. In the second
step, the model is extended to incorporate automatic relevance determination
(ARD) through a sparsity promoting prior, in order to ﬁnd the most active
sources. The associated learning rule for tuning the hyperparameters is derived.
Lastly, the model is extended to utilize the assumed relatively static sparsity
pattern of the EEG signal through a multiple measurement vector (MMV) for-
mulation of the ARD algorithm. The results is a multiple response automatic
relevance determination (M-ARD) algorithm. The ARD algorithm was orig-
inally developed for determining relevance of features in neural networks by
MacKay et al. (1994) but have seen many uses for sparse problems since then.
A variant of the MMV approach to the ARD was ﬁrst proposed by Wipf et
al. (2005) in the form of multiple response sparse Bayesian learning (M-SBL).
Extensive numerical experiments on synthetic data, with a Gaussian random
forward model, verify the ability of the ARD to ﬁnd sparse solutions.
Fur-
ther simulations show the improvements and superior recovery performance of
the MMV approach in M-ARD over the single measurement vector approach in
ARD. Experiments with synthetic data and a realistic head model show the lim-
itations of the M-ARD. However, it is also demonstrated that the performance
is on par or close to state-of-the-art algorithms, while showing a computational
complexity reasonable for real-time usage.
Keywords: EEG, inverse problem, Bayesian inference, ARD, M-ARD, SBL,
M-SBL

Chapter 1
Introduction
The desire to understand the human mind is becoming increasingly more in-
teresting to researchers and doctors, as well as large corporations. One of the
approaches to understand what goes on inside the human head is the analysis
of the neural activity.
Functional brain imaging, also known as functional neuroimaging, is one ap-
proach to this where the purpose is to measure brain functions in order to di-
agnose diseases and understand the relation between brain activity and mental
functions. In the neurological and cognitive psychology research, it is, among
others, used for ﬁnding correlations between what a human subject see, feel,
smell, sense etc., and the neural activity in the brain those events give rise to.
Functional neuroimaging is also widely used for diagnosing brain related dis-
eases/dysfunctions, e.g. epilepsy and Alzheimer’s [RTL06; Smi05; AAA10].
One of the methods of neuroimaging is electroencephalography (EEG) [NS06].
While there exists a variety of methods for functional neuroimaging [Dar+04],
this thesis will solely focus on EEG. The motivation for this is that EEG can be
performed with a relatively small setup compared functional magnetic resonance
imaging (fMRI), positron emission tomography (PET) imaging, or magnetoen-
cephalography (MEG) imaging which in contrast all require large and expensive
clinical setups.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
In a time where everything is becoming more mobile, it is also interesting to
pursue a mobile environment for analyzing the brain as this makes it possible
to perform EEG in real-life scenarios and not just in a clinical setting. This
also makes the technology available to more people, including people in poor
and less developed countries where expensive equipment is not available. It has
been shown that mobile EEG can be performed [Sto+14] and is currently used
in the Bhutan Epilepsy Project [Pro15].
Performing EEG with a mobile setup means making it smaller and less com-
plicated than a clinical setup. First of all, this means fewer measuring sensors,
which is the a major bottleneck of EEG. EEG is performed by measuring an
electrical potential on the scalp of a subject with an array of sensors.
The neural activity in the brain is modeled as a number of dipole sources,
where the electrical potentials measurable on the scalp mainly are caused by
small currents in the dipole sources. While the measured signal can be used for
some insights, it does not clearly say anything exact about the neural activity.
This is due to the fact that when observed from the scalp the measured signal
is a mixture of sources. The task of determining the contributions from each
source in the measured electrical potential is a severely ill-posed linear inverse
problem. This problem is generally known as EEG source localization.

1.1 The linear inverse problem of EEG source localization
3
1.1
The linear inverse problem of EEG source lo-
calization
In order to understand what an ill-posed linear inverse problem is, imagine a
simple system consisting of a very simple “brain” modeled as two dipoles and
just one sensor on the scalp for measuring. For the purpose of explanation,
let us just say that the dipoles emit a signal with varied amplitude, meaning
it can have any value including zero. Seen from the dipoles, the two emitted
signals form a combined measurable quantity through a linear combination; this
is a forward problem. The signals emitted from the dipoles are, however, not
directly observable but only determinable through noisy measurements on the
scalp, this is an inverse problem. Furthermore, the problem is ill-posed as there
exists an endless number of ways to combine the signals from the dipoles, that
will result in the same measured signal by the sensor. Thus, there does not exist
a unique solution to the inverse problem. See Figure 1.1 for illustration.
The linear inverse problem describing the relation between sources and sensors
has the following form
y = Ax + ε,
(1.1)
where y ∈RN is a matrix of N measurements, A ∈RN×M is the so-called
forward model ‘mapping’ from the dipole sources to electric potentials, x ∈RM
is the sources we wish to determine, and ε ∈RN is the noise in the system.
The ill-posed nature of the problem is visible through the forward model as
N << M.
In general, in order to be able to solve an ill-posed inverse problem, the problem
needs to be constrained.
This is usually done by regularizing, e.g.
such as
Tikhonov regularization [TA77], which leads to solutions with smaller norms
being more probable.
Although the number of dipole sources in the EEG model extends to the order of
103-104, the model of brain activity is spatially sparse [BML01; Del+12], mean-
ing that far from all possible dipole sources are active simultaneously. Essen-
tially, if we model the brain as thousands of dipole sources that all, potentially,
contribute to a signal measurable by a few sensors, the sparsity means that the
majority of the sources, x, will in fact be zero and not have contributed to the
measured signal y. If there are at most k ≤M non-zero entries in x, it is said
to be k-sparse. We denote the non-zero entries of x as the support.
The sparsity can be exploited as it has been shown that under some assumptions
of the forward matrix A and in a noiseless system, given that the source model
==PAGE:20==
4
Introduction
is suﬃciently sparse compared to the ratio between number of samples and
sources, the exact reconstruction of x is possible [CRT06; DT10].
The sparsity is a model assumption and it is not necessarily true that the non-
active sources have exactly zero contribution. The exploitation of the assumed
sparse solution will, however, still lead to a solution of the most active (ampli-
tude wise) sources while diminishing the role of the less active. It is also argued
that the sparse model is a suitable assumption in order to focus on the most
predominant brain activity [NS06]. Furthermore, it is a needed assumption if
we want any chance of solving the ill-posed linear inverse problem.
Returning to the simple “brain” problem of measuring a signal mixture from two
dipoles. Introducing the sparsity principle, such that only one dipole is active
at a given time, we can now infer that the measured signal was produced by one
of the dipoles and not a mixture, eﬀectively reducing the amount of possible
solutions to the set of solutions dependent on only one dipole.
Furthermore, the pattern of the sparsity is temporally smooth, meaning that
the support of the sparsity pattern changes slowly over time [BML01]. This
temporal smoothness of the support makes it natural to extend the problem
of EEG source localization to include multiple responses simultaneously. This
is also referred to as a multiple measurement vector problem (MMV) [Cot+05]
where multiple measurement vectors, or responses, of successive time steps t =
1, ..., T are available. The extended linear inverse problem for MMV is:
Y = AX + E,
(1.2)
where Y ∈RN×T now is a matrix of N measurements over T time steps, and
X ∈RM×T also represents a matrix of M sources over T time steps.
There are a variety of other ways to optimize the EEG measurement as to ease
the ill-posed linear inverse problem. Among these are combined EEG and MEG
[Bai+99; Mol+08] or just by using more sensors.
However, as this thesis is
an attempt to make a generalized and light-weight solution that can be imple-
mented on consumer electronic smart phones, those kind of improvements are
less relevant and will not be further examined.

Solving the linear inverse problem can be a computationally hard task, all de-
pending on the size of the model, with regards to number of sensors and number
of sources being modeled, and the number of responses considered simultane-
ously.

1.2
Literature review
The purpose of this section is to give an overview of the literature regarding
the linear inverse problem of EEG source localization. First, a short review of
methods for solving the single measurement vector (SMV) sparse linear inverse
problem is presented, followed by a short review of those the methods that
naturally extend to the multiple measurement vector (MMV) formulation.
Minimum-norm solutions to the inverse problem is in the category of optimiza-
tion / search methods. The minimum-norm methods have been shown to achieve
sparse solutions [MO95]. The minimum 2-norm is the ℓ2-norm that minimizes
∥x∥2 with the constraint y = Ax. Another approach is the counting function
∥· ∥0 which returns the number of non-zero elements in a vector x ∈RN and,
thus, provides a measure of the degree of sparsity. In order to achieve a sparse
solution, it seems reasonable to try to minimize ∥x∥0 with respect to a data
ﬁtting constraint such as a regularized least squares solution

minimum-norm solution, “recursively enhancing the values of some of the so-

Current solutions such as [Sto+14] implement low resolution electromagnetic
tomography (LORETA) method for source reconstruction. The general idea of
LORETA is to get the ‘smoothest’ distribution of active dipole sources (also
referred to as current density distribution), that can explain the measured data
from a model in which each dipole has three current moments [PML94; Pas+02].
This solution was shown by [Sto+14] to be possible to implement on a mobile
device for real-time source reconstruction including implementing an adaptive
model for the noise estimation.
A widely used approach to obtaining sparse solutions to the linear inverse prob-
lem is the Bayesian approach where prior distributions over the sources allow
the incorporation of sparsity assumptions. Several methods have been devel-
oped that rely on the framework of Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [Tip01;
WR04] which is by some referred to as Automatic Relevance Determination
(ARD) [Sat+04; Bis06; WN08]. We will investigate the method of ARD more
closely in the thesis, and will therefore not go into more detail now.
Many of the algorithms have naturally been extended to a MMV approach,
where the solutions to each measurement vector is assumed to have a shared
common support. M-FOCUSS [REC04; Cot+05] is the extension of the FO-
CUSS algorithm from a single measurement vector (SMV) problem to a MMV
problem. Similarly, the Bayesian approach of SBL is extended to M-SBL [WR07]
where the relative low frequent changes in the support are exploited through the
MMV approach. In this thesis, we wish to investigate those methods thoroughly
and evaluate the possibility of developing a fast and eﬃcient solution intended
for use in a real-time setting on a mobile device.
Many researches have been studying the boundaries for which it is possible to
solve the linear inverse problem, a guarantee for when it is or is not possible,
so to say. Further reading into this can be found in [EK12] but will not be
discussed further.

Chapter 2
Theory:
Electroencephalography
This chapter will give a brief introduction to the theory of electroencephalogra-
phy (EEG) and the issues that arise from performing source localization on the
measured signal.
2.1
EEG and source localization
EEG is the measurement of electrical potentials on the scalp of a human subject
where the electrical potential is mainly caused by currents in the cortex (neural
activity). The currents are very small and have to pass through several layers
of brain tissue, cerebrospinal ﬂuid, skull, and skin before it is measurable as
an electrical potential on the scalp. On top of that, the measurement is very
susceptible to noise and other electrical potentials e.g. created from movement
of facial muscles.
The EEG measurement does not disclose exact information about which parts of
the brain are active. Therefore, in order to determine from where the measured
signal originates, source localization is performed. Localizing the sources gives
insight into the neural activity and allow us to study the brain in further details.
==PAGE:26==
10
Theory: Electroencephalography
Superﬁcially explained, the brain consists of billions of neurons that produce a
very small electrical potential when active. The current produced by individual
neurons is so small it is beyond measurement in EEG. Therefore, we model
thousands or millions of neurons as a current source dipole where the neurons
have similar spatial orientation and ﬁre simultaneously [NS06]. The combined
electric potential is what is be measured by EEG. The source localization is
thus mapping the electric potential measured on the scalp to the source dipoles
which we model the brain as.
Performing source localization is not an easy task. It is done by solving the
severely ill-posed linear inverse problem of
y = Ax + ε,
(2.1)
where A ∈RN×M is the forward model mapping from the source dipoles to the
sensors. The forward relation is referred to as the head model.
The ill-posed nature of the problem is due to the relation between the amount
of current source dipoles in the brain and the number of sensors on the scalp.
In the common model of the brain, the number of sources dipoles is in the area
of 1.000-10.000, whereas the number of sensors usually is in the area 10-100
[BML01]. The number of sources is varied depending on the complexity of the
model and desired resolution while a high resolution requires a large amount
of sensors. In the mobile setup, where there are few sensors (around 20), the
model of the brain is simpliﬁed to get a smaller number of sources, around 1.000
or even less.

2.2 EEG head model imperfections
11
2.2
EEG head model imperfections
In order to better understand the challenges of the EEG source localization
problem, we must study the forward model which in the case of EEG is called
the head model. The head model is a model of how an electrical potential from
the current source dipoles pass through the conductive layers of the head until
reaching the scalp where non-intrusive sensors are able to measure the electrical
potential.
The very simplistic head model, consisting of a set of nested concentric homoge-
neous isotropic spheres, has been shown to have a closed-form solution [BML01].
However, the human head is in reality not at all like this and, thus, the solution
does not truly apply.
To create a precise head model it is required to know the exact shape and
thickness of the layers (e.g. brain tissue, skull etc.) as well as the conductive
properties of the diﬀerent layers. As this is both diﬃcult and not possible in
vitro, an imperfect and approximate head model is used. Furthermore, as all
heads are anatomically diﬀerent when it comes to shapes, thickness of layers,
and conductivity, it is obviously optimal to use a personalized head model for
each subject instead of one shared approximate model, as this thesis will be
dealing with.
Better and personalized head models can be created from high-resolution vol-
umetric brain images obtained by scanning the subjects head with magnetic
resonance (MR) or computed tomography (CT) imaging [BML01].
This is,
however, not a simple and easy process, and requires much more resources and
falls out of line with the simplistic approach of the mobile EEG solution.
Recently, interesting results have been shown in an attempt to infer a more
precise forward model for a given subject from the measured EEG signal and a
corpus of forward models [HHH16].

This section aims to derive a method for solving the ill-posed linear inverse
problem where no unique solution exists. We will start with the simple solution

Achieving sparsity
When iteratively updating the hyperparameters of αi, a large proportion of
them will go towards inﬁnity subsequently leading to the corresponding poste-
rior distributions of wi being sharply peaked around zero. Besides from being
irrelevant to the model, the large hyperparameters pose a computational prob-
lem when determining Σ = (A + βΦ⊺Φ)−1 due to A becoming ill-conditioned.
The hyperparameters, associated parameters, and basis functions can therefore
advantageously be pruned from the model when the corresponding αi becomes
suﬃciently large with respect to some arbitrary threshold (e.g. 106). The prun-
ing has the obvious and positive side eﬀect of speeding up the following itera-
tions, reducing the overall computation time.
3.3
Multiple response Automatic Relevance De-
termination
In the EEG signal, it is generally assumed that when an area of the brain is
activated, the area will remain active for a period of time. We say that the EEG
signal exhibits temporal smoothness, such that the indexes of active sources, i.e.
the support, do not change during this interval. Combining this with the sparsity
pattern, we assume that a number of responses share a common subset of active
sources. The amplitude of the signals from the active sources are not aﬀected
by this temporal smoothness.
This property makes it natural to extend the ARD algorithm to take multiple
responses into consideration simultaneously. Informally, this acts as a method

4.1
Experiment setup and methods
The general experiment setup is as follows:
A forward model A ∈RN×M comprised of i.i.d. random numbers drawn from
a normal distribution, N(0, 1), where M denotes the number of parameters /
basis functions and N denotes the number of samples. In the EEG context, the
number of samples is equivalent to the number of sensors measuring on the scalp
and the number of basis functions / parameters is equivalent to the number of
sources in the brain we are modeling. The dimensions of the forward model
are varied for the diﬀerent experiments in order to explore the properties of the
algorithms.
The number of model parameters w are varied for the diﬀerent experiments as
well as number of non-zero model parameters denoted by k. In general, all non-
zero parameters are drawn from a Gaussian distribution, N(w|0, α−1), where
α = 2 has been used.
The input signal x in the experiment is simply the parameters, such that the
sources are given by
x = w
(4.1)
The measured signal is then given by
y = Ax + ε,
(4.2)
where the noise ε is Gaussian noise on the form N(0, σ2), where the value
σ = 0.2. We can here also deﬁne noise precision hyperparameter β = 25.

4.2
Analysis of the evidence approximation
In section 3.1, we derived two versions of evidence approximation maximization;
one algorithm that assumes a shared prior for all parameters and another that
assumes separate priors for each the parameters. As the algorithms are derived
with the purpose of solving ill-posed linear inverse problems, it is interesting to

To evaluate this, experiments have been designed to explore the signiﬁcance of
the number of samples against the number of parameters being estimated, and
the number of non-zero parameters against the total number of parameters.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This thesis has taken a Bayesian inference approach to derive a solution to the
severely underdetermined linear inverse problem of EEG source localization.
The solution has been derived through three stages of assumptions.
The ﬁrst step is the introduction to Bayesian inference and the beneﬁts of this
over a simple maximum likelihood approach along with the derivation of the
evidence approximation maximization (EA) algorithm. The evidence approxi-
mation maximization leads to the most probable hyperparameters given the data
and the initial algorithm conditions. The hyperparameters govern the prior dis-
tribution of the parameters and the modeled noise distribution which together
with the data can be used to determine the most likely posterior distribution
of parameters. In the EEG source localization problem, those parameters rep-
resent the localized sources. The prior distribution of the parameters and the
noise are assumed to be Gaussian distributions.
The EA algorithm was shown to only perform well in cases with more samples
than parameters, and for a dense model.
Furthermore, it is very prone to
overﬁtting when the number of samples equal that of the parameters, although it
did show improvement over the simple maximum likelihood algorithm in regards
to this.
The second step introduces the principle of sparsity in the evidence approxima-
tion through a sparsity promoting prior and the Automatic Relevance Deter-
==PAGE:94==
78
Conclusion
mination (ARD) algorithm is derived. The sparsity promoting prior introduces
individual Gaussian prior distributions for each parameter. This leads to pa-
rameters with prior distributions sharply peaked around zero to be pruned from
the model. Essentially, the ARD algorithm is likely to ﬁnd sparse solutions.
This approach is driven by the model of the brain which assumes that some
areas of the brain will be signiﬁcantly more active than others.
It is demonstrated that the ARD algorithm ﬁnds sparse solutions to the sparse
linear inverse problem. It is also shown that the sparsity and the ability to
determine it relax the necessity of having more samples than model parameters
to some extent. Furthermore, it is shown that ARD experiences similar issues
as the overﬁtting of the EA algorithm, however, this happens when the number
of samples is small compared to the number of model parameters. This issue
results in a very underestimated noise variance.
The multiple response ARD (M-ARD) algorithm is derived by extending the
ARD algorithm to consider multiple responses simultaneously. This approach is
driven by another assumption from the model of the brain, namely the assump-
tion about relatively low frequent changes in the exhibited sparsity pattern, i.e.
common support across some number of responses.
It is shown that increasing the number of simultaneous responses leads to in-
creasingly better performance in terms of normalized mean square error of es-
timated parameters. This trend continues until some point where the gradual
improvement starts to recede. This is also shown to be true for rather noisy
systems. It is concluded that the extension of the ARD algorithm to consider
multiple responses simultaneously makes the algorithm able to achieve better
parameter estimates.
A drawback of the multiple responses is the noise variance estimate. The in-
creased number of responses was seen to amplify the issue making the noise
variance extremely underestimated, which in severe cases leads to a singular co-
variance matrix, thus making the parameter estimation impossible. This argues
the case that estimating the noise variance from training data alone should not
be done but instead estimated by other means. Through numerous experiments
it is shown that the M-ARD algorithm is very sensitive to the correctness of a
provided noise variance estimate. Emphasizing the importance of other ways of
determining this.
The limitations of the M-ARD algorithm are presented through numerous ex-
periments with very underdetermined problems that are made resemble real-life
EEG expectable dimensions. Using a setup with 22 measurements (sensors),
768 sources, 32 active sources with a sinusoidal input, and a Gaussian forward
model, experiments were performed for a various number of responses as well as
==PAGE:95==
5.1 Future work
79
noise levels. The experiments show M-ARD to achieve smaller and smaller er-
rors, measured as normalized mean square error, when the number of responses
is increased. Even in the noisy case. However, when measuring the ability to
recover the active sources, the results clearly show that M-ARD is not capable
of this for a problem of that size.
The M-ARD algorithm is compared to the MFOCUSS and T-MSBL algorithms
through the above mentioned experiments. All algorithms were tasked with the
same problem and compared against each other in terms of number of simultane-
ous responses, noise level, and time usage. The conclusion of these experiments
is that they all beneﬁt a great deal from an increased number of responses, and
they seemingly achieve similar results under the given circumstances. Regard-
ing computational complexity, the MFOCUSS is the fastest while M-ARD and
T-MSBL use in the same order of time.
In order to further demonstrate the limitations, all three algorithms are shown
to perform even worse when the above mentioned experiment is repeated with a
subsample of a real head model instead of the Gaussian forward model. In this
case, all of them achieve errors on par with assuming the entire signal is noise.
It is concluded that all algorithms, M-ARD being no exception, are very sensitive
to the provided noise variance estimate being correct.
Overall, the M-ARD has shown interesting results in terms of recovery per-
formance and relatively low computational complexity.
It is, however, very
sensitive to initial conditions and small variations of the problem variables. It is
therefore deemed necessary to perform more work on this topic before anything
absolute can be concluded.
5.1
Future work
The thesis is concluded with a list of work that would be interesting to carry
out in the future:
• The M-ARD algorithm, achieved poor results when given the task of solv-
ing the EEG source localization problem with a subsample of a real head
model. It would be interesting to obtain a proper simpliﬁed head model
based on actual basis function centers.
• Following the experiments with a properly simpliﬁed head model, it would
be interesting to measure the localization error as it is speculated that

• As mentioned throughout the thesis, the intended use is a real-time im-
