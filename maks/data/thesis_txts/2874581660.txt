Abstract 
This project evaluates a siting method for Battery Energy Storage System installations and 
analyzes the economic feasibility of implementing them in existing Wind Farms in the Electric 
Reliability Council of Texas (ERCOT) wholesale power market. To do this, we gathered Real 
Time and Day-Ahead Settlement Point prices in 2020 and 2021 and ran statistical calculations 
to determine which active wind farms in ERCOT have the most volatile prices. We then conduct 
economic assessments to determine if installing Battery Energy Storage Systems would be 
economically feasible at these wind farms. We then present the results of the economic 
assessments that show which analysis methods and battery sizes provide the best economic 
results.

==PAGE:15==

As stated from [5], which gives the best description of the US wholesale power markets: 
 
“The goal of wholesale electricity markets is to produce a least-cost economic dispatch of 
generation resources that meets demand and ensures system reliability. Energy prices are 
determined by the balance between supply and demand based on bids and offers submitted by 
market participants. 
 
ISOs/RTOs within the United States use locational marginal pricing (LMP) to price energy 
transactions at each network node. LMP includes the marginal cost of energy plus the cost of 
transmission congestion and losses. ISOs/RTOs use LMPs as a market-based mechanism to 
price the efficient use of transmission infrastructure in cases where congestion prevents the 
least-cost dispatch from reaching the nodes where power is needed. Security-constrained unit 
commitment and security-constrained economic dispatch are unit commitment and economic 
dispatch algorithms used to calculate LMPs while taking generation and transmission limitations 
into account. 
 
ISOs/RTOs in the United States run day-ahead and real-time markets. These two terms refer to 
the time at which the buy-and-sell transaction is made relative to the delivery of energy. Most of 
the energy volume is traded in day-ahead markets, where market participants submit bids and 
offers for electric energy delivered on the next day. In real-time markets, the volume of energy 
traded is the difference between energy commitments cleared in day-ahead markets and the 
energy actually needed in the system to meet demand. Real time prices are based on actual 
operating conditions and costs (Hausman et al. 2006). In real-time markets, LMPs are 
calculated in 5-minute intervals or more frequently if the network is experiencing extraordinary 
conditions (Price, n.d.). In day-ahead markets, the commitment and dispatch interval is typically 
one hour. Day-ahead markets and other longer-term procurement options help participants 
hedge against the volatility of real-time markets (Turner 2016; Hausman et al. 2006). Longer-
term price certainty helps generation plant developers secure long-term financing (Hogan 
1993).”

2.2.3 Hubs 
 
The last locations where SPPs are calculated in of ERCOT are in Hubs. These are geographic 
groupings of electrical substations that encompass the 345 kV ERCOT transmission system. 
These 345 kV transmission lines are the long-haul lines that transmit power from the primary 
generation areas to the load centers, thus essentially encompassing the pricing point between 
the generation and load centers in ERCOT. There are five regional Hubs in ERCOT: North, 
South, West, Houston, and the Panhandle Hub and two average Hubs: ERCOT Hub Average 
and ERCOT Bus Average.

2.2.4 LMPs 
 
LMPs are the building blocks to calculating SPPs at Resource Nodes, Load Zones, and Hubs in 
both the Day-Ahead Market (DAM) and Real-Time Market (RTM). LMPs represent the marginal 
cost to serve the next increment of load at an electrical bus. These electrical busses could be 
load substations, where electricity is stepped down from transmission voltage to lower voltage 
distribute power locally to business or neighborhoods, or it could be at a generator substation, 
where power is stepped up to connect to the grid transmission system.  
 
LMPs reflect the costs to deliver energy to specific locations in ERCOT and due to their 
geographic nature, they also reflect the overall impacts of transmission congestion [13]. Figure 
7 below depicts the LMPs for all resource nodes, load zones, and hubs in the RTM1. Here, we 
can see LMP prices vary from $-62.70/MWh, depicted in the blue areas in southern region of 
ERCOT, all the way up to $142.92/MWh, depicted by the red areas in the south-central region.

2.2.5 SPP’s at Resource Nodes, Load Zones, & Hubs in DAM & RT 
 
Since DAM offers and bids are transacted hourly, the corresponding SPPs are needed for each 
hour for those transactions. The SPP for each Resource Node is the LMP at the Resource 
Node’s electrical bus for that hour. For Load Zones, since they are geographic groupings for 
load, the SPP is the load-weighted average of LMPs within each Load Zone. Finally, for Hubs, 
the SPP is the simple average of the LMPs at the 345 kV hub busses defined for that hub. 
Figure 8 shows the DAM LMP map of for the operating day of June 13th, 2022 at 19:00, as of 
June 12th at 12:28 in the DAM.

In the RTM, SPPs are calculated by averaging 5-minute LMPs over a 15-minute period and 
include an On-Line Reserve Price Adder. This price adder represents the additional value of 
having reserves available for dispatch under constrained conditions. The RTM SPP for a 
Resource Node is the 15-minute average of the LMPs at the Resource Node bus plus the On-
Line Reserve Price. Since Load Zones and Hubs are regional and include multiple busses that 
encompass them, the RTM SPP is the sum of 15-minute average for each bus within them plus 
the On-Line Reserve Price Adder. Figure 9 shows the RTM SPP map as of 21:47 on June 12th,

2.3 ERCOT DAM & RTM Overview 
 
Due to the complex nature of the DAM and RTM operations in ERCOT, we will only cover the 
basic overview of the operations in each market.  
 
The DAM is a co-optimized market where energy, Ancillary Services (AS), and congestion 
financial transactions are made to procure the energy needed for each hour the next operating 
day. These transactions include offers and bids for energy, ancillary services, or both, and are 
made by Qualified Scheduling Entities (QSEs), who represent both generation resources and 
Load Servicing Entities (LSEs). Each transaction has to be reviewed and cleared by ERCOT. 
Most of the energy and AS are procured in the DAM as a hedge against volatility in the RTM 
due to congestion constraints [6-7]. 
 
The RTM is operated in a much shorter time scale and is designed to ensure the grid is 
operating reliably to balance generation, load, and congestion. RTM operations initiate during 
the adjustment period, which is immediately after the DAM close and up to one hour before the 
next operating hour. Here, AS are dispatched and QSE’s can submit and update energy offers 
and bids, which ERCOT reviews and clears based on economics and reliability needs [6,8].

3.2 Day-Ahead & Real Time Settlement Point Prices 
After developing the framework of Resource Nodes with their corresponding Load Zones and 
generation types, we could then use this to link to the DAM and RTM SPPs in ERCOT. The 
DAM and RTM SPP data is posted on ERCOT’s website, however, there is only 30 days’ worth 
of data provided locally. To get the two-years of DAM and RTM SPP data needed for our 
analysis, a formal data request had to be made through the ERCOT website, where we 
requested the DAM and RTM SPP data for the years 2020 and 2021 and Quarter 1 of 2022 
[17]. This data was provided in a cloud-based share folder over a two-week period and 
contained around 4 GB of data.  
 
The initial challenge we faced was the file structure of the data. For the DAM SPP data 
provided, there was one zip folder for each day and within each zip folder, there was a single 
csv and html document. In these documents, there was 24 hours of DAM SPP for each 
Resource Node, Load Zone, & Hub for each hour of that day, which resulted in 365 zip files for 
each year.  
 
For the RTM SPP data, since RT SPPs are made every 15 minutes, as opposed to hourly for 
DAM SPPs, each zip file contained the 15-minute SPPs for each Resource Node, Load Zone, & 
Hub. This means that there are 96 individual zip files, each with one csv and one html file, for 
one full day of pricing data and equates to 35,040 zip files for one full year.  
==PAGE:25==
 
15 
 
It would have been unfeasible to extract this data manually. To extract this data, a python code 
had to be written to read through each zip folder, extract the csv data from it, and compile that

5. Case Simulations & Analysis 
Now that we have determined the most volatile wind resource nodes in ERCOT in our 
assessment period, we will now evaluate if adding BESS to them would be profitable or not. To 
make this analysis, we use the Evaluation Methodologies in Section 5.1 to run case studies for 
each Resource Node, which are provided in Sections 5.2 & 5.3.  
 
5.1 Evaluation Methodology  
 
5.1.1 CorRES 
 
Since there is no public information available that would provide the wind farms actual 
operational data of the power generation and curtailment, we would need to estimate the 
generation output of each for our BESS optimization modeling and economic analysis. To do 
so, we used the CorRES software to generate an hourly timeseries of the power output during 
our two-year analysis period at each wind farm.  
 
CorRES is a VRE simulation tool, developed by the Department of Wind Energy at the 
Technical University of Denmark, that uses both meteorological reanalysis data and stochastic 
simulations to generate a VRE power generation time series or forecast at any specific location. 
The meteorological reanalysis data is derived from the Weather Research and Forecasting 
(WRF) model, a mesoscale model that is designed for atmospheric research and weather 
forecasting purposes, that generates an hourly meteorological time series from a 10 km x 10 km 
grid encompassing the area of interest.  
 
The stochastic simulations are composed of fluctuation simulations and forecast error 
simulations. These simulations are utilized to compensate for the underestimated sub-hourly 
variability in wind speeds from the WRF model and provide a more representative time-series 
when the WRF data is adjusted with these simulations [18,19].  
 
The inputs needed for a CorRES simulation are the wind farms coordinates, the total nominal 
installed capacity, and the turbine power curve. With these inputs, CorRES then generates an 
hourly timeseries of the available power generation over the specified timeframe. 
 
 
5.1.2 HPP Optimization Model 
 
The HPP optimization model used to calculate the revenue of the HPP used in our research 
was developed by Rujie Rhu, a PHD student at DTU and one of my co-supervisors in this 
project. The optimization model is python-based and its objective function is to maximize the 
HPP revenue from the forecasted VRE production and market price in the DAM, which the 
model uses to generate the optimal charge and discharge schedule of the BESS within the 
specified operational constraints of the HPP via the simplex solver of IBM ILOG CPLEX 
Optimization Studio [20].

Using the CorRES data and market price data for each WPP, we used the HPP Optimization 
Model to run case studies to assess the economics of adding different BESS capacities and 
durations to the WPP. We use two different evaluation methods in each case study: the Day-
Ahead Schedule Method and Perfect Foresight Method.  
 
In the Day-Ahead Schedule Method, we upload the WPPs hourly DAM SPP timeseries and the 
hourly CorRES WPP generation timeseries in the model and set the BESS and WPP capacities 
and BESS parameters before running the model, which determines the BESS’s hourly charge & 
discharge schedule for each day over the two-year analysis period. This method is the more 
conservative of the two as the BESS wouldn’t be participating in the RTM, where SPPs are 
more volatile, which would theoretically correspond to higher energy arbitrage revenue that the 
BESS could realize.   
 
For the Perfect Foresight Method, we average the 15-minute SPPs in the RTM for each WPP 
into an hourly timeseries so it can be modeled with the hourly CorRES timeseries data. Using 
the hourly RTM data in the model is the only difference between these two methods as the input 
parameters and model operations are the same to determine the BESS charge and discharge 
schedule for each operational day in the analysis period. Even though this method follows the 
same modelling steps as the Day-Ahead Schedule Method, fundamentally, this method 
assumes the BESS charge & discharge schedule has “Perfect Foresight” of the next operating 
day’s SPPs.  While this method is used in some literature, as it depicts the maximum possible 
revenue that could be realized by the BESS, it is likely not fully achievable in practice.  
 
One of the primary features of the optimization model is that the BESS charge and discharge 
schedule can be modified by adjusting the Optimal Marginal Degradation Cost variable. While 
the BESS charge and discharge schedule is mainly influenced by the SPP prices and the WPP 
generation for that operating day, having this feature is important to limit the BESS daily cycling 
rate. The Optimal Marginal Degradation Cost variable is set as a percentage of the BESS 
capital cost and its importance in the modeling is that in our profitability analysis assumptions, 
we’re assuming a fixed annual OPEX rate of 2.5% of the CAPEX cost per year. This OPEX rate 
assumes that the BESS would be limited to one cycle per day, as this is a typical value for 
warranty guarantees and for project lifecycle assumptions [22]. Therefore, in order for our 
profitability analysis assessments to be aligned with this assumption, we would have to adjust 
the Optimal Marginal Degradation Cost variable in each case study scenario target the BESS 
average daily cycling rate to once per day.  
 
As an additional note for these case studies, the BESS is only charging from the WPP (i.e., non-
grid-charging) and we are not modeling additional revenue from AS as we did not have enough 
time to assess that in this study.

capacities in the 1 hr & 2 hr durations have the highest NPV. Therefore, we can conclude that in 
this study, a BESS sized at 25-50% of the WPP nameplate capacity with a 1-2hr duration would 
likely be the most profitable option.

Key Takeaways: 
• 
Finding the resource nodes with the highest SPP standard deviation could be a key 
driver for siting, or at least screening, potential BESS projects, as the existing BESS 
generators in ERCOT have the highest SPP standard deviation of all the other power 
generator types  
• 
Lower BESS capacities and durations (25% of WPP capacity and 1-2 hr durations) 
provide more promising economic results than higher capacity and durations BESS 
sizes  
• 
CAPEX costs are a key driver of economic assessments. If BESS CAPEX trends 
continue to fall as indicated by projections, project economics will continue to improve  
• 
Future studies will need to be conducted to assess the value of BESS in the AS market, 
as that would improve the project economics 
• 
Future studies would need to be conducted to determine how applicable the Perfect 
Foresight Method is in practical applications in the RTM and what percentage of the 
projected revenue is realized
