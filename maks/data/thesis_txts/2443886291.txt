Abstract
Copepods are a diverse group of multicellular aquatic organisms. A cardinal characteris-
tic that differentiates between species is the maximum size of adults. This has a profound
effect on their demography and life history traits, such as growth and reproduction.
To explore the demography of copepods, size-based McKendrick-von Foerster equa-
tion was implemented. Parameters were extracted from data found in various literature
sources, that describe certain physiological aspects of these organisms as a function of
their size. These were further on incorporated in McKendrick-von Foerster differential
equation to model their population growth rate and size distribution. A comparison
was made with larger marine animals such as fish and sharks to see whether there are
fundamental differences among these organisms and can the differences be attributed to
the adult size.
Another aspect of this work was to construct a resource-consumer model to explore
the level of interactions and dependence between different life stages of copepods and
algae species they feed on.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Copepods are small crustaceans inhabiting many aquatic ecosystems, predominantly
marine environments. They are an important link between primary producers and higher
trophic levels since many larvae and juvenile fish species are feeding predominantly on
copepods. They are a major control of the growth of phytoplankton population and
they contribute to the nutrient cycle by excreting nitrogen and phosphorus compounds,
thus increasing the growth of primary producers. (Harris, 2001)
The main trait that differentiates between copepod species is the size of the adults.
Another important aspect of their demography is the size distribution. These are closely
related to their growth, reproduction and adult to egg ratio. The aim of this work is to
investigate how the demography of copepods varies primarily based on the maximum
size of the population. Further on I am going to compare the demography of copepods to
other organisms, such as fish and sharks to evaluate, if there are fundamental differences
between them or can these be attributed to the differences in the adult size.
The main question of interest throughout this work is:
What is the demography of copepods and how does it compare to the demography of fish
and sharks?
To answer the question I am going to apply a size-structured model, the McKendrick-
von Foerster differential equation, which shows the size distribution of a population. To
do this, it is important to derive various parameters from available data, that describe
different aspects of copepods. These will include growth rate, reproduction rate, mor-
tality and the adult to egg ratio and parameters related to their feeding.
In addition, I will explore the interactions between a copepod population and their
environment, more precisely, how their survival success is dependant on the availability
of resources they consume.
This will be done by finding a numerical solution to a
system of differential equations by integrating McKendrick-von Foerster equation and
an equation modelling dynamics of algae, that is the food source for a copepod species.

3.1
Copepods
Figure 3.1: Megacyclops viridis (Cyclopoid copepod).
Copepods are aquatic organisms predominately abundant in marine environment. They
belong to the phylum Crustaceans. The name Copepod is derived from the Greek words
kope – oar and podos – foot, which refer to flat paddle like swimming legs of these
animals. (Mauchline, 1998)
In an article written by Humes, 1994 he estimated that there are 11 500 known
copepod species accounting for 15 % of the total number of species of copepods.
These animals inhabit many different environments, from tropical to polar regions
and they live in various depths ranging from intertidal zones to deep sea. Copepods
are a major link between primary producers and higher trophic levels. By grazing, they
control the phytoplankton production, being also a major food source to earlier stages
of fish, thus contributing significantly to the marine ecosystem as well as commercially
exploited fish stocks. (Harris, 2001)
Most copepod species are microscopic. Some planktonic cyclopoids are only 0.2mm
in length, while some calanoid copepods, such as Valdiviella can be more than 20mm
long. Parasitic copepod species are generally larger. A parasite belonging to the genus
Penella, commonly living on fish and whales, can be as long as 30 cm. (Harris, 2001)
Copepods usually have a segmented cylindrical body shape. A common characteristic
is at least two pairs of swimming legs at some stage in their lifetime, as well as segmented
==PAGE:14==

antennules and a head that contains a median naupliar eye. Most of the copepod species
are transparent, but some species can have blue, red, orange or black pigments. (Harris,
2001)
Throughout their lives, copepods go through various stages. The earlier stages –
nauplii – are characterised by having fewer appendages. In calanoid copepods there are
six naupliar stages. Copepods advance throughout stages by shedding their exoskeleton.
After the sixth stage, the copepods go through a metamorphosis and into the first out
of 5 copepodite stages, throughout which they develop adult-like characteristics. In the
final stage, they become adults are ready to produce offsprings. (Harris, 2001)

adulthood (Raisuddin, Kwok, Leung, Schlenk, and Lee, 2007).
The majority of copepod species spread their eggs directly in the water, however,
pacticoids, cyclopoids and some calanoids tend to carry their eggs in sacs. Females in
food saturated conditions can produce more than 100 eggs in a day. Egg size ranges from
0.2 to 0.6 mm, with sac spawner eggs being generally relatively larger in size. In the
tropical and subtropic regions the breeding of copepods are not restricted to seasonality,
thus the breeding happens continuously throughout the year in most species. In areas
with high seasonality, offsprings are produced annually. (Harris, 2001)

Among different groups of fish, teleosts are the most abundant in terms of biomass
and number of species. The second most abundant group of fish is cartilaginous fishes,
having two subgroups - Holocephali and the more dominant - Elasmobranchii, which
includes sharks, skates, rays and sawfish.
Generally, sharks are predators that feed mainly on fish, though the largest sharks
feed on zooplankton, similarly as teleosts from the smaller end of the spectrum. The
maximum size differs, with the smallest adult elasmobranch weighing 100 grams, but
the largest one can grow over 10 tons.
A major difference between teleosts and elasmobranchs is the adult to offspring size
strategy. Teleosts tend to have small offsprings, despite their adult size. In contrast for
sharks, similarly as for copepods, offspring size scale with the size of adults. The ratio
between the maximum size and offspring size for elasmobranchs is approximately 227
(K. H. Andersen, 2019). A simple lifetime reproductive output model can be used to rep-
resent the fitness of a population. The lifetime reproductive output is maximised when
the offspring size is as small as possible. Even though the survival is lower for smaller

contrast to having larger ones. This strategy is adopted by fish, who generally make off-
springs weighing only 1 milligram. This argument, however, does not take into account
survival affected by density dependence in early life stages. When density dependence
is added to the lifetime reproductive output model, the strategy to produce offsprings

predator is around 1cm in size, which is the size of a fish larvae. Organisms that are
smaller are blind and rely on tactile sensing to catch prey. If fish larvae would be smaller,
they would have to adapt another sensory organ to catch prey, otherwise, they would
be outcompeted by tactile sensing organisms. Another reason is that fish larvae feed by
using suction, but this is not effective for fish larvae that are smaller than 1 cm, due
to scaling of the hydromechanics at low Reynolds numbers China and Holzman, 2014.
Therefore it is not feasible for fish to make smaller offsprings.

3.3
Size spectrum
A notable pattern in nature was described by R.W. Sheldon in 1972 (Sheldon, Prakash,
and Sutcliffe, 1972) stating that if all organisms in an ecosystem are partitioned in
logarithmically distributed size intervals, the total biomass in each interval is roughly
equal. He observed this in microorganisms that the biomass was independent of the size
of an organism and soon he realized that this regularity emerges also in larger animals
such as fish, and even extends to whales. It is known now as the Sheldon spectrum,
named after its discoverer.
This consistency emerges only when the size of an organism is considered and not
the specie it belongs to. Even though the species that are smaller in size will generally
be more in numbers, there are also rare species in all size groups. Because of this, the
Sheldon spectrum would not be as obvious. Sheldon could observe this regularity by
noting the number of organisms with a given size and not the species name, otherwise,
he most likely would have not gotten to this conclusion.
The Sheldon spectrum is represented as histogram with logarithmically increasing
size intervals. For example, the smallest interval could contain all the organisms that
are in the size range from 0 to 2 grams, the next one consists of organisms having the
size in the range from 2 to 4 grams, the following one from 4 to 8 grams and so on. As
a result, the total biomass in each of the size groups will be constant.

The McKendrick-von Foerster equation is a first order partial differential equation. The
purpose of it is to analyse age and time-based structure dynamics. The equation repre-
sents the distribution of individuals within a population based on their age at a certain
time point. Here, however, I will be replacing age with weight. Similarly, as age is
increasing along with time, the same can be said for the weight until a certain limit.

3.5
Growth rate
Now that an introduction to the McKendrick-von Foerster equation has been given, I
will describe in more detail the mortality and growth equations.
In biology, many physiological changes in organisms are related to their body size.
Julian Huxley, a British biologist, described in his work (Huxley, 1932) the size-related
variations using the allocation equation, which is in the form:
Y = Y0mb
where Y is some variable that is dependent on the body mass m. Examples include
growth rate, metabolic and reproduction rates. In the equation Y0 is a normalization
coefficient and b represents a scaling exponent (Brown, 2004). Brown, Gillooly, Allen,
Savage, and West, 2004
The growth rate is designated to show how effectively an organism allocates the
acquired energy to somatic growth (measured as mass per time). Growth rate is based
on a biphasic growth model, which means that the organisms, while being in their
development stages, are using the acquired energy for somatic growth but as they reach
an age of reproduction, the available energy is allocated for producing offsprings.

3.9
Consumer-resource model
When comparing individuals within a community, one of the apparent distinction is
their size. The way individuals interact with the surrounding environment might differ
depending on their life phase. Certain processes such as energy intake and mortality
depend upon the size of an organism. Moreover, the dynamics of the whole population
are a cumulative result of the actions of individuals. Physiologically structured popu-
lation models (PSPMs) have been designed to reflect the population dynamics based
on differences in individual physiological traits. The model described in this chapter is
constructed to reflect the resource and consumer interactions, where the consumer is
a copepod population, but the resource is algae Chlamydomonas rheinhardii they feed
from. By grazing the consumer is reducing the amount of the resource and in such way
influencing its own future. (de Roos, 1997, Roos and Persson, 2001)
The structure of the model is based on the daphnia model by Roos and Persson,
2001, but adapted to be weight dependent instead of length. Another adjustment here
is that copepods, unlike daphnia, stop growing once the adulthood has been reached.

CHAPTER 4
Materials and Methods
In order to successfully describe in mathematical terms the copepod demography, I have
derived various parameters that can be implemented in the following models. To do this,
the first step is to gather literature sources to find the necessary data. Further on, I
have applied different strategies, depending on the case, to get the parameter values.
The parameters of interest include growth coefficient, reproduction and recruitment effi-
ciencies, mortality rate and adult to offspring ratio. In this chapter I have described the
methods for finding the parameters, so they could be used in modelling the population
growth and size distribution of copepods. In addition, I have derived from data the
factor for maximum ingestion rate and clearance rate in order to implement these in a
model describing copepod and algae Chlamydomonas rheinhardii interactions.
To obtain the growth coefficient and reproduction efficiency, I have plotted the data
points extracted from literature sources for copepod growth rate and reproduction rate
as a function of body weight. Then I have fitted a regression line using the least squares
approach by setting the value of the slope to be equal to the scaling exponent n. By
doing this, the parameter of interest can be derived from the fit, this will become more
apparent later on. In the same manner I have derived the adult to offspring ratio and
factors for maximum ingestion rate and clearance rate.
To acquire the physiological mortality, a more analytical approach is used, since it
is fairly difficult for practical reasons to measure the mortality of marine organisms. As
a result, the mortality in many cases is estimated indirectly. Finally, the recruitment
efficiency was derived from copepod mortality and egg hatching times.
All the results have been compiled in the table 4.1 at the end of this chapter.

4.3
Recruitment efficiency εR
Recruitment efficiency shows the fraction of eggs that have survived for long enough to
hatch. It can be derived if the egg mortality and hatching times are known. According to
the paper by Kiørboe and Sabatini, 1994 the average hatching time t is 1 and 3 days for
free-spawning calanoid and egg-carrying cyclopoid copepods respectively. The average
post-hatching mortality µ is 1 per day for calanoid copepod eggs and 0.1 per day for
the cyclopoid copepods. By averaging these values, the egg hatching time is 2 days and
the mortality is 0.55 per day. To calculate the recruitment efficiency these numbers can
be inserted in the equation for survival e−µt, and by doing this the final value becomes
0.33.
4.4
Physiological mortality µ
In order to derive the physiological mortality, it is important to define the mortality
rate. The explanation for the mortality rate and the derived parameters is based on the
book by K. H. Andersen, 2019. Since in nature the main cause of death generally is
predation by other animals, the mortality here is defined based on predator-prey inter-
actions in a marine size spectrum. It incorporates a parameter describing the predator
size preference of prey Φ, the expected feeding level f0 and h, which is the coefficient for
maximum consumption rate Cmax = hm3/4. The maximum consumption rate describes
the maximum amount of food that an organism is able to consume in a certain time
period, whereas the consumption rate is the amount of food an organism actually con-
sumes, because the availability of resources is generally limited. The expected feeding
level f0 is a factor for Cmax. The value of f0 is between 0 and 1, it should be higher
than standard metabolism in order for the organism to be able to sustain itself, but less
than 1, since in nature it rarely happens so that an organism has unlimited amount of
available food. Therefor, a reasonable chioce for f0 is 0.6 (K. H. Andersen, 2019). Φ is
a constant that describes the prey size preference and is roughly equal to 0.17 (K. H.
Andersen, 2019). The mortality rate here is defined as:

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
I have explored the size based demographic models of copepods and how these compare
with other organisms such as sharks and fish. I would like to summarise the key insights
in this work.
When considering individual growth, the smaller is the organism, the faster its mass
will increase relative to the initial size, thus the copepods will reach adulthood faster
than sharks and fish.
The time dependant number spectrum is decreasing with a slightly lower rate for
copepods than for fish and sharks. This is due to a higher ratio between the population
growth rate and growth coefficient for copepods.
When the number spectrum for a depleted population is compared with a steady
state, the spectrum of a growing population will be declining faster, since in a growing
population the imbalance between the adults and juveniles is higher.
A notable difference among sharks, fish and copepods is their strategy for offspring
size. Copepod and shark offspring weight is proportional to the adult size, in contrast,
fish tend to produce offspring weighing around 1mg, despite the adult size. A reason
for this might be that for a simple lifetime reproduction model an optimal strategy is to
produce small offspring. This applies, however, if there is no density-dependence, which
is the case for fish at earlier life stages. When the density dependence is considered in
the model, it is more beneficial to produce offspring proportional to adult size, as can be
observed with shark species. For copepods, a different explanation might apply. Preda-
tors have a size preference when it comes to prey. Due to cannibalism among copepods,
it is beneficial to produce offspring larger than the preferred size of prey. Interestingly,
copepods and sharks employ the same offspring size strategy, despite differing greatly
in size, but possibly for different evolutionary reasons.
For copepods and sharks, the highest population growth rates will be for the species
with the maximum size in the smaller end of the range. Fish, however, will have the
optimum rate neither in the smaller nor higher end of the spectrum, but rather in the
middle.
Overall, copepods among all three groups of organisms will have the highest popula-
tion growth rates. This can be seen in the equation 3.13, that a population of smaller
species will have a higher growth rate.
Variations in parameters can drastically affect a population. When the value of phys-
iological mortality goes above a certain threshold or below a threshold for reproduction
efficiency, the copepod population may eventually become extinct.
The consumer-resource model has shown the level of dependency of the copepod
population from the resource they feed on. With the carrying capacity of algae initially
sufficient enough, the population can grow in numbers until the resource is depleted,
thus reducing the population density until a point, where the resource is able to regrow.
==PAGE:50==
42
6 Conclusion
This pattern can persist indefinitely. When the carrying capacity becomes lower, the
population goes to an equilibrium, where it neither dies out nor is able to produce high
numbers of individuals. Copepods face more tragic fates, when the food source is limited,
causing the earlier stages to die out first, followed by the rest.
Despite the fact that individuals with a higher mass are more robust, smaller species
as a whole community are able to recover more quickly after being in a depleted state.
Copepods are a vital food source to other animals and contribute significantly to the
food chain. If we are able to recognize damage in a smaller population, like copepods,
mathematical modelling can become a great tool to understand the possible implications
and how these can extend to higher trophic levels. This way, we can use these insights
to recognize issues beforehand and provide solutions to protect aquatic environments.
