Abstract
This research develops a greenfield optimization model to determine the most
cost-effective mix of offshore and onshore wind, fixed-tilt and tracker PV, battery en-
ergy storage systems (BESS), and Power-to-X (PtX) technologies required to achieve
a fully renewable power system in Denmark. The analysis is conducted from a soci-
etal perspective and explicitly considers the transmission grids of both DK1 (Western
Denmark) and DK2 (Eastern Denmark). The model explores scenarios using 2024
weather and load data as a baseline and projections to 2030, assessing optimal tech-
nology portfolios, geographic siting, and capacity requirements to advance energy
self-sufficiency. It further evaluates the integration of 2024 levels of electric vehicles
under a vehicle-to-grid (V2G) scheme, as well as the influence of PtX on renewable
energy valuation, and its impact on system costs and the societal cost of carbon
(SCC).
The results indicate that achieving the final 3% of self-sufficiency imposes dis-
proportionately high system costs, primarily due to Dunkelflaute—extended peri-
ods of low wind and solar generation. Under idealized copper-plate conditions, the
model finds clear benefits in geographically dispersing generation capacity, which
smooths the overall production profile. However, introducing realistic transmission
constraints substantially reduces the ability to capture these benefits. Tracker PV
deployment is found to be limited, occurring mainly in niche cases within highly
flexible systems, where it can support the baseload operation of PtX facilities. The
analysis shows that if 2024 levels of EVs had participated in V2G schemes, the
potential system value could reach 2 Bn EUR, enabling owners to recover up to
22% of the vehicle’s purchase cost through flexibility services. PtX is found to sig-
nificantly improve the SCC, increasing the effectiveness of decarbonisation under
the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), while also creating additional value and
mitigating greenhouse gas emissions through the production and sale of e-jet fuel.
The study further finds that insufficient transmission capacity increases total
system costs by 10–20 Bn EUR due to additional capacity requirements, with the
societal cost of achieving a 97% self-sufficient system ranging from 500 M EUR
to 1.4 Bn EUR. High PtX penetration mitigates these costs, placing them at the
lower end of the range and enhancing the market value of renewables. Overall, the
findings underscore the complex interplay between renewable generation, storage,
flexible demand, and grid infrastructure, highlighting the need for coordinated policy
measures—including sufficiently high ETS pricing—to achieve a cost-effective and
reliable renewable energy transition. Future work should incorporate more detailed
grid data, multiple weather years, and explicit market dynamics to enhance realism.

1. Introduction
Since the adoption of the Paris Agreement at COP21 in 2015, global efforts to
mitigate climate change and limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial
levels have intensified. Achieving this target by 2050 demands informed decision-
making regarding the scale, location, and timing of renewable energy deployment.
A key challenge is to determine the renewable capacity required for a net-zero power
system in a socio-economically efficient manner - minimizing both total system costs
and carbon emissions while maintaining reliability.
This requires determining the optimal total generation capacity, selecting the most
suitable mix of generator types, and combining them to ensure availability, cost-
effectiveness, and low carbon emissions. To date, temporal dynamics have been ex-
amined primarily from the demand-side perspective. Take Denmark as an example:
wind resources are strongest along the west coast of Jutland, and solar irradiance
peaks on Lolland and western Zealand. While it may seem logical to concentrate
wind and solar installations in these areas, doing so risks excessive simultaneous
production, surpassing demand or transmission capacity.
A geo-temporal approach to capacity planning might mitigate such effects by lever-
aging correlational and anti-correlational weather patterns to induce beneficial off-
sets in peak production, driven by the temporal progression of weather systems. By
distributing wind generation from west to east, staggered compared to stacked peaks
might be obtainable, leading to a more stable output profile. Similarly, dispersing
solar installations reduces the impact of localized cloud cover, and broader spa-
tial planning enables better alignment between generation patterns and electricity
demand. Furthermore, technology selection influences cost outcomes and requires
evaluation across a broad set of options, including combinations with energy stor-
age, to determine the most effective configuration. To support this process, models
such as Balmorel, have been widely used to optimize energy system investments
and dispatch. Balmorel, both a Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) and
Linear Programming (LP) model, effectively simulates the European energy mar-
ket but primarily adopts a system-wide perspective, accounting for large-scale zonal
connections but assumes regional copper plate modeling.

1.1. Objective
Hence, this research aims to investigate and expand the understanding of geo-
temporal anticorrelatory effects within the context of Denmark’s transmission grid,
using the DK1 (Western Denmark) and DK2 (Eastern Denmark) zones as a case
study. Building on this, the study develops a greenfield optimization model that ne-
glects existing capacities to determine the most cost-effective mix of wind (onshore
and offshore), solar (fixed-tilt and tracker photovoltaics), and battery energy storage
systems (BESS) required for a fully renewable Danish power system in a hypothetical
2024 using historical weather and load data, assessed from a societal perspective. By
moving beyond copper plate assumptions and explicitly incorporating grid topology,
transmission constraints, localized weather patterns, and demand distribution, the
model identifies the optimal geographical deployment of these technologies and eval-
uates how spatial diversification mitigates variability, curtails losses, and enhances
system reliability.
Special emphasis is placed on exploring the interactions between geographically dis-
persed wind and photovoltaic (PV) assets, BESS and electric vehicles (EVs) as
flexible demand and distributed reserves, and Power-to-X (PtX) technologies, to
assess their combined impact on system cost efficiency, renewable energy valuation,
and societal carbon costs (SSCC) both for the 2024 scenario and projected 2030 sce-
narios. This high-resolution, localized approach aims to address gaps in the existing
literature and provide actionable insights for policymakers, system planners, and
investors seeking to design resilient, low-carbon, and cost-optimal power systems.

2. Literature review
This literature review examines geo-temporal optimization in renewable energy sys-
tems, focusing on existing models, their capabilities, and limitations. It assesses
current optimization approaches, identifies methodological gaps, and informs the
development of improved methods to address spatial and temporal challenges in
energy system modeling.
As power systems grow increasingly complex, the need for data-driven models for
renewable energy deployment has intensified. Models such as Balmorel represent
power systems across countries and regions, incorporating location-specific produc-
tion data [1]. However, Balmorel employs a "copper-plate" approach within regions,
omitting internal transmission infrastructure and representing only aggregated inter-
regional and cross-border connections. Each region is assigned a single aggregated
transfer capacity rather than individual transmission lines, limiting the model’s abil-
ity to capture intra-regional grid dynamics.
Traditionally, power producers maximize output by targeting high mean resource
availability, such as siting wind turbines in areas with the highest average wind
speeds.
Recent research, however, has explored the potential of exploiting cor-
relations and anti-correlations between production profiles to achieve more stable
generation patterns.
Page 3 of 70
==PAGE:13==
[2] investigated wind–solar combinations in Germany and found no significant com-
plementarity between wind–solar, solar–solar, or wind–wind generation beyond sea-
sonal variation.
By contrast, [3] reported beneficial interactions between similar
technologies in Poland. These conflicting findings underscore the uncertainty sur-
rounding the value of geo-temporal optimization, suggesting that its effectiveness
may be highly country- or biome-specific. No comparable studies for Denmark were
identified.
[4] analyzed large-scale renewable deployment across Europe with the objective of
minimizing both positive and negative residual load. The model selected an optimal
mix of 74% wind and 26% PV for an overall renewable penetration of 82%, identi-
fying North Sea offshore wind as a “no-regret” option but also highlighting risks of
surplus production due to correlation with neighboring onshore wind. The analysis
excluded tracker PV and BESS and treated Europe as a copper-plate, ignoring grid
constraints and cost minimization in the objective function.
Similarly, [5] evaluated hybrid renewable systems in China combining combined
heat and power, wind, PV, and BESS. The study found solar irradiance and wind
speed to be major cost drivers, with other weather characteristics having secondary
effects. Although vast spatial scales were considered, the optimization again omitted
transmission and distribution grid constraints.
Other works [6–9] examined the potential of leveraging large geographical distances
to smooth production through regional weather diversity, effectively creating “nat-
ural storage”. While demonstrating potential benefits, these studies often involved
unrealistic spatial scales and overly simplified grid representations with few nodes.
Further studies supporting geo-temporal optimization [10–15] typically focus on min-
imizing variance in combined production profiles rather than optimizing for social
welfare or matching generation to demand. Although such variance reduction can
improve production stability, generation alignment with load profiles is crucial to
reduce curtailment and ancillary service activation. Importantly, none of these stud-
ies incorporate grid constraints, despite the fact that the geographical placement of
both grid infrastructure and loads directly influences optimal capacity siting.
In summary, most existing research on renewable energy production and storage
Page 4 of 70
==PAGE:14==
capacity optimization overlooks grid constraints, limiting its applicability to real-
world systems. The reviewed studies also feature a narrow technology catalogue,
thereby missing potential synergies between technologies. Moreover, none examine
the integration of PtX technologies, their geographical placement, or their impact
on system cost, operation, and decarbonization.
This study addresses these gaps by incorporating transmission network limitations,
expanding the technology portfolio, and considering the spatial integration of PtX
within a geo-temporal optimization framework for renewable energy capacity.

3.2. Investment problem
This section introduces the investments problem and working hereof.
Initially,
the problem was formulated as a MILP, in which a binary variable ensured that
simultaneous charging and discharging of the BESS was prevented, alongside
the requirement that wind unit installations be in whole integers. However, this
formulation was subsequently relaxed to an LP due to the computational complexity
associated with solving a problem of this scale.
As discussed in [16], social welfare in electricity systems is maximized by minimizing
both the cost of consumption and externalities such as greenhouse gas emissions.
However, [17] argue that in a fully renewable power system, marginal costs approach
zero, undermining the effectiveness of liberalized electricity markets in signaling in-
vestment needs. Moreover, a fully renewable system eliminates operational green-
house gas emissions. Given that electricity demand must be met in every hour,
no emissions are produced, and the system has no fuel-related operating costs, the
primary economic cost becomes the investment in renewable generation and storage
capacity.
Assuming demand is inelastic and fixed, the utility of electricity con-
sumption is constant. Therefore, maximizing social welfare reduces to minimizing
the total system cost, specifically, the investment cost and the operational cost of
renewable generation technologies and battery energy storage systems. The opti-
mization model is therefore structured to minimize total investment and operational

5. Numerical results
5.1. Structure of the numerical analysis
The numerical analysis is conducted in a three-step approach. As the system is
greenfield, its behavior and requirements are initially unknown.
Therefore, the
first part of the analysis focuses on establishing the base scenario. This involves
determining the degree of self-sufficiency, appropriate size of BESS, and deciding
Page 28 of 70
==PAGE:38==
5.2
Scenarios
whether EVs should be included in the base case. These factors are explored to de-
fine a consistent modeling foundation. Once these parameters are settled, the base
scenario itself is examined in detail. This is followed by a sensitivity analysis, inves-
tigating how deviations from the base assumptions affect the system’s geographic
layout, capacity composition, and operational behavior. Finally, with a granular
understanding of the individual system components and their spatial distribution, a
system-level perspective is adopted to compare the scenarios and assess their overall
performance.

model, which are subsequently determined through simulation. While the proposed
optimization problem is capable of sizing and siting various production technologies,
sizing the BESS is somewhat more complex. The BESS includes both a power and
an energy component, each contributing to the total CAPEX. Simultaneously opti-
mizing both power capacity and depth leads to a nonlinear optimization problem,

Notably, the most significant reduction in BESS capacity occurs when moving from
100% self-sufficiency to 99.3% self-sufficiency. In this step, BESS capacity drops by
68%, caused by only a 0.7% reduction in self-sufficiency. From a BESS perspective,
it is evident that the final steps toward 100% self-sufficiency are disproportion-

decreases, offshore capacity increases. If 6TWh or more of deferred load can be
realized, offshore wind is not required. Once again, the largest increase occurs when
moving from 99.3% self-sufficiency to 100%, during which offshore capacity doubles.
BESS and offshore generally follow the same trend, with the exception of the self-
sufficiency level at which they are introduced. Offshore is the most expensive pro-
duction technology per MW available, and BESS does not generate power. Hence,
these technologies are only used when there is a substantial need for constant out-
put or for output during periods when other technologies are not producing. BESS
enables storage of overproduction for later use, while offshore generally offers a more
stable production profile than onshore, as can be seen imagined from comparing the
offshore CF as shown in Appendix I to Appendix G, which is more vulnerable to
no-wind conditions.

can also be seen in the use of PV tracker when limited or no BESS is installed.PV

further reduced and BESS increases, PV tracker is no longer utilized. This outcome
stems from the underlying need for system flexibility. While storage remains neces-
sary regardless of tracker deployment, in scenarios with substantial storage capacity,
the broader production profile offered by tracker PV does not offset its higher cap-
ital costs when compared to the more cost-effective generation achievable through

5.5. Nodal operation
The following section delves into the specifics of nodal operation for the highlighted
nodes identified through the geospatial analysis.
5.5.1. 2024SMPG
The nodal power flow of the central Jutlandish node Tange are analyzed to assess
its role in system-level balancing, particularly during Dunkelflaute periods of 2024.
Detailed results of Tange’s operation, including imports, exports, transit flows, PV
generation, BESS discharge, and local consumption, are provided in Appendix N.
The analysis highlights that, while transit flows through Tange remain nearly con-
stant due to the high consumption at the connected Bjørnholdt node, the substantial
BESS capacity at Tange plays a key strategic role. Rather than primarily storing
PV generation, the BESS is actively used to stabilize the grid, discharging during
periods of high system stress and uptaking power from the whole of Jutland when
power is abundant. Tange’s connection to all three 400kV Jutland corridors fur-
ther reinforces its position as an optimal location for centralized energy storage and
strategic system balancing.

5.7. Solver parameters
The following section outlines the solver parameters, computational requirements,
and workarounds employed to achieve optimal solver performance. Due to the size
of the model, great amounts of time have been spent fine-tuning solver parameters
and coefficient statistics. With over 30 million rows and 20 million columns, using
crossover in Gurobi to obtain polished solutions is very time-consuming. If the full
year is temporally connected due to the BESS, this further increases solve time.
Hence, a framework has been developed where the BESS SOC is reset at intervals,
effectively turning the problem into an equal-probability stochastic programming
problem. As seen in Appendix P, there is no difference between using crossover or
not. Also, varying the temporal length of SOC linkage does not significantly affect
results. Slightly lower CAPEX is observed with longer durations, but the difference
is minor. The model is therefore run with SOC linkage broken every two months,
Page 62 of 70
==PAGE:72==
resulting in six equally probable scenarios that can be solved more efficiently
in parallel by Gurobi.
Crossover is disabled.
While this leads to less "pretty"
solutions, it is acceptable, as duals are not used and the focus is on the primal results.
Appendix Q lists the Gurobi settings, matrix coefficients, and the computational
resources used. Due to the scale of the problem, the High Performance Computer
(HPC) at DTU has been employed [57]. Given the problem size and memory de-
mands, running multiple problems in parallel for sensitivity analysis has required
up to 0.5TB of RAM to obtain reasonably fast results—still taking up to 10 hours,
as also reflected in Appendix Q.

6.2. Model limitations
The model aims to approximate real-world behavior and system responses but
remains a simplified representation developed over a few months. It is currently
formulated as a greenfield model to simplify the approach. In reality, if Denmark
were to pursue rapid decarbonization, existing generators, such as gas plants for
backup and biomass for baseload, would need to be included.
Therefore, the
model should be viewed as a worst-case scenario, likely overestimating the total
cost of achieving a high security of supply and a low-carbon grid, since it does
not account for existing infrastructure. Furthermore, although the current model
includes a relatively comprehensive catalogue of production technologies, it should
be expanded to incorporate additional options such as CHP, different ESS etc. For
simplicity, the model does not consider the interconnectors linking Denmark to the
Netherlands, the UK, Sweden, Norway, and Germany. Given Denmark’s status as
one of the most interconnected countries globally, including these interconnectors
would allow for cheaper balancing when surplus power is available elsewhere.
However, this is omitted due to difficulties in accurately determining the carbon
intensity of imported electricity and because power flows on interconnectors are
driven by price differences, an aspect the model currently does not capture. To
realistically incorporate interconnectors without explicit power prices, the model
would likely need to be expanded to a European scale, where interconnector
capacity is small relative to the internal load of the control area. This represents
a valuable potential extension, comparable in complexity to integrating electricity
prices into the model.
Page 66 of 70
==PAGE:76==
6.2
Model limitations
It is important to note that the model focuses solely on meeting load rather than
price-driven dispatch, which is the norm in real-world electricity markets. Operating
purely on prices would likely yield different system outcomes.
Nevertheless, an
internal price mechanism emerges implicitly:
the system’s total cost creates a
virtual price signal, where consumption during hours of low production effectively
raises costs by necessitating additional capacity.
This mechanism approximates
aspects of a merit order dispatch system despite the absence of explicit electricity
prices.
The grid representation in the model is a simplified approximation of real-world
behavior. In reality, voltage increases along longer transmission lines, and power
flow includes not only active but also reactive power. While reactive power does
not perform useful work, it is essential for certain grid functions. Additionally, due
to lack of access to detailed grid data, the line capacities used in the model are
estimates rather than actual values. Future research could benefit from obtaining
accurate grid data to enable more precise power flow approximations and enhance
the model’s realism.
Additionally, the model is based on a single year of weather data, which is not
representative of average conditions. To improve the robustness of the decisions,
the model should incorporate multiple weather years to capture broader weather
patterns rather than relying on the variability of a single year.
As mentioned earlier, there is a significant difference between LCOE and OLCOE,
as well as between the socio-economic discount rate and the discount rate used by
companies. Extending the model to incorporate business investment behavior could
enhance its realism by reflecting the viability of projects as actual business cases,
thereby improving the likelihood of implementation.
Overall, the research demonstrates a promising approach to exploring the synergies
between weather and production technologies. Further research is needed, including
Page 67 of 70
==PAGE:77==
expanding the model’s geographic scope to account for interconnectors, integrating
price mechanisms, and adopting a business perspective on investment decisions
regarding location, technology choice, and scale.
7. Summary and conclusions
This research developed a greenfield optimization model to determine the most cost-
effective mix of offshore and onshore wind, fixed-tilt and tracker PV systems, BESS,
and PtX technologies needed to achieve a fully renewable power system in Denmark.
The model was designed to explore scenarios reflecting both the state as could have
been in 2024 and a prospective outlook towards 2030. Central to the investigation
were questions regarding the optimal location, technology portfolio, and capacity
requirements for advancing towards increased energy self-sufficiency and a 100%
renewable supply. Additionally, the research sought to evaluate the impact and value
of integrating electric vehicles through V2G capabilities, as well as to understand
how PtX influences renewable energy valuation, the societal cost of carbon and total
system cost.
The results reveal that achieving the final 3% of self-sufficiency presents a dispro-
portionately high system cost, driven largely by the meteorological phenomenon
known as Dunkelflaute, characterized by prolonged periods of low wind and solar
generation. These conditions necessitate significant overcapacity and storage so-
lutions, highlighting the critical need to complement intermittent renewables with
dispatchable generation and energy storage to maintain system reliability. Under
assumptions of a copper-plate system, the model finds clear benefits in geographi-
cally dispersing generation capacity, which produces staggered and more balanced
production profiles compared to stacked otherwise. This is achieved primarily by
distributing offshore wind resources from the southwest to the northeast across the
North Sea. Additionally, spacing wind production from west to east further cre-
ates a staggered effect.
However, when realistic grid constraints are introduced,
these geographic balancing advantages diminish due to transmission limitations.
The constraints imposed by the transmission grid effectively limit geographic re-
source optimization to balancing generation behind substations, thereby avoiding
Page 68 of 70
==PAGE:78==
additional grid connection costs. Nevertheless, distinct geographic technology pref-
erences remain evident. Broadly, the farther south and east the location, the stronger
the case for solar power, whereas in northwestern Jutland, onshore wind emerges
as the optimal choice. Even when cable costs are taken into account, offshore wind
farms are best positioned far from shore, maintaining the previously noted south-
west–northeast orientation. However, the introduction of large-scale flexible demand
shifts the optimal placement of offshore wind capacity inland, as under such con-
ditions, total generation capacity becomes more critical than production stability.
Tracker PV systems were identified as the least-utilized technology across most sce-
narios, except under conditions of high flexible demand and limited BESS, where
their stable output supports more efficient PtX operations.
In terms of electric vehicle integration, the model estimates a substantial poten-
tial value of approximately 2 Bn EUR for V2G applications in 2024, considering
both technical system constraints and behavioral factors affecting EV owners. This
translates into a significant economic incentive, with owners potentially recovering
up to 22% of the vehicle’s purchase price through participation in grid flexibility ser-
vices. This finding underscores the important role that flexible demand, particularly
through EVs, can play in enhancing system efficiency and cost-effectiveness.
The Societal Cost of Carbon analysis indicates that deep decarbonization becomes
progressively more costly under realistic grid constraints compared to copper-plate
assumptions. Furthermore, the 2024 ETS price levels were found to be insufficient to
sustain the degree of self-sufficiency actually observed in 2024. In contrast, projected
2030 levels would incentivize approximately 97% self-sufficiency. PtX technologies
and flexible demand significantly reduce the societal cost of carbon, up to 50%,
by enhancing system flexibility and offsetting emissions, with additional reductions
achieved through avoided jet fuel emissions. These findings highlight the importance
of coordinated policy measures, such as sufficiently high ETS pricing, to facilitate
a cost-effective and societally optimal renewable energy transition. Moreover, the
analysis suggests that the projected 2030 ETS level of 149EUR/tCO2, in combina-
tion with substantial PtX deployment, would be sufficient to incentivize the 100%
decarbonization of Denmark.
PtX technologies also emerge as a key factor in shaping the economic landscape of
Page 69 of 70
==PAGE:79==
References
a renewable energy system. The introduction of PtX markedly reduces the subsidy
gap needed to support investments with lower capacity factors, cutting the required
subsidy from 1.4 Bn to 650 million euros. By converting surplus renewable electric-
ity into valuable fuels such as e-kerosene, PtX not only mitigates curtailment but
also generates additional economic value, thereby lowering the societal cost of transi-
tioning to renewable energy while also generating value from the sales of e-kerosene.
Furthermore, higher PtX penetration is associated with reduced overall system costs
and greater renewable energy value to investers, reinforcing its importance in future
energy planning.
The inclusion of grid constraints in the modeling framework reveals a significant
increase in total system costs, on the order of 10 to 20 Bn EUR depending on the
level of deferred load, compared to idealized copper-plate assumptions. The societal
cost of reaching a 97% self-sufficiency target under these conditions ranges from 500
million to 1.4 Bn euros, with higher PtX deployment contributing to cost reductions.
These findings highlight the importance of accounting for grid limitations in system
design and policy development.
In summary, the model successfully addresses the research objectives by identifying
cost-optimal technology mixes and locations, quantifying the benefits of electric ve-
hicle flexibility, elucidating the value added by PtX, and illustrating the significant
cost implications of grid constraints. The insights gained emphasize the complex
interplay between renewable generation, storage, flexible demand, and grid infras-
tructure, all of which must be carefully considered to facilitate a cost-effective and
reliable transition to a fully renewable energy system. Future work could further
enhance the model by incorporating more detailed grid data, extending temporal
coverage to multiple weather years, and integrating explicit price mechanisms and
business investment behaviors to improve realism and applicability.

A comparison between Table R.19 and Table R.20 reveals distinctions that stem
not from differences in model structure, but rather from the methodologies used
to compute the LCOE and the OLCOE. Whereas LCOE is calculated based on
theoretical generation potential, disregarding curtailment.
OLCOE accounts for
curtailed energy and thereby reflects the cost per unit of delivered electricity more
accurately.
Because the model consistently selects high-yield, anti-correlated sites to optimize
generation, the LCOE remains relatively stable across scenarios. The minor fluctu-
ations observed in LCOE values are primarily driven by changes in the technology
mix and total installed capacity, which alter the weighted average cost and thus the
overall LCOF.
By contrast, OLCOE exhibits more pronounced variability between scenarios, re-
flecting the operational realities of curtailment. In scenarios where certain technolo-
gies, such as offshore wind, are predominantly deployed as reserve or backup, asset
utilization decreases, leading to a higher OLCOE. This divergence underscores the
importance of accounting for operational patterns in cost assessments.
This distinction carries substantial implications for investment planning. Renewable
energy investors typically expect, at minimum, to recover costs at the LCOE. How-
ever, in systems characterized by significant curtailment, recovery must occur over
fewer delivered megawatt-hours, requiring a higher market price per MWh, captured
by the OLCOE. In an ideal, uncurtailed system, the required remuneration aligns
closely with the LCOE; in curtailed systems, it must instead approach the OLCOE.
Page 117 of 70
==PAGE:127==
The resulting gap between LCOE and OLCOE thus represents a deadweight loss or
the subsidy required to sustain investment viability in a self-sufficient, renewables-
based power system. This cost reflects the true societal burden, as it constitutes
non-market-based funding that must be injected into the market.
Crucially, the integration of PtX technologies substantially narrows this gap.
When sufficient PtX capacity is available, curtailment is reduced, improving asset
utilization and causing the OLCOE to converge toward the LCOE. In such cases,
only onshore wind requires a marginal subsidy to remain viable. This convergence
highlights the potential of PtX to reduce societal costs while maintaining investor
incentives. Beyond this, PtX provides additional value through the production and
sale of synthetic fuels and contributes to broader environmental goals by enabling
deeper decarbonization across sectors.
