Summary
In this thesis a controller design for disturbance rejection on a planar manipulator is
proposed. The design utilizes a prediction of the forces about to act on the system to
enhance disturbance rejection. It separates quadratic and nonlinear elements into dis-
tinct subsystems, a predictive controller and trajectory optimizer respectively, to reduce
computational complexity.
The controller uses a form of modified Model Predictive
Control (MPC), enhanced to take a prediction of the disturbances.
A linearized model of a single revolute joint manipulator is used in the design of
a predictive controller. Using this controller, disturbance rejection is greatly improved
compared to a comparative full state feedback controller. The system is expanded to
two revolute joints, and the nonlinear dynamics of motion are introduced. The two
controllers performance are reassessed on the nonlinear system, and various techniques
to improve the state prediction are discussed.
To handle the nonlinear complexities of optimal configurations for rejecting forces
affecting the end-effector, a trajectory optimizer is designed. It must calculate the opti-
mal configurations for a number of expected forces, then generate a reference signal and
disturbance estimate for the controller. Solution constraints are introduced, to limit the
solutions found to what the manipulator can actuate. After these topics are discussed
in detail and a cost function for the optimizer is derived, the issue of solution vetting is
discussed. An example force series is designed, and the system tested on it.
Once the trajectory optimizer is designed, the two subsystems are tested in combi-
nation the example force series. This test illustrates the difference in performance when
disturbance estimates are expected, unexpected, or expected in error.
Finally, the results are summarised and discussed, and some avenues of future work
are laid out.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The idea for this project first came to me after falling down a flight of stairs. My vision
was blocked and I miscounted the steps. When there was a step less than expected, I
tumbled. In the years of thinking that followed, I came to the realization that I had
relied on an expectation of my environment, and when that expectation was wrong it
caused me to fall. I realised that it is this expectation which allows me to interact with
my environment dynamically. When a ball is thrown to me, it is my expectations which
allow me to catch it without hurting my shoulder or elbow by distributing and softening
the jerk of the impact. Could robots benefit from mimicking our predictive behaviour
by estimating the influence of its environment acting on it?
Figure 1.1: The four stages of industrialisation.
Humanity is moving into its fourth industrial revolution, where robots and humans
can operate safely in the same workspace. To ensure a safe and efficient working envi-
ronment, it is important that the robot and human co-workers are able to interact, both
statically and dynamically. It might, for a static example, be necessary for a robot to
hand over a tool, or to receive one it is handed. Only one part acts at a time, making
such a task relatively easy and mostly safe. It stands only to improve in efficiency.
If I throw a ball and a robot has to catch it, this is a dynamic system, where multiple
elements are in motion at the same time. In this scenario, the forces involved could
be large enough to cause damage to the catcher. In order to safely perform its task in

this dynamic environment, it is necessary for the robot to know how its actions affect
its environment and itself, in order to be capable of optimizing them. This requires
a precise model of its environment to base a prediction on, and a control architecture
which will take the prediction into use.
One application for this technology is when manipulators operate on heavy loads.
One of the proposed solutions for removing space debris in low Earth orbit is the use of
satellites for junk removal [Ame16]. This would require precise control with destructive
impact forces. You might think of this problem as a giant robot trying to catch very
large, hugely dense, and incredibly fast projectiles. By predicting the forces, the manip-
ulator can be proactive - distributing the forces evenly to reduce strain and pre-empting
the disturbances to reduce their impact on the system.
This requires a prediction of the forces about to act on the manipulator, and knowledge
of how to optimize its movements to cancel out those forces, with this thesis focusing on
the latter. For this thesis, it is assumed that an accurate prediction of the forces acting
on the manipulator is available.
To accomplish this, two subsystems will be designed, namely the trajectory opti-
mizer, and controller. The proposed design of the system can be seen in figure 1.2,
where ˆF represents a prediction of the forces acting on the manipulator. The trajectory
optimizer takes this prediction, calculates the optimal configurations to handle them,
and provides the controller with a disturbance estimate for each individual joint. This
task constitutes a nonlinear optimization problem.
A control scheme capable of handling predictions is necessary to make use of the
disturbance estimate provided by the trajectory optimizer. One such scheme is Model
Predictive Control (MPC)[CB07], where a model of the manipulator is used to predict
the operating states forward in time a number of samples. By including the disturbance
estimates in this prediction, the controller can calculate the optimal input sequence. All

1.1
Literature Review
Optimal control was first introduced in 1956, and have been in constant development
since the 1960s, when Kalman introduced the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR). At
the time, the control scheme had little impact on the field of process control, mainly due
to the prevalence of PID controllers. This was due to a lack of operational constraints,
and because the principle of optimality was considered too impractical for actual imple-
mentation. In the late 70s, multiple publications presented industry specific applications
of MPC, such as Dynamic Matrix Control by Cutler and Ramaker [Cut80] and Model
Algorithmic Control by Richalet [Ric+78]. Control theories around this time focused on
using system pulse- and step responses, to predict the system behaviour. One issue of
the finite horizon predictive controllers is that it does not have the inherent stability of
the infinite horizon LQR, so while stability could be obtained by adjusting the weights,
it was not guaranteed.
The second generation of MPC attempted to tackle this problem with methods such
as Quadratic Dynamic Matrix Control by Garcia and Morshedi [GM86], but the com-
putationally difficult quadratic programs deterred wide-spread application, as digital
computers were bulky, slow, and prohibitively expensive. Variants of MPC were used
for solving slower problems, such as controlling large-scale chemical processes, when
there is ample time to solve the quadratic problems.
MPC has seen more widespread use in the 21st century, with the availability of
cheap processing power and advances in quadratic programming. As more transistors
can be crammed onto chips, the technology can be applied to systems which require an
ever increasing sampling frequency. In robotics and mechatronics, the technology have
started to see its use. A few examples being for biped walking motion [Wie14], motor
drive control [Gey10], jerk reduction control in diesel engines for hybrid trains [Bau+04],
and most notably in the control of the new dexarm manipulator in development by
the European Space Agency [Fer+05][Cam+06][MRR08]. The dexarm is designed to
mimic the human arm, and will be used on the International Space Station for tasks
which would otherwise require the dexterity of a human operator, specifically on the
outside of the station.
Projects in the field of MPC tend towards practical applications on specific systems,
either through a real implementation or a high-fidelity model, as the scheme requires a
precise model of the system to function. Furthermore, the computational requirements
of the control scheme generally dissuades the use of the scheme for systems with a
combination of fast and slow dynamics, as a prohibitively long prediction horizon is
required.

1.2 Project Description

1.2
Project Description
Torque rejection in the joints is complicated by the load inertia being dependent on
the manipulator configuration. To evenly distribute the disturbance torque it is, nec-
essary to consider all joints in a unified model. Further complicating the problem, the
static force/torque relationship for revolving joints are nonlinear. This creates a nonlin-
ear optimization problem. Nonlinear problems are notoriously difficult to solve in hard
real-time, as the solution time is not bounded. The MPC prediction horizon further
complicates the problem, creating a combinatorial nonlinear optimization problem. For
time horizons longer than a few samples, the problem might become computationally
infeasible. Therefore, separating the nonlinear elements from the controller is required.
To compare the controller performance, a static LQ regulator will be designed on
approximately the same cost function as the MP controller, so that the two controllers
have similar performance for unexpected disturbances. Controller performance will be
evaluated and compared both on a simplified linearized model, and a Matlab simulation
with nonlinear dynamics of motion included.
1.3
Problem Specification
1. The control effort of the model predictive controller must be less than or approxi-
mately equal to the control effort of a comparable static linear quadratic regulator.
2. When the disturbances can be expected:
a) The displacement of the manipulator with a model predictive controller must
be less than the system with the comparable static LQ regulator.
b) The settling time of the manipulator with a model predictive controller must
be less than system with the comparable static LQ regulator.
3. When the disturbances cannot be expected:
a) The displacement of the manipulator with a model predictive controller must
be less than or approximately equal to the system with the comparable static
LQ regulator.
b) The settling time of the manipulator with a model predictive controller must
be less than or approximately equal to the system with the comparable static
LQ regulator.

1.4
Thesis Overview
This thesis is separated into five sections, each relating to a part of the development
process. These sections are:
Chapter 2: Theory
In this chapter, the necessary background theory is provided on the subjects of system
dynamics, linear quadratic regulators, model predictive control, quadratic optimization
and nonlinear optimization.
Chapter 3: Single Joint
A MP controller and LQ regulator are designed for use on a single revolute joint, and
the performance of the two controllers are compared.
Chapter 4: Double Joint
The system and MP controller is expanded to have two revolute joints, and the controller
is used on a nonlinear simulation of the system.
Various techniques are applied to
improve the reference tracking of the model predictive controller.
Chapter 5: Trajectory Optimizer
The cost function for the trajectory optimizer is derived, and the topics of trajectory
generation and solution vetting is discussed. Finally, its performance is assessed on an
example force series.
Chapter 6: Optimizer and Controller Combined
In the final chapter, the trajectory optimizer is used on the example force series, and
the resulting reference and disturbance estimate are provided to the controller. Both
subsystems are timed and the results are discussed.

2.2
Performance Criteria
In control theory there are several criteria by which performance is evaluated, most
notably rise time, overshoot, settling time, tracking error, and control effort. Focusing
on one typically comes at the expense of others, and as such a major part of control
design is balancing the different aspects through manual tuning and experimentation
on models of the system, or through optimization algorithms. Optimal controllers are
designed around minimization of a given performance index.
In robotics, where the controller operates on electro-mechanical systems, too high
controller gain can cause chattering. Chattering is when a controller performs constant
micro-adjustments typically in response to measurement noise. This causes strain on the
system, and is undesirable behaviour. As such, the controller should have the smallest
possible gain that achieves satisfactory performance.
The primary concern of this thesis is in disturbance rejection, in which scenarios only
settling time, max settling value, and control effort is considered. For reference tracking,
which is considered in chapter 4, settling time, max settling value, control effort, rise
time, and overshoot are used to evaluate performance.
Control Effort
There are several ways of indexing control effort. In this thesis control effort is measured
as the integral of squared control input, and is referred to as Summed Quadratic Control
Effort (SQCE). In discrete notation it is given by
SQCE =

where u(k) is the control input at a kth sample and N is the length of the signal. This
measure of control effort penalises greater values more, which is appropriate as large
spikes in motor torque cause greater strain on the drive shaft of the DC motor.
For systems excited by disturbance steps, the length of the signal affects the SQCE, as
the controller converges on a non-zero value. As such, only the control signal until the
system has settled is considered.

Benefits & Downsides
The most prominent benefits of LQR control are:
• Good robustness properties [HJS08, p. 324].
• Inherently multivariate.
• Through extensions or changes to the performance index the LQ regulator can be
very versatile, and can be used for dynamic control or with time-varying systems.
There are few downsides to the LQ regulator, but two specifically stick out:
• The LQ regulator cannot include constraints on states or the control signals.
Weights must instead be placed conservatively or signals limited artificially using
saturation, which the controller cannot compensate for.
• It cannot, baring modifications, include predictive signals, which is paramount for
this project.

The upside of convex problems is that they are easier to solve, as any local minimizer

using only the first order optimality conditions, as there are neither saddle-points nor

anteed to be positive definite. In both cases, however, the quadratic problem is convex.
Numerically, errors can cause the product matrix to not be positive semi-definite, but
this is very unlikely.
For quadratic programming Matlab provides access to two algorithms, namely a
convex interior point algorithm [PW00] and a reflective trust region algorithm. Rather
than go into details on their operation, it is noted that convex interior point is typically
faster than the reflective trust region, but may have slight inaccuracies, due to the fact
that the internally calculated barrier function keeps iterates away from the inequality
constraint boundaries. The reflective trust region is able to handle nonconvex problems,
but can use only solution bounds, or only linear equality constraints. As MPC can
utilize both inequality and equality constraints and is guaranteed to be convex, the
interior point algorithm is chosen.

Nonlinear Optimization
The nonlinear problem with linear equality and inequality constraints is the optimization
problem defined by
min
x
ϕ(x)
s.t.
Aieqx ≤bieq

(2.85)
lb ≤x ≤ub
Where few or no assumptions are made on the function ϕ(x). This presents a problem,
as no guarantee can be made on the solution space. Further, the execution time cannot
be guaranteed to be bounded. Further still, as the problem is not necessarily convex
the local minimizer found is not guaranteed to be the global minimizer. For example,
both cosine and sine have multiple points which could be considered optimal, as they
are at their lowest once every cycle. More complex functions can have arbitrarily many
minimizers, any or one of which might also be the global minimzer. For problems with
multiple local minimizers, it is necessary to thoroughly examine the entire feasible so-
lution space, greatly increasing execution time, whilst still not guaranteeing that the
solution found is a optimal solution.
One approach, which is utilized for the trajectory optimizer, is the use of the heuristic
Particle Swarm optimization algorithm described in [KE95]. The algorithm scatters sev-
eral trackers across the feasible region, and utilizes optimization techniques to converge
on minimizers throughout the feasible region, with trackers repelling other trackers. The
best solution is found by evaluation at the minimizers found. Even this computationally
expensive algorithm cannot guarantee that the global minimzer is found, nor that it will
find the same potentially sub-optimal solution every time it is run. For these reasons
convex problems are generally preferred, especially for implementation in robots, where
the controller must evaluate frequently in a hard real-time application.
Feasibility
One advantage of the nonlinear solvers are the fact that they are not limited to have
feasible starting points. For the extreme cases where the quadratic optimizer would fail
from being outside the feasible region, a nonlinear optimizer would still be able to find
a solution. As the forces involved in taking the optimizer into this infeasible region are
so great, however, there is little to benefit from this consideration.

3.4
Offset & Erroneous Estimates
The introduction of disturbance estimates presents a new source of faults, as the estimate
is made by a fallible computer system. In general it is assumed that all estimates are
accurate, but such an assumption does not hold in a real application. Thus it is relevant
to examine what effect offsets in the estimate has, and the effect of erroneous estimates
- where estimates are provided, but no force affects the system. For this section alone
the predictions are assumed to be inaccurate, to study the effect of such signals.
3.4.1
Pulse Estimation
In figure 3.8 the effect on the estimates can be seen for errors in a expected pulse, and
in figure 3.9 the input sequences are shown. In these figures δ(t) indicates a pulse at
time t, and are indicated with a dashed blue line. The expected time of the signal is
given by ˆtd, and is indicated by a vertical gray line. As such δ(td +2Ts) indicates a pulse
affecting the system two samples later than expected.
It can clearly be seen that the MP controller uses increased controller effort when
the estimate is off, while also having a greater offset. The worst scenario in terms of
displacement comes when the estimate is early. In this scenario the motor must cancel
out the angular velocity induced by the run up to the estimated disturbance, and then
when the disturbance hits, the resulting velocity cause greater displacement. In terms
of control effort δ(ˆtd + 2Ts) and δ(ˆtd + Ts) are the worst. After performing its proactive
control leading up the expected disturbance, the controller has two and one samples
respectively where it believes it necessary to correct for its previous effort, and as with
the displacement the system must then provide additional effort when the disturbance
hits unexpected.
It is worth noting that while the performance of the system in any of the delayed
or erroneous scenarios is worse than for the accurate estimate, it performs equivalently
to the LQ regulated system as seen in figure 3.2. The offset estimates are a problem
in that it increases control effort, but offset estimates are still preferable to unexpected
disturbances. While the erroneous is naturally undesirable, it would be entirely the fault
of the predictor making the estimate, and is therefore not a part of this thesis.

4.2.1
Coupled Dynamics
The linearized double joint manipulator can be seen in figure 4.2 when excited with a
unexpected pulse disturbance of 200Nm. In figure A.9 the system when excited with a
step disturbance can be seen.
The matrices in equation (4.23) indicate that the motion of one joint induces torque
on the other. The MP controller can utilize this to reduce the motor torque of the
disturbed joint and compensate in part using the reactive forces from the other joint.
While the effect of these forces are reduced by the gearing and as a result is less efficient,
the optimal controller will still leverage some control effort in the undisturbed joint, to
reduce the displacement on the disturbed one.
In figure 4.2 it can clearly be seen that a disturbance on the first joint affects the
second joint as well. Such coupled dynamics would be troublesome if the system had
been designed with multiple distributed SISO controllers, as effort on one joint would
displace the other, leading to dampened oscillating between the two joints. The MIMO
controller on the other hand can take the coupled dynamics into account. An optimal
MIMO controller, such as the MP controller, can calculate the input for both joints
while taking the coupled dynamics into account, and reduce the total displacement in
the process, which is seen in figure 4.3 for the expected pulse disturbance.
Consider the input in figure 4.2. After the initial pulse of motor effort, the two motor
torques start to fluctuate with notably different phase. When the speed of one joint is
at its highest, it utilizes both joints to decelerate the one which was disturbed the most,
thus achieving a lower performance index.

4.3
Simulation on Nonlinear Model
The simulation is carried out using Matlabs Ordinary Differential Equations (ode)
solvers. Specifically the stiff ode15s solver is used, as the simulation contains elements
of varied frequencies, necessitating the use of a stiff solver to reach a solution in a rea-
sonable time frame.
As the linked dynamics relies heavily on second order terms of the angular velocity,
using those dynamics in control might present an issue, as the quadratic terms are not
represented in the linearized model. This will cause the model to become less precise
as the velocity of the joints increase. In other words, as the system leaves its linearized
region, the MP controllers heavy reliance on the model of the system for predictions
might lead to inaccuracies and negate the benefit predictive control.
In addition to expected pulse and step disturbance tests, a third test is designed,
intended to take the system as far away from its linearization point as possible. This
’stress test’ consists of a reference change from 0 to 4π on both joints, as this is the
largest possible reference change which could be imposed by the trajectory optimizer.
This reference change will increase the velocity of the joints as much as possible
within the constraints. The high velocities - which you will recall is the bane of the
linearized model - will hopefully expose the weaknesses of the MP controller, if there
are any. Further, as this is a reference step on the second joint as well, the load inertia
of the first joint will change throughout the test.
To fully grasp the problem the system must be seen in motion. Animated .gif files
are included in the attachments under ’Animations’ and ’Unmodified Nonlinear Stress’.
In the animation the state and input predictions are indicated by dashed lines in the
corresponding colors.
In figure 4.5 a still image of the system with the MP controller can be seen. On the
flanks of the displacement, when the velocity is at its highest, the prediction start to err,
resulting in sudden and abrupt changes to the input. This can clearly be seen in the
animation, especially around the two second mark.
Furthermore, the momentum of the system, which is a nonlinear term and thus unac-
counted for, makes the system harder to stop. As such this causes additional overshoot
when compared to the linearized stress test response seen in figure A.10 of appendix A.
When the nonlinear simulation of the system is subjected to expected pulse or step
disturbances however, this problem is less apparent, as can be seen in figure 4.6 for a

a certain velocity is unavoidable when disturbances are introduced. This induced ve-

velocity of more than 20 radians per second, which makes the control problem infeasible.

or soft constraints.

apply around expected disturbances, or to scale with the size of the disturbance. Soft
constraints add additional virtual states to penalize high velocities, complicating the
optimization problem.
Neither technique is employed, as the forces necessary to facilitate such disturbances are
outside the scope of the project.

5.3 Trajectory Generation

5.3
Trajectory Generation
Once the solution to the optimization problem is found, a reference trajectory must be
constructed so that the controller can ensure that the system is at the optimal config-
uration when a force affects the system. The reference must be maintained for a set
amount of samples after the disturbance, as the predictive controller might otherwise
proactively move the manipulator away from the optimal configuration in the critical
moment.
The generating scheme will hold the configuration found by the optimizer for 20%
of the difference in time till the next disturbance. After this rest period, the reference
will conduct a step change to the next optimal configuration in the prediction. The step
reference change is used, as it gives the MP controller free reign to calculate the optimal
input sequence according to its cost function.
An alternative approach would be to stagger the reference changes for the two joints, to
eliminate one source of uncertainty caused by the unmodelled linked dynamics. These
dynamics, however, have limited effect on the settling time of system controlled by the
constrained controller, and so the simple reference step is deemed most appropriate.
Rather than conduct a step change on the reference, the reference could be sloped to the
next optimal configuration. This would potentially alleviate issues from the unmodelled
coupled dynamics, as the controller would move at lower velocities. On the other hand,
it would take agency away from the controller, disallowing it to operate according to its
cost function.
5.4
Solution Vetting
As mentioned it cannot be guaranteed that an optimal solution is found. Nor can it
be guaranteed that it is the same suboptimal solution found between iterations of the
trajectory optimizer. Therefore, it is necessary to consider a scheme for solution vetting.
If not properly vetted, the solution might change rapidly between two opposite solutions,
locking the manipulator in place and wasting control effort.
Comparing the new solution to the reigning is a simple, but effective approach. Once
a solution is found it is maintained until a subsequent solution is found with sufficiently
reduced performance index. This requires the reigning solution to be re-evaluated every
time the trajectory optimizer is executed, as the force estimate may have changed be-
tween iterations.

The system clearly benefits greatly from the MP controller when an accurate distur-

While these results are phenomenal, they depend on an accurate prediction of the
forces about to act on the system. Even though design of the predictor has not been the
focus of this thesis, it is worth considering the effect on the system when the disturbance
estimate are delayed or made in error.
When the estimate is delayed the displacement increases with the size of the offset. As
the controller attempts to rectify the effort it has already asserted, the control effort is
likewise increased. The worst case scenario is for estimates made in error. In such a sit-
uation the controller induces a response on the system similar to that of an unexpected

disturbance for no benefit, as the system was not disturbed. From this examination it
is clear that the effectiveness of the controller proposed in this thesis depends on the
accuracy of the force predictor. A delayed estimate of a disturbance give better rejection
than an unexpected disturbance, albeit not as good rejection as an accurate estimate.
Doubled Jointed Manipulator
With the controller performance evaluated in a simple system, the model is expanded to
two revolute joints. The second joint adds a complex coupled dynamic, where motion
of one link has a reactive effect on the other - even in the linear case. This allows the
MP controller to leverage the motion of one joint to control the other, as can be seen in
figure 4.3 on page 61.
Nonlinear Dynamics of Motion
When nonlinear dynamics of motion are introduced the linearized coupled dynamics be-
come less dependable. When moving at high velocities, especially, the system behaves
different compared to where it was linearized. As the MP controller relies on an accurate
model of the system, the calculated control sequence becomes less reliable, resulting in
increased control effort and longer settling times. To improve controller performance
constraints are introduced on the joint velocities, to keep the system within its linearized
region.
When constraints are placed on the system under stress conditions, it closer approx-
imates the linearized model used by the controller, and so allows the system to settle
in the same time using greatly reduced control effort. The constraints have no effect
on disturbance rejection for realistically sized disturbances, as the velocity under those
conditions never approach the constraints.
Trajectory Optimizer
The trajectory optimizer calculates the optimal configurations for the handling of ex-
pected forces on the end-effector. It then creates a reference signal which ensure that
the manipulator are in these configurations, along with the predicted disturbances in
the joints given the expected force and configuration.
The trajectory optimizer optimizes using four terms to create a weighed cost function,
which it solves using Matlabs implementation of the Particle Swarm algorithm. The
terms are:
• A reference tracking term, penalizing solutions further from the original reference.
• A torque difference term, penalizing the difference in torques of the two joints.
• A absolute torque magnitude term, penalizing solutions with large torques presents.
==PAGE:113==
7.2 Future Work
95
• A singular avoidance term, penalizing the singular solutions.
These terms are used to calculate the optimal solutions for the given expected force
series. In addition to minimizing the cost function, the solutions must also satisfy con-
straints limiting how far apart they can be. A displacement- and kinematic heuristic is
presented in this thesis, and both are used to some extend.
The solutions are then used to construct a reference signal and a disturbance estimate,
and these are provided to the controller.
To avoid wasted effort, the subject of solution vetting is discussed. By evaluating the
found solution against the one found previous iteration, and only updating if the new
solution is sufficiently better, the trajectory optimizer can maintain the same reference
signal for the controller between iterations.
Combined System
The trajectory optimizer is used on an example force series of three 10kN pulses, at
sample 50 (t1 = 2s), 80 (t2 = 3.2s), and 100 (t3 = 4s). The resulting optimal trajectory
and force predictions are used by the controller in three separate simulations. The first
with accurate disturbance estiamtes, the second without disturbance estimates, and the
third with erroneous disturbance estimates. Comparison of the three tests clearly shows
that the system with accurate estimates has greatly improved performance, and that
erroneous estimates pose a smaller problem to the manipulator than unexpected distur-
bances.
The execution time of the combined system is fast enough to be implemented on
high-end processors in a distributed implementation given current technologies. In a
few years a dual core processor with high enough performance should be available for
the algorithm to be implementable cheaply. It is also possible that the computational
load could be reduced, either by lowering the sampling frequency, implementation of
the routines used in a faster processing language, or by reducing the prediction horizon.
7.2
Future Work
Model Improvement
As this thesis shows the model predictive control scheme works well under idealised
conditions, and so the next step is to implement the trajectory optimizer an controller
on either a real system, or a high fidelity model with measurement noise and complex
nonlinearities, such as the Coulomb friction and the resulting dead zone [KE04].
One way to approach this could be by the use of describing functions and quasilinear
modelling, which would base the linearized model on the probability distribution of the
==PAGE:114==
96
7 Conclusion
states to include more of the linked dynamics [Ste94].
An alternative approach could be to model the continuous nonlinear dynamics using the
state prediction of the MP controller, and introduce the discrepancy into the system in
the same manner as the disturbances. This would allow the controller to optimize the
trajectory while taking additional nonlinearities into account. This approach is likely
infeasible for discontinuous nonlinearities, such as the Coulomb friction.

Introduction
Robots can be implemented in most ﬁelds of manual labour to increase eﬀectiveness and improve
safety, especially when the work is dangerous or strenuous. For many ﬁelds, such as disaster
relief, interaction with an unknown and potentially hazardous environment is necessary to ensure
the continued operation of the robot. In the case of space robotics, robots typically have long
limbs and are required to handle increasingly greater loads (300-500 kg) while becoming lighter
(15-30 kg) to reduce costs. For loads of this size it is imperative to avoid conﬁgurations that
concentrate the strain on a single joint, as this can cause damages.
For my thesis I will be considering systems where moments of impact are inevitable, either
due to the objective of the robot or from exogenous sources. It is assumed that the moments
and magnitude of impacts can be estimated, as the estimation is not the focus of this thesis.
These forces could be actively handled by the robot to minimize the eﬀect of the impact on
its joints, both in terms of displacement and strain. My hypothesis is that Model Predictive
Control (MPC) over a relatively long time horizon can be used to distribute and reduce the
strain on individual joints. This could reduce the weight and/or cost of individual joints, as
their mechanical torque tolerances can be lowered. It could also potentially improve the lifespan
of the robot, which is essential in ﬁelds where maintenance is expensive such as underwater or
space robotics.
These problems are further complicated by non-linear behaviour. Typically these dynamics are
considered insigniﬁcant enough to be neglected and idealised linear models are instead used for
controller design. Using Model Predictive Control it might be possible to counteract some of
these nonlinearities by including their causes in the cost function of the controller.
In this report the problem will be described. With this in mind the learning objectives will be
evaluated, and a project plan proposed. Finally I will consider and discuss the possible risks
that the project faces.

Project Description
Optimal torque disturbance rejection is a diﬃcult problem. Torque rejection in the joints is
complicated by having a load inertia which is dependent on the robot conﬁguration. To evenly
distribute the disturbance torque it is necessary to consider all joints in a uniﬁed model. To
make matters worse the static force/torque relations for revolving joints are nonlinear. This
creates a nonlinear (NL) optimization problem. NL problems are notoriously diﬃcult to solve
in hard real-time, as an upper bound for the solution time cannot typically be guaranteed.
The MPC time horizon further complicates the problem, creating a combinatorial nonlinear
optimization problem. For time horizons longer than a few samples, the problem might become
computationally infeasible. This thesis will examine approaches to solving this problem. Com-
putational feasibility will be considered with regard to contemporary computers and a 10-year
projection of computational power.
To compare the design a LQR controller will be constructed on approximately the same cost
function as the MPC. Controller performance will be evaluated and compared ﬁrst on the
linearised model, and secondly on a model containing some of the major nonlinearities of a
DC-motor.
Main Objective
Design a model predictive controller for optimal distribution and rejection of torque disturbance
in robotic manipulators.
What will I examine?
The eﬀectiveness of utilizing predictive control in torque distribution and rejection.
How will I examine it?
By comparing the performance of a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) on both a idealised
linear system and a system containing some of the major nonlinearities of a DC-motor.
What can it be used for?
Even if the model predictive techniques improve disturbance rejection it is likely that the
calculations will be too time consuming to be of practical use. As technology advances however,
it is probable that the solution proposed in this thesis can improve the performance of robotics
manipulators signiﬁcantly.

Risks
Modelling
It is possible that the model proves more complex than anticipated, although early research
suggests otherwise. Implementing the simulation with the nonlinear model could also prove
diﬃcult, but this diﬃculty should already be accounted for in the schedule.
Computational Feasibility
It is possible that the nonlinear optimization is not only too slow to be of use in practice, it
might also be too slow to be used in simulation. If this is the case I will attempt to construct a
more simpliﬁed representation in order to optimize it as a quadratic problem, but depending on
what adaptation I make use of, this approximation might also be computationally infeasible.
If all else fails the performance of the MPC and LQR controllers have to be compared without
the use of a the joint position optimizer.

Project Revision & Evaluation
In retrospect, too little time was allotted for development of the nonlinear model. The issue,
however, was alleviated by having included a four-week writing and working buﬀer into the
schedule, the updated version of which can be seen in ﬁgure 1. Mostly the project went according
to plan, with the exception that more research became necessary as the project developed.
All in all the project has been developed without much trouble, with the exception that writing
and rewriting took longer than expected. This is likely due to the fact that I had failed to take
into account that the many varied plots found throughout the thesis took a surprisingly great
amount of time to make.
Use of a disciplined structured, ﬂexible Matlab workspace and extensively saving the results
of all simulations and optimizations, has purchased much time. Even now, at the end of the
project, the well-deﬁned structure and iterative looping script design allows for addition or
fundamental changes without requiring further work, a great boon for myself in the results and
thesis stages of the project plan, as it allowed me to tweak or adjust without wasting time.
