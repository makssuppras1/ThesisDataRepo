Abstract
Pesticide contamination is the most frequent cause of drinking water abstraction well closure in
Denmark. The limited knowledge regarding the fate and behaviour of the pesticides in the subsur­
face presents a challenge for risk assessment. This thesis focused on the pesticide contamination
of Slimminge well field in central­eastern Zealand. Metabolites of the pesticide dimethachlor,
used for oilseed rape production from 1980­1990, have been detected in a northern, a middle
and a southern abstraction well, leading to ongoing investigations to identify all potential sources.
Dimethachlor metabolites have been found in the clayey till above the primary aquifer at an ex­
pected point source upstream of the abstraction wells. The primary aquifer in the area is constituted
by the Lellinge Greensand formation, which is characterised by significant heterogeneity. Knowl­
edge regarding the Greensand formation is limited, making it challenging to predict the spreading
of pesticides in the primary aquifer. This thesis aimed to enhance the conceptual understanding
of pesticide fate and transport from source to receptor, specifically focusing on the contamina­
tion with the dimethachlor metabolites, dimethachlor OA, dimethachlor ESA and CGA 369873 in
Slimminge.
The findings revealed that the clayey till at the site was relatively homogeneous, containing a few
sand lenses of unknown extent, and that the Greensand formation is characterised by having thin
high permeable layers or fractures creating preferential flow paths for contaminants. The centre
of the pesticide plume was found to be located in the deeper part of the clayey till at the site. The
horizontal distribution of the contamination towards the north has yet to be delineated, suggesting
a potentially larger source area.
Flow and solute transport models were created in both 2D and 3D using COMSOL Multiphysics.
A 2D top view and a 3D model showed that, under the current hydraulic conditions, the highest
concentrations of DMC metabolites were expected in the middle well with smaller amounts in the
northern well. This has been confirmed by measurements. However, simulations showed that no
contamination reached the southern well, while all three metabolites have been measured here.
Simulations showed that the pumping rates of the different abstraction wells would affect the pes­
ticide plume, and that changes could lead to a re­direction. The pesticide transport was modelled
through the clayey till and the Greensand formation with a 2D side view and a 3D model to esti­
mate the expected size and duration of the contamination, based on the current measurements of the
metabolites and the sale period of dimethachlor. The models showed that the highest concentration
in the abstraction wells would occur within the next five years from 2023. The effect of hetero­
geneity in the primary aquifer on the fate and transport of the metabolites was evaluated through
several model scenarios. It was found that adding thin highly permeable layers increased the veloc­
ity significantly in the primary aquifer resulting in earlier breakthroughs at the abstraction wells,
higher concentrations and potentially larger capture zones than previously expected. A Compar­
ison of simulated and observed concentrations in the abstraction wells showed that dimethachlor
OA was overestimated, while dimethachlor ESA and CGA 367893 fell within the lower range
of measurements. The overestimations of dimethachlor OA could be due to uncertainties in the
degradation rates, and the different distribution of dimethachlor metabolites suggests presence of
additional sources. However, there are uncertainties in input estimations. The lack of metabolites
from the site in the southern well could result from changes in the flow field over time.
Overall, this thesis has led to an enhanced conceptual understanding of the pesticide fate and trans­
port from the site to the abstraction wells. The results indicate that the point source does not pose
a risk to the wells as the pesticide concentrations are expected to decrease in the coming years, the
water supply should thus continue to be able to dilute the pesticide concentration to below the qual­
ity criteria. However, ongoing monitoring and a catchment scale risk assessment are recommended
as changes to the flow field or unknown upstream sources could affect the final concentration in
the abstracted water.

Introduction
In Denmark, the drinking water supply is almost entirely based on groundwater, which undergoes
minimal treatment, such as aeration and filtration, before distribution to the consumers (Thomsen
et al., 2021). Therefore, protecting the groundwater resource is crucial for ensuring a safe and
reliable drinking water supply.
To ensure the availability of drinking water, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency must
map all areas with special drinking water interest, OSD areas, so that action plans can target vulner­
able and essential water resources (Miljøstyrelsen, 2021a). In 2015, around 40 % of the Danish land
area is mapped as OSD (GEUS, 2023b). Agricultural activities occupy two­thirds of the land area,
meaning most groundwater recharge occurs beneath cultivated areas. Due to intensive agriculture,
pesticides, and especially pesticide metabolites, now pose the largest risk to the groundwater re­
source (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023b; Roost et al., 2022). Pesticide metabolites often present a challenge
due to the limited knowledge regarding their fate and behaviour in the subsurface (Hintze, 2020).
To secure drinking water quality, the Danish Ministry of Environment and Food has defined quality
criteria of 0.1 µg/L for individual pesticides and 0.5 µg/L for the sum of pesticides. These quality
criteria apply to drinking water and groundwater (Miljøministeriet, 2023; Miljøstyrelsen, 2021b).
In 2021, pesticides were detected in 58.5 % of the groundwater sampled in the national monitoring
program, GRUMO, where the quality criteria were exceeded in 26.4 % of the samples for individual
pesticides and 12.1 % of the samples for the sum of pesticides (Thorling et al., 2023). This has
resulted in pesticide contamination being the most frequent cause of the closure of drinking water
abstraction wells in Denmark (Region Syddanmark, 2018). As utilities are forced to close the
abstraction wells, finding new and uncontaminated resources becomes increasingly challenging.
To address this issue, investigation of the source of the contamination and its fate and transport
is essential to estimate the duration of the impact and target efforts to minimise the risk to water
supply (Danske Regioner, 2021).
At an OSD area near the village Slimminge, located in central eastern Zealand, Denmark, pesticide
contamination has been detected in several of the abstractions wells in the area. This has led to the
closure of one of the local waterworks’ abstraction wells (Orbicon, 2018). Furthermore, pesticides
have been detected in abstraction wells belonging to Slimminge well field, located northeast of the
village. This is a large well field owned by the water utility company HOFOR. Region Zealand
and HOFOR are working together to find all the potential contaminant sources in the area (WSP,
2023).
Most of the abstraction wells in the area are screened in the Greensand formation, which consti­
tutes the primary aquifer in the area (Orbicon, 2018; GEUS, 2023c). The Greensand formation is
characterised by a large inhomogeneity and complex layering (Jensen, 2003; Tyge, 2023a). There
is limited knowledge regarding the presence and distinct hydrological characteristics of the vari­
ous layers in the Greensand formation in the area (Tyge, 2023a). The heterogeneity and potential
presence of high­permeability layers, such as fractures, that can create preferential flow paths for
contaminants, make it challenging to predict and control the spreading of pesticides in the primary
aquifer.
In the abstraction wells belonging to HOFOR, there have been detected several different types of
pesticides including the metabolites N,N­dimethylsulfamide, DMS, 2,6­dichlorobenzamide, BAM,
and the dimethachlor metabolites dimethachlor OA, dimethachlor ESA, (2,6­dimethylphenyl carba­
moyl)­methanesulfonic acid and [(2,6­Dimethyl phenyl)­(2­sulfoacetyl) amino] acetic acid (GEUS,
2023c). Dimethachlor metabolites have only been analysed in some of the Danish water supply
wells since 2018 and were first included in the Water Works Well Monitoring Programme in 2019
(Thorling et al., 2019, 2021). The extent and duration of the contamination is therefore uncertain.

When there is limited knowledge regarding the geology, hydrogeology and contaminant physical­
chemical properties, modelling can be used to provide a conceptual understanding of contaminant
transport in the subsurface by investigating the effect of different input parameters (Roost et al.,
2022). Modelling can therefore be used as an essential aid in risk assessment.
1.1
Objectives of the study
The purpose of this study is to investigate and improve the understanding of the transport pathways
and mechanisms between the contaminant point source, aquifer, and drinking water supply. The
study focuses on the current pesticide contamination with dimethachlor, DMC, metabolites in the
Slimminge area. Specifically, the study will focus on a point source located west of Slimminge
well field, where contamination of DMC and DMC metabolites has been detected. Flow and solute
transport modelling will be used in combination with field data, to gain a better understanding and
prediction of the possible fate and transport for the pesticide contamination.
This study will therefore focus on the following objectives:
• Building an updated conceptual model of the local geology, hydrogeology and spatial dis­
tribution of the DMC metabolites. This will be based on a literature review as well as field
and laboratory work.
• Evaluation of the fate and transport of the pesticides, including the effect of hydraulic con­
ductivity, pumping rate, and geological heterogeneity, by design and implementation of flow
and solute transport models.
• Application of the enhanced conceptual understanding of pesticide fate and transport to as­
sess the risk posed to the local water supply.

Background
2.1
Risk assessment
In Denmark, the regions are responsible for handling the risks from contaminated sites, where
the polluter cannot be held responsible. The process involves several steps, including tracing and
mapping of contamination, risk assessment and potential remediation (Olsen et al., 2022). The
steps are shown in Figure 2.1, where it is seen that during each step, some sites will be omitted,
and in the end, it is only a few sites that will be remediated.
The regions are categorising all types of potentially contaminated and contaminated sites on two
levels, knowledge level 1, V1, and knowledge level 2, V2. Firstly, they conduct a desktop study,
where sites are mapped as V1 if there is knowledge of activities that may have caused contamina­
tion. Hereafter, preliminary investigations are conducted at the sites, and if soil contamination is
documented, the sites are mapped as V2 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023c). By the end of 2020, 18,659 sites
were mapped as V1 and 19,774 as V2 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023a). This systematic approach is crucial
for prioritising which contaminations need further action (Olsen et al., 2022).
If a site is mapped as V2, the region assesses whether there is a need for more detailed inves­
tigations, where the risks are evaluated. Afterwards, it is decided if remediation is needed. This
approach aims to protect human health, the aquatic environment in surface water, and groundwater
used for drinking water (Olsen et al., 2022). The regions prioritise contamination that poses a risk
to groundwater in OSD areas and important drinking water catchment areas.
Figure 2.1: The steps of the regions’ effort to detect, investigate and remediate contaminated sites.
Modified figure from Overheu et al. (2011).
Groundwater contamination can originate from either point sources or diffuse sources. Point
sources define sources of limited aerial expanse, but where there typically are high concentra­
tions, while diffuse sources cover a larger area with lower concentrations. If the groundwater is
contaminated, the responsibility for managing the contamination depends on whether it originates
from a point or diffuse source. The regions are responsible for the point sources, and the munic­
ipalities and the Danish Environmental Protection Agency, EPA, are responsible for the diffuse
sources (Roost et al., 2022).

Risk assessment of point sources can be approached on a local scale and a catchment scale. The
local scale focuses on the risk from individual contamination sources. In contrast, a catchment
scale approach is a tool for prioritising all different sources in the catchment to ensure that all sites
contributing to downstream contamination are accounted for. A catchment scale approach still
requires risk calculations for each individual source (Overheu et al., 2011).
If the groundwater contamination poses a risk to the drinking water supply, the risk assessment is
traditionally based on a comparison of the contaminant concentration in the groundwater with the
quality criteria at a selected point of compliance. If the groundwater quality criterion is exceeded,
it will usually result in further field investigation or remedial action (COWI, 2001). Estimation of
the contaminant mass discharge, CMD, is another vital tool for a risk assessment. CMD [M/T] is
the amount of contaminant mass discharged from a source over time. In contrast to a concentration
at a selected point of compliance, CMD has the advantage that it can be directly compared to other
CMD’s. A larger CMD will affect an aquifer more than a smaller one. This is not always the case
for concentrations. CMD is therefore often an important addition to the risk assessment (Overheu
et al., 2011).
Assessing the risk of groundwater contamination requires knowledge of the type of contamina­
tion, including the size of the source area, physicochemical properties, and concentrations of con­
taminants. Furthermore, it requires knowledge of geology and hydrogeology in the area, such as
the porosity, hydraulic conductivity, and organic carbon content of the different layers, net pre­
cipitation, hydraulic gradient, and redox conditions (COWI, 2001). The risk assessment is often
supported by the use of models (Locatelli et al., 2017). Model simulations can be used to provide
insights and support investigations of contaminant transport and fate. Combining fieldwork and
modelling simultaneously during a risk assessment can give an initial conceptual understanding,
which can improve the precision of future field investigations and measurements, while additional
data continuously can improve the model (Mosthaf et al., 2016). Through scenario modelling, a
range of potential outcomes and future scenarios can be assessed. Furthermore, the effect of differ­
ent geological conditions and different transport and fate scenarios can be investigated by varying
input parameters such as dispersivity, sorption, degradation and CMD (Andersen et al., 2008).
The contaminants that pose the highest risk to drinking water nationally are pesticides (Danske
Regioner, 2020). In recent years, there has been an increase in the detection of pesticides in water
supply wells, and pesticides are now the most common reason for the closure of drinking water
abstraction wells (Olsen et al., 2022; Region Syddanmark, 2018). This is among other reasons due
to a broader range of pesticides being included in the Water Works Well Monitoring Programme,
presented in Miljøministeriet (2023), than previously (Olsen et al., 2022).
2.2
Pesticide contamination
Pesticides are a large group of chemicals used to protect plants by controlling pests. Widely used
pesticides include herbicides, fungicides and insecticides (US EPA, 2023). Pesticides and their
metabolites can be present in groundwater due to various activities such as agriculture, forestry,
gardening, and landscaping (Thorling et al., 2023). Pesticides in groundwater can come from
either point sources or diffuse sources. Point sources are typically sites where pesticides have been
handled and spilt, often at storage and washing areas in agricultural or horticultural areas. Diffuse
sources usually occur in relation to agricultural application of pesticides (Roost et al., 2022).
Pesticides can have several potential degradation pathways depending on the specific site condi­
tions. Certain pesticides can degrade into metabolites that are more problematic than the parent
compound (Ottosen et al., 2013). The pesticide metabolites are often more problematic as they
tend to be more persistent and mobile than their parent compound, which increases the risk of
leaching to the groundwater (Kiefer et al., 2019). As a result, pesticide metabolites have gotten

2.2.1
Legislation
In Denmark, the general political view is that groundwater should only need a simple water treat­
ment with filtering and aeration before it is sent to consumers (Bennekou & Weile, 2020). Quality
criteria have been defined for both drinking water and groundwater to ensure safe drinking wa­
ter. In Denmark, the quality criteria for drinking water and groundwater requires that the concen­
tration of individual pesticides and pesticide metabolites does not exceed 0.1 µg/L and the total
concentration of all pesticides and metabolites should not exceed 0.5 µg/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 2021b;
Miljøministeriet, 2023). These values are set in accordance with the requirements for the quality of
water intended for human consumption in the EU Drinking Water Directive (European Parliament,
2020).
2.2.2
Risk assessment and challenges
Even though pesticide contamination is a widespread issue, and the pesticide approval phase re­
quires in­depth investigation of the properties of the compounds, a lot is still unknown regarding the
fate and behaviour of metabolites in the subsurface (Hintze, 2020; Ottosen et al., 2013). Pesticides
cover a large group of substances that each behaves differently due to their individual chemical and
physical properties (Olsen et al., 2022). Field­based studies investigating the fate of metabolites in
soil and groundwater are scarce. As a result, investigations are often based on model simulations or
laboratory studies (Hintze, 2020). For many problematic pesticide metabolites, properties such as
degradation rates have not been tested under natural conditions and have mainly been tested for the
aerated topsoil (Ottosen et al., 2013). Generally, the degradation of pesticides is faster under aer­
obic conditions than anaerobic, which means that the redox conditions are an essential parameter
for the transport of pesticides (Ottosen et al., 2013) Each of the pesticides behaves differently due
to their varying physicochemical properties. It is, therefore, necessary to evaluate each pesticide
as an individual compound during a risk assessment (Ottosen et al., 2013).
2.3
Dimethachlor
This thesis focuses on the pesticide dimethachlor, DMC, and selected DMC metabolites. This
section will provide relevant fate and transport properties for DMC and its metabolites. For a more
comprehensive description of the current knowledge, reference is made to the work conducted by
Schott (2023).
2.3.1
History and use
DMC (cas number 50563­36­5) is a pre­emergence herbicide belonging to the herbicides group
chloroacetanilide (PPDB, 2023a; Roost et al., 2022). It is currently approved for use in several EU
member states, including France and Germany, and in the UK for oilseed rape protection (PPDB,
2023a; Roost et al., 2022) The herbicide is the active ingredient in the pesticide product Teridox
500 EC, which was sold in Denmark from 1980 to 1990, with the largest sale in 1990, after which
DMC was no longer approved in Denmark. DMC has only been used for agricultural practices and
even though the herbicide was only applied for a decade, the metabolite, DMC ESA, is currently
being measured in 2.2% of the quality controls of water supply wells (Roost et al., 2022).
2.3.2
Physiochemical properties
DMC has a low volatility, a high water solubility, and a high sorption to soil (PPDB, 2023a; Roost
et al., 2022). DMC is non­persistent in aerobic soil and has a half­life of 2.3­15.6 days (EFSA,
2008; PPDB, 2023a). Based on the short half­life, DMC is not expected to leach to the groundwater
(PPDB, 2023a; Roost et al., 2022). EFSA (2008) have presented data from anaerobic laboratory
experiments conducted to determine DT50 values for DMC. A DT50 of 11.3 d was obtained from
an experiment conducted with clay loam at 25 ◦C and a value of 19.4 d with silt loam at 20 ◦C.

2.3.3
Dimethachlor metabolites
Several degradation products have been identified for DMC. This thesis focuses on the two major
metabolites DMC ESA (also called CGA 354742) and DMC OA (also called CGA 50266) as
well as CGA 369873, which is a degradation product of DMC ESA. The compound CGA 369873
is the sodium salt of the metabolite (2,6­dimethylphenylcarbamoyl)­methansulfonic acid. These
substances are very similar, and will henceforth both be referred to as CGA 369873. The chemical
names and structures for these metabolites are shown in Appendix A.
A full overview of all known degradation pathways for DMC can be seen in Appendix B. It is seen,
that DMC under aerobic conditions can degrade via four different routes (A, B, C and D). DMC
ESA is formed under degradation pathway A. This is a gluthathione pathway, where the chlorine
atom on DMC is replaced by sulphur. DMC ESA can be further oxidised to form Dimethachlor­
carbonyl sulfonic acid, which then can be degraded to among others, CGA 369873. CGA 369873
can be degraded to CGA 37732 or CO2.
The second major metabolite, DMC OA, is formed under pathway D. On this pathway, dichlori­
nation and subsequently hydroxylation of the acetyl group on DMC leads to the formation of CGA
39981. CGA 39981 can then be oxidized DMC OA. Under anaerobic conditions, DMC can by
reductive dechlorination be degraded to CGA 42443. If CGA 42443 is exposed to aerobic con­
ditions it can be oxidised to form CGA 39981, which then can be oxidised to DMC OA, as with
pathway D. Degradation of DMC OA leads to high amounts of non­extractable residues, NER,
and CO2 (EnviPath, 2023). NER refers to a residue that cannot be disassociated or separated by
extraction without undergoing changes or modifications (IUPAC, 2006). Unfortunately, there are
knowledge gaps regarding the precise degradation pathways and the composition and behaviour
of NER (Schott, 2023).

3.1
Geology
The geology in the area around the site and Slimminge well field can be divided into the Quaternary
deposits, which consist mainly of clayey till, and Pre­Quaternary deposits, which consist of the
Greensand Formation and Danian limestone (NIRAS, 2023).
3.1.1
Quaternary deposits
The geology in the area has been investigated several times (NIRAS, 2023; Orbicon, 2018; Schøller,
2018; WSP, 2023). In the initial phase of this study, a geological profile of the geology from the
site to Slimminge well field was created by Tyge (2023b), as part of a project with the consultancy
company NIRAS and Region Zealand, see Appendix B. No wells have been constructed on the
field between the site and the abstraction wells. Therefore, the profile is based on knowledge on
the geology obtained from pre­existing wells located at the site, from the abstraction wells and
general knowledge of the geology at the well field.
From the geological profile in Appendix B, it is seen that the Quaternary deposits have a thickness
of 10­15 m and consist primarily of clayey till in the area around Slimminge well field. Locally,
smaller deposits of meltwater sand are embedded in the clayey till. These sand deposits are not
expected to be connected (NIRAS, 2023). The Quaternary deposits lie directly on top of the pre­
Quaternary units in most of the area. Occasionally, the layers are separated by meltwater sand and
8

gravel (Orbicon, 2018). Generally, the deposits just above the Greensand Formation are affected
by Pre­Quaternary deposits and typically have a greenish­grey colour with clusters of Greensand
limestone. The central part is lighter and more sandier, and above that a homogeneous silty, slightly
sandy grey/dark grey clayey till is observed. The near­surface layers consist of shifting layers of
sand, gravel and clayey till (NIRAS, 2023).
3.1.2
Pre­Quaternary deposits
The pre­Quaternary deposits are located at approximately 15 m DVR90, marking the top of the Se­
landian Greensand. The Selandian Greensand is also known as the Lellinge Grønsands Formation,
and will henceforth be referred to as the Greensand formation. It is a marine deposit characterised
by the content of the mineral glauconite, which only forms in marine environments. The formation
lies on top of the Danian limestone and has a thickness of approximately 25 m in the area around
Slimminge (Orbicon, 2018;NIRAS, 2023).
The Greensand limestone consists of largely varying deposits with marl, sand, clay and silt (Klit­
ten, 2003; NIRAS, 2023). Jensen (2003) found that the Greensand formation is largely heteroge­
neous as tests had shown that it constituted of several smaller permeable layers (aquifers) sepa­
rated by less permeable (aquitards) of varying thickness. Furthermore, Klitten (2003) found that
the Greensand formation can be characterised by the presence of 8 high­permeable layers based
on log­stratigraphy. The three lowermost layers are relatively thick clay and marl layers with a
few layers of hard limestone. The three intermediate layers are frequently shifting layers of thin
clay/marl layers and more or less prominent limestone layers. The two uppermost layers are more
clayey with fewer prominent hard limestone layers (Klitten, 2003). The layers which consist of
more silt, marl and sand are generally weakly to strongly hardened, while the more clayey layers
of the Greensand formation are unhardened. The limestone layers are typically strongly hardened
and some even silicified (Orbicon, 2018). The hardened layers are typically very permeable while
the marl layers have a significantly lower permeability (Jensen, 2003). The top of the Greensand
formation might be glacially eroded, and it is unknown which of the 8 high permeable layers, de­
scribed by Klitten (2003), are present in the area (Tyge, 2023a). However, resistivity logging from
two wells in Vigersted, close to Slimminge, shows respectively 7 and 8 significant resistivity peaks
throughout the formation (Klitten, 2003). These can be interpreted as approximately 0.5 m thin,
porous and more permeable layers occ. The layers are embedded within more dense 2­5 m thick
layers. It is expected that thinner high permeable layers will be present at the Greensand beneath
the site and Slimminge wellfield (NIRAS, 2023).
3.2
Hydrogeology
The Selandian Greensand and the Danian limestone constitute the primary aquifer in the area (Or­
bicon, 2018). The Greensand formation has large heterogeneity with layers of varying permeability
(Tyge, 2023a). The formation is found to have a very high water­bearing capacity due to both the
primary porosity and secondary fracture porosity (Jensen, 2003). The most permeable horizons are
mainly due to fractures in the harder limestone layers (Klitten, 2003). This results in the hydraulic
conductivity, K, of the Greensand formation generally being higher and the effective porosity gen­
erally lower than that of other Danish limestone aquifers (Kidmose et al., 2022). The general size
and orientation of the fractures are unknown (Tyge, 2023a). Kidmose et al. (2022) found that water
abstraction mainly occurs in the fractured part of the aquifer as the hydraulic conductivity in the
matrix is very low compared to the fractured part. To account for both the primary and secondary
porosity an effective porosity of 0.1­0.11 is estimated for the Greensand formation (Kidmose et al.,
2022; Tuxen et al., 2013). It is currently unknown if there is a coherent secondary aquifer in the
sand lenses in the Quaternary deposit (WSP, 2023).

Boundary conditions
To solve the partial differential equation, it is necessary to establish boundary conditions for each
side of the model domain (Sonnenborg & Henriksen, 2005). For this study two types of boundary
conditions were used: Dirichlet and Neumann no­flow boundary. The Dirichlet boundary condi­
tion specifies a fixed hydraulic head, which can be determined based on measurements of hydraulic
head (Mosthaf, 2021b; Sonnenborg & Henriksen, 2005). The second type of boundary, Neumann,
specifies a no­flow boundary (Sonnenborg & Henriksen, 2005). No­flow can result from geologi­
cal conditions such as an impermeable layer and non­geological conditions such as a groundwater
divide or no flow perpendicular to flow lines (Mosthaf, 2021b; Sonnenborg & Henriksen, 2005).

5.2
2D side view model
The top view model does not account for the geological variation in the primary aquifer. However,
as described in section 3.1, the geology varies significantly within the Greensand formation. In the
side view model, both the clayey till and the Greensand formation are included. The aim of this
model is to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the metabolite transport from the source
through the clayey till and into the primary aquifer, as well as their subsequent transport through the
Greensand formation and towards the wells. Different geology scenarios for the Greensand forma­
tion are investigated with the side view model to provide insights into the influence of geological
heterogeneity including the presence of high permeable layers, such as fractures. Moreover, the
side view model aims to provide a more detailed understanding of the risk posed to the well field
considering the expected concentration development and contamination duration in the abstraction
wells. In contrast to the top view model, a constant contamination flux will not be used in the side
view model. Instead, an input concentration based on the current measurements and the projected
spill duration at the site will be integrated.

The initial understanding of the geology in the area is described in section 3.1. In the following

6.1
Characteristics of the Greensand Formation in the study area
As described in subsection 3.4.2, the Greensand formation starts at approximately 10 m DVR90.
The knowledge regarding the Greensand Formation in the study area is limited, primarily due to
the limited samples from the Greensand below the site and between the site and the abstraction
wells. However, borehole profiles and flow logs from nearby wells indicate significant stratifica­
tion within the formation as described in section 3.1.
During this study, the heterogeneity of the Greensand formation was further investigated. Ad­
ditional information regarding the abstractions wells was collected from HOFOR. HOFOR per­
formed a flow log in 2013 in the southern well in the model domain, abstraction well 212.1678.
The location of the well is shown in Figure 3.1. The flow log can be seen in Figure 6.1 and shows
that the flow occurs within a few distinct horizons and/or fractures. Approximately 25% of the flow
originates from a depth of 10.5 m DVR90, while an additional 30% comes from 6.4 m DVR90 and
45% from 5 m DVR90. The matrix thus only accounts for a few percentages of the overall flow,
suggesting that the flow predominantly occurs through thin layers or fractures with high perme­
ability. No other flow log data has been found for the three abstraction wells. However, the results
from the flow test in 212.1678 fit with other flow logs from the Greensand formation, presented
in Klitten (2003), which also showed the presence of thin high permeable layers between low
permeable layers. Furthermore, at another site near Slimminge, measurements of the hydraulic
conductivity in the Greensand formation showed high variation with values ranging from 10−3
to 10−6 (Orbicon, 2018), which support shifts in permeability through the formation. Figure 6.1
gives more information on the specific depth and potential thickness of these highly permeable
layers at the top of the Greensand formation. It was therefore used to estimate the placement of
high permeable layers in the modelling.

• It is assumed that the wells have operated at a constant pumping rate throughout the years.

now closed, have been utilized previously. The streamlines might therefore have changed
over time.
• Unknown pesticide point and/or diffuse sources are contributing to the contamination and
not solely the investigated site.

Sensitivity analysis
A local sensitivity analysis was performed for selected input parameters for the top view model by
changing the parameters individually ±10% and evaluating the effect on the final concentration
in the abstraction wells as described in section 4.3. The sensitivity indices of the parameters are
shown in Figure 9.3. The results of the local sensitivity analysis revealed that the porosity, the
degradation rate, and the hydraulic conductivity were the most sensitive of the tested parameters.
Based on these results, it will be advantageous to prioritise a thorough investigation of these pa­
rameters to improve the reliability and accuracy of the model predictions. The results emphasize
the relevance of considering the variation in porosity and hydraulic conductivity throughout the
Greensand formation. In Figure 9.3, it is seen that Kd has a very low sensitivity index. It should
be noted that the sensitivity analysis evaluates the sensitivity of the concentrations after 40 years.
Here sorption is no longer of great importance as the system has reached a steady state. If the sen­
sitivity at the breakthrough time was analysed instead, the sensitivity index for Kd would likely
have been higher.

the point source to the abstraction wells. The illustration is based on the results presented in chap­

conceptual understanding, presented in chapter 5, the metabolites have after 40 years leached out
of the upper part of the clayey till. The plume’s centre is now located in the lower part of the clayey
till, specifically the lower parts affected by the underlying Greensand formation. At the site, the
main contaminants are DMC OA and DMC ESA. Other metabolites include the degradation prod­

been measured more than 1,000 times higher than the groundwater and drinking water criteria. The
vertical distribution of the contamination has been delineated through data from earlier investiga­

site has not yet been achieved to the north.

the southern well increased, the plume would get a more southern direction and some would reach
the southern well 212.1678. At the same time, an investigation of the effect of the pumping rate
showed that the final concentration in the abstraction well increased if the pumping rate decreased.

well.

based on model simulations showing that the currently measured concentrations in the abstraction
wells are already at the higher end of what will be measured in the well. The model simulations
indicated that the contamination from the site will affect the abstraction wells for the next 70­80
years but with a decline in the concentrations within the next few years. HOFOR is abstracting
very large amounts of groundwater and has so far been able to reduce the concentrations through
dilution. It is expected that the utility company will be able to maintain the ability to reduce the
concentration below the quality criterium through dilution. Although the contamination from the
site is not expected to be a significant risk for HOFOR’s water abstraction wells, continuous mon­
itoring is recommended to track any changes as instability in the flow field could affect the final
concentration in the well. A catchment­scale risk assessment is recommended to ensure that any
additional contamination including both point and diffuse sources is not overlooked, as this could
lead to a higher concentration in the abstraction wells over time compared to what is expected
solely from the site.There is a potential risk of concentration increasing in one well if the pumping
rate decreases in another well. To better understand and manage the risks associated with pesticide
contamination, it is essential to conduct more detailed investigations. More samples should be
conducted at the site to delineate the contamination to the north to know the full scale of the con­
tamination and to be able to estimate a more precise CMD from the site. At this stage, remediation
actions are not recommended.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to enhance the conceptual understanding of pesticide fate and transport, specifi­
cally focusing on the pesticide contamination of the Slimminge well field in central­eastern Zealand.
This was done through literature review, field and laboratory work, and design and building of
flow and solute transport models in COMSOL Multiphysics ®. The work focussed on the three
dimethachlor (DMC) metabolites, DMC OA, DMC ESA, and CGA 369873.
The key findings of this study are as follows:
• The clayey till beneath the source area is relatively homogeneous, only containing a few
sand lenses of unknown extent. The vertical distribution of DMC metabolites showed that
the centre of the contamination plume is concentrated in the deepest part of the clayey till,
just above the primary aquifer. The horizontal distribution towards the north remains to be
delineated, suggesting a potentially larger source area.
• The heterogeneous primary aquifer constituted primarily of Lellinge Greensand, consists of
thin highly permeable layers or fractures in­between low permeable layers. The groundwater
flow in the area predominately occurs within the high­permeable layers, while the matrix
only contributes to a few percentages of the overall flow.
• Flow and solute transport models showed that, under the current hydraulic condition, the
contribution from the site to the contamination of the three abstraction wells will be mainly
to well 212.1389, and that the concentration of DMC metabolites either has peaked or will
peak within the next few years from 2023. Hereafter, the concentrations are expected to
decline for the next 70­80 years until they are fully washed out of the system.
• Changes in the abstraction wells’ pumping rates can affect the final concentration in the
abstracted water and influence the direction of the pesticide plume.
• The thin highly permeable layers in the Greensand formation could lead to larger capture
zones than previously anticipated and will dominate the solute transport pathways and sig­
nificantly increase pesticides’ transport times, resulting in earlier breakthroughs at the ab­
straction wells.
• Based on the enhanced understanding of the pesticide fate and transport, the investigated
point source will not pose a significant risk to the abstraction wells as the concentrations
are not expected to increase to an extent the water supply will not be able to dilute the
contamination to below the quality criteria. While remediation is not recommended for the
site, it is recommended with ongoing monitoring, especially if the pumping rates change
significantly.
12.1
Future work
This study modelling DMC contamination and as with many pesticides, very little is known about
its metabolites’ physicochemical properties. For future work, modelling performance could be im­
proved with better estimates of degradation rates as it would decrease uncertainties in the results.
Furthermore, the CMD and the pulse input concentrations were estimated based on the current
knowledge of the contamination at the site. A full delineation of the contamination to the north
would further improve this estimate. This study indicated that additional sources may contribute to
the contamination found in the abstraction wells. Investigation of the effect of a diffuse source in
the modelled scenarios could be relevant for a catchment scale risk assessment. Furthermore, test­
ing the effect of including other abstraction wells in simulations could be relevant as the flow fields
could be more complex than previously assumed. This could be some of the previous abstraction
wells in the south to investigate how the plume could have moved in the past.

M.2
Pump test in well 212.1349 used for calibration of hydraulic
conductivity
A pump test was conducted in 2003 in well 212.1349 by Ke­Partner a/s Brøndboreafdelingen
(2004). The pumping rate was 40 m3/hr and lasted for 6 hours. The test resulted in a drawdown of
1.2 m. The pump test was simulated with the 2D top view model by adjusting the pumping rate of
well 212.1349 and turning only this well on and off. The corresponding change in the hydraulic
head at the well was calculated for different values of K, and the resulting drawdowns are shown
in Table M.2. This test indicates that a K of around 55 m/d gives a change in head closest to the
observed.
