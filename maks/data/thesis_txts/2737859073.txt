Abstract
With the incorporation of an increasing amount of intermittent electricity production, energy
storage systems (ESS) has garnered a significant attention due to their viability in addressing
various grid-balancing challenges. However, many different types of ESS exist with differing
suitabilities in relation to solving different challenges. In this study, the operation behaviours
and investment attractiveness of different types of ESS in the Nordic Region are examined and
compared. Specifically, the study is based around three types of ESS, namely a short duration
battery (SD-battery), a long duration battery (LD-battery) and an electrolyzer with storage
system (EwS-system).
By developing a distinct revenue maximizing model for each type,
insights are obtained about how a large number of technology specific considerations impact its
utilization under the current market conditions and regulations. While obvious differences exists
between the battery-systems and EwS-system, distinguishing between technical considerations
for the battery types also proved to be important. The most impactful distinction is shown to
be market qualifications, where the LD-battery misses a lot of revenue due to exclusion from
the FCR-D market. Additionally, large revenue differences occur depending on their strategies
for recovering potential activation.
Findings indicate, that even with perfect price foresight, revenues are mostly comprised of the
availability payments from delivering services, which can be increased considerably by stacking
both frequency containment services and frequency restoration services. Based on the revenues
from the modelling, all three technologies are deemed profitable if the market conditions are
to remain as currently over the full lifetime of the technologies. However, the current market
conditions can not be considered representative in the long term. Thus, to investigate the effects
of more stabilized market conditions, a future market scenario is estimated by replacing the very
favorable Nordic FCR market with the German FCR market. While the market regulations are
slightly different, this approach offers a much more balanced bidding behaviour for the ESSs,
where the SD-battery obtains an IRR of 17.7% and the LD-battery an IRR of 12.0% - with the
values being quite sensitive to capital cost and tariffs. However, the EwS can no longer recover
the cost of buying energy to produce hydrogen. Only an exemption of tariffs can enable the
EwS system to turn a profit.

Introduction
Due to climate change, a great amount of pressure is applied to the integration of renewable
energy sources into the energy system.
While renewable energy technologies such as solar
and wind power are vital to decarbonizing the energy system, their inherent intermittency in
production introduces significant challenges in balancing supply and demand in the electricity
grid[1]. Therefore, the need for solutions to provide energy flexibility is higher than ever. Energy
flexibility refers to adjustable production or consumption and can be used to maintain stability
in the power grid. Historically, energy flexibility could mainly be supplied by conventional
power plants with adjustable production. However, with the increasing amount of intermittent
production, solutions have to be found in other places. While some energy flexibility can be
provided by solutions such as flexible demand, energy storage systems (ESS) have been revealed
to be a crucial component of maintaining balance in modern energy systems. ESS refers to a
range of technologies which can store energy, such that it can be used at a later point in time,
thereby smoothing out fluctuations between production and consumption.
From the perspective of the transmission grid, various services are required to mitigate grid
instability and large electricity price deviations. These services include peak shaving, frequency
regulation and load leveling. Many different types of ESS technologies exist, each with different
characteristics and capabilities. Among the ESS technologies, battery types have been recog-
nized for their ability to efficiently deliver many of these services. Batteries which can charge
or discharge their full energy capacity within a relatively short duration (SD-batteries), such at
lithium-ion batteries (Li-batteries), are known to be relatively affordable in addition to having
fast response times and efficiencies. Thus, the technology is an ideal candidate for deliver-
ing short-term balancing, such as frequency regulation. Battery technologies which requires
a longer duration for charging or discharging their full energy capacity (LD-batteries), such
as Vanadium-redox-flow batteries (VRF-batteries), are able to deliver services which require
charging or discharging over an extended period of time. On the other hand they are known
to be more expensive and have slightly lower efficiencies in comparison.
In addition to the battery type ESSs, green hydrogen production and storage systems have
gained an enormous amount of attention in recent times. With the launch of the RePowerEU
plan, Europe has committed to increasing the amount of renewable hydrogen production. This
establishes hydrogen systems as a cornerstone of Europe’s future energy system[2]. Through
real-time adjustment in hydrogen production, it has been revealed that an electrolyzer with
storage system (EwS-system) can provide many of the services demanded by the transmission
system[3]. This dual-capability of EwS-systems, which enables them to produce hydrogen while
also offering grid balancing integration, positions them as a very versatile solution in the energy
transition.
Each of the mentioned technologies can potentially play vital roles in the energy system, but
the exact utilization ultimately depend on the technologies’ economic viability. It is therefore
crucial to understand and evaluate how the operation of the different ESS, each with individ-
ual characteristics, affect profitability both under current and future market conditions. This
information, can help investors make more informed decisions, as potential risks and returns of
each technology would be more clarified. Additionally, policy makers and grid operators can
use the information to enable a better utilization of resources and potentially offer more cost
effective solutions from the system perspective.

1.1
Literature Review
In the literature, previous attempts have been made to characterize and compare ESS in terms
of both operational behaviour and economic attractiveness. Due to the complex nature of ESS
modelling, [4] has reviewed various challenges and desirable behaviours for different types of
analyses. The paper provides the basis for how battery modelling is approached, and describes
how different capabilities of the storage technologies can be captured, including efficiency and
degradation. Additionally, the implication of a having a price-taker model assumption is de-
scribed, namely that the result can only be considered accurate if the modelled ESS is not large
enough to impact the market price itself. Battery energy storage systems are investigated in [5]
from a business perspective. The study is employing an empirical methodology and examines
price patterns in the frequency containment services in eastern Denmark from 2015 to 2020.
Using the prices they found that the revenue which could be gained for a battery mainly consist
of the availability payments for reserving capacity and not for the actual energy delivery. They
also found a significant increase in revenue by stacking multiple different frequency containment
services which the battery can deliver.
In [6], the value of delivering frequency restoration services for a battery is examined in different
setups. They conclude that higher revenue potentials could be obtained by delivering frequency
containment services instead, but does not investigate the benefits of stacking both.
An assessment of battery profitability is made in [7], where multiple revenue streams are con-
sidered in the German market while taking into account battery degradation. In the study it is
established that when assessing profitability of ESS, the internal rate of return (IRR) is more
appropriate compared to other profitability measures such as net present value (NPV).
The importance of considering strategies for recovering potential activation when delivering
frequency regulation services is described in [8]. The study examines different strategies for
recovering the state of energy, which have large impacts on revenue.
In terms of EwS-system modelling, [3] examines how the frequency regulation services of Eastern
Denmark can be delivered while producing hydrogen. The study finds a considerable revenue
increase in the short run, but does not examine the lifetime of the system. The study is very
accurately modelling the operational states and non-linear efficiencies of the electrolyzer. In [9],
the required complexity of EwS-system models is investigated, for a wind farm connected EwS-
system. They find that linear approximations will underestimate the hydrogen production. At
the same time, excluding the standby- and off- states negatively impacts revenue. Using a
simple linear model without the standby-state, they estimate that revenues decreases with 2%
and hydrogen production with almost 15%.
In [10], various types of electrochemical batteries and hydrogen energy storage technologies
are examined, for which they have conducted a comprehensive data collection of parameters
and costs from many recent studies. The study compares the levelized costs for which Li-
batteries had the lowest of the common battery types, although it is associated with some
uncertainty. In this regard the VRF-battery had the most uncertainty but with the potential
to outperform the other batteries. For the hydrogen technologies, they found that the setup with
the lowest levelized cost of storage, was to use an alkaline electrolyzer and do hydrogen delivery
by pipeline. Lastly, [11] and [12], also estimates battery costs, but also provides projections of
future developments.

1.2
Contribution and Research Question
This study aims to model and compare the operational dynamics and economic feasibility of
different types of ESS’s within a multi-market framework. The focus is on three types of ESS:
SD-batteries, LD-batteries, EwS-systems. The study will be guided by two research questions.
The first question relates to the modelling of each technology:
How can the operational dynamics of different energy storage technologies be modelled within a
multi-market framework to estimate realistic annual revenues?
Addressing this question involves capturing the unique operational characteristics of each ESS
technology. This study assumes perfect price foresight and simplifies all dynamics to be linearly
described and of continuous nature. However, it integrates a large number of elements, which
in the literature are deemed impactful. These factors include efficiencies, battery degradation
and complex market regulations in addition to uncertain activation and associated recovery
strategies. Additionally, the analysis includes the stacking of multiple markets, combining both
frequency containment and restoration services. By doing so, it aims to provide a reflected
and comparable estimation of how these technologies perform in real-world market conditions,
offering insights into their revenue potential.
The second question builds upon the findings of the first and aims to examine the full life-
time of the technologies to determine and compare the profitability of potential investments.
Additionally, it addresses the sensitivity to changes in market conditions:
How does the energy storage technologies compare as investment options in current and future
conditions?
Using the insights from the literature, the technologies are compared based on their internal
rate of return. The novelty of this study lies in its many modelling considerations and how
the impacts of future conditions are examined. By incorporating multiple factors and ensuring
the models are comparable, this research provides a thorough evaluation of the operational and
economic viability of different ESS technologies.

ally, opportunities for future work are presented.

Market and Technology Overview
This chapter contains background knowledge relevant for the project, which serves as the foun-
dation for the modelling of energy storage technologies. Relevant markets in which revenues can
be obtained for storage technologies will be presented along with their participation criteria and
characteristics. Subsequently an overview of three representative energy storage technologies is
provided. The necessary considerations and revenue impacting technical parameters for both
operation and long term analysis are described. Finally, ESS costs and parameter configuration
is determined.
2.1
Energy Market Overview and Data
Energy markets refers to all markets in which energy, or services related to energy are traded.
Physical products such as gas, heat and electricity can be traded, but also grid balancing services
and rights to capacity lines are traded on the markets. Through the participation on one or
multiple energy markets, energy storage technologies can be used to obtain revenue. Energy
storage technologies are especially significant in relation to the electricity grid, as balancing of
supply and demand has to be made in real time. The wholesale market refers to all markets
related to the trading of electricity and balancing of the grid. It can be divided into three
parts with differing purposes: the day-ahead market (DA-market), the intraday market, and
the ancillary service markets. In the DA-market and intraday market, energy is traded, while
in the ancillary service markets, both energy and availability of capacities are traded to ensure
grid stability.
Denmark contains two different electricity price zones, ’DK1’ and ’DK2’.
While it is not
uncommon for countries to have different price zones, Denmark is somewhat special as each
zone is a part of different synchronous areas. DK1 is part of the Continental Europe synchronous
area, while DK2 is in the Nordic synchronous area. While the zones are interconnected and
has a similar structure, the design and regulations of their ancillary service markets differ. In
this study, technologies are, as a starting point, modelled based on the DK2 price zone.
The recent energy crisis in Europe has significantly affected the energy market, leading to high
volatility and price spikes. In figure 2.1 a historical overview of the average monthly DA-market
prices and standard deviations can be observed. Prices can be seen to somewhat stabilize and
return to more reasonable levels in 2023, making it a suitable year to choose as a starting point
for this study.
In the following sections, each market relevant to the analysis is shortly described. For each
market, hourly price data is required for the analysis along with activation data for the ancillary
service markets.

2.1.2
Ancillary Service Markets
Ancillary service markets are different types of markets meant to ensure reliable operation of
the electricity system. While many services exist, this study only deals with the services related
to the transmission system - i.e. services procured by the transmission system operator (TSO).
On the ancillary service markets both capacities and energy is traded. When a technology
participates on a capacity market, an amount of power is reserved, which can potentially be
activated if the grid requires it. The activated energy is further described in section 2.1.3.
Historically, ancillary service markets in Denmark have been referred to as the primary, sec-
ondary and tertiary reserve - each with different purposes[16].
The primary reserve refers
to the ’Frequencey Containment Reserves’ (FCR), the secondary reserve refers to the ’auto-
matic Frequency Restoration Reserves and the tertiary reserve refers to the ’manual Frequency
Restoration Reserve’ (mFRR). FCR services requires the fastest response time but is the least
energy intensive service, while mFRR is the opposite.
The mFRR and aFRR services exists both in the Central European synchronous region and the
Nordic synchronous region. These are both used to restore the grid frequency to normal levels
in case of imbalances. Both services are asymetric markets, meaning that both up and down
regulation can be traded, but for mFRR only up regulation is procured in the DK2 price zone.
As the name suggests, the aFRR service is used as an automatic response, while the mFRR is a
manual signal sent by the TSO. The danish TSO, Energinet, uses the mFRR service proactively
to counter expected imbalances[17].
The primary reserves of the Nordic region are split up to more products, namely: the ’Fast
Frequency Reserve’ (FFR), the ’Frequency-controlled normal operation reserve’ (FCR-N) and
the ’Frequency-controlled disturbance reserve’ (FCR-D) which includes both up and downwards
regulation. The role of the FCR services is to reduce the magnitude of the grid frequency
deviations. In this way, the aFRR and mFRR services has time to respond on longer duration
deviations. An overview is provided in table 2.1. Note that FFR is not included in the analysis
of this study.

2.1.3
Activation
When participating on a capacity market, you have to be able to deliver energy to balance
the grid - in other words, the technology can be activated proportionally to the bid size. This
activated power is priced at the balancing price.
While the activations from the delivered services are unknown, the expected magnitude and
frequency of activation differs for each service. In general, primary reserves are more frequently
activated while the second and tertiary reserves has a higher total magnitude upon activation.
As the conditions in the electricity grid changes and market designs are continually improved,
the future activation behavior is not necessarily gonna stay the same. Nevertheless, for the
analysis, hourly data for 2023 describing the expected activation for each delivered service is
required to give an indication of the dynamics. It should be noted that each service’s activation
behavior cannot be fully described on an hourly scale, however, the amount of both upwards and
downwards activation can be measured. As mentioned, the activation magnitude is assumed
to be proportional to the bid size of any delivered service, making the required data a set of
fractions.

2.2
Energy Storage Technologies and Parameters
The ESS term refers to a broad range of technologies for which energy can be stored and utilized
at a later point in time. There is no universal standard of how to optimally operate a storage
technology to gain the most revenue, as both markets conditions and technology capabilities
differ a lot. However, technologies with similar capabilities can also be considered likely to
operate similarly given the same market conditions. Many different classifications of ESS types
can be made, but this study deals with examining SD-batteries, LD-batteries and EwS-systems.
For each ESS type, a representative technology has been selected to illustrate its operational
principles and costs. The technology chosen for SD-batteries is a Li-battery, for LD-batteries it
is a VRF-battery and for the EwS-system an alkaline electrolyzer connected to a compressor,
storage and pipeline.
Understanding the operational dynamics of these technologies requires a thorough grasp of
their limitations and capabilities, typically defined by a specific set of technical parameters.
While the exact choice of parameters depends on the nature of the technology, the technical
parameters ultimately define which markets the technology can participate on, but also how well
it can be utilized on those markets. In the following sections, the three chosen energy storage
technologies will be reviewed along with the technical parameters describing their potential for
energy market qualifications and performance.

The Li-battery and VRF-battery are based on a the same system layout, in which they are

in figure 2.3. It can be seen that an efficiency is applied to all charged and discharged energy.

3
ESS Modelling and Operation
In this chapter, the modelling of ESSs is addressed. The modelling aims to provide insights
into how different categories of energy storage technologies are best utilized on the energy
markets. Additionally, an expected yearly revenue for each technology is required to assess the
investment potential in chapter 4. Both an SD-battery and an LD-battery is examined. As
explained earlier the SD-battery is based on a Li-battery and the LD-battery on a VRF-battery.
Subsequently, an alkaline EwS-system is modelled using simple assumptions, to compare the
operational behavior.
All technologies are modelled using a shared set of assumptions, which are presented in the
beginning of the chapter. Afterwards, models of the battery technologies are presented. Each
battery type has their own characteristics and limitations, necessitating two distinct models to
capture the differences in operational decisions and market participation strategies. Despite
the differences, many aspects of the modelling for the battery types remain similar. Therefore,
the chapter will introduce the battery system overview and assumptions, followed by a general
battery model formulation, containing the core mathematical framework applicable to both
battery types. Following the general model, the chapter will explore the specific modifications
and constraints required to more accurately represent each battery type. After the battery
models, the model for the EwS-system is presented and formulated. The chapter concludes by
examining model results with a comparison of each model’s operation.
3.1
Modelling Framework and Assumptions
There are many ways of approaching the modelling of energy storage operation. The choice of
approach ultimately defines the scope of the analysis and the validity of the results. Therefore,
the models’ system and boundaries are developed in a way that balances the complexity required
to capture operation dynamics and the generalizability required for long term analysis. Each
model is set up as a continuous linear optimization problem which makes decisions for each
hour. The models aim to maximize the revenue gained within a predetermined time frame.
Prices are assumed to be known giving the models perfect-foresight, which is used to determine
the optimal strategy of a technology under the given assumptions.
While the prices are known, the activations from the capacity market bids are not. Thus, in
the problem formulation it is assumed that a bid can only be made such that the technology
operation stays within a feasible range regardless of the potential realized activation. A rolling
horizon approach is used to estimate the operation over the full year. This is done both to
minimize the size of the problem, but also to simulate a limited forecast horizon. In figure
3.1, a visualization of the rolling horizon approach can be observed. The model has a perfect
forecast of prices over the optimization horizon, in which it determines the optimal decisions for
the battery. Over the control horizon, those decisions are locked in, but the decisions outside
the control horizon are discarded. Instead the starting state of energy (SoE2) is updated, and
the model is run again with a new optimization horizon. This is done until the end of the
year is reached. For the analysis, the model’s optimization horizon has been chosen as 8 days
(T = 8 · 24), while the control horizon is set to 7 days.
2Often ’state of charge’ is used to represent the current energy level of an ESS as a percentage. The SoE

The assumptions make the model quite transparent, but they introduce some limitations to
the model. Firstly, as the models operate on an hourly basis, all dynamics happening within

minimum bid size. This can have a very large impact for technologies with a very small energy or
power capacity, however, the limits should not be violated often if the technology is sufficiently
large.
Another aspect that cannot be captured without some form of price uncertainty is
the effects of market gate closure times, as such a model would rely on gradually revealing
information. This approach deals with different choices of strategy which is outside the scope
of this study.
3.2
General Modelling of Batteries
While two different battery technologies are modelled, many aspects of the models are similar.
Therefore, a general model formulation applicable to both battery types is first presented and

Investment Potential
In this chapter, the investment potential of the three technology types will be examined and
compared. Using simple assumptions, cash flows over the full lifetime of each technology can
be estimated and used to compare the economic attractiveness from an investor’s point of view.
This entails that the chapter does not intend to give a very detailed description of the lifetime
cash flows, but aims to give a comparable estimate of each technology’s economic performance.
4.1
Methodology and Assumptions
To assess the economic potential of the technologies, a discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis is
performed. A DCF analysis is a project-evaluation method to determine the present value of
a set of cash flows[30]. First of all, it is important to note that everything is based around an
estimation of the future, thus, it is uncertain if conditions will stay as expected for the whole
lifetime.
The method takes into account the time value of money, which means that further into the
future you go, the less impactful a cash flow is for the profitability. This is because there is a
cost associated with raising capital. The exact cost varies for each individual company as it
reflects how much a future cash flow from the project is worth to the company. It depends on
both debt, equity, lost opportunities and risks. To reflect the cost, a yearly discounting factor
(D) is applied to all cash flows. In this study it is set to 8%.
To determine the value of the cash flows, a set of parameters and assumptions are required.
All costs associated with installation is considered included in the CAPEX and all operational
costs not accounted for in the modelling is considered fixed by the year. The company tax rate
(TX) in Denmark is 22%[31] and the inflation rate (I) is assumed to be fixed at 2% for the
future, corresponding to the target of the Central European Bank[32]. When the technology
reaches the end of its lifetime, the decomissioning costs and scrap value are assumed to be
net zero. A technology in operation is depreciated linearly over its lifetime. In this study, the
expenses related to financing of an asset are not included.
The yearly revenue for each technology was calculated in chapter 3 and is considered fixed in real
terms for every year in operation. In the analysis it is assumed that the investment potential
is calculated before a potential investment is made. Therefore, to simplify the calculations, the
full cost of a technology is paid at the end of the planning year (year 0) and starts operation
at the start of the following year. The operation then lasts until the end of the lifetime (LT).

The investment can be considered profitable if the NPV is above 0. However, the NPV metric

to provide insights into how the future market conditions and tariff adjustments could impact
the long term profitability and viability of the energy storage technologies.

6
Discussion
In the previous chapters, results related to the modelled operation, investment potential and
outlook for the three technologies was presented. In this chapter, the insights are synthesized
and discussed in relation to the research questions.
6.1
Modelling Insights
Through each of the three models developed in the technology modelling, an attempt has been
made to capture the the operational behaviours of an SD-battery, an LD-battery and an EwS-
system. The models aim to make bidding decisions within a complex multi-market framework
while taking into account the operational criteria. The behaviours of the technologies have
been captured by implementing different technology specific considerations.
While each model takes into account the different limitations and characteristics of their re-
spective technology, they have all been developed with shared set of modelling assumptions and
simplifications. This is done with the intention of capturing the effects of a large number of
factors, while maintaining a format in which the technologies’ bidding and operational decisions
can be compared. While the assumptions open up for some opportunities they also introduce
certain limitations to the model.
6.1.1
Technology-Specific Considerations
From the modelling, it is evident that technology-specific considerations are crucial for ac-
curately representing operational dynamics. The distinct characteristics of the technologies
and market participation requirements necessitate tailored modelling approaches. While the
Li-battery and EwS-system could participate on all markets, the VRF-battery was assumed
to not be able to deliver services on the FCR-D market. It is obvious that the battery- and
EwS-system have some inherent differences, however, the distinctions made between short and
long duration batteries proved to also be very important. When modelling battery types, it
is essential to differentiate between LER and non-LER units due to their differing operational
constraints and recovery strategies. The recovery strategy of each technology turned out to be
a very important consideration as it can have a large impact revenue. While the technologies
did not have a very large total cost for the recovered amount of energy, differences in revenue
occurred due to the capacity reservation for potential recovery. The VRF-battery, categorized
as a long-duration battery, benefits significantly from a simple recovery strategy that reserves
less capacity. This approach resulted in more than double the revenue compared to using the
same recovery strategy as the short-duration Li-battery. The degradation cost consideration
turned out to not be very significant for both batteries with the chosen parameters, as they
both have a high number of lifetime cycles. With that said, adding the degradation cost def-
initely changed some decisions which reduced the cycling. Thus, it can be theorized that the
consideration can be very important for batteries in which the number of cycles becomes a
problem.

6.1.2
Modelling Limitations
Since the models are used to examine operation over at least a year, they are simplified to
be fully linear and continuous. Additionally, perfect foresight was assumed for prices in the
planning horizon, but to not capture an unrealistic behavior of the technologies, activation was
considered uncertain. Because of the uncertainty in the model, the realized revenue obtained
could only be calculated after the decision making.
Making the model linear and continuous introduced some limitations to each model. Firstly,
any non-linear behaviours in efficiencies had to be linearized. It is generally not considered
to cause large inaccuracies for batteries, but for the electrolyzer, the behaviour could be very
inaccurate.
By assuming that all variables had to be continuous, the models were unable to enforce the
minimum bid sizes present for some markets. The effect of this limitation should not be very
impactful for the revenue, as the technologies were scaled to minimize the amount of impossible
bids. A more impactful limtiation is present in the EwS-system’s model, which can cause a
large overestimation of electricity and tariff costs. When using only linear variables, the standby
and off-states of the electrolyzer can not be implemented. Therefore, the model is required to
always be in the on-state, thus, it has a minimum consumption of electricity in each hour. This
means, that even in hours where no considerable revenue is possible, the system will consume
electricity and be charged tariffs - resulting in a net negative revenue.
Perfect foresight of prices undoubtably overestimates the revenue which a technology can obtain,
as it completely eliminates the element of risk associated with bidding decisions. With that
said, the fact that activation is considered uncertain, the bids has to take into account both
the feasibility and riskiness of potential activation. Since the models does not account for the
stochastic behaviour of activation, it was proposed to introduce an expectation, or very rough
forecast, for activation on each service. However, for markets where the activation fluctuates a
lot, the average activation proved not to be a very good indicator of the actual activation. It
was shown that in some cases, it was actually better to not expect any activation compared to
using an oversimplified forecast.
6.2
Investment Attractiveness
To compare the three technologies as investment options, the lifetime cash flows for each tech-
nologies had to be accounted for. For all the technologies, the cash flows were simplified to be
represented by an investment cost, fixed operational expenses and yearly revenues. This en-
tails, that the analysis is based on an assumption of market conditions and dynamics remaining
constant throughout the whole lifetime. By taking into account tax, depreciation and inflation,
the lifetime cash flows could be obtained. Using these along with a reference discount rate,
the value of each investment could be estimated. While costs related to financing (i.e. loan
payments) is not included in the yearly cash flows, the discount rate should to some extent
reflect the costs associated with raising capital.
By using this method, all three technologies were shown to be profitable, with IRR estimates
of 64% for the Li-battery, 10% for the VRF-battery and 12% for the EwS-system - which are
all above the reference discount rate. Comparing these estimates with the literature, the VRF-
battery’s IRR is within an expected range but the IRR’s for the Li-battery and the EwS-system

6.2.1
Bidding Behaviour and Future Market Expectations
In the revenue calculation, the value of stacking markets was examined.
As expected, the
revenue increased proportionally to how many markets were considered, however, it increased
by a very large amount when the FCR-D market was included. It could be observed that
the technologies would almost exclusively gain revenue on this market, which is suspicious,
as literature examining the markets for previous years did not find the FCR-D market to be
particularly more attractive[5]. The prices in 2023 for the DK2 FCR-D services can therefore
not be considered representative for the full lifetime of the technologies. This explains why
the Li-battery and the EwS-system gained such high IRR’s, as the analysis is assuming market
conditions to be constant.
To better represent the expected market conditions for the future, the FCR market of DK2 was
substituted with Germany’s more mature market. With the Central European regulations, the
VRF-battery also qualifies for participation, but still favors the FRR services slightly more than
the Li-battery. In general the revenue distributions are much more balanced between markets,
than previously. The Li-battery’s IRR is lowered to 17.7% while the VRF gains a higher IRR
of 12.0%. However, without the favorable FCR-D market, the EwS-system will not be able
to recover the minimum cost of buying electricity, which means the plant is not expected to
be profitable. The main reason is that the hydrogen produced sells for much less than the
associated costs of buying electricity with tariffs.
This study have not taken into account the speed of which the market will transition, which
would require a market saturation analysis. However, since the markets use marginal pricing,
has a limited procurement and the technologies are profitable, a fast transition is to be expected.
6.2.2
Sensitivity of Technology Costs, Availability Price and Tariffs
Using the future market expectations, the results’ sensitivity to change in a selection of pa-
rameters was examined. It became clear that the profitability of the battery technologies are
heavily dependant on technology capital costs. This means, that an accurate estimation of the
cost is very important, making the obtained IRR estimates somewhat uncertain, since the total
CAPEX was simplified to scale linearly with energy and power capacity. The availability price
of the FCR market can change the IRR of the batteries. With a 25% decrease accross all FCR
prices, the technologies the IRR approaches levels where the profitability can be questioned.
By changing the other availability prices individually, the impacts were not especially high.
Both consumer and producer tariffs are very significant when assessing profitability of battery
technologies. While increasing producer tariff will negatively affect the IRR, changes in con-
sumer tariffs will ultimately have a higher impact. Interestingly, the EwS-system starts to be
profitable when the consumer tariffs approaches zero.
Like it was theorized in the literature[3], a grid connected EwS-system can definitely increase
the revenue to some extent by participating on ancillary service markets - however it requires
very favorable prices or policy changes to make it profitable with the examined setup. Another
approach could be to change the system setup, such that a power purchase agreement with a
local power producer could be made.

Conclusion and Future Research
In this study, the operation and profitability of different types of ESS in the Nordic Region
has been explored. The analysis is based on the 2023 market conditions in DK2, but also
investigated a future, more stabilized, scenario by replacing the frequency containment reserve
markets with the German FCR market and conducting sensitivity analysis.
Through the first research question, the operational dynamics of different ESSs were investi-
gated. Three different models have been developed with the aim of capturing the behaviour
of an SD-battery, an LD-battery and an EwS-system. Through the modelling, insights were
obtained about how different technology specific considerations affected each ESSs utilization
on the different energy markets.
From the models it was clear that each technology had their own operational distinctions based
on both their system layout and technical parameters. It was found that by stacking markets
additional revenue could be obtained for every technology. The significantly largest amount of
revenue was gained through the availability payments from the ancillary service markets. The
most impactful distinction between the battery types was that the LD-battery was excluded
from participation on the FCR-D market, which resulted in much lower revenues. Additionally,
the strategy for recovering potential activation proved to have a large effect - especially for the
LD-battery, which could more than double its revenue by employing a less strict strategy. In
the models, all captured dynamics were assumed to be of linear and continuous nature, which
simplifies the modelling, but introduces some inaccuracies, especially for the EwS-system.
In the second research question, the lifetime investment potential of each of the ESSs was
examined.
In the current DK2 market conditions all three types of ESS was shown to be
profitable, provided that the market conditions would remain the same throughout their whole
lifetime. The SD-battery demonstrated an exceptionally high IRR of 63%, while the LD-battery
had an IRR of 9.6% and the EwS system 12.4%. It was indicated that the current market
conditions would not be representative in the long-term.
Therefore, the Nordic frequency
containment markets were replaced with the current German FCR market to represent more
stabilized market conditions. For these market conditions, the ESSs revenues were much more
balanced between the different markets.
The batteries’ IRRs were estimated as 17.7% for
the SD-battery and 12.0% for the LD-battery, however, the EwS-system would not be able
to recover the costs of producing hydrogen. Finally, the IRR for each battery was shown to
be rather sensitive to changes in CAPEX-estimates, tariffs and FCR-prices. The EwS-system
could only approach profitability by being exempt from tariffs.

Future Research
Further research can be conducted in extension of this study, for which some paths are suggested
below. Some of them can improve the validity of the results and others increase the explanatory
power of the model.
Comparison of Prices Accross Multiple Years
In the results for the current market conditions it was clear that the FCR-D service was espe-
cially favorable for the technologies. Since other studies did not encounter this in previous years
an obvious extension would be to compare the revenue modelling with prices across multiple

Optimal Technology Composition and Sizing
In this study, parameters and sizing of the technologies were simply based on rough estimates.
Optimizing the technology composition and sizing might show some different operational dy-
namics and revenues.
Employment of Different Recovery Strategies
The recovery of activation proved to be a significant factor affecting revenues. More effective
strategies are already present in the literature[8] and can be utilized for more favorable revenue
estimations.
Including Non-Linear Constraints and Integers
A lot of dynamics can not be represented linearly and continuously. For example the hydrogen
storage modelling are underestimating revenues by the simplified assumptions.
Representing the Activation Stochastically
In this study it was examined if a simple mean activation forecast could provide enough infor-
mation to increase revenues. The effect was not large, and was in some cases detrimental to
the revenue. A better approach would be estimating the activation stochastically, such that
the model could make decisions based on the potential realizations.
Utilizing Price Forecasts and Gate Closures
The assumption regarding perfect information on prices makes the model much simpler to work
with. However, for a more realistic operation, the model can employ price forecasts - either
with simple expectations or with forecasts of stochastic nature. Further expanding upon this,
the model can take into account multiple stages, in which certain recourse decisions can be
made upon the realization of information.

A.2
Activation Data Description
Figure A.1: Historical activation for the capacity services.[17]
Looking at the historical activation on figure A.1, it is clear how the approximate activation
distributions should look like (allthough the year is 2022). As explained in the report, the DK2
aFRR activation data does not match the expected distribution, and is therefore substituted
with the DK1 data.
Relative activation is thereby obtained as the fraction between DK1
activation and procurement.
Both the DK1 and DK2 mFRR data does not match the expected activation. It is clear from
the data that not all activation signals are captured, as the mean activation is only 2.78%. By
very roughly approximating the hours and activation in each segment on the shown figure, the
expected mean is determined to be around:
(20
44 · 0.05) + ( 8
44 · 0.15) + ( 9
44 · 0.3) + ( 4
44 · 0.5) + ( 3
44 · 0.8) = 21.1%
The collected data shows very rare activation, so every hour is set to the daily mean. Then it is
scaled with the factor 21.1
2.78. As a few incidences of activation over 100% are now present, these
are simply set to 1. In this way, a very (very) rough approximation of the real activation is
made. On figure A.2, the time series and distribution of the approximated mFRR activation is
shown. While the approach is very flawed, this distribution is much closer to the expectation.
The approximation is made in the file ”Load data.py”.
