Abstract
Seaweed is a crop that does not require arable land, freshwater, or fertiliser. Seaweed could
therefore be a sustainable source of biomass e.g. for the production of biofuels. One of the
main types of polysaccharides present in brown seaweed is alginate, which makes up 30-60 %
of the polysaccharide content, the rest being mannitol and glucan, which can readily be fer-
mented and used industrially. To fully utilise seaweed as biomass the degradation of alginate
has to be further investigated.
The last step of alginate degradation to be completely understood is the conversion of 4,5-
unsaturated monouronates to 4-deoxy-l-erythro-hexoseulose uronic acid (DEH), which is catal-
ysed by the enzyme KdgF. This project investigated the structure and function of KdgF from
the bacterium Bacteroides eggerthii.
13C-15N-labelled BeKdgF was produced recombinantly in E. coli and puriﬁed using aﬃnity
chromatography. A 1H, 13C, and 15N chemical shift assignment of BeKdgF was obtained for
the backbone (> 92 % assigned) and sidechains (> 59 % assigned). The chemical shifts were
used for a dihedral torsion angle prediction, which showed the secondary structure for 89 % of
the BeKdgF amino acid residues as being 50 % β-strands and the rest loops, bends, or random
coils with no α-helices present.
The dynamics of BeKdgF at ps and ns scale was investigated using NMR spectroscopy, which
showed that BeKdgF has ﬂexible terminals, while the rest of the protein was rather feature-
less regarding dynamics. The rotational correlation time of BeKdgF was calculated as τc =
15.3 ns ±0.7 ns, which correlates with the molecular size of ≈25−30 kDa indicating that
BeKdgF is in a dimeric form.
Upon titration with ZnCl2, BeKdgF seems to aggregate, which might be indicative of tetrameri-
sation in the presence of Zn2+ ions. The pKa values of seven amino acid residues were found
using pH NMR titration, including a pKa-value of 5.9 ±0.1 for the active site residue D102. An
NMR aﬃnity analysis showed no binding between BeKdgF and d-mannuronate and possibly
a weak binding between BeKdgF and l-guluronate.
This project further characterised the structure and function of BeKdgF, which can contribute
to the industrial use of seaweed and alginate as biomass.

Introduction
1.1
New sustainable sources of biomass are necessary
There is a growing demand for biomass. A report from the Climate Knowledge and Innova-
tion Community at the European Institute of Innovation & Technology (EIT Climate-KIC)
concluded that the biomass use in Europe will increase 70−150 % by 2050, as biomass can
replace fossil fuels and thus help reduce the CO2 emission of EU (Material Economics 2021).
But this increase in biomass consumption is ≈40−70 % more than can be sustained by current
EU resources, and it is not given that biomass import can be increased, as countries around
the world face a need to reduce pressure on their nature (Material Economics 2021).
Seaweed grows faster than land plants and does not require arable land, freshwater, or fer-
tiliser (Enquist-Newman et al. 2014). Therefore, seaweed has a large potential as a sustain-
able addition to conventional sources of biomass e.g. to produce biofuels to replace fossil fuels
(Duarte et al. 2017). Seaweed production also functions as a ’CO2 sink’ by binding CO2 in
biomass that gets deposited as sediment (Duarte et al. 2017).
1.2
Seaweed and alginate
Seaweed and seaweed components have always been utilised by humans for example as food
or food ingredients (e.g. (Rhein-Knudsen and Meyer 2021; Qin et al. 2018)). One seaweed
component with a large range of uses is alginate. Alginate is a family of polysaccharides that
constitute around 20−40 % of seaweed on a dry weight basis (Qin 2008), corresponding to
30−60 % of the polysaccharide content, where the rest of the polysaccharides in seaweed are
mainly mannitol and glucan (Enquist-Newman et al. 2014). Mannitol and glucan can readily
be fermented and used as biomass (Chades et al. 2018; Motone et al. 2016). Alginate is widely
used in the food industry for example as a thickener and gelling agent (Qin et al. 2018). In the
pharmaceutical industry, alginate is used, amongst others, to form hydrogels and for drug de-
livery (K. Y. Lee and Mooney 2012; Qin 2008). But alginate must be converted to compounds
that can be used in the current biomass industry, to make it feasible to use seaweed for biofuel
production.
Alginate is produced by seaweed and some bacteria species (Haug et al. 1967; Gorin and
Spencer 1966). Alginate consists of (1→4)-linked β-d-mannuronate (M) and its C-5 epimer α-
l-guluronate (G). The monosaccharides are ordered in blocks of G (PolyG), blocks of M (PolyM),
and blocks with alternating monosaccharides (PolyMG) (Haug et al. 1967; Atkins, Mackie, and
Smolko 1970). The diﬀerent polymer blocks can be seen in Figure 1.

1.2.1
Alginate degradation
Alginate can be degraded by marine bacteria (e.g. (Preston, Romeo, and Bromley 1986; Dou-
bet and Quatrano 1984)). Humans do not express alginate-degrading enzymes (Stender et al.
2019), but certain bacteria from the Bacteroides genus from the human gut microbiota have
been found to grow on alginate (e.g. (Li et al. 2016; Bai et al. 2017)). Alginate-degrading bac-
teria break down the polysaccharides to oligomers and 4,5-unsaturated monouronates using
enzymes called alginate lyases (Kim, C.-G. Lee, and E. Y. Lee 2011).
The monouronates are converted ﬁrst to 4-deoxy-l-erythro-hexoseulose uronic acid (DEH) by
a process previously assumed non-enzymatic (Kim, C.-G. Lee, and E. Y. Lee 2011), but it has
been discovered that this process is catalysed by the enzyme KdgF (Hobbs et al. 2016). DEH is
converted to the linear sugar 2-keto-3-deoxygluconate (KDG) by the enzyme DEH reductase
(Preiss and Ashwell 1962). An overview of the degradation of alginate to KDG is shown in
Figure 2. KDG is a key metabolite since it can go directly into the metabolic pathway being
an energy source for organisms as well as the feedstock for biofuel production (Conway 1992).

1.2.2
Formation of DEH
Figure 3: Conversion of 4,5-unsaturated monouronate to DEH can be catal-
ysed by base or the enzyme KdgF.
DEH is formed by ring-opening of a 4,5-unsaturated monouronate as shown in Figure 3, which
can occur spontaneously in the presence of water (Arntzen et al. 2021). The time scale of this
reaction is on the order of minutes to hours depending on the pH (Hobbs et al. 2016). With
such a slow reaction it might not be realistic that organisms have used spontaneous ring-
opening in their metabolism, so it would be plausible that an enzyme is used to catalyse this
reaction.
It has been proposed that the enzyme catalysing the conversion of monouronates is KdgF by
the reaction seen in Figure 3 (Hobbs et al. 2016). Since this enzyme has not been accounted for
previously, it has not been included in industrial microbial platforms for alginate degradation
(e.g. (Enquist-Newman et al. 2014; Wargacki et al. 2012)). With KdgF catalysing this reaction,
KdgF could be the last uncharacterised link in the enzyme chain degrading alginate from
complex polysaccharides to e.g. ethanol. The subject of this project is a further investigation
of the enzyme KdgF.

1.3
Protein structure
Protein structure can be described on four diﬀerent levels called the primary, secondary, ter-
tiary, and quaternary structure of a protein (Branden and Tooze 1991). The primary structure
refers to the amino acid sequence of a protein (Stollar and Smith 2020). Two generic amino
acids linked by a peptide bond can be seen in Figure 4. The backbone of a protein refers to the
main chain of amino acids joined by peptide bonds, and the sidechain is characteristic for each
amino acid (Stollar and Smith 2020). Amino acid nomenclature denotes the sp3-hybridised
carbon of the backbone as Cα, the ﬁrst carbon of the sidechain as Cβ and so on (Stollar and
Smith 2020).
Figure 4: Two generic amino acids linked by a peptide bond. The circles
show the backbone (blue) and sidechains (red) of the amino acids. The dou-
ble bond character of the amide is shown with a dashed line. The φ and ψ
angles are indicated with arrows.
The secondary structure of a protein refers to how the amino acid chain folds as a result of
hydrogen bonds between backbone carbonyls and NH groups (Stollar and Smith 2020). Since
the amide bond has double bond character as a result of the resonance structure, there is not
free rotation around the bond (Stollar and Smith 2020). Rotation can therefore only take place
around the bond between Cα and the carbonyl (rotation angle φ), and around the bond between
Cα and NH group (rotation angle ψ) (see Figure 4) (Stollar and Smith 2020). Because of steric
clashes between sidechains, not all φ and ψ angles are possible (Ramachandran, Ramakrish-
nan, and Sasisekharan 1963). Two types of folding maximise backbone hydrogen bonding
while avoiding steric clashes: α-helices and β-sheets (Stollar and Smith 2020). α-helices are
right-handed coils where the NH-group of an amino acid residue forms a hydrogen bond to
the carbonyl of the amino acid residue four residues before (see Figure 5a) (Stollar and Smith
2020). β-sheets are two or more peptide chains (β-strands) that form hydrogen bonds to each
other, and the strands are either parallel or antiparallel arranged (see Figure 5b) (Stollar and
Smith 2020).

The tertiary structure of a protein refers to its three-dimensional structure and the combina-
tion of the diﬀerent secondary structural elements that are linked by loops and bends (Stollar
and Smith 2020). The tertiary structure is generally a result of sidechain interactions (Stollar
and Smith 2020). The primary, secondary, and tertiary structure describes one subunit of a
protein, whereas the quaternary structure of a protein describes how more than one subunit
combine to form a larger protein complex (Stollar and Smith 2020).

1.3.2
Determination of protein structures
General information about the structure of a protein can be obtained with techniques like cir-
cular dichroism and ﬂuorescence (Stollar and Smith 2020). But to determine the full structure
of a protein the most widely used experimental techniques are X-ray crystallography, cryogenic
electron microscopy (cryo-EM), and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy (Stollar
and Smith 2020).
To make an X-ray crystallography analysis a protein is crystallised to form an ordered arrange-
ment of protein molecules (Allen, Seng, and Larson 2009). Then an X-ray beam is focused on
the crystal, and the X-rays get diﬀracted as a function of electron density in the protein (Allen,
Seng, and Larson 2009). The resulting diﬀraction pattern can be used to calculate the electron
density map for the protein, into which a structural model consisting of amino acid residues is
ﬁtted (Allen, Seng, and Larson 2009). The advantage of X-ray crystallography is that proteins
of a wide range of sizes can be analysed (Stollar and Smith 2020). Protein structures with a
resolution better than 1 ˚A is rare (Allen, Seng, and Larson 2009). The largest disadvantages
are that the protein needs to be in a crystal form, which is not the native state of the protein,
and in a crystal dynamic elements of the protein are not well-characterised (Stollar and Smith

For cryo-EM the protein sample is cooled to and kept at temperatures below 150 °C and struck
by an electron beam, generating a set of images of the protein (Stollar and Smith 2020; Hen-
derson 2004). These images are grouped and averaged to calculate a 3D image of the protein
(Stollar and Smith 2020). Compared to X-ray crystallography, cryo-EM has the advantage that
diﬀerent conformations can be determined from the same sample (Stollar and Smith 2020).
Cryo-EM also requires less sample than both X-ray crystallography and NMR (Stollar and
Smith 2020). With cryo-EM 3D maps with a resolution of down to 1.2 ˚A can be achieved (Strack
2020). A disadvantage of cryo-EM is that it is diﬃcult to obtain the structure of proteins with
a size lower than ≈100 kDa because they lack recognisable features that can facilitate initial
image alignment, but new techniques are breaking this size limit (Wu and Rapoport 2021). A
pitfall of cryo-EM is noise overﬁtting. If using a structure template for data analysis, there
is a risk of selecting noise biased towards the desired structure and thereby observing noise
instead of protein particles (Henderson 2013).
In NMR spectroscopy the spin magnetic moment of magnetically active nuclei (e.g. 1H, 15N,
or 13C) is exploited by subjecting a molecule to a strong magnetic ﬁeld and nudging nuclei
with a radiofrequency pulse (Stollar and Smith 2020). The nuclei generate an oscillating
electrical current, which is recorded as the Free Induction Decay (FID) and translated via a
Fourier transformation to an NMR spectrum (Stollar and Smith 2020). Besides determin-
ing the structure of a molecule in solution, NMR spectroscopy is useful for investigating the
function of a protein, for example by characterising interactions between a protein and other
molecules or investigating protein motions in solution (Stollar and Smith 2020). Structural
investigations with NMR spectroscopy is mainly possible for proteins smaller than 30 kDa,
where X-ray crystallography and cryo-EM can be used for structural determination on much
larger proteins (Takeuchi, Baskaran, and Arthanari 2019).
NMR spectroscopy and structural and functional characterisation of proteins using NMR spec-
troscopy will be further described in the following section 1.4.

1.4
NMR spectroscopy for protein analysis
Some nuclei including 1H, 13C, and 15N have intrinsic angular momentum (spin) and magnetic
moment (Claridge 2016). In NMR spectroscopy these properties are used to gain information
about the nuclei by subjecting a sample to an external magnetic ﬁeld (B0) (Rhodes 2017).
There will be a slight excess of nuclei that are oriented parallel to B0 instead of anti-parallel
(Claridge 2016). A radiofrequency pulse is applied to the sample, which changes the distribu-
tion of the nuclei, and the signal measured in NMR spectroscopy occurs as the nuclei relax back
to equilibrium (Claridge 2016). The nuclei relax by dipole-dipole, chemical shift anisotropy,
spin rotation, and quadrupolar mechanisms (Claridge 2016). The relaxation is described by
the longitudinal relaxation time (T1) and the transverse relaxation time (T2) (Claridge 2016).
In NMR spectroscopy the chemical shift (δ) describes the resonance frequency of nuclei rela-
tive to the magnetic ﬁeld that is a combination of B0 and the local magnetic ﬁeld generated
by the electron environment around the nuclei (Claridge 2016). Another information learned
with NMR spectroscopy is the spin-spin coupling between magnetically active nuclei, which
arises from the polarisation of the spins in chemical bonds (Rhodes 2017).
Using NMR gives the advantage of being able to observe a protein in solution at room tem-
perature, which is closer to its natural conditions, as well as it is very easy to change the
environment and conditions (Takeuchi, Baskaran, and Arthanari 2019). There are even ex-
amples of proteins observed while being in living cells (Sakakibara et al. 2009). The dynamic
behaviour of the protein can also be observed (Takeuchi, Baskaran, and Arthanari 2019). The
structure of a protein can be determined using NMR spectroscopy (Frueh 2014), and NMR is
an essential tool for characterisation of protein motions and interactions (Stollar and Smith
2020).
1.4.1
Challenges in protein NMR
The main disadvantage of NMR spectroscopy concerning proteins is that typically structural
determination is limited to proteins smaller than 30 kDa, which is especially due to the fast
transverse relaxation of large molecules (Takeuchi, Baskaran, and Arthanari 2019). Large
molecules have a slower tumbling rate resulting in more eﬃcient transverse relaxation, which
is a disadvantage, as it is during T2 that spin manipulation can be performed (Frueh 2014).
This results in lower correlation eﬃciency and thus lower signal-to-noise ratio, and therefore
also that some signals cannot be detected (Frueh 2014). The short T2 also results in line
broadening, which degrades the maximum resolution of the spectra (Frueh 2014).
The slow tumbling of large proteins reduces the longitudinal relaxation resulting in a long T1,
meaning that a longer time is needed for magnetisation recovery between recordings (Frueh
2014). Therefore the signal-to-noise ratio obtained within a certain time is lower for large
proteins than small molecules, and so the sensitivity of the NMR experiment is lower (Frueh
2014).

1.4.2
Coupling constants
Spin-spin coupling, also called J-coupling, is used for structure elucidation and magnetisa-
tion transfer in multidimensional NMR spectroscopy (Rhodes 2017; Sattler, Schleucher, and
Griesinger 1999).
1J, 2J, 3J and so on denotes the coupling constant between two nuclei, where the superscript
shows the number of bonds between the nuclei (Cavanagh et al. 2007). The size of the cou-
pling constants are independent of the strength of the magnetic ﬁeld, and 1J and 2J coupling
are largely independent on conformation (Sattler, Schleucher, and Griesinger 1999). As it
can be seen in Figure 7, the coupling constants between diﬀerent types of nuclei in a protein
are quite diﬀerent, and can therefore be used to distinguish between diﬀerent correlations
(Sattler, Schleucher, and Griesinger 1999). Therefore, the coupling constants 1J and 2J are
used when transferring magnetisation between nuclei to identify nuclei correlations in protein
NMR (Sattler, Schleucher, and Griesinger 1999).

1.4.3
NMR experiments for structural analysis
There is a vast amount of NMR experiments used to analyse the structure of proteins. In this
section, only the experiments used in this project are described. These include some of the
most common structural NMR experiments and thus give a good introduction to the possibil-
ities of structural analysis with NMR.
The simplest NMR experiment is the 1D single pulse experiment (Claridge 2016). A 90° pulse
is applied to the sample, followed by a detection period, t2, where the signal (FID) is generated
(Cavanagh et al. 2007). The real component of the FID is transformed to a frequency domain
for the 1D NMR spectrum by a Fourier transformation (Cavanagh et al. 2007).
Figure 8: Basic pulse sequence of a 2D NMR experiment, where the ﬁrst
pulse represents the preparation period (p), t1 describes the evolution pe-
riod (e), the second pulse represents the mixing period (m), and the FID is
shown in the detection period (d) (Claridge 2016).
2D NMR experiments have the same basic format which can be described as four periods:
preparation (p), evolution (e), mixing (m), and detection (d) (see Figure 8) (Claridge 2016). The
preparation and mixing periods consist of one or more pulses and/or time periods, and during
the detection period with the length t2, the FID is recorded (Claridge 2016). The length of
the evolution period, t1, is varied which inﬂuences the amplitude of the FID, so the resonance
intensity as a function of time represents a second FID for the t1 domain (Claridge 2016).
Two-dimensional homonuclear experiments
The 2D NMR experiments can be divided into homonuclear and heteronuclear experiments
depending on the type of nuclei involved in the experiment. For smaller proteins (MW ≤
10−12 kDa) homonuclear experiments can be suﬃcient to ﬁnd the protein structure (Ca-
vanagh et al. 2007). A combination of e.g. Correlation Spectroscopy (COSY), Total Corre-
lation Spectroscopy (TOCSY), and Nuclear Overhauser Enhancement Spectroscopy (NOESY)
experiments are typically used (Marion 2013). COSY experiments detect two or three-bond
correlations between protons based on coherence transfer through coupled spins (Cavanagh
et al. 2007). TOCSY experiments detect multiple-bond correlations between all protons in the
same spin system, which in proteins correspond to each amino acid residue (Cavanagh et al.

2007). NOESY experiments use dipole-dipole cross-relaxation correlations observed by the
nuclear Overhauser eﬀect (NOE) to detect correlations between proton that are less than 5 ˚A
apart in space (Cavanagh et al. 2007).
When proteins are larger than 80−100 residues, characterising the structure of a protein us-
ing only homonuclear NMR is diﬃcult or impossible due to increasing linewidth and spectral
overlap, thus heteronuclear experiments are used in addition (Marion 2013).
Two-dimensional heteronuclear experiments
Heteronuclear experiments investigate the correlations between 1H and a heteroatom, usu-
ally 13C or 15N, which is directly bonded to the proton (Cavanagh et al. 2007). Typically, 1H
polarisation is ﬁrst transferred to the heteronucleus, spin manipulations are made, and then
the coherence is transferred back to 1H magnetisation for detection, as proton detection in-
creases sensitivity (Cavanagh et al. 2007). The magnetisation is transferred via the coupling
constants described in section 1.4.2 (Cavanagh et al. 2007).
In this project, 1H-15N correlations are investigated using Heteronuclear Single Quantum Cor-
relation (HQSC) and TROSY experiments, and 1H-13C correlations are investigated using only
HSQC experiments. The method for structural characterisation of proteins will be explained
in section 1.4.4. The 2D heteronuclear experiments are the basis of 3D NMR experiments
(Cavanagh et al. 2007).

Three-dimensional heteronuclear experiments correlate backbone and sidechain 1H, 13C, and

Course of action for structural characterisation
Figure 9:
Flowchart over

In general, a protein structure characterisation using NMR
spectroscopy is done in steps shown in Figure 9 (Frueh 2014).
When analysing a protein using NMR spectroscopy, the ﬁrst
step is to make sure that the sample is pure, correctly folded,
and in a concentration of ≈0.1−2 mM, which can be assessed
using a 1D 1H and a 1H-15N-HSQC spectrum of the protein (Ca-
vanagh et al. 2007). By reacquiring these spectra after some
time, the protein stability can also be assessed (Frueh 2014).
The second step of structural characterisation is to identify the
backbone 1H-15N peaks of the protein. The starting point of
this is using a 1H-15N HSQC spectrum sometimes in combina-
tion with the HNCO spectrum (Frueh 2014). Each peak in the
1H-15N HSQC should be marked. Only 1H-15N HSQC peaks
that also show a 13C peak in HNCO stem from a backbone H-N
group, so the HNCO spectrum can be used to ﬁlter out peaks
not belonging to the protein backbone in crowded 1H-15N HSQC
spectra (Frueh 2014).
When all spin systems have been marked, the 3D spectra listen
in Table 1 are used to assign the backbone resonances of C’, Hα,
and Cα to the spin systems (Frueh 2014). It can be an advan-
tage to assign some sidechain chemical shifts at this point, as
they aid in the identiﬁcation of amino acid residue sequences.
When backbone resonances have been assigned to spin systems,
sequential amino acid residues can be identiﬁed using the in-
formation about the chemical shift of residues and their neigh-
bours to link residues to each other. Certain amino acid residues have unique chemical shifts
that can be used as checkpoints, making it possible to correlate fragment chains with the
amino acid sequence (Frueh 2014).
After the backbone of the protein has been mapped, the sidechains can be characterised by
using additional 3D NMR spectra, where the most important spectra are the 1H-15N NOESY
HSQC and HC(C)H-TOCSY (Frueh 2014). To adjust the 13C chemical shifts to more accurate
values, an aliphatic 1H-13C HSQC spectrum is used, since the sensitivity of this spectrum
is high (Frueh 2014). When the protein resonances have been assigned, the structure of the
protein can be characterised using computational methods (see section 1.4.5).
If a full structural determination is desired, distance constraints are necessary to determine
the fold of a protein (Marion 2013).

1.4.5
Determination of secondary structural elements
The software TALOS-N takes the amino acid residue type and the assigned chemical shift
values of the protein and uses an artiﬁcial neural network to predict the distribution of the
dihedral angles φ and ψ of the residue (Shen and Bax 2013). In this project, TALOS-N was
used to predict the dihedral torsion angles of BeKdgF. The dihedral angles can only take cer-
tain values in a protein (see section 1.3), so the dihedral angle analysis is also a validation of
the resonance assignment.
The dihedral angle values correlate to the secondary structure of the protein (Ramachandran,
Ramakrishnan, and Sasisekharan 1963). Based on the dihedral angle prediction, TALOS-N
determines the possibility of each amino acid residue being part of the diﬀerent secondary
structural elements (Shen and Bax 2013). In the prediction made by TALOS-N, the χ1 ro-
tameric state of the sidechain is included as a factor (Shen and Bax 2013).
TALOS-N also calculates the chemical shift derived order parameter called the random-coil-
index-derived order parameter (RCI-S2) (Shen and Bax 2013), which is used as an indicator
for protein ﬂexibility (Berjanskii and Wishart 2008).
1.4.6
Dynamic analysis of proteins using NMR
In section 1.4.1, the relationship between molecule size and the relaxation times T1 and T2
was described. The relaxation of individual amino acid residues in a protein can be mea-
sured, thus the dynamics of diﬀerent parts of the protein can be characterised (Kay, Torchia,
and Bax 1989). If a part of the protein has a shorter T1 and a longer T2 than the average of
the protein, that part of the protein is more mobile than the rest of the protein (Kay, Torchia,
and Bax 1989). It is relevant to measure both T1 and T2 as they are sensitive to diﬀerent
motional frequencies (Kay, Torchia, and Bax 1989). The T1 time provides information about
motional frequencies in the range of 10−8−10−12 s−1, where T2 is additionally also sensitive
to motions in the range of 10−6−10−3 s−1, so by measuring both relaxation times, information
about protein dynamic at a wide span of motional frequencies can be obtained (Kay, Torchia,
and Bax 1989).
The NOE of individual amino acid residues can also be measured, and a low NOE value indi-
cates that an amino acid residue is mobile in the protein (Kay, Torchia, and Bax 1989). The
combination of T1, T2, and NOE values gives a good indication of the dynamic properties at
the ps to ns timescale for a protein (Kay, Torchia, and Bax 1989).
T1 and T2 can be used to calculate the rotational correlation time (τc) (Kay, Torchia, and Bax
1989). τc for a protein in solution is deﬁned as the time it takes the protein to rotate one
radian (Rossi et al. 2010). There is a correlation between molecular weight and τc, which is il-
lustrated for known monomeric targets from the Northeast Structural Genomics Consortium
(NESG) in Figure 10 (Rossi et al. 2010).

1.4.7
pH titration using NMR spectroscopy
As there is an interest in understanding the energetics and electrostatics of proteins and pro-
tein complexes, ﬁnding the pKa values of proteins are often desired (Webb et al. 2011). For
measuring site-speciﬁc pKa values of amino acid residues, the method of choice is NMR spec-
troscopy observed pH titrations, where 1D or 2D spectra are recorded as a function of sample
pH (Webb et al. 2011). As the pH changes, the charge of ionisable groups changes, which
aﬀects the chemical shift of nearby nuclei through inductive eﬀects (Hass and Mulder 2015).
Plots of chemical shift vs. pH typically show Henderson-Hasselbalch titration curves, which
can be ﬁtted to the modiﬁed Hill equation (see section 3.2.12) (Webb et al. 2011).
Most titrating protons are unobservable due to fast exchange with solvent water, so the titra-
tion can be followed indirectly by surrounding nuclei, which enables observing the titration
event through several nuclei, but also introduces an uncertainty, as a given nucleus might be
aﬀected by several diﬀerent titration events (Webb et al. 2011). Correct measurement relies
on precise and correct peak assignment, and as the pH is changed, peaks move (often in non-
linear paths) which further complicates the tracking of chemical shifts (Webb et al. 2011).

Aim of study
This project aimed to characterise the structure and function of KdgF from Bacteroides eg-
gerthii. The project was divided into ﬁve objectives. To enable the investigation of KdgF, the
ﬁrst objective was to make a resonance assignment of BeKdgF. The second objective was to
predict and evaluate the secondary structural elements, quaternary structure, and dynamics
of the enzyme. As a previous study showed that KdgF has a higher activity with divalent
metal ions present, the third objective was to investigate the eﬀect on the protein structure
of the addition of zinc ions to BeKdgF. The fourth objective was to determine the pKa values
of relevant amino acid residues. Finally, the ﬁfth objective was to investigate interactions be-
tween BeKdgF and monomeric d-mannuronate and l-guluronate as analogues to the natural
substrates of the enzyme.

3.2
Methods
3.2.1
Recombinant protein expression
The protein production and subsequent puriﬁcation were based on the method developed by
Rønne et al. except for the use of M9 media instead of LB media for the main culture and
protein expression.
Escherichia coli (E. coli) BL21(DE3) T7 Express cells (New England Biolabs) containing a
pET15b-USER-BeKdgF plasmid (Rønne et al. In preparation) were plated on a petri dish con-
taining LB medium, agar (1.5 %, OXOID), and ampicillin (100 µg/mL, Sigma Aldrich). The
plate was inoculated with the transformed cells overnight at 37 °C.
One colony was transferred to a preculture containing LB media (25 mL) and ampicillin
(14 µg/mL, Sigma Aldrich) and incubated overnight (30 °C, 225 rpm).
Preculture (5 mL) was added to M9 media (500 mL) along with ampicillin (35 µg/mL, Sigma
Aldrich), and the culture was incubated (30 °C, 225 rpm) until the optical density of the me-
dia was between 0.6 and 0.8 at 600 nm. The culture was cooled on ice for 5 min. Protein
expression was induced by addition of isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (VWR) to a ﬁnal
concentration of 0.5 mM, and the main culture was incubated overnight (16 °C, 190 rpm). The
cells were separated from growth media by centrifuging (10 min, 4 °C, 5000 x g).
The cell pellet was resuspended in lysis buﬀer (25 mL) with one cOmpleteT M EDTA-free pro-
tease inhibitor tablet (Roche) and then lysed on an ice bath using sonication (total 10 min with
the following setting: 40 % amplitude, a cycle of 10 s sonication, 40 s pause) using a Branson
Soniﬁer equipped with a microtip. The cell lysate was centrifuged (30 min, 4 °C, 16000 x g),
and afterwards the supernatant was sterile ﬁltered with a 0.22 µm Stericup® Quick Release
Vacuum Driven Sterile Filter (Merck).
3.2.2
Aﬃnity chromatography
BeKdgF had been expressed containing a His-tag. Since histidine has a high aﬃnity for Ni2+,
the protein could be puriﬁed using a Ni2+ aﬃnity column.
3 mL cOmpete His-tag Puriﬁcation Resin (Roche) was placed in a Econo-Pac® chromatography
column (Bio-Rad) and rinsed with ethanol (20 %, 20 mL) and UPW (20 mL) and equilibrated
with 20 mL lysis buﬀer. The lysate was loaded onto the column for 30 min while gently mixing
the lysate and resin every few minutes. The resin was washed with ﬁrst lysis buﬀer (3 x 7.5
mL) and then washing buﬀer (8 x 7.5 mL). The protein was eluted using elution buﬀer in four
fractions of 7.5 mL and one fraction of 15 mL. All protein elution fractions were stored at 4 °C.

Secondary structure of BeKdgF determined for 89 % of residues
Overall secondary structure elements of BeKdgF were obtained by analysing the chemical
shift data. Here TALOS-N (Shen and Bax 2013) was used to make a dihedral torsion angle

ondary structural elements was also predicted based on the dihedral angle analysis. Both

secondary structure could be reliably predicted for 89 % of the amino acid residues. The di-
hedral angle analysis shows that ≈50 % of the amino acid residues have a high probability of
forming β-strands and the rest of the amino acid residues form random coils, loops, or bends
with a small possibility of a three amino acid residue α-helix present at residues 11−13.

4.6
The closest homolog to BeKdgF is YeKdgF
There are three published structures of KdgF from two diﬀerent species (Hobbs et al. 2016).
A sequence similarity analysis was performed to investigate how close in amino acid sequence
BeKdgF was to the published structures. Another purpose was to identify whether the active
site residues from the closest homolog were conserved in BeKdgF.
The sequence of BeKdgF was searched against the PDB database (Berman et al. 2000) using
the BLAST tool (Altschul et al. 1990). The lowest E-value was found for YeKdgF, so YeKdgF
is the closest published homolog to BeKdgF, and they have a 46 % sequence similarity. All
proposed active site and metal coordinating amino acid residues in YeKdgF are preserved in
BeKdgF, where the active site residues therefore supposedly are D102, F104, and R108.

The amino acid residue D102 is supposedly part of the active site in BeKdgF based on it being
conserved from a vital amino acid residue in YeKdgF (Hobbs et al. 2016). The conversion
of 4,5-unsaturated monouronates to DEH is supposedly base-catalysed (Hobbs et al. 2016;
Rønne et al. In preparation), and as D102 has an acid/base functionality, D102 is suggested to
be the catalytically active residue in BeKdgF. To further investigate the reaction mechanism
and support the claim of D102 being the catalytically active residue, the pKa value D102 was
determined by performing a pH titration and monitoring chemical shift changes with NMR
spectroscopy.
The pH titration was performed in the pH range 4.15−8.02. At pH lower than 5, the protein
became unstable, which was indicated by the appearance of new peaks, low signal intensities,
and some white precipitate observed in the NMR tube which indicates protein aggregation.
The titration was not continued above pH 8.02 as the maximal chemical shift change had been
reached. A change in protonation state could be reliably measured for D102 as well as six other
amino acid residues (E13, E17, E21, E66, D110, and E114) in the pH range 4.15−8.02. The
chemical shift change was used to calculate the pKa value of the residues. The pKa of D102
was determined to be 5.9 ±0.1. The observed 15N chemical shift change plotted as a function
of pH can be seen in Figure 18. The calculated values of the six other amino acid residues and
the plots of their chemical shifts vs. pH can be seen in Appendix F Table 8 and Figure 28.

5
Discussion
This project aimed to characterise the structure and function of the enzyme BeKdgF. One
objective was to produce isotopically labelled BeKdgF for subsequent resonance assignment,
structural assessment, and functional characterisation using NMR spectroscopy. The func-
tional characterisation probed dynamic information about BeKdgF, interactions with divalent
metal ions, pKa of the catalytic residue, and substrate interactions.
5.1
Successful production of isotopically labelled BeKdgF
The basis of this project was the production of 13C-15N-labelled BeKdgF without which the
NMR spectroscopy analysis would not be possible. The enzyme was produced by recombinant
expression in E. coli. The production of isotopically labelled BeKdgF in M9 media gave a yield
of ≈34.4 mg/L cell culture, which is slightly higher than the yield of 23.1 mg/L cell culture
obtained for non-isotopically labelled protein (Andersen 2019). The M9 media contained 50 %
more glucose than the LB media used for the production of non-isotopically labelled BeKdgF
(Andersen 2019), which could explain the higher yield.
When evaluating the purity of elution fractions containing BeKdgF using an SDS-PAGE anal-
ysis a band was observed at 14 kDa (monomer) and 28 kDa (dimer). Such observations could
be caused by a high enzyme concentration or the presence of a dimer. The dimerisation will
be further discussed in section 5.4.
The 1H-15N HSQC spectra show no change for several weeks, which indicates that BeKdgF
is stable in the phosphate buﬀer at 25 °C. Stability in HEPES buﬀer was not systematically
assessed, however during the studies using HEPES buﬀer there was no indication that the en-
zyme should not also be stable at these conditions. The peak dispersion in the 1H-15N HSQC
spectrum corresponds with the protein being properly folded in the buﬀer systems at 25 °C.
5.2
Resonance assignment of BeKdgF was almost completed
The ﬁrst step in the characterisation of the structure and function of BeKdgF was to perform
a resonance assignment of the enzyme to obtain chemical shift values for the backbone and
sidechain nuclei. The chemical shifts were necessary for the structural determination and as
a starting point for functional analysis.
The resonance assignment of BeKdgF was almost complete since there was a high degree of
assignment of backbone resonances (> 92 %) and a partial degree of sidechain assignment
(> 59 %). In the 1H-15N HSQC spectrum some peaks could not be assigned to any speciﬁc
amino acid residues, and three of the unidentiﬁed peaks were found to be a fragment of se-
quential spin systems.
Two protein fragments could not be found in the 1H-15N HSQC spectrum (H52-F53-H54 and
P86-D87-V88-L89). Although there were two missing protein fragments and one unidentiﬁed
fragment of spin systems, the unidentiﬁed fragment could not be convincingly assigned as any
of the missing protein fragments. The two missing protein fragments are both located in loop

5.3
Chemical shift data indicate the same fold as other KdgF
One objective in this project was to evaluate the secondary structure of BeKdgF in solution
compared to the crystal structure. The chemical shift data obtained through the resonance
assignment was analysed using the software TALOS-N (Shen and Bax 2013) allowing for an
evaluation of the secondary structure elements in the enzyme.
The dihedral torsion angle analysis of BeKdgF using TALOS-N could predict the secondary
structure of 89 % of the amino acid residues. The analysis shows a high degree of β-sheets
in the protein. This is consistent with the reported crystal structures of KdgF from Yersinia
enterocolitica and Halomonas Sp (Hobbs et al. 2016) and the unpublished crystal structure of
BeKdgF (Rønne et al. In preparation). A comparison of the secondary structural elements of
BeKdgF found by NMR spectroscopy and X-ray crystallography is shown in Figure 19, where
it is shown that there is a good overlap between the β-sheets predicted based on the resonance
assignment and the ones found using X-ray crystallography. Residues 24-31 were in the crys-
tal structure found to be a β-strand, where the TALOS-N prediction only show residues 24-27
as part of the β-strand. Only a few chemical shifts have been found for residues 28-31, which
makes it diﬃcult for TALOS-N to predict the secondary structure.

The torsion angle prediction by TALOS-N shows that the terminals of the protein are disor-

5.4
BeKdgF is a dimer in solution
The quaternary structure of BeKdgF was assessed based on the rotational correlation time of
the enzyme, which was found during the dynamic analysis. The rotational correlation time
of BeKdgF was found to be τc = 15.3 ns ±0.7 ns. When this value is compared to τc of other
proteins (e.g. (Rossi et al. 2010)), it is clear that 15.3 ns is a very large value for a globular
protein with a molecular weight of 14.1 kDa. It corresponds better to a globular protein with
a molecular weight of ≈25 kDa, which would be approximate twice the size of a monomer of
BeKdgF (see Figure 20).

BeKdgF and what molecular weight that time corresponds to.
The rotational correlation time indicates that BeKdgF in solution is a dimer. The 1H-15N
spectra show only one set of peaks for the enzyme, so BeKdgF is most likely a homodimer.
The X-ray crystal structures published show HaKdgF and YeKdgF as dimers with the two
monomers related by C2 symmetry (Hobbs et al. 2016), so the results presented in this project
are in accordance with the crystal structures of KdgF. A proposed dimeric conformation of
BeKdgF can be seen in Figure 21.

5.5
The eﬀect of zinc on the quaternary structure
To investigate if the dimerisation of BeKdgF was inﬂuenced by the presence of metal ions, a
ﬁve-fold amount of EDTA was added to the enzyme to remove metal ions. No chemical shift
changes in the TROSY spectra were observed when adding EDTA, as well as the line width
stayed the same indicating that BeKdgF stays a dimer. EDTA forms complexes with the metal
ions of the solution, so this result indicates that metal ions are not essential for the dimerisa-
tion of BeKdgF.
As the presence of divalent metal ions increases the activity of KdgF (Hobbs et al. 2016),
ZnCl2 was added to a sample of BeKdgF to assess the eﬀect on the enzyme. When ZnCl2 was
added, no peaks moved in the TROSY spectra, which could have indicated the metal-binding
site. Instead signals disappeared. When a 1:1 ratio of ZnCl2 to BeKdgF was reached, prac-
tically all signals had vanished. One reason for this signal disappearance could be that the
presence of Zn2+ induces aggregation of BeKdgF, which causes line broadening rendering the
enzyme nondetectable with NMR.
One possible explanation of the aggregation could be that BeKdgF forms a tetramer when
ZnCl2 is present. A tetramer of BeKdgF would have a molecular weight of ≈56 kDa, making
the molecule too large to be observed by the NMR techniques used. A suggestion for the struc-
ture of the tetramer is shown in Figure 22. The tetramer was generated by the two 2-fold axes
in P6222, with the Zn atom, having an occupancy of 0.25, at the intersection of the symmetry
axes.
Some peaks in the TROSY spectra disappeared at lower ZnCl2 concentrations than others.

pKa of key residue in the catalytic mechanism was determined
In YeKdgF amino acid residue D100 was found to be vital for the activity of the enzyme and
therefore proposed to be part of the active site (Hobbs et al. 2016). This amino acid residue
is conserved in BeKdgF as residue D102 along with the rest of the active site, as explained in
section 4.6. As D102 has an acid/base functionality, it could be the catalytically active residue.
The pH titration of BeKdgF was observed by recording a series of TROSY spectra of a BeKdgF
sample in the pH range 4.15−8.02. During the pH change, it was observed that D102 and six
other residues (E13, E17, E21, E66, D110, and E114) titrated in the pH interval. The pKa
value of D102 was determined to be 5.9 ±0.1. Bacteroides is one of the dominant bacteria
species in the human gut (Bai et al. 2017). The pH of the human colon is ≈6.6 (Evans et al.
1988), which means that in the native environment of BeKdgF D102 will be deprotonated ﬁve
out of six times.
The proposed reaction of BeKdgF is base-catalysed with the mechanism shown in Figure 23
(Rønne et al. In preparation). That mechanism would require D102 to be deprotonated, which
corresponds well with the D102 pKa value of 5.9 ±0.1 which was obtained with the NMR
titration.

The proposed substrates for KdgF are 4,5-unsaturated monouronates (Hobbs et al. 2016).

6
Conclusion
In this project, the structure and function of KdgF from Bacteroides eggerthii have been stud-
ied using NMR spectroscopy. First 13C-15N-labelled BeKdgF was produced and puriﬁed re-
sulting in a yield of 32 mg/L cell culture. The isotopically labelled protein was used to obtain
a 1H, 13C, 15N resonance assignment of BeKdgF, which also allowed for characterising the sec-
ondary structure and functional aspects of the enzyme. The assignment was almost complete
for backbone resonances (> 92 % assigned) and partially complete for sidechain resonances
(> 59 % assigned). Using the chemical shift assignment torsion angles were predicted using
TALOS-N, and based on this prediction the secondary structure was determined for 89 % of
BeKdgF amino acid residues in solution. Furthermore, the prediction shows that ≈50 % of
the secondary structure of BeKdgF is β-sheets with the rest of the protein having no secondary
structure. The high degree of β-sheets is in good agreement with previously reported struc-
tures.
Dynamic information for BeKdgF was probed at the ps-ns time range. The analysis shows that
the terminals are more dynamic than the rest of the protein. This is also in good agreement
with the order parameter calculated with TALOS-N based on the chemical shifts, which also
indicate that the terminals are disordered while the rest of the protein is well-ordered. The
rotational correlation time of BeKdgF was found via the dynamic analysis to be τc = 15.3 ns
±0.7 ns, which corresponds to BeKdgF being a dimer in solution. Furthermore, there is also
a clear indication of the protein being a homodimer, as only one set of signals in the 1H-
15C HSQC spectrum is detected corresponding to the number of amino acid residues in the
monomer. An EDTA test shows that the dimerisation of BeKdgF does not rely on the presence
of metal ions.
Interactions with divalent metal ions were investigated by performing a titration with ZnCl2,
which showed that addition of Zn2+ induced aggregation of BeKdgF, and at a 1:1 ratio of
BeKdgF to ZnCl2 practically all peaks had disappeared in the NMR spectra.
The pKa value of the active site residue D102 was found to be 5.9 ±0.1 based on a pH titration.
Moreover, the pKa values were determined for six other amino acid residues also titrating in
the same pH range. Substrate binding was probed by investigating the eﬀect of the addition of
d-mannuronate and l-guluronate monomers to BeKdgF. The NMR analysis shows no binding
between BeKdgF and d-mannuronate, and between BeKdgF and l-guluronate there could be
a weak interaction.
The resonance assignment and functional studies of BeKdgF presented here enables fur-
ther investigation of the enzyme with NMR spectroscopy. Furthermore, the increased un-
derstanding of the biological role of KdgF aids in the industrial use of alginate from seaweed
as biomass.

7
Future perspective
7.1
Knowledge about KdgF
7.1.1
Full structural characterisation
In this project, the backbone and a large part of side-chain resonances in BeKdgF have been
assigned, but the full structure has not been determined using NMR spectroscopy. The struc-
ture has been determined using X-ray crystallography (Rønne et al. In preparation), but it
might still be relevant to conﬁrm the X-ray crystal structure with NMR. As enzymes naturally
are found in solution, an NMR-determined structure would be closer to the native structure
of the enzyme. However, the secondary structural element analysis based on the resonance
assignment indicates that there are no important diﬀerences between the crystal structure
and the NMR structure of BeKdgF in solution.
7.1.2
Other methods for characterisation of quaternary structure
The quaternary structure of BeKdgF has in this project been suggested to be a dimer based
on the rotational correlation time and previous X-ray crystal structures. This result could
be conﬁrmed by making a size-exclusion chromatography (SEC) analysis and using multi-
angle light scattering (MALS) detection of the protein (Hong, Koza, and Bouvier 2012). A
SEC-MALS analysis can provide information about the size, concentration, and shape of a
protein (Hong, Koza, and Bouvier 2012). The SEC-MALS analysis of BeKdgF could also be
performed with and without divalent metal ions present. This would help to understand what
the structure and size of the formed aggregate are, and so conﬁrm if BeKdgF tetramerises in
the presence of Zn2+-ions. Whether this aggregate is formed with other divalent metal ions
than Zn2+ would be relevant to investigate as well.
Other methods for quaternary structural analysis could be used instead of or along with SEC-
MALS, e.g. small-angle X-ray solution scattering (SAXS) (Mertens and Svergun 2010) or
circular dichroism (CD) (Greenﬁeld 2006).
7.1.3
Enzyme stability
During the pH titration, it was observed that BeKdgF is not stable at low pH. What happens to
the enzyme structure at low pH could be investigated using CD (Greenﬁeld 2006). Similarly,
the eﬀect of diﬀerent buﬀer conditions could be investigated. The melting point of BeKdgF
could be found using a thermal shift assay (Sviben et al. 2018). Information about the stability
of the enzyme will aid in investigating how to use KdgF industrially.

Substrate investigation
7.2.1
Further analysis of aﬃnity for l-guluronate
To fully characterise the possible binding of M and G, higher concentrations of the compounds
could be investigated. This is most relevant for G, as there were some changes in the spectra
of BeKdgF when 75.5 mM G was present. By making a titration with higher concentrations
of G, maybe a binding could be observed. As G is similar in structure with 4,5-unsaturated
guluronate, which is one proposed substrate for KdgF (Hobbs et al. 2016), the two compounds
might interact with BeKdgF in the same manner. BeKdgF would not be able to catalyse the
conversion of G, but just bind the compound, making an investigation of the binding possible.
7.2.2
Aﬃnity for alginate dimer
The aﬃnity between BeKdgF and M and G was investigated in this project. M and G are sim-
ilar to the substrates of BeKdgF in the sense that the molecules have the same conﬁguration
at C1-3, but are diﬀerent at C4-6. To investigate the aﬃnity of BeKdgF to the other end of the
4,5-unsaturated monouronates, C4-6, an alginate dimer (e.g. Figure 25) could be used. This
alginate dimer would consist of a 4,5-unsaturated uronate bound to an M or G monosaccha-
ride. If there is a high aﬃnity between BeKdgF and the alginate dimer, then BeKdgF probably
binds to C4-6 of the substrate.

7.3
Reaction analysis with 4,5-unsaturated monouronate
The reaction catalysed by KdgF has been investigated using a linked assay with the oligosac-
charide lyase Alg17c and HaKdgF, where Alg17c catalysed the degradation of dimannuronate
to 4,5-unsaturated mannuronate, and HaKdgF catalysed the double bond depletion (Hobbs et
al. 2016). The reaction was followed by measuring absorbance at 230 nm (Hobbs et al. 2016).
A similar coupled assay with BeKdgF could be made, where the reaction would be followed
using time-resolved NMR spectroscopy, which would show the formation of intermediates and
products and enable characterising the reaction kinetics.
A similar study was made by Arentzen et al. (2021), where PolyM and a PL17 enzyme were
combined and the reaction was followed with NMR spectroscopy resulting in the identiﬁcation
of six reaction intermediates and products. In the study, the linearisation of DEH was base-
catalysed and not catalysed by KdgF (Arntzen et al. 2021), so if a similar experiment with
KdgF is performed, the reaction kinetics could be compared to that of the reaction without
KdgF to further observe the eﬀect of KdgF.

7.4
Mutation study for investigation of the active site residues
In this project, it has been proposed that the catalytically active amino acid residue of BeKdgF
is D102, as it has an acid/base functionality and is conserved compared to YeKdgF, where it
is part of the active site. If the reaction analysis described above in section 7.2 is performed,
probably the reaction catalysed by BeKdgF will be characterised. Then it would be interesting
to make a mutant of BeKdgF, where D102 is changed to asparagine, as asparagine has the
same structure as aspartate, but asparagine does not have an acid/base functionality. If the
enzyme BeKdgF D102N is still catalysing the conversion of 4,5-unsaturated monouronates to
DEH, D102 would not be the catalytically active residue, or the reaction would not be base-
catalysed.
The two other proposed active site residues in YeKdgF are also conserved in BeKdgF as F104
and R108. Similarly as with D102, whether F104 and R108 are vital for the activity of BeKdgF
could be investigated by assessing the activity of BeKdgF mutants.
7.5
Degradation of alginate
The purpose of investigating the enzyme BeKdgF was to gain knowledge about the degra-
dation of alginate to optimise the use of alginate as biomass. It should now be possible to
investigate the full degradation of alginate to e.g. KDG by combining for example alginate
lyases, KdgF, and DEH reductases. This investigation has been started by Rønne et al. (in
preparation). By degrading alginate with diﬀerent enzyme combinations and measuring the
reaction rate and degree of degradation, the eﬀect of using KdgF could be characterised, and
alginate degradation could be optimised. This investigation would open up the possibility of
using alginate and seaweed industrially as biomass.
