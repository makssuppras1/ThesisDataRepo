Summary (English)
In the present project a set-up has been created in order to test 4 dierent mast
sections comprised of circular cylinders. A force transducer has been designed,
which can be used for measuring horizontal wind loads on the mast sections.
In the report the design, calibration and verication of the force transducer
is accounted for. Furthermore the issues of blockage are investigated. Several
methods for blockage correction are investigated, and a Blockage Tolerant Test
Section has been built and veried, and used in the present experiments. The
Blockage Tolerant Test Section showed to eliminate blockage eects signicantly.
The tested masts consists of two masts with triangular cross-section and two
with rectangular cross-section. Two dierent bracing congurations exists, with
approximately the same solidity area. The masts were tested for an angle of
incidence α = 0◦to 120◦with an interval of α = 10◦for the triangular masts,
and for the rectangular masts with an angle of incidence α = −10◦to 100◦
with an interval of α = 10◦. The masts were tested in both smooth ow and
turbulent ow, and all ow regimes were subcritical. The turbulence in the ow
was generated by means of a turbulence grid in the start of the test section.
The results showed, that drag coecients for all masts tested in smooth ow was
comparable with the drag coecient proposed by ESDU, but that the Eurocode
methods underestimated the drag coecients. For the turbulent ow the drag
coecients were larger than both the drag coecients proposed by ESDU and
Eurocode. A reason for this behaviour can not immediately be identied, but
the tendency is backed up by previously conducted experiments with masts
sections in turbulent ow in the subcritical regime. The results are on the other
hand a little uncertain because of the used set-up, and the ow conditions are

not immediately applicable to full-scale, but still some tendencies can be seen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A mast structure is dened as a tall thin structure created to support equipment,
which needs to be placed high above ground. This equipment could be antennas
for telecommunication, weather stations etc. A mast can either be guyed or
cantilevered. The cantilevered mast supports itself laterally, meaning that all
wind force is obtained in the foundation of the mast. The guyed mast is, on
the other hand, supported laterally by several cables, meaning that most wind
forces are obtained in the foundations of these cables. The guyed mast is the
most material economic mast type, as it only needs to support its self-weight,
and therefore it is also the type that the tallest masts in the world have been
built as. The tallest mast ever to be built is the Warszawa Radiomast which
stood at an astonishing height of 646 m.
The task of determining the aerodynamical coecient on a three-dimensional
lattice mast section (in the following just called mast section) has, according
to [20] attempted to be solved analytically, but these results have been in poor
agreement with experiments, and therefore the codes and standards for deter-
mination of the drag coecient on a mast section, are all based empirically on
experiments done on mast sections in dierent congurations. The traditional
approaches have been either the summation method or the bulk method [18].
The summation method involves determining the force of each element of the
mast section and determining the overall force on the mast by summation, and
the bulk method involves nding the overall force of the mast section in cal-

culation methods as a function of the shape and solidity of the mast section.
The latter method is the most widespread, and the one that most codes and
standards propose.
1.1
Project purpose
The purpose of the present project is to determine the drag coecient of a mast
section comprised of cylinders experimentally, and compare it with the drag
coecient proposed by the common codes and standards. Especially the drag
coecient of the mast sections in turbulent ow in the subcritical regime is of in-
terest, because several experiments have shown, that this drag coecient might
be larger than the drag coecient in smooth ow. Four generic mast sections
with approximately the same solidity ratio will be tested and compared. It has
been decided to test the masts in two ow conditions, smooth and turbulent,
and for angle of incidence intervals of 10◦. The masts will be tested in uniform
ow, as this will be realistic conditions for the masts sections located high above
ground.
All experiments have been conducted with existing material from the wind tun-
nel facilities, and therefore there has been no aim at attaining specic ow
conditions which could correspond to full scale conditions.
To measure the drag coecients, a force transducer will need to be built, and
the necessary considerations with such designing, along with the subsequent
calibration and verication of it, will be accounted for. Furthermore, a blockage
tolerant test section will be designed, and veried.

The friction drag is dependent on the boundary layer of the body. The boundary
layer is a thing layer of air near the surface of the body, in which the velocity
will decrease along the height to the surface. The boundary layer can either
be turbulent or laminar. The laminar boundary layer will create less friction
drag than the turbulent. The nature of the turbulent boundary layer will be
explained in section 2.3. Laminar layers can on the other hand transition into
a turbulent ow, which will create a 'free shear layer', which will separate the
wake and outer ow.
Depending on the shape and size of the body, the pressure and friction compo-
nent will play dierent roles. On a streamlined airfoil there will not be much
pressure at the windward side of the body, as most of the wind will be guided
around. Therefore the positive stagnation pressure will only be present at a
small area. Furthermore the ow separates near the rear, which means that the
negative pressure will also only be present at a small area. Considering both
of these factors, it means that the pressure drag component is relatively small.
On the other hand the wind will travel a long distance over the surface of the
airfoil, meaning that the skin friction will give the body drag, and therefore the
friction drag component will be relatively large.
The contrary example of an airfoil is the at plate, at which the pressure will
be large, as the plate will decrease the incoming wind velocity to zero at a big
area, thereby having a big area of stagnation pressure. When the wind goes
around the plate, its velocity will be increased, causing the pressure to drop,
and thereby giving a negative pressure at the leeward side. Both of these factors
contribute to a relatively large pressure drag component. On the other hand,
there is very little area parallel to the wind direction, meaning there is not much
skin friction, and thereby a relatively small friction drag component.

4.2
Blockage correction
When testing scaled structures in wind tunnels, it is of great importance to
know, if the size of the structure compared to the size of the wind tunnel will
inuence the results. This phenomenon is termed blockage, and needs to be
investigated and solved whenever testing. Both positive and negative blockage
exists. Positive blockage is the case in which walls conne the ow around the
tested body.
Negative blockage is the case in which an open test section is
used, and the ow therefore is slowed down by the surrounding stagnant air.
Ideally the masts should be tested in an unconned ow, meaning a wind tunnel
with big enough dimensions that the vicinity of walls will not inuence the ow
around the mast, resembling what would be expected in a full scale situation
with a free-standing structure.
Both negative and positive blockage will distort the force measured by the trans-
ducer, and thereby give incorrect drag coecients.

For the sake of measuring the forces applied on the tested masts, a force trans-

be used to back-calculate the force applied to it.
The bending of the pipe is measured by 8 strain gauges, which measured the
elongation of the pipe in a chosen direction. The strain gauges are mounted in
pairs in the two principal cross section directions, on two horizontal planes on
the pipe. This way the force in the two principal directions can be measured, and
used to determined the force resultant for any angle of application. The strain

9.1
Uncertainties in the experiments
Because of the simplicity of the used force transducer and the data-logging sys-
tem compared to commonly used equipment, uncertainties in the experimental
measurements will inevitably occur.
As the data logging system only logs with 2 Hz, compared to the minimum
1024 Hz of commonly used systems, there was a need for relatively long sample
durations to obtain a reliable mean value of the drag coecients. In order to
obtain a stable mean value that did not deviate when longer sample duration
was done, it was shown that logging for at least an hour was necessary. As this
was too long, it was shown that logging for 10 minutes gave a reliable mean
value, that deviated only 2% from the mean value when logging for 2 hours.
The drag coecient of the cube tested in smooth ow in the BTTS was seemed
deviate −3.29% from what the literature proposed, and the drag coecient of
the cube tested in turbulent ow in the BTTS seemed to deviate −4.65% from
what the literature proposed.

deviances of maximum 6.2% when the calculated force resultant was compared

experiments in smooth and turbulent ow, with the biggest deviance happening
in the smooth ow.

9.4
Smooth vs. turbulent ow
One of the main observations which can be made from the experiments is that,
for ow around the mast with its members in the subcritical ow regime, the
drag coecient of the mast will be larger in turbulent ow than in smooth ow.
According to classical theory regarding ow around a cylinder in subcritical ow,
there should be no dierence in the drag coecient whether the ow is turbulent
or smooth. Turbulence in the ow will only shift the transition to critical ow
to a lower Reynolds number. Therefore the present experiments suggest, that
the drag coecients of a lattice mast section might behave a dierently than
cylinders. This fact is backed up by previously conducted experiments as will
be explained in the following.
The experiments conducted by NMI likewise showed, that the drag coecients
for mast sections tested in turbulent ow were always larger than for smooth
ow. At most, the turbulent drag coecients were 9% larger than the corre-
sponding smooth ones for a tested mast section with solidity ratio φ = 0.163
and decreased with increasing solidity ratio to a deviance of 3% for a solidity
ratio of 0.316. The tests done by [19] also showed, that the drag coecient was
larger in turbulent ow than in smooth ow in the subcritical ow regime.
For the NMI-experiments the ow regime is not described, and for both of
the above experiments the integral length scale is larger than in the present
experiments (0.11cm compared to 0.03cm. The comparability with the present
experiments is therefore uncertain, but the tendency of larger drag coecients
does, under all circumstances, substantiate the observation from the present
experiments.
When studying the sources and references of ESDU, it is seen that the graph
for determining the basic drag coecient with zero incidence and no shielding
is based on the case of a cylinder. On the other hand, the eects of shielding
are determined from experiments conducted on mast sections in smooth ow
[20], and for plane lattice frames shielded by other plane lattice frames in tur-
bulent ow [18]. As the present experiments showed larger drag coecients for
turbulent ow, this might mean, that the shielding eects are dierent when
considering turbulent ow, something which was not considered in ESDU.

9.5
Summary
In the present report a set-up has been produced, in order to test 4 dierent
mast sections, with triangular and rectangular cross-sections. The built force
transducer showed to be reliable, with maximum deviations of 6.2%. A Blockage
Tolerant Test Section was furthermore built, which proved to eliminate blockage
eects in the wind tunnel very well.
The results showed, that drag coecients for all masts tested in smooth ow was
comparable with the drag coecient proposed by ESDU, but that the Eurocode
methods underestimated the drag coecients. For the turbulent ow the drag
coecients were larger than both the drag coecients proposed by ESDU and
Eurocode. A reason for this behaviour can not immediately be identied, but
the tendency is backed up by previously conducted experiments with masts
sections in turbulent ow in the subcritical regime.
This conclusion should though be drawn cautiously, as the used test-setup posses
some uncertainties, and with a geometric scale between the integral length scale
and the mast section not immediately common. To verify the present conclusions
it would be necessary to conduct the experiments again with a more commonly
used set-up, and with integral length scales comparable to nature.

Building the BTTS
Fig. A.1: 80 NACA-proles were cut out of a wood plate of 0.6 cm thickness by
laser-cutting. They were glued together using an alu-angle, so that they were
perfectly aligned before being glued.
Fig. A.2: When all pieces had been glued together, the resulting prole was
sanded to remove any glue sticking out and to make the surface smooth.
Fig. A.3: A mould was made by putting 4 pieces of cardboard together. The
NACA-prole was then put in and clay was formed around it in the bottom as a

Afterwards a two-component silicone rubber was put in the form, where it cured
for 6 hours. After it had cured the now solid silicone rubber mould was put in
the bottom, and the process of pouring silicone rubber in was repeated. After
another 6 hours the silicone rubber had cured and the mould was then complete.
Fig. A.4: The mould parts were fastened with clamps, and a two-component
epoxy resin was poured in. A 6 mm steel reinforcement bar was inserted in the
mould beforehand to give the nished prole stiness. After 12 hours the epoxy
was cured.
