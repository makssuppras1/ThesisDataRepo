Summary
Two of the very popular topic on the global agenda in these years are the environment
and autonomous cars. This project is addressing both of these topics. The purpose of
this project has been to provide a computer vision soft- and hardware solution for a
fuel-efficient car.
The car is the DTU Dynamo developed by DTU Roadrunners. The car can already
drive autonomously on a track under certain conditions.
The car have successfully
participated in the Shell ECO-Marathons autonomous completion. But it is only able
to drive autonomously, if there are barriers at the side, marking the edge of the track.
The goal of computer vision system developed in this project, is to enable the car to see
the racetrack without barriers and get it location relative to it.
This task is not only useful for a fuel-efficient car in a competition on a race track,
it could also be used on other vehicles driving on asphalt in a nature environment, eg
an autonomous winter service vehicle spreading salt at the bike lanes in a park.
This project is using the ZED2 Stereo camera together with a Jetson Nano. A few
different algorithms has been tested the locating the track.
The solution of this project has been tested on recordings from the car at a track
without barriers at Roskilde Racing Center, with great success.
The computer vision system has now the foundation to be fused with the LiDAR
and the rest of the autonomous system on the DTU Dynamo, so that DTU Roadrunners
can continue the work at developing the capabilities of a fuel-efficient autonomous car.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This project will investigate the possibilities in using a camera to detect the boundaries
of the race track and locate the car relative to it. The camera is mounted on a car,
and the purpose of detecting the boundaries of the track is to enable the car to drive
autonomously around the track. If the car is to be able to drive autonomously on the
track, then it needs to know its location relative to the edge of the track. It needs to
know if it is close to the left side of the track, close to the right side, or in the middle
of the road. It also needs to know its orientation; is it heading towards one of the edges
or is it following the track in the right direction.
1.1
Motivation for doing this project.
DTU Roadrunners is a team of DTU students that in many years has participated in the
Shell Eco Marathon competition. The purpose of the competition is to design and drive a
car that is as fuel efficient as possible. A few years ago Shell introduced some autonomous
challenges. The challenges were to follow a short part of the track with barriers at each
side, drive through a gate on the track and park at marked parking spots. These are
good starting tasks for designing an autonomous car, but if fuel efficiency needs to be
considered and maybe compared to when the car is driven manually, the car must be
able to drive the whole track without barriers. DTU Roadrunners decided to try to
solve this task, even though it is not a part of the competitions at Shell Eco Marathon
yet. The tasks that up until now has been part of the competition, have already been
solved by DTU Roadrunners in previous years. We wanted to take it a step further. We
wanted to investigate the possibilities in Fuel efficient Autonomous driving. This project
is a step in that direction.

CHAPTER 2
Design
The vehicle used in this project is the DTU Dynamo built by DTU Roadrunners. The
Dynamo is a four wheel, one person vehicle, which is improved every year by DTU
students.
Figure 2.1: The Dynamo with the ZED camera and the LIDAR mounted on top.
The electrical system in the car can be divided into two parts. The Autonomous
system and the regular system. The autonomous system can easily be removed from
the car during the non-autonomous competition. The regular system handles things
like break pressure sensor, injection control, front/back and signal light, gearshift, and
user input/output from/to the driver.
The autonomous system communicates with
the regular system via a CAN bus.
The autonomous system consists of three main
components and some sensors. A custom made Autoboard that handles power and date
from different sensors. The Intel NUC is connected to the LiDAR and runs most of
the software for the autonomous system. The Jetson Nano is used for image processing.
The Jetson Nano is described further in section 2.2.

2.1
Camera
The purpose of this project is to calculate the distance from the vehicle to the edge of
the track. A camera projects the 3D world to a 2D plane. A mono camera can therefor
reconstruct the direction to the 3D world point, but not how far in that direction the
point is. The distance can be obtained if the same 3D point is projected in another
camera and if the relative position between the two cameras is known. The old camera
on the vehicle (USB8MP02G-L75 mono camera from EL [Top18]) was only a mono
camera . It was therefor decided to get a stereo camera for the car.
There are some commercially available stereo cameras. Companies have taken two
camera sensors and put them into the same device, which is then ready to plug in to a
computer. It is of course also possible to make our own stereo setup by mounting two
separate cameras on the car. This solution gives flexibility to customize the position
of the cameras with different angles, the distance between them (base line) etc. One
of the benefits of getting a commercially available stereo camera is that the camera
doesn’t need to be calibrated to obtain the relation between the two cameras every
time it is mounted on the car. Mounting and calibration of two mono cameras is a
bit difficult and might cause a lot of trouble.
Another benefit is that commercially
available stereo cameras often come with a Software Development Kit (SDK) that have
some of the functions like rectifying images customized to that camera. Therefor it was
decided to use a commercially available stereo camera. Cameras from two companies
were considered. Intel RealSence and the ZED cameras from Stereolabs.
A comparison of the cameras can be seen in table 2.2. The ZED2 had the longest
depth range and the additional sensors might benefit the performance. Therefor this
camera was chosen.
The ZED2 use both USB 3.0 and 2.0 to send video and motion sensor data. Some
extension cables which have only USB 3.0 internal wires or fiber extenders will work for
image transmission but sensors data from IMU or Barometer will not be available.

2.1.1
ZED SDK
The ZED2 was chosen do to the long depth range. The smaller resolution in respect to
the old mono camera, is not expected to be a big problem since the edge of the road is
not a small object and hence should be easily detectable.
The ZED SDK requires CUDA in order to work. CUDA is an interface from Nvidia
enabling parallel processing on a Nvidia GPU. This means that if one wants to use the
ZED SDK one need to have a computer, that have an NVIDIA GPU. A Jetson Nano
was bought to be used on the car. However since the development of this project is
student driven, and student often use their own computers to develop, it is a bit of a
disadvantage, that they have to have a NVIDIA GPU on their computer or develop
directly on the Jetson Nano.
2.1.1.1
Parallel processing
Image processing is a topic that often is mentioned when talking about parallel process-
ing. Parallel processing is to perform multiple calculations at the same time instead of
doing the calculations one after another. There are many calculations in image process-
ing which are being calculated on every pixel, independent of the results from the other
pixels. An example is linear filters where pixel values are changed based on a weighted
sum of the surrounding pixels.

Jetson Nano
Since a Nvidia GPU was needed to run the ZED SDK, a Jetson Nano was added to the

2.2.1
Power
The Jetson Nano can run on a usb power supply, or jack barrel plug. A USB power
supply delivers up to 2 A, which is sufficient for running the Jetson Nano. But when the
camera is plugged in, the camera will be drawing 380 mA from the Jetson Nano. Some
USB power supplies deliver less than 2 A, in which case the Jetson might shut down
when the camera starts to draw power.
2.2.2
SD card
The Jetson uses the jetpack operation system. The operating system can be downloaded
from the Jetson web page and flashed to an SD card. While developing on the Jetson,
there has been a few times where the Jetson would not boot. The solution has been to
flash the SD card with a new image of the operating system. Therefor is it recommended
often to take a backup image of the SD card. The documentation recommends at a
minimum a 16 Gb SD-card of class 10 or higher. The OS and the ZED SDK take up
around 14 Gb, so it could be recommended to get a bigger SD card.

2.3
ROS
The autonomous system developed for the Dynamo in previous years are based on ROS.
ROS is a way to share information, between programs in real time. ROS is a collection of
software and libraries for programming robots. The ROS tutorials on the ROS webside
[Ano20] is a good place to start to get familiar with ROS.
The ROS system handling most of the autonomous system, is running on the Intel
NUC computer. So algorithms running on the Jetson module need to communicate with
this platform.
2.3.1
ROS on multiple machines
Since the camera is running on the Jetson Nano board and the the other part of the
autonomous system is running on the NUC, it was necessary to make a connection
between these, so they could communicate with each other. It is possible to run ROS
on multiple machines. So the Jetson is setup to run ROS together with a Master ROS
running on the NUC. This setup enables both computers to be able to subscribe to the
same topic.
The first step in this process is to get the two computers on the same bidirectional net-
work. The systems can be setup in the following way, assuming that the two computers
are physically connected to another by Ethernet.
• Get the ip adresses of the NUC and the Jetson e.g. by the ifconfig command in
the terminal.
• Open the the text file ~/.bashrc on both computers
• Asuming that the ip of the NUC is 192.168.1.3 and the Jetson is 192.168.1.1, add
the following 3 lines in the end of the file on the NUC:
– export ROS_MASTER_URI=http://localhost:11311
– export ROS_HOSTNAME=192.168.1.3
– export ROS_IP=192.168.1.3
• Add the following 3 lines in the end of the file /.bashrc on the Jetson: (note that
localhost is replaced with the IP of the NUC)
– export ROS_MASTER_URI=http://192.168.1.3:11311
– export ROS_HOSTNAME=192.168.1.1
– export ROS_IP=192.168.1.1
Since the NUC is setup to be master is it not needed to run roscore on Jetson.

3.1
GrabCut
The GrabCut algorithm was developed to segment the foreground from background in
still images. In this project it was investigated whether this algorithm could be used to
segment the asphalt from the rest of the picture.
The algorithm works by defining a part of the image, that definitely is background
and/or parts that definitely is foreground. The region close to the car is defined as
background.
The GrabCut algorithm was published by Rother et al. [RKB04]. It is an extention
of the graph-cut algorithm. They have enhanced the alogtithm by iterartive estimation
and incomplete labelling.

3 Road detection Algorithms
3.1.1
Graph Cut
The graph cut algoritm works on gray scale images. The user defines a foreground and
background region, on which the gray level distribution is obtained by computing two
histograms, one for the foreground and one for the backgound.
3.1.2
GrabCut
The only user input needed for the GrabCut algorithm is a region, that for sure is
background TB. The foreground is initialized to null TF = ∅. All the rest of the pixels
are categorized as undetermined TU = FB
The GraphCut algorithm uses a histogram. But because the GrabCut uses color
images instead of only grayscale, is the histogram replaced with a Gaussian Mixture
Model.
In the OpenCv function is it also possible to define pixels the for sure is foreground.
Figure 3.1: Left frame captured by the camera (rectified)
3.2
Canny edge detection
The Canny edge detector is a method to detect edges in an image. It start by filtering
out noise with a Gaussian filter. Then it computes the gradient of the image. This is
done by convolution two kernels to the image and calculating the gradient strength and
direction of the edge. In order to remove everything that is not considered a part of an
edge from the image, a non-maximum suppression is then supplied. The final step is to
apply an upper and a lower threshold.
• If the gradient is above the upper threshold, is it an edge.

• If the gradient is under the lower threshold, is it rejected.
• If the gradient is between the two thresholds, is it only accepted if the neighbouring
pixel also is an edge.

3.3
Region of interest
Some parts of the image can be disregarded as containing the edge of the track, therefor
a region of interest is defined. Take a look at image 3.1. Because the camera is mounted
on the top of the car, the front of the car can be seen in the bottom center of the picture.
One can also see, that each side of the road is going towards each the same point when
they are going towards the horizon. So it makes sense to disregard the top corners of
the frame.
The region of interest is selected by manually making an array of points in a polygon
that defines the region of interest. This polygon is then drawn to a blank canvas the
same size as the image. The openCV function fillPoly is used for this task. Thereby a
mask image has been made that is black in all pixels except within the region of interest,
which is white. If a bitwise AND operation is performed between this mask and the
image with the canny edges, all the edges outside the region of interest will be removed.
Another approach is to use an Image Editor like GIMP to draw the Region of Interests
on a layer over the original image, then save the layer as an image and load it as a mask
in the script. This approach requires the resolution of the mask and the image taken
by the camera to be the same. With the polygon approach the points can be scaled by
comparing the shape of the captured image with the shape of the image used to define
the points.

3.5
Rejecting outliers, averaging and
creating coordinates
After the lines have been computed by the Hough line transform, the left and right edge
of the track can be found. This is done by computing the slope of the lines and sorting
them depending on the sign of the value. If the slope is negative it is categorised as a
candidate for the left edge of the track, otherwise it is categorised as a candidate for the
right edge of the track.
Outliers are rejected by if the difference between the slope and the mean value for
the slops are more than m times the standard deviation of the slopes.
The line is an outlier if
(s −slope_mean) ≥m · slope_std
(3.9)
where s is the slope of the line in question, slope_mean is the mean value of all the
slopes, m is a value that define how far from the standard diviation the slope is allowed
to be before it is rejected (eg. m= 1.5), and slope_std is the standard deviation of the
slope.
3.6
Closing
Closing is a morphological function which is a combination of the two other morpholog-
ical functions dilation and erosion. Closing is a dilation followed by an erosion.
3.6.1
Dilation
Dilation adds elements to a binary image. Dilation is performed by moving a kernel
through a binary image. The kernel is an array of 0 and 1 and may be any size. The
process of dilating an image can be illustrated by overlaying the kernel on the image. If
at least one of the 1’s in the kernel is laying over a 1 in the image, the anchor point will
also be a 1. The anchor point is often the centre of the kernel, but can be defined to
other points instead. The process is repeated for all the pixels in the image.
3.6.2
Erosion
Erosion removes elements from a binary image. Erosion can be said to be the opposite
of dilation. Instead of adding 1’s to the image, it removes 1’s from the image if at least
one of the 1’s in the kernel is overlaying a 0.

3.6.3
Closing
Closing is a dilation followed by an erosion. This means that first the object in the
binary image will grow bigger; thereby objects that are close to each other will be joined
in the same object, and afterwards the object will shrink in size, but the objects that
were joined by the erosion will continue to be one object.

3.8
Triangulation
Triangulation can be used to get the distance and orientation of points in the image.
One camera can obtain a direction in which the real world point projected in the
camera is, but it does not know how far in that direction. If the same point is located
in another camera, that camera will also know in which direction the point is relative to
this camera’s position. It is possible to conclude that the point is in the intersection of
these two directions. If the position of camera one in relation to camera two is known,
triangulation can be used to obtain this 3D world coordinate.
The ZED camera uses the color images for depth perception. The ZED SDK com-
putes a depth map by using triangulation. It outputs the z value for each x,y value in
the left image. The SDK has two sensing modes, Standard or Fill mode. According to
the documentation, the STANDARD mode preserves the distance metrics and shapes,
and runs faster than the FILL mode. Therefor standard mode is chosen for this imple-
mentation.
The depth sensing has four depth modes, Ultra, Quality and Medium, and Performance
mode.
The minimum range can be defined in the script. By choosing a minimum range at
e.g. 1 meter, the performances will be increased in respect to the standard minimum
range at 0.3 m.
The unit can be set to SI units or imperial unites.

working good at large parts of the track. The intersection of the projection of the two
sides on the image gives, a clear indication on the direction the track is going.

This is the reason why the algorithm detects the trees instead of the road.

very well. It would definitely be relevant to use this on the car, together with the rest
of the system.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project has a few different ways of detecting the boundaries of a race track, been
presented. Two of the algorithms, the contour search and the Hough based, worked well,
but failed when there was lines painted on the track. Even though the algorithms are not
performing perfectly in every conditions, and some improvements can be made, would it
still be beneficial to use the algorithms with the car. Especially if the work is continued
and fused the LiDAR algorithm. It could be possible that the LiDAR works good where
the camera is lees perfect and vice versa. The best of the two algorithms is the contours
search. Even though the Hough algorithm does a bit better job at detecting the road,
when there at lines on the asphalt, is there overall more places where it is very wrong.

Future work
8.1
Future work on the algorithms
8.1.1
Improvements to the algorithm described in this
project
This project focus on finding the edge of the track in each frame, regardless of where it
was in previous frame. An approach could be to find the edge in the first frame, and try
to apply some tracking algorithms.
Since the change form frame to frame is minimal, assuming that the edge in the
previous frame is known (at least to some certainty) this could be used for as a mask
for the next frame. e.g. the edge in the new frame is within a radius of r pixels from the
previous frame.
One could use previous data where the edges are found categorize small elements of
it as features and use the ”bag of features” approach to find the edges.
The normal openCV function is used for canny edge detection. Future work could
be to use the OpenCV that takes advantage of cuda.
The integration with the LIDAR data and the rest of the autonomous system, is also
one of the things that should be looked into in the future.
