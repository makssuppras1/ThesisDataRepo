1. Preface 
This 35 ECTS points master's thesis is the result of experimental work by Christine Thue Poulsen, carried out 
in the first half of 2019. The work took place at the Department of Chemistry at the Technical University of 
Denmark. It is part of the project focusing on a sterol library in the Laraia group.  
The chemical structures represented in this thesis are numbered 1-81 based on their chronological 
appearance. Systematic names for compounds have been generated by ChemDraw® provided by 
Cambridge Soft PerkinElmer. NMR spectra of the synthesised compounds can be found in appendix. 
All compounds were synthesised as racemic mixtures and not separated, hence all stereocentres assigned 
for the compounds in this thesis are relative.

3. Abstract 
The use of natural products (NP) as a source of therapeutics has been known since ancient times, and is still 
applicable today. Enlarging the pool of therapeutics and expanding on the success of NPs, makes 
investigating the bioactivity of NP-derived or NP-inspired compounds an area of great interest.  
Ursodeoxycholic acid  (UDCA) is a sterol (a bile acid) which has been shown to have a wide range of 
applications as a therapeutic and shows exciting biological activity, hence synthesising a compound 
collection derived from the scaffold of UDCA, the cis-decalin, and exploring the possibilities for such a 
scaffold are of highly interest and relevance. Such a compound collection have been synthesised during the 
project.  
The compound collection are made from the precursor 1 and the chemistry involved in modifying the 
precursor yielding the compound collection included modifications and ring expansion of the B-ring of the 
cis-decalin scaffold (Scheme 1). The library precursor was synthesised from 2-methylcyclohexane-1,3-dione 
2 and methyl-vinyl-ketone 3 in a six step sequence. It was not possible to isolate 1 as a single isomer, as 
diastereoisomers formed in two of the reactions, which were then carried forward. From the precursor a 
lactam formation at the B-ring was carried out by a Beckmann rearrangement 4. A quinoline 5 was also 
synthesised from the precursor. Another precursor 6 was synthesised by α-bromination of 1, which was 
used for synthesising an aminothiazole, isolated as the formate ion 7. Finally a reduction of the protected 
Wieland–Miescher ketone (WMK) 8 yielded two diastereoisomers 9 and 10 which were separable, and had 
surprisingly not been reported in the literature.

4. Resume 
Naturstoffer har været brugt siden forhistorisk tid som en kilde til lægemidler og bruges stadig i høj grad i 
dag. Det er interessant at undersøge bioaktiviteten af naturstof derivater, og stoffer inspireret af 
naturstoffer, da det kan lede til nye lægemidler, samt øge den succes som naturstoffer har i 
lægemiddelindustrien 
Ursodeoxycholic acid  (UDCA) er en sterol, af typen galdesalt, der har vist sig at have en lang række 
anvendelsesmuligheder som lægemiddel. Derfor vil det være interessant og af stor relevans at syntetisere 
et bibliotek baseret på UDCAs struktur kerne(scaffold): cis-decalin, samt undersøge bioaktiviteten af dette. 
Et sådan bibliotek er blevet syntetiseret i dette projekt. 
Biblioteket er lavet ud fra præcursoren 1, hvor forskellige typer af reaktioner er blevet udført for at 
modificere og ringekspanderer B-ringen i cis-decalin. hvilket resulterer i biblioteket (Skema 1). Bibliotekets 
præcursor blev syntetiseret fra 2-methylcyclohexane-1,3-dione 2 and methyl-vinyl-ketone 3 via en seks-
trins syntese. Det var ikke muligt i dette projekt at isolere 1 som en enkelt isomer, da der dannes 
diastereoisomerer i to af de fortløbende reaktioner. Fra præcursoren blev der syntetiseret en lactam i B-
ringen via et Beckmann rearrangement 4.  En quinoline 5 blev også syntetiseret fra præcursoren. En 
supplerende præcursor 6 blev syntetiseret via en α-brominering af 1 der herefter blev brugt til at 
syntetisere en aminothiazole der blev isoleret som format ion 7. Derudover blev den beskyttede Wieland-
Miescher keton (WMK) 8 reduceret til de to diastereoisomerer 9 og 10, som var mulige at isolere 
individuelt. Disse var overraskende nok ikke rapporteret i den eksisterende litteratur.

7. Natural products - a source of therapeutics 
In drug discovery and chemical biology there is a growing demand for molecules with clearly defined 
structure, selectivity, mechanism of action, potency and availability. However, since the current theoretical 
estimate for drug-like molecules stands upwards of 1060, there is a need for a strategy for developing new 
drug-like molecules, as it will be impossible to investigate all the possibilities, both due to time issues but 
also due to insufficient amount of atoms in the whole universe to synthesise all possible compounds1,2.   A 
feasible strategy could be to look at nature and the molecules that originate from here: natural products 
(NPs). As Aristotle said "Nature creates nothing without a purpose"1.  
NPs are the oldest type of therapeutics as they were the core of ancient natural medicine. The Greek 
physician Hippocrates used powdered willow bark to treat pain in the fifth century before Christ. In 1829 
salicin 11 was isolated from willow bark (Figure 1). Inspired by salicin 11 both sodium salicylate 12 and 
salicylic acid 13 was developed in 1875,and used as pain relievers (Figure 1). In 1897 Felix Hoffman, who 
worked at Bayer, started looking into an replacement of salicylic acid, as it had a side effect of being a 
stomach irritant. His work led to the synthesization of acetylsalicylic acid 14, which Bayer sells under the 
trade name Asprin; a widely used drug for several therapeutic means3. The scaffold of all four molecules 
are the same (Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1, different compounds from the salicylic family 
  
NPs cover small-molecule secondary metabolites created by nature. Some differences between NPs and 
typical synthetic drug-like molecules from combinatorial chemistry are the prevalence of stereogenic 
centres and the molecular complexity for NPs2. NPs (on average) contain higher concentration of oxygen 
but lower concentration of nitrogen. Furthermore the fractions of chiral centres and sp3-hybridized 
bridgehead atoms are also higher for NPs. With 38% of all known NP containing arene systems, NP are 
more prone to have aliphatic rings over aromatic rings4. From the above it is clear the NPs often are non-
planar, which corresponds with the fact that they are evolved to fit into the 3-dimentional (3D) receptors 
and ligand binding site, interacting with multiple proteins. The 3D structure of the proteins are defined by 
secondary structural elements such as α-helices and β-sheets of the backbone of the protein, folding the 
protein to a joined unit (Figure 2). Within the protein, subfolds are found  and the size and shape of these 
determine the size and shape of the ligand binding site. The type of amino acid side chains, the sequences 
of the amino acids and the chemical nature within the subfold, defines the type of ligand the can 
bind/interact with the ligand binding site. This is illustrated in the figure below. The folding of proteins are 
very conservative from natures site, leading to very similar folding across different proteins. It is only

For drug discovery purposes, this non-planar structure of NPs is well suited for adding and changing 
different substituents to the molecule4.  In many cases retaining the core scaffold but changing the 
substituents, will also change the bioactivities for the molecules. The molecule is likely still to interact with 
the ligand binding site if the scaffold is retained or similar, as the binding may occur if the size and 
properties of new NP substituents match with the amino acid residues in the ligand binding site1.  
 
When discussing NP in drug discovery, there are also classes of molecules called NP-inspired molecules and 
NP-derived molecules. For NP-derived molecules the scaffold is identical to the one from the NP, likewise 
the stereochemistry is retained. For NP inspired molecules the scaffold only closely relates to the one of the 
NP and the stereochemistry may be varied as well1. Figure 3a highlights an example of a NP and how a NP 
derived molecule could look like. Note that the scaffold is retained, as is the stereochemistry. Furthermore 
the figure shows an example of a NP and how a NP inspired molecule could look like, here the scaffold and 
some of the stereochemistry are changed as well (Figure 3b).

drugs (defined as drugs that modulate a so far unparalleled biological pathway or drug target) from 1999-
2013, of which 13% were natural product or compounds derived thereof and 15% were natural substance-
derived molecules (including peptides)6. The full overview of the distribution of the type of molecules of the

9. Steroids and sterols 
Steroids all consists of the same scaffold of four rings, three six-membered rings and one five-membered 
ring, but vary in substituents and functional groups. Gonane 33 is referred to as the steroid nucleus as it is 
the parent structure of the steroids7 (Figure 7). There are several chiral centres in steroids, but only one 
isomer occurs naturally for a given steroid8. A subgroup of steroids are sterols, which is also call steroid 
alcohols, and are the term for alcohol-containing steroids9. 
 
Figure 77, Gonane 33 which is the steroid nucleus. 
 
 
Due to the extensive hydrocarbon skeleton, steroids are very hydrophobic, which is an important 
characteristic as many e.g. the hormonal steroids, have to cross membranes in order to bind to intracellular 
receptors. Many have polar side groups, which are important for binding, but do not significantly change 
the overall hydrophobic nature of the hormonal steroids8. Bile acids (vide infra) are polar derivatives of 
cholesterol, and contain both polar and nonpolar regions, making them effective detergents. 
Sterols are found, amongst other places, in the membranes both of animals (zoosterols), plants 
(phytosterols) and microorganisms (mycosterols) such as yeast. The most common zoosterol is cholesterol 
which is present in the human body in approx. 250 g scale, whereas others are present in less than 1mg 
scale. Cholesterol is a precursor for many other sterols and steroids in the human body and has a number 
of non-hormonal roles (vide infra), hence the big amount found in the human body8,10. 
9.1. Structure and nomenclature of steroids 
The carbon atoms in steroids are numbered as shown for cholesterol 34 (Figure 8). The four rings are called 
A-D, which also can be seen for cholesterol below.  
The stereochemistry is described by α and β, were α describes substituents that are situated below the 
plane of the rings, and β describes substituents that are situated above the plane. For example the methyl 
groups (C18 and C19) are β-substituents in cholesterol.  
The position of double bonds are described by Δx, were x is the number of the carbon for the double bond. 
If there is any ambiguity, both the numbers of carbons are noted, for example with cholesterol, if only 
noted Δ5, it is not clear whether the double bond is between C4 and C5 or between C5 and C6, hence the 
correct notation would be Δ5(6) as the double bond is between C5 and C68,11.

9.2. Chemical biology of steroids  
9.2.1. Cholesterol 
Cholesterol is an important constituent in cell membranes as it modulates membrane fluidity. It constitutes 
almost 25 % of the membrane lipids in some nerve cells, but is on the other hand almost nonexistent in 
other cells12.  
Due to its hydrophobicity, it cannot dissolve in blood, hence it needs a transport molecule; which is usually 
low-density lipoprotein (LDL), high-density lipoprotein (HDL) or bile acids (vide infra). LDL is used for 
transporting cholesterol (and triglycerides) from the liver to the peripheral tissues. Cells that need 
cholesterol produce LDL receptors placed in the cell membrane, which bind LDL and enable them to be 
endocytosed into the cell where they release cholesterol. HDL are used for the transportation back from 
the tissue to the liver. It is called HDL as they contain higher proportions of protein that LDL. HDL increases 
in size as the travel around the body and picks up cholesterol from the various tissues. 
High levels of LDL or low levels of HDL have been showed to associated with mortality due to coronary 
heart diseases. High levels of LDL or too low levels of HDL means that cholesterol will be retained in the 
arteries as LDL transports it there and there is not enough HDL to transport it away. Cholesterol in the 
arteries can cause formation of fatty plaques, which can cause atherosclerosis if the arteries become too 
narrow, or heart attack if a clot rips off and ends in the heart, or a stroke if the clot ends in the brain. Hence 
lowering the levels of LDL or increasing the levels of HDL, the risk of strokes, heart attacks and 
atherosclerosis will be lower13.  
Cholesterol can be obtained from the diet, but can also be synthesized in cells. Cholesterol is synthesized 
from Acetyl Coenzyme A 35 and 36 in a long pathway of several step, which can be grouped into three 
stages (Figure 10):  
a) Stage one takes place in the cytoplasm and is the synthesis of the key building block; an activated 
isoprene unite, more specific isopentenyl pyrophosphate 37. 
b) Stage two takes place in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), and is the condensation of six of the 
building blocks formed in stage one, to form one molecule; squalene 38.  
c) Stage three also takes place in ER, here squalene cyclises to the tetracyclic product lanosterol 39 
which subsequently is converted into cholesterol 3411.

9.2.2. Lowering cholesterol levels 
In normal functioning cells the levels of cholesterol should be at a normal level, however problems arise if 
the diet is too rich in cholesterol or the cells does not function correctly. If the diet is the problem, the 
obvious way would be to reduce cholesterol in the diet. If this is not possible or not enough, blocking the 
synthesis of cholesterol can be an alternative. Drugs for this have been designed. Statins are an example of 
this, as they target the enzyme (3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase (HMGR)) which 
catalyzes the rate determining step for the synthesis of cholesterol. The discovery of statins started when 
the HMGR enzyme was identified as a potential target. On the path to find a lead compound which would 
inhibit HMGR, researchers began by looking at compounds produced by microorganisms i.e. NPs. Their lead 
compound and first active inhibitor was compactin 41, which was isolated from Penicillium citrinum (Figure 
11a). With a 10000 fold higher affinity for the enzyme compared with the natural substrate, compactin was 
a highly potent inhibitor. Although it started clinical trials it never reached the market. Later the potent

inhibitor mevinolin 42, was isolated from the fermentation broth from Aspergillus terreus, having a closely 
related structure to compactin (Figure 11a). After clinical trials it entered the market in 1987 as the drug

structures (Figure 11a). These are called type 1 statins, and they all had some side effects and were found 
difficult to synthesise due to a large number of asymmetric centres.

9.2.5. Bile acids 
Bile acids play several major roles in the body, some of which are mentioned here. An important role is the 
solubilisation of cholesterol hence making it possible to transport it from the hepatocytes to the intestinal 
lumen leading to excretion14. The bile acids  solubilise cholesterol by forming mixed micelles containing bile 
acids, monoglycerids, phospholidpids and cholesterol.18 
Another important role is the lipid transport, where the bile acids promote absorption of lipids from the 
diet. The lipids are absorbed due to formation of mixed micelles formed by the bile acids in the biliary 
canaliculus. They are made of bile acids, phospholipids and cholesterol, hence called mixed micelles. The 
absorption of lipids, also include the absorption of fat soluble vitamins, such as vitamin A, D, E and K1. Bile 
acids also are important for cholesterol biosynthesis, as there is a negative feedback regulation of the 
cholesterol and bile acid biosynthesis. This negative feedback mechanism seems to act on the 
concentration of bile acid in the hepatocytes. When the concentration is high, the biosynthesis is low (as 
bile acid is synthesised from cholesterol, both biosynthesis are regulated in parallel), and opposite if the 
concentration is low, the biosynthesis is high, as there then is a high need for more bile acid. This feedback 
mechanism is important as it always makes sure that there is enough bile acid, to have sufficient uptake of 
lipids and nutrient from the diet. It also makes sure that there is not too much bile acid stored, as bile acids 
are cytotoxic when present in abnormally high concentrations14. Recently bile acids were found to serve as 
signalling molecules, capable of activating several different receptors19. 
Bile acids are formed in the hepatocytes located in the liver, in a process coordinated by multiple enzymes. 
Two bile acids are made in the hepatocytes (for humans); chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA) 53 and cholic acid 
51 (Figure 14+Figure 15). CDCA contains two hydroxy groups, one at C3 and one at C7. Cholic acid has an 
additional hydroxy group at C12. These bile acids are termed the primary bile acids, as they are formed in 
the hepatocytes. 
After the conversion to bile acid, the molecules are conjugated with glycine (for humans mainly) or taurine, 
yielding a strong acid, which at the pH found in the small intestine is fully ionized (pKa of glycocholic acid 54 
is 3.7720, and pH is 4-7, as pH>pKa it is deprotonated) (Figure 15). This conjugation, and ionization, yields a 
molecule that is amphipathic, membrane dissolving and a water-soluble detergent. The conjugation 
happens before the bile acid leaves the hepatocytes. From the hepatocytes the bile flows down the biliary 
tract (due to osmosis) and on to the gallbladder were the bile acid pool is stored until needed. The bile 
acids are needed when a meal is eaten, and at this time, cholecystokinin is released, which triggers the 
gallbladder to deliver the bile acids to the duodenum. In the intestines the bile acids perform their job, as 
mediators for uptake (vide supra). The bile acids are absorbed from the distal intestine and returned to the 
liver, where they can be secreted into the bile again. This cycle of secretion, absorption and resecretion is 
termed the enterohepatic circulation (EC). During this EC the structure of the bile acids are altered in the 
distal intestine by bacterial enzymes. One alteration is the deconjugation, where the glycine/taurine are 
removed from the bile acid. In this case most of the unconjugated bile acids is reabsorbed to the liver, 
where it gets reconjugated in the hepatocytes and enters the EC as normal. Another modification is by 
bacterial dehydratases which remove the hydroxy group at C7, forming deoxycholic acid (DCA) 55 from 
cholic acid, and lithocholic acid 56 from CDCA (Figure 15). DCA and lithocholic acid are termed secondary 
bile acids, as they are formed from the primary bile acids. The secondary bile acids are to some extend

reabsorbed in the small intestine, hence getting excreted. The excretion of lithocholic acid is very fortunate, 
as it is highly hepatotoxic14.

In a healthy person the uptake of conjugated bile acids in the hepatocyte is rapidly followed by elimination, 
making sure the concentration does not get too high. If the elimination is impaired bile acids accumulate 
followed by intrusion in the mitochondria, leading to damage and ultimately cell death due to oxidative 
stress through generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS)14,19.  
There are several ways disturbance in the EC can happen e.g. biliary obstruction/cholestasis. It can occur 
when e.g. a gallstone blocks the common duct, retaining the bile acids in the hepatocyte leading to cell 
death (as described above). If there is full blocking of the common duct, there will be no bile acids present 
in the small intestine hence the dietary triglycerides and fat-soluble vitamins are insufficiently absorbed.  
 
As bile acids perform multiple functions, it is also possible to use them for medicinal purposes, known as 
bile acid therapy. Bile acid therapy can be divided into two, replacement therapy which aims to correct a 
bile acid deficiency, and displacement therapy where the composition of the circulating bile acids are 
changed. The purpose of the displacement therapy can be to either decrease the biliary cholesterol 
secretions to modulate  cholesterol metabolism or to decrease cytotoxicity of the bile acids. One of the 
earliest examples of displacement therapy was the administration of CDCA, which caused a reduction in

biliary cholesterol secretion along with degradation of gallstones. Since CDCA is cytotoxtic in high 
concentration, it was wanted to use another bile acid, and for this purpose ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) 57, 
which is a noncytotoxic bile acid, was used (Figure 16). UDCA has identical structure as CDCA except the 
hydroxygroup of C7 is β oriented instead of α. UDCA is also used to prolong the interval from diagnosis to 
liver transplantation for patients with the autoimmune disease primary biliary cirrhosis14.  
Bile acids are being associated with colorectal carcinogenesis, and are assumed to act as promoters but not 
directly as carcinogens (in humans). A possible reason for this may be the generation of ROS which cause 
oxidative DNA damage, which then can cause mutations which can lead to unusual expression of tumor 
suppressor genes or oncogenes. Studies with UDCA suggest that it can have a protective effect against 
colorectal carcinogenesis, and several studies have investigated the potentially use of UDCA as 
chemoprevention.

The Robinson annulation consists of two steps, a conjugated addition (Michael addition) followed by an 
intramolecular aldol condensation. The Robinson annulation is a well proven way to form the Wieland–
Miescher ketone (WMK), which is a common precursor of the A and B ring scaffold of sterols.

as a base, to form the stable enolate of the di-ketone, which then acts as an nucleophile and carries out a 
conjugate addition onto the enone, forming the tri-ketone 5821.

11.1.2. 
Step 3: Protection of the ketone 
It was desired to protect the ketone over the enone to selectively reduce the enone to the alcohol to 
achieve the desired scaffold. The chosen protecting group is a dioxolane, which protects against 
nucleophiles and bases and it is also easily removed again with water and acid21. 
 
The protection is an acid-catalysed dioxolane formation, which goes through a hemiacetal intermediate 
(Scheme 7). First step is the formation of the oxonium ion by protonation of the ketone, which is then 
attacked by the alcohol, and after deprotonation of the adduct the hemiacetal is formed. The alcohol of the 
hemiacetal are hereafter protonated to yield the oxonium ion after elimination of water. Again the 
oxonium ion is attacked by the alcohol to yield the dioxolane after deprotection. This formation of a cyclic 
acetal are often easier to make than acyclic ones, as the alcohol that attacks the oxonium ion of the 
hemiacetal is close by ready to react, and gets water less of a chance to hydrolyze the acetal21. Even though 
the removal of water in the reaction is still very important as it is all in equilibrium, and removal of water 
will drive the reaction towards the dioxolane. Since this reaction is kept at RT, it is not possible to use a 
Dean-Stark apparatus as for the previous reaction, hence molecular sieves are used. Though sieves are used 
to remove the water, the reaction will not go to completion, the equilibrium will stay at a steady-state, and 
the nonprotected WMK needs to be removed by purification.

To obtain the cis-decalin system as seen for bile acids, a hydrogenation was carried according to several 
papers which claimed to be able to obtain the cis-decalin system selectively25–28. 
Hydrogen is a reducing agent in the presence of a metal catalyst. The catalyst is often finely divided on a 
solid support, as the hydrogenation takes place on the surface of the metal (Scheme 8). The first step of the 
hydrogenation is the adsorption of hydrogen onto the metal surface, breaking the H-H bond hence 
distributing the H atoms on the surface ready to react with the substrate. Next the alkene binds to the 
metal and the hydrogen gets transferred from the metal to the alkene, yielding the alkane21.  
Scheme 8, Path of a hydrogenation21.

the flat surface of the catalyst hence forming the cis-decalin21 (Figure 17). Sterics are also important, and in 
this case the dioxolane hinders the catalyst from binding as easily from the topface hence forming the 
trans-decalin.

Step 5: Reduction of ketone 
To obtain the desired alcohol 1 the ketone 60 was reduced using sodium borohydride. 
The mechanism involves a hydride transfer from the boron to the carbon of the ketone; electrons in the σ-
bond act as nucleophiles through the hydrogen, and moves away from the rest of the BH4 anion becoming 
bonded to the ketone through the π* orbital, which is the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)21 
(Scheme 9). The negative charge on the oxygen can be stabilized by the sodium ion, before it gets 
protonated either by the protic solvent (in this case MeOH) or when quenched with water.  
 
Scheme 9, A general mechanism for the reduction of ketones with NaBH4. 
 
The stereochemistry of the major product places the hydroxyl group is trans to the methyl group (Scheme 
10). To see this more clearly, the ketone is drawn in its chair conformation below. An axial attack is the 
most thermodynamically favourable hence leading to the equatorial positioning of the hydroxyl group, 
which also is the most stable conformation.   
 
Scheme 10, Chair conformation of the two possible alcohols after reduction.

structure of 60, the methyl group might hinder an attack a bit, which would lead to an attack opposite of 
the methyl group yielding the cis-configuration (Figure 18). Since the methyl group does not block 
completely, the steric hindrance are not that big, and it would be presumed that the axial attack would still

Intramolecular Heck reaction 
Indoles and their derivatives are  commonly used heterocycles in drug discovery and are found in many 
classes of natural products, including alkaloids. The intramolecular Heck reaction is a way to form pyrrole-
containing multicyclic systems including indoles and aza-indoles.  
 
 
First the imine is formed through condensation of the amide and the ketone (Scheme 15). The imine then 
tautomerise to the enamine, which participates in the catalytic cycle. The enamine enters the catalytic cycle 
in the oxidative addition, the next step which is the carbometallation, the new C-C bond is formed. At the 
β-hydride elimination step the indole is separated from the catalyst which in the last step is regenerated by 
reductive elimination21.

Baeyer-Villiger oxidation 
To access analogues with a lactone scaffold, ring expansions using the Baeyer-Villiger oxidation were 
attempted with two different starting materials and oxidizing reagents. 
This reaction is a ring expansion of the Baeyer Villiger oxidation type. For this reaction peroxide was used as 
the oxidizing agent, and a fluorinated tetraarylborate catalyst. The fluorinated tetraarylborate catalyst 
coordinates to the carbonyl to make it more electrophilic hence promoting the nucleophilic attack of the 
hydrogen peroxide. The fluorinated tetraarylborate catalyst is highly lipophilic, which makes it suitable for a 
two phase system, as the catalyst makes it possible for the reaction to occur in the organic phase  or in the 
phase interface, which is in contrast to other Lewis- or Brønsted acids which serve as catalysts in the 
aqueous phase or the phase interface39.

The reaction are assumed to go via an ionic mechanism, and not a radical mechanism (Scheme 21). This is 
due to the reasoning that there is no radical initiator present, as the reactions are carried out in darkness, 
hence the reaction would not start if it went by a radical mechanism. The mechanism is simple, as the 
ketone is on its enol -form, and the electrons attacks the electrophilic bromine ending with a proton 
transfer yielding the brominated compound and bromobarbituric acid, which can provide a second 
equivalent for bromination.

expected product would be 78 (Figure 21a). However, taking the mechanism into account, in principle the

Hantzsch thiazole synthesis 
The Hantzsch thiazole synthesis is a reliable route to form thiazoles. 2-aminothiazoles are used widely in 
medicinal chemistry, as 2-aminothiazoles with antimicrobial, antibacterial and antifungal activity are 
known. Recently anticancer, antitumor, antidiebatic and anticonvulsant activity have also been reported for 
2-aminothiazoles43. As 2-aminothiazoles shows such a wide range of biological activities, it could be of 
interest to fuse them with a sterol scaffold. 
The Hantzsch-Thiazole reaction was carried out inspired by Zhang et al.44 (Scheme 22) 
 
Scheme 22, The Hanztsch-Thiazole synthesis. 
 
An α-halo-carbonyl reacts with a thioamide (Scheme 23). Sulfur attacks the α-C, by a nucleophilic attack, 
resulting in the release of HX and the acyclic intermediate. The sulfur attacks the alkyl halide as they are 
'soft' nucleophiles and electrophiles respectively. The cyclisation happen as nitrogen attacks the ketone 
(which are 'hard' nucleophiles and electrophiles respectively)21. Finally elimination of water forms the 
thiazole driven by the formation of an aromatic system.

12. 
Conclusion and future work 
A summary of the different reactions which have been part of the project is shown in Scheme 25 along with 
suggestions for future work to elaborate the sterol library (in blue). 
For the optimization of the library precursor, it was possible to obtain higher yields for the formation of the 
WMK. It was also possible to skip a purification step at this point. After the protection the purifications step 
was optimized using a dry column instead of flash column, hence better separation was seen.  
A great deal of time was spent optimizing the hydrogenation. Both hydrogenation with and without flow 
was tried. Different conditions, like variations in the solvent, the catalyst type and -loading, pressure, flow 
rate and the temperature. The optimal conditions were found to be with flow hydrogenation, but as the 
results were not reproducible a standard hydrogenation were chosen as the optimal one. The procedure 
was tested for scalability with success. Even though the hydrogenation step was optimized, it was still 
yielding the trans-isomer and not the cis-isomer exclusively.  
For the reduction of the ketone, formation of diastereoisomers were also seen, yielding four different 
diastereoisomers. After the reduction a purification step was needed as there were impurities which was 
not able to purify off after deprotection. 
From the precursor 1 four analogue reactions were attempted where only two was successful, yielding 
three pure analogues as single isomers. No attempts for optimization was made. The bromination of 1 
yielding in the second precursor 6 was successful, and it was possible to isolate 6 as a single isomer. Only 
one attempt on making analogues from 6 was made and it was successful, yielding in an aminothiazole 
formate ion as a single isomer.  Further a reduction of 8 gave the two allylic alcohols, which it was possible 
to isolate as single isomers.  
In further pursuit of analgoues with the cis-decalin system, optimizing the hydrogenation and reduction to 
only yield one stereoisomer or finding a way to separate the isomers would be highly recommended. If 
possible, then maybe the Beayer-Villiger oxidation would only yield a pair of regioisomers. Also attempting 
to make the Beayer-Villiger oxidation using microwave could be of interest if the starting material could be 
isolated as a single diastereomer.  
As the Hantzch-thiazole reaction was successful and the 2-aminothiazole are seen in many type of drugs, 
pursuing other analogues could be of interest. This could be done by using other thioamides with different 
substituents instead of thiourea, which are commercially available.  
Optimizing the intramolecular Heck-reaction would be of interest, as this type of chemistry allows one to 
make several compounds. Further reactions with the brominated precursor would also be of interest, it 
could be e.g. synthesising quinoxalines. 
All successful reactions made with 1 could be made with the deprotected 10 and 9. 
During the project it took at lot longer to optimize the hydrogenation than planned. It was assumed to be a 
reaction which would yield a single isomer exclusively as this have been reported several times. This was 
not the case hence taking up a lot of time, which was planned to be used for synthesising analogues. Also 
the reduction and the bromination was more challenging than planned, hence pushing the plan even more.

General directions 
Commercially available reagents were used without further purification (except for methyl-vinyl-ketone 
which was distilled prior to use and non-anhydrous ethylene glycol which was distilled with toluene prior to 
use) and all solvents were of HPLC quality. Dry solvents were obtained directly from a Pure Solv installation 
from Innovative Technology, Tronyx. 
Reactions were monitored by thin layer chromatography (TLC) and/or reversed-phase ultra-performance 
liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (RP-UPLC-MS). Analytical TLC was conducted on Merck 
aluminium sheets covered with silica (C60 F254). The plates were either visualized under UV-light or stained 
by dipping in a developing agent (KMnO4 [0.75 g KMnO4, 5 g potassium carbonate, 0.6125 mL 10% sodium 
hydroxide and 100 mL water]) followed by heating.  
Flash column chromatography was performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 μm) silicagel, with 
pressure applied from a handpump. 
Chromatography using drycolumn was performed using Silica gel 60 (0.015-0.040 mm) from Merck, with 
housevacuum. 
Celit® 512 from Fluka/Honeywell was used for dryloading samples for chromatography and for filtering of 
catalysts. 
For the recording of 1H NMR and 13C NMR a Bruker Ascend with a Prodigy cryoprobe (operating at 400 MHz 
for proton and 100 MHz for carbon) was used. The chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million 
(ppm) and the coupling constants (J) in Hz. For spectra recorded in DMSO-d6, signal positions were 
measured relative to the signal for DMSO (δ 2.50 ppm for 1H NMR and δ 39.43 ppm for 13C NMR). For 
spectra recorded in CDCl3, signal positions were measured relative to the signal for CHCl3 (δ 7.26 ppm for 1H 
NMR and δ 77.0 ppm for 13C NMR). 
Analytical RP-UPLC-MS (ESI) analysis was performed on a S2 Waters AQUITY RP-UPLC system equipped with 
a diode array detector using an Thermo Accucore C18 column (d 2.6 μm, 2.1 x 50 mm; column temp: 50 °C; 
flow: 1.0 mL/min). Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% HCO2H in MeCN) were used in a linear 
gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 min and then held for 0.1 min at 100% B (total run time: 2.6 min). The LC 
system was coupled to a SQD mass spectrometer. 
Preparative RP-HPLC was carried out on a Waters Alliance reversed-phase HPLC system consisting of a 
Waters 2545 Binary Gradient Module equipped with an xBridge BEH C18 OBD Prep Column (130 Å, 5 μm, 
30 x 150 mm) operating at 20 °C and a flow rate of 20 mL/min, a Waters Photodiode Array Detector 
(detecting at 210-600 nm), a Waters UV Fraction Manager, and a Waters 2767 Sample Manager. Eluents A1 
(0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B1 (0.1% HCO2H in MeCN) were used. 
Analytical LC-HRMS (ESI) analysis was performed on a Waters Alliance 2695 system. Samples were injected 
directly and the LC system was coupled to a Waters LCT Premier XE Micromass equipped with a Lock Mass 
probe operating in positive electrospray mode. Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% HCO2H in 
MeCN) were used in a 1:1 ratio for a total run time of 2 mins. 
Microwave reactions was done on a Biotage® robot Eight with Initiator+ system. 
For freeze-drying samples a Scanvac CoolSafe™ 110-4 Pro was used.
