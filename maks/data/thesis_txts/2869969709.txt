Abstract
Globally, the use of timber and other biobased alternatives in the building industry is
increasing as they contribute to urban sustainability.
A commonly used construction
material is cross-laminated timber, CLT. From a fire safety perspective, this introduces
new challenges for engineers, particularly due to concerns about continued burning and
structural degradation of the elements even after a heating phase.
Eurocode 5, EN 1995-1-2, provides calculation methods for the fire design of timber ele-
ments, but these primarily consider standard fire exposure for a limited duration and do
not account for ongoing heat penetration during cooling.
This thesis presents an experimental and numerical investigation of the thermal and me-
chanical performance of encapsulated CLT elements exposed to a fully developed fire.
The experimental investigation includes two studies: a Cone Test, which provided data for
validating a numerical thermal model, and a Mobile Furnace Test, which provided data
for evaluating strength reduction. In both experiments, two sample cases were examined -
CLT encapsulated with either one or two layers of type F gypsum boards - each subjected
to a heating and cooling phase.
The Cone Test included four tests for each sample case, with exposure to a constant heat
flux of 75 kW for 15 and 45 minutes, respectively. The results showed high consistency
across both sample cases, making them well suited for comparison with numerical thermal
models. The Mobile Furnace Test included two tests per sample case, exposed to the
ISO 834 fire curve for 21 and 55 minutes, respectively. The reduced cross-sections and
bending capacities were determined using the Reduced Cross-Section Method (RCSM)
and the Advanced Calculation Method (ACM) as provided in both the current and revised
versions of Eurocode 5, EN 1995-1-2. The greatest reductions were observed during the
cooling phase when using the ACM, where the capacity dropped to 44.7% and 39.5% of
the original capacity for the two sample cases, respectively. In the RCSM, the capacity
dropped to 84.8% and 76.6% when using the current EC5 and to 66.6% and 62.9% when
using the revised version.

The numerical study involved thermal heat transfer models in Abaqus FEA Learning
Edition. Separate models were created for heating and cooling to best match the Cone
Test results. The models successfully captured overall temperature trends, particularly
during heating, though deviations appeared at the gypsum-CLT interface. A sensitivity
study showed that thermal properties of gypsum significantly influenced heat transfer,
while CLT variations had minimal impact.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The building industry accounts for more than 40% of human-caused greenhouse gas emis-
sions, making it one of the most environmentally impactful sectors [1]. As a result, there
has been a strong push toward sustainability, leading to a shift in material choices. Glob-
ally, the use of green materials such as timber and other biobased alternatives has been
increasing, as these materials store carbon within structural elements and contribute to
urban sustainability [2]. While this transition is a step in the right direction, it also intro-
duces new challenges for engineers. Incorporating more natural and inherently combustible
materials into structures may increase the overall fuel load, raising concerns about con-
tinued burning and structural degradation even after the fire has been extinguished. This
risk is particularly significant when biobased materials are initially exposed or become
exposed due to the failure of protective layers.
Since the late 1990s, cross-laminated timber (CLT) has become an increasingly popu-
lar engineered wood product in Europe, with its use expanding to mid-rise and high-rise
buildings [3]. However, this growth in application has amplified concerns about fire safety,
as timber gradually loses strength and stiffness when subjected to elevated temperatures
and moisture exposure [4]. Various approximate structural fire design methods exist, with
Eurocode 5, EN1995-1-2, providing calculation approaches for the fire design of timber
structures based on standardized tests. However, these methods primarily consider nor-
mative fire exposures for a limited duration, corresponding to the required fire resistance
classification of the timber element [4]. In reality, heat continues to penetrate the material
even after exposure, meaning that timber elements may reach higher temperatures than
initially assumed following a design fire scenario.

1.2. AIM AND METHOD
It is widely known that timber begins to char at high temperatures, typically around
the 300°C isotherm. However, what is less commonly known is the significant reduction
in mechanical properties that occurs at temperatures lower than 300°C, especially for
compression.
Studies indicate that at just 100°C, the compressive strength of timber
is reduced by approximately 75%, while its elastic modulus decreases by 65% [5]. This
suggests that even if the timber remains below the charring threshold and no visible
degradation occurs, the structural integrity may still be severely compromised, increasing
the risk of failure [5].
This issue is particularly critical for columns and walls, which primarily experience high
compressive loads, but it also affects beams and slabs where compression occurs on the
top surfaces.
Fire design methods typically assume exposure from below, potentially
overlooking the risk of failure in the top layer when exposed to fire from above. This
study focuses on the fire performance of encapsulated timber elements, examining both
temperature development and mechanical response throughout the entire duration of a
standard fire, including the critical cooling phase.
1.2
Aim and Method
The aim of this project is to investigate the thermal and structural response of encapsulated
CLT elements exposed to a fully developed fire from above, with a particular focus on the
effects that arise at the end of heating and during the cooling phase. To achieve this, a
combined experimental and numerical approach is applied.
The experimental investigation consists of controlled fire tests to measure temperature
distributions and assess mechanical performance using the methods outlined in both the
current and revised Eurocode 5, EN1995-1-2.
The numerical study involves finite element modeling in Abaqus FEA to simulate heat
transfer, allowing for a direct comparison between experimental results and computational
predictions.
Combined, these methods provide a comprehensive understanding of the fire performance
of encapsulated CLT elements and contribute to the development of improved fire design
approaches.

Theoretical Background
This chapter presents the fundamental theories and principles relevant to this study, form-
ing the foundation for both an experimental and numerical investigations. It provides an
overview of fire behavior and thermal response, as well as mechanical and thermal proper-

2.1
Fire
A fire occurs when a combustible material comes into contact with oxygen in the presence
of heat [6]. In a closed environment, a fire progresses through three distinct phases. Ini-
tially, the pre-flashover fire phase marks the ignition and growth of the fire as it gradually
expands in size and intensity. Once the fire has spread to all combustible materials within
the enclosed space and reaches peak intensity, it enters the flashover phase. Following
the consumption of most of the available fuel, the fire enters the post-flashover fire phase,
characterized by gradual decay. This final phase of a fully developed fire is of primary
concern when designing structures to withstand fire conditions [6]. The different phases
of a fully developed fire are illustrated in Figure 2.1.

2.1.1
Design Fires
When designing a structure to withstand the effects of a fully developed fire, selecting an
appropriate design fire is essential. This can be achieved using either a physically based
fire model, which accounts for the entire duration of the fire, including the cooling phase,
or a nominal fire curve, which analyzes the temperature exposure for a specified period
without considering the cooling phase [8].
In practice, nominal design fires are most commonly used, as physically based fire models
are more complex and time-consuming to develop. Several nominal fire curves are avail-
able, depending on the type of fire exposure anticipated for the structural elements. The
primary nominal fire curves include the standard fire curve, the external fire curve, and the
hydrocarbon fire curve. These different nominal design curves are depicted in Figure 2.2.
Most national standards are based on either the ASTM E119 curve or the ISO 834 curve.
The ASTM E119 curve is primarily used in the United States and countries that follow U.S.
construction codes, whereas the ISO 834 curve is internationally recognized and widely
adopted in Europe, Asia, and other regions, including Denmark.

Timber differs significantly from other common construction materials such as steel and
concrete. It exhibits anisotropic mechanical properties, meaning its strength and stiffness
vary depending on the direction of the load relative to the grain, resulting in very different
behaviors when forces are applied in different directions.

better under compression loads than tensile loads. Furthermore, the strength of timber
changes over time, as it gradually decreases under long-duration loads, also known as creep.
This makes timber a unique and complex material that requires careful considerations in
structural applications [6].

2.2.2
Cross-Laminated Timber - CLT
Cross-laminated timber (CLT) is a prefabricated engineered wood product composed of
at least three orthogonally bonded layers of coniferous or deciduous wood lamellas, which
are glued together [13]. The thickness of the lamellas typically ranges between 20 and 60
mm, varying across the different layers of the CLT panel.

==PAGE:30==

Each lamella is strength-graded according to national standards and typically all layers
consist of the same strength class. To optimize the structural performance of a panel,
higher-strength wood can be used in the outer layers and aligned with the primary load-
bearing direction, where stresses are generally the highest [14].
Figure 2.4 shows the
principles of a CLT panel.
Figure 2.4: Cross-Laminated timber panel; a) Layup and b) Axis direction. Figure 5

Due to the arrangement of lamellas in a crosswise manner, almost all the load carrying
capacity lies within the longitudinal piles. The strength contribution from the crosswise
layers are typically neglected or assumed to contribute with 1/30th of the longitudinal
layers [16].
A critical factor in CLT panel production is the moisture content of the wood. Depending
on the type of adhesive used, the moisture content of each lamella must be maintained
between 8% and 15% at the time of bonding, with a maximum variation of 5% between
adjacent lamellas. To ensure optimal bond strength, the moisture content should be as
close as possible to the equilibrium moisture content of the finished structure [14].
2.2.2.1

2.2.3
Behavior of timber at elevated temperatures
Temperature variations caused by fire exposure can induce both imposed and constrained
expansions and deformations, potentially leading to structural effects of actions such as
forces and moments [13].
A well-known phenomenon that happens to timber when exposed to fire is the fact that it
starts to undergo charring. This phenomenon consists of three sequential sub-processes:
drying, pyrolysis, and oxidation. When the temperature in the timber rises, the water
molecules that are bonded within the timber begin to absorb the heat and break which
causes the water to evaporate and the timber to dry out. Once the timber is dry, the
heat starts to break the long polymer chains, which are the load-bearing part of the
timber. The chains turn into non-load-bearing char and flammable volatiles; the pyrolysis
process. These volatiles then migrate toward the surface, where they ignite and become an
additional heat supplier to the timber. Consequently, the char layer propagates deeper into
the wood until an equilibrium is reached—either when the char layer becomes suﬀiciently
thick to insulate the remaining load-bearing timber or when the entire section is converted
to char, causing a complete loss of load-bearing capacity [18].
Since the boiling point of water is 100°C, this marks the onset of the drying process. The
transition from intact timber to char is defined by the 300°C isotherm, as stipulated by
EC5 [17]. This indicates that the pyrolysis process - and the associated strength reduction
- occurs within the temperature range of 100°C to 300°C.
EC5 [17] provides three primary methods for the structural fire design of timber members:
the Reduced Cross-Section Method (RCSM), the Reduced Properties Method (RPM) and
an Advanced Calculation Method (ACM) given in EC5 Annex B.

Finite Element Modeling
Finite Element Modeling (FEM) is a widely used numerical method for solving complex
engineering problems, particularly in cases where analytical solutions are not feasible.
FEM is primarily applied to field problems, including stress analysis and heat transfer in
solids and structures. In its most basic form, the finite element method is an approxima-
tion technique that subdivides a complex problem domain into numerous smaller, simpler
elements. Each of these finite elements has a well-defined behavior that can be described
using relatively simple equations, allowing for an approximate solution to be constructed
for the entire problem [20].
A variety of software programs are available for performing complex FEM analyses, one
of which is Abaqus FEA.

2.4.1
Finite Element Analysis
The process of a Finite Element Analysis (FEA) is summarized in Figure 2.10. The first
step involves identifying the physical problem to be analyzed. A corresponding math-
ematical model is then developed, incorporating key assumptions and defining essential
parameters such as geometry, material properties, boundary conditions, and loading condi-
tions. Once an appropriate mathematical model is established, the finite element solution
must be configured by selecting suitable element types, mesh density, and solution param-
eters. These choices significantly influence the accuracy and reliability of the results.
After obtaining a solution, the accuracy of the model must be assessed using predefined
accuracy criteria. If the model satisfies these criteria, it is considered suﬀicient. However,
if the criteria are not met, adjustments to the finite element solution parameters - such
as mesh refinement - are required. This iterative process continues until the accuracy
criteria are met. If the results still prove inadequate, the mathematical model itself must
be refined to better represent the physical problem.

2.4.2
Elements
In FEM, it is crucial to understand what an element is and how it behaves in order to
make the right decisions when setting up the finite element solution. The behavior of an
element is defined by five key aspects: the element family, the degrees of freedom, the
number of nodes, the formulation, and the integration, all described in [22].
2.4.2.1
Element Families
Different types of element families exist, each corresponding to a specific geometry type.
The most commonly used families are shown in Figure 2.11. Broadly, element families
can be categorized into two groups: structural families, which include shell, beam, plate,
membrane, and truss elements, and continuum families, which consist of solid continuum
and infinite elements. In a stress/displacement analysis, both family groups can be uti-
lized, whereas in a thermal analysis, only the continuum families and shell elements are
applicable.
Additionally, elements differ in dimensionality. Beam and truss elements are one-dimensional,
plate, shell, and membrane elements are two-dimensional, and solid continuum elements
are three-dimensional. The geometry of an element determines its dimensionality, which
in turn affects the number of nodes and, consequently, the degrees of freedom.
Figure 2.11: Examples of different element families. Figure 21.1.1-1 from [22].
2.4.2.2
Degrees of Freedom
Each element is composed of multiple nodes, which serve as connection points in the
elements. Each node is assigned a certain number of degrees of freedom (DOF), which
represent the fundamental variables computed during a FEA. In a stress/displacement
analysis, the DOFs correspond to both translational and rotational components in the
nodes, while in a heat transfer analysis, they represent the temperature at each node.
Performance of Encapsulated Timber Elements in Compression Exposed to a Fully Developed Fire

Translational DOFs exist in three directions: x, y, and z. Similarly, temperature DOFs
are defined along these three axes. Rotational DOFs are also present, corresponding to
rotations about the x-, y-, and z-axes. Selecting the appropriate element type is crucial for
accurately capturing the behavior of the system in the analysis. For coupled thermal-stress
simulations, both translational, rotational, and temperature DOFs must be included. A
shell element, for instance, is suitable for such coupled analyses.
2.4.2.3
Number of Nodes and Order of Interpolation
All degrees of freedom are computed at the nodes of each element. To determine dis-
placement, temperature, or other degrees of freedom within the element, interpolation is
used between the nodal values. The interpolation order depends on the number of nodes
within the element. If an element has only corner nodes, as shown in (a) in Figure 2.12,
linear interpolation is applied in each direction, classifying it as a linear or first-order ele-
ment. If the element also includes midside nodes, as seen in (b), quadratic interpolation
is used, making it a quadratic or second-order element. In the case of modified triangular
elements, such as (c), a modified second-order interpolation method is applied, leading to
the designation of a modified second-order element.

Formulation
Each element family is governed by a specific mathematical framework that defines its
behavior. In convective heat transfer analysis, Abaqus employs the Eulerian description,
where the elements remain fixed in space while the material flows through them. For all
other stress/displacement elements, Abaqus uses the Lagrangian description, in which the
elements deform along with the material, capturing its movement and response to external
forces [22].

determine temperature [26].

3.1.4
Cone Test: Discoveries
Firstly, the CLT top surface reached 300°C after 15 minutes for samples with one layer of
gypsum and after 45 minutes for those with two layers. While the test conditions did not
fully replicate the growth phase of a standard fire curve, the results still differed from the
values of 21 minutes (1 layer) and 49 minutes (2 layers), 2.3.
Secondly, notable temperature rises of more than 60°C was observed at multiple depths
during the cooling phase in both sample cases. This is particularly interesting because the
cooling phase is not typically considered in design calculations, highlighting a potential
gap in standard fire design methodologies.
Lastly, the experimental set-up was a simple and well-defined arrangement that yielded
highly consistent results across both sample cases. Standard deviations were extremely
close to the mean values, indicating low variability and high reproducibility. This reliability
makes the Cone Test results particularly suitable for comparison with thermal model
simulations in Abaqus, where similarly controlled conditions are implemented.

3.2.2
Mobile Furnace: Experimental Set-Up
For this experiment, CLT deck elements measuring 100×45×14 cm were used.
Each
element consisted of five lamellas with a thickness distribution of 40-20-20-20-40 mm and
belonged to strength class C24. The same type of gypsum board as in the Cone Test was
utilized - Type F gypsum, with dimensions 90×60×1.25 cm.
Since the goal was to measure temperatures in the middle of the CLT, each element was
cut in half, resulting in two pieces of 50×40×14 cm. On one of these pieces, seven holes
(Ø1.5 mm) were drilled 30 mm into the sample along the 50 cm side. The hole positions
were 5, 15, 30, 50, 70, 100, and 130 mm from the exposed surface, spaced 10 mm apart.
A drill stand was used to ensure that the holes were drilled straight.
To secure the TCs inside the sample, a milling cutter was used to create shallow grooves
connecting the drilled holes to the unexposed surface of the CLT. The TCs were then
inserted into the holes, bent along the milled grooves, and extended out from the unexposed
side.
This design ensured that the TCs did not interfere with the joint between the
two CLT pieces when they were glued back together, forming a new CLT element with
dimensions 90×50×14 cm. The decision to glue the 50 cm sides together was based on two
factors, one ensuring that the sample matched the furnace opening width and two to utilize
the pre-existing straight surface and thereby avoiding cutting cracks or irregularities.

Experimental Discussion
Two experiments were conducted: the Cone Test and the Mobile Furnace Test. The CT
aimed to provide an initial understanding of heat transfer through encapsulated CLT and
to establish simple boundary conditions that could be implemented in a thermal model. In
contrast, the MFT was designed to simulate real heat transfer conditions under a standard
fire. The results from these tests informed a further investigation into the reduced cross-
sections and bending capacities, using different methods presented in both the Old and
New EC5.

3.3.1
Cone Test vs Mobile Furnace
The temperature distributions from both tests exhibited similar trends in temperature rise.
However, the CT resulted in higher peak temperatures within the CLT compared to the
MFT. This difference is reasonable, as the heating exposure in the CT lacked the growth
phase characteristic of the standard fire curve used in the MFT. Despite the differences in
heating conditions, the in-depth temperature profiles from both tests followed the same
patterns, with most maximum temperatures occurring during the cooling phase. This
suggests that, even though the samples were exposed to heat from different directions, the
overall thermal response remained the same.
The in-depth temperature profiles also provided valuable insights into the evaporation of
water at 100°C, as indicated by a prolonged plateau around this isotherm. As moisture
within the CLT evaporates, steam pressure increases, eventually leading to crack openings.
This phenomenon was observed in both tests. In the CT, cracks appeared on the top
surface at the glued interfaces, while in the MFT, large cracks emerged in the top lamellas,
extending through the entire first lamellae layer. Since two different CLT types were used
in the experiments, direct comparisons are diﬀicult. However, the presence of cracks in the
load-bearing lamellas raises concerns about their ability to retain or regain full strength
after exposure.
3.3.2
Reduced Cross-Section vs Advanced Calculations
Even though EC5 introduces two different methods for calculating the reduced cross-
section of an exposed timber element, the choice of method significantly influences the
results. The most commonly used approach is the simplified RCSM, where the effective
charring depth is determined and subtracted from the original cross-section. In the New
EC5, the zero-strength layer has been more than doubled, leading to significantly greater
effective charring depths and, consequently, a more pronounced reduction in cross-section
and bending capacity.
The calculated bending capacities for the sample encapsulated with one protection layer
and exposed for 21 minutes were 27.9 kNm using the Old EC5 and 21.9 kNm using the New
EC5. For the sample encapsulated with two protection layers and exposed for 55 minutes,
the corresponding results were 25.2 kNm and 20.7 kNm, respectively. In comparison, the
bending capacity of the CLT without exposure to heat or any cross-section reduction is
32.9 kNm. These results clearly indicate that the New EC5 leads to a significantly greater
reduction in capacity, raising questions about the validity of the Old EC5 methods and
the underlying data on which they are based. Given that numerous buildings have been
designed according to the Old EC5 requirements, these findings suggest that additional
protection may be necessary for existing designs.

The RCSM is based on a theoretical framework that defines a depth comprising both
the charring depth and the pyrolysis zone (zero-strength layer), which combined form the
total zero-strength depth. The charring depth is assumed to correspond to the depth of
the 300°C isotherm. Using the results from the MFT, it was possible to evaluate whether
the theoretical charring depths align with the actual charring depths observed in the two
sample cases. By adding the zero-strength layer from both the Old and New EC5 to the
measured charring depth, the effective charring depths could be compared. For the sample
protected with one gypsum layer, the theoretical effective charring depths were 7 mm and
16 mm according to the Old and New EC5, respectively, while the actual depths were mea-
sured as 7.25 mm and 16.25 mm. This indicates strong agreement between theoretical and
actual results. For the sample protected with two gypsum layers, the theoretical effective
charring depths were 10.9 mm and 19.9 mm, whereas the actual values were 7 mm and 16
mm. In this case, the theoretical depths exceed the actual measurements, indicating that
the EC5 provides more conservative estimates for samples exposed to prolonged heating
durations.
It is well known that EC5 methods account only for the heating phase in their calcula-
tions. However, as observed in the experiments, temperatures continued to rise during
the cooling phase, raising concerns about whether EC5 calculations adequately capture
this delayed temperature increase. When considering the cooling phase, the actual effec-
tive charring depths increased to 8.5 mm and 17.5 mm for the sample with one gypsum
layer and to 8 mm and 17 mm for the sample with two layers. Comparing these values
to the theoretical depths, the same trend is evident: both the Old and New EC5 fail to
provide conservative estimates for the sample with one gypsum layer when considering
the cooling phase (theoretical values of 7 mm and 16 mm). However, for the sample with
two protection layers, both EC5 versions provide conservative results (10.9 mm and 19.9
mm).
A fundamental limitation of the RCSM in both the Old and New EC5 is that it does not
account for mechanical degradation caused by temperatures below 300°C. EC5 explicitly
states that the compressive strength of wood decreases to 1/4 of its original value at 100°C.
During the tests, a substantial portion of the cross-section reached this temperature, par-
ticularly during the cooling phase. To address this, the actual reduced bending capacities
were calculated based on the reduction factor, considering temperatures at the end of the
heating phase, at an intermediate stage of the cooling phase, and at maximum recorded
temperatures.
The reduced capacities at the end of the heating phases (21 and 55 minutes) were de-
termined to be 23.3 kNm and 18.8 kNm, respectively. When compared to the capacities
obtained from the RCSM — 27.9 kNm (Old EC5) and 21.9 kNm (New EC5) for the sample
with one gypsum layer, and 25.2 kNm (Old EC5) and 20.7 kNm (New EC5) for the sample

with two gypsum layers - it is evident that the New EC5 provides suﬀiciently accurate
predictions for the case with one protection layer. However, in all other cases, the RCSM
is inadequate when accounting for strength reductions due to temperatures below 300°C.
More noticeable results emerge when analyzing reduced capacities during the cooling
phase. For the sample with one protection layer, the reduced capacity was determined 30
minutes into the cooling phase, yielding a value of 14.7 kNm - more than a 50% reduction

a reduction of over 60% of the original capacity. These findings suggest that the RCSM
is maybe too simple, as it fails to fully capture the extent of mechanical degradation oc-

Another notable finding was that the reduced capacities during the cooling phase closely
aligned with those obtained when considering the maximum temperatures recorded through-
out both the heating and cooling phases. When using the maximum temperatures, the
reduced capacities were found to be 13.5 kNm and 11.5 kNm for the two sample cases,
respectively. Since the results based on maximum temperatures represent the worst-case
scenario, it is concerning that they closely resemble those observed during one point in
the cooling phase. Although temperatures eventually stabilize at ambient conditions, the
extent to which the reduced strength is regained remains uncertain. Even if full strength
recovery occurs, there will still be a period during which the strength is reduced by 50%
or more.
This study includes an analysis of only one test with a single gypsum protection layer and
one test with two layers. Even if additional tests were conducted and lower temperatures
were observed in the cooling phase, the disagreement remains significant. The reduced
capacities of 14.7 kNm and 13.0 kNm during cooling are still substantially lower than the
21.9 kNm and 20.7 kNm predicted by the RCSM in the New EC5. While the reduction
factor used in the ACM may be a conservative estimate provided in EC5, it is still assumed
to be based on experimental data that reflect the behavior of timber at elevated temper-
atures. The strength reduction factor in EC5 indicates that compressive strength is the
most affected by high temperatures, whereas tensile strength is less impacted. However,
existing literature suggests that timber behaves in a more brittle manner under tensile
loading, particularly when loaded perpendicular to the grain [6]. This raises important
questions and highlights the need for further investigations into this aspect.

Numerical Part
In addition to an experimental investigation, this project also includes a numerical anal-
ysis. Two thermal heat transfer models were developed with the goal of replicating the
same temperature distributions as observed in the experimental investigation. The results
from the Cone Test were used as a reference, as the controlled conditions in this experi-
ment provided a more predictable and reliable basis for comparison and for determining
appropriate boundary conditions for the models.
For this project, the software Abaqus FEA Learning Edition was used to develop and
analyze the thermal models. While this edition had a few limitations, none of them com-
promised the accuracy of the results.
This chapter reviews the key decisions made in developing the thermal models and outlines
their construction. The results from each model are then presented and analyzed, followed
by a discussion on how these decisions influenced the output. This leads to considerations
for future research and potential subject for further investigation.
4.1
Implementation of Model
The process of implementing a thermal model in this project involved an initial learning
phase where smaller assignments were conducted using Abaqus FEA Learning Edition.
This phase was essential for becoming familiar with the software and understanding its
various features. Additionally, it served as a way to validate the final models and justify
that the decisions made throughout that modeling process was reliable.

Validation Models
The learning phase involved conducting both steady-state (SSA) and transient-state (TSA)

4.1.2
Final Models
The aim of the numerical heat transfer analysis was to develop a model that accurately
replicated the experimental results obtained from the CT. A separate model was created
for each sample case, with model case ABA1 corresponding to CT-1 and model case ABA2
corresponding to CT-2. Due to limitations in Abaqus, it was necessary to create separate
models for the heating and cooling phases, meaning that two models were produced for
each sample case. The primary objective of these models was to determine the temperature
distributions along a centerline through the materials. In principle, this could have been
achieved using a 1D model, but to enhance the visual representation and ensure a more
realistic setup, a 2D model was chosen. The following steps were carried out for both
ABA1 and ABA2 in the heating and cooling models:
Step 1: Parts
First, the parts were defined. Two distinct parts were created - one representing the gyp-
sum and the other representing the CLT. Both were modeled as 2D deformable shell panels.
Since the width of the materials did not influence the results, each part was assigned a
width of 1 cm. The CLT part was modeled with a thickness of 10 cm, corresponding to
the samples used in the Cone Test. The gypsum layer was modeled with a thickness of
12.5 mm in ABA1 and 25 mm in ABA2, matching the respective test configurations.
Each part was assigned specific material properties based on the values provided in EC5 for
gypsum plasterboard and CLT. These properties, also presented in Figure 2.5-Figure 2.7
for CLT and in Table 2.3 for gypsum, were defined as temperature dependent. The density
values at ambient temperature were set to ρCLT = 420 kg/m3 for CLT and ρgyp = 830
kg/m3 for gypsum, matching the materials used in the Cone Test. In the models simu-
lating the cooling phase, the gypsum properties were assigned as constant, corresponding

emissivity of 0.7, also at ambient temperature.
Additionally, a boundary condition was assigned at the interface between the gypsum and
CLT layers to enable heat transfer between the materials. This was implemented using a
node-to-surface tie constraint with a specific distance of 0.6 mm.

Each model, whether for heating or cooling, consisted of two steps: an initial step and a
heating/cooling step.
In the heating models, the initial step included a predefined temperature field where all
surfaces were set to an ambient temperature of 20°C. The heating step introduced a heat
flux load applied uniformly to the top surface of the gypsum. This surface heat flux was
set to 75 kW, corresponding to the conditions used in the Cone Test. The heating duration
was assigned as 15 minutes for ABA1 and 45 minutes for ABA2, matching the exposure
times from the experimental setup.
In the cooling models, the initial step consisted of a predefined temperature field derived
from the final temperature distribution output of the heating model. This was achieved by
importing the last increment of the heating step as an initial condition. The cooling step
was then simulated over a period of 160 minutes for ABA1 and 200 minutes for ABA2,
ensuring that the full duration of the cooling phase was captured.

4.3
Numerical Discussion
By utilizing a simplified experimental setup from the Cone Test, it was possible to develop
a thermal model in Abaqus under similar conditions. This approach allowed for validation
of the finite element modeling against a realistic experiment. Two distinct thermal models
were created, with one model dependent on the results of the other. This methodology
was necessary to accurately represent both the heating and cooling phases, as the thermal
properties differ between these stages. Since Abaqus only permits a single set of material
properties, this sequential modeling approach was used to overcome that limitation.
4.3.1
Boundary Conditions
Even though the boundary conditions implemented in the thermal model appeared simple
and straightforward, careful consideration was required to ensure they were well-founded.

To assess their impact on the results, multiple simulations with varying conditions were
conducted.
As mentioned in 2.1.2, EC5 recommends a convective heat transfer coeﬀicient of 9 W/m2K
to account for the combined effects of convection and radiation.
This coeﬀicient was
therefore applied as a surface film condition on both the top surface of the gypsum and
the bottom surface of the CLT. To verify the suitability of this assumption, a convection
study was performed based on the theories of external free convection flows outlined in
[29], Chapter 9.6. The study indicated that while a convective heat transfer coeﬀicient of 9
W/m2K was indeed appropriate for the top surface, a lower coeﬀicient of 4 W/m2K would
have been more accurate for the bottom surface. However, this study was conducted after
most simulations had already been completed. A single additional simulation was carried
out using the lower convective heat transfer coeﬀicient at the bottom surface, but the
results remained unchanged, confirming that the original assumption was suﬀicient.
Additionally, also noted in
2.1.2, the emissivity of surfaces in fire conditions typically
ranges between 0.7 and 1.0.
In the thermal models, an emissivity of 0.7 was applied
as a surface radiation condition on both the top surface of the gypsum and the bottom
surface of the CLT. A sensitivity analysis was performed by increasing the emissivity to
0.9 at the top surface, which resulted in lower overall temperatures. The best agreement
with experimental results was found when maintaining an emissivity of 0.7, reinforcing its
appropriateness for the model.
4.3.2
Minimum Time Increment
In addition to the boundary conditions another very important step had to be solved, the
time increment. Since the thermal models incorporate two different materials with different
thermal properties that vary in response to heat exposure, determining the criterion for the
minimum time increment was challenging. To address this complexity, a simplification was
applied in this project. Firstly, the mesh configuration for each material was predetermined
due to the Abaqus Learning Edition’s restriction of a maximum of 1000 elements. Secondly,
the minimum time increment was initially determined based on the thermal properties of
CLT at ambient temperature, as this provided the most conservative estimate of 1.4 s.
However, after encountering the error message ”time increment required is less than the
minimum specified,” adjustments had to be made to allow the model to complete the
simulation.
Reducing the minimum time increment would have required a finer mesh, which was not
feasible due to the element limit.
Consequently, the decision was made to bypass the
minimum requirement and instead conduct an oscillation study to validate the results of
the model. The oscillation study ultimately confirmed that exceeding the requirement did
not introduce any concerning oscillations when analyzing temperature variations at every

4.3.3
ABA1 vs. ABA2
The two thermal model simulations for the heating and cooling phases were developed to
replicate the conditions of the Cone Test samples. ABA1 represented a sample with a 12.5
mm gypsum layer and a 100 mm CLT layer, while ABA2 consisted of a 25 mm gypsum
layer and a 100 mm CLT layer.
All material properties were initially based on those
provided in EC5, yielding results that closely matched the experimental data, particularly
in the early stages of the heating phase. However, during the cooling phase, modifications
to the thermal properties were necessary to better align the model with experimental
observations.
The adjustments involved changing the gypsum properties from temperature dependent
to constant values. In both cases, it was found that the temperature in the middle of the
gypsum layer reached 600°C or higher. Based on this observation, the constant thermal
properties were set at that temperature, assuming that all water had evaporated and that
the density would not recover. For the CLT, the thermal conductivity and specific heat
capacity remained the same as in the heating model, but the peak values in specific heat
around 100°C were removed. This change had a significant impact on the results, as the
peaks in specific heat are primarily caused by moisture evaporation, which was assumed
to have already occurred in most of the CLT during heating. This also supported the
decision to keep the density constant for CLT in the cooling phase, with a value set at
95% of its original density.
Overall, ABA1 tended to overestimate the experimental results, while ABA2 tended to
underestimate them.
This highlights the significant influence of the gypsum layer on
the output of the models and emphasizes the complexity of accurately representing this
material in thermal simulations. Various studies have investigated the behavior of gypsum
in greater detail, exploring how to best replicate its thermal response by refining material
properties and boundary conditions. However, as this project is primarily focused on the
behavior of CLT, an analysis of gypsum was beyond the scope of this thesis and was not
investigated further.
Aside from the overall overestimation in ABA1 and underestimation in ABA2, both mod-
els faced challenges in accurately capturing the transition between materials. In ABA1,
heat transfer appeared to occur too rapidly, while in ABA2, it was too slow. The most sig-
nificant deviations from the experimental results were observed at the end of the heating

4.3.4
Sensitivity Study
A sensitivity study was conducted to better understand how modifications in thermal
properties would affect diffusivity and improve the alignment of the thermal model with
experimental results at the end of heating and the beginning of cooling for ABA2. Four
different studies were carried out, adjusting the diffusivity to both higher and lower val-
ues. Given the significant influence of specific heat capacity on the results, a decision
was made to alter only this property by ±10% in one set of studies. In the other two
studies, thermal conductivity and density were modified to achieve the most substantial
changes in diffusivity: in one case, conductivity was increased by 10% while density was
decreased by 10%, and in the other case, the opposite changes were applied. Recognizing
the considerable impact of the gypsum layer thickness, these four variations were applied
separately to the gypsum properties (Case 1), the CLT properties (Case 2), and both
materials simultaneously (Case 3).
In Case 1, decreasing the specific heat capacity by 10% resulted in a temperature gra-
dient that closely matched the experimental gradient in the CLT at the end of heating.
However, in the early cooling phase, none of the modifications led to a match with the
experimental results. The most pronounced effect was observed when increasing density
while decreasing conductivity, which reduced diffusivity. Overall, conductivity and den-
sity changes had the most significant impact on the final results in Case 1.
Case 2 showed no substantial changes when modifying the CLT properties. All variations
remained closely aligned with the original ABA2 results, reinforcing that the gypsum layer
had the most significant influence on the temperature profiles.
In Case 3, where both gypsum and CLT properties were adjusted simultaneously, the
results predictably clustered around those of ABA2, as both materials were modified in
the same manner. Again, no study successfully replicated the experimental gradient in
the early cooling phase, but at the end of heating, the model where specific heat was
reduced by 10% provided the closest match to the experimental results, consistent with
the findings from Case 1.

CHAPTER 5
Final Conclusion
This thesis has presented both an experimental and numerical investigation into the ther-
mal and mechanical performance of encapsulated cross-laminated timber (CLT) elements
exposed to a fully developed fire from above.
The experimental investigation included two main studies: the Cone Test, which provided
controlled thermal data for validating numerical heat transfer models, and the Mobile
Furnace Test, which assessed the strength reduction in exposed CLT elements. In both
experiments, two sample cases were examined - CLT encapsulated with either one or two
layers of type F gypsum boards - each subjected to both a heating and cooling phase.
The Cone Test included four successful trials for each sample case, with exposure to a
constant heat flux of 75 kW. The top surface of the CLT reached 300°C after 15 minutes
for the single-layer case and 45 minutes for the double-layer case, showing deviations
from the analytically predicted penetration times of 21 and 49 minutes, respectively. The
tests yielded highly consistent results across both sample cases, with standard deviations
extremely close to the mean values, making them particularly suitable for comparison with
numerical thermal models in Abaqus.
The Mobile Furnace Test proved to be a more complex experimental setup, resulting
in only one predominant successful test per sample case.
The samples were exposed
to the ISO 834 fire curve, reaching 300°C on the top surface of the CLT after 21 and 55
minutes, respectively. Similar temperature trends to those in the Cone Test were observed,
showing a rise in temperatures during the cooling phase. The reduced cross-sections and
bending capacities were determined using both the Reduced Cross-Section Method and the
Advanced Calculation Method provided in the current and revised versions of Eurocode
5, EN1995-1-2. The results showed significant differences between these methods, with
the Advanced Calculation Method predicting the most significant reductions in capacity,
especially during the cooling phase, where bending capacities dropped to less than 45%
Performance of Encapsulated Timber Elements in Compression Exposed to a Fully Developed Fire
==PAGE:116==
96
of the original capacity in both sample cases. In a comparison between the current and
revised Eurocode 5, EN1995-1-2, calculation methods also revealed notable differences,
with the current method predicting capacity reductions of no more than 84% of the original
capacity, whereas the revised method indicated reductions of up to 67% for both sample
cases. However, neither of these methods captured the severe reductions observed in the
Advanced Calculation Method.
The numerical investigation involved the development of thermal heat transfer models
in Abaqus FEA Learning Edition, aimed at replicating the thermal behavior observed
in the Cone Test. Separate models were created for the heating and cooling phases, as
modifications to the temperature dependent thermal properties were required for the cool-
ing phase. The models successfully captured the overall temperature distribution trends,
particularly in the initial heating phase. However, deviations were observed, primarily
at the interface between the gypsum and CLT layers. The model including one layer of
gypsum tended to overestimate temperatures, whereas the model with two layers of gyp-
sum underestimated them. A comparison of temperature profiles at the end of heating
and the beginning of cooling showed that neither model fully replicated the experimental
temperature gradients. A sensitivity study revealed that the thermal properties of gypsum
had a significant influence on the heat transfer, while variations in CLT properties had
minimal impact. Adjustments were made to the specific heat capacity, the density, and
the conductivity, and while some modifications improved accuracy in the heating phase,
none fully replicated the cooling phase behavior.
Overall, this research provided valuable insights into the thermal and mechanical response
of CLT in fully developed fire conditions when considering the methods provided in the
European Standards, Eurocode 5, EN1995-1-2. By integrating experimental findings with
numerical modeling, this study contributes to the ongoing development of more accurate
fire design methods for CLT structures. A more precise prediction of temperature distri-
butions will allow engineers to better assess strength reduction, leading to safer and more
reliable designs for CLT structures.

Numerical Modeling
In Abaqus a steady state analysis is performed to see if the same results are obtained. The
steel beam is modeled in 2D with the left and right ends of the beam assigned with an
adiabatic boundary condition. Firstly, a model with the respective length and height of
the beam is created and the thermal properties of the material are added to the beam. As
this is a heat transfer analysis, the absolute zero temperature and the Stefan-Boltzmann
constant also need to be added to the model. This is done in the model attributes. Then,
the steps of the procedure are created. The initial step includes the predefined field of
the ambient temperature before heating; 20°C. The second step is the heat transfer step,
which includes surface interactions, boundary conditions, or loads cases depending on how
the heat is applied to the beam. In Abaqus, the convection and radiation are modeled
as a surface interaction. The convection is applied to the model using a ”Surface Film
Condition”, and the radiation is applied to the model as a ”Surface Radiation”. The mesh
is then added to the model, dividing the model into smaller elements. This model was
divided into elements with nodes located every 10 cm along the height and length. Finally,
a job can be made with the requested field and history output. In this case, the nodal
temperature and the heat flux vector were requested. When everything is set, the job can
be submitted and the results obtained.
