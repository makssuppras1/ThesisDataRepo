Preface
This master thesis, Dynamic strain estimation by digital twins, marks the end of the
Master of Science (MSc) in Civil Engineering program at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU).
The project is credited 35 ECTS points for both students. It revolves around a continuous
feed of data used to update a Finite Element (FE) model and how it is used to estimate
dynamical strains using modal expansion. The project works with both simulated and
experimental data generated from a 5-story shear frame mounted on a shaking table that
makes the structure vibrate. A sensor registers the displacements at each level, and the
data is analyzed with different Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) methods. From a
preliminary analysis, one OMA is selected and used throughout the project to update the
model and ultimately predict the displacements on the first floor of the shear frame.
The appendix mainly includes plots, tables, and figures for the data set with high damping,
whereas the results presented in the main part of the project originate from the data set
with no damping. The appendix also contains handwritten derivations and pseudo-code,
and all the code used for calculations in this thesis is available at Github.
This thesis is written by Adam Alexander Hempler-Christiansen and Frederik Emil Ser-
ritzlew, who are both students at DTU Construct, and with Jan Becker Høgsberg from
the Department of Civil and Mechanical Engineering as advisor.
We would like to thank Jan Becker Høgsberg for his amazing guidance and for always
being able to allocate time for discussions and writing helpful notes along the way. Jan
has an incredibly engaging and simple way of explaining things, along with a great portion
of patience, making the process profoundly pleasant and meaningful.
A special thanks also go out to Mads Greve Pedersen from the Department of Civil and

Resumé
Konstruktioners tilstand overvåges ofte ved at indsamle realtid-operationel data fra tilgæn-
gelige steder på konstruktionen ved hjælp af standard sensorer som lasere, accelerometre
og strain gauges. Dataene bruges derefter til at opdatere en Finite Element (FE) model,
og baseret på et begrænset sæt af sensorer kan teknikker til respons-estimering anvendes
til at beregne responset på steder uden sensorer.
Denne afhandlings formål er at bruge sensitivity metoden til at opdatere en FE model og
udføre virtual sensing ved brug af Modal Decomposition and Expansion (MDE). Herefter
laves en udmattelsebedømmelses af resultaterne fra virtuel sensing.
To forskellige tilgange til modelopdatering af en FE model er undersøgt kombineret med
to forskellige eksperimentelle datasæt, et uden dæmpning og et med høj ikke-proportional
dæmpning. Desuden bliver MDE teknikken valideret og derefter brugt til at forudsige fly-
tningerne på første sal af en femetagers forskydningsramme. Kvaliteten af resultaterne
diskuteres i sammenhæng med udmattelse, hvor der bliver anvendt henholdsvis tre og fem
sensorer til Operationel Modalanalyse (OMA), modelopdatering og MDE.
I den første del af denne afhandling diskuteres forskellige teknikker inden for OMA. Det
drejer sig om Enhanced Frequency Domain Decomposition (EFDD) i frekvensdomænet,
Stochastic Subspace Identification - Covariance Driven (SSI-COV) og Stochastic Subspace
Identification - Data Driven (SSI-DAT) i tidsdomænet. Disse teknikker anvendes til at
analysere simulerede data genereret med Newmark-β integrationsmetoden og det vises at
alle tre metoder giver nøjagtige resultater. Da MDE er teknikken, der bruges til at udføre
virtuel sensing, og SSI-COV opnåede højere nøjagtighed for svingningsformerne, er denne
valgt som metoden at fortsætte med.
I anden del af denne afhandling præsenteres to forskellige FE modelopdateringsmetoder.
Forskellige målfunktioner bliver opstillet, og ved hjælp af Matlab’s fminsearch-algoritme
bliver least-square optimeringsproblemet løst. Herefter implementeres sensitivity meto-
den, hvor tre forskellige residualer bliver brugt til at definere afvigelsen i egenværdier
og svingningsformer fra FE modellen og fra OMA. Ved at kombinere nøjagtigheden af
egenværdier og svingningsformer bliver de bedste resultater for både målfunktioner og
sensitivity metoden opnået. Generelt opnås en lidt højere nøjagtighed med sensitivity
metoden for egenfrekvens, og for svingningsformerne giver målfunktionerne en lidt højere
nøjagtighed. Yderligere har, sensitivity metoden længere beregningstid og er mere kom-
pleks at implementere.
I den sidste del af denne afhandling bliver virtuel sensing udført ved brug af MDE-
metoden. Metoden bliver først verificeret ved brug af simuleret data. Herefter anven-
des metoden på det eksperimentelle data, hvor målinger fra henholdsvis fem og tre sen-
sorer bruges til OMA og modelopdatering, og de resulterende modale parametre anvendes
senere. Det viser sig at være nødvendigt at opdele signalet i fem frekvensbånd, for at opnå
de mest nøjagtige prædikterede flytninger. For fem sensorer konkluderes det at målfunk-
tionen med egenværdier og svingningsformer er den bedste, da denne giver den højeste

nøjagtighed i svingningsformerne. Det konkluderes yderligere, at ved brug af tre sensorer
opnås en lidt ringere fase-nøjagtighed og en lidt større amplitudefejl.

sensorer forværres resultaterne betydeligt ved at indføre ikke-proportional dæmpning i

De prædikterede flytninger bliver derefter oversat til værdier, der bruges til udmattelses-

Abstract
Structures’ structural health is often monitored by collecting real-time operational data
from accessible locations using standard sensors such as lasers, accelerometers, and strain
gauges. The data is then used to calibrate a Finite Element (FE) model, and using a
limited set of sensors, response estimation techniques can be utilized to calculate the re-
sponses at unmeasured locations.
This thesis aims to update an FE model using the sensitivity method and perform virtual
sensing based on the Modal Decomposition and Expansion (MDE) technique. Hereafter
a fatigue evaluation of the results from virtual sensing can be performed.
A data set with no damping and one with high non-proportional damping, combined with
two different approaches for updating an FE model, is investigated. Moreover, the MDE
technique is validated and used to predict the displacements on the first floor of a five-
story shear frame. The quality of the results is then discussed in the context of fatigue,
where three and five sensors are used for the Operational Modal Analysis (OMA), model
updating, and MDE.
In the first part of this thesis, different OMA techniques are discussed. The Enhanced
Frequency Domain Decomposition (EFDD) in the frequency domain, and the Stochastic
Subspace Identification - Covariance Driven (SSI-COV) and Stochastic Subspace Identifi-
cation - Data Driven (SSI-DAT) in the time domain. These techniques are used to analyze
simulated data generated with the Newmark-β integration scheme and prove that all three
methods provide accurate results. Since MDE is the technique of choice for performing
virtual sensing, and SSI-COV obtained a slightly higher mode shape accuracy, this is
chosen as the method to continue with.
In the second part, two different FE model updating schemes are presented in the second
part of this thesis. Different cost functions are created, and using Matlab’s fminsearch-
algorithm, the least-squares optimization problem is solved. Afterward, the sensitivity
method is implemented, where three different residual types are used to define the devia-
tion in the eigenvalue and mode shapes from the FE model and the eigenvalue and mode
shapes obtained from the OMA. Combining the accuracy of the eigenvalues and mode
shapes yields the best results for both the cost functions and the sensitivity method. The
sensitivity method generally obtains slightly higher accuracies for the natural frequencies,
while the cost functions obtain higher mode shape accuracies. It is also worth noting that
the sensitivity method has longer computation times and more complex implementation.
In the last part of this thesis, virtual sensing is performed with MDE. To verify the
method, it is first performed on simulated data. Hereafter the method is performed on
experimental data, where the measurements from five and three sensors are used to do
OMA and model updating, where the resulting modal parameters are applied. Conse-
quently, dividing the signal into five frequency bands is necessary to obtain the best results
for the predicted displacements. For the five sensors, it is proven that the best method for
the cost function is the combined eigenvalue and mode shape since this has the highest
V
==PAGE:10==
mode shape accuracy. Only using three sensors has a small impact on the final phase
accuracy and gives a slightly higher amplitude error. For both schemes, introducing non-
proportional damping to the setup worsens the results considerably.
The predicted displacements are then translated into values used for fatigue evaluation,
and it is be concluded that the quality of the fatigue estimations is significantly worsened
by a relatively high amplitude error from the virtual sensing. Therefore, much empha-
sis should be put on reducing the amplitude error before the fatigue calculations are
performed.

Introduction
Modern structures are becoming increasingly slender and flexible to save material because
they are considered more visually appealing. Consequently, dynamic loads are also be-
coming more relevant as these can make slender and flexible structures oscillate, which
in the long run will accumulate as damage in the structure, ultimately making it yield.
Therefore, monitoring these structures, which are prone to swaying and exposed to cyclic
loads, is critical. This is under the umbrella term Structural Health Monitoring (SHM)
and is typically done by mounting sensors on the structure to measure displacements,
accelerations, or strains. From these measurements, analytical tools can be applied to get
valuable insight into the health of the structure, along with the ability to predict future
results in different conditions. Typically structures will be most vulnerable to failure at
certain critical points. These points might be located in hard-to-reach places, such as
around the mudline on Offshore Wind Turbines (OWTs). Predictions about the state of
the structure at those critical points can still be made using virtual sensing in combination
with a Finite Element (FE) model. The FE model has to be calibrated sufficiently well
for the method to produce satisfactory results, which is ensured by updating the model
based on data from the measured locations. In this test setup, the predicted signal will
be compared to a measured signal, and the deviations will be discussed.
This thesis will deal with these aspects, as it aims to update an FE model using the
sensitivity method and perform virtual sensing based on the Modal Decomposition and
Expansion (MDE) technique. Hereafter perform a fatigue evaluation of the results from
virtual sensing. This is done for a simulated data set and two experimental data sets, one
with no damping and one with non-proportional high damping. The analyses are made
for both data sets, but the results in the text are only presented for the data set with
no damping unless stated otherwise. The results for the data set with high damping are
included in the appendix. For the sake of continuity, an overview of the progression of
this thesis will now be presented:
• Chapter 2 to 5 will introduce and highlight background knowledge, along with any

Signal processing
Signal processing is an essential step in OMA, as the information of interest has to be
isolated and extracted from the noise and distortion in the measured signal. This infor-
mation is referred to as modal information, and the part of the signal, which carries this
important information, can be amplified by performing signal processing. The noise can
be reduced, making the analysis easier and the results clearer.
Noise and distortion are inevitable when conducting measurements in the real world, as
several different factors can affect the measured signal. The effect of some of these sources
can be reduced or even entirely mitigated by taking certain precautions when setting up
the equipment or when performing the analysis. Others are so inherently rooted in some
part of the process that they will always be present to some extent. Some sources of noise
and error include:
• Environmental noise in the form of electromagnetic interference (EMI) and am-
bient temperature fluctuations, referred to as thermal noise.
• Equipment noise, for example, voltage and current noise in the instruments, can
also be noise from other parts of the acquisition process.
• Human errors can be misreadings of the instruments, errors in setup or calibration,
or errors in data entry or analysis.
3.1
Detrending
Detrending measurements aim to remove trends or systematic variations from a signal.
Trends refer to any consistent, long-term changes in the measured signal, such as gradual
increases or decreases over time, and this is important to consider as sensor noise can be
rather prominent in low-frequency regions. One of the most common causes for this is
direct-current (DC) offsets in signals due to temperature effects on measurement equip-
ment [6]. This may have a large effect on real outdoor structures. However, the effect is
expected to be negligible for the indoor test setup considered in this thesis.
A few different methods exist to perform the detrending of time series, including a linear
detrend and a constant detrend. The linear detrend involves fitting a linear least-squares
line to the data and subtracting it from the data. The constant detrend is performed by
subtracting the mean of the data from all data points. This thesis uses linear detrending
since it may be the superior of the two, as suggested in [7].
Another option would be to first segment the data, i.e., by a Hanning window with 50%
overlap, and then perform constant detrending on the segments, as detrending in small
sets would be more appropriate to correct minor DC errors. All the filters could also be
applied for the segmented data instead. Still, it was discovered through tests that this
was not necessary in the case of this thesis, as the processed signal was of sufficiently
good quality. This can also be seen in figure 3.1, which shows the impact of detrending
the signal.

3.2
Filtering & down-sampling
Filtering and down-sampling are often used in relation to OMA, as the measured signal is
often sampled with a higher frequency than what is needed for the analysis, and it is not
the entire frequency range that is of interest. The Nyquist-Shannon theorem can be used,
which states that the sampling frequency has to be at least twice the highest expected
value of maximum frequency [8]. This ensures that the upper bound of the observable
frequency range, the Nyquist frequency, is equal to the maximum frequency value. This
is important to consider, as an inadequate sampling frequency can result in aliasing and
unreliable measurements.
To avoid signal acquisition difficulties, long computation times, and other problems, as
discussed earlier in section 3.1, different filters can be applied to limit the effects of these
problems on the signal. The filters are divided into analog and digital filters, where the
only analog filters used in OMA are the anti-aliasing filters, which must be applied before
the Analog-to-Digital converter (ADC) [6]. After this, one of two main types of digital fil-
ters can be applied to filter and down-sample the data. The finite impulse response (FIR)
and the infinite impulse response (IIR) filters are alternatively referred to as Moving Aver-
age and Auto-Regressive filters, respectively. A more detailed description of the two types
of digital filters can be found in [9], but to sum up the main differences, it comes down to
the length of the impulse response, as their names suggest. This results in IIR having a
higher efficiency than FIR filters as it needs fewer coefficients to perform the same filtering
tasks, but also a tendency to be more unstable as ripples may occur. As this thesis aims
to implement an automatic model updating algorithm, an IIR filter will be used due to
the sheer amount of data, it should be able to handle, and a down-sampling factor of 2 is
also applied to reduce the computation time even further. In the case of the experimental
data analyzed in this thesis, which is sampled at 100 Hz, a down-sampling factor of 2
ensures that the Nyquist frequency fN = fs
2 is higher than the maximum frequency of
interest. This is also the case for the simulated data, as the frequency rate here is 1000 Hz.
After selecting the filter type, it should be decided what frequencies should be excluded
from the signal. A low-pass filter excludes all frequencies above a user-specified cut-off
frequency, a high-pass filter excludes the frequencies below a user-specified cut-off fre-
quency, and a band-pass filter excludes all frequencies outside a user-specified frequency
band. The high-pass filters are generally used to remove DC offsets, whereas low-pass fil-
ters are used to exclude high-frequency components in the signal. This is done by setting
the filter coefficients to attenuate the unwanted bits and pass through the remaining part
of the signal. However, the filter’s effect also depends on the order of the filter, as a filter
with a higher order will have a steeper cut-off. The order must be chosen as a balance
between computation time and precision.

Operational modal analysis
The structural response of a vibrating structure can be analyzed in different ways. When
estimating the modal properties of a structural system, the two main categories considered
are Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) and OMA, sometimes referred to as output-only
modal analysis. The difference between the two categories lies in the knowledge about
the excitation force, which is unknown in OMA and is known in EMA. Because of this,
OMA is often used when the structure is subjected to ambient or operational excitation,
as these can not be measured. The load is assumed to have white noise characteristics in
an OMA setup. This is a key assumption for OMA, as the input load is assumed to affect
the system equally within a frequency range, both in space and time, not carrying any
information of importance. A strong structural response can be interpreted as a structural
modal response [6]. This makes OMA well-suited for in-situ testing as the tests are rather
cheap, fast to conduct, and do not interfere with the normal use of the structure. For the
rest of this thesis, only OMA methods will be discussed and considered, as this is what is
used for SHM of structures and, in general, sees the most use when analyzing vibration
data from structural systems.
Many different OMA algorithms exist, but they all operate on the same principles. Based
on the structural response, they estimate the Correlation Functions (CF) or Spectral
Density (SD) functions. As a result of the forcing function being unknown, Frequency
Response Functions (FRF) can not be calculated since they describe the connection be-
tween the force and the response. Therefore, based on some assumptions described in the
section 4.2, it can be shown that all important information about the structure can be
described completely by the second-order statistical properties, such as variance, covari-
ance, and correlation values.
The methods are generally developed to perform the identification within one of two
domains, the frequency domain or the time domain. However, it should be noted that
methods for modal identification have also been developed in other domains, such as the
continuous wavelet transformation method in the joint time-frequency domain. However,
this will not be discussed any further in this thesis. For more information, the reader is
advised to look into the following article [12].
Within the time domain, popular OMA techniques include Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD),
Eigensystem Realization Algorithm (ERA), and Stochastic Subspace Identification (SSI).
An advantage of identifying in the time domain includes a minimal degree of bias in the
data and, therefore, might be more accurate in certain situations [13].
In the frequency domain, the most used OMA techniques are the Enhanced Frequency
Domain Decomposition (EFDD), the Least Squares Complex Frequency (LSCF) method
and the Complex Mode Indicator Function [7]. The main advantage of analyzing in the
frequency domain is that each mode is dominant within a small frequency band, known
as natural modal decomposition. It makes the identification of the modes easier. A draw-
back of working in the frequency domain is that the Spectral Density (SD) estimations
introduce a bias [6]. However, it is considered easier to distinguish between closely spaced
11

modes in the frequency domain, which might be worth considering. This advantage is
obvious when considering the stabilization diagram in figure 4.2 and the FDD plot in
figure 4.1.
Another distinction that can be made between the different methods is whether it is
a parametric or non-parametric technique. Parametric identification techniques include
fitting a parametric model to the vibration data, where some regression is applied. These
procedures typically perform better but are considered more complex and computation-
ally demanding [7]. The opposite of this is the non-parametric techniques, which rely
on applying numerical tools to the vibration data, such as Singular Value Decomposition
(SVD). These are particularly useful to get a quick insight into the dynamic properties of
a system [7].

4.2
Assumptions in OMA
When performing OMA, several assumptions are necessary to consider the uncertainty
of the nature of the excitation force. The structural response is the only information
that can be used for system identification. Therefore, it is necessary to have some general
assumptions of the excitation force and the structure to have some foundation from which
the theory behind the techniques can be developed.
4.2.1
Assumptions for the system
Several assumptions are made about the system in relation to OMA. Specifically, the
system is assumed to be linear and time-invariant.
The first assumption about linearity means that the system satisfies the superposition
principle. Explicitly, the output caused by multiple inputs would be identical to the sum
of the responses caused by the individual inputs.
Time invariance is also essential, as the system parameters do not change during the
measurement time.
A change in the structural properties of the system would result
in changes in the response, which would be difficult to distinguish from real structural
responses. Therefore, it is also often assumed that the response is stationary, but this
might be violated due to changes in the loading conditions. Most systems are close to
being time-invariant, which is usually assured by considering the appropriate lengths of
data sets. A response is categorized as stationary when the mean and variance do not
change over time. In fact, only wide-sense stationarity is necessary, and it has been proven
that most of the theory still holds for weakly non-stationary responses [6]. This means it is
possible to have a time-invariant system with a non-stationary response, but a stationary
response will always demand a time-invariant system. Stationarity goes hand in hand
with in-variance since a time-invariant system might have a stationary response. Still, a
time-varying system will result in a non-stationary response as the physical parameters of
the system change. Too long measurement periods can therefore violate the assumption
of time-invariance, while too short measurement periods can violate the assumption of
stationarity.
4.2.2
Assumptions for the surroundings
The assumptions of the system’s surroundings are almost exclusively related to the excita-
tion force, and the most important assumption is that the input to the system is assumed
to have Gaussian White Noise (GWN) characteristics with zero mean in the frequency
range of interest. This implies that all modes are equally excited, and the output spec-
trum, the signal intensity as a function of frequency, contains all the information about
the structure [7]. The input loads are not driving the system at certain specific frequen-
cies. Therefore, any strong structural response can be interpreted as a structural modal
response. This is often true, but noise, harmonic loads, and disturbances such as heavy
machinery, rotation of equipment, or pedestrians, might drive the structure at a specific
frequency. The corresponding deflection shapes are often denoted Operational Deflection
Shapes (ODSs). A mode shape is also a deflection shape, but it consists solely of resonant
vibrations, whereas an ODS contains both forced and resonant vibration components.

4.3
Correlation functions & spectral density functions
Since the excitation force is unknown, it is impossible to calculate the FRF. Furthermore,
first-order statistics are unimportant since the excitation force is assumed to have a zero
mean. The validity of this assumption is given by the central limit theorem and is more
thoroughly explained in [6]. As a result, second-order statistics will be used, from which
the CFs and SD functions are calculated and used in the time domain and frequency
domain, respectively.
The CFs can be interpreted as a similarity measure for two signals and are formulated as
follows
Rxx(τ) = E[xs(t)xs(t + τ)]
Ryy(τ) = E[ys(t)ys(t + τ)]
Rxy(τ) = E[xs(t)ys(t + τ)]
(4.3)
here xs(t) and ys(t) are sample functions of two stationary random processes, which

as auto-correlation functions of the sample functions, while Rxy is the cross-correlation
function of the sample functions. In the case of this thesis, the sample functions will
contain the time histories for each sensor, and since GWN is assumed, the CF will be
identical to the covariance functions. Hence the name of the OMA method in section 4.5.2

The first step in EFDD is to perform the normal peak-picking FDD. Hereafter, the nearest
singular vectors are compared to the singular vector selected from the peak-picking by
the use of the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC). As long as the MAC value is higher
than a user-specified threshold, called MAC Rejection level, the singular vector and the
corresponding singular value are included in a so-called Single-Degree-of-Freedom (SDOF)
Spectral Bell function. The singular vectors within the MAC rejection level will be av-
eraged to estimate the mode shape, where the closest singular vectors will have a larger
weight. Hence the name Bell Function, as the contribution from the singular vectors will
taper off and be zero for everything outside the MAC rejection level. In section 4.6, the
MAC rejection level is set to 0.85, which will be used for the simulated data.
The mode’s natural frequency and damping ratio can be determined by translating the
SDOF Spectral Bell function to the time domain. The result is an SDOF CF, and by
using regression analysis, the natural frequency and the damping ratio can be estimated
[19].

4.5.1
Stochastic state-space formulation
The dynamic behavior of a system is described by the EOM given in equation (2.7). How-
ever, this formulation has certain limitations regarding experimental modeling, including
being given in continuous time. In contrast, the data is available at discrete time steps
and the implementation of noise to the system. Both of these drawbacks can be solved
by converting the EOM to the discrete-time stochastic state-space model as
xk+1 = Axk + wk
yk = Dxk + vk
(4.11)
where a more detailed derivation can be found in the following articles [20, 21]. Here
xk is the discrete-time state vector, yk is the sampled output vector, A is the discrete
state matrix, D is the discrete output matrix, wk is the process noise and vk is the
measurement noise. Disturbances and model inaccuracies are given in process noise, and
sensor inaccuracies apply in measurement noise. Furthermore, the equation containing
the discrete state matrix is generally referred to as the state equation, and the second
equation is called the observation equation. The state equation describes the system’s
dynamic response. The observation equation defines the portion of the system’s dynamic
response that can be observed in the model’s output [7].
An OMA technique that identifies the state-space model is the SSI method, which is
formulated in an SSI-COV and a Stochastic Subspace Identification - Data Driven (SSI-
DAT). The state-space model is said to be realized, which is analogous to solving the
EOM. This thesis will not use SSI-DAT because of the long computation time shown in
table 6.3. Therefore, the theoretical background for the SSI-DAT will not be covered.
However, for more details on the method, the reader is referred to [6, 7].

4.5.3
Poles & stability
It is important to have a sufficiently high model order to establish the stability of poles
and identify the natural frequencies. The model order can be calculated theoretically, but
the gap between the sequence of the singular values in the matrices is not clear enough
because of noise and modeling inaccuracies. Therefore, an overfit of the model order is
needed to ensure all the physical modes are included. However, too much overfitting can
result in spurious poles, which must be separated from the physical ones. The spurious
modes are categorized as noise modes or mathematical modes. Noise modes are poles
from the physical excitation of the system, while mathematical modes are theoretical in
nature, affected by computational noise and modeling inaccuracies. The spurious modes
are the reason for the overestimation of the model order. To better estimate whether a
pole is physical or spurious, a stabilization diagram can be created where the poles are
plotted for different model orders as a function of frequency. Here the physical poles will
be categorized as stable, which means they will be aligned throughout the model order.
The spurious poles will be more scattered. The physical poles can start at low and high
model orders, depending on the modes’ excitation level. To determine the stability of a

Data simulation & modal precision
Data simulation is of great importance, as this enables the evaluation of the accuracy of
the analytical tools and compares them directly since the end result is known. Therefore,
before the measured data is analyzed, some test data is simulated and analyzed in order
to get an idea of how the different OMA methods compare.
There are different ways to simulate data, and the EOM seen in equation (2.7) is mathe-
matically an Initial Value Problem (IVP), where the structure is assumed to be at rest at
t = 0. This equation can be solved analytically and numerically. Still, numerical solvers
are often preferred as a closed-form solution may be very complicated, and numerical
solutions can offer much more efficiency [26]. The common factor for all solution methods
is that the system matrices are assumed to be exactly known, which is obviously not true
in real life. As the system matrices are assumed to be known, the structural response can
then be simulated when an external load is applied. However, before the data simulation
is discussed, a short description of analytical and numerical techniques is presented below.
Analytical approaches have the common factor of starting off with some version of the
EOM, which could be damped or undamped and free vibrations or loaded, and then as-
suming a solution on a specific form, could be on exponential or harmonic form [27]. In
most cases, structures are damped in some way or another, and the load is seldom pure
harmonic, which makes the free vibration solution combined with the impulse response
function a decent choice for an analytical solution technique. As mentioned earlier, ana-
lytical solutions can often become rather complex and difficult to evaluate, but also rigid
or sensitive, as a small change in the initial conditions will change the entire solution.
However, the analytical solutions are closed-form solutions, so there will be no round-off
errors, and the results can be considered exact.
The numerical approaches are all numerical integration techniques, as numerical integra-
tion is closely related to solving a differential equation as described in [26]. The solution
techniques can be divided into explicit schemes and implicit schemes.
Explicit techniques revolve around determining a solution at a given time step, computed
directly from the values of the solution at previous time steps, without requiring a solution
of additional equations or by iterative techniques. Examples of this are the Explicit Cen-
tral Difference Method, also called the leapfrog algorithm, and the Runge-Kutta method.
On the other hand, implicit techniques implement values from the previous time step
along with values from the current time step into equations and then try to solve those
equations. This means that implicit methods are typically more computationally expen-
sive than explicit methods. However, they also tend to be more stable and accurate [28].
Examples of implicit techniques are the Linear Acceleration method and the Newmark-β
method.

5.2.1
Modal assurance criterion
The MAC was introduced in 1982 by Randy J. Allemang and Donald L. Brown and is
a statistical measurement of the correlation between two sets of mode shapes [31]. This
has later become the industry standard for comparing modal vectors due to its simple
implementation, being easy to interpret, and the fact that it is more sensitive to large
differences and rather insensitive to small differences [32].
There exist a few different types of MAC indicators. Still, the traditional MAC is cal-
culated as the normalized scalar product of the two sets of vectors φA and φB, which
results in the MAC matrix

Model updating & digital twins
With the introduction of the FE-model and its modal parameters in chapter 2, along with
the experimental results from the OMA analysis in the previous chapter 6, a deviation in
the estimation of the modal parameters is noticed. The modal parameters from the OMA
are considered true values. This chapter briefly describes how a mathematical model can
be calibrated to decrease this deviation in the results. This is an important step, as the
virtual sensing performed in chapter 10 depends on the precision of the FE model.
7.1
A digital twin in short
When designing a physical system, the initial stage usually involves conceptual ideas that
are transformed into a digital model. These models are mathematical representations of
the physical system and rely on assumptions and simplifications. These, amongst other
modeling errors, are highlighted and presented below, and for a more extensive list, the
reader is advised to the following article [34].
• Idealization errors
– Simplifications which could involve the frequency range of interest
– Erroneous modeling of joints and boundary conditions, which are assumed to
be rigid
• Discretisation errors
– Too coarse FE mesh, resulting in modal data that has not fully converged yet
– Truncation errors
• Assumption errors
– Precision of measurements of geometrical and material properties
– Non-structural mass
Typically the assumption errors are the ones that can be corrected by model updating.
Since the model’s accuracy directly depends on the fidelity of these assumptions, it is of
great interest to make the digital model as identical to the real structure as possible to re-
duce the discrepancies between the model predictions and the experimental observations.
A very efficient way of obtaining a realistic model is to design a model and then update it
based on real-time measurements. This is only possible if the structure or system has been
built or implemented. For example, an FE model, whose initial parameters are continu-
ously updated based on real-time measurements, is referred to as a digital twin. Digital
twins exist within many different industries, including HVAC-related systems where IFC
models are updated based on indoor climate measurements, in healthcare where human-
like models are designed and can be used for patient monitoring and surgical planning,
36
==PAGE:64==
Dynamic strain estimation by digital twins
and within transportation and logistics where digital models of supply chains or ware-
houses are updated. A visualization of the general workflow of a digital twin is presented
in figure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Main principles for digital twins.
As the figure above shows, working with a digital twin is an iterative process. Initially,
both a digital and physical model are created.
Then the physical model is mounted
with some measurement equipment. Different analytical tools can be applied to these
measurements to transfer the raw data into something that can be used to update the
digital model. The big advantage of having a digital twin is that it can be used for testing
and predictions, and these predictions can then be compared to the experimental data.
The digital model is then calibrated according to the discrepancy between the simulated
data and the experimental data, and this process is then repeated until a sufficiently
accurate digital model is achieved or indefinitely if used as a monitoring system. This has
a huge financial potential, as it can replace certain physical testing of systems and enhance
the quality of improvements and monitoring performed on the system. The following four
steps can summarize the entire monitoring cycle for real-life applications.
1. Collection of continuous data from the structure, typically acceleration, displace-
ment, or strain measurements
2. Identification of parameters
3. Tuning of the FE model based on the modal parameters
4. Prediction of structural response at different locations on the structure
The same is true for the example with human-like digital twins, condition monitoring for
structures is essential, as being able to predict future service problems by interpreting live
data would allow very efficient maintenance and operation. As stated by C. D. Foster
and J. E. Motterheads from 1990, "if changes are identified in the structural parameters
of a foundation, then such information can perhaps lead immediately to the part of the
structure which is responsible for the observed changes." [35].

7.2
Model updating with vibration data
Within the field of structural engineering, FE models are typically updated based on dis-
placement or acceleration measurements, or in some cases, both. In the case of this thesis,
a vibration analysis was performed on displacement measurements exclusively.
Vibration testing of structures is essential to consider, as vibrations can cause discomfort,
for example, when a high-rise building sways during a storm, and fatigue where structures
break when exposed to vibrations for a sufficiently long time. Structures start to sway as
a result of being exposed to dynamic loads. However, in real life, most structures, e.g.,
large OWTs, will be exposed to dynamic loads, such as waves and turbine dynamics, and
quasi-static loads, characterized by long periods. An example of a quasi-static load could
be loads that originate from thrust forces [36]. However, regarding the structure and
load described in section 2.1, only dynamic response is considered in this thesis. With
vibration tests, it should be highlighted that cases where a sensor is located close to the
vibration nodes, might include more measurement noise than other sensors. This is due
to a higher signal-to-noise ratio, which is then magnified when expanding as discussed in
chapter 10 [37].
As discussed earlier in chapter 4, the parameters of interest concerning vibration analysis
are the modal parameters and, in the case of this thesis, mainly the natural frequencies
and the mode shapes. These will also be the governing parameters used to define the
difference between the physical and digital models. The experimental data are analyzed
with OMA, and the natural frequencies and mode shapes are hereby determined. From
the digital model, the system matrices are used to solve the generalized EVP as discussed
in section 2.3, and the natural frequencies and mode shapes are again determined. Mass,
stiffness, and damping parameters can be very important in monitoring structures since
they relate to precise locations on a physical system. The idea is then to adjust the sys-
tem matrices, typically the mass matrix M and stiffness matrix K, of the digital model
in order to make the modal parameters from the two models agree with one another. It
makes sense to change the mass and stiffness matrices, as the precision of these is often
limited by measurement equipment, inherently difficult to quantify, and might change
with time.
The question remains exactly how the discrepancy between the modal parameters is used
to update the digital model.
In this thesis, two different methods will be discussed.
First, a method based on cost functions is used, where the cost is minimized with the
fminsearch in Matlab. Secondly, the sensitivity method, which is considered the in-
dustry standard for model updating, will be implemented based on vibration data [34].
A more detailed explanation of the theory behind them is first covered, and later, the
results from the two methods will be discussed in chapters 8 and 9.

Model updating using cost functions
With an idea of, what a digital twin is, the next step is to look at the different methods for
updating the FE model. A good starting point is relating the deviation in measurements
between the FE model and OMA to some cost and then applying mathematical tools in
order to minimize the total cost. This type of approach is the most common one, and
in this chapter, different cost functions will be formulated and evaluated based on their
performance.
8.1
Local stiffness optimization
As seen in chapter 2, the global stiffness matrix is created from the local inter-story stiff-
nesses kj. These values are determined analytically, but the real values will differ from the
analytical ones due to imperfections and assumptions. Moreover, the local stiffnesses are
more suitable for optimization than the local masses since mass can easily be measured,
whereas the stiffness is more complex in nature and difficult to estimate. Furthermore,
stiffness is more sensitive than mass, as it depends on strains, while mass depends on
displacements. This is valid for linear-elastic materials, where the stiffness is proportional
to the volume strain, which is the first derivative of displacement [38]. Therefore, errors
in the stiffness are introduced due to differentiation, making stiffness a suitable parameter
to update. Another thing to consider is the geometrical and material properties of the
system, such as the bending stiffness EI and length of the columns L, as these are also
possible optimization parameters.
Intuitively, it seems reasonable to describe the discrepancy between the experimental and
theoretical values by some error or cost and then let a minimization algorithm try to
reduce the total cost by changing certain parameters. The local stiffnesses make up good
parameters to change, and the modal parameters seem appropriate for parameters to
define the cost. However, as the modal parameters are estimated by solving the EVP,
using the eigenvalues in the cost function is convenient instead of the natural frequencies.
The following sub-chapters will investigate how the local stiffnesses are adjusted based on
eigenvalues, mode shapes, and a combined eigenvalue-mode shape cost function. Further-
more, it will also be investigated what effect it has by using different accuracy measures
for mode shapes and discuss if one seems to be superior.
8.1.1
Minimization algorithm
There is a plethora of multivariate minimization algorithms, including quasi-newton meth-
ods, simulated annealing, and gradient-descent-based methods, where the latter is proba-
bly the most used type and essential what lies behind the sensitivity method discussed in
chapter 9 [26]. However, to make the first attempt at updating an FE-model as simple as
possible, the Matlab’s fminsearch function has been used for minimizing the cost func-
tion. This function uses the Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm, which is a non-derivative
method that uses a so-called direct search approach. The reader is referred to [39] for a

9.2
The sensitivity matrix
As stated in the previous section, the sensitivity matrix is of great importance, and, as seen
from equation (9.2), it describes the sensitivity of the residual parameters relative to the
optimization parameters. Therefore, a high value in the matrix will indicate that a residual
parameter changes a lot based on changes in the optimization parameter. The residual
parameters should be sensitive to changes in the optimization parameters, and this must
be justified by a physical understanding of the structure and the test arrangement. The
optimization parameters of choice are often stiffnesses, for the same reasons as discussed
in chapter 8, but the residual parameters can be a bit more tricky to select.
A big
advantage of the method is that it allows the user to use a mixture of different residual
parameter types as long as the number of residual parameters, q, exceeds the number of
optimization parameters p. There is, therefore, a large number of combinations of residual
and optimization parameters that can be used. This thesis will not cover this, as the focus
is on local stiffnesses and modal parameters.

Virtual sensing
In chapter 7, it was discussed how a calibrated FE model could be achieved through
model updating based on vibration data. The importance of a precise FE model is also
emphasized, as the quality of the predictions would be higher. This is even more true
when predictions are wanted at places where no sensors are available and, as stated in
A. Iliopoulos et al. 2016, "The issue of limited information due to limited availability of
operational data is overcome by using an updated and properly calibrated finite element
model." [48].
The process of attaining or predicting measurements at places with no
sensors is called virtual sensing, indicating an artificial or simulated sensor measurement.
Regarding FE models, virtual sensing is most often performed with one of two methods,
MDE or Kalman filtering. This thesis will use and discuss the MDE approach in the
following section, while Kalman filtering is debated in section 13.2. Furthermore, virtual
sensing will only be used to predict the displacements on the structure’s first floor. This
means all figures that include predicted displacements will be for the first floor.
10.1
Modal decomposition and expansion
As the name suggests, the MDE technique involves decomposing the structural response
into mode shapes and modal coordinate components, where a limited number of mode
shapes is used to predict displacements. However, this decomposition has already been
covered in equation (4.6) and will therefore be skipped. Instead, the importance of the
modal coordinates is discussed.
The modal coordinates express how much different mode shapes contribute to the overall
structural response of the structure. Because of this, it is possible to get an insight into
what mode shapes have a significant contribution at specific frequencies. With this, the
mode shapes that have a considerable contribution to the total structural response can
be selected, as this would yield a better expansion [27, 48, 36].
After the modal decomposition, the modal expansion begins. The total number of DOFs

10.2
Quality indicators
To evaluate the precision of the predicted signal compared to the measured signal, dif-
ferent quality indicators have been developed. These are available in both the time and
frequency domains, but only the time domain versions will be considered in this thesis.
Frequency and time domain values will coincide for an identical frequency range, making
the calculation in both domains redundant [48].
10.2.1

Traditionally, the Time Response Assurance Criterion (TRAC) is used to compare to
what degree two time-domain signals are in phase. TRAC values range from 0-1, where
0 indicates zero correlation, and 1 indicates the signals are completely in phase with one
another. One TRAC value will be calculated for each DOF over all time, which can be
seen below

value. Due to this, some indicator of amplitude deviation should also be calculated in
combination with a TRAC value.

10.3
Multiband approach
When all modes contribute somewhat equally to the total structural response, leaving
out any of the modes might significantly worsen the results of the expansion described
in section 10.1. A way to avoid this is by dividing a signal into frequency bands and
performing the virtual sensing on the individual bands. This approach is called multiband
MDE and is often used for real applications.
As an example, OWTs are often exposed to both dynamic and quasi-static loads, as
mentioned at the beginning of this thesis. Here it is often convenient to divide the signal
into a low-frequency, which accounts for the measurements induced by the quasi-static
loads, and a high-frequency band that accounts for the remaining loads. The multiband
estimation of the predicted displacement will then be given as the summation of the
predicted displacements in each frequency band, which is seen below
qp = qp,low + qp,high
(10.8)
where qp,low are the predicted displacements from the low-frequency band and qp,high are
the displacements from the high-frequency band [50]. However, in some cases, where there
is a lot of energy in all modes considered in the decomposition and expansion, dividing
the signal into a low- and high-frequency band is insufficient. It might be necessary to
divide the signal into multiple frequency bands. Sometimes, a frequency band per mode
might be necessary. This can be seen in
qp = qp,1 + qp,2 + ... + qp,n
(10.9)
where n is the total number of modes included in the expansion. Again, the total pre-
dicted displacements are given as the summation of the predicted displacements from each
frequency band.

Fatigue evaluation
The results from the previous chapter about virtual sensing are often further processed in
relation to fatigue and lifetime estimations of structures, as the predicted measurements
can provide insight into a system’s health. Therefore, this second to last chapter will
round off the calculations by introducing a way of transferring measurements into mean-
ingful quantities related to fatigue estimations and how these are affected by deviations
in predicted measurements from virtual sensing.
11.1
Rainflow counting
When structures are exposed to dynamic loading, fatigue is essential to pay attention
to, as cyclic loading will result in cracks initiating and propagating. This is one of the
main threats when discussing SHM related to bridges, cranes, wind turbines, and offshore
structures.
Most often, the objective is to estimate the lifetime expectancy of the structure, but
before this can be done, a way of quantifying and measuring accumulated damage is
needed. Traditionally this is done by categorizing stress cycles by their magnitude and
then counting the number of occurrences of each stress cycle in the structural response.
One of the most used methods to count the number of cycles, which does not have to
be stress cycles, is the Rainflow counting algorithm [51]. The scheme aims to determine
the number of stress cycles and their magnitudes to assess fatigue damage and predict
the fatigue life of a structure. Furthermore, there are a few different implementations of
the scheme, and the one that will be used in this thesis is referred to as the Pagoda roof
method. This version is considered the industry standard and is used in the ASTM E1049-
85 - Standard Practices for Cycle Counting in Fatigue Analysis, which is an American
standard used in construction. The method is summarized in the following steps [52].
88
==PAGE:116==
Dynamic strain estimation by digital twins
Steps for the Pagoda roof method:
1) Process the data so that only "tensile peaks" and "compressive valleys" remain.
2) Turn the time history 90◦, so that the earliest time is at the top.
3) First, consider the tensile peaks. Each tensile peak is imagined as a source of water
that "drips" down the time history.
4) Count the number of half-cycles by looking for the termination of the flow. The
flow is terminated in three cases.
a) It reaches the end of the time history.
b) It merges with a flow that started at an earlier tensile peak.
c) An opposite tensile peak has greater or equal magnitude.
5) Repeat step 5 for compressive valleys.
6) Assign a magnitude to each half-cycle equal to the stress difference between its start
and termination.
7) Pair up half-cycles of identical magnitude, but opposite sense, to count the number
of complete cycles. Typically, there are some residual half-cycles.
A short example will illustrate how the steps are performed, where a fictional stress
history is considered and visualized in figure 11.1. If the reader is comfortable with the
methodology, skipping to section 11.2 is advised.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to estimate the dynamic strains based on a digital twin. However, this
was taken a step further to get an insight into the use of predicted measurements. In
addition to this, the effect of introducing non-proportional damping to the system was
also investigated.
It is concluded that SSI-COV, SSI-DAT, and EFDD perform well in estimating the modal
parameters for the experimental data. All three methods have a mean natural frequency
and mode shape accuracy of 99.9% or greater, making it impossible to denote one method
as superior, as they have their strengths and weaknesses. Yet, the three methods per-
form differently for the mean damping ratio estimates and computation times, with the
SSI-based techniques able to estimate the higher damping ratios more accurately.
In
comparison, EFDD is able to estimate the lower damping ratios better. Additionally, the
computation times for the SSI-COV and EFDD are much shorter when compared to SSI-
DAT. Because of this, either SSI-COV or EFDD should be used when considering large
data sets. In this thesis, SSI-COV is chosen, as this provides slightly better estimations of
mode shapes, which is important as MDE is performed later. Furthermore, this provided
an opportunity to get acquainted with the technique.
Following the OMA segment, different cost functions and residual types are implemented
for updating the FE model. Combining an eigenvalue and mode shape deviation as the
squared error yields the best result compared to the different cost functions. However, it
is worth noting that the cost function solely based on eigenvalues also produces surpris-
ingly good results. When applying the sensitivity method, almost the same conclusion
is reached. The eigenvalue and mode shape residual yields the best result together with
the eigenvalue residual, which is surprisingly accurate for both natural frequencies and
mode shapes. The only difference between the two residuals is that the eigenvalue resid-
ual alone did not hold the criteria of having more residual parameters than optimizing
parameters. Comparing the results from the cost functions with those from the sensitivity
method, the sensitivity method generally yields the best natural frequency estimations.
In contrast, the cost functions yield the best mode shape estimations. Nevertheless, this
result is hugely affected by the weighting of the residuals, which can be tweaked for both
techniques. Additionally, implementing the cost functions is much easier than implement-
ing the sensitivity method, and the computation times are much longer for the sensitivity
method. Still, the sensitivity method has been proven to work for real-sized structures,
which cannot be said about the cost functions.
Lastly, virtual sensing is used to predict the displacements on the first floor of the five-story
frame, and it is concluded that multiband MDE with a band for each mode is necessary
to achieve acceptable results. It is also proven that updating the FE model with the cost
function eigenvalue and mode shape case and using the resultant mode shapes leads to
the best results. Moreover, using the measurements from three sensors instead of five has
a small impact on the TRAC and MAE, making them slightly worse. However, the small
worsening is substantial when using the predicted displacements for fatigue evaluation.

Introducing non-proportional damping to the system turns out to have a slim to non-
existent effect on all results, apart from the virtual sensing. The damping increases the
amplitude error in the MDE results, which worsens the fatigue estimations.

Further studies
Many choices have been made during the work of this thesis, as the same analyses could
have been done in many different ways. Most choices have been made based on a balance
between performance and simplicity of implementation, but sometimes choices have been
made to be able to progress in the work. This will leave some questions unanswered
or otherwise interesting topics or problem statements not investigated. As a result, this
section will present a few ideas for further studies, which could be interesting to examine
in future work.
13.1
Experimental setup
In section 2.1, it was explained why the experimental setup was designed in the way it
was and what the original objective with it was. This entails that some aspects of the
setup are disadvantageous when considering the scope of this thesis. This mainly includes
the equally distributed energy present in all five modes and the lack of a strain gauge at
the bottom of the setup.
Having a lot of energy present in all five modes has the drawback of restricting the
possible accuracy obtained by performing modal expansion. Due to a limited number of
mode shapes that can be included in the expansion, there will always be some energy that
will not be represented in the predicted signal. This will be a substantial part when all
modes have a lot of energy.
Moreover, the main objective of this thesis was to investigate the accuracy of dynamic
strain estimations based on virtual sensing, which is rather challenging to do when the
real strains are unknown. For systems that can be represented similarly to the 1D FE
column presented in figure 2.2, the point of interest is often at the base, as this is often
the most critical spot. Therefore, it would have been a good idea to place a strain gauge
at the bottom of the frame, which is mounted to the shaking table, to evaluate the strain
estimations from virtual sensing.
13.2
Kalman filters
An alternative to using the MDE method for virtual sensing is to use a Kalman filter
approach, a more recently developed method that should be rather competitive and in-
terchangeable with the MDE approach [54]. The paper used both algorithms to estimate
strains on an OWT on a monopile foundation, both in parked and rotating conditions.
The conclusion was that both methods achieved good results, and it was unclear if one
should be superior to the other. Another paper investigates the performances of the MDE
and the augmented Kalman filter approaches for simulated data of a truss structure, with
the objective of potential implementation in a wireless fatigue strain-digitizing setup [55].
Here a lot of emphasis was put on the ease of implementation and low computation times,
and it wasa discovered that both schemes generate promising results. Still, MDE might
97
==PAGE:125==
Dynamic strain estimation by digital twins
produce slightly better results since it achieved a higher minimum TRAC value. How-
ever, i was still concluded that "The two strain estimation algorithms presented for the
real application are still competitive and interchangeable." [55]. A third paper investigates
how the two algorithms perform on an experimental setup of a cantilever beam, excited
both by impulse loads and pseudo-random loads [56]. It concludes that if the number of
installed sensors is no less than the number of considered modes, MDE is the preferable
method. This is the case for the first test of the setup in this thesis. Still, when only
three sensors are used in the OMA, and a greater number of modes are considered, the
paper suggests a modified Kalman filter would be the best choice due to insensitivity to
the sensor distribution.
All this shows high uncertainty in the performance differences between the two approaches.
When considering digital twins, a trade-off between accuracy and computation is essential,
and this could be interesting to investigate for non-simulated data in the future.
13.3
Experimental mode-shapes vs FE mode-shapes
When performing the MDE, not only the mode shapes from the FE model are available,
but also some from the OMA, depending on the number of measured locations. This
means the expansion can also consist of a mixture between the experimental and FE
mode shapes or solely experimental mode shapes. One article indicates that it might be
wiser to perform MDE from experimental mode shapes, as it can improve the accuracy
of stress estimations [57]. Still, it comes at the cost of fitting errors. This results from
reduced modeling errors, as the experimental mode shapes are closer to the actual system
than the FE mode shapes. However, as the author states, "To the best of the author’s
knowledge, however, the two approaches have never been compared in a study" [57].
An interesting study would therefore consist of comparing the precision of the two types
of mode shapes used in MDE to see if using one over the other would be more effective.
Otherwise, it could also be interesting to combine the two types of mode shapes by
substituting some of the mode shapes in the expansion with the experimental mode shapes
and see how this would affect the performance of the virtual sensing.
For example,
the lower mode shapes from the experimental analysis could be used, as these typically
contribute more to the total structural response and are closer to the actual system.

determined from S-N fatigue curves, sometimes referred to as Wöhler curves. Depending
on different factors, the life expectancy can be very sensitive to the stress ranges since
it might be scaled by either a factor 3 or 5 [61]. Therefore, a slight deviation in the
predicted measurements might be amplified compared to actual measurements, resulting
in very different lifetime estimates.
