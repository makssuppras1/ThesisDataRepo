Abstract
This thesis studies the implementation of a recycling process for glass fibres rein-
forced polymer (GFRP) composite panels by dissolution of the recyclable thermoset
polymer matrix with acetic acid.
An experimental investigation allows to set-up a
procedure in order to reach clean recycled glass fibres. In addition, the development of
a kinetic model of the dissolution reaction helps to understand and predict the dissolu-
tion evolution for various temperatures, studying the impact of different experimental
parameters on the properties of the fibres. Recycled fibres are observed on optical and
scanning electrons microscopy and the amount of remaining resin after the dissolution
procedure is quantified by a loss of ignition measurement. Finally, the recycled fibres
are characterised by performing single fibre test.

Introduction
Wind energy is a key part of mitigation of climate change ; to reach the European commis-
sion energy and climate target, more than 30% of the European electricity demand will have
to be covered by wind energy, and more than 40% in 2050 [1]. Currently, only 19% of the
EU’s electricity demand is covered by wind farms [2]. To meet the EU target a significant
number of wind turbines needs to be produced in the coming years, which will result in a
rising demand for materials.
In that respect, the current trend is to increase the size of wind turbines, and so of
blades [10]. A growing production of blades come with a rising demand for composite ma-
terials, while trying to lower the energy and carbon footprint of blades production. Hence
it is essential to ensure that this material can be recycled.
A wind turbine is mainly composed of metal; 80 to 88% of the total turbine mass is
steel and iron materials [3] [11]. Then, fibreglass and resin, the components of glass fibres
reinforced polymer (GFRP), and plastic counts as 5 to 10% of the total turbine mass. The
remaining is aluminium, copper, alloys and electronics. Concrete material is not considered
in these numbers. The recycling technology already exists for metal [20] : already 40% of
the steel produced comes from recycled material in the current market. Steel is mainly re-
cycled with electric arc furnace (EAF) [20]. Unfortunately, GFRP is known to be complex
to recycle [4] [10] [12]. The polymers and composite materials in wind turbine are mostly
found in blades. A turbine blade is composed of two faces gathered by an adhesive joint and
made stiff by a box beam or integral webs linking the upper and lower faces [22]. A wind
turbine blade is typically made of GFRP composite [10]. Glass fibres correspond to 75% of
the blade weight. Fibres are essential for their stiffness. The typical glass fibres used in the
industry are the E −glass, made of borosilicate glass fibres [10]. In the current market, the
composite panels are mostly made with thermoset polymer matrix [12], the most used one
being an epoxy matrix.
Many recycling technologies for GRFP have been investigated and these are generally
ranked according to the waste management hierarchy [5] [4] [24] [25]. The waste manage-
ment hierarchy applies as “a priority order in waste prevention and management”[6] and
is used in EU waste policy and legislation. First, prioritizing prevention of waste by de-
signing blades using easily recyclable material or less material, then reusing of the blades
for the same application. After that blades can be repurposed, for instance GFRP blades
could be used as furniture, something they were not initially designed for [15]. Then come
recycling, which can be mechanical, thermal or chemical. Mechanical recycling consists in
shredding the blades to obtain chopped fibres [7] that can then be used in new composites
to produce new material. Thermal recycling aims to extract the fibres by using a pyrolysis
treatment [8] [9]. Chemical recycling results in using solvent to dissolve the polymer matrix
and to save both the fibres and the matrix [4].
Recycling of wind turbine blades remains a challenge because thermosets polymers can
not be remolded to form a new product [10] [12] and because of the poor quality of recycled
3
==PAGE:6==
materials, especially recycled glass fibres. For example, for mechanical recycling processes,
a lot of wear is caused to the equipment due to the presence of glass fibres. For thermal
recycling processes, high temperatures are required to separate the composite constituents re-
sulting in a significant reduction in the mechanical properties of recycled glass fibres [16] [21].
Besides, recycling of GFRP is complex because of the variable amount and quality of mate-
rial to recycle, making the implementation of a procedure a difficult task [14].
In order to lower the energy and carbon impacts of wind turbine blades, different com-
posite materials for the production of blades have been considered. Natural fibres such as
sisal, flax, hemp and jute are studied [13]. These are cheap and available, however, they
show quality variations, a high moisture uptake and a low thermal stability. Other material
strategies have aimed at easily recycled and repairable fibre reinforced polymer composites.
This is done for example with the investigation of infusible thermoplastic or recyclable ther-
moset [22]. This would allow to separate the composite constituents in a easier way and to
have recycled materials with good properties [18]. The French polymer company Arkema has
developed the thermoplastic polymer Elium that aims to be used for recyclable composite.
However, the mechanical performances of the thermoplastic composites for blades are still
behind the classical ones made with thermosets [27]. Thus, a main focus is given to new
recyclable thermoset resin. A thermoset can be made recyclable by introducing degradable
or dynamic covalent bonds in the polymer [23]. To dissolve these new recyclable resin, a
chemical recycling process is typically needed. This may include the use of an acid and
heat [18]. While being an interesting technology, these materials and processes are still in
development so knowledge is still missing. To plan future wind turbine blade recycling so-
lution ahead, more knowledge is for instance needed on the recycling procedure. Very little
literature is available on these recycling processes and despite being promising, the advan-
tages are yet to be fully characterized [18] [19]. In this regard, experimental data is needed
to better understand, model and optimize such recycling processes as well as to understand
the impacts and the potential of the out-coming recycled materials.
This thesis studies glass fibre reinforced polymer composites manufactured with a recy-
clable epoxy resin, the Recyclamine resin from Aditya Birla Chemicals [26]. The aim of the
study is to model the recycling process of the composite investigated and determine the im-
pacts on the outcoming recycled glass fibres. To do so, this study focuses on an experimental
implementation of a recycling procedure and on the characterisation of recycled glass fibres
properties.
The recycling process investigated is a dissolution process triggered by an acidic environ-
ment, using acetic acid 32%, at a temperature up to 90°C. A first experimental campaign
is dedicated to the development of a lab scale recycling process and the development of
methods to characterize the advancement and the completion of the recycling process. To
optimize future dissolution processes, it is essential to understand when the dissolution pro-
cess is completed. In this study, the time of completion is defined as the shortest time at
which clean recycled glass fibres are obtained. The cleanliness of the recycled glass fibres is
assessed with a loss of ignition measurement, which quantifies the amount of remaining resin
present on the fibres after the dissolution procedure. In addition, a second experimental
4
==PAGE:7==
campaign is performed to define the understanding and the characterization of a complete
dissolution. Here the experiments are conducted on loose glass fibres that went through the
same acidic and heating treatment. Then, to model the dissolution process, a third experi-
mental campaign is performed, where three recycling process temperatures are investigated:
70, 80 and 90°C. The progress of the reaction is followed with a qualitative assessment us-
ing optical and scanning electrons microscopy and a quantitative assessment using a loss of
ignition measurements. This experimental campaign provides the data for a comparative
study of kinetic models which enables the characterization of the temperature-dependency
of the chemical reaction. A kinetic model is then selected to model dissolution processes at
different temperatures and predict their time of completion. The time of completion pre-
dicted for the three different temperature is then used in a fourth experimental campaign.
The objective of this last campaign is to validate the model selected and to characterize the
impact of different recycling temperature on the mechanical properties of recycled glass fibres.
This thesis is structured in the following way. First the method section presents the
experimental plan, the dissolution procedure, the different characterisation techniques and
the kinetic modelling.
The results section presents the initial composite material, then
the procedure of glass fibres recycling by dissolution of the resin. Then the results section
presents the kinetic model of resin dissolution and finally the recycled glass fibres mechanical
properties. The strength and limitations of the approach followed in this study are discussed
in the discussion section. In the end, the main results of this study are summarized in the
conclusions as well as future work needed in this field. Altogether this study contributes to
a more thorough understanding of recycling processes for recyclable resin composites and
future wind turbine blades.

Methods
2.1
Experimental plan
Firstly, the experimental study aims to implement an experimental protocol of the dissolu-
tion of the resin to reach clean fibres. Then, the dissolution reaction is studied through the
development of a kinetic model and the study of the stage of reaction at different dissolu-
tion time, for various temperatures. Finally, one may assess on the impact of the recycling
procedure on the mechanical properties of the glass fibres. More precisely, the investigation
focuses on a potential impact on the Young modulus and maximum strength of the fibres
with rising temperatures.
In that respect, the experimental plan of this thesis is divided into four experimental
campaigns :
- Procedure development (I) : it aims to implement the dissolution procedure, dealing
with the experimental set-up and the first considerations on experimental parameters.
Four trials are performed in order to adjust the technical parameters. The first ap-
proach of the dissolution experiment gathers default parameters : a 100mm x 30mm
5
==PAGE:8==
x 5mm composite sample immersed in acetic acid 32% during 4 hours, at a targeted
temperature of 90°C and without stirring.
For each trial, a qualitative study of the fibres is first conducted aiming to assert if
the dissolution is complete : fibres are observed through optical and scanning electrons
microscopy, looking for resin residues. Then, a loss of ignition test is performed to
validate the complete dissolution of the resin.
- Loose fibres characterisation (II) : the objective is to set a reference for the study by
performing the experiment on loose glass fibres : a loss of ignition measurement will
be conducted; it will provide a reference on what percentage of weight loss is expected
for recycled fibres supposed to be clean. Then, the density of loose fibres is measured.
This value is needed to analyse single fibre test results. Finally, single fibre tests will
be performed on these fibres. The Young modulus will give a reference to what is
expected for recycled glass fibres.
- Temperature effects (III) : Once the experimental set-up has been figured out in I,
campaign III studies the dissolution of the resin in composite samples at 90°C with time.
It includes four dissolution experiments; III-01, III-02, III-03 and III-04, respectfully
with duration times of 30min, 1h, 2h and 4h. Then, the experiments are repeated at
70°C (III-05, III-06, III-07 and III-08), then at 80°C (III-09, III-10, III-11 and III-12).
- Model development and validation (IV) : this experimental campaign reproduces the
satisfying results from campaign III in order to perform mechanical tests on the recycled
glass fibres.It also aims to add experimental points to the model at 70°C to validate
its prediction and to reach clean fibres at this temperature.

2.2
Dissolution procedure
Section 2.2 describes the default dissolution protocol, with different temperatures Texp and
duration times texp :
After measuring the dimensions and mass of the composite sample, it is placed in a 1L
glass bottle with 500mL of acetic acid 32%, a magnetic stirrer and a glass lid. The glassware
is positioned on a heater and stirring device, a thermocouple is also inserted in the bottle
and connected to the device to automatically control the temperature.
The dissolution procedure is performed at the temperature Texp, set on the heating de-
vice, during texp. After this dissolution time, the sample is cooled down and filtered with
distilled water on a ceramic filter.
During campaign I, the experiments are performed one by one. For all the next cam-
paigns, up to four dissolution experiments are performed at the same time. Figure 1 presents

2.3
Characterization techniques
2.3.1
Density determination of glass fibres by gas pycnometry
Gas pycnometry allows for the measurement of a sample’s volume. Knowing the mass, the
density can then be determined.
Indeed, the pycnometer presents two known volumes, Vcell and Vadded.
First, a solid
sample, with a known mass, is placed in Vcell. The valve is closed and Vadded is at Patm, Vcell
is pressurizes with nitrogen at 19 psi. This pressure, P1, is measured, relative to Patm.
Then, the valve opens; Vcell and Vadded are connected and the relative pressure P2 is
measured. Using the Ideal Law of Gases, the volume of the sample is calculated:
Vsample = Vcell −
Vadded
(P2/P1) −1
(1)
The density determination by gas pycnometry procedure relies on first, the equipment
preparation. Then, on the calibration procedure which uses a small sphere with a known
volume. It calibrates both volumes Vcell and Vadded and must be run at the beginning of a
measurement series.
The sample needs to be placed under vacuum conditions for at least 16 hours, usually
overnight, to allow it to be completely dry. A maximum of fibers are rolled around the spool
in the bucket and the combined mass of the spool, bucket and sample is measured.
To get a satisfying density measurement, at least three valid measurements on the fibres
from the same sample must be performed.
A measurement is accepted if the volume stabilizes after 50 runs. Otherwise, it must
be repeated. Moreover, a few single runs (not more than five) may be excluded from the
analysis.

2.3.2
Measurement of resin residue content by loss of ignition test
Loss of ignition (LOI) measurement is a method aiming to assess the loss of weight of a
sample between before and after burn-off. The method used here relies on the one used for
composite sample. It has be adapted to the study of fibres with resin residues and is based
on the standard ASTM D4963/D4963M-22. The objective here is to quantify the mass of
resin left on the fibres after the dissolution process, wresin.
The standard recommend three samples per analysis, and each sample should weight at
least 3g. Each sample is composed of loose glass fibres pulled from the fabric that went
through the dissolution process. The samples are dried, the crucibles annealed then cooled
down. Empty crucibles are weighted, then crucibles with the samples.
The crucibles with the samples are put into a preheated muffle furnace at 625°C for 2
hours. After burning off the matrix, the crucibles are placed in a desiccator containing a
moisture absorbing material for about 1 hour.
The crucibles with the fibres are weighted. The mass ratio of the weight of the sample
before and after the process will give the percentage of resin relative to the sample.
The LOI value obtained through the procedure is the percentage of remaining resin
compared to the sample mass after dissolution, it is calculated the following way :
LOI(%) = wbefore −wafter

2.3.3
Microscopy
Recycled fabrics and fibres are observed by optical microscopy in order to have a first look
at the fabric and at the fibres. More precisely, one may want to have a first look at resin
residues, but also to know if fibres are still gathered as bundles and if the stitching is still
holding the bundles.
No pre-treatment is needed for this observation and the samples can be easily observed
after the dissolution process on an optical microscope.
Scanning electrons microscopy is necessary to look for organic residues on the fibres after
the dissolution procedure.
Only some fibres need to be observed with SEM. For that, some chopped fibres are col-
lected from the sample and placed on a carbon tab.
For SEM, samples are usually coated either with gold or with carbon. Gold being too
heavy (ZAu = 79), it would not allow to observe the differences between glass fibre (ZSi =
14)and organic material mostly made from carbon (ZC = 6). Thus, carbon coating is used
here. Carbon coating allows to enhance conductivity of the sample, while being electrons
transparent.
The output images from SEM are the result of the interaction of the electrons probe with
the atoms of the sample.
Secondary electrons (SE) have very low energy, around 50eV. Thus, SE can only escape
from a 5-50nm range of the sample thickness, as figured in Figure 4. Its signal is localized at

Backscattered electrons (BSE) are reflected from the sample by elastic scattering. They

2.3.4
Single fibre tensile test
Single fibre tests are performed using a semi-automated “Favimat+” machine, combined
with a sample loading system, Robot 2 (TexTechno, M¨onchengladbach, Germany) [32]. In-
deed, after extracting single glass fibres from a bundle with painting brushes, single fibres
are hung in a magazine with a capacity of 25 fibres. Each fibre is clipped to the magazine
from one end, and held vertically straight by a small clip-on weight of about 0.1g at the
other end. Then, the magazine is placed in the testing region of the machine. From this
point on, the test is automated; a robotic device comes to pick the single fibres, one by one
for placement in the testing region.
Once held in the testing clamps, the fibre diameter is measured thanks to vibroscopy [32]
: a tension force is applied to the fibre and acoustic resonance frequency is measured, leading
to the linear density T[kg/m] determination. Then, using the fibre density ρ0 measured with
gas pycnometry, the cross-sectional area of the fibre is calculated, A0 = T
ρ0.
The tensile test is then performed by first reducing the tension force to zero, and then
displacing the upper clamp upwards with a speed of 1mm/min [32].

4
Discussion
4.1
Procedural improvements
4.1.1
Comparison to literature
The recycling procedure developed by Dubey et al in [18] on the Recyclamine paper is not
described completely ; the composite sample is immersed in a “recovery solution” at 80°C for
6 hours for the solvolysis to happen. The container is a metallic box and no stirring seems
to be added. In addition, no investigation of the time of completion seems to be conducted.
Literature is missing considering recycling procedure on recyclable thermosets.
4.1.2
Limitation and suggestion for improvement
A recycling procedure of the composite sample has been developed in this thesis and is pre-
sented in Section 2.2. However, a future work would be to upgrade the experimental protocol
of this procedure.
The glassware, heating and stirring devices are satisfying. However, a first optimization
would be to reduce the amount of acid solvent. The volume of acetic acid is currently 500
mL. It is observed during the procedure that almost no evaporation occurs during the heat-
ing. In that respect, one might want to reduce the volume of solvent in order to reduce even
36
==PAGE:39==
more the material-impact of this procedure.
In addition, it has been noticed that, for some experiments stopped at an early stage of
dissolution, the composite panel would stick to the bottom of the glassware. The solvent
flow is not homogeneous, even with the magnetic stirring. Then, developing an experimental
set-up allowing to have a acid solvent flow homogeneously around the sample would be
preferable.
4.1.3
Discussion of the sizing effect and of the indicator for end of reaction
As explained by Thomason et al [30], sizing, or size, of fibres is a complex and mostly con-
fidential part of fibres production. It is composed of a film coating, which aims to protect
and lubricate the fibres, while holding them together before the composite processing, and
of a coupling agent. The coupling agent is almost always an alkoxysilane compound and is
used to band the fibres to the matrix resin.
Peterson et al [31] and Thomason et [30] explain that sizing can be removed from the
fibres by solvent extraction or during a 600°C thermal treatment. However, only the organic
part of the sizing can be removed. Indeed, the surface of the fibres will be silica-rich due to
the presence of thermal degraded silanes [30].
Peterson et al [31] describes the sizing removal procedure by soxhlet extraction, in 200mL
of acetone for 24 hours before letting dry at room temperature for at least 24 hours.
Solvent extraction allows to remove around 80% of the sizing [31].
Thus, it is fair to assume that the dissolution procedure described in Section 2.2 may
remove as much as 80% of the sizing. However, it is difficult to know exactly how much of
the sizing has been removed. Future work could consider to what extend a new sizing can be
applied on the recycled fibres. A heterogeneous fibre surface could make sizing more complex.
The indicator for a complete dissolution is defined as the amount of remaining organic
element present on the loose fibres after the dissolution procedure, described in Section 3.2.2.
During the dissolution procedure, the loose fibres are in direct contact with acidic solvent.
On the other hand, during the dissolution procedure of composite samples described in
Section 2.2, glass fibres located in the bulk of the sample for example, are not in direct
contact with solvent. Hence, one may wonder if a greater amount of sizing is removed on
loose fibres that what is actually removed on the fibres in the composite sample. Thus, the
reference for cleanliness of fibres might be too low.
In order to define this indicator for complete dissolution, a LOI measurement could have
been performed on virgin fibres, fibres with sizing. Then, the reference for cleanliness would
be too high.
In that respect, these limits in wresin could maybe be considered, and the reference would
work on a range of cleanliness.
Furthermore, the time of completion is defined as the shortest time at which clean recycled
37
==PAGE:40==
fibres are obtained. Dissolution procedures have been conducted at different time, however,
the completion time can not be experimentally assessed. For instance, at 90°C the model
suggests that the time of completion is around 3 hours but the experiment is conducted for
4 hours. More data is needed to investigate exactly at what dissolution time wresin would
intercept the reference limit for clean fibres.
4.2
Model
4.2.1
Comparison to literature
Both the kinetic models used by Pinero et al [28] and Keith et al [29] are developed to study
the solvolsis of polymer resin on carbon fibres reinforced polymer composites.
The model presented is analytical, fast and easy to implement with an Excel software.
More advanced models are being developed, using finite elements modelling, considering
more parameters [35].
4.2.2
Limitation and suggestion for improvements
In the model studied by Pinero et al [28], wsolvent is assumed to be constant. This assumption
can be questioned, as we observed evaporation of half of the solvent volume in one of the
first dissolution procedure, not described in this report.
In the model developed by Keith et al [29], an average weight of the composite is con-
sidered to determine M. It is reasonable because standard deviation of composite sample
weight is 0.05g but it could still be challenged.
In both models, the kinetic model assumes that the degradation process happens at the
surface of the resin. However, the interface between solvent and resin is not the same at the
surface of the composite sample or in the bulk : diffusion mechanism should be considered.
4.2.3
Discussion of the accuracy of the model
As pictured in Figure 18, even if the determination coefficient is satisfying, there is still an
existing difference between the model and the experimental values, mostly at the end of
reaction.
The experimental values at 6 and 8 hours suggest that the reaction would be faster than
what the model predicts. Then the constant rate keff would be higher.
Hence, future work should focus on finding a kinetic model that would fit better the
experimental data.

4.3
Recycled materials
4.3.1
Glass fibres properties
In the study of Feith et al [33], E-glass fibres are coated with a silane-based sizing and present
a Young modulus of 74 GPa. In this thesis, the recycled fibres present an average modulus
of 84.5 GPa,
In their study, Feith et al [33] presented E-glass fibres with a single fibre strength of 2.3
GPa, which is a typical value for commercially-produced fibres. The recycled fibres produced
with the recycling procedure developed present the same magnitude of strength.
4.3.2
Influence of the dissolution procedure on the mechanical properties of
recycled glass fibres
First, it is interesting to compare recycled fibres to virgin fibres. However, as explained in
Section 3.4.1, here the fibres tested went through an acidic treatment at 90°C for 4 hours.
Hence, heating could already have lower the mechanical performance of these fibres.
Feith et al [33] explain that the elastic modulus is not affected by thermal treatment. In
this thesis, the Young modulus is quite stable : the standard deviation is only 1GPa. Thus,
the results presented in Figure 23 confirm that the Young modulus would not be influenced
by a heating procedure in the range 70-90°C.
Thomason et al [16] report that strength of fibres is mostly determined by the extrinsic
strength; which is controlled by the presence of flaws and mostly cracks. Feith et al [33]
add that an elevated temperature treatment on glass fibre leads to a structural relaxation
of internal deformation states. Hence, the fibres strength decreases rapidly with increasing
temperature and with heating time. In the study of Thomas et al [17], compared to virgin
fibres, 65% of the fibres strength is lost when fibres are treated above 400°C for 4 hours.
In the same way, Thomason et al [34] present that the strength of silane-coated glass is
relatively stable up to 300°C. At higher temperatures, there is a significant reduction of the
fibre strength.
In addition, Dubey et al [18] present that, for a dissolution procedure at 80°C, the me-
chanical performances of the recycled fibres are lower by 10%.
In the light of these studies, the single fibre test results presented in Figure 24 are inter-
esting : the maximum strength values are overlapping for the 4 samples. The temperatures
are far below the ones studied by Thomas et al and Thomason et al thus a different be-
haviour was expected. Here, no clear influence of the heating temperature can be defined
in this study, in the range of 70-90°C. More data is needed to be able to compare recycled
fibres with virgin fibres and then conclude on the agreement with Dubey’s conclusions or not.
Moreover, the 70°C procedure lasted 20 hours while the 3 others lasted 4 hours. But, the
data overlapping, the influence of the heating time can not be considered.

5
Conclusion
In conclusion, a recycling procedure of glass fibres reinforced polymer composite has been
implemented at the laboratory scale. The dissolution of the recyclable thermoset polymer
matrix is performed in accurate glassware, with magnetic stirring, in a acidic environment
using acetic acid 32% at temperatures from 70 to 90°C.
After the dissolution experiments for various temperatures, the implemented procedure
provides fibres with the same amount of organic element than what initially exists on virgin
fibres. Hence, this recycling procedure allows to reach clean fibres.
Two kinetic models have been investigated. The first-order Arrhenius model gives more
satisfying results and a better fit to the experimental data with determination coefficients
all greater than 0.94. On the other hand, the second-order non-stationary model lacks con-
sistency and does not fit properly the experimental data. Thus, the first-order model could
help predicting the completion time of the dissolution process and the rate of the chemical
reaction with temperature. Nevertheless, this model is still not strong enough and better
kinetic models should be investigated.
The mechanical properties of recycled fibres have been characterized for different disso-
lution paths ; the Young modulus and maximum strength of the glass fibres appear mostly
unchanged in the range of temperature 70-90°C. This is a promising result for these new
recyclable composites.
Finally, this work focuses on one specific composite material. Nevertheless, the overall
objective of this study is to implement a recycling procedure that could be use to test any
new recyclable thermoset resin and to assert on the impact of the recycling procedure on the
composite fibres.
