Abstract
In the marine environment, oceanic circulation is an essential means of transport. Ocean currents determine
transportation routes of several migratory species and the dispersal of larvae, propagules and drifting abiotic
particles. The knowledge of how the circulation inﬂuences the connectivity between ocean regions on a
global scale is however scarce. In this project, the transportation of particles between ecological provinces of
the Atlantic Ocean has been studied through particle tracking simulations with the ocean circulation model,
Nucleus for European Modelling of the Ocean (NEMO). The aim of the project was to identify connectivity
patterns and connectivity times between Atlantic Ocean ecological provinces, in order to determine major
routes and timing of particle transport. The results show a clear trend of transportation from equato-
rial regions towards the North Atlantic within the time scale of weeks and months. Major trans-Atlantic
connections were identiﬁed from east to west along the equator, and from west to east with the North At-
lantic Drift. Connections between productive coastal provinces were observed, indicating exchange of biotic
particles enhancing the productivity of coastal ecosystems, potentially also in trans-Atlantic connections.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the global ocean, the passive movement of particles is a key link to understand the dynamics of ecological
processes. Oceanic circulation drives the dispersal of matter, and drift with the ocean currents is an essential
means of transport for both living organisms and non-living substances. For larvae and propagules, oceanic
transport may determine larval dispersal and connections between spawning grounds and nursery areas
(CIESM, 2016), and ocean currents are essential for the travel routes of long-distance migratory species
(Putman, 2018). Smaller organisms such as bacteria or waterborne pathogens are also subject to horizontal
transport and dispersal by oceanic circulation (Frank et al., 2016; Kough et al., 2014). In addition to
living organisms, the global ocean is responsible for transporting a wide range of abiotic substances, as for
example nutrients (Talley et al., 2011) and environmental pollutants such as marine plastic (van Sebille
et al., 2012).
To understand the oceanic drift and how it determines links between geographical regions, oceanic
general circulation models (OGCMs) can be used to estimate the transport patterns and migration routes
of any type of particle in the marine environment. By coupling particle tracking algorithms with OGCMs,
patterns of connectivity can be identiﬁed. Knowledge about connectivity patterns, mass transport and
timescales of connectivity can contribute to explain a wide range of questions relevant for understanding
patterns and trends of dispersal, and is of great importance for the purpose of eﬃcient conservation and
management of the marine environment. For example for ﬁsh stock management, mapping links between
spawning and nursery areas can contribute to an eﬃcient management strategy, by identifying sensitive
areas (see Hidalgo et al., 2017). Modelling oceanic particle transport might also explain spread of invasive
species (e.g. d’Auriac et al., 2017), and dispersal or accumulation of marine debris (van Sebille et al., 2012).
The idea of studying and managing the marine environment with respect to spatially connected areas
has evolved during the last two decades (Bryan-Brown et al., 2017). Recognizing marine ecosystems as
sets of connected habitats has improved the design of marine protected areas (MPAs), has changed the
understanding of population management, and provided solid basis for ecosystem-based management of
the marine environment (Hidalgo et al., 2017). Despite recent interest in connectivity studies, few studies
address questions of connectivity patterns on a global scale. Global connectivity studies may reveal trends
and patterns that can improve the understanding of long-range oceanic transport and explain the ecology
of migrating populations. With oceanic surface circulation lasting up to multiple decades (J¨onsson and
Watson, 2016), global connectivity is not only explained by spatial distributions, but also by the timescales
of connectivity between regions, where connectivity time can be viewed upon as an alternative measure of
distance. Studying the transition time between ecological regions of the global ocean may contribute to the
understanding of migration of planktonic species and unwanted spread of anthropogenic litter, pollutants
or invasive species. Connectivity timescales of the global ocean is however currently largely unexplored.
1
==PAGE:12==
When studying the timing of connection between geographic areas, the particles released in an ocean
model will in theory eventually reach every other geographical point, if enough time is provided (J¨onsson
and Watson, 2016). For this reason, it is relevant to study particle transport from one area to another,
and use the minimum connectivity time to explain the timescales of connectivity. The main arguments
for studying minimum travel time rather than average travel time are that 1) only the minimum time of
connection can explain which areas that are connected faster than others, and 2) organisms with high
reproductive output, like phytoplankton and bacteria, is better described by the minimum travel time, as
their reproductive potential decreases with lower dispersion rates, and that only a few individuals are needed
to make a connection between two areas (J¨onsson and Watson, 2016).
Studying connectivity on a global scale has the advantage of showing the big picture of connections
between geographical areas of the world oceans, which is essential for understanding long-distance species
migration and transport of debris and pollutants. One of our major oceans, the Atlantic Ocean, is an almost
entirely enclosed ocean basin, with interaction with the other major oceans in the northern and southern
ends. There are numerous connectivity studies performed on a regional scale within the northern part of
the Atlantic Ocean, particularly in the region of the Caribbean Sea and north of the United Kingdom, but
in the southern hemisphere, the knowledge is limited (Bryan-Brown et al., 2017). Although there is a
small number of modeling studies addressing global connectivity patterns (e.g. J¨onsson and Watson, 2016;
Villarino et al., 2018; Wood et al., 2013; Froyland et al., 2014), no study has to my knowledge looked into
the relation between transport between ecological provinces on a global scale.
In this project, the connectivity of the Atlantic Ocean is studied by using an oceanic global circulation
model in combination with particle tracking tools. The project aims to quantify large-scale oceanic connec-
tivity, identify connectivity network patterns, and identify the timescales of travel of particles between the
biogeographical provinces of the Atlantic Ocean. This study will aim at explaining the eﬀect of global-scale
particle dispersal in the Atlantic Ocean from an ecological perspective, focused towards implications for
living organisms such as ﬁsh larvae, invasive species or phytoplankton communities, and for dispersal of
marine pollutants. I will attempt to answer the questions
1. How, and to what extent are the biogeographical provinces of the Atlantic Ocean connected?
2. What are the timescales of connectivity between biogeographical provinces in the Atlantic Ocean?

Chapter 2
Background
The following sections will provide background information on NEMO as a modelling tool, and a description
of the basis for partitioning of the Atlantic into ecological provinces.
2.1
Ocean dynamics
Before explaining the setup of the OGCM (oceanic general circulation model) used for simulating ocean
dynamics and particle trajectories, fundamental oceanographic physical processes and drivers of oceanic
circulation will be described. Following the principles of classical mechanics, the ocean can be seen as a
mass where the velocity of the body remains constant unless external forces acts on the body (Newton’s ﬁrst
law of motion). Oceanic circulation is forced by a number of processes, where the more dominant forces are
driven by the earth’s gravitational force, the spin of the earth, and the properties of seawater (Talley et al.,
2011). Essential forces driving the oceanic circulation such as density diﬀerences in the water masses or
wind forces are linked to forces generated by solar radiation and planetary rotation. Solar radiation aﬀects
the ocean through heating of water masses and the atmosphere, leading to water movement due to density
diﬀerences. In the following section, the eﬀect of some of major forces on ocean dynamics will be described,
based on information from Talley et al. (2011).
Driving forces
One of the major forces driving oceanic circulation is density driven circulation is caused by density diﬀerences
in seawater, caused primarily by changes in temperature and salinity. Temperature diﬀerences are linked to
changes in solar radiation in terms of direct heating of water masses, or indirectly by ice-melt causing inﬂow
of cold water. Salinity diﬀerences are primarily caused by evaporation and precipitation, or freshwater runoﬀ
from land or ice-melt. Density diﬀerences between water masses may generate convection, where lighter
density water rises and the heavier sinks. The vertical movement of water plays a key role in global scale
circulation. Another force that particularly aﬀects circulation in surface water is wind. When wind force
hits the ocean surface, surface tension lets the wind force act on the water, generating motion. Another
force aﬀecting ocean circulation is tidal forces, which inﬂuences ocean circulation as it creates ebbing and
ﬂowing of water. This process generates movement in the water, particularly in shallow water areas. Tides
are generated by gravitational forces primarily from the moon, but also from the sun, pulling the water in
their direction.

The driving forces acting on the ocean cause a highly dynamic, irregular and non-linear turbulence ﬁeld,
generating eddy structures of large consequences for transport of matter and energy in the ocean. The
motion of the ocean also generates oceanographic fronts that take place at sharp gradients in either tem-
perature, salinity or momentum, as for example between subtropical and subpolar gyres. The interference
of the water masses generates tension and turbulence, leading to downwelling and upwelling. Upwelling

subduction of water to deeper layers. When oceanic circulation interferes with the surrounding continents,

eastern boundaries, surface water may be dragged away from the coast, causing upwelling along the coast-
lines. With a basic understanding of the driving forces of the ocean, section 2.2 will explain the foundational
structure of the oceanic circulation model NEMO Ocean Engine.
The meridional overturning circulation
Changes in temperature and salinity drive the thermohaline circulation, also named the meridional over-
turning circulation (ﬁgure 2.1). A main force driving this circulation is deep water formation, which takes
place in polar regions (ﬁgure 2.1, yellow dots). The cold climate and ice formation producing cold and
highly saline water masses, causes the water to become dense and sink. The continuous process of deep
water formation in the Arctic forces the dense water to ﬂow southward through the Atlantic Ocean basin,
while warm surface currents are forced towards the north. From looking at the circulation patterns of the
thermohaline circulation, the expected general transport within the Atlantic Ocean, is a northward drift from
the southeastern Atlantic towards the equator, entering the Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico before continuing
north towards the Arctic. From north to south, the was transport in subsurface layers is expected to follow
the western coasts, before joining the circumpolar current of the Southern Ocean.

2.2.1
Primitive equations
The following section will go through the primitive equations of the NEMO model as they are presented
in Madec (2016). The fundamental equations describing ocean circulation in the NEMO model are based
on conservation laws for conservation of momentum and conservation of mass.
For the ﬂuid motion
calculations, Navier-Stokes equations describe the momentum conservation, and the equation of state
explains the conservation of mass.
To simplify the equations (and also reduce computing power), six
general assumptions are made, to create a foundation for the model equations:
1. Spherical earth approximation. Assuming that the earth is round lets the gravitational forces be
parallel to the earth’s radius.
2. Thin shell approximation. It is assumed that the ocean depth is negligible compared to the earth’s
radius, letting the ocean be a spherical shell, aﬀecting the deﬁnition the vertical dimension.
3. Turbulent closure hypothesis. Turbulent ﬂuxes are expressed by large scale features.
4. Boussinesq hypothesis. Assuming that density variation only is important in the terms related to
buoyancy, ρg, in the Navier-Stokes equations.
5. Hydrostatic hypothesis. The vertical momentum equation be expressed by the vertical pressure
gradient and buoyancy force.
6. Incompressibility hypothesis. The divergence of the three-dimensional velocity vector is assumed
to be zero.
The primitive equations in the NEMO model are deﬁned on the basis of the above assumptions. In the
following, the primitive equations are described (see ﬁgure 2.2 for an overview).

Parameterization of turbulence
While the primitive equations used in NEMO describe the ﬂuid behaviour at the scales of kilometres
in the horizontal plane, meters in the vertical plane and minutes in the temporal dimension, physical
dynamics generating energy ﬂuxes which inﬂuence large-scale dynamics may occur at smaller scales (down
to millimetres and seconds) which requires a ﬁne mesh to be resolved. The deviations in scales represents
a problem of closure of the primitive equation. To close the system, turbulence is generally parameterized
using a turbulent diﬀusion coeﬃcient multiplied by the current gradient:
∇KH∇U
(2.15)

some cases be formulated and solved, depending on the grid spacing. Meso-scale turbulence (<100 km
in diameter, and timescales less than a month (Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory, accessed 2018-
03-22) can be solved in some cases. Sub-mesoscale turbulence is however always parameterized. For the
vertical turbulence, the resolutions used in the NEMO model will not be suﬃciently ﬁne to compute vertical
turbulence, and the parameterization is always used.
2.2.2
Discretization
By discretizing the primitive equations, the change in mass and momentum ﬂuxes in space and time is
calculated in a step-wise manner. The concept of discretization is that the volume of the body where the
primitive equations are solved is split into boxes, where each box has incoming and outgoing ﬂuxes for each
parameter in three spatial dimensions (i, j, k), that also change over time (t). Figure 2.3 illustrates how
information is stored in each box/cell.
In ﬁgure 2.3, T represents all scalar variables, e.g. temperature, salinity or density. In ﬁgure 2.3, u, v,
and w represent velocity vector points in three directions. u, v, and w are seen at two places in the ﬁgure,
indicating velocity directed in and out of the cell. When a volume is split into cells like demonstrated in
ﬁgure 2.3, the properties of each cell (T, u, v, w) depend on the properties of the cell in the previous time
step, and the properties of the neighbouring cells, which determine the velocity ﬁeld and thereby in- and
out-ﬂow of ﬂuxes (e.g. heat and salt ﬂuxes). By structuring a mathematical problem this way, information
is transferred between neighbouring cells, and the spatial and temporal change in the model variables can
be computed.

2.2.4
Vertical grid
The vertical dimension can be discretized in several ways. The vertical coordinate options in NEMO are
based on two types of vertical coordinates: z-coordinates where the vertical domain is divided into layers
with a ﬁxed depth, and terrain-following coordinates, where the vertical layers are stretched to ﬁt with the
bathymetry. In NEMO, one can choose to work with hybrids of the two types. The following section is
based on information from Naval Postgraduate School (accessed 2018-02-18).
z-coordinate
The simplest way to deﬁne a vertical grid is using the z-coordinate, which divides the ocean into ﬁxed level
sections (ﬁgure 2.5, left). This division of the water column is straight forward and easy to work with,
but in areas with sloping topography, the z-coordinate have disadvantages in computing velocities near the
bottom, potentially causing unrealistic velocities along the surface boundary. In NEMO, the z-coordinate
can be split into full step or partial step, where partial step allows increased resolution of vertical steps to
increase the accuracy, e.g. around uneven or steep bathymetry.
s-coordinate
The s-coordinate (σ-coordinate) follows the slopes of the terrain with the same number of vertical grid
points at all water depths (ﬁgure 2.5, middle). The s-coordinate allows a ﬁner resolution at the bottom
and surface, which makes it suitable for coast- and shelf problems. It does however have problems with
sharp topographic changes between grid points and might produce unrealistic ﬂows as it does not take into
account the pressure changes along the grid steps.

2.3
Particle tracking methods
In order to estimate pathways in the ocean, one can either use and Eulerian framework where the changes
in velocity ﬁeld is seen with respect to a ﬁxed position, or the Lagrangian framework where changes in
velocity ﬁeld is observed from a point following the ﬂuid motion Talley et al. (2011). Lagrangian particle
tracking is the simulation of virtual particles within a ﬂuid, where the particle position changes in response
to changes in the velocity ﬁeld in Eulerian simulations, often computed by OGCMs (van Sebille et al., 2018).
Trajectories can be simulated either with oﬄine or online methods (van Sebille et al., 2018). For the oﬄine
10
==PAGE:21==
method, trajectory estimates are computed from stored samples of the velocity ﬁelds from a Eulerian model.
In online simulations, particle trajectories are simulated along with the Eulerian model, where the particle
position is updated for every new computation in the Eulerian model. Several models are available for
computation of Lagrangian trajectories both online and oﬄine, as summarized in van Sebille et al. (2018).
In this project, online Lagrangian particle tracking methods are used for computing trajectories with NEMO.
2.3.1
Particle tracking with NEMO
NEMO’s particle tracking module, key ﬂoats, allows online particle tracking, and was used to simulate
particle trajectories in this project. For numerical integration of particle trajectories, NEMO oﬀers a choice
between the commonly used Runge-Kutta 4th order method (RK4), and an algorithm developed by Blanke
and Raynaud (Madec, 2016). The second option, named Blanke’s scheme, has the advantage of being
consistent with the numerical solutions of the NEMO code. But, due to occasional issues with Blanke’s
scheme, and the conﬁdence in the performance of the RK4 method, RK4 was chosen for computation of
the particle trajectories. The particle tracking input ﬁle follows the Ariane convention, which is a tool for
computing oﬄine trajectories (Ariane accessed 2018-05-23).
Runge-Kutta 4th order
Numerical analysis with the Runge-Kutta method is based on the Euler method, where the change in
velocity in the three-dimensional velocity ﬁeld is discretized over time. Although based on the same velocity
ﬁeld, the RK4 method signiﬁcantly improves the numerical solution by combining the Euler method with
the mid-point method, where the velocity ﬁeld from the Euler velocity is sampled multiple times (4 times
in the RK4 case). Finally, the weighted average of these sub-samples is the velocity ﬁeld for the subsequent
time step. Compared to the Euler method, RK4 increases the precision of the particle trajectories with 4
orders of magnitude, computing smoother and more accurate representations of the particle trajectories.
The Runge-Kutta method can be used with further increased accuracy, but the RK4 is commonly used
because it is considered the best trade-oﬀbetween computational cost and precision (Rodriguez, accessed
2018-09-05).
2.4
Description of biogeographical provinces
In order to quantify connectivity, the area of interest must be divided into sub-regions. The classical way
of deﬁning locations for a connectivity study is to divide the area of interest into a grid of squared cells. In
order to work with a set of regions that are partitioned based on ecological properties, the area can be split
into ecological regions, where the location is deﬁned based on environmental characteristics rather than a
ﬁxed mesh (e.g. Reygondeau et al., 2013; Frank et al., 2016). The interest for separation the ocean into
ecological regions dates back to the late nineteenth century, but its development has been slow compared to
that of terrestrial systems, mainly because collection of data from the ocean is time-consuming and costly
(Longhurst, 2007). Among the many attempts of an ecological partitioning of the ocean (e.g. Semina,
1997; Spalding et al., 2012), a popular and well validated biogeographical separation of the global ocean
is described by Longhurst (2007). The Longhurst province deﬁnitions are based on long-term data sets on
temperature and salinity gradients, bathymetry and chlorophyll a. Separating the regions by ecological or
geochemical boundaries is particularly applicable in global scale connectivity studies where there are strong
gradients between the regions of the domain.
In the following sections, the twenty-two biogeographical provinces of the Atlantic Ocean will be de-
scribed, focusing on physical oceanography and productivity within each province. The provinces are deﬁned

ARCT
ARCT is one of the two provinces bordering to the Arctic Ocean, along with the Atlantic Subarctic Province
(SARC). The province covers the Greenland Coastal current in the west. ARCT is divided from other
provinces by a polar front, where Arctic and Atlantic water masses meet, resulting in cold (0-4◦C) and
saline (34.6-34.9 ppt) water.
ARCT includes the anticyclonic subpolar gyre south of Greenland.
The
ecology of ARCT is characterized by low diversity but a high productivity with a clear seasonal signal with
a summer bloom and winter mixing.
SARC
SARC is divided from ARCT by the oceanic polar front, and covers the Norwegian Current transporting
warm water masses towards the north. SARC is subject to unstable eddy ﬁelds related to a salinity front
where freshwater discharge from the Baltic Sea. Further oﬀshore, large scale eddies occur regularly. The
SARC ecosystems are highly diverse, and controlled by the seasonality in light climate with production
peaks in summer and autumn.
NWCS
NWCS stretches from a cold-climate to a subtropical region, and as a consequence, the province covers
a great variety of habitats. NWCS is also one of the more productive regions of the ocean. The strong
climatic gradient also promotes diﬀerent types of biological growth. The regions within NWCS have two
things in common: the seasonal cycles in productivity with a signiﬁcant spring bloom, and the equatorward
current, transporting low-salinity Arctic water along the shelf of the American continent. Depending on
the state of the Gulf Stream, warm water may ﬂow onto the shelf. In the southern NWCS, the mixing of
warm tropical water causes a nutrient ﬂux onto the shelf. The province is aﬀected by the North Atlantic
Oscillation (NAO) where a low NAO index allows great volumes of water from the Labrador Current to
ﬂow south, pushing the Gulf Stream southward and further oﬀshore. The cold Labrador water also greatly
aﬀect stratiﬁcation dynamics by the melt of pack-ice.
NADR
The North Atlantic Drift province, NADR, covers the region of transport of warm Atlantic water from west
to east, and is deﬁned as the zonal ﬂow between SARC in the north and NAST in the south. In northwest,
the polar front of ARCT makes a strong climatic divide. The North Atlantic Drift originates where the Gulf
Stream divides, and mesoscale eddies from the Gulf Stream remain in the drift. In autumn, wind speeds
within NADR are among the highest on the globe, deepening of the mixed layer. The North Atlantic spring

NECS
NECS, covers large parts of the shelf regions connected to the European continent. NECS is characterized
by its geology, where glacial deposits have formed the landscape with shallow banks and deep basins. NECS
consists of multiple sub-regions. Characteristics that are general for the entire regions is the inﬂuence by

freshwater input from the Baltic Sea. The water transport of the province has in general a weak poleward
direction of the drift. Locally, the shelf water transport is wind driven. NECS is inﬂuenced by the interannual
variability of the North Atlantic Oscillation. NECS is strongly regulated by four ecological seasons including
a high biomass spring bloom, a summer stratiﬁcation, autumn breakdown of stratiﬁcation and a light limited
winter.
GFST
The northern border of GFST is where the warm western boundary jet current, the Gulf Stream, lets go
of the shelf and enters the cold water from the southward ﬂowing Labrador Current. This region is highly
dynamic because of the jet intensity and the strong horizontal temperature gradient, which in winter goes
from 18◦C in the south to pack ice only 300-400 km north. The ecology of the Gulf Stream water and
habitat is similar to the water of the Sargasso Sea. Rings of cold-core eddies contain biota much like the
American slope water, and the primary production within these rings are higher than in the surrounding
water. The seasonal signal in GFST is weak, but the biomass is generally higher in spring and summer.
There is a clear nutrient limitation in the GFST province, and the production is limited by light in the winter
months.
NAST
The provinces NAST(E) and NAST(W) together cover the area of the Subtropical Gyre system of the
North Atlantic, south of the eddy ﬁeld of the Gulf Stream. The southern border is set to the Subtropical
Convergence (STC) at 25-30◦N at the 20◦C surface isotherm and where the signiﬁcant mixing ends. The
NAST provinces occasionally receive cold-core eddies transported from the GFST province, carrying slope
water biota. Throughout NAST, the numerous seamounts elevated from the otherwise deep ocean ﬂoor can
produce warm-core cyclonic eddies or Taylor columns, controlling patch formation of chlorophyll and driving
upwelling processes. NAST(W) and NAST(E) are split in western and eastern parts of the basin at the
Mid Atlantic Ridge, that causes the water in the western part of NAST to recirculate in the western part of
the basin, causing the water masses of NAST(W) and NAST(E) diﬀerent characteristics. In NAST(W), a
unique feature is the abundance of the characteristic Sargasso seaweed, (Sargassum), making the province
biologically and ecologically distinguishable from its neighbours. NAST(E) comprises parts of the calm
Canary Current, where winter mixing does not penetrate as deep. Tidal mixing contributes to vertical
exchange of water, as well as circulating eddies, driving upwelling and downwelling processes. Lower wind
stress reduces the depth of the winter mixing in the entire NAST. The production in NAST is nutrient
limited and peaks during winter and spring. There is high abundance of pelagic ﬁsh within the entire NAST
region, but NAST(E) is generally more productive than NAST(W).

NATR
The NATR province covers the southern part of the North Atlantic Gyre, south of the STC. In the south,
a salinity gradient sets the zonal border between the saline NATR water (>37 ppt) and the less saline
equatorial water of WTRA (<36.25 ppt). NATR is located below the trade-wind belt of the North Atlantic,
and the ﬂow within the province is therefore mainly wind driven down to 200 m depth. Satellite images of
indicate low chlorophyll levels in NATR. In summer, deep mixing combined with a constant irradiation might
enhance primary production slightly. Nevertheless, major parts of the province appear to be oligotrophic
throughout the year.
CARB
CARB includes the two basins of the Caribbean Sea and the Gulf of Mexico. Although the two basins
might be viewed upon as two individual provinces, the circulation between them is highly integrated. The
circulation in CARB is mainly driven by trade winds, that generally are stronger in boreal spring and summer,
occasionally developing into hurricanes mixing the water column. CARB is characterized by mesoscale eddy
activity, and the circulation is complex because of the varying topography within the archipelago and the
many sources of surface water. In periods where the Brazil Current retroﬂection transports water into
CARB, the highly turbid water inﬂuenced by the Amazon delta carries algal biomass into the province.
Several regions in CARB has permanently high levels of chlorophyll, and the seasonality of the region is
weak. Nutrient upwelling is seen in particular in the Mexico Gulf where oceanic inﬂow brings nutrient rich
water to the surface layer and the photic zone. Further, eddies and loops within the gulf contributes to
upwelling and mixing of the in-ﬂowing water. The nutrient rich water of the CARB region does not only
enhance primary production, but also growth of marine organisms up to the highest trophic levels, like
whales and other cetaceans.
CNRY
CNRY borders the convergence zone where the Canary Current converges with the anticyclonic wind stress
about 200-400 km from the coast. There is a wind driven vertical nutrient transport enhancing the pro-
ductivity of the coastline. Generally, CNRY is characterized by upwelling all the way from north to south,
stronger in seasons with strong winds. CNRY is located by the Sahara desert, which provides CNRY with
mineral rich dust, providing the province with iron. With no iron-limitation, upwelling of nutrients and an
intensive light climate, the CNRY province has a high potential of primary production.
WTRA
The region of the Western Tropical Atlantic, WTRA, is a highly dynamic region with a complex surface
circulation. The northern boundary of WTRA is at the Subtropical Convergence (STC) and the southern
part of the North Equatorial Current (NEC). The dynamics of WTRA are driven by trade winds and by
the change of sign of the Coriolis acceleration at the equator. WTRA covers large parts of the equatorial
current system including the North and South Equatorial Currents (NEC, SEC) ﬂowing towards the west
and the North Equatorial Counter Current (NECC) and the Equatorial Under Current (EUC) ﬂowing in
the eastward direction, causing vertical mixing and upwelling from shear stress, inﬂuencing the ecology in
WTRA. The eﬀect of tropical instability waves (TIWs) on sea surface temperature (SST) is mirrored in
chlorophyll concentrations, resulting in a highly dynamic primary production.

GUIN
GUIN is characterized by upwelling of nutrients along the coast, as well as multiple rivers (including Niger and
Congo), releasing fresh, turbid water onto the shelf. GUIN alternates between productive and oligotrophic
scenarios. The seasonality in GUIN is aﬀected by dry and wet seasons along the Guinea coast, where heavy
rainfall during wet seasons may strongly reduce the salinity of the surface water. Seasonal cloud cover
might restrict the incoming light in the period April to September. The subsequent dry season evaporates
water and creates a steep pycnocline below the shallow mixed layer. GUIN receives upwelling cells mainly
during the boreal summer period, and also receives chlorophyll by the Canary Current.
ETRA
ETRA covers the oceanic part of the Gulf of Guinea. ETRA is almost symmetrical around the equator,
and therefore it includes many of the equatorial processes mentioned in the section on the WTRA province.
Seasonality in temperature gradients is highly inﬂuenced by the trade winds. Similar to WTRA, ETRA
sees a complex thermocline around equator where the Coriolis acceleration changes sign. The seasonal
alternation of leads to a change in signs of the vertical motion of the water ﬂuxes, generating motion and
mixing within the ETRA. Ekman pumping is causing upwelling of nutrients in the region of the Angola
dome within ETRA, enhancing growth of algal biomass. The velocity of the mass transport varies with
the index of the North Atlantic Oscillation, aﬀecting the thermocline and thereby the productivity of the
province. The primary production is more or less constant through the seasons.
BENG
BENG is restricted to the upwelling eddies of the Benguela Current ﬂowing on the south African wide
and deep shelf.
The anticyclonic circulation of the South Atlantic transports water into the Benguela
current that is carried by jet currents northward along the African continent before it again joins the gyral
circulation. The Agulhas retroﬂection occasionally pinches oﬀrings that might enter the Benguela system,
but the frequency of such event is highly variable between decades. The intersection of warm tropical
surface water from the north and cool water from the south generates turbulent mixing where the Angola
and Benguela currents meet. Upwelling cells occur near the coast when winds from African deserts blow
strongly.
SATL
The South Atlantic Gyral Province, SATL, is deﬁned by the anticyclonic motion of the large gyre system
of the South Atlantic, bordering to the eddy ﬁelds of the boundary currents along the Brazil and Bengali
coasts. The South Atlantic is not fully enclosed as the North Atlantic, and interaction with the retroﬂecting
Agulhas Current greatly inﬂuences the dynamics of the South Atlantic. The gyre of the South Atlantic is
not centered, but with a southwest position within the ocean basin. There are indications of a nutrient
limited primary production in SATL, seen in satellite imagery, with low chlorophyll levels towards the centre
of the gyre. At latitudes associated with the South Subtropical Convergence Zone, the frontal dynamics
result in permanently high chlorophyll levels. At this latitude, there is a clear seasonal signal.
GUIA
GUIA covers the wide continental shelf of northern South America. GUIA includes the North Brazil Current
(NBC) which greatly aﬀects the dynamics of the province by its warm and shallow northward ﬂow. The
province has a seasonal character, inﬂuenced by coastal eddies during winter, and a surge during summer,
16
==PAGE:27==
transporting large volumes of water northwards.
The GUIA province ecosystems are inﬂuenced by the
coastal upwelling, but the eﬄuent of the Amazon and Orinoco rivers determines the chlorophyll biomass.
Despite the high levels of nitrate and cDOM (colored dissolved organic matter) in inshore ecosystems, high
turbidity restricts the growth of phytoplankton by blocking the incoming irradiance. In contrast, ecosystems
seaward of the chlorophyll plume are limited by nutrients, restricting primary production. In between these
two extremes, a linear ecological zone accumulates chlorophyll, where the main growth limiting factor is
light.
BRAZ
In BRAZ, the seaward boundary of BRAZ remains narrow and close to the coast with a width of only 10
km in the north. BRAZ has a varied topography, characterized by submarine canyons and valleys. Where
the northward Falkland current encounters the Brazil Current, and important region of conﬂuence takes
place, generating motion aﬀecting the dynamics of BRAZ. BRAZ has a clear seasonal signal with large mass
transport in the austral summer, but with limited ﬂow during winter caused by changes in the wind ﬁeld.
BRAZ is not a major upwelling region, but decrease in wind speed in the region allows rapid production by
primary producers, which is quickly grazed down by herbivores. In the BRAZ province, chlorophyll levels
are only of signiﬁcance close to the coast, with a peak during austral summer, resembling that of NECS.
SSTC
SSTC covers the subtropical convergence zone circulating around the Antarctic continent. The province is
deﬁned by fronts in the north the south, both associated with the surface water properties of the subtropical
gyre of the Atlantic Ocean (and the other world oceans at other longitudes). The location of the zone is
quite predictable, but the ﬂow of the zone is meandering, and is dynamically shifting. Despite the nutrient
limitation within the province, the primary production is high during periods of the intense light climate in
the period June to November.
FKLD
FKLD is located in a region of subtropical convergence, generating energetic eddies, depending on local
wind force. The seaward boundary of FKLD is set around the wide Falkland plateau, and the southern end of
the FKLD province has a fjord-like topography, with complex circulation systems. The province is generally
inﬂuenced by tidal mixing the whole year through, and by seasonal cycles similar to the processes seen in
NECS, with summer stratiﬁcation and winter mixing. The cold and nutrient-rich subantarctic water carried
by the Falkland Current is a key feature enhancing and controlling the distribution of the primary production
in the FKLD province, and shelf-edge upwelling also contributes to the productivity of the province.
SANT and ANTA
SANT and ANTA are mainly related to the Southern Ocean and the circumpolar current around Antarctica
ﬂowing from west to east. As will be seen later in the analysis of the mass transport between regions,
SANT and ANTA are in minimal connection to the other provinces of the Atlantic Ocean. There is also a
strong divide between the Southern Ocean and the adjacent ocean basins both ecologically, chemically and
physically. Nevertheless, it has been included in the project not to exclude potential sources or receivers of
particles, given the geographically long boundaries with SSTC and SATL.

Materials and methods
In the following section, the materials and methods of the project will be described. The materials have

3.1
The gyre conﬁguration
Before working with a large global scale model, the ﬁrst step of the project was to learn how to use NEMO:
from software installation and compilation, to building conﬁgurations and running simulations through the
DTU high performance computing centre (DTU HPCC). Important parts of the work with the test case
was to learn to control the model input through editing model namelists and XML ﬁles, and to learn how to
handle NEMO input and output, troubleshoot issues with NEMO, and to practise visualization in Matlab.
The gyre conﬁguration is a comparatively simple conﬁguration, with a small domain size and short run
time, and is therefore ideal to be used as a test case. After installing and compiling NEMO (procedures
can be found in appendix A.1), test runs were run with the gyre conﬁguration, as will be described in the
following sections.
A gyre is a wind driven circulating current system. The gyre conﬁguration is built to simulate seasonal
patterns in a box model based on synthetic wind ﬁelds, representing gyre systems in an idealized North
Atlantic or North Paciﬁc basin. The conﬁguration applies seasonal forcing and is therefore ideal for studying
mesoscale eddies and how these aﬀect large scale circulation and seasonal patterns in major ocean gyres.
The initial condition in the basin is at rest (zero velocity), with an analytical temperature proﬁle, and with
constant salinity. In the model conﬁguration, the sign of the zonal wind stress changes sign at 22◦N and
36◦N, forcing a subtropical gyre in the south and a sub-polar gyre in the north. The seasonal oscillation in
parameters included in the conﬁguration, for example solar radiation and precipitation, is also included in
the conﬁguration.

==PAGE:29==
Spatial and temporal domain
The space domain in the gyre conﬁguration is a 3180 × 2120 km rectangular basin, centred at 30◦N with
a 45◦rotation, stretching from 15◦N to 50◦N and -85◦E to -50◦E. The default grid resolution is 1◦(106
km), which corresponds to a grid with 32 points in the ﬁrst lateral direction (longitude) and 22 points in
the second lateral direction (latitude). The depth of the basin is 4 km. The vertical resolution is set to
30 steps, with a full-step z-coordinate (see section 2.2.4), resulting in a vertical step of ≃133 m. The
domain boundary is deﬁned as a closed boundary with vertical walls and a ﬂat bottom. The default time
resolution in the conﬁguration is 2 hours (7200 seconds). The conﬁguration is set to run the simulation for
4320 time steps by default, which results in 1 year (4320 × 7200 s ≃11.8 months). To be able to study
the abilities and performance of the NEMO model at diﬀerent spatial resolutions, both the horizontal and
temporal resolutions will be modiﬁed, as will be explained later in this section.
Figure 3.1: The computational domain of the gyre conﬁguration. Each dot represents the center position
of a grid cell in i and j directions.
Mesh
The choice of mesh in the namelist parameter jphgr msh determines which of the ﬁve available mesh types
that is used. In the gyre conﬁguration, the mesh type is deﬁned as a 45◦rotated beta-plane. The plane is
rotated 45◦counterclockwise in order to make the domain cover the largest possible area of the simulated
jets, which tend to be rotated from the orientation of longitude and latitude coordinates. The beta-plane
approximation simpliﬁes the calculations related to the Coriolis force: Instead of letting the Coriolis force
depend on latitude, the mean force of the domain is used across the domain. The rotation geographical
position of the mesh points are illustrated in ﬁgure 3.1.

Boundary conditions
The model boundaries occur between all interfaces between the modelled waterbody and its surroundings,
including land-ocean, solid earth-ocean, atmosphere-ocean and sea ice-ocean interfaces. The boundary
conditions play a key role in determining the ﬂow within the domain, as it deﬁnes the ﬂow in and out of
all grid cells along the edges of the domain. The horizontal boundaries can be deﬁned to be either open,
letting velocity, heat or mass ﬂow in and out of the system, or closed, which makes the ﬂow ﬁeld act as
within a sealed box. The gyre conﬁguration boundaries are deﬁned as closed boundaries in both the surface,
bottom and side walls, which makes the gyre system act as within a sealed container. The boundaries can
however be deﬁned with open boundaries allowing ﬂuxes of momentum, mass and heat.
Initial conditions
By default, NEMO is initialized at rest, meaning that at t = t0 there will be zero velocity in the entire
domain. The velocity dynamics are forced by wind velocity input ﬁles, and the initialization of variables
can be controlled by input ﬁles through a namelist parameter. By default, temperature is initialized from
an analytical proﬁle, and salinity is initialized at 35.5 psu.
3.1.1
Temporal stabilization of the gyre conﬁguration
Starting from rest, the gyre system will still be in a transient state after 1 year of simulation. To be able to
analyze the system, it will be run until a steady state is reached, simply by increasing the number of time
steps. The simulation speciﬁcations of the conﬁguration are modiﬁed by editing namelists (explained in
appendix A.3). After running simulations with step-wise increasing time spans, it was concluded that the
system has reached a steady state when the simulation runs for about 30 years. This was concluded from
looking at velocity-, temperature- and salinity output from a 50-year simulation. On the basis of the ocean
dynamics described in section 2.1, running test examples with the idealized case, the gyre conﬁguration,
the output from the model is expected to return certain characteristics. Given that the domain has a
closed boundary and is located in the northern hemisphere, a circulating motion with a clockwise rotation is
expected. The seasonal ﬂuctuation in solar radiation is likely to result in seasonal cycling in both momentum
and tracers. Seasonality is also expected seen in water temperatures, and possibly also slightly for salinity,
due to seasonality in evaporation and precipitation. To assess the validity of the gyre conﬁguration test
runs, the parameters velocity, temperature and salinity are compared to the expectations to the output.
Summarized, these expectations are:
1. temperature and salinity in steady state
2. seasonal variations in temperature and salinity
3. steady state velocities (u, v) possibly with seasonal ﬂuctuation
4. circular ﬂow-patterns, due to the formation of a gyre
From the results it was found that the potential temperature in the upper 130 m reaches a steady state
after 10 years (ﬁgure 3.2, top), and has a clear seasonal pattern (ﬁgure 3.2, bottom). When plotting the
annual change temperature, it follows a clear 12-month cycle, which stabilizes with an average temperature
peak of 21◦C during summer, and a winter average down to 1◦C. In ﬁgure 3.3, the temperature forcings
in the gyre conﬁguration is compared to the simulated temperature. Here it is seen that the temperature
values derived by the model are within the applied temperature forcings in winter and summer, at latitudes
across the domain.

DTU HPCC benchmark
NEMO can be parallelized, which means decomposing the domain and running sub-domains on multiple
computers with MPI (Message Passing Interface).
With high performance computers (HPC) available
through DTU’s HPC centre, the MPI functionality is used for running NEMO with massively parallel
processing (MPP), allowing fast computations. The HPC cluster lets the users occupy up to 100 cores per
job and 8/20 processes per node, and oﬀers unlimited space for temporary storage. As seen in table 3.1,
the number of points to compute quickly becomes high when increasing the simulation resolution, which
is reﬂected by the computing time, which is more than four times higher when the resolution is increased
from 1◦to 1/2◦and is forty times higher when increasing from 1◦to 1/4◦. By increasing the number of
processors and nodes, the run time will be reduced until the number of processors becomes so high that
the computational cost from communication between processors slows down the computation. To set a
benchmark for the DTU HPC cluster, the R05 simulation was run for a range of processors (from 2 to
24). Attempts of running the R05 simulation failed with more than 24 processors, most likely because of a
number to processors disproportionate to the number of points in the computation. The result from this
test is seen in ﬁgure 3.8.

The test of the HPC-cluster reveals that for a simulation with a number of points in the order of 109, the
simulation is the most eﬃcient when ran on around 20 processors. It is seen that increasing the number

3.2
Setup of the global conﬁguration
3.2.1
The global conﬁguration, ORCA
The NEMO OPA ORCA conﬁguration is used to simulate ocean physics and particle trajectories. The
ORCA2 conﬁguration is a global conﬁguration with a 2◦horizontal resolution, which will be used for the
global simulations in this project. As described in section 2.1, acting forces such as solar radiation and
freshwater input drives the dynamics of the ocean, resulting in physical processes on a wide range of scales.
The coarse grid of the conﬁguration (2◦×2◦) is not suﬃciently ﬁne for resolving small scale physics, and all
eddy coeﬃcients are therefore parameterized. In ORCA2, the coeﬃcient for eddy viscosity (friction induced
by transfer of momentum from turbulence) changes with latitude, with a lower eddy viscosity near equator
(except from along western boundaries). The eddy diﬀusivity of tracers (mixing of the tracers due to eddy
motion) is deﬁned to act along isopycnal surfaces (layers of constant density). Eddy-induced velocity is also
parameterized, and depends on the changes in baroclinic instability (misallignment of pressure and density
gradients). By default, the solid boundaries along the coastline transfers no ﬂux of either salt or heat.
Neither does the boundary along the ocean bottom. Along continental shelves however, a linear fraction is
applied to the momentum to account for frictional forces from the bathymetry. The vertical coordinate is
a partial-step z-coordinate (explained in section 2.2.4).
ORCA tripolar grid
A unique feature of NEMO’s global conﬁguration, ORCA, is that it is built with a tripolar grid, improving
the global scale computational mesh. In the northern hemisphere, the geographical north pole is exchanged
with two poles, both placed on land. This means that all three poles are situated outside of the ocean
basins, resulting in the beneﬁt of one continuous mesh across the entire domain of the global ocean Madec,
2016 (see ﬁgure 3.10). In contrast, regular geographical mesh-grids create a point of singularity at the north
pole, which causes implications with model computations. The tripolar mesh is applied from a latitude of
20◦N, allowing the more southern equatorial dynamics to be computed in a regular meshgrid, for a better
numerical solution (Madec, 2016). It should be noted that the mesh size in the equatorial region is ﬁner
than for other latitudes. Also, the discrepancy between the grid and the real geographical location results in
a mean grid spacing of 1.3◦(NEMO Web Page accessed 2018-07-23), but the horizontal resolution is still
described as a 2◦resolution. It should be mentioned that NEMO allows grids with considerably ﬁner mesh
sizes than 2◦, but that the 2◦resolution was in this project chosen because of the match with available
physical input ﬁles.
3.2.2
The ORCA2-LIM conﬁguration
The ORCA2-LIM conﬁguration is a global conﬁguration (ORCA) coupled with a sea-ice model (LIM),
including interaction with ice and melting icebergs.
Domain deﬁnition
The ORCA2-LIM conﬁguration has 31 vertical levels from the surface to 5000 metres depth, where the
upper layers have a ﬁner vertical grid spacing than the deeper levels. The vertical coordinate is a partial-
step z-coordinate (explained in section 2.2.4). The conﬁguration time step depends on the resolution of the
spatial domain. For the ORCA2-LIM conﬁguration, the 2◦spatial resolution allows a computational time
step of 96 minutes.

Building the conﬁguration
The ORCA2-LIM conﬁguration acts as the base of the trajectory simulation conﬁguration that is built. Two

key ﬂoats is added to activate the particle tracking algorithm, and key xios2 is added to allow use of XML
ﬁles to control the model output. Other keys activate computational solutions for forces such as geothermal
heating, mixing coeﬃcients and eddy-induced velocity, as well as computational solutions. A list of all the
active keys in ORCA FLOATS and their functionality can be found in appendix A.5. The method of building
the global conﬁguration is similar to the method of building the gyre case conﬁguration (see appendix A.2).

In this case, the new conﬁguration is named ORCA FLOATS. When activating key xios2, the model requires
input of XML ﬁles compatible with the correct version of XIOS, XIOS 2. These ﬁles can be copied from

restart ﬁles are created from the last time step of a simulation.
When starting from restart ﬁles, the
simulation continues from where the simulation before was ended. Using restart ﬁles as input in a new
simulation, requires that restart ﬁles from previous simulations has been written (deﬁned in the namelist),
and subsequently given the right ﬁle name.
When computing with massively parallel processing (MPP), one restart ﬁle is created for each number
of processors used. Before these restart ﬁles can be used, the related ﬁles need to be merged into one
single ﬁle. NEMO has created a tool speciﬁcally for merging output ﬁles computed on multiple processors,
named rebuild nemo, found in the tools directory. The rebuild tool can be used for concatenating any type
of NetCDF output ﬁle which is divided into multiple ﬁles when simulating with MPP (like for example
the mesh mask). The process of building the rebuild tool is explained in appendix A.6. This method is
used when running particle tracking simulations from diﬀerent times of the year, and when initializing the
physical ﬁles for all simulations.

3.3
Setup of the connectivity computations
Before particle trajectories can be simulated, an input ﬁle that deﬁnes the initial position of the released
particles must be created. To achieve optimal results, the particles will be released within the borders of
the biogeographical provinces, and within the borders of the Atlantic Ocean coastlines. This section will
describe how the biogeographical provinces have been deﬁned, followed by how the particle tracking input
ﬁle is generated.
3.3.1
Deﬁning areas of biogeographical provinces
Twenty-two biogeographical provinces was deﬁned within the region of the Atlantic Ocean, from the Arctic
to Antarctic regions (ﬁgure 2.6). The provinces are based on the deﬁned regions described in section 2.4.
The provinces within the Atlantic Ocean stretches from where the Atlantic Ocean meets the Arctic Ocean
in the north to the Southern Ocean in the south. The eastern and western borders are primarily deﬁned by
land boundaries. The two exceptions are in the southern part of the domain where the province boundaries
are drawn across the Drake Passage and the Agulhas Basin (see ﬁgure 2.6). The provinces SST, SANT and
ANTA are by Longhurst (2007) deﬁned to be circular around the Antarctic continent, but is here restricted
to the Atlantic part only. The twenty-two provinces exclude only the truly polar biomes bordering to the
Atlantic Ocean.
Deﬁning province coordinates
The coordinates of each province have been retrieved from a combination from the map from Longhurst
2007 and the Webmap available through Matlab (Esri ArcGIS Online). Since the provinces are not strictly
deﬁned by geographical coordinates in nature, and since their borders most likely change between seasons
and years, the province map deﬁning the provinces used for this project is a simpliﬁed resemblance of
the map by Longhurst (appendix B.2). Each province has been deﬁned as a polygon where the shape
and position of the polygon nodes have been conﬁrmed by comparing the Longhurst map to geographical
coordinates from the Matlab Webmap. Longhurst provided valuable descriptions of oceanographic features
and their approximate positioning in relation to land boundaries. With the approximate location of the
relevant node, the Webmap was used to retrieve the geographical coordinates (longitude and latitude) for
each point. For simpliﬁcation, and, due to uncertainties, most coordinates were rounded to the nearest
degree. The ﬁnal deﬁnition of the provinces are depicted in ﬁgure 2.6. A list of the coordinates for every
province polygon is attached in appendix B.1.
3.3.2
Generating the particle initialization ﬁle
The initialization ﬁle, named init ﬂoat ariane, is generated according to the description of the ariane tool
convention in the NEMO OPA manual (Madec, 2016). Input in the format of Ariane requires horizontal

contains the start position of a given number of particles in three dimensions, where steps in the i and j
directions represent the longitudinal (west to east) and latitudinal (south to north) dimensions respectively.
The vertical coordinate is set to a depth of 1.5 m for all particles, in the surface layer of the simulation
(0-10 m depth). The ﬁle will be structured as a ﬁle with ﬁve columns, where columns 1-3 contain indices
in i, j and k directions, column 4 is set to 1 for the ﬂoat to follow the w-velocity, and column 5 is a dummy

3.4
Output processing
All physical input and output ﬁles are in the format of NetCDF ﬁles, common in meteorology and oceanog-

that are not in the format of NetCDF are those related to the particle trajectories, which are written in
ASCII format. For processing the ocean physics output, Matlab has been used for visualizations, and NCO
(NetCDF Operators) for extracting the information of interest from ﬁles too large to handle by Matlab. For
particle trajectory data, Matlab has been used for data processing and visualizations. In order to transform
the information from the particle trajectories into illustrations of minimum time of connectivity between
Atlantic Ocean provinces, Matlab functions for reading output ﬁles and computing connectivity probabilities
and minimum times from particle trajectory ﬁles were developed.
3.4.1
Calculation of connectivity probability
A common method for calculating connectivity probability is to compare the transport of particles between
two areas at the ﬁrst and last time step (e.g. Rossi et al., 2014). Given the large size of the areas and
long time span of the simulations in this study, comparing particle transfer only between the ﬁrst and last
time steps would lead to a low number of connections, and possible links between regions occurring at
any other time step might be overlooked. If the particles are to represent living organisms that may settle
at any given point in space or time, a more detailed representation of the connectivity probability will be
appropriate. For this reason, the average connectivity probability between every province combination is
used to represent the connectivity probability. The calculation of connectivity probability is described in the
following.
Let A and B represent two province areas, and t0 . . . tend represent all time steps of the simulation.
Area A contains n particles at time t0. The fraction of the n particles in A at t0 that are positioned within
B at t1 will represent the probability of particle transfer from A to B in the time interval t0 −t1. The
calculation is repeated for all combinations of the Atlantic Ocean provinces, resulting in a 22 × 22 matrix
where each matrix cell represents the connectivity probability from one province to another, at the ﬁrst time
step. This procedure is repeated for particle transfers at all time steps, calculating connectivity probabilities
between A at t0 and B at t1, t2 . . . tend for all province combinations. The ﬁnal connectivity probability
is calculated by ﬁnding the average probability for each province combination across all time steps. This
results in a single 22 × 22 matrix which represents the probability of particle transfer in the given time
period. As an example, the simulated 5-year trajectories returned data for 365 time steps. In this case,
the described procedure ﬁrst generated 365 connectivity probability matrices (22 × 22), one for each time
step. The ﬁnal matrix of connectivity probability (22 × 22) contained the average probabilities of the entire
5-year period. (The Matlab script for the described calculation can be seen in appendix C, cmdraw.m.)
3.4.2
Description of output processing functions
Tools for processing and visualization of the trajectory data ﬁles have been designed and constructed in
order to transform the simulated trajectories into data-sets and ﬁgures that can be used for the analysis of
connectivity and connectivity times. The overall routine of the output processing is as follows: The ﬁrst
step of processing the trajectory simulations is to read the output ﬁle, trajec ﬂoat, into Matlab. This is done
using the function ﬂoat reader.m, which writes the input into a structure, which contains all the raw data.
These data are organized and prepared for connectivity analysis by running the data through the sorting
function (cmsort.m) producing a connectivity matrices that subsequently can be used for visualization by e.g.
cmdraw.m and mintdraw.m. In this section, the constructed functions will be described and demonstrated.
All functions are written in Matlab. For details in the code, scripts are attached in appendix C.
38

Reading raw data - ﬂoat reader.m
The function ﬂoat reader.m reads the NEMO particle trajectory output ﬁle, trajec ﬂoat, writing the data
into a structure array where all particle variables are stored at time steps t0 to tend. The variables stored
are all of what the raw ﬁle contains: longitude, latitude, depth, nis (for isobar ﬂoat), particle ID number,
temperature, salinity, and the date of the write of particle position.
Sorting trajectory data - cmsort.m
The cmsort.m function is designed to organize the particle trajectory data into a structure used in con-
nectivity analyses. The function labels every particle with a unique ID, sorts the position of each particle,
and identiﬁes which province area the particles are in at every time step. Knowing the position of the
province borderlines and the ID and position of every particle at all time steps, the movement of particles
between provinces, the connection between the provinces, and the time of connection can be extracted.
The function requires an input ﬁle in the format written by ﬂoat reader.m. As output, cmsort.m returns a
structure named T, where the particles within the provinces are stored at time steps t0 to tend.
Drawing the connectivity probability matrix - cmdraw.m
The cmdraw.m function determines the probability of connectivity between each province combination as
described in section 3.4.1. The input to the function is the structure array, T, deﬁned by the function
cmsort.m. The output is a matrix containing the probability of connectivity between all combinations of
provinces, and a ﬁgure representing the connectivity matrix values. The cmdraw.m function bases the
connectivity matrix ﬁgure on all the simulated trajectories, including particle trajectories that extend to
outside of the domain. For this reason, certain connections may show low connectivity probabilities to the
Atlantic Ocean provinces, and rather be more connected to the neighbouring ocean basins, such as the
Indian Ocean and the Atlantic Ocean. (A demonstration of cmdraw.m can be seen in appendix C.2.)
Drawing the minimum connectivity time matrix - mintdraw.m
The minimum time of connectivity is deﬁned as the shortest time it takes for a particle to move from
one area to another. The minimum time of connectivity is found by the following procedure: The particle
content of every province combination is compared once for every simulation time step. If a particle within
area A at time t0 is positioned within area B at t > t0, the relevant time step of this event represents
the minimum connectivity time between area A and B. The minimum time of connection is written into
the data set only if the connection is the ﬁrst between the relevant provinces. The input to the function is
the structure T deﬁned by cmsort.m. The function mintdraw.m identiﬁes the shortest travel time between
provinces as explained above, and draws a matrix representing the minimum time of connection between
all province combinations. (A demonstration of mintdraw.m can be seen in appendix C.2.)
An overview of other visualization tools can be seen in appendix C.

each cell represents the average connectivity over a period of 5-years (as explained in section 3.4.1). A
strong characteristic of the connectivity probability ﬁgure is the cluster of connections from the southern and
equatorial regions (CARB, WTRA, GUIN, ETRA, SATL, GUIA) to provinces in the northern hemisphere
(SARC, NADR, GFST, NAST(W), NAST(W), NATR, CARB). The lack of connections in the reverse
direction clearly illustrates the transport from the equatorial region to the northern hemisphere.
The
abundance of connections in the left side of ﬁgure 4.2 shows that there are many provinces functioning
as sources for the North Atlantic, seen from the cluster of connections in the lower left side of ﬁgure 4.2.
Another distinct pattern is the upper left part of ﬁgure 4.2, where there is a high number of connections
from northerly provinces (ARCT, SARC, NWCS) towards provinces related to the North Atlantic gyre
(NAST(E) in particular).
These regions receive particles from the entire Atlantic, north of the SSTC
province boundary and BENG and BRAZ. In the southern hemisphere, SATL is destination for particles
from the entire southern hemisphere. The matrix of connectivity probability shows a clear asymmetry of
the connectivity in the Atlantic, favouring particle transport towards the northern hemisphere. Out of the
484 possible combinations, 123 connections were made between the 22 provinces in the Atlantic Ocean,
within a period of 5 years. (The values from the connectivity matrix can be seen in appendix D.1.)
Source provinces
By looking at the connectivity probability matrix (ﬁgure 4.2) the provinces acting as strong sources can
be spotted where the shaded cells are in high numbers horizontally. WTRA is seen to be a source for
many provinces, particularly for NATR where the probability of connectivity is about 0.4. The eastern
neighbours ETRA and GUIN are also strong sources of particles. Together, WTRA, ETRA and GUIN is
responsible for particle transport to the entire tropical and subtropical Atlantic Ocean in a 5-year period.
Other characteristic source-provinces are NWCS, providing the entire northern hemisphere with particles
and GUIA and CARB transporting matter to most of the northern hemisphere. NADR is a source with a
reasonably high connectivity probability to the north-eastern provinces SARC, NECS and NAST(E) (about
0.3 connectivity probability), which in turn has ARCT as its source province.
Destination provinces
The provinces acting as destinations can be read from the connectivity matrix by looking for strong patterns
along vertical lines. The two major destinations for particle transport in the Atlantic Ocean are the North
and South Atlantic gyres. In the North, this gyre system is comprised by NAST(W), NAST(E) and NATR,
which has a high connectivity probability from many of the surrounding provinces, primarily for particles
from CNRY (0.88), GFST (0.48), and WTRA (0.43), but also from CARB, NWCS, NADR and GUIA
(about 0.25). In the southern hemisphere, SATL stands out as a major destination, with high connectivity
probabilities from both BENG (0.85), BRAZ (0.80) and FKLD (0.74), receiving particles from almost the
entire system of the southern hemisphere, even SANT (0.03) and ANTA (0.0001).

Spread potential
The spread potential of the provinces is here determined by the number of provinces that each province
spreads to. A map of the number of connected provinces (ﬁgure 4.5) shows how the central equatorial
provinces WTRA and ETRA spreads to 16 and 14 other provinces respectively (>60% of the twenty-two

and GUIN have a comparatively intermediate spread potential, while the extreme low spread is seen for
BENG and NECS that spreads to 3 and 4 other provinces respectively. Generally, the number of connected
provinces is higher in the northern than southern hemisphere, and the equatorial provinces have the highest
spread. Apart from the extremes (SATL, NATR, WTRA, ETRA) in the ﬁgures of retention (4.3) and spread
potential (4.5), certain coastal provinces stand out as sheltered areas. NECS is a good example of a region
with a low number of connections to other provinces and a comparatively high retention of particles.
Leakage of particles from the domain
A connectivity analysis in a closed system would result in a connectivity matrix where the connectivity
probabilities of each individual row or column sums up to 1. Figure 4.2 indicates that certain rows or cells
do not add up to a probability of 1 (e.g. SARC, SANT and ANTA, see values in appendix D.1), suggesting
a leak of particles from the deﬁned domain. This leak from the poleward provinces is to be expected, given
the strong connection between high-latitude provinces and the Arctic and Southern Oceans, and because
all simulated trajectories are included in the connectivity data sets, regardless of whether the end position
of the particles are positioned outside of the domain (section 3.4.2). Implications caused by the particle
leakage are described in section 5.2.

4.7
Long-term connectivity patterns
The 10-year particle tracking simulation was run with identical setup as the 1- and 5-year simulations,
except from the frequency of writing in the output ﬁle, which was reduced to 15 days to keep the ﬁle size
within reasonable limits. The minimum connectivity times for 10 years of particle drift is seen in ﬁgure 4.11.
Despite the changes in write frequency, the results are nearly identical to those of the 5-year simulation
(ﬁgure 4.8). There are however new connections occurring for time spans >5 years (blue colored cells),
particularly for connections where GUIA and BRAZ is acting as source to regions of the North Atlantic and
equator. Within 10 years, particles from GUIA reach to distant locations, as SARC and NECS in the north,
and CNRY in the east. Particles from BRAZ reach to NWCS, GFST and NAST(W) and the entire belt
of provinces around the equator. The long minimum time of connection between BRAZ and e.g. WTRA,
which is geographically close, is probably due to the counterclockwise spin of the South Atlantic gyre, which
leads the particles in a large swirl before reaching the equatorial region and the coast of Central and North
America. Other links occurring later than 5 years that are worth noting is the minimum time from CARB
to SARC (about 7 years), the slow time of connection from ARCT and SARC to the North Atlantic gyre
(NAST(W) and NATR), and the surprisingly late connection between the bordering provinces NADR to
NWCS (about 6 years), that most likely is caused by the one-directional ﬂow of the North Atlantic Drift.
Interestingly, ﬁgure 4.11 also reveals where provinces do not interact even after long time steps. For
example, the strong jet of the GFST province could be expected to feed the entire north-eastern Atlantic
with particles, but after 10 years, the minimum connectivity time results show that no released particle
from GFST have yet reached NECS. Similarly, no particle from NAST(W) reaches either NADR or NECS
in the east, while no particle from NAST(E) reaches GFST and CARB in the west. The case of CNRY is
also unique, as the particles reach only the nearest neighbours, and that even after 10 years, the particles
have not reached outside of NATR (see ﬁgure 4.10). In the southern hemisphere, a similar pattern is seen
for BENG, where all the particles are fed into SATL, with no particles left for other near neighbours, such
as ETRA and SSTC. Finally, the FKLD province not is connected to the southerly ANTA province, but
sends the particles primarily to SATL, and a small portion to SSTC and SANT. ANTA provides only a
limited portion of its particles to the other provinces of the South Atlantic (see ﬁgure 4.10), but still ANTA
interacts with both SSTC, SANT and FKLD.

(see ﬁgure 4.11), which all follow the general circulation patterns of the Atlantic Ocean. In the north, the
Greenland Current from north to south causes the ARCT province to provide NECS, GFST, and NAST(E)
with matter without receiving anything in return. The same goes for particles from SARC to NWCS, GFST
and NAST(E) (although these connections are slow (>2 years) and of low probability (<0.002)). NWCS
transports particles to NADR, NECS and NAST(E) with help from the velocity of the North Atlantic Drift
and the Gulf Stream, without receiving particles. GFST, CARB, NAST(W) and NAST(E) provides NADR
with particles, while CARB and NAST(E) also contributes to NECS. In the equatorial region, one-way
relations are seen for WTRA, ETRA, SATL and GUIA which all are connected to GFST, which passes on
the received particles further north or east. Similar relations are seen between equatorial and North Atlantic
gyral provinces, where the link of most interest is the connection from WTRA to NATR. These provinces
share a geographically long border, zonally just north of equator, where particles are transported only in
from WTRA to NATR, and not the reverse direction. This feature is consistent across the seasons and over
long time periods (see ﬁgure 4.11). Similar patterns are seen for more separated regions (e.g. CARB to
NAST(E), ETRA to NAST(W), CNRY to NAST(E), ETRA to CNRY), underlining the northward transport
of particles along the entire equatorial belt.
4.9
Vertical displacement
The 5-year particle trajectories were analyzed for vertical transport. As previously mentioned, all simulated
particles were released in the surface layer. Throughout the 5 years of travelling, the mean depth of the
particles trajectories were found to 85 m. After 1 year, the mean depth of particles was 40 m (at this time
step only). At the ﬁnal time step of the simulation, the mean depth of the ﬁnal position was found to 143
m, illustrating a general sinking of particles with time. The particle at the greatest depth at the ﬁnal time
step was located at 1053 m depth. This particle was released in ARCT between Iceland and Greenland,
and trapped in a major vortex (see ﬁgure 4.12, particle 1). This point represents an outlier, as only three
particles out of 25 104 were found to have their end positions at >600 m depth, within the Irminger Sea.
Only 69 particles had end positions below 400 m. Throughout the simulation however, multiple 79 particles
reached below 1000 m. Figure 4.12 presents vertical trajectories of selected particles to give examples of
the transport in the third dimension. The presented trajectories remain in shallow layers (above 10 m) for
at least the ﬁrst year of simulation, except from particle 5, which is oscillating between surface and 200
m depth (ﬁgure 4.12), clearly inﬂuenced by the dynamic Guinean Coast (ﬁgure 4.12). The majority of the
particle trajectories show a trend of descending towards depths. In contrast, particle 4 shows a v-pattern,
starting oﬀand ending in the surface, reaching no deeper than 30 m. Particle 5 stabilizes at comparatively
shallow depths, probably due to its position close to the coast. Particles 6, 8, and 10 do all have end
positions in the centre of the North or South Atlantic basins, where the vertical subduction does not seem
to be settled, even after 5 years (ﬁgure 4.12). In contrast, particles 7 and 9 show stable depths, especially
particle 7 which remains at 200 m depth on its northward journey 4.12.

Chapter 5
Discussion
An oceanic global circulation model has been used for particle tracking to identify connectivity patterns
between biogeographical provinces of the Atlantic Ocean. The particle trajectory simulations have provided
estimates of short-term, long-term, and seasonal patterns of connectivity and connectivity timescales. In
the following, I will attempt to answer the working questions of how the biogeographical provinces in the
Atlantic Ocean are connected, and under which time frames inter-province connections occur. The results
will be discussed in relation to examples of marine species in the Atlantic, in order to identify potential
impacts from oceanic circulation on marine ecosystems within the Atlantic Ocean.
5.1
Connectivity patterns in the Atlantic Ocean
5.1.1
Connectivity probability
From the connectivity probability results, it is seen that the majority of the particles accumulate in the
North and South Atlantic gyres over extended time periods (ﬁgure 4.2). There is a clear trend of particle
transport from the equatorial regions towards provinces of the North Atlantic, strongly indicating asymmetry
in the patterns of connectivity within the Atlantic Ocean basin. On the basis of coastal retention in the
North Atlantic, it can be hypothesized that coastal provinces of the northern hemisphere sustain rich and
diverse populations, because retention of particles possibly increases settlement and growth of species with
a pelagic larval phase. As an example, the rich life of populations of ﬁsheries species in the North Atlantic
(Trenkel et al., 2014) centers around the NECS province, mirroring the comparatively high retention within
this province. For other coastal provinces, there is a substantial transport of mass towards GUIN, GUIA,
NECS and SARC, while GUIA and NWCS also function as source to several provinces. The connectivity
probability results reveals a trans-Atlantic transport of particles, suggesting a possible exchange of biota
between eastern and western coasts of the Atlantic Ocean. The connectivity probability results do also
suggest that a large number of provinces are not connected within the simulated time periods (grey cells
in ﬁgure 4.10).
5.1.2
Minimum connectivity time
The minimum time of connectivity in the Atlantic Ocean was on average found to 8 months, and the
median minimum connectivity time between provinces of the Atlantic Oceans were found to be 10 days
(4.1). The dominance of fast minimum connectivity times is most likely a consequence of the high number
of shared borders between the provinces, where particles cross the borders very quickly, with almost no
57
==PAGE:68==
exceptions. When excluding the 10-day minimum times from the data set, the median of the values of
minimum connectivity times was found to be 13 months (400 days), which is considerably longer, but
still within the timescale of months. In the study of global ocean minimum connectivity times by J¨onsson
and Watson (2016), the median value of minimum time was found to 3.5 ± 2.3 years. These results are
however not easily compared to the present results, as they are based on diﬀerent methods of computing
the minimum time. In J¨onsson and Watson (2016) the Atlantic Ocean is split into grid cells of 2◦×2◦,
much smaller than the province separation in this study. As the probability for reaching a smaller cell is
lower, the minimum time of connection of two cells is likely to cause a prolonged average minimum time,
which explains the diﬀerence in median values. The mean value of all minimum connectivity times >10
days is 1.5 years (541 days), which is somewhat closer to the median value from J¨onsson and Watson
(2016). Although the two studies are not directly comparable (described in more detail in section 5.2),
the ﬁndings are in the same order of magnitude. The presented estimates suggest a rapid exchange and
dispersal of particles within the Atlantic Ocean among the connected provinces, even when excluding the
majority of the fastest connections. For populations of Atlantic migratory ﬁshes such as herring, mackerel,
blue whiting, blueﬁn tuna and swordﬁsh (Trenkel et al., 2014), transport of early life-stage individuals can
potentially determine the population dynamics in terms of recruitment success and horizontal dispersal,
seemingly within timescales of months. The presented results indicates that drifting biotic particles as
for example ﬁsh larvae, phytoplankton or microbial communities may be dispersed to a large number of
locations only within months, potentially leading to a wide habitat range.

Short and long minimum connectivity times
For the shorter minimum connectivity times that are in the timescales of days or weeks, the connection
between provinces might lead to exchange of species with a pelagic larval stage. Certain species of molluscs,
polychaetes, crustaceans, echinoderms, ﬁshes and certain types of algae have pelagic larval duration (PLD)
of weeks and months (Shanks, 2009). The presented results show that given the right location of spawning,
larvae or spores with PLDs corresponding to the connectivity time between two provinces may be dispersed
58
==PAGE:69==
long distances, and settling in the destination province, if the biological timing is right.
For microbial
communities, marine viruses have been observed to transfer DNA between biomes (Sano et al., 2004) under
ideal conditions. In a case of a short minimum connectivity time between two provinces, this indicates that
viral diseases may spread between provinces, given the right ecological conditions.
An ecological aspect of the longer minimum connectivity times is the transport of species with longer
PLDs. An example of such species is the European eel, Anguilla anguilla, where the spawning takes place in
the Sargasso Sea and larvae are transported with the ocean currents towards the European shelf (Schmidt,
1922). The dispersal of the European eel depends on ocean currents throughout the leptocephalus larval
stage, which may last up to 280 days (Arai et al., 2000). The results show that such larval transport
is possible. As larger individuals of the European eel (>60 mm) are found closer to the European shelf
(Schmidt, 1922), this supports the ﬁnding of passive larval drift from west to east in the North Atlantic.
In comparison, the habitat of the blueﬁn tuna also stretches across the Atlantic Ocean, where spawning
grounds have been found to be in the Gulf of Mexico and the Mediterranean (e.g. Rooker et al., 2007). For
blueﬁn tuna however, the dispersal only depend on oceanic transport only during the ﬁrst weeks, as larvae
of blueﬁn tuna rapidly develop into swimming individuals (Mariani et al., 2010).
For timescales longer than general timescales of PLDs, understanding the connectivity timescales can
be used to estimate or predict the spread of marine organisms using marine substrate as a transport
mechanisms. Several groups are known to spend parts of their life cycles on ﬂoating objects of for example
ﬂoating macroalgae, drift wood, propagules, drift seeds or plastic debris (Winston, 2012). Rafting with
ocean currents may let passenger organisms travel long distances. For bacterial communities, plastic debris
has shown to be habitat for whole ecosystems of bacteria (Zettler et al., 2013). With the long half-life of
plastics compared to natural substrates, and the connection across Atlantic provinces, it can be argued that
plastics have the ability to function as vectors for bacterial transport, even across ocean basins.
It can be hypothesized that if the conditions are right for growth and reproduction in the destination
province, a colony of the non-indigenous species is able to settle and reproduce in the new location. Partic-
ularly with the eﬀect of climate change increasing seawater temperatures and altering chemical composition
of seawater, one can speculate in such rafting events may introduce tolerant alien species to new locations.
Considering the relatively rapid ﬂux of matter from tropical and equatorial regions towards North Atlantic,
it can be speculated that tropical species favouring warmer water might be transported along this route. It
should however be kept in mind that climate change may also alter ocean circulation patterns, potentially
equally important to global ecosystems as temperature increase or ocean acidiﬁcation (van Gennip et al.,
2017).
5.1.3
Coastal provinces
As previously mentioned, the connectivity between the coastal provinces have shown to be extensive, and
comparatively fast. In the following, the connectivity relations of the coastal provinces of the Atlantic Ocean
will be described and discussed.
Coasts of the North Atlantic
Along the western coastline of the Atlantic, GUIA is well connected to the coasts of the northern hemisphere,
reaching CARB in <10 days with a connectivity probability of 0.23, and reaching NWCS and GFST within
1 year. The stable mass transport from GUIA to CARB and NWCS over the seasons shows that GUIA
represents a strong and stable contribution of water to the coastal region along the North American coast.
The ecology of CARB may therefore be inﬂuenced by the transport of planktonic species as well as abiotic
particles such as organic matter from the GUIA coast.
In the mid-west Atlantic,
CARB covers both the Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean Sea.

is only seen for particle releases in February and March, where the minimum time of connectivity is rapid
(25 and 10 days respectively, ﬁgure 4.9). A seasonal pattern like this may be crucial for the direction of
organism dispersal. As an example, two bivalve species with spawning seasons at diﬀerent times of year
was spreading to habitats in opposing directions, due to seasonality in current direction, at similar latitudes
in the Paciﬁc Ocean (Carson et al., 2010). With the distinct seasonal window of particle exchange from
CARB to GUIA, a similar divide of dispersal between neighbouring provinces might also occur for the CARB
province, for example aﬀecting the dispersal of coral larvae, which is known to be dispersed between the
two regions (Wood et al., 2013).

east, demonstrating that a trans-Atlantic transfer of biota cannot be neglected. The particle transport from
west to east corresponds to the patterns of the thermohaline circulation and the North Atlantic Drift (ﬁgure
2.1). The high levels of chlorophyll a across the Atlantic including NWCS, NADR and NECS shows that
biotic particles are most likely not food limited on the journey from west to east (ﬁgure 5.1), which may
increase the chance of survival in the eastward drift across the Atlantic. A colony rafting across the Atlantic
could potentially introduce tropical non-indigenous species from the Caribbean to the European shelf, if
surviving the light and temperature climate across the North Atlantic.
These far-reaching connections
support the hypothesis of the north east Atlantic being inﬂuenced by the particle transport from the central
west coast of the Atlantic Ocean. This will however remain a hypothesis, as unassisted transfer of an
invasive species has to my knowledge never been observed. With respect to the potential consequences of
climate change both for oceanic circulation (van Gennip et al., 2017) and the expected northward species
migration (e.g. Beaugrand et al., 2002), a future climatic scenario might open up for a stronger connection
between the tropical CARB towards the European shelf sea, introducing species that are better suited to
the warming climate.

The oceanic provinces in the Atlantic Ocean can be divided into two gyre
systems, separated by the equatorial currents. The northern gyre consists
of NAST(W), NAST(E) and NATR, in the following described as the North
Atlantic Gyral Province, NATL. The major gyres are known for their low

satellite images (e.g. ﬁgure 5.1). The suction of the gyres from all surround-

suggest that there is an insigniﬁcant eﬀect from the potential biological connectivity from coast to gyres
in the Atlantic Ocean, because the low primary productivity in the two major gyres cannot sustain a rich
ecosystem. It should be noted that the phytoplankton biodiversity is noticeably higher in the NATL than
SATL region (Barton et al., 2010). This may be due to limitation of nutrients such as iron, but this remains
a question. From a marine litter point of view however, the gyral accumulation is of great signiﬁcance, as
slowly degradable garbage clusters together and remains trapped within the gyre systems (van Sebille et al.,
2012), possibly causing damage to marine organisms by entanglement or ingestion.

southerly ARCT and northerly NADR. Despite its retention, ARCT is still
source to 7 other provinces, possibly due to the southward ﬂow of the Greenland Current. Particles retained
within ARCT may be subject to vertical subduction in the Irminger basin, as particles reached 1000 m
depth (ﬁgure 4.12, particle 1), possibly involved in the formation of deep water known to cause a downward
transport of particles such as nutrients. This observation corresponds to the expected vertical motion from
thermohaline circulation, bringing surface water to great depths. Although retained within ARCT during
a 5-year period, such particles are possible transported great distances with the deep circulation following
the meridional overturn (ﬁgure 2.1).
The dominance of northward ﬂow of the adjacent SARC, causes a highly limited connection from SARC
to other regions of the Atlantic, where the main transport is lead into the Barents Sea and the Arctic
Ocean. A substantial amount of particles leaves the northern boundary of the domain, particularly from
SARC. (The leakage of particles will be discussed further in section 5.2.) NADR shares the high chlorophyll
levels with ARCT and SARC, but is considerably much more connected to the rest of the Atlantic Ocean,
partly due to its central position in the North Atlantic. NADR connects to all the surrounding provinces
within <10 days, except from NWCS where no particle enters before roughly 6 years, most likely due to the
eastward direction of the North Atlantic Drift. NADR receives the majority of its particles from ARCT and
transports particles to SARC in the north and NAST(E) in the south with probabilities of 0.19 and 0.27
respectively.

5.2
Limitations of the study
First of all, it should be pointed out that all models have limits and they are generally ideal representations
of the real processes occurring in nature. A model will always be a simpliﬁcation of the processes that
occur in reality.
In search for the best trade-oﬀbetween precision and practicalities, one might have
to compromise between required computational power, computational techniques and model resolution,
imposing limitations on the produced results. In the following, some of the limitations of the study will be
described and discussed.
Model setup
The NEMO global conﬁguration ORCA, was run with a 2◦horizontal resolution. To this date, OGCMs
run with considerably ﬁner grid resolutions, and the ORCA conﬁguration can be set up to compute oceanic
circulation with a horizontal grid resolution of 1/4◦, from the pre-deﬁned resolutions (Madec, 2016). The
beneﬁt from using the pre-deﬁned 2◦conﬁguration was that all input ﬁles required for the simulation was
provided, allowing a smooth and trustworthy setup of the global conﬁguration. A coarse resolution has
the disadvantage of smoothing the dynamics, representing the average value across a larger area. The
result from the averaging of physical ﬁelds is likely to inﬂuence the transport path of passively drifting
particles. This can possibly make the model miss out on connections between regions that perhaps would
have been picked up by a ﬁne-resolution model setup. The smoothing of the velocity ﬁeld could for example
be an explanation of why the expected connection between GFST and NECS is missing. Also, the 2◦×2◦
resolution does not resolve processes on mesoscale level. When eddy features are not resolved in the model,
areas aﬀected by mesoscale processes may not be realistically represented, as for example for the Gulf
Stream region and the equatorial region. Figure 3.13 clearly demonstrates the diﬀerence in intensity of
the velocity ﬁeld and the level of detail of meanders in features in the observational and modelled data.
Increasing the horizontal resolution could increase the precision of the velocity ﬁeld, and thereby also the
accuracy of the particle trajectories and connectivity results (Putman and He, 2013).
The temporal resolution in terms of frequency of writing the particle trajectory output ﬁle could have
been increased in order to reveal more detailed information of the ﬁrst 10 days of connections. In the results
of minimum connectivity time, a large amount of the minimum time connections occur within only the ﬁrst
month. On this basis, it could be argued that a ﬁner frequency would reveal a more detailed picture of the
minimum connectivity times, but it would not be expected to change the overall conclusions. In order to
study large-scale trends over multiple years, the 5-day frequency of write in the trajectory output proved to
be suﬃcient (see appendix D.4), as well as it ensured reasonable ﬁle sizes, necessary for post-processing of
the results.
A clear limitation of the study for the purpose of explain transport of living organisms is that by
simulating purely passive drift with ocean currents, important factors such as swimming, buoyancy, mortality,
temperature tolerance and prey availability are not taken into account. In order to increase the model
accuracy for e.g.
ﬁsh larvae, key behavioural traits should be included in the model.
Species speciﬁc
transport mechanisms can however be diﬃcult to identify (Kool et al., 2012). As an example of potential
transportation routes, the oceanographic model may still indicate overall patterns of dispersal for organisms
that to a large degree depend on ocean currents as means of transport. The model runs with average
conditions of ocean physics, with a climatology that does not account for extreme events or interannual
variability. This means that in nature, oceanographic events deviating from the average ocean state might
lead to variation in connection strength and timing between provinces. Also, the model was not set up to
resolve tides. Accounting for tidal dynamics would perhaps have resulted in changes in the connectivity
results, inﬂuencing the connectivity relations of the NECS and FKLD provinces which are subjects to
particularly strong tides (Longhurst, 2007).

Seeding of particles
In the simulation of particle trajectories, the initialization of particles occur only at the ﬁrst time step. This
represents a weakness in the simulated results, because the continuous leak of particles leave the Atlantic
with a noticeably lower number of particles than at the point of initialization. As seen in ﬁgure 4.1, the
majority of the remaining particles swirl within the two major gyres, which restricts the possibility for new
connections across province borders.
A continuous release of particles would allow the particles to hit
diﬀerent geographical points due to temporal variation in the velocity ﬁeld, which is demonstrated by the
seasonal release simulations (section 4.6). The one-time seeding of particles might restrict the outcome
of the connectivity analysis, possibly being the reason for the limited number of new connected provinces
from 5 to 10 years. Either a repeated pulse of particle releases, or an incoming ﬂux of particles through
boundaries with incoming water masses, would improve the model by being a more realistic representation of
the continuous particle ﬂuxes in the Atlantic. The main reason for the one-time pulse of seeding is because
of the limited options of the NEMO particle tracking tool. The sensitivity to timing of particle dispersal is
a limitation of the study, as certain connections might have been overseen, due to the single-pulse release
of particles. A model allowing a continuous release of particles may signiﬁcantly improve the accuracy of
the result, but would however introduce other drawbacks, such as increased computational time.
Method for calculating connectivity probability
The method of calculating connectivity probability as the average over all time steps (explained in section
3.4.1) was chosen in order to represent the overall probability of connectivity between the Atlantic provinces.
In a biological context, this can be seen as a limitation when identifying biological relations such as dispersion
of eggs and larvae, because a long-term average value will not show potential high-connectivity probability
events occurring over short time scales. An example of such an event could for example be a large group
of larvae passing through a province in a period of a few weeks. If coinciding with the timing of larval
settlement, such a connection would be of great importance from an ecological perspective, but would
perhaps be overlooked by the presented method of calculating connectivity probability.
The beneﬁt from the presented method is that it provides an estimate of the probability of connectivity
for all connected provinces within the entire time of simulation. While the commonly used method of
comparing the ﬁrst and last time steps would cause connections in any other time step to be overlooked,
the presented method results in a comprehensive representation of connectivity. Although it may have
implications for the understanding of certain ecological processes, the method used for the analysis gives a
representation of a broader spectrum of potential connectivity links, suitable for large-scale and long-term
connectivity analysis, as in the aim of the project.
Method for studying minimum time of connectivity
A limitation regarding the method of studying minimum connectivity time has appeared to be the use
of biogeographical provinces as regions of comparison for the connectivity analyses. The method has a
clear weakness in the level of detail, particularly because of the large size of the provinces. For example,
when the minimum time from NWCS to NECS is estimated, one cannot tell if the particle that crosses
the border originates from a coastal or oceanic part of NWCS. The argument for using the provinces was
nevertheless that a partitioning of the ocean in relation to ecological properties was a more biologically
applicable method than a squared grid insensitive to ecological properties of the province. In hindsight, it
appears that a combined-grid approach of estimating the minimum connectivity time perhaps would provide
a more accurate result. As for example in J¨onsson and Watson (2016), the minimum connectivity time is
estimated using a regular square grid for estimating the minimum connectivity time. This grid is much ﬁner
67
==PAGE:78==
than the very coarse biogeographical partitioning used to produce the presented results. The squared grid
approach allows the use of a shortest-path algorithm (e.g. Dijkstra’s algorithm), which ﬁnds the fastest path
between all the grid points, regardless of the number of grid cells the particles needs to pass from source to
destination. This provides a considerably more detailed picture of the minimum connectivity times compared
to the method used in this project. The partitioning of the Atlantic Ocean in this study was chosen in order
to provide a more general picture of global-scale connectivity. With the choice of a partitioning into major
ecological provinces comes a low level of accuracy. A suggestion for an improvement of the poor-accuracy
issue could be to run the connectivity analysis in two steps, where 1) the traditional square-grid connectivity
is estimated, and 2) the square-grid is subsequently separated into the biogeographical provinces, where
the provinces now consist of a cluster of square cells, allowing a more detailed description of connectivity
probability and minimum connectivity time between the Atlantic provinces.
Vertical subduction
The main focus of the analysis has been for the horizontal dimension. Vertical subduction is a central
component of oceanic circulation (Talley et al., 2011), and the transport of large water masses towards
deeper layers is likely to determine the vertical position of the particles. When describing living organisms
and dispersal between regions, the vertical position of the particle may inﬂuence the success of migration,
as water depth determines e.g. light availability. It should be noted that most eggs and larvae are slightly
positive or neutrally buoyant, letting them ﬂoat at corresponding density levels (MacKenzie and Mariani,
2012). In order to realistically represent drift of eggs and larvae, such properties should be accounted for.
A deeper analysis of the vertical dimension in relation to the horizontal would potentially provide a more
detailed picture of connectivity of living organisms. Further, connectivity patterns in the vertical dimension
may also reveal interesting patterns of connectivity between the surface and deep layers, as brieﬂy described
here, and as described by Frank et al. (2016).
Validation and veriﬁcation of the results
In relation to connectivity modelling studies, Hidalgo et al. (2017) stresses that the position of the particle
initialization determines both the direction and distance of dispersal of a drifting particle. Here, the ocean
physics are veriﬁed by comparison to satellite images and observations (ﬁgure 3.13), but no other veriﬁcation
or validation the particle trajectories or connectivity result is performed. Although the number of particles
have been checked for their robustness (section 3.3.2) there is a risk that the particle initialization inﬂuences
the trajectories and the ﬁnal connectivity results. To back up the ﬁndings, the results could be veriﬁed by
for example comparing trajectories and connectivity results to expected dispersal paths from information
on ocean circulation and velocities. Ideally, but at a high cost, observational data (e.g. satellite images,
zooplankton and phytoplankton sampling or GPS data) could be used to validate the model output and
connectivity results (Kool et al., 2012). The lack of empirical evidence to support the presented results is
an apparent limitation of the study. Additionally, running statistical test of the connectivity results would
provide an estimate of the level of accuracy in the results of minimum connectivity time and connectivity
probability. As presented in Hidalgo et al. (2017), an R package, ConnMatTools, is available for statistical
validation of connectivity results.
Particle leakage
Because the Atlantic Ocean is not an entirely enclosed ocean basin, but rather a world ocean in connection
with the global circulation, the global particle simulations lead to a leak of particles out of the Atlantic,
which is the domain used for connectivity analyses (ﬁgure 5.2). A major disadvantage with the loss of

particles is that with a reduced number of particles, the number of particles representing connections within

kept in the data sets used for the analysis in order to explain the weak connectivity between high-latitude
provinces and the rest of the Atlantic Ocean. Particularly large numbers of particles is seen to leak from

5.3
Future research
The previous section has lead to several suggestions for research to further improve the accuracy and validity
of the global ocean connectivity results. First of all, similar modelling studies should be performed with
a higher resolution model (preferably eddy-resolving) to allow particle trajectories to follow the energetic
current systems that are observed in the circulation systems of the Atlantic Ocean. Second, it would be
highly useful to compare the methods of analyzing connectivity in regards to squared grid versus ecological
province partitioning. Third, if achievable, it would be a great advantage to be able to analyze global con-
nectivity patterns during shifting seasons with a dynamic model (similar to what is described in Reygondeau
et al. (2013)), adding the seasonal (and perhaps also interannual) dimension to global connectivity pattern
analysis. This could potentially also allow prediction of connectivity patterns in response to climate change.
Fourth, a more thorough understanding of the vertical transport of particles could possibly reveal patterns
of vertical connectivity, as for example identiﬁcation of vertical structures (such as fronts) functioning as
ecological barriers. Finally, including parameters on swimming behaviour, mortality and growth could ex-
plain how planktonic species migrate across major ocean basins with improved accuracy. To this date, only
a limited number of published studies on global connectivity studies are available. There is an apparent
need of a deeper understanding of global ocean connectivity in terms of dispersal routes, spatial scales of
connectivity, and connectivity timescales.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The analysis of the presented results on Atlantic Ocean connectivity patterns have shown clear character-
istics, particularly in the diﬀerence between coastal and oceanic connectivity, and in diﬀerences between
the northern and southern hemispheres. Two major trans-Atlantic paths of dispersal are identiﬁed, one
from western to eastern coasts in the northern hemisphere and a second from eastern to western coasts
along the equator. These connections occur within the timescales of months. In combination with high
productivity and biodiversity in the provinces involved, the comparatively rapid trans-Atlantic connections
suggests that transport of living organisms between continents potentially is possible, for example for lar-
vae of ﬁsh and corals or rafting bacterial communities. As expected, the two major gyre systems of the
Atlantic Ocean accumulates large amounts of the released particles. While regions of low chlorophyll a
suggests that the major gyres do not sustain large abundances of living organisms, the gyral suction causes
high densities of non-living substances, as for example plastic debris. There is a strong asymmetry in the
connectivity patterns within the Atlantic Ocean, favouring particle transport towards the north, particularly
at longer timescales (>1 year). This results in more complex connectivity patterns in the North Atlantic
compared to the South Atlantic where the particles are primarily transported to the South Atlantic Gyre
or the Indian Ocean. As expected, the shortest minimum connectivity times were seen for neighbouring
provinces. Given that PLDs commonly last for days or weeks, the majority of successful exchanges of biota
is likely to take place between province neighbours. For species with longer PLDs, or for rafting organisms,
the long-distance transport is possible. Finally, the time of release of particles have shown to determine
province connections, in some cases with clear seasonal patterns.
The general ocean circulation model NEMO has been used to track particles in order to determine connec-
tivity patterns between ecological provinces of the Atlantic Ocean. The results have provided information
on connectivity probability and minimum connectivity times, indicating key transport routes and trans-
portation times between biogeographical provinces of the Atlantic Ocean. In summary, the results show an
asymmetric transport of particles from equatorial regions towards the northern hemisphere, and two strong
trans-Atlantic connections between coastal regions. While interaction between the productive coastal re-
gions may play a critical role in distribution and dispersal of living organisms, the gyral accumulation of
particles is likely to sustain non-living particles such as plastic debris. This study of large-scale connectivity
provides a picture of the transport and timescales of transportation within major dispersal pathways in the
Atlantic Ocean. By this, the project has contributed to identify global-scale ecological connections, which
to this date remains a largely unexplored topic.

A.3
NEMO namelists
A namelist is a ﬁle containing information about model input parameters, that can be changed in order
to modify the model simulation parameters (grid speciﬁcation, boundary conditions, parameter deﬁnition
and much more). For example, to change the conﬁguration so that the simulation will run for a period of
time that is long enough for the output variables to reach a balanced state, the conﬁguration namelists are
modiﬁed.
namelist ref and namelist cfg
The ﬁrs namelist read by the program is the reference namelist, namelist ref, which contains the variables
that are initialized to default values. The conﬁguration namelist, namelist cfg, is read subsequently. The
information in namelist cfg contains changes that will be made to the parameters in the reference namelist,
and overwrites these. namelist cfg is therefore the ﬁle where the parameter are changed. I have chosen
not to make changes to the reference namelists, to avoid potential errors.

Following the format of a namelist ﬁle in NEMO, the rebuild namelist is written. The ﬁle looks like below

&nam rebuild
filebase = ’mesh mask’
ndomain = 12
/
The ﬁle starts with ”&”, marking the start of the namelist named nam rebuild. The ﬁlebase parameter

the namelist ﬁle ends with a ”/” to mark the end of the namelist. The ﬁle, nam rebuild, will now be placed
in the directory of the ﬁles to merge. This example of nam rebuild would write mesh mask ﬁles 1-12 into
a ﬁle named mesh mask.nc.
Run REBUILD NEMO
When running the build command (below), the current directory must be the directory of nam rebuild
and the ﬁles to merge. Following the example of the namelist above, this command reassembles the 12

mpirun -n 1 ~/ NEMOGCM/TOOLS/REBUILD_NEMO/rebuild_nemo.exe

D.4
Particle write frequency
The below ﬁgures show the relative diﬀerence in connectivity probability and diﬀerence in minimum connec-
tivity time from simulated trajectories where the frequency of writing the ﬁle was 2 and 5 days respectively.
The relative diﬀerence was found by dividing (5d−2d) output with the 2d output. It is seen that the relative
diﬀerence is small, with a peak where ETRA, BENG and GUIA functions as source provinces. These three
provinces are connected to the highly energetic equatorial current systems, which may explain the larger
diﬀerence in these regions. The minimum connectivity time diﬀerence shows that the majority of diﬀerences
are between 2 and 5 days or 4 and 10 days, reﬂecting the time step diﬀerence. In the two cells of larger
diﬀerence (NWCS-ARCT and SSTC-SANT), the diﬀerence were found to be between 4 and 15 days for
the former and between 4 and 20 days for the latter, which is a subtle diﬀerence, given that the general
minimum time is on a scale of several months.
