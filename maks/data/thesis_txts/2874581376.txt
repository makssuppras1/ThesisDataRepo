Summary
In this thesis we review the roughness extension model by Knopp et al. (2008) and apply
it to 3 distinct ﬂow problems. Flow in a closed channel, ﬂow over a ﬂat plate, and the
ﬂow over an airfoil. We verify and validate the new roughness extension model on the 3
ﬂow problems and we go over the eﬀects of roughness on an airfoil such as Leading Edge
Roughness (LER). We found the new roughness extension model by Knopp et al. (2008)
is suitable for solving 2D ﬂow problems accurately. We create an empirical data driven
model to post-process the lift to correct for the mismatches we found. This correction is
not fully validated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With the increasing focus and interest from the global audience on clean energies as we
transition from fossil fuels, wind energy has emerged as one of the most economical ways
to cleanly add new generating capacity to the grid. Other clean energy sources like solar
and hydro are very competitive as well and wind turbine design needs to be constantly
improved to keep up with the rising demand.
As a wind turbines operates, their blades, specially the leading edge, naturally accu-
mulate dirt, insects, ice, smog and a variety of other contaminants that will increase the
roughness of the blade and can lead to rapid ageing and erosion over time. Manufacturing
imperfections can also aﬀect the aerodynamic properties of the blades. According to
Sareen et al. (2014) the increase in leading edge roughness (LER) represents an Annual
Energy Production (AEP) loss of at least 3 to 5 % and can be as high as 25% in extreme
erosion cases.
The aerodynamic diﬀerences (lift and drag) between a clean or smooth airfoil compared
to an eroded or rough airfoil can lead to severe AEP losses (as mentioned earlier) which
translate to millions of dollars wasted. It is therefore important to understand the eﬀects
of roughness on wind turbine blades so someday we may be able to mitigate those risks.
A way to understand the inﬂuence of roughness on airfoils is through Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) which is a lot cheaper and faster compared to wind tunnel
experiments and therefore it has turned into a major design tool in an engineer’s toolkit.
Several models have been developed to simulate and understand ﬂow over rough surfaces;
of course a CFD code that models roughness is only useful if it is an accurate representation
of reality. Unfortunately the fast pace of the wind industry forces us to use models that
are not always fully vetted.
The objective of this thesis is to verify and validate one of the newer models: the
SST k−ω turbulence model extension by Knopp et al. (2008) as implemented on EllipSys.
Code review is out of the scope of the present work as it is not available.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Introduction
There are many ways to solve CFD ﬂow problems; each come with its advantages and
limitations. Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) and Large Eddy Simulation (LES)
are often too computational expensive, and although computational power has grown
signiﬁcantly over the past few years, the problems we are attempting to solve have grown
too. It is therefore desired to develop models that can inexpensively solve the ﬂow
problems. The Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) does this as it solves for the
mean ﬂow but uses a model to account for turbulence eﬀects.
In this chapter we will go over the existing SST k −ω model, and brieﬂy discuss
other roughness models like the Wilcox (1998) correction. Finally we will go over the
roughness extension proposed by Knopp et al. (2008) which will be the main focus of
this work.
2.2
SST k −ω model
The SST k−ω turbulence model attempts to predict turbulence by two partial diﬀerential
equations for two variables: the turbulence kinetic energy (k) and the speciﬁc rate of
dissipation (ω). The shear stress transport (SST) formulation uses the k −ω model in
the inner parts of the boundary layer but switches to a k −ϵ behaviour as it transitions
away from the boundary layer into the free-stream.

2.4
Other corrections & their limitations
Wilcox’s correction modiﬁes the speciﬁc rate of dissipation (ω) near the wall and imposes
a turbulent kinetic energy (k) equal to zero at the wall. A decrease in dissipation will
lead to an increased turbulence compared to a smooth wall, hence higher momentum
transfer and higher friction coeﬃcients. The Wilcox correction fails when used with the
SST model as it underestimates the velocity shift when roughness is increased. It also
requires very ﬁne grids, thus increasing the computational cost.
Hellsten et al. (1997) correction modify the SST limiter in the wall region. This
corrects the issues by Wilcox, but gives slightly lower friction levels, and often fails with
enlarged roughness.
2.5
Roughness Extension by Knopp et al.
Knopp et al. (2008) (sometimes abbreviated kea through this work) presents a new
extension for the SST k −ω turbulence model to account for surface roughness for
transitionally and fully rough surfaces. It is based on the equivalent sand grain approach
and accounts for theoretical considerations on the log-layer solution for fully rough
surfaces.
In computational models which use the equivalent sand grain approach, the rough
surface is replaced by an eﬀective smooth surface, and instead, modiﬁed boundary
conditions are imposed.
The new model is based on the Wilcox correction by Hellsten et al. (1997), and
attempts to overcome 3 shortcomings of that approach:
1. Grid requirements for the near-wall mesh increase with roughness leading to high
computational costs as ﬁner resolutions are required.
2. For transitionally rough surfaces the predictions for skin friction often fail to provide
satisfying and accurate results.
3. The Wilcox roughness modiﬁcation cannot be used in conjunction with the original
SST model and thus requires an additional modiﬁcation of the SST model.
The new method claims:
1. It allows for the simulation of ﬂows over rough surfaces at the same grid resolution
requirements as for smooth walls maintaining the same computational cost.
2. It gives improved predictions in skin friction for transitional roughness Reynolds
numbers compared to using only the roughness extension by Wilcox (1998).
3. It does not require a modiﬁcation of the SST k −ω model.

2.6
EllipSys
EllipSys is a 2D and 3D ﬂow solver developed by DTU. EllipSys uses a numerical method
to solve the incomprehensible Navier-Stokes equations. All simulations will be carried
out using the following solver parameters:
• QUICK on all grid levels for the convective terms
• SIMPLE for the pressure correction
• Residual limit of maximum 10−5
• Drela−Gile for the transition model
We will be using hyperbolic grid generation with a dissipation factor of 1.

3.5
Conclusion
Both high and low Reynolds number cases have good agreement between the experimental
results by Nikuradse (1933) and the simulations in Knopp et al. (2008).
An average error of 5.8% is found between the experimental results and our simulations.
Using a case deﬁned grid will help reduce the error as well.
The new model works very well on the channel ﬂow, although it is under predicting
the friction velocity sometimes. The under prediction may be withing the uncertainty of
the original measurements by Nikuradse (1933), however we cannot verify this as the
uncertainties from such experiment are not available.

5.4

results using the experimental data from Lujström.
The lift correction we are proposing is an empirical data driven model. However there
is a limited amount of data and further testing is required. The correction is proposed to
set the framework for future studies.

then we will validate that model against case with kr = 3.08 ∗10−4 to make sure we are

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
We have thoroughly evaluated the new extension model proposed in Knopp et al. (2008)
in a variety of setting ranging from diﬀerent Reynolds and roughness Reynolds numbers
to diﬀerent geometries and ﬂow types.
We have successfully replicated the results from Knopp et al. (2008) and have seen the
new SSTk −ω extension perform very well in the channel and ﬂat plate ﬂow problems
as it correctly predicts the roughness eﬀects on the ﬂow. The airfoil ﬂow problem was to
some extent accurate as well taking into consideration the quality of the experimental
data. Moreover the roughness extension accomplished its claims and some more.
The roughness extension:
• successfully predicted the ﬂow conditions with the same grid requirements as a
smooth ﬂow problems
• did not require any additional modiﬁcations to the SST model
• was able to provide accurate results for transitional roughness
• was able to replicate the results for smooth walls as well
As a further matter we were able to understand the impact of roughness on a an
airfoil, particularly the impact of the leading edge roughness compared to the roughness
on the rest of the blade and how the increasing roughness aﬀects lift and drag coeﬃcients.
More experimental data on diﬀerent airfoils and ﬂow conditions could help further
validate the extension by Knopp et al. (2008).
