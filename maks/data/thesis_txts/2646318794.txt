Preface
This thesis is the final project of the MSc. Eng. Architectural education at the Technical
University of Denmark. The project accounted for 30 ECTS and was carried out between
the 3rd of August 2020 to 3rd of February 2021. The supervisor was associated professor
Lars Schiøtt Sørensen, from the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark. The external supervisor was certified fire safety engineer Christian
Warneboldt Green. The thesis assesses an alternative, natural ventilation solution for a
single stair building’s smoke control system in Denmark. The project compares a natural
smoke shaft performance according to Approved Document B clause 3.49-3.59 [1] with a
pressurized staircase according to DS/EN 12101-6 [2].
References are indicated using the IEEE method with the corresponding bibliography at
the end of the report.

Abstract
The aim of the project is to assess whether a natural smoke shaft constructed according
to Approved Document B clause 3.49-3.53 [1] would meet the safety standards of the
prescriptive solutions for single stair buildings in Denmark. To assess the performance
of the natural smoke shaft, 16 simulation scenarios were conducted. These simulation
results were compared to 16 similar simulation scenarios using the already prescriptive
solution, a pressurized staircase according to DS/EN 12101-6 class B [2].
To measure performance, the following parameters have been varied: the building height,
the level of the fire-initiated apartment, fire size, and wind-driven fire. The building height
was varied between a height of 27 m (24 m to the top floor) and a height of 48 m (45
m to the top floor) to illustrate the lower and upper limit for the prescriptive solution for a
pressurized staircase according to the Danish building regulation [3]. The placement of
the fire-initiated apartment was set to one of two heights: either the ground floor or 2/3 of
the building’s height. The fire size varied between a smoldering 5 kW fire, a small furniture
fire of 500 kW and a whole apartment fire of 5000 kW. In the simulation for the 5000 kW fire,
the windows in the apartment were set to break if the temperature or pressure exceeded a
certain pre-defined threshold. In the fires where the windows break, two scenarios where
simulated: one where no wind affect was considered and another where a simplified wind
pressure was modeled to create a wind-driven fire.
The results have been evaluated according to the thresholds given in chapter 8 in the
building regulation guidance of chapter 5 [4]. The results show no critical conditions occur
in the escape route during the simulation time for the pressurized staircase. For the natural
smoke shaft, in the building of 27 m height, critical conditions occurred in all cases with
a 5000 kW fire. However, for the tall building model, critical conditions only occurred with
a wind-driven fire scenario, if the fire-initiated apartment was placed at 2/3 of the building
height.
The assessment of the two systems’ performance was established based on risk, mon-
etary, and floor-area analyses. Though the simulation results showed that critical condi-
tions occur in the natural smoke shaft, especially in the low building, the risk assessment
showed that the risk level is similar for both smoke control systems. The risk assessment
was based on the low building’s performance. The monetary analysis indicates that the
natural smoke shaft is a less expensive solution of the two smoke control systems. The
floor-area analysis showed that the natural smoke shaft takes up 60% less square meters
on each level compared to the pressurized staircase.
The findings indicate that the natural smoke shaft in certain cases could be a substitution
for the pressurized staircase. However, further investigation is needed to determine the
solution’s robustness and applicability, as the effectiveness of the natural smoke shaft
greatly depends on the stack effect. This means that things such as the building height
and weather conditions will influence the performance of the system.

Introduction
Tragic events such as disaster fire accidents, often creates reflection upon how such an
event could occur. They may lead to innovation in the building designs of the future or
lead to law changes to prevent against such events. One of the most recent examples
is the Grenfell Tower fire in England, where the single staircase layout was insufficient
in providing a safe escape route due to smoke accumulation. Multiple fire safety mea-
sures failed and influenced other measures, and the smoke control system did not follow
regulations. The system was a cross between a mechanical smoke shaft and a pressur-
ized system. [24] Nevertheless, statistics show that nine out of ten pressurized staircases
fail to operate correctly during a fire, resulting in staircases getting filled with smoke [25].
When smoke enters the staircase, it limits the possibility to escape through the staircase,
which for a single staircase building can result in people getting stuck in the building.
1.1
Fire safety regulation and prescriptive solutions during
time
A smoke control system aims to ensure a safe evacuation of people and limit fire and
smoke spread within the required safe egress time. Additionally, the system contributes
to better working conditions for firefighting intervention. The primary purpose of fire safety
in a building according to BR18 clause 82 [5] is precisely what the smoke control system’s
aim is, meaning that the smoke control system is of significant importance.
A building can be designed according to prescriptive solutions to ensure tenable condition
for evacuation during a fire. The definition of prescriptive solutions is, as the name indi-
cates, pre-accepted solutions which can be applied if the building fulfils the requirements.
The benefits of using the prescriptive solutions are that you do not need to prove that the
solution is safe, as the prescriptive solution ensures that the level of safety is sufficient.
Although the exact level of safety cannot be guaranteed, as prescriptive solutions often
are based on previous experience, usually collected over time and lessons learned from
previous fires. This makes it a valuable guideline because it allows us to build upon ex-
isting experience/knowledge with fires. [26] [8]
Building traditions vary between countries and the prescriptive solutions follow these vari-
ations. Despite these variations, the principles of the prescriptive solutions concepts are
similar.
This section will present the timeline of the prescriptive solution in Denmark according
to means of escape and fire fighting in residential buildings up to today. The section

in means of escape in residential buildings and ensure acceptable condition for firefight-

1.1.1
Prescriptive solutions in Denmark during time
Up until the year 1850, there were no collected regulations as we know today. At that
time, the only way of ensuring consistency in construction was through direct command
by the king. Another stakeholder of your building would be the local community, as they
would fear fire occurrence and spread. Therefore, they would be interested in the design
and construction of the building. [8]
Two great fires in the 1800s and the constitution in 1849 changed this perspective. Copen-
hagen got passed the first construction law in 1856, and other parts of Denmark followed
in subsequent years. The construction law was based on experience from the fires. In the
different parts of Denmark, the construction law was established based on the construc-
tion law from Copenhagen but was adapted to the local settings. However, there were
some national requirements according to the number of stairs in a building. Before 1728,
a block of flats was nearly unknown, but after the great fires, it started to be widespread
as a lot of families had lost their homes after part of Copenhagen had been burned down.
In the years after the great fires, the so-called Ildebrandehuse with several stories were
built. [27] [8]
The construction law from 1856 required access to two stairs in a block of flats. The re-
sult was long corridors connecting tiny apartments; this was inappropriate. In 1889 the
requirement was altered so the access from an apartment to the two stairs should be
direct and unhampered. Although in a regulation from the 4th of July 1795 there was a
desirable matter of an additional staircase in buildings, the requirement could not be met
in many buildings because they were too small, whereas a number of exceptions were
given. [8]
Kartoffelrækkerne in Copenhagen is an example of the possible dispensation for the two-
staircase requirement, as the dispensation could be given if the building consisted of a
maximum of three stories. The experiment houses, Forsøgshusene, on Mariendalsvej
at Frederiksberg, was built in 1906.
It was a five-stories building and had one non-
combustible staircase and escape balconies from each apartment. This made it possible
to escape from an apartment by jumping from balcony to balcony. [8]
From the 22nd of January 1929, the Home Office approved a general dispensation from
the two-staircase requirement. If the building was maximum of three stories high, the re-
quirement was a non-combustible staircase, and taller buildings were in addition required
to provide escape balconies from each apartment. [8]
During the 1950s the first 28 high-rise buildings in Denmark with 8 to 13 stories were built
at Bellahøj. The height of the buildings was a challenge for ensuring the fire safety of
the building. Therefore, escape balconies and airlocks had to be introduced between the
apartments and the staircase. The airlock solution was met with suspicion, as it would

The application of the natural smoke shaft according to the regulation in England
The natural smoke shaft can be used in residential buildings with the top floor below 11

distance between the flat door and staircase door from 4.5 m to 7.5 m [1]. It is required

above ground, a protected firefighting shaft is required in the building [1]. A smoke control
system, e.g. a natural smoke shaft, could be provided to protect the firefighting shaft [1].
According to BS 9991 [18], a natural smoke shaft is also suitable as a firefighting shaft in
residential buildings with a floor height above more than 30 m ground [18].
In non-residential buildings there is a general requirement of a minimum of two escape
stairs in a building with a top floor height more than 11 m above ground [1]. Hence, it is not
an requirement to provide a smoke control system to ensure means of escape in these
situations. However, a smoke control system is required if the top floor exceeds 18 m
above ground, to ensure tenable conditions for firefighting operations [1]. In this instance,
a natural smoke shaft can be used.
According to BS 9991 section 0.7 [18], the natural smoke shaft can be applied to residen-
tial buildings of any height. However, the solution should be evaluated by a fire safety
engineer if the building exceeds a height of 50 m.
1.2
Project objectives
The accessibility of escape routes is crucial for the fire safety in a building, so the oc-
cupants in the building can safely leave the building without being exposed to critical
conditions such as elevated temperatures, toxic smoke, and radiation from the fire for the
duration of the evacuation [5]. In Denmark, it is required to design buildings with escape
windows, so occupants can exit the building safely if escape routes are blocked. How-
ever, a building can be designed without escape windows if the safety level is obtained in
another manner [5]. If a building’s top floor is located 22 m above ground, it is not possible
to use the escape windows, as the length of the fire brigades’ ladder is limited. [3] For
that reason, the reliability and durability of the escape routes in the building are essential.
In a single staircase building, the staircase is essential to ensure a safe, swift, and suc-
cessful evacuation. Hence the Building Regulations in Denmark require installation of a
smoke control system in single staircase buildings where emergency escape windows are
not an option or if the top floor is located between 22-45 m above the ground. The smoke
control system is often executed by pressurizing the staircase or by integrating an airlock
besides the staircase, as these are prescripted solutions. [3]
The pressurized staircase is a mechanical solution creating an overpressure in the stair-
case, to keep it clear of smoke. This mechanical solution contains a number of compo-
nents to obtain and maintain the overpressure.[2]
The airlock solution demands that the staircase must be located alongside the façade [6],

which is also where the most valuable square meters in the building are located. This
location also limits the flexibility of the layout of the building. Therefore, the pressurized
staircase is more often used than the airlock solution to protect the staircase in case of a
fire.
In England, a different solution is often used to keep the staircase clear of smoke. The
solution is built around a vertical shaft located next to the lobby where the smoke can
escape , like a chimney.
This solution can either be driven by natural ventilation (natural smoke shaft) or by a me-

solutions are more straightforward than a pressurized staircase. In England, the mechan-
ical smoke shaft is used more often of the two, as this solution takes up less space for the
vertical shaft. However, the disadvantage of a mechanical system (pressurized staircase
or the mechanical smoke shaft) is that they contain components, each containing a failure
probability. This means that if one of the components fails, the whole system might fail,
which decreases the reliability of the system.
Whereas the natural smoke shaft system only contains a fire detector in the lobby, vent
to the smoke shaft from the lobby at the fire-initiated story, vent at the top of the smoke
shaft- and staircase. Activation of a detector in the lobby should activate an automatic
and simultaneous opening of the vents [18] [19] . The number of components which the
system includes is less than for a pressurized staircase. Therefore, the natural smoke
shaft system might have a lower risk of failure.
A pressurized staircase is often an expensive solution. Therefore, it would be beneficial
to determine whether a natural smoke shaft system can compete with a pressurized stair-
case according to the level of safety, construction and maintenance costs.
This project aims to examine the benefits, weaknesses, and limitations of the natural
smoke shaft system. So that it can be compared with a pressurized staircase. This will
provide the basis for an assessment of the natural smoke shaft system, so it can be as-
sessed whether a natural smoke shaft can be an alternative to a pressurized staircase in
a single stair building in Denmark.
Above-mentioned is the topic for the thesis, and the following research question has been
formulated:
Does a natural smoke shaft system have the same level of safety as a pressurized stair-
case, and which solution is the most beneficial?

The smoke control systems
This chapter will present the principles behind the two smoke control systems. Smoke
is a substantial killer in residential fires [20], and the first objective of the smoke control
system is to keep the escape route clear from the smoke and ensure tenable conditions
for people to escape through. The tenable conditions will be presented in chapter 5 [4].
The flow of air and smoke occurs due to pressure differences and the buoyancy of smoke.
A pressure difference occurs when there is a difference between the inside temperature
and the outside ambient temperature. The stack effect happens when the temperature
inside the building is higher than the ambient temperature, resulting in hot smoke flows up-
ward and out. If a fire occurs in a room below the neutral plane, the smoke flows upward,
e.g., through the staircase and into the rooms above the neutral plane. Alternatively, if
there is an opening at the top of the stairs, the smoke will escape through this. The driv-
ing force is the pressure difference, because the atmospheric pressure decreases with
altitude. The principle is shown in Figure 2.1. [20]
Figure 2.1: Natural stack effect. The illustration assumes uniform temperature, uniform openings over the
building height, steady-state conditions and no wind. Where pin = inside pressure, pout = outside pressure,
and ∆p = pressure difference [20]
11
==PAGE:28==
The reverse stack effect occurs when the temperature inside the building is cooler than
the ambient temperature. This temperature differences create a lower pressure inside the
building than outside above the neutral plane. This results in hot smoke flows downward
and out. If a fire occurs in the compartment above the neutral plane, the smoke will
flow downward and out in the rooms below the neutral plane. This occurs because the
air density inside the building is higher than the density of the ambient air. Resulting in
a higher negative pressure inside than outside in the building above the neutral plane.
Below the neutral plane the pressure inside is higher than outside, which creates a flow
outward. The principle is shown in Figure 2.2. [20]
Figure 2.2: Reverse stack effect. The illustration assumes uniform temperature, uniform openings over the
building height, steady-state conditions and no wind. Where pin = inside pressure, pout = outside pressure,
and ∆p = pressure difference [20]
When a fire is ignited, hot smoke is produced, creating a plume and a smoke layer below
the ceiling as hot smoke rises due to buoyancy.[20]
In the fire compartment, the fire builds up a pressure difference between the compartment
and the adjacent areas. This results in the smoke escaping to the adjacent areas with
lower pressure as the pressure wants to equalize.[31] The pressure difference between
the fire compartment and the adjacent areas for a fully developed fire is shown in Figure
2.3. [20]

The natural smoke shaft is activated when a smoke detector in the lobby detects smoke.
This detector will send a message to the fire indication panel (FIP), which will send a mes-
sage to the fire fan control panel (FFCP). This fire fan control panel will send a signal to
the vents to open. Then the lobby vent, shaft vent, and staircase vent will open simulta-
neously. As the lobby vent is placed above the staircase doors height, the smoke will flow
into the shaft.
The natural smoke shaft’s driving force is the stack effect, which is dependent on the
weather conditions, temperature and wind, smoke buoyancy, and possibly mechanical
climate ventilation.
[32] The stack effect creates a suction in the shaft like a chimney. The most significant
stack effect occurs when the temperature inside is much greater than the outside tem-
perature. This means that the system is sensitive to temperature conditions. If the tem-
perature outside is much higher than inside a reverse stack effect can occur, resulting in
smoke being pushed down into the shaft instead of out. However,in the Nordic countries,
the temperature inside is typically higher than outside. The hot smoke from the fire will
2.1. NATURAL SMOKE SHAFT
13
==PAGE:30==
increase the strength of the stack effect. In a fire compartment the smoke’s buoyancy will
make the smoke flow upward and create a ceiling jet, when the smoke layer becomes
below the height of the door, the smoke will start to flow into adjacent areas. If there is
climate ventilation in the building, this system can force the smoke to flow in a specific
direction or into the system, spreading the smoke to adjacent parts of the building. How-
ever, the climate ventilation system is usually shut down when a fire is detected so that it
will not affect the fire’s behavior and the performance of the smoke shaft.
The size of the smoke shaft depends on the required venting action. As the most signif-
icant pressure difference from the stack effect occurs at the bottom of the building, the
largest venting action will occur at this level. Hence, this level will determine the size of
the smoke shaft. [31] The stack effect in the shaft with an opening at the top of the shaft
is shown in Figure 2.5.

The smoke shaft size depends on the pressure loss across the lobby vent, the friction loss
through the shaft, the momentum pressure loss inside the shaft, and the pressure loss
through the shaft vent. The shaft can be seen as a chimney where the smoke has to flow
through.[31]
When the smoke flows into the shaft, it passes the lobby vent, which means that the
smoke has to flow through a decreased area; this results in a local pressure loss. As the
smoke has passed the lobby vent, the smoke flows through the shaft, and this means that
pressure loss will occur due to friction losses between the shaft surface and the smoke.
There is a requirement for leakage to ensure that the smoke does not spread from the
shaft to adjacent floors; however, a leakage will lead to pressure loss due to momentum
loss. At the top of the shaft, the smoke has to pass through the shaft vent, meaning that a
local pressure loss also will occur here.[31] There is a requirement for the minimum size
of the shaft’s internal restrictions in order to ensure that too large pressure losses do not
occur. [31]
The leakage is an essential parameter for the size of the smoke shaft. The taller the build-

ing, the smaller the leakage should be to ensure the smoke’s momentum so that it flows
through the shaft and out. This means that the more airtight the shaft is, the smaller the
shaft size, and the taller the building can be. [31]
The lobby vent should be located close to the ceiling because the hot smoke stratifies
near the ceiling due to buoyancy. [33]
According to the British Standards [18][19], the shaft’s size should be 1.5 m2 if the shaft
is used in a residential building to protect escape and routes and firefighting shaft, and it
should be 3.0 m2 if the shaft is used in a non-residential building to protect the firefighting
shaft. The journal article Basis for the Design of Smoke Shafts [31], states that the shaft
size can be 1.4 m2 if the size of the lobby vent, shaft vent, and shaft are the same and the
shaft is airtight, then the size should be efficient for up to a building height of 304.8 m. If
the size of the vents is smaller, then a larger size of the shaft is required. This size does
approximately fit with the requirement in the British Standard for residential buildings. [31]
[18][19] BRE’s research claims that the shaft should be of a size of 3 m2 to protect the
firefighting shaft for buildings up to 101 stories [34]. The research is based on physical
scale modeling and computational fluid dynamics models. This size corresponds to the
one which is a requirement by the British Standard [18][19].
The smoke shaft needs to extend a minimum of 2.5 m above the ceiling of the top story [1].
The shaft extension aims to ensure that other constructions on the roof will not limit the
smoke’s possibility to discharge through the shaft, e.g., by creating adverse wind effects
[35]. Another reason why the shaft should be extended corresponding to a height of one
story is to ensure that the shaft can draw smoke out from the top floor [34].
The hot smoke must not ignite the smoke shaft, therefore the shaft must be constructed
in A1 non-combustible material.
2.2
Pressurized staircase
This section will describe the principles of the pressurized staircase.
As earlier mentioned, the pressure increases in the fire-initiated apartment, leading to
an outflow of smoke from the compartment to the adjacent areas due to the pressure
difference and the smoke buoyancy. [32][31] To keep the staircase free of smoke, the
staircase can be pressurized. When pressurizing the staircase, the pressure inside the
staircase will rise, so the pressure is higher than in the adjacent areas. Hence the smoke
will flow into the release shaft instead of the staircase, meaning that the escape route will
be kept free from smoke. The system can also be designed so the smoke will flow into a
vent to the free or a mechanical extraction system instead of a release shaft. [2] [32]
In Figure 2.6, the principle of the staircase’s pressurization and the requirements for the
system are shown.
2.2. PRESSURIZED STAIRCASE
15
==PAGE:32==
Figure 2.6 shows the requirements for the pressurized staircase for a class B system. This
class B indicates that the pressurized staircase can be used to escape and for firefighting
operations. The pressurized staircase is activated in the following manner: a detector
in the lobby detects the smoke and sends a message to the fire indication panel. The
fire indication panel sends a message to the fire fan control panel. This fire fan control
panel activates the fan, the supply dampers in the staircase, the damper to the release
shaft on the fire-initiated floor, and the damper at the top of the shaft. Air is blown into
the staircase from supply dampers on multiple floors. This creates overpressure in the
staircase. It results in smoke being forced into the release shaft instead of into the stair-
case. If the staircase door is closed, the staircase’s pressure must not be too high, as it
can become impossible to open the staircase doors, making it impossible for people to
escape. Therefore, a maximum force of 100 N may be applied on the staircase door by
the overpressure. To ensure that the door is not affected by too great a force, a relief vent
is placed at the top of the staircase. The relief vent will open if the pressure is too high
in the staircase, in such case making it impossible to open the door. The relief vent will
usually be dynamic and open quickly until the pressure has decreased. [2]
If a staircase door is kept open, then the pressure in the staircase will decrease. This
is why there are two demands to determine the required air supply: a) when the door is
open and b) when the doors are closed. These demands are highlighted with grey text
in Figure 2.6. When determining the air supply for the pressurization, the airflow paths
should be identified. This means that for the situation a), the staircase door is open on
the fire-initiated floor, an adjacent floor, and the final exit door at the ground floor; these
open doors create a flow path where the air can escape. Additionally, there will be air
flows through the leakage. Leakage can occur through gaps around doors, windows, lift
landing doors, mechanical extraction systems, walls etc. For situation b), where the doors
are closed, the pressurization air can solely escape through the leakage areas. The air
supply should be determined for both systems, and the largest value plus a safety factor
becomes the required supply volume flow for the pressurization. This volume flow can be
determined according to the formulas in DS/EN 12101-6. [2]
The supply air should be discharged evenly in a building with a height above 11 m. In
buildings below 11 m in height, one supply damper is enough at the top of the staircase.
The supply damper should not be placed too closely to the final exit door on the ground
floor, as this can lead to the air flowing directly to the outside instead of creating the
overpressure in the staircase in case the exit door is kept open. Therefore, a supply
damper should be placed a minimum of 3 m above the exit door. The system should be
supplied with a secondary fan, which can take over if the primary fan fails to operate.
The release shaft and dampers’ size can be determined according to the formulas in
DS/EN 12101-6, depending on the required volume flow. Likewise, all the components
should follow the requirements according to the DS/EN 12101 series. Additionally, the
pressurized staircase should be tested before the building can be commissioned. [2]

The Danish building regulation requires installing a smoke control system in single stair

The apartment door to the lobby and the door from the lobby to the staircase are kept

By placing the fire-initiated apartment at different levels, it will be possible to consider if

3.1.2
Temperature
The smoke movement in a building is influenced by the stack effect, as mentioned in
Chapter 2. The pressure difference, created by the stack effect, is controlled by building
height and the thermal difference between outside and inside; therefore, the tempera-
ture conditions have a significant influence on whether the systems will function or not.
In Denmark, the outside temperature is rarely higher than the inside temperature. It has
therefore been decided to work with as realistic temperature conditions as possible.
The inside ambient temperature is set to 20°C, which is the temperature used to calcu-
late the thermal loss in buildings according to DS 418 [37]. Simultaneously, the outside
ambient temperature is set to 8.6°C, which is the 10-year average temperature according
to Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) [38].
3.1.3
Wind
The wind is a driving force. It can influence the stack effect, and thereby influence the
smoke control systems’ effectiveness. If a fire is wind-driven, the wind will push the smoke
into the building. However, it is imperative that the wind does not affect the smoke control
systems. When there is a wind pressure on the top of the building / on the roof, it is likely
that this wind pressure will have a larger force than the smoke itself, and thereby, the
wind will push the smoke down and into the building. Therefore, it is a requirement that
the shaft vent in the natural smoke shaft should not be affected by adverse wind effects.
[18] [19] For the pressurized staircase, the condition is that the air intake and exhaust
shall be designed to be independent of the wind and the direction of the wind. [2]
Usually, suction will occur on a building roof. However, wind pressure can occur, if the

3.1.4
The fire scenario
Three different types of fires have been modeled in the middle of the fire-initiated apart-
ment to determine the two different smoke control systems’ performance. The reason is
that different kinds of fuels change the properties of the fire, including smoke production.
A fire occurs due to a chemical reaction between pyrolysis gases and oxygen [40] . Before
a fire, a pyrolysis process is going on. The pyrolysis is a decomposition of the material
due to heat. However, the pyrolysis process can happen in the absence of oxygen. [41]
The product of a pyrolysis process is both a pyrolyzate gas and solid products (see equa-
tion 3.1) [41].
Fuel(solid) + Heat →Pyrolyzate(gas) + Char(solid) + Ash(solid)
(3.1)
Oxidation of the solid char product from the pyrolysis process is a heterogeneous oxida-
tion which leads to smoldering combustion (see equation 3.2) [41].
Char(solid) + O2(gas) →Heat + CO2 + H2O + other gases + Ash(solid)
(3.2)
Oxidation of the pyrolyzate gas from the pyrolysis process is a gas-phase oxidation which
leads to a flaming combustion (see equation 3.3) [41].
Pyrolyzate(gas) + O2(gas) →Heat + CO2 + other gases
(3.3)
A smoldering fire’s characteristics are that the combustion process happens slowly, the
temperature of the smoke is low, and it is flameless. Typically, smoldering fires occur in
porous fuels, e.g., polyurethane foam, which produces many toxic gases, e.g., carbon
monoxide, due to the incomplete combustion process. A fire in a residence is often a
smoldering fire [42]. If the level of oxygen available for the smoldering fire increases,
the smoldering fire can convert to a flaming fire [27][41]. The flaming fire can convert to
a flashover fire if the temperature at the ceiling level reaches 500-600°C or if the heat
flux to the floor exceeds 20 kW/m2. A flashover fire’s characteristics are that all exposed
surfaces reach ignition temperature more or less simultaneously, whereas the fire spread
rapidly.[27]

Verification
FDS is a well-known and verified computational fluid dynamic (CFD) program for the sim-

mathematically correctness, whereas FDS is for the purpose of this project considered to

Results & Discussion
This chapter examines the simulation results and discussion. Initially, the basis for the
evaluation is presented, then the results of the simulation scenarios are presented. Along

4.1
Acceptance criteria
In this section, the criteria for evaluation of the smoke control system’s performance are
presented. The acceptance criteria presented are the criteria found in the BR18 demon-
stration guidance [4]. As the aim is to evaluate whether the natural smoke shaft can be
applied in Denmark as a smoke control system to protect single stairs against smoke
entrainment. The acceptance criteria are set to ensure tenable conditions in the escape
route during an evacuation, and are listed below.[4]
• Visibility at 2 m above floor level should be greater than 10 m
• The free height to the smoke layer should minimum be 2 m
• The temperature in the smoke layer and air may not exceed 80°C at 2 m above floor
level
• Exposure to radiation 2 m above floor level should not exceed
2.5 kW/m2 for the long term
10 kW/m2 for a maximum of 4 s (short term)
The acceptance criteria should be evaluated at the following height above the floor level
as described in the equation 4.1, but as BR18 states [5], the elevation above the floor
level for the critical conditions should be minimum at 2 m; hence the acceptance criteria
are evaluated at 2 m above the floor level.
H∞= 1.6 m + 0.1 · H = 1.6 m + 0.1 · 2.7 m = 1.87 m < 2 m
(4.1)
where
H∞is the minimum height which should be smoke free [m]
H is the room height [m]
The acceptance criteria thresholds have been set to ensure that people can escape with-
out being harmed by the fire. When a fire is ignited, smoke is produced while the burning
41
==PAGE:58==
material decomposes. Due to the buoyancy, the smoke rises into a plume where the am-
bient air is entrained. When the smoke hits the ceiling, a ceiling jet is formed. As the fire is
raging, more smoke is formed, and the smoke layer gets thicker. When the smoke layer
height in the fire-initiated room gets below the doors’ height, the hot smoke containing
toxic by-products is spreading to the surrounding areas. [52]
A significant killer in domestic fires is not the heat from the smoke or fire but the toxic
gases produced by the burning combustibles. These toxic gases can be irritant or nar-
cotic depending on which gas it is (e.g., CO, HCN…). Before the toxic gasses affect the
evacuees, the smoke lowers the visibility and therefore reduces movement speed of the
evacuees. Therefore, an acceptance criterion is set to ensure that the smoke layer height
is approximately above the height and visibility criteria of a human to ensure that occu-
pants can find their way out. [52]
If the smoke is hot, there is a risk of evacuating occupants being exposed to the heat both
by convection and radiation. The high temperatures can cause skin burns or hyperther-
mia, which can lead to incapacitation and death. If the core temperature of a human body
exceeds 42.5°C, it can results in unconsciousness and death. Likewise, a higher temper-
ature at the escape routes makes it more difficult for occupants to escape because they
will walk slower, which will increase the evacuation time. Therefore, a threshold for the
temperature is set to 80°C at 2 m height in the escape routes. For this case, a threshold
of 2.5 kW/m2 or 10 kW/m2 is also set for the maximum radiation a human may be exposed
to from the hot smoke layer, for long and short term, respectively. [52]

4.4
Summary
In this chapter the results from the simulations were presented and discussed. Results
showed that wind-driven fires have an essential impact on the performance of the natural
smoke shaft. However, the wind has been added in a simplified manner, whereas no final
conclusion can be drawn based on the wind scenarios. This indicates that the natural
smoke shaft’s performance should be investigated further according to the wind effect.
The pressurized staircase should also be tested in the same manner to conclude whether
the wind impacts the smoke control systems. When discounting the wind scenarios, the
simulation results showed that the conditions were tenable at the stairs’ flight in simulation
series NSS45, PS24, and PS45.
Nonetheless, critical conditions did occur in the simulation series NSS24 for the large
fire. These results show that the building’s height impacts the performance of the natural
smoke shaft, as the stack effect is less intense the lower the building. However, the crit-
ical conditions only occur on the fire-initiated floor, whereas the smoke does not spread
to the adjacent floors within the simulated time.
For all the scenarios except for the small fire scenarios, smoke was entrained in the stair-
case just before the smoke control system was fully operating. When the smoke control
systems started to function, the staircase’s entrained smoke got sucked into the shaft.
The volume flow to the shaft was most considerable for the pressurized staircase. The
reason is that the smoke is forced into the shaft by the mechanically driven fans, and the
system gains strength from the stack effect. For the natural smoke shaft, the lobby vent
had the largest volume flow into the tall building’s shaft when the fire was placed at the
ground floor. This is again due to the strength of the stack effect. This investigation shows
that the larger the volume flow to the shaft is, the more efficient the smoke control system
is. It showed that for the scenarios where there was no smoke entrained in the middle of
the flight of the stairs on the fire-initiated floor, the volume flow to the shaft was greater
than for the scenarios where smoke was entrained at the flight of the stairs.

Conclusion
The project aimed to assess whether a natural smoke shaft would be an applicable alter-
native for a pressurized staircase to protect the escape routes in single stair buildings in
Denmark. Thirty-two simulation models have been established to determine the perfor-
mance of both a natural smoke shaft following Approved Document B clause 3.49-3.53
[1] and a pressurized staircase following DS/EN 12101-6 [2]. Different simulation scenar-
ios were carried out to determine the performance of the smoke control systems. In the
scenarios, the variable parameters were the smoke control system, the building’s height,
the level of the fire-initiated apartment, the fire size, and whether the fire was wind-driven.
The smoke control system was either a natural smoke shaft or a pressurized staircase.
Two different building heights were modeled, one of 27 m and one of 48 m, corresponding
to 24 m and 45 m to the top floor from the ground. The building heights were chosen so
they relate to the lower and upper limit of the prescriptive criteria where a pressurized
staircase can be applied in a single stair building according to the Danish Building Regu-
lation [3]. A fire-initiated apartment was modeled on two different levels: the ground floor
and approximately 2/3 of the buildings height, to measure the fire-initiated level’s influence
on the smoke control system. The fire size was varied between a smoldering 5 kW fire,
a small furniture fire of 500 kW, and a whole apartment fire of 5000 kW. In the simulation
for the 5000 kW fire, the windows in the apartment were set to break if the temperature or
pressure exceeded a pre-defined threshold. In the fires where the windows would break,
two scenarios were simulated, one where no wind affect was considered and another
where a simplified wind pressure was modeled to create a wind-driven fire.
The simulation results showed that critical conditions occurred in some scenarios with the
natural smoke shaft. In comparison, no critical conditions occurred in scenarios with the
pressurized staircase as the smoke control system. The natural smoke shaft scenarios
where critical conditions occurred were the low building for all the large fires independent
of the fire-initiated apartment location. Critical conditions also occurred where the large
fire was wind-driven in the low building. In the tall building, critical conditions only occurred
in one scenario. The scenario was the one with a large fire located in an apartment ap-
proximately 2/3 of the building height, and it was wind-affected. However, the wind was
added in a simplified manner, which means that a final conclusion should not be based
on the wind-driven fire scenarios. Hence, the outcome from these simulations should be
that a wind-driven fire will affect the natural smoke shaft’s performance, and further inves-
tigations should be carried out.
The simulations indicate that the fire size significantly affects the natural smoke shaft’s
performance. This could be related to the shaft’s capacity and suction effect. Another pa-
99
==PAGE:116==
rameter which effects the performance of the natural smoke shaft is the building’s height.
The reason is that the stack effect and the smokes buoyancy drives the smoke through
the shaft, meaning that the taller the building is, the greater the suction in the shaft will
be. This is why the simulations generally show an increased effectiveness of the natural
smoke shaft in the tall building simulations. The reason critical conditions occurred in one
wind-driven fire scenario in the tall building is that the wind forced an increased volume of
smoke into the building, meaning that if the suction in the shaft is not great enough, the
smoke will be forced into the staircase.
Risk , monetary and floor area assessments were established. The risk assessment was
based on the simulation results for the low building. It showed that the natural smoke shaft
has a similar risk level as the pressurized staircase, although the pressurized staircase
performed better in the simulations. The risk level is similar because the natural smoke
shaft itself has a lower probability of failure than the pressurized staircase.
The construction- and maintenance costs cannot be completely conclusive as they are
based on a price from one contractor. It is estimated that the construction cost will be
27% less expensive for the natural smoke shaft, and the maintenance cost is 33% less
expensive than the pressurized staircase. The costs not included in the maintenance
estimation are: reparation of malfunctioning components, quarterly system test, and ac-
credited inspections and service of the automatic fire alarm. Although adding these costs
would change the percentage reduction proportion, the fewer and generally simpler com-
ponents in the natural smoke shaft system will likely result in a cheaper maintenance
regardless. However, the construction- and maintenance costs are based on only one
contractors prices, whereas these values are more of a crude estimate of the cost.
There will be a reduction of 60% of square meters on each level by using the natural
smoke shaft as the smoke control system. This means that the natural smoke shaft takes
up fewer square meters, resulting in more useable square meters in the building.
This project shows that the benefit of a natural smoke shaft to protect a single staircase
might be that it is less expensive to install and maintain, and has a risk level similar to that
of a pressurized staircase. The natural smoke shaft’s weakness is that it is sensitive to
weather conditions; both wind and temperature can affect its performance. The limitations
of the smoke shaft are that it might not be feasible in low buildings. The weather conditions
and the optimal building height for tenable performance by the natural smoke shaft need
to be investigated further. On the other hand, the pressurized staircase is also sensitive
to weather conditions, as the stack effect affects the system. However, the pressurized
staircase has the benefit of having fans, which create pressure to protect the staircase
from smoke; though the pressurized staircase is sensitive to pressure losses, which could
occur if multiple staircase doors are open. In contrast, multiple open staircase doors will
not affect the natural smoke shaft.
Based on this study, it can be concluded that a natural smoke shaft may be beneficial in
certain cases because it has comparable risk levels and is less expensive both in con-
struction and maintenance than the pressurized staircase. However, further investigation
is needed to determine its robustness and application, as there is a potential risk that its
performance will be significantly affected by certain conditions, such as placement rela-
tive to other buildings, wind direction and weather in general.

6.1
Further study
The robustness of the natural smoke shaft should be investigated further. Things such as
temperature and wind influence the effectiveness of the natural smoke shaft. Therefore,
the natural smoke shaft should be tested with a reverse stack effect, meaning that the
initial inside temperature is lower than the outside temperature. The robustness of the
smoke control systems should also be investigated for different wind effects and different
sizes of wind-driven fires. Likewise, the system should be tested for cases where fire
occurs on multiple stories. The optimum building height for the natural smoke shaft should
be investigated by simulating different building heights to find the optimum levels where
the natural smoke shaft has an acceptable performance.
Furthermore, a more detailed risk assessment should be made, where the likelihood of
the critical weather conditions is considered.
