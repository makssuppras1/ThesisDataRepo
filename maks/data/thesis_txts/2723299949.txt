Introduction
In this thesis, we look into an attack on the Code-based cryptosystem BCH-McEliece, which
uses BCH codes instead of the originally intended Goppa codes. The BCH-McEliece variation
is closely related to the original design of McEliece, which currently is competing against
other cryptographic systems to become the new standard for Post-Quantum Cryptography
(PQC). The attack which will be described in detail is proposed by Faugère et. al[1].
Before the attack by Faugère et. al[1] is described, we present some of the required theory
related to the domain of the thesis. The presented theory covers concepts from discreet
mathematics, coding theory and cryptography. Once the presented theory has been covered,
the algebraic modelling for recovering the cryptographic key and the attack by Faugère et.
al[1] will be presented. Once the attack has been presented, the design choices for the two
implemented algorithms will be covered. The algorithms will be used to find the longest
L sets.
These L sets are used throughout the attack[1].
Finally, the findings from the
experiments will be presented and discussed.

1.1
Motivation
Prior to the modern era, cryptography focused on message confidentiality, which converted
comprehensible messages to incomprehensible ciphers and back again, thus rendering the
confidential ciphers unreadable for any interceptors, eavesdroppers or adversarial parties
whom didn’t possess the secret knowledge required to turn the cipher back into its compre-
hensible form.
The classical types of ciphers include Transposition- and Substitution ciphers which respec-
tively rearranges the order letters in the messages or systematically substitutes letters with
other letters. One of the earliest accounts of cryptography being used were by the roman
historian Gaius Suetonis Tranquillus who reported that Julius Caesar used substitution ci-
phers to communicate with his generals, which fittingly was called the Caesar cipher.
However as times progresses, such simplistic ciphers were no longer able to confidentially
communicate messages between parties and thus new systems had to be developed. During
the 20th century many mechanical encryption devices were invented. One of the more note-
worthy rotor machines were the Enigma machine which was used by German government
officials and military personnel from the late 1920’s and throughout World war II.
However as time has moved on, and we entered the "Information Age", cryptology had to
be improved in order to withstand the test of time. By then modern cryptology had drawn
in aspects from all kinds of disciplines such as mathematics, physics, information theory,
digital signal processing, electrical engineering and many more.
Over the last couple of years has NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) been
in charge of defining the standards and requirements within the United States for the cryp-
tology used within computer systems. In addition to having to define and propose standards
for today’s cryptology, NIST has also called for proposals for the Post-Quantum Cryptol-
ogy (PQC), as more research has been put into the development of Quantum computers,
i.e. machines capable of exploiting quantum mechanical phenomenon to solve mathematical
problems. For instance quantum computers would be able to completely break the existing
Public-key cryptosystems, such as the RSA, DSA and Elliptic curve cryptosystems, using
Shor’s Algorithm.

2.1
Modular Arithmetic
Modular arithmetic is a system of arithmetic for integers, where numbers wrap around once
they reach or surpass a certain value, called the modulus. In modular arithmetic, the result
of an operation will always result in an integer in the range of [0; m −1], where m is the
modulus.
Definition 2.1 (Modular Arithmetic). Two integers a and b are said to be congruent
modulo m, if m is a divisor of their difference, i.e. let a and b be integers and let m
be a positive integer. We then say:
a ≡b (mod m) if m divides (b −a)
This means that for a positive modulus m and any integer a it holds a ≡a+m mod m and
similarly km ≡0 mod m for any integer k. Using the knowledge about modular arithmetic,
we are able to define sets based on the modulus m which consists of all positive integers less
than m.
Definition 2.2. Let m be a positive integer. The set Zm is defined as the set of
numbers between [0; m −1]:
Zm = {0, 1, ...m −1}
Modular arithmetic is used in many areas of mathematics, including number theory, cryp-
tography, and computer science. It is also used in everyday life, for example in calculating
the day of the week for a given date, or in clock arithmetic (where the modulus is 12 for
hours and 60 for minutes).
In cryptography, modular arithmetic is used to create and decrypt codes. For example, the
RSA algorithm, which is widely used for secure communication, relies on the difficulty of
factoring large numbers to encrypt and decrypt messages. Whereas, in computer science,
modular arithmetic is used to implement efficient algorithms for performing arithmetic op-
erations on large numbers, such as multiplication and division. It is also used to implement
hash functions, which are used to store and retrieve data in computer databases.

2.2
Fields
A field is any set of elements on which addition, subtraction, multiplication and division are
all defined and behave as the corresponding operations would on rational and real numbers.
Fields provides us with a basic building block within mathematics as it allows us to assign
certain characteristics and properties to a set of numbers. A fields which is restricted to a
finite set of members is known as a finite field or Galois field.
Definition 2.3 (Field). A field F is defined as a set of elements that is closed under
the additive and multiplicative operators and satisfies the following properties:
1. Additive associativity: ∀a, b, c ∈S | (a + b) + c = a + (b + c)
2. Additive commutativity: ∀a, b ∈S | a + b = a + b
3. Additive identity: ∃0 ∈S | ∀a ∈S | 0 + a = a + 0 = a
4. Additive inverse: ∀a ∈S, ∃(−a) ∈S | a + (−a) = (−a) + a = 0
5. Left and right distributivity:
∀a, b, c ∈S | a · (b + c) = (a · b) + (a · c) ∧(b + c) · a = (b · a) + (c · a)
6. Multiplicative associativity: ∀a, b, c ∈S | (a · b) · c = a · (b · c)
7. Multiplicative commutativity: ∀a, b ∈S | a · b = b · a
8. Multiplicative identity: ∃1 ∈S | ∀a ̸= 0 ∈S, 1 · a = a · 1 = a
9. Multiplicative inverse: ∀a ̸= 0 ∈S, ∃a−1 ∈S | ∀a ̸= 0 ∈S, a · a−1 = a−1 · a = 1
Unlike rings, a field has to adhere to all 9 properties, whereas rings have to adhere to
properties 1-5 in order to become rings and can optionally satisfy properties 6, 8 and 9 to
become a division algebra or skew field.
2.2.1
Galois Field
A finite field, also called Galois field, is a field with a finite field order i.e. number of ele-
ments. The field order of Galois fields is always expressed as either a prime or a power of a
prime. Such Galois fields are denoted as GF(pn), or alternatively written as Fpn.
GF(p) is called the prime field of order p, and describes the field of residue classes modulo p,
i.e. a = b in GF(p) is equivalent to a ≡b mod p. It should be noted that a ring of residues
modulo x is distinct from the Galois field with x elements, e.g 2 · 2 ≡0 mod 4, since 2 has
no reciprocal, which is one of the nine properties which fields has to adhere to, namely the
9’th property. Therefore it should be noted that Galois fields are denoted GF(pn) instead
of GF(k) where k = pn.

2.3
Error Correcting Codes
Error correcting codes are an encoding scheme used in conjunction with binary data, such
that if some bits are erroneously flipped during transit or during storage, the recipient will
be able to retrieve the original data from the encoding. Error correcting codes are typically
used in the transmission and storage of binary messages where data corruption may occur,
i.e.
misinterpretation during message decoding from transmission or data corruption on
storage devices. As the name implies, error correcting codes are used to ensure accuracy and
integrity of data in noisy media that can introduce errors to the data.
Error correcting codes are accomplished through the addition of redundant information to
the original data.
Error correcting codes comes in different implementations, however the two main categories
of error correcting codes are: Block codes and Convolutional codes. The differences between
block codes and convolutional codes lies in the encoding approach.
Block codes divide the data into blocks of a fixed size and add extra bits, called parity bits,
to each block. The parity bits are chosen such that the resulting block of data satisfies

a convolutional encoder, to the data. The convolutional encoder generates a sequence of
symbols that can be used to detect and correct errors.
2.4
Linear Codes
Definition 2.4 (Code). An error correcting code or block code C of length n over a
finite alphabet Σ is a subset of Σn. The elements of C are called the codewords in C,
and the collection of all codewords is called a codebook.
The alphabet of C is Σ, and if |Σ| = q, the code C is then a q-ary code. When q = 2,
C is a binary code. The length n of the codewords of C is called the block length of C.
Apart from being able to describe the entire alphabet as q-ary code, general codes do not
provide any innate structure over the alphabet. However, one important subclass of codes
called Linear codes is able to provide the structure which general codes lack. Contrary to
general codes, linear codes are defined over alphabets Σ which are finite fields Fq with q
elements, where q is a prime power.
Definition 2.5 (Linear code). If Σ is a field and C ⊂Σn is a subspace of Σn then C
is said to be a linear code.
Since C is a subspace of the alphabet Σ, there exists a basis vector set c1, c2, ..., ck where k
is the dimension of the subspace. Thus, taking these basis vectors it is possible to express
the whole codebook as linear combinations of said basis vectors. Alternatively, these vectors
can be placed in matrix form as columns in a n × k matrix, which is commonly denoted as
a Generator matrix.

2.9
McEliece
The McEliece cryptosystem is a public-key cryptography (PKC) system proposed by Robert
J. McEliece in 1978 and is the first cryptosystem which is based on the algebraic structure
of error correcting codes.
As of today, the McEliece cryptosystem is one among a handful of other algorithms which
have entered into the "Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization" competition organized
by the National Institute of Standards and Technologies (NIST), which are compet-
ing to become part of the new standards for "Post-Quantum Cryptography" (PQC) public
key encryption. The classic implementation of the McEliece has as of July 5 2022, made it
into the fourth round of the PQC standardization round and continues to compete against
2 other code-based implementations namely "BIKE" and "HQC".
The McEliece cryptosystem consists of two algorithms: Key generation and Encryption/De-
cryption. In the Key Generation algorithm, the user generates a public key and a private
key.
The public key is a random looking generator matrix for a Goppa code, given by
an arbitrary Goppa polynomial with degree t. The private key consists of the previously
mentioned Goppa polynomial, and the matrices S and P. In the Encryption/Decryption
algorithm, the sender encrypts the message by adding a randomly chosen error vector to the
original message. The receiver is then able to recover the original message using the public
key and error vector. More formally, the general algorithm for the McEliece cryptosystem is
as followed:
• Choose System Parameters: n, t ∈N where t ≪n
• Key Generation: Given the parameters n, t generate the following matrices:
G : k × n generator matrix of code G over F of dimension k with a minimal hamming
distance of d ≥2t + 1
S : k × k random binary non-singular matrix
P : n × n random permutation matrix
Gpub : k × n computed as SGP
• Public Key: (Gpub, t)
• Private key: (S, DG, P), where DG is an efficient decoding algorithm for G
• Encryption: (E (Gpub,t)) used to encrypt a given plaintext m ∈Fk based on a randomly
chosen vector z ∈Fn with a weight of t. The ciphertext c can be computed as followed:
c = mGpub ⊕z
• Decryption: (D(S,DG,P)) used to decrypt a ciphertext c. First calculate
cP−1 = (mS)G ⊕zP−1

2.9.1
Advantages and limitations
The McEliece cryptosystem has several advantages over other PKC systems. For starters,
the cryptosystem remains resilient against attacks from quantum computers, due to the al-
gebraic structure of error correcting codes being robust against quantum algorithms, unlike
RSA which has become obsolete due to Shor’s algorithm being able to crack the RSA system
in O((logN)2(loglogN)) operations. Additionally, computational complexity of the McEliece
cryptosystem is relatively low compared to other PKCs, which makes it suitable for resource
constrained environments such as embedded systems and mobile devices.
However, despite having all the aforementioned advantages and benefits compared to other
PKCs, it remains impractical for real world cases, due to its key sizes. A typical public key
for the McEliece cryptosystem is several hundred kilobytes in size, which makes it impractical
to use in applications due to the large memory requirement compared to other PKC systems
used today.

Remaining Steps in the Attack
2.13
Remaining Steps in the Attack
2.13.1
Gröbner Basis
The objective for the Gröbner bassis algorithm is to find vector spaces of maximal dimension
in ZFqm(Pu ∪L). As the set ZFqm(Pu ∪L) contain at least one vector space of dimension
|L|, it is possible to fix |L| variables and find a solution in each vector space of maximal
dimension. The Gröbner basis performed is |L| times, where each time |L| variables are fixed
differently, in order to find |L| sets of solutions. These solution sets are then used to obtain
a basis for each of the maximal vector spaces.
2.13.2
Fröbenius Alignment
The goal of the Fröbenius alignment is to take |L| solutions sets given by the Gröbner basis,
and pick one solution from each solution set, such that the collected vectors W forms a basis
for spanFqm{x ℓ: ℓ∈L}. This can be solved using a brute-force search, however the runtime
of this is exponential, thus will become infeasible when |L| grows. Hence [3] develops an
algorithm with polynomial runtime in |L|.
2.13.3
Detanglement
The Detanglement step in the attack on BCH-McEliece is the step in the attack where the
secret key x can be recovered from the knowledge of a basis W for spanFqm{x ell | ℓ∈
L}. The idea of the step is an iterative approach, where linear operations and vectorspace
intersections are used to iteratively decrease the size of W while maintaining the structure
of the vectorspace.

Greedy Algorithm
3.2
Greedy Algorithm
The greedy algorithm is based on the concept of a Best-First, Greedy or Fast algorithm, as
the algorithm use no additional information and simply adds elements together which fulfil
the u-sum condition one after another.
In the initial state of the algorithm L = {}, then for each x ∈Su, we first check if the value
itself passes the u-sum condition. If the value x passes the u-sum check, we then iteratively
check for each element in Su (other than the x) if they pass the u-sum check, thereby in-
creasing the L set element by element by each value which passes the u-sum check.
The algorithm performs this procedure for each element x ∈Su, meaning that the output
from the algorithm is a greedily chosen L set for each x ∈Su. By "greedily chosen" it is
meant that, the algorithm does not consider the case where the addition of one element x1
may produce a shorter L set compared to the addition x2, i.e., the algorithm naively adds
elements together when it creates the L sets.

3.3
Frontier Algorithm
3.3
Frontier Algorithm
The idea behind the frontier algorithm was to construct the L set in an organized and struc-
tured way. This algorithm guarantees to find all L’s of maximal size, by exploring all subsets
of the set Su which passes the u-sum condition. In order to achieve this two types of data
structures were used to; remove redundant u-sum checks on subsets and explore the subsets
in an organized fashion. The data structures used to do this were: a queue (frontier) and a
dictionary (foundGroups, lSets).
As mentioned before the frontier is used to systematically explore the L sets. The frontier
contains the tuples of sets and values (set, val) which we want to perform the u-sum check
on. New tuples are added to the frontier whenever the subset we are currently evaluating
passes the u-sum check. Whenever we expand the frontier the new tuples added to the
frontier are all the tuples in the set {(set ∪val, x) | x ∈Su}.
Dictionaries are general purpose data structures used for storing a group of objects. The
objects in the dictionary are stored using distinct keys, where each key has a single associated
value. In the algorithm we use the distinct subsets of Su as keys in the dictionaries to swiftly
obtain information about a given subset. The purpose of each dictionary is defined as follows:
• foundGroups: contains all explored subsets of Su as keys with True, False or None as
their values denoting if they have passed the u-sum check
• lSets: contains all L sets of Su as keys and length of each L sets as its value.
As mentioned above foundGroups contains all explored subsets of Su which either have been
evaluated by the u-sum check or not. Whenever a subset is explored, the value associated
with the subset is initialized to None as the subset has not been u-sum checked. Once the
subset has been evaluated the associated value will be updated based on the result from the
u-sum check.
It should be noted that lSets can be contructed from foundGroups by taking grabbing all
keys which values are equal to True. The purpose of lSets is merely for convenience, whereas
foundGroups is used to remove redundant u-sum checks on subsets we have previously eval-
uated.
The frontier algorithm works by initially looking at the empty subset of Su and add it to the
frontier queue. The algorithm then pops the (subset, val) tuple from the frontier and check
the dictionary foundGroups to see if the subset previously have been evaluated in regards to
the u-sum check, in order to avoid redundant computations. Here three different scenarios
can occur:
1. subset has passed u-sum check
2. subset has not passed u-sum check
3. subset has not been evaluated

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have presented the mathematical background behind the cryptographic
encryption scheme that is McEliece, including galois fields, coding theory and cryptography.
From the presented material, we have described the cryptographic attack on the variation of
McEliece which uses BCH codes instead of the intended Goppa codes. Thereby, we have ob-
tained insights in regards to the benefits of Goppa codes and more importantly the strengths
associated with carefully selected parameters for the cryptographic system.
Additionally, we have presented two different algorithms for finding L sets, which are used
to compute the dimension of vector spaces in the solutions set of the polynomial system
used for the key recovery. The presented algorithms have been described in texts such that
design choices for the algorithms’ design should appear clear.
Finally, we have conducted experiments, which objective was to
1. Benchmark the performance of the algorithms.
2. Investigate how L changes when r is not q in BCH-McEliece.
These experiments provided material for understanding the growth of L sets and the asso-
ciated Su and S sets based on different values for r. Here we found that choosing r ̸= q
produced L sets of smaller length, which would be easier to break compared to codes where
r = q.
