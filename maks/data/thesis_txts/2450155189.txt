Abstract 
 
Drinking water is one of the most important resources required for survival by human 
beings, and yet, 884 million people lack access to safe and clean drinking water. Majority 
of the population without access to clean and safe drinking water resides in rural areas or 
low socio – economic zones where the water sources are usually contaminated with 
pathogenic bacteria. Removal of these pathogens would go a long way in providing clean 
drinking water, however, these areas do not have the required capital or technology to set 
up conventional drinking water treatment. For this reason, various studies have assessed 
the utility of alternate drinking water treatment methods such as filtration, chlorine tablets 
and solar disinfection for removal of pathogens. 
 
In this thesis, the feasibility of two solar disinfection (SODIS) reactors for household water 
treatment to cater to a community. The reactors were evaluated based on their efficiency 
for inactivation of Escherichia coli, total coliforms, Enterococci and Vibrio spp. and the 
costs incurred for running a reactor for one year. The reactors used for solar disinfection 
were raceway pond reactor (RPR) and compound parabolic collector (CPC). The test water 
was bottled mineral water inoculated with urban wastewater influent. The solar 
disinfection experiments were conducted at the University of Cadiz in March – April 2019. 
The cost estimation was performed for CPC and RPR reactors with a treatment capacity 
of 100 m3/h, which would cater to approximately 1500 – 2000 people.  
 
The inactivation was evaluated as a LOG10 reduction in the pathogen concentration in the 
test water. Results of inactivation showed a better performance by the CPC. It required a 
smaller amount of time and a lower dose to reach the detection limit whereas the in the 
RPR the detection limit was reached more consistently, albeit with a higher dose required 
and a longer time period. The cost estimation, however, showed that the investment and 
operation and maintenance (O&M) costs of the RPR would be much cheaper compared to 
the CPC. This study concluded that a raceway pond reactor would therefore be a more 
feasible option for household water treatment using solar disinfection. 
 
Keywords: Solar disinfection, drinking water treatment, pathogen removal, cost 
estimation

1 Introduction 
 
Water is one of the most important resources required by human beings. Humans use water 
for a variety of household purposes such as cooking, drinking, cleaning, washing. Water is 
used not only for household purposes, but also in industries such as manufacturing and 
textiles. Water has an important role to play in every walk of life. It is one of the most 
important resources required for survival and sustenance as human beings cannot live 
without drinking water for more than three or four days. However, due to the rapid increase 
in human activities and the subsequent effects on the environment, easy availability of 
drinking water is under threat. The increased levels of pollution have contaminated water 
sources and made them unfit for drinking without proper treatment.  
 
According the United Nations (UN), the current population of the world is 7.7 billion (United 
Nations, 2017), out of which 3.4 billion live in rural areas (United Nations, 2018). These 
areas usually lack sophisticated water treatment facilities and people are forced to obtain 
drinking water from sources that might be contaminated. According to a report by the WHO 
and UNICEF, 8 out of 10 people in rural areas still lack access to improved water sources 
(WHO & UNICEF, 2017). An improved water source, as defined by the WHO, is a source 
of water that, by nature of its construction, adequately protects water from outside 
contamination, especially faecal matter (WHO & UNICEF, 2012). Sustainable Development 
Goal target 6 calls for universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water. 
The target is tracked with the indicator of “safely managed drinking water services” – 
drinking water from an improved water source that is located on premises, available when 
needed, and free from faecal and priority chemical contamination. As per the latest report, 
2.1 billion people still lack access to safely managed drinking water services. However, out 
of this 2.1 billion, 1.3 billion had access to a basic drinking water service, which is an 
improved water source not more than 30 minutes away. (United Nations, 2018). This means 
that 844 million still lack access to a basic drinking water service. These people either have 
to travel long distances to acquire drinking water from an improved water source or obtain 
their drinking water from unprotected wells and springs or untreated surface water from 
lakes, rivers and canals.  
 
Safe drinking water is critical to the development of a healthy child. Ingestion of 
contaminated water from unprotected wells and springs or untreated surface water leads to 
sickness and diseases like cholera, diarrhoea, dysentery, hepatitis A, typhoid, and polio. 
Diarrhoea is one of the top three leading causes of child death in developing countries and 
this is often triggered from consuming unclean water. Further, every 2 minutes a child dies 
from a water-borne disease. Whether they are consuming contaminated water or suffering 
from dehydration due to diarrhoea, a lack of access to safe water is responsible (WHO, 
2018). Access to clean drinking water would mean reduced child and maternal mortality 
rates. It means reduced physical injury from constant lifting and carrying heavy loads of 
water A lack of access to safe water and sanitation significantly affects the health of women 
as well. Burdened daily by water collection and finding a place to go, women and girls spend 
large amounts of time carrying heavy vessels and walking long distances. The physical strain 
of these activities impacts their health and if pregnant, the health of their unborn children 
(Graham et al., 2016).

When water comes from improved and more accessible sources, people spend less time and 
effort physically collecting it, meaning they can be productive in other ways. This can also 
result in greater personal safety by reducing the need to make long or risky journeys to collect 
water. Better water sources also mean less expenditure on health, as people are less likely to 
fall ill and incur medical costs and are better able to remain economically productive. With 
children particularly at risk from water-related diseases, access to improved sources of water 
can result in better health, and therefore better school attendance, with positive longer-term 
consequences for their lives (WHO & UNICEF, 2017). 
 
Universal access to safe and clean drinking water is a fundamental need and a human right. 
Hence, it is very important to make sure people from all walks of life have access to clean 
drinking water. As most of those who currently lack access reside in rural and low-income 
areas it is important to develop or set up treatment methods which are simple and incur lower 
costs. Conventional drinking water treatment methods such as artificial UV disinfection, 
ozonation and chlorination require a large amount of capital investment, sophisticated 
equipment and skilled operators and hence might be unfeasible to set up in rural areas. To 
know what kind of treatment method is needed in rural areas, it is important to know what 
kind of contaminants need to be removed. The water sources most used in these areas are 
either unprotected wells and springs or untreated surface water sources and hence are usually 
contaminated by faecal bacteria due to presence of unsatisfactory sanitation (WHO & 
UNICEF, 2012). Hence, appropriate household water treatment and storage (HWTS) 
interventions are required to reduce the risk of contaminants affecting human health. The 
HWTS methods must, however, satisfy certain criteria in order to be accepted. The criteria 
are listed below – 
1. Low cost – Usually only the poorest communities tend to be affected. 
2. Easy to use – Complicated protocol will reduce acceptance. 
3. Sustainable – Required equipment must not be expensive or difficult to obtain 
 
The methods which fulfil these criteria are boiling, filtration and use of chlorine tablets. 
However, one more technology has recently seen an increase in popularity. This method is 
solar water disinfection. 
 
Solar disinfection uses the ultraviolet radiation present in visible light to harm the DNA or 
pathogenic bacteria. This method has been successfully studied by many researchers using 
plastic bottles and bags as the reactors for individual households and in the event of natural 
disasters or war. Flow reactors and advanced oxidation processes have also been tested to 
treat larger volumes or to enhance the efficiency of this technology. Despite the extensive 
research in this field, there remains a large gap between laboratory and field experiments to 
upscale SODIS for treatment of larger volumes. Due to the higher costs incurred by flow 
reactors or enhancement technologies, they would not be feasible for use in individual 
households. However, they might provide a sustainable soluble at a community scale. This 
has created the need to study the feasibility of solar disinfection for household water 
treatment at a community scale.

2 Objective and Scope of Research 
 
The primary objective of this research is to evaluate solar disinfection of drinking water as 
a household water treatment method to cater to a community of up to 2000 people. The study 
was conducted in two parts – efficiency of solar disinfection to remove pathogens and 
evaluation of investment and operation and maintenance costs of a solar disinfection reactor.  
 
Two reactors, namely, a raceway pond reactor and a compound parabolic collector reactor, 
were tested for disinfection of drinking water contaminated with pathogenic bacteria. An 
estimation of the investment and operation and maintenance costs for 100 m3/h of 
contaminated drinking water for a duration on one year was also done for both reactors. 
 
The results of this research will form the basis for selection of reactor with the potential to 
be upscaled to a large – scale solar water disinfection unit.

3 Solar Water Disinfection 
 
Solar water disinfection, or SODIS has been known of for more than 30 years. It is a simple 
and easy technique which involves putting water in a transparent container and placing it 
under sunlight for a certain amount of time depending on the solar intensity and sensitivity 
of the pathogens. Due to its simplicity and ease of use, SODIS has spread throughout the 
world and is in daily use in more than 50 countries in Asia, Africa and Latin America 
(McGuigan, et al., 2012)  
 
The disinfection capabilities of sunlight have been known for many years, but only in the 
last few decades has this idea come into practice. Communities on the Indian sub – continent 
used to place open trays of water in the sunlight to be blessed nearly 2000 years ago (Baker 
& Taras. 1981). In the late 1870s by Downes and Blunt (1877), studied solar disinfection 
and indicated the importance of parameters such as radiation intensity, exposure time and 
wavelength. However, it was only in the 1980s that Aftim Acra discovered solar disinfection 
as a low – cost and effective method for drinking water treatment (Acra, et al., 1984). Since 
then, there have been numerous studies and field experiments that have provided an in – 
depth knowledge about the advantages and development of SODIS as a means of treating 
drinking water effectively.  
 
3.1  Mechanism of Solar Disinfection 
 
Solar ultraviolet radiation is effective for pathogen inactivation due to its ability to damage 
pathogens in a number of ways. The radiation can cause damage through direct inactivation, 
direct action of photons on the DNA of microorganisms, or by photosensitisation 
mechanisms. Irradiation of DNA by UV light releases some energy which is absorbed by 
pyrimidine rings of thymine and cytosine bases. This leads to the formation of pyrimidine 
dimers that hinder DNA replication and prevent the microorganism from reproducing 
(Goodsell, 2001). UVA radiation is not energetic enough to directly modify DNA bases. 
However, it is absorbed by photosensitisers present in water (humic acids and chlorophylls), 
which react with oxygen to produce reactive oxygen species (ROS). These ROS include 
singlet oxygen, superoxide, hydrogen peroxide, and hydroxyl radical. These highly reactive 
oxygen molecules cause damage to DNA and oxide amino acids in proteins and 
polyunsaturated fatty acids in lipids, thereby inducing cellular membrane damage. However, 
the repair of cell damage is possible by photo-reactivation (Thoma, 1999; Kim, et al., 1994; 
Todo, et al., 1997; Sancar, 1994, 1996) or dark repair mechanisms (Lindahl & Wood, 1999; 
Prakash, et al., 1993; Lehmann, 1995; Seeberg, et al., 1995); cell damage can also be 
prevented by antioxidant enzymes (Fridovich, 1995; Cabiscol, 2000). Some enzymes can 
repair pyrimidine dimers in the presence of blue light or even enable the cell to scavenge 
reactive oxygen species. Figure 1 shows an illustration of the inactivation mechanism of UV 
light.

Bacteria disinfection by thermal inactivation has been attributed to the high absorption of 
far-infrared radiation by water. Temperature triggers both synergetic and antagonist effects 
between optical and thermal processes. At temperatures above 45 ˚C, a strong synergistic 
effect has been observed (McGuigan, et al., 1998; Wegelin, 1994; Vivar, et al., 2017). This 
synergistic effect was attributed to a slow pasteurisation effect and the inhibition of the DNA 
repair mechanisms (McGuigan, et al., 1998), while temperatures of 20 – 40 ˚C were reported 
to enhance the bacteria growth, thus hindering the disinfection process (Vivar, et al., 2017; 
Giannakis, et al., 2015).  
 
The solar disinfection technique has been shown to be highly effective against a wide range 
of waterborne pathogens, such as bacteria, viruses, and protozoa (Wegelin, 1994; Sommer, 
1997; Boyle, et al., 2008; Heaselgrave, et al., 2006; Schwab & Harding, 2012; McGuigan, 
et al., 2006; Meierhofer & Landolt, 2009). The treatment time required, however, varies, 
depending on the resistance provided from the microbes. Table 1 shows the microbial 
species known to be inactivated by SODIS.

However, microbial resistance is not the only parameter that needs to be studied when talking 
about SODIS. The intensity of UV radiation received, water temperature, container 
characteristics, water composition and reactor design are also important parameters that 
affect solar disinfection (Acra, et al., 1984). 
 
3.2 Solar Disinfection Reactors 
 
3.2.1 Plastic Bottles and Bags 
 
The most commonly used solar disinfection method is to fill water in transparent 
polyethylene terephthalate (PET) bottles and leave it under sunlight for 6 h to 48 h depending 
on the intensity of sunlight. Polyethene terephthalate does not transmit UVB radiation, but 
their thin walls allow them to efficiently transmit 85 – 90 % UVA radiation (McGuigan, et 
al., 1998). Plastic bottles have been used for numerous solar disinfection field tests all over 
the world with positive results. In southern India, Rose, et al., (2006), used 2L PET bottles 
to test the effect of SODIS water on the incidence and severity of diarrhoea in 100 children 
under 5 years of age. A 40% reduction in the risk of diarrhoea was recorded. However, in 
PET bottles, only UVA radiation acts to aide inactivation of pathogenic bacteria. PET bottles 
are also easy to use and clean with transportation of these bottles is neither tedious nor costly. 
Plastic bottles, in spite of their advantages, however, are not the best SODIS technology. 
Polyethene terephthalate has the potential to release photoproducts after lengthy exposure to 
strong sunlight. Research involving chemical analysis has been conducted concerning the 
potential leaching of photoproducts into water. So far, results show that in some cases 
photoproducts such as terephthalate compounds remain on the surface of the container but 
do not migrate into the water (Wegelin, et al., 2001). Other compounds such as the carbonyls 
and plasticisers are found in the water but are well below the limits set for drinking water 
quality (Reed, 2004). Despite these studies, doubts continue to linger about the safety of 
water disinfected in plastic bottles in light of the disclaimers made by manufacturers in the 
bottling industry who instruct users not to reuse plastic bottles. The PET bottles also have to 
be replaced every 6 months. These bottles also have a limited capacity which is usually 2 L.

Disposal of these bottles produces lots of plastic waste which is extremely harmful for the 
environment. Due to these drawbacks, other methods have been researched. Polyethylene 
(PE) bags and hybrid polyethylene and ethylene vinyl acetate (PE/EVA) bags have been 
tested to compare with PET bottles (Gutierrez-Alfaro, et al., 2017). A study by Gutierrez-
Alfaro, et al., 2017 compared PE/EVA bags, PE bags and PET bottles filled with simulated 
field condition water. Both bags reached the detection limit for E. coli and Enterococcus, 
whereas the PET bottles did not. PE bags showed the best results among the three materials 
tested. This study also conducted ageing tests and found that PE bags observed 100% 
breakage, but PE/EVA bags remained intact and still exhibited a better disinfection 
efficiency compared to new PET bottles. Moreover, the PE and PE/EVA had a much higher 
capacity of 4L compared to the PET bottles of 2L.  
 
3.2.2 Flow Reactors 
 
PET bottles and PE or PE/EVA bags are highly effective techniques when the need is for 
individual households or in case of natural disasters or even in war – ravaged areas. 
However, if a larger volume needs to be disinfected, PET bottles or PE/EVA and PE bags 
will not be feasible. In such cases, flow reactors would be more effective as they have a 
larger capacity. Caslake, et al., (2004), developed a SODIS system based on a PVC circuit 
covered by an acrylic layer for the disinfection of drinking water in remote locations in 
developing countries. The system worked without catalyst and was used for disinfection of 
contaminated river water in Peru. In spite of high turbidity, a 4 – log reduction for total 
coliforms was obtained. The author, however, did not provide data on the dose required or 
time taken.  
 
Ubomba-Jaswa, et al., (2009), studied the limitations of large volume SODIS treatment when 
scaled-up through the use of larger batch volumes or continuous flow recirculation reactors. 
Two types of solar reactors were used in this study for disinfection tests, a borosilicate glass 
tube used as batch system (0 L min−1 flow rate, 2.5 L total volume), and two higher volume 
(14 and 70 L) solar photo-reactors fitted with compound parabolic collectors (CPCs) and 
recirculation systems at two flow rates (2 and 10 L min−1). The authors studied the effects 
of the total volume of treated water and the flow rate on inactivation. It was observed that 
an increase in the flow rate has a negative effect on inactivation of bacteria, irrespective of 
the exposure duration. It was also found that at a given time – point, to ensure complete 
inactivation, maximum exposure to UV radiation is required rather than repeated sub – lethal 
doses over a long period of time. The authors also observed that the inactivation patterns of 
E. coli under varied UV irradiances and found that an uninterrupted solar UV dose greater 
than 108 kJ m-2 regardless of irradiance is enough. These results were used to explain the 
reason for the frequent complete inactivation observed in static batch systems compared to 
continuous flow or recirculating reactors. When water with bacteria remains static under 
sunlight it is completely illuminated leading to complete inactivation whereas for continuous 
flow systems, approximately 2 log CFU /100 mL remained even after a 5 hour period 
(Ubomba-Jaswa, et al., 2009). This study helped form a basis for those attempting to upscale 
SODIS using continuous flow or re – circulatory flow reactors.  
 
Another continuous flow reactor was test by Gill and Price, (2010), which had a capacity to 
deliver 10 L/min of treated water. This pilot was set up in rural Kenyan village to treat 
surface water for a small collection dam. The reactor used was approximately120 m long 
and had a 47 mm diameter Pyrex tubing at the focus of a CPC reflector (concentration factor

= 1.0), assembled in eight panels. While only preliminary measurements were made soon 
after the reactor was completed, these indicated that coliform populations were being 
reduced from 102 to 0 CFU/mL after a 20 min single pass residence time. No details of the

determined. Pansonato, et al., (2011), reported 2 log reductions in E. coli concentration using

therefore, treated a higher volume of polluted water in the same time. The system also 
incorporated an electronic UV-A sensor which controlled the discharge of the treated water 
into a clean reservoir tank provided a pre-defined UV-A dose was received.  
 
Recently, researchers have also studied the potential of raceway pond reactors as an alternate 
for CPC reactors in advanced oxidation processes. Raceway pond reactors were originally 
used for the growth of microalgae and disinfection of aquaculture effluents. Carra, et al., 
(2014) studied raceway pond reactors as a photo – Fenton reactor as they have a larger 
capacity and are relatively cheaper to build and operate. The authors used 5.5 mg Fe/L and 
a reactor depth of 15 cm to successfully remove micropollutants from water. Esteban Garcia, 
et al., 2018 also used raceway pond reactor as a photo – Fenton reactor for inactivation of 
wild bacteria present in wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) effluent. The authors used 50 
mg/L H2O2 and 20 mg Fe2+/L in a 5 cm deep reactor and achieved disinfection in 80 minutes. 
However, to the best of my knowledge, it was found that raceway pond reactors have not 
been used for solar disinfection of drinking water.  
 
3.2.3 Advanced Oxidation Processes 
 
Another method employed to improve the inactivation efficiency of SODIS is to combine it 
with photo catalytic materials or oxidising agents such as TiO2, ZnO, graphene and hydrogen 
peroxide. When a semiconductor is irradiated with illumination above band-gap, the 
radiation energy is absorbed, and electrons are promoted from the valence band to the 
conduction band giving rise electron–hole pair formation. If the charge carriers reach the 
semiconductor/water interface they may participate in redox reactions. When an electron 
acceptor (normally dissolved oxygen) and electron donor are adsorbed close to the surface 
of the semiconductor particle, a series of electron transfer reactions occur so that different 
reactive oxygen species (ROS) are produced. The ROS are very active, indiscriminate 
oxidants, especially the hydroxyl radical (Fujishima, et al., 2000). The ROS not only destroy 
a large variety of chemical contaminants in water but also cause fatal damage to micro-
organisms (Blake, et al., 1999). The photocatalytic properties of many semiconductors 
(TiO2, ZnO, Fe2O3, etc) have been investigated. Amongst these, TiO2 is the most widely 
used semiconductor for water treatment purposes. TiO2 is a wide band semiconductor whose 
band – gap energy of 3.2 eV corresponds to photons of wavelength shorter than 390 nm, that 
is, in the UVA and UVB spectrum. TiO2 may be utilised in aqueous suspension or may be 
immobilized on a supporting solid substrate. Most studies have reported that suspension 
reactors are more efficient due to large surface area available for the reaction. The main 
drawback of using nano- or micro-particles in suspension is the requirement for post-
treatment retrieval or recycling of the catalyst, potentially making the treatment more 
complex and expensive. Therefore, treatment reactors utilising immobilized TiO2 have 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
 
gained more attention. Unfortunately, immobilising TiO2 on a solid substrate reduces the 
surface area available for reaction and limits the mass transfer of reactants to the 
photocatalyst surface in addition to making it more expensive (Malato, et al., 2009). Most 
studies on photocatalytic disinfection suggest the hydroxyl radicals as the primary species 
responsible for microorganism inactivation. The oxidative effect of TiO2 photocatalysis 
occurs by direct contact of the catalyst particle with the bacteria, so the first damage should 
occur at the outer membrane (Sunada, et al., 2003). The capability of TiO2 to inactivate a 
wide range of pathogens has been a fruitful field of research since 1985 (Matsunga, et al., 
1985. E. coli is the most frequently studied microorganism for water disinfection studies 
(Malato, et al., 2009), as it is an indicator of faecal contamination and reference for water 
quality. Different types of titania catalysts have been also evaluated for disinfection, using 
either suspensions or immobilized systems. Suspensions present better disinfection 
capacities compared with immobilized titania.  
 
Other technologies used to improve solar disinfection efficiency include chemical additives 
such as lime juice and riboflavin, solar mirrors and thermal enhancers.  
 
3.2.4 Cost Estimation  
 
Cost estimation is a very important factor to consider when upscaling of SODIS technologies 
is studied. The cost estimation includes cost of construction, operation and maintenance.  
 
With the increasing need for alternate methods of drinking water treatment, numerous 
researchers have been conducting studies aimed at finding technologies that are efficient as 
well as sustainable. Ubomba – Jaswa, et al., (2010) studied SODIS using a CPC reactor for 
the purpose of household use. The author used a CPC reactor with a 1.3 m long borosilicate 
glass tube with an internal volume of 2.5 L. The total volume that was treated was 25 L. This 
reactor was used in a batch process with 2.5 L disinfected at a time. The author successfully 
achieved complete inactivation in approximately 2 hours in sunny conditions using this 
reactor. The author also estimated the cost of such a reactor. The prototype reactor would 
cost 200 USD, assuming an operational life of 10 years. The cost of 1 L of treated water 
would be approximately 0.002 USD. The author stated that this reactor had no operational 
costs. (Ubomba – Jaswa, et al., 2010) 
 
Carra, et al., (2014), while studying raceway pond reactors as solar photo – Fenton 
technology, found that using CPC reactor for solar photo – Fenton would cost upwards of 
400 EUR/m2 for purchase and installation, whereas, a RW reactor would only cost 10 
EUR/m2.  
 
The most detailed cost estimation for a CPC and RW reactor was conducted by Rivera 
(2017). The author designed a continuous flow CPC and RW reactor with pre – filtration for 
a flow rate of 100 m3/h. The author first conducted laboratory tests to determine the optimum 
design parameters. These design parameters were used to design large scale SODIS reactors. 
After the design was complete, the author created a budget for the complete reactor system 
which included the investment costs and operation and maintenance costs. The author 
reported an investment cost of 439033.45 EUR for a CPC reactor and 119156.32 EUR for 
an RPR. The O&M costs for RPR were 15260 EUR and 16253.35 EUR for a CPC. The 
author calculated these costs for Spain.

4 Materials and Methods 
 
To determine the SODIS reactor best suited for household water treatment, laboratory scale 
disinfection experiments were conducted using a compound parabolic collector (CPC) 
reactor and a raceway pond reactor (RPR). The reactors were evaluated based on their ability 
to inactivate certain pathogens and the costs of setting up a large-scale reactor. Pathogen 
inactivation was tested for four pathogens, namely, E. coli, total coliforms, Enterococci and 
Vibrio spp. The test water containing these pathogens was prepared and then placed in the 
reactors, which were then exposed to natural sunlight. Samples were collected at regular 
intervals and then filtered and incubated to grow colony forming units for enumeration. The 
colony counts were then processed using mathematical and statistical tools for proper 
representation of the obtained results. Photocatalysis experiments were also conducted using 
ZnO to check for any potential enhancement in pathogen activation. A cost estimation was 
also conducted for the two SODIS reactors. 
 
Disinfection experiments were also conducted using artificial UV lamps (UVC and UVA) 
to learn how to conduct disinfection experiments and compare different UV lamps. 
 
The materials and methods used in this study have been described in this chapter.

4.2 Preparation of Phosphate Buffer 
 
During the process of filtering the samples using membrane filtration, it is important that the 
sample be uniformly spread over the membrane so as to prevent growth of colonies on top 
of each other. For this purpose, phosphate buffer solution was used as it does not alter the 
bacteria concentration of the sample. Phosphate buffer solution was used when the sample 
volume to be filtered was below 1 mL. 
 
The stock solution of the phosphate buffer was prepared by suspending 8.5g of potassium 
dihydrogen phosphate in 100 mL of sterilized water. Sodium hydroxide was added to the 
solution till the pH reached 7.2 while stirring continuously. 
  
To prepare phosphate buffer for use, 1.25 mL of the stock solution was mixed with 1 L of 
sterile water and then autoclaved to make it sterile. The solution was stored in a refrigerator.

4.6 Artificial UV Disinfection 
 
UV disinfection is one of the conventional disinfection methods used in drinking water 
treatment. The spectrum of UV radiation used in conventional treatment plants is 200 – 280 
nm with 254 nm as the desired wavelength. This spectrum falls in the UVC region. However, 
the solar radiation received on the surface of the Earth does not contain UVC radiation and 
hence, for this research, UVA radiation was also tested as a means for disinfection of 
drinking water. The UVA and UVC disinfection apparatus and methodology is described in 
this section. The artificial UV disinfection experiments were conducted in the laboratory in 
the Water Building at Aalto University, Espoo, Finland. The UV disinfection experiments 
were conducted to form a comparison between conventional UV disinfection and solar 
disinfection.

4.7 Solar Disinfection  
 
Solar disinfection, or SODIS, makes use of both UVA and UVB radiation found in sunlight. 
The spectrum of UV radiation which aides SODIS ranges from 280 – 400 nm. The reactors 
used for SODIS in this project were the compound parabolic collector (CPC) reactor and 
raceway pond reactor (RPR). During the solar disinfection experiments, the intensity of solar 
radiation was measured continuously at 10 second intervals. The solar disinfection 
experiments were conducted on the roof of the INMAR building at the University of Cadiz 
in Puerto Real, Cadiz, Spain.  
 
4.7.1 Raceway Pond Reactor 
 
Raceway pond reactor is an open channel reactor which can be used either in a batch process 
or a continuous flow process. In a raceway pond reactor, the complete volume in the reactor 
is illuminated throughout the experiment. As discussed in Chapter 3.2.2, the studies 
conducted using this reactor have focussed more on the disinfection of urban wastewater 
effluent or aquaculture effluent. Through this study, an attempt has been made to use a 
raceway pond reactor for the disinfection of drinking water. Figure 8 shows a pilot raceway

According to a WHO publication (WHO, 2011), which compared different household water 
treatment methods, if the test water is 1% wastewater v/v, a LOG10 reduction of 4 or more 
is highly protective for consumption. A LOG10 reduction between 2 and 4 is deemed 
protective. Hence, a LOG10 reduction of 4 was selected as the detection limit for Total 
coliforms.  
 
While conducting experiments, it was important to make sure the results were reliable. The 
filtration equipment was sterilised before and during each experiment to prevent 
contamination. During preparation of medium for cultivation of bacteria, the temperature 
and pH was checked according to the protocol. The experimental set up was cleaned before 
every experiment, and the experimental schedule was strictly followed for each trial. 
Samples were always filtered in triplicates.  
 
The results were also validated by forming a comparison between the measured results and 
those found in literature. However, due to differences in the water matrix, environmental 
conditions and the experimental set up it is difficult to form a proper comparison. Hence, the 
literature closest to this study was used to make sure that the results obtained were reliable.  
 
Despite the steps taken, there were still possible sources of error such as, improper counting 
of colonies. The colonies were counted using the naked eye which makes it possible to have 
an incorrect count. In some cases, colonies were growing on top of each other which made 
it difficult to correctly estimate the actual number colonies. Loss of test water due to leakage 
in the reactor reduced the volume being disinfected which may affect the inactivation of 
bacteria.  
 
For the enumeration of colony forming units, it is important to know what the colonies look 
like. The E. coli colonies and Total coliform colonies were grown using the same medium. 
The E. coli colonies were blue in colour and the pink colonies were Total coliforms. Vibrio 
spp., grown on TCBS agar were found to be yellow and green. Both yellow and green 
colonies together were counted as Vibrio spp. Enterococci, grown on Slantez – Bartley 
medium, were counted as all the colonies from red to brown in colour. The colony forming 
units of each pathogen are shown in Figure 16.

The primary goal of this study was to evaluate two solar disinfection reactors for household 
treatment of drinking water for a community based on inactivation efficiency and costs of 
setting up the reactors. Evaluation of two reactors, CPC and RPR, showed that even though 
the CPC reactor required a smaller dose and less time, the extremely high costs would made 
it unfeasible for rural areas.  
 
Raceway pond reactors, to the best of my knowledge, have not been used for solar based 
disinfection of drinking water. These reactors, albeit not as good as CPC reactors, are 
sufficient for inactivation of pathogenic bacteria. They would also incur much lower 
investment costs compared to the CPC reactor. Raceway pond reactors would also be easier 
to construct and operate and would not require any skilled labour.  
 
The photocatalytic solar disinfection tests conclusively showed that there was no 
improvement in the reactor performance despite the use of a photocatalyst. Hence, it can be 
said that the use of photocatalysts is not required for HHWT as preparation of these materials 
requires expensive equipment and skilled labour, both of which are hard to find in rural 
areas.  
 
Thus, it was concluded that a raceway pond reactor would provide the best possible solution 
for household water treatment for a community of around 2000 people.
