Abstract
This study compared the short­read sequencing technology NovaSeq 6000 system by Illumina to
the long­read sequencing technology PromethION 2 by Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT)
to assess their ability to detect antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs), recover genomes, and the
estimation of related abundances. A new measurement, Base pairs Per Kilobase of reference se­
quence per Million bacterial base pairs (BPKM) for comparing abundance captured by short­read
and long­read sequencing technologies, was tested and showed promising results.
Key findings indicate that longer reads provide better gene coverage and yield more high­ and
medium­quality metagenome­assembled genomes (MAGs). Abundance measurements of ARGs
were highly correlated between short and long reads, and a similar number of novel bacterial
species was discovered. This study emphasized several advantages of using long­read sequencing
technology with a few exceptions where short and long reads showed comparable performances.

Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) persists to be one of the most pressing threats to global health,
affecting humans, agriculture, livestock, and aquaculture.
Next­generation sequencing technologies have made it possible to study antimicrobial resistance
genes (ARGs) in environmental samples as they allow access to the genomic data without the
need to isolate and culture microorganisms prior to analysis, especially when the bacteria cannot
be cultivated under laboratory conditions [12]. Short­read sequencing technologies, such as the
platforms from Illumina, have been the standard within metagenomic sequencing and the study of
microorganisms. However, recent advancements in long­read sequencing technologies have sig­
nificantly improved the previously criticized accuracy and throughput. With the growing devel­
opment of bioinformatic tools specialized for long reads, third­generation sequencing is becoming
more applicable. Longer reads have the potential to offer new insights into the complex microbial
communities such as the discovery of novel species.
This project seeks to investigate the advantages and disadvantages of sequencing metagenomic
samples using the short­read sequencing technology NovaSeq 6000 system by Illumina and the
long­read sequencing technology PromethION 2 system by Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT).
The technologies will be compared in terms of their ability to discover AMGs, recover genomes,
and estimate related abundances.
1.1
Sample introduction
The analysis has been performed on DNA extracted from fecal matter of four pigs. The pigs were
in the weaning stage of life when the samples were provided. Of the four pigs, three of them were
considered healthy and one was considered to be of poor health. Before sequencing, one of the
healthy pig samples was spiked with a mock microbial community from the ZymoBIOMICS® Gut
Microbiome Standard [31], which can be used as a reference in the comparison of the two technolo­
gies. Each pig microbiome was sequenced in triplicates in order to evaluate the reproducibility.
This gives a total of 4 · 3 = 12 microbiome samples sequences by the two technologies. It is
important to notice that the same 12 samples are sequenced by the Illumina and ONT platforms,
making it suitable for direct comparison of the two.
Figure 1.1 illustrates the workflow of acquiring the metagenomic dataset. Beginning with sample
collection, followed by DNA extraction and library preparation, which makes the DNA ready for
sequencing, resulting in the raw reads.
The samples will be referred to by the ID of the pig from which they came, followed by an under­

1.2
Library preparation
Library preparation is an essential step in preparing the extracted DNA to be sequenced. The in­
gredients and handling of the DNA may be different prior to sequencing with Illumina and ONT,
but the steps are analogous. The first step in library preparation is the fragmentation of the DNA.
Fragmentation can be done in a variety of ways such as by mechanical force or enzymatic di­
gestion. Different methods will affect the fragment distribution and how much material is lost
differently. After the fragmented library is repaired (from protruding 3’ and 5’ ends), adapter lig­
ation is performed. Distinct upstream and downstream adapters for each fragment are required for
every sequencing platform. Lastly, a size selection and cleanup step can be carried out by using
magnetic beads. For the DNA intended for sequencing by Illumina, a length of 300 base pairs is
generally targeted [21]. If working with multiple samples, sample­specific indexing primers can be
incorporated into the adapters (multiplexing). This allows for the sequencing of multiple samples
at once, while still being able to separate the samples (demultiplexing) afterwards.
==PAGE:9==
1.3
NovaSeq 6000 System by Illumina
The NovaSeq 6000 system is a second­generation sequencing platform from Illumina, also known
as a short­read sequencing technology (Read length approx. 300 bases). The system uses the
Illumina sequencing by synthesis (SBS) chemistry, facilitating the massively parallel sequencing,
allowing the sequencing of billions of DNA fragments in the range of 50­500 bases.
The first step in the sequencing is loading the library onto the flow cell. The flow cell contains
surface­bound oligonucleotides, which are complementary to the adapters, binding the DNA frag­
ments to the flow cell. Polymerases, dNTPs, and buffers are then introduced to the flow cell,
inducing each fragment to copy itself thousands of times. This amplification method is called
bridge PCR, and will create clusters of DNA. The DNA undergoes linearization and denaturation,
resulting in single­stranded templates for sequencing to obtain the first read. For paired­end se­
quencing, the templates are used to perform bridges synthesizing the second strand. The original
strand is then removed, leaving the complementary strand as a template for the second read [4].
Within each of these clusters, templates are sequenced by the Illumina SBS chemistry. This chem­
istry is a reversible terminator­based method which will use a single base extension and a com­
petitive addition of nucleotides. Following the incorporation of each nucleotide, the clusters are
excited by light, resulting in the emission of a fluorescent signal [16]. Red or green clusters are
interpreted as C and T bases, respectively, where both colors represent A bases and no color repre­
sents G bases. The number of cycles will determine the read length, and the emission wavelength
and signal intensity will determine the basecall. The system is known to have a low basecall error
rate of 0.1% [26].
1.4
PromethION 2 by Oxford Nanopore Technologies
PromethION 2 is a third­generation sequencing platform by Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT),
also known as a long­read sequencing technology (read length >4 million bases). This platform
sequences DNA through so­called nanopores. A single strand of DNA is passed through a mem­
brane via a nanopore, while a voltage difference is established across the membrane. The electrical
resistance in the pore will change depending on the bases present, creating electrical signals for
the bases. This entire electrical signal, called the ’squiggle’, is the raw sequencing data. The
basecaller, Dorado, transforms the raw signals into DNA sequences in real time through the appli­
cation of machine learning [33]. The basecalling algorithm more specifically uses neural networks,
trained on a diverse dataset, including DNA or RNA from plants, animals, bacteria, and viruses.
The latest version of Dorado is reported to have an error rate of 0.25% [28].

1.6
Tools
1.6.1
Trimming & Quality control
The first step of the analysis is pre­processing of the raw reads and the evaluation of read quality.
Quality control is an essential step as low quality reads can affect the downstream analysis. A bad
read can both be a result of factors related to library preparation and sequencing. In the process
of library preparation, issues like primer/adapter contamination or polymerase errors might arise,
while sequencing can be affected by factors such as cluster density, optical detection, and phasing
errors, potentially resulting in inaccurate basecalling. When the samples have been sequenced,
fastq files will be created, including the Phred quality score (Q), a measurement of the confidence
of correct basecalling. The Phred score ranges from 0­40, where 40 is the highest confidence. The
Phred score is on a logarithmic scale meaning that Q = 40 equals 1 out of every 10,000 bases being
incorrectly called, also referred to as the error rate (P).
The formula for the Phred score:
Q = −10 log10(P)
(1.1)
The quality score can be used to remove poor quality bases at the end of the reads, or entire reads,
in a procedure called trimming [34].
There are currently many different trimming tools available, and the selection of one depends on
the sequencing technology. The reads from ONT were basecalled using Dorado Basecall Server
==PAGE:11==
v7.2.13 and “high­accuracy model”. Only high­quality reads (>10 Q score) were selected and all
adapters and barcodes were trimmed prior to analysis. The integrated trimming and quality control
features of ONT make it standard in the scientific field to avoid employing additional trimming
methods.
Illumina, on the other hand, does not provide this option, and the reads will need trimming from
an exterior trimming tool. fastp and BBDuk, two short read trimming tools, were selected. The
reads generated by the tool that yielded the highest quality were utilized. The performance of the
tools was based on the before trimming and after trimming quality report of the reads created by
FastQC.
fastp
fastp is an ultra­fast bioinformatics tool for processing fastq data. It offers methods for quality con­
trol, read filtering, and base corrections while incorporating features from various other trimming
and quality control tools. fastp features automatic adapter sequence detection for single­end and
paired­end reads from Illumina. Adapter sequences are detected in single­end reads by the assem­
bly of high­frequency read tails. Paired­end reads identify the overlap between each pair, treating
bases outside this overlapped region as adapter content, thus enabling trimming even for adapters
as short as a single base. fastp also provides polyG and polyX tail trimming, read filtering, read
pruning by a sliding window, and other operations [9].
When using fastp it is important to know some of its shortcomings. The sliding window has a size
of 4 bases and will look at the mean Phred quality score. From a purely mathematical standpoint,
it is not possible to directly sum values on a logarithmic scale, but it is faster than having to convert
Phred scores to error rates. With a window size of 4, even a single low­quality base at the start
or end of a read will result in all 4 bases being trimmed if their average quality falls below the
threshold. This can lead to excessive base removal and should be considered when using fastp.
BBDuk
BBDuk is a trimming tool from the BBtools package, employing the techniques K­trimming and
Q­trimming. K­trimming is an adapter trimming method using Kmers, which utilizes the kmers
as a quick way to look up adapters from an adapter content file. Q­trimming applies a Phred score
algorithm to trim bases with a low Phred score. It will convert Phred scores to error rates and
calculate the average error rate of two regions of the read, the discarded and retained regions. The
algorithm will start from the 3’ end of the read and continuously discard bases until the region has
an average error rate under the threshold and the retained region above the threshold.
Similar to fastp, BBDuk has its limitations. This is seen if a read is shorter than the sequencing
cycles, in this case 151, where adapters will start to be sequenced and if these adapters are less than
11 bases, the read will not be trimmed as K­trimming has a Kmer range of 19­11 bases (standard
setting) [5].
FastQC
When the reads have been trimmed, FastQC can evaluate the quality of the reads. FastQC will run
a set of analyses and point out potential problems. By giving the fastq files of the raw reads and
==PAGE:12==
reads trimmed by fastp and BBDuk, it is possible to compare the quality reports. FastQC will give
you basic statistics, base and sequence quality, GC content, and adapter content among some. If
something looks unusual, FastQC will highlight it [2].
1.6.2
Mapping & Alignment
KMA is a program that performs Kmer alignment and mapping of raw reads against redundant
databases. KMA is suitable for both short and long reads, and is therefore used for both se­
quencing technologies. KMA uses a Kmer seed and extend method to speed up mapping and
the Needleman­Wunsch algorithm to align the extensions from the Kmer seeds. The ConClave
novel sorting scheme is used when reads map to multiple templates (Multi­mapping), and will
ensure that the reads are mapped against the template(s) with the highest Conclave score [11].
1.6.3
Assembly
For assembly of the reads created by Illumina and ONT, SPAdes created for short reads and Flye
created for long reads.
metaSPAdes
SPAdes or metaSPAdes is a toolkit used for assembly and analysis, primarily of second­generation
sequencing data. metaSPAdes is an additional pipeline developed to handle metagenomic data
[1]. The first step in SPAdes is the construction of the de Bruijn graph, which relies on Kmers.
The size of the Kmer will affect how tangled or fragmented the graph will be. In the next step,
the de Bruijn graph is transformed into the assembly graph by a number of graph simplification
procedures. Paths in the assembly graph are constructed corresponding to long genomic fragments
within a metagenome. metaSPAdes tries to overcome the challenges related to the assembly of
metagenomic data, such as the microdiversity challenge. Then focusing on the reconstruction of a
consensus backbone of a strain mixture, leading to some strain­specific features from rare strains
being overlooked [27].
metaFlye
Flye or metaFlye is a de novo assembler used for third­generation sequencing data. Like metaS­
PAdes, metaFlye is the specific pipeline for metagenomic data [19]. This assembler was designed
to handle some of the challenges related to long­read metagenome assembly, such as the highly
nonuniform coverage of the composition and species with highly similar genomes. [20] In ad­
dressing the issue of uneven coverage, metaFlye employs a repeat graph rather than utilizing the
de Bruijn graph. This graph incorporates approximate sequence matches rather than exact Kmer
matches, leading to a higher noise tolerance of the reads and thereby better assembly [19].
1.6.4
Binning
Binning is the method of recovering genomes from a complex mix of metagenomic contigs. The
binning of the contigs is performed using the binning tool MetaBAT 2 and the output is often re­
ferred to as a bin or metagenome­assembled genome (MAG). The key aspects of the MetaBAT
2 algorithm are normalized tetra­nucleotide frequency scores, a graph structure, and an iterative
==PAGE:13==
graph partitioning procedure for clustering and steps to recruit smaller contigs. The adaptive bin­
ning algorithm will do the parameter tuning to ensure accurate binning [17].
1.6.5
Bin quality
After binning the contigs, CheckM2 can be used to assess the quality of the bins. In contrast
to CheckM1, which employs comparative genomics to determine lineage­specific marker gene
sets, CheckM2 relies on machine learning for evaluating bin completeness and contamination.
This approach integrates extra genomic information such as multi­copy genes, biological pathways
and modules, as well as other genomic features like amino acid counts and the number of coding
sequences. By using machine learning, the tool is more accurate and sensitive for novel lineages
that lack high­quality genomic representation [10].
1.6.6
Taxonomic classification
GTDB­tk is used to classify the taxonomy of the MAGs from MetaBAT 2. The tool assigns objec­
tive taxonomic classifications to bacteria and archaea, based on the Genome Database Taxonomy.
The process involves predicting genes with Prodigal and identifying 120 bacterial or 122 archaeal
marker genes using HMMER (profile hidden Markov models [14]). The domain with the highest
proportion of marker genes is selected. Marker genes are then aligned, concatenated, trimmed,
and placed into domain­specific reference trees using pplacer. Query genomes get classified based
on this placement, along with their relative evolutionary distance (RED) and their average nu­
cleotide identity (ANI). A query genome assigned to a genus is designated to the species of the
nearest reference genome if the alignment fraction exceeds 65% and it falls within the species ANI
circumscription radius, generally 95% [8].
To be consistent with community standards, it is only recommended to apply GTDB­Tk to genomes
that meet the criteria of a High­quality draft (HQ) and Medium­quality draft (MQ) MAG. HQ
MAGs will be >90% complete and <5% contamination, and should encode the 23S, 16S and 5S
rRNA genes and tRNAs for at least 18 out of the 20 amino acids possible. A MQ MAG must be
≥50% complete and <10% contaminated. MAGs with <50% completeness and >10% contamina­
tion are classified as Low quality (LQ), and will not be used in the analysis [6].

Materials & Methods
This section outlines the various methods employed in the project and explains their applications.
A handful of bioinformatic tools have been used to convert the raw reads into useful data. All tool
commands are listed in the Appendix 6.1.
2.1
Pipeline
To provide a clear and structured overview of the workflow in this project, a diagram illustrating
the project pipeline was created, see figure 2.1. The pipeline highlights the key steps, covering the
stages from the data preparation and processing to analysis and interpretation.
Figure 2.1: Project pipeline: A visual representation of the project pipeline and work flow. The
pipeline shows the different steps in the data processing of the Illumina (IL) and ONT reads along
with the tools used.
The pipeline begins with Library and Sample Preparation, where the data sequenced by the Il­
lumina platform undergoes quality control and trimming. This ensures standardized raw sample
reads for comparative analysis, enhancing data integrity and reliability. In this project, various
computational tools have been employed (see figure 2.1) to (a) identify ARGs and conduct AMR
analysis, (b) recover genomes, and (c) estimate the abundance of recovered MAGs.
To proceed, the Illumina­generated reads need to be preprocessed, which involves trimming and
performing quality control. Raw forward and reverse reads are used as input in both fastp and
BBDuk, trimming the read until the quality is above a threshold of Q=20. A minimum read length
8
==PAGE:15==
after trimming of 50 bases was set. BBDuk will search for Kmers of size 19 and down to size 11.
After trimming, FastQC was used to determine the quality of both trimmed and raw reads, to select
the output to use for further analysis.
2.1.1
Identification of ARGs (a)
To identify the ARGs, the reads are aligned against the SILVA and PanRes databases by KMA,
where SILVA is a database of rRNA gene sequences of bacteria, archaea and eukaryota domains.
Bacteria is the only domain of interest in this project [30]. PanRes is a database containing a col­
lection of ARGs from multiple other collections of resistance genes [23]. KMA works differently
when aligning short and long reads. Short reads are ensured to only map to a template once, and
long reads are allowed to map to multiple templates, as there might be more information within
the long reads. The output file consists of information regarding the alignment, which can then be
used to calculate gene coverage and abundance.
2.1.2
Genome recovery (b)
To retrieve the genomes, the initial step involves assembling the reads from Illumina and ONT,
respectively, by employing two different assemblers: metaSPAdes for short reads and metaFlye
for long reads. The coverage depth of the contigs is obtained from BBMap and KMA, by aligning
the reads to the contigs, which are then used as part of the binning. For short reads, BBMap was
used, and for long reads, KMA was used. Binning of the contigs was performed by MetaBAT 2,
with a threshold of bin size >200 kb and contig lengths of >1500 bp. CheckM2 was used to assess
the quality of the MAGs, filtering away LQ bins. GTDB­Tk takes HQ and MQ MAGs as input
and will return the taxonomy of the classified MAGs.
2.1.3
Abundance of MAGs (c)
An indexed database of MAGs from MetaBAT 2 binning, described in figure 2.1 part (b), is created
to facilitate abundance estimation by mapping the reads back to the MAGs by KMA.
2.2
Calculating ARG abundance
An essential aspect of AMR analysis is to estimate the abundance of the discovered ARGs in each
sample. The inherent characteristics of compositional data, however, pose analytical difficulties.
The raw abundance and total read count per sample are uninformative. They merely represent the
relative abundance of a random subset of the true DNA content present in the environment. The
relative information lies in the ratios between the components, and the data must be transformed
in order to obtain meaningful results [7].
Fragments Per Kilobase of reference sequence per Million bacterial fragments (FPKM) is a com­
mon normalization method for calculating abundances, see equation 2.1. While FPKM is an ef­
fective method for estimating the abundance of ARGs, it is mainly applied to short reads. If the
AMR in the samples is to be compared between Illumina and ONT, measuring in fragments will
affect the abundance of ARGs differently. The number of fragments from Illumina is significantly
greater than that of ONT, and the abundances will end up on different scales.
==PAGE:16==
For a more accurate comparison of the ARGs abundances, the alternative normalization method
called Base pairs Per Kilobase of reference sequence per Million base pairs (BPKM), see equation
(2.2) below. BPKM normalizes the abundance based on total base pairs, providing a more robust
metric which accounts for variations in read length across different sequencing technologies.
2.2.1
Collecting metagenomic composition
Calculating FPKM involves aligned fragment counts (fragmentCountAln) collected from the out­
put file, created during mapping using KMA. When calculating BPKM, the total base pairs (bp­
Total) are collected from the same output files. The length of the ARGs is also needed.
2.2.2
Zero replacement
For each sample, a list of ARGs ID’s will be listed along with the alignment or base pair count.
Compositional datasets may contain zero values for various reasons, which is the case in this anal­
ysis. There are essential or structural zeros, that are truly zero, and then there are rounded and
count zeros, that can occur due to values below the detection limit, imprecision, or insufficient
sample size. Since these values are not truly zero, replacing these zeros with suitable small values,
called zero replacement, is recommended as part of the data analysis [22].
The Bayesian­multiplicative treatment was used for the zero replacements, which takes the relative
proportion of the data into account when performing the zero replacement. The non­zero values are
then scaled multiplicatively, ensuring that the replaced values do not distort the relative proportion
[24]. The counts are now ready for normalization.

Coverage over 100%
While calculating the gene coverage, several coverages exceeding 100% were discovered. The
coverage was calculated as follows:
Coverage = Reference covered positions
Gene length
(3.1)
The only way the coverage can exceed 100% is if the gene length is smaller than the reference cov­
ered positions, which is nonsensical. It became apparent that insertions were causing the coverage
to exceed 100%. KMA will count the insertions as part of the reference covered positions and if
the gene already has a high coverage, this can lead to a coverage over 100%.
An example of this can be observed in the ARG with ID: pan_11582_v1.0.1 in sample 1035842_1,
figure 3.3. The gene has a length of 246 bases, but the reference covered positions is 255 bases,
which is 9 bases higher than the gene length. By looking at the alignment for the particular gene,
exactly 9 insertions are present in the template, marked in red.
==PAGE:22==
Figure 3.3: Alignment of gene: pan11582v1.0.1 with 9 insertions shown in red.
To overcome this problem, all genes with a coverage >100% were set to exactly 100%. Later in
the project, an in­house program was developed to correct for the insertions, and this script is used
in the remaining analysis. Due to limited time, recalculating the updated coverages was, however,
not feasible.

4
Discussion
The field of metagenomics is constantly evolving and the introduction of long­read sequencing
technologies seems promising. Many of the results created in this project highlight the opportuni­
ties of using long reads, which have also been described in previous studies [18].
4.0.1
ARG coverage
In the discovery of ARGs, the short and long reads were aligned to the PanRes database. The short
reads discovered more ARGs in all samples compared to the long reads. By further examination
of the results, many of the genes were of low coverage. Short reads can thus be advantageous in
identifying a wide array of ARGs, while still being aware of the fact that the matches with low
coverage tend to be of low statistical significance [12].
The longer reads generated matches of considerably higher coverage, supporting the claim that
longer reads provide more contiguity. Research also indicates that longer reads enhance the detec­
tion of variants, such as large insertions or deletions (indels), complex rearrangements, structural
variations, areas with high GC content, and repetitive regions [18], enabling the differentiation of
genes with differences as small as a single base.
The choice of setting gene coverages >100% to 100% is unlikely to significantly impact the results,
but it could potentially yield a larger number of completely covered genes, if enough insertions are
present in a sequence.
4.0.2
BPKM vs. FPKM
Following the discovery of ARGs, it becomes possible to assess the total resistance within a sample,
as well as evaluate the effectiveness of the two technologies in capturing this information.
A standard way of calculating the abundance is through the ALR variant FPKM. However, a certain
skepticism was associated with employing this technique. When applied to data from a single
sequencing method, it poses no concern. Yet, when employed to compare short­read and long­
read sequencing technologies, a crucial feature significantly influences the results. The length of
a fragment varies considerably based on the technology, and disregarding this factor can lead to
information loss during transformation.
The calculation of AMR abundance, using FPKM, results in a total resistance in each sample
which is significantly greater for ONT compared to Illumina. This outcome is anticipated, given
the division of the number of fragments mapped to a gene by a considerably smaller total fragment
count. The Pearson and Spearman’s rank correlation also suggest little to no correlation between
the abundance values from Illumina and ONT. Some variation between the two technologies is
to be expected, although not to such an extreme degree. BPKM, conversely, exhibits a strong
correlation between the two, with the values being more proximate. This suggests that using base
pairs instead of fragments is more reliable when working with varying read length.
30
==PAGE:37==
While the total resistance value does not directly reflect the abundance of individual genes, it still
contains valuable insights. A single total resistance value per sample facilitates easier comparison
between the two technologies and can indicate the extent to which Illumina and ONT capture
resistance. A higher total resistance for all samples captured by ONT could imply that long reads
better capture ARGs of low abundance.
It is important to highlight that FPKM and BPKM were calculated on counts from all gene matches
irrespective of the coverage percentage. Therefore, the results may have differed if genes with low
coverage had been excluded.
4.0.3
Assembly­based bacterial classification & ARG detection
Using KMA to align reads to databases is one way of bacterial classification and ARG detec­
tion, but this approach is limited to the detection of genes and genomes that are already known.
Assembly­based approaches offer an alternative that enables the discovery of new genes and species
by assembling reads into contigs. These contigs can subsequently be organized into bins that cor­
respond to entire genomes.
The precision of the assembly has a direct impact on the outcome of the binning process. Species
within a microbial community often share highly conserved regions, and with varying levels of
abundance in metagenomic samples, it can cause nonuniform read counts, resulting in numerous
assembly challenges. If these challenges are not handled correctly, it can lead to fragmented contigs
and possibly trigger intergenomic assembly errors [27].
Closely related species can result in the production of chimeric contigs containing a mixture of
sequences from multiple genomes, evidently failing to capture the true diversity and abundance in
the sample. This could potentially lead to falsely identifying novel species and hinder the discovery
of the actual species present in the chimeric contigs. Chimeric contigs can also merely be a result
of sequencing errors [3]. Short reads are more likely to make chimeric contigs because short reads
are more fragmented. This makes the assembly process more complicated, whereas fewer and
longer reads facilitate more efficient assembly [18].
Some of these mentioned assembly challenges might be what diverged the results of the binning
between Illumina and ONT in table 3.4. In every sample except one, ONT achieved a greater recov­
ery of both HQ and MQ MAGs compared to Illumina. This supports the assertion that longer reads
facilitate easier assembly and result in less fragmented contigs than those obtained with Illumina.
The increased fragmentation of Illumina contigs might have contributed to the lower number of
MAGs compared to ONT before undergoing quality control, as binning required a minimum contig
length of 1500 base pairs.
4.0.4
Variation between replicates
The recovered HQ and MQ MAGs highlighted a large variation between some of the replicates
in a sample from Illumina. This observation is especially prominent in sample 1035842, where
a difference of 73 MAGs is observed between the replicate with the most recovered MAGs and
the replicate with the least recovered MAGs. Although the replicate exhibited a notably lower
==PAGE:38==
count of HQ and MQ MAGs, it nonetheless had a similar overall number of MAGs (HQ, MQ,
LQ) to the other two replicates. The distinction lay in the quantity of incomplete MAGs. This
deviation was absent in the equivalent replicate sequenced by ONT and also did not appear in
the AMR analysis. Hence, it is probably not a result of incidents during library preparation or
sequencing. A more plausible cause for this anomaly may be attributed to the assembly process.
Minor discrepancies between replicates are commonly noted in all results. They initially appear
in the raw data, presenting as variations in reads and base counts. Such differences can likely be
attributed to the processes of library preparation and sequencing and should be taken into account
during comparisons.
4.0.5
MAG abundance distribution & outliers
Returning to the recovered MAGs, a somewhat similar abundance distribution was observed be­
tween Illumina and ONT (figure 3.8) in all samples but 1035844. The increased variance in the
distribution and its distinct separation from the other samples may be attributed to changes in the
pig microbiome during illness, which Illumina is capable of detecting. The distributions are diffi­
cult to compare directly due to the uncertainty arising from the fact that the abundances have not
been normalized. Still, a similar pattern is observed.
It is also noteworthy that when filtering away the LQ MAGs, only ONT is able to capture the high
abundance of S. alactolyticus. There is a chance that the species can be found in the LQ MAGs
from Illumina, and after BLASTing one of the high abundant outliers, S. alactolyticus did appear
in the result. However, due to the low quality of this MAG, the findings are less reliable compared
to those obtained from ONT.
It is challenging to draw conclusions from the obtained MAG abundances since the actual abun­
dance and composition of the samples are unknown. The spike­in mock microbial community of
sample 1035845 can, on the other hand, provide some insight.
4.0.6
Mock microbial community as a reference
When comparing the abundances of the spike­in mock microbial community (table 3.6) to the
same species captured by Illumina and ONT, the main difference was ONT’s inability to capture
Bifidobacterium adolescentis. This does not necessarily imply that ONT entirely failed to capture
the bacterium, as it might have been categorized in one of the lower quality MAGs and thus not
included in GTDB­Tk classification. By examining the output from mapping the reads to the
SILVA database, two cases of Bifidobacterium adolescentis were found in the ONT output and
one in the Illumina output. The strains were, however, different from the one classified by GTDB­
Tk.
Variations in the abundance of species, which were reported to have equal abundance in the mock
microbial community, were also observed. This could potentially be explained by the influence
of the existing bacterial composition within the pig microbiome. If several of the 17 bacterial
species from the mock microbial community are already abundant in the microbiome, it could lead
to skewing of the abundance pattern. Prevotella, Clostridium, Lactobacillus, and Roseburia are
identified as part of the ”core” pig microbiome [15]. Additionally, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii is
==PAGE:39==
likewise noted for its high abundance in weaning pigs [25]. While directly comparing the preva­
lence of 17 bacterial species does not lead to definitive conclusions, it does suggest that Illumina
and ONT capture relatively similar patterns of these species, with even closer similarities observed
at the genus level.
To achieve a more precise comparison between the two technologies, it might have been more
optimal to conduct the analysis using a simulated dataset, where both composition and abundance
are known. However, a simulated dataset will likely not capture the complexity of a metagenomic
dataset.
4.0.7
Novel species
The degree of success GTDB­Tk achieved in classifying Illumina and ONT MAGs is roughly
comparable when considering the initial MAG starting point. One distinction may be that the
novel species captured from the Illumina MAGs are close to the number of novel species captured
by ONT. This could indicate that they both identify the same novel species, or alternatively, some
other factor prevented the Illumina MAGs from being classified at the species level.
4.0.8
Bioinformatic tools and availability
A factor that will clearly influence the analysis and comparison is the selection of bioinformatic
tools. Some tools may work for both short and long reads, while others are specifically designed
for one read length, such as metaSPAdes and metaFlye. The results of data processing are highly
dependent on the tools chosen, and choosing inappropriate tools can result in incorrect outcomes.
Certain tools may offer a long reads option; however, the algorithm is not optimized for long
reads, rendering the outcomes unsuitable for publication. When choosing between short­read and
long­read sequencing technologies, it is crucial to ensure that the selected sequencing methods
are compatible with the tools relevant to the research objective. While the creation of various
bioinformatic tools is undoubtedly increasing, the array of tools available for short­read sequencing
remains considerably larger compared to long­read sequencing, as this approach has long been the
standard. [18].
==PAGE:40==
5
Conclusion
This study highlighted some of the advantages of using long­read sequencing technology, sup­
ported by previous studies.
The results indicate the superiority of long reads in terms of their ability to cover a larger percentage
of the ARG, and a higher yield of HQ and MQ MAGs. A larger total resistance was likewise
observed, potentially suggesting that the continuity of longer reads enhances the detection of low­
abundant ARGs, although it was recognized that numerous factors impacted the estimation. Short
and long reads were similar in their detection of novel species and species from the spike­in mock
microbial community. Overall, long­read sequencing technology appears promising, however,
researchers need to remain aware of the tools available that align with their research objectives.
