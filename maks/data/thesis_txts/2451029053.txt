Abstract
Although water resources are one of the greatest concerns of our society, they keep being
degraded because of anthropogenic activity. To address this issue, the European Union es-
tablished in 2000 the Water Framework Directive (WFD) aiming at restoring the quality of
surface water and groundwater and at keeping them from over-utilization. However, WFD
2021 targets are very unlikely to be achieved. Among many other sources, urban outlets such
as wastewater treatment plants outlets, combined sewer overﬂows (CSOs) or separate system
sewer overﬂows (SSSOs) are important emitters of various groups of contaminants such as
organic pollutants, Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrcarbons (PAHs), micropollutants and also of sus-
pended particles (SS). The difﬁculty to efﬁciently assess contamination released by urban out-
lets makes restoration planning even harder. In this study, an approach combining ﬁeld work
and modeling using sediments as passive samplers aiming at backtracking pollutant loads from
urban outlets was tested. The pollutants selected to perform it were heavy metals due to their
relative stability in water and sediment and their expected presence in urban outlet outﬂows.
The study area was located in the Usserød stream in Denmark. Metal concentrations, selected
on the basis of other studies in Danish catchments, were measured in water and bed sediments
at 9 sampling locations (7 for sediment). These measurements were made along a 205m stretch
of the Usserød stream that included the main outlet of a WWTP. After laboratory analysis,
the results revealed that 5 out of 7 metals were detected in water. 2 metals, Zn and As, were
detected in concentrations above the recommended guidelines values in water. Soil guidelines
were exceeded in sediments for 2 compounds (As and Cd) at few sampling locations. Zn was
exceeding the maximal allowable concentration (MAC) at every station except the one located
in the tributary (Dønse stream), entering 170 downstream the WWTP outlet. Zn was exceeding
the MAC by a factor of 5 in the sample taken from the WWTP outlet, right before it enters the
stream. Decreasing concentration in the rest of the stretch would suggest that the WWTP is the
main source of Zn in this segment of Usserød. Arsenic was exceeding the quality criteria in wa-
ter at all locations except in the WWTP outlet and on the right side of the stream after the outlet.
It was also above the soil cut-off criteria at two sampling locations (Dønse and the furthest one
downstream). These results could suggest that As was getting released from geogenic source.
High metal concentrations in sediments were found upstream the WWTP. It could be explained
by the presence of the WWTP overﬂow outlet and a dam 150m upstream. Calculation of Toxic
Units revealed that the level of heavy metals in Usserød and Dønse exposed aquatic organisms
to potential toxicity. Field measurements and calculations estimating Zn partitioning were used
to parameterize a model studying Zn fate in the studied stretch of Usserød. Zn was selected as it
was the only compound found to exceed the MAC. The investigation of Zn fate within a model
primarily built for organic pollutants allowed to see whether this model could be used for pre-
diction of heavy metals fate. Zn fate was investigated through ﬁve different scenarios exploring
the inﬂuence of key factors, events and processes on Zn concentration in water and sediments
such as overﬂows, mean particle size, resuspension and particles fraction of organic carbon
(foc). The simulated sediment concentrations presented a high dependency on mean particle
size and foc, pointing the importance of accurately estimating these parameters. However, the
resuspension process seemed to have very little inﬂuence on the model results. Simulated Zn
total concentration in water only showed short-term signiﬁcant differences in behavior between
the different scenarios. Even though the software used was primarily designed to model organic
pollutants fate, this study demonstrated the possibility of using sediments as passive samplers
when combining ﬁeld measurements and modeling.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background overview
Due to increased anthropogenic activity, water resources are getting degraded. As a conse-
quence, water resources, which are naturally precious and even scarce, are being even more un-
der pressure. Many studies have reported the different sources (agriculture, industry, domestic
water, urban runoff, sewer overﬂows, etc.) as well as the different contaminant groups (pesti-
cides, organic, heavy metals, Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs), Emerging Pollutants
(EPs), micropollutants, etc.) (Marsalek and Rochfort, 2004; Kronvang et al., 2003; Eriksson
et al., 2007; Bigi, 2017; Geissen et al., 2015; Grandclement et al., 2017). The protection of
these resources along with the known adverse effects of contamination on the water biota and
human health lead the European Union to adopt the Water Framework Directive (WFD) on the
22nd of December 2000. This directive aimed at restoring surface water and groundwater to
good ecological and chemical status and to keep the groundwater intake at sustainable level by
2015 (European Parliament, 2016). However in 2015, not even 50% of the total surface water
bodies had reached a good ecological status. The target was then postponed to 2021, but it is
still very unlikely that it will be achieved (EEA, 2018).
An important source of micropollutants, especially in a developed country like Denmark, are
urban point sources (Eriksson et al., 2007; Fauser et al., 2009). This term refers to outlets which
emit water from urban facilities such as waste water treatment plants (WWTPs), combined
sewer systems or separate sewer systems. Depending on the type of sewer system, these events
are called combined sewer overﬂows (CSOs) or separate sewer system overﬂows (SSSOs). For
the case of WWTPs, even though water is thoroughly treated, some pollutants like pesticides,
PAHs and heavy metals, can still go through the treatment without being completely removed
and be emitted in surface water (Fauser et al., 2009; Boutrup et al., 2015; Pot´e et al., 2008).
However, discharges of nearly untreated waste water can even happen in case of overloading
of the sewer system during rain events. CSOs can happen during heavy rain events whereas
SSSOs happens every time it rains. It is widely recognized that overﬂows can be a source of
great pollution with harmful effects on the environment (Bi et al., 2015; Becouze-Lareure et al.,
2016). It can be an important source of nitrate, phosphate, fecal contaminants but also heavy
metals (Rizzo et al., 2018; Kom´ınkov´a et al., 2016; Marsalek and Rochfort, 2004; Barco et al.,
2008).
To be able to efﬁciently plan restoration measures aiming at minimizing the impacts of urban
point discharges in view of meeting European Union targets, it is necessary to have a more ac-
curate assessment of their contribution to stream pollution (Sonnenberg et al., 2011). However,
pollution emitted from such urban point sources is complicated to assess. Firstly, even though
WWTPs have to provide concentration data for speciﬁc mandatory compounds, they usually
disregard other pollutants such as heavy metals or pesticides. Nevertheless, ﬁeld measurements
are relatively easy to implement as WWTPs outﬂows are continuous sources, which is not the
case for CSOs and SSSOs. The unpredictible nature of CSOs, and to a less extent SSSOs, make
measurements very tricky to implement (Cosco et al., 2018). For that reason, there is a scarcity
of data which makes the modeling of pollutant loads subjected to high uncertainties (Vezzaro
et al., 2009; Sonnenberg et al., 2011). Thus, more efﬁcient methods are needed to be able to
evaluate the impacts of these urban point sources.
1
==PAGE:19==
Birch et al. (2013) have explored the use of ﬂow-through passive samplers measuring heavy
metals concentrations in stormwater runoff in combination with modeling. They showed the
potential of this approach for long-term monitoring and results interpretation. Sediments can
be considered as natural passive samplers as they act like sinks for contaminants, and particu-
larly heavy metals (Salomons and Stigliani, 2012; Brils, 2008; Kronvang et al., 2003). Heavy
metals are a quite reliable source of information as they are not affected by degradation pro-
cesses (Chapra, 2008). Moreover they are good indicators for urban outlets contamination as
they are commonly found in their outﬂows (Boutrup et al., 2015; Vezzaro et al., 2018). Field
measurements of sediments are also easy to implement as they can be done after and not during
the overﬂow events. Then, modeling could be used to turn the information from this passive
sampling into a way of backtracking pollutant loads from discharges.
1.2
Project aim
The aim of this Master Thesis is to evaluate the possibility of using heavy metals concentrations
in sediments as ”passive samplers” to back calculate the urban point discharges pollution loads.
To do so, a combination of ﬁeld data measurements and modeling will be used. Heavy metals
were chosen because they are present in urban point source discharges (WWTP efﬂuent, CSO,
SSSO) and are inert in water and sediments, which make them a reliable source of informa-
tion. Then, this approach was tested using data from Usserød stream, located in the Hørsholm
municipality in Denmark. The main goals are the following:
• Quantify contamination of heavy metals in an urban stream water and sediments focusing
on urban outlets
• Build a model predicting the presence of heavy metals originating from urban point dis-
charges and assess the model’s robustness for the intended purpose
• Assess whether heavy metals are posing a risk to the environment in Usserød

2
Combining ﬁeld work and modeling: Description of the
approach
Combining ﬁeld work and modeling is a choice which takes the advantages of both methods.
Field work is the way to get the most accurate data wherever samples can be taken. How-
ever, ﬁeld measurement are extremely time-consuming, expensive, only give a limited amount
of data, do not take into account time variations and can be not applicable in every case. In-
deed, for instance, whereas it is easy to get water measurements from continuous ﬂows such as
WWTP outlets, the same is not true for discontinuous ﬂows such as CSOs and SSSOs. To get
measurement in these cases, it is necessary to go on the ﬁeld right when the overﬂow is hap-
pening. Unfortunately, overﬂows cannot accurately be predicted therefore it makes the logistic
of ﬁeld measurements very complicated. That is why data about pollutants concentrations in
overﬂows are very rare.
Modeling does not know such logistic-related issues, however it can be very data-intensive.
Building a model usually requires a lot of data for model calibration and validation. In Den-
mark, the concentrations in COD, BOD, total N, NH3-N, total P and suspended solids (SS)
are the only concentrations mandatory to measure in WWTP outlets (Retsinformation, 2017b).
For a WWTP of more than 50000 persons equivalent (PE), which is the case of the Usserød
one, only 24 measurements of these mandatory compounds per year are required (Retsinfor-
mation, 2017b). Other micropollutants such as heavy metals are usually never measured. What
is known about overﬂows is basically the volume which had been discharged once it had hap-
pened. The pollutant loads can only be estimated based on data found in the literature, but
highly varies depending on the type of catchment and land use (Vezzaro et al., 2018, 2019).
The study site, a ca. 200m reach of the Usserød stream, is situated within a peri-urban catch-
ment. It is necessary to know what type of contamination occurs in this type of area to select
which compounds would be interesting to measure and model. Peri-urban catchments are com-
prised of both agricultural and urban activities, leading to the potential for complex contamina-
tion of streams due to the presence of multiple chemical stressors. In agricultural catchments a
high number of different types of pesticides are usually detected (Sonne et al., 2017; McKnight
et al., 2015; Kronvang et al., 2003). The use of manure and fertilizers can also be sources of
copper (Cu), nickel (Ni), zinc (Zn) and nitrogen (Sonne et al., 2017; Kronvang et al., 2003). Wa-
ter contaminants encountered in urban areas come from various sources. Roads and pavements
runoff provide signiﬁcant amount of PAHs, heavy metals like lead (Pb), copper and zinc, chlo-
rinated ethenes, sodium chloride but also of nutrients and organic matters (Brown and Peake,
2006; Hnatukov´a et al., 2009; Sonne et al., 2017; Koeleman, 2002; Marsalek and Rochfort,
2004). As a result of the use of weed killers, pesticides can also runoff from urban areas like in
agricultural catchments (Koeleman, 2002). Pollutants originating from sites contaminated by
old industrial activities can also inﬁltrate into groundwater and surface water (Cozzarelli et al.,
2016; Bigi, 2017). In addition, urban point sources such as WWTP, CSO and SSSO outlets can
usually be found in peri-urban areas. Their discharges include all the contaminants that can
be found in urban areas (heavy metals, pesticides, phenols, solvents, PAHs...) and thus deeply
affect the water ecosystem (Koeleman, 2002; Becouze-Lareure et al., 2012, 2016).
The next step was to decide what type of pollutants to measure from the ones listed above and
what kind of measurements to make in order to get reliable and representative data with easy
3
==PAGE:21==
access. Heavy metals appeared to well ﬁt these requirements. First, they are present both in
WWTP outﬂows at normally low concentrations, and in overﬂows at high concentrations (Vez-
zaro et al., 2018). In addition, unlike organic compounds, heavy metals are inert in water and
sediment, which means that their concentrations will not be impacted by degradation processes
such as photolysis, hydrolysis or biodegradation (Chapra, 2008). The evolution of heavy met-
als concentrations will mostly depends on sorption, settling, resuspension and transportation
downstream (Zoumis et al., 2001; Mulligan et al., 2001). It is also known that sediments often
behave like sink for contaminants, including heavy metals. Hence sediments can be considered
as passive samplers as they keep traces of contamination, discharged continuously or not. For
these reasons, it is considered possible to get reliable and representative data from heavy metals
sediment concentrations. It also allows to avoid all the complications related to implementing
ﬁeld measurements during overﬂows. The effects of a WWTP outlet were ﬁrst studied because
as it can be considered as a constant source of pollutants, the effects on sediments heavy metals
concentrations should be easier to see than for variable sources like overﬂows. However, it has
to be highlighted that heavy metals fate is affected by many other processes which were not
taken into account in the scope of this project. As it was mainly an exploratory work about
using sediments as passive samplers to backtrack pollutants loads from urban outlets, only a
simpliﬁed version of their behavior was considered to make their modeling easier.
Thus, a model representative for a ca. 200m long stretch of the Usserød stream was built. In
this model, two types of urban outlets were speciﬁcally be investigated: a WWTP outlet and
a CSO outlet. The goal of the model was to evaluate the possibility of estimating pollutant
loads emitted by urban outlets by calibrating it with sediment data. It would then avoid getting
ﬁeld measurements which are complicated to implement. Water and sediment concentrations
in heavy metals obtained through ﬁeld work and laboratory analysis were used to parameterize
the model.

Whereas the adverse effects of heavy metals are known, humans and aquatic organisms are

Seeing the available amount of data about toxicity values on different platforms such as the

tend to accumulate (Goodyear and McNeill, 1999; Baby et al., 2010). The aquatic organisms
take the heavy metals through the body surface, gills an food (Baby et al., 2010). They will tend

copper), neurotoxic effects (lead, copper), skin or mucosal irritation (copper, chromium), can-
cers (arsenic, nickel) or hepatic damages (cobalt, copper) (J¨arup, 2003; Sharma and Agrawal,

3.2
Processes affecting the fate of heavy metals
3.2.1
Physico-chemical processes
Unlike other contaminants, especially organic ones, heavy metals cannot degrade into harmless
compounds (Holm and Christensen, 1997; Bubb and Lester, 1991). Moreover, it is persistent
in aquatic systems and many studies showed that they are very likely to bioaccumulate. Hence,
the main processes which affect heavy metals fate are precipitation, sorption and complexation.
Arsenic and chromium can also be affected by redox processes and methylation, which can
reduce their toxicity (Holm and Christensen, 1997; Mason, 2012).
The word ”sorption” actually gathered two different physico-chemical processes: adsorption,
which refers to a bonding on the particles’ surface, and absorption, which refers to a bonding
within the solid particles’ structure. Adsorption is the most important of the two processes
(Christensen and Holm, 1999; Selim, 2012). In general, the main parameters impacting heavy
metals sorption are (Christensen and Holm, 1999; Allison and Allison, 2005; Bubb and Lester,
1991; Selim, 2012; Lin et al., 2002):
• Kinetics: sorption is considered as being a fast process. For instance, equilibrium be-
tween sorbed and solubilised cadmium was reached in 1 hour, though most of the sorp-
tion occured in the ﬁrst 10 minutes. That is why equilibrium state is usually assumed.
• pH: pH is the most important parameter affecting sorption. At low pH, the H+ protons
will compete with the metals for sorption sites, decreasing the sorption. At higher pH,
iron and manganese oxides, which can create strong bonds with metals, are more likely to
see a change in their charges resulting in an increase in sorption. That is why regression
equations only depending on pH can be used to estimate Kd.
• Cations: it is expected that cations such as sodium, calcium or magnesium would com-
pete for sorption sites under reducing conditions. As a consequence, the mobility of
heavy metals is increased. However the effect is expected not to be as important as pH.
• Metals: in a natural media and especially in a contaminated environment, heavy metals
are almost never found individually but in a mixture of different heavy metals at different
concentrations. Competition can also occur between them, reducing sorption. However
this is true only if the metals are competing for the same sites, which may not be the case
for all heavy metals.
• Sediment composition: it is expecting that sediment characteristics will have an effect
on heavy metals sorption. Indeed, different types of sediment can have different sorption
capacity depending on the compound. For example, it has been shown that copper is
likely to form complexes with organic ligands whereas lead is more easily bound to
inorganic solids. Zn has also shown a tendency to bind to ﬁne sand particle.

If SI = 0 (i.e. K = IAP), equilibrium is reached. If SI > 0 (i.e. IAP < K), the solution is

slow process but it can be stimulated, by for instance adding lime or magnesia which contain
dissolved metals: when added to the solution, the metals in solution will react with hydroxide
ions to form metal hydroxide solids. Both sorption and precipitation can compete at higher
concentrations and they can also be both affected by complexation (Holm and Christensen,
1997; Christensen and Holm, 1999; Abi D et al., 2011).
Heavy metals can also bound in complexes, which can be inorganic ligands (chloride, sulfate...)
or organic compounds (ﬂuvic acid, cellulose, dextrin...). This process is called complexation
and is likely to occur in situation of organic carbon contamination. Complexation depends on
the compound’s concentration, the pH and the type of ligands. This process can either increase
or decrease the heavy metal mobility. If the ligand has an afﬁnity for soil surfaces, such as
hydroxide ions, the metal sorption will increase. On the other hand, in case of ions with low
afﬁnity for soil surfaces, such as chloride ions, heavy metals will remain in the aqueous phase
rather than settling (Christensen and Holm, 1999; Trivunac and Stevanovic, 2006; Rieuwerts
et al., 1998).
Reduction and oxidation processes are characterised by electron(s) exchange(s) from an atom
to another. They can inﬂuence the fate of heavy metals in water as they inﬂuence their solu-
bility and their mobility. For instance, reducing environments increase Fe and Mn solubility
while it decreases Cr one. Even if some compounds are not directly impacted by redox con-
ditions (when considering conditions encountered in natural environments), the availability of
complexing agents adsorbing Fe and Mn (hydr)oxides and redox-sensitive anions which can
generate precipitation, will indirectly affect them. Fe and Mn oxides and hydroxides have
a great power of heavy metals adsorption, as adsorbants or coprecipitating matrices. Under
slightly reducing or oxidizing conditions, with a medium to acidic pH range and slight alka-
linity, Fe and Mn solids can precipitate as carbonates, considerably reducing their ability to
adsorb heavy metals (Bourg and Loch, 1995; Rieuwerts et al., 1998).

3.2.2
Physical processes
As said in the previous section, heavy metals can form complexes or adsorb onto suspended
solids. Hence they will tend to settle in the stream bed sediments (Bubb and Lester, 1991;
Est`ebe et al., 1998; Saget et al., 1995). Heavy metals settling depends on physical and hy-
drodynamic factors such as the particles size, ﬂow velocity and direction, the water depth, the
morphology of the stream, the turbidity... (Bubb and Lester, 1991; Chapra, 2008).
Once settled, heavy metals are not permanently bound to the sediment layer. They can be
remobilized by different various chemical and physical processes. Physically, they can get
resuspended because of turbulent events such as storms or boat trafﬁc (Bubb and Lester, 1991).
Benthic organisms can also cause resuspension of sediments through their feeding activities
(Reynoldson, 1987).

4.1
Study site: Usserød stream
The ﬁeld campaign was conducted along a 205m reach of the Usserød stream, which is located
in the Hørsholm municipality, 30km north of Copenhagen, in Denmark. The stream is 2-4 m
wide and 0.5-1.5 m deep, and drains a peri-urban catchment of 126km2. The map of the entire
catchment can be seen in Appendix A.2. The total length of the stream is around 9 km. It has

starts approximately at 3,8 km downstream the lake. At around 175m from the ﬁrst sampling
station, Usserød is joined by its tributary, the Dønse stream.
Right after the Usserød WWTP, the annual average ﬂow is of 640 l/s, monthly ranging from
138 l/s to 1057 l/s (2014-2018) (Hydroinform (2019)). At the point of the urban outlet, the
WWTP outﬂow contributed from 30% to 97% to Usserød ﬂow in 2018. The annual average
ﬂow of the Dønse stream right before it joins Usserød is 183.6 l/s, with monthly variation from
1.32 l/s to 998 l/s (2016-2018) (Hydroinform, 2019). The average annual precipitation in the
area is 732 mm/y (2006-2015) (DMI, 2019).

4.2
Investigation in the Usserød stream
4.2.1
Field campaign overview
This project was conducted in conjunction with the Danish Innovation Foundation project
Riverscapes. Overall, very little information about chemicals in Danish surface water exist
(Bigi et al., 2018). Hence, no information about heavy metals contamination in Usserød could
be found.
As explained in Section 2, one of the main point of the ﬁeld investigation was to get data
to base the model on. Heavy metals concentrations were chosen for their reliability, their
ease to measure and their presence in the urban point sources outlets. It also allowed to get
an overview of the contamination in heavy metals in this part of the stream. Contamination
could be originating from different sources such as the Usserød WWTP outlet, overﬂow outlets,
the dam and catchment runoff on heavy metal concentrations in Usserød. The investigation
consisted in making the following measurements:
1. The concentrations in heavy metals As, Cr, Cd, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn in Usserød stream
water
2. The concentrations in heavy metals As, Co, Cr, Cd, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn in Usserød stream
bed sediments
3. The concentrations in Mn and Fe in water and Mn in sediments to get a view of the redox
conditions in Usserød
The sampling stations can be seen in Figure 4. Sediment and water samples were extracted at
respectively 7 and 9 different stations. Extra water samples were taken to investigate the loca-
tion of the point of fully mixed conditions (FMC). To check whether a point of the stream is
fully mixed, the concentrations along the width has to be less than 5% different to the average
one (Fischer et al., 1979). Rønde et al. (2017) found out that in case of groundwater plume
constantly discharging into a stream, the point of FMC was located approximately ten times
the width of the stream where the mixing occurs. It is in compliance with Danish guidelines for
determining impacts from groundwater plumes emanating from contaminated sites discharging
into streams. To check whether a similar relation could be applied in the case of urban outlets,
three water samples along the width were taken at ten times the width of the stream where the
WWTP emits in Usserød. As the width of the stream was of approximately 3 meters at that
point, the water samples had to be taken approximately 30 meters downstream. However, due
to lack of tools to measure distances during the ﬁeld excursion, the FMC water samples were
taken approximately 1 meter upstream station D, i.e. around 50 meters after the WWTP out-
let instead of 30 meters. Moreover, it has to be noted that these samples were taken after the
stations further upstream, i.e. stations E, F, G and H. Then it is possible that the data had been
skewed due to resuspension of the sediments when walking in the stream and taking sediment
samples.

4.3.1
Preparation of the sediment samples
The following hours after the sediment samples had been collected, the plastic bags containing
the sediments were placed into a freezer at -40 °C over the night.
Before analyzing the sediment samples, the water needed to be removed. To do so, the day
after, the sediment samples were placed into a vacuum machine at -98°C for three days (see
Figure 6a). This treatment removed water from the sediments. At the end of the three days,
all the water which had been removed from the samples could be seen as an ice layer on the
bottom of the vacuum machine (see Figure 6b).
The next step was to crush the samples into thin powder. Even if a very little portion was
used, 100g was necessary for each sample because the crush container had to be full enough to
correctly grind. On the other hand if it was too ﬁlled, the media was not going to be ground
efﬁciently. First, the sediment sample was sieved through a 2mm grid. This removed big
particles such as rocks, shells or vegetables. Then the sediments were placed into the grinding
container as seen in Figure 6c. The container was placed into the Vibratory Disc Mill (VDM)
machine for 24 seconds at 710 Hz (see Figure 6d). If the sample looked very heterogeneous
with big particles, time could be doubled. Between each sample, the whole setup was carefully
cleaned with a vacuum cleaner, an air gun and paper so that no cross-contamination occurred.
The obtained powder was split up using a plastic ruler (see Figure 6e) to get a small vial for the
heavy metal analysis. The samples were then placed under a desiccator which controlled the
humidity level so that the powder did not get water from the atmosphere (see Figure 6f).

should not be applied on it. For this same method, less than 0.13 pH unit of difference could be

to stabilize, the difference could only be due to the stabilization time and not to the changing
of media.

ﬂuents. However Boutrup et al. (2015) reported that Zn concentrations in WWTPs’ efﬂuent
witnessed a signiﬁcant reduction from the period 2004-2006 to 2011-2014: while its occur-

found to be exceeding in various types of surface waters such as a stream located within a

exceeding guidelines in Danish streams, unlike As, which was found in average to be above the
quality standard. This observation supports the idea that As may naturally be at a high level in
Denmark. It is however complicated to evaluate how much the As background concentration
contributes as no data could be found about As background concentrations is Denmark.
It is important to notice that guidelines are subjected to change. The last update is from 2017,
which came to replace the guidelines from 2010. Thus concentrations which were meeting
the requirements in 2010 could fail to meet the ones from 2017, or the other way around.
Indeed, whereas the general quality requirement for As is of 0.6 µg/L today, which made As
concentrations exceeding it in Usserød, the criterion was only of 4.3 µg/L in 2010. Legally,
As concentration would have by that time not caused any problem. On the other hand, Ni
quality guideline recorded an important increase, going from 2.3 µg/L in 2010 to 8.6 µg/L in
2017. Even though Ni concentrations were exceeding none of these criteria in Usserød case, the
concentrations are much closer to the 2010 limit. It shows that it is necessary to be careful to
have the last updated guidelines but also to see beyond the guidelines: they are only indicators
of the thresholds suspected of being hazardous for the environment however danger could occur
at lower as well as higher levels.

less than 5 times Zn detection limit. The same comments can be made about sediment results:
concentrations usually showed low RSD and higher RSD values seemed to be recorded for
concentrations closer to the detection limit.

It has to be highlighted that these calculations are based on very rough estimations of resus-

of magnitude of the range of the inﬂuence of this process on the measurements.
4.5.4
Solubility product calculations
It is important to know whether precipitation can occur or not. Indeed as heavy metals pre-
cipitate, they form solids and then tend to settle (Saget et al., 1995). Hence the bioavailability
decreases but the contamination in sediment increases. The likeliness of precipitation can be
estimated using the Saturation Index (SI), explained in Section 3.2.1.
The calculations were made for two compounds, Zn and Cu, at the stations where pH values
were available (station A, B and D). The precipitations reactions are the following:
Zn(OH)2 = Zn2+ +2OH−
Cu(OH)2 = Cu2+ +2OH−
The trickiest part was to evaluate the activities of the ions. The activities depend on the concen-
trations of all ions in solution. However, there was no access to this value in this case. Kielland
(1937) showed that for solution with an ionic concentration of 0.001, the activities of Cu and
Zn ions are comprised within the range [0.903;0.905] at 25°C, and go down to [0.37;0.405] for
a ionic concentration of 0.2. Crocket and Winchester (1966), for ionic concentrations varying
from 0.003 to 0.01 and temperatures from 25°C to 50°C, estimated the activity of Zn2+ to be
within the range [0.68;0.80]. Hence, these values were taken as an estimation of the ranges
within which activities could be found.
Thus, two different cases were considered for the calculations: the case of an inﬁnite dilution
meaning that γion = 1, and the case of a non-inﬁnite dilution with a value of activity coefﬁcient
γion = 0.5. The ionic product of water logKw was taken equal to 14.8 (estimated from Covington
et al. (1966); Holzapfel (1969) for a water temperature varying between 3 and 5°C). The results
can be seen on the graphs in Figure 10. Only the most critical case, i.e. when activities are equal
to 1, is shown. The case where γion = 0.5 and the calculations steps can be found in Appendix
A.6.
The results of the calculations can be seen in Figure 10. For the three stations A, B and D
the concentrations in OH−, Zn2+ and Cu2+ should not lead to precipitation as they are situ-
ated under the solubility product equilibrium line. However, the values are very close to the
solubility product equilibrium in the case of Cu. Indeed, Holm and Christensen (1997) state
that at pH 8 the solubility of Cu2+ is in equilibrium with Cu(OH)2 at concentrations lower
than 5 µg/L. As Cu concentrations were already reaching values higher than 3 µg/L, they may
exceed this threshold during overﬂow events or WWTP malfunctioning, which could lead to
Cu precipitation in Cu(OH)2.
Nevertheless, when the concentrations are corrected with the activity coefﬁcient, the actual
available concentration is decreased. Hence, it makes the points move away from the equilib-

assessing the likeliness of a risk for the ecosystem. In that case, it can be said that the risk for

4.5.7
Estimation of the ratio dissolved/sorbed of heavy metals concentrations in water
The values of concentrations measured in the stream were for the dissolved phase. As the values
of concentrations used as input for the model were total concentrations in water, it was neces-
sary to get an estimation of the ratio solubilized/sorbed concentrations for each compound. It
is important to notice that the dissolved phase also includes the metals sorbed to dissolved or-
ganic carbon (DOC). The Kd values that had to be used to calculate this ratio were the ones
for SS/water phase. To get the total concentration in water, the sorbed concentration has to be
added to the solubilized concentration. The following equation describes this relation:
{HM}tot = XHM +SHM
(4.3)
Where {HM}tot (in mg/L) is the heavy metal total concentration in water, XHM (in mg/L)
the heavy metal sorbed concentration on SS and SHM (in mg/L) the heavy metal solubilized
concentration. At a steady state, XHM and SHM concentrations relation can be expressed using
Kd (in L/kg) and the total concentrations of suspended solids in water XTSS (in mg/L):
XHM = KdXTSSSHM
(4.4)
Hence the total concentrations could be calculated for the three stations for which the concen-
trations in total suspended solids (TSS) were collected by the Riversacpe project: stations A, B
and D. The Kd values used were the ones reported in Allison and Allison (2005). The graphs
presenting the ratio in % of each phase of the total water concentration for each compound can
be seen in Figure 11. Cd and Pb were not displayed as their concentrations were under the
detection limit.
It can be said that most of the compounds were expected to be mostly present in the solubilized
phase. For some of the compounds the pattern was very clear at each station (Ni, Cu, As,
Cd) and the solubilized phase represented at least 60% of the total concentration. For Cr and
Zn not such a clear pattern could be found: for station D the solubilized phase was slightly
above 50% whereas for stations B and A the sorbed phase represented almost 60% of the total
concentration. However it has to be highlighted these results can just provide a general idea of
the real distribution as the Kd values used were not measured speciﬁcally for that case but taken
from the literature. Indeed, Smolyakov et al. (2010) reported that Cu had higher tendency to
sorb than Zn, whereas the previous graphs show than it would be Zn and similar results were
found by Vukovi´c et al. (2011). Moreover, steady state was assumed whereas it could be not the
case, especially for the compounds emitted by the WWTP outlet (Zn and Cu mainly). Beside
estimating the total concentration in water for the model inputs, the idea is also to use these
results to compare them with the model outputs.

5
Modeling: Building of a predictive model based on ﬁeld
measurements
5.1
Modeling goals
The main goals of the modeling part was to evaluate the possibility of using heavy metals
concentrations in sediments to backtrack pollutants loads from urban point sources. A model
of Usserød was created to study the fate of zinc. This compound was selected because it is the
only one which was exceeding the MAC guideline.
5.2
Introduction to WEST and the IUWS MP library
The software DHI WEST was used for the modeling part. Its main application is to model
WWTP systems, thus many processes mostly concern organic compounds. Micropollutants
(MPs), which include heavy metals, are not considered in the basic library of WEST. Hence
another model library which enables the software to track the fate of MPs, and in this project
speciﬁcally of heavy metals, was developed by Vezzaro et al. (2014). The name of the library
is WEST.IUWSMP2014.BlockLib. The objectives of this library are:
• Estimating micro pollutants occurrence and fate in urban water systems allowing the
assessment of mitigation strategies for control of MP emissions
• Helping the optimization of monitoring programs
• Assessing the fulﬁlment of the water bodies ecological statues.
This library was developed as part of the European project ScorePP. This model is based on
the River Quality Model No. 1 made by the IWA Task Group on River Quality Modeling and
developed to include MPs fate modeling (Vezzaro et al., 2009).
5.3
Model description
The elements used to set up the model were rivers and river inputs. The rivers are modeled as
continuous stirred-tank reactor (CSTR) made of two compartments: bulk water and sediment.
These two compartments are connected with each other through settling, resuspension and dif-
fusion. These processes as well as the ones affecting heavy metals concentrations are described
below. A mass balance for the water ﬂow is made between each river stretch using the physical
parameters of the stretch entered in the model and the Manning equation.
All the processes affecting the ratio sorbed/dissolved phase and modeled in WEST and their
associated parameters can be seen in Table 17 and 18. A schematic view of the processes taking
place can be seen in Figure 12.
5.3.1
Physico-chemical processes
As WEST is primarily dedicated to organic pollutants, many processes that are implemented
in the software do not concern heavy metals. For instance, they are not concerned by decay
processes such as biodegradation or hydrolisis which would make the total amount of the com-
pound decrease (Chapra, 2008; Srivastava and Majumder, 2008). Therefore these processes
were canceled in the model. On the other hand, some other processes which would affect
heavy metals fate such as speciation or precipitation, are not taken into account in the model.
Thus using this model lead to simpliﬁcations about heavy metals behavior in surface water.

5.6
Description of the modeling scenarios
Five different scenarios were tested with Zn:
1. Under normal conditions
2. Under overﬂow conditions
3. Under overﬂow conditions with an increase in mean particle size
4. Under overﬂow conditions with the activation of the resuspension process
5. Under overﬂow conditions simulated for four foc values covering a range of commonly
found foc values
5.6.1
Modeling under normal conditions
The ﬁrst simulations were made under normal conditions, i.e. without taking into account
overﬂows neither resuspension. Resuspension was canceled at ﬁrst to simplify the analyse of
the model functioning. WWTP ﬂow data from year 2018 was used for the WWTP outﬂow. For
Usserød and Dønse ﬂows data from 2018 were also used. The only urban outlet considered in
this scenario was the WWTP outlet. The simulations lasted 150 days.
5.6.2
Modeling of wet weather discharges
The main goal of these simulations was to see how the overﬂows affected the concentrations
in sediments. Sediment concentrations behavior could give indications about when would be a
good timing to make measurements and/or how long the effects of an overﬂow remain measur-
able in the ﬁeld.
The overﬂows happening during heavy rain events are characterized by an increase in ﬂow and
an overall increase in pollutants load (Vezzaro et al., 2018). As it was shown in Figure 2 many
outlets, of known or unknown type, were discharging in this speciﬁc stretch of Usserød. It was
known from the WWTP data that the bypass pipe of the WWTP was located upstream station
H. However, if the overﬂows were input at station H in the model, it would not be possible to
compare the effects of the overﬂow with an Usserød stretch not impacted by the overﬂow. For
that reason, the overﬂows were modeled as being emitted from the WWTP outlet at station G.
The overﬂows data from 2018 were given by the WWTP services. The overﬂow volumes were
added to the WWTP ﬂow at the exact time they were measured. In total, over the 150 days
covered by the simulations, 8 overﬂow events occurred including 7 in 5 consecutive days. The
graphs showing the WWTP ﬂow and overﬂow events ﬂow can be seen in Figure 30 in Appendix
42
==PAGE:60==
A.12. During the overﬂow periods, the TSS concentration was increased from 2.1 mg/L to 280
mg/L. This value is situated within the range of mean values that were measured for CSOs TSS
concentrations (Vezzaro et al., 2018). The concentrations in heavy metals was also increased
to 500 µg/l of total Zn concentration. This value is situated approximately in the middle of the
total concentration range given in Vezzaro et al. (2018) for CSO events.
5.6.3
Changing in particle size
The model takes as an input a mean particle size and from it makes a Gaussian particle size
distribution. It is known that grain size can have great impact on particle settling as it is one
of the main parameter used to calculate the settling velocity (Dietrich, 1982; Chapra, 2008).
Moreover, it has been shown that it can also inﬂuence heavy metals distribution in sediment
and sorption (Lin et al., 2002; Mckay et al., 1989). By default, the particle mean size is set to
4.5×10−5m. What was expected to make sediment concentration in Zn increase was increasing
mean size article as suspended particles, on which Zn sorbs onto, would settle faster. As this
project particularly focused on sediment concentration, the particle size was arbitrarily set to
4.5×10−4m.
5.6.4
Modeling of resuspension
The resuspension process was not taken into account except for this scenario. To do so, the
critical bottom shear stress also called critical erosion stress (τcrit,res) was set at a very high
threshold (1000 N/m2). As the shear stress depends on the ﬂow, this threshold would never be
reached under common conditions as set in this model. Another option was to set the erodability
constant E0 to 0 g/(m2d), i.e. the bed stream sediments cannot physically not be resuspended.
To determine the threshold at which the particles should resuspend, simulations with overﬂows
were made and the bottom shear stress was plotted. Resuspension should not occur all the time
but only for some speciﬁc events when the stream ﬂow was particularly strong. It also needed
to be adapted to each stretch as each stretch had its own physical and chemical characteristics.
5.6.5
Variation the fraction of organic carbon (foc)
Foc measures were not performed on the sediment samples in this project. As explained in
Section 5.4, the value of 8.5% for foc was taken because it both ﬁtted Kronvang et al. (2003)
and Hnatukov´a et al. (2009) papers which were considering streams quite similar to Usserød.
However ﬁrst, Kronvang et al. (2003) gives a range of value which goes from 5.5% to 16.1%,
which is quite wide. Moreover, Freitas et al. (2015) who studied an efﬂuent of the river Tames
also impacted by a WWTP outlet, found values of foc in sediment comprised in the range
[1;6.5]. Hence, even within the same river stretch, foc is a value which is subjected to change.
This parameter is of a great importance as it leads some of the most important processes for
heavy metals in streams which are sorption and desorption. Then foc values of 5% and 15%
were considered. At a foc of 5%, resulting KOC values is higher than at 8.5%, and at a foc of
15% KOC values is lower. Hence, to only get to the extreme conditions, only the highest KOC
values at foc of 5% and the lowest at foc of 15% were simulated. A summary of the KOC values
assiociated with their foc values tested can be seen in Table 22.

6
Perspectives
The suggestions made below could help improving the global understanding of Usserød con-
tamination and the model accuracy and reliability.
6.1
Field work part
• It was observed that concentrations in heavy metals were higher upstream the WWTP
outlet. A possible explanation is that pollution comes from the WWTP overﬂow outlet
and/or the dam located around 150m upstream the WWTP outlet. To check this hy-
pothesis, more water and sediment measurements should be made between the overﬂow
outlet/the dam and the outlet of the WWTP.
• Neither Kd nor foc measurements were made from sediment samples. However they are
very complicated to evaluate only from literature data as they highly depend on the type
of surface water and catchment. As they lead the crucial process of sorption, for model
purposes it would be relevant to make lab measurements.
• Performing a grain size analysis could also be of interest. Even if for the current model,
the process of sorption does not depend on grain size, still it was shown that grain size
had great impact on settling and then on sediments pollutant concentrations.
• Precipitation is an important process affecting heavy metals concentration in water. It
is all the more important that it is more likely to happen during overﬂow events when
concentrations in pollutants greatly increase. Measuring ionic strength as well as the
concentration of the compounds which can precipitate with heavy metals would help
assessing the likeliness of precipitation.
6.2
Modeling part
• Only Zn fate was simulated in this project. Other compounds such as As, which was the
other compound exceeding guidelines in Usserød, should also be studied. Overall, only
few parameters among many others in a complex model were investigated. More time
should be spent on pointing which parameters could be of importance and then examine
their inﬂuence on the model results. For instance, the resuspension process could be
further explored by studying the inﬂuence of the erodability constant.
• The software used was primarily developed to model organic pollutants fate, hence it not
optimized to model heavy metals fate as they are controlled by different processes. If the
same model keeps being used, then it should be considered to include some important
processes determining heavy metals concentration such as precipitation, complexation or
redox processes. The model should also be enabled to receive either Koc or Kd values
as input depending on the type of micropollutants considered. Indeed, Kd is the relevant
parameter to consider for heavy metals but not necessarily for organic pollutants.
• Combined with more measurements upstream the WWTP outlet, the Usserød upstream
input could be replaced by an extra river stretch that would linked the WWTP overﬂow
outlet to station H. It would allow simulating the overﬂows where they occur in reality.
• The simulations were run only for a period of 150 days. Longer simulations (at least one
year) would help understanding the long term behavior of sediments pollutant concentra-
57
==PAGE:75==
tion and assessing how well the model ﬁts to reality. However it would also require more
ﬁeld measurements to compare the results with.
• The library used to create the model allowed simulating only one pollutant at a time.
This simpliﬁcation neglects the potential interactions that exist between the pollutants. It
could become very important in case of high concentrations of species that can combine
and precipitate (even though precipitation is not yet included in the current model).
• The current model considers sediments as a dense compartment of suspended solids.
However, Kd values are different depending on the phases considered, for instance if it
is water/SS, water/sediment or sediment/pore water. It could be considered to enable to
input different Kd values depending on the compartment (water or sediment).
• The main issue regarding pollutant concentration in sediments is the way the model cal-
culates it: sediment concentration decreases because particles settle at a higher rate than
sorbed pollutants. This could be improved for example by setting a maximum limit to
sediment volume or height, above which sediment particles would get resuspended. It
would avoid modeling an inﬁnite increase in sediment mass resulting in an artiﬁcial de-
crease in pollutant concentration, as it was observed.

7
Conclusion
The objective of this study was to evaluate the possibility of using sediments as passive sam-
plers to backtrack pollutants loads from urban outlets. To do so, an approach combining ﬁeld
measurements and modeling was carried out. Field measurements evaluated heavy metals oc-
currence in water and sediments in a speciﬁc stretch of Usserød stream, situated upstream and
downstream a WWTP outlet. Then, the model was calibrated to study Zn fate using these data
and real ﬂow and overﬂow data from 2018. The goal was to see whether real overﬂow events
could be clearly identiﬁed by just looking at Zn sediment concentration.
Water and sediment samples were respectively taken a nine and seven different locations. Zn
and As were found to exceed respectively the MAC and the general quality requirement in water
at almost all locations. Sediment concentrations were exceeding the soil quality criteria in Cd
upstream the WWTP and the soil cut-off criteria in As at the station the furthest downstream
and in the bottom layer of Dønse station, an Usserød tributary.
• Overall, sediments presented higher concentrations upstream the WWTP outlet. The
overﬂow outlet from the Usserød WWTP and a dam are located approximately 150m
upstream the WWTP outlet, which may explain the accumulation of heavy metals before
the WWTP. Then, the WWTP outﬂow would wash off the contaminants by resuspending
them. Zn was exceeding the MAC guideline at every Usserød station, but not in Dønse.
In the WWTP outlet, Zn water concentration was more than three times higher than
usptream the WWTP outlet, resulting in a signiﬁcant increase in Zn water concentration
downstream the outlet. Zn was gradually decreasing along the stretch, suggesting that
the main inﬂow of Zn in Usserød came from the WWTP outlet. Arsenic was found to
exceed the general quality requirement in water at every station except in the WWTP
outlet and right after it, on the right side of the stream. As it was also exceeding in
sediments at two stations downstream, these results indicated that As may come from
diffuse geogenic source or may be mobilized from contaminated aquifer due to reduced
conditions. Cu was never found to be exceeding the water guidelines, however it showed
concentrations close to the guidelines. The WWTP outlet and Dønse tributary reached the
highest Cu concentrations. High concentrations in Dønse could be due to the numerous
outlets bringing runoff from the roads into the stream.
A model of Usserød was implemented emulating the stretches deﬁned by the sampling stations.
From the ﬁeld data were estimated the parameters which were useful for the model such as
concentration inputs, Kd values and sorbed/dissolved phases ratio in water. Zn fate was studied
following ﬁve different scenarios: ﬁrstly under normal and overﬂow conditions, then under
overﬂow conditions with an increase in the mean particle size, then with the activation of the
resuspension process and ﬁnally comparing four different foc values.
• The model revealed clear differences between the scenarios with and without overﬂows.
Under overﬂow conditions, whereas Zn water concentrations showed high peaks which
faded in less than a day, Zn sediment concentrations presented signiﬁcant steps with
long-lasting effects. These results showed that indeed sediments could be good passive
samplers as the effects of several overﬂow events would be measurable in the ﬁeld with-
out being time-constraining.
• The model also suggested that the resuspension process does not signiﬁcantly impact
59
==PAGE:77==
Zn concentration in Usserød. However, the drop in metals sediment concentrations be-
tween the stations upstream and downstream the WWTP showed that the WWTP may
resuspend sediments, which was not evidenced in the model.
• While mean particle size and foc values had low impact on Zn water concentration, large
differences could be seen for Zn sediment concentrations. Thus, these parameters are
crucial to determine in a view of using the model results to evaluate contaminants loads.
• Even if the library used is at ﬁrst not made to model heavy metals fate, it still allowed to
show that this kind of model is able to depict clear differences in sediment concentrations
between scenarios with/without overﬂows. Then it would be possible to estimate over-
ﬂows occurrence by comparing ﬁeld measured sediment concentrations and the sediment
concentrations given by the model under normal (i.e. without overﬂows) conditions.
The toxic risk related to the presence of heavy metals in the stream was assessed making toxic
units (TU) calculations. An exploratory work evaluating the likeliness of precipitation was also
performed.
• Even though contamination in Usserød was relatively low, TU calculations showed that it
was still posing a risk to the environment. At every station and no matter the type of end-
point chosen (LC50, EC50 mortality and EC50 immobilization), log ∑TU was always
higher than -2, suggesting a risk of lethal effects for the biota. Furthermore, concentra-
tion measurements were made at speciﬁc time of a speciﬁc day. As highlighted by the
model results, Zn water concentration, and very likely any heavy metal concentration,
was subjected to variations, even within the same day. Moreover, Zn water concentration
was soaring during overﬂow events. During these events, contamination can have very
serious acute effects on the biota.
• Solubility calculations were made to evaluate the likeliness of Zn and Cu precipitating
respectively in Zn(OH)2 and in Cu(OH)2. It showed that under the conditions observed at
the time of the ﬁeld investigation, Zn and Cu should not precipitate in these compounds.
The likeliness of precipitation in other compounds could not be studied due to lack of
concentration data. However, precipitation could happen during overﬂow events as con-
centrations in heavy metals are expected to be much higher. Then, it could be that the
conditions are such that some heavy metals precipitate. In that case, the amount of heavy
metals in water would decrease, reducing their bioavailibity and then limiting the toxic
effects. Nevertheless, contaminants would still be present in sediments and subjected to
remobilization in water.

A.6
Solubility product calculations
The two precipitation equations that are considered here are:
Zn(OH)2 = Zn2+ +2OH−
Cu(OH)2 = Cu2+ +2OH−
Their associated solubility products are (Holm & Christensen, 1997):
Ksp = [Zn2+][OH−]2 and log(Ksp) = log(Zn2+)+2log(OH−) = −15.5
Ksp = [Cu2+][OH−]2 and log(Ksp) = log(Zn2+)+2log(OH−) = −19.32
where the values into brackets represent activities.
To plot the solubility product equilibrium, the two pH values 4 and 10 were used. Then the
concentrations in [OH-] at these two values were calculated and from them, using the above
equations, were deduced the logarithms of Zn and Cu concentrations under these conditions at
equilibrium.
The concentrations in [OH-] at stations A, B and D were calculated from the pH data obtained
from the Riverscapes project. In the case of ideal solution, the activity is taken equal to the dis-
solved concentration measured in Usserød. Otherwise, the dissolved concentration is corrected
with an activity coefﬁcient as explained in Section 3.2.1. For the latter case, γ =0.5. The values
calculated and used to make the graphs can be seen in Table 33.
