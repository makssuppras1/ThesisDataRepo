2 | Abstract  
 
It is essential to recognise humanity as a considerable impactor on the Earth if destabilisation of the 
Earth System (ES) should be avoided. As a result, a framework based on Planetary Boundaries (PB) 
has been introduced, where a share of safe operating space (SoSOS) can be assigned to products and 
services. This project conducted an absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA) on the 
Danish company Arla and their annual supply of either petrochemical or bio-based high-density 
polyethylene (HDPE) bottles to the United Kingdom (UK). This was followed by a scenario analysis, 
where five improvement options were investigated. Additionally, a comparative life cycle assessment 
(LCA) of a petrochemical and bio-based HDPE bottle in a closed loop recycling system was studied 
to investigate which bottle performed environmentally better than the other. The AESA found that 
Arlas supply of HDPE bottles cannot be considered absolute sustainable with an exceedance of the 
SoSOS for 10 out of 13 impact categories for the petrochemical HDPE bottle and 11 out of 13 impact 
categories for the bio-based HDPE bottle. The five improvement options lowered the environmental 
sustainability ratios (ESRs) in the scenario analysis. However the best-case scenario still indicated 
that the assigned SoSOS was exceeded for more than half of the impact categories. The comparative 
LCA showed a 2 % difference in the emitted greenhouse gasses (GHG) for the petrochemical and bio-
based HDPE bottle and could not draw a conclusion on which type of bottle was preferable. The case 
study aimed to investigate if the supply of HDPE bottles could be considered absolute sustainable 
and if the environmental performance could be improved depending on the origin of the HDPE. 
Thereby, the eco-effectiveness and the eco-efficiency of the supply of HDPE bottles were assessed.

4 | Introduction  
Earth is resource constrained and has a certain capacity to assimilate emissions and waste (Ryberg 
et al., 2018). The environmental pressure on the Earth System (ES) is affected by human activities, 
which have become the main driver of global environmental change. These activities have the 
potential to push the ES outside the stable state known as the Holocene (Rockström et al., 2009; 
Hauschild M., Z., 2015). It is therefore essential to recognise humanity as a considerable impactor 
on the Earth, if destabilisation of the ES should be avoided (Steffen et al., 2018). As a result, a 
framework based on Planetary Boundaries (PB) has been introduced (Rockström et al., 2009; Steffen 
et al., 2015). The PBs indicate levels in which anthropogenic perturbations can occur with low risk 
of destabilising the ES. These levels are also known as the safe operating space for human activities 
(SOSPB). Human activities include all kinds of products and services that fulfil the needs of a growing 
society. Thus, it includes the supply of food packaging that ensures safety and easy distribution of 
food.  
The production, use and disposal of food packaging is associated with a multitude of 
environmental impacts (Pauer et al., 2019). These impacts vary depending on the material that is 
selected for the packaging component, which have been a much-debated topic since the 1970s 
(Hauschild et al., 2018). In order to analyse the different material compositions of packaging, life 
cycle assessments (LCA) can be performed. LCA is a standardised quantification method (EC-JRC, 
2010; ISO 2006b) to calculate environmental impacts that often are compared to those of similar 
products or services. Thus, LCA works as a ‘relative’ sustainability assessment, evaluating which 
product is performing environmentally better than the other (Hauschild, 2015). 
One of the concerns related to packaging is the environmental impacts of non-renewable 
materials, which have led to a growing demand of materials derived from renewable sources 
(Verghese, 2012). According to a research report from the European Union 2017, bio-based plastics 
have the potential to become an important strategy in the transition towards a sustainable 
development (European Union, 2017). Previous LCAs have found that bio-based plastic performs 
better than non-renewable thermoplastics, also known as petrochemical plastics, in terms of the 
emitted greenhouse gasses (GHG) (e.g., Tsiropoulos et al., 2015; Nguyen et al., 2020; Hottle et al., 
2017). However, LCAs vary in scope definitions and can easily favor one over another if only GHG 
emissions are considered or if the study only performs a cradle-to-gate assessment (Hottle et al., 
2017). Thus, it is essential to assess the potential environmental impacts from cradle-to-grave, that 
is from extraction and supply of raw materials to end-of-life (EoL), to ensure that adequate decisions 
are made (Tsiropoulos et al., 2015).   
 Assessing the environmental impacts, in relation to relative sustainability, allows for the selection 
of plastic with the best environmental performance per packaging component provided. However,

how do we know if the best environmental performance is actually good enough or just less bad? 
Since the population numbers and consumption rate is continually increasing, there is a strong need 
for assessing not just the eco-efficiency between products but also the eco-effectiveness in terms of 
fulfilling the needs of a growing society within the absolute limits of the ES (Hauschild 2015). In 
other words, we need to identify an absolute sustainable solution for providing plastic and then we 
adjust the environmental performance.  
This has led to the development of the absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA). 
AESA is an LCA-based method, where quantified environmental impacts are related to absolute 
environmental boundaries, such as the PBs (Ryberg 2020). Using the PBs as absolute limits enables 
a top-down approach, where (1) the carrying capacity of the global ecosystems towards 
environmental impact is determined, (2) the SOSPB is established and (3) the share of safe operating 
space (SoSOS) within the SOSPB is assigned to the investigated products or services. Hence the 
quantified environmental impacts are related to the assigned SoSOS and thereby giving an indication 
of the absolute sustainability. This approach is emerging within the field of environmental 
sustainability assessment (Bjørn et al., 2020) and only a few practical studies exist (Algunaibet et al., 
2019; Brejnrod et al., 2017; Chandrakumar et al., 2019; Ryberg et al., 2018; Ryberg et al., 2020; Wolf 
et al., 2017). Nevertheless, the AESA has the potential to be an effective decision-making tool given 
that it combines multiple global environmental pressures within one integrated framework (Ryberg 
et al., 2018). The AESA indicates if the provided product or service can be considered 
environmentally sustainable in absolute sense and even if the assessment suggests otherwise, it can 
still provide valuable perspectives when setting absolute improvement targets (Ryberg et al., 2020). 
So far, an AESA has never been performed on food packaging, more specifically on 4-pint high-
density polyethylene (HDPE) milk bottles (hereinafter referred to as HDPE bottles). The present 
case study intends to fill this gap, beginning with a comparative LCA of a petrochemical and bio-
based HDPE bottle in a closed loop recycling system. Next, an AESA is performed on the Danish 
company Arla and their annual supply of either petrochemical or bio-based HDPE bottles to the 
United Kingdom (UK) market. This is followed by a scenario analysis, where five improvement 
options are defined and investigated by carrying out an AESA for each scenario. The case study ends 
with a discussion and conclusion on how Arla can address the key issues presented in the LCA and 
AESA.

5 | Methods 
5.1 | HDPE milk bottle case study    
A case study on Arla’s food packaging was defined based on an annual production of HDPE bottles 
produced for containing milk. The geographical placement was specified to the current market of the 
UK, which was supported by Arla due to an internal interest in investigating a closed loop system. 
Two scenarios were defined for the product: (1) 100 % petrochemical HDPE bottle; (2) 60 % bio-
based HDPE bottle. The material composition for each scenario was defined to be 60 % virgin HDPE 
and 40 % recycled HDPE (rHDPE) (Arla, 2019). The rHDPE was assumed to be petrochemical, which 
explains the content of 60 % and not 100 % for the bio-based HDPE bottle. 
The functional unit (FU) was defined as “annual supply of 4-pint HDPE milk bottles in the United 
Kingdom” (see section S3-1 in SI for annual supply). The FU was specified to cover an annual supply 
of HDPE bottles, as it eases the assigning of the SoSOS and the interpretation of the absolute results 
in the AESA. Furthermore, the environmental impacts that are associated with the FU were 
quantified using the Planetary Boundaries-based life-cycle impact assessment (PB-LCIA) 
methodology in the AESA, which requires annual elementary flows in the inventory (Ryberg, 
Owsianiak, Richardson & Hauschild, 2018).  
The conducted LCA fulfils the requirements of the ISO standard and the guidelines of the 
International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) handbook (EC-JRC, 2010; ISO 2006b). The 
decision context of the case study was defined as “accounting of environmental impacts”, which is 
referred to as Situation C1 in the ILCD handbook. Therefore, the modelling of the life cycle inventory 
(LCI), that is the input (resources) and output (emissions) when fulfilling the FU, was modelled by 
an attributional modelling framework to give the best representation of the actual physical flows 
from the FU.  The product system was modelled in SimaPro version 9.1.1.1. The data used to model 
the foreground system was based on information provided by Arla and data from ecoinvent v3.3 
using a “cut-off”-based attributional inventory model (Wernet et al., 2016). The background system 
was also modelled by ecoinvent v3.3 using a “cut-off”-based attributional inventory model and was 
supported by specific LCI data from Akmalina & Pawitra, 2020 (see section S2-1 in SI for LCI). 
For modelling of biogenic carbon, land use (LU), direct land use change (dLUC) and indirect land 
use change (iLUC) ISO 14067;2018 – carbon footprint of products was applied. Biogenic carbon is 
carbon derived from biomass and was important to consider, since the bio-based HDPE bottle is 
produced based on biomass as feedstock. According to ISO 1467;2018 section 6.4.8, all GHG 
emissions within 10 years after the inception of the product should be treated as if they had occurred 
in the beginning of the assessment period. By this approach, it was assumed that the HDPE bottle 
had outlived its purpose in the closed-loop recycling system within a period of 10 years. Therefore, 
the carbon dioxide to soil or biomass was added to the emitted GHG.

5.2 | System description 
The product system for petrochemical and bio-based HDPE bottles was assessed from cradle-to-
grave. The petrochemical HDPE was defined as chemicals derived from petroleum fractionated from 
crude oil. The bio-based HDPE was based on ethanol derived from food crops, more specifically on 
Brazilian sugarcane, which was subsequently dehydrated to yield bio-ethylene (Ronca, 2017). 
Regardless of whether the feedstock is bio-based or petrochemical, the further conversion of ethylene 
to HDPE remains the same (Tsiropoulos et al., 2015). This can be seen in Figure 1 that shows the 
system boundaries for the case study, including both the foreground and the background system. 
The background system is characterised by the extraction and supply of raw materials, 
manufacturing of virgin plastic granules and waste treatment scenarios. The foreground system is 
characterised by a closed-loop recycling system, where the HDPE bottles are manufactured, used 
and collected as post-consumer waste in the material recycle facility (MRF). The MRF sorts the waste 
by type, size and density and delivers pre-baled HDPE bottles to the plastic recycle facility (PRF), 
where the bottles are shredded, extruded and sliced into food grade rHDPE granulate. The inherent 
properties of recycled material in a closed loop system are not considerably different compared to 
the virgin material and thus can be used for identical products (Huysman et al., 2015). Therefore, 
the rHDPE granules are used to make new HDPE bottles that fulfil the FU.  
In order to produce the HDPE bottle, three bottle parts were defined for the system: body, cap 
and label. The body and cap were made out of HDPE, while the label was made out of polypropylene 
(PP) (LIoyd, 2011). The rHDPE produced from the PRF was only used in the bottle bodies, as the 
caps were produced from virgin HDPE only (LIoyd, 2011). The production method for all three bottle 
parts was based on assumptions and therefore modelled with the available processes in ecoinvent 
v.3.3 (see section S2-1 in SI for LCI). Due to lack of data for the production of the three bottle parts, 
the electricity use, outputs to technosphere and emissions to nature that were already integrated in 
the production methods from ecoinvent v.3.3 were assumed to be representative for the whole 
product system (see S1-1 in SI for list of assumptions). Furthermore, transportation was modelled 
for the closed-loop in the foreground system (see section S2-2 in SI for details). 
The recycling rate of HDPE bottles in the UK is 85 % (Dairy UK, 2018). This was interpreted as 
85 % of the HDPE bottle bodies and caps. The remaining 15 % were assumed to be waste alongside 
with the bottle label and treated through incineration, open burning or sanitary landfill, which are 
the predetermined scenarios for treating plastic waste in ecoinvent v3.3. Furthermore, it was 
necessary to define a production of bio-ethylene to model a bio-based HDPE bottle, as the process 
was not defined in ecoinvent v.3.3. Akmalina and Pawitra 2020 presents main inventory data (per 
kg product), which was used as input to model the production of bio-ethylene (see section S2-1 in SI 
for LCI). In the inventory data, the catalyst and the ions were excluded in the modelling of bio-
ethylene, as they could not be found in the database and were assessed to have a low impact.

5.3 | Life cycle impact assessments 
For the comparative LCA of petrochemical and bio-based HDPE bottles, the environmental impact 
scores were calculated using the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) method ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint 
(H). This method converts the LCI into life cycle impact scores by translating emissions and resource 
extractions by characterisation factors that indicate the environmental impacts per unit of stressor 
(e.g.  Kg CO2-eq to air) (Huijbregts et al., 2017). According to ISO 14040, the selection of the impact 
categories shall reflect the environmental issues related to the product system (ISO, 2006a). For this 
case study, 6 midpoint impact categories were identified to describe the product system of petroleum 
and bio-based HDPE bottles, respectively (see Table 1).   
Additionally, a substance and contribution analysis were performed for global warming. This was 
done in order to investigate the GHG emissions and study the differences between the petrochemical 
and bio-based HDPE bottle. Thereby it was also possible to compare the obtained results with known 
literature.

The environmental impact scores for the AESA were quantified by using the PB-LCIA 
methodology (Ryberg, Owsianiak, Richardson, & Hauschild, 2018). The methodology uses 15 of the 
PBs as absolute environmental boundaries (Steffen et al., 2015), while excluding two of the PBs. The 
ones excluded were the PB ‘introduction of novel entities’, which is still to be defined, and the PB 
‘change in biosphere integrity’, which is not yet based on sufficient knowledge regarding of the effect 
of anthropogenic pressures on biodiversity (Ryberg et al., 2018). The PB-LCIA methodology is based 
on LCI information that is defined as mass per year, which is opposed to traditional LCIA 
methodologies, where LCI information is given as mass (Ryberg et al., 2018). This allows the impacts 
of the product systems to be expressed directly in the metrics of the PBs’ control variables (e.g. ppm 
CO2 for climate change as atmospheric CO2 concentration), or in other words, as annual pressures 
or as environmental states in a long-term (steady state) perspective (Ryberg, Owsianiak, Richardson, 
& Hauschild, 2018). 
Table 2 provides an overview of the applied impact categories and their associated control 
variables in the PB-LCIA methodology. Furthermore the PBs from Steffen et al, 2015 are listed and 
a natural background level is defined (Ryberg et al., 2018), which is the part of the PBs that are 
occupied by nature. Accordingly, the SOSPB was defined by subtracting the natural background level 
from the PBs. This can serve as a limit for the total human activities if the stability of the ES is to be 
maintained. Out of the SOSPB, a SoSOS was calculated for the providing of FU (Ryberg et al., 2020).

5.4 | Assigning the share of safe operating space 
Products and services, which provide a FU to humans, have the right to occupy a SoSOS based on 
their value and importance (Ryberg et al., 2020). For this case study, a SoSOS was assigned to Arla 
based on the FU of supplying the UK with HDPE bottles. This was done by a two-step approach 
presented in Hjalsted et al., 2020: (1) downscaling to the individual level, where a global SoSOS was 
assigned to the UK (termed aSworld→UK) using an egalitarian (equal per capita) sharing principle, (2) 
upscaling to a higher organisational level, where a national SoSOS was assigned to Arla (termed 
aSUK→Arla) using final consumption expenditure data. The total SoSOS assigned to Arla (termed 
aSworld→Arla) based on the FU was found by multiplication of aSworld→UK and aSUK→Arla (see Table 3).  
Step 1 for calculating the SoSOS included an equal per capita approach, which means that all 
humans are considered equal and thereby assigned an equally large SoSOS (Ryberg et al., 2020). The

population of the UK was found to be 66.834.400, while the population of the world was found to be 
7.673.533.970 (World Bank, 2019). By dividing the UK population with global population the 
aSworld→UK was found to be 0,87 %. The aSworld→UK then had to be distributed among the activities (such 
as products and services) that are annually used by the British population in order to calculate the 
aSUK→Arla. This was done based on final consumption expenditure (FCE) data, which was found to be 
336.184.000.000 EUR for the UK (Household final consumption expenditure 2018 Q4, 2019). Final 
expenditure consumption data for Arla’s HDPE bottles in the UK can be found in section S3-1 in SI.  
The SoSOS was used in the assessment of absolute environmental sustainability, where the SOSPB 
was multiplied with the aSworld→Arla to give the SoSOS assigned to Arla relative to the safe operating 
space for human activities (termed SoSOSPB, Arla) (see Table 3) (Ryberg et al., 2018). The 
environmental impacts scores quantified by using the PB-LCIA methodology (termed ISPB) were 
divided by the SoSOSPB, Arla to indicate the environmental sustainability ratio (ESR). If the ratios were 
equal to or below one for all evaluated impact categories, the providing of the FU could be considered 
absolute sustainable (Fang et al., 2015).

5.6 | Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis  
The input parameters that were used to model the foreground system are presumed to resemble a 
closed loop system in the UK. Some of these parameters are specifically known to be true. However, 
a share of the parameters is not well known and thus uncertain. To identify the uncertainty of the 
results and find which input parameters the results are most sensitive towards, a sensitivity and an 
uncertainty analysis were conducted.  
The quantification of the sensitivity was based on a perturbation analysis (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
This method adjusted the input parameters of the foreground LCI with a percentage of 10 %. By 
varying an input parameter with a fixed percentage it was possible to observe a change in the model 
output relative to the results, where the input parameters were unchanged. This information was 
used to calculate normalised sensitivity coefficients (Scoef), which are the ratio of the relative change 
in output divided with the relative change in input (see section S1-2 in SI for details).  To evaluate 
the sensitivity of the impact scores in the baseline scenarios, the Scoef were calculated for both LCIA 
methods that were used, which were ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint (H) and the PB-LCIA methodology. 
Additionally, Scoef were calculated for the modelling of bio-ethylene that were based on one scientific 
paper (Akmalina & Pawitra, 2020) and for the data that were provided by Arla and used to calculate 
the SoSOS.   
The uncertainty analysis was performed qualitatively, which, in contrast to a quantitative analysis, 
cannot be expressed as a value range around the result. Three steps were accomplished to perform 
the analysis: (I) identification of uncertainty sources, (II) characterisation of uncertainty in terms of 
magnitude and/or scientific evidence, (III) a qualitative assessment of the likelihood that the 
uncertainty source could affect the result of the case study (see section S1-2 in SI for details).

7 | Discussion  
7.1 | Interpretation of the results  
Sustainable food packaging can be defined as effective, efficient, cyclic and safe (Verghese, 2012). To 
assess the sustainability of providing HDPE bottles, there is a need for assessing the eco-efficiency 
as well as the eco-effectiveness. By complementing a comparative LCA with an AESA, it was possible 
to assess both and be the first study to perform an AESA on food packaging.  
The research presented in the current case study examined both the eco-efficiency and the eco-
effectiveness of the providing of HDPE bottles. First, the relative sustainability of the bottle was 
assessed by comparing an origin of petrochemical or bio-based HDPE. Here, the result on which 
bottle that was performing environmentally better was inconclusive, when all the impact categories 
were considered. Second, the AESA was performed on a baseline scenario and five other 
improvement scenarios, to indicate the absolute sustainability relative to the PBs. The result showed 
that the ESRs for the baseline scenario exceeded the assigned SoSOSPB, Arla for more than half of the 
impact categories for both the petrochemical and bio-based HDPE bottle. Additionally, the 
improvement scenarios lowered the ESRs, however the best-case scenario still indicated that the 
assigned SoSOSPB, Arla was exceeded for more than half of the impact categories.  
==PAGE:21==
Master’s thesis 
 
Ida Løkken Witschas  
Page 21 of 32 
 
Exceeding an assigned SoSOS is not distinctive for this case study only. Other studies (e.g. 
Brejnrod et al., 2017; Ryberg et al., 2018; Ryberg et al., 2020; Wolff et al., 2017) also show an 
exceedance of several impact categories, yet the level of exceedance varies depending on the 
investigated area. Brejnrod et al., 2017 has found a worse-case exceedance of 1563 % for climate 
change, while Ryberg et al., 2020 has found an exceedance of a factor 10 (approximately) for climate 
change. In general, it is likely that most products that require a relative amount of energy would 
exceed their SoSOS, simply because the providing of energy is based primarily on an unsustainable 
system (Ryberg et al., 2018). Since the results of the current LCA reveal that the providing of HDPE 
bottles requires a certain amount of electricity, the exceedance of the SoSOSPB, Arla is not unexpected. 
Although the exceedance might be evident for some of impact categories (e.g. climate change), the 
added value of applying the AESA is that it enables an evaluation of the necessary improvements 
required of the system, in order to meet the specified targets of the assigned SoSOSPB, Arla.  
An activity can only be considered absolute sustainable if it does not exceed its assigned SoSOS. 
However, the activity cannot be accounted full responsible for the exceedance of the PBs, which is a 
result of other activities that are not acting within their assigned SoSOS as well (Ryberg et al., 2018). 
Unfortunately, some PBs have already been exceeded (e.g. climate change, biogeochemical flows and 
land-system change) due to anthropogenic perturbations (Steffen et al., 2015). If all the activities 
reduced their contribution to a level that does not exceed their assigned SoSOS, it would be possible 
to reduce and maintain environmental pressures that are within the SOSPB and maintain the stability 
of the ES (Ryberg et al., 2018). This assumes that previous boundary transgressions have not already 
generated abrupt or irreversible changes to the ES (Ryberg et al., 2020).  
The ES is recourse constrained, which incites for new solutions preferably within renewable 
sources. The transition towards bio-based plastics has so far been based on the significant savings of 
GHG emissions (e.g. Daioglou et al., 2014; Hottle et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2020; Tsiropoulus et al., 
2015), which goes against the findings of this study. The difference in impact score for global 
warming between the petroleum and bio-based HDPE bottle was observed to be 2 %, which is less 
than expected. Current science on the field have found that changing from petrochemical HDPE to 
bio-based HDPE offer significant savings in GHGs (Nguyen et al., 2020) with up to 140 % 
(Tsiropoulus et al., 2015). Additionally, it is found that switching from fuel to biomass in general has 
the potential to reduce annual emissions by up to 20 % (Daioglou et al., 2014). Although, the finding 
of this case study differs from these sources, it should be noted that the assessment of biogenic 
carbon varies. Both Nguyen et al., 2020 and Tsiropoulus et al., 2015 include GHG credits for biogenic 
carbon. According to Pawelzik et al., 2013, the treatment of biogenic carbon storage in LCAs is critical 
for quantifying GHG emissions for bio-based materials, as the biogenic carbon is reversible and adds 
carbon emissions at EoL. If the biogenic carbon were accounted for as carbon storage in this case 
study, the bio-based bottle would have performed 17 % better than the petrochemical bottle for global 
==PAGE:22==
Master’s thesis 
 
Ida Løkken Witschas  
Page 22 of 32 
 
warming. Therefore, the choice of emitting or storing the biogenic carbon at EoL in a cradle-to-grave 
assessment, is recognized to affect the final ranking the petrochemical and bio-based HDPE bottle.  
7.2 | Limitations    
It is important to recognise that this case study cannot deduce comprehensive information of all 
bioplastics or plastic bottles. Both the LCA and the AESA applies to a specific product or service, 
where input parameters can vary decisively from one product system to another (type of bioplastic, 
production methods, recycling rate etc.). Therefore, this case study should only be used as a relative 
and absolute indication of the fulfilling of the specified FU.  
In the modelling of the bio-based HDPE bottle, the input parameter of bio-ethylene needed to be 
established in the ecoinvent database v.3.3. This was done based on one scientific paper (Akmalina 
and Pawitra, 2020), where LCI data from a bio-ethylene production was obtained. In the uncertainty 
analysis (see section S1 – 3.2 in SI for uncertainty results), the modelling of the bio-ethylene 
production is assessed to be very likely to affect the conclusion of the case study. Thus, a presentation 
of new LCI data on the production of bio-ethylene may be crucial to this case study. Accordingly, the 
reader should be aware of this limitation and keep it in mind, when interpretating the results.  
One of the main uncertainties related to the AESA is the choice of sharing principles for assigning 
the SoSOS (Ryberg et al., 2018). For this case study, an egalitarian sharing principle in connection 
to the FCE was used, which always favour the activities that generate a high economic output. This 
is illustrated through the improvement scenario #5, where the average price for the HDPE bottle was 
doubled because of carbon taxes. A doubling of the price provides a larger SoSOSPB, Arla and thereby 
acknowledge a higher environmental pressure for fulfilling the FU. In other words, the more we 
spend on a product or service, the more value it has and should be assigned more space for operating. 
Since the SoSOSPB, Arla is based on economic allocation, the assigned share to Arla requires regular 
recalculation in order to reflect reality.  
In the AESA, only impact categories related to the ES identified in the PB-LCIA methodology were 
included. This implies that it does not assess impact categories such as human carcinogenic toxicity 
and fossil resource scarcity, which is used in the comparative LCA. The impact categories that are 
included in comparative LCA is limited by choice in order to best reflect the environmental issues 
related to the product system. 
The scenario analysis showed that scenario #2 and #3, where a 100 % HDPE recycling rate and 
an increase of rHDPE in the bottle body was implemented, made no significant changes in the ESRs 
from the baseline. Since the case study did not investigated the difference between a closed loop 
recycling system and a product system with no recycling at all, it is not possible to draw a conclusion 
on the importance of recycling. However, Hottle et al., 2017 concludes that recycling has major 
benefits due to avoidance of creating new plastic from virgin resources. This is valid no matter the 
impacts that are related to the shipping or demand for recycled material (Hottle et al., 2017).

7.3 | Recommendations  
On the way to absolute sustainability, it is important to recognise that the reduction of environmental 
pressures do not happen from one day to another. It is a matter of commitment and requires 
ambitious improvement targets. Arla has announced an ambition of being carbon net zero by 2050 
for all their global operations, including packaging (Arla Food UK Press Office, 2019). Furthermore, 
they have stated that their sustainable packaging should be 100 % recyclable by 2025 and contain no 
petrochemical virgin plastic by 2030 (Arla, n.d.).  
Switching from petrochemical to bio-based plastic comes with a cost of additional land use and 
other environmental impacts related to the production of biomass. The results of the comparative 
LCA showed no preference towards the bio-based HDPE bottle, except for the use of fossil resource 
scarcity. In addition, the AESA showed that, the petrochemical HDPE bottle exceed less SoSOSPB, Arla 
than the bio-based HDPE bottle. These results do not indicate the need for switching from the 
petrochemical HDPE to bio-based HDPE. However, since the results contradict current 
recommendations, the product system should be investigated further.  
If Arla should be able to fulfil the FU within the SoSOSPB, Arla and achieve their goal of being carbon 
net zero by 2050, the focus should be on reducing the GHGs emissions contributing to climate 
change as well as emissions from biogeochemical flows. The substance and contribution analysis 
show that many levers for making the environmental improvements lie outside the direct influence 
of Arla. Worth mentioning is the electricity grid mix associated with background system, which is 
heavily reliant on fossil fuels. In order to change these circumstances and become full responsible 
for each link of the life cycle, a full transparency within the value chain is recommended.  
8 | Conclusion  
An AESA was conducted to evaluate if the Danish company Arla and their providing HDPE bottles 
to the market of the UK could be considered absolutely sustainable relative to an assigned SoSOS. 
Additionally, a LCA was performed to compare the origin of petrochemical and bio-based HDPE for 
one bottle. Thereby it was possible to assess both the eco-effectiveness and eco-efficiency of Arlas 
HDPE bottles. It was found that Arlas supply of HDPE bottles cannot be considered absolute 
sustainable and that focus should be on the reduction of impacts related to climate change and 
biogeochemical flows. A scenario analysis for the AESA was carried out to assess five improvement 
options towards the baseline. The improvement scenarios lowered the ESRs, however the best-case 
scenario still indicated that the assigned SoSOS was exceeded for more than half of the impact 
categories. The comparative LCA of which product was performing environmentally better than the 
other was inconclusive and showed no preference towards the bio-based HDPE bottle. Although, 
Arlas supply of HDPE bottles cannot be considered absolute sustainable, the AESA allows for the 
identification of the environmental impact categories where reductions are needed and thereby 
function an effective decision tool for setting absolute improvement targets.

The sensitivity was evaluated by calculating the normalised sensitivity coefficients (Scoef), which is 
the relative change in output divided with the relative change in input, according to Eq. S1 (Ryberg 
et al, 2018).

S1 – 2.2 | Uncertainty analysis 
I. 
The first step of the uncertainty analysis was to identify potential uncertainty sources. This 
was done regardless of the perceived importance of the sources and served the purpose of 
generating a list that would be classified into two different categories: model parameters for 
LCI and method-related decisions. 
II. 
The uncertainty sources were characterised by assessing either their relative magnitude (low, 
medium, high) or by scientific evidence. The assessment was done independently of the 
results from the sensitivity analysis.   
III. 
A qualitative evaluation was performed on how likely the uncertainty source was to affect the 
final conclusion of the case study. This is indicated by “not likely, likely or very likely”. The 
justification was done considering the results from the sensitivity analysis.

chosen as input parameter for the modelling of bio-ethylene. Furthermore, the production of sodium 
hydroxide includes a chemical production of sodium chloride, which contributes to the sensitivity

S1 – 4 | Detailed description of scenario analysis  
The performed scenario analysis defines five potential system improvements to the baseline (see S3-
2 in SI). The baseline scenario was defined as scenario #1 and the best-case scenario was defined as 
#6 (scenario #2-#5 combined).  
Scenario #2 was based on the recycling rate of HDPE bottles, which is defined to be 85 % in the 
baseline scenario #1 (Dairy UK, 2018). This was changed to 100 %, which means that there was no 
waste treatment input (municipal incineration, sanitary landfill and open burning) for HDPE. 
Accordingly, the only input for waste treatment was the 0,0001 kg label for one bottle. Furthermore, 
the HDPE collected in the MRF increased from 85 % of 0,036 kg (0,0306 kg) to 100 % of 0,036 kg 
(0,036 kg). The corresponding amount of rHDPE produced from PRF was changed from 0,02891 kg 
to 0,03401 kg (see S2-1 in Supporting Information S2 for calculation of PRF).  
In scenario #3, the rHDPE content in the HDPE bottle body was changed from 40 % to 60 %. 
According to Arla 2020, the recycled content depends on material availability, processability and 
cleanliness and the current processing upper limit on the material quality for rHDPE is therefore 50 
%. Above this stage, the purity of the material results in leakage of the blow moulded bottle. However, 
60 % rHDPE could be achievable, if the material collection and sorting were improved, which would 
provide more clean and available material (Arla, 2020).  
Scenario #4 was based on changing the electricity mix from non-renewable to renewable energy. 
The new input parameter for electricity was “Electricity, high voltage {GB}| electricity production, 
wind, 1-3MW turbine, offshore | Cut-off, U”. For both the bio-based and petrochemical HDPE bottle, 
the electricity was changed in the following input parameters: HDPE for bottle body, rHDPE for 
bottle body, blow moulding, HDPE for bottle cap and PRF. It should be noted that the bio-based 
HDPE for bottle body and cap was changed further, due to the electricity used in sodium hydroxide, 
which is an input parameter to the modelling of bio-ethylene.  
Scenario #5 was defined by doubling the average price for a HDPE bottle of milk due to potential 
carbon taxes. By changing the price from 1,265 EUR to 2,53 EUR the assigned share of safe operating 
space to Arla (aSWorld→Arla) doubled. This means that Arla’s assigned share relative to the safe 
operating space available for human activities (SoSOSPB, Arla) doubled and the ESR decreased 
significantly (see S3-2 in SI for ESR values).
