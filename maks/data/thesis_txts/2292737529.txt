ABSTRACT
The future of computational devices are closely tied to scientiﬁc research done today. In
previous decades, major technological advancement has allowed for controlled experimen-
tation on the atomic scale, where quantum physics predict novel behaviour of nature.
In the applied ﬁeld of quantum optics current interests point towards the use of solid
state dynamics within optical micro-cavities as the basis for eﬀective quantum computa-
tional devices, as these cavity systems bring with them possibilities for storing or emitting
enhanced information. In this context the diamond-based Nitrogen Vacancy center has
attracted much attention because of its long coherence times and stable single-photon
emission.
This master’s thesis addresses the plan to construct an optical ﬁbre-based micro-cavity
for use with NV centers. It is to be applied in future scientiﬁc research in the labora-
tories of the Quantum Physics and Information Technology department at the Technical
University of Denmark. Due to delays on external projects vital for the completion of
the micro-cavity a perspective was put towards immediate use in magnetrometry. A ﬁnal
micro-cavity was thus not completed, but this thesis addresses the planning and technical
diﬃculties in creating such a device.
During the project of this thesis a design-plan was made for building an optical micro-
cavity.
A simple prototype was constructed and applied in an experimental context.
Towards a goal of doing magnetrometry using NV centers, measurements were done to
determine and characterize ﬁbre-ﬂuorescence in the optical ﬁbres used. Characterization
of two NV centers were done and ﬁtted to a three level model, which validated the use of
this model.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
In our modern day society, technology is rapidly evolving with close ties to information
technology, as is often famously described by Moore’s empirical law from 1965 on electronic
computer hardware[1]. This law may now have become more a rule of industry than a law
of physics, but serves well to show how present day technology evolves. Time has shown
that it is possible to eﬃciently scale down transistors to a degree that we today deal with
mass-production of sizes in the range of 10 nanometers1 - a scale on which nanotechnology
clearly has become a reality. The lower limit on the size of these conventional transistors
is, however, being reached as the single atom scale of a few nm becomes inﬂuential[2].
This does not reduce demands for better and ever faster technology to solve complicated
and numerically hard problems of information science in the present day; examples of
these range from the microscopic density functional theory to the macroscopic celestial
mechanics to everyday weather-forecasts.
A solution to this problem may lie in the recent decade’s paradigm shift in information
processing: the transition from electronics to optics. Light is a complicated phenomenon
and has through it’s scientiﬁc history been hard to model. It was not until James Clerk
Maxwell, standing on the shoulders of previous scientists, famously published Maxwell’s
equations that a consistent framework was put to light. His description is often called the
classical description of light and is only an approximation to how light actually behaves
at very small sizes. In this regime light behaves accordingly to quantum mechanics (with
sub-ﬁeld domains illustrated in the Venn diagram of ﬁg 1.1). At times a quantum mechan-
ical description may seem to bring along illogical behaviour as famously disputed early
in its infancy[3], but repeated conﬁrmation has lead to increased research throughout the
20th century. In this area, research is currently being done on how to realize an actual
quantum computation device, with the frontrunner being quantum bits, qubits for short,
intended to replace conventional bits. Qubits require super-positions of states[4], which
is a unique quantum mechanical phenomenon and possible to achieve on the atomic scale
using single atoms or photons.
However requirements for the commercialization of qubits is a huge technical challenge as
any interaction with the environment will result in rapid state de-coherence and loss of
quantum information. With the photon as the mediator of quantum information a require-
ment is often put on single photons sources[5]. To solve the problems of de-coherence and
ﬁnding single-photon sources, early magneto-optical traps and low cooling environments
has been developed in combination with optical cavities[6]. However these methods are
often very cumbersome.
Another promising and well-studied candidate for quantum information and processing
comes from synthetically produced diamond containing Nitrogen impurities[7]. These im-
purities can form what is known as Nitrogen Vacancy (NV) centres - a type of color centre
within the diamond lattice. It possesses some basic properties ideal for use in quantum

1.2
Project description and outline of the thesis
The goal of this master’s project has been to attempt the construction of a ﬁbre-based
micro-cavity for collection of NV center photo-luminescence. For some structure to be
retained, this experimental may be viewed in two major parts: construction of a stage-
system for a micro-cavity, and the installation and characterization of the optical system
surrounding it.
Establishing an entirely new experimental system, bottom-up, has included much work
on basic optical systems and lab practices and entails a lot of diﬀerent aspects, which has
been approached in lesser or greater extend throughout this project. Throughout this
establishment various characteristics and problems were addressed and explained. The
construction of a cavity-stage for holding a micro-cavity was realized through a design
process that framed the requirements for success. Furthermore to fully characterize the
free space system, prior to cavity-coupling, an investigation on the NV centers was done.
Much regarding the cavity-design as whole rests on previous projects - in particular the
construction of mirrors on a cleaved optical ﬁbre tip[14].

foundation for work in experimental optics and with optical cavities. A short account

cavity stage system, the design process is explained in chapter 4 with speciﬁcs on the
actual fabrication considered from section 4.2. This all sets the framework for which the
experiment will be chronologically outlined and characterized in chapter 5. To ﬁnalize the

To understand the approach to experimental optics and the work done in this thesis, this
initial chapter will introduce, using a bottom-up approach, the reader to some of the most
essential concepts as described by physics.
As a preliminary section, in order to explain the very basic workings of light, ray optics is
introduced in sec. 2.1 leading directly to the formulation of optical transformation matri-

in sec. 2.2. Tools for ﬁbre optics will shortly be summarized in sec. 2.3, for in the end to

With some of the basic principles regarding experimental optics and optical cavities ex-
plained in previous chapters, the ﬁrst steps can be taken towards developing a micro-cavity.
How to construct an optical cavity from scratch is essentially an engineering design pro-
cess. Such a design process is often iterative; it takes and combines theory, practice and
evaluation in search of the optimal solution. Much may start out as estimates and qual-
iﬁed guesses from research and brainstorming processes, but becomes increasingly hard
facts and optimized solutions. The design process of this experiment, from thought to
ﬁnal device, is what will be considered in this chapter.
4.1
Design steps
A concrete process-scheme is necessary to establish the criteria for success as well as
constraints for the project at hand. As an explanation of the systematic design process
used in this project, the reader is referred to the ﬂowchart in ﬁg. 4.1 inspired by the
book ”Engineering design process” by Y. Haik and T. M. Shahin. The process illustrated
involves 5 essential steps:
1. Deﬁnition of the task
2. Speciﬁcation of the requirements
3. Conceptualizing the design.
4. Constructing of a model (mock-up or prototype).
5. Finalization of the task in the form of a product.
Deﬁnition of the task should be a short and precise statement explaining the needs and
scope of the task - thus keeping in mind the use, how and why. Specifying requirements
may result in a need to do some background research to understand what is feasible and
what is not. The requirements themselves mush be unambiguously deﬁned and speciﬁed
to a precise limit - usually in the form of a metric and a value. The conceptualization may
initially be a very creative process through, for instance, brainstorming of ideas that ﬁt
the general requirements, but must be looked upon critically compared with requirements
to whether it is an optimal and feasible solution. When a valid concept has been decided
upon and the requirements further speciﬁed, a sketch is often necessary to quantify di-
mensions and working principle together with a mock-up or prototype.
Prior to the development process it is good to know the source of several pitfalls or faulty
designs, which may occur under way. Mistakes may range from common to more serious
ones. Typical mistakes include error in calculations, measurements, or construction. Fun-
damental errors are that of false assumptions, incorrect reasoning or misunderstanding of
the problem. However even a proper design-plan may fail if unforeseen issues arise.

4.3
Cavity mirrors
The stage-design accommodates for cavity mirrors of a special design as described earlier
(speciﬁcations given in app. E). The ﬁrst of the two mirrors is a plane 12.8 mm mirror
produced by LayerTech from a grating of dielectric material. This coating must transmit
the excitation beam to allow excitation of the NV centers through the mirror substrate, but
be fully reﬂective with respect to the broadband emission. The grating also ensures that
the surface of the mirror has maximum cavity-ﬁeld amplitude (extending perpendicular
from the surface by ∼200 nm) for optimal coupling with the NV center. The holder for
the ﬂat mirror, viewed in ﬁg. 4.4(c), has a circular opening where the mirror is placed and
held tight by a M2 nylon-tipped screw. Attaching this to the 2D-stage puts is vertical to
the optical table.

which is outside the scope of this project, but will be brieﬂy mentioned because of the
close ties. The mirror is in short fabricated using a CO2 laser, which when aligned to
the ﬁbre tip can shoot a high intensity pulse that causes a curvature, corresponding to
the Gaussian intensity proﬁle of the pulse, in the glass substrate of the ﬁbre. Clean ﬁbres
characterized by curvature, diameter and roughness are then shipped to LayerTech for

4.4
Depositing NDs on the cavity mirrors
NDs are produced by Microdiamond Ag in Switzerland to contain impurities of Nitrogen
which may result in the formation of single NV centers in a given ND. In order to get
single NDs into the cavity, they must be placed on the ﬂat mirror and then moved by the
piezo-stage exactly opposite of the ﬁbre-tip. The way they are placed, in this project, is
using one of two ways: 1. Evaporation method and 2. Spin-coating method:
1. 1) 20 µL of ND solution + 5 mL of deionized water is mixed. 2) a droplet is placed
on the substrate surface. 3) wait-time of a few hours, which can be accelerated by
dynamic evaporation using air-ﬂow.
2. 1) 20 µL of ND solution + 1 mL of deionized water is mixed. 2) The solution is put in
an ultrasonic bath for ∼30s. 3) a wait time of 2 min. 4) The substrate is placed on
a spinner with a single droplet is placed on top and then spin-coating is performed
using ∼2000 rpm for 10s.
Procedure 1. yields big lumps of NDs on the surface - good for a deﬁnitive surface signal,
whereas 2. yields an approximate density of 50 ND per 20x20 µm. To prepare the surface
of the mirror a plasma-clean technique is performed.
4.5
Magnetrometry using ﬁbre-ferrules
As the completion of the micro-cavity is closely tied to external development of the ﬁbre-
mirror, another perspective has been put for immediate use of the setup, as earlier ex-
plained.
This is done by depositing gold layers on ceramic ﬁbre optic ferrules of zirconia. The gold
layers are deposited in such a way that a current can be lead through closely around the tip
by wire bonding to an external microwave source. By applying a 2.87 GHz magnetic ﬁeld
this simple modiﬁcation makes manipulation of the NV centers magnetic spin-properties
possible for use in magnetrometry. This design is illustrated in ﬁg 4.6.
Steps were taken to create gold-wiring by sputtering layers of gold through heating in a
vacuum chamber. A short manual was written for explaining this process - see app. H.

intensities, the LP ﬁlter can be applied.

6. DISCUSSION AND EVALUATION
This section is a summary of remarks on the experimental results and the future steps
towards completion of the micro-cavity setup.
At the time of writing the micro-cavity was, as explained, not ﬁnished due to delays in
fabrication of the ﬁbre-mirrors. This project may, however, serve as a starting point for
future ﬁbre-based micro-cavities and in particularly for the continuation of the presented
setup.

setup, one can use the measurements done on the given ﬁbre types in sec. 5.2.2 and NV

7. CONCLUSION
The results presented in this thesis were achieved at the laboratories at DTU during the
development of an optical ﬁbre-based micro-cavity for use with NV centers. This was
done based on recent perspectives put to NV center coupling to cavity ﬁelds[38] and sim-
ilar work done[12, 13].
First basic optical theory was introduced, followed by a design-process. Based on this pro-
cess a micro-cavity concept developed with starting point in previous work[14]. Through
estimations based on theoretical calculations, a list of requirements and limits were set to
central parameters of the cavity-stage concept. With limitations on equipment in mind
important parameters were realized and the cavity-stage system was assembled from plas-
tic parts fabricated using a 3D printer.
A complimentary optical setup was constructed for excitation and detection of NV center
photo-luminescence. For characterization of this an automated beam-proﬁler was made,
using a 2D piezo-stage, yielding very high resolution nanometer-scale measurements of
tightly focused beams. Fibre-mirrors for the micro-cavity were developed externally and
were not ﬁnished during this project. As an intermediate aim the cavity-stage system
was introduced into the optical system for doing magnetrometry measurements using NV
centers. Thus a parallel investigation was done, which respect to making an extension
of the previous design.
This led initially to a characterization of optical ﬁbre ﬂuores-
cence, which showed a comparatively large background signal drowning any NV center
photo-luminescence.
Doing magnetrometry as designed in this thesis was at the time
not previously done elsewhere, but was due to complications in fabrication and ﬁbre-
ﬂuorescence not completed. A similar experiment by other research groups veriﬁed the
possibility at the same time of this project[37]. Single NV centre photo-luminescence was
instead detected using a confocal microscope setup and modelled to a three level system.
This resulted in estimations of The NV center transition rates. With the transition rate
from shelving to ground state as inﬁnitesimal, this gives support to previous models[9],
which neglect this factor.
7.1
Outlook
This project set out to create an optical ﬁbre-based micro-cavity. With a ﬁnished cavity
design and stage-system incorporated into an optical system for detection important steps
have been made. However, completion of this unﬁnished setup requires a few steps.
Alignment of the system must be optimized by including new lab equipment to replace
inappropriate parts, such as the objective holder and camera for absolute ﬁbre positioning,
that was initially accepted.
Optical ﬁbres used in this experiment show signiﬁcant background ﬂuorescence hindering
the detection of single NV center photo-luminescence. These must be exchanged if not
simply replaced by the ﬁnished dielectric coated ﬁbre-mirrors.
Fabrication of the ﬁbre-ferrules must be completed to continue the steps towards mag-
netrometry measurements. With the ultimate goal of exploiting the micro-cavity eﬀects

2 Handling Instructions
Respect Screw-in Depths
For some positioners, the allowable screw-in depths are quite small. By using too long screws, 
you may hit internal components and damage the positioner. Therefore, measure the screw length 
beforehand and shorten the screws, if necessary. The screw-in depth are given in the drawings of 
the current catalogue. If you are not sure that the screw will fit please contact the SmarAct sales 
team.
Screw-in depths for an SLC-1730
positioner
Never loosen screws that hold the
guideways
Never loosen screws that adjust the
actuator
Do Not Diassemble Positioners
Disassembling a positioner will destroy its preload and the connection between the actuator 
and the slide. We also recommend not to disassemble a manipulation system,which consists of 
several positioners. Therefore, the screws of the positioners and manipulators are sealed with a 
droplet of TorrSeal.
Keep Torques and Forces Low
Generally, caution should be used to not apply high torques or forces to the slides with respect
to the guides. The max. forces and torques are given in the cataloue. For non-magnetic positioners
(ending -NM) the values should be devided by 2. Therefore, if a device shall be mounted to a 
positioner, please hold the positioner directly and not indirectly via other positioners which are 
connected to it. E.g. when mounting something to the Z positioner of an XYZ manipulator hold the 
Z positioner directly and not the base.

H. GOLD-DEPOSITING FOR USE IN MAGNETROMETRY
H.1
Steps of operation
This guide is written for depositing 100 nm layer of gold. Figures H.1 to H.4 are marked
with central control-options.
H.1.1
Vacuum pump
The preliminary assumption is that the vacuum chamber has been pumped.
1. Open external source of dry and liquid N2 using valves to allow usage of N2.

Deposition machine
1. Choose the material to deposit (ﬁg. H.3 (2)): Gold is ’4B’ on the switch.
2. Set the initial settings (ﬁg. H.3 (1)): Gold density
3. Start depositing: Turning the big knob to 10% of 400 A (eﬀectively 40 A) will start
heating the gold and a layer thickness in k˚A will soon start to accumulate and given
on the interface (on ﬁg. H.2 (1)). These settings give around a deposition-rate of 7
˚A per second.
4. End depositing: Turn the big knob back and end the heating.
5. Wait a couple of minutes for water cooling.
