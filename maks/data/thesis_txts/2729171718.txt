Industrial Symbiosis Design and Analysis: Implementation of Power-to-X technologies in Bioprocesses 
 
Abstract 
The global economy, as highlighted by the 2023 Circularity Gap Report, is only 7.2% circular, 
indicating a decrease in circularity despite advances in sustainable and circular manufacturing 
techniques. This study examines the role of bioprocesses in the sustainability and circularity of 
the manufacturing industry, focusing on challenges such as the low costs of fossil raw materials 
and scalability. Using Unibio A/S, a biotechnology company, as a central case, and Kalundborg 
Industrial Symbiosis (KIS) as a model of successful industrial symbiosis, this report evaluates 
the integration of Power-to-X (Pt-X) technologies with existing bioprocesses. A new framework, 
based on low computational demand software (MA.tools), was proposed to facilitate the 
analysis of technological synergies within industrial parks. Using a case-study, the software 
examined potential co-benefits and challenges of implementing Pt-X with Unibio’s upstream 
manufacturing process and carbon capture and utilization (CCSU) technology. The results 
underline the potential trade-offs, especially concerning energy consumption and waste 
recovery. Several avenues for improvement and deeper analysis are recommended, including 
more robust tecno-economic and life cycle assessments, optimizations for both process 
simulations and the proposed industrial symbiosis network, and the importance of data quality 
and availability.

1. Introduction 
According to the latest Circularity Gap Report from 2023 the global economy is only 7.2% 
circular. Since global material use has been increasing – it has tripled since 1970 – the 
circularity metric is taking the opposite direction and has shrink from 9.1% to 7.2% in just six 
years [1]. The results of the report become even more unreasonable when the contribution of 
developing countries, such as Denmark is analysed. Denmark that has created the most 
evolved symbiotic park in the world (Kalundborg Industrial Symbiosis) is only 4% circular [2]. 
The same report pointed out the relevance of an advance circular manufacturing to achieve 
better circularity metrics. According to future estimations, the material extraction is not set to 
decrease and by 2050 the world economy will be running on the extraction of at least 170 Gt of 
material (figure 1). 
 
 
Figure 1 Estimation for 2050 of material extraction in a linear economy. Source: Circle Economy 
 
Among the different manufacturing sectors, the bioprocesses are being characterized as a vital 
component for a future manufacturing industry based on sustainability and circularity. Studies 
suggest that an integration of biological and chemical engineering processes is crucial to 
achieve resource efficiency and contribute to a circular manufacturing [3]. However, such 
development steps are currently limited by different aspects, in particular the low costs for fossil 
raw materials and scalability issues [4], [5]. A switch from fossil raw materials to a bio-based 
economy will not be feasible if such problems are not overcome. 
 
A relevant company that is playing a significant transition in the chemical-biochemical industry is 
Unibio A/S. Unibio is a young biotechnology company that is entering the food market with the 
production of high value protein through an innovative fermentation process. Their process, as 
many other bioprocesses, are phasing difficulties to be economic competitive in Europe 
considering the high manufacturing and workforce expenses. The company is preparing to open 
production plants in regions such as Saudi Arabia due to raw materials attractive prices and 
availability [6]. On the other hand, integrated biorefineries such as Zeit in Germany have 
evolved in the last years, from a bottom-up approach, and today are producing food and fuels in 
a synergetic interaction capable of making the processes more economic attractive and robust 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
2 
 
[7]. Similarly, Unibio and its surrounding industries are studying synergies through 
implementation of novel technologies and processes.  
The industrial park where these synergies are being study is the Kalundborg Industrial 
Symbiosis (KIS). There, a collaboration between industries started 50 years ago, and today the 
park is the location of the most developed case of Industrial Symbiosis (IS). Different challenges 
from the local industry have been solve there without the need to find solutions in a different 
part of the globe. This saved money and made the park more open to further collaborations, 
fuelled by its own actors needs and problems. From the different synergies being study at KIS, 
one is of particular interest for this study - the Pt-X Cluster Zealand project. Here, the 
implementation of Power-to-X (Pt-X) technologies through synergies with bioprocesses is being 
analysed to solve common issues. From this project possible future partnerships and 
investments might arise based on the feasibility to integrate the novel technology (Pt-X) with 4 
specific enterprises located at Kalundborg.  
 
The integration of novel technologies, such as Pt-X, in the chemical industry have been 
performed through systematic computer-aided frameworks, superstructure optimizations, and 
tecno-economic analysis capable of identifying the most sustainable and economical process 
design. If complex industrial networks, such as the ones in KIS, are replicated with the previous 
systematic frameworks, the task becomes computational demanding. Moreover, the task 
becomes even more challenging when companies avoid sharing the sensitive data of their 
processes due to legal and intellectual property (IP) protection rules [8]. 
 
To tackle the complexity of integrating technologies with a network of industrial processes, a 
initial framework based on a low computational demand software (MA.tools) is proposed in this 
report. Its purpose is to analyse the implementation of synergetic technologies in industrial 
parks and their potential co-benefits.The framework is then tested with a case-study, developed 
from a collaboration with the company Unibio. The case is based on the process design of a Pt-
X technology with Unibio’s upstream manufacturing process, together with a carbon capture 
and utilization (CCSU) technology. The process was model with AVEVA process simulation 
software. By applying the proposed framework, the case-study model is integrated with a group 
of pre-selected processes and a hypothetical Industrial Symbiosis (IS) park was designed and 
analysed.

1.1 Background  
 
1.1.1 Industrial Symbiosis  
The concept of Industrial Symbiosis (IS) bases its definition on the natural sciences term used 
to describe intimate association between two species and the benefits that one or both gain 
from it. Chertow has defined IS as “traditionally separate industries in a collective approach to 
competitive advantage involving physical exchange of materials, energy, water and/or by-
products” [9]. It also emphasized that these collaborations are offered by geographic proximity. 
Also, the 2023 Circularity Gap Report on Denmark, refers to industrial symbiosis has a crucial 
method to achieve an advance circular manufacturing [2].    
  
According to the previous definition of IS, wastewater treatment plants that receive waste from 
different factories can be consider a symbiotic interaction where different companies have the 
common investment to treat their liquid waste. So, a symbiosis can sometimes be interpreted as 
a partnership where corporates invest in shared projects for mutual benefit. The same can be 
said for biogas plants, which part of its feedstock is commonly composed by bioresidues from 
nearby factories. In these two examples, a reduction of waste is achieved through integrated 
site-level shared technologies. Another form of symbiotic partnership could be sharing 
infrastructures or even workforce. As conceptualize in literature, the ambition for IS is often 
characterised to achieve full use of waste flows and recycling of materials, ideally in a cascade 
[10].  
 
The existence of industrial symbiosis (IS) networks relates to a complex number of factors. 
According to literature some of the reasons are the existence of common goals, for example the 
reduction of waste disposal costs [9]. Overall, identifying with an issue is central to mobilize 
==PAGE:14==

symbiotic collaboration among corporations. A definition and characterization of different levels 
of IS networks was suggested by Valentine’s ground research [11]. He divided the evolution of 
industrial symbiosis in 3 stages – cautious engagement, the cooperative escalation and 
operational collaboration [9]. These stages are characterized on table 1 with specific key 
performance indicators (KPIs). In the same study, the Kalundborg Industrial Symbiosis (KIS) 
was referred as the most developed case of IS and one of the rare cases to reach the stage 3 of 
development.

1.1.2 The Kalundborg Industrial Symbiosis (KIS)   
.  
The Kalundborg Industrial Symbiosis (KIS) is considered one of Denmark’s Industrial hotspots. 
This title is given not only by the existence of a mature network of environmental transactions, 
but also due to the positive effects that this industrial site has done to the country and its 
municipality. According to Copenhagen Economics KIS has develop Kalundborg’s employment 
rate, GDP and even the creation of infrastructure, with several joint initiatives such as the 
Knowledge Hub Center, Helix lab and many more [11]. The numbers are clear, and in 2015 
after the financial crisis, Kalundborg was one of the few regions in Denmark with an increase in 
the number of industrial jobs. Kalundborg also stands out in terms of company’s value creation. 
Between 2011-2015 the region had a 32% GDP increase while the rest of the country had only 
4%. Copenhagen Economics attributes this numbers to the different initiatives happening in 
Kalundborg such as KIS.  
  
According to the previous mention Valentine’s ground research, the motivations of KIS are 
specific to the companies involved as well as to the Kalundborg community [9]. There is an 
environmental awareness among the key executives within KIS that try to correlate the business 
goals with the well-being of the community. Nevertheless, KIS collaborations do not get out of 
the business norm - they are commercial transactions that benefit both parties economically. 
Certainly, these environmental collaborations have delivered significant improvements to the 
companies and the surrounding communities, however, as Valentine’s interviews conclude, the 
business nature of this networks make them superficial, and their full potential is almost never 
achieved [9]. All environmental networks are performed by the exchange of specific resource(s). 
The different resource layers can be specified as energy, water and material layers. According 
to a report conducted by Copenhagen Economics, in 2015 more than 50% of socio-economic 
GDP gain come from energy resources networks, while only 13% were based on material 
resources [11]. On the other hand, the water stream is considered, according to a 2015 report 
from the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), the resource with the highest 
developing interest [12]. The conclusions from the study suggest that several interchanges can 
still be created and explored.  As shown in figure 3 there are several actors and companies 
participating in the KIS cluster. During the present work 3 out of the 20 symbiotic partners 
(highlighted in dash red) are refer in this study. In the following section 2 of them are described 
and their relationship with industrial symbiosis.

Kalundborg Bioenergi   
In 2018 KIS started the construction of the Kalundborg Bioenergi - a biogas facility that 
processes bio industrial waste from other firms. By 2019 this facility was the biggest biogas 
plant in eastern Denmark. Annually, the plant treats 300,000 tons of biomass from NovoNordisk 
and Novoenzymes solid waste with a production capacity of 8 million cubic meters of biogas. 
The biogas is then purified to methane, which can produce up to 220,000 kWh of electricity per 
day. Local farmers receive 250,000 tons of digestate, a by-product that is rich in phosphorous 
compounds and has good fertilizer applications [11]. A project like Kalundborg Bioenergi is 
highly interactable considering its capacity to treat biowaste, while delivering clean methane and 
fertilizers to the agriculture industry. It is also important to state that its environmental 
performance can also be increase through extra symbiotic networks. For example, the 
emissions of biogenic CO2 can be captured and directly used by succinic acid fermentation 
processes or used in chemical conversion processes (i. e methanol) as it will be later showcase. 
  
  
Unibio S/A  
Unibio is a pioneering company within the biotechnology industry sector. The company has its 
headquarters in Denmark, with its innovation center and power plant located in Kalundborg. The 
firm has developed an innovative fermentation process that converts natural gas or methane, 
into high-quality-protein for fish and animal feed. Their process mimics certain microbial 
organisms, that exist in nature, which consume gases emitted by decaying organic material. 
The produced single cell protein (SCP) has been approved by EU for animal and fish meals and 
is specifically produced by a technology developed between DTU and Unibio – the Uloop 
reactor. The company is currently developing the technology through different collaborations 
and licenses with other biotech companies, with the goal to scale up the process and increase 
its productivity [6].  A similar process to the one Unibio’s uses is given in figure 4. Here the 
Uloop is represented as a normal fermenter since its design details are not relevant to this 
study.

As seen in figure 3 the process has different resources needed to successfully run a protein 
production facility. According to previous meetings with Unibio’s senior fermentation engineer 
Michael Elleskov, oxygen is one of the most expensive raw materials. Currently the oxygen is 
produced by a vacuum pressure swing adsorption (VPSA) technology. According to literature, 
the use of a pure oxygen supply is crucial to reach good performance parameters (gas 
utilization) that cannot be reach while using air as supply. Hence, the oxygen supply can have 
critical impacts on the efficiency and economics of the process [13]. While applying this 
technology a study has estimated that the capital cost for the air separation is between 14-20% 
of the total capital expenditure (CAPEX) investment [13]. The process also has biogenic CO2 
emissions produced during the fermentation reaction, which could be used as resource and 
contribute to a lower environmental impact of the process (similarly as the CO2 presented in 
biogas). Another crucial resource for the process is the methane which currently comes from the 
grid, however it could also be directly sorted from biogas sources through upgrading 
technologies. Nowadays the company has only 2 symbiotic exchanges with the park: the 
indirect use of the methane from the biogas plant, and the treatment of wastewater by the 
regional wastewater treatment facility. 
  
  
1.2 Challengers and Needs for IS Implementation   
The barriers to symbiosis success are broad and will depend on the development stage of the 
network. According to some studies the lack of infrastructure and resources represented the 
main obstacle [15]. On other terms, being too dependent on one actor might create vulnerability. 
DONG Energy power plant (Asnæs) was phased out due to a Danish government strategy, 
which compromise the steam network between DONG Energy (now Ørsted) and Statoil and 
Novo Nordisk [9][15]. Another big barrier is the lack of time - staff members of the company are 
assigned internal challenges and have very little time to innovate within the network’s boundary 
[9]. Another significant barrier is the antitrust and Intellectual Property (IP) protection rules, 
which require extra efforts to tackle the challenges. This affects the exchange of relevant data, 
such as detail information about the waste composition [8]. More importantly, during innovation 
projects when cost savings are not obvious, collaborations loose track and motivation [11].   
  
Several strategies were developed by KIS to overcome these challenges. Among them the 
creation of Knowledge Hub Zealand, a full-scale center test that gives the needed infrastructure 
for companies to innovate and create new processes, including symbiotic networks. The center 
allows for a close collaboration between industry and universities to together test and create 
new production processes [11]. The barrier created by the firms lack of time and resources is 
tackle by the creation of a facilitator. As a neutral partner it can dismantle the long-term 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
7 
 
investment barriers with enough knowledge of future local development plans, environmental 
control and capable of connecting different partners without external interests[16]. For the 
facilitator to perform its job efficiently it not only requires infrastructure or human resources, it 
will also require methods and tools to access the latest technologies and processes. In 2015 the 
Symbiosis Center Denmark was developed for this purpose and is actively screening and 
developing new symbiotic exchanges for the region of Zealand, in Denmark [17].   
  
All the previous solutions will contribute to foster IS networks, however a stage 3 IS cannot be 
achieved without specific technologies and their development. On table 2 an overview summary 
of the relevant technologies and tools for IS implementation is given. The content of the table is 
based on a Horizon 2020 report from the European Commission developed to review and study 
28 industrial symbiosis projects [8]. According to the report, a successful implementation of IS 
cases requires investment in these technologies. The task is not trivial since sometimes the 
technology needs to be developed and adapted to the specific symbiotic interactions. This 
means a specific technology should be shaped to the industrial park needs and not the 
opposite. Moreover, the report emphasizes the development of methods and tools to identify 
opportunities for IS establishment. For example, the development of digital twins will be crucial 
to track the diverse operational characteristics in different industrial sectors, such as renewable 
energy (RE) supply fluctuations.

The previous mention technologies as well the projects that they resemble, require performance 
metrics that analyse their capabilities to develop industrial symbiosis networks. To document 
and analyse the maturity of industrial symbiosis projects and their technology readiness level 
(TRL), KIS has developed a measurement metric: the symbiosis readiness level (SRL) [16]. 
This performance metric could be applied to specific technologies, projects, regions or even 
countries (see appendix C for the metric details). The previous mention EC report stated that 
only 2 industrial sites were documented to reach high SLRs: KIS and Smart Delta Rivers (SDR) 
in Netherlands. In addition, most documented symbiotic projects are in early phases, which 
would highly benefit from innovative supporting implementation frameworks.  
 
  
  
1.3 Frameworks for IS Analysis and Implementation  
 
The scientific community as contribute with different frameworks that can help symbiosis 
establishment. Agudo et al has proposed a symbiotic readiness measurement tool, the tool is 
presented as a checklist framework with no use of software [18]. Silk et al have proposed 
computer-aided decision support frameworks to tackle challenges on identifying and developing 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
8 
 
resource purification of complex streams. The framework was created to facilitate the

1.4 IDENTIFY: A Power-to-X Industrial Symbiosis Network   
The basis of Power-to-X (Pt-X) principle comes from the local availability of renewable energy 
produced by photovoltaics and wind (onshore and offshore) power, and sometimes all the three 
at the same time [19]. Generally, this energy is used to produced oxygen and hydrogen through 
an electrolysis process. The hydrogen is the product with the highest desirability and application 
of this process, while the surplus oxygen and heat are considered by-products. The hydrogen is 
used directly as an energy source or it can be converted, frequently in combination with CO2 to a 
variety of different products (i.e methanol). The technology is suggested as a solution to avoid 
feeding excess electricity into the power grid, which according to the German energy law 
provisions has a negative economic effect [19]. It is also consensual that not every sector of our 
society can be electrified. Heavy transportation vehicles, the aviation or the shipping sector are 
some of the obvious examples that require fuels in the liquid or gas form. Hence, power-to-x is 
being studied as a solution to deliver the require fuels through sustainable energy sources [13].  
  
Pt-X technologies are currently not economic competitive due to price advantages that 
traditional fossil-based products have [20]. Not only it relies on renewable energy (RE) 
production excess, as it needs significant amounts of pure water. The development of 
==PAGE:19==

technologies such as Pt-X are expected to contribute to a more sustainable production of 
commodities (called “X”), while delivering an application to the surplus of RE production that 
characterizes the current wind and solar energy production systems [21].  
  
Generally, a well-integrated and developed Pt-X industrial park requires specific raw materials. 
Provision of CO2, pure water or N2 are require to design one step Hydrogen-to-X systems. 
Literature studies have analysed the flexibility of this technology to produce methane, methanol, 
syngas and ammonia (the main one-step products), which contribute to an increase of the use 
of RE energy and decreases the high costs related to storage tanks [20]. Still, the flexibility of 
their production is not fully studied. Overall, Pt-X industrial processes have their main purpose 
the production of hydrogen however, oxygen is also a valuable commodity that can benefit the 
Pt-X system into becoming cost-effective. Normally this co-production of oxygen is ignored and 
released to the atmosphere for lack of feasible applications [19][14]. Studies have suggested its 
use in wastewater treatment plants and medical purposes [22], [23]. Nowadays, several projects 
are discovering the benefits of using this extra source of O2 within biorefineries or other 
industrial processes, such as in oxyfuel combustion [14]. According to literature this can 
massively increase the efficiency of the process and contribute for the commercial success of 
the projects [19][20]. Nevertheless, according to Mucci et al only a few studies have analysed 
the integration of Power-to-X processes. Arnold et al has qualitatively analysed synergies 
between Pt-X and a biorefinery but no specific process modelling was conducted neither 
conclusions regarding optimal process routes were made. Tecno-economic assessments (TEA) 
and life cycle assessments (LCA) methods were not performed to this integration processes 
[19].

Unibio production site is in the region of Kalundborg and its currently part of its symbiosis

The development of IS faces many barriers, among them the physical and economic visibility to

2.1.1 Case-Study Process Integration  
As mention, some of the main bottlenecks phased during single cell protein’s (SCP) 
fermentation are the expensive supply of pure oxygen and its significant CO2 emissions. The 
project is based on the use of Pt-X systems as a possible way to tackle the process 
dependency on oxygen (one of the most expensive commodities). Simultaneously, the 
suggested process tries to decrease its carbon footprint through the carbon capture technology. 
 
The proposed process flowsheet is presented in figure 7. In this figure the numbering of the 
streams and the different equipment will help the reader identify their corresponding physical 
values. To model the thermodynamic behavior of the different streams and mixtures, the 
different sections used the following cubic equations of state, respectively: Soave-Redlich-

kg/h of hydrogen and consuming 1096 kg/h of water (10% excess). Following the fermentation 
process supplied by the electrolysis oxygen (S10), an exhaust gas (S19) at 43C is collected and 
mixed with 15% excess air (S18). This mixture is combusted in an adiabatic chamber, with a

temperature of the flue gas is used to produce 3056.6 MJ/h of high-quality steam that supplies 
all the heat needed by the stripper unit. Subsequently, the cooled flue gas (S22) goes to a flash

unit and a CO2 stream (S38) of 574 kg/h, with 99.039wt% purity is produced. Finally, the purify 
CO2 is pressurized to 3 bar (S38) and is ready to be stored or used for further chemical 
applications.

3. Methodology 
The methodology section is divided in 2 parts. Initially the method of Metabolic Analysis (MA) is 
introduced together with the software that applies its theory. The relevant components of the 
software are described and when its possible specific examples are related to this study are 
given. The chapter ends with a framework suggestion based on the the tools and methods 
applied in this report. Some terms use in this section are related to the Industrial Ecology 
discipline, see table 14 in appendix B for a clear understanding of these terms. Abbreviations for 
the different processes are also used and enumerated in appendix B. 
 
3.1 Metabolic Analysis 
Metabolism is defined as “the aggregate of all physical and chemical processes taking place 
within an organism or group of organisms”[21]. An organism can be described as a complex 
system with regulation mechanisms influenced by interior and exterior sources. A factory and 
the processes that it embeds are considered, by this definition, an organism. Such organism is 
regulated by markets, stakeholders, sometimes other factories, and many other inter-dependent 
parameters. An industrial actor is normally in a geographic region with proximity to other 
production industries (i.e agricultural, chemical, biological). The case of Kalundborg Symbiosis 
is an example of a symbiotic network between different organisms with focus on the circularity 
of materials and energy. This specific industrial park has evolved in the last 50 years into an 
industrial organism with an extensive network of resources between its actors. As pointed out in 
previous studies on the park, KIS has similarly evolve, as biological organisms have evolved to 
optimize their metabolism according to their surroundings and ecosystems. One could visualize 
the analogy between both concepts by comparing a factory to one of the many cells present in 
the human body. A cell regulates part of its mechanism based on the environment conditions 
and the presence of metabolites (i.e high concentration of glucose in cold temperatures), which 
will trigger different metabolic pathways and determine the outputs needed for the cell 
maintenance. Similarly, a biogas factory outputs are also influenced by the raw materials that 
are available. Generally, the feedstock of biogas plants is a mix of different biomass leftovers 
from industrial and agriculture processes [22]. The fluctuation in this feedstock affects the 
performance of the plant, and if substantial changes happen, different “industrial pathways” 
might have to be activated to guarantee the factory productivity and service to the community.  
To analyze the complexity of biological organisms specialists in the area of bioinformatics have 
emphasize the relevance of using mathematical models and computer-aided frameworks for the 
analysis of network based pathways [23]. Just as for biological organisms, it is also useful and 
relevant to analyze the metabolism of industrial processes. Industrial ecology books have 
emphasized the relevance of such applications to understand the possible interactions between 
separated industrial processes [21].The similarities between industrial and biological organisms 
shouldn’t be interpreted has having a similar motivation for the applications of metabolic 
analysis. The industrial applications are focused on the mass and energy flows of the system, 
while the biological gives more importance to the changes on pathways. For instance, industrial 
pathways such as the ones in KIS can be model with a relatively low computational power 
compared to biological metabolic pathways happening inside microorganisms and between 
different cells. Nevertheless, the intensity of the industrial system analysis depends on the 
objectives of the MA application. As shown later in the report the complexity can become high 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
18 
 
and tricky if different scenarios are studied. Overall, MA application in industry, translates into 
understanding and mapping the processes happening inside an industrial system. 
The Theory 
To apply the method the data of all the processes is organized in a stoichiometric matrix (S-
matrix) that should have information of all the qualities (columns) that are entering or leaving a 
certain process (rows). Another important concept used in MA is the pathway vector (p), that 
translates into the global arrangement of transformations capable of converting resources into 
the final services. If new transformations inside the industrial organism want to be studied and 
analysed, they can be added to the S-matrix and the pathway vector, as a new row. The 
multiplication of these two matrixes generates the flow matrix of the system (F-matrix). This 
matrix is considered the outputs of the method. 
 
To make the theory of MA more related an example is given with the previous model process 
for SCP production. Figure 8 shows part of the S-matrix for the 3 model processes in this study 
(the values in the matrix are rounded to zero decimals). The matrix is simplified and only the 
most important 7 qualities are selected (first row). Notice that the data is organized based on 
the stoichiometry within each process (𝑣𝑖). For instance, in the electrolysis transformation 
process (𝑣1) the flow of qualities is based on the production of 1kg of hydrogen, which will 
produce 8 kg of oxygen and consume 9 kg of water. The other two process are the production 
of SCP in the Uloop Reactor (𝑣2) and the capture and purification of the emitted biogenic CO2 
(𝑣3). This matrix is a simplification example, normally the matrix has more processes and their 
respective qualities.  
 
The pathway vector (p) dictates the intensity of each process (𝑣𝑖). The example below (𝑝1) 
considers the annual production of SCP by the company Unibio (9000 tons/year), a PEM 
electrolyser process with a 25 MW capacity to supply the oxygen to run the protein process, and 
a CCSU system to collect and purify the 18,000 tons of CO2 emissions. It is important to state 
that different pathway vectors can be model based on the objectives to use the MA.

3.2 MA.tools Software 
The theory behind MA is applied through the software MA.tools. The software developed with R 
programming language, was developed by Andreas Ibrom in the Department of Environmental 
Engineering, at the Technical University of Denmark. The tool has the capacity to read the 
extensive data present in the S-matrix. The software generates the F-matrix as an output. 
 
The tool is configured to collect data from every process in a pre-design table in Microsoft 
Excel. The excel books are designed equally to every node. There, the processes occurring 
within each node, their qualities and respective flows are defined by the user (see appendix B 
for details). The user also specifies the sources of each quality. This means the input 
information also includes the origin or destination of a flow. For example, the water used in the 
electrolysis is imported from the environment, while the surplus heat is dissipated and lost to the 
environment. The same is performed for collocation between qualities. 
 
The R script also verifies if the input data is consistent. It guarantees that there are no repeated 
qualities within a process, that all units are equal to every quality or that the fractions of a flow 
sum up to 1 unit, so there is no missing allocation of a resource. The script was built on several 
functions developed by Ibrom. These functions allow to preserve the data and all the relevant 
information that it contains. In this work the following outputs were used as the basis to achieve 
the desired results: 
 
• 
S-matrix: A similar matrix as the one in figure x is generated. 
• 
F-matrix: The desired output of the software. It is achieved by the multiplication between 
the S-matrix and the path vector. 
• 
Wise budget: This output guarantees that the system is correctly characterized, and no 
inconsistencies are found. Hence, the wise budget serves as a final validation tool. Due 
to the complexity of the model system several inconsistencies had to be corrected 
through additional iterations on the input data. 
• 
Network Diagrams: The script generates several diagrams that help the user visualizing 
the interactions happening within the system. A diagram is generated for each node and 
for each quality. This is useful as a qualitative tool to see if any quality is being disposed 
while another process is using it as an input (see appendix B for details).  
 
Overall, MA.tools collects a set of data from different sources and tries to generate a 
quantitative analysis of its material and energy flows. The information taken from this tool can 
be apply for different purposes. A straightforward approach can be to track possible materials 
within an industrial network that can be reused and not disposed. An advantage of MA.tools is 
that it gives a quick way to perform quality screenings to extensive database of processes. 
Previous work has been developed to use the outputs of the tool to perform life cycle 
assessment (LCA) analysis on KIS [24]. In the present work the tool was used to model different 
industrial pathways created by the implementation of Pt-X and CCSU technologies. The 
flexibility of the path vector (one of the inputs of the tool) allowed to simulate different

combinations of pathways and analyse their effects on each node, the environment and the 
whole park performance.

In the ethanol process, the biomass feed goes under pretreatment before the hydrolysis step, 
which is then followed by the fermentation and production of the ethanol. Next the stream is

3.3.2 Anaerobic Digestion  
The anaerobic digestion (AD) process is based on two tecno-economic assessment papers that 
model a typical Danish biogas plant [22][27] . The plant processes a mix of 80% manure and 
20% industrial or municipal biowastes, referred as simple “biowaste” in this report. It handles 
100,000 tons of this feedstock annually, equivalent to a mass flow of 12.08 tons per hour. The 
plant is designed to operate 345 days a year over a 20-year lifespan. 
 
The feedstock first undergoes blending and then preheating to 55°C. This preheated material is 
sent to a thermophilic anaerobic digester, which operates at 55°C and atmospheric pressure, 
with a hydraulic retention time (HRT) of 20 days. A desulphurization unit featuring a biotrickling 
filter is included to treat the biogas produced.

3.3.3 Biogas Upgrading Section (BAS Node) 
The biogas produced, in the previous process, primarily consists of methane (65mol%) and CO2 
(35mol%). The way CO2 is treated in the biogas depends on its intended application. For 
instance, when biogas is used for generating heat and power, there is generally no need to 
remove the CO2. However, if the end goal is to upgrade the biogas to biomethane, which has a 
methane content of 95–97%, the CO2 must be removed. Additionally, any sulfur content also 
needs to be eliminated before the gas can be either injected into the natural gas grid or used for 
upgrading.  
 
As for the methods used to upgrade biogas, current practices often involve chemical or physical 
processes. These can be energy-intensive and may involve the use of hazardous chemicals. 
The focus of these methods is predominantly on removing the CO2 component but not on 
utilizing it. In contrast, biological processes are emerging as environmentally friendly 
alternatives. Not only do they tend to consume less energy and avoid hazardous chemicals, but 
they also have the added advantage of converting the CO2 present in the biogas into methane. 
Therefore, biological methods are gaining increased attention for their dual benefits of 
environmental friendliness and resource utilization. In the next section two distinguish process 
routes, model in literature studies, are selected as possible industrial pathways for biogas 
upgrading. In figure 11 the mass balance and energy requirements for both processes are 
provided.

Biodiesel Production 
In this biodiesel production process, Waste Cooking Oil (WCO) and 99% pure methanol 
(MeOH) are the main feedstocks. The process can be divided into 4 key steps: raw material 
preparation, transesterification, esterification, and products purification. The biodiesel 
production process begins with the mixing of WCO and 99% pure methanol at elevated 
temperatures. This mixture is sent to a transesterification reactor operating at 69°C and 1 bar 
pressure, where a three-step transesterification occurs, converting triglycerides into BDF and 
producing glycerol (GLY) as a by-product. Methanol is used in excess to drive the reaction 
towards the desired products. The reactor output is then heated and sent to a flash tank that 
separates methanol and water from the BDF and GLY mixture. 
 
The methanol-water mixture undergoes further separation and distillation to achieve 99.99% 
pure methanol, which is recycled. Meanwhile, the BDF and GLY are separated and cooled. The 
BDF is purified through a series of distillation columns, separating it from remaining methanol 
and waste oils. The GLY is similarly purified to a 99.8% purity level. This process not only 
produces high-purity biodiesel and glycerol but also includes steps for recycling methanol, 
making it a resource-efficient setup. 
 
Succinic Acid Production 
The production of succinic acid starts with mixing glycerol, water, ammonia, and CO2 at 25°C 
and 1 bar pressure, followed by heating to 37°C. The mixture undergoes fermentation using 
engineered E. Coli at 37°C and 1 bar for 72 hours, producing Bio-SA, acetic acid, and by-
products like biomass and water. Sodium hydroxide is added for pH control. Post-fermentation, 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
25 
 
gases are removed in a flash tank, and the liquid goes through microfiltration and nanofiltration 
at specific temperatures and pressures. The filtered product is then acidified with sulfuric acid to 
free up Bio-SA and prepare it for crystallization. 
 
Sodium sulfate, a by-product, is filtered out and dried to 98% purity. The Bio-SA is concentrated 
to a 19% solution via evaporation at 100.9°C, with evaporated water being purified and 
recycled. Finally, the concentrated Bio-SA is cooled to 4°C for crystallization, yielding Bio-SA 
crystals with 99.6% purity. It's noteworthy that about 16% of Bio-SA is lost during the purification 
and recovery processes.  
 
 
3.3.5 Methanol Production 
.The data for this process is retrieved from a tecno-economic environmental assessment study. 
Methanol production through hydrogenation (also called direct synthesis) involves three primary 
stages. The selected process consumes approximately 81,157 kg/h of CO2 and 11,055 kg/h of 
H2 . In the initial stage, feed gases undergo multi-stage compression and heating to the reactor's 
needs. The second stage the reactor operates under conditions of 76 bar and 210°C. A 
commercial catalyst, specifically Cu/ZnO/Al2O3, facilitates the reactions within the reactor. The 
third and final stage focuses on the separation of methanol from water. This is achieved through 
a distillation column operated at ambient pressure.  
The CO2 conversion rate to methanol is approximately 21%. The unreacted gaseous stream, 
rich in H2 and carbon oxides, is compressed and recycled to the reactor. In the distillation 
column, the methanol is extracted to achieve a purity of 99.96wt%. This results in a production 
rate of 1320 t/day of methanol, and a water output of 775.34 t/day. 
Overall, this methanol synthesis process is characterized by its comprehensive stages of 
compression, reaction, and separation.

3.7 Suggested Framework 
This work presents a simple decision-support framework to analyse the implementation of 
technologies in an IS background. The proposed methodology is generic and so it can be 
applied to different technologies with potential for enhancement of IS projects. The design of the 
framework took inspiration in a literature developed decision-support framework, for the 
assessment of resource recovery projects [32].The framework is the suggested strategy to 
answer the current project research questions. A visualization of the framework system is 
provided in figure 16. The framework suggested in the present work can be integrated in the 
phase 2 of implementation - INVENT - described previously in section 1.3. 
 
The red dashed line separates the work developed in this project from background decisions 
not related to the scope of the project. The section “Needs Finding” was previously conducted 
through the already mention Pt-X Cluster Zealand project. Here a generation of ideas was 
performed and culminated in the formulation of a problem: bioprocesses and Pt-X phase 
difficulties to compete with fossil-based products. Simultaneously the selection of CCSU and 
electrolyser technologies was previously made to this study. After, ideas for process integration 
were performed and one of them was selected and model in this work: oxygen deliver for 
protein production through electrolysis with a post-combustion recovery of the CO2 exhaust 
gases. In the following sections the framework is explained in 4 interconnected intervals. 
 
 
Figure 16 Proposed Framework to analyse the implementation of technologies in an IS background

3.7.1 Interval 1: Process Selection 
Based on the problem formulation a selection of relevant processes is conducted. The selected 
processes are obviously the facilities present in the IS park that is being analysed. Also, extra 
cases based on the analysed technologies should be selected to design the different 
opportunities and interactions between them and the park.  
 
Option gate A 
Between the different processes selected it should be decided which ones will be model through 
process simulation software. The processes that go this path will have a robust analysis on the 
integration between their manufacturing steps and the exchanges with the potential symbiotic 
technologies. The processes that go the other path will be integrated with the technologies 
based on more assumptions and uncertainties. The flexibility of this option gate allows to 
achieve initial results without the need to model an extensive complex network that might not 
have the results required to process for more deep analysis. 
 
3.7.2 Interval 2: Process Integration 
In this section of the framework viable technologies are identified based on their TRL and 
capacities to be integrated with a diverse group of industrial processes. Process design 
decisions are also performed here, normally based on its optimal construction. The selected 
technologies an design are then model with simulation tools with the purpose to identified 
bottlenecks and relevant parameters for optimization. 
 
The rest of the processes that were not model will be based on a set of assumptions that allow 
their integration with the selected technologies. The lack of accuracy at this point should not be 
a concern considering that the purpose of the framework is to make an initial evaluation of the 
potential that the technologies have to generate and promote IS networks. 
 
3.7.3 Interval 3: IS Network Design 
Here the design and analysis of the industrial pathways starts by organizing the selected data in 
groups. Each group represents one of the processes that each company/facility has. The design 
of MA.tools software dictates that the data must be stored and organised on functional units 
(FU), which normally are the final product of each process. The rest of the flows present in the 
process are normalised based on the selected FU. 
 
Objectives 
The goal of this section is to formulate the obvious symbiotic exchanges and quantify their 
benefits and trade-offs through simple material flow analysis. Here potential new resources 
exchange might be identified. On other cases, the outputs of the framework will be reductant 
and extra analysis will be needed to formulate meaningful conclusions. 
 
Data Consolidation (Decision gate B) 
The section finalises with a data consolidation function incorporated within the MA.tools 
software. If errors are found another iteration to the pathway vector must be performed until 
consolidated results are achieved. The consolidation is based on the resource interchanges 
between each process/node or the environment. It guarantees that the flow that leaves a certain 
node is the same as the flow that enters the recipient node. The decision gate B has 4 different 
exists. The other three are based on the relevance of the first results achieve from the 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
33 
 
framework. If the outputs suggested a lack of potential of the designed IS park, the decision can 
be either change the selected processes and technologies or exit the framework. If the results 
show potential further analysis is conducted on section 4. The term “potential” is used because 
the metrics used to evaluate the IS network should be based on the problem statement and the 
goals to use the framework. Some constraints could be, for example, the minimization of CO2 
emissions, the use of excess RE. 
 
3.7.4 Interval 4: Data Generation 
The last section of the framework focus on generating more results and performing iterations to 
the previously model IS park. During iterations different constraints can be selected based on 
the problem formulation.  
 
Objective 
To generate data outputs that can be used in other methods or frameworks to analyse with 
more details the synergies between the technology and the IS network.  
 
Scenarios Generation (Decision gate C) 
The generation of more data is based on further iterations to the path vector of MA.tools. To 
generate accurate and quality data the formulation of different scenarios that characterise the 
different technologies must be done carefully. When enough data is collected and consolidated 
the framework reaches the end. A final decision must be made based on the results: what tools 
and methods are needed to proceed with the analysis to the IS network.

4.1.3 Design Alternatives and Limitations 
The previous described process is designed on different assumptions that might affect its 
results. Some of these assumptions were done to simplify the initial process design and 
modelling and should be mitigated according to their sensitivity to the output results. See 
section A.1 the appendix for an overview of the assumptions made. Overall, the modelling of the 
system was performed with a relevant number of uncertainties which decreases the accuracy of 
the results.  
 
To increase the accuracy and relevance of the results future work should be perform, such as: 
energy integration of the process and sensitive analysis to the parameters that were model with 
high uncertainty. Optimization of relevant parameters would also increase the relevance of the 
work. By analyzing the performance of each technology (table 3) one can see the optimum 
range is far from the results achieved in literature. Moreover, economic and sustainability 
assessments are other methods that are out of the scope of the project. However, they would 
allow a comparation of the results with the current production system used by Unibio or other 
relevant processes.

Another limitation of the suggested process is the selected technologies and the design choices 
made. Even though the design of the process was made with two mature technologies, other

electrolysis technology portfolio is also becoming larger and companies like Dynelectro are

4.2.2 Pathway Analysis (Scenario S2, S3 and S4) 
The results in this section are achieved through the framework’s interval 4. 
 
In this section a careful analysis is performed to 3 new industrial pathways created by the new 
available sources of H2 and CO2 in the park. The selected pathways are the biomethanation 
upgrade of biogas (dashed green), the production of e-methanol through CO2 hydrogenation 
(dashed orange), and lastly a mixed of the 2 pathways. The performance of these scenarios is 
first analysed individually and afterwards compared. Some pathways might be better integrated 
to the industrial park than others. Some can give more resources to the park, while others can 
also find co-benefits within the available processes. Either way both are win-win situations for 
both parties (the park and the technology manufacturer). The goal here is to analyse how 
different pathways influence the symbiotic exchanges.

Mix Pathway (Scenario S4) 
With the purpose to make the park more integrated with a bigger diversity of qualities, a fourth 
scenario is studied and analyzed. Scenario S4 is a combination of all previous three scenarios. 
Here, all available pathways are activated.  
 
In the previous scenarios each section of the park was analyzed without constraints. To make 
the analysis more realistic some constraints are imposed to this scenario: 
 
1. Minimum methanol production to guarantee park self-sufficiency. 
2. Parks CH4 production increases to guarantee that all the produce methane from 
scenario S0 is supplied to the local grid. 
3. Industrial Park needs to keep being CH4 self-sufficient, and supply UNIBIO. 
4. The excess renewable energy is set to the electricity consumption of the previous 
scenarios: 24.5 MW. This means if higher capacities of electrolysis are required, a new 
energy source has to be found. 
 
Considering the four constraints it is clear from simple mass balances that there is no sufficient 
H2 production from the previous capacities to supply the two selected pathways. To overcome 
this issue the industrial park has available 2 industrial pathways that could give a solution: 
 
a. The electrolysis capacity increases, and the extra energy needed is supply by the local 
energy grid or any available combined heat and power (CPH) plant in the park; 
b. The biogas capacity of the park is increased by supplying bigger amounts of the 
feedstock. This would require bigger amounts of biowaste coming from other available 
sources of biowaste in the park (i.e SUCEL, UNIBIO). 
 
In this section the first pathway is selected and analyzed, however all identified pathways 
should be analyzed and compared. According to the F-matrix outputs the following chart (figure 
31) is generated with the purpose to compare the different pathway fluctuations in relationship 
to relevant qualities. Methane and CO2 are analyzed due to their relevance and importance to 
the park: both are the building blocks to produce protein and methanol, respectively.  
 
The electricity consumption of the park is not sensitive to the pathway changes showing that the 
production of a commodity “X” will not be an energy burden to the park, rather it will make the 
park self-supply to methanol (S3) or will increase the chances of collocation by increasing the 
park needs for ammonia and producing fertilizer (S2). What is more beneficial depends on 
socio-economic parameters that are not consider in this work. The modest energy requirements 
of the biomethanation and methanol processes, allows the park to keep its energy consumption 
somehow stable while increasing the park self-sufficiency towards relevant qualities (e.g CH4 
and MeOH). On the other hand, when the technology capacity has to adapt to specific 
constraints the performance of the park shows high sensitivity towards the electricity needs and 
the availability of specific qualities (CO2).

4.2.4 Technologies Applications to KIS or Similar Parks 
To analyze the effects of Industrial Symbiosis on the define process a reference to the 
availability of RE excess should be consider. The RE excess fluctuates throughout the year and 
it varies from region to region, making any specific analysis unreachable for this report. For the 
region of Kalundborg, the value was recently estimated by Khalil et al and the hydrogen 
production potential was estimated for the region [39]. According to the study the region had the 
potential to produce 337 tons of H2 per year, which equivalents to just 10% of the model 
scenarios S1-S3. Moreover, the fluctuations on the RE supply will make it mandatory to build 
storage reserves of oxygen and hydrogen to guarantee their supply is not affected by the 
unpredictability RE. Hence, the RE energy excess can be consider the biggest limitation of this 
study since no considerations about the parameter were taken.  
 
Another relevant parameter not considered is the infrastructure needed to support the 
technologies integration, for both H2 and CO2 storage. Infrastructure such as storage tanks, 
pipeline planning and integration with the energy system are out of the project scope, however 
they impose limitations to the results [36]. For instance, if the model park had both pathways 
activated it would required a pipeline infrastructure to supply the H2 to the respective facilities, 
much like how Smart Delta Rivers is doing  [8]. This of course requires significant investment.

4.2.5 Framework SWOT Analysis 
 
Strengths  
The framework allows to efficiently map several parallel industrial pathways. It allows the user to 
have a full picture of all the interactions between the processes and an industrial park. Moreover 
it is not limited by lack of data neither by IP protection rules since literature can mitigate 
problems with missing data.  
 
Weaknesses 
Only a limited number of technologies are directly related to Industrial Symbiosis. The current 
framework does not apply to technologies that do not have the potential to create synergies in 
industry. Hence, its application might be limited to just a few available technologies with high 
TLR. The results as discussed previously are sometimes also limited and reductant. As 
mention, complex qualities such as wastewater, heat or biowaste are not analysed by the 
MA.tools with the required details. This brings a lack of applications to some of the outputs of 
the framework. 
 
Opportunities 
The framework outputs bring several opportunities and applications. Some of them were 
already mentioned in this report: LCA and TEA methods can use the framework outputs into 
their analysis. Optimization methods can enhance the results of the framework. Lastly, the 
software MA.tools has several features that can be developed in the future. This would highly 
benefit the framework since most of its abilities are based on this software. Previous mention 
frameworks could also give opportunities to sections integration between them [4][32][40]. 
 
Threats 
The proposed framework value is dependent on the interest of companies to share technologies 
and infrastructure. Some companies might be cautious or doubtful on pursuing investment in 
shared assets. Moreover, different frameworks based on stronger softwares with higher TLR 
levels might make the capacities of this framework irrelevant. After a careful desk research, no 
frameworks related to the topic were found in literature.

5. Conclusion 
Collocations created by IS have contributed to make bioprocesses more economic competitive 
with fossil fuel cheap products, the case of KIS is the reference industrial model that resembles 
this concept. Similarly, technologies such as Pt-X and CCSU can open more doors to develop 
IS networks and benefit the transition to a more circular economy. Considering the IS network 
analysed in this report, Pt-X and CCSU projects show potential to contribute for industrial 
symbiosis. However, the generated results did not allow to reach a clear conclusion about their 
exact capacities. Rather the developed work allowed to create the initial structure of a 
framework that could facilitate the integration of synergetic technologies in industrial parks. 
 
The initial integration of the technologies was successfully performed with important qualities 
being supplied to the park (oxygen) and waste streams recovered by the technologies (CO2 
exhaust gases). The trade-offs of integrating the processes are clear: less waste and more 
collocations came with high energy costs. Further analysis is required to understand if the 
created collocations can tackle the high energy and economic demand that their implementation 
brings. In this case-study possible co-benefits were suggested regarding the use of wastewater 
streams that could supply both technologies. Nevertheless, no analysis is performed on the 
impurities present in the wastewaters, neither on the processes required to purify them due to 
framework limitations. Beyond the commodities mentioned, the studied technologies hold the 
potential to produce a full portfolio of blockchain chemicals. Delving into the various production 
pathways could reveal the genuine potential of these technologies in fostering and enhancing 
symbiotic networks. For instance, producing ammonia, ethylene, propane, or even carbon fibers 
can be achieved using the principal products of both technologies: H2 and CO2. A deep 
exploration of these industrial pathways would spark a valuable discussion on the true capability 
of electrolysers and CCSU technologies in advancing industrial symbiosis. 
 
The following question arise while analysing the results of this report: 
“Does a diverse production of commodities through hydrogen, with the purpose to increase the 
SRL of a park, bring monetary and sustainable benefits? Or the diverse integration of the 
technology is not feasible, and the hydrogen should simply be used to produce one-step 
products as the current Pt-X projects are aiming for?” 
 
The results of this work are based on a software that is still on an early stage of development 
and different applications and improvements can be thought for both the software and the 
framework. Below different future work opportunities are suggested. 
 
• 
The process simulation conducted in this work is limited and would require several 
improvements to reach stronger conclusions about the challenges to integrate 
electrolysers and amine scrubbing system with a protein production process. The 
application of sensitive analysis methods to relevant parameters would give a better 
understanding and relationship between the different sections of the process. A pinch 
analysis and energy integration of all process should also be conducted to analyse 
more efficiently the energy demand of all system. 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
51 
 
• 
The Pt-X and CCSU projects offer a spectrum of products that can be tailored to market 
demands. Notably, the technologies produce methane, methanol, and pure CO2, which 
have the potential to enhance park performance by fostering collocations and reducing 
imports and waste. However, a more comprehensive evaluation is required to discern 
the sustainability benefits and challenges of these technologies. Incorporating techno-
economic and life cycle assessments would solidify the findings and lead to more 
concrete conclusions. 
• 
During the generation of scenarios, in particular scenario S4, it was analysed the 
capacities that the tool has for modelling the industrial system robustness to outside 
constraints. From this analysis it would be interesting to see the software being 
developed to analyse the capacity that industrial parks have towards changes in the 
supply of materials. In the report, it was analyse how different industrial pathways inside 
the park can respond to common challenges: a blockage to natural gas suppliers for 
example could allow the software to analyse an industrial park and visualize what 
solutions inside the park could be found to mitigate the problem (i.e use of hydrogen to 
upgrade the biogas into bigger quantities of methane). 
• 
The current work misses both an optimization to the process integration simulation and 
to the industrial symbiosis network that was proposed and model. For the last one the 
optimum pathway ratios will be dependent on what parameter(s) are selected for 
optimization. A possible parameter would be the maximization of the collocation’s 
streams, which could be combined with economic or sustainability parameters (i.e H2 
minimum price, greenhouse gas emissions).  
• 
Data accuracy and availability are crucial factors in the proposed framework. The 
introduction of specialized industrial databases would streamline data gathering, 
facilitating more insightful results. Integration with other tools, frameworks, and data 
analysers will be crucial to refine the outputs of the framework or the software.

similar to the biomass of cells were found in the library of

compound was created based on the thermodynamic 
properties of biomass found in modelling books. This might 
explain the lack of accuracy of the energy balance of the 
Uloop. (The other equipments energy balances and 
performance were not effected by this assumption). 
•

combustion of the methane. It also only models the 
combustion of methane and leaves out the presence of 
heavier hydrocarbons (present in natural gas) and the 
formation of other gases except from CO2 and H2O (i.e CO, 
NOX). 
• 
The calculation of the theoretical air is based on 
stoichiometric calculations applied to combustion reactions. 
The amount of excess air is based on the percentage used 
on natural gas power plants.

Appendix B 
Metabolic Analysis Model Relevant 
Data   
In enclosure B the input data used in the Metabolic Analysis software (MA.tools) is provided. 
The Matrix of processes (MAp) are provided with important transboundary flows (TBF) 
highlighted in green. The data presented refers either to scenario S1 or S4, as a reference and 
example for the reader. The terms highlighted in red are data points that were not found or not 
accurately estimated. The glossary of relevant terms common in the industrial ecology discipline 
is given below. 
 
Table 14 Glossary for relevant terms. 
Term 
Meaning 
Nodes 
Compartments of the system, within a node several 
processes can be present. The processes from the 
same node are normally interconnected in this report.  
Process node 
Parts of the node. SUCEL node has two processes: 
biodiesel and succinic acid production. 
Quality flow 
Refer to any consumed, produced or transfer of 
material and energy. Composition and thermodynamic 
details are not specified in qualities. 
Industrial pathway 
An industrial pathway is defined as a combination of 
processes needed to achieve a service. An example is 
the 2 industrial pathways available in the BAS node. 
Example: The biogas has available two parallel 
industrial pathways that can be activated individually or 
simultaneously to produce concentrated methane. 
Collocation 
When a quality is not wasted or when a service is 
interchange within two nodes. In both ways emissions 
are mitigated (less transportation, less waste). In this 
work the contribution of collocation is divided to both 
nodes. 
Importations 
Any qualities that enter the industrial park. 
Exportations 
All qualities that leave the industrial park and have 
service value to the society. 
Waste 
Gas, liquid waste that leaves the industrial park and is 
emitted to the environment. Hazardous and non-
hazardous waste are not separated in this report for 
simplicity reasons.
