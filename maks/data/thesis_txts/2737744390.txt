Abstract  
This thesis explores the potential of a support system for heat pump technology to address 
electricity shortages and enhance government revenue through differentiated energy taxation. 
With Iceland's unique energy landscape, characterized by a reliance on geothermal and 
hydroelectric power, the nation faces challenges due to increased electricity demand driven by 
industrial growth and technological advancement. This study investigates the feasibility of 
adopting heat pump systems in non-residential buildings to improve energy efficiency and 
manage electricity demand effectively. 
Three primary objectives guide this research: assessing the current energy consumption 
patterns in non-residential buildings, evaluating the energy and financial impacts of heat pump 
installations through a detailed case study, and exploring potential support systems that 
leverage increased government revenue from energy savings. Through quantitative analysis 
and a case study approach, the research provides insights into the effectiveness of heat pumps 
in reducing electricity consumption, thereby offering a viable solution to Iceland's electricity 
constraints. 
Furthermore, the thesis examines the economic implications of tax reforms and subsidies on 
heat pump installations, revealing that strategic support systems can significantly enhance the 
attractiveness of these technologies, leading to increased adoption and financial benefits both 
for building owners and the government. This study contributes to the broader discourse on 
energy efficiency and sustainable development, proposing policy recommendations to support 
Iceland’s transition towards a more sustainable and economically viable energy future.

Chapter 1- Introduction 
1.1. Background - Energy system in Iceland 
Iceland is a small island located between the North Atlantic and Arctic Oceans, on the Mid-
Atlantic ridge that separates the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates (Denk, Grímsson, 
Zetter, & Símonarson, 2011). Because of the country's positioning, Iceland has access to 
geothermal heat where the tectonic plates meet. This geothermal heat is used for both heating 
and electricity generation in Iceland. Unlike other nations that may rely more heavily on fossil 
fuels this unique access to geothermal energy has facilitated a clean, sustainable energy 
landscape for Iceland (Shortall & Kharrazi, 2017). However, while the country primarily relies 
on geothermal energy for heating, approximately 15% of houses that are located far from the 
geothermal resources and do not have access to district heating are heated with electricity 
(Government of Iceland, 2016). Electricity has the potential to power everything from 
household appliances and lighting to industrial machinery and technological devices, making 
it an invaluable part of modern life. However, using electricity for heating can be less efficient 
and more costly than other sources. Whilst, there is currently an electricity shortage in Iceland 
due to rapidly increasing demand driven by population growth and the expansion of energy-
intensive industries like aluminum smelting and data centers, and a growing tourism industry. 
Technological advancements, and the move to digitisation has also led to higher consumption 
of electricity in residential and commercial sectors. 
 
Iceland has a closed electricity system with no grid connections to other countries, and despite 
increasing energy demands, the construction of new power stations has been minimal, due to 
heightened concerns over nature conservation in recent years (Ingolfsdottir A., Gunnarsdottir 
G., 2020).  Maximizing energy efficiency for electric heating is therefore key to meet growing 
demand. Currently, geothermal sources account for about 30% of Iceland's electricity 
production, with hydropower contributing the up to 70% of the baseload during the summer 
months, with capacity significantly lower during winter months. Historically, this seasonal 
variation is managed using reservoirs; however,  the stress on resources during winter 
underscores the feasibility and desirability of supporting alternative energy-efficient 
technologies and practices (Ministry of Industries and Innovation, n.d.) A potential solution to 
address this electricity shortage and improve energy efficiency is the adoption of heat pump 
technologies. 
 
Heat pumps are an efficient technology that can be used to manage and reduce electricity load, 
especially during critical winter periods. Heat pumps work by extracting heat from the air, 
ground, or water outside a building and using it to heat the interior. In a relatively cold climate 
like in Iceland, heat pumps can provide a reliable and sustainable heating solution (Gibb, 
Rosenow, Lowes, & Hewitt, 2023).  Modern heat pumps are designed to perform efficiently 
even in low temperatures, making them a viable option for maintaining comfortable indoor 
environments during harsh winters, reducing  the reliance on fossil fuels, lower energy costs, 
==PAGE:6==
 
2 
and contributing to a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions (U.S. Department of Energy, 
2023). Heat pumps have the ability to significantly reduce electricity consumption for heating 
by more than 25% (R.S.Adhikari et al., 2012). However, as electricity in Iceland is relatively 
cheap, heat pumps currently have a long payback period. Therefore people are less inclined to 
consider this option due to this low cost of electricity. Currently, households in residential 
buildings heated with electricity are eligible for subsidies for their heating because of the high 
cost compared to geothermal heating.  Purchase and installation of heat pumps is also available 
through this subsidy system. However, these subsidies are limited to houses where people have 
a legal address. This excludes companies, institutions, summer houses, and other non-
residential entities from benefiting from these subsidies (Landsvirkjun et al., 2023). With a heat 
pump installation, both individuals and society benefit since the electricity saved is then 
available for use elsewhere in the closed grid. This available electricity could be used, for 
example, to charge new electric cars, and meet electricity needs for new houses being built for 
the growing population.  
  
1.2 Statement of Objective 
In Iceland, electricity is subject to two different VAT rates (24% and 11%), with electric  
heating falling within the lower VAT rate of 11%, and electricity for all other purposes, such 
as TV charging, dishwasher, the fridge, etc. is subject to the higher rate of 24% (KPMG, 2024). 
By establishing a support system for non-residential buildings, such as companies and 
institutions, to install a heat pump, the electricity saved and made available could be redirected 
to other purposes where the VAT rate is 24% rather than 11%, resulting in an increased revenue 
for the government (the government would get more 
tax revenue for the same kWh) and potentially reducing the necessity for new power stations. 
Within this context, the aim of the thesis is to evaluate how heat pump technology can address 
Iceland's electricity shortage and enhance government revenue through increased energy 
taxation, utilizing a support system for their adoption in non-residential buildings. To this end 
this thesis seeks to understand the following research question: 
 
"How can the implementation of a support system for heat pump installation in  
 
non-residential buildings contribute to enhancing energy efficiency in Iceland, 
addressing electricity shortages, and increasing government revenue?”

Chapter 2 – Literature review  
This Chapter aims to review existing literature on the adoption of heat pump technology in 
non-residential buildings and explores the role of government subsidies in promoting 
renewable energy initiatives. Specifically, the literature review provides an overview of how 
government subsidy mechanisms can positively impact various sectors i.e. their effects on 
enhancing economic viability, supporting sustainable development, and fostering innovation. 
It also addresses the electricity shortage that Iceland faces and examines the potential of 
subsidies to mitigate these issues and support the energy sector's transition towards 
sustainability. 
 
2.1 Subsidy polices 
In recent years, global environmental awareness for the need to reduce our energy footprint has 
increased, emphasizing the need to optimize energy consumption, reduce overall energy use, 
and enhance energy conservation and emission reduction efforts (World Economic Forum, 
2022). At the same time, the significant global energy crisis observed over the last number of 
years has emerged as a central challenge for nations worldwide. This crisis is driven by the 
shortage of fossil fuel reserves and by an ever-increasing demand for energy, largely due to the 
world's growing population. The situation has been further compounded in the aftermath of the 
COVID-19 pandemic and ongoing geopolitical conflicts, including wars, which have disrupted 
energy supply chains and reduced energy supplies worldwide  significantly (Ozili & Ozen, 
2023 & Zakeri et al., 2022). 
As a result, countries have increased their investment in renewable energy sectors to an 
unprecedented level, recognizing its potential to stimulate regional economic growth. Due to 
the necessity of energy in our everyday lives, but the high cost of moving away from fossil 
fuels, governments are increasing putting in place economic incentives to facilitate the energy 
transition (Qadir, Al-Motairi, Tahir, & Al-Fagih, 2021). These economic incentives include:  
1) Subsidies and Grants: Governments provide direct financial support to renewable 
energy projects through subsidies and grants, reducing the initial investment costs for 
developers and making renewable energy more competitive against fossil fuels. These 
subsidies can take various forms, including direct payments, rebates, and low-interest 
loans, which significantly lower the barrier to entry for renewable energy projects. For 
example, the U.S. government offers the Investment Tax Credit (ITC) for solar energy, 
which covers a percentage of the installation cost, making it a viable option for many 
(ManagEnergy, n.d.). 
2) Feed-in Tariffs (FiTs): A feed-in tariff (FiT) is a policy mechanism designed to 
encourage the development of renewable energy sources by offering producers a 
guaranteed price for their energy that is higher than the market rate. These tariffs 
typically involve long-term contracts, often spanning 15 to 20 years, which provide 
financial stability and predictability for renewable energy projects. FiTs are widely 
==PAGE:10==
 
6 
implemented in the U.S. and internationally, with significant use in countries like 
Germany and Japan (Kenton, 2024).  
3) Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS): RPS mandates require that a certain 
percentage of electricity sold by utilities comes from renewable energy sources. This 
policy creates a guaranteed market for renewable energy, driving its adoption. States 
like California have aggressive RPS targets, which have spurred significant investments 
in solar, wind, and other renewable energy sources (California Public Utilities 
Commission, n.d.). 
4) Carbon Pricing: Implementing carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems puts a price on 
carbon emissions, thereby incentivizing reductions in greenhouse gas emissions and 
promoting cleaner energy sources. For example, the European Union’s Emissions 
Trading System (EU ETS) is a cap-and-trade system that has successfully reduced 
emissions from large power plants and industrial facilities (World Bank, n.d.), 
(European Commission, n.d.). 
2.1.1 The Effectiveness of Government Subsidies 
Numerous studies have sought to understand the effectiveness of government subsidies to 
facilitate the green energy transition. A study conducted in 2019 by Yang,et al. investigated 
the threshold effect of government subsidies investment in 92 Chinese companies from 2007 
to 2016. It was found that when the levels of energy consumption and bank credit are high, and 
the level of economic development is below a certain threshold, the effectiveness of 
government subsidies in promoting investment in renewable energy is significantly increased. 
Another interesting finding was that when the intensity of energy consumption exceeds a 
certain threshold, tax incentives have a more substantial impact on renewable energy 
investment compared to government monetary subsidies. Moreover, government monetary 
subsidies significantly promote renewable energy investment only when the level of economic 
development is below the threshold. Finally it showed that government subsidies significantly 
enhance renewable energy investment in large enterprises only when bank credit exceeds a 
certain threshold, with no notable impact on medium-, small-, and micro-sized enterprises. 
Furthermore, irrespective of whether the intensity of energy consumption and the level of 
economic development surpass these thresholds, government subsidies are more effective in 
encouraging investment in medium-, small-, and micro-sized enterprises compared to large 
ones (Yang, He, Xia, & Chen, 2019). 
An article published by Nordic shows the effectiveness of government subsidies in the Nordic 
region for green energy transitions has been significant. These policies have successfully 
increased the development and implementation of renewable technologies, especially wind and 
solar energy. The subsidies have been instrumental in reducing costs, fostering market 
competitiveness against fossil fuels, and driving technological advancements and export 
capacities, particularly in Denmark and Sweden, where wind energy has flourished. For 
example, Denmark introduced the Electricity Supply Act and Heat Supply Act in the 1970s, 
which laid the groundwork for later expansions in renewable energy policy. The overall policy 
==PAGE:11==
 
7 
environment has been vital in meeting both national and EU renewable energy targets (Nordic, 
n.d.).  
A recent study on policy uncertainty in Europe, by Sendstad et.al (2022), emphasizes the 
critical impact of policy changes on renewable energy investments. The study illustrates how 
unexpected major changes or reductions in subsidies significantly hinder investment rates in 
renewable energy within the EU, particularly affecting solar and wind sectors differently. This 
underscores the importance of stable and credible government commitments to subsidy 
schemes for maintaining investor confidence and achieving investment targets (Sendstad, 
Hagspiel, Mikkelsen, Ravndal, & Tveitstøl, 2022). 
2.2 Energy technology and heat pumps 
As well as increasing the use of renewable energy, energy efficient technologies can play a 
pivotal role by enhancing energy efficiency and supporting the transition to a green economy. 
Technologies such as heat pumps, solar panels, and wind turbines harness renewable resources 
and optimize energy use, offering both environmental and economic benefits. Among these, 
heat pumps stand out due to their unique ability to transfer heat rather than generate it, making 
them a cornerstone in energy-efficient building design (International Energy Agency, n.d.). 
 
Heat pumps have existed for more than a century in applications such as refrigerators and air 
conditioners. Heat pumps operate by using electricity to move heat from cooler to warmer 
areas, thereby cooling down a warm space and warming up a cooler one, see figure 1. As noted 
in the Introduction, heat pumps used in heating buildings extract heat from outside to heat the 
inside during colder months and reverse the process to cool the inside  during warmer months. 
This efficient heat transfer mechanism  maintains comfortable indoor temperatures more 
effectively than traditional heating or cooling systems. There are three primary types of duct-
connected heat pumps, air-to-air, water-source, and then geothermal. Each type extracts heat 
from its respective source i.e. air, water, or the ground, outside your residence and amplifies it 
for indoor heating (U.S. Department of Energy, n.d.).

In 2023 a study done by Vonžudaitė et al, was published that focused on optimizing heat pump 
systems in buildings to minimize costs and CO2 emissions, considering various building types 
and heat pump models. Using mixed-integer nonlinear programming and multi-objective 
optimization, the study identified air-to-water heat pumps and electric heaters as the most 
efficient solutions for space heating, achieving CO2 neutrality in the majority of buildings 
analyzed. They further found that as well significant cost reductions, a move to these 
technologies would have substantial environmental benefits. Using a series of case studies, the 
study found that moving to heat pump systems in renovated and unrenovated buildings 
achieved substantial energy savings, with low energy costs of 0.0831 EUR/kWh, short payback 
periods, and high returns on investment.  
Regarding the environmental impact, the research used a life-cyle assessment of heat pump 
systems, to examine immediate and long term impacts on CO2 emission. They found both 
immediate and long term benefits to the environment through heat pump adoption. These 
findings collectively highlight the role of heat pumps in promoting sustainable and 
economically viable heating solutions, underscoring their capability to achieve significant 
energy savings and attractive investment returns. The holistic approach adopted by the study 
offers a compelling argument for the widespread adoption of heat pumps, reinforcing their 
critical importance in advancing energy efficiency and sustainability goals within the 
construction and real estate sectors.

Iceland is facing its challenges within the global energy crisis. Iceland's electricity system is 
currently operating at its  maximum capacity, with a projected shortfall in meeting the demand 
necessary to support average industrial growth, particularly concerning the energy transition 
(Arnason, 2023). A study done in collaboration with researchers from MIT Energy initiative 
and the Institute for Research in Technology (IIT) at Comillas University published in 2017,

power plants will only increase by about 0.9%. It is predicted that in 2026, the maximum 
generation capacity will then be estimated to be about 2.6% higher than the total demand for 
energy, or about 6.5% higher than the demand for priority energy according to the Electricity 
Forecast (Efla, 2022).

reductions in their energy usage. Whilst, the average energy consumption for heating decreased

conditions. The findings highlight that heat pumps not only maintained optimal functionality 
across Iceland's diverse and often harsh climates but also provided significant savings on

2.4 Conclusion 
To conclude, research has found that governments can play a critical role in fostering renewable 
energy investments through subsidies and that strategic governmental interventions can 
substantially enhance renewable energy investments. However research also highlights the 
differential effects of tax incentives and monetary subsidies on the uptake of government 
incentives. In Iceland, the availability of subsidies for residential buildings has proven effective 
in areas devoid of geothermal resources, yet there is a notable gap in support for industrial 
buildings, suggesting an area for policy enhancement. Globally, mechanisms such as Feed-in 
Tariffs, Renewable Portfolio Standards, and direct financial subsidies have successfully 
increased the development and implementation of renewable technologies like solar and wind 
energy. These measures not only foster market competitiveness against fossil fuels but also 
drive technological advancements and broader economic benefits. 
 
However, this review also identifies a critical need for consistent and reliable policy 
frameworks to maintain investor confidence and ensure the continued expansion of renewable 
energy sectors. The uncertainty surrounding subsidy schemes can significantly impact 
investment decisions, as evidenced by fluctuating support levels affecting renewable energy 
projects across Europe. Finally, the integration of heat pump technology supported by 
government incentives can significantly contribute to energy sustainability and economic 
stability. For regions like Iceland, where energy demand is rapidly growing, strategic 
investments in heat pump technology and a clear, supportive policy landscape are essential to 
meet future energy needs and environmental goals. This review underscores the importance of 
continued research and policy development to fully leverage the potential of heat pumps in 
enhancing global energy security and sustainability.

Chapter 3 - Data and method 
3.1 Data on Electric heating usage 
To conduct this study, data on electric heating usage in Iceland was required as well as 
information on areas where electricity is used for heating purposes. Initially, data on electric 
heating usage was obtained from the Icelandic National Energy Agency (NEAI). However, it 
became evident that further detailed information from electrical utility companies in Iceland 
was required to accurately determine the electricity consumption for heating in non-residential 
buildings in the whole country. To facilitate this data collection and help identify the electrical 
utility companies, NEAI provided data indicating the regions in Iceland where electricity is the 
primary source of heating. This mapping exercise identified the four key electricity utility 
companies catering to these areas: Rarik, Orkubú Vestfjarða, HS veitur and Veitur. Data on 
service coverage and the annual electricity usage of the non residential buildings that use 
electricity for heating was obtained from these 4 companies. This data collection effort not only 
illuminated the geographical distribution of electric heating reliance but also underscored the 
importance of targeted interventions to enhance energy efficiency across these regions. It was 
found that Rarik and Orkubú Vestfjarða accounted for 96% of service coverage in these areas, 
significantly more than HS Veitur and Veitur, each contributing about 2%. Consequently, the 
latter two were excluded from detailed study considerations. 
 
There are five categories for electricity usage in iceland, termed A, B, C, N and H: 
A: This rate includes the general VAT of 24% without any subsidies for electric heating. 
B: Applies to combined metering of general use and electric heating, with 15% of the 
usage taxed at the general VAT of 24% and 85% at a reduced VAT of 11% for electric 
heating, excluding any subsidy for electric heating. 
C: Similar to B, this rate is for combined usage but includes a subsidy for the electric 
heating, with the same VAT distribution. 
N: Taxed at 11% for electric heating and includes a subsidy for electric heating. 
H: Also taxed at 11% for electric heating, but excludes any subsidy for electric heating. 
This study specifically targets category B and H, considering it covers all institutions and 
companies that are not serviced by district heating and use electricity for heating.  In contrast, 
category A consists of buildings that benefit from geothermal district heating, and therefore are 
not using electricity to heat the building. Residential buildings without district heating 
connections but hold a legal resident registration, thereby qualifying for subsidies, are 
classified under category C. Category N caters to locations served by electric boiler district 
heating. In these electric heated regions, few towns benefit from heating provided by these 
==PAGE:17==
 
13 
utilities, so they are excluded from this study. Given this context, our research will exclusively 
investigate category B and N, as it represents the sector of interest for assessing the impact and 
potential of heat pump installations in non-residential buildings not served by district heating. 
 
In most of the buildings, the electricity used for heating is measured separately from other uses. 
However, for some buildings where electricity is measured as a whole, an systematic 
assumption is made by the authorities that 85% of the total electricity consumption is attributed 
to heating, based on calculations from the utility companies. 
3.2 Electricity cost  
To be able to calculate the monetary value of electricity savings it is important to understand 
the structure of the electricity system in Iceland. The sale and distribution of electricity are

Chapter 4 - System analysis 
This chapter outlines the operational framework, key components, and interactions of a 
potential support system designed as a strategic interventions. These interventions aim to 
promote the adoption and efficient use of heat pumps in non-residential buildings in Iceland. 
Utilizing analytical methods to examine the system from various perspectives, it aims to deliver 
a thorough understanding of how the electricty system operates in Iceland, its effects on 
stakeholders, and its contribution to Icelands energy goals. The analysis sets the foundation for 
identifying areas for improvement and potential growth, ensuring the system's effectiveness in 
promoting heat pump installation. The chapter begins by explaining the current system and its 
boundaries. It then explores how the system can be enhanced with heat pump installations and 
subsidies, detailing the flow of electricity and its interaction with policy. Following this, the 
Business Model Canvas  is used to identify and visualize the key elements of the support 
system, such as value propositions, customer segments, and revenue streams. This management 
tool facilitates an examination of how these elements interact to create value within the system.  
 
4.1. System boundaries: Current and Proposed Structures 
Understanding the system boundaries is crucial to comprehend the structure of the proposed 
support system. It provides clarity on how electricity flows from production to consumption 
and the interactions with governmental fiscal policies. Establishing these boundaries helps 
delineate the scope of the support system, encompassing the journey of electricity from its 
generation by the producers to its end users. This clarity is essential for evaluating the systems 
effectiveness, its economic impact, and its role in mitigating Icelands energy challenges.

and potentially increasing government revenue through a restructured energy tax system 
necessitate an innovate strategy. To effectively outline and develop such a comprehensive 
strategy, the Business Model Canvas (BMC) serves as an invaluable tool. The BMC is a

It allows for a detailed visualization of the key components of a business or project, spanning 
areas such as customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue 
streams, key activities, key resources, and key partners. By using the BMC, stakeholders can 
identify and understand the interconnections between different elements of the system, 
ensuring that the proposed solutions are not only effective but also aligned with broader 
sustainability and efficiency objectives. The use of the BMC facilitates a structured exploration 
of how the proposed support system can deliver value to its primary customers, the non-
residential building owners. It helps in mapping out the mechanisms through which the system 
can encourage the adoption of heat pump technology, thereby contributing to Iceland's energy 
efficiency and sustainability goals. Furthermore, the BMC aids in identifying the roles and

challenges.

Value proposition  
The support system for heat pump installations in non-residential buildings in Iceland offers 
three key value propositions: increased energy efficiency, enhanced sustainability, and 
increased government revenue. By adopting heat pump technology, electricity consumption for 
heating is reduced, contributing to alleviating the electricity shortage.This not only supports 
Iceland's sustainability goals by ensuring a more efficient utilization of green energy but also 
maintains the sustainability benefits of electrical heating by increasing the availability of 
electricity. Moreover,  the system introduces a financial model that increases government 
revenue without raising taxes. Redirecting savings from lower VAT rate consumption to higher 
rate uses allows for a boost in government funds, which can be reinvested into further energy 
efficiency initiatives and/or making the subsidies sustainable. These propositions aim to 
achieve good energy management by combining economic benefits with environmental care. 
 
Customer 
The customer segment for the proposed support system for heat pump installations are the 
owners of non-residential buildings in Iceland. This diverse group includes entities such as 
businesses, educational institutions, governmental agencies, and various organizations that 
operate facilities outside the residential spectrum. For these building owners, the transition to 
heat pump technology represents an opportunity to reduce their energy consumption and costs 
associated with heating. 
 
Key activities  
The key activities for the support system mainly include a few steps, starting with the 
installation of heat pumps in non-residential buildings. This step is crucial for directly reducing 
electricity used for heating. Alongside, engaging with building owners is essential, providing 
them with insights into the economic and environmental benefits of heat pumps, which 
encourages adoption. Additionally, creating partnerships with government agencies, energy 
companies, and suppliers plays a pivotal role, since these collaborations ensure smooth 
implementation.  
 
Key resources  
The key resources vital to the support system include government and industrial contacts, 
alongside financial resources. Government and industrial contacts are crucial for navigating 
the regulatory landscape, securing subsidies, and establishing partnerships that facilitate the 
programs implementation. These contacts serve as a bridge between the project and the 
necessary regulatory approvals, support mechanisms, and collaboration with energy companies 
and suppliers. Financial resources are also essential for the upfront investment required for the 
installation of heat pumps, as well as for managing the operational costs associated with 
running the support system. These resources ensure the projects viability, enabling it to offer 
subsidies to non-residential building owners and to cover any unforeseen expenses.  
 
Customer relationship 
For this support system, maintaining strong customer relationships is important. Ongoing 
support and education are critical to ensuring that the building owners are fully informed about

Education efforts aim to empower owners with knowledge about the long-term economic

Customer channel 
To effectively connect with customers, establishing clear communication with the building

Key partners  
The key partners for the system are instrumental in ensuring the programs success and its

Energy companies and utility providers are also important partners since the adoption of heat 
pumps will affect them. The Icelandic National Energy Authority (Orkustofnun) acts as a

standards and policies, while also contributing valuable data and insights into energy usage and 
efficiency. Heat pump suppliers are a key partner as well since they provide the heat pump 
systems tailored to the specific needs of non-residential buildings in Iceland.  
Cost structure  
The cost structure of the support system covers several key components, including government 
subsidies, development costs, and marketing and engagement expenses. Government subsidies 
represent a significant part of the financial framework, providing essential funding to offset the 
installation costs for building owners and encourage wider adoption of heat pump technology. 
Development costs cover the expenses associated with setting up and running the support 
system. Marketing and engagement expenses are crucial for raising awareness among building 
owners about the benefits of heat pumps and the available subsidies, involving costs related to 
advertising and informational materials.

Revenue stream  
The main revenue stream for the system is derived from value-added tax (VAT) revenue. This 
innovative financial model leverages the increased use of heat pumps to potentially shift energy 
consumption patterns, thereby increasing the government's VAT revenue from electricity 
usage. As buildings transition from traditional heating methods to more energy-efficient heat 
pumps, the electricity saved—previously taxed at a lower rate for heating—can be redirected 
towards other uses taxed at a higher VAT rate. This not only promotes energy efficiency and 
sustainability but also creates a sustainable revenue stream for the government without 
imposing new taxes. The additional VAT revenue generated can then be reinvested into further 
sustainability initiatives, creating a virtuous cycle that supports the country's environmental 
and energy goals.

5.2 Model testing 
This section describes a sensitivity analysis that specifically evaluates the influence of varying 
policy scenarios on the outcomes of the proposed support mechanisms for heat pump 
installations. Rather than a traditional model sensitivity analysis, this analysis is designed to 
demonstrate how sensitive the outcomes are to changes in variables such as subsidy rates, 
installation costs, energy prices, energy savings, and tax rates. Using a one-at-a-time (OAT) 
methodology, the analysis varied one key variable at a time within a range deemed realistic by 
historical data from Orkustofnun, keeping other variables constant. This focused approach 
allowed for a precise understanding of the individual impact of each variable on the support 
system's performance under various hypothetical policy scenarios. Results were systematically 
recorded in structured data tables using Excel, facilitating an organized evaluation of how each 
variable adjustment influences key outcomes like the payback period and overall cost-
effectiveness of heat pump installations.  The analysis revealed that certain variables, such as 
the subsidy rate and energy price, had quite an impact on the payback period and overall cost-
effectiveness of the system. For instance, increasing the subsidy rate by 10% reduces the 
average payback period by approximately 20%, enhancing the attractiveness of heat pump 
installations to building owners. 
These findings underscore the importance of carefully setting policy parameters to ensure the 
economic and operational success of the support system. They also highlight the need for 
ongoing monitoring and flexibility in policy design to accommodate changes in economic 
conditions and energy markets. Overall, the sensitivity analysis not only validated the resilience 
of the support system but also provided critical insights that will inform future policy 
adjustments, ensuring that the system remains effective under a wide range of conditions.

period, this would amount to a total of 2,350,857 ISK in tax revenue. 
5.4 Discussion 
The DSS facilitates strategic planning by illuminating the paths toward achieving energy 
efficiency and economic viability. It allows for the incorporation of real-world energy usage 
data, which helps ensure that decisions about energy policies are based on real, observed 
information. The interactive nature of the model ensures that it is not a passive storehouse of 
data but an active participant in the policy-making process, offering a robust platform for 
discussions and new discoveries. By providing immediate feedback on how changes in the 
subsidy level affect both the adoption rate of heat pumps and the associated fiscal benefits, the

shortages and transitioning to more efficient energy use.

Chapter 7- Discussion and recommendations 
 
The analysis presented in this thesis underscores the critical role of financial incentives in 
promoting the adoption of heat pump technology in non-residential buildings across Iceland 
that don't have access to distinct heating. The 20, 15, and 10-year support scenarios all 
demonstrate the importance of a support system to lower initial financial barriers, making the 
installation of heat pumps more feasible and attractive for building owners. 
 
The first objective of the study was achieved by assessing the electricity consumption for 
heating in non-residential buildings across Iceland, highlighting the significant energy usage 
in these buildings and the potential for substantial electricity  savings. Through additional data 
collection on the financial analysis focused on examining the pricing structures and their impact 
on the cost of electricity, showed that adopting heat pumps could lead to considerable revenue 
savings. These findings underscore the potential for electricity savings in Iceland and the 
resulting economic benefits. 
The second objective of the study was achieved by evaluating both the energy-saving and 
financial impacts of heat pump installations through a case study of Snæfellsnes Town Hall. 
This evaluation highlighted significant reductions in energy consumption and demonstrated the 
financial feasibility and benefits of adopting heat pump technology. The installation of the heat 
pump system led to a 70% reduction in energy consumption at Snæfellsnes Town Hall, 
showcasing the substantial potential for energy savings in non-residential buildings. The initial 
installation cost of 7,516,542 ISK was significantly reduced with the support system. Different 
support scenarios (10, 15, and 20 years) were analyzed, showing effective cost reductions and 
shorter payback periods, enhancing the financial attractiveness of heat pump installations. The 
study highlighted a potential increase in government revenue through energy tax savings, 
supporting the economic benefits of energy-efficient investments. The positive feedback from 
Mayor Kristinn Jónasson emphasized the broader potential for a support system, encouraging 
other non residential building owners to consider adopting  this energy-efficient solution with 
the help of a support system. These findings underscore the practical and economic benefits of 
installing heat pumps in non-residential buildings, contributing to Iceland's goal of maximizing 
energy efficiency amidst rising electricity demand. 
Developing a DSS, objective 3 demonstrated the potential of extended subsidy plans to 
significantly reduce the financial barriers to adopting heat pump technology in non-residential 
buildings across various regions. By analyzing 20-year, 15-year, and 10-year subsidy scenarios, 
this research has shown that longer-term financial support not only accelerates the adoption of 
heat pumps but also ensures their economic viability. These findings underscore the importance 
of a government-led subsidy system in not only alleviating the energy shortages but also in 
advancing the shift towards renewable energy sources. 
Overall the findings presented here align with existing research that underscores the importance 
of well-designed government subsidies in promoting renewable energy adoption. Previous 
studies indicate that strategic subsidies can significantly boost investment in green 
==PAGE:36==
 
32 
technologies, especially when considering regional disparities and economic thresholds (Yang 
et al., 2019; Sendstad et al., 2022). This thesis confirms that financial incentives are important 
and highlights their impact on adoption rates in the renewable energy sector. For instance, the 
effectiveness of government subsidies in promoting renewable energy investments in the 
Nordic region has been well documented, showing a strong correlation between these 
incentives and increased development and implementation of renewable technologies, 
especially wind and solar power (Nordic, n.d.).  
In the specific context of Iceland, the challenges and opportunities presented by its isolated 
energy grid and its reliance on sustainable energy practices further illustrate the critical role of 
these subsidies. As detailed in recent studies, Iceland's electricity system is currently operating 
at its maximum capacity, with projections indicating a shortfall in meeting future demand 
necessary to support industrial growth and energy transition efforts (Arnason, 2023). This 
scenario underscores the urgency for strategic interventions, such as subsidies, to support the 
adoption of efficient technologies like heat pumps which have proven particularly effective in 
Iceland’s unique climatic conditions (ManagEnergy, n.d.). 
7.1 Recommendations 
As a means to guide government actions aimed at enhancing energy efficiency and assisting 
with addressing the energy shortages that Iceland faces, as part of its transition to renewable 
energy, this Section discusses a series of recommendations derived from the comprehensive 
analysis conducted in this thesis. 
Recommendation 1: Enhance Subsidy Programs for Non-Residential Buildings 
The Icelandic government should expand its subsidy programs to include non-residential 
buildings. This expansion can drive the adoption of heat pump technologies, the initiative can 
facilitate significant energy savings and efficiency improvements. Increasing the installation 
of heat pumps can significantly reduce the load on the national grid by providing a more 
efficient method of heating and cooling buildings. This reduction in grid load can free up more 
energy capacity for other uses, which can be sold at a higher tax rate and thereby increasing 
the overall energy efficiency and economic benefit to the country. 
Recommendation 2: Fairness and Equity in Subsidy Allocation 
A further consideration is the Fairness and Equity in Subsidy Allocation. The analysis revealed 
that regions like Orkubú Vestfjarða Rural, which constitute only 4% of the user base, would 
benefit significantly from this kind of a  support system . This raises the question: is it fair to 
allocate substantial resources uniformly across regions, when some areas, like Orkubú 
Vestfjarða Rural, have a much shorter payback period and could benefit disproportionately 
from the same level of support. 
Providing uniform subsidies across all regions ensures that every area receives the same 
financial support, promoting fairness and straightforward implementation. This approach 
==PAGE:37==
 
33 
simplifies the administrative process and guarantees that all regions can benefit equally from 
the financial incentives, thus promoting widespread adoption of heat pump technology. While 
it might be interesting to consider tailored subsidies, the small percentage of users that would 
benefit from such an approach suggests that a uniform subsidy system is more practical and 
equitable. 
Recommendation 3: Enhancing Public Awareness and Engagement 
A critical aspect of successful subsidy programs is public awareness and engagement. The 
government should initiate comprehensive educational campaigns to increase public 
knowledge about the benefits of heat pump technology. These campaigns should cover how 
heat pumps work, their environmental advantages, and the long-term cost savings they offer 
compared to traditional heating systems. Additionally, these campaigns could highlight the 
specifics of the subsidy program, detailing who is eligible, how to apply, and the expected 
benefits. By making this information easily accessible and understandable, the government can 
ensure that individuals and businesses are well-informed and motivated to participate in the 
subsidy programs. This proactive approach in educating and engaging the public will not only 
drive the adoption of heat pump technology but also foster a more energy-conscious culture 
across all sectors of society (United Nations, n.d.).
