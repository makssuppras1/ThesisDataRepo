Abstract 
 
The incorporation of makerspaces in the educational setting can inspire creativity, 
hands-on and teamwork. This research aims to develop the virtual network of a 
makerspace in a recycled commercial container which will be offered to schools 
with the goal of improving resource sharing, student engagement, and 
interdisciplinary collaboration.  A design-based research approach was used to 
evaluate existing makerspaces in schools across Copenhagen. The study 
analyzed different virtual chains of these makerspaces and the feedback received 
from users of these spaces, such as teachers. The results showed that virtual 
makerspaces facilitate access to shared resources, promote peer learning, and 
support the development of STEM-related skills. However, challenges were also 
identified, such as the difficulty of digital accessibility and the technical challenges 
faced by some teachers. The findings highlight the potential of virtual makerspaces 
as a scalable model for integrating digital fabrication and innovation in schools. 
 
Keywords: makerspace, virtual chain, education, teachers, STEM, STEAM, digital 
learning.

1. Introduction 
 
This thesis is part of a larger project developed by a group of DTU collaborators, 
students, teachers, and external partners, called Gnist. The objective is to create 
a virtual network for a makerspace housed in a recycled shipping container. This 
container, equipped with several 3D printers, a laser cutter, and a dedicated 
platform to support and tutor teachers, is designed to serve as an additional 
educational tool integrated into their curriculum. 
A key goal is to establish a value chain that simplifies both modeling and print 
management software, making it as user-friendly as possible for students and, 
especially, for teachers—many of whom have limited experience with technology, 
particularly in printing and modeling. 
Simultaneously, the project aims to develop an intuitive platform that provides 
guidance and support for teachers, ensuring that this service enhances their 
teaching experience rather than adding complexity. Ultimately, this initiative seeks 
to promote the seamless integration of technology into the educational curriculum. 
Nowadays, the access to any type of knowledge through the internet to watch 
tutorial videos or consult social media to get answers or recommendations to solve 
problems is very accessible. This way of accessing knowledge has allowed us to 
learn things in a practical way and has encouraged doing them ourselves. This 
learning lets us redesign and improve the results and allows us to share what we 
know how to do with others, a key action for learning that represents great value in 
society. Sharing what, how, with, what and why you make things is what is known 
as Maker Culture. This culture is the result of the ideas and knowledge that have 
transcended around the world of the Maker movement, which emerged in the 
United States based on the idea that anyone can carry out their own projects and 
products, with the help of technology. In addition, it is characterized because 
people do things with their hands, a philosophy known as DIY which means do (D) 
it (I) yourself (Y). But it's also possible to do things with others, known as DIWO, 
and work in environments where communication has a very important role and that 
occurs through social networks, whether real or virtual (Peppler and Bender, 2013).  
Makerspaces have their roots in this DIY Movement, which was born in the 2000 
decade, when the MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology) created places 
called FabLabs, spaces developed to provide access to technological tools for 
digital manufacture. These FabLabs, short for Fabrication Laboratories, were 
designed by Neil Gershenfeld. This professor conceived these spaces as those 
one where anybody could create anything they wanted using equipment controlled 
by computers providing access to prototype tools for personal fabrication (Agudo-
Martínez, M. 2017).

Transforming education would be one of the main challenges that maker education 
has. Pedagogically, its origins can be traced back to the ideas of John Dewey and 
Seymour Papert. Dewey defended the idea that active participation in one’s own 
learning is essential, and Papert emphasized the importance of construction over 
the simple transmission of knowledge (constructionism). Maker education aims to 
bridge the gap between formal and informal learning, encouraging individuals to 
consider the several places and methods through which they can learn. (Papert 
1993).  
The concept of maker education combines the terms “maker” and “education” with 
“maker literacy”, which is defined as the deep demonstration of one’s skills. Maker 
education combines various teaching approaches, including project-based, inquiry-
based, and collaborative learning. It is viewed as a blend of information and 
communication technology (ICT) and active teaching methods, primarily utilizing 
technological tools and online environments. Maker education is seen as a means 
of cultivating a maker mindset in learners, encouraging ongoing development, and 
facilitating inquiry-based learning, so it promotes innovation and awareness as 
additional key components (Halverson 2014).  
A makerspace is a learning environment where students engage in creative 
activities and learn through exploration, design, play, and experimentation with a 
variety of tools and materials. They collaborate, ask questions, solve problems, 
and develop their own inventions using techniques such as analysis and problem-
solving (Gantert, 2022).

In the United States, K–12 schools align their curricula with the Next Generation 
Science Standards’ focus on the importance of design and technology (NRC, 2012) 
and engineering (Schunn, Silk, & Apedoe, 2012) and the new media arts standards 
across the arts disciplines (NCCAS, 2014), the multidisciplinary design work often 
seen in makerspaces inspiring educators. 
 
In Europe, the maker culture has gained impetus, for example, the Innokas 
Network project in Finland. This initiative, organized by the Faculty of Education 
Sciences at the University of Helsinki, has been a pioneer in the integration of 
makerspaces in schools. The purpose of the project is to lead schools towards 
innovation through technology by providing training, consultancy and events that 
promote the use of digital tools in education. They offer training and technological 
resources to more than 1,000 educators around Finnish schools (Myllimaki M, 
2025). 
 
In Spain, there is a Project called Aula del Futuro that has modernized the 
educational system by promoting the digitalization of classrooms and the 
implementation of makerspaces within schools. The Future Classroom Lab is an 
inspiring learning environment that aims to challenge those who access it to rethink 
the role of pedagogy, technology, and spatial design within the classroom itself. In 
these spaces, students can explore the competencies and roles of 21st-century 
students and teachers in teaching and learning (Díaz, 2022).  
 
This program seeks the enhancing of students' digital, collaborative, and 
interdisciplinary skills. The Aula is divided into six reconfigurable learning zones: 
research, creation, presentation, interaction, exchange and development, each of 
which highlights a specific aspect through different physical spaces, resources, 
roles and styles (European Schoolnet, 2016), and has been designed, due to its 
spatial layout and resource allocation, to work on or develop a specific type of 
activities, challenges, or tasks with the intention of making students the 
protagonists of their learning process. (García Tudela, 2021). 
 
 
1.1. Context and Statement 
 
As Preddy L. said, "A makerspace is a destination where students-sometimes 
alongside staff, parents, and mentors-can create, problem solve, and develop 
skills, talents, thinking, and mental rigor." (Preddy L, 2013) 
 
A makerspace is a place for students to implement their ideas, individually or in 
teams. It is defined as “a creative and uniquely adaptable learning environment 
with tools and materials, which can be physical and/or virtual, where students have 
an opportunity to explore, design, play, tinker, collaborate, inquire, experiment, 
solve problems and invent” (Loertscher et al., 2013). The tools used in 
makerspaces are referred to as digital fabrication or digital manufacturing

1.2. Justification and Importance 
 
Implementing virtual makerspaces in schools is revolutionizing the learning and 
teaching experience because through this hands-on project-based learning 
experience, students develop technological and creative skills and give teachers a 
new tool to use during their classes. As Kurti said (Kurti, 2014) state: makerspaces 
allow for an experiential learning approach that enhances students' understanding 
through practical involvement. 
 
Virtual makerspaces have a number of significant advantages over traditional 
makerspaces. By facilitating access to tools and resources from anywhere, virtual 
makerspaces offer the possibility to collaborate and learn remotely, which is 
particularly relevant in today's context where education in the wake of the COVID-
19 pandemic has changed significantly. Virtual makerspaces provide a flexible and 
accessible solution that can adapt to the changing needs of schools and students. 
 
Additionally, makerspaces offer more sustainable and cost-effective solutions than 
sustainable makerspaces. The virtualization of these spaces makes it possible to 
reduce the physical space and costly materials, such as the hardware needed to 
create a makerspace, and makes it possible to implement a makerspace in a larger 
number of schools with limited resources. These features allow more students to 
have access to the creative and technological opportunities offered by virtual 
makerspaces.

2. Theoretical Framework 
 
2.1. Definition and Evolution of Makerspaces 
 
 
Makerspaces are also known as creation spaces, are physical places typically 
located in schools, colleges, and universities. They are designed to allow students 
to explore areas of knowledge through a wide variety of tools and materials, 
thereby building and creating their own learning projects.  
  
They are teamwork learning environments in which students take part on hands-
on projects inspiring creativity, invention and problem-solving. These facilities are 
fully equipped with various tools and materials that encourage students to design, 
build and experiment to foster practical application of the theory taught in the 
curriculum.  
 
==PAGE:16==

These types of spaces are increasingly common in schools around the world. They 
offer significant learning benefits, and more and more strategies are being 
developed to maximize the learning experiences within them.  
 
The concept of these spaces stems from "Maker Education," whose objective is to 
provide students with the autonomy necessary to investigate and read up on their 
own ideas and to see themselves as people who can imagine, create, build, and 
solve problems.  
 
This approach is complemented by the collaboration with others to achieve a 
purpose, which gives these spaces a sense of community. In conventional schools 
and/or universities, learning tends to take place through reading and analyzing 
knowledge found in books and teaching pedagogies rather than through physically 
"making" or "creating something." Therefore, the new concept of makerspaces, or 
makerspaces, transforms the way we learn. Over the last decade, makerspaces 
have established themselves in a variety of informal and increasingly formal 
educational settings (Peppler, 2016). 
 
According to Dougherty (2012), makerspaces are collaborative environments 
where students experiment with 3D printing, robotics, and programming to develop 
innovative projects. In education, these spaces promote active learning and 
problem-solving through hands-on activities. 
 
For instance, libraries and museums have designed makerspaces to promote 
creative activity, resource sharing, and active engagement with materials, 
processes, and ideas in their collections and exhibits, and also for reflection activity 
and boost to become aware of knowledge (Britton, 2012; Honey & Kanter, 2013).  
 
Some aspects that differentiate the classes taught in makerspaces to traditional 
classes are: 
 
● Learning approach: traditional classes are teachers-centered meanwhile 
makerspaces are student-centered with a focus in experiential learning. 
● Unlike traditional classes, makerspaces offer an informal and flexible 
environment which inspires students to creativity.   
● Makerspaces provide an assessment based on projects and peer 
evaluations, far away from the standardized assessments elaborated in 
traditional lessons.  
 
When we talk about skills that cannot be acquired in a traditional classroom, we 
are referring to activities aligned with the NGSS (Next Generation Science 
Standards) Science and Engineering Practices (2015), such as problem definition 
and solution design; to activities related to "collaboration and communication" skills

Traditional makerspaces are set at universities, schools, library and community 
centers. However, access to these spaces is often limited to urban and affluent 
areas.

socioeconomic

because

As Barton said: Makerspaces going mobile are a step in the right direction for 
breaking barriers in education (Barton, 2018).

•

FryskiLab, first mobile fabLab in Europe.

2.2. Importance of Technology in Education 
 
 
Digital tools have revolutionized the educational landscape by improving student 
motivation, autonomy, and creativity. These tools foster a sense of autonomy and 
self-directed learning as students take control of their learning process. Developing 
innovative ways in which access to this wide variety of resources is key to providing 
learners with the opportunity to explore and learn critical 21st century skills 
autonomously. 
 
In the past, education was lesson-centered where students passively received 
information, but modern technology has enabled lessons to be more active and 
student centered with gamified learning, virtual simulation, for example. This has 
made learning more interactive and engaging for students, leading to increased 
student participation and knowledge retention. 
 
Technology is crucial to competency-based education and the development of 21st 
century skills. Competency-based education emphasizes pupils gaining specific 
skills and knowledge at their own pace, and technology provides the tools that 
support this approach. Online educational platforms and applications, and digital

necessary skills for success in STEM and STEAM disciplines.  
 
Furthermore, makerspaces provide an inclusive atmosphere where students of any 
background can access and engage with new technologies. They encourage 
students to apply mathematical and scientific concepts to real-world problems 
while incorporating artistic and creative elements.  
 
These creative and technical skills learnt and reinforced through makerspaces 
enable students to be better equipped to face different challenges in the 
increasingly technological world of work, especially those related to STEM. In 
addition to the technical skills students develop, makerspaces also encourage the 
development of other equally important real-world skills such as problem solving, 
collaborative work and innovative thinking. 
 
In addition to students, the inclusion of technology in the classroom also provides 
benefits for teachers. Thanks to artificial intelligence, learning platforms and digital 
classroom tools, teachers can tailor learning to the needs of their students. In 
addition to improving classroom management, automating administrative tasks, 
etc., technology tools relieve teachers of excessive burdens.

2.3. Theoretical Foundations of Virtual Chain  
 
The Virtual Chain Model concept for educational spaces is based on Design-Based 
Research (DBR), a methodological approach that integrates iterative design, 
implementation, and evaluation in real environments (Brown, 1992; Collins, 1992). 
Design-based research is especially relevant to technological educational 
innovations as it builds a bridge between theory and practice, allowing for 
researchers to improve their designs based on both empirical findings and 
contextual information. This approach ensures that the design of the virtual chain 
develops dynamically and responds to the setbacks detected in traditional 
makerspace chains and promotes better learning for students. 
 
The DBR method, in maker environments, ensures an efficient workflow by aligning 
the technological tools in a virtual system. By following an iterative design 
methodology to develop virtual chains, technical challenges can be identified that 
would discourage users from using a makerspace, such as the need to carry out 
manual installations of various tools. If these issues are addressed in each 
iteration, the design can be improved to provide the most accessible experience 
possible for users. 
 
To ensure that a virtual chain is accessible and intuitive for the main users who 
may have little technological experience, we must take into account the concepts 
of Human-Centered Design (Norman, 2013). The HCD of Donald Norman 
prioritizes the needs, abilities, and limitations of users during all stages of design, 
which allows for accessible and user-friendly designs. One of the advantages of 
virtual chains for makerspaces is that they reduce the technical barriers that users 
may face by providing a virtual environment with pre-configured tools and 
resources available to create more accessible experiences. This accessibility 
reduces the difficulty related to the use of these maker environments, promoting 
the participation of students and teachers. 
 
This accessibility not only facilitates the integration of these spaces but also allows 
for a more equitable education. Traditional makerspaces often require advanced 
equipment and technical expertise, and some schools are unable to cope with the 
barriers this can cause. The digitization of traditional makerspace chains 
overcomes these technological barriers because it can be implemented on 
standard hardware, which means that even if there are institutions with low 
resources, they can also take full advantage of all the possibilities and benefits that 
an educational makerspace can offer. 
 
The virtual chain model is based on digital learning environments by Siemens and 
(Molotsi A, 2020) and distributed cognition (Holland & Hutchins, 1995), which 
provides a strong rationale for its design and implementation. Siemens, through 
his connectivism, stresses that digital networks are important for facilitating 
==PAGE:22==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
20 
knowledge sharing and collaborative learning, emphasizing that tools are needed 
to connect educators, learners, and resources. Under the framework of this theory, 
it could be said that the virtual chain is the point where students, teachers and the 
resources of a makerspace converge, allowing the enjoyment of a more efficient 
and deeper educational experience. 
 
Similarly, Hutchins' theory underlines how cognition extends beyond individuals, 
encompassing tools, artefacts, and collaboration with others. The Virtual Chain fits 
into these frameworks by functioning as a centralized digital interface for modelling, 
slicing and resource management, thereby enhancing the cognitive and technical 
capabilities of its users. Under this concept the virtual chain of a makerspace 
provides a digital environment where users can collaborate simultaneously, 
leverage integrated tools and access educational resources without unnecessary 
technical distractions. By alleviating the complexity of workflows and easing the 
technological challenges in the system, the model would allow teachers and 
students to focus on the problem-solving and creative aspects of projects. 
 
Taken together, these design theories and methods allow us to perceive the ability 
of virtual chains to transform traditional makerspaces into environments that are 
more accessible, efficient, and user-friendly. This enhances the impact that 
makerspaces also have in educational environments. 
 
 
2.4. Concept of a Virtual Chain in a Makerspace 
 
 
A virtual chain is a digital structure that integrates the tools, resources and 
processes needed to create a cohesive learning experience. This chain connects 
all the necessary elements of a makerspace, allowing users to interact with these 
resources both physically and digitally. 
 
There are different types of makerspaces: physical, hybrid and virtual. 
 
Physical Makerspaces are the traditional ones; located and designed in schools, 
universities, public libraries, community centers where teachers and students have 
physical access to resources and tools.   
 
Physical Makerspaces have to face major limitations. The challenge for many 
institutions who recognize and assume the educational benefits and the basic 
philosophy of self-directed learning inherent in Makerspaces are often challenged 
with their implementation from: limited available space and suitable space, cost to 
renovate and maintain Makerspaces, staffing support and specialty expertise, 
availability hours and security, uncertainty how a Makerspace should be configured 
==PAGE:23==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
21 
and the dimension allocated to serve the programs; if the space should be singular 
disciplinary or multidisciplinary and if so, what disciplines they have to include; how 
do student’s share their ideas and their work and balance between the openness 
of free collaboration and the protection of intellectual property. 
These major limitations lead the institutions to consider implementation of virtual 
or hybrid Makerspaces, which can act in support of the physical Makerspace, and 
even, can help them initially start the process to eliminate the risks of poorly 
designing the physical Makerspace without enough experience of what will work 
for that institution.  
So many institutions are opting for hybrid or virtual spaces to begin this type of 
education or to support the one already started in physical spaces. Virtual platforms 
achieve the goal in a fraction of the time and cost required to establish a physical 
space. It is common for many institutions to start by setting up virtual spaces and 
later when they are functioning, deploy physical spaces. 
 
Virtual Chain is designed for quick and easy deployment. Maintenance is easy and 
can be managed centrally or dispersed to many stakeholders. This enables them 
to evolve and grow based on actual student usage patterns that are often changing 
and unpredictable and they have infinite possibilities to change and adapt to the 
type of student.   
 
The virtual chain of makerspaces is composed of different elements. On the one 
hand, the necessary platforms for resource management, such as platforms that 
manage inventory, equipment maintenance, or material planning. Then there are 
the platforms for collaboration between students and teachers, such as Moodle, 
Google Classroom or Tinkercad. Finally, there are resources that offer project 
databases, digital simulations for students on their work, or tutorials for both 
students and teachers. 
 
Two crucial characteristics of virtual networks are that they bring greater ease and 
accessibility, i.e., from anywhere and at any time users are able to access the 
resources and tools they need (Leminen, 2024). This is essential for educational 
institutions where the space available for a makerspace is not sufficient to 
accommodate all the students needed. This digital format of makerspaces also 
improves the maintenance of resources as users will always have the tools and 
materials updated without wasting time doing it themselves manually. 
 
  
Virtual networks also play a role in promoting teamwork, problem solving and 
creativity as they can provide a platform for students and teachers to collaborate 
with each other and share projects, ideas, and resources (Duytam L, 2023). 
 
==PAGE:24==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace

In short, we can consider the advantages of the virtual makerspace over physical 
spaces as: 
• No space or participant limitations.  
• They eliminate geographical and economic barriers, allowing low-income 
students and creators to access advanced digital tools. 
• Open all day/seven days a week and all year,  
• A staff template that is perfectly acceptable to the institution.  
• Better control over access to all or part of the Makerspace to enable 
outside communities to participate and support students.  
• Allow access and inclusion of different student groups who may struggle 
with access due to location, but also enable the formation of dedicated 
spaces for underrepresented groups.  
• Facilitate networking and the expression of interest and research projects 
more efficiently, allowing students to participate in multiple projects, either 
individually or as part of a team.  
• Provides updated posts on project progress and creates a repository of 
student projects over time, serving to inspire and enable future projects 
and networking with others around the same idea. 
• Monitor attendance and participation by group or student. 
• Makerspaces can be transformed online, created in just a few days, 
modified on the fly, and combined with student events and allowing for live 
interactions. 
• Supports effective communication whether by chat, comment, video call or 
calendar scheduling for in-person meetings. 
• 2023 Ellen MacArthur Foundation report on the circular economy highlights 
that virtual makerspaces can reduce the environmental footprint by 
minimizing the waste of physical materials. 
• Studies from the University of Cambridge concluded that the use of 
simulations in design reduces material waste by 50%–80% compared to 
physical prototypes. 
 
 
Finally, virtual learning chains are more sustainable than traditional learning chains 
as they reduce the need for physical resources and help to manage the use of 
materials more efficiently, thereby creating increasingly sustainable learning 
environments. This is a great advantage, especially in the current global context 
where efficient resource management and sustainability are becoming more and 
more important (Shi, 2022).

2.5. Implementation of Virtual Chains in Makerspaces 
 
There have been several success cases of digital chains in makerspaces, over 
time this type of system gets more implemented. The following are two case 
examples showing the integration of the virtual chain in makerspaces. 
 
The first case that can be consulted is the Innokas Network Project in Finland. 
Despite good results in international comparisons, Finnish universities face 
difficulties in attracting students to STEM disciplines, which impacts the country's 
economic development due to a shortage of technology and ICT experts.  
 
This initiative has been coordinated by the Faculty of Educational Sciences at the 
University of Helsinki to enhance the access to technological resources. Its goal is 
to guide school to innovation through technology, offering training, consulting, and 
events through Finland. The program has included 1000 teachers from 13 
universities to identify challenges, test ideas and finalize solutions. Thanks to this 
project, Finnish schools have learned to combine technology with their curriculum, 
based on collaborative project-based learning. The Innokas network has promoted 
the development of communities where teachers share ideas, experiences, and 
resources to support each other in the learning environment, all of which has 
allowed the quality of Finnish education to increase. Although the benefits of STEM 
activities will be confirmed in the long term, current indicators show a significant 
impact on the education and training of young people and their families. (Myllimaki, 
2021). 
 
Other case of success is the Future Classrooms Program in Spain, this program 
aims to modernize and digitalize the education system. The Future classroom is 
understood as an initiative to redefine educational spaces through technological 
resources and promote a different way of learning. However, to create a classroom 
of the future tailored to the school's reality, it is necessary to start with an analysis 
of the specific characteristics of the school, the available resources, and the 
training of the teachers (Tena & Carrera, 2020). 
 
Digital competency development in education, school digitization, digital resource 
development, and advanced digital methodologies and competencies are all 
included in the program. Thanks to this program, traditional classrooms have been 
transformed into environments where interactive and flexible learning is promoted, 
in which students experiment with technologies such as 3D printing, robotics and 
augmented reality (García-Tudela PA, 2023). 
 
Although the success stories in the digitization of makerspaces continue to grow, 
it should be noted that there are several problems that the education system faces 
in implementing digitization. Technological infrastructure is one of them, for 
example, schools must have the technological facilities to support the use of 
==PAGE:26==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
24 
advanced tools and platforms, this can be complicated by the fact that some 
schools have limited financial resources for technology. Another problem 
encountered is the lack of teacher training to be able to use or make the most of 
these tools. 
 
There is another challenge that is the most significant - resistance to change. There 
are educators accustomed to traditional teaching methods who are reluctant to 
include technology as part of their curriculum or to use it as a tool. In the face of 
this, it is essential to demonstrate the many benefits and possibilities that 
technology can bring to teaching, and to offer support and training to those 
teachers who are struggling with the transition. 
 
While the above initiatives show the potential of makerspaces, the proposed virtual 
chain differentiates itself from them in key respects: 
 
● Unlike Innokas Network, that focuses on general digital education and 
training for teachers, this Virtual Chain Model proposed will be designed for 
makerspaces and their users. It assures that digital tools are not only 
accessible but are integrated into the learning-by-doing process in a 
structured way. 
● The Virtual Chain Model objective is connecting all the elements (design, 
modelling, resource management and collaborative work) into a structured 
digital workflow, differentiating itself from, for example, the Future 
Classroom Program that promotes digital literacy. 
 
 
 
3. Research Methodology 
 
This study follows a qualitative research approach, as it seeks to get a better 
understanding of the needs and challenges faced by schools’ makerspaces. The 
focus on the experiences and perceptions of teachers and pupils allows for an in-
depth exploration of the topic at hand.  
 
To structure the research and design phases, this thesis follows the Design 
Thinking methodology approach. This methodology consists in 5 phases: 
emphasize, define, ideate, prototype and test. This approach is broadly known for 
its people-centered problem-solving orientation (Brown, 2009).  
 
By encouraging iterative experimentation and active collaboration between all 
involved, the design thinking process fosters creativity and innovation. Its emphasis 
on understanding and addressing user needs ensures that the resulting solutions 
==PAGE:27==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
25 
are not only practical, but also meaningful and impactful. As Brown (2009) points 
out, this method has been widely adopted in various fields because of its ability to 
bridge the gap between theoretical concepts and real-world applications, making it 
particularly suitable for projects that aim to integrate user-centered research with 
practical design strategies. 
 
Following the phases of this methodology different tasks have been carried out: 
 
• Empathize: to emphasize with the users to get a deeper and understanding 
of their needs and experiences (Gibbons, 2016), interviews, cases studies 
were used as data collection techniques.  
• Define: through all the information gathered during the previous phase, the 
main problems are determined (Gibbons, 2016). 
• Ideate: several solutions are proposed to solve the problems identified 
during the definition phase (Gibbon, 2016).  
• Prototype: a prototype of the final solution was made in order to test it 
(Gibbons, 2016).  
• Testing: the final solution was evaluated to get hold of errors and to continue 
with an iterative cycle until the design is totally validated (Gibbons, 2016).  
 
There are several benefits that Design Thinking provides: 
 
• User-Centered Solutions: this is because the needs and experiences of the 
users are the core of the whole methodology. By the empathy phase, the 
solutions offered are not only user-centered but also highly effective and 
meaningful (Allabarton, 2024). 
• Better understanding of the users: the methodology helps understand the 
user in depth (Allabarton, 2024). 
• Usability of products: thanks to focusing on the user, testing and iterations 
the product or service designed is accessible, user-friendly, and more 
usable than with other methodologies (Allabarton, 2024) 
• Boosted innovation and creativity: this methodology leads to out-of-the-box 
solutions that more effectively meet the user’s needs (Allabarton, 2024). 
 
 
In conclusion, because of the benefits and the approach that Design Thinking 
provides, this methodology was used. The goal is to design a virtual chain focused 
on the users; this case, teachers and students, as accessible and efficient possible 
by deeply understanding the needs and challenges these users face in school 
makerspaces every day.

7.1. Virtual chain concept in makerspaces  
 
As explained during chapter 2 in section 2.3, a virtual chain is A virtual chain is a 
digital system that connects resources, tools, and users through a virtual network 
(Diaz A., 1996), facilitating interaction and collaboration in remote or hybrid 
environments. 
 
A virtual chain is made up of different components: 
 
• User interface (UI): It provides intuitive and user-friendly access, allowing 
users to have the chain's resources at hand without difficulty. A well-
designed interface is crucial to facilitate interaction and learning in virtual 
environments (Oliver, Kevin & Moore, Robert & Evans, Michael., 2017). 
• Collaboration tools: These platforms allow users to work together in real 
time, sharing ideas and documents (Steyn and Semolic, 2018). 
• Remote access: allows users to interact with the platform from any 
connecting device, this is an essential feature for makerspaces (Orwin, L. 
M. K., 2020) 
• Resource management: allows to organize and store key materials such 
as tutorials, tools and guides in the cloud (Oliver, Kevin & Moore, Robert & 
Evans, Michael., 2017). 
 
Virtual makerspaces in European schools integrate technologies such as 3D 
printers, robotics and collaborative platforms. These tools enable students to 
develop skills such as teamwork and problem-solving, even remotely 
(Makerspaces in Schools: Practical Guidelines For School Leaders And Teachers 
- FCL, n. d.).  
 
A virtual chain within a makerspace refers to the digital tools, platforms and virtual 
processes that complement and optimize the functions of a physical makerspace. 
this includes: 
==PAGE:45==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
43 
• 
Digital design and prototyping tools: CAD software, digital simulators, or the 
platforms necessary to use the physical hardware equipped in the 
makerspace.  
• 
Collaborative platforms: for example, Trello or Google Workspace facilitate 
project management and real-time collaboration, so users can work as a 
team even remotely (AR Makerspace Collaborations, n.d.). 
• Educational resources: online tutorials, digital guides, databases, and 
materials... 
• Platforms to manage projects and organize ideas. 
 
These virtual chains extend the reach of makerspaces, making them more 
inclusive and accessible, as they allow users with different levels of experience and 
knowledge to actively participate in learning and creation. 
 
 
7.2. Implementations of a virtual chain 
 
As mentioned in the previous section and decided in chapter 6, redesigning the 
virtual chain of a makerspace to make it easier to access and use will improve the 
accessibility of makerspaces and address the challenges mentioned in chapter 5. 
 
The virtual chain as explained in the previous section is the virtual system that 
includes the platforms, digital tools and management tools that complement the full 
functionality of the activities of a makerspace. 
 
There are three options when implementing a virtual chain in a makerspace: local 
installation, virtual machine, and cloud-based platform. 
 
Local Installation  
 
This method of implementation consists of installing and hosting the chain on the 
school's physical servers. Although it is limited to the local environment, it is still a 
virtual network because: 
 
• Resources are managed within a virtual system on local servers.  
• Users avoid having to manage different physical resources as they interact 
with the system through a digital interface. 
• It is not accessible remotely, but users can interact with the platforms 
through the school's internal networks. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:46==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
44 
Virtual machine 
 
This type of implementation consists of the creation of a virtual environment hosted 
on a server (local or remote). This pre-configured environment allows schools to 
customize their virtual chain according to their needs, and users can access it from 
any device connected to the network. 
 
Cloud-based Platform 
 
This type of implementation is based on the use of cloud-based platforms to host 
the virtual chain. It offers the greatest scalability, as it allows multiple institutions to 
access and share resources without the need for local physical infrastructure.

A virtual machine (VM) is a software environment that emulates a complete 
computer system, allowing you to run an operating system and applications as if 
they were on a physical computer (Microsoft Azure, n.d.). In essence, it is a 
Image 5. Implementation comparison

“computer within a computer”, using physical hardware resources (such as CPU, 
memory, and storage) to create an independent virtual environment. The main 
features of a virtual machine are:  
 
• Independence: each machine operates in isolation, even if it shares the 
same physical hardware with other VMs. 
• Flexibility: it allows running different operating systems on the same physical 
device. 
• Portability: virtual machines can be easily moved between different servers 
or devices. 
• Security: VMs are ideal for testing software or handling potentially 
dangerous data without affecting the host system. 
• Scalability: virtual machines can be cloned as many times as needed.

7.5.1. Identified errors 
 
During the development of the initial prototype, a configuration error was detected.  
 
Because the virtual machine was configured in Microsoft Azure thanks to a 
sponsorship for startups, it did not allow to configure a machine with more specific 
features necessary for the needs of a makerspace. The virtual machine lacks a 
graphics card, which is essential for the use of the modeling and rendering 
programs needed to perform the functions of a makerspace. Because of this the 
Bambu Studio slicer program has not been tested to work. 
 
Due to this latter fact, it has not been possible to check if the 3D printing machines 
can be connected to the virtual machine and if they work correctly. 
 
 
7.5.2. Key challenges 
 
During the configuration of the virtual machine that were identified which require 
further analysis and iterations in the following prototypes. 
 
Differentiated access according to operating system 
 
Once the machine was configured when the remote access tests were carried out, 
it was found that depending on the operating system of the computer used to 
access it, the login is different. 
 
If you want to access from a Windows device, access is easy with the RDP file as 
explained in section 7.4.3 but if you access from an iOS device access requires 
the Bastion port needed for these devices due to security issues. 
 
This could complicate the user experience, especially if users are unfamiliar with 
the functionality of both methods. 
 
Simultaneous connection 
 
When testing the machine by creating different users, it was found that it is not 
possible to access the virtual machine simultaneously by several users. This is a 
challenge in relation to accessibility as it prevents collaboration between users. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:70==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace 
68 
Online access  
 
 
The virtual machine of the initial prototype is an online machine, i.e. it needs a 
stable internet connection to be able to use it efficiently, as users without internet 
cannot access the VM or work on it. 
 
This internet connectivity can be a challenge as in educational environments 
connectivity is not always stable or reliable, and therefore this could affect the user 
experience and accessibility. 
 
 
7.5.3. What VM improves 
 
It is essential to compare what improvements the virtual machine brings compared 
to the traditional makerspace chain. 
 
There are some features that the model proposed has succeeded on covering such 
as:  
 
• Centralized access: the VM provides a centralized system where users find 
everything they need already configured and installed, this avoids the need 
for users to get involved in complicated installations that some software 
require. This enables both teachers and students to have direct access to 
software such as Tinkercad and educational materials. 
• Scalability: its modular feature provides the opportunity to expand the model 
to cover the complete workflow of a makerspace and allows the expansion 
of the virtual chain depending on the school’s needs. The VM can be cloned 
easily so it is a better solution than configuring computers one by one like in 
the traditional chain of makerspaces. 
• Reduces 
dependency 
on 
technological 
infrastructure: 
traditional 
makerspaces require computers to be configured locally, this limits its 
implementation in schools with low technological resources. VMs can be run 
in hardware with standard specifications so they guarantee compatibility 
with any equipment of less capacity.  
 
Aspects that remain unchanged with the virtual machine 
 
• Even though the VM allows pre-configured software, in this case in this case 
as Tinkercad would be used it does not mean a change as this platform 
requires a login to use it. This process would be the same if the user uses 
this tool on his own computer. 
• In both cases, an internet connection is required to use the program or 
download updates, both in the traditional chain and in the VM. This 
==PAGE:71==
Modelling the digital value chain for an educational makerspace

dependency in connectivity is a crucial aspect when comparing it to the 
traditional chain. 
 
Aspects in which a traditional chain is better 
 
• In a traditional chain in a makerspace everyone uses their own computer 
simultaneously, this is a limitation that has been observed in the proposed 
VM as it allows hosting different users, but they cannot use the same virtual 
machine at the same time.  
 
 
7.5.4. Proposals for improvement 
 
Once these challenges and errors have been identified, the following actions are 
proposed to be carried out in the following iterations to improve the prototype. 
 
 
Configuration error with Microsoft Azure 
 
To solve the virtual machine bug regarding the inability to use rendering programs, 
the following should be done: 
 
• Consider and investigate other virtual machine platforms, such as VMware 
or Google Cloud, that allow the inclusion of specific graphics card features 
or GPU support. 
• Integrate a virtual GPU into the already configured virtual machine, such as 
NVIDIA, but bear in mind that this upgrade is a financial investment. 
 
 
Differentiated access according to operating system 
 
The challenge of users having to log in to the VM depending on their computer 
could be improved in the following way: 
 
• Research the features of AnyDesk or Teamviewer that allow a universal 
solution for remote access. 
• Create detailed tutorials to guide the user according to the operating system 
they use to facilitate access. 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:72==

Simultaneous connection 
 
As mentioned in section 7.5.2 the impossibility of simultaneous connection 
prevents collaboration between users, there are certain measures that need to be 
taken to face this challenge: 
 
• Investigate the possibility of setting up a more advanced type of virtual 
machine that does allow simultaneous connections. 
• Consider the possibility of creating different virtual machines within the 
same makerspace with the same functionalities so that users can work in 
parallel.   
 
 
Online access  
 
The configuration of a virtual machine that requires a stable connection to the 
internet can cause problems in the accessibility of the makerspace, for which the 
following solutions are proposed: 
 
• Design a VM that doesn't strictly need internet access, that syncs when you 
have internet available so you can store things in the cloud. 
• Consider the possibility of using programs that do not need internet access, 
as for example Tinkercad is web-based and cannot be used without internet. 
• Try other possibilities such as the use of VPNs for greater connection 
stability in schools.

8. Testing 
 
Although during the development of the initial prototype it has been possible to 
identify initial errors and challenges, due to time constraints, it has not been 
possible to carry out extensive testing with teachers and students in the school 
makerspaces. 
 
However, according to the theoretical framework of Design Thinking (Brown, 2019) 
the last phase of the methodology is the testing of the solution developed during 
the other phases (Dam, R. F., 2025). This phase is used to evaluate the 
functionality and accessibility of the solution developed in previous phases.  
 
Next, the tests that have been devised to be able to examine the extent to which 
the virtual machine can improve accessibility in educational spaces are then ideally 
proposed. 
 
 
8.1. Testing Objectives 
 
The objectives of these tests must be established in order to have a clear vision of 
the results to be obtained. 
 
• Evaluate whether the proposed solution improves accessibility within the 
makerspace, assessing to what extent it solves the problems mentioned 
during chapter 4. 
• Obtain direct feedback from makerspace users, both teachers and students, 
on what aspects need to be improved in terms of functionality or 
accessibility. 
• Evaluate the technical aspects that characterize the proposed solution, the 
virtual machine, in terms of remote accessibility, compatibility with the tools 
selected for the makerspace and system performance.

8.2. Planned testing for future iterations 
 
In order to achieve the testing objectives (chapter 8.1) it is necessary to carry out 
a series of tests that provide us with the type of information we need. These tests 
are the most important since this entire solution has been developed on the basis 
of improving the accessibility of school makerspaces.  
 
User experience tests 
 
The following tests will help us evaluate the extent to which the developed solution 
improves accessibility in terms of user experience. 
• Test the ease of use and access by end users, these users should have 
different levels of technical knowledge. 
• Observe the time it takes the user to log in and start using the offered tools. 
• Check that the workflow proposed for the users is the most appropriate. 
• Evaluate whether the instructions and educational resources are 
appropriate for users to easily use the platforms. 
 
During these tests it is essential to receive feedback from users on the virtual chain 
developed for them, collect opinions, listen to the challenges they faced during use 
and consider their proposals for improvement. This evaluation is crucial within the 
Design Thinking methodology that advocates design towards the user by 
empathizing with the user (Brown, 2019). 
 
 
Functionality tests  
 
It is very important to evaluate at a technical level the functions that the virtual 
machine provides, a good functioning of the virtual machine will contribute to the 
improvement of the accessibility and the easy use of the virtual chain. 
 
• Verify and evaluate the compatibility of the virtual machine with the different 
operating systems. 
• Verify that the devices in the computer the virtual chain is opened work 
properly when connected. 
• Test compatibility and functionality with 3D printers or equipment available 
in the makerspace.

• Simulate network outages to evaluate how the machine behaves and 
whether users can easily reconnect to the VM. 
Simultaneous testing 
 
One of the challenges in the development of the first prototype (chapter 7.5.2) is

• Examine whether the system prioritizes resource sharing fairly.

focused on validating the functionality and accessibility of the prototype, however 
due to time constraints it was only possible to propose the necessary tests to 
iteratively advance the development and implementation of the solution proposed 
in chapter 7. Despite these, the tests planned to evaluate accessibility, user 
experience, functionality and connectivity offer a clear view on the areas where 
improvements are needed in future iterations. 
 
The analysis of the challenges and bugs identified during the prototyping phase 
(chapter 7) was key to come up with effective testing strategies. Among the most 
important aspects to be evaluated in future developments, the following stand out: 
 
1. Optimization of the simultaneity of users.  
2. Hybrid configuration for offline accessibility.  
3. Improved remote access and multiplatform compatibility.  
4. The configuration of virtual machines that meet the requirements of a 
makerspace in terms of modeling and printing.  
 
Therefore, although this chapter could not be completed as a practical phase in the

testing phase is indispensable to ensure that the virtual machine prototype meets 
the needs of the users and the goals of the school makerspace.

9.  Discussion and Analysis 
 
This discussion and analysis chapter aims to reflect on the results obtained 
throughout the development of the initial prototype and how these results align with 
the objectives that were set out at the beginning of this project. It seeks to identify 
the aspects that were successful, the challenges that were encountered and the 
proposals for improvement. It also looks at how the Design Thinking methodology 
has benefited or limited this project. 
 
 
9.1. Reflection on the obtained results 
 
Thanks to the development of the initial prototype, it has been possible to identify 
aspects that need to be further developed to ensure that the virtual chain proposed 
for the makerspace makes it a more accessible and functional space for teachers 
and students.  
 
Although, significant advances have been made, such as the configuration of 
modelling tools on the machine and the inclusion of educational resources, due to 
time constraints the following iterations of the prototype and the testing phase could 
not be carried out, the analysis and evaluation of the first prototype has allowed us 
to identify what technical aspects are strictly necessary, what challenge the virtual 
machine that was developed may pose in relation to accessibility and the 
limitations that this system entails. 
 
For example, it was discovered that the operation of the machine would be 
compromised without a graphics card, as the tools implemented in a makerspace 
depend on it, especially the rendering, modelling and printing tools, and that it is 
necessary to ensure a stable internet connection, otherwise the accessibility of the 
users to this virtual space would be affected. These results build a good basis on 
which to develop the following iterations.  
 
 
9.2. Comparison with initial objectives 
 
Since the beginning of the project, the main objective has been to improve the 
accessibility of makerspaces through the creation of a more user-friendly virtual 
chain. Although significant progress was made in this objective through the 
creation of a virtual machine and its respective first functional prototype, such as 
such as the configuration of modelling tools on the machine or educational 
resources, there are objectives that are still pending due to temporary and technical 
inconveniences.

For example, although adequate remote access to the virtual machine was 
developed, it was not possible to solve the challenge of not being able to use the 
virtual machine simultaneously. Also, the connectivity dependency of this prototype 
remains a problem to be addressed in future iterations.  
 
 
9.3. Advantages and limitations of the use of Design Thinking 
in this project 
 
The Design Thinking methodology used in the development of this project has 
proved to be very effective, as it allowed us to focus the project on the needs of the 
users and empathize with their problems in order to devise solutions focused on 
them.  
 
The initial phases of Empathy and Definition allowed us to identify and understand 
in depth the key problems that today's makerspaces suffer from related to the 
accessibility and functionality of school makerspaces. Thanks to the analysis of 
these problems, it was possible to propose solutions focused on the users and then 
develop the solution based on the advantages it could bring to improve 
accessibility.  
 
However, the iterative process that characterizes this method was limited by the 
time available, which prevented a practical testing phase from being carried out. 
This highlights how important it is to determine more extensive planning in order to 
complete all phases of the design process and validate the proposed solutions.

Through this analysis and final discussion, the impact of the identification of the 
problems that makerspaces face today, the proposal of a series of solutions that 
could be implemented to improve accessibility in educational environments and the 
development of the initial prototype where the first problems of functionality and 
accessibility were identified, which involved the solution of a virtual machine as the 
center of a more user-friendly virtual chain, has become evident. But these results 
serve as a starting point for further iterations in the future.  
 
Analyzing these results with the initial objectives set out at the beginning of the 
project, it becomes clear how important it is to prioritize the resolution of certain 
aspects such as the need for simultaneity in the same virtual space, the need for a 
suitable GPU to be able to use the tools or the stability of connection on the 
devices.

10. Conclusion 
 
 
10.1. Summary of key learning 
 
As this project has developed, a number of key learnings have emerged that have 
contributed significantly to understanding and improving accessibility in school 
makerspaces. Among the lessons learned are the following: 
 
Identification of key problems 
 
During the empathy phase, the challenges that makerspaces face today were 
discovered, such as the lack of training of teachers to use these spaces, the 
limitation of technological resources or the difficulty for teachers to integrate this 
tool as part of their curriculum, all of which prevents these makerspaces from being 
used to their full potential. 
 
Proposal of innovative solutions 
 
The ideation process allowed the generation of different ideas to improve these 
problems that limit the accessibility of makerspaces. Thanks to the impact-effort 
and dot thinking techniques, the most appropriate proposal was selected: the 
development of a more user-friendly virtual chain.  
 
Development of the initial prototype 
 
After evaluating different possibilities of virtual chains, the advantages that a virtual 
chain based on a virtual machine can offer were discovered. During the prototyping 
phase, a basic prototype of this machine was developed, identifying the bugs and 
challenges in the solution of improving accessibility through a virtual machine.

Importance of Iteration 
 
The Design Thinking methodology highlighted the importance of continuous 
iteration to improve the design and functionality of the prototype. Although time 
constraints prevented extensive testing, a solid foundation for future improvements 
was established. 
 
 
10.2.  Implications and Future Recommendations 
 
The implications of the results obtained after the development of this project for the 
field of study and the applied context are significant. This project demonstrates that 
the implementation and improvement of virtual chains around accessibility can 
transform today's educational makerspaces, making them more efficient and 
inclusive spaces. 
 
The following are some recommendations for future research and projects: 
 
• Extensive testing: extensive end-user testing with both teachers and 
students is vital to the success of proposals such as these in order to 
validate and evaluate the functionality and accessibility of the proposed 
virtual chain. this should include testing in different domains, user 
experience evaluations, technical functionality testing and connectivity 
testing.  
• Investigation of more advanced virtual machines: there is a need to 
investigate in more detail the possibilities and opportunities that virtual 
machines can offer, especially those that allow simultaneous connection of 
users and those that can work without the need for a constant internet 
connection. This would enhance collaboration and inclusion in school 
makerspaces.  
• Development of educational resources: although general guides and video 
tutorials were included in the initial prototype, the development of self-
developed educational resources is essential, ensuring that teachers have 
all the tools they need to make the most of the opportunities that a 
makerspace can provide in their curriculum.

Welcome to the Virtual Makerspace! 
Dear Educator, 
Welcome to our Virtual Makerspace! This space has been designed to support you in exploring 
and integrating 3D design and digital fabrication into your classroom. Whether you're a 
complete beginner or have some experience, this resource hub will guide you step by step. 
What You’ll Find Here 
Inside this folder, you will find: 
• 
Tutorial Videos: Step-by-step guides on how to use Tinkercad, from basic shapes to more 
complex designs.  
• 
Introduction to 3D Printing: Essential tips on how to operate a 3D printer and prepare your 
designs for printing.  
• 
Tinkercad Shortcut: A direct link to Tinkercad, an easy-to-use online 3D modeling tool. 
In your desktop you will find Bambu Studio so you can start preparing your files whenever you 
like! 
 
Getting Started 
1. Watch the First Tutorials: Begin with the “Getting Started with Tinkercad” and “Basic 
concepts of Tinkercad” video to understand the basics of 3D modeling, posted in the “First 
Steps”.  
2. Explore and Experiment: Try creating simple shapes in Tinkercad before moving on to more 
advanced models.  
3. Learn About 3D Printing: If you are new to 3D printing, check out the introduction videos, 
with the “Getting Started with 3D printing” to understand how printers work and how to 
prepare files for printing. 
4. Learn how to prepare your files for printing: if you need some help figurín out how to use 
the slicer, check out our videos on “How to use Bambu Studio” 
5. Log into your Google account provided for the makerspaces so you have everything save 
and sound.
