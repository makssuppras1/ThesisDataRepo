Abstract
The ﬁeld of plasmonics is very versatile and structures with highly tunable optical properties
can now be realized. As the limits of fabricational methods are continually being pushed, the
demand for a deeper understanding of the quantum mechanical eﬀects in true nanometer sized
objects is increasing. Many novel experimental setups have been used to investigate this regime,
but as of yet no clear answer has been given towards the question of which quantum mechanical
eﬀects dominate the optical response of these nanostructures.
The aims of this study is to fabricate a novel system that combines two eﬀects: The large
dependence of the optical response of dimer structures on the interdimer distance and the
vibrations of a micromechanical resonator.
With the dimers situated on the resonator, the
vibrations will modulate the optical response of the coupled plasmonic structures. This ability
to modulate the response of the system for diﬀerent dimer structures in close proximity could
open new ways to probe the limits beyond classical electromagnetism and provide valuable
insight into the realm of quantum eﬀects.
A scheme is presented for high-yield, reproducible fabrication of Si3N4 micromechanical strings
with gold dimers situated on them using focused ion beam milling. The strings are 14.6 µm long
and have an average width of 247 nm with an achieved average gap size between the dimers of
118 nm. The dimers themselves have a deviation in their dimensions of less than 20 nm (∼10 %).
The modulation of the optical response is sought experimentally proven by thoroughly cali-
brating and benchmarking the optical system used. This process is successfully concluded by
measuring the dark ﬁeld scattering spectra from gold nanoparticles and comparing these to nu-
merical simulations. No conclusions are drawn as to the presence of optomechanical coupling of
the fabricated structures since the obtained spectra can not be unambigously separated from the
noise. The source of this noise is identiﬁed as proximity scattering and reﬂection from nearby
structures and a scheme to remove this noise is proposed.
In the end suggestions for improving the fabrication method and future work is given.

Resumé
Studiet af plasmoner er et alsidigt felt og strukturer med yderst modulerbare optiske egen-
skaber kan nu realiseres.
Da grænserne for fremstillingsmetoderne hele tiden ﬂyttes, stiger
nødvendigheden af en dybere forståelse af de kvantemekaniske eﬀekter i objekter på den sande
nanometerskala. Mange nye eksperimentelle metoder er blevet brugt for at undersøge dette
regime, men der er endnu ikke noget klart svar på spørgsmålet om, hvilke kvantemekaniske
eﬀekter der dominerer i disse nanostrukturer.
Målet med dette studie er at fabrikere et nyt system, der kombinerer to eﬀekter: Den store
afhængighed af den optiske respons af dimer-strukturer på den indbyrdes afstand og vibra-
tionerne af en mikromekanisk resonator. Med dimerne placeret på denne resonator vil vibra-
tionerne modulere den optiske respons af den koblede, plasmoniske struktur. Denne evne til
at modulere systemets respons for forskellige dimer-strukturer med små indbyrdes afstande kan
åbne for nye måder at probe grænserne ud over den klassiske elektromagnetisme og give værdi-
fuld indsigt i kvanteeﬀekternes verden.
En reproducérbar måde at fremstille mikromekaniske Si3N4-strenge med guld dimere på ved
bruge fokuseret ion-stråle fræsning med højt udbytte præsenteres.
Disse strenge er 14.6 µm
lange og har en gennemsnitlig bredde på 247 nm med en opnået gennemsnitlig afstand mellem
dimerne på 118 nm. Dimerne selv har en afvigelse i deres dimensioner på mindre end 20 nm
(∼10 %).
Modulationen af den optiske respons forsøges eksperimentelt eftervist ved tilbundsgående kali-
brering og benchmarking af det benyttede optiske system.
Denne proces bliver succesfuldt
tilendebragt ved at måle dark ﬁeld spredningsspektrene fra guld nanopartikler og sammenligne
dem med numeriske simuleringer. Ingen konklusioner bliver draget vedrørende tilstedeværelsen
af optomekanisk kobling i de fabrikerede strukturer, da de målte spektre ikke entydigt kan ad-
skilles fra baggrundsstøjen. Kilden til denne støj bliver identiﬁceret som spredning og reﬂektion
af lys fra nærliggende strukturer og en plan for at fjerne denne støj fremlægges.
Slutteligt præsenteres ideer til forbedring af fabrikationsmetoden samt forslag til fremtidigt
arbejde.

Introduction
The unique optical properties of metal nanoparticles have long been used to change the behaviour
of macroscopic objects. One of the earliest and most famous examples is the Lycurgus Cup from
the 4th century A.D. where the presence of minute concentrations of silver and gold nanoparticles
in the glass change the color of the cup from red when light is transmitted through to green/blue
when viewed in reﬂection. The beautiful stained glass mosaics in grand ediﬁces around the world
also owe their appearance to the presence of metal nanoparticles.
These, albeit impressive,
achievements were not based on an understanding of the fundamental properties of the light-
matter interaction, but rather a series of trial and error experiments through many generations
of master craftsmen.
Today we know the strong optical response from e.g. a metallic nanostructure is caused by the
joint behaviour of the free electron gas in the material. The collective oscillation known as a
plasmon was ﬁrst proposed by Pines and Bohm in 1952 [1]. The interesting optical properties of
spherical metallic nanoparticles was actually described over a hundred years ago [2, 3] although
the nature of the localized surface plasmon was unknown at that time. Likewise, the strong
conﬁnement and guiding of electromagnetic waves along the surface of a conductor has been
known since the turn of the 20th century when Sommerfeld studied radio waves propagating near
an inﬁnite wire [4]. The excitation of these guided surface waves was realized experimentally
in the optical regime in 1968 by Kretschmann et al. [5] using prism coupling, and a uniﬁed
description of these phenomena known as surface plasmon polaritons, i.e. a polarized charge
wave coupled with the photon ﬁeld, was established.
The ﬁeld of plasmonics has seen an ever increasing interest spurred along by the advances in
micro- and nanoscale fabrication since then. This has presented the possibility to create large
ﬁeld enhancements between dimers in close proximity [6] and to tune the optical response of
nanostructures [7].
The developments have ensured that plasmonics has found use in many diverse areas such
as biomedical sensing using surface enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) [8], with a sensitivity
even reaching the single molecule limit [9]. The eﬀect has also been used in reverse where the

spatial origin of the SERS signal was used to directly probe the electric ﬁeld enhancement in
nanostructures [10]. Other such areas include cancer treatment [11], increasing the eﬀectiveness
of photodetectors [12], making optical circuitry [13], and for colour printing with a previously
unreachable resolution [14].
Recently, the plasmonic properties of metallic nanoparticles and thin ﬁlms have been utilized to
transduce optical energy to mechanical vibrations of a resonator [15], and high optomechanical
coupling strengths have been achieved for such systems [16]. The photothermal tuning of the
resonance frequency of such a resonator has also been used for ultra-sensitive mass as well as
species detection [17].
The aim of this work is to utilize this transduction to continuously modulate the optical response
of a dimer structure, which is situated on mechanical resonators in the form of strings cut from
an Si3N4 membrane. The goal is to fabricate structures where the average distance between
the dimer structures can be reduced to the regime where quantum eﬀects start to appear in the
optical response. This response is strongly dependent on the interdimer distance, and combined
with the mechanical vibrations of the structures the response will be heavily modulated and can
shed more light on which quantum mechanical process (and in which regime) is the dominant:
Quantum tunneling of electrons or non-locality of the electron gas. Very recent studies suggest
that neither of the two competing theories alone can explain the observed phenomena [18].
As such new methods are required to fully understand and engineer the optical properties on
the true nanoscale. Changes in the vibrational frequency could potentially give information on
the electrostatic forces between the dimers and give insight into the charge equalization from
tunneling currents, besides the information available from the optical properties.
This thesis is divided into ﬁve chapters:
Chapter 1 Here the fundamental theory about plasmonic excitation and the foundations of
Mie theory is presented along with the theory of coupled plasmonic structures in order to
interpret experimentally obtained spectra. Following this is a derivation of the mechanical
vibrations of beams and ﬁnally a description of the state of the art in the ﬁeld of quantum
plasmonics.
Chapter 2 The experimental methods both with the scanning electron and optical microscopes
used are explained here as well as the post-processing performed on experimental spectra.
An account of the computational methods in the form of COMSOL simulations is also
given and the parameters used are presented.
Chapter 3 All the steps taken to prepare the investigated samples for optical measurements
are presented. The samples used to benchmark the optical system are single Au nanopar-
ticles on Boroﬂoat glass, while an account is given of the design and fabrication of the
optomechanical systems to be studied.
Chapter 4 The experimental results from the benchmarking of the optical setup using single
Au nanoparticles as well as the results from measurements on the optomechanical string
structure fabricated are presented here.
Chapter 5 Conclusions and outlook for future work.

Chapter 1
Theory
In this chapter the theoretical groundwork will be laid to interpret experimental optical spectra.
First, a brief introduction will be given to the ﬁeld of plasmonics followed by a description
of the basic plasmonic resonance of a small sphere in the electrostatic approximation. Next,
an introduction to the exact results for a scattering sphere in the framework of Mie theory is
given as an outlook beyond the electrostatic approximation. A derivation of the optomechanical
coupling strength between two plasmonic nanoparticles is made thereafter. Following this is
the theory of doubly-clamped vibrating beams and the frequencies and maximum deﬂection
associated with the thermally induced vibrations. Finally, the recent developments in the ﬁeld
of quantum plasmonics is reviewed to give an understanding of the regime beyond the classical
treatments presented in the previous sections.
1.1
Fundamentals of plasmons
A plasmon is a collective excitation of the free electrons in a material (most commonly a metal
although heavily doped semiconductors are also known to support plasmon modes). For increas-
ing frequencies the material becomes increasingly transparent until the electron cloud simply
oscillates with the electric ﬁeld as a bulk plasmon; the transitional frequency between the reﬂec-
tive and transparent regime is known as the plasma frequency. On the interface between two
materials with a sign diﬀerence in their respective dielectric constants, e.g. a metal and a di-
electric, another type of plasmon known as a surface plasmon polariton (SPP) strongly conﬁned
to the surface of the metal can be excited, see Figure 1.1. This excitation is a coupling between
the incident excitation ﬁeld and the charge oscillations on the surface of the metal. However,
these modes can be diﬃcult to excite since there is a large momentum mismatch due to the
diﬀerent dispersion relations of the SPP’s and the ﬁeld in the dielectric; special techniques such
as coupling through a grating or a prism has to be employed to eﬀectively excite the SPP’s (see
e.g. [19]).

1.2. PLASMONIC RESONANCE
If the physical dimensions of the metal structure is reduced well below the wavelength of the
incident radiation a special type of SP can be supported namely the localized surface plasmon

of a constant electric ﬁeld and as such no special techniques are required to excite the LSP’s.

1.3
Mie theory
One of the most important exact solutions in the theory of absorption and scattering of light is
that for a spherical particle of arbitrary size and dielectric function. The result was ﬁrst pub-
lished by Lorenz in 1890 [2]. However, his results did not receive widespread recognition partly
because they were published in Danish, but also because they were not founded in Maxwell’s
equations but rather in a mechanical viewpoint relying on the presence of ether. The more
well-known treatment was published by Gustav Mie in 1908 [3] giving the theory its name.

isotropic medium can be written as

1.6
Quantum plasmonics
So far all derivations done in this work have only considered classical eﬀects. Indeed, for par-
ticles or structures larger than ∼10 nm the local Drude response model provides an adequate
description for most metals [19]. However, if there is a characteristic length scale a (such as
nanoparticle radius or dimer gap) under this size limit the quantum nature of the electron gas
can no longer be ignored [33].
As the fabrication methods are constantly improving, the experimental probing of dimer struc-
tures with subnanometer gaps has been realized in several diﬀerent ways: Using spherical Au
nanoparticles to form a nanocavity between two atomic force microscope tips [34], and in scan-
ning tunneling electron microscopes using electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) measure-
ments on Ag nanoparticles [35]. Several methods have also been devised to separate Au nanopar-
ticles from an underlying Au substrate (the nanoparticle on mirror conﬁguration): Utilizing the
two-dimensional material graphene [36], monolayers of molecules [37], and self-assembled alka-
nadithiols [38].
In a purely classical framework the optical response of the usual bonding dipolar and quadrupo-
lar plasmon (BDP and BQP) as well as higher-order modes redshift and diverge as the gap size
decreases, as predicted by theory in Figure 1.8a. These trends are also observed in experiments
for gap sizes down to approximately 0.5 nm [35]. However, a decrease in the strength accom-
panied by a blueshift of the BDP and BQP is observed when gap sizes are reduced below this
limit in experiments, a trend not captured by the classical calculations. Including quantum me-
chanical eﬀects through time dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) the calculation now

tomatically incorporated, and these computationally heavy methods do seem to capture the
non-classical eﬀects of the plasmonic response [40]. This incorporation presents a problem inas-
much as to pinpoint the exact nature of the observed response, and calculations based on ﬁrst
principles are not suited for describing large plasmonic systems. In an eﬀort to compensate for
this, the quantum corrected model (QCM) was developed by Esteban et al. [39]. Here, the space
between the dimers is described with an eﬀective dielectric constant based on DFT calculations
of the overlap of the electronic wavefunction in the gap to include the eﬀects of quantum tun-
neling. The QCM shows good agreement with quantum mechanical calculations and has been
able to reproduce the experimental observations in the optical spectra [34, 35], as well as the
change in the enhancement factor in SERS [41].
But the QCM is not the only competitor to explain these results. It has been argued that the
physical basis of the QCM is incorrect [42] as it allows for dissipation in the tunneling gap, a
process that is widely accepted as being elastic [43]. Furthermore, it is questioned whether the
time-scale for tunneling currents is relevant in optical experiments. Earlier work has reported
tunneling time scales in the picosecond range [44] and with ﬁeld switching on the femtosecond
scale quantum tunneling currents can be a too slow mechanism to cancel the charge buildup.
As such, recent years have seen an increase in the support for the generalized non-local optical
response (GNOR) model. Here, non-locality can be introduced both as a phenomological extra
contribution to the usual delta-function response of the dielectric function [33], but also founded
in a derivation in the framework of the hydrodynamic model. A Navier-Stokes-like equation is
used to account for the quantum pressure exerted by the electrons on each other in regions of
high charge density which sheds additional light on the strength and dependence on physical
parameters [42] (for a thorough treatment of the GNOR, see the review by Raza et al. [45]
and references therein). The GNOR model has shown good agreement with the observation
of multipole plasmons in small (20 nm > a > 4 nm) Ag nanoparticles and the disappearance
of these modes for even smaller particles [46]. The observed blueshift in EELS measurements
of single Ag nanoparticles is also in agreement with the model [47, 48], a blueshift which has
also been observed in traditional optical spectroscopy of various noble metallic nanoparticles
[49]. The GNOR model tends to underestimate the magnitude of this shift, however, and other
explanations such as electron spill-out [50, 51] and changes in the electronic band structure [52]
have been proposed.
Both theories show promising results at the moment, but more investigation is required to
determine which of the two is the governing mechanism in nanoscale systems. Recent work has
indeed suggested that neither method completely captures the eﬀects when gap distances are
reduced to the single digit Ångstrom regime [18].

2.1
Scanning electron microscopy
2.1.1
Focused ion beam
FIB is a versatile technique that can be used to deposit, ablate and/or analyze material at
speciﬁc sites on a sample. It is done by accelerating ions from a source towards a target where
the high kinetic energy of the ions can either break atomic bonds of a chemisorbed precursor
(deposition) or the substrate (milling). The ablated atoms can then be analyzed with e.g. a
mass spectrometer to investigate the composition of the sample.
A FIB system is typically implemented by placing an ion gun at an angle θ in a scanning electron
microscope (SEM) alongside the electron beam, see Figure 2.1. The ions can, like the electrons,
be used for imaging, but the much heavier ions damage the sample with each scan. Furthermore,
the resolution is lower than with the electrons for two reasons both related to the much higher
mass of the ions:
1. The ions penetrate much further into the sample thus generating electrons from a larger
area of the sample.
2. The ions are harder to focus than the electrons and thus the minimum achievable spot
size is considerably larger.

2.3.1
COMSOL and the ﬁnite element method
COMSOL Multiphysics (henceforth COMSOL) is a commercially available software package
that utilizes the FEM to solve problems numerically in such diverse ﬁelds as electrochemistry,
heat transfer, structural mechanics and optics (among others). COMSOL v. 5.1 was used for
all the simulations done in this work.
In the FEM the geometry under investigation is discretized into a ﬁnite set of 2D or 3D elements
(hence the name) known as the mesh. At the corners of these mesh elements are points known
as nodes, which are all the points at which the solution to the given problem is calculated.
Knowing the values of physical properties such as the electric ﬁeld or the temperature at these
nodes thus opens for the evaluation at any given point using interpolation. As is clear from

this description the meshing of the geometry is crucial. When e.g. simulating structures in
the framework of classical electromagnetics, Maxwell’s equations are directly solved with the
appropriate boundary conditions and relevant dielectric functions, and as such the method is
accurate with suﬃciently ﬁne meshing. A rule of thumb from COMSOL says the number of mesh
points N needed when doing electromagnetic simulations is N = λ/5 as a compromise between
computational cost and numerical accuracy [55]. However, for regions with a strongly varying
ﬁeld (such as the gap between two metallic dimers) ﬁner meshing may be needed otherwise large
numerical discrepancies can arise.

The solver used was MUMPS, a direct solver using matrix inversion to solve Maxwell’s equations.
MUMPS is fast but the memory requirement is very high due to the solution approach. An

points (e.g. the touching point between sphere and substrate), and also by changing the distance
between the sphere and the substrate (in both the ±z direction) but only minor improvements
were gained towards solution convergence. As such, MUMPS was used for all calculations at
the cost of numerical accuracy.

Chapter 3
Sample preparation
Contained in this chapter is a description of the steps taken to prepare the samples used in
this work. First is the process of preparing the single Au nanoparticles used to benchmark the
optical system. Following this is a description of the process ﬂow used to fabricate the wafers
containing the optomechanical chips, the considerations on the design and speciﬁc geometry of
the individual chips and the process of drawing and specifying the mask design. The chapter
is completed by accounting for the considerations and steps used when milling the dimers on
beams from the Si3N4 membranes as well as the results obtained.
3.1
Single particles
The single Au nanoparticles used in this work were commercially available oﬀ-the-shelf products
from BBI Solutions with diameters 100 nm and 150 nm in a solution. To corroborate optical
measurements on single particles with measurements of their size, a substrate was ordered
from DTU DanChip in Boroﬂoat glass with Au markings in a square pattern on top cut into
1.5×1.5 cm2 pieces. The wafer was coated with resist prior to cutting to protect it from damage
and stray particles. Figure 3.1 shows one of the markings, from which it is seen the markings
themselves measure 80 × 60 µm2; the center spacing is 500 µm in each direction. The numbering
starts in the center of the wafer, and the two dots indicate the direction of increasing numbers
and thus from which part of the wafer the piece originates; in this case the top right.
The transfer of particles was done by ﬁrst removing the resist with acetone. Thereafter the
nanoparticle solution was diluted 10 times with demineralized water to lessen particle conglom-
eration on the substrate, followed by a simple dropcast and evaporation of the solution on a
hotplate at 50 ◦C.
Finding particles near one of these markings and obtaining spectra from them thus opens the
possibility to reﬁnd the particles in an SEM for imaging. Figure 3.2 shows an example where
ﬁve Au nanoparticles were refound in the SEM after acquiring an image with the optical setup.

3.2
Optomechanical chip design
3.2.1
Process ﬂow
The process ﬂow for cleanroom fabrication of the chips has been developed by Luca Carnevali
(M.Sc. student, Politecnico di Torino), Andrea Casci Ceccacci (PhD student, DTU Nanotech)
and Associate Professor Silvan Schmid (DTU Nanotech). The cleanroom fabrication itself up
until the cutting of the wafer was carried out by Cristoforo Decaro (M.Sc.
student, DTU
Nanotech). The cutting of the wafer and all subsequent work was performed by me. In the
following, the fabrication steps will be brieﬂy reported; the full ﬂow process can be seen in
Appendix A.
First, a 100 nm Si3N4 layer is deposited using low pressure chemical vapour deposition (LPCVD)
as the low pressure helps to increase ﬁlm uniformity across the wafer.
Next, a layer of SiNx is deposited on the top side of the wafer using plasma enhanced CVD
(PECVD) to protect the Si3N4 in the following steps. Resist is added to the backside, openings
are deﬁned with UV lithography, and an opening to the substrate is made in the Si3N4 layer.
Then a backside etch is performed all through the Si wafer followed by an etch of the SiNx layer
leaving the membrane suspended.
The ﬁnal steps serve to deposit the 50 nm Au layer. Resist is added to the front side and openings
deﬁned with UV lithography. The metal is subsequently sputtered on and excess Au is removed
in a lift-oﬀprocess.
Finally, the wafer is cut into individual chips ready for further processing.
3.2.2
Considerations
In the following, speciﬁc terms will be used extensively. For clarity, these are deﬁned below:
Membrane This references speciﬁcally to the unsupported center area of Si3N4 on each chip.
Contacts The large Au patches in the top and bottom right of each chip.
Wire The long stretch of Au across the membrane between the contacts.
Template The small patch of Au in the center of the membrane.
Strings The FIB milled structures from the Si3N4 membrane.
Dimers The Au structure on the strings.
It has been shown that it is possible to strongly modulate the optical response of two coupled
dimers by utilizing the mechanical motion of the system [32]. In order to use this modulation to
probe the realm of quantum plasmonics, it was necessary to design a chip where the dimers are
(or could be brought) in close proximity to each other while at the same time being allowed to
oscillate around their equilibrium positions. Previous work has shown that high Q mechanical
resonators in the form of strings can be made from low-stress (σ ≈200 MPa [30]) Si3N4 mem-
branes using FIB milling [15]. Placing dimers on these strings will cause them to oscillate under

Chip geometry
The chips were made on a 350 µm thick 4" wafer as this allows the fabrication to be done in
batch and thus a single run of several wafers can readily supply hundreds of chips. The chip
size was chosen to be 5 mm; this choice is somewhat small but the chips can easily be cut from
the wafer in groups (e.g. 2 × 2) increasing the eﬀective size of the chip to be handled. This also
allows for multiple membranes on the same stub when doing FIB milling thus reducing the time
spent on transferring samples to and from the SEM chamber (each transfer takes 30-45 minutes
with venting, pumping and realigning the sample and beam).
The contacts were made as large as possible since this would ease wirebonding the chip for
sending current through the wire. Wanting to maintain a 50 µm safety distance to the edge of
the chip to ease the separation without risk of damaging the contacts and the same distance to
the membrane, the contacts were chosen to be 2.02 × 2.20 mm2 and 1.90 × 2.20 mm2 resulting
in a wire length of 400 µm and 600 µm for the 300 µm and 500 µm membrane, respectively.
Since the aim was to fabricate nanoplasmonic systems using FIB, the dimer template in the
center was designed to have the minimum size available which was 2 × 2 µm2. Reducing the
added mass on the membrane as much as possible, the wire width was chosen to also be the
minimum feature size of 2 µm; a quick calculation yields a resistance of about 150 Ωfor the
entire wire making it easily possible to pass currents through with an applied voltage.

3.3
FIB fabrication
3.3.1
General considerations
In order to maintain the optimum working distance from the ion gun, the structure to be milled
on the sample has to be placed in the eucentric plane. In this plane, objects keep their position
when undergoing rotations about a certain axis (here, the stage’s rotation axis) and is achieved
by rotating the sample and adjusting the stage height until the structure is centered before and
after rotation. This also ensures that focusing and correction of the astigmatism is uniform
across the area of the sample being milled.
As the Si3N4 membrane is non-conductive, charging and the resulting deﬂection of the Ga+
beam can severely hamper the end result when milling. The most obvious solution to overcome
this problem is to use as low a current as possible to allow time for charge cancellation and
diﬀusion in the substrate. The choice of using a low current also has the added advantage that
the full width half maximum of the Gaussian spot decreases [60].
The most important parameter to control when optimizing the focusing of the ion beam is
the astigmatism; without proper pre-fabrication alignment nanoscale precision is impossible to
achieve. Figure 3.8 shows the eﬀects of astigmatism on the end result: The two trenches were
milled using the same pattern and parameters, but the lower had a higher degree of astigmatism
correction. However crucial this is, the process of correcting the astigmatism can be diﬃcult as
the imaging itself destroys the object used for focusing. A lower current increases the longevity

Strings
Milling the strings provided diﬀerent challenges than milling the dimers. As was seen in Sec-
tion 1.5 the amplitude of the vibrations increased with increasing string length and decreasing
width (the height was ﬁxed to the thickness of the membrane), hence the goal was to create as
long and thin strings as possible to allow for maximum modulation of the optical response.
However, when FIB’ing the strings it was not possible to mill from one end to another simul-
taneously on multiple pattern elements. Since making one string required two trenches in the
membrane one trench would have to be milled before the other. Milling too long stretches, how-
ever, resulted in the string bending around its longitudinal axis as can be seen in Figure 3.16.
Here the 40 µm long strings were milled in stretches of 10 µm each with the top trench being
milled ﬁrst. As is evident the string is prone to bending towards the bottom trench making
the gap larger on one side of the string than the other. Furthermore, this bending associated
with each of the four stretches induces inconsistent string widths as the string bends into the
ion beam when it is gradually released from the membrane on the other side. The maximum
distance possible to mill without introducing this distortion was about 8 µm.

3.3.3
Final proces and results
With all the above considerations taken into account, the pattern seen in Figure 3.17 was
devised to make two 15 µm strings with nanorods; to fabricate smaller structures an extra set of
rectangles can be inserted as the ones in Figure 3.13. The colours indicate the diﬀerent z-depths
used, although these were subject to change depending on the speciﬁc alignment of the ion beam
during fabrication; a more focused beam resulted in a shallower depth needed. The much higher
depth needed for milling the gap between the dimers is due to the rectangle being only 3 nm
wide. With the 3 × 3 nm2 grid this means there is only one milling point in the short dimension
and thus no adjacent points to cause proximity milling as is the case with the other parts of the
pattern. The red rectangles used to mill the membrane all overlap 200 nm since the outermost
parts of the patterns experience less milling for the same reason. Overlap thus ensures these
regions are also milled all through the membrane. All patterns were milled from top to bottom.
The pattern was milled in the following order:
1. Remove excess Au from the wire (the blue pattern); the distance between the two blue
rectangles is 220 nm.
2. Mill lower (left) half of the wires (the red pattern). The spacing between the rectangles is
300 nm.
3. Next the upper (rightmost) part was milled in two stretches to minimize distortion.
4. Make the ﬁnal cut between the dimers. Leaving this for last adds stability to the strings
during milling.
Milling the entire structure takes just under 14 minutes. Since tryouts had shown the minimum
gap size achievable when also milling through the membrane was ∼100 nm, it was decided to
make fairly large nanorods to increase the likelihood of optical coupling of the structures where
the gap size as a rule of thumb has to be less than the structures themselves.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and outlook
5.1
Conclusion
The aim of this work was to fabricate micromechanical resonators combined with plasmonic
nanoscale systems to investigate the realm of quantum plasmonics. Although this goal was not
achieved, I have taken important steps towards reaching this goal.
I have presented the basic theoretical background for understanding the plasmonic response of
metallic nanoparticles. I have also presented the theory for understanding coupling between
plasmonic structures and the vibrations of microfabricated beams. Combining this, the optome-
chanical coupling between two spherical particles of radius 25 nm has also been estimated to give
a wavelength modulation on the order of ∼2 nm for these dimers placed on vibrating strings.
This modulation is expected to increase drastically as the gap size diminishes.
In addition to this I have made a COMSOL platform for simulating diﬀerent metallic structures
on various substrates.
Besides aiding in the work of benchmarking the optical system, this
platform was used to model the optical response of the fabricated structures.
Within this
framework the response for even very large nanostructures (V ≈8 µm3) of arbitrary shape can
be calculated without using high performance computers.
Furthermore, I have improved on an existing optical setup by expanding its capabilities, cali-
brated it and benchmarked it against the calculated optical response of spherical Au nanoparti-
cles. By combining these measurements with SEM size measurements of the particles, I showed
excellent agreement between theoretical predictions and experimental results. This solidiﬁed
that the artifacts previously present in the optical setup had been successfully eliminated.
In this work I have also devised a scheme for fabricating micromechanical resonators in the
form of strings cut from a Si3N4 membrane.
The high yield, low fabrication time of about
14 minutes, and reproducibility of these strings as well as a consistent minimum string dimension
of about 240 nm are among the most notable features from the fabrication, as well as the excellent
agreement between pattern design and actual structures. Additionally, I have shown that high

reproducibility was attainable for the Au structures on the strings with a small deviation (<
20 nm/ ∼10 %) of the attained sizes.
Lastly, I have shown that an optical spectrum is clearly visible from the fabricated Au structures
on the strings even when considering the high amount of scattering from the string edges. I
was, however, unable to obtain a spectrum where all sources of noise was removed, since the
scattered and reﬂected light from the nearby Au wires from which the dimers were fabricated
was too bright.
5.2
Future work
The natural next step for this work would be to test the hypothesis of the light pollution from
the Au wires by removing these and repeating the measurements of the fabricated structures.
To further increase the signal to noise ratio, measurements in a cross-polarization scheme could
also be interesting to pursue.
Another interesting road to follow would be the development of a MATLAB routine to convert
drawn images directly into scripted FIB patterns to exploit the full resolution of the Helios
system. Achieving this would allow for easier fabrication of more exotic structures and types
of resonators. The more precise control of the patterns is also expected to further increase the
yield and reproducibility.
A new design could also be envisioned where the strings are merely made using three parallel
cut lines in the membrane thus removing the pads that supported the Au structures.
This
would also reduce the already low fabrication time. Incorporating the Au wire on one string will
possibly make it to heavy to vibrate signiﬁcantly, but if one string is statically deﬂected and the
other free to vibrate as the ones fabricated in this work, the modulation can still be realized.
A revised fabrication method of the string resonators may also be required to reach the quantum
limits since the proximity milling caused the Au dimer structure to be removed from the edge
of the strings. This could either be realized by milling from the backside as has previously been
done [16], or using a He+ ion beam instead of Ga+ since these have shown the possibility of
milling gaps as small as 6 nm [64]. Another way could be to change the design altogether, as
recent work has shown promising results for controlling gap sizes of a diﬀerent structure using
only electrostatic gating [65].
An interesting theoretical point could be to do further calculations on the optomechanical cou-
pling strength G and investigate how the non-local response and quantum tunneling scale with
this parameter. Engineering G could thus give valuable insight to the impact of these diﬀerent
mechanisms.
