1 Abstract 
The global health-crisis caused by snakebite envenoming is escalating. In tropical regions 
of the world, snakebite leads to more than 125,000 deaths and 400,000 amputations every 
year. The cobra Naja melanoleuca is known for its lethal venom, which derives its toxicity 
from potent type I and II α-neurotoxins that target nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, 
leading to progressive paralysis. Presently, the only existing treatment of snakebite 
envenoming is animal-derived antivenom. However, there is a shortage of effective 
antivenom, the price of treatment is high for most victims, and the heterologous nature of 
the antivenom is associated with severe side-effects.  
This thesis aimed at identifying human antibodies with neutralizing effects against the 
medically important toxins of N. melanoleuca venom through phage display selection. 
Whole venom was separated into fractions by RP-HPLC and biotinylated. 7 of 20 toxins 
present in nine selected venom fractions were properly biotinylated, and slight over-
biotinylation was observed for 10 toxins. In a mass-spectrometry based characterization of 
toxins in each fraction, most toxins were estimated to belong to the three-finger toxin 
family. Noteworthy differences in the venom chromatograms of this study and a previous 
study of N. melanoleuca venom supported an existing proposition that this species ought to 
be reclassified into several sub-species. 
Biotinylated venom fractions were used for discovery of selective heavy-domain 
antibodies from the Predator library and human single-chain variable fragments from the 
IONTAS library through phage display selection. Counts of 104-106 CFU/mL were observed 
for plates with phage-infected E. coli cells from panning round 3, indicating successful 
infection with toxin-selective binders in accordance with similar studies. This indication 
was confirmed through a selective plate binding assay which enabled the distinction of 
selective toxin-binding antibodies from false positives. Finally, enzyme-linked 
immunosorbent assays (ELISA), further confirmed the results of the selection. Collectively, 
the results pointed towards selection of toxin-binders for venom fractions M1 (Predator 
library), M8 (κ and λ-strains IONTAS), M19 (κ-strain IONTAS) and M22 (pooled strains, 
IONTAS). It is therefore recommended that these successfully selected libraries be used in 
further sub-cloning studies. Based on mass-spectrometry of the selected fractions used for 
phage display, it is likely that antibody-fragments found in this study bind to three-finger 
toxins, as well as possibly phospholipase A2s.  Additional indications of binders from the 
IONTAS library were found, although further confirmation is required before continuing 
with these libraries. 
Based on the discovery selective binders towards medically important toxins in N. 
melanoleuca, it is anticipated that the work of this study will help pave the way for the first 
polyvalent snakebite antivenom consisting of oligoclonal mixtures of recombinant and 
human antibodies with high efficacy and low immunogenicity in human recipients.

3 Introduction: Discovering Toxin-Binding Antibodies  
 
Out of the vast number of snake species that are known to exist, approximately one-fifth 
are recognized as venomous1. The danger and lethality concerned with the bite of a 
venomous snake is widely acknowledged, but only few are aware of the scale of impact of 
snakebite envenoming.  
This thesis is part of a focused research project which aims towards the formulation of an 
oligoclonal recombinant antivenom against snakebite. Presently, the only treatment 
options which have proven effective are based on antisera derived from immunized 
animals, and an increasing shortage of this type of antivenom2 in rural areas has caused 
the present situation of snake-bite treatment to leap from bad to worse. In cases where 
antivenom is available and administered to the patient, it is known to occasionally cause 
physical side-effects due to immunogenicity. Furthermore, the price of antivenom is often 
an issue for those inflicted and in poor areas it may not be available at all. These issues 
with current antivenom have caused scientists to aim for new treatment options, to ease 
the burden of snakebite in the tropical parts of the world.  
The use of recombinant antibodies may serve as a solution to the antivenom crisis. The 
objective of this thesis is therefore to discover heavy domain antibodies and single chain 
variable fragments with selective binding abilities to the medically most important toxins 
in venom from the black-and white-lipped cobra. This cobra, also known as N. melanoleuca, 
is a highly-feared snake with a potent venom. A synthetic and a naïve human antibody-
library will be screened for potent binders through phage-display selection consisting of 
two to three rounds of panning against important toxins in the hope of finding selective 
binders.  
 
4 Theory 
 
4.1 Snakebite – A Neglected Tropical Issue 
Recently announced as one of the most neglected tropical diseases by the WHO, the threat 
of snakebite envenoming now seems more real than ever. Snakebite is most common in 
the rural areas of the world, and numbers from the WHO point towards more than 5.5 
million envenomings per year, resulting in more than 125,000 annual deaths3,4. The impact 
on surviving victims is massive, and many end up maimed or with amputations, unable 
to take up work, or otherwise restricted in their normal lives.  
The threat of snakebite is worldwide, although venomous snakes are most often found in 
tropical parts of the world, including, but not limited to South Asia, Southeast Asia and 
Sub-Saharan Africa5,6. India, as an example, has the highest number of envenomings per 
capita5, estimated to be up to 163.000 each year.

 Families of Venomous Snakes 
Overall there are four main families of venomous snakes; Viperidae, Elapidae, 
Atractaspididae, and Colubridae. However, Elapidae and Viperidae are known to be of 
the highest concern due to the number of envenomings caused by underlying species as 
well as the severity of these envenomings7, and only these families will therefore be 
described further. Elapid snakes are usually found in tropical areas of the world. They 
vary greatly in size and are known by their short, and permanently erect fangs situated in 
the front of the mouth, which can inject deadly venom into prey. With more than 300 
species divided into around 60 genera, this family of snakes includes mambas, cobras and 
coral snakes. Some of the worlds’ deadliest snakes such as the black mamba (Dendroaspis 
polylepis) and the inland taipan (Oxyuranus microlepidotus) are of the family Elapidae.  
Elapid venom, although often containing a great variety of toxins, is usually mainly 
neurotoxic, and many elapid venoms can, therefore, cause paralysis and even death8. 
Viperidae is another of the venomous snake families. As opposed to elapids they are 
known for their long, mobile fangs which can be moved from the roof of the mouth to the 
front, ready to bite. Vipers live in both temperate and tropical environments and inhabit 
very different places, including grass lands and dry steppe, mountains, forests, and 
savannas. The venom of a viper typically contains protein-degrading enzymes and is 
different from the Elapids in the sense that the viper-venom is most commonly hemotoxic 
and myotoxic, leading to hemolysis and muscle necrosis. Bites from a viper will typically 
result in painful wounds with swelling, at the bite site caused by tissue necrosis. Some 
viper venoms also cause neurotoxic symptoms9.   
 Snake Venom Complexity 
Snake venoms are immensely diverse substances, and the matter is made even more 
complex by each different snake species containing an individual set of toxins. The 
complexity of each venom is said to bear part of the reason behind the lack of development 
of new antivenoms, and present antitoxins only target one or very few toxins10. However, 
new tools within transcriptomics and proteomics have made it possible to create broad 
assessments of the different toxins present in snake venoms, as well as an understanding 
of the relevance of different groups of toxins11. A thorough understanding of the target is 
essential to the development of recombinant antivenoms.    
There are undoubtedly many relevant snake toxins to inhibit in snakebite victims. 
Laustsen et al. (2015)12 describe two key properties when evaluating a toxin in a specific 
venom: 
1) Intrinsic toxicity  
2) The abundance of the toxin.  
The ratio of toxin abundance (%) divided by the estimated LD50 in CD-1 mice is termed the 
“toxicity score” and is likely to be a better tool to determine the importance of an 
individual toxin in a whole venom than the LD50 alone, where lethality, but not abundance

of toxin in the venom, is considered. Some of the toxin families most relevant to this thesis 
are described below. 
   
 
 Toxin families 
Snake venom toxins have been studied for decades and can be divided into a few 
superfamilies of enzymes and non-enzymatic proteins 13. Some common classes of snake 
venom toxins are listed below: 
➢ Myotoxic peptides (Non-enzymatic. 43–45 residues with three disulfide bonds) 
➢ Dendrotoxins (Non-enzymatic. 57–60 residues with three disulfide bonds) 
➢ Three-finger toxins (Non-enzymatic. 60–74 residues with four-five disulfide 
bonds)  
➢ Phospholipase A2 (Enzymatic. Around 120 residues with six-eight disulfide bonds) 
➢ Hemorrhagins (Enzymatic. 200–800 residues with several disulfide bonds) 14  
The most relevant toxin families in most elapid venoms, meaning those that are both in 
abundance and have severe effects, are the three-finger toxins and phospholipase A2s 
(Figure 1).

 Three-Finger Toxins 
 The three-finger toxins (3FTxs) form a broad superfamily of proteins which are non-
enzymatic. These types of toxins are most commonly found in Elapidae snake venom 
although they are also reportedly found in Viperidae and Colubridae15,16. Toxins of this 
family are made up of 60-74 amino acid residues with up to 4-5 disulfide bonds17. Four of 
these bonds are conserved throughout the family and they cause the toxins to fold in a 
similar manner. The structures of the 3FTxs contain three β-stranded loops, in popular 
terms called the “fingers”, extending from a core18. Although carrying a great resemblance 
in structure, the different toxins of this family differ greatly in functional activities which 
makes them very interesting from a molecular evolutionary point of view19. Some 3FTx 
are known to act synergistically with other toxins, as has been observed in mamba 
venom20,21.

Three-finger toxins are known to inflict a wide range of actions. Among the most 
commonly known are those of α-neurotoxins which bind to, and powerfully block 
nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChR). Blocking of a muscle nAChR will block the 
transmission of neuromuscular signals leading to paralysis22.  Other functions of 3FTx 
include L-type calcium channel blockers, platelet aggregation inhibitors, and 
acetylcholinesterase inhibitors among others, which cause symptoms like uncontrollable 
twitching of muscles, increased or decreased heart rate, and blood clotting20 (Table 1).  
 Phospholipase A2 
The phospholipase A2s (PLA2s) are enzymatic toxins which are present in many forms in 
snake venom.  They typically exist as monomers, although complexes of PLA2s are also 
known. They are capable of hydrolyzing phospholipids in the cell membrane at the sn-2 
position of the glycerol backbone, thus causing the release of lysophospholipids and fatty 
acids. Snake venom PLA2s are among the major toxic and potent proteins in snake venom, 
and they play an important role in the digestion of prey in addition to causing a variety of 
painful symptoms. Despite sharing 40-99% identical amino acids, the different isoforms of 
PLA2s are capable of inflicting many different pharmacological effects on the recipient, by 
affecting normal physiological processes23,24. PLA2s are especially known for inducing 
tissue necrosis, as well as some neurotoxic effects (Table 1)

4.2 Naja melanoleuca 
The venom used in this study derives from the black and white-lipped cobra, also known 
as the black cobra or the forest cobra, however, the Latin alias Naja melanoleuca is 
commonly used to avoid confusion26. N. melanoleuca is the largest cobra in Africa, 
measuring up to 3.1 m in length27, and is known to be an agile climber and more aquatic 
than other cobra species. Its primary habitats are moist areas such as rivers, forests and 
==PAGE:12==
 
 10 
 
suburban areas in Western, Central and Southern Africa2829. Some N. melanoleuca 
specimens are black with white scales lining the lips, others are black with yellow bands, 
and some matte brown with black tips30 (Figure 2). The colors vary with region, and thus, 
although known as one species, it has been proposed that N. melanoleuca snakes from 
different regions might actually be so different, both in appearance and venom 
composition that they ought to be reclassified31. This will be elaborated on later. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2: Naja melanoleuca, also known as the black and white-lipped cobra32 
 
An impressive venom yield of up to 1 g per milking27 and an LD50 of 0.66 mg/kg makes N. 
melanoleuca cobra a snake with a potentially lethal bite33 and it is therefore greatly feared 
among African farmers and respected among herpetologists. Furthermore, it has been 
classified as a category 1 snake of highest medical importance by the WHO3034. The early 
symptoms of envenoming by N. melanoleuca include blurred vision, drooping eyelids, and 
drowsiness. These symptoms are followed by weakness of muscles innervated by the 
cranial nerves, and difficulties in swallowing and speaking. Eventually, muscle 
respiratory paralysis may occur, in which case the patient must receive artificial 
respiration aids, to avoid a fatal outcome35. 
 
  N. melanoleuca Venom  
 N. melanoleuca has been chosen as part of this study partly due to its potent venom 
composition. The venom is categorized by the WHO as a syndromic category 3, meaning 
that the venom leads to progressive paralysis by being highly neurotoxic36. A recent 
toxicovenomic profiling of the venom of a Ugandan specimen is one of the few sources of 
existing data on the N. melanoleuca venom. This study will be described in further detail 
later.

4.3 Antivenom 
 Traditional Antivenom Production 
The present treatment for snakebite victims is a parenteral administration of heterologous 
antivenom which is derived from immunized animals. The antivenom consists of 
antibodies with the ability to neutralize certain venom toxins by a mechanism in which 
the molecular region responsible for the toxic effect is blocked. The method of production 
was developed in the late 19th century by scientists Calmette and Phisalix and Bertrand, 
and relies on the repeated immunization of horses or sheep with snake venom, resulting 
in a build-up of toxin-neutralizing antibodies in the blood, which is collected. The plasma 
is then separated and purified (Figure 4) Generally, the immunization is performed using 
whole venom from either an individual species, resulting in a monospecific antivenom, or 
a mixture of venom from multiple species giving rise to a polyspecific antivenom. In the 
earliest days the serum was unpurified, causing many unwanted effects, but with the 
introduction of purification methods, the content of antibodies or antibody fragments 
against the clinically relevant toxins is higher, although contaminant proteins are still 
present6. Current antivenoms in production contain either whole IgG molecules, F(ab’)2 
fragments or monovalent Fab fragments39.

The major reduction in fatalities due to timely administration of antivenom should not be 
underestimated, and some antivenoms are effective in neutralizing certain snake venom 
toxins, especially systemically acting toxins40. However, as the antivenom is derived from 
animal sources, incompatibility between the human immune system and heterologous 
antibodies is a recognized risk factor. Both early and late adverse reactions are known to 
occur in some antivenom recipients and the effects can be severe. Usually, the reactions 
are urticaria, generalized rash, chills, nausea, colics, vomiting, and in some severe cases, 
bronchospasm and hypotension4139. Additionally, existing antivenoms contain numerous 
antibodies which are non-specific to non-toxic antigens, thereby diluting the toxin-specific 
antibodies. Limitation of access to antivenom partly due to the reduction of production 
has also emerged as a problem42. Another drawback includes costs of treatment, which lies 
in the range of $56-640 for a whole treatment43. This price often exceeds available funds for 
the victims who are most commonly poor farmers in rural areas, and for the public health 
systems in developing countries44. Price and quality therefore represent two major issues 
within the current antivenom technology. 
 
 Antivenom Against N. melanoleuca Venom 
There are several antivenoms on the market known to be effective against N. melanoleuca 
venom. These include SAIMR Polyvalent Snake Antivenom (South African Vaccine 
Producers), Snake Venom Antiserum (African, VINS Bioproducts), and Snake Venom 
Antiserum (Central-Africa, VINS Bioproducts), and all are polyvalent antivenoms 
obtained from immunization mixtures of 4-10 different venoms from the Dendroaspis, Naja, 
Bitis, Haemachatus, Echis, and Vipera genera45. In a study from 2017, these polyspecific

from a Ugandan specimen. All tested antivenoms showed some binding affinity towards

towards the presence of cross-reactivity between similar toxins which could be useful in 
the development of an oligoclonal antivenom33.  
 
 Recent Molecular Advances Within Antivenom Research  
Monoclonal antibodies, produced via hybridoma technology was shown to neutralize a 
toxin more than 25 years ago. Current antivenoms still consist of whole IgGs or IgG 
fragments from immunized animals, however, due to the many drawbacks, alternative 
strategies using different molecules are being investigated as replacements. These different 
strategies include the use of small organic molecules, metal ions, carbohydrates, 
nanobodies, peptides, and antibodies. Similar for most of these are the challenges 
concerning stability, size, and efficiency of the product, biodistribution, as well as 
affordable production and comprehensiveness of the strategy.  
Small molecules, such as antibody fragments, have been investigated as inhibitors of 
enzymatically active toxins in the recent years, with limited sucess46. The advantage of 
using small molecules to target toxins is their ability to penetrate tissue which is hard to 
reach for larger molecules, and though they have shown to be able to target locally acting 
toxins, such as phospholipase A2s in a few cases47, they are still not clinically accessible38. 
Exclusively using small molecules for toxin-inhibition has been deemed a precarious idea, 
as small molecules like scFvs are known to have short half-lives, and their size may limit 
their ability to accommodate all the desired functions. If one small molecule can only target 
one toxin, it will require a huge number of different molecules to create a potent antivenom 
against multiple species, making this strategy highly comprehensive with increasing 
production costs.  
The use of smaller peptides is also considered for antivenom development, although 
presently no peptide-based antivenom is available in clinics. Peptidic antitoxins have 
shown binding properties against α-cobratoxin48, suggesting that the use of these have 
some potential as antitoxins. In theory, production of a peptide-based antivenom might be 
relatively inexpensive compared to the present alternative, as well as having a fast bio-
distribution in the victim. Additionally, there is a possibility that these may be developed 
to be distributed in other ways than the usual intravenous injection, for instance by 
subcutaneous administration49, which would be a major step forward, as this could 
potentially eliminate the need for medically trained personnel50. Peptides and peptide 
fragments do, however, have a very short half-life which largely limits their use.  
The use of human antibodies overcomes this particular problem. Since snake venom is 
rarely injected directly into a vein but is instead left to diffuse slowly through tissue and 
therefore reside in the patient for long periods of time, the half-life of antibodies is critical. 
IgGs have shown to have excellent half-lives. One equine IgG antivenom was showed to 
have a half-life between 60 and 190 hours39 which makes it possible to target “late” toxins. 
Furthermore, they can bind with high affinity and have the obvious advantage of being 
completely compatible with the human immune system51. Smaller fragments like scFvs or 
==PAGE:17==
 
 15 
 
single-domain antibodies, however, have the advantage of penetrating tissue faster, which 
may be a valuable asset when targeting larger molecules at the bite-site50. Researchers have 
experimented both with the use of full IgG antibodies, and antibodies consisting of 
different formats and combinations of heavy and light chains and variable fragments 
(Figure 5). A mixture of whole IgGs and smaller antibody fragments may be the solution 
to reaching both systemically and locally acting toxins.

Some of the main challenges lie within the identification of potent human antibodies that 
can inhibit multiple relevant toxins, as well as the enabling low-cost production. However, 
human monoclonal scFvs have already been found to have some neutralizing effects on a 
lethal neurotoxin from Naja kauthia53 and research within spider and scorpion venom has 
yielded neutralizing scFvs from a human library, isolated by phage display with some 
success54. Furthermore, Laustsen et al. (2017)55 have found two selective binders from the 
IONTAS library used in this study, able to fully inhibiting two elapid toxins in vivo.  
 
4.4 Analytical Methods 
Phage display is the main method that will be used to discover toxin-binding antibodies in this 
thesis. It is an increasingly used technique based on genetically engineered bacteriophages in 
the screening of millions of mAbs to find binders of any given specificity. These binders may 
then be amplified and used both in drug discovery to find high-affinity binders to different 
targets and to discover new biomarkers for research use56.

Several rounds are necessary to deselect unwanted binders and amplify wanted binders, so the 
phage antibody-binders with the wanted selectivity are obtained.  
Prior to panning, the antigen for which antibody binders are wanted is immobilized on a solid 
surface. The immobilization takes place via a biotin-linker, as this has been shown to yield 
better selection results for toxins-binding scFvs, compared to direct coating without a linker57. 
The immobilization surfaces are often either microtiter plate wells, magnetic beads, PVDF 
membranes, column matrices, immunotubes, or whole cells in solution56,58. A large library of 
antibody-displaying phages (in the scale of 1014 clones) is then added in the panning step, left 
to bind the target for a period of time, and unbound phages are then washed away. 
Subsequently, the bound phages are eluted, by cutting the binding between antibody-fragment 
and antigen with a digestive enzyme such as trypsin.  The phages are collected and can be 
amplified in a bacterial host such as E. coli to achieve a stock of the selected antibodies. The 
process is repeated if further rounds are considered necessary to increase the binding affinity 
or specificity of the selected antibodies. Due to the risk of obtaining “false positives” or “non-
selective binders”, meaning phages that bind to the surface material instead of the displayed 
antibody, several deselection steps are usually performed during these rounds. When sufficient 
rounds of selection are completed, the activity of the analyte can be measured through an 
enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)56,59,60. The ELISA will not be described further in 
this section, as the method is described in the method section and  reviewed extensively in 
literature61.  
 
In this study, a binding assay, named the “Plate Binding Assay” was invented to obtain 
indications of selectively binding phages in addition to the ELISA. The assay consists of one 
round of phage display, however, phages are panned against both toxin, streptavidin and PBS, 
left to bind, washed away, eluted, and amplified in E. coli on plates. Colonies are then counted 
on the plates, indicating one of the following:  
Selective binding: growth exclusively observed on the plate containing binders panned against 
toxin. Plates with streptavidin binders and PBS-binders are empty 
Unspecific streptavidin binding: growth observed on the streptavidin-plate, perhaps also on 
the toxin-plate, but none on the PBS-plate. 
Non-specific binding: growth observed on all three plates, including the PBS-plate.  
 
 
 The M13 Phage 
The phage antibody libraries in this study are comprised of phages containing antibody-
encoding genes, either single-chain variable fragments (scFvs) or single-domain antibody 
fragments such as VHs, which are incorporated into the DNA of bacteriophages. The use of 
bacteriophages enables the selection of good binders with a phage that contains both the 
antibody fragment on the outside and the genetic information which encodes this fragment 
==PAGE:19==
 
 17 
 
on the inside. This makes it ideal for the continuous work, as the ssDNA of binders can 
easily be obtained for further research56. 
In this thesis, libraries containing the M13 phage were used for phage display. M13 is a 
filamentous bacteriophage, containing a circular ssDNA molecule, and a large majority of 
the coat protein pVIII62. M13 is extensively used as a vector in cloning and DNA-
sequencing.  
M13 infects E. coli cells by one of the coat proteins called PIII which attaches to the tip of 
the F pilus on the cell. The F pilus then retracts, allowing the phage DNA to get in contact 
with the bacteria and be translocated into the bacterial cytoplasm. Once inside the bacteria, 
the genome of the phage is converted to dsDNA by enzymes, and rolling circle replication 
takes place. All proteins necessary for the assembly of the virion are transported to the 
periplasm of the cell where they are assembled, and coated with pVIII before release. An 
interesting property of this phage is that it is able to infect a cell without lysing it, and the 
cell can, therefore, continue to grow, although slower while infected, all the while releasing 
new M13 virions 63.  
 
Figure 6: The M13 bacteriophage, carrying a scFv-encoding gene and displaying the fragment on the pIII coat protein. 
 Modified from Laustsen (2016) 50 
 
M13-antibody phage libraries are constructed by fusing the DNA of a protein, peptide or 
antibody with the phage genome, enabling the phage to display the peptide sequence of a 
protein fragment on one of the five capsid proteins on their outer coat. Fusion with any of 
the coat proteins encodings in the genome is possible, however, the terminal pIII coat 
protein is most commonly used. By fusing a fragment with the coat protein, the fragment 
will be displayed on all pIII proteins on the phage surface, which can be undesirable due 
to the risk of blocking viral assembly or reducing infectivity. For this reason, a phagemid 
is often used, to limit the display of a fragment to a single pIII protein. A phagemid is a 
small plasmid vector used to transform the bacteria. The M13 phagemid carries the pIII 
gene, and the antibody fragment encoding gene, as well as a phage packaging signal and 
antibiotic resistance. The addition of a helper-phage is necessary when using phagemids, 
as they must provide the other essential coat proteins necessary to accomplish the full life 
==PAGE:20==
 
 18 
 
cycle of the phagemid. Without these proteins, the phage cannot assemble within the 
bacteria. The helper phages also carry a mutated gene which prevents efficient replication, 
and the transformation efficiency is therefore much higher for the phagemids 59,62,64,65.  
 
 Antibody Libraries 
John McCafferty et al. (1990) reported the first development of an antibody library more 
than 25 years ago66.  Antibody libraries contain millions of antibody-displaying phages, 
and they are commonly used in the pharmaceutical industry to identify specific therapeutic 
antibodies. There are three types of such libraries: immune, naïve and synthetic libraries. 
The immune library is a well-known type of antibody library which originates from an 
immunized donor. They are made from B-cells from immunized individuals which will 
have an enhanced concentration of antibodies specific to the antigen in question. This is 
advantageous, as even a small library will be likely to lead to potent binders, but the 
process of immunization is unfortunately very time-consuming. Additionally, the issues of 
immunogenicity due to heterogeneity when using animal-derived antibodies, and the 
major ethical issues when considering human immunization, arise65.  
Synthetic antibody libraries are different to immune libraries in that they contain 
synthetic phagemids which are derived from non-immunized donors. These are commonly 
built artificially via the assembly of V-gene segments and D/J segments, thus forming 
antibodies which may recognize a specific antigen. In synthetic libraries, a predetermined 
level of randomization is introduced to the key amino acids, typically in the antigen-
binding CDR region60. One of the phagemid antibody libraries utilized in this study, 
dubbed the Predator library67, is a synthetic library consisting of heavy domain antibodies 
and has been designed to mimic the natural human immune response. 
Antibody libraries can also derive from non-immunized donors. In these so-called naïve 
libraries, the constructed antibodies have not yet encountered an antigen. Naïve libraries 
are typically constructed by amplifying heavy and light chain genes, combining them 
randomly and cloning them into a combinatorial library of scFv or Fab antibody fragments, 
with the overall affinity of the library proportional to the size of the library. The V-genes 
are obtained from B-cells, which are isolated from the spleen, bone marrow cells, tonsils or 
peripheral blood lymphocytes of non-immunized donors. This library of proteins can be 
used to generate antibodies to a large number of antigens, including toxin antigens60. The 
main phage library used in this thesis, the IONTAS library, is a naïve library. It is a stock 
of human scFvs from naïve human donors consisting of both lambda (λ) and kappa (κ) 
light chains from which binders are found separately during the first round of selection. 
 
4.5 Experimental Plan 
The main objective of this thesis is the identification of recombinant antibodies against the 
most medically relevant toxins from N. melanoleuca. To find potent binders to the relevant 
toxins, venom must first be divided into smaller fractions. After dissolving the powdered 
venom from two N. melanoleuca specimens from Cameroun, venom is fractionated using 
==PAGE:21==
 
 19 
 
reverse-phase high-pressure liquid chromatography. The fractions are then evaporated, 
freeze-dried and stored until use. The most important fractions are then identified from the 
chromatogram and selected for further use. 
Prior to phage display experiments, venom fractions are subjected to biotinylation, to 
improve the binding abilities of the toxins to the wells. After leaving biotin to bind, 
biotinylated toxins are purified by centrifugation. 
Phage display selection against the isolated fractions is carried out over three rounds, using 
the Predator and IONTAS phage libraries displaying heavy-domain and scFv-antibodies. 
After rounds of panning, binding, elution, and amplification, the selected binders are 
evaluated via a count of colonies, and measured in a plate-binding assay followed by a 
polyclonal ELISA.

 Biotinylation 
Toxins were biotinylated, thus attaching streptavidin-binding biotin molecules to the 
toxins. This favors strong attachment and lifts the toxin up from the wells during phage 
display selection and ELISA-analyses.  
Biotinylation: The concentrations of toxins were measured using a nanodrop 
spectrophotometer. Biotin was then added in a 1:4 toxin to biotin ratio and left at room 
temperature for 30-45 minutes.  
Purification: Purification of the biotinylated toxins was carried out using ultrafiltration 
spin columns (Vivacon® 500, Sartorious). Columns were centrifuged at 14000 g for 30 
minutes and the filtrate collected. Columns were washed 3 times with PBS and 500 μl PBS 
was added to each column. They were centrifuged for 30 min. at 4000 g, and the filtrate 
collected again. Washing and centrifugation was repeated twice. Columns were then 
inverted and 100 μl PBS was added to each column. They were centrifuged at 2500 g for 30 
minutes and the filtrate collected in a new tube. 50 μl PBS was added to the columns and 
centrifuged for 2x30 min. at 2500 g. Columns were then centrifuged for 2x15 min. at 5000 
g, until about 30 μl were left on top of the filter.

20 seconds. The flow through was saved. Samples were then washed twice with 20 µl 
equilibration solution and spun for 30 seconds. Finally, samples were eluted slowly with 
10 µl elution solution, using a syringe and kept.

With more than 30 different toxins known to be present in the venom of N. melanoleuca, the 
first step in the discovery of toxin-binding antibodies was to separate the venom into 
smaller fractions. Venom from N. melanoleuca was fractionated by RP-HPLC into 
approximately 33 different fractions (Figure 8). Some of the most prominent fractions, 
namely peak 1, 2, 3, 8, 9, 18, 19, 20 and 22 (hereafter named M1, M2, M3, M8, M9, M18, 
M19, M20 and M22) were selected for further analysis, and only these fractions will 
henceforth be discussed. Some of the fractions that eluted later, such as peak 28, were also 
prominent, however, based on a previous toxicovenomic study of N. melanoleuca venom 
from a Ugandan specimen by Lauridsen et al. (2016)33, fractions appearing earlier were 
more likely to be lethal toxins of highest medical value, and these were therefore 
prioritized.

A comparative analysis of the chromatogram obtained in this study and one from the study 
by Lauridsen et al. (2016) (Figure 9)  show that chromatograms of separate specimens from 
the same species, obtained in identical manner (with the exception of a 5 nm wave-length 
deviation) are slightly different. Many of the prominent compounds seem to be present in 
both venoms, such as fractions M1, M8, M18, M19, and M20, likely corresponding to peak 
3, 8, 14 and 15 in the previous study, all of which were shown to mainly contain 3FTxs. 
However, peak 9 of the present study, a rather prominent peak, is non-existing in the 
analysis from 2016. So is peak 3, and peaks around peak 21/22/26 in this study. The 
differences in the two chromatograms clearly indicate that venom compositions in N. 
melanoleuca specimen from different regions are subject to variation.  
Since there is very little existing data on venom from N. melanoleuca, any speculations on 
the subject of differing venom compositions are currently left as such. However, according 
to Dr. W. Wüster and Dr. N. Casewell, who confirmed the ID of the specimen from this 
study by analysis of the Cytochrome B and ND4, N. melanoleuca is a cryptic species 
containing multiple sub-species, and as such ought to be reclassified. Looking at the venom 
composition differences of subspecies of N. melanoleuca can support this reclassification. 
Based on the results it would presently be unwise to directly convey a toxicovenomic study 
from one specimen of N. melanoleuca to identify toxins from another. 
 
Subconclusion 
The most prominent fractions of this study, namely M1, M2, M3, M8, M9, M18, M19, 
M20, and M22 were selected for use in phage display selection. As a small note, 
differences in chromatograms between two related specimens indicate that N. 
melanoleuca as a species ought to be reclassified.

In some cases, it was not possible to determine whether a peak represented a new toxin or 
a biotinylated toxin, such as is the case of fractions M2 and M8, which both contain peaks 
with the molecular weight 10147.175. In M2, the peak seems to indicate the attachment of 
three biotin molecules to a toxin, whereas it appears to be an unbiotinylated toxin in 
fraction M8. This could be a peculiar coincidence, but may also reveal the presence of an 
extra toxin in M2. 
7 of the 20 toxins, from fractions M1, M19, M20, and M22 appear to contain a singly 
biotinylated toxin, leaving them in the optimal state for immobilization in a well via 
streptavidin binding. Fraction M8 may also have an advantage to the remaining fractions, 
as attachment of biotin seems to have failed, leaving it without the advantage of biotin, but 
still with the natural binding abilities of the toxin. 10 of the 20 toxins appear to have been 
over-biotinylated, indicated by attachment of two or more biotin molecules (Table 7). The 
extent of biotinylation has previously been shown to have a great effect on the yield of 
phage binders. Singly biotinylated toxins are far more advantageous than both over-
biotinylated toxins (>1 biotin) and unbiotinylated toxins.  However, non-biotinylation is 
preferred to over-biotinylation57. The high level of biotinylation found in this study may 
have caused alterations in the antigen surface, and may additionally have caused any 
displayed antibodies on the phages to interact sub-optimally with the antigen during 
phage display, as suggested by Laustsen et al. (2017) and McCafferty (1996) 57,68.  
 
Subconclusion 
Based on the biotinylation, 7 toxins from venom fractions M1, M19, M20, and M22, as 
well as possibly 2 toxins from M8, are expected to be most successful in the following 
phage display screening.

6.4 Discovery of Human Antibodies Using Phage Display 
 
 
 
 
Phage display selection was carried out using phages containing antibody-encoding genes 
and displaying antibody fragments on the PIII pili. Phages were panned against the 
selected fractions from N. melanoleuca venom and left to bind. Unbound phages were then 
washed away, and binders were eluted and left to infect E. coli. By successful infection E. 
coli acquires ampicillin resistance and is able to grow on plates containing this antibiotic, 
unlike uninfected cells. Pannings were repeated two or three rounds. Phage display 
experiments were carried out using the Predator Library containing single domain 
antibodies and the IONTAS scFv human library. Results of the colony counts between 
rounds are displayed separately for each experiment (Figure 11), however, a general 
tendency of increasing colony counts with every round of phage display is evident. The 
growth of smaller colonies (Figure 12) was taken as a positive sign, as E. coli infected with 
fully functionally antibody-displaying phage virions have a growth disadvantage69. 
The first experiment using the Predator Library (PL) only revealed growth on the output 
plate in the first round. In round 2, the increase in colonies was apparent, reaching colony 
counts above 5x104 CFU/mL. In the second experiment using the same library and 
screening three additional toxins, a tenfold increase from round 1 to round 2 was observed 
for M1, from 1×104 CFU/mL to1×105 CFU/mL, and a twofold increase for M8, from 
0.5×105 CFU/mL to 1×105 CFU/mL. No growth was observed for two of the fractions in 
round two, which could be caused by a failure to infect E. coli cells with phages. Only two 
rounds were performed with this library, as the following ELISA showed no potential 
antibody-binders from round 2 for any of the toxins, despite promising colony counts. 
Experiments were therefore initiated with a new library instead of continuing the existing 
ones. However, as will be explained later, and can also be seen in Appendix 1, the low 
absorbance observed from these initial ELISAs turned out to be largely due to the 
antibodies used in the detecting ELISA.

superiority, in terms of counted colonies, was observed for the κ-strains in this round, 
which is unusual, according to Dr. Aneesh K. Vellatt, senior research scientist at Iontas Ltd.

that the remaining four fractions, M9, M18, M20, and M22 would have displayed similar 
high counts in round 3, but a manual error led to insufficient aeration of cells in the 
overnight cultures for binders to these fractions, and cell density was visibly lower.

6.5 Indications of Selective Binding in a Plate Binding Assay 
 
 
A binding assay was carried out as a reaction to unsuccessful ELISAs to validate the 
binding specificity of the selected phages against their intended venom fraction. Each 
library of selected antibodies was panned separately against the venom fraction, 
streptavidin and PBS, and left to bind. Unbound phages were then washed away, and the 
binders eluted and left to infect E. coli cells which were then plated on antibiotic plates. 
Growth on the plates which contained phages panned against toxin, and a lack of growth 
on the other two plates, confirmed that the selected antibodies were indeed toxin binders 
instead of non-specific binders selected by mistake.

6.6  Confirmation of Selective Binding by ELISA 
 
 
Enzyme-linked Immunosorbent Assays were carried out to confirm selective binding of 
antibody-displaying phagemids to toxins in each of the selected venom fractions. Phages 
were panned against toxins, streptavidin, and PBS, and absorbances were measured to 
determine the degree of binding. Selected phages from both experiments from the Predator 
library and the IONTAS library were analyzed.  
An average of four ELISAs were carried out for each of the libraries of selected phages, due 
to continuous improvements of the protocol. An overall evaluation of each library was 
made from these analyses based on average absorbance values. A cut-off value for 
minimum absorbance was set to 0.4 as well as the requirement of minimum 50% higher 
absorbance for toxin-coated wells compared with control-wells with streptavidin and PBS. 
Although results varied greatly, the ELISAs imply that selective binders were found to four 
of the nine different venom fractions, namely fractions M1, M8 (κ and λ-strains), M19 (κ-
strain) and M22, deriving from ten different selected phage-libraries from both round 2 
and round 3 (Figure 15). The selective M1-phages originated from the Predator library and 
the remaining from the IONTAS library.

Indications of selective binders were clear; however, signal strengths were not outstanding. 
The fractions for which selective binders were most abundant, namely M1, M8, M19, and 
M22, most likely contain toxins from the 3FTx family, based on the previous findings in the 
study by Lauridsen et al. (2016)33. According to the same study, and based on their toxicity 
scores, the most therapeutically relevant targets of N. melanoleuca to be neutralized with 
antivenom were alpha-neurotoxins, namely Short neurotoxin (P01424), Long neurotoxin 2 
(P01388), and homologs to Long neurotoxin OH55 (Q53B58) and Alpha-neurotoxin NTX1 
(Q9YGJ6). M1 and M8 are likely to contain the short neurotoxin and alpha-neurotoxin, 
making neutralization of one of these highly lethal toxins a possibility. As for fractions M19 
and M22, based on evaluation of the older study, selected antibodies are likely to bind to 
cytotoxin or phospholipase A2, two equally interesting toxins-targets for neutralization. 
 
Reviewing the collected results of both biotinylation, plate binding assay and ELISA, a 
general pattern of agreement is found (Table 11). Singly-biotinylated toxins in the optimal 
state for binding typically yielded phage-binders in the phage-display selection, confirmed 
by both plate binding assay and ELISA. Unfortunately, not all selected libraries were 
subjected to the plate binding assay, which might have further confirmed the outcome of 
the ELISA. A few inconsistencies were seen, such as fraction M9 which, despite slight over-
biotinylation, yielded excellent results in the plate binding, but upon testing the selected 
binders in an ELISA showed no signs of selective binders. Fractions with similar conflicting 
results should be re-assessed in additional ELISAs or plate binding assays. 
 
Subconclusion 
Based on this evaluation it is recommended that M1 (Predator Library), M8 (κ and λ 
libraries), M19 (κ-library) and M22 (pooled κ and λ library) are used in further 
subcloning studies.

6.7  Experimental Challenges 
It is fair to say that a few of the experiments conducted in this study had to be repeated. 
For the most part, the challenge of starting up a new laboratory was the cause of failure, 
leading to delays or destroyed experiments. An outline of the experiments, their proposed 
reason for success or failure as well as any action taken to correct errors is displayed in 
Appendix 1 – Overview of Experimental Issues  
One of the major causes of frustration in this study was the use of faulty detection 
antibodies for the ELISA. After several failed attempts at ELISA, the first antibody was 
found to be faulty, even when tested at high concentrations (1:20 dil.). New antibodies were 
ordered in response. However, ELISA-experiments continued to yield low absorbance-
levels, so an empty Maxi-Sorp plate was used to test every step of the ELISA protocol, but 
to no avail (absorbances <0,15), leaving the secondary antibody from Thermo Scientific as 
the primary suspect for error. Yet another (secondary) antibody was ordered in response. 
==PAGE:51==
 
 49 
 
Higher signal intensities were finally obtained using M13 Phage coat protein Antibody 
(Invitrogen) and peroxidase-conjugated affinipure goat anti-mouse antibody (Jackson).  
Along with the change in antibody, the use of a phosphate/citrate buffer was introduced in the 
ELISA-protocol, in substitution for H2O which had worked with other types of detection 
antibodies in similar previous studies. However, with repeated obvious failures, the buffer was 
introduced to decrease pH to 5, in order to optimize the activity of the enzyme in the O-
Phenylenediamine assay. The positive effect was evident.  
Yet, with functional detection antibodies and the proper buffer in place, signals were often low 
(<0.4) and varied greatly in nearly identical ELISA-experiments.  Though differing 
concentrations of detection antibodies and phages may bear some of the blame for the 
inconsistency, other reasons were sought. In 1996 John McCafferty, one of the apostles of 
phage display, described several factors that influence the outcome of experiments:  
➢ Introduction of deselection steps to remove unwanted phage-antibodies 
➢ Affinity of the displayed antibody fragments  
➢ Antigen immobilization and presentation 
➢ Clonal variation  
Deselection steps were incorporated in phage display experiments and ELISAs. Unspecific 
binding leading to false positives was a reoccurring phenomenon in the phage display 
selection, and though efforts were made to reduce the level of non-selective binders by 
several deselection steps, such as blocking of potential non-selective binders with 2-6% 
milk powder in PBS, interchanging coating of wells with Streptavidin and NeutrAvidin 
and deselection of phages with Streptavidin-beads, unspecific binding was still apparent. 
Another measure to improve the deselection could be to pre-block phages with 
Streptavidin-beads in rounds two and three, in addition to round one. Furthermore, the 
coating of wells in round three could be changed from Streptavidin to NeutrAvidin, as the 
continued presence of streptavidin-binders is more likely than those that bind 
NeutrAvidin.  
Increased and improved washing rounds were introduced in ELISAs to remove unwanted 
phage-antibodies, but the efforts were not rewarded. Accidental spreading of phages to 
other wells in late steps of the ELISA was suspected, as this could have caused the observed 
inconsistency and signals for unspecific binders. Thus, extra care was taken during the 
addition of phages, but to no avail. 
Affinity of displayed fragments and especially low affinity of fragments for some of the 
selected binders is a possible explanation for low signals, and might well be the reason for 
less successful findings for some fractions. However, strong binders from the IONTAS 
library have recently been found for other Elapid snake toxins, and converted to IgG-
format55, supporting the presence of high-affinity fragments in the library, as well as 
increasing the expectations for this experiment. 
As touched upon earlier, Antigen immobilization and presentation is largely affected by 
the strategy of immobilization which in this study depends on the level of biotinylation57. 
The slight over-biotinylation of toxins may have impacted the selection of phage-
==PAGE:52==
 
 50 
 
antibodies negatively. It is a proposed flaw of ELISA that some toxins may bind better to the 
plate than others, leading to a lower signal for the poorly binding toxins69. 
Clonal variation may further affect antibody fragment translation, folding, transport, and 
stability of the fusion, thus creating a bias in amplification of undesired fragments. 
 
In the same study by McCafferty (1996), he showed that lowering the concentration of 
phages in phage display in some cases yielded more binders than when the proportion of 
input phages was increased and proposed that this trait was likely due to aggregate 
formation of the phage after binding71. Since the phage display protocol was previously 
discussed with McCafferty himself, the concentration of phages was not lowered here, but 
the input of phages in the ELISA was decreased several times, from 50 μL down to 0,5 μL, 
partly to save phages, but also in an attempt to optimize conditions for binding. The impact 
of this change was minimal.  
 
 
Subconclusion 
Although several deselection steps were performed, further steps can be introduced to 
decrease the selection of unspecific binders, and NeutrAvidin-coating may be 
considered for selection in round 3. A buffer (pH 5) is critical for enzymatic activity in 
O-Phenylenediamine assay, as is the choice of detection antibodies for ELISA. Slight over-
biotinylation is also thought to have impacted the success of this study. 
 
 
6.8 Next Step: Generation of Expression Plasmids 
Future studies should aim at isolating DNA from the polyclonal phage libraries of the 
binders found for fractions M1, M8, M19, and M22, by isolation and digestion of plasmids 
followed by gel-purification. Digested inserts should be ligated into a pSang10 vector, and 
transformed into the competent BL21(DE3) E. coli cells. Individual colonies can then be 
picked and screened for expression of scFv or VH binders by monoclonal ELISA. In case of 
positive signal, future studies could also test cross-reactivity between selected antibodies 
and different toxins, both in the venom of N. melanoleuca, but also between different snake 
species, as a single antibody has previously been shown capable of inhibiting multiple 
antigens in scorpion venom72,7374. Following expression, the binding strength of selected 
clones should be measured, and fragments potentially converted to a different format such 
as IgG if this is deemed advantageous. In vivo neutralization can then be tested in CD-1 
mice.

7 Future Challenges and Considerations on Recombinant 
Antivenom 
 
7.1 Perspectives on Antibody Formats in Antivenom 
The phage libraries used in this study contained antibodies of different formats and origins, 
both recombinant heavy-domain antibodies, and human single-chain variable fragments. 
The potential of these antibody libraries has been proven by the many applications of both 
libraries in phage display, and the findings of selective inhibiting binders within toxin and 
breast cancer studies 5575.   
If successful toxin-neutralizing binders are found based on the selective binders found in 
this study, the question arises of which format to use in a formulation. Since binders have 
been found in two different formats, the possibilities are several. 
The difference in size for the targeted toxins is an important factor when contemplating 
optimal format, as larger toxins are slower in diffusing from the bite site to the circulation. 
Thus, antibodies neutralizing these larger locally-acting toxins such as large PLA2s and 
metalloproteinases - the 2nd and 3rd most abundant toxin families in N. melanoleuca - may 
need different abilities such as faster diffusion through tissues than those targeting 
systemically acting toxins like the neurotoxins likely present in fractions M1 and M8.  
Many studies have pointed out that a mixture of high molecular weight antibodies (IgG, F(ab’)2) 
and smaller fragments (Fab, scFv, VH) might be the better solution in the treatment of 
envenoming76,77. Larger fragments containing the Fc-region have longer serum half-lives, but 
smaller fragments with shorter half-lives are better able to penetrate deep tissues due to their 
small size46. This trait could be a major advantage in the neutralization of non-systemic toxins 
in the venom of N. melanoleuca which conventional antivenom is known to neutralize poorly 
in bite-victims, mainly due to the speed of which the toxins act, once injected into the 
body7879.  
The present lack of functional antibodies against non-systemic toxins in current 
antivenoms greatly advocates for the use of smaller fragments with the ability to penetrate 
tissues faster than larger molecules. However, one of the most critical aspects of producing 
antivenom is the stability of the antibodies, and in addition to their short half-life in the 
system, small fragments lack thermostability. This feature leads to additional cold chain 
requirements which will cause difficulties in many rural areas of application.  
 The use of camelid VHH has recently been proposed as a solution to many of the issues 
with existing antibody formats. In addition to their ability to penetrate deep tissues due to 
their low mass, they are known to be more thermostable than larger formats, able to target 
cryptic enzyme clefts that are usually unreachable to regular VHs, and additionally are 
easily expressed and purified from E. coli. VHHs have been shown to neutralize several 
lethal doses of α-cobratoxin, however, the rapid clearance of this format prevents the sole 
use of these antibodies. Furthermore, the issue of immunogenicity in humans persists, 
although less critical than for most animal-derived antibodies80. Perhaps coupling to 
human Fc-region may help elongate the half-life of camelid VHH.     
==PAGE:54==
 
 52 
 
 
Immediate neutralization of lethal neurotoxins upon diffusion into the bloodstream is of 
utmost importance in many Elapid species. Large-mass IgGs are slow to penetrate tissues 
and therefore mainly act systemically. These are presently considered the favored format 
in our group at the Tropical Pharmacology Lab for inhibition of systemically acting toxins 
due to their long half-lives, which are advantageous since many snake venom toxins persist 
in the body up to several days, slowly diffusing from the “depot” of venom at the bite site. 
And with more than 57% of the toxins in N. melanoleuca venom being low-mass neurotoxins 
of the 3FTx family, selective scFvs and VHs from this study will likely be attached to an IgG 
scaffold if they are found to have toxin-neutralizing abilities and high binding affinities. 
However, with the ultimate goal of creating a polyspecific antivenom for the use against 
several different snake-species with both systemically and locally-acting toxins, a mixture 
of recombinant or human fragments and whole IgGs, and maybe even camelid fragments 
may be the way forward.  
 
7.2 Final Remarks on Recombinant Antivenom 
Neutralizing lethal toxins is a priority in the development of therapeutic antivenom, and 
one which many of the existing equine antivenoms have accomplished. Yet, even with 
functional antivenoms, the drawbacks of using horse-serum derived antibodies cannot be 
overlooked, giving part of the purpose to the present study. Although this study is not the 
first to show indications of selective human antibodies with binding abilities towards snake 
toxins, it is the first to do so specifically for N. melanoleuca venom.   
The use of phage display has recently grown to be a widely used tool within drug-
discovery, and within antivenom research, it has proved a promising strategy48,54,76,81,82. The 
concept of screening recombinant phage-libraries is under rapid development, giving rise 
to the hope of a non-immunogenic alternative to the century-old equine antivenom of 
today.  
By avoiding adverse effects due to the heterologous nature of horse-derived antibodies, 
and with a formulation exclusively consisting of components which are useful for the 
neutralization of toxins, antivenom consisting of human antibodies will almost certainly 
be safer than the present alternative. Additionally, without the need of venom and animals 
for immunization, resources are saved. A more specific antivenom will also result in the 
requirement of a much lower dose for treatment in victims, which can reduce costs. 
By including neutralizing antibodies against a variety of species, with the primary focus 
on Elapids, it is the aim to create an oligoclonal antivenom which can effectively treat bites 
from several different snakes; a great advantage since it takes valuable time to identify the 
species from the venom injected into a patient. The advantage of having one antivenom 
which effectively treats a large palette of toxins is evident, and may additionally lower 
production costs.  
==PAGE:55==
 
 53 
 
Presently, the shortage of antivenom is a pressing problem. Manufacturers of the existing 
antivenom are pulling out of the market, especially in Africa, with no immediate 
replacement strategy in place for victims in these areas83,84. This development, along with 
the critical issue of high costs of the current antivenom leaves victims of snakebite in rural 
areas in a very unfavorable position. The cost of production of recombinant antivenoms is 
therefore sure to be a key determinant of the success of this strategy, although there is a 
lack of consensus among scientists regarding this issue. Supporters of existing antivenom 
claim that producing recombinant antivenom will be priced at tens of thousands of dollars 
per dose, whereas those working with recombinant antivenom propose that a pan-African 
antivenom treatment would cost $30-150, a positive indication compared to current prices 
of conventional antivenom ($60-600)83,85 
 
Although the pursuit of a novel recombinant antivenom is a complex journey with many 
obstacles along the way, there is hope that the next few years will lead to great 
breakthroughs within antivenom research. Very recently, 2 scFvs deriving from the 
human IONTAS library were tested in mice and found to provide full protection against 
Dendrotoxin 1 and α-cobratoxin. This finding underlines the potential of using human 
antibodies in a future antivenom. But whether a future oligoclonal antivenom will be 
based entirely on human antibodies, camelid domain antibodies, a mixture or something 
entirely different will be interesting to see. There is hope for the millions of snakebite 
victims around the world.    
 
8 Conclusion 
 
The global issue of venomous snakebite has reached a critical state, and current 
antivenoms are often unavailable, too expensive or cause adverse side-effects in victims. 
Phage display selection has recently proven itself a promising new approach for the 
development of novel antivenoms. By applying this technique, it is the ambition to replace 
traditional animal-derived antivenom with the production of an oligoclonal recombinant 
antivenom through cell cultivation. Using venom of the cobra N. melanoleuca as the target, 
this thesis used phage display selection to discover of toxin-binding antibodies, with the 
aim of contributing to an oligoclonal antivenom which may ultimately target multiple 
Elapid venoms.  
Initially, a comparative analysis of the venom used in this study with a previous 
toxicovenomic study of venom from the same species showed that although similar, 
differences in the two venoms were apparent. This finding supports a very recent opinion 
that N. melanoleuca ought to be reclassified into several sub-species. This analysis also 
indicated that the majority of selected toxins were of the neurotoxic 3FTx family as 
expected, although due to the distinctions in the two venoms, a more specific 
characterization of individual toxins was not possible in this study. 
==PAGE:56==
 
 54 
 
Biotinylation of venom fractions prior to phage selection was somewhat successful, 
leaving 7 of 20 toxins properly biotinylated and in the optimal state for immobilization in 
wells. 11 toxins were deemed over-biotinylated which may have caused poor interaction 
between displayed antigen and antibody-displaying phages as proven by previous 
comparable studies.  
Throughout rounds of phage display selection, antibody-displaying phages from the 
Predator and IONTAS libraries with high affinity for N. melanoleuca toxins were selected. 
Results throughout panning rounds were found to be promising, with a plate growth of 
104-106 colony-forming units per milliliter, a finding which was in accordance similar 
studies. A confirming Plate Binding Assay likewise indicated successful selection of toxin-
binding antibodies for six of the nine venom fractions. Lastly, an ELISA was performed to 
confirm the indications of the Plate Binding Assay. This lead to varying indications of 
success, mainly due to repeated errors with the detection antibodies in use. However 
successful finding of selective binders to some of the same venom fractions was confirmed.  
Based on a collective evaluation, it is concluded that selective binders have been found for 
fractions M1 (Predator library), M8 (κ and λ-strains IONTAS), M19 (κ-strain IONTAS) and 
M22 (pooled strains, IONTAS). It is therefore recommended that these libraries are used 
in further sub-cloning studies. Additionally, since binders are found in VH and scFv format 
the target likely being systemically acting neurotoxins, it is proposed that the fragments 
be coupled to a human Fc-region for prolongation of the antibody half-life.  
The findings of this study confirm phage display as a potent method for the discovery of 
toxin-binding antibodies. The selection of antibodies against N. melanoleuca toxins enable 
the initiation of expression studies, and will hopefully lead to the revelation of toxin-
inhibiting abilities through in-vivo testing in mice. The development of an oligoclonal 
antivenom with the potential of improving the lives of millions of snakebite victims, has 
been brought a small step closer.
