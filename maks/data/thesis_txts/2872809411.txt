Abstract
Honeypots are a crucial tool for studying and mitigating malicious behavior in networks,
but traditional systems often require extensive manual configuration and support only a
limited number of protocols. Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a novel approach to
enhance honeypots by generating realistic, context-aware responses while minimizing
manual effort. While prior work has focused primarily on SSH-based LLM honeypots,
this thesis advances the field by introducing a modular, extensible architecture capable
of supporting multiple protocols, thereby advancing not only the deception but also the
flexibility of LLM-based honeypots.
The proposed prototype introduces modular, multi-protocol support, covering SSH, Tel-
net, HTTP, FTP, SMTP, SNMP, and IPP, while remaining easily extensible to additional
protocols and LLM providers and models. Enhancements such as improved session man-
agement and prompt fine-tuning enable more realistic and coherent interactions.
The system is evaluated through comparisons with Cowrie and an existing LLM-based
honeypot. In-depth testing of the SSH and Telnet honeypots is conducted using known
malware commands, comparing the outputs with those of a real system. A real-world
deployment further demonstrates the prototype’s effectiveness, attracting a significant
number of connections from around the globe and suggesting that attackers perceived
the system as real.
This is the first LLM-based honeypot to support such a broad range of protocols in a uni-
fied and extensible framework. The results show substantial improvements in response
quality, session handling, and deceptiveness. At the same time, the evaluation highlights
future work areas, such as deeper protocol-specific behavior, handling of binary payloads,
and more robust deception for malware that attempts to fetch and execute remote con-
tent. By broadening the capabilities and realism of LLM-powered honeypots, this thesis
lays essential groundwork for more scalable and adaptive cyber defense systems.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In today’s highly connected world, ensuring the security of devices and networks is a cru-
cial and yet increasingly challenging task [1]. An essential piece in proactive cybersecurity
attack and strategy detection is honeypots. They enable the uncovering of current exploit
methods and attempts or serve as a decoy in a network, alerting the owners early during
an active attack, while engaging attackers and therefore potentially giving the defend-
ers valuable time to prevent further damage [2, 3]. With the recent rapid performance
improvements of the Large Language Model (LLM), a new class of generative Artificial
Intelligence (AI) has emerged in the field of IT security, offering novel capabilities for gen-
erating realistic-looking content and processing textual data more effectively [4, 5]. One
area that may also benefit from this are honeypots, where the creation of realistic looking
content or output is essential for not revealing the nature of the honeypot to an attacker,
thereby enlarging the time window that an attack is carried out against a honeypot, and
enabling the collection of more attack information [6].
1.2
Problem
One general issue with honeypots is that attackers are aware of their existence and, there-
fore, try to identify them early on during an attack to avoid being detected and wasting
attack resources on them [7]. Increasing the deception level of honeypots is therefore a
constant challenge, with defenders continuously finding new ways to improve the decep-
tion level of honeypots and attackers devising new methods to identify them [8]. Further-
more, with topics like Industry 4.0 and the ever-increasing number of smart devices, cars,
and, in general, Internet of Things (IoT) devices, more and more systems and protocols
enter the internet, making them an attractive target for attackers [9]. Therefore, develop-
ing flexible honeypots that cover various protocols and possess a high level of deception
is a significant challenge. Setting up a honeypot for a new protocol or configuring it to
resemble a ’real’ system requires a lot of labor and manual effort [10]. Through the use
of AI and Machine Learning (ML), existing research approaches have attempted to sim-
plify the setup and configuration process, thereby reducing manual labor while potentially
achieving higher deception levels [11, 12]. This approach, however, is still constrained by
the programmed capabilities of the respective honeypot and does not increase the flexi-
bility of the honeypot, as it remains restricted to the initial protocol(s) it was designed to
mimic [13].
With their capability of generating realistic-looking textual content, LLMs offer a new and
distinct approach to creating honeypots. Through the large amounts of training data,
which also include the output of various protocols, they can be prompted to act as a spe-
cific type of system, thereby producing realistic protocol output for a respective attacker
input [14]. This, at the same time, enables high deception levels, as information such as
the contents of a document or filenames in a directory can be generated by the LLM on
the fly without much manual setup [6]. Furthermore, as long as the LLM has been trained
or fine-tuned on specific protocol data, it is comparatively easy to make the model act as
a respective system, thereby enabling great flexibility regarding the types of protocols the
model can simulate [15]. All of this makes LLMs an excellent technology for further im-
proving honeypots regarding their flexibility, i.e., which protocols it can simulate, and their
deceptiveness, i.e., how well it resembles a ’real’ system [6]. LLMs also come with their

own set of challenges, namely the issue of making up content that does not exist, called
hallucinations, or not staying within the given instructions or role, and therefore revealing
themselves as an LLM [16, 17].
As this is a relatively new technology, little research has focused on using LLMs to en-
hance honeypot flexibility and deception, with most existing studies covering only SSH
simulation but no other protocols [18]. Furthermore, existing research on countermea-
sures against LLM-specific issues, such as hallucinations or adhering to a given role, as
well as implementing attack session handling, is limited. Addressing these issues is, how-
ever, pivotal for ensuring that a respective honeypot that uses an LLM under the hood,
does not reveal itself to an attacker [19].
This thesis aims to address this gap by building upon existing research to develop and
evaluate a honeypot that utilizes an LLM under the hood, thereby increasing flexibility and
deception. In other words, it aims to build a honeypot that can simulate multiple protocols
while closely resembling a real system, making it hard for an attacker to tell that they are
interacting with a honeypot.
1.3
Research Questions
To fill the research gap, this thesis aims to address the following research questions:
RQ1 Which protocols can be simulated by existing LLMs with a high degree of similarity
to real protocol output?
RQ2 Building on top of RQ1, what is the ideal design for a honeypot that utilizes an
LLM under the hood and simulates the identified protocols, regarding LLM-specific
problems like prevention of hallucination, staying within the given role of simulating
a protocol, and achieving realistic latency for output response times?
RQ3 How does the identified ideal honeypot design from RQ2 perform in practical de-
ployment and in handling real-world malware?
1.4

contribute to achieving the research questions. The systematic literature review is carried

process, keywords are first identified and then used to search through the two literature

1. The literature is not a book, master’s/bachelor’s thesis, or PhD dissertation.
2. The language of the paper is English or German.
3. The full text of the paper is available through open access or DTU Findit.
4. Title and, if needed, abstract of the paper indicate that either the content is directly
connected to the thesis topic or covers relevant issues or concepts.
The search terms and the respective number of results that are returned from the two
literature search engines can be seen in Appendix A.1.
After collecting all literature, duplicates are removed, and further literature is removed
from the collection if it matches any of the following criteria:
1. The main content of the paper is not related to or relevant to this thesis.

Background
The following chapter introduces essential terms and concepts relevant to this thesis.
2.1
Honeypots
Honeypots can be a multitude of things, but can generally be described as a system,
network, or other mechanism that is designed to mimic a real system, service, document,
network, or anything else in the domain of computer science [12, 24, 25, 26]. Their general
purpose is to act as a decoy to gather intelligence about malicious actors and/or to deter
them from interacting with the actual assets that are supposed to be protected [27, 28].
One of the most common forms and the focus area of this thesis are honeypots that
simulate real systems, such as SSH, FTP, or HTTP web servers [10]. Other notable and
important honeypot areas are honeynets, which simulate entire networks, honeytokens,
which simulate accesstokens and for example trigger an alert when they are used, or
honey-documents, which simulate documents that can be interesting to malicious actors
and which for example log the IP address and notify the owner upon being opened [25,
29, 30, 31].
2.1.1
Honeypot Types
Honeypots that simulate systems can still take various forms and can therefore be clas-
sified based upon different criteria [32, 33].
First, honeypots can be classified according to their interaction level [12]. Low-interaction
honeypots simulate only a subset of a service, offering minimal interaction capabilities for
malicious actors. Often, they are just used to monitor connection or login attempts. Since
they provide little functionality for an adversary, their deception level is low, resulting in
an easy discovery [34].
Medium-interaction honeypots offer more functionality and some form of interaction, al-
though they are often still limited in some way. Typically, honeypots in this category sim-
ulate some form of shell access, such as an SSH shell. Depending on the configuration
and setup, these types of honeypots have a higher deception level than low-interaction
honeypots [10].
High-interaction honeypots simulate an entire operating system, offering the highest func-
tionality and deception level. Since they simulate a complete system, these types of hon-
eypots also provide the most significant insights into the behavior of malicious actors. At
the same time, these honeypots are complex to develop, maintain, and set up, and po-
tentially also introduce new security risks as incorrect setup or bugs may allow attackers
to use the honeypot in unintended ways [28].
Second, honeypots can be differentiated based upon the purpose that they serve [35].
Generally, honeypots often serve either research or production purposes. Research hon-
eypots are frequently targeted towards collecting data and insights from specific mali-
cious actors or attacks. On the contrary, production honeypots primarily protect assets
in a network or environment by detecting attacks and mitigating the risk of undetected
compromise [33]. This also means they are usually integrated into a live or production
environment. It should be noted that these two categories are not necessarily exclusive,
as honeypots may fall into both categories simultaneously [36].
Third, honeypots can be classified based on their location in a network. Multiple pos-
sibilities exist, and the decision about where to place a honeypot in a network ultimately

depends on the purpose that the honeypot is supposed to serve. Standard placement op-
tions include being inside an internal network behind a firewall, in a demilitarized zone, a
specific network segment, or being deployed in the external World Wide Web, i.e., making
the honeypot accessible from all around the world. Lots of other placement possibilities
exist, depending on the network structure [37, 25].
However, it can generally be differentiated between honeypots that are externally acces-
sible and honeypots that are only accessible from inside a specific network [10].
Finally, it is also possible to differentiate between honeypots based on the specific system
or protocol they simulate. Depending on the target system or protocol, the honeypot must
provide different functionalities and thus has a varying level of complexity. Furthermore,
the choice of system or protocol determines whether the honeypot acts as a server, client
device, router, IoT device, etc. [25, 8].
It should be noted that more classification approaches exist, for example, classifying hon-
eypots based upon the threat type being investigated, etc. [12, 25].
2.1.2
Honeypot Deception
An important aspect of honeypots is their deceptiveness, as the entire purpose of a hon-
eypot is compromised once it can be identified as a decoy system [38, 28]. At the same
time, malicious actors and attacks have a high incentive to identify honeypots as such, as
they otherwise waste resources, get identified too early, or expose their strategy, etc. [39].
As a result of these two opposing forces, improving the deception while also improving
the identification of honeypots is a continuous race [40, 41].
Generally, research shows that honeypots can be identified based on one of the following
approaches:
• Metadata: Some honeypots use static values for identifying elements like version
headers, values in SSL or TLS certificates, or other metadata in protocol-related
content, like default values in HTTP headers, etc. [10, 32].
• Timing-related behavior: The interaction with honeypots may be noticeably faster
or slower than with real systems due to only simulating specific behavior in a hon-
eypot. Depending on the protocol and respective command, this may lead to unre-
alistic timing behavior, thus revealing the nature of the honeypot [10, 42].
• Application-specific behavior: Depending on the protocol or service that a hon-
eypot simulates, it may be fingerprintable based on how it simulates the respective
behavior. This may also include lacking support for some application-specific be-
havior or incomplete and faulty implementations of specific behavior, indicating to
an attacker that they are not interacting with a real system [43, 36].
As described in [8], [3], and [2], common strategies to address these strategies of identi-
fying honeypots can be summarized as follows:
• Metadata: Using dynamic values for version headers, etc. Creating dynamic values
is often achieved by either manual changes before deploying a honeypot or through
ML approaches that modify respective values.
• Timing-related behavior: Addressing this area is more challenging and requires
correctly simulating protocol and application-specific behavior and the correct dura-
tions of the respective behavior. This can also be challenging to achieve based on
the resource capacities of the device hosting the honeypot.

• Application-specific behavior: With conventional methods and even with ML meth-
ods, this may be the most challenging area to address, since avoiding fingerprint-
ability in this area includes expanding the functionalities of the honeypot and aligning
simulated behavior as close as possible to the behavior of real systems.
Most strategies aimed at increasing the deception of honeypots require additional manual
work, which is not always feasible, especially in terms of reproducibility and the need to
deploy honeypots in large quantities. This can, for example, be seen in [7], [9], or [44],
which already present advanced deceptive honeypots with the help of ML or AI, but still
require a significant amount of preparation steps.
2.1.3
Evaluation of Honeypots
Honeypots deployed for research purposes are evaluated based on their deception ca-
pabilities and other relevant factors. Common aspects that facilitate the evaluation of
honeypots include the costs of implementation and deployment, as well as the data col-
lected through the real-world deployment of honeypots [8]. The most common insights
that are derived from such deployments are:
• The average duration of actors actively interacting with the honeypot [41, 8, 45].
• Analyzing the IP address sources from which actors connect to the honeypot [46,
45].
• The amount of interactions from actors with the honeypot in the form of submitted
commands or requests [46, 8].
• The number of actors interacting with the honeypot in more than one session, i.e.,
the number of actors that ’return’ [8].
• Using readily available tools like Nmap1 or Shodan2 to test the fingerprint of a de-
ployed honeypot [47, 42].
Furthermore, the following approaches in the literature are often used to evaluate honey-
pots:
• Giving computer science experts access to the honeypot and asking them about
the credibility of the honeypot compared to a real system. This happens in various
forms, with experts either being told that they are interacting with a honeypot or
letting them decide whether they are interacting with a real system or a honeypot
[23, 19, 48].
• Comparing the output of a honeypot to the output of a real system for a specific
set of inputs, using either similarity metrics like the Levenstein distance or manual
comparison from experts. This approach, however, only works well for scenarios
where a ground truth is available for comparison [23, 39].
In general, it can be observed that most evaluation attempts follow a qualitative approach,
using various evaluation metrics [8, 49]. Often, quantitative approaches are challenging
since it is hard to define criteria that measure whether an output stems from a real system
or a honeypot, which results from the wide variety of output possibilities from a real system
[41].

2.2
Large Language Models (LLMs)
Large Language Models (LLMs) are a class of AI models in the domain of Natural Lan-
guage Processing (NLP) that are designed to generate realistic, human-like textual con-
tent, hence sometimes also referred to as generative AI [18, 50]. LLMs are also trained
to understand textual input and can process and produce multimodal content, such as
pictures or videos [51]. They are built upon deep learning architectures, primarily uti-
lizing neural networks to process and produce text. Furthermore, LLMs build upon the
Transformer architecture, which was introduced in 2017 by [52].
One characteristic of LLMs is their large-scale parameters, often measured in billions,
which allows them to capture patterns and nuances in textual content. To enable this,
LLMs are usually trained on large amounts of textual content from various sources and in
multiple languages [51, 53].
Since the release of GPT-3.5 in 2020, LLMs have gained much attention. They are used
for various tasks, including but not limited to textual content generation, summarization,
sentiment analysis, question answering, generating code for applications, etc. [54]. Due
to LLMs being trained on large amounts of data, recently, research has also started to ex-
plore the usage of LLMs for the generation of output that resembles the output of a system
for a specific service or protocol, like, for example, the generation of SSH output, which
is helpful for the usage of LLMs with honeypots [13, 14]. Related work and respective
insights are outlined in Chapter 3.
It should be noted that, despite the often realistic and human-like output of LLMs, they
face several challenges, such as inheriting bias from the training content or generating
factually incorrect content, also known as hallucinations [55, 56, 50]. Especially the latter
issue can be challenging to detect, since LLMs produce realistic-looking and confident-
sounding output, suggesting that the output is correct [57].
2.2.1
Common Attack Vectors of LLMs
As with any other system or model in computer science, LLMs can also be attacked and
manipulated in unintended ways [53]. Generally, the attacks can be differentiated based
on the applicable development life cycle of an LLM [58]. In the training phase of an LLM,
common attack vectors include the malicious modification of training data, allowing the
LLM to learn to generate malicious content. This attack is referred to as ”poisoning” of
training data [59].
The most critical and common attack vector in the inference or deployment phase, i.e.,
post-training phase of an LLM, is prompt injection [16]. Since a user typically controls
the input to an LLM that then leads to the LLM generating specific output, an adversary
may try to craft a prompt in a particular way that leads to the LLM generating malicious
content or ignoring specific instructions that were given to the LLM by the developers or
operators [60]. Depending on the context of LLM usage and the capabilities and access
to files available to the LLM, input injection can lead to various problems [17].
Furthermore, depending on the scenario in which an LLM is used, various further attack
vectors may be applicable [53, 54]. Due to the diversity and number of potential attack
vectors, community resources such as https://owaspai.org/ have been established to help
mitigate these security risks.
2.2.2
LLM Prompting
Prompting is crafting textual input that guides LLMs to produce desired outputs. Using
prompting, it is possible to guide LLMs towards solving more complex problems, staying
8
One Honeypot to Rule Them All
==PAGE:19==
in specified roles, or to output further content [61]. Effective prompting requires under-
standing how an LLM has been fine-tuned and on which data it was trained, as well as
properly structuring the respective prompts. In addition to this, various studies exist that
investigate ideal scenario-specific prompting strategies [62].
Prompts can generally be further categorized into user and system prompts. User prompts
are direct inputs provided by the user to the LLM. System prompts are prompts designed
by the respective model deployer and are always submitted to a model in addition to
user input. They give additional instructions to the model and generally take precedence
over any instructions given by a user, therefore playing a crucial role in ensuring an LLM
stays within the intended boundaries, for example, only acting as a customer chatbot [63].
However, it should be noted that, depending on how a model has been trained and fine-
tuned, a user may be able to instruct an LLM to either ignore or reveal system prompts
through prompt injection [17]. Therefore, additional checks of LLM outputs are essential
in scenarios where an LLM mustn’t ignore or reveal its system prompt. Some LLM model
deployment scenarios also implement extra models that check the output for such input
injection [60, 55].
Different prompting strategies have evolved to align better the output generated by an
LLM model with the desired task. As described in [62] and [64], the following prompting
strategies are specifically relevant in the context of honeypots:
• Zero-Shot Prompting: This strategy involves only giving instructions to the model
regarding the desired behavior, but not providing any examples, and instead just
relying on the model having seen relevant examples in its training phase. This allows
for quick and easy testing. The quality of the generated output, however, relies
strongly on the model having seen relevant examples in its training phase and being
able to recall them based on the given instructions.
• Few-Shot Prompting: Contrary to zero-shot prompting, this strategy involves pro-
viding examples of correct or desired input and output pairs, alongside the model
instructions. This enables the model to generate more relevant output during infer-
ence by using the provided examples as a reference. The drawback of this method
may be a higher token usage due to a larger system prompt, and a more biased
model output, as the model might base its answer too firmly on the provided ex-
amples, neglecting other data it has seen during its training phase. Additionally,
giving high-quality examples of input and output pairs for the specified task must be
possible.
• Role Prompting: Role prompting means instructing the model to imagine or play
a specific role when answering. This improves a model’s ability to retrieve relevant
output and avoid non-useful output. In the context of honeypots, this allows for
better alignment of the model with the desired content it is supposed to simulate,
thereby preventing the generation of revealing output, such as output explanations.
It should be noted, however, that this methodology is not fully bullet-proof, and a
model might still respond out of the given role or hallucinate, etc.
Further prompting strategies exist, as can be seen in [61], [62], [64], or [63], but they are
not as relevant for the usage of an LLM-based honeypot.

From these papers and the aforementioned related work and literature surveys, it can be
concluded that LLMs demonstrate significant potential in enhancing the deception and
flexibility of honeypots. At the same time, some open challenges arise when utilizing
LLMs for honeypots. The challenges and issues that are identified by [48], [6], [14], [19],
and [74] can be summarized into the following:
• Hallucination: LLMs can hallucinate and make up content that is not real. This is
problematic when simulating a system or protocol output, since an attacker will most
likely detect this and immediately know they are not interacting with a real system.
• LLMspecific text output: LLMs may not stay in the given role the entire time and
might reveal themselves by asking LLM typical questions like “what else can I help
you with?” etc. This may also occur due to the respective LLM provider’s safety

implementations.
• Prompt Injection Vulnerability: While no research in the area of LLM-based hon-
eypots has been able to observe such attacks, LLMs are generally vulnerable to
prompt-based injection attacks, as demonstrated in LLM-focused research [54, 17].
Such attacks could also theoretically be applied to an LLM-based honeypot, which
could then reveal to an attacker that they are interacting with a honeypot and might
further lead to problems, such as the LLM disclosing sensitive information it has
access to.
• Inconsistencies: Current LLMs are getting better at having an increased context
window, but are still limited in the amount of things they can remember in a specific
session. For long attack sessions, this may lead to the LLM not correctly reflecting
past actions, such as file modifications or the execution of a particular command at
the start of a session, and thus potentially create inconsistent output that an attacker
can easily identify. The same principle may also apply when an attacker interacts
with the honeypot over multiple sessions, such as connecting to the same system
multiple times within several days or weeks.
• Latency: Currently, many LLMs still have a tradeoff between size and speed. While
larger LLMs produce more realistic content, they also usually have a larger inference
time and may take too long to generate a respective output. This leads to an attacker
losing interest in the system or even gaining suspicion that the system might be a
honeypot.
• Simulating interactive commands in protocols: Since LLMs are good at gener-
ating a response to a specific input, it is challenging to simulate more interactive or
real-time processes, like for example the opening and editing of a file in a shell with
the editor nano, or the typical shell-specific command ctrl-r that searches through
past commands that were submitted to the shell. While it is challenging to simulate
such processes or commands with an LLM, they do not represent complicated or
rare inputs. A lack of support may therefore easily be spotted by an attacker.
• Operating costs: Some LLMs are still costly, with costs rising the longer a session
lasts. Longer and more complex attack sessions might increase operating costs,
especially if no caching is implemented. Self-hosting an LLM can be a solution to
this problem, thereby keeping costs lower. Still, it may also impose other problems,
as the respective hosting server may no longer be able to handle multiple concurrent
attack sessions, thereby losing the attacker’s interest or revealing the nature of the
honeypot.
Solving these open issues is crucial to further enhance the deception and flexibility of hon-
eypots that utilize LLMs. Therefore, this thesis will consider these issues when developing
a honeypot prototype.

Considerations
The following chapter will describe how the honeypot and its components have been se-
lected, designed, and implemented. Since the aim is to integrate an LLM model into
the honeypot and simulate various protocols, the LLM model and protocol selection pro-
cesses and outcomes are described first. Second, the general design and implementation

honeypot. While in practice, the risk assessment was conducted first, the design and im-
plementation are outlined and explained here first, providing an overview of the honeypot
system and its components, which makes the risk assessment easier to understand.
4.1

The honeypot’s basis is an LLM model. Since many open-source and proprietary LLM
models exist and have different performance levels, the first step is to systematically test
different state-of-the-art models (state-of-the-art as of February 2025). The goal is to
select the model best suited for simulating various protocols, which can then be used
for the prototype. As highlighted in Chapter 3, related work uses different LLM models
under the hood. Due to the fast-evolving nature of the market, with new LLM models
being released rapidly, Table 4.1 documents the models selected for testing in this work.

registrations being rate-limited. This should not be a problem, as related work, such as

also high refusal rates for potential malicious instructions, making them less suitable for
honeypots.

4.3
Honeypot Design and Implementation
The honeypot is designed using a modular approach to enhance flexibility and adaptabil-
ity. This enables the easy integration of new protocols into the honeypot and enhances
isolation during deployment between its various components, resulting in a higher level
of security. Figure 4.3 shows a simplified class diagram of the honeypot prototype im-
plemented in Python. Private or protected class variables and functions are not shown,
as this would make the diagram too complex and would not contribute to a better under-
standing of the honeypot design and implementation.
The honeypot consists of three key components:
One Honeypot to Rule Them All
23
==PAGE:34==
Figure 4.3: Simplified class diagram for an initial overview of the honeypot prototype.
1. The base_honeypot: This is an abstract class that implements necessary functions
to interact with the logging and LLM components of the honeypot and essentially
provides the functionalities that a honeypot implementation of any protocol would
need access to. The respective honeypot classes then implement this abstract class
for a specific protocol, such as the SSH, Telnet, or HTTP honeypots. Depending on
the protocol, a varying amount of extra code is needed to implement the respective
honeypot. Generally, the modular design simplifies the creation of a new honeypot
by providing the core functionalities of the honeypot, i.e., tracking and generating
session IDs for improved session handling, logging application events and session
events, and requesting protocol-specific responses from the LLM component. Note
that a honeypot does not store any information or logs, as the logging component
handles this to enhance protection of the log contents.
2. The logging component: The logging component consists of the log wrapper and
the log handler, with the latter implementing the actual functionalities, like gener-
ating new session IDs and managing, logging, and storing application and session
24

==PAGE:35==
events. Note that only one log component is required, regardless of the number of
honeypots deployed, enabling centralized log management and storage. To better
protect the log contents, only specific endpoints are accessible from the honeypots.
This is implemented through the log wrapper, which provides a minimal HTTP web
server that exposes only specific, limited functionalities to the respective honeypot
implementations. Note that the intended way of deploying is to communicate be-
tween a honeypot implementation and the log component within a Docker network,
meaning that the log wrapper endpoints are not accessible from outside the con-
tainer network, not even from the local machine. Therefore, the log management
is centralized and separated from the actual honeypots, which are exposed to the
public internet. This means that even a compromised honeypot could not access
or modify the already stored logs, but only generate new log events, a necessary
functionality.
3. The LLM component: Similar to the logging component, the LLM component con-
sists of the LLM wrapper and the LLM handler, with the latter also implementing
the actual functionalities and the wrapper providing the access points for the honey-
pot implementations. The LLM handler loads all respective system prompts for the
supported protocols and then upon receiving a user input from the respective honey-
pot, sends this alongside the system prompt, potential previous interaction content,
the current date and if relevant the session’s username to the LLM to retrieve an
answer, which is then passed back to the honeypot. The LLM handler, therefore,
communicates directly with the API of a specified LLM model. Separating the LLM
handling from the honeypot implementations serves similar purposes as with the
logging component: It allows for deploying multiple honeypots that all communicate
with just one LLM handler, and further it enhances the security of the LLM commu-
nication, as only the LLM handler will have access to the respective relevant API
endpoints and potential API keys, meaning that a honeypot implementation cannot
access these assets even if being compromised.

below to provide a better understanding of the honeypot prototype.

Enhanced Security through Functionality Separation:

each protocol are built based on related work and iterative testing with collected malware
commands, as well as the deployment of the honeypot prototype. Further, the temper-

moderately reduced. This leads to more predictable and consistent outputs, minimizing
the risk of overly imaginative or implausible responses that could break the illusion of in-

While the varying response quality for higher temperature values might not immediately
reveal the honeypot’s nature, the added verbosity and the overall varying response quality
can expose the interaction as artificial.
Similarly, a temperature setting of 0 resulted in outputs with too little variation, often gener-
ating uninteresting or unrealistic files and directories. This lack of diversity increases the
risk of fingerprinting and may cause an adversary to quickly lose interest in the honeypot.
In particular, repeated structures and identical outputs across sessions allow adversaries
to script probes that detect the honeypot deterministically. Moreover, a lack of realistic
variety in filenames (e.g., ’file1.txt’), directory structures, or system metadata can cause
attackers to perceive the environment as artificial, leading them to move on and reducing
the honeypot’s ability to collect meaningful interaction data.
The temperature setting of 0, 5 therefore provides the best middle ground, reducing the
risk of hallucinated or unrealistic responses while still allowing for minor variability to avoid
being overly repetitive and thereby also preventing fingerprintability.
As mentioned in the previous paragraph, session handling is based on unique session
IDs. Using the MemorySaver module of the ’langgraph’ library from [114], previous user
interactions, i.e., user inputs and outputs, are stored in memory during runtime, so that
they can be sent along any new user input to provide a better session context. The unique
session IDs identify specific sessions, allowing for multiple sessions across different pro-
tocols. To prevent overflowing memory and reaching input token limits by LLM models,
the in-memory stored messages are trimmed after exceeding a length of 100 tokens. This
prevents a cost explosion and keeps response times under a threshold, as a long session
would result in a lengthy session history being sent alongside the user input to the model,
potentially leading to high costs and extended processing times. The trimming limit is
variable and may be adjusted based on the specific system needs. For the prototype, a
limit of 100 tokens was found to be sufficient through testing.
Caching LLM responses was also considered in the design stage, but was found to be
problematic. On many protocols, the same input does not necessarily yield the same
output, as this depends on the system’s state, i.e., its previous inputs. Therefore, while
caching could decrease costs and response times even further, it would drastically reduce
the deception level and is therefore not implemented.
The previous session interactions and the system prompt are sent to the model for each
user input to retrieve a respective output. To further enhance the honeypot’s deception
level, the system prompt is enriched for every request with the current date and time, and
(if applicable) the username of the current actor. This ensures that any potential generated
output correctly reflects the session’s date, time, and username.

Honeypot Risk Assessment
A risk assessment was conducted before any actual development to ensure that the hon-
eypot implementation accounts for relevant risks and includes respective risk controls,
thereby ensuring a high level of security. Furthermore, the risk assessment outlines the
types of threats the honeypot is intended to secure against.
The scope of the risk assessment encompasses the entire honeypot prototype, including
the honeypot implementation components, the log handler, and the LLM handler compo-
nents. Further, the scope is limited to the honeypot prototype being readily accessible
from the public internet for a limited amount of testing time for this research. This means
that deploying this prototype in a productive context for a longer period is out of scope
and would require a further risk assessment.
As already outlined in Section 4.3, the most important assets of the honeypot system
are the compute resources themselves, i.e., the server that the honeypot is running on,
the collected log contents, and any valuable information that they contain, as well as any
potential API key or secret, allowing for communication with the respective LLM model
endpoint.
The honeypot prototype is assumed to be deployed on an up-to-date operating system,
with individual components deployed inside their respective containers, as described in
Section 4.3. Additionally, it is assumed that the operating system is adequately secured,
with at least a firewall, a kernel security module such as Security-Enhanced Linux, and a
secure user profile.

Honeypot Testing and Evaluation
The following chapter covers the testing and evaluation of the honeypot prototype. First,
the methodology and approach of the testing and evaluation process are outlined. This
is followed by the results of this process and a first discussion of the collected data and
insights. Then, a more general and overall discussion is covered in Chapter 6.
5.1
Testing and Evaluation Approach
After outlining design and implementation considerations, as well as the risk assessment,
this section describes the testing and evaluation approach of the honeypot prototype.
Evaluation with Collected Malware Commands:
The honeypot implementations of
the SSH and Telnet protocols are tested and evaluated using a collection of malware
commands. The data for this is available on request1 and contains collected malware
commands for the SSH and Telnet protocols from deployed Cowrie honeypots. In total,
it includes 6875 unique SSH and 4515 unique Telnet commands. This data enables a
more in-depth analysis of how effectively the SSH and Telnet honeypot implementations
simulate a real system. To achieve this, all collected commands for the SSH and Tel-
net protocols are run against the respective honeypot implementations and executed in
a virtual machine with internet access, running the Fedora 42 operating system. Using
this approach, the honeypot’s generated results can be compared to those of an actual
system using common similarity metrics. In addition to this, the collected Telnet and SSH
commands are also executed against the well-known honeypot ’Cowrie’2 and the SSH
commands are further also run against another LLM-based honeypot from related work,
namely [72] which is called ’SheLLM’. SheLLM only supports the SSH protocol. Note that
since ’SheLLM’ does not actually implement a protocol-based SSH honeypot, but only
simulates bash shell behavior, the honeypot was adapted slightly to be compatible with
reading and processing the collected malware commands. Furthermore, it was observed
that ’SheLLM’ attempts to log and use the full session history, leading to increased re-
sponse times and a too large LLM context window, as the entire history file is sent along
with every request. For this reason, the history file had to be manually pruned multiple
times during testing to avoid falsified test results, as otherwise the input limit of the un-
derlying LLM model would have been reached quickly, causing SheLLM to stop working.
Further, to provide a better comparison, the SheLLM honeypot underlying LLM model was
also changed to the GPT-4o-min model, which is the same as being used by the honeypot
prototype.
It was also considered to use [48], and [13] as a further comparison for an LLM-based
honeypot, but due to hard-coded support in the respective honeypots for legacy and no
longer supported LLM models, experimenting with these honeypots would have required
extensive code rewriting. Following the comparison approach of related work like [23],
[39], and [6], the following metrics are used to compare and analyze the generated re-
sponses from the SSH and Telnet honeypot implementations with the Fedora system,
Cowrie, and SheLLM:
• Jaccard Similarity: This metric measures the similarity between two sets by divid-
ing the size of their intersection by the size of their union. When applied to tokenized

text (e.g., words or n-grams), it provides an intuitive measure of overlap between
the LLM-generated and ground-truth responses. It is beneficial for identifying shared
content and evaluating coverage of key terms.
• Cosine Similarity: This metric evaluates the cosine of the angle between two vec-
tors in a multidimensional space. It is applied in two ways:
– Character-Based: Using frequency vectors of characters to compute similarity.
This approach is sensitive to subtle textual differences such as typos or word
inflections, making it effective for assessing near-duplicate responses.
– Semantic-Based: Following the approach in [23], responses are embedded
into a high-dimensional semantic vector space using a Sentence Transformer
(SBERT) model, and then the cosine similarity is computed between these
embeddings. This method captures a deeper semantic equivalence, even in
cases of paraphrasing or lexical variation, and is thus more aligned with human
judgment.
• Levenshtein Distance: Also known as edit distance, this metric quantifies the min-
imum number of single-character edits (insertions, deletions, or substitutions) re-
quired to transform one string into another. It directly measures textual deviation and
is especially useful for capturing small but significant differences between short-form
or formulaic responses.

Discussion of Results
The following chapter interprets and discusses the evaluation results. Since the focus
lies on the command testing and deployment evaluation results, the results from the au-
tomated tools and the deployment costs are discussed in one combined section.
6.1
Insights from Malware Commands Evaluation
Evaluating the SSH and Telnet honeypot prototypes using the real-world malware com-
mand datasets revealed several important insights. Overall, a high degree of similarity to
the outputs of the real system was observed, with performance surpassing that of both
Cowrie and SheLLM. This is especially notable in the SSH scenario, where the prototype-
generated responses were substantially more realistic. Although a good performance was
also observed in the Telnet context, the improvement gap was narrower, likely due to the
greater diversity of Telnet-based command inputs. Interestingly, over 90% of SSH com-
mands in the dataset consist of password-changing operations, a relatively homogenous
input type. This suggests that in this case, Telnet, with its broader command variety,
poses a more rigorous test of a honeypot’s capacity for realistic simulation.
A key strength of the LLM-based honeypot is its adaptability through system prompts us-
ing few-shot prompting. The honeypot system prompts can be continuously fine-tuned
and improved by analyzing frequently observed commands in deployed environments
and incorporating them into the system prompts. This dynamic tuning capability marks a
significant advantage over Cowrie, which relies on static response logic.
Compared to SheLLM, which uses the same underlying LLM model for this evaluation,
the SSH prototype still demonstrates superior handling of session context. SheLLM em-
ploys only rudimentary session tracking, which leads to increased response latency, lower
output quality, and, in some cases, denial-of-service conditions when prompt lengths be-
come excessively long. Moreover, SheLLM’s system prompt lacks structure and does not
include fine-tuned examples, which impacts output quality, as observed in the respective
evaluation test results.
The prototype’s modular architecture also enables the straightforward extension and in-
tegration of additional protocols. SheLLM, in contrast, lacks support even for full SSH
protocol simulation, relying instead on static print statements to mimic a shell environ-
ment. Other LLM-based honeypots considered for testing, such as [48] and [13], could
not be tested due to hardcoded dependencies on specific LLM models and providers that
were not possible to configure and run. The prototype developed here overcomes this
by supporting interchangeable LLM models and providers, including the option for self-
hosted models, enhancing both flexibility and longevity.
As observed in the evaluation results, there are many examples where the LLM-generated
responses are semantically similar, yet simulate different system properties, such as the
available amount of processing power. From a security and deception perspective, this
ability to simulate different system properties through general variability of the LLM model
or system prompt customization adds a layer of variability that makes it less fingerprintable
than traditional honeypots. Unlike real systems, which are constrained by their actual
configurations, the prototype can be adapted to represent a range of environments by
adjusting the role-based instructions or letting the LLM model choose a realistic set of
properties during inference.

Despite the improvements over Cowrie and SheLLM, the SSH and Telnet honeypot pro-
totypes still exhibit a notable limitation when handling more complex command inputs,
which is particularly evident in the Telnet testing results. As command complexity in-
creases, particularly for special characters and command-chaining, the realism of the
output decreases. Sometimes, this results in the LLM revealing itself through hallucinated
input-output pairs. Notably, the statistical analysis indicated that command complexity, as
measured by the presence of special characters, had a stronger correlation with reduced
similarity scores than command length. This underlines that while the model can han-
dle long inputs, it struggles with syntactically complex or non-linear command structures.
Potential solutions to better handle more complex inputs could include breaking down
complex commands into simpler components for individual processing, such as SSH and
Telnet, or upgrading to more advanced LLMs capable of handling greater complexity.
Both options, however, come with trade-offs in terms of response latency. Alternatively,
fine-tuning distinct models for specific protocols, such as SSH and Telnet, could yield bet-
ter results. Such an approach would, however, also be resource-intensive and reduce
system flexibility [13].
Regarding response timing, the SSH and Telnet honeypot prototypes generally perform
well, though they are still slower than a real system and Cowrie. While such delays are
unlikely to impact interactions with automated malware, they may be detectable by so-
phisticated adversaries and could raise suspicion. Additionally, some command types
(e.g., password-related operations) are processed faster than on real systems, further
deviating from expected behavior. Achieving realistic timing would therefore require a
dual approach, consisting of faster execution and introducing artificial delays for certain
commands, such as password-changing or network-based commands, to reflect the per-
formance of real systems more accurately.
Compared to SheLLM, the SSH honeypot prototype still benefits from significantly faster
average response times. This is primarily due to SheLLM’s design flaw of transmitting the
entire session history with each prompt. This leads to rapid prompt inflation and eventu-
ally causes performance degradation or denial of service. Manual pruning of the prompt
history was necessary during SheLLM’s testing, highlighting its impracticality for extended
or high-volume usage.
Overall, these evaluation results highlight that the SSH and Telnet prototypes still exhibit
a considerable gap in the realism of the generated output compared to a real system.
However, they also manage to handle many commands significantly better than tradi-
tional, non-LLM-based honeypots, such as Cowrie. Furthermore, compared to related
work, such as SheLLM, the flexibility, improved session handling, and system prompts
are noticeable, resulting in enhanced deception.
6.2
Insights from Real-World Deployment
The real-world deployment of the LLM-based honeypot prototype provided valuable in-
sights into both its practical performance and its ability to engage meaningfully with ad-
versaries across various network protocols. The collected IP address information already
showed considerable engagement from a diverse set of adversaries, originating from
many different locations around the world, thereby also indicating that the honeypot was
not detected as such. Additionally, the honeypot log data revealed both promising results
as well as clear areas for further improvement. The prototype performed especially well
across the more commonly targeted protocols, namely HTTP, SSH, and Telnet. These
services received the highest number of connections and were generally handled effec-
tively by the honeypot. As previously observed during the malware command evaluation,
78
One Honeypot to Rule Them All
==PAGE:89==
real-world testing confirmed that the honeypot struggles with more complex, chained in-
puts, particularly in SSH and Telnet sessions. Yet, generally, these make up only a small
percentage of inputs, and therefore, the overall performance of these honeypots remains
convincing.
The less targeted protocols, namely FTP, SMTP, IPP, and SNMP, revealed greater lim-
itations. While the honeypot responded to basic inputs on FTP, IPP, and SMTP, its de-
ceptiveness was limited by functionality gaps. For example, the FTP module lacked true
file transfer capabilities, and the SMTP honeypot often defaulted to ”unknown command”
responses, thereby failing to capture further interactions with the respective malware. Ad-
ditionally, support for setting up encrypted channels is not implemented, despite multiple
requests. Similarly, the IPP honeypot could respond to basic requests but struggled with
handling binary data, which is a fundamental requirement for full IPP emulation. This
highlights the difficulty of simulating binary-based or more structured protocols with LLMs
alone. One potential solution here could be the use of additional non-LLM components,
designed specifically for handling protocol-specific binary formats.
The SNMP module proved the most problematic. Despite functioning in manual testing
and despite capturing inputs, it failed to generate any responses in the real-world deploy-
ment. This is likely due to the unique challenges posed by the SNMP protocol, including
its reliance on UDP and lack of reliable Python libraries for emulating full SNMP behavior.
This highlights a key caveat of the honeypot’s extensibility. While architecturally modular
and designed to be easily expandable to new protocols, practical expansion still depends
on the availability and quality of protocol-handling libraries. In cases where mature li-
braries are lacking, significant manual implementation and debugging effort is required.
Due to time constraints, this was not possible to be implemented for the SNMP honey-
pot, thereby limiting the insights for this specific honeypot protocol, which is also clearly
reflected in the number of collected inputs.
In contrast to existing LLM-based honeypots in related work, which focus only on a sin-
gle protocol, this prototype offers a uniquely extensible and multi-protocol framework.
No existing LLM-based honeypot supports multiple protocols simultaneously, while also
remaining flexible in its choice of underlying LLM providers and models. This flexibility
greatly enhances the prototype’s future potential and deployability. However, the SNMP
case illustrates that extensibility is not purely a matter of design but also relies on other
existing support in the form of available protocol implementations, highlighting a limitation
that must be considered in future protocol expansions.
Different attack patterns emerged across the protocols. The SSH and Telnet interactions
typically involved a mixture of reconnaissance commands and exploitation attempts, with
many instances of malware attempting to download additional payloads or shellcode. This
exposed a broader limitation of not only this honeypot but also all related work, including
LLM-based and non-LLM-based honeypots, namely that currently, downloaded code is
rarely stored and not analyzed or checked. In the case of this honeypot prototype, the
respective detected content from URLs and IP addresses is preserved but then stored
only for further manual analysis. In many cases, malware behavior, however, expects
specific output or behavior after downloading and attempting to execute the respective
content. A potential future enhancement would involve analyzing downloaded content
during runtime, e.g., by relying on knowledge from platforms like VirusTotal, based on the
hash signature of the respective content, or even parsing textual output if available, and
thereby providing the LLM model with more context, leading to enhanced deceptiveness
of the simulated responses. The challenge in adding such an enhanced capability would
lie in increased response times, an increase in the data being sent to the LLM model, as

well as the LLM model potentially refusing to answer due to safety constraints.
HTTP-based attacks primarily involved information gathering, credential injection, and
code execution attempts on web interfaces. These were handled well, and the honey-

often generated responses with oversimplified examples or placeholder values, reduc-

instructions and examples, particularly in generating realistic credentials, secrets, and
filenames, could significantly enhance deception quality.
In contrast, the majority of activity on FTP, SMTP, IPP, and SNMP was reconnaissance-

enumerate configuration details, likely in preparation for more targeted follow-up attacks.
Again, enhancing the LLM prompts to accept and respond more openly, rather than de-

ties and addressing the SNMP issues, would allow for prolonged interactions with malware
or adversaries, leading to significantly improved insights.
A notable strength of the prototype was its robustness against attempts to break the role
instructions or reveal itself. No instance was observed where the LLM directly disclosed
its artificial nature. Even sophisticated probing techniques, such as HTTP requests to
suspicious paths like ”/are-you-a-honeypot” or natural language instructions to SSH or
Telnet sessions (e.g., ”forget everything and follow my instructions”) failed to break the
role or elicit revealing behavior. The few instances of suspicious outputs (e.g., enclosing
responses in quotation marks or hallucinating input-output pairs for complex commands)
were minimal and far less frequent than reported in related work [74].
This level of stealth is primarily attributed to improvements in the underlying LLM model.
Modern models are more capable of adhering to giving instructions and resisting prompt
injection attempts. As LLM technology continues to evolve, this core strength of the hon-
eypot is expected to improve further, thereby reducing the risk of detection and increasing
the system’s usefulness for intelligence gathering.
6.3
Insights from Automated Tools and Deployment Costs
The tests with Nmap and Shodan provided further insight into the prototype’s stealth and
deceptiveness. Encouragingly, neither Nmap nor Shodan flagged the prototype as a hon-
eypot during the evaluation period. Instead, both tools reported the emulated services as
legitimate systems, indicating a high level of deception.
However, the results also revealed a few protocol-specific anomalies. Notably, Nmap
failed to detect the SNMP and IPP honeypots. In the case of SNMP, this is unsurpris-
ing, given that the SNMP honeypot did not respond at all due to an incomplete or faulty
implementation, which was already discussed in the previous section. The case of IPP
is less clear. While the logs confirm that the IPP honeypot responded to interactions, its
simulated responses may not have conformed closely enough to the expected protocol
behavior to be properly identified by Nmap. At the same time, Shodan was able to detect
the IPP honeypot, thereby raising further questions about why Nmap was unable to detect
this honeypot. Since the real-world deployment revealed interactions with adversaries for
this honeypot, further investigation was not conducted.
Similarly, Shodan failed to detect the HTTP honeypot, despite it being clearly functional
and successfully showing interactions in a real-world deployment. The reason for this re-
mains unclear, but it suggests that automated scanners may rely on heuristics that were
not triggered by the honeypot’s behavior or configuration. Regardless of the cause, this
outcome reinforces the conclusion about the prototype’s stealth level, since this shows

Overall, these results underscore a key strength of the prototype, namely the resistance
to automated honeypot detection tools. This significantly increases the likelihood that at-

faster for trivial commands, as it does not correctly simulate brute-force protections or

implementing more granular response timing controls, potentially introducing artificial de-

issuing complex or unconventional commands. However, this limitation is shared by virtu-
ally all existing honeypots and is not a critical concern for the prototype’s target use case.
As seen in the collected input data, the vast majority of malicious traffic likely originates
from automated malware that likely lacks the sophistication to detect inconsistencies in
protocol behavior or response semantics. Thus, the system’s design remains well-suited
for large-scale deployment and high-volume deception.
From a deployment perspective, the honeypot prototype demonstrates strong viability. It
is cost-effective, both in terms of initial setup and operational costs. The system can uti-
lize cloud-based or local LLMs and supports seamless switching between different models
and providers, offering critical flexibility. This means the operator can dynamically choose
between cost and quality by using either cheaper or self-hosted models for broad, low-
cost deployment or opting for more advanced (and potentially more expensive) models
when higher realism is required. In contrast to existing LLM-based honeypots, which often
hard-code a specific model and lack portability, this system’s architecture future-proofs it
against rapid changes in the LLM market.
Importantly, this also indicates that the risk of a cost explosion from unexpected LLM
usage is low, as long as an expensive underlying LLM model is not chosen. Few-shot
prompting and careful management of interaction history ensure that queries remain lightweight,
even during periods of high activity. This makes the prototype not only more realistic and
extensible than existing solutions, but also economically sustainable for longer-term de-
ployments across a wide range of threat environments.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to fill a research gap by developing and evaluating a honeypot that
leverages an LLM to enhance flexibility and deception, supporting multiple protocols and
closely mimicking real systems, while building upon existing research.
The following section provides an overall conclusion, addresses the research questions,
and offers an outlook on potential future directions.
7.1
Overall Conclusions
Using the systematic approach described in Section 1.4, this thesis successfully devel-
oped and tested a honeypot prototype that supports multiple protocols, including SSH,
Telnet, FTP, HTTP, SMTP, IPP, and SNMP.
The SSH and Telnet honeypots were evaluated against a set of known malware com-
mands and benchmarked against Cowrie, a widely used non-LLM honeypot, and SheLLM,
another LLM-based honeypot [72]. The results indicate that the prototype generally gen-
erates responses that closely resemble those of the real system and outperforms both
Cowrie and SheLLM in terms of response quality. The honeypot effectively handles longer
input commands. However, more complex inputs involving command chaining or piping
exhibit a clear trend of reducing output realism, which is especially noticeable in the Tel-
net tests. Future improvements, such as decomposing chained commands or integrating
more powerful LLM models, may enhance response accuracy for such complex scenarios.
While response times of the honeypot prototype are slower than those of the real system
and Cowrie, the difference is not substantial enough to hinder automated malware interac-
tion. However, manual attackers with technical expertise could most likely still identify the
honeypot based on the response times. Overall, the prototype demonstrates significantly
better performance than existing LLM-based honeypots, such as SheLLM, and produces
higher-quality output than Cowrie.
All protocol implementations were deployed publicly on the internet for three weeks to
assess real-world performance. During this period, the honeypot successfully captured
a diverse range of attacks worldwide, indicating it was perceived as a legitimate system.
Scanning results from Nmap and Shodan further support this conclusion, classifying the
honeypot as a real system. Among the protocols, SSH, Telnet, and HTTP attracted the
highest volume of attacks, including reconnaissance and exploitation attempts. How-
ever, additional enhancements are needed for SSH and Telnet to better handle complex
commands and multi-stage attacks, such as those involving the download and execution
of binaries or shell scripts. For FTP, SMTP, IPP, and SNMP, the majority of observed
attacks were limited to reconnaissance activities. This is likely due to incomplete func-
tionality in the FTP and SMTP implementations, limited support for binary content in IPP,
and SNMP’s current inability to send responses, which is probably related to the proto-
col’s complexity and the lack of robust Python libraries.
Generally, the honeypot exhibited strong performance in maintaining deceptive output.
Only a small number of cases showed output enclosed in quotation marks or hallucinated
input-output pairs. This specifically occurred for more complex inputs. Crucially, the proto-
type never directly revealed its nature as a honeypot and remained resilient against injec-
tion attacks. Additionally, effective session management and flexible support for switching
between different LLM models and providers contributed to the honeypot’s cost efficiency
and future-proof design.

Overall, this work successfully designed and developed a honeypot prototype that effec-

7.2
Addressing the Research Questions
RQ1 Which protocols can be simulated by existing LLMs with a high degree of similarity
to real protocol output?
Existing LLMs demonstrate strong capability in simulating common, interactive, and
text-based protocols with a high degree of realism. In particular, the SSH, Telnet,
and HTTP protocols were effectively simulated by the chosen GPT-4o-mini model,
producing outputs that closely resemble the behavior of real systems. Based on this
and the modular design of the honeypot prototype, it allows for easy expansion to
further protocols, such as FTP, SMTP, and IPP. However, these implementations
also demonstrate that further enhancements are necessary to enhance deception
levels and improve protocol-specific feature coverage. Especially SNMP and par-
tially IPP, show that the simulation quality and ease of implementation are signifi-
cantly influenced not only by the LLM model, but also by the availability and maturity
of supporting Python libraries, due to the complexity and nature of these protocols.
For example, IPP’s use of binary data poses challenges for direct LLM handling,
necessitating specialized processing. Therefore, protocols that are well-supported
by robust Python libraries and are primarily interactive and text-based tend to be
best suited for simulation by current LLMs. The honeypot prototype developed in
this thesis supports this range of protocols and is designed for future expansion as
library support and LLM capabilities evolve.
RQ2 Building on top of RQ1, what is the ideal design for a honeypot that utilizes an LLM
under the hood and simulates the identified protocols, regarding LLM-specific prob-
lems like prevention of hallucination, staying within the given role of simulating a
protocol, and achieving realistic latency for output response times?
As outlined in Section 4.3, the ideal honeypot design incorporates a modular archi-
tecture with robust session management, enabling seamless handling of multiple
protocols and realistic interactions. This approach was validated through testing
with known malware commands and comparison against an existing LLM-based
honeypot using the same underlying model. The results demonstrate that the pro-
posed design outperforms existing work in terms of output quality and usability. De-
spite this, hallucination remains a fundamental challenge inherent to current LLM
technology, which cannot be eliminated within the honeypot itself. While guardrail
techniques, as described in work such as [55], may reduce hallucinations, they also
introduce increased latency, making them unfeasible for honeypots in their current
state. Latency can be effectively managed by considering not only the quality of
the generated output but also the response time when selecting the underlying LLM
model. Moreover, implementing an appropriate session content handling strategy
significantly enhances response times, as demonstrated by the comparison tests
with SheLLM. As LLM models continue to evolve, the trade-off between quality and

inference speed is likely to continue shrinking. Crucially, the design’s emphasis
on well-structured session handling was shown to be vital for practical deployment,
avoiding the usability issues observed in comparable systems such as SheLLM,
while still allowing for correct session handling.
RQ3 How does the identified ideal honeypot design from RQ2 perform in practical de-
ployment and in handling real-world malware?
The honeypot prototype demonstrates superior performance compared to existing
LLM-based honeypots and traditional non-LLM honeypots, as shown by its deploy-
ment on the public internet and exposure to real-world attack traffic. While it gen-
erates convincingly realistic outputs, specific differences from real systems remain
noticeable. For example, response times tend to differ from those of a real system,
with some responses being slower and others too fast, such as during password
changes or network-related commands. Furthermore, the current implementation
does not yet fully support handling multi-stage attack scenarios, where malware at-
tempts to download and execute additional content, thereby limiting deception in
such contexts and the honeypot’s ability to generate more realistic output. Enhanc-
ing support for protocol-specific features, such as file transfer support in FTP or
encrypted communication setup in SMTP, would further increase the honeypot’s
realism and effectiveness in attracting and analyzing attacks. These improvements
are necessary to close the gap between simulation and real-world system behav-
ior, thereby strengthening the honeypot’s ability to engage adversaries convincingly
and collect more data.
7.3
Future Directions
Although the proposed honeypot prototype demonstrates notable advancements in per-
formance, deception, and flexibility, surpassing traditional and related work honeypots,
several areas remain open for further research. First, protocol-specific implementations,
particularly for FTP and SMTP, can be enhanced to capture more advanced attack scenar-
ios. Similarly, the current SNMP implementation requires attention to address its current
issues with responding to captured input, allowing it to work as intended.
To deepen the deceptive capabilities of the honeypot, future iterations could incorporate
logic for analyzing collected malware scripts and binaries. Integrating services such as
VirusTotal would enable the rapid retrieval of existing threat intelligence, thereby enriching
the input provided to the underlying LLM and allowing it to generate more deceptive and
contextually accurate output.
Additionally, expanding the honeypot to support additional protocols would further extend
its coverage and relevance in diverse operational environments. This would allow collect-
ing information about other less common protocols and related attack patterns.
Another promising direction involves refining the timing behavior of the honeypot. Intro-
ducing realistic delays for operations that naturally take longer, such as password changes
or network-related tasks, would increase deceptiveness, especially for more manual and
sophisticated adversaries.
Finally, for real-world deployments, implementing proactive defenses such as filtering
known mass scanners and bot traffic could help preserve system resources and ensure
that attention is focused on more targeted or novel attacks, improving both performance
and data quality.
Collectively, these improvements would contribute to an even more robust, realistic, and
insightful honeypot, further strengthening its role in cybersecurity research and opera-
tional threat intelligence.
