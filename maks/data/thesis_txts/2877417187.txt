Abstract
This Master’s thesis investigates frontal midline theta activity measured with electroen-
cephalography (EEG) as a neural marker of cognitive function and recovery following
neurological injury. Frontal midline theta is closely associated with working memory,
attention, and executive processes, making it a promising indicator of preserved cog-
nition and change over time. The study reveals that healthy participants exhibited
robust increases in frontal theta power with increasing task difficulty, whereas patients
showed reduced and variable activation at discharge, with normalization at follow-up.
Methodological comparisons revealed that frontal-channel theta power provided the
most consistent and physiologically interpretable measures, outperforming global aver-
ages and CSP-derived features. Linear mixed-effects modeling and post hoc contrasts
identified significant effects of group, task load, and time, supporting the sensitivity of
frontal theta to cognitive engagement and neurological status. These results indicate
that frontal midline theta is a reliable biomarker of working memory-related cogni-
tive processes and may serve as an early predictor of recovery in patients following
neurological injury.

Introduction
1.1
Foundations of Frontal Theta Activity in EEG
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive technique that records the brain’s
electrical activity from the scalp following patterns as on Figure 1.2. The measured
signals arise from coordinated electrical activity of brain cells in the cortex, and they
provide a window into the dynamic processes underlying perception, attention, memory,
and higher-order cognition. Because EEG captures oscillatory activity across distinct
frequency bands, it provides a sensitive marker of how the brain responds to specific
cognitive demands. In particular, it allows researchers to study not only whether the
brain is active, but also how different neural processes are engaged depending on the
task.
Figure 1.1: Schematic illustration of a standard EEG electrode montage on the human
scalp. Frontal, central, parietal, and occipital electrodes are shown.
In the context of cognitive neuroscience and clinical research, memory and con-
trol tasks are often employed to seperate task-related brain responses from baseline
or passive processing. Memory tasks require the active encoding, maintenance, and
retrieval of information, while control tasks provide a condition where external stimuli
are present but no active memory engagement is required. This contrast helps identify
whether participants are truly engaging working memory networks or simply respond-
ing to sensory stimulation.
Among the various frequency bands, theta activity (4–8 Hz) has received particular

attention. Theta oscillations, especially those originating from the frontal midline re-

are robustly associated with working memory, attention regulation, and executive
functions, and have been linked to activity in the medial prefrontal cortex [2]. For ex-
ample, frontal-midline theta power has been repeatedly shown to increase with working-
memory load and to reflect active engagement of memory networks [3], such as digit
span or n-back paradigms, making it a valuable electrophysiological marker of cogni-
tive effort and information processing. For this reason, studying frontal midline theta
responses is especially relevant in patients with neurological conditions where the as-
sessment of preserved cognitive function is clinically challenging.
While frontal midline theta is a well-established marker of cognitive engagement, its
reliable detection in EEG data critically depends on the analytical methods used to
extract and quantify neural activity. Different approaches, such as computing spectral
power across all electrodes, restricting analyses to anatomically motivated frontal re-
gions, or applying spatial filtering techniques like Common Spatial Patterns (CSP), can
emphasize distinct aspects of the signal and lead to different interpretations, therefore
finding the method where the frontal midline theta brain response is most visible is
the goal. This methodological challenge is especially relevant in clinical populations,
where increased noise, altered brain organization, and variable injury profiles compli-
cate signal interpretation. Consequently, identifying analysis strategies that balance
sensitivity, interpretability, and robustness is essential for establishing frontal midline
theta as a valid and clinically useful marker of cognitive function and recovery.

1.2
Aim
This Master’s thesis examines frontal midline theta activity measured with electroen-
cephalography (EEG) as a marker of cognitive function and recovery following neu-
rological injury. Because frontal midline theta is closely linked to working memory,
attention, and executive processes, it represents a promising neural indicator of pre-
served cognition and change over time.
The primary aims of this thesis are to:
• characterize how task-related EEG features differ between healthy individuals
and patients at hospital discharge, and how these features evolve by a 3-month
follow-up;
Based on existing literature, linking frontal midline theta to medial prefrontal ac-
tivity and cognitive engagement [2] [3], the following hypotheses are tested:
• H1: Theta-band power (whether measured globally, over frontal channels, or via
CSP-derived features) is expected to be reduced following neurological injury and
shows partial recovery at the 3-month follow-up.
• H2: Memory tasks are expected to showcase stronger theta-band modulation
than control tasks in healthy participants compared to patients at discharge,
reflecting reduced cognitive engagement after injury.
To address these aims, an EEG analysis pipeline is first developed and validated
using data from healthy participants, comparing multiple feature extraction approaches,
where the goal is to build and test a reliable analysis pipeline. The most robust and
interpretable method is then applied to patient and healthy controls data from the
hospital, where EEG recordings are collected at discharge and follow-up to evaluate
group differences and recovery-related changes.
1.3
Theoretical background
This section explores different methods used to extract EEG signals and to investigate
frontal midline theta activity associated with memory and cognitive control tasks.
Cluster Permutation Test: Cluster-level permutation test provides a non-parametric
solution to the multiple-comparisons problem existent in high-dimensional EEG data
by evaluating clusters of adjacent data points rather than individual samples [4]. This
approach is designed for one-sample inference and is particularly appropriate for within-
subject contrasts in EEG data, where the objective is to test whether a subject-level
difference deviates systematically from zero.In this framework, the data are arranged
as a two-dimensional array of shape
(nobservations, nsamples),
where each observation corresponds to an independent subject and each sample repre-
sents a time point. The values typically reflect condition-wise differences (e.g., memory
minus control) computed at the subject level, yielding a single time-resolved difference
waveform per subject. At each sample, a test statistic ( a t-statistic) is computed across
9
==PAGE:10==
observations. Samples exceeding a predefined threshold are identified and grouped into
clusters based on temporal adjacency. For each cluster, a cluster-level statistic is calcu-
lated as the sum of the test statistics within that cluster, reflecting both the magnitude
and temporal extent of the effect. Statistical significance is assessed through permu-
tation testing. Under the null hypothesis that the mean of the observations equals
zero, permutations are generated by randomly flipping the sign of the subject-level
difference waveforms. For each permutation, cluster-level statistics are recomputed,
and the maximum cluster statistic is retained to form a null distribution. Observed
cluster statistics are then compared against this null distribution to obtain corrected
p-values. This cluster-based permutation procedure controls the family-wise error rate
while maintaining sensitivity to temporally extended effects, making it well suited for
detecting sustained modulations of frontal midline theta activity in memory and cog-
nitive control paradigms.
Common Spatial Patterns (CSP): CSP constitute a supervised spatial filtering
technique widely used in EEG analysis to enhance discriminative neural activity be-
tween experimental conditions. CSP seeks spatial filters that maximize the variance of
the projected signal for one condition while minimizing it for another, thereby empha-
sizing task-relevant oscillatory patterns.
Let X ∈RC×T denote an EEG epoch with C channels and T time samples. For
a two-condition setting, CSP operates by estimating class-specific covariance matrices,
typically denoted as Σ1 and Σ2. These covariance matrices can be computed by con-
catenating epochs within each class and estimating covariances. The CSP filters are
obtained by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem of the form:
Σ1w = λ(Σ1 + Σ2)w,
(1.1)
where w represents a spatial filter and λ the corresponding eigenvalue. The resulting
filters project the sensor-level data into a low-dimensional CSP space in which variance
differences between conditions are maximized.
Each CSP component corresponds to a spatial pattern and an associated spatial
filter. Within the MNE-Python framework, CSP is implemented as a modular decom-
position method that provides access to spatial filters and spatial patterns [5]. This
formulation supports flexible choices regarding covariance estimation, regularization,
normalization, and feature representation, making CSP well suited for exploratory and
hypothesis-driven analyses of task-related EEG activity.
Linear Mixed Model (LMM): Linear mixed-effects models (LMMs) provide a flex-
ible statistical framework for analyzing data with hierarchical or repeated-measures
structure. In experimental designs where multiple observations are obtained from the
same participant across conditions or time points, LMMs allow for the explicit mod-
eling of both fixed effects of experimental factors and random effects associated with
individual variability.
10
==PAGE:11==
Let Yij denote a continuous outcome measure for participant i at observation j.
Fixed effects represent systematic, population-level influences of predictors, while ran-
dom effects account for correlations among repeated observations from the same par-
ticipant. The fixed-effects structure may include main effects and interactions among
predictors, allowing for the examination of how the outcome varies across different con-
ditions or factors. Participant identity is modeled as a random intercept, permitting
each participant to have an individual baseline while assuming that fixed-effect slopes
are common across all participants.
Formally, the model can be expressed as:
Yij = β0 + β1X1ij + β2X2ij + β3X3ij
+ β4(X1ij × X2ij) + β5(X1ij × X3ij)
+ β6(X2ij × X3ij) + β7(X1ij × X2ij × X3ij)
+ bi + εij,
(1.2)
where β0 represents the fixed intercept, βk denote fixed-effect coefficients, bi is a
participant-specific random intercept assumed to be normally distributed with mean
zero, and εij is the residual error term. Model parameters in linear mixed-effects mod-
els are typically estimated using maximum likelihood (ML) or restricted maximum
likelihood (REML). REML, in particular, is preferred for estimating variance compo-
nents (e.g., the variance of the random intercepts and residuals) because it accounts
for the loss of degrees of freedom caused by estimating fixed effects. This produces
unbiased estimates of the variance components, meaning that the estimated variabil-
ity attributable to random effects and residual error more accurately reflects the true
population values, even in small or unbalanced datasets [6].
To test the significance of fixed effects, it is necessary to determine appropriate de-
grees of freedom for the test statistics. In mixed models, variances are often unequal or
complex, making exact degrees of freedom difficult to compute. The Satterthwaite ap-
proximation provides a method to estimate effective degrees of freedom in such cases.
This approach treats the sampling distribution of an estimated effect as if it were a
t-distribution with a fractional number of degrees of freedom that accounts for the un-
certainty in variance estimates [7]. For two groups with unequal variances, the standard
error of the difference is computed using Satterthwaite’s formula:

1.4
State of the Art
Electroencephalography (EEG) has been widely used to investigate neural mechanisms
underlying cognitive functions, particularly working memory and attention. Oscillatory
11
==PAGE:12==
activity in the theta band (4–8 Hz), especially over frontal midline regions, has been
connected with active cognitive engagement and medial prefrontal cortex activity [2,
3, 8]. Increases in frontal midline theta have been observed as a function of memory
load, suggesting that theta power is a reliable marker of task-related neural recruitment
during working memory tasks [8, 9].
Several studies have demonstrated that frontal theta not only reflects memory load
but also varies with individual cognitive capacity and style. Gevins and Smith [10]
showed that EEG measures of working memory correlate with differences in cognitive
ability, highlighting the sensitivity of electrophysiological markers to inter-subject vari-
ability. Similarly, Lindborg et al. [11] demonstrated that theta-band oscillations are
modulated by task-specific audiovisual integration, emphasizing the task-dependency
of neural dynamics.
Methodologically, extracting meaningful EEG features requires careful considera-
tion of spatial and spectral analysis techniques. Common approaches include averaging
power across all channels, restricting analyses to frontal regions, or using spatial filter-
ing methods such as Common Spatial Patterns (CSP) [12, 13]. CSP has been widely
used in brain-computer interface research to maximize class separability [12, 13], but it
is highly sensitive to noise and individual differences, which can limit its interpretability
in clinical populations. Nonparametric statistical methods, such as cluster-based per-
mutation tests, provide robust tools for identifying significant task-related differences
in EEG signals [14].
EEG data analysis in clinical populations must account for substantial inter-individual
variability. Mixed-effects modeling has therefore been recommended as a robust sta-
tistical framework for EEG studies, as it allows separation of systematic experimental
effects from subject-specific differences and accommodates repeated-measures designs
common in longitudinal and clinical research [15].
Overall, the literature establishes frontal midline theta as a sensitive and physio-
logically meaningful marker of working memory and cognitive engagement. However,
clinical application requires methods that are robust to inter-subject variability and
noise, while maintaining neurophysiological interpretability. These considerations di-
rectly motivate the analytical approach adopted in this thesis, focusing on frontal theta
power as a reliable indicator of cognitive function and recovery following neurological
injury.

Methodology
2.1
Participants and Data Acquisition
Healthy Control Data
The healthy healthy control dataset provides the foundation for developing and validat-
ing the EEG analysis pipeline. It originates from a study by Pavlov et al. [16] and
consists of raw 64-channel EEG recordings collected from 86 neurologically healthy
participants. In addition to EEG, the original dataset includes cardiovascular (electro-
cardiography and photoplethysmography) and pupillometry measurements; however,
only the EEG data were analyzed in the present thesis.
Participants completed approximately 4 minutes of eyes-closed resting-state record-
ing, followed by performance of a classic auditory digit span working memory task with
serial recall. During the task, sequences of 5, 9, or 13 digits were presented auditorily at
a fixed stimulus-onset asynchrony (SOA) of 2 seconds. Depending on the experimental
condition, participants were instructed either to actively memorize the digit sequences
(memory condition) or to passively listen without memorization (control condition).
Each trial consisted of:
• a 3-second baseline period,
• digit presentation with a 2-second SOA,
• a 3-second retention interval following the final digit.
Participants completed 108 memory trials and 54 control trials across the three
sequence-length conditions. The clear manipulation of cognitive load and the explicit
contrast between memory engagement and passive listening make this dataset well
suited for detecting task-related frontal midline theta activity. Its high signal-to-noise
ratio and controlled experimental design creates it an ideal benchmark for developing
and validating the EEG analysis pipeline prior to application in clinical data.
Patient Data
The healthy controls and patients dataset extends the analysis to a clinical population.
It derives from a case-control study including 30 critical illness survivors from four ICUs
(neurologic/neurosurgical, cardiologic, cardiothoracic, and general ICU) at Rigshospi-
talet, Copenhagen University Hospital, along with 30 age-, sex-, and education-matched
healthy controls.
Patients were assessed twice:
• at ICU discharge (±24 hours),
• at a 3-month follow-up visit.
13
==PAGE:14==
Both patients and hospital-matched healthy controls performed an N-back working
memory task while undergoing combined NIRS–EEG measurements.
Patients were
screened daily for eligibility; healthy controls were recruited from the community or
from families of admitted patients.
The EEG portion of this dataset includes markers for several experimental condi-
tions, including tongue movement (real or imagined) and N-back memory tasks with
varying load (1-back, 2-back, 3-back). For the purposes of this thesis, only the working
memory conditions (marker codes 5–7) were analyzed. Tongue movement conditions
were excluded.The N-back task is a widely used paradigm for assessing working memory
and cognitive load. In this task, participants are presented with a continuous sequence
of stimuli and are required to indicate whether the current stimulus matches the one
presented N items earlier in the sequence. As the value of N increases (e.g., from
1-back to 3-back), the task places progressively greater demands on working memory,
attention, and executive control processes. During the N-back tasks, each memory trial
lasted 45 seconds and was divided into 3-second epochs corresponding to individual
digit presentations. Control intervals immediately followed each memory block, lasted
15 seconds, and were segmented into 3-second epochs. This design enabled direct con-
trast between active memory engagement and resting baseline. Figure 2.1 illustrates
the task structure.

Role of Healthy Data in Pipeline Development
Analysis began with the ’healthy healthy control’ dataset because it provides high signal-
to-noise ratios and consistent task responses, making it ideal for establishing a robust
pipeline. The goal in this phase was to optimize methods for detecting theta-band
responses to memory demands and for identifying the electrodes and components most
sensitive to working-memory-related theta activity.
In the digit span task, digits were presented every 2 seconds, and sequence length
varied across three conditions (5, 9, and 13 digits). EEG was recorded from 64 elec-
trodes arranged according to the extended 10–20 system at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz.
Analysis focused on theta-band activity (4–8 Hz).

2.2
EEG Preprocessing and Signal Preparation
EEG preprocessing was performed to enhance signal quality and ensure comparability
between healthy control and patient recordings. Given the increased noise, movement
artifacts, and variability typically present in clinical EEG, preprocessing steps were
designed to balance noise reduction with preservation of physiologically meaningful
activity, particularly in the theta frequency range.
EEG signals were re-referenced to the common average reference, since the
original recordings were referenced to electrode FCz. Because the primary focus of this
study is on frontal lobe activity, continuing to use FCz as the reference could have
artificially reduced the differences in activity of interest in the frontal midline region.
The common average reference provides a more balanced reference point by minimizing
bias toward any single electrode, thereby improving spatial resolution across the scalp.
To reduce computational demands while maintaining signal fidelity, the data were
downsampled in accordance with the Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem [17]. This
theorem states that the sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency
present in the signal to avoid aliasing. Since the analysis targeted the theta frequency
band (4–8 Hz), a sampling rate of at least 16 Hz would be sufficient; however, to en-
sure a safe margin and preserve signal quality, the data were downsampled to 25 Hz.
This choice allows accurate capture of theta oscillations while reducing file size and
processing time.
A band-pass filter was then applied between 4 and 8 Hz to isolate the theta
frequency range of interest. This filtering step ensured that the analysis focused specif-
ically on oscillatory activity known to be involved in working memory and attention
processes, while suppressing activity from other frequency bands.
Next, for the ’healthy healthy controls’ dataset, task information for each participant
was extracted from the corresponding .tsv files. These files provided:
1. The onset time of each stimulus,
2. Whether the trial belonged to the memory or control condition,
3. The sequence length (5, 9, or 13 digits),
4. The digit number within the sequence.
Based on this information, epochs were created around each stimulus onset, with
time zero aligned to the presentation of the digit. Each epoch extended from –0.25
to 1.5 seconds relative to onset. Baseline correction was performed using the pre-
stimulus interval (–0.25 to 0 s), ensuring that subsequent analyses measured relative
changes in brain activity rather than absolute voltage fluctuations.
For the ’healthy and patients’ data, the memory task consisted of 1-back, 2-back,
and 3-back conditions, with each digit displayed for 3 seconds. For each n-back test, a
45-second segment beginning from the presentation of the first digit was extracted and
divided into 3-second epochs, corresponding to the duration of each digit display. This
resulted in 15 epochs per n-back test, and a total of 45 memory epochs per participant.
For the control data, a 15-second segment before each n-back test was selected and
18
==PAGE:19==
divided into 3-second epochs, yielding 5 control epochs per n-back test and 15 control
epochs in total.
2.3
Theta Power extraction
The analysis pipeline for ’healthy healthy controls’ included several complementary
steps to characterize task-related theta activity. Statistical evaluations of theta power
were first performed across individual digit presentations to quantify memory-related
modulations.
Time–frequency representations were then used to compare pre- and
post-stimulus activity, enabling examination of both phase-locked (evoked) and non-
phase-locked (induced) components of the EEG signal. Analyzing these components
separately allowed assessment of which representation most effectively captures frontal
midline theta activity, particularly with regard to subsequent application in patient
data.
Evoked activity reflects stimulus-locked responses that are consistent across trials,
whereas induced activity represents ongoing oscillatory dynamics that vary from trial to
trial. As highlighted by Lindborg et al. [11], distinguishing between evoked and induced
activity is essential for a comprehensive characterization of neural dynamics, ensuring
that observed effects are not solely driven by averaged event-related responses. Impor-
tantly, the order in which averaging and power estimation are performed has method-
ological implications. Averaging EEG signals across channels before power computation
can reduce random noise but may also cancel meaningful neural activity due to polarity
differences across electrodes. In contrast, computing power at the single-channel level
prior to averaging preserves oscillatory magnitude regardless of signal polarity, at the
cost of retaining more noise. This trade-off was explicitly considered when selecting
the final approach for quantifying frontal midline theta activity, with priority given to
preserving physiologically meaningful task-related effects. Based on these considera-
tions, subsequent analyses focused on non-phase-locked (induced) theta activity. Theta
power was therefore computed at the single-trial level prior to averaging across trials,
channels, and participants. This approach preserves oscillatory magnitude independent
of phase consistency and is better suited to capturing frontal midline theta dynamics
associated with working memory and executive engagement. Importantly, this method
proved more robust in the presence of inter-trial variability and noise, which is partic-
ularly relevant for patient EEG data. Consequently, all further power-based analyses
and group comparisons in this thesis were conducted using induced theta-band power
estimates, as suggested in [3] .
Following preprocessing, time-frequency analysis was conducted to examine theta-
band responses. Spectral power was estimated using Morlet wavelets, which are widely
applied in EEG research due to their favorable trade-off between temporal and frequency
resolution. This method is particularly well-suited for detecting transient oscillatory
activity, such as the frontal midline theta response. Importantly, Morlet wavelet de-
composition allows the tracking of how theta power evolves over time within each trial,
rather than only providing averaged activity across the task.
In addition to wavelet-based analysis, theta-band power was also estimated using the
Hilbert transform. For each epoch, the EEG signal x(t) was first band-pass filtered in

Wavelet- and Hilbert-based power measures were subsequently averaged across time

per epoch per participant. These epoch-level values were further averaged across mem-

cognitive states. When a single numerical estimate of theta power per epoch was re-
quired, the Hilbert transform was used due to its computational efficiency and precise
estimation of instantaneous amplitude within a predefined frequency band. In contrast,
when temporal composition of the signal was of interest, the Morlet wavelet approach
was applied, as it allows simultaneous examination of multiple frequencies and provides
a flexible trade-off between temporal and spectral resolution. Together, these com-
plementary analyses ensure that both transient, trial-specific dynamics and broader
oscillatory trends are captured in the quantification of theta-band power.

To further evaluate the discriminative power of theta-band features, a supervised

either across all channels or restricted to the predefined frontal electrodes, and the log-

selected due to its ability to handle high-dimensional feature spaces and to find opti-

the number of training samples. Logistic regression was also trained for comparison,
but it generally yielded lower classification accuracy, likely because it is less robust to
multicollinearity and noise in small-sample, high-dimensional EEG data. Model per-
formance was evaluated using a stratified 5-fold cross-validation scheme, ensuring that
each fold contained a proportional representation of both memory and control trials.
This approach reduces potential bias during evaluation and ensures that performance
metrics reflect the classifier’s ability to discriminate both conditions.
Given that the dataset contained substantially more memory trials than control
trials, class imbalance was explicitly addressed. Two complementary strategies were
considered:
1. Class-weighted loss [18]: This approach was applied by assigning higher weights
to the underrepresented control class. Misclassifying a control trial incurred a
larger penalty, thereby encouraging the model to treat both classes equitably.

Cluster Permutation Test
The next idea was to identify time intervals where memory and control trials differed
significantly.
A simple approach would be to run multiple t-tests with a threshold
of p set to 0.05; however, this leads to the multiple comparisons problem, which is
particularly severe in EEG data because the signal is distributed across many electrodes,
time points, and frequency bins. Importantly, standard t-tests assume that observations
are independent, but in EEG data, this assumption is strongly violated: signals are
highly autocorrelated over time, neighboring electrodes exhibit spatial correlations,
and frequency bins are often interdependent.
Traditional correction methods (e.g.,
Bonferroni or FDR) are not only overly conservative but also fail to account for the
spatiotemporal dependencies inherent in EEG activity [14].
To address this, cluster-based permutation testing on ’healthy healthy dataset’ was
created, which evaluates clusters of adjacent significant samples instead of isolated
points. This approach leverages the fact that true neurophysiological effects are ex-
pected to occur in contiguous regions (e.g., across neighboring time points or electrodes),
while random noise is unlikely to form large clusters.
The procedure was as follows:
1. Epoch extraction and difference computation for within subject test:
For each participant, epochs were extracted for both memory and control con-
ditions. The average theta response (4–8 Hz) was computed separately for each
condition, and a difference waveform (memory – control) was obtained.
This
resulted in a distribution of difference values across participants for every time
point.
2. One-sample t-tests across subjects: At each sample point, a one-sample t-
test was performed to test whether the across-subject distribution of differences
deviated significantly from zero.
3. Cluster formation: Adjacent significant samples (threshold p set to 0.05) were
grouped into clusters. Adjacency was defined based on temporal continuity and
spatial neighborhood relations among EEG electrodes.
4. Permutation procedure: To approximate the null distribution, condition labels
(memory vs. control) were randomly sign-flipped within each participant (reflect-
ing the within-subjects design). For each permutation, new cluster statistics were
computed as the sum of t-values within each cluster.
22
==PAGE:23==
5. Statistical inference: From each permutation, the largest cluster statistic was
retained to form a null distribution of maximal cluster sizes. Observed cluster
statistics were then compared against this null distribution to obtain corrected
pvalues, thereby controlling for family-wise error rate (FWER).
The main advantage of this method is that it provides robust control over Type I
error while preserving sensitivity to true neurophysiological effects. Instead of flagging
isolated points that may arise from noise, cluster-based permutation testing identifies
contiguous regions of theta modulation that reliably distinguish memory from control
conditions. Such clusters reflect effects that are both statistically significant and neu-
rophysiologically interpretable.
Cluster-based permutation testing was employed to identify temporal regions in which
theta power differed significantly between memory and control conditions. These sig-
nificant clusters show time regions that maximize task-related variance. Rather than
averaging theta power across entire epochs, theta power can be averaged within these
clusters for each participant, thereby concentrating the analysis on the most informative
portions of the signal. The resulting cluster-averaged theta features can then be used
for subsequent statistical comparisons and as input to classification models, with the
goal of strengthening discrimination between memory and control conditions by em-
phasizing periods of maximal task-related modulation. By computing memory–control
theta differences only within these clusters, sensitivity to task-related neural activation
may be enhanced while minimizing the influence of unrelated background activity.
Common Spatial Patterns (CSP)
Common Spatial Patterns (CSP) were used to derive spatial filters that maximize differ-
ences in theta-band power between memory and control conditions. All CSP analyses
were performed at the participant level to account for inter-individual variability in
EEG topography.
CSP input representation
For each participant, preprocessed EEG data were represented as theta-band amplitude
envelopes (4–8 Hz), as described in the preprocessing section. Prior to CSP computa-
tion, several preprocessing steps were applied to ensure that the input data captured
physiologically meaningful task-related oscillatory activity and allowed for stable co-
variance estimation.
The data were band-pass filtered in the theta range (4–8 Hz) to isolate oscillatory
activity associated with working memory and executive processes, which are known to
be reflected predominantly in this frequency band. Before analyzing the EEG signals,
each channel was adjusted to have an average value of zero within each epoch (zero-mean
normalization). This step removes slow shifts and constant offsets in the signal that can
interfere with later analysis. The signals were then processed with the Hilbert transform
to extract the amplitude envelope, which reflects how strong the theta oscillations are
over time at each electrode. Removing the average first helps the Hilbert transform
estimate oscillation strength more accurately, because offsets can falsely increase the
measured amplitude. By using the amplitude envelope instead of the raw voltage signal,
the analysis focuses on changes in oscillation strength rather than on precise timing or

phase. This is important for detecting induced, non–phase-locked frontal midline theta
activity. The analytic signal can be computed using the Hilbert transform, and the
resulting envelope may be used as input to covariance estimation. This approach allows
CSP to capture condition-specific differences in oscillatory power dynamics rather than
phase-locked activity. After these preprocessing steps, each epoch was represented as a
matrix of dimensions C × T, where C denotes the number of EEG channels and T the
number of time points.
CSP computation
For each condition, covariance matrices Σmem and Σctrl were estimated from the en-

number of EEG channels is large (e.g., C = 63) relative to the number of available sam-

unstable eigenvalue decompositions or, in some cases, failure to estimate the covariance
matrix altogether. Shrinkage regularization improves numerical stability by combining
the empirical covariance matrix with a scaled identity matrix, thereby reducing estima-
tion variance and ensuring that the resulting covariance matrices are positive definite.
This is particularly important in high-dimensional EEG data and in clinical recordings,
where noise levels are higher and the number of clean epochs may be limited. Regu-
larization therefore enables robust CSP filter estimation and improves generalization of
the resulting spatial patterns across trials and conditions.

Selection of the Optimal Analysis Method
Several complementary analysis approaches were evaluated to determine the most suit-
able method for quantifying task-related frontal midline theta activity: (i) theta power
computed across all EEG channels, (ii) theta power restricted to anatomically defined
frontal channels, (iii) theta power extracted from CSP-projected components, and (iv)
theta power summarized within significant temporal clusters identified using cluster-
based permutation testing. Each approach emphasizes different aspects of the EEG
signal and differs in sensitivity, robustness, and neurophysiological interpretability.
Method comparison was conducted using data from healthy participants, where
working memory networks are assumed to be intact and reliably engaged. The prede-
fined selection criteria were: (1) the ability to reveal statistically meaningful differences
between memory and control conditions, (2) suitability for supervised classification of
task states, and (3) interpretability in terms of known frontal midline theta mechanisms,
assessed through spatial specificity and scalp topographies.
Theta power computed across all channels provided a useful global measure of task-
related modulation, but focusing on predefined frontal electrodes enhanced the ability
to discriminate between memory and control conditions. CSP-based features explicitly
maximize variance differences between conditions and were therefore well suited for
classification. However, the resulting spatial patterns were often broadly distributed
and not consistently centered over frontal midline regions, limiting their interpretability
with respect to canonical working memory networks. In contrast, theta power extracted
from predefined frontal electrodes provided a hypothesis-driven and anatomically con-
strained measure of frontal midline theta activity. This approach balances sensitivity
to task-related effects with robustness to noise and preserves direct neurophysiological
interpretability. Based on these methodological considerations, frontal-channel theta
power was selected as the primary feature representation for subsequent statistical
analyses and longitudinal comparisons in patient data.

the following hypotheses were evaluated in hospital data:
• H1: Theta-band power (whether measured globally, over frontal channels, or via

shows partial recovery at the 3-month follow-up.
• H2: Memory tasks are expected to showcase stronger theta-band modulation

Random Effects
The model included a random intercept for each participant, allowing each individual to
have their own baseline difference in theta power. This accounts for variability between
participants while modeling repeated measures within each participant.
Model Estimation and Convergence
The model was estimated using restricted maximum likelihood (REML), which is the
standard approach for fitting linear mixed-effects models and provides reliable estimates
of both fixed and random effects. To ensure that the model fitting algorithm successfully
reached a solution, the convergence of the model was monitored. In particular, the
function isSingular(model) was used to check for singularity in the random-effects
structure. Singularity occurs when one or more variance components of the random
effects are estimated to be zero or very close to zero, which can indicate that the
model is too complex for the available data or that some random-effects terms are not
supported by the dataset. Any warnings about singularity were carefully examined to
verify that the model results were valid and interpretable.
Statistical Inference
Fixed-effect significance was assessed using Type III ANOVA, which provides p-values
for each main effect and interaction. To interpret significant interactions, the post-
hoc comparisons were performed using the emmeans package. These comparisons allow
identification of which specific n-back levels, groups, or time points drive observed
effects.

loads, and time points. Contrasts with p-values below 0.05 were interpreted as statis-

3.3
Selection of Frontal Channels as Optimal Fea-
ture Set
Based on the method comparison results (Figures 3.5 and 3.8), frontal-channel theta
power consistently showed the largest and most reliable differences between memory
and control tasks across participants. While all-channel averaging captures more global
brain activity, it can weaken task-specific frontal midline signals that are known to un-
derlie working memory processes. In contrast, CSP-based features exhibited substantial
inter-subject variability, likely due to their sensitivity to noise, limited trial numbers,
and individual differences in spatial activation patterns.
These observations are further supported by the classification results summarized in
Table 3.1, where frontal-channel features achieved the highest or near-highest classifica-
tion accuracy for a large proportion of participants, with more consistent performance
across individuals compared to CSP. Although all-channel features occasionally yielded
very high peak accuracies, frontal-channel theta power provided a more stable and
physiologically interpretable representation of memory-related neural activity.
Consequently, frontal channels were selected as the optimal feature set for all subse-
quent analyses. This choice ensures robustness, consistency, and interpretability when
comparing cognitive engagement across groups. In the following sections, frontal theta
power is therefore used to investigate: (i) differences between healthy controls and pa-
tients at baseline (discharge), and (ii) longitudinal changes in patients from baseline to
follow-up, providing insight into trajectories of cognitive recovery.

3.4
Group and Effect Comparisons
To investigate factors influencing memory-related theta activation (defined as the mean
difference in theta-band power between memory and control conditions), a linear mixed-
effects model (LMM) was fitted. The model was designed to account for both within-
subject and between-group variability. The n-back level was included as a repeated-
measures fixed effect to capture within-participant changes in cognitive load. Group
(Patients vs. Healthy) was modeled as a between-subject fixed effect, while Time (Base-
line vs. Follow-up) was included as a within-subject fixed effect to assess longitudinal
changes. All two-way and three-way interactions between n-back, Group, and Time were
specified to examine whether task difficulty modulated group differences and recovery
trajectories.
To account for inter-individual variability and repeated observations, random inter-
cepts were included for each participant. The resulting linear mixed-effects model was
defined as:
MemoryPctDiff ∼n-back × Group × Time + (1 | Participant).
(3.1)
where the term (1 | Participant) specifies a random intercept for each participant,

by influential outliers. As shown in Figure 3.13, the residuals closely follow the unity line

4
Discussion
The present study investigated frontal midline theta activity as an electrophysiolog-
ical marker of cognitive engagement and recovery following neurological injury.
By
systematically comparing feature extraction methods, task demands, and participant
groups across time, this work aimed to evaluate both the physiological relevance and the
methodological robustness of frontal theta measures. The following sections integrate
the main findings in relation to the stated hypotheses, interpret their implications for
cognitive neuroscience and clinical EEG research, and discuss methodological consid-
erations and limitations that inform the use of frontal midline theta as a longitudinal
biomarker of working memory and recovery.
4.1
Frontal Midline Theta as a Marker of Cognitive
Engagement
Consistent with prior literature linking frontal midline theta to medial prefrontal ac-
tivity and cognitive engagement [3], healthy participants exhibited robust task-related
increases in theta power during memory versus control tasks. This effect was most
pronounced when focusing on frontal channels, compared to global (all-channel) av-
erages or CSP-derived features. CSP-based measures, while theoretically capable of
optimizing variance between conditions, showed high inter-subject variability and did
not reliably correspond to frontal theta activation, highlighting the advantage of frontal
channel averaging for capturing physiologically meaningful working memory signals.
Our method comparison and classifier analyses confirmed that frontal-channel theta
power provides the most consistent and interpretable measure of memory-related activa-
tion. Across participants, frontal features produced higher or comparable classification
accuracy relative to CSP and all-channel approaches (Table 3.1), supporting their use
for group-level and longitudinal analyses. These findings underscore that frontal mid-
line theta is a reliable electrophysiological marker of task engagement, suitable for both
healthy and patient populations.
4.2
Patient versus Healthy Control Comparisons
At hospital discharge, patients exhibited reduced and highly variable frontal theta acti-
vation relative to healthy controls, particularly during moderate to high cognitive loads
(e.g., 2-back and 3-back tasks). Estimated marginal means and post hoc analyses re-
vealed a significant difference between patients and healthy participants at Baseline,
while confidence intervals overlapped at the 3-month follow-up (Tables 3.4 and 3.5).
These findings indicate partial recovery of frontal theta power over time, consistent
with improvements in cognitive function.
The observed variability in patient theta responses likely reflects variability in injury
severity. Some patients retained memory-related theta patterns similar to healthy con-
46
==PAGE:47==
trols, whereas others showed markedly reduced or even inverted activation, highlighting
the potential utility of frontal theta as a sensitive marker for identifying individuals at
risk for persistent cognitive deficits.
4.3
Task Difficulty and Cognitive Load
Theta modulation increased with task difficulty. Post hoc comparisons of n-back levels
revealed that the 3-back condition elicited stronger theta responses than 2-back or 1-
back trials (Table 3.3), supporting H2 that memory tasks evoke greater frontal theta
engagement in healthy individuals. In patients, moderate cognitive loads (e.g., 2-back)
were sufficient to detect differences between memory and control epochs, suggesting that
even partially impaired neural systems can recruit frontal networks under moderate
demands. This emphasizes the importance of selecting task difficulty that balances
sensitivity to cognitive engagement with feasibility for impaired populations.
4.4
Methodological Considerations
The results highlight the importance of method selection in EEG analyses. Frontal-
channel averaging provided robust, physiologically interpretable estimates of working
memory engagement, whereas all-channel averages weaker, less specific local effects
and CSP-based features introduced inter-subject variability. Limitations of the present
study include the relatively small overall sample size, particularly within the patient
group, which restricts statistical power and limits the generalization of the findings. At-
trition at follow-up further reduced the available data, with substantially fewer patients
completing the three-month assessment compared to baseline, introducing missing data
and potential selection bias. Considerable inter-individual variability was observed, not
only among patients but also within the healthy control group across time points,
reflecting natural differences in neural responses as well as measurement noise. In ad-
dition, the patient cohort was heterogeneous with respect to demographic and clinical
characteristics, including age, sex, and background factors, which may have contributed
to variability in EEG measures and recovery trajectories. Methodological constraints
also include variability in EEG recording quality and the need to balance task diffi-
culty against patient fatigue, as overly demanding tasks may reduce engagement and
attenuate theta responses. Finally, while the percent-difference metric was chosen for
its intuitive interpretability and normalization to individual baselines, it is sensitive
to small control-condition values and may distort comparisons across participants; fu-
ture work could consider log-transformed ratios or other scale-invariant measures to
improve robustness. Despite these limitations, the present work demonstrates a struc-
tured framework for using EEG-derived frontal midline theta metrics. Despite these
limitations, the present work demonstrates a structured framework for using EEG-
derived frontal midline theta metrics.
4.5
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study shows that frontal midline theta activity provides a reliable
electrophysiological marker of memory-related cognitive engagement. Healthy partic-
ipants consistently exhibited robust increases in frontal theta power during memory
tasks, whereas patients at hospital discharge showed reduced and highly variable re-
47
==PAGE:48==
sponses (supporting H2: Memory tasks are expected to showcase stronger theta-band
modulation than control tasks in healthy participants compared to patients at dis-
charge, reflecting reduced cognitive engagement after injury).
Importantly, frontal
theta activity partially normalized at follow-up (supporting H1: Theta-band power
is expected to be reduced following neurological injury and show partial recovery at
the 3-month follow-up) , with moderate task demands (2-back) being sufficient to elicit
detectable engagement in patients, while higher cognitive loads (3-back) revealed max-
imal theta modulation in healthy individuals and those at follow-up. Methodologically,
frontal-channel theta power proved more consistent and physiologically interpretable
than global power measures or CSP-derived features. Together, these findings support
the use of frontal midline theta as a sensitive and meaningful biomarker of working
memory function and cognitive recovery, and suggest that EEG measures obtained at
discharge may contribute to early assessment of recovery trajectories and the develop-
ment of individualized rehabilitation strategies.
