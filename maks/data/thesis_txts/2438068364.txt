Abstract
In the following thesis, a novel ﬁlter aimed to reduce speckle from Sentinel-
1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) images is presented. The ﬁlter is based
on a similarity measure developed for change detection in the covariance
matrix, and is utilizing a non-local (NL) approach in both the temporal and
spatial domain in order to reduce the speckle noise and achieve better co-
herence estimations. The ﬁlter is compared to de-facto standard ﬁlters and
new state-of-the-art ﬁltering algorithms on both simulated and real Sentinel-
1 images and the results are discussed. The ﬁlter shows promising results.
Both in terms of speckle reduction, texture preservation and speed is the ﬁl-
ter comparable to more advanced methods. There does, however, in some
cases appear to be an issue with bias when incorporating the temporal dom-
ain. This issue is discussed and further improvements suggested.

Introduction
Earth Observation (EO) and remote sensing have over the last 50 years ena-
bled us to observe and study the Earth on a larger scale than previously
possible. It has permitted us to make new discoveries and provided us with
a greater understanding of the planet. Today, EO is the main source of in-
formation for an abundance of scientiﬁc ﬁelds, and data from EO sources
are helping create day-to-day weather forecasts, large-scale climate models,
updated geographical information and even the documentation of humani-
tarian crisis [1], [2]. In Denmark, remote sensing has been widely adopted
for agricultural management, marine monitoring and coastal mapping [3].
The most widely known EO product is probably the photograph. Visual sa-
tellite images are intuitive to understand as they express what we can see
with our own eyes, and are widely used to observe the lower layers of the
atmosphere (clouds) or when the circumstances permit it to observe what
lies underneath. Another well known EO product is radar images. While
not as intuitive to understand, radar images provide a volume of beneﬁts
over visual images and are therefore often used in conjunction with, or as a
stand-alone data-source.
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is a high-resolution radar imaging sy-
stem operated from a moving platform such as an airplane or a satellite.
A SAR utilizes a stable oscillator to transmit and then coherently combine
several received pulses of a given target. This coherent combination ena-
bles the radar system to synthetically create a large antenna aperture with a
much higher resolution than by using a single image alone [4].
SAR systems use microwaves to form images.
This leads to several
advantages over visual imaging systems. Mainly, microwaves penetrate
cloud-covers enabling SARs to observe targets independent of weather. Se-
condly, it permits observation regardless of sunlight. This not only gives
SAR systems night operational capabilities, it also enables observations of
sea-ice in areas far north or south independent of the seasons. SAR systems
are great at deriving surface characteristics as a radar does not see color the
way visual imaging systems would, but rather the coarseness of a target.
SAR imaging systems are therefore widely used within agriculture, hyd-
rology, forestry, oceanography and ice monitoring [5]. The SAR principles
have even been used for several deep-space missions and helped us gain

Early SARs operated using a single polarized wave and thereby creating
a greyscale image of the target dependent on the backscattered signal. These
systems include the European ERS and American SIR systems [5]. Later sys-
tems in the 1980’s and 1990’s extended this principle by using multiple cohe-
rent polarizations which gives rise to additional information regarding the
target characteristics [8]. This principle is called Polarimetric SAR (PolSAR).
Further, by observing a target from slightly different directions it is possible
to construct a phase- or path-difference image proportional to an elevation
map of the target [9]. This principle is called Interferometric SAR (InSAR).
PolSAR and InSAR capabilities enable users to solve problems that are im-
possible with other technologies such as the classiﬁcation of agricultural tar-
gets or mapping of subsidences. These applications have led to PolSAR and
InSAR gaining wide popularity in many scientiﬁc ﬁelds.
Despite the obvious advantages of an active radar imaging system com-
pared to conventional passive imaging systems, SAR systems also have
some massive drawbacks. Mainly the problem with speckle noise. Speckle
noise is caused by the apparent roughness of a distributed target compared
to the wavelength and resolution of the imaging system. This phenomenon
gives rise to many individual complex wave contributions from a single
resolution cell being added in-phase at the receiver. The result is a chaotic
noise pattern in the image which is dependent on the underlying backscatter
of target [10].

3
This speckle is evidently degrading the resolution of the image, and thus
speckle ﬁltering is an important task used to improve the quality of SAR
images. However, the speckle noise phenomenon is fundamentally different
than the noise appearing in traditional images. Therefore, conventional de-
noising algorithms cannot be used. Instead, a more system-speciﬁc approach
considering the unique statistics of the speckle should be used.
SARs has been used for several decades now, and the speckle ﬁltering
issue has been well documented by the SAR community. If searching for
speckle ﬁlters one will ﬁnd an abundance of different implementations and
solutions. However, the subject is by no mean exhausted and the problem is
far from solved. With the introduction of non-local (NL) ﬁltering in image
processing a new family of denoising algorithms have emerged [11]. This
has evidently led to a new family of speckle ﬁlters [12]. NL speckle ﬁlte-
ring has been widely researched within the last decade. Initially, ﬁlters for
single polarization images were introduced. But as the process was veri-
ﬁed the focus has changed, and the main topic the last couple of years has
been within NL speckle ﬁltering of multi-channel images such as PolSAR
and InSAR. This is indeed an ongoing research topic and new papers on the
subject are published every year.
Historically, users of SAR images suffered from the availability of high-
resolution frequent images. With SAR satellites being operated by only a
handful of large operators images could be costly. Naturally, SAR users
- often limited by funds - have been forced to work with as few images as
possible in order to reach a certain goal. But recently, this issue has changed.
In April 2014, the SAR satellite Sentinel-1A was launched as part of
the European Copernicus-program. In April the 2016, an identical satellite
Sentinel-1B followed [13]. The system achieved full operational capabilities
in 2017 [14]. In Interferometric Wide-Swath (IW) mode, the system almost
covers the entire European continent and offers dual polarized SAR data
with a coverage frequency of six days and resolutions down to a few me-
ters over land in IW mode [15]. The Sentinel-1 system is a successor to the
old ERS and Envisat missions. However, contrary to previous missions the
Sentinel-1 system, funded by the EU and ESA, is based on free online data
dissemination. This means that everyone can access the data without the
need of pre-ordering or paying for the images. The fact that Copernicus pro-
gram offers free SAR data, and with high-frequency world-wide coverage
has actively kick-started the interest in new ways of exploiting SAR images.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Scope
Developing an optimum speckle ﬁlter for several different systems is difﬁ-
cult. Several research groups have been trying to solve this problem [16].
When trying to develop panacean ﬁlters the complexity is often increasing.
As a result, primitive spatial ﬁlters developed in the 1980’s are still widely
used within the community. As the Sentinel-1 system is gaining wider sup-
port, the need for a system-speciﬁc solution to the speckle problem growing.
An obvious approach is the utilization of the high time resolution and free
data dissipation of the Sentinel-1 system. This, in combination with the re-
cently introduced NL technique, could improve the quality of SAR images.
The scope of this thesis is three-fold. First of all, a review of historical
and contemporary despeckling algorithms will be given. Hopefully, this
will shed light on the main challenges in speckle ﬁltering. Secondly, ba-
sed on the review, a NL ﬁlter operating on Polarimetric SAR Interferome-
try (PolInSAR) Sentinel-1 images will be presented. The algorithm is ba-
sed on a novel optimization of the similarity measure used in NL ﬁltering
which results in a simple implementation while providing a high quality. At
last, the proposed method will be compared to historical and contemporary
speckle ﬁlters and the results will be evaluated.
While it is unlikely that this thesis will provide a ﬁnal solution to the
speckle problem, it is the hope that some of the methods developed here
could contribute to the ongoing development of speckle ﬁltering algorithms.
Especially for polarimetric and interferometric the Sentinel-1 data.
Outline
The outline of this thesis is as follows. Chapter 2 includes an introduction to
SAR and speckle, as well as presenting most of the theory used in the the-
sis. 3 contains a presentation of historical and contemporary solutions to the
speckle problem as well as the common methods used for the evaluation of
speckle ﬁlters. Chapter 4 presents a detailed description of a new speckle ﬁl-
tering method optimized for Sentinel-1 applications. An introduction to the
data processing is given in Chapter 5. All important results are presented in
Chapter 6 and the results are discussed in Chapter 7. Chapter 8 contains a
conclusion to the thesis. Further, Appendix A includes an overview of the
different datasets used in the thesis. Appendix B includes results for the in-
dividual datasets. An investigation of the sensitivity of the suggested ﬁlter
to different ﬁlter parameters are presented in Appendix C and Appendix
D includes a short investigation of the improved detection of grass-cutting
from Sentinel-1 by applying the suggested ﬁlter.

Theory
The following chapter will brieﬂy present the main concepts behind Synthetic
Aperture Radar (SAR) in order to gain a broader understanding of the pro-
blematic speckle noise. Further, an overview of the different imaging modes
will be outlined.
A Note on the Theory
It is not within the scope of this thesis to give a detailed explanation of pulse
radar theory and system principles. An introduction to basic radar prin-
ciples can be found in [17]. Further description of digital pulse coding is
found in [18]. For a comprehensive introduction to radar systems and the-
ory see [19]. Further, as the focus of this thesis is on the reduction of speckle
in SAR images, explanations regarding pre-processing, calibration, focus-
sing and other image-distortions in SAR-systems will not be covered any
further than the brief explanations hereunder. For a detailed explanation
regarding SAR processing, distortions and theory, the reader is directed to
[4, 5, 8, 9].
2.1
Synthetic Aperture Radar
RADAR is an acronym for RAdio Detection And Ranging. A traditional
pulse radar is used to detect the range to a target by transmitting a radio
pulse (typically in the MHz −GHz range) and waiting for the return of the

Speckle
A major drawback in SAR imaging systems is the inherent speckle noise
present in the images [10]. The word, noise, in the previous sentence is
italicized because speckle is in fact not noise in a strict sense but rather an
unwanted signal-dependent contribution1. Speckle appears in all active co-
herent imaging systems when imaging distributed targets. As most natural
sceneries can be classiﬁed as distributed (ﬁelds, water, forests) the problem
with speckle is so severe for SAR systems that it outweighs other electrical
noise sources [16]. Speckle noise is said to be fully developed if a certain set
of circumstances are true [16]; First, individual scatterers have to be large
compared to the wavelength of the system. Secondly, each resolution cell
must contain a large number of individual scatterers. And thirdly, all the in-
dividual scatterers within a resolution cell must be approximately the same
size. Figure 2.2 illustrates the phenomenon in a SAR context. Here, a single
resolution cell containing several scatterers is highlighted. As each scatterer
is reﬂecting the incoming complex signal individually the received signal
at the SAR-system is a sum of several contributions. Due to each scatte-
rer being much larger than the wavelength of the signal, the phase of each
contribution is highly varying and may be considered uniformly distribu-
ted [−π; π] [10]. Thus the measured complex signal C can be regarded as a
stochastic signal, dependent on the underlying reﬂectivity of the individual

Speckle Filtering
The following chapter will provide an introduction to different speckle ﬁl-
tering techniques.
Firstly, simple homomorphic and spatial ﬁltering ap-
proaches will be presented. Then, the more advanced wavelet and non-
local (NL) methods introduced recently will be covered. Afterward, the the-
ory behind multichannel ﬁltering is presented. The chapter will be conclu-
ded with an introduction to assessment of ﬁltering techniques.
Naturally, due to the scope of the subject, only the relevant theory behind
speckle ﬁltering will be presented. The chapter hereunder should serve as
an introduction to the different methods in order to connect the different
chapters. For a comprehensive introduction to speckle reduction methods
see [16]. Further, in an attempt to simplify the following chapter all deri-
vations of ﬁlters have been omitted. Instead, the reader is referred to the
respective articles originally suggesting the ﬁlters.
3.1
Introduction
The theory presented in Chapter 2 outlines an important fact regarding
speckle noise; It is statistically, fundamentally different than Additive White
Gaussian Noise (AWGN) normally considered in electrical engineering and
image processing. Therefore a new family of ﬁlters is required to process
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) images. These are often dubbed speckle
ﬁlters5.
The ideal speckle condition is considered ﬁrst. Here, the SAR is imaging
a homogeneous area with fully developed speckle. Due to the fact that the
noise-intensity follows a negative exponential distribution (Equation 2.7),
the Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE) of the underlying backscatter
can be estimated by averaging the intensity image. In other words, σ0 can
be estimated by averaging L independent measurements or looks as this will
preserve the mean value while reducing the variance of the signal by a factor
L [8]. This procedure is called multi-looking.
5Speckle ﬁltering is also known as despeckling, denoising or simply reconstruction. All four
terms will be used interchangeably throughout the thesis

Ideal multi-looking requires L independent samples which can be acqui-
red by making L observations. Alternatively, multi-looking can be perfor-
med by averaging the spectral components of the image in the focusing pro-
cess [4]. However, both of these methods have drawbacks. Acquiring many
individual observations can be difﬁcult due to satellite re-visit intervals and
price, and spectral averaging is naturally degrading the bandwidth of the
image. Instead, by assuming spatial homogeneity multilooking can be per-
formed as a simple spatial averaging by convolution with a desired ﬁlte-
ring kernel. Indeed, if an area is perfectly homogeneous and the individual
pixels are uncorrelated, spatial averaging will be sufﬁcient at reducing the
variance of the speckle.
However, the assumption of homogeneity will not hold as the imaged
area is increasing, and thus spatial averaging tends to degrade textural in-
formation in the image. This will often appear as blurring of edges and struc-
tures, sometimes called over-ﬁltering. The non-homogeneity of SAR-targets
also leads to another very fundamental problem: Speckle will not be present
everywhere in the image. As stated in Chapter 2 fully developed speckle is
only appearing under certain conditions. Granted, these situations are plen-
tiful for natural targets such as water, ﬁelds and forests. But when imaging
structures consisting of large individual scatterers such as cities, the speckle
phenomenon is not fully-developed. In some case, speckle will not be pre-
sent at all. Hence, a speckle ﬁlter shall treat different areas differently in
order to avoid information loss over areas without speckle. I.e. a speckle
ﬁlter is by nature required to be adaptive. Several ﬁlters in the literature
consider three distinct classes in SAR images; Homogeneous regions which
contain a lot of speckle, textured region with a low speckle level, and strong
scatterers without any speckle [16]. The latter are sometimes denoted per-
sistent scatterers or point targets.
To avoid any confusion regarding the goal of a speckle ﬁlter, a set of rules
or requirements that a speckle ﬁlter should abide will be deﬁned. A set of
rules for speckle ﬁltering was suggested by Gragnaniello et al. [30] and Di
Martino et al. [31]. However, a slightly different set of rules will be used for
this thesis. In general, a speckle ﬁlter should:
1. Minimize the variance in homogeneous areas
2. Not introduce bias
3. Preserve edges and points-targets
4. Not introduce artefacts
This problem can be solved in several ways. According to Di Martino et
al. [31], SAR despeckling methods can be loosely grouped according to three

3.2
Homomorphic Filters
As mentioned above speckle noise has a multiplicative characteristic, while
noise in traditional image processing has an additive characteristic. The-
refore, some early speckle ﬁltering methods suggested performing a sim-
ple mathematical transformation in order to transform of the multiplicative
speckle noise in Equation 2.9 into additive noise using a logarithmic trans-
formation,
z(s) = x(s)u(s)
log
−→z′(s) = x′(s) + u′(s)
(3.1)
The transformed SAR image could then be denoised using traditional met-
hods and an inverse transform could reconstruct the denoised image.
This very simple approach enables the image to be processed with tradi-
tional algorithms normally used for images corrupted by AWGN and initi-
ally gained some traction within the community. However, the method has
two important ﬂaws. First of all, as fully developed speckle is not present
globally in the image, the method will cause distortions in the ﬁltered image
as regions without speckle will be ﬁltered an equal amount to regions with
speckle. Secondly, it has been shown that log-transformation of the speckle
noise does not result in a Gaussian noise distribution [32].
These unfortunate attributes result in bias and artifacts in the despeckled
image [16], and as a result, homomorphic ﬁltering is rarely used indepen-
dently but rather as part of other ﬁlter methods. For example within Wa-
velet ﬁltering [33]. Recently, some authors have suggested an alternative
transformation method in order to utilize gaussian denoisers for speckle ﬁl-
tering. These methods will be discussed in Section 3.8.
3.3
Spatial Filters
Spatial ﬁltering is probably the most well-known family of image ﬁltering
methods. Spatial ﬁltering is achieved by performing a spatial averaging of
the intensity image. In its simplest form, this is achieved by convolving
the noisy image with a ﬁlter kernel such as the boxcar (square) or a Gaus-
sian kernel. As explained earlier, this approach assumes homogeneity in the
image which often results in some sort of blurring of high-frequency infor-
mation such as edges or point targets. In Figure 3.1 a noisy and boxcar ﬁlte-
red image is seen. It is noticed how the boxcar ﬁlter is great at reducing the
variance over the homogeneous dark area, while it completely destroying
edges and textures by blurring the image.

3.3.1
Lee Filter
The ﬁlter suggested by Lee in [34] under the name local statistics ﬁlter is a
local Linear Minimum Mean Square Error (LMMSE) ﬁlter. Here, the local
mean and variance are calculated in a 7 × 7 kernel, ∆s around the central
pixel s. These local statistics parameters are then used to create a noise-free
estimate of the underlying signal by using a ﬁrst order Taylor-expansion,

The ﬁlter was further reﬁned in [35] where it gained the more well-
known form. Here, the 7 × 7 kernel used to calculate the local statistics, was
replaced by one of several edge-aligned ﬁlter kernels based on underlying ed-
ges found in the noisy image. These changed proved for much better edge
preservation.

3.4
Wavelet Filters
In order to overcome the typical shortcomings with spatial ﬁlters, several
authors have suggested using ﬁlters based on wavelets instead. Wavelet
ﬁltering is achieved by performing an integral transformation of the signal
z(n) with a ﬁnite energy basis function ψ(n), called a wavelet. The wavelet
should fulﬁll certain conditions [38, eq. 2.16-2.18]. Where a Fourier trans-
formation of a signal provides information regarding the frequency com-
ponents of the signal, a wavelet transform provides information regarding
the presence of certain details, their sizes, and position based on the chosen
basis- (wavelet-) function [38]. By choosing a basis-function that includes
high-frequency components, point targets and edges can easily be represen-
ted by only a few wavelet components.
Wavelet representation has been widely used for image compression as most
information in common images can be expressed using only a few wavelet
coefﬁcients [31]. Similarly, wavelet transformation can be used to suppress
noise by thresholding certain coefﬁcients while preserving local sharp transi-
tions. Where a Fourier transformation is said to provide a single frequency
representation of a given signal, the Wavelet Transform (WT) is said to pro-
vide a multiresolution representation [16].
Examples of pure wavelet ﬁlters for SAR images includes the WCS ﬁlter
[39], and the segmentation-based Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) ﬁlter [20].
Wavelet ﬁlter generally outperforms spatial ﬁlters in terms of speckle re-
duction and edge preservation [33]. However, they tend to introduce visual
artifacts in the despeckled images [31], and today, wavelet processing is ge-
nerally used in combination with other techniques such as NL ﬁltering.

3.6
Multitemporal Filters
As mentioned in Chapter 2, ideal multilooking can be achieved by avera-
ging L independent samples from L independent observations. Similarly,
if a user has acquired L coregistered images of the same target, these ima-
ges can be averaged in the temporal domain. This is called multitemporal
ﬁltering. Multitemporal ﬁltering can be performed in a series of ways de-
pending on the application. The simplest method is a linear combination of
all the images on a pixel-to-pixel basis. However, more advanced methods
exist as well.
One multitemporal ﬁlter presented in [42] suggested using the Lee ﬁlter
in the temporal domain instead of the spatial. To prove the concept a set of
18 images acquired over a period of 19 months was used to create a recon-
structed image. The approach showed great noise reduction but requires
very long observation times when using a satellite with low revisit interval.
To overcome this disadvantage Quegan et al. suggested integrating data
from several satellite systems in order to achieve a higher time resolution
[43]. Here, the image is reconstructed by weighted averaging of several ima-
ges from different sources. The ﬁlter is implemented in a recursive fashion
so only minimal calculations is required to update the reconstructed image
when a new image is acquired.

Obviously, these despeckling approaches will only work as long as the
target remains completely stationary over the observation period. If change
is occurring between images the reconstructed signal will feature noise simi-
lar to how long exposure results in spatial distortions in slow-shutter pho-
tography.
In order to avoid these spatial distortions, an adaptive neighborhood ﬁl-
ter (3D-ANF) was suggested for speckle ﬁltering in [44]. The algorithm uti-
lizes a combination of multitemporal- and spatial ﬁltering by performing
region-growing in a stack of coregistered images. Another solution was
suggested with the DespecKS algorithm [45]. This ﬁlter is using the Kolmo-
gorov–Smirnov test to test whether pixels from different images were drawn
from the same underlying statistically homogeneous distribution. This ap-
proach enables ﬁltering not only in the temporal but also in the spatial dom-
ain, and with fantastic results as well.
Both the 3D-ANF ﬁlter and the DespecKS algorithm behaves as proto-
NL ﬁlters for multitemporal images and show powerful speckle reduction.
However, they both lack a robust similarity measure and have never gained
much traction as they were quickly surpassed by the NL ﬁlters mentioned
earlier.
Recently, Chierchia et al. suggested extending the SAR-BM3D algorithm
to multitemporal images [46]. The algorithm showed improvements over
the original SAR-BM3D algorithm, especially in terms of the preservation
of dark edges. However, the improvement comes at a greater cost of com-
putational complexity.
3.7
Other Filter Types
Although the most common despeckling approaches have been presented
above, several alternative methods exist in the literature. While often expe-
rimental, it is worth noting some of these ﬁlters in order to gain a deeper
understanding of the challenges that exist regarding speckle ﬁltering.
Some recent interesting approaches to speckle ﬁltering relies on sensor
fusion in order to create a prior used for estimating the underlying signal.
These methods include the optical-driven despeckling approach suggested
in [47]. Here an optical image is used to estimate pixel similarity before
actual ﬁltering is performed in the SAR image. The authors suggest that
their approach is outperforming the SAR-BM3D and PPB ﬁlters. However,
they are dependent on cloud-free optical images to perform the ﬁltering.
Another approach gaining popularity in recent years is the scattering-
based ﬁltering methods [48]. The approach relies on introducing physical-
based similarity criterion in order to improve existing NL ﬁltering algo-

3.8
Multichannel Filtering
Thus far all ﬁlters considered have originally been developed for ﬁltering
single polarization or span images. Though single polarized SAR images
have been and still are widely used, many applications today requires multi-
channel images. However, single-polarization speckle ﬁlters can not na¨ıvely
be used to ﬁlter each channel in a multichannel SAR image individually as
this would ruin the polarimetric or interferometric properties. Further, one
should ensure that the different bands in a multichannel image are not being
mixed together. According to Lee [49], multichannel ﬁltering must abide by
the following principles:
1. ”To avoid crosstalk between polarization channels, each element of the cova-
riance matrix must be ﬁltered independently in the spatial domain.”
2. ”To preserve polarimetric (and interferometric) properties [...] All terms of
the covariance matrix should be ﬁltered by the same amount.”
The Lee ﬁlter was adapted to polarimetric images using the above prin-
ciples in [49]. Here, a span image is created from the sum of the different in-
tensity channels. This span image is then used to ﬁnd suitable edge-aligned
windows as described earlier. At last, using this window, each element of
the covariance matrix is ﬁltered individually but equally.
Another approach to polarimetric speckle ﬁltering based on simulated
annealing was suggested in [50]. The ﬁlter proved to greatly reduce vari-
ance for homogeneous regions while preserving structures. However, the
ﬁlter was shown to induce radiometric distortion in the ﬁltered images.
A more recent approach to multichannel speckle ﬁltering was suggested
in [51], called NLSAR. Here, the NL approach is expanded by the intro-
duction of several novel contributions such as adaptivity, automatic tuning,
and automatic bias reduction. The general scheme of the algorithm is seen

3.9
Evaluation of Despeckling Algorithms
With several algorithms suggested for SAR image despeckling an important
question arises: How can despeckling algorithms be assessed? As speckle
is inherent in SAR images, no true reference signal exist which makes eva-
luation very difﬁcult. Historically, SAR images were often evaluated and
segmented by experts looking at the images. These experts could then qua-
litatively determine if a ﬁltering was good, i.e. if it made their job easier.
Today, visual assessment is still widely used within the community. Espe-
cially to evaluate the preservation of edges and points, and to assess the
introduction of potential artifacts.
In order to ease the visual assessment, some authors have suggested
plotting the ratio image. That is the pixel-wise ratio between the ﬁltered
and original image. For areas with fully developed speckle, the ratio image
should resemble a perfect speckle pattern with no textural information [31].
In Figure 3.6 the ratio between the original and boxcar ﬁltered image from
Figure 3.1 is shown. It is noticed that the ratio image contain a lot of structu-
ral information which suggests over-ﬁltering and smearing. Naturally, this
qualitative approach complicates the comparison of different ﬁlters, and
hence quantitative analysis methods are much preferred.
Quantitative despeckling assessment can be performed in two ways,
with or without reference [16]. With-reference methods rely on a reference
image, often an artiﬁcially created SAR image. A general with-reference ap-
proach was suggested in [31]. In the article, several test images generated
using a SAR simulator is presented. Each image is representing a distinct
case, such as a homogeneous area or a building. And for each case, a whole
series of indexes were suggested. Each index (or ﬁgure of merit) is repre-
senting a speciﬁc feature of the ﬁlter, such as edge-preservation, bias, or
noise-reduction. The idea is, that if everybody were using the same refe-
rence images, and thus indexes, comparison of ﬁlters is made easier.

Filter Description
The following chapter will present a description of the suggested speckle
ﬁlter. The structure of the chapter is as follows: First, the rationale behind
the ﬁlter will be presented hereunder. Thereafter, the basic ﬁltering met-
hod will be presented. In the end of the chapter, a description of the ﬁlter
expanded for Polarimetric SAR Interferometry (PolInSAR) images is given
together with a optimized version of the ﬁlter.
As shown in Chapter 3, research conducted within the last decade have
moved towards speckle ﬁltering using non-local (NL) approaches [12]. Most
recently, for Polarimetric SAR (PolSAR) or Interferometric SAR (InSAR) ima-
ges [55] [52]. NL ﬁltering combined with multitemporal images have also
very recently been suggested [46]. Although these methods show very pro-
mising results the algorithms are often very complex and require rigorous
implementations. As a result, many authors publish accompanying tool-
boxes for speckle ﬁltering with their articles in an attempt to reach the com-
munity [51, 40, 52, 46]. Even though these toolboxes guarantee reproduci-
bility, they may also discourage some users to embrace the development as
the solutions are difﬁcult to understand.
Perhaps, a simple NL ﬁlter optimized for the widely used Sentinel-1 sy-
stem could ﬁll a gap in the current landscape of despeckling algorithms.
Further, utilizing the high time resolution of the Sentinel-1 system with a
simple NL ﬁlter, might even result in a solution that could compare to the
quality of the advanced NL methods.
Hence, based on the review of speckle ﬁltering algorithms in Chapter 3,
it was decided to implement a simple multichannel Spatio-Temporal Non-
Local (STNL) speckle ﬁlter for Sentinel-1 images. The method relies on a
NL approach in both the spatial and temporal domain in order to optimize
the quality of the reconstructed image. Further, by taking advantage of the
theory developed for change detection in polarimetric Synthetic Aperture
Radar (SAR) images, a more efﬁcient similarity measure could be develo-
ped. The hope is, that by utilizing redundant information in several images
may prove to give results better than or comparable with the advanced NL
methods.

4.1
A Spatio-Temporal Non-Local Approach
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the multitemporal approach has some limita-
tions. A na¨ıve linear combination of several images totally disregards the
change that could have happened in between image acquisitions which can
lead to noise in the reconstructed image. Luckily, NL ﬁltering should solve
this problem. As mentioned earlier one of the main principles within NL ﬁl-
tering is the similarity measure. By selecting the correct similarity measure,
areas that have changed between acquisition times should show a very low
similarity, and thus be automatically avoided for ﬁltering.
So the problem can be reduced to determine the correct similarity mea-
sure. Here we can utilize the knowledge from another very important ﬁeld
within SAR imaging, namely change detection. Change detection is the abi-
lity to statistically determine whether a pixel s in image I and the same pixel
s in image II have changed between acquisitions. Change detection is a po-
werful tool for a wide range of applications, for example for segmentation
and edge-detection for agricultural applications [24]. Further, methods for
testing the similarity between covariance matrices have been developed for
SAR applications with the purpose of change detection. Obviously, these
methods can be utilized for NL ﬁltering.
Based on the original NL approach by Buades [11], a speckle ﬁlter have
been developed. As in the original ﬁlter, the method is based on ﬁnding
similarities in the image and thus recreating a pixel by weighted averaging
based on the similarity. To improve this method, the algorithm has been ex-
panded for the multitemporal case by searching for similarities in a stack of
images. This will, of course, result in increased execution times. So in order
to minimize computational cost, especially for large images, a search-area is
deﬁned in the neighborhood of s where we look for patch similarities. Furt-
her, as the original approach was developed for Gaussian distributed noise,
a new similarity measure was used.
The algorithm is outlined in Algorithm 1 and in Figure 4.1.
Given a stack of N images, [J(I)J(II)...J(N)] we wish to reconstruct the
ﬁrst image J(I). For each pixel s ∈J(I) we do the following: First we deﬁne
a patch ∆s of size P × P centred around the desired pixel s. Then we de-
ﬁne a search area (or neighborhood) of size N × N × N, called τ where we
search for patch similarities. For each pixel t ∈τ we calculate the simila-
rities between the two patches, S(∆s, ∆t), and calculate the corresponding
weight w(t) from the similarity. After we have iterated over τ we now have
a weight-map w(τ), of weights for the entire neighborhood. Then, the re-
constructed pixel, ˆx(s), is the weighted average of all pixels in τ. By sear-
ching an area of τ, the theoretical ENL for the ﬁltered image could be up

Two problems with NL ﬁltering for SAR images not mentioned so far

SAR images is much larger than normal images. Very bright point-scatterers
can appear with a backscatter value being many dB’s higher than the back-
ground [51]. When ﬁltering areas near point-targets even a very low weight
for those pixels can contribute to the reconstructed pixel.

function. The very high dynamic range for point-targets also another pro-
blem called the rare-patch effect [46]. Rare patches, such as point targets,
will likely not have any similar patches in the neighborhood and thus not
be subject to any ﬁltering. For a single pixel this is not a problem, but if
large kernels are chosen for the similarity measure the effect may lead to an
unﬁltered halo in the proximity of the rare patch. This problem is generally
solved by some kind of adaptivity. In our case, by incorporating a multi-
temporal approach, the hope is that rare patches can be ﬁltered using the
same patches in the underlying images. This will, of course, requires that
the patches are present in the underlying images which are not certain.

5.1.1
Pre-Processing
All data was downloaded from the Copernicus open access hub 8. Each da-
taset consists of one month, or ﬁve Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) images
collected into 4 polarimetric-interferometric images. A summary of the do-
wnloaded data is given in Appendix A.
The data is in the Single-Look Complex (SLC) Interferometric Wide-Swath
(IW) format. Contrary to the most common Sentinel-1 format Ground Range
Detected (GRD), SLC data is not projected on to the ground range using an
Earth ellipsoid. Thus, SLC data is provided in slant-range geometry as des-
cribed in Chapter 2 and will appear geometrical distorted compared to the
map in Figure 5.1. The pixel spacing of the unprocessed SLC IW data is
approximately 2.3 × 14.1 meters in range and azimuth respectively [57].
Prior to the speckle ﬁltering the data was processed using SNAP9 ver-
sion 6.0.3. SNAP is a software application developed by ESA for processing
of Sentinel data. In SNAP, the data was co-registered in order to transform
all images to the same coordinate system. To ensure consistency, all ima-
ges were coregistered to the same reference image. As Sentinel-1 IW images
consist of different image bursts acquired at slightly different times, the data
requires deburst. The deburst process collects the different image bursts into
one image, removing overlaps. After the deburst of the data, the desired
subset was selected. As to ensure that the images were not taking up too
much memory. The size of the chosen subset is 1024 × 615 pixels.
SNAP saves the data in SLC format, hence before ﬁltering, transforma-
tion into the Polarimetric SAR Interferometry (PolInSAR) covariance matrix
is required. This is done by spatial averaging as described in Chapter 2. Gi-
ven a covariance matrix of dimensions D × D the number of samples L used
in the spatial average must be L > D [51]. Thus in our case, the spatial aver-
aging is achieved using a simple 5 × 1 (range × azimuth) ﬁlter. This results
in a theoretical Equivalent Number of Looks (ENL) of 4.4 and approximate
pixel spacing of 11.5 × 14.1 meters (range × azimuth) in slant-range [57].

5.1.3
Filter Parameters
The general approach to implementing the Spatio-Temporal Non-Local (STNL)
and Optimized Spatio-Temporal Non-Local (STNLo) ﬁlters are given in Chap-
ter 4. However, several of the details of the implementation have not yet
been discussed. Mainly, the selection of the ﬁltering parameters h, P and N.
As the speed of the algorithm is proportional to both P and N squared,
these values should be as low as possible. However, a larger value for N is
expected to increase ﬁltering quality as a larger neighborhood is used for the
averaging process. This will especially be true for very large homogeneous
areas. Generally, a search area of between 21 × 21 to 39 × 39 is suggested by
the literature [12]. In terms of the patch size P, a larger patch size does not
necessarily mean better performance. Setting the patch size too small may
result in reinforcement of the noise as similar noise-patches are easily found.
Further, by choosing a small patch size, the local geometry such as edges
can be difﬁcult to determine and thus result in bad ﬁltering performance of
edges. In the other extreme, by setting the patch size too large will result in
very few similar regions found, especially of textured areas.
It has been suggested that the parameters should be chosen locally for
each pixel [51]. However, this will naturally increase the complexity of the
algorithm. Instead, it is suggested to choose a global setting by a numerical
optimization tailored for the Sentinel-1 system.
In order to determine the global settings for the algorithm, a synthe-
tic PolInSAR dataset was created. Based on the process outlined by Lee
[5, 28], several homogeneous areas of a Sentinel-1 image was selected and
the average covariance matrix of each area was calculated. These average
covariance matrices were then used to create new samples by using a mul-
tivariate random distribution. This process can then be repeated for a stack
of images, thus simulating the natural change between image acquisitions.
Further, in order to simulate the occurrence of strong scatterers, two buil-
dings from the Sentinel-1 image was chosen and superimposed on the si-
mulated image. Simulated samples are then mapped to a synthetic image by
using a suitable template. This process has the advantage that a clean image
can be generated together with the noisy images. This image can then be
used to determine the ﬁltering quality of a given process. The areas used
to generate the simulated images are shown in Figure 5.3 together with the

Matlab Implementation
The STNL, and STNLo-algorithms described above was implemented using
Matlab 2017a together with a small SAR-processing library. The library in-
cludes methods to read and write Sentinel-1 data in formats used by SNAP,
functions to perform despeckling on SAR data using different methods for
comparison, functions to generate, display and save SAR images in different
formats, and functions used to evaluate the performance of despeckling al-
gorithms. The ﬁlter was implemented in two versions, the simple and the
optimized version.
The code was partially vectorized using the build-in Matlab function
im2col. This restricts the implementation to using only three for-loops, one
for each pixel dimension. Further, the ﬁrst for-loop was parallelized using
the parfor capabilities of Matlab. A small function enabling circular ele-
ment access, access, was implemented to avoid rigid implementation near
image edges. This may result in overﬂow of one image edge to another.
Noisy images are loaded in their full size. Thus the processing is limited
by the RAM on the computer used.
Attached to this thesis, two folder containing Matlab code have been
uploaded. The folder matlab test contains two test-examples than can be
run by the user. The folder, matlab full, contains the full code required to
ﬁlter the Sentinel-1 images. However, due to the size of the data, Sentinel-1
data is not included in the upload, and matlab full can not be run. Instead,
matlab full should be considered as a separate appendix. Data can howe-
ver, be provided separately upon request.

Results
In order to assess the quality of the newly developed ﬁlter, a series of tests
have been performed. The tests have been performed on simulated and real
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) data and compared with different standard
and advanced despeckling algorithms. Section 6.1 contains the results from
the simulated data. Section 6.2 contain results of the Sentinel-1 data. A
summary of the results for the individual datasets is found in Appendix B.
In general, the different months proved not to affect the performance of the
ﬁlter signiﬁcantly, and thus only the average results are presented here.
In case of the simulated images both the Spatio-Temporal Non-Local
(STNL) and Optimized Spatio-Temporal Non-Local (STNLo) ﬁlter have been
assessed. Due to computation times, only the STNLo have been applied on
Sentinel-1 images. The ﬁltering has been performed for both single and mul-
titemporal images. Unless otherwise stated, all shown images of the STNL
and STNLo ﬁlters, have been generated using 4 Polarimetric SAR Interfe-
rometry (PolInSAR) images. In other cases, the number of images will be
denoted by a parenthesis after the name.
The ﬁlter has been compared with several alternative speckle ﬁltering
methods introduced in Chapter 2. Two advanced and two simple PolInSAR
ﬁlters have been chosen for the comparison. The simple ﬁlters are the box-
car ﬁlter and the Lee ﬁlter. The advanced ﬁlters are the NLSAR ﬁlter and
the BM3D ﬁlter implemented using the MuLog method. The implementa-
tion of these last two ﬁlters has been downloaded from the original authors
website10. All calculations was performed using 12 cores at the High Per-
formance Computing (HPC) service at DTU 11.
The results are assessed with the methods introduced in Chapter 3. Here,
both the bias and the speckle coefﬁcient, σv, requires measurements in a
running window. However, in order to reduce the computational cost, these
methods were implemented using a random uniform sampling of the entire
image. The sampling was limited to 1000 points in a window of 5 × 5 pixels.
Further, the top 1% percentile were omitted to avoid biased results.

A Note on Computation Times
As part of the assessment, computation times of the different ﬁlters will be
shown.
As the NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters have been downloaded by the authors
of the respective ﬁlters, users do not have any control over the implementa-
tion. Further, these implementations have all been done in different langua-
ges and optimized for different machines. This is expected to have a great
impact on the computation times for the different ﬁlters. Further, the im-
plementation of the NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters incorporates look-up-tables to
reduce the amount of computations for pixels previously compared. Thus a
strict comparison on the basis of the results below cannot be made. Instead,
the computation times in this chapter should merely be seen as a guideline.
All computations have been processed on the same machine with the same
amount of cores available. It is unknown if the BM3D and NLSAR imple-
mentations are utilizing all available resources.
In order to gain broader insight into the computations times, a brief over-
view of the complexities is presented here. The computation time of the
STNLo ﬁlter in terms of Big-O notation have been derived in Chapter 4 to,
TSTNL = O(M2N2NP2).
(6.1)
The speed of the NLSAR ﬁlter is derived by the authors to[51],
TNLSAR = O(M2N2
mP2S).
(6.2)
Here, N2
m is the maximum neighborhood size and S is the number of dif-
ferent weights used for ﬁltering. In the normal conﬁguration NmS > NN
and thus the STNLo ﬁlter is expected to be faster.
The speed of the original BM3D algorithm is given in [41, Sec. IV A,
Table I]. The analysis is a little complex, but can be simpliﬁed for the normal
proﬁle to approximately,
TBM3D ≈O(M2 · 25000).
(6.3)
Now for SAR images in the MuLog case, this algorithm has to be repeated
D2 times, where D is the dimension of the covariance matrix. This results in
approximately 400.000 calculations per pixel. Further, the complexity of the
MuLog algorithm should be added. Comparing this with the complexity of
the STNLo algorithm given above, O(M2N2NP2) = O(M2 · 152 · 4 · 52) =
O(M2 · 22500). All in all, the expected computation time for the STNLo ﬁlter
is faster than the MuLog BM3D.
When running the code on a normal laptop, the computation times will
differ greatly from the results given below. Especially the STNLo algorithm
will suffer from a reduced number of cores due to the implementation.

Discussion
The following chapter presents a discussion of the results from Chapter 6,
as well as general observations and issues met during the development of
the ﬁlter. The structure of the chapter is as follows: Initially, the results of
the simulated and Sentinel-1 images are discussed. This will be followed by
a discussion of suggested improvements to the STNLo ﬁlter.
7.1
Speckle Filtering of Simulated Images
The results from the weight-map in Figure 6.1 largely appear to indicate
that the ﬁlter is behaving as expected. Similar regions are detected and used
for ﬁltering, while dissimilar regions are given a low weight. The region,
a, next to the building appears to undergo only a reduced amount of ﬁl-
tering, with similar pixels only found in two of the four images. This is
possibly due to the fact, that the building has been superimposed from real
Sentinel-1 images, different look-angles could inﬂuence the similarity of the
patches. The problem might also be caused by the change happening bet-
ween the images. From Chapter 5 it is recalled, that the simulated images
are each generated from individual Sentinel-1 images. Therefore, changes
in the Sentinel-1 image between acquisitions causes a change in the simu-
lated images. Figure 6.1 also suggests that over-ﬁltering might appear in
some cases. For the boundary region b, pixels outside the boundary have
been used for ﬁltering as well, indicated by the light-grey area in Figure 6.1.
An explanation could be, that the ﬁlter bandwidth parameter being set too
high, and thus, dissimilar pixels are weighted as well. The same phenome-
non appears for the textured area d.
The quantitative assessment of the simulated images suggests that the
STNLo ﬁlter can compete with the advanced NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters. Both
in terms of computation time, Tc, ENL and image quality, IQ, the STNLo ﬁl-
ter scored high results. The optimized version of the ﬁlter, STNLo, showed
to be a great improvement over the STNL ﬁlter, both in terms of speed and
robustness. It is expected, that the removal of the elements in the covari-
ance matrix, not only improved the speed due to the decreased number of
multiplications required for the similarity measure; this optimization also
77
==PAGE:92==

improved the robustness as the omitted elements added extra noise to the
similarity measure, thus making it harder to ﬁnd similar areas. The ENL
for the STNLo is very small compared to the theoretical achievable ENL of
N × N × N = 152 · 152 · 4. However, this very high ENL is only expected to
appear over very large homogeneous regions.
The DG used to investigate the speckle reduction proved to give varying
results. Almost all ﬁlters were found to give an increase in speckle noise
which was very unexpected. Especially for the BM3D ﬁlter which showed
to be the second worst in terms of DG after the boxcar ﬁlter. Clearly, one
should be critical to these results as they differ greatly from the visual asses-
sment of the images. It is unknown what exactly causes these discrepancies.
Possibly, the chosen image template is ill-suited for these kinds of compari-
sons, or perhaps an error in the assessment causes the problem. As reported
in [53], MSE-based image assessments can be prone to varying results and
thus more advanced assessment methods are needed. MSE-based methods
to evaluate the performance of speckle ﬁlters are widely used in the commu-
nity [33], [20], [21]. However, authors often fail to document the methods
used to generate the simulated images.
Visual assessment of the simulated images suggests that the STNLo ﬁlter
is outperformed by the NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters. Both the NLSAR and
BM3D show much better preservation of the edges and the textured region.
However, the BM3D ﬁlter does show some indications of over-ﬁltering for
the coherence images, with darkening of the colors and reduction of the
building sizes. The Lee ﬁlter was shown to introduce artifacts and add a
level of foggy texture over homogeneous areas. This is likely due to the edge-
aligned kernels used for ﬁltering.
7.2
Speckle Filtering of Sentinel-1 Images
As with the simulated images, the results of the Sentinel-1 images suggests
that the STNLo ﬁlter can compete with the more advanced methods. For the
quantitative assessment, the STNLo ﬁlter scores higher than the NLSAR and
BM3D in terms of the speckle coefﬁcient σv. This suggests that the ﬁlter is
better at lowering the speckle noise. However, the boxcar ﬁlter still appears
to achieve the highest σv. This is likely due to the fact, that the boxcar ﬁlter
is performing indiscriminate ﬁltering of the entire image. Also on areas
containing structure.
From the investigation of the bias, it appears that the NLSAR ﬁlter has
introduced a large bias to the ﬁltered image. What causes this bias have not
been investigated. Surely, bias is not expected to be an issue, as the NLSAR
ﬁlter contains a bias reduction step countering this exact problem [51]. Pos-
sibly, the problem is caused when merging the NLSAR code with the code
developed for this thesis. The downloaded NLSAR ﬁlter is developed to
==PAGE:93==
7.2. SPECKLE FILTERING OF SENTINEL-1 IMAGES

be used together with its own toolbox. But in order to ease the compari-
son between the ﬁlters, parts of the NLSAR toolbox was merged with the
toolbox developed for this thesis. The positive high bias for the boxcar ﬁlter
is most likely due to the method used to measure bias. It is recalled from
Chapter 3, that bias is measured by calculating the mean value of kernels,
5 × 5 pixels in size. As strong scatterers are smeared, some kernels placed
close to bright scatterers might measure an increase in mean value compa-
red to the original. The STNLo (4) ﬁlter shows a small offset of 0.02. This
is possibly introduced when using several images in the ﬁltering process.
This will be discussed further in Section 7.3. Besides the STNLo (4) ﬁlter, all
ﬁlters exhibit low offset, comparable to the bias introduced by the Lee ﬁlter.
As the Lee ﬁlter has been widely used by the community for decades, it is
assumed that this offset is within the accepted limits.
In terms of the bias reduction in the coherence magnitude, γ0, the BM3D
ﬁlter is by far the superior. The STNLo and the NLSAR ﬁlter show similar
performance, both better than the Lee and boxcar ﬁlters.
In terms of the coefﬁcient of variation, Cv, the STNLo ﬁlter is performing
slightly better than the NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters, with good preservation of
the variance in area a and b. The boxcar ﬁlter is shown to destroy textural
information by severely lowering Cv for area a and b. The Lee ﬁlter appears
better at preserving the variation than the BM3D and almost as good as the
NLSAR ﬁlter. However, the ﬁlter does appear to introduce artifacts due to
the edge-aligned kernels used for ﬁltering. The best Cv reduction for area c
and d is shown by the boxcar ﬁlter. This is most likely due to the indiscrimi-
nate ﬁltering.
The visual inspection of the backscatter images suggests some over-ﬁltering
by the STNLo ﬁlter. The ship (point target) have been reduced in size, and
the area south of the pier have lost texture. However, the STNLo ﬁlter ap-
pears to preserve the texture area of the city better than the BM3D and NL-
SAR ﬁlter. This is especially clear when comparing the shipyard in the un-
ﬁltered image with the STNLo, BM3D and NLSAR images.
The ratio images support this conclusion. The ratio image for the STNLo
ﬁlter shows large homogeneous grey regions over the city areas. This indi-
cates that no ﬁltering has been performed on these areas. From Chapter 2 it
is recalled, that fully developed speckle only appear over distributed targets
and is not expected over cities. The ratio images for the BM3D and NLSAR
ﬁlter both results in perfect speckle patterns over the entire image. Howe-
ver, for large areas in the selected images speckle noise is not expected to be
a problem. This indicates that the BM3D ﬁlter and NLSAR ﬁlter have remo-
ved speckle in areas without speckle. This, in turn, suggests that the BM3D
ﬁlter and the NLSAR ﬁlter have removed textural information in the ima-
ges. This is most visible for the backscatter images of the shipyard. Here, it

variations are noise. Indeed, the boxcar ﬁlter is expected to destroy texture
in the ﬁltered image, and seeing the BM3D ﬁlter behave similarly could sug-
gest that this is the case for the BM3D ﬁlter as well. However, further inves-
tigations into the subject are required before any deﬁnite conclusions can be
made. The NLSAR, Lee and STNL ﬁlter are hard to discriminate in terms of
phase quality. The NLSAR ﬁlter appear to generate smoother edges.
7.3
Inﬂuence of the Number of Images
In general, increasing the number of images does not appear to improve the
overall ﬁltering quality in terms of the quantitative assessments.
Even though an increase in images appear to improve the speckle re-
duction in terms of ENL, σv and variance reduction of homogeneous areas;
the improvement comes at a cost of increased bias and computation costs.
This is clear when observing Figure 6.11. Here, the backscatter of the water
regions has greatly changed between acquisitions which results in a change
in color for the ﬁltered image. Naturally, the similarity measure should have
countered this issues by weighting dissimilar areas very low. However, it
is possible that many very small contributions from dissimilar areas have
inﬂuenced the ﬁnal results to such a degree, that the ﬁltered result is biased.
7.4
Edge Preservation
Investigation of the simulated images suggested that the STNLo ﬁlter decre-
ased the width of dark lines in the image. This has been further investigated
and is presented Figure 6.12. Here, subset D is shown for the December data-
set containing a dark line through the image. From the backscatter images
it is clear that all ﬁlters except the BM3D ﬁlter appear to smear the line. This

7.5. COMPUTATION TIMES
81
is also apparent from the ratio images where the road is clearly visible for
all ﬁlters except the BM3D ﬁlter. From Figure 6.1, it is seen how pixels far
away from the centered reference pixel are weighted with a low weight. As
mentioned above, many dissimilar pixels with low weight might inﬂuence
the ﬁnal result and thus results in reduced edge preservation. However, the
edge-preservation of the STNLo ﬁlter appear to be just as good as the the
NLSAR and Lee ﬁlters.
7.5
Computation Times
The improvement in computation time gained by the optimization in Chap-
ter 4 was larger than expected. Likely, this is due to the reduced number
of complex elements in the covariance matrix. Thus reducing the required
number of multiplications when calculating the determinant.
For the Sentinel-1 image the computation time of the NLSAR ﬁlter had
surpassed the STNLo ﬁlter. This unexpected result indicates that the com-
putation time for one NLSAR ﬁlter scales differently regarding the image
size than expected. This is clearly to the advantage of the STNLo ﬁlter as
SAR processing are often expected to be performed on much larger images
than in this example.
This investigation shows that the suggested ﬁlter can compete with the
advanced NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlter in its current form in terms of com-
putation time. Regardless of the inefﬁcient implementation. Naturally, it
should be kept in mind that the processing was performed using a high-
performance machine. Currently, the STNLo ﬁlter is implemented in a par-
tially vectorized Matlab code. Using a more efﬁcient language such as C
or C++ is expected to improve the speed greatly suggesting that the STNLo
ﬁlter will be even faster yet.
7.6
Suggested Improvements
Currently, the main problem with the ﬁlter is the bias introduced when per-
forming multitemporal ﬁltering. This could be solved by implementing a
new weight function. Currently, the weight function is weighing all con-
tributions, even those with very low similarity. This is naturally a problem
when large areas are used for ﬁltering, as contributions from many dissimi-
lar pixels can add up to a large bias. This is especially problematic for areas
exhibiting major variation in the covariance matrix between image acquisi-
tions. A threshold for determination of similar covariance matrices has been
used for change detection [24]. By incorporating a similar threshold in the
weight function, the bias problem could be reduced. That is, by developing
a weight function with a hard cut-off.

82

An efﬁcient implementation is required, in order to efﬁciently test the
computation time of the ﬁlter. It is suggested that further improvements
should include a C/C++ or equivalent implementation.
This would also
make a comparison between the ﬁlters easier. Further, the computation time
could be improved by reusing similarity estimated for some pixels.
Another improvement could be the predetermination of the underlying
target distribution. This has been done successfully in [20] and [30]. This
approach exploits the detecting the underlying variance in order to deter-
mine the best ﬁlter parameters. This approach might improve the ﬁltering,
but naturally at a cost of increased complexity.
The multitemporal ﬁlter suggested by Quegan [43] has a recursive form.
This means that initially only a single image is used for reconstruction. Ad-
ditional images can then be added afterwards in order to improve the ﬁrst
estimate. A similar approach could be used for the STNLo ﬁlter. This could
make it easier to update the image estimate as more images were made avai-
lable.
7.7
Summary
The optimization of the covariance matrix proved to results in a large re-
duction in computation times. Further, the optimization also appeared to
increase the robustness of the algorithm as noisy elements in the covariance
matrix have been deleted.
Tests showed that the suggested ﬁlter behaved as expected, by identi-
fying similar regions in several images and using these to reconstruct indi-
vidual pixels.
Quantitative assessment showed that the performance of the STNLo ﬁl-
ter was comparable with the advanced NLSAR and BM3D ﬁlters on all pa-
rameters. And both qualitative and quantitative assessment showed that
the STNLo ﬁlter outperformed the boxcar and Lee ﬁlters on almost all para-
meters except computation time.
Visual assessment of the simulated images showed poor performance by
the STNLo ﬁlter, especially when compared to the more advanced BM3D
and NLSAR methods. This bad performance was, however, not visible in
the real Sentinel-1 images. It is expected that the ideal speckle case of the
simulated images results in false positive results for the BM3D and NLSAR
ﬁlters. From Chapter 5 it is recalled, that the simulated images contains
speckle in the entire image except for the superimposed buildings. In rea-
lity, the speckle problem is more complex. Especially when considering the
relatively low resolution of the Sentinel-1 system. This was also suggested
by visual assessment of the Sentinel-1 images where the BM3D and NLSAR
ﬁlters appears to indiscriminately remove speckle everywhere in the image.
This, despite large areas in the image could be classiﬁed as heterogeneous.
==PAGE:97==
7.8. CLOSING REMARKS
83
Here, the STNLo ﬁlter appeared better at preserving the texture.
The advanced BM3D ﬁlter showed very good results on all parame-
ters. However, visual inspection of the coherence images suggests that some
over-ﬁltering appear. It is unknown what causes this over-ﬁltering. But it
is recalled that the methods rely on separation and individual ﬁltering of
the individual elements in the covariance matrix. Perhaps, this leads to the
destruction of the interrelationship between the elements, resulting in the
unequal ﬁltering of the different elements.
Tests showed that the STNLo ﬁlter introduced bias when ﬁltering was
performed using several PolInSAR images. This is likely due to the choice
of weight function.
7.8
Closing Remarks
Despite the bias problem, the ﬁlter can still be considered valuable. As bias
only appeared to be problematic for the multitemporal case, the ﬁlter can
still be applied for single PolInSAR images. The performance of the STNLo
(1) ﬁlter showed to be just as good as the BM3D and NLSAR ﬁlters on a
wide range of assessment indices. Furthermore, the required implementa-
tion of the STNLo ﬁlter is relatively simple, compared to the more advanced
NLSAR and BM3D.
Several quantitative assessment indices were used to evaluate ﬁlter per-
formance. As mentioned in Chapter 3, no standard evaluation method for
SAR ﬁlters is widely used by the community. Hence, authors often have the
liberty to pick and choose the evaluation methods best suited for speciﬁc
ﬁlters. In the evaluation above, several methods from the literature have
been used to evaluate the performance of the STNLo ﬁlter compared to the
other ﬁlters. However, several additional methods of evaluation exist. Eva-
luation methods not used in this thesis should not be seen as an attempt
to under-report the ﬁndings, but rather as a necessity in order to an avoid
overly complex result chapter.
The STNLo ﬁlter introduced in the thesis was developed in an attempt to
present a simple, yet powerful speckle ﬁlter by utilizing redundant informa-
tion from the high revisit frequency of the Sentinel-1 system. This was achie-
ved by introducing an optimized similarity measure for the polarimetric-
interferometric covariance matrix. This optimization was performed by omit-
ting certain elements and rearranging the covariance matrix. As mentioned
in Chapter 4, some applications might require these additional elements.
Hence, not all applications can be used in combination with the STNLo ﬁl-
ter.
The optimization does, however, prove to greatly reduce computational

cost. This optimization beneﬁts the suggested STNLo ﬁlter, but could just
as well be used in for the NLSAR and BM3D algorithms. The optimization
suggested here could actually be used as an important optimization step for
all non-local (NL) ﬁlters operating on dual-polarized PolInSAR data.

8
Conclusion
The following thesis has presented a review of speckle ﬁlters for Synthetic
Aperture Radar (SAR) applications. The review has shown that big advan-
cements in speckle ﬁltering algorithms have been achieved in the last de-
cade. Especially, the introduction of non-local (NL) ﬁltering methods have
shown great improvement in the ﬁltering quality. However, the review sho-
wed that this improvement had been achieved at the cost of a high ﬁlter
complexity. Furthermore, despite the surge in new algorithms none seemed
to be directed speciﬁcally at the new European Sentinel-1 system.
Thus, it was decided to develop a simple ﬁlter Polarimetric SAR Inter-
ferometry (PolInSAR) ﬁlter, based on the NL principle and optimized for
the Sentinel-1 system. The developed ﬁlter exploits a similarity measure de-
veloped for PolInSAR change detection in order to detect similar pixels in
a time series of Sentinel-1 images. Further, by introducing a novel optimi-
zation of the covariance matrix the computation time and robustness was
improved greatly.
The proposed method has been compared with historical and contem-
porary speckle ﬁlters using both simulated in real Sentinel-1 images. The
suggested ﬁlter have been investigated with positive remarks. The ﬁlter
performance was comparable with more advanced NL methods and better
than the simple spatial methods.
The beneﬁts from utilizing the spatial domain were not as unambigu-
ously advantageous as expected. In some cases, the introduction of several
images in the ﬁltering showed to introduce bias to the results. This is most
likely caused by the weight function used by the ﬁlter. It is expected that an
improved weight-function with a hard cut-off could improve the problem.
Despite this problem, the ﬁlter showed great performance when using only
a single image.
The suggested optimization of the similarity measure proved to greatly
reduce the computation time in the NL ﬁltering. This novel approach can ea-
sily be introduced in many other NL ﬁltering methods. It is hoped that this
method could contribute to the ongoing development of speckle ﬁltering al-
gorithms, further improving the quality of SAR applications, beneﬁting all
users of SAR data.

Inﬂuence of Filter Parameters
In order to achieve a better understanding of the sensitivity of the STNLo
ﬁlter, a test series showing was performed. The series consisted of three
individual tests. In each test, one of the three variable parameters P, N
and h was varied and the corresponding images was plotted. While one
parameter was changed, the remaining parameters was initialized to their
respective standard value: P = 5, N = 15 and h = 85. The tests were
performed on a stack of 4 PolInSAR images of size 124 × 124.
(a) P = 5, N = 15, h = 25
(b) P = 5, N = 15, h = 50
(c) P = 5, N = 15, h = 75
(d) P = 5, N = 15, h = 100
(e) P = 5, N = 15, h = 250
(f) P = 5, N = 15, h = 500
Figure C.1: Inﬂuence of ﬁlter bandwidth parameter, h on the backscatter
and coherence images. If h is large, dissimilar pixels are used for the recon-
struction resulting in smearing.

25 to 500. The resulting ﬁltered images are shown in Figure C.2. It is noticed

plotted in Figure C.2. It is noticed that when P increases, the smoothing

D
Detection of Grass Cutting
In collaboration with the Danish company DHI-Gras it was decided to look
into the possibility of using the coherence images to detect grass cutting
events. The detection of grass cutting is important as some farmers receive
subsidies from the European Union which requires them to cut their grass
ﬁelds at certain dates. Detection of grass cutting events in Estonia has pre-
viously been investigated using coherence in [27]. The authors showed a
signiﬁcant correlation between coherence and grass-height.
By utilizing visual images from the Copernicus mission Sentinel-2, grass
cutting can easily be estimated using the normalized difference vegetation
index (NDVI),
NDVI = NIR −Red
NIR + Red.
(D.1)
NDVI is used to assess whether an area contains green vegetation. So a large
decrease in NDVI for a grass ﬁeld between two image acquisitions indicate
a grass cutting event.
However, as Sentinel-2 is an optical mission cloud cover can obstruct the
ground vision, and thus it is desired to estimate cutting dates using Sentinel-
1 instead.
Processing
A study area was selected over southern Funen and the island of T˚asinge,
Denmark. Data from the Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 systems was acquired
from the Sentinel data-hub from June and July 2017.
Two images were acquired using the Sentinel-2 system from June 19th
and July 9th 2017. Using the sentinel processing tool SNAP, NDVI images
were created. Using this NDVI data, 10 grass ﬁeld-patches (main group)
showing a large decrease in NDVI between the two acquisitions was identi-
ﬁed. It is expected that this decrease is due to a grass-cutting event. Another
10 ﬁelds-patches (control group), showing either an increase or no change in
NDVI between acquisitions was also identiﬁed. A summary of the selected
patches and the associated crops is seen in Table D.1.

The Equivalent Number of Looks (ENL) is 4.4. The selected ﬁeld-patches is

As the investigation is of the coherence between different satellite pas-
ses, the 7 SAR images were paired in successive pairs of two and collected
in a covariance matrix. Each covariance matrix was then ﬁltered individu-
ally using 2 different speckle ﬁlters. The ﬁrst ﬁlter used was the BM3D ﬁlter
developed by Dadov et al. [41] for image processing and later expanded
to SAR images by the MuLog method [52]. The second ﬁlter used was the
STNLo ﬁlter developed for this project and described in the thesis Speckle
Reduction in Synthetic Aperture Radar Images - A Spatio-Temporal Non-Local
Approach. The STNLo ﬁlter was used with both a high and low ﬁlter band-
width parameter to simulate different levels of ﬁltering.

In Figure D.6 the average coherence for each of the patches is plotted indi-

how all patches in the main group show an increase in coherence towards
the end of the observation period.
We want to investigate whether this increase is easier to detect after ﬁlte-
ring. This is done using a very simple approach. In Figure D.5 red lines are
marking the dates for the Sentinel-2 images used to calculate the NDVI. It is
noticed that the ﬁrst Sentinel-1 image pair has been acquired before the ﬁrst
Sentinel-2 image. This measurement is then used as a reference to calcu-
late the mean, m0 and standard deviation, s0 for both the main and control
group. For each of the successive dates, the number of ﬁelds exhibiting an
average coherence larger than m0 + 5s0 is counted. The results given in Table
D.2.
From Table D.2 we notice that using the raw dataset, 10 deviations are
found in the main group. This number is 16 for the BM3D and STNL2 and
18 for the STNL1. The number of deviations found in the control group is
fairly constant for all datasets except the STNL1 ﬁlter.
Further, for each ﬁeld, the maximum value is determined. The maxi-
mum detected value was found at the same date for both the noisy and
ﬁltered images. The estimated cutting date is then assumed to be the date
before the maximum. These results are outlined in Table D.3.
Discussion and Conclusion
The investigation has been carried out based on the assumption that a de-
crease in NDVI constitutes a grass-cutting event. However, this relationship
might not always hold. In [27] it is suggested that humidity has a great effect
on the coherence magnitude. This relationship has not been investigated
here.
In [27] it was determined that there is a signiﬁcant correlation between
the coherence and the grass-height for Sentinel-1 images, thus indicating
that coherence images can be used for the detection of grass cutting. In the
results above, we see an indication that ﬁltering of the coherence images
could correct the coherence bias. Thus making the separation between low-
coherence and high-coherence areas easier. This very simple investigation
indicates that ﬁltering of the coherence matrix in SAR images could improve
the detection of grass cutting events using Sentinel-1 images. However, in
order to establish this relationship fully, further investigations are required.
