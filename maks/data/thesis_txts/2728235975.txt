Abstract
Traditional authentication approaches, including passwords and conventional biometrics,
possess inherent limitations regarding security and user­friendliness. Electroencephalog­
raphy (EEG) captures the brain’s electrical activity via non­invasive electrodes placed on
the scalp. Each individual’s brain response to specific stimuli or concepts arises from a
unique interplay of genetics, life experiences, and neural networks, thereby ensuring the
distinctiveness of EEG signals. In light of this, recent research has explored the potential
of EEG as a biometric tool for identification and authentication.
In this study, we introduce a comprehensive EEG authentication framework that employs
motor imagery, encompassing the entire spectrum of EEG signal processing and authen­
tication. Our framework integrates channel selection and deep learning classification to
provide end­to­end authentication. Through our evaluations, we explore various determi­
nants of authentication precision, such as temporal considerations and channel count. Ad­
ditionally, we propose evaluations encompassing both internal and external classification
tests, noise resilience assessments, and experiment for distinguishing synthesized sig­
nals. We evaluate our framework for rigorous testing using two public datasets: Physionet
and BCIC­IV­2a. Within the Physionet dataset, our method obtains promising outcomes,
and when compared with many state­of­the­art methodologies, it consistently outperforms
while requiring fewer channels. However, the BCIC­IV­2a dataset reveals considerable
inter­user variability. In scenarios with noise interference, our framework robustly miti­
gates disruptions, achieving optimal accuracy. Moreover, when confronted with synthetic
signal attacks, all tested models successfully discerned these fabricated signals.
Keywords: Biometric, EEG­based authentication, Motor Imagery

Introduction
EEG­based authentication is an innovative and promising biometric direction that uses
electroencephalography (EEG) for identifying and authenticating individuals. EEG refers
to the recording of electrical activity generated by the brain. This activity generates dis­
tinctive, complex, and unpredictable patterns that vary significantly between individuals,
making it an attractive solution for biometric authentication.
Biometric authentication technologies, such as fingerprint and face recognition, are widely
known and used, but they have limitations in terms of security and privacy. EEG­based
authentication, on the other hand, offers a unique alternative that utilizes the individuality
and complexity of brain signals. The premise of EEG­based authentication relies on the
understanding that brainwaves are unique to each individual, just like fingerprints or retinal
patterns. These brainwaves can be captured non­invasively using EEG, a process that
needs placing sensors on the scalp to record electrical activity.
Unlike other biometric methods, EEG­based authentication offers potential benefits, in­
cluding resistance to forgery, high levels of uniqueness, and continuous authentication.
In principle, brain signals cannot be replicated or stolen in the same way that a fingerprint
or face image can, making the system robust against conventional attack methods.
One key advantage of EEG­based systems is their dynamic nature. Instead of authenti­
cating a user once, as traditional systems do, EEG­based systems could potentially mon­
itor user authentication continuously. The user’s EEG signals could be continually ana­
lyzed, providing ongoing verification of the user’s identity and offering enhanced security.
Despite its potential, EEG­based authentication is not without challenges. Practical imple­
mentation requires dealing with issues such as signal quality, comfort and usability, and
ensuring privacy. Furthermore, the system’s performance can be affected by various fac­
tors, including user mental states and environmental conditions, which could potentially
cause variations in EEG signals. This research investigates some aspects of EEG­based
authentication, such as the factors influencing authentication accuracy and the tempo­
ral impact on performance. Additionally, it studies the methods specifically designed for
EEG­based authentication. Efforts are made to enhance the current methods to achieve
higher accuracy with minimal data.
The field of EEG­based authentication, though in its infancy, offers exciting prospects in
biometric security. It represents a shift toward harnessing our brain’s unique and intri­
cate electrical patterns to secure our digital identities. As technology continues to evolve
and improve, it’s conceivable that EEG­based authentication could become a standard
practice in the near future, offering unprecedented security levels in the digital age.
1.1
Motivation
EEG (Electroencephalogram) based authentication utilizes the distinctive nature of indi­
vidual brain waves. As each person’s brainwave pattern is unique, EEG signals offer an
exceptional biometric measure for user authentication. This method not only affords a
superior level of security but also ties authentication to the immediate presence of the
user, thereby diminishing the potential for misidentification or fraudulent activity. Various
deep learning models have been developed to this end.

Wu et al. [1] proposed an advanced EEG authentication framework on Motor Imagery,
achieving outstanding classification results on two public Motor Imagery datasets. Song
et al. [2] introduced an EEG decoding method named Spatial­Temporal Tiny Transformer
(S3T). This method employs an attention mechanism to transform data into a highly dis­
tinctive representation, utilizing both spatial and temporal information. The mechanism
adeptly perceives the global dependencies of the signal and the significance of differ­
ent feature channels for classification tasks. Applied to the classification of four different
imagined movements, S3T’s results exceeded those of most state­of­the­art models for a
majority of subject data.
Inspired by these advancements, we try to improve upon Wu et al.’s model [1], by inte­
grating attention mechanisms such as Squeeze­and­Excitation (SE) block [3], Convolu­
tional Block Attention Module (CBAM) [4] and Receptive Field Block (RFB) [5]. These
mechanisms, regarded as general modules, can be seamlessly integrated into state­of­
the­art models, either supplementing or replacing original components. Their efficacy in
improving model performance has been proved in both computer vision (CV) and natural
language processing (NLP) fields. Importantly, these enhancements do not significantly
increase the model’s complexity or computational demands. Specifically, we incorporate
the SE block subsequent to the frequency­decomposed feature maps. This adjustment al­
lows for the modeling of interdependencies between frequency bands, thus adaptively re­
calibrating the features. This recalibration emphasizes beneficial features while suppress­
ing extraneous ones. Before directing the feature maps into the standard convolutional
layer for temporal feature extraction, we integrated a CBAM, enabling the optimization of
feature maps by extracting information between frequency bands and channels. Here too,
crucial information is emphasized while unimportant details are suppressed. Additionally,
we experiment with embedding RFB between the channel compression and temporal fea­
ture extraction phases. The RFB, with its diverse receptive field sizes, extracts a range of
features and subsequently merges them, enhancing both feature discrimination and over­
all model robustness. We conducted several ablation studies to check the crucial role of
RFB in this context. Additionally, we recognize that EEG­based authentication faces chal­
lenges such as noise and synthetic signal attacks [6]. Therefore, we implemented three
distinct attack scenarios to evaluate the robustness of our system.
1.2
Contributions
The significance of this study is that it further enhances the accuracy and robustness of
EEG­based authentication systems. The key contributions of our research include:
• Channel selection: We introduce three distinct channel selection strategies to in­
vestigate their impact on authentication accuracy. A comparative analysis between
our proposed models and the original models, based on these strategies, is pre­
sented to illustrate the effectiveness of our approach.
• Deep Learning Classification Model: Our research explores the integration of
various attention mechanisms within the existing model framework, aiming to ele­
vate its performance. Through our exploration, we identified optimal positions for
the inclusion of attention blocks, enabling the enhanced model to achieve superior
accuracy even with a reduced number of channels.
• Evaluation and Comparison: Our evaluation employs two widely recognized datasets:
Physionet and BCI. We offer an extensive comparison of our approach with eight
other studies and methodologies, testing them on the Physionet dataset for both in­
sider and outsider tests as proposed by Wu et al. [1]. This comparative evaluation

2.1.1
Introduction to EEG
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non­invasive neurophysiological technique utilized to
measure the brain’s electrical activity. Multiple electrodes are placed on the scalp to detect
and record voltage fluctuations resulting from the synchronized activity of neurons in the
brain in response to both internal and external stimuli. EEG signals encompass diverse
features that can be analyzed to extract valuable information about brain function. These
signals vary due to factors like gender, age, disease, and other individual differences.
EEG signals encompass diverse features that can be analyzed to extract valuable infor­
mation about brain function. These signals vary due to factors like gender, age, disease,
and other individual differences. Each individual’s EEG signal differs significantly, even
when they perform the same task, yet it exhibits relative stability and repeatability within
the same person. Consequently, EEG signals possess distinct and consistent character­
istics. The amplitude of recorded signals exhibits rhythmic voltage fluctuations ranging
from 5 to 100μV, while their frequency ranges between 0.5 and 40 Hz. Technological
advancements over the years have significantly improved the acquisition and analysis of
EEG signals. The development of digital amplifiers, advanced electrode placement sys­
tems, and signal processing techniques have improved the quality and accuracy of EEG
measurements. Consequently, EEG signals have found applications in diverse research
domains, including the diagnosis and study of neurological conditions, person authenti­
cation, mental state recognition, and affective computing.
2.1.2
EEG Frequency
The EEG signal is commonly characterized by rhythmic activity and transients. It exhibits
varying frequencies across different neural states of brain activity. Rhythmic activity within
specific frequency ranges represents distinct brain activity states. Rhythmic activity waves
are categorized into five frequency bands [7]:
Delta (1 ­ 4 Hz) exhibits slowest waveform with the highest amplitude. It is typically
observed in babies and adults during deep sleep;
Theta (4 ­ 8 Hz) is commonly observed in young children, drowsy adults, and during
meditation. The amplitude of Theta waves is typically below 100μV;
Alpha (8 ­ 12 Hz) typically dominates and is observed bilaterally in the posterior re­
gions of the head. It appears during relaxation and with closed eyes. The amplitude
of the Alpha band decreases when eyes are opened or during mental exertion. Their
amplitude is generally below 50μV;
Beta (12 ­ 25 Hz) is typically observed bilaterally with symmetrical distribution, par­
ticularly in the frontal region. It is closely associated with motor behavior and is
generally reduced during active movements [8]. Low­amplitude beta waves are of­
ten linked to active, busy, or anxious thinking, as well as active concentration and
focused attention. The amplitude of Beta waves is typically below 30μV;
Gamma (over 25 Hz) exhibits the lowest amplitude. They are typically observed dur­
ing multiple sensory processing tasks, such as cognitive or motor functions;

2.1.4
EEG Acquisition Protocols
Neuronal activity within the brain significantly correlates with human mental states, being
sensitive to both external environmental stimuli and endogenous autonomous regulation.
Thus, it becomes essential to design a clear and well­defined collection protocol for the
purposeful gathering of EEG signals. EEG can be categorized into spontaneous (resting­
state potentials and mental tasks without external stimuli such as focusing) and evoked
EEG (stimulus­induced, with specific tasks like motor imagination and vision for imag­
ing tasks). These categories help the investigation of EEG signals exhibiting temporal
characteristics across spontaneous states, environmental perception, and complex cog­
nition. For example, resting states or mental tasks without external stimuli only require
EEG recording devices, whereas tasks provoked by external stimuli require additional
equipment to implement suitable stimulation. However, simple tasks like resting states
are easily affected by environment noise and artifacts, while mental tasks and externally
stimulated tasks generate a higher signal­to­noise ratio (SNR) [13]. Each person has their
own special brain neural pathways and thinking patterns which results in different types of
task signals. Even in the same type of task, the EEG signals responding to the different
tasks are also different. Therefore, the selection of an appropriate acquisition protocol
significantly impacts the recognition results and should be customized to specific tasks.
The following subsection describes the protocols mainly used in EEG authentication.
Resting states
Resting states are the most commonly employed protocol for EEG signal acquisition [14],
[15], [16], [17]. Many studies have proved that the EEG signals generated by different in­
dividuals in a completely resting state contain unique characteristics that can distinguish
one subject from another [18], [19], [20]. This protocol requires the subject to be com­
pletely relaxed without having to perform any specific tasks or receive any external stimuli.
Typically, the subject sits in a quiet environment, and the EEG signal is then recorded.
Two types of this protocol include closed eyes (”REC”) and open eyes (”REO”), there
are differences in the dominant frequency band and the more efficient EEG channels
[13]. With REO, the best results are derived from the central region, while REC provides
superior results from the parietal region. Furthermore, during REC, the alpha (α) band
tends to be the dominant frequency band [21], [22].

The popularity of this protocol lies in its simplicity and the lack of need for any special
requirements or instructions. However, it is essential to ensure a quiet environment and
that the individual remains free of mental activity or preoccupation to avoid skewed results.
Despite this, the resting state can be a baseline EEG activity for other tasks during the
preprocessing stage.
Mental imagination
Employing a protocol that requires subjects to imagine specific physical movements or
other tasks is a common approach for collecting EEG signals. For instance, studies by
[23], [24], [25], and [26] ask individuals to move hand or foot imaginarily or do both imag­
inary and physical body movement [27], [28]. The motor imagery (MI) induction task in­
duces corresponding EEG signals when a subject imagines the movement of a particular
limb or tongue. Contemporary neuro electrophysiological research indicates that the EEG
signals generated by limb movement or motor imagination demonstrate significant indi­
vidual differences [29]. Prior studies have indicated that the resting potential exhibits less
noise than that generated by the motor imagination task, and the EEG signals induced by
the motor imagination task are less noisy than those induced by actual movement tasks
[30]. Due to the individual variability inherent in the EEG signals of motor imagination
tasks, these signals have been increasingly utilized for authentication in recent years.
This method has shown strong applicability across a wide range of patients, including
those with physical disabilities and visual impairments.
However, in some instances, motor imagination tasks may fail to induce distinct signals,
leading to task blindness [31]. With the advancement of technology, recent years have
seen the appearance of imagination tasks similar to the motor imagination protocol for
authentication purposes. [25] and [32] incorporated protocols that required individuals to
silently sing a favorite song, mentally count numbers, or focus on a preferred thought,
termed a ”pass­thought”. Other mental tasks considered by [33] include mental multi­
plication, visualizing the rotation of a given geometric figure around an axis, mentally
counting numbers, and composing a letter to a friend. Tasks involving imagining a per­
son, a gesture, or the taste of food [34], as well as generating words beginning with ran­
domly selected letters [23] have also been used. While mental imagination tasks do not
need specialized devices for stimulation, similar to visual stimuli, they require rudimentary
equipment to provide cues that help individuals initiate the task.
Event­Related tasks
The Event­Related Potentials (ERPs) task represents a distinctive type of evoked task.
Event­related potentials (ERPs) are very small voltages generated in the brain structures
in response to specific events or stimuli [35]. They can be recorded by the electrodes
placed on the scalp. In addition, they have high time resolution, so the brain signals can
be quickly received by the recording equipment. ERPs waveforms consist of a series
of positive and negative voltage deflections. Common ERPs components are generally
categorized into a sequential order: P100, N100, N200, N250, P300 [33], N400 [36], and
others.
The Visually Evoked Potentials (VEPs) are the most common form of ERPs, illustrating
specific neural activities triggered by the nervous system in response to external visual
stimuli, such as graphical or flashing inputs [37]. To generate robust and correlated sig­
nals, exogenous evoked events often use periodic sensory stimuli, such as Steady­State
Visually Evoked Potentials (SSVEPs). The stimuli with a frequency range of 4 to 60 Hz can
induce enhanced stationary periodic oscillations with the same frequency and its higher
harmonics in the brain waves recorded over visual areas [38], [39]. Such stimuli may
originate from a panel of flickering LEDs. The rate of increase in the power spectrum of

2.1.5
Multitask and Task­Independent
Researchers think that EEG signals induced by multiple tasks contain more individualized
feature components, thereby resulting in higher recognition accuracy than those induced
by a single task. Consequently, several protocols have been designed to stimulate multi­
task performance [41]. For instance, [42] developed a protocol merging visual and audio
tasks, while [25] incorporated imaginary text, mathematical operations and visual task.
However, EEG signals evoked by multi­tasking are inherently more complex than those
induced by a single task, which not only increases the decoding difficulty but also causes
mental fatigue and resistance among the subjects. Despite its capacity to enhance recog­
nition accuracy, multi­tasking also involves varied methods that different researchers use
for different tasks, which reduces the method’s transferability across tasks. Thus, some
researchers strive for a task­independent approach that can achieve robust authentication
results irrespective of the tasks performed by the subjects [43].
2.1.6
Preprocessing
Time­Domain processing
Traditional signal analysis is waveform analysis. Waveforms represent the signal as a
time­dependent function and contain comprehensive information about the signal. A sig­
nal’s properties are initially expressed in its temporal characteristics, such as the time
correlating with the signal amplitude, the length of a repeating cycle with an identical
waveform, and the signal waveform’s rate of change (for instance, the pulse duration of
a pulsed signal or the steepness of the pulse’s rising and falling edges). Detailed study
of these time­domain data enables a profound understanding of these waveform char­
acteristics. These features vary over time, exhibiting the signal’s non­stationarity. Tech­
niques such as statistical methods and signal smoothing — incorporating mean, median,
variance, and normalization — are frequently used to derive crucial information from the
signal, with the ultimate aim of enhancing the signal­to­noise ratio.
Frequency­Domain procesiing
Time­domain and frequency­domain analyses offer two perspectives on the observation
of analog signals. Time­domain analysis investigates the dynamic relationship between
the signal and time, while frequency­domain analysis shifts this signal relationship to the
frequency axis. In general, time­domain representations offer more visual and intuitive de­
pictions, whereas frequency­domain analyses provide a more concise view, often leading
to deeper, more convenient problem exploration. Frequency­domain analysis employs
the Fourier transform. According to Fourier’s theorem, any continuously measured time

==PAGE:17==
series or signal can be decomposed into an infinite superposition of sinusoidal signals of
varying frequencies. Thus, an EEG signal can be viewed as a compound signal com­
posed of various superimposed sinusoidal signals. Utilizing the Fourier transform, this
compound signal can be decomposed back into individual sinusoids of different frequen­
cies, thereby obtaining frequency­domain information. Frequency­domain analysis is ap­
plicable not only to task state data but also commonly used for analyzing resting state
data. Power spectrograms in the frequency domain represent the EEG signal distribution
across different frequency bands. This is achieved mainly through methods such as filters
and power spectrum estimation. Power spectrum estimation is a technique that describes
the random features of the EEG signal within the frequency domain and further support
other frequency­domain analysis methods such as the Chebyshev filter, Butterworth fil­
ter, and AR parameter model estimation. The Chebyshev filter, when applied for low­pass
filtering of EEG signals, extracts EEG signals within specific frequency bands.
Time­Frequency Domain Analysis
Despite its utility, the Fourier transform has a limitation: it is applicable only to steady­state
data, while EEG data is inherently non­stationary. Frequency domain analysis alone fails
to reflect frequency changes over time. Consequently, a single dimension of time do­
main or frequency domain analysis cannot fully capture signal characteristics, and then
time­frequency analysis is needed. When applied to each subject, channel, and seg­
ment of the EEG signals, the Fourier transform provides energy levels at each frequency
point, paving the way for statistical analysis. Time­frequency analysis combines both
time­domain and frequency­domain information. This is achieved by applying a window
to the EEG data, assuming the data within this window to be in a steady state, and then
conducting a Fourier transform to extract frequency­domain information within that time
window. By sliding the window forward along the time axis and repeating the same pro­
cess for the data within each time window, frequency information over time is derived,
obtaining a time­frequency plot. This plot’s horizontal axis represents time, its vertical
axis indicates frequency, and the power value corresponds to each time­frequency point.
Window size impacts time and frequency accuracy: larger windows offer lower time ac­
curacy and higher frequency accuracy, suitable for analyzing low­frequency slow waves.
Conversely, smaller windows ensure higher time accuracy and lower frequency accuracy,
ideal for analyzing high­frequency fast waves. Depending on the research requirements,
the window size can be either fixed or adaptive. For instance, while the Short­Time Fourier
Transform uses a fixed time window, the Wavelet Transform employs a time window that
scales with frequency change, offering more flexible usage.
Spatial­Domain Analysis
When collecting EEG signals, multiple electrodes are placed to gather more comprehen­
sive information. The associated information among the corresponding electrodes is spa­
tial information and the analysis of it can reduce noise, detect and remove artifacts, and
analyze the signal components with important characteristics. Commonly utilized spatial­
domain analysis techniques include Common Average Reference (CAR), Principal Com­
ponent Analysis (PCA), Independent Component Analysis (ICA), and the Laplacian Spa­
tial Filter. CAR computes the average value across all electrodes, enabling noise reduc­
tion by subtracting this average value from all electrode measurements. Consequently, it
extracts the most important information within the EEG signal and eliminates artifacts.
2.1.7
Feature Extraction
Time­domain feature extraction
Time domain feature extraction is a more primitive technique that analyzes signals/data
based on time. This allows us to quantify how the signal changes over time. This is espe­
cially important in the case of EEG signals, as they are often recorded over multiple hourly
A Novel Process for EEG­based Authentication with Motor Imagery Brain­Computer Interface
9

time frames. Typically, windowing and segmentation of the signal is required for time do­
main feature extraction. In this way, a localized feature will be extracted for each window,
and the researcher will be able to view how these features change over each window.
Windowing and segmentation are especially important for processing physiological sig­
nals because they are inherently nonlinear and nonsmooth. Common EEG time­domain
techniques include autoregressive modeling, fractal dimension based on Higuchi’s algo­
rithm, statistical characterization, and detrended fluctuation analysis.
Frequency­domain feature extraction
Frequency domain analysis techniques focus on extracting features from the sine wave
signals that make up the data. This is usually done by first converting from the time domain
to the frequency domain and then doing further analysis. Common EEG frequency domain
techniques include Fourier transform (FT), power spectral density (PSD), band power
(BP), and Hilbert­Huang transform (HHT).
Decomposition domain feature extraction
Decomposition feature extraction is useful because it allows simultaneous filtering of the
signal. The basic premise is that (1) the signal is decomposed using a selection method,
(2) the desired components are selected, and (3) the unwanted components are elimi­
nated. This also leads to data compression, making it ideal for more modern applications.
Common EEG decomposition domain techniques include adaptive Hermite decomposi­
tion, localized eigen­scale decomposition, wavelet transform (which also includes con­
tinuous wavelet transform (CWT) and discrete wavelet transform (DWT)), and empirical
modal decomposition (EMD).
Time­frequency domain feature extraction
Feature extraction using spectral and temporal features of a signal alone is usually inef­
fective because their signals do not correspond. To overcome the limitations of a single
domain, time­frequency analysis techniques do a good job of combining the two. Common
EEG time­frequency feature extraction techniques include short­time Fourier transform,
S­transform (an extension of the morlet wavelet transform), and matched tracking.
Spatial domain feature extraction
Spatial domain feature extraction, also known as spatial filtering, is one of the most com­
monly used techniques for EEG signal classification. Specifically, the technique uses a
common spatial pattern (CSP), a supervised spatial filter. The spatial filtering method
transforms the EEG waves into a unique space. In this unique space, the variance of one
group is amplified while the variance of the other group is smaller. However, the pure CSP
technique has limitations; the desired performance cannot be achieved because it is lim­
ited by the optimal frequency band for a particular subject. Therefore, researchers have
been developing variants of CSP to overcome these limitations. Some of the methods
that have been improved based on CSP are Common Spatial Spectral Pattern (CSSP),
Common Sparse Spatial Spectral Pattern (CSSSP), Subband Common Spatial Pattern
(SBCSP), and Regularized Common Spatial Pattern (RCSP).

2.2.1
Introduction to Authentication
Computer authentication is an important aspect of computer security, encompassing the
verification of the identity of a user, process or device, and is often a prerequisite for
granting access to information system resources. In a world where digital data and its
manipulation and access are becoming critical, the need to ensure the confidentiality,
integrity and availability of information makes this concept significant.
The authentication system needs the use of user­provided credentials to verify the identity
of a prospective user. These supplied credentials are cross­checked against a repository

==PAGE:19==
of stored credentials within the computer system’s database, establishing user identity.
Upon confirming a match between the prospective user and the identified account, ac­
cess to the system is granted. However, should there be a mismatch, the prospective
user’s access request is consequently denied. Robust authentication is of quite an im­
portance in preventing potential attacks on the computer system. In the contemporary
era, individuals utilize machines on a daily basis to manage work assignments, store per­
sonal data, and safeguard intellectual property. Given that a significant number of these
machines are interconnected through computer networks, they are potentially accessi­
ble from external computers. Without stringent authentication protocols, an illicit entity
could exploit another individual’s access and extract valuable information. This principle
extends beyond network communications and is equally applicable to physical device se­
curity. Unrestricted access to a device risks information compromise in the event of the
device being misplaced or stolen.
The origins of computer authentication can be traced back to the advent of shared main­
frame computing systems in the 1960s, when the need to distinguish between users and
their respective privileges arose. The earliest and most basic form of computer authenti­
cation was username­password authentication. Despite its simplicity, this form of authen­
tication has been important because it is cost­effective, easy to implement, and straight­
forward for users. However, because username­password authentication is susceptible
to brute­force breaking attacks, dictionary attacks, and user errors such as the use of
predictable or weak passphrases and mishandling of passphrases, more sophisticated
authentication methods have emerged.
In the late 1980s, the concept of two­factor authentication (2FA) surfaced as a complement
to the traditional username­password paradigm, integrating something the user knows
(e.g., a password), something the user possesses (e.g., a smartcard or token), or some­
thing the user himself or herself has (biometrics). Such a system greatly reduces the risk
of unauthorized access because it requires intruders to bypass multiple layers of defense,
each based on a different principle. For instance, using an ATM card to withdraw money
from an ATM machine requires you to have your card and know a secret PIN to access
your account.
Biometric authentication uses an individual’s unique physiological or behavioral attributes,
such as fingerprints, iris patterns, or voice characteristics, and became widely used in the
late 20th century. With the proliferation of personal devices equipped with appropriate
sensors, biometrics offer a high degree of security and user convenience. However, con­
cerns around privacy, the possibility of false positives or false negatives, and the risk of
permanent disclosure (since biometric traits cannot be reset like passwords) are ongoing
challenges to their widespread acceptance.
Smart card­based authentication, using a physical card embedded with a chip carrying
user credentials, is another noteworthy development. It has seen widespread application
in corporate environments and banking systems due to its robustness against common
attacks. Yet, it still bears the risk of physical theft or loss, besides the cost and complexity
involved in its deployment.
Digital certificates, employing Public Key Infrastructure (PKI), provide another means for
authentication. PKI involves the use of a pair of keys ­ a public key and a private key. The
private key is kept secret by the user, and the public key is published in a certificate that
is signed by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA). This technology is especially beneficial
for ensuring both identity authentication and data integrity in digital communications.

Multi­factor authentication (MFA) is a more recent development that mandates the use
of multiple evidence types from different categories for successful authentication. This
method offers a superior level of security as it combines several independent credentials:
what the user knows (password), what the user has (security token), and what the user is
(biometric verification). The emergence of MFA is a response to the heightened security
requirements of our interconnected era.
Moreover, decentralized authentication methods like blockchain­based authentication sys­

important as our digital lives expand further.
2.2.2
Biometric Authentication
Driven by technological advances and increased demand for secure and user­friendly au­
thentication systems, biometric authentication has become an important method of estab­
lishing identity in a range of applications. Unlike traditional methods, such as password­ or
token­based systems, biometric authentication, which is based on an individual’s unique
physical or behavioral attributes, is an attractive alternative to overcome some of the lim­
itations inherent in traditional systems. Biometric authentication stems from the need for
robust, reliable and efficient systems for personal identification. Traditional systems based
on what an individual knows (passwords, PINs) or has (ID cards, keys) have significant
limitations. Passwords can be forgotten, stolen or easily guessed. Physical tokens can be
lost or copied. In addition, these systems cannot be non­repudiation, i.e., they guarantee
that a person involved in a transaction cannot later deny his or her involvement.
In authentication, biometrics refers to the automatic recognition of an individual based on
his or her unique physical or behavioral characteristics. The basic principle of biometric
authentication is that every individual can be accurately identified by his or her inherent
physical or behavioral characteristics. Physical biometrics include fingerprints, facial pat­
terns, iris patterns, and hand geometry. Behavioral biometrics include voiceprints, gait
and signature dynamics. Recently, more advances have been made in biometrics such
as heart patterns, brain waves (EEG), and even DNA sequences, which are more private
and difficult to spoof. Biometric features used for biometric authentication should fulfill a
number of requirements, including 1) universality, 2) permanency, 3) uniqueness, and 4)
collectability [44]. Universality means that each biometric feature that is considered as
an authentication factor is possessed by every individual. Permanence indicates that the
feature does not change over time. Uniqueness requires that the feature is distinguish­
able for each different user. Finally, collectability means that it should be able to be easily
collected from the user.

Biometric systems have two modes of operation: identification and authentication. Identi­
fication answers the question ”Who am I?” by comparing the captured biometric trait with
all the biometric traits in the database. Authentication answers the question ”Am I who
I claim to be?” by comparing the captured biometric with a specific biometric previously
stored in the system. The complexity and computational requirements of each model vary
greatly.
A typical biometric system consists of four main components: a sensor that collects bio­
metric features, a preprocessing module that enhances the collected data, a feature ex­
tractor that recognizes the identifying features, and a matcher that compares the extracted
features with a stored template.
A biometric system’s performance is affected by several factors, such as its ability to re­
sist circumvention, the stability of the biometrics over time, the accuracy of the system
under various operating conditions, and the time required for identification or authenti­
cation. User acceptability and societal implications, such as privacy concerns, also play
an important role in the practical implementation and widespread adoption of biometric
systems.
2.2.3
EEG­based Authentication
EEG signals, the bioelectrical activities produced by the human brain, contain unique
characteristics that make them private, difficult to imitate, and impossible to steal. These
attributes, combined with the necessity for a live individual to record the data, set EEG
signals apart from more conventional biometric identifiers such as face, iris, voice, and fin­
gerprints. As a result, the scientific community has prioritized EEG signals as an emerging
category of biometric identifiers, primarily employed for person identification tasks.
Furthermore, EEG signals encapsulate an individual’s biometric information, correspond­
ing to specific mental tasks, similar to passwords. Hence, the utilization of EEG signals
for personal authentication integrates the benefits of both password­based and biometric­
based person authentication.
Implementing an EEG­based personal authentication system involves three primary steps:
preprocessing, feature extraction, and classificaion, which are utilized in both the registra­
tion and authentication phases. During the registration phase, EEG signals are recorded
as the user performs specific tasks such as imagining the movements of hands, feet, fin­
gers, or tongue. This EEG data is then preprocessed to reduce noise, followed by the
extraction and selection of salient features via a feature selection algorithm. These se­
lected features are then used to train a classifier, constructing a unique model for the
individual. In the authentication phase, the user intending to access the system must
provide EEG signals by repeating the tasks performed during the registration phase. The
provided EEG data undergoes the same preprocessing and feature extraction procedures
as during registration. The resultant feature vector is input into the classifier to match the
model corresponding to the claimed identity. Based on the matching scores and pre­
determined thresholds, the system determines whether to accept or reject the asserted
identity.

3.1
Shallow Classification
Shallow methodologies can generally be conceptualized as including two stages: first,
the extraction of distinctive features, and second, the implementation of a classification
technique to the procured features. Notably, given the nature of the signal, the features
and classifiers may significantly vary across different applications. In the context of EEG
biometrics, the input signal represents a time series of potential fluctuations recorded
from various brain locations. Consequently, features that can be extracted from the time,
frequency, or spatial domains are useful. Examples include autoregressive model co­
efficients in the time domain, power spectral density in the frequency domain, wavelet
coefficients in the time­frequency domain, or common spatial coherence in the spatial fre­
quency domain. Importantly, the applied method should be able to classify these feature
types. A review of the most prevalent techniques for feature extraction and classification
in EEG authentication is presented below. It’s crucial to realize that these methods, at any
level, can be utilized individually or fused together to improve results. At the feature level,
features extracted using diverse methods can be directly fused or through specific fea­
ture reduction tools such as canonical correlation analysis. At the decision level, matching
scores from various classification techniques are combined to augment confidence.

Other methods
In EEG signal analysis, the non­stationary nature of these signals has rendered the ”wavelet
transform” (WT) a prevalent tool for feature extraction within the time­frequency domain.
The Wavelet Transform dissects a signal into a weighted amalgamation of functions,
termed wavelets. Each of these wavelets is essentially a shifted and scaled version of
the original function, known as the parent wavelet. These wavelets provide approxima­
tions of the signal at progressively reduced resolutions. Through Wavelet decomposition,
a feature vector is defined by the coefficients of wavelets per channel. The study in [56]
introduces a method for extracting salient features from noisy signals, harnessing the
Multi­Objective Flower Pollination Algorithm in conjunction with the Wavelet Transform
(MOFPA­WT). Additionally, statistical properties of wavelet coefficients, such as mean,
standard deviation, RMS, and entropy, are tapped as features, as underscored by [57].
The work of [38] dives into the extraction of EEG features of steady­state visual evoked po­
tentials (SSVEP) within the time­spatial domain, employing the Short Time Fourier Trans­
form (STFT). The authentication process leveraged the k­nearest neighbors (KNN) algo­
rithm, yielding an acceptance rate ranging from 60% to 100%.
Many investigations adopt a hybrid approach, drawing from multiple methods to distill
features from diverse domains. For instance, [58] rolled out an economical, consumer­
grade EEG­based personal identity authentication system. This system wields a linear
support vector machine (SVM) to segregate three distinct sets of features culled from each
electrode: the sixth­order autoregressive (AR) coefficient, the power spectral density, and
the cumulative power across five frequency bands. The empirical evidence pointed to a
100% classification accuracy for each subject across all tasks. Further, [57] channeled
statistical features such as mean and standard deviation, utilizing both HMM and SVM for
identity authentication. When put to the test across 30 subjects, the framework exhibited
a Half Total Error Rate (HTER) of a mere 2%.

3.1.2
Classification methods
similarity­based methods
The similarity­based approach represents one of the simplest and least complex classi­
fication methods. This approach involves the comparison of two vectors; if their similar­
ity score exceeds a predefined threshold, they are classified as belonging to the same
class. Essentially, this involves comparing feature vectors generated during the registra­
tion phase with those created during the enrollment phase. This technique is primarily
utilized for authentication rather than identification. For instance, Fraschini et al. [59]
used Euclidean distance to compute eigenvector centrality within a resting brain network.
They subsequently employed this eigenvector centrality as a unique ”signature” for each
individual, enabling person identification. Similarly, Das et al. [60] computed the co­
sine and Euclidean distance between different subjects’ visually evoked potentials (VEP)
waveforms to distinguish users. Their approach, utilizing 17 channels, yielded the low­
est Equal Error Rate (EER) of 13.55%. Furthermore, Maiorana et al. [51] employed the
Manhattan distance as one of the features in their study aimed at evaluating the stability
of EEG features.
Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)
Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is a well­established linear learning method that iden­
tifies a linear combination of features to distinguish between various categories. In the
LDA approach, samples are projected onto a straight line in such a way that samples
from the same category are as close together as possible, while those from different cat­
egories are as distant as possible. This method facilitates efficient sample classification.
LDA is a prevalent shallow classifier in EEG­based authentication research. For instance,
Kong et al. [61] calculated the phase lock value (PLV) to measure phase synchronization,
thereby constructing a coherence matrix to create a weighted undirected network. They
utilized the node degree of the brain network to generate feature vectors, achieving an
impressive 95% accuracy rate in LDA classification. Narayan et al. [62] extracted six
types of features and employed LDA for their classification, with their best model attain­
ing an accuracy of 91.6%. To address the issue of diminished classification accuracy due
to the temporal variance of EEG motor imagery signals from the same subject, Yang et
al. [63] proposed an adaptive approach, Decision Surface Adaptive LDA (DSALDA). This
method, based on the common spatial pattern (CSP) and decision surface, employs the
updating of the LDA classifier’s decision surface threshold by retaining the CSP spatial
features from different days’ test data. After conducting experiments with EEG data from
24 subjects over different days, their results showed a substantial 35% improvement over
the conventional CSP­LDA method. In their study, Jayarathne et al. [64] utilized the Com­
mon Spatial Pattern (CSP) technique to extract spatial features from EEG signals within
the 8­30 Hz frequency band. These extracted features were then classified using Linear
Discriminant Analysis (LDA) for the purpose of authentication. Experimental trials were
carried out on 12 subjects, achieving the highest accuracy rate of 96.97%.

spectral information, coherence, the number of interrelationships, and mutual informa­

3.2
Deep classification
To ensure good classification outcomes in shallow classification, it is important that the
feature set used for identification be highly discriminative. Although it is feasible to de­
sign feature extraction and classification methods for task­specific EEG signals, general­
izing these methods to EEG signals for other tasks to achieve good performance remains
a challenge. Traditional machine learning processes are easy to information loss, and
specific feature extraction methods often require the domain expertise of professionals,
producing single and insufficient features. Furthermore, the majority of existing machine
learning methods mainly focus on static data and try to accurately classify time­varying
EEG signals. Hence, many deep learning models are proposed to decode EEG signals.
These models can efficiently capture high­dimensional feature representations of signals
and the potential relationships of internal features through a nonlinear deep structure. For
EEG signals characterized by complex information content, high temporal variability, and
insignificant features, deep learning can extract deep dimensional and significant feature
representations. These models can be applied directly to raw data, bypassing complex
preprocessing and feature extraction processes. Depending on the requirement, they can
serve as feature extractors, classifiers, or a combination of both.
3.2.1
Classification methods
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
Deep learning network models typically consist of numerous layers, enabling the extrac­
tion of deeper internal features through more complex structures. As a consequence,
these deep learning networks exhibit increased complexity and a greater number of pa­
rameters. If the data required for deep learning is sizable, the learning process can be­
come protracted and complex. The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a type of deep
neural network characterized by its convolutional kernel. The presence of this convolu­
tional kernel means that the convolutional layer is partially connected to the preceding
layer and shares parameters, thus considerably reducing the total number of network pa­
rameters. Owing to its unique attributes, CNNs have been successfully used in a range
of applications, including image recognition. This broad application stems from their abil­
ity to extract significant features possessing specific and potentially unidentified internal
structures.

4.1
Proposed Framework
In this section, we elaborate on the comprehensive framework developed for the authen­
tication tasks. We present the methodological approaches employed and describe the
details of each constituent part.
4.1.1
Preprocessing
The EEG signals are recorded using multiple electrodes placed on the scalp, resulting in
data from multiple channels arranged in a specific order predetermined by the collector.
Furthermore, the EEG data is labeled at each moment to indicate the subjects’ state, in­
cluding the rest state, cue state, and imaging state. During each trial, the subject engages
in a motor imagery task, which involves imagining a specific limb movement, with rest pe­
riods both before and after the imagination period. Consequently, we filter the data based
on the labels and retain only the segments relevant to motor imagery, forming our original
EEG data. For instance, when the imagination period lasts approximately 4 seconds and
the sampling frequency is 160 Hz, each trial corresponds to approximately 640 samples.
4.1.2
Channel Selection
Channel selection is one of the influencing factors in the system’s recognition capabil­
ity. Consequently, we designed diferent channel selection strategies to evaluate whether
channel selection would have a significant impact on recognition accuracy.
Physionet EEG Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset
For the channel selection strategy, we choose three pairs of channels based on physiolog­
ical evidence to gather input data. The first group consists of the C3, Cz, and C4 channels
due to the presence of MI RH activity predominantly above the left motor cortex (C3) and
MI LH activity across the C4 channels. Clear brain activity related to MI foot movement
can be observed through the Cz channel. Additionally, based on prior research [81], our
second group comprises 12 channels: FC1, FC2, FC3, FC4, C3, C4, C1, C2, CP1, CP2,
CP3, and CP4. The third pair comprises 18 channels situated around the motor cortex
region, responsible for simulating motor movements [82]. These channels are FC3, FC4,
C5, C3, C1, Cz, C2, C4, C6, CP3, CP4, Fz, T7, T8, P7, P3, P4, and P8, following the
order in the Physionet EEG Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset.
BCI competition IV­dataset 2a
As we have already utilized the Physionet dataset to assess the impact of channels, we
have chosen not to repeat this experiment with the BCI dataset. Instead, we employ all
the channels provided by the BCI dataset to evaluate the system’s accuracy.
4.1.3
Deep Learning Classification
Our deep learning classifier framework is an extension and enhancement of the EEG­
based authentication model Mixed_FBCNet [1]. This model is an application and im­
provement of FBCNet [74] on authentication. It refines FBCNet by incorporating a hybrid
temporal feature extraction structure. In light of these advancements, we integrate some
attention modules into Mixed_FBCNet to improve its performance further. This framework
can be segmented into the subsequent six stages:
1) Frequency bands decomposition: Initially, the framework decomposes EEG data
into several frequency bands utilizing multiple narrow­band filters.

The input EEG data is denoted as E = {(Xi, yi)|i = 1, 2, ..., n}, where Xi ∈RC×T signifies
the EEG data of the i­th trial, and yi ∈{0, 1} corresponds to the associated label. Here,

the number of channels, while T denotes the time points within a single trial.
The filter frequency bands adopted in this study are derived from Mixed_FBCnet, which
include δ(1 −3Hz), θ(4 −7Hz), α(8 −13Hz), β(14 −30Hz), etc. The frequencies below
the power line noise ­ 60Hz are segmented into 9 non­overlapping intervals ranging from
4Hz to 40Hz, each having a bandwidth of 4Hz [74]. Each frequency band is obtained
by a narrow­band filter. Consequently, the raw EEG signal of the i­th trial can be decon­
structed into nine distinct frequency band signals, represented as XFB ∈RC×T×B. Here,
B = 9 denotes the number of narrow­band filters and frequency bands. The subsequent
depthwise separable convolution operation is executed on these signals to extract deeper
features associated with different spectral intervals.

Receptive Field Block
Receptive Field Blocks (RFBs) are introduced by [5] to enhance the deep features de­

to rapid and accurate detectors. It is inspired by the structure of Receptive Fields (RFs)

RFB Net model exhibits substantial performance enhancements while maintaining com­

restrictions on the network architecture. Due to the RFB module’s exemplary performance

4.2
Attacks
User authentication systems require substantial security and robustness to function ef­
fectively in real­world scenarios. For large­scale, remote user authentication services,
it is vital to address both verification accuracy and security/privacy concerns. However,
certain vulnerabilities can compromise the integrity of online user authentication systems.
These systems are particularly susceptible to attacks due to the use of unsecured trans­
mission channels. For instance, an attacker could intercept biometric signals of an autho­
rized user during transmission over a communication channel, then reuse these biometric
data to trick biometric recognition systems. Furthermore, previous research has explored
various attacks on biometric­based security systems. For instance, Chuang et al. [25]
propose impersonation attacks—based on knowledge of cognitive states or secret pass­
thoughts—as an open problem. Subsequently, [32] presents an analysis of their system’s
susceptibility to such impersonation attacks. Further, [92] investigates the vulnerability of
EEG­based biometric systems to hill­climbing attacks, suggesting potential countermea­
sures.
Spoofing attacks on EEG­based authentication systems generally fall into four categories:
1) Impersonation, which involves mimicking a registered subject’s signal by performing the
same cognitive task; 2) Replay attacks, which involve reusing a stolen signal from a regis­
tered subject; 3) Signal conversion, which involves modifying an available signal to mimic
a genuine one (e.g., through hill­climbing); and 4) Signal synthesis, which involves gen­
erating an artificial signal based on available samples [93]. While effective countermea­
sures exist for the first three categories [94], [95], synthesized signals pose a challenge
for detection. [6] proposes an attack method based on signal synthesis using Adaptive
Neuro­Fuzzy Inference System (ANFIS) as a generative model. Here, an adversary with
access to a subject’s EEG data sample can derive data features, construct a generative
model of the relatively low entropy features, and use these to generate time­domain data
by inverting the feature extraction process. Consequently, while the resulting signal dif­
fers from the original, it retains intrinsic features recognizable as a registered entry by
the classifier in spoofing attacks. This model assumes that an adversary has access to a
registered subject’s EEG sample.
Inspired by [6], we propose three kinds of attacks to assess the susceptibility of our au­
thentication system. They are classified as follows:
1) Temporal Noise Attack (TNA): n this attack, Gaussian white noise is added to the
snooped time domain signal.
2) Spectral Noise Attack (SNA): This attack involves adding Gaussian white noise to
derived spectral feature vectors from the snooped time­domain signal sample, and
then transforming it back into the time­domain.
3) Spoofing with Temporal Synthesis (STS): This attack trains Machine Learning (ML)
generative models using the snooped time­domain data and generates a time­series
that could potentially resemble the original signals.
TNA and SNA are used to assess the influence of noise on our system. These attacks
operate under the assumption that attackers can access the transmission channel be­
tween the user and the authentication system. The final attack, STS, is a type of spoofing
attack. This attack presupposes that the adversaries can access the database housing
the registered user data.

4.2.1
EEG signals Generative Model
Numerous models have achieved impressive accuracy in the application of EEG for motor
imagery classification, user identification, and authentication. Despite this, they encounter
inherent limitations such as poor quality and accessibility of EEG data, challenges in col­
lecting prolonged recordings, low spatial resolution, and the necessity for large volumes
of data to train precise models. Giulio’s [96] method for synthesizing EEG data via De­
noising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) has demonstrated superior performance
to Generative Adversarial Networks [97] in image generation. The models are trained
on extensive emotionally­labeled EEG datasets featuring Electrode­Frequency Distribu­
tion Maps, with the training data further enriched by data from the generated datasets.
The data is then classified using a specific classifier, trained exclusively on real data, that
achieved an average accuracy exceeding 90% when applied to the generated samples.
The authors also compared the performance of classifiers trained on a mix of synthetic
and real data with the performance of models trained using only real data. The hybrid
models consistently surpassed the performance of the original classifiers, suggesting the
synthetic data encapsulates information not present in the real data.
Inspired by these findings, we aimed to emulate spoofing attacks utilizing DDPM­generated
signals. The training data for DDPM is real data of particular subjects. The generated data
serves as an attack.
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) are a subclass of generative models
employing a parameterized Markov chain and variational inference to yield synthetic sam­

a process of diffusion, which entails a gradual infusion of Gaussian noise into the data.
This diffusion process is articulated as a latent variable model utilizing a fixed Markov
chain to map onto the latent space.
The primary goal of training a diffusion model is mastering the inverse process, thereby
facilitating synthetic data generation. Over time, the chain methodically infuses noise into
the data, eventually obscuring the original signal and leaving only noise. Figure 4.7 shows

motor imagery tasks. The term ’trial’ refers to the recorded sample of a subject consis­

Experimental data is derived from various body part movement imaginings. As verified in
[1], all classes of motor imagery contain unique characteristics that can distinguish sub­
jects. We train the authentication system of a subject using the designated subject’s data
as positive samples and other subjects’ data as negative samples. To verify the system’s
classification capability, we utilize the model trained specifically for the designated subject
to classify the data from him(her) and other individuals. As a result, each subject has a
separately trained model for authentication. To ensure verification fairness when assess­
ing the performance of models corresponding to different subjects, we use data from the
same subjects.
To evaluate the performance of each authentication system, we employ the Equal Error
Rate (EER). This metric, vital in biometric security systems, measures the system’s effi­
cacy in correctly identifying individuals. It represents the point at which the False Accep­
tance Rate (FAR) and False Rejection Rate (FRR) intersect, implying an equal probability
of incorrect acceptance of a non­matching individual and incorrect rejection of a match­
ing one. EER is an important measure, providing a balance between security and user
convenience. Generally, a lower EER value corresponds to high accuracy in a biometric
system. In order to avoid accidental best results, we record the best 3 results and then
average them for the final result.
5.1.3
Configuration
Our training and testing datasets comprise multiple trials, with each trial being assigned a
label of either 1 or 0. Each trial includes multiple channels, the indices of which correspond
to their respective positions within the dataset.
For the Physionet dataset, signals are sampled at a rate of 160Hz, resulting in a 4­second
trial signal that encompasses 640 time points. We employ a window size, w, of 128 for
temporal extraction. This leads to a filter length of w/p = 32 in the standard convolution
layer and a max pooling layer kernel size of (1, 4). Consequently, the number of trial data
time points is reduced to T/w = 5. Variance is then calculated within a window of w = 128
by the variance layer.
The BCI dataset differs in that a single trial is comprised of 1125 time points (spanning
4.5 seconds), and the window size is set at 225. This results in a temporal convolution
filter length of w/p = 45 and a max pooling window of p = 5, thereby also reducing the
trial data time points to T/w = 5. In this instance, the variance layer calculates variance
within a window of w = 225.
The classification model is implemented using PyTorch. Batch normalization and swish
nonlinearity are applied following each convolution layer, with the exception of the SE
block, CBMA, and RFB. The reduction ratios are 4 in both SE block and CBMA. To pre­
vent overfitting, we incorporated a dropout function with a 0.5 probability during temporal
convolution. The training process spans 200 epochs, with the optimal model for each sub­
ject, as determined by the best 3 accuracy, selected from the last 100 epochs. Learning
rates were set at 0.001 for the initial 100 epochs, decreasing to 0.0002 for the remaining
epochs. The weight decay is 0.01. The batch size for both the training and verification
phases was 4.

5.2.3
Performance Comparison
In this section, We compare the performance of several representative and state­of­the­
art methods developed for EEG authentication and Motor Imagery classification. The
results presented here were obtained by replicating the experiments on 1­10 subjects
under identical environmental conditions. Both the data processing techniques and the
number of utilized channels were according to as designed in the original papers.
Kumar et al. [57] utilized a classifier implemented by Support Vector Machine (SVM)
and Hidden Markov Models (HMM) for the purpose of identity authentication. Their best
experimental result showed a half total error rate (HTER) of 2%. In line with their methods,
we filter signals within the gamma band and calculate the mean and standard deviation
of the data, as demonstrated in [57], to serve as statistical features for classification.
Jayaratne et al. [64] provide a representative baseline for the common spatial coherence
features. Their method uses the Common Spatial Pattern (CSP) values as the primary
features to train the model, followed by the application of Linear Discriminant Analysis
(LDA) for the classification of a specific user. This approach achieved the best perfor­
mance of 96.97% within the 8 to 30 Hz Alpha and Beta combination band. Similarly, we
implement the methods and classify using EEG data within the 8 to 30 Hz range and
derived from 64 channels.
Sun et al. [77] introduced an EEG­based user identification system employing a 1D­
Convolutional Long Short­Term Memory neural network (1D­Convolutional LSTM). The
effectiveness of this method was substantiated by a publicly accessible database com­
prising EEG data from 109 subjects. The empirical outcomes revealed that, when using a
mere 16­channel EEG signal set, the proposed network could attain an average accuracy
of up to 99.58%. We reimplement this network and do the experiment with 64 channels
for authentication.
Das et al. [73] introduced a framework named MI_CNN, a convolutional neural network
consisting of four convolutional layers. This framework is specifically designed for auto­
mated feature extraction and individual identification, achieving an accuracy of 99%. For
replication of this network and subsequent experimentation, we employ a motor imagery
(MI) dataset from 17 channels, with data appropriately normalized.

6
Discussion
The influence of channel selection
From the results presented in Section 5.2.1, it is evident that the number of channels
plays an important role in determining the system’s accuracy. Generally, as the number
of channels increases, so does the accuracy. Specifically, there is a marked improvement
in the insider test accuracy when the channel count increases from 3 to 12, with only a
marginal enhancement observed between 12 to 18 channels. Conversely, for the outsider
test, 18 channels obtain slightly worse results compared to 12 channels. This suggests an
optimal channel threshold beyond which incremental additions do not significantly improve
accuracy. Notably, a system with fewer channels is advantageous as it necessitates lesser
data, leading to faster processing and a more user­friendly experience. Nevertheless, it’s
important to acknowledge that some subjects did not achieve an EER of zero, indicating
that a universal channel selection may not suitable for all. This underscores the potential
need for personalized channel configurations customized to individual subjects.
Time influence
The BCI dataset, collected over different days, offers insights into the influence of time on
results. The outcomes exhibit variability across subjects. For instance, while Subject 1
achieves an EER of zero, Subject 5 records an EER of 100%. Such findings suggest that
our model is capable of classifying cross­session EEG signals. However, the efficacy of
this classification is subject­dependent. While the EEG signals for certain subjects remain
relatively stable over time, others exhibit significant variations.
Comparison results
Section 5.2.3 presents the results of our proposed models alongside some state­of­the­
art methods. The findings reveal that models from the FCBNet family consistently out­
perform others, even when utilizing fewer channels. While most contemporary methods
rely on 64 channels, the FCBNet family operates with merely 12. In the insider test, the
outcomes of the FCBNet variants are comparable, all falling under 1%. Among them,
FBCNet achieves the best performance. In the outsider test, both Mixed_FBCNet and
SCM_FBCNet emerge as top performers, while FBCNet lags behind.
Given the slight differences observed among the FBCNet family models, we decided to
further reduce the channel count for comparison. This exercise revealed that SRCM_FBCNet
outperforms its counterparts in both tests, underscoring the efficacy of the RFB in extract­
ing more information from fewer channels to differentiate users.
Security Assessment
We introduced three types of attacks to evaluate the robustness of both our models and
their predecessors. In the cases of both temporal noise attack (TNA) and spectral noise
attack (SNA), SRCM_FBCNet exhibits exceptional performance, suggesting that the RFB
offers a degree of noise resistance. Moreover, during the spectral noise attack (SNA), all
models successfully differentiated between synthetic and original signals. However, it
should be noted that these synthetic signals were generated by a diffusion model trained
over 500 steps. The efficacy of signals generated by model trained beyond this remains
uncertain in terms of their ability to deceive the system.

7
Conclusion
Our research aims to enhance the model used for user authentication and assess its ro­
bustness against various attacks. On the Physionet dataset, the model proposed in our
study performs well, similar to previously proposed models utilizing data from 12 channels.
When restricted to just three channels, performance differs between the model integrat­
ing the Receptive Field Block (RFB) and the model without the RFB. The former model
demonstrates superior performance, suggesting that the RFB is a beneficial module for
extracting additional features to facilitate subject differentiation under channel limitations.
The performance of the model without the RFB across all subjects in the BCI dataset is
inconsistent, although it does demonstrate good results for certain subjects. This discrep­
ancy indicates that the model can perform well over time but is dependent on individual
signals. Some subjects’ signals may remain relatively constant over time, and additional
factors such as channel selection and signal denoising may influence the results.
Temporal Noise Attack (TNA) and Spectral Noise Attack (SNA) results reveal that the
RFB module also enhances the model’s resistance to noise. Furthermore, the Spoofing
Temporal Synthesis (STS) attack demonstrates that the system maintains a robust au­
thentication capability, distinguishing real and synthetic signals effectively. Notably, the
synthetic signals significantly deviate from the original signals, making them easily iden­
tifiable for the system. Future research should explore whether synthetic signals, with
adequate training, could potentially deceive the system.
