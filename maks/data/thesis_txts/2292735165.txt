Abstract. 
Cropland expansion has large environmental impacts on a global scale. Large areas have been, and 
continue to be, converted into cropland. Some of the substantial impacts from this conversion are on 
carbon stock and water resources. Especially the impact on water resources has not been included 
sufficiently in life cycle assessments (LCA). The objectives of this study is to assess the impacts of cropland 
expansion on carbon emissions and water resources and evaluate the methodology of this assessment.  
The assessment has been performed using geographic information systems (GIS). A dry region in Sahel in 
Africa was chosen to evaluate the local impact on water resources and evaluate methodology for assessing 
the impact in other regions. Two methods were applied and compared: a method based on the Budyko 
approach (Zhang) and a method based on MODIS ET satellite data (MODIS-ET). Both methods estimate the 
impact of cropland expansion on the hydrological cycle by quantifying changes in annual 
evapotranspiration (ET) and runoff (calculated as the difference between annual precipitation and ET). 
Zhang was only applied to a basin inside Sahel and MODIS-ET was applied to the basin and a larger area 
with homogenous climate. 
 The effect on carbon was calculated using carbon maps and biomass stock. It was calculated globally (using 
deforestation statistics) and for Latin America (using actual expansion data). These calculations were 
compared to another method based on deforestation statistics and standard mean biomass stock instead 
of spatial data on carbon stock. The calculated emissions were then converted to a carbon emission factor 
(CEF) to estimate the annual impact of cropland occupation. The calculated impact was compared to a 
method that used deforestation statistics and was based on mean biomass growth estimates instead of 
spatial data on biomass carbon stock.     
No types of cropland expansion was found to significantly increase ET or decrease runoff in Sahel. Cropland 
was found to have a significantly lower ET (143,1mm/y, p=0,005) and higher runoff (104mm/y, p-
value=0,03) than savannah in the driest third part of Sahel. The transition estimates of ET for the applied 
two methods on the investigated basin was found similar. But LU ET estimates from MODIS-ET was less 
than 60% of the modelled ET value from Zhang. 
The average global Carbon stock emission from cropland expansion was calculated to be ranging from 19,8 
to 186,7 t C·ha-1  dependent on the region and biome. The carbon emission for South American forest was 
119,0 t C·ha-1. This carbon emission estimate was twice as high as the estimate derived using actual 
agricultural expansion data (60,3 t C·ha-1). Comparison to the method that used standard mean biomass 
stock estimates showed good correlation. The global CEF was calculated to be 0,15 to 1,41 t C·ha-1·y-1 
dependent on region and biome. These estimates are very low compared to the study that used standard 
mean biomass growth estimates.  
It can be concluded that cropland expansion in a dry area is not found to have a water depleting effect. On 
the contrary we found higher runoff on cropland than savannah in the driest region. Further investigations 
are needed to examine this aspect and how it can be incorporated into LCA. It can be concluded that using 
statistics does not provide accurate carbon emission estimates in certain regions and biomes. Further, 
carbon impact is higher if derived using biomass growth than biomass stock. More research is needed to 
establish improved methods to calculate CEF from biomass stock and biomass growth respectively.

Resume. 
Globalt har udvidelse af opdyrket jord store miljøpåvirkninger. Store arealer er blevet og bliver 
fortsat ryddet og opdyrket. Væsentlige miljøpåvirkninger ved opdyrkning omfatter 
kulstofudledning og påvirkning af vandressourcer. Specielt er påvirkningen af vandressourcer ikke 
inddraget tilstrækkeligt i livscyklus vurderinger (LCA). Formålet med dette speciale er at vurdere 
hvilken effekt en udvidelse af det opdyrkede areal har på kulstofudledning og vandressourcer og 
evaluere de metoder der anvendes til at vurdere påvirkningen.  
Vurderingen er blevet foretaget med geographic information systems (GIS). Som grundlag for dels 
at vurdere den lokale påvirkning af vandressourcer, dels anvendeligheden af metoderne til at 
vurdere påvirkningen i andre regioner, blev et tørt område i Afrika valgt (Sahel). To metoder blev 
anvendt og sammenlignet: en metode baseret på Budyko tilgangen (Zhang) og en metode baseret 
på MODIS evapotranspiration (ET) satellit data (MODIS-ET). Begge metoder bedømmer 
påvirkningen af opdyrkning på vandressourcerne ved at bestemme ændringen i den årlige ET og 
afstrømningen (beregnet som forskellen mellem den årlige nedbør og ET). Zhang blev kun anvendt 
på et afvandingsområde i Sahel og MODIS-ET blev anvendt på dette område og et større område 
med ensartet klima.  
Udledningen af kulstof blev beregnet ved hjælp af kulstof kort og indholdet af biomasse i 
systemet. Resultaterne blev beregnet på globalt plan (ved hjælp af statistik over skovrydning og 
kulstof kort over indholdet af biomasse) og for Sydamerika (ved at anvende kort over udvidelse af 
landbrugsarealet og kulstof kort over indholdet af biomasse). Disse resultater blev sammenlignet 
med en anden metode der anvender skovrydningsstatistik og en standard for det gennemsnitlige 
indhold af biomasse i stedet for kulstof kort. Den beregnede udledning blev omdannet til en 
”carbon emission factor” (CEF) for at estimere den årlige påvirkning fra at opdyrke jorden. Den 
beregnede påvirkning blev sammenlignet med en metode der benytter statistik over skovrydning 
og er baseret på estimater for den gennemsnitlige biomasse vækst i stedet for indholdet af 
biomasse fra kort. 
Opdyrkning medførte ikke signifikant forøgelse af ET eller formindsket afstrømning I Sahel. 
Opdyrket land viste sig i den tørreste tredjedel af Sahel at have en signifikant højere afstrømning 
end savanne (104 mm, p-værdi=0,03) og en signifikant lavere ET (143,1mm/y, p=0,005). 
Sammenlignelige ET estimater blev fundet ved de to metoder der anvendtes på det undersøgte 
afvandingsområde. 
Den gennemsnitlige kulstof udledning ved rydning og opdyrkning af land blev beregnet til at 
variere fra 19,8 til 186, t. kulstof/ha, afhængig af området og biosystemet. Den gennemsnitlige 
kulstof udledning fra Sydamerikansk skov var 119,0 t C·ha-1.  Dette kulstof estimat var dobbelt så 
højt som estimatet frembragt ved brug af kort over udvidelse af landbrugsarealet (60,3 t C·ha-1). 
Sammenligning med metoden som brugte standard gennemsnitlige biomasse indhold viste en god 
korrelation. Den globale CEF blev beregnet til at være mellem 0,15 og 1,41 t. kulstof/ha afhængig 
af område og biosystem. Disse estimater er meget lave sammenlignet med den undersøgelse der 
anvendte standard gennemsnitlige biomasse vækst estimater.

1 Background 
The limited land resources of the world (only 1/3 of the globe is covered with land) are facing increasing 
pressure. There are many factors contributing to this. While rising population demands more land to be 
used for food and feed, in recent years, a new pressure has also emerged in the form of utilization of land 
for bioenergy production (Gibbs et al. 2010).  
There are two solutions to increasing cropland production. The first is to intensify production on existing 
areas to raise yields. The second is to expand the cropland area. This expansion results in a series of 
cascading effects involving “intermediate” land use changes (LUC), (meaning croplands are transformed 
into other types of croplands) ultimately incurring conversion of natural/semi-natural ecosystems into 
cropland (net expansion of agriculture at the frontier with nature) (Kløverpris 2010). 
Between 1961 and 2007 4 million ha of cropland were added on annual average globally. There are huge 
regional differences since the area of cropland is currently decreasing in the developed world but is 
increasing in the developing world (Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012). However, overall it is increasing 
(Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012). 
This kind of LUC has several environmental impacts. If natural vegetation is cleared carbon emissions occur. 
This depends on the ecosystem type (forest or shrubland, etc.) and the geographic location (Tonini et al. 
2015). The carbon emissions associated with LUC are still poorly understood although they represent one 
of the biggest anthropogenic sources (Harris et al. 2012). 
LUC also has an effect on water resources. Changing land use (LU)  directly affects evapotranspiration (ET) 
resulting in a change in runoff and may make water more scarce for human uses (Costa et al. 2003) or, it 
may increase runoff and consequent flooding risk (Nunez et al. 2013). The effect of agricultural expansion 
on water resources depends on where it takes place and how the expansion occurs (Scanlon et al. 2007). 
The problem is further complicated since the effect of using a certain amount of water depends very much 
on where in the world the production takes place (Pfister et al. 2011). 
Because the environmental effect of LUC differs according to where the change occurs, it is important to 
consider the geographic location when evaluating the environmental performances of agricultural systems. 
Taking spatial effects into account will ensure that the effects are incorporated correctly into life cycle 
assessments studies (LCA) (Pfister et al. 2011). LCA is a method to evaluate the environmental performance 
of a product or system throughout its entire life cycle (Nunez et al. 2013). Many LCA studies overlook the 
environmental effects of LUCs due to the lack of a link between the usage of land in one specific region and 
the induced expansion of cropland in other regions of the world. This is termed indirect LUC (iLUC), 
(Schmidt et al. 2012). Improving the knowledge about LUC effects has therefore a great impact on the 
science of LCA, and may benefit both LCA of bioenergy and of agricultural systems.

2 Litterature review 
In the following chapter, the results of previous research will be presented for cropland.  Since many 
studies have investigated agricultural expansion in general, including pasture expansion and cropland 
expansion, these results will be presented as agricultural expansion in this section. 
2.1 Previous research on cropland expansion  
Many authors have examined how the LUC due to cropland expansion have occurred around the globe until 
today. Alexandratos & Bruinsma (2012) found that using a period from 1967 to 2007, expansion of cropland 
has mainly occurred in Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa and East Asia (Malaysia and Indonesia included). 
The biggest total expansion has occurred in Africa (108 Mha), closely followed by Latin America (97 Mha) 
compared to 58 Mha in East Asia. However, the regions show quite the same rate of cropland expansion 
over this period (around 0.9 percent). If only looking at the last 25-years, Africa have had the highest rate, 
and Latin America the lowest. The study also projected future expansion until year 2050, and predicts that 
East Asia will experience little further LUC, while the two other regions will both have an expansion of 
about 50 Mha. However, it has to be remembered that East Asia includes China as well, which is predicted 
to decrease its area of cropland1. The expansion in the developing world is mainly taking place in the tropics 
(Foley et al. 2011, cited from FAOSTAT 20112).  
Most studies have only investigated net expansion of agricultural land and not the source of the land (Gibbs 
et al. 2010). Gibbs et al. (2010) found that more than 80 % of the expansion of agricultural land in the 
tropics during the 80’s and 90’s has occurred on former tropical forest. All regions showed a high 
proportion of forest conversion. It is important to be aware that Gibbs et al. (2010)’s definition of forest 
land was >10% tree cover which is less strict than in most other classifications. The ratio between how 
much of the replaced forest that was disturbed or intact, varies widely and the variation is generally greater 
within than between regions. Shrubland (used collectively for cerrado, woodland, savannah and grassland 
with less than 10 % tree cover) is converted to cropland in substantial amount in South America and East 
Africa. Disturbed forest is converted to agricultural land in regions with low forest cover such as West Africa 
and South Asia (e.g. India). Intact forest is converted to agricultural land in regions that are still rich in 
forest. Brink et al. (2009) investigated LUCs in Africa from 1975 to 2000 and agrees with the trends in 
agricultural land3 expansion stated by Gibbs et al. (2010) but uses a different definition of forest (>40% tree 
cover), concluding that forest is the source of 58 % of the land for agricultural expansion. Brink et al. (2009) 
further found that most substantial agricultural expansion took place in Sahel.  
The results from Gibbs et al. (2010) indicate that many of the areas (within the regions) that were 
agricultural expansion hotspots in the 80’s are also hotspots in the 90’s. This also corresponds to the 
findings of Fearnside (2000), where it is stated that the great majority of deforestation occurs at edges that 
progressively expand on the forest land. This seems to indicate that these trends will also be valid for 
results for the 21th century. 
Grasser et al. (2015) examined the pasture and cropland expansion in Latin America from 2001 to 2013. 
This study also included the southern portion (e.g. Argentina) while many studies have focused only on the 
Amazon. Furthermore, it also separated expansion of pasture and cropland. It was established that there

The use of remote sensing in LUC investigations generally increases the accuracy of the LUC estimates 
dramatically (Gibbs et al. 2010). There are many global LUC maps available for covering changes in 
cropland. This includes the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) land use land cover 
(LULC) maps (MCD12C1 and MCD12Q1), maps produced by the European Space Association (ESA) and the 
Globeland 30 project. However, as outlined below, all these maps are not adequately precise, not updated 
or have other issues.  
The problem in using the MODIS maps are that they are mainly to be used as input to models, not change 
detection (Sulla-Menashe 2015, personal communication). The uncertainty of the maps at a pixel level is 
substantial, thus change detection at a pixel level can provide misleading results.   
European Space Association (ESA) have produced LU products for the entire world. They have actually 
produced two different map series of global LU maps. The first of these series is belonging to the “Climate 
Research Data Package (CRDP)”. Unfortunately, these maps are not updated in terms of cropland and 
grassland data (Bontemps 2015, personal communication4). This update will not be finished before autumn 
2015, and prior to this, the map should not be used for change detection of cropland and grassland. The 
second map they have produced is the Globecover map. However, according to Willaarts (2015, personal 
communication), the map series were not accurate enough which was the reason why further elaboration 
based on this and other maps were undertaken, as explained below.   
The Globeland 30 project is probably overall the best map with a spatial resolution of just 30m. 
Unfortunately, only the 2010 map has been released for download during the elaboration of this project. 
The 2000 map (!) should be ready for download in the middle of this year (Peng 2015, personal 
communication). It is however already available for visual investigation at the project webpage 
(globallandcover.com n.d.).   
Thus, there are no global LU maps that can be used for change detection. The only larger scale maps that is 
found to be usable are two maps that are both for the region of Latin America. The last, and possibly best 
of these maps (Willaarts 2015, personal communication), was developed by Graesser et al. (2015). This 
map only covers Latin America but is suitable for doing pixel based change detection analysis of cropland. 
However, the map has been in the process of getting updated during 2015 (Graesser 2015, personal 
communication). It was therefore not to be used in any analysis at the time of this project.  
The other map for Latin America was developed by Willaarts et al. (2014). This change detection map was 
produced as a combination of two different maps where each of the maps where elaborated to fit 
together. These maps were from 1993 (Global Land cover produced by the USGS) and 2009 (produced by 
ESA). Willaarts et al. (2014) tried to correct some of the errors that these maps originally contained. Thus, 
this map should be the only map usable for mapping large scale change detection of cropland at the pixel 
level. 
Even though there are no global LUC maps that can be used to estimate cropland expansion, there are 
other approaches to solve this problem. One solution is to average maps over a longer time period to 
minimize errors. Apart from using pure remote sensing investigations, other studies have attempted to

2.2 Previous research on carbon emissions from cropland expansion 
Many papers have investigated carbon emissions in relation to LUC and methods for their estimation.  
2.2.1 Modelling Carbon fluxes 
Houghton (2003) investigated, through statistics, which effect human induced LUCs had on carbon emission 
to the atmosphere during a period of 1850-2000. He found that LUCs represents a carbon sink outside of 
the tropics, but including the tropics has the implication that there is a yearly transfer of 2,2 Pg C year-1 
from LUC globally. These results indicate that it might not be meaningful to investigate non-tropical regions 
in terms of C emission from LUC. However these results might cover considerable regional differences. 
Fearnside (2000) examines the total greenhouse gas emissions from the tropics and the type of these. 
Virtually all cleared areas are burned in most tropical countries where deforestation occurs, such as Brazil.  
Even though not all biomass is burned, almost all the rest is still released to the atmosphere through decay. 
This means that all the carbon, at least from the above ground biomass (AGB) is released to the 
atmosphere5. Thus, it is not likely that e.g. timber is incorporated into wood products storing the carbon for 
many years. It is therefore realistic to assume that all carbon is simply lost to CO2. However, because a lot 
of the biomass decays, rather than burns, some of the emissions will in reality not occur in the year of the 
clearing.  
In a literature review in Fearnside et al. (2009), it was found that the amount of biomass that is burned and 
decayed respectively depends on the ecosystem. Initial burn efficiency in forest is 39,4%, and then 11,4 % 
more is burned through the first decade. Initial burn efficiency in cerrado (South American savannah)  is 
65,0 % and 5,7 % more is burned through the first decade. The rest is left for decay. Thus, using the case of 
forest, about 40 % of the carbon emission happen in immediate connection to the clearing, while about 10 
% happens during the following 10 years, and the rest will be released gradually during a longer period 
from decay.  
How much of the below ground biomass (BGB) that is emitted upon cultivation is complicated to establish 
and elaborate from the different studies. Not many articles have investigated the effect of cropland 
expansion on BGB, and the few conducted studies have rather focused on change in root morphology than 
the loss of biomass and ultimately carbon from the change (Jackson et al. 2000). The topic is further 
complicated because it is often unclear whether academia stating “below ground biomass” include soil 
carbon in this terminology.  
Most articles that have used the emissions from BGB in the conducted modelling, e.g. Searchinger et al. 
(2008), divide up the carbon compartment into soil and plant. From this and from looking into the sources 
used by Searchinger et al. (2008) (Guo & Gifford (2002), Murty et al. (2002) it is apparent that in 
Searchinger et al. (2008) soil carbon only means the carbon that is in the soil and the BGB is not included.

Thus BGB is here included in what is termed the plant or vegetation carbon which is removed to make 
room for cultivation. Searchinger et al. (2008) did the modelling assuming that 100% of the vegetation 
carbon stock is released upon cultivation.  
This has been the approach by the majority of studies, which have thus added estimates from above, and 
BGB together and therefore treated the carbon from the BGB similarly to AGB (Saatchi et al. 2011, Harris et 
al. 2012). Harris et al. (2012) also assumed all of the BGB and AGB was cleared. This is also stated to be the 
case by Baccini et al. (2012), although their map only includes AGB.  
Different approaches exist to map biomass, thus also carbon, in vegetation biomass. While it is not possible 
to measure biomass directly from space, space remote sensing can still be used to estimate it (Dong et al. 
2003). Dong et al. (2003) investigated the relationship between satellite measurements of Normalized 
Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), at a high spatial resolution, and inventoried biomass for temperate and 
boreal forest. The authors found that there was a link between biomass and NDVI and that this link is 
present at all investigated latitudes (29° to 72°). Van Tuyl et al. (2005) used leaf area index (LAI) in 
combination with ground inventories to establish both NPP and carbon storage in forests in Westen 
Oregon. A literature review performed by Drake et al 2002, evaluated that this is not possible in the tropics 
because of the thick canopy structure making LAI saturate much faster than the actual biomass (Drake et al. 
2002). This is also the case for NDVI (Mitchard et al. 2013) (Harris et al. 2009). 
Thus, in the tropics there is a need for a different approach. Mapping biomass with the technology “Light 
Detection And Ranging” (LiDAR) has been used successfully in many investigations to estimate the biomass 
(and carbon) of a small scale system (e.g. Drake et al. 2002). However, large scale satellite driven LiDAR 
system will not be ready for usage before the end of this decade (end of the 2010’s)(Saatchi et al. 2011, 
cited from Shugart et al. 20106). The solution for studies until then has been to combine inventory samples 
and LiDAR samples and then scale up using satellite imagery (e.g. Saatchi et al. 2011;Baccini et al. 
2012)(Harris 2015, personal communication). When the AGB has been estimated, the BGB was calculated 
based on established relationships (Mokany et al. 2006). This relationship finds that the BGB is about 25 % 
of the AGB. 
The carbon map from Saatchi et al. (2011) combines global coverage and includes BGB based on the above 
relationship. The carbon map from Baccini et al. (2012) also has global coverage and though the data is only 
including AGB, BGB can be found readily from the above relation by individual geographic information 
system (GIS) elaboration by the user of the data. 
Harris et al. (2012) used the maps from Saatchi et al. (2011) to investigate carbon emissions from tropical 
deforestation. It was concluded that the carbon emission from the tropical forest of Africa is relative 
smaller than in the other areas because the tropical forest here is more dry. Furthermore, only areas with 
more than 25 % forest cover was included in the forest definition used by Harris et al. (2012), leaving out 
large areas of savannah from the assessment. 
Establishing the amount of the soil carbon that is released upon cultivation is complicated because 
different studies uses different sampling depths. The greatest amount of soil carbon is by far within the first 
top m of soil (Fearnside 2000). Many modelling studies have incorporated soil carbon by assuming that 25 
% will be released following cultivation (Searchinger et al. 2008; Müller-Wenk & Brandio 2010). This also 
corresponds well to what was found in Davidson & Ackerman (1993). This study found that the average 
emissions from all the investigated soil profiles amounted to 24 %. However, the analyzed depth was not

consistent and varied between 19 and 150 cm. Their analysis also showed that most of this carbon loss 
occurs within a few years of the beginning of the cultivation. Murty et al. (2002) found that the cultivation 
emitted 22% of soil carbon. Murty et al. (2002) also did a small meta-analysis regarding the temporal

cultivation effect on the carbon in 0-30 cm depth, and 30-60 cm depth are quite similar.  
The importance of soil carbon for the total carbon emissions is highly dependent on the location. Fargione 
et al. (2008) established that it is very important to consider whether the rain forest is standing on peatland 
when assessing the carbon emission from LUC. The CO2 emissions from peatland rainforest was found to be 
almost 5 times higher than regular rainforest, because the peatland has to be drained prior to cultivation, 
resulting in large emission from decaying peat. This is also the reason why some studies have only included 
the soil emission from peatland (Tonini et al. 2015). Whether the land is peatland mainly needs to be taken 
into account in Malaysia and Indonesia (Edwards et al. 2010). A problem is however that these peatland 
forests are characterized as forest (and not wetland) in land cover maps such as MODIS, therefore 
underestimating the release of soil carbon upon clearing of the forest. Some studies have not included soil 
carbon at all in the assessment of carbon emission from deforestation, because they were assumed to be 
uncertain and small in many areas (Harris et al. 2012). 
According to the European Commission, the best available spatial data on global soil carbon properties is 
the Harmonized World Soil Database (HWSD). This map is also one of the few soil carbon datasets that is 
presented as spatial data (ec.europa.eu 2014). This map was used in Harris et al. (2009). The map also exist 
in an updated version, which is however not available for download.  
Besides from spatial data, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) provides default numbers 
for determining mean emission factors for the different ecosystems on a regional scale. Both estimates for 
soil and vegetation are available from IPCC (2006).   
2.2.2 Modelling carbon impacts in LCA 
Different approaches exist to include CO2 emissions from LUC in an LCA perspective. Searchinger et al. 
(2008) used “the annualization method”. This method attributes the carbon emissions from the LUC to a 
certain period afterwards where the area is presumed to be occupied by crops. This period was set to 30 
years in Searchinger et al. (2008).  The implication of this is that the production over a 30 year period from 
the LUC is divided with the emission arising from the LUC. The chosen accounting period is often referred to 
as “the amortization period” (Schmidt et al. 2012). The 30 year amortization period in Searchinger et al. 
(2008) was chosen because changes need to happen fast to mitigate climate change.  
There are some negative consequences of modelling climate change impact with the annualization method. 
The choice of the amortization period is rather arbitrary and at the same time, it heavily influences the 
impact per unit of crop (Kløverpris & Mueller 2013).  
Schmidt et al. (2012) and Kløverpris & Mueller (2013) (in the following referred to as both articles) used a 
different approach compared to Searchinger et al. (2008). Both articles built their approach on the 
atmospheric residential time of CO2. Kløverpris & Mueller (2013) named the method “baseline time 
==PAGE:15==
Side 15 af 87 
 
accounting”. Both articles modelled the effect of cropland expansion as the cultivated area is taken into 
production one year prior to what it would have been otherwise. This implies that the area would have 
been taken into production later on anyway due to future cropland expansion.  
The effect of cropland expansion is thus modelled as not causing an increasing amount of emissions, but 
rather that the emissions occur before they would have done without cropland expansion. The net 
emission from cropland expansion is therefore 0. However, because the emission occurs earlier with 
cropland expansion, the impact is more severe. Earlier emission causes the effect to be more severe 
because only a restricted time period is taken into account in the assessment. Thus, the scenario with 
cropland expansion caused one more year of warming impact than the scenario without cropland 
expansion.  
Different time periods can be used to assess the global warming potential (GWP). Kløverpris & Mueller 
(2013) uses the typical approach which takes the global warming potential over a 100 year period in 
account. This is termed GWP100. It is important to be aware, that choosing a longer accounting period 
means that the effect of speeding up the emissions with one year is smaller, and thus that the impact of 
cropland expansion is smaller. 
Kløverpris & Mueller (2013) modelled the difference between the annualization method used by 
Searchinger et al. (2008) and the proposed baseline time accounting using the emission data from 
Searchinger et al. (2008). The results showed that the baseline time accounting method consistently 
resulted in lower carbon impacts per unit of produced crop – only about 25-30% of the impact from using 
the annualization method.       
Other articles have not used emissions from the carbon stock resulting from LUC, but have instead utilized 
the growth of the ecosystem to evaluate the impact of cropland. Tonini et al. (2015) averaged the growth in 
the individual investigated ecosystems over 100 years and used this to generate a carbon emission factor 
(CEF, t CO2-C·ha-1·y-1). The growth estimates were taken from region and biome specific data from IPCC 
(2006).  Thus, while the area is occupied by cropland, it will not accumulate biomass and sequestrate 
carbon, and this lack of sequestration is the impact of the cropland occupation.  
2.3 Previous research on the effects of cropland expansion on water resources 
2.3.1 Modelling of water fluxes 
The effect of cropland expansion on water resources has been investigated in many regions, at many scales 
and for many different types of LUC.  
It has been shown in several studies that forests use more water than agriculture (e.g. Legesse et al. 2003). 
Scanlon et al. (2007) conclude in a global study that conversion to rain feed agriculture mainly increases the 
water recharge but lowers the quality. Conversion to irrigated agriculture generally decreases water 
quantity as well as quality. This does not only hold true for forest land but also for savannah (Leduc et al. 
2001). Leduc et al. (2001) performed an investigation of the reason for a rise in the water table in Sahel in 
Niger in a semi-arid area a 30-year mega drought period. They concluded that this was due to LUC to rain 
feed crops (from savannah). Scanlon et al. (2005) further found that cropland expansion in the High plains 
of USA increases runoff also compared to natural grassland. The reason seems to be because of the shorter 
growing season of crops compared to grassland (Zhang & Schilling 2006). Therefore, the results of cropland 
expansion on the water cycle differ in terms of groundwater recharge and ET in different regions with 
different land use transitions. Thus, there is strong need for site-specific analysis to incorporate the effects 
of these transition into LCAs reliably.  
==PAGE:16==
Side 16 af 87 
 
Irrigated areas are mainly expanding in developing countries but primarily in land scarce regions where 
there is a need to optimize yield (Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012). Therefore it does not seem likely that 
expansion of irrigated land will happen where there is plenty of land to be taken from clearing natural 
ecosystems. Taking the case of Sub-Saharan Africa, the expansion of irrigated land constituted 2,8 % of the 
total expansion of cropland from 1961 to 2005/2007. The expansion of irrigated land is projected to be less 
than 2 % of the total expanded cropland between 2005/2007 and 2050 (Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012).   
Evaluating the effect of a LUC on water resources has been done by looking at the difference in ET between 
the natural vegetation and the replacing crop. (Nunez et al. 2013). The use of water through ET is also 
termed as the green water flow, representing water that is in the system but cannot be directly used by 
humans (Falkenmark & Rockstrom 2004). Blue water on the other hand, is surface water and groundwater 
(Nunez et al. 2013). It can be assumed that non-irrigated crops do not use blue water (Pfister et al. 2015, 
personal communication). 
The Runoff from an area can be calculated by substracting ET from precipitation, assuming no soil storage 
and including groundwater recharge as runoff (Renner et al. 2014). Thus, areas with higher ET will have 
lower runoff.    
Global ET data is available from using inputs of satellite imagery. The MODIS MOD16A3 product has data on 
global annual ET from 2000 to 2014. This ET data is based on the Penman-Monteith equation. MOD16A3 
also provides PET. For more information about the MOD16 datasets and the algorithm used to derive the 
datasets, please see Mu et al. (2013).  
Some studies have found that MOD 16 ET deviates from actual measurements of ET. This was concluded to 
be the case for South Africa in Ramoelo et al. (2014), where MOD 16 was compared with measurement in 
two savannah ecosystems. MOD 16 was found to underestimate the actual ET by between 2 and 7 mm/8 
days7. They actually state that the correlation between MOD 16 and the actual observations are poor. The 
conclusion of a poor correlation were also drawn in Garcia et al. (2012) for the Sahelian region. Here the 
bias was -0,56 mm/day (r2=0,32) .  
However, the problem was mainly that MOD16 failed to capture the dynamics of ET throughout the year, 
an aspect that is less important for this study where annual data is used. This was also performed with the 
8 day MOD 16 dataset – not the annual. Some sources states MOD16 as overpredicting the ET in some 
areas. Measurements performed in a Sahelian site (Bamako) from 2006 to 2009, found that in this 
particular site, MODIS was higher than measured ET by a factor 1,33 (Garcia 2015, personal 
communication). The advantage of this approach is that it is linked to the historical and current land 
situation based on actual reflectivity properties of the land. Global remote approaches, like MOD 16, are 
being refined continuously being a promising source of ET data.  
Another way to estimate ET is based on hydrological models of varying complexity. For instance, Zhang et 
al. (2001) investigated how the ET of a basin would respond to LUC using an approach based on the 
relationship between annual rainfall and potential evapotranspiration at the catchment level (Budyko 
1958). By knowing the percentage of the basin that was composed of short vegetation and trees, the 
average ET for the total basin can be estimated, if the precipitation for the basin is known as well. Zhang et 
al. (2001) also found that the potential evapotranspiration (PET) could be assumed to be a constant value

for each investigated LU in the modelling of ET on the basin level. The approach was based on global basin

To use the approach from Zhang et al. (2001) there is a need for global precipitation data. Precipitation 
data is available from many sources. However, the data needs to be at a resolution compatible with the 
scale of the remotely sensed data and the scale of analysis, be available globally and it must be possible to 
extract yearly precipitation values if the data should be used in LUC investigation at a larger scale. Most of 
global precipitation data is not in high enough spatial resolution for evaluating the effect of LUC.  The only 
global dataset that was found to have the necessary spatial resolution to be used in LUC investigations was

Toner & Schilling (2009) investigated how to develop an index to separate the changes due to interannual 
climatic variability from the effect of LUC. This index is based on how LUC and climatic changes affects the 
PET, the actual ET and the precipitation. They found that climatic change shifts these in a different way 
than LUCs. Unfortunately, their finding can only be used to state that an effect is primarily due to LUC or 
climate change, but it is not possible to state that a certain percentage of the effect is coming from LUC and 
climate change respectively. Therefore, the approach cannot be used when the exact effect of LUC is 
needed and trends are not enough. 
It is important to note that changing the LU in one region might affect other regions as well. The LUC results 
in an alteration of the vapor coming from one region. Deforestation will lead to a lower transfer of vapor 
from the biosphere to the atmosphere, while irrigation will result in increased transfer of vapor to the 
atmosphere (Gordon et al. 2005). Therefore, looking into selected regions will not provide the whole aspect 
of effects coming from LUC. In the case of deforestation, more water will be available in that basin (less ET 
leading to more run off) but the neighboring basin might have less rain (due to less water vapour in the air). 
This makes the understanding of the effects of LUC more limited.  
2.3.2 Modelling impact of water resources in LCA 
LCAs have often only investigated environmental performance in terms of carbon, leaving water less 
investigated (Pfister et al. 2011). The traditional ignoring of water seems to be due to the industrial origin 
of LCA (Heuwelmans 2005). 
The effect of using a certain amount of water is highly dependent on which region the water usage takes 
place. The reason is that water scarcity8 differs greatly on a regional scale. Unless differences in water 
scarcity is included in the assessment, the true environmental impact of water use will not be established. 
Pfister et al. (2009) investigated how to assess the environmental impacts of freshwater consumption and 
suggested a method for how to incorporate this environmental effect into LCA . The article presents the 
Water stress Index (WSI), which is based on the ratio between annual freshwater withdrawals to 
hydrological availability. Pfister et al. (2009) further suggested that there should be made a distinction 
between comsumptive and degradative use of water. Consumptive water use means that other users can’t 
use the water, e.g. because it is evaporated. Degradative water use means that the quality of the water is 
decreased but the water is still available for other users. The latter will be the case for water used on

croplands (Scanlon et al. , 2007). Decreasing the quality of water can also be seen as decreasing the 
quantity. 
LCA studies have until the last few years only focused on blue water since green water use has been 
perceived as less of an environmental problem (Nunez et al. 2013). But the majority of the regions of 
interest, such as Africa, has little irrigated areas and are not projected to get more in the future 
(Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012). Moreover, green water use does contribute to the alteration of water 
resources (Nunez et al. 2013).  Nunez et al. (2013) presented a framework for how to assess the 
environmental impact of changing the ET on an area (changing the LU) and incorporate this into LCA. They 
calculated the ET of the Potential Natural Vegetation (PNV) and used this in case studies comparing it to the 
new crop vegetation.   
Incorporating increased risk of flooding from LUCs due to increased runoff into LCA is a topic that has so far 
been largely unexplored in studies. This impact can be very detrimental though (Nunez et al. 2013). 
Heuwelmans et al. (2005) investigated the topic, however only in a theoretical way because the necessary 
research for conducting case studies on the basin level was not available at that time. There is not retrieved 
later research on the topic.   
It also has to be recognized that many hydrological impacts occurs because of extreme events – not 
average flow. This is not well reflected in LCA studies because there is lack of incorporation of temporal 
variation (Heuwelman 2005). There is therefore not really a framework for incorporating effect of increased 
amount of water from LUC. 
      
2.4 Conclusions from literature review. 
A synthesis of the previous research shows that cropland expansion mainly occurs in the tropical regions. 
The expansion is split into three big areas; Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa and (South) East Asia. The 
biggest cropland expansion historically have occurred in Latin America and Sub-Saharan Africa and this is 
also where it is projected to be the biggest change in future, but they are not expected to be as drastic. 
South East Asia has seen less LUC and is not projected to have big changes in the future. The majority of the 
agricultural expansion in the tropics takes place on forested land, but other biomes are also important, 
dependent on the forest definition used. Areas experiencing large scale LUC, seems to mostly continue to 
be hotspots. 
Carbon emissions from Sub-Saharan Africa is low compared to the other regions because it is dryer and has 
less biomass. At the same time, it means that Africa is more interesting in relation to depletion of water 
resources.  The structure of the tropical forest makes it hard to use traditional remote sensing approaches 
to map biomass more precisely but new remote sensing technology in combination with other methods 
have produced precise biomass maps. Together with soil carbon maps, they show the entire carbon stock 
of the tropical ecosystems. Soil is especially important to take into account when the cultivation happens 
on peatland. It is realistic to assume that practically all the CO2 from the AGB and BGB will be released 
following tropical LUC and not stored in products. Reported soil carbon losses are not completely 
consistent, but around 25% release of the top meter carbon upon cultivation seems to be an average 
estimate. Different approaches exist for including carbon emission from LUC in literature.  
Most agricultural expansion happens in the tropics, but the tropics do not experience much water  
==PAGE:19==
Side 19 af 87 
 
In principle the tropics should not experience major water stress related to crop production, but there are 
smaller hotspots. Studies seem to indicate that expansion of cropland will have a positive impact on the 
quantity of fresh water resources avaible to human uses – even in semiarid savannah landscapes and 
natural grasslands. This is only the case for rain feed crops and not for irrigated crops. However, irrigated 
areas seem to be very rare, and continue to be very rare, in most of the regions of interest in respect to 
cropland expansion (Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012). It is therefore most likely that the normal effect of 
agricultural expansion in the tropics will be increasing runoff for a region leading to more flooding, instead 
of more drought. Different methods can be employed to estimate the change of ET due to cropland 
expansion on water resources. Some of these approaches works on the basin level, while yet others work 
on a general level. They rely on inputs available from satellite data, especially MODIS. There is no 
framework yet for how to include increased water from cropland expansion in LCA. Cropland expansion 
resulting in higher ET and less water availability can be assessed with the WSI.

3 Objectives 
It has been demonstrated that cropland expansion occurs on a very large scale, is very unevenly distributed 
and its effect also varies considerably dependent upon location. Furthermore, and very importantly, it is 
projected that it will continue to occur in the future. However, effects of cropland expansion are still not 
accurately taken into account in LCA studies. The environmental effects of LUCs are still poorly understood, 
even though the effect are considered to have significant impacts. There is, to this day, scarce knowledge 
on which impact cropland expansion may have locally in terms of water resources, as the LCA research has 
been much more focused on carbon impacts.  
In the attempt of filling this gap in the scientific knowledge, the purpose of this project is to investigate how 
the expansion of the area of cropland affects CO2 emissions and water resources and how the impact can 
be included in LCA. This project aims at answering the following questions: 
 
What are the carbon emissions from historical cropland expansion globally? 
 
What is the local change on the water resources from a hypothetical cropland expansion scenario 
in a water scarce region in Africa, and are different methologies comparable? 
 
What is the impact of the carbon emission from historical cropland expansion globally? 
 
What is the impact of the change from hypothetical cropland expansion scenarios to the water 
resources?

4 Materials and Methods 
As previously mentioned, the carbon and water assessment are performed in different ways and on a 
different scale. This is due to the nature of the problems, CO2 emissions being more of a global 
environmental issue and the complexity of the assessment. 
4.1 Water fluxes   
To evaluate the impacts of cropland expansion on water resources, we consider two variables: annual ET 
and runoff (precipitation-ET). The ET reflects the direct change on transpiration and soil evaporation due to 
cropland expansion (green water fluxes). The annual runoff shows the change in surface water and 
groundwater available for freshwater use, assuming that there is no change in soil water storage. This is 
especially interesting if the precipitation varies between the LUs.  
The analysis of water fluxes will be performed as a “what if” analysis, prognostic. The meaning of “What if” 
is that hypothetical future LUCs are investigated (“What if” this actually happened). This means that it is 
modelled which effect possible cropland expansion would have on an area. The analysis will be done 
according to two different approaches. 
 
Method 1 (Zhang): Modelling ET and runoff changes on a basin level with an equation for annual ET 
based on the Budyko approach from Zhang et al. (2001). 
 
Method 2 (MODIS-ET): Evaluation of natural vegetation ET and runoff compared with cropland ET 
and runoff from MODIS satellite data. 
The reason to have two different methods is to validate the reliability of the MODIS-ET method by 
comparing assessments from both methods. Another reason is to have a methodology that is tied to 
historical data on land use, meteorological and vegetation information (MODIS-ET) and another (Zhang) 
which is independent of historical land use data, dependent only on climatic data and could be potentially 
applied to future climate change scenarios. 
The two methods will be compared by investigating the same area. However, MODIS-ET will also be applied 
to a larger region. 
4.1.1 Selecting regions of interest. 
The only historical change detection maps available when the water analysis was carried out9 were the 
MODIS maps (MCD12C1, and MCD12Q110). However, both these maps have many inconsistencies (as 
previously mentioned). Thus, it was decided to use the maps to focus on a particular region of interest 
which were coherent with the literature review (Brink et al. 2009), rather than on the globe.

Africa from west to east and from between 14° N and 18° N. It comprises annual rainfall level increasing 
from 200 mm/y to 1000 mm/y, which makes it an ideal region to test the impact on water resources.

The method works by calculation of ET twice – with two different LU combinations. The first calculation is 
performed on the 2012 land use of the southern basin – this is termed the baseline scenario. The second 
calculation is performed after having hypothetically increased the cropland area by 20 %. This expansion is 
assumed to take place on land previously covered with savannah. Each calculation gives the average ET in 
the basin with the respective land cover. Subtracting these results yield the change in average ET between 
the two LU situations. It is known how many ha that have changed in the basin and how many ha the total 
basin consists of. This can then be scaled to a change per ha which is of interest in this study. 
The result from Zhang will present the difference of mean ET in the basin as a consequence of cropland 
expansion, from which the runoff can be calculated. There is no standard error generated. The reason is 
that only one calculation is made and the whole basin is involved (all spatial data is included in the 
analysis). Since all data is included, this theoretically means that there is no standard error.

4.1.3 MODIS-ET 
Introduction of MODIS-ET approach 
The MOD16 ET product provides ET values at a pixel level every 8th day, monthly and annually for the last 
15 years. This allows analysis of the effect of LUC based on these historical estimates. 
The effect from LUC on availability of freshwater resources can be found by looking first at the difference in 
temporally averaged ET of the natural vegetation and comparing it to the temporally averaged ET of the 
new ecosystem, in this case cropland. To separate the effect of LUC from the effect of climate variability, 
the uncertainty associated to interannual variability was during the historical period considered. 
First we selected homogeneous areas of a particular LUC that did not change during the whole period. This 
was done by taking two LU maps of extreme temporal values (in this case 2001 and 2012) for the region of 
interest. For each type of investigated LU, those areas that have that the same LU in both extreme 
temporal cases are extracted (Figure 4.6) and spatially averaged across a region for each year. 
 
Figure 4.6: How areas of interest is extracted. Case example for cropland (Own Production). 
 
The reason for choosing this method, is that the available LU maps have the before mentioned issues when 
used for pixel based change detection. By selecting pixels that have the same LU in these two extreme 
==PAGE:31==
Side 31 af 87 
 
temporal values, there is insured a bigger chance of choosing pixels that actually have that LU, and has had 
this LU throughout the period. By aggregating spatially, some errors can be averaged out. The pixels of each 
LU that fulfills this criterion are then compared in term of their ET and precipitation. 
Application of MODIS-ET 
It was chosen to use MCD12Q1 as LU map, being the LU map with the best spatial and temporal resolution, 
despite its mentioned shortcomings. This map is less suited for a global analysis but well suited for smaller 
scale investigations.  
Since the MOD12Q1 maps are in small tiles (tiles are small sections of the globe on the map), these sections 
have to be combined, in order to work with the data in GIS. For instance, the BSh region is composed of the 
tiles h16v07 to h21v07 plus h20v07. Therefore a dataset with these tiles was created for both the years 
2001 and 2012 and the part of the tiles that was inside the BSh region was extracted. The ET from MOD16 
and the LU from MCD12Q1 are extracted from the areas that have the same LU in the two years.  
For the identified relevant areas, ET data are used for the whole period, including both 2001 and 2012 but 
also the years in between. The mean ET for every year for the particular LU investigated is extracted. The 
process is repeated for the different LUs. Thus, 12 years of ET means for every LU are extracted. The mean 
and standard error are then compared for the different LUs and it is detected whether the observed 
differences are significant at a chosen 5 % level of significance. The standard error accounts for differences 
in ET related to climatology. The analysis will be done for cropland but also for the other relevant LUs of the 
region. 
Furthermore, only relevant LUs (terrestrial ecosystems) are investigated. In addition, the relevant LUs are 
only investigated if they are represented with more than 100 pixels. Pixels belonging to the MODIS Mosaic 
category (mixed of LU; grass, crop, shrub, savannah, forest) are not investigated. 
 
An additional aspect of the analysis was to (a) split the study region in three zones with similar annual 
precipitation within each of the zones and perform the same procedure on these three individual areas (b) 
investigate whether the precipitation is different among the investigated LUs. This will help to make sure 
that when we compare changes in ET among different LU the rainfall is not introducing an additional error 
as ET is very dependent on mean annual precipitation (as seen from figure 4.3a and 4.3b). It is also 
interesting to assess the relationships between precipitation and ET for the different LU. This is assessed by: 
 
i: performing a regression analysis to examine the relation between precipitation and ET for the 
different LUs.      
 
ii: Extracting the average precipitation for investigated LUs.  
Precipitation data from iridl.ldeo.columbia.edu (2015) was used. The results for the whole BSh, and divided 
in the three precipitation zones, were then compared and evaluated how much the results changes due to 
the further homogenization of the precipitation of the LUs. 
The regression analysis was performed to investigate how much the value of ET could be predicted from 
just precipitation. Based on Figure 4.3, it can be argued that both a logarithmic and a linear relation should 
be tried for the precipitation pattern in BSh. Although these relations are based upon the basin level, they 
are based upon basins that are fully covered by forest and grass, and it is accepted to extent the trends for 
LUs in general.  
==PAGE:32==
Side 32 af 87 
 
Precipitation for the different LU in BSh are calculated as described for ET. Then runoff is found from 
subtraction.  
In order to minimize the precipitation differences between the LU inside each of the three zones, the 
extreme high and low precipitation values for BSh were removed before the three zones was created. One 
possibility is to split according to quantiles and remove a certain quantile of high and low values. This was 
attempted in a preliminary investigation, but was not suited because of an uneven distribution with more 
extreme high precipitation values than low precipitation values. Therefore, the extreme values were 
instead manually excluded, before BSh was split into the three zones of precipitation level.  
The same procedure was then followed for extraction of precipitation and ET as in the analysis of the entire 
BSh.  
Comparison of MODIS-ET with Zhang 
To compare the results from MODIS-ET with Zhang, the MODIS-ET values were extracted also for the same 
basin as Zhang. This is performed to compare and attempt to validate the results of the two different 
methods. It is only done for the transition from savannah to cropland. 
The analysis of the cropland expansion consequence on ET is performed in two ways. First, the average ET 
for the 12 year period is found within the southern basin. Using 12 year data should increase precision of 
the transition consequence. Secondly, the ET data from 2012 are used in order to compare the ET levels 
found in Zhang with the level from MODIS-ET. The reason for this is that the input to the Zhang model is 
2012 data. The mean ET estimates for different land uses between MODIS-ET and Zhang is therefore best 
compared using only 2012 data. Thus, the two methods serve different purposes.

Cregional average= Ʃ δ·Cvegetation

expansion consequence in terms of Runoff (mm/y) is multiplied by the WSI value identified for the basin 
where the expansion occurs.  This will express the cropland expansion impact on water resource depletion.. 
Calculating the increased impact on water resources will only be relevant in case cropland expansion results 
in ET increases.

Since there at present is no way of incorporating effect of increased water flow into LCA, there cannot be 
made an impact assessment of this scenario. 
4.3.2 Impact assessment, MODIS-ET 
As in the case for the modelling with the Zhang equation, an impact assessment with the WSI is made. 
However the application of the index is quite different from the Zhang approach. Since the study region in 
MODIS-ET is outlined from climate data, the zones will not fit together. This means that the BSh region will 
span over many WSI values. If this method was analyzing historical changes, this would not be a problem 
because then those changes that happened within the respective WSI zone could be weighted with this 
value, while changes in other areas would be weighted according to their WSI value. However, since this 
method is not modelling historical changes, the changes are not in their exact position within the 
==PAGE:41==
Side 41 af 87 
 
delineated zone, but rather “somewhere within” this zone that is established to be homogeneous. Thus, it 
is not known which change that takes place within which WSI zone. 
The way to solve the problem is to delineate new zones of interest, based on the WSI, within the area that 
was analysed with MODIS-ET. This means that every part of the BSh region that has a distinct WSI value will 
get attributed a WSI weighted water amount per ha of cropland expansion. Thus, the change in ET that is 
estimated for cropland will be valid for all BSh, or the three smaller zones, but the impact of this change will 
be according to the WSI zone.  
Similar to the Zhang approach, there cannot be made an impact assessment for increases in water flow. 
 
4.4 Carbon impact assessment  
After the carbon stock datasets are combined to show the total carbon emission due to cropland expansion 
for the whole world, it needs to be taken into account how this would be used in an LCA analysis. A 
transformation is performed to convert the Δ carbon stock from cropland expansion given in t C/ha into t C 
· ha-1·y-1 of cropland occupation. This end unit will allow the user of the LCA system to calculate the impact 
of the cropland system on a yearly basis.  
In order to do this transformation, the timing of the emissions is important. In this study, it is assumed that 
all carbon stock loss is released in the year of the clearing and that none of it is incorporated into timber 
products. This is a simplification because not all of the carbon is released in the initial burning. However, 
based on calculations shown in appendix 1, it is shown that the difference between calculating the exact 
timing of all the emission in a similar way to the results found by Fearnside et al. (2009)15 compared to 
assuming that all the emissions take place one year earlier was very small16. Kløverpris & Mueller (2013) 
also states that since the time shift is the same the actual time of the emissions does not need to be taken 
into account since the difference will be small. Thus, the simplified approach is used in this report and it is 
assumed that all the emissions happen instantly.  
To calculate the annual carbon impact from cropland expansion, the methodology from Schmidt et al. 
(2012) is used. This means that the effect of cropland expansion is equal to that the emissions occurs one 
year earlier than they would otherwise. This can be visualized by moving the graph on figure 4.13 one year 
to the left. GWP is investigated over a 100 year period (GWP100).

The problem is only for East Europe and Rest of Europe since United States and rest of North America has 
no actual cropland expansion. FAO (2010) found that 0,5 % of the total world expansion occurred in the 
two European regions. The global CEF will be multiplied with 1,005 to account for the European Regions as 
well, assuming that it is roughly corresponding to the average CEF in all the other regions combined. 
The assessment of the carbon impact with agricultural expansion data from Willaarts et al. (2014) will only 
be calculated for the four individual CEFs – for shrub and forest in Central and South America. The result 
will be compared with the numbers from both Tonini et al. (2015) and the world carbon modelling carried 
out in this study.

different layer files imported into the program. These layers files are laid on top of each other and can be

4.5.1 Statistical considerations 
In remote sensing investigations, results differ from other investigations, such as forest inventories, due to 
the scale of the investigation. Many of the results of this study will only be generated as a mean17. More 
precisely this is the spatial means for the different LUs. The reason for this is that the means can be 
assumed to be the “real means” (Garcia 2015, personal communication). If computing the standard error 
for the mean, this will be tiny because there are so many pixels that constitute the mean and sample size is 
therefore huge. In other words, sample mean is approaching population mean.  
For the temporal variation, standard error, standard deviation and confidence interval are calculated. Here 
sample size is not very large – 2001-2012 represents a sample size of 12. A general level of significance was 
chosen at 5 %.

5 Limitations 
It will not be considered whether the grown agricultural products will result in CO2 savings. It will therefore 
not be included whether these agricultural products could replace fossil fuels and in this way contribute to 
mitigating climate change. This has been researched in other articles such as Searchinger et al. (2008). 
The only global warming impact that will be considered is the carbon emission from the cropland 
expansion. This means that the release of other greenhouse gases will not be covered in this report. The 
same is the case regarding changes in surface albedo due to LUC. 
The main focus of this report is expansion of cropland. However, much of the work and conclusions in this 
report is applicable to pasture expansion as well.  
This study will focus on rain-feed cropland expansion. The reasons for this is that only a very small part of 
the total cropland expansion in Africa is found to be composed of irrigated cropland. The MODIS maps does 
not distinguish between rain-feed and irrigated cropland (Alexandratos & Bruinsma 2012).

7 Discussion 
A major difference between the results from the water and the carbon is the scale of the investigation. 
LUC effects on Carbon has been investigated on a large continental scale, while the effects on water 
has been investigated on a smaller scale. The background for this is first of all that the effect on water 
is primarily a local effect while the effect on carbon is a global issue. Secondly, the carbon results are 
being compared to another study that used this level of investigation. The water analysis is being 
compared to a different method carried out in the same study. Also, water is based on potential 
cropland expansion while carbon is based on actual expansion data.  
 
7.1 Analysis water fluxes 
7.1.1 Zhang 
Results from the application of Zhangs method 
When applying Zhang to the southern basin, we found a transition consequence from savannah to cropland 
of 31,1 mm/y lower mean ET and 16,3 mm/y higher mean runoff for cropland. Thus, conversion from 
savannah to cropland was not found to have a water depleting effect.  
A clear positive aspect about the Zhang modelling is that you get the direct change in ET from the cropland 
expansion. Precipitation differences do not have to be taken into account, and the effect per expanded ha 
can be found directly and used in LCA. 
However, it has to be remembered that this positive aspect is due to the performed method of modelling. 
The expansion scenario has been performed with 20 % more cropland, assuming that these 20% cropland 
will have the same properties as existing cropland – which means also having the same precipitation. 
However, this will probably not be the case since cropland is expanded on savannah, and savannah seems 
to have a higher average precipitation than cropland in the basin. This is according to table 6.4 from Zhang, 
and a higher precipitation estimate for savannah is therefore inserted into the model. Thus, this 
assumption represents an uncertainty in the modelling. This uncertainty will be even greater if a bigger 
basin is chosen. It has to be remembered that the precipitations differences are not significant from table 
6.16 of precipitation zone 3, which is the main zone that the southern basin is situated in. The southern 
basin chosen in Zhang had a rather narrow shape from south to north. Thus, there might be precipitation 
differences inside the basin which also seems to be the case from the figure in appendix 2 (although it 
spans less than the 166 mm interval of each precipitation zone). Just because precipitation differences 
were not evident between land uses in precipitation zone 3, it does not mean that we can be sure that it is 
not the case for only the basin. 
The above mentioned observation shows that caution must be exerted when using Zhang to estimate the 
consequences of cropland expansion.   
The inputs to Zhang is only based on one-year data. More year should have been used instead to have 
more precision of the estimates. The same time frame as the one in MODIS-ET should have been used 
which would also have made the two method easier to compare. It was not possible to redo the calculation 
for the 12 year period in the time available for this project. 
The reason why the locally calibrated w for the northern basin turned out to be negative is the relationship 
between ET, PET and precipitation. If PET is very high and precipitation and ET are rather low, then 
==PAGE:69==
Side 69 af 87 
 
equation (4.2) (Or rather the numerator) will turn out negative. The reason why the problem is especially 
large on the local scale could be that BSh in Sahel lies close to the equator with a very high PET. Thus, it 
could seem as if PET should not be added to the Zhang equation as a variable in high solar radiation areas. 
Apparently, the mistake performed in the method carried out in this survey is that the original constant 
values for PET from Zhang should have been used. On the other hand, constant PET values were only 
available for forest and grass and not the other LUs. It would have been necessary to guess PET for several 
LUs. The constant value for PET that is used in Zhang et al. (2001) for grass and forest is different because 
of differences in albedo and aerodynamic resistance. It could have been tried to estimate this for the 
remaining LUs. This would imply giving an ecosystem such as savannah a value between 1100mm/y and 
1400mm/y. While this could have been performed by simple assuming a linear relationship (savannah 
being comprised of grass and trees), it would have been harder for crops and shrubs (since not composed 
of grass and trees). Thus, the problem would have been the same as in the conducted analysis anyway, but 
with PET instead of w. Furthermore, the approach with having PET as a variable is used in Willaarts (2012). 
Willaarts (2012) succeeded in calculating w from the different inputs (PET, ET and precipitation). Thus, the 
applied approach is judged acceptable. 
To attribute a value of w to savannah and woody savannah we assumed that there is a linear relationship 
between the amount of trees and the ET of an area. This may be a too simplified approach, but the 
equation from Zhang et al. (2001) is based on this particular linear relation anyway. This linear relation is 
debated scientifically and not all studies have found this (Sahin and Hall 1996).   
The mosaic category is included as having w=0,5 because it is assumed to be mainly a combination of 
cropland and grass. From looking at Figure 5.1, ET for mosaic might seem a bit higher than for both 
cropland and grass, though it has not been tested statistically. It has been not tested since the mosaic 
category is less relevant in our investigation. The assumption of including mosaic as having w=0,5 does not 
seem to violate the calculations. 
Willaarts presented a value for shrubland of w=0,7 which was for a Spanish environment and therefore not 
necessarily a better value than w=0,5 used in our study.   
  
7.1.2 MODIS-ET  
Evaluating the effect of cropland expansion with MODIS-ET we found significantly lower ET on cropland 
compared to savannah in precipitation zone 1 (p=0,005). Testing the difference in terms of runoff also 
yielded statistical significantly difference (p=0,03), runoff being higher for cropland. It seems as if the 
differences in ET might be more pronounced compared to testing runoff. Thus, cropland expansion does 
not here seem to have a water depleting effect. 
When carrying out MODIS-ET it is critical that the main source of variability is due to LUCs, therefore the 
pixels are homogenized as much as possible. This is done both in terms of selecting the overall area, the 
BSh region, but also by splitting up this rather homogeneous area into three parts. However, these parts 
could still encompasses precipitation differences (potentially up to 166 mm within each part). It has to be 
remembered that the precipitation is generally declining from South to North in BSh.  None of the LU 
precipitations was found to be significantly different from cropland precipitation. Thus, the chosen 
approach should be acceptable for evaluating the consequences of cropland expansion in the three 
precipitation zones. 
==PAGE:70==
Side 70 af 87 
 
Another approach would have been necessary if precipitation differences had been found. This would not 
be possible before more accurate historical LULC map are available to apply the MOD16 ET to assess pixel 
based changes. Then the only source of change is LU (accounting for climatic variability). 
When the three precipitation zone graphs are compared, savannah ET is dependent on precipitation 
differently from the other categories. ET for savannah is more constant from the dry to the wet zone than 
the other LUs. Thus, savannah exhibits a low spatial variation. In terms of temporal variation, the situation 
is the opposite. Savannah is the LU that has the highest annual variability in ET in all three precipitation 
zones. Thus, in years where there are more than average rain, savannah adapt relatively more to this and 
increases ET. In years with less rain than average, ET declines relative more. However, being situated in an 
environment with generally larger precipitation does not affect savannah ET much. As seen from figure 4.3, 
trees exposed to higher precipitation should increase ET relatively more than grass. The reason why 
savannah in areas with larger precipitation do no exhibit higher ET is not clear. Assuming that the MOD16 
product is reliable, this could be related with different grazing and management pressures in wetter areas. 
Savannah has a higher ET than woody savannah in the three precipitation zones. As mentioned, it is well 
established in literature that increasing the amount of trees increase ET. It is especially striking because 
woody savannah has a lower precipitation in all zones than savannah, but a higher runoff. There is no 
logical explanation for this. The reason could be that there is generally not a lot of woody savannah in the 
area, which is also the reason why it is not even included in precipitation zone 1. Thus, the estimates for 
woody savannah are attached with more uncertainty than the other LUs. This also suggests the need to 
validate the MOD16 product with field data. 
A possible reason why cropland ET is so much more explained by precipitation than for the other LUs 
could be that cropland is the only LU that is not natural vegetation and therefore is less adapted to 
Sahelean conditions (Paruelo et al. 1999). The other LUs, except from pasture grassland, are bound to 
be where they have developed naturally, while cropland can be introduced to wherever it suits 
humans. This could mean that cropland inhabits a broader and more mixed environment, possibly 
giving a more pronounced regression trend.  
It is apparent that grassland and cropland are significantly different when looking at the entire BSh, but 
not when looking at the individual regions. The reason for this could be that the majority of grassland 
are located in the dryer zones of BSh, while cropland will mostly be placed in wetter zones. When it is 
not possible to cultivate successfully, there is more cattle and grazing. So, the land use is linked to the 
precipitation level. When splitting up the dataset, the dry grassland is only compared to the dry 
cropland.  
PET is included as a variable in the Zhang modelling instead of the constant value from the original 
Zhang et al. (2001). PET is not used as a variable in MODIS-ET because of the conclusions from Zhang et 
al. (2001) that PET is not very important for ET. It is more complicated to take the variable PET into 
account in MODIS-ET, since the modelling performed on the precipitation and the ET would have to be 
redone for PET as well. Secondly, the differences in PET are assumed to be small because the region of 
BSh extent so few latitudinal degrees closer to the equator. This would especially be the case after 
having been split into three zones of mean annual precipitation. Results on PET (Shown in appendix 3) 
generally confirms this. 
 
==PAGE:71==
Side 71 af 87 
 
Comparison 
It is reasonable to compare the methods (Zhang and MODIS-ET) in terms of their ET estimates. Two issues 
should be discussed when comparing the results from the two methods. First, the ET for the LUs in 
each of the two method. Secondly, transition values. 
The ET estimates from MODIS-ET are much lower, about 300 mm/y, compared to Zhang for the 
southern basin (using table 6.18b). As a results, MODIS-ET ET estimates are only 56,6 % (for short 
vegetation) and 59,4 % (for savannah) of the ET estimate from Zhang. This seemed to correspond with the 
findings from Garcia et al. (2012) and Ramoelo et al. (2014). 
This highly questions the sources stating that MODIS overestimates ET in Sahel. The reason why the 
Zhang ET values are so high is because PET is so high, as already discussed. If the constant PET values 
from Zhang et al. (2001) had been used, this means that the ET would have been substantially lower.  
However, the transition values in Zhang for savannah to short vegetation and MODIS-ET savannah to 
cropland in the southern basin are found to be acceptably close to each other (using table 6.18a). 
Zhang equation estimated that transferring 1 hectare of savannah to short vegetation, incl. cropland, 
means a decrease in mean ET of 31,0 mm, while MODIS-ET estimates the decrease in mean ET for 2001-
2012 to be 26.9  mm/y for savannah to cropland and 23,6 mm/y for savannah to short vegetation. Thus, 
when looking at the transition values, the puzzling difference in mean ET between Zhang and MODIS-ET 
seems to be eliminated. A systematic error can be suspected. The reason for this seems to be that the PET 
is very high for the LU in the Zhang modelling, resulting in high ET. This makes the ET of the LU more similar 
(because they will all be close to the precipitation). This smaller difference means that the relations end up 
being the same.  
Since Sahel is a dry area, it makes sense that the ET is as high as found from Zhang. The plants will use as 
much of the water as possible. Thus, ET modelled from Zhang could be more reliable than MODIS. 
A consequence of the Zhang modelling is that there is only a mean value for the transition since 
standard error is tiny. As mentioned in the theory the reason is that sample mean is approaching 
population mean. However, this makes comparison to other studies harder. In a different framework, 
there could have been produced a temporal analysis of the basin using MODIS-ET and Zhang. This 
would allow comparison by having 12 temporal means for each method. This would have been similar 
to the approach for MODIS-ET in the three precipitation zones. The comparison of the two method can 
therefore not determine whether they are significantly different.  
Having carried out the results, we investigated whether the modelling assumptions from Zhang seemed 
valid. It was assumed that grass, shrubland and cropland would have similar ET. According to the results 
from MODIS-ET, we found grass ET to be consistently, but not significantly, lower than open shrub and 
cropland within each of the three precipitation zones. It was also found that cropland is never significantly 
different from grass and open shrub within each precipitation zone. However, Cropland and grassland is 
significantly different in the region of BSh as a whole (p=0,043). The reason for this seems to be that grass 
in located in drier areas than crop. This highlights the need for splitting up the entire BSh into three 
precipitation zones. Thus, our assumptions from Zhang that grass cropland and shrubland would have 
similar ET seems to be valid. 
==PAGE:72==
Side 72 af 87 
 
To see how big the difference is between the calculation with only cropland and with all the categories that 
Zhang uses as short vegetation, this calculation was performed (table 6.18). This makes the transition value 
smaller because as mentioned mosaic has a higher ET than shrub, grass and crop.  
Investigating the effect of cropland expansion in a BSh climate is harder than in other environments. This is 
due to that there is no forest present the region. Thus, the investigated transitions will have a smaller effect 
in terms of mm, making it harder to get significant tests. It is also harder because the plants should use 
most of the available water. This is not the case from looking at the ET graphs though. Even though the 
investigated differences in ET and runoff are smaller, the impact of these differences will be highly 
dependent on the area in Sahel as only some of the areas has a high WSI value.  
An important aspect in relation to the water modelling is whether/how the conclusions for the region of 
BSh can be extended to other climatic regions. To judge this, it is observed how the pattern between 
cropland ET and the ET of the other LUs changes over the 3 zone precipitation gradients. The only clear 
difference is the already mentioned observation that savannah ET is much higher compared to cropland ET 
in the driest zone. Interestingly, this is the opposite of what was found in Zhang et al. (2001) where it is 
found that the difference is especially big in high rainfall areas. This somewhat questions the results from 
this analysis.  
A reason that could explain this is that the position of the savannah might be relatively different dependent 
on which climatic zone that is investigated. Savannah could be found spread out all over the regions in 
precipitation zone 2 and 3, but only being found in the southernmost part of precipitation zone 1 (the least 
dry are of precipitation zone 1). This is also what the precipitation results seem to indicate. 
Expanding the findings from the BSh region to other climates is problematic since there is not seen a 
specific trend in the relationship according to which of the precipitation zones that is looked at. It could 
seem as if the consequences are more severe in precipitation zone 1, but this is not the case for grassland.   
When the LUC results in a lower ET than the cleared vegetation, the results of the WSI are not meaningful. 
However as mentioned in the background, there is simply no available indicator that can be used to 
address the impact of the increase in run off. Since the basic Zhang equation can never result in an increase 
in ET from expansion of short vegetation (since cropland belongs to the categories that has the lowest w), 
this means that the WSI and basic Zhang approach is not really compatible with each other. Since no 
transition in MODIS-ET was found to significantly reduce runoff and increase ET within the three 
precipitation zones is was also not relevant to apply to WSI to this method. 
It is important to remember that aside from differences in how much water LU systems use, there is also a 
difference in terms of how the LU affects the water quality. This has not been addressed at in this survey. 
As mentioned in the background chapter, agriculture generally lower the quality of water. While this may 
seem out of the topic in the discussion of this study, quality and quantity should be seen as connected as 
was mentioned earlier referring to Pfister et al. (2009). Taking this into account could change the 
conclusion drawn for water use of cropland relative to other LU systems.

7.2 Carbon fluxes 
Discussion of results 
The CEFs found for the different regions are quite different from the CEF found by Tonini et al. (2015). The 
estimates from our study are lower with no exception. This is surprising considering that soil carbon is only 
taken into account for peatland in Tonini et al. (2015). In our study, carbon stock emissions are based on 
high resolution mapped data with information about the carbon stock loss for every pixel, which is then 
averaged for two major categories, shrubland and forest. The average emission per ha is then estimated 
accordingly to the share coming from shrubland and from forest for that respective region.  
Tonini et al. (2015) had the same approach but did not use a carbon stock map. Instead, Tonini et al. (2015) 
used results based on averages for the different biomes in each region to find an overall emission factor. 
However, the amount of deforestation occurring in each biome was only estimated based on the relative 
share of that biome within the region – not actual deforestation data for that specific region for that 
specific biome. Another difference is that Tonini et al. (2015) used growth of the ecosystem, not carbon 
stock to calculate CEF.  
The main difference between the studies, apart from that our study includes soil carbon and doesn’t cover 
all land surfaces, is therefore that in Tonini et al. (2015) there is used an emission factor for every biome, 
which is then added up according to the total deforestation and the proportion of the respective biome 
within the region. In that sense, the results from our study should be more precise, at least for the regions 
where all land surfaces could be mapped. 
However, both investigation are suffering from the same basic issue in two different forms. The problem is 
either the assumption that deforestation of a certain type of biome will happen according to its share of 
the LU in a region (Tonini et al. 2015); or that the CO2 emission from deforestation is found by averaging 
the carbon stock for a certain biome type (forest or shrub)(this study). In this study, it means that included 
in finding the average carbon there are many areas which have not actually experienced cropland 
expansion in this period. 
The reason why the world carbon modelling values from our study are lower than in Tonini et al. (2015) 
seems to be that our study used the stock, while Tonini et al. (2015) used the growth of the biome. The 
elaboration of results from IPCC (2006) was also calculated with carbon stock. This seems to be the reason 
why IPCC (2006) and our results correspond rather well. 
The optimal solution would be to have precise LU maps for the whole world, so only the carbon stock of the 
areas that were actually experiencing change, would be taking into account. As stated from the beginning, 
such maps do not exist today, at least not available for research.     
If comparing the regional carbon impact results from Tonini et al. 2015 and this study with the impact from 
the actual expansion in South America, the impact from Tonini et al. (2015) is much higher. This further 
strengthen the conclusion that the reason why Tonini et al. (2015) is much higher is because it is based on  
biome growth.  
 
For Central America there seems to be a difference between the data indicated by Tonini et al. (2015) and 
the results from our world carbon modelling. This can be seen because MCD12C1 from 2001 only identified 
==PAGE:74==

a very little area as being shrubland (60 pixels) compared to forest (8126 pixels). Considering that only 0,73 
% of the area is shrubland based on the Modis map, it seems strange that it constitutes 4 % of 
deforestation. On the other hand, the resolution of MCD12C1 is coarse and has general issues as well as 
mentioned earlier in this report.  
There are only 135 pixels of shrubland in central America that are situated in areas of agricultural 
expansion, and these have been resampled from a LU map of a much bigger resolution. The shrubland 
results for Central America are therefore not reliable. 
The carbon stock estimate for forest in South America that experienced agricultural expansion from 1993 to 
2009 was found to be lower than the regional average for our world carbon modelling forest average in 
South America. The reason for this difference is that a lot of the high carbon density forest is situated far 
into the Amazon forest. These areas of forest are still quite untouched by LUC. However, when making the 
calculation on a continental basis, these areas of course count as well. This means that the forest category 
has its carbon stock emissions raised although it is not relevant for the ongoing LUC. This is an useful 
observation that contributes to the statement from Fearnside et al. (2000) that deforestation happens 
progressively at the forest edge. These deforestation edges do apparently not form in the middle of the 
forest – clearing are made at the borders with other nature types such as savannah, cropland etc.    
A main reason for this difference between the estimates using statistics and actual deforestation data is 
due to the classification of the LU data into scrubland and forest by 10 % tree cover. The implications of this 
is that savannah and forest are treated in the same category though there is a big difference in how much 
carbon the two ecosystem holds on average. If they are not in the same category, then it will not be the 
same problem that a lot of the forest is undisturbed because it will be the average that is important and 
this could probably be assumed to be quite similar for the forest areas that have experienced change and 
the total forest area. There is given a certain percentage of how much of the new agricultural land that 
comes from shrubland and forest, but there is no further division in this. If the separation had instead been 
divided into scrubland (10 %> tree cover) savannah (e.g. 10-45% tree cover) and forest (45 %< tree cover) 
then this classification could have captured the carbon stock much better. 
Comparing the regional average world carbon emission with the results elaborated from IPCC (2006), there 
is no consistency in the relationship between the results from the two studies. For two regions, all carbon 
stocks are lower than in the IPCC (2006) (Central America and South America) and for the other 4 regions 
they are either similar or higher. Regrettably, soil carbon is not included in results from IPCC (2006), making 
it hard to draw final conclusions from this. However, the comparison still yields important information.  
It is disturbing that very different conclusions are drawn from comparing the world carbon modelling 
emission with IPCC (2006) estimates and the world carbon modelling impact with the data from Tonini et 
al. (2015). It is important to remember that Tonini et al. (2015) is based on IPCC (2006) estimates as well. 
However, Tonini et al. (2015) used the carbon emission estimates based on biome growth. The results from 
IPCC (2006) used in our study is based on the carbon stock estimates – not growth. The carbon impact from 
this study is relatively much lower to the carbon impact from Tonini et al. (2015), while the carbon emission 
correspond more with IPCC (2006) estimates. This seems to question how exact the stock and growth of 
the ecosystem have been calculated in IPCC (2006). The consequences of this is that LCA studies that have 
chosen to use IPCC (2006) data will find different impact dependent on whether they have used growth or 
carbon stock as indicators of carbon emissions from cropland expansion.

Discussion of method 
As stated previous, the MODIS maps, especially MCD12C1, should not be used for pixel based change 
detection. Based on this, it should be reasonable to use the 2001 MODIS LU map as input for the modelling 
of the effect of the changes between 1993 and 2009 (input is what the MODIS LULC maps are supposed to 
be used for). However, there would still be the risk that some of the pixels that is stated to have undergone 
LUC from 1993 to 2009 have already undergone their change on the 2001 map. 
In that case, the effect of the change is evaluated for areas that have already undergone the change and 
therefore have a different amount of carbon than the ecosystem that previously occupied the area.  
It is to be remembered that this will only be a problem if the pixel belongs to the LU class of grassland. In 
that case, there is a risk that the pixel between 1993 to 2001 has changed from its previous state into 
grassland. It is not a problem for cropland since cropland is not included in the LU classes constituting 
shrubland according to Gibbs et al. (2010). It also has to be remembered that there is no problem for the 
forest carbon stock analysis.  
The problem is constituted of two issues: first, that it is unknown whether the grassland on the LU map is 
from natural grassland or pasture. Since it can be assumed that these pastures and natural grasslands have 
similar properties, the assessment should not have too big consequences. Secondly, the consequence is 
that for the assessment of the carbon stock in shrubland, the distribution of the categories could be 
altered. The understanding of this is that due to pastural expansion, less percentage of the Gibbs et al. 
(2010) shrubland category is composed of MCD12C1 shrubland. This is confusing but it has to be 
remembered that the shrubland category use by Gibbs et al. (2010) is composed of the MODIS LU classes: 
open shrubland, closed shrubland and grasslands on MCD12C1. Since these probably do not have the same 
carbon stock, the distribution is important.  
It is assumed that the cropland expansion and pasture expansion will have the same distribution and as a 
result occur on areas containing the same average carbon stock. This assumption can however be debated 
(Grasser et al. (2015)). An important aspect to consider is that the two surveys are less easy to compare 
than it would apparently seem. The best example is that a lot of the cropland expansion takes place on 
pastureland. This is not taken into account in Willaarts et al. (2014)21 since cropland expansion on pasture is 
not agricultural expansion, because the land was occupied by agriculture in the first place.  
In the carbon analysis, the MODIS LU map and the total carbon map has a very different spatial resolution. 
This means that when they are combined, ArcGIS attributes the nearest pixels to each other. It also means 
the program adopts the resolution of the dataset with the higher cell size, in this case the MCD12C1. This is 
not the best solution for this. Preferably the cell size in the MODIS LU map should have been changed to 
the “finer” cell size of the carbon maps (the resolution was the same for the soil carbon and vegetation 
carbon). When the tool “Combine” is used, it means that there is used a “nearest neighbor” resampling 
operation. This means that the pixel that has center closest to the center of the new pixel is being used as 
the new pixel. Further more the value is truncated down to the nearest number (e.g. 17,9 is turned into 
17). While the last issue results in a slight underestimation of the carbon stock, which shouldn’t be too big 
of a problem, the first issue could seem to be of major concern.

 Normally it would not be a problem but in this case, there is a large difference in spatial resolution 
between the LU (Cellsize=5560 m) and the carbon stock map (cell size= 1035). This means that out of the 
approximately 6 x 6 =36 carbon pixels that constitute one pixel in the new combined map, only one of 
these are used. This means that only about 3-4 % of the information in the carbon stock dataset is actually 
used! This is therefore an error. If there had been enough time, then the modelling of the carbon should 
have been redone but there was no time for this in this project, since the issue was discovered too late in 
the process.  
For the South American Continent, values without this interpolation method was calculated. This 
calculation can be seen in appendix 4. These calculation seem to indicate that this is actually not a major 
issue, since the difference is very small (about 0,01 t CO2 eq/ ha). Due to that the regions are quite large, it 
could seem like differences are averaged out because so many pixels are investigated. It can be argued that 
had a smaller continent been chosen for comparison, the difference would maybe have been bigger. 
 In summary, it seems like this issue was not a major problem for the analysis since the CO2 eq emission 
doesn’t increase substantially (increase in forest emission but decrease in shrubland emission). This 
questions whether the results actually are more precise than the results from Tonini et al. (2015). 
It can be discussed whether it is realistic to assume that all carbon is emitted from the belowground 
biomass when clearing an area. As mentioned in the introduction, this is not a subject that has been 
researched intensively. However, the largest part of the root biomass can be assumed to be within 30 cm of 
the surface (Husch et al. 2001, cited from IPPC 1999). This implies that the far majority will be within the 
depth, which is assumed to be affected by cultivation.   
Former studies do not fully agree on how to incorporate soil carbon in modelling the effects of cropland 
expansion. The problem is that the used soil map was divided into 0-30 cm and 30-100 cm, and this was not 
the investigated intervals used by all studies. The assumption that cropland expansion results in emissions 
from 25% of the top and 25 % of the bottom soil carbon, may have been a too simplified approach. It was 
chosen to apply the used method even though it can be assumed that the effect of cultivation will be 
greater in the top part of the soil. Emission of 25 % of the total soil carbon has been found to be reasonable 
in articles and it has been used in many other surveys as well (such as Searchinger et al. 2008). 
Wood product incorporation is not included, since it is only assumed relevant for a very small portion of the 
cleared timber. If incorporation occurs, then the carbon emissions are overstated. Since it seems impossible 
that none of the wood is used in this matter, e.g. being used locally as building material, there should be a 
small overstating of the emissions.   
Other situations that lead to overstating of carbon emissions are when some of the expanded cropland is 
used to produce biofuels, which could replace fossil fuels. This will further depend upon the growth of the 
expanded crop. 
Since not all the biomass is burned, some of the emissions will not occur in year one. As calculated in 
Appendix 1 this effect was deemed very small. This timing is also valid for soils. In this study, the emissions 
from soil carbon were assumed to happen at the same time, as the emission from the plant biomass 
(assumed to be the time of the clearing), which is not entirely the case. Not taking this into account results 
in a slight overstating of carbon emissions. 
Cropland expansion results in emissions of other greenhouse gases apart from CO2. When biomass is 
burned, there is also released CH4 and N2O. When biomass decays, there is released CH4 (Fearnside et al. 
2009). According to Tonini et al. (2015) there will also be released NOx. This means that the greenhouse gas 
==PAGE:77==
Side 77 af 87 
 
emissions from cropland expansion are higher than indicated in this report. As has been stated, there are 
several aspects of greenhouse emissions that are not being covered by our study. The total implication of 
this is not possible to state. 
The modelling of changing carbon stock emission from cropland expansion could have been done in a 
different way. This is both in terms of which combination of depth of soil and percentage that was used, 
but also in which way the modelling was done. An alternative approach could be only to consider the 
production (growth) of the ecosystem. This is the approach used by Tonini et al. (2015). The method from 
Searchinger et al. (2008) could also have been used, but this method had the previously mentioned 
problematic assumptions. One of the assumptions with the baseline time accounting method used in this 
study is that it is assumed that one extra unit of expansion will not change the total expansion – only the 
time before it is reached. This is why cropland expansion can be modelled as not causing more carbon 
emissions, but only making them occur before. The rationale behind this is that protection policy will 
ensure that there will be a minimum area of natural ecosystems left. If this assumption is non-valid then 
the method will underestimate the carbon emissions from LUC (Schmidt et al. 2012).   
The impact from soil carbon emissions and biomass carbon emissions are treated collectively in this study. 
However, the emission differs from each other in the sense that the amount of soil carbon that is emitted is 
dependent on the years of cultivation. This is in contrast to the biomass carbon, which is cleared to make 
room for cultivation and which is burned/decayed no matter whether the areas is actually used for 
cropland occupation. 
The choice of accounting period for the global warming potential has a big influence on the impact of the 
cropland expansion. If a longer period is chosen, e.g. GWP 500, this means that emissions from the 
expansion is lowered. The reason for this is that the effect of one more year of warming is smaller when 
that last year has a smaller fraction left of the CO2 pulse.  
Another side effect of LUC that hasn’t been taken into account is the warming potential of 
evapotranspirated water. Since water vapor is a greenhouse gas, plant transpiration also results in the 
emission of greenhouse gases. Since the cropland ecosystem replacing the original ecosystem will typically 
have a lower ET, it means that this will lower the global warming potential of the LUC.

8 Conclusion 
The purpose of this study was to calculate the impacts of cropland expansion on carbon emissions and 
water resources and compare methodologies for estimating this. Other land use changes and other impacts 
to global warming were not considered. 
The effect on water resources was evaluated using hypothetical cropland expansion scenarios for the BSh 
climate in Sahel, Africa. Using the Zhang method, on a basin level, we found that savannah to cropland 
(short vegetation) transitions would results in 31 mm annual decrease in ET. Using MODIS-ET on the same 
basin as Zhang was applied to, the transition from savannah to cropland was found to decrease annual 
mean ET by approximately 30 mm. The correlation was found even though MODIS-ET consistently showed 
ET  less than 60% of ET derived using Zhang.  Using MODIS-ET in the region of BSh , cropland expansion did 
not have a significant water depleting effect. No transition significantly increased evapotranspiration or 
decreased runoff.  Transition from Savannah to cropland in the driest zone yielded significantly higher 
runoff (104 mm/y, p=0,03) and lower ET (143,1mm/y, p=0,005). The transition was not significant in the 
other investigated zones. Since no transitions to cropland significantly increased ET or decreased runoff, 
the WSI was not applied. There is at present no framework for including the effect of increased runoff into 
LCA. Thus, the impact of this effect could not be evaluated. The conclusion from the BSh is not easily 
broadened out to other climatic zones since there was no consistent trend for the three investigated zones 
with a gradual change in climate.  
Using deforestation statistics, the global average regional carbon emission from cropland expansion was 
calculated to range from 19,8 to 186,7 t C·ha-1 dependent on the region and the biome converted to 
cropland. The average carbon emission for South American forest was 119,0 t C·ha-1. This carbon emission 
estimate was twice as high as the estimate derived using actual agricultural expansion data (60,3 t C·ha-1). 
The reason is the agricultural expansion pattern in South America. The largest stock of carbon in South 
America is in the Amazon. However the majority of the cropland expansion took place outside of the 
Amazon. Both these calculations were performed using biomass stock and spatial carbon data. Comparing 
these calculations to methods, using mean regional IPCC estimates on biomass stock emission from 
cropland expansion, yielded comparable results. The calculated carbon emission was converted to CEF to 
estimate the annual impact of cropland occupation. We found that using global carbon stock emissions to 
calculate global CEF on a regional level, the carbon impact ranged from 0,15 t C·ha-1 ·y-1 to 1,41 t C·ha-1 per 
expanded ha of cropland. The average global impact was 0,75 t C·ha-1 per expanded ha of cropland. The 
calculated CEFs are much lower in comparison with a study that used biomass growth instead of biomass 
stock.  
We conclude from this study that cropland expansion in a dry region in Africa do not seem to deplete water 
resources. On the other hand, there is a need for a framework to include the effect of increased runoff 
from cropland expansion into LCA. This is important for capturing the true environmental impact of crop 
production. Concerning carbon, we conclude that the conversion to an annual impact needs to be reviewed 
to provide consistent impact estimate for different biomass indicators. It is also concluded that there is a 
need to develop better global LUC maps suitable for doing pixel-based change detection of cropland since 
the presently available maps are not well suited for such analysis.

9 Perspectives 
We found decreased ET and increased runoff from cropland expansion in the driest regions examined. This 
could increase the risk of flooding. Future studies should address the inclusion of this aspect into LCA. From 
our results, water depletion is not a problem from cropland expansion in dry areas.  
Sahel is projected to experience increasing precipitation because of climate change (Dong & Sutton 2015). 
This makes it more relevant to consider flooding in the region. 
Incorporating the coupling between the local and the regional/continental effects of cropland expansion 
might affect drawn conclusion. Even though cropland expansion will increase local water, it might still 
increase regional risk of drought. 
Cropland expansion might also have an effect on albedo – this implies increased or decreased 
reflection/absorption of incoming radiation, leading to cooling/warming depending on the land surface. 
This is an aspect our study has not focused on, but also needs to be considered. A methodology for how to 
incorporate the CO2 eq effect of changes in albedo was presented in Muños et al. (2010). 
The drawn conclusions state that using biomass stock and biomass growth estimates from IPCC (2006) 
yields different yearly impacts of cropland expansion. It is puzzling that there is such a large disagreement 
between the carbon impact derived from these two carbon inputs.   
Future research within agricultural change detection should be directed at using the improved change 
detection maps that will be available in the future. This includes both the ESA map and the Globeland 30 
map. As already mentioned, these should be available soon after the completion of this project. 
The water modelling of hypothetical cropland expansion should be continued to include the rest of the 
world. Prefeably it should therefore be done for all climatic zones in which cropland expansion have 
occurred/are expected to occur.
