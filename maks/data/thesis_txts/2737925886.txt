Abstract
This thesis investigates the development and application of a Self-tuning Adaptive Sliding Mode Control framework
designed for perturbed systems, with the goal of improving control system resilience and performance in dynamic
environments.
Traditional Sliding Mode Control (SMC) techniques, while eective in managing nonlinearities
and disturbances, often suer from reliance on xed parameters that do not adapt well to varying operational
conditions. To address this limitation, the thesis proposes an innovative approach that integrates non-homogeneous
Super Twisting Sliding Mode Control (STSMC), multiple barrier functions, and dynamic adaptation of control
gains.
The research commences with a comprehensive literature review of SMC principles and existing adaptive tech-
niques. Building upon this foundation, the thesis introduces novel algorithms for self-tuning adaptive control capable
of autonomously adjusting parameters based on real-time feedback. The stability of these algorithms is rigorously
analyzed and validated using the Lyapunov stability theorem. Extensive simulations and experimental validations
across diverse scenarios demonstrate the framework's robustness and stability. Key contributions include simplify-
ing parameter tuning with more understandable parameters and ensuring predened performance levels specied
by end users. Furthermore, the thesis acknowledges limitations and outlines future research directions in adaptive
control strategies aimed at advancing capabilities in complex and uncertain environments.
In conclusion, this thesis advances adaptive control methodologies by introducing a versatile framework that
enhances system resilience, adaptability, and performance across various operational domains. It lays a foundation
for future developments in autonomous and robust control systems.

Motivation and Background
The eld of control systems engineering has undergone signicant advancements in recent decades, particularly with
the evolution of advanced control techniques designed to manage uncertainties and disturbances inherent in dynamic
systems. Among these techniques, Sliding Mode Control (SMC) has emerged as a robust methodology capable of
addressing nonlinearities and external perturbations, thereby ensuring system stability and robust performance.
However, conventional SMC approaches often rely on xed parameters that may not suciently accommodate
the variability in system dynamics and the uncertainties encountered in real-world applications. Moreover, the
process of tuning these parameters is complex and typically requires a rigorous theoretical foundation, which can
hinder the swift deployment of these algorithms in industrial applications.
This inherent limitation has spurred researchers to explore alternative approaches, particularly self-tuning and
adaptive strategies.
These strategies aim to autonomously adjust control parameters in response to real-time
feedback and evolving system conditions. By continuously adapting to changes in the operational environment,
self-tuning adaptive control algorithms oer the potential to enhance performance, resilience, and adaptability
across diverse applications.
The pursuit of self-tuning adaptive control is motivated by the need to overcome the practical challenges asso-
ciated with xed-parameter SMC techniques. These challenges include the diculty in accurately controlling the
systems under disturbances with unknown or variable bounds and the limitations in manually adjusting parameters
to maintain optimal control performance over time.
By enabling control systems to dynamically optimize their parameters based on observed data and operational
feedback, self-tuning adaptive approaches promise to improve the reliability and eciency of control systems in
complex and dynamic environments.
This thesis aims to contribute to this evolving eld by investigating and
developing a Self-tuning Adaptive Sliding Mode Control framework.
Through theoretical analysis, algorithm development, simulation studies, and experimental validations, this
research seeks to demonstrate the eectiveness and robustness of the proposed framework. Additionally, the thesis
will discuss practical implications, potential limitations, and avenues for future research in the realm of adaptive
control strategies for dynamic systems.

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW
Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1
Context
Control systems play a critical role in managing the behavior of dynamic systems across various industrial and tech-
nological applications. SMC has emerged as a robust methodology capable of handling uncertainties, disturbances,
and nonlinearities inherent in many real-world systems [1, 2]. The core principle of SMC involves driving the system
state along a sliding surface towards a desired equilibrium, ensuring robust stability despite disturbances [1, 3, 4].
Traditional SMC techniques typically rely on xed control parameters that are designed based on a priori
knowledge of system dynamics [5, 6, 1, 3]. However, these parameters may not adapt well to varying operational
conditions, leading to suboptimal performance in practical applications [7, 8, 9]. This limitation has spurred the
development of adaptive control strategies, aiming to dynamically adjust control parameters in response to changing
system behavior.
2.2
Current state of research
In recent years, signicant research eorts have focused on enhancing the adaptability and robustness of SMC
through various adaptive techniques with higher order adaptive sliding mode [10] systematic design [11] or with new
methodologies [12]. Adaptive Sliding Mode Control (ASMC) methods aim to improve performance by automatically
adjusting control parameters based on real-time measurements or system feedback.
One prominent approach for enhanced performance and robustness is the use of STSMC, which incorporates
higher-order sliding surfaces to achieve faster convergence and reduced chattering compared to traditional SMC.
Adaptive STSMC techniques are discussed [13, 14] where dynamical gains are employed based on a Lyapunov
design to improve control performance and adaptability. Dynamic gains using an equivalent control method are
proposed by [15], providing an alternative perspective on the adaptation of control parameters dynamically. A
more complex STSMC approach is introduced by [7] with xed parameters involving four distinct parameters
aimed at enhancing robustness and performance. A dynamic gain strategy without reliance on Lyapunov designs
is explored by [16] , demonstrating an alternative approach to achieving robust control in SMC. A more intricate
algorithm positioned between the complexities [7] and [16] is proposed by [17], contributing further nuances to the
application of dynamic gain strategies in SMC. Finally, variations with self-tuning capabilities are introduced by
[18, 19], which adapt control gains dynamically based on system conditions, thereby enhancing adaptability and
robustness in challenging operational environments. STSMC has been successfully applied in diverse elds such as
robotics, power systems, and aerospace, demonstrating its eectiveness in handling complex nonlinear systems with
uncertainties [13, 20, 21].
Moreover, the concept of barrier functions has gained attention as a means to enforce constraints and improve
robustness in adaptive control frameworks. Barrier functions in the context of rst-order sliding mode control,
incorporating positive denite and semi-denite characteristics, is explored by [22, 23], contributing to improved
stability and performance under varying operating conditions. The use of barrier function is extended to STSMC
by [24, 25], employing positive denite barrier functions to enhance control robustness and constraint enforcement.
The usage of barrier-function based higher-order sliding modes is explored by [26, 27] oering enhanced control
strategies that integrate robustness and constraint management capabilities.
By incorporating barriers, controllers can maintain stability and accuracy while accommodating varying levels
of system disturbances and uncertainties [28, 29, 30]. The integration of self-tuning mechanisms into STSMC, using

2.3
Gaps and Challenges
In the current landscape of research on Adaptive STSMC (ASTSMC), several gaps and challenges are apparent.
One notable gap is the limited exploration and application of semi-denite barrier functions. While barrier functions
are integral to ensuring robustness and stability in control systems, their adaptation to STSMC frameworks remains
underexplored. Existing algorithms often lack a generalized approach and tend to be complex to tune eectively,
hindering widespread adoption in practical applications [40, 41].
Moreover, the treatment of regions outside the barrier function's domain is typically sparse.
Implementing
barrier functions eectively involves addressing these regions to maintain control performance across the entire
operational range of the system. Additionally, the discretization of barrier-function-based STSMC has not been
extensively studied or implemented, posing challenges in translating continuous-time control strategies into discrete-
time systems for digital implementation.
Furthermore, there is a notable absence of research into the use of multiple barrier functions within STSMC
frameworks. Multiple barriers could potentially enhance control performance by providing distinct control objectives
or handling multiple constraints simultaneously. Exploring these aspects could signicantly advance the applicability
and robustness of STSMC in various real-world scenarios.

5.3
Multiple Barrier Functions
The use of multiple barrier functions in discrete time control systems is pivotal for enhancing performance and
robustness, particularly in handling disturbances eectively. This approach addresses the limitations posed by the
sampling time, which can sometimes be too large to adequately manage disturbances and noises, thereby aecting
the system's stability and performance.
5.3.1
Managing "Large" Sampling Times
In discrete time systems, the interval between samples can be suciently large that disturbances push s out of the
intended region of operation, specically out of the barrier function, denoted by ѕµвИТ. When this happens, the system
may lose the benets provided by the barrier function, relying only on the outermost control algorithm to maintain
stability.

5.3.2
Concept of Multiple Barrier Functions
To mitigate this issue, multiple barrier functions are implemented. This involves using an additional, more relaxed
barrier function, denoted by ѕµ+. This relaxed barrier function serves as an intermediate constraint that helps to
maintain the system's state within a manageable range even when disturbances occur.
Inner Layer (ѕµвИТ) The primary barrier function intended to maintain s within the closest proximity to the desired
sliding manifold.
Outer Layer (ѕµ+) A secondary, more relaxed barrier function that acts when s is pushed out of the inner layer
due to disturbances or large sampling intervals.
5.3.3
Mechanism of Action
When a disturbance pushes the sliding manifold out of the rst barrier function ѕµвИТ, the system's state enters the
region governed by the outer barrier function ѕµ+. This outer barrier function then exerts a corrective inuence,
constraining the sliding manifold to this new, broader layer. Subsequently, the control algorithm works to repush
the state back inside the inner layer ѕµвИТ, thus restoring the desired operational conditions.
5.3.4
Generalization to Multiple Layers
The concept of using multiple barrier functions can be generalized to N layers, represented by the set E =
{ѕµ1, ѕµ2, ..., ѕµN}, where 0 < ѕµ1 < ѕµ2 < ... < ѕµN. Each layer provides a successive level of control, with increas-
ing relaxation.
Hence, ѕµ1 is the innermost barrier function, oering the most stringent control and ѕµN is the
outermost barrier function, providing the broadest constraint. As disturbances push the sliding manifold through
these layers, each successive barrier function constrains s within a broader, but still controlled, region. This layered
approach ensures that the system maintains stability and performance across a wide range of disturbances and
sampling times.
5.3.5
Benets of Multiple Barrier Functions
Enhanced Robustness: Multiple barrier functions provide a graduated response to disturbances, enhancing the
system's ability to remain stable despite large sampling times or signicant perturbations.
Improved Performance: By utilizing a hierarchy of constraints, the system can eectively manage disturbances
and maintain desired performance levels without being overly conservative.
Flexibility: The generalization to N layers allows for tailored control strategies that can be adjusted based on the
specic requirements of the system and the nature of the disturbances encountered.
In conclusion, the implementation of multiple barrier functions in discrete time control systems is crucial for ensuring
robust performance and stability. By providing a layered approach to disturbance management, these functions
enable the system to handle larger sampling times and maintain control eectiveness across a range of operational
conditions.

8.1
Fine-tuning
As demonstrated in the previous section, several parameters must be considered to ensure optimal performance and
robustness. This section provides guidelines to assist the end-user in ne-tuning the parameters of the algorithm.
Firstly, ќ± must be chosen with care. A too low ќ± will result in an underconstrained system, thereby necessitating
that the constraints originate from the layers. If the system lacks precision, i.e., does not reach the innermost layer,
it would be prudent to increase ќ± before adjusting other parameters. However, it is crucial to remember that the
constraints induced by this parameter increase rapidly, potentially leading to overconstraints and erratic behavior
in discrete time.
Secondly, regarding ѕµ1, this parameter is exible for the end-user if sucient layers are present to ensure
performance and robustness. As previously noted, achieving a given accuracy may not always be feasible due to
noise or perturbations. Therefore, while it is possible to set this parameter as low as desired, it is vital that the other
ѕµ values are not too close to it and that enough layers are maintained. Otherwise, the system will be dominated by
the dynamic algorithm.
Since the system may be governed by the dynamic algorithm in cases of initial conditions, strong perturbations,
or incorrect parameter choices, it is advisable to saturate the gains. Moreover, the maximum dynamic gains should
be selected in accordance with ќ±. A simple yet eective approach is to dene a constant and raise it to the power of
ќ±. Otherwise, this parameter remains exible for the end-user, who should bear in mind that a too low maximum
will result in very slow convergence or even failure to converge in the presence of a non-vanishing perturbation,
while a too high maximum will lead to signicant chattering and poor precision, potentially preventing the system
from reaching the barrier function.
Regarding the sampling time, this parameter should be set as low as possible within the limits of the hardware.
Although the sampling time can be adjusted as desired, lower sampling times will yield better performance.
Finally, concerning ќЈ, this parameter is also exible for the end-user (considering the aforementioned conditions).
However, it is important to keep in mind that when ќЈ is used within its set range, the algorithm's power diminishes,
making it more susceptible to noise or perturbations that could push it out of the current layer.

8.3
Limitations
While the study presents valuable insights and guidelines for optimizing barrier-function based STSMC, several
limitations must be acknowledged. These limitations highlight areas for potential improvement and further research.
Firstly, the analysis primarily focuses on a specic set of parameters (ќ±, E, and ќЈ) and their impact on sys-
tem performance. However, other parameters and factors may also inuence the behavior of the systems. The
interactions between these parameters and the broader control system dynamics warrant further investigation.
Secondly, the study assumed ideal conditions for measurement and actuation. In real-world applications, sensors
and actuators are subject to various imperfections, such as noise and latency. These factors can signicantly aect
the performance of the control system, and their impact needs to be rigorously analyzed - as it can be observed in
the experimental validations.
Additionally, the experiments conducted to validate the theoretical ndings were performed under controlled
conditions with specic initial settings. The robustness of the proposed tuning guidelines across a wider range
of scenarios, including extreme and unpredictable conditions, has not been fully explored.
This may limit the
generalizability of the results to other contexts or systems with dierent characteristics.
The dynamic algorithm used in the study, while eective in many scenarios, may not be universally applicable.
Its performance can be highly sensitive to saturation tuning, and in certain cases, it may not achieve the desired
level of accuracy or stability. Further research is needed to develop more adaptive and robust algorithms that can
better handle a variety of operational conditions.
Another limitation is the computational complexity associated with the discrete-time implementation of the
proposed control algorithm. The requirement for low sampling times to achieve optimal performance can impose
signicant demands on hardware resources. This aspect needs careful consideration, particularly for systems with
limited computational capabilities. Moreover, for systems with limited memory, it is not feasible to add an unlimited
number of layers, and this constraint should also be considered.
In conclusion, while the study provides a solid foundation for optimizing barrier-function based STA, addressing
these limitations will be essential for enhancing the robustness, applicability, and practical deployment of the
proposed control strategies. Future research should aim to expand the scope of parameter analysis, incorporate
real-world imperfections, explore adaptive algorithms, and ensure the feasibility of real-time implementation in
diverse operational environments.
8.4
Possible Improvements
Revised version of the algorithm The algorithm presented in (5.28) assumes that A0 is capable of reaching
the innermost layer, which is not always feasible in real-world scenarios. Therefore, an enhancement could
involve revising this algorithm to allow the end-user to select the specic layer at which to cease the use of
this algorithm and transition to the appropriate barrier function. Additionally, since only a version with two
layers has been presented, it could be valuable to assess the benets of switching when the sliding manifold
reaches the innermost barrier function, as well as when it encounters the barrier function immediately below
its current position.
More complex STSMC In this study, only the classical non-homogeneous STSMC has been presented. A mod-
ied version of this algorithm could potentially yield better results.
Systematic tuning A systematic tuning algorithm could be derived from the knowledge the end-user has about
the system, such as the noise variance and the type of perturbation the system is expected to encounter.
Adaptive Parameter Adjustment An adaptive mechanism could be developed to automatically adjust the con-
trol parameters in real-time based on the system's performance and environmental changes. This approach
could potentially improve the system's resilience to varying conditions.
