Abstract 
 
This project demonstrates different patterning methods for silicon dioxide in the nano- and micro-
scale. The primary task in this project is the nano-patterning of silicon dioxide for selective-area 
growth of nanowire solar cells. This can be carried out by using either nanoimprint lithography 
technology or electron-beam lithography technology. For silicon dioxide micro-patterning, 
photolithography technology is used for selective-area growth of cubic silicon carbide.  
For silicon dioxide nano-patterning using nanoimprint lithography, holes with diameters of 50-60 
nm are successfully defined in a 30-nm-thick silicon dioxide layer on a silicon substrate. The wafer 
will be used as a patterned substrate for gallium arsenide phosphide nanowire growth using 
molecular beam epitaxy. In the fabrication process, a silicon stamp consisted of nanopillar arrays 
with a structure diameter of 50-60 nm was initially fabricated using electron-beam lithography 
followed by deep reactive-ion etching. Thereafter, the stamp was employed in the nanoimprint 
process using a hot-embossing imprinter. A wafer with a layer of silicon dioxide on top and coated 
with nanoimprint resist was prepared. During nanoimprint, the nano-pattern was transferred to the 
nanoimprint resist layer successfully. Finally, by applying reactive-ion etching and wet etching on 
silicon dioxide, the nano-pattern was transferred to the silicon dioxide layer with a desired hole 
diameter of 50-60 nm. During the electron-beam lithography process, writing current and exposure 
dose were optimized. The conditions of nanoimprint lithography and other steps were also 
optimized to obtain the expected hole size on the silicon dioxide layer. 
In addition, the nano-patterned silicon dioxide layer can also be produced using electron-beam 
lithography. In this case, electron-beam exposure using different doses was performed on positive 
electron-sensitive resist to create different sizes of nanohole arrays. After development, holes with 
diameters ranging from 35 to 50 nm were successfully created in the resist. Optimization of 
exposure conditions and etch of silicon dioxide will be carried out in the future. 
For silicon dioxide micro-patterning, micro-structures with sizes ranging from 1 μm to 10 μm were 
successfully fabricated using photolithography followed by reactive-ion etching. The wafers will be

used for cubic silicon carbide selective-area growth. The purpose is to investigate the growth 
quality of cubic silicon carbide for various silicon dioxide structures.

1 Introduction 
Silicon dioxide (SiO2), also known as silica, is an oxide of silicon and it is a material possessing lots 
of advantages which enable it to engage in various applications ranging from solar industry to 
materials science. SiO2 can be easily manufactured by oxidation of silicon (Si) which is an 
abundant, cost-efficient material. In addition, SiO2 is known for its hardness which can provide 
protection for critical films or structures underlying. Furthermore, the unique property of 
transparence allow SiO2 to be one of the most promising and indispensable materials in solar 
applications. Besides, advanced and mature processing technologies of SiO2 enable it to obtain 
design flexibility as well as a wide range of application possibilities. 
Patterning of SiO2 at a nanometer level or a micrometer level is a widely used method in SiO2 
processing technology. For nano-patterning of SiO2, technologies including nanoimprint 
lithography (NIL) technology or electron-beam lithography (EBL) technology can be employed. In 
NIL, fabrication of a stamp by EBL needs to be carried out first and the stamp will be used in the 
following NIL process. In nano-patterning of SiO2 by EBL, the structures can be directly formed in 
a simplified process. For production of micro-patterned SiO2, photolithography technology is a 
common way to realize formation of micro-structures.  
At the beginning of this chapter, nano-patterning of SiO2 for fabrication of nano-structure solar cells 
is introduced in §1.1. Secondly, §1.2 provides information regarding micro-patterning of SiO2 for 
testing of cubic silicon carbide (3C-SiC) growth. The outline for the remaining thesis chapters is 
finally provided in §1.3. 
1.1 Nano-Patterning of Silicon Dioxide for Solar Cells   
There is an increasingly urgent need in the world for clean and renewable energy sources since 
generating electricity using fossil fuels including coal, oil and natural gas leads to severe global 
warming effect which exerts a negative impact on the environment, climate and even human health. 
Solar energy, known as clean, practical, reliable and renewable resource, possesses plentiful 
availability and is quite a potential substitute for conventional resources. Based on photovoltaic 
effect, a solar cell can convert the sun's energy into electricity without generation of harmful gases. 
Other advantages including easy installation, low maintenance and good robustness make solar cells 
become a quite promising and attractive source of electricity.  
==PAGE:26==

In the manufacture of solar cells, it is well known that Si is an abundant, cost-efficient and robust 
material but with poorer efficiency due to its indirect band structure in comparison with III-V 
semiconductor compound materials which normally possess direct bandgaps. Combination of a Si 
substrate and III-V semiconductor compound materials into one cost-efficient and high-efficiency 
solar cell has always been a dream for solar cell manufacturers. However the lattice and thermal 
expansion mismatch existing between high-efficiency III-V compounds and low-cost Si substrates 
prohibits the integration. Taking advantage of III-V nanowires can be a way to bypass the limitation 
caused by material misfit since small contact area between the III-V nanowire and the Si substrate 
can enable strain induced at the heterointerface to be relaxed within the first few monolayers [1]-
[4]. By this method, finally in this project, combination of a Si substrate and III-V nanowires into 
one cost-efficient and high-efficiency solar cell can be successfully realized. The Si substrate 
contains a p-n junction and is also a solar cell. By the combination of Si substrate and nanowires, a 
tandem solar cell can be produced. 
The used Si substrate contains a p-n junction itself which has the lowest bandgap energy in the final 
cell and it has a crystal orientation of 〈111〉 instead of the standard orientation of 〈100〉. The 
formation of the p-n junction is carried out through highly n-type doping of the surface of a p-type 
Si substrate. The reason why a 〈111〉 oriented Si substrate is hired is that the nanowires can grow 
perpendicular to the surface of such a substrate [5]-[6]. Nanowires grown on a 〈100〉 oriented 
substrate tend to develop in four different 〈111〉 directions and end up with an angle of 
approximately 35℃ relative to the substrate surface. The Si substrate is oxidized in order to form 
the SiO2 layer and later holes at the nanometer level are defined in the SiO2 layer.   
Cooperators of the project employ ternary gallium arsenide phosphide (GaAsP) as the primary 
material of nanowires since it is one of the most promising III−V compound semiconductors in 
photovoltaic applications. The nanowires made of GaAsP can be grown from predefined holes in a 
SiO2 layer on the Si substrate. During growth using molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), firstly, a 
vertical core growth of p-type gallium arsenide phosphide (p-GaAsP) is applied and after reaching 
the desired height, shell growths surrounding the core are performed. The first and second shells are 
made of intrinsic gallium arsenide phosphide (i-GaAsP) and n-type gallium arsenide phosphide (n-
GaAsP) respectively to form a p-i-n junction in the radial direction. The outermost n-type gallium 
indium phosphide (n-GaInP) shell which possesses the largest bandgap energy functions as the 
window layer. Its role is to absorb high-energy photons in the solar spectrum while let the other

photons pass through and transmit all the way to the other absorbing layers. Hence, after nanowire 
growth, a tandem solar cell is formed and the simplified structure is shown in Figure 1-1. 
Generally, in a planar p-n junction or an axial p-n junction of a nanowire, carrier separation and 
collection happen in the same direction as the photon absorption, which results in a trade-off 
between short path of carrier diffusion and good absorption of light. If electron-hole pairs are 
generated outside the diffusion length away from the p-n junction within which they are separated 
and collected more easily, a loss of the electron-hole pairs can occur due to carrier recombination 
since the diffusion length defines the average distance a carrier can move from generation to 
recombination. One of the most valuable characteristics of radial p-n junctions is separating light 
absorption and carrier collection into two orthogonal directions. The electron-hole pairs are 
collected radially while the photons are absorbed in the axial direction, which enables the generated 
carriers to diffuse for a much shorter distance to get collected before the recombination can occur 
and in the meanwhile a sufficient solar absorption is guaranteed [7]-[8]. The height of the nanowires 
can be as long as desired to have a good absorption of solar light and at the same time the 
thicknesses of the core and shells can be designed to be thin enough in order to guarantee an 
efficient collection of carriers.

1.2 Micro-Patterning of Silicon Dioxide for Growth of Cubic Silicon 
Carbide  
3C-SiC is a very promising material which possesses a large bandgap, high electron mobility, high 
breakdown field, temperature stability and chemical inertness. Nevertheless, it is difficult to 
heteroepitaxially grow good-quality 3C-SiC on a Si substrate. In the area near the interface between 
SiC and Si, large amount of defects including misfit dislocations, twins, stacking faults and 
threading dislocations can be generated, which significantly degrade the material quality. 
Fortunately, this problem can be solved by employment of a process containing firstly a selective 
epitaxial growth on a micro-patterned SiO2 layer on the Si substrate and secondly epitaxial lateral 
overgrowth (LEO) [12]-[13].  
In this project, the fabrication process of a micro-patterned SiO2 layer on top of a silicon substrate 
mainly includes photolithography and SiO2 etch. Testing of 3C-SiC growth on different micro-
structures in the SiO2 layer will be carried out by our cooperators aiming to find out the best 
structure for high quality 3C-SiC growth on the Si substrate.

2.1.1.1 Spin Coating 
In the first step of wafer preparation, a 4-inch Si wafer was selected. The wafer was baked using a 
hotplate for 30 minutes at 200℃. Hydrophilic surfaces, most notably SiO2, usually accelerate the 
formation of a moisture layer absorbed from ambient humidity and leads to a bad adhesion of resist 
spun on the wafer surface. During development, this moisture layer enables developer to penetrate

into the interface between resist and substrate and cause resist layer delamination. Furthermore, 
when an etching process is proceeding, this moisture layer offers the etchant an opportunity to 
diffuse into the interface and result in an undesired undercutting. Consequently it is extremely 
important to apply a treatment to the SiO2 surface before coating. Although Si is not as water-
absorbing as SiO2, the existence of native oxide formed on the Si surface can also contain some 
moisture which can deteriorate the adhesion of resist. Hence the wafer was baked at high 
temperature for dehydration.

2.1.1.2 Electron-beam Lithography and Silicon Etch 
In the following step of EBL, an electron-beam writer was used to accomplish the electron-beam

Result Analysis 
One-shot exposure is a much more time-efficient way to write dot pattern over a large area than a 
multi-shot exposure. By applying different exposure doses under a certain beam current, different 
pillar diameters can be obtained after development and Si etch. However, the decision about which 
dose to be used still cannot be made. The stamp will be used in the following nanoimprint step to 
produce holes with a diameter of 50-60 nm in a SiO2 layer. Pattern transfer can happen for several 
times in the process, which can probably lead to structure diameter alteration. The first possible 
change after fabrication of stamp happens in the nanoimprint process. Afterwards etch of residual 
resist could possibly enlarge the imprinted structures. Furthermore, either the dry etch of SiO2 or the 
wet etch of SiO2 could result in further change in structure size. Since the nanostructures are 
extremely tiny and delicate, several-nanometer change in diameter can make a not negligible impact 
on the final wafer. Hence the evolution of structure diameter change from step to step needs to be 
carefully inspected in order to make the right decision about which electron-beam exposure dose 
should be selected to produce the desired holes in SiO2.  
2.1.1.3 Molecular Vapor Deposition 
The last but not the least step of stamp fabrication is to deposit a FDTS (chemical formula 
Cl3Si(CH2)2(CF2)7CF3) layer by Molecular Vapor Deposition (MVD) which can create molecular 
films on the surface of the Si stamp functioning as the anti-stiction coatings in nanoimprint 
lithography. These self-assembled monolayers are formed with the presence of a molecular vapor of 
chemicals [22]. After deposition the silicon surface can be coated with a 10 - 15 Å thick monolayer 
of anti-stiction layer which has a contact angle of 110° with water. A surface deposited with a layer 
of such can be considered as a hydrophobic surface which can help avoid resist stiction. 
During a nanoimprint process, the substrate surface to be imprinted is in direct contact with the 
structured surface of the Si stamp.  The high temperature applied during imprint gradually melts the 
solid resist layer on the substrate surface into the molten phase. Without the presence of the anti-
stiction coatings, melting resist in high temperature can stick to the structured stamp surface and 
stay there even after the temperature is cooled down. The nasty appearance of stamp without the

The fabrication process of nano-patterned SiO2 layer using nanoimprint is shown in Figure 2-28. 
Firstly, a Si substrate containing a p-n junction and has a crystal orientation of 〈111〉 is cleaned by 
RCA method which is a standard way to treat wafer before putting into an oxidation furnace. 
Werner Kern developed the basic procedure in 1965 when he is working for Radio Corporation of 
America (RCA) [23]. It consists of Standard Clean 1 and 2 (SC-1 and SC-2) and 5% HF solution. 
==PAGE:64==

The SC-1 contains H2O, NH4OH and H2O2 with a volume ratio of 5:1:1 and is used for removal of 
light organics, particles and metals. The SC-2 contains H2O, HCl and H2O2 with a volume ratio of 
5:1:1 and is used for removal of alkalis, metal hydroxides, and residual metals. 5% HF solution 
aims to remove native silicon oxide formed at the Si surface. Sample surface is initially oxidized by 
H2O2 and then the generated oxide is removed by acids and bases. By combination of oxide 
generation and dissolution, the sample surface can be effectively cleaned. 
Afterwards, the cleaned sample is sent to a furnace for dry oxidation. The wafers are placed on a 
quartz carrier and transported into a quarts tube. The chamber temperature is heated up by 
resistance heating system and a reaction shown below happens at 1050 ℃ for 17 minutes in order to 
form a 36-nm-thick SiO2 layer.

2.4 Conclusions 
Fabrication process of nano-patterned SiO2 layer using NIL technology includes fabrication of Si 
stamp using EBL and fabrication of nano-patterned SiO2 layer using NIL. For stamp fabrication 
==PAGE:80==
                                                                                         Advanced Micro/Nano-Patterning Of Silicon Dioxide 
56 
both the processes assisted with negative and positive electron-beam resists were studied and 
applied in order to fabricate a Si stamp with 100-nm-high pillars on its top. The fabrication process 
using a negative tone photoresist is preferred since no inverse pattern transfer needs to be carried 
out in its procedure hence resulting in a more straightforward process with fewer steps when it is 
compared to the process using a positive resist. However, one of the drawbacks about this negative 
resist is that the employed mr-EBL 6000.1 is a less used resist in DTU Danchip and consequently 
very little information and experience can be provided. Furthermore, this is a highly sensitive resist, 
meaning that a small fluctuation in the current or the dose during exposure can lead to a different 
result.  
At the beginning of the electron-beam exposure, the most challenging problem was the extremely 
long exposure time. Many improvements were done on pattern designs, writing methods, exposure 
conditions and writing routines in order to shorten the time. The most efficient way to create a time-
efficient process is to employ the one-shot exposure which can save the writing time and improve 
the exposure efficiency dramatically in comparison with the initially used multi-shot exposure. By 
extension of the shot pitch to the size of the designed nanostructures, each 50-nm square can be 
exposed by only a single shot.  
For the stamp fabrication assisted with one-shot exposure and negative resist mr-EBL 6000.1, dose 
tests using doses ranging from 10 to 40 μC/cm2 under a certain current of 2 nA or 6 nA were 
carried out to determine which dose and writing current to be used. For 6 nA, pillars exposed using 
doses lower than 30 μC/cm2 have significantly degraded quality and low yield of pillars while the 
severe degradation happens at doses smaller than 28 μC/cm2 for 2 nA. In addition, a higher dose 
can result in a larger structure size. For doses ranging from 26 to 40 μC/cm2, in the case of 6 nA, 
diameters of pillars from different doses change from 40 to 70 nm while in the case of 2 nA, the 
pillar diameters are decreased by approximately 5 nm. Besides, the most important advantage of 2 
nA is the higher yield of pillars at low doses than 6 nA. Heights of pillars from different doses are 
not identical with each other due to the loading effect. For doses ranging from 26 to 40 μC/cm2, the 
pillar heights go from 80-85 nm to 100-105 nm. 
For nanoimprint, EVG was employed at last in consideration of its vacuum system. After 
nanoimprint, hole diameters in the resist layer get an increase of less than 5 nm in comparison with 
the corresponding pillar sizes of the Si stamp. The imprinted depths of holes are different from dose 
to dose due to the corresponding different pillar heights resulting from loading effect in Si etch 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
 
57 
Chapter 2 Fabrication of Nano-Patterned Silicon Dioxide Using Nanoimprint Lithography Technology 
process. And the hole depth is in accordance with the pillar height. The following etch of the 
residual resist gives an another increase of less than 5 nm. Hence there is a total increase of smaller 
than 10 nm in comparison with the pillar diameters of the Si stamp.  
Finally after the etch of SiO2, the hole diameters in SiO2 are decreased by 5 to 10 nm in comparison 
with the hole sizes after the etch of residual imprint resist. Based on the inspection, a writing current 
of 2 nA and an exposure dose of 36 μC/cm2 were selected for the final stamp in consideration of 
the desired diameter and satisfying yield of desired pillars. After the NIL process, 50-60 nm holes in 
SiO2 were successfully produced.

3 Fabrication of Nano-Patterned Silicon Dioxide Using Electron-
Beam Lithography Technology 
In addition to the fabrication of nano-patterned SiO2 using NIL technology, the 30-nm-thick nano-
patterned SiO2 layer can also be fabricated using EBL technology. A fabrication process assisted 
with either a negative or a positive tone resist can be employed. In this chapter, both processes are 
introduced and analyzed. 
In the chapter, §3.1 explains the fabrication process assisted with a negative tone resist while §3.2 
presents the fabrication process using a positive tone resist. Finally the results are analyzed and 
compared in §3.3. 
3.1 Fabrication Process Using a Negative Tone Photoresist 
Fabrication process of nano-patterned SiO2 using a negative tone photoresist is explained in Figure 
3-1. To begin with, a 36-nm SiO2 layer is formed on a Si wafer by dry oxidation and afterwards the 
wafer is baked at 200 ℃ for 30 minutes to avoid moisture. Then a spin coating using the same 
parameters shown in Table 2-1 is applied to obtain 80-nm-thick mr-EBL 6000.1 on the SiO2 
surface. Next, after a 3-minute softbake at 110 ℃, the wafer is exposed to the electron beam and has 
the desired pattern written on the negative resist. Layout, dose distribution and writing condition are 
the same as what is demonstrated in Figure 2-15, Table 2-10 and Table 2-11 respectively. 
Thereafter, the exposed sample is developed in developer mr-Dev 600 for 40 seconds and then 
baked at 110 ℃ for 3 minutes.  
Afterwards, a 20-nm Al layer is deposited on the structured resist layer by Alcatel. Al is directly in 
contact with the SiO2 surface in the areas without resist while in the areas covered by mr-EBL 
6000.1 Al is deposited on the resist. Following, in the process of lift-off, the wafer is dipped in 1165 
remover assisted with ultrasonic power under 60 ℃ for one hour. During this process, Al on the 
resist layer can be removed while Al on the Si surface can survive. By doing this an inverse pattern 
made of Al can be produced. After lift-off, the SiO2 is etched by RIE using the recipe shown in 
Table 2-20 to achieve an etch depth of 30 nm. Next, Al is removed by a wet etching process. 
Finally, the residual SiO2 is etched away using the etchant made of SioEtch and DI-water with a 
volume ratio of 1:14.

This is probably because mr-EBL 6000.1 is not a perfect choice for lift-off since resist profile after 
development is very sensitive to the dose during exposure. Usually, a re-entrant cross sectional 
profile of resist after development is desired since this prevents metal from depositing on the 
sidewall. Metal resting on sidewall can cause difficulties in lift-off by keeping remover 1165 away 
from resist. Unwanted and desired cross sectional profiles of resist after development are shown in 
Figure 3-2 and Figure 3-3 respectively. It can be seen that during Al deposition, metal can easily 
deposit not only on top of resist but also on the sidewall in the case shown in Figure 3-2. 
Consequently, during lift-off, remover 1165 cannot find an entrance to the resist below the metal 
layer, which can lead to a failed lift-off. As for the case in Figure 3-3, top part of resist shadows the 
bottom part. Hence metal deposition on sidewall can be avoided to a large extent. During lift-off, 
resist can be exposed to remover 1165 and get dissolved.

3.3 Conclusions 
50-60 nm holes in SiO2 may be produced using EBL technology. In the case of the negative tone 
photoresist mr-EBL 6000.1, lift-off is one of the most critical steps to realize pattern transfer. Due 
to probably the not suitable profile of mr-EBL 6000.1 after development, the process failed in the 
step of lift-off. As for the process assisted with the positive tone resist CSAR 62, the fabrication 
method is more straightforward and requires fewer steps. According to the inspection after 
development, the yield of holes is higher than mr-EBL 6000.1. Hole diameters in CSAR 62 exposed 
using doses ranging from 280 to 490 μC/cm2 change from 35 to 50 nm depending on which dose is 
being used in EBL process. The same as the mr-EBL 6000.1, higher doses provide larger hole 
diameters in the layer of CSAR 62. Nevertheless, in addition to the relatively high cost and low 
efficiency of fabrication, the success of NIL method stops the further processing of this method 
including SiO2 etch.

4 Micro-Patterning of Silicon Dioxide Using Photolithography 
Technology 
3C-SiC contains excellent properties which make itself a promising material in different 
applications. In this chapter, the fabrication process of a micro-patterned SiO2 layer on top of a Si 
substrate is introduced. The process primarily including photolithography and RIE process aims at 
testing 3C-SiC growth on different structures in the SiO2 layer so as to find out the best structure for 
3C-SiC growth on the Si substrate.  
One of the most challenging problems is the exposure to realize micro-structure transfer from mask 
to substrate with acceptable accuracy. In addition, the etch depth of SiO2 layer needs to be precisely 
controlled to avoid both residual SiO2 on Si substrate and overetch which can lead to damage of the 
Si surface. 
The chapter is arranged as follows. §4.1 describes the fabrication process of micro-patterning of 
SiO2 including photolithography and RIE process. In addition, results and measurements are also 
presented after each step. In §4.2, previously demonstrated results are analyzed and conclusions are 
draw eventually. 
4.1 Fabrication Process  
Wafers with a layer of SiO2 on top of Si substrate are prepared to be further processed by steps 
demonstrated in Figure 4-1. As the beginning step, photolithography is carried out to define a 
patterned layer of photoresist AZ5214E on SiO2 surface. Afterwards, etching of SiO2 by RIE 
process is applied in order to transfer structures from patterned resist layer to the SiO2 layer. 
Eventually, after removal of the resist, these Si samples covered by a structured layer of SiO2 are 
produced.  
Filmtek is used to measure SiO2 thickness in order to obtain wafers with proper oxide thickness. 
Afterwards the selected wafers are treated by chemical vapor in HMDS oven to get a better 
photoresist adhesion during spin coating using SSE spinner. Next, ultraviolet-light (UV-light) 
exposure using KS aligner and development in developer AZ351B are carried out to transfer 
patterns from the structured mask used in UV-light exposure to the resist layer on the SiO2. By 
employment of RIE process, finally structures can be formed in the SiO2 layer. The cooperators of 
this project will perform testing of 3C-SiC growth on different structures. Afterwards, ideas

4.1.2.2 UV-light Exposure 
UV-light exposure can be employed to realize selective dissolution of photoresist in developer. 
Ahead of exposure, a mask which has part of areas transparent and the other areas lightproof is 
installed on top of the sample to be exposed. During exposure, photons transport through the 
transparent areas to have the underneath photoresist exposed while the other parts of resist are 
protected by the lightproof areas of the mask from being exposed to photons. During the exposure 
of a positive resist, original cross-links in the resist are destroyed and the resist starts to become 
dissolvable in developer.  
The most critical structures on the used mask are lines and squares shown in Figure 4-2 and Figure 
4-3 respectively. Lines are used for formation of SiO2 strips and squares are employed to produce 
SiO2 structures which can function as holes. In addition to these two, the other types of structures 
which can contribute to testing of 3C-SiC growth are also present. For all the structures described 
above, there are both dark field and bright field regions on the mask and they can end up with 
completely inverse products. Either dark or bright field can work for SiO2 strips while the other 
structures including squares can only be fabricated using the dark field region. Figure 4-2 is a 
picture showing structures in bright field while Figure 4-3 is for dark field with the yellow parts as

5 Conclusions & Outlook 
This chapter provides the conclusions of the findings in this project and they are highlighted in §5.1. 
Some improvements for future research are demonstrated in §5.2.  
5.1 Conclusions 
For nano-patterning of SiO2, 50-60 nm holes in SiO2 layer can be successfully defined by 
combining EBL and NIL technologies.  
By the employment of EBL technology, a Si stamp with pillars on top can be fabricated and can 
make contributions to the following NIL process. In the fabrication process of stamps, both the 
negative resist mr-EBL 6000.1 and the positive resist CSAR 62 were tried. For exposure of the 
same area, mr-EBL 6000.1 presents a more efficient writing time because of its high sensitivity to 
electrons. Furthermore, mr-EBL 6000.1 allows a simplified process with fewer steps while the 
employment of CSAR 62 asks for more steps including metal deposition and lift-off which increase 
the risk of failure. For EBL, one-shot exposure can improve the exposure efficiency dramatically in 
comparison with the multi-shot exposure. For one-shot exposure, writing currents of 2 nA and 6 nA 
and various exposure doses ranging from 10 to 40 μC/cm2 were tested. For hole formation after 
development, a relatively low current of 2 nA demonstrates a higher yield than 6 nA. And the 
employment of higher exposure doses presents a higher yield than the lower doses. However there 
is always a trade-off between a high yield and a small structure size since a higher dose also means 
a larger structure diameter after development. For 6 nA, pillars exposed using doses lower than 30 
μC/cm2 have significantly degraded quality and low yield while the severe degradation happens at 
doses smaller than 28 μC/cm2 for 2 nA. In the end, one-shot exposure using negative resist mr-EBL 
6000.1 with a writing current of 2 nA and an exposure dose of 36 μC/cm2 was used for fabrication 
of the final Si stamp. Si pillars with diameters of 50-60 nm were produced eventually. 
For NIL process, EVG NIL is the employed equipment in consideration of the vacuum system it 
can provide. Under vacuum, gap between the stamp and substrate can be eliminated to avoid the 
formation of bubbles in the resist layer. During nanoimprint, the resist thickness on the substrate 
should be around 10 to 20 nm thicker than the pillar height of the stamp in order to avoid contact 
between pillar tip and SiO2 surface. After nanoimprint, hole diameters in the resist layer get an 
increase of less than 5 nm in comparison with the corresponding pillar sizes of the Si stamp. And 
==PAGE:104==
                                                                                         Advanced Micro/Nano-Patterning Of Silicon Dioxide 
80 
the hole depth is in accordance with the pillar height. The following etch of the residual resist gives 
an another increase of less than 5 nm. Hence there is a total increase of smaller than 10 nm in 
comparison with the pillar diameters of the Si stamp. The employment of an exposure dose of 36 
μC/cm2 and a 25-minute imprint process with chamber heated to 130 ℃ and a piston force of 15 
kN gives hole diameters of 65-70 nm after the removal of residual imprint resist.  Finally after the 
etch of SiO2, holes of 50-60 nm in SiO2 can be produced. 
Holes with diameters of 50-60 nm in SiO2 may also be produced by the employment of EBL 
technology. In this method, positive resist CSAR 62 is preferred due to its simpler fabrication 
process and high yield of desired structures after development in comparison with the negative mr-
EBL 6000.1. Similarly, dose test for one-shot exposure was applied under a writing current of 6 nA 
and ends up with holes of 35-50 nm in the CSAR 62 layer. This method did not proceed further in 
consideration of its high cost and a low efficiency of fabrication in comparison of the fabrication 
process using NIL. 
For micro-patterning of SiO2, micro-structures in SiO2 can be successfully defined by the 
employment of photolithography technology. The employment of vacuum contact during the UV-
light exposure allows precise transfer of patterns. Drawing a vacuum between mask and wafer prior 
to exposure can minimize the air gap between mask and wafer hence ending up with a high 
resolution by mitigating the diffraction effect. After the etch of SiO2, different micro-structures can 
be produced in SiO2 layer. The etch time should be precisely controlled to avoid both a layer of 
residual SiO2 and an overetch which can harm  the  Si surface. 
5.2 Outlook 
For nano-patterning of SiO2 using NIL, some improvements for better wafer quality can be made 
for future research.  
Wet Silicon Etch for Improvement of Stamp Roughness In the stamp fabrication, Si pillars were 
formed only using DRIE Pegasus. In the future, wet etch of Si using KOH can be used as the step 
after DRIE to smooth the stamp surface. Furthermore, for smaller structure production in SiO2 using 
NIL, the pillar size of the Si stamp can be further reduced by this method. 
Flexible Stamp for Nanoimprint In NIL process, one of the available options is to employ a 
flexible stamp made of polymer instead of the Si stamp. One of the advantages is that a flexible 
==PAGE:105==
 
 
 
81 
Chapter 5 Conclusions & Outlook 
stamp has a smaller possibility of fragmentation during imprint. Furthermore, unlike the hard 
stamps, the surfaces of the flexible stamp and the substrate do not have to be perfectly flat due to 
the flexibility of stamp. This is good for transfer of structures in a large area. Besides, the flexibility 
of stamp enables the stamp and the substrate to fit more closely to each other so as to avoid bubbles 
formed in the resist layer. 
For micro-patterning of SiO2, the roughness of Si and SiO2 after dry etch was removed using RCA 
cleaning method. Instead of this, another way using wet etch of SiO2 is also applicable. 
Wet Etch of Residual SiO2 Layer In selective SiO2 etch, the exposed SiO2 layer was removed by 
RIE and afterwards the surface roughness was improved using RCA cleaning method. A better way 
for the same purpose is to leave a 5-nm SiO2 after RIE. After removal of the covering resist, the 5-
nm residual SiO2 can be eliminated using a wet etching process to expose the Si under the 5-nm 
SiO2 layer and increase the surface smoothness at the same time.
