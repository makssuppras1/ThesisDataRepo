Abstract
In this thesis a leading edge morphing device based on the NACA 64418 airfoil
was designed, analyzed and constructed for wind tunnel experiments. The
prototype utilizes a camber morphing actuation mechanism that allows the nose
of the wing device to be deflected downward to achieve delayed stall with respect
to angle of attack, effectively moving the transition between laminar and
turbulent flow further aft. This feature results in a parallel shift of the lift to
drag ratio for increasing angle of attack, thus offering favourable control
conditions for a range in angle of attack which may be considered in future
wind turbine blade design. Experimental results show an increase in maximum
lift coefficient of 8.5% for maximum leading edge morphing, and a maintained
lift to drag value parallel shifted 3 degrees in angle of attack.

Introduction
2.1
Motivation
The increasing demand for clean, renewable energy is evident in Figure 2.1, where the global
cumulative installed wind capacity is seen. Comparing this fact with the rule of thumb that
states that the energy production of a horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) approximately
is equal to the square of the wind turbine rotor radius, it is clear that the size of wind turbines
will keep increasing [24]. Furthermore, another common agreement is that the wind turbine
rotor diameter roughly is approximate to the height of the tower.
Clearly then, enormous wind turbines, producing upwards of 20 MW, will be seen in the
future, and this implies a major concern in the art of aerodynamic and structural design of
the ever growing turbines.
The longest wind turbine blades have masses of up to 35 tonnes which inflict a tremendous
amount of stress on the blade root and the hub to which it is attached - let alone the loads
that must be overcome due to aerodynamics while in service. Alleviating as much of this
stress as possible is key when designing new blades, allowing for a rotor radius even larger
for future systems.
One way of relieving the structure is by introducing variable geometry blades that allow the
them to alter their shape to accommodate for turbulence and aerodynamic optimization.
This optimization can lead to an increase in annual energy production (AEP), which is the
overall goal in the quest of improving the wind energy technology and industry.
Figure 2.1: Wind capacity installed worldwide from 2001-2017. Source: GWEC

Furthermore, including the morphing ability to wind turbine blades opens the possibility
that the service expectancy of wind turbines can be prolonged, which would result in more
energy harvested over the course of a wind turbine’s life expectancy.
By far, the majority of research into airfoil morphing has been carried out in aircraft aero-
nautics context. The gains and potential achievements of developing wind turbine blades
with morphing camber abilities are promising, so this thesis is dedicated to the investigation
of a technology that is primarily used in aeronautics relations, for possible implementation
in the future wind energy industry.
Figure 2.2 shows how the free wind speed is slowed near the ground due to wind shear -

What is desired then, is a rotor that is able to decrease the difference in free wind speed

2.2
Concept & Theory
2.2.1
Wind Turbine Control
The power source for wind energy is intermittent as free wind speeds vary greatly. For low
wind speeds, the designed power rating may not be met while for very high wind speeds, the
enormous forces on the machine can cause failure, so wind turbines in service need controlling
and maintenance to function optimally. Cut-in, rated and cut-out wind speed are the terms
used for the regions in wind speed in which a wind turbine operates.
For example, the
DTU 10MW RWT has cut-in wind speed of 4 m/s at which speed power production is
commenced, rated wind speed 11.4 m/s at which the turbine is producing power optimally
and cut-out wind speed of 25 m/s at which power production is halted and the rotor brought
to a standstill for safety [7].
Wind turbines are designed to produce a specific power output, Prated. For higher wind
speeds, the power output is limited to Prated through different strategies;
• Stall regulation
• Pitch regulation
The stall regulation strategy works by fixing the blade to the rotor hub in a specifically
designed pitch angle, so that for high wind speeds, the angle of attack is increased to a
point beyond stall (cf. Figure 2.9), whereby the lift coefficient decreases and drag coefficient
increases, causing a maximum shaft torque, thus limiting output power.
Most modern wind turbines use pitch regulated power limitation.
With this mechanism
installed, the entire blade is able to rotate about its longitudinal axis, hence actively ad-
justing the angle of attack with the relative wind speed. In this manner, the blades can be
pitched continuously so that the power output matches the rated power by decreasing the
angle of attack. For wind speeds above cut-out speed, the blades are pitched in a way that
result in stall, which is labelled active stall [24]. Some modern wind turbines make use of a
technology that allows a smooth power production decrease when above rated wind speeds,
e.g. 25 m/s. This is done in order to deliver a better transition of produced power to the
electrical grid as opposed to an abrupt halt.
2.2.2
Airfoil Design
Wind turbine blades are designed to optimize power extracted from the wind source. To
accomplish this, many factors play a role in the design phase, as there are limitations to the
final structure of the blades. Modern wind turbine blades have reached sizes where logistic
considerations restrict size upscaling, as the blades have to be transported from factory to
site, be it onshore or offshore. This is specifically important for the inner part of the blade,
where the chord length is greatest.
The enormous weight of modern wind turbine blades requires structural focus for the inner
part of the blades, where structural design is favoured over aerodynamics.

2.2.4
Fundamental Aerodynamic Concept
When a body moves through a fluid, the fluid exerts a force on it, according to Newton’s
third law of motion. The aerodynamic lift generated by a wing is always perpendicular to
the incoming free flow, while the aerodynamic drag generated is always parallel to the free
flow. The shape of the body in question is essential for the generated lift and drag, as is the
angle of attack.

2.2.6
Blade Element Momentum Theory
When a wind turbine is in operation, each part along its blades moves at different speeds
because of farther travel in the wind for greater radii. This local vector is called the tangential
wind speed and lies as the name suggests in the direction of the rotor plane. The vector
quantity is ωr(1 + a′), as seen in Figure 2.9. The free stream wind that interacts the rotor
disc perpendicularly has magnitude V0(1 −a). The vector sum of this and the tangential
wind speed is the quantity the airfoil experiences as the incoming wind, Vrel.
The angle between the rotor plane and the relative incoming wind is denoted by ϕ, the flow
angle. The angle between the airfoil chord line and the rotor plane is the local pitch angle
θ, while the angle between chord line and Vrel is the angle of attack, α.
For elements close to the tip of the blade, the tangential speed is much larger than the free
stream wind speed during operation, so it is evident from Figure 2.9 that the local pitch
angle must be small, while larger local pitch angles are required when blade part is closer
to the rotor hub, in order to maintain constant angle of attack for all airfoil sections along
the blade. This is the reason why turbine blades twist along the blade.

The lift coefficient increases approximately linearly as function of angle of attack, with slope

dα = 2π/rad until flow separation takes place, and then decreases. The behaviour of this
curve is very dependent on airfoil shape.
Drag increases linearly for small angles of attack but then increases partly due to increased
frontal area. When flow separation happens, the drag figure takes a complex nature

With the concept stated above, there will for any airfoil be an angle of attack where flow
separation will occur. At this point, the lift coefficient will decrease abruptly while the drag
coefficient will increase. For some angle of attack then, Cl
Cd will attain a maximum value. In
other words, there exists an angle of attack for which the highest amount of lift for the least

p. 56].

2.3.1
Morphing Actuation Techniques
2.3.1.1
Piezoelectric Actuators
Piezoelectric actuation is a two-way process in which electricity can be converted to mechan-
ical energy, and vice versa. The most commonly used material in context with the study
of smart structure actuation is the piezoceramic PZT (Lead Zirconate Titanate), which is
often stacked and amplified to improve strain [16, 47]. This actuation technique has poten-
tial in aerospace technology, and has been studied for aircraft wings and helicopter blades
especially. An example of the latter is that of Straub et al. [42], where a full scale 5-bladed
helicopter rotor fitted with active piezoelectric hinged blade flaps underwent an extensive
series of tests.
With 33% chord length and 25% span, it was shown that vibration was
significantly decreased by 80%, achieved with blade deflection of 2◦. The use of the flaps
also accounted for an increase in rotor thrust of over 10%.
Sahng Min Lim et al. constructed a biomimetic wing to investigate use intended for UAV
wings [30]. The aft part of the built airfoil was composed of sandwiched layers of glass/epoxy,
carbon fibre and PZT piezoceramic for actuation. It was found, that the displacement of the
trailing edge linearly followed that of the applied voltage until 150 V, then became higher
that expected voltage input. Ultimately, the displacement reached a maximum of 5 degrees
at 300 V.

In his PhD thesis, Poonsong reviews the RAINBOW (Reduced And Internally-Biased Ox-

2.3.1.2
Shape-Memory Alloys
Smart materials such as shape-memory alloys employ temperature to alter its shape, such
that the alloy configuration returns to its programmed form when heated above a certain
transition temperature. The temperature of the material is usually changed by applying
an electric current through the material, and the process is reversible. This concept seems
reasonable then to implement in the wind energy industry because of its versatility and
strength, and studies have considered it as a good alternative to use within blade design for
a wind turbine [8, 10, 13, 28, 29].
A study made by Lara-Quintanilla et al. saw the experiment of an SMA setup to import the
actuation technique specifically to wind turbine blades. By inserting wires of SMA material
(Nitinol type) into channels allowed the deformation of a polymer beam around the neutral
axis seen in Figure 2.13, by contracting and relaxing the appropriate wires. The system is
dependent on cooling the SMA wires when they are to be expanded, and for this reason, the
guiding channels also had the function of cooling the wires with pressurized venting air.

2.3.1.3
Mechanical Actuators
Traditional mechanical actuation systems like hydraulic and pneumatic actuation is a promis-
ing alternative for method of actuation regarding the morphing capabilities of a wind turbine
wing. These systems offer high reliability. The large size of a wind turbine blade requires
high actuation force displacement and as stated in [10, 11] a frequency of 0 −6 Hz should be
met for optimal aerodynamic and load alleviation abilities. Barlas reports in the study, that
a complete damping of aerodynamic disturbances can be achieved if an actuation frequency
of 12 Hz is obtained.
A drawback of a hydraulic actuation system is the need for inspection and maintenance of
moving parts and actuation system as a whole. Furthermore, Barlas and van Kuik report of
leakage problems in hydraulic hoses [10]. In a hydraulic actuation system, the mechanism
itself would be displacing the morphing blade internally, and this puts requirements for
reliability of the actuation system even greater, since accessibility of the actuation mechanism
is very limited once the blade is glued together at the end of the manufacturing process.
The Induflap2 research project developed at DTU Risø uses a pneumatic system for ac-
tuation of a silicone, partly hollow trailing edge flap that has a length of 10% chord (see
Figure 2.14). Simulation test results on the 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine (RWT) for this
project suggest that the overall blade mass can be reduced by 19.6%, the AEP increased by
0.51% and the lifetime blade root flapwise fatigue reduced by 23.1% [7, 9, 12, 35].

2.3.2
Materials and Structural Design for a Morphing Airfoil
The enormous loads a wind turbine blade must withstand requires structural rigidity, and
this conflicts with the concept of the blade being able to morph, since this is sought to be
done with the least possible amount of force. The load carrying region of the blade airfoil is
the integral webbing and spars that are positioned near the middle of the chord [24]. This
is also a reason why previous work with morphing airfoils involves concepts with variable
geometry trailing edge, since this area of the airfoil focuses on aerodynamic optimization
rather than load.
The key focus of a wing with morphing abilities is then the out-of-plane stiffness which
should be as high as possible to carry aerodynamic and structural loads along the blade,
and at the same time have low in-plane stiffness in order to make force requirements of
blade morphing as low as possible. These properties are the center of attraction in the study
by Dayyani et al., in which the optimal material for such operation is investigated [18].
Regarding wind turbine morphing blade material in particular, a comprehensive paper by
Lachenal, Daynes and Weaver in [28] was carried out. In this review they address materials
with so called anisotropic properties. These materials’ stiffness is direction dependent so
that the skin of the airfoil can be designed with low stiffness in the direction of deformation
and high stiffness in load direction. This type of design appears feasible for application in
wind turbine blades as it offers strength and allows morphing of airfoil while keeping a low
weight.

3.3.1
STAR-CCM+ CFD Analysis
A performance evaluation of the proposed wing design is useful; not only for the performance
itself, but also to get indications of where the design can be improved.
Also, the study
can give a good indication of how much droop nose flex (DNF) is needed for optimal
aerodynamic performance. If a high DNF angle is required for a specific AOA, the airfoil
material must have matching properties - i.e. thickness of material, composite composition
- to achieve that flexibility.
To do this, a CFD analyses of the proposed design has been carried out in Star-CCM+
and XFOIL. The objective of this operation is to get figures of the ratio between lift and
drag as the angle of attack varies and the airfoil is morphed. The analysis was designed as
follows;
• subject airfoil NACA 64(3)-418 to 50 m/s airflow under normal atmospheric conditions
and let angle of attack range between 0-25 degrees
• morphing airfoil nose should be able to droop 60 degrees
• droop angles to investigate range from 0 to 60 with increments of 5 degrees
• all 13 droop angles should be subjected to the mentioned angles of attack
• store lift, drag, lift/drag-coefficients and Reynolds numbers for 2D simulations
This implies producing 338 2D simulations of the different angles of attack and droop angle
combinations.

of the resulting morphed wing without having a physical prototype. To accomplish this
Solidworks’ flex feature was employed. This tool allows a CAD-body to be flexed between
fixation and flex planes and imitates the droop nose design. Note in Figure 3.4 that the flex
feature does not alter the shape or outline of the airfoil body that is behind the stationary
plane; this is advantageous as this is an intended mechanism objective of the proposed
prototype design.

In order to have an independent measure of the magnitude of actuation, an optical laser
for displacement readings is installed in the prototype wing. The optical laser is a Micro-
Epsilon OptoNCDT 1302-50, which uses optical triangulation to determine displacement
of moving objects. The optical laser is fixed in place to the suction side inner shell and
measures the distance to an object that is protruding from the linear motor slider. Thus,
it measures the actuation displacement of the slider in the linear motor. The independent
measurement of actuation - designated offset - is an important control parameter in wind
tunnel experiments.
Regarding aerodynamic results, the measurement technique uses pressure taps to record
local pressure differences at 32 different locations along the circumference of the airfoil. The
concentration of pressure taps is greater near the leading edge - on both suction- and pressure
sides - to capture rapid changing flows near the nose of the wing. To convert the analog
signals from pressure differences in the taps, a pressure transducer is installed in the wing.
The transducer model used in the prototype is a Measurement Specialties - Pressure
Systems esp32 hd. Another identical transducer captures the wake of the wing through a
wake rake placed downstream of the prototype wing in the wind tunnel.

CHAPTER5
Results & Findings
Construction of the wing prototype allowed the conduct of a series of wind tunnel tests
to investigate if the CFD simulations carried out (see Section 3.3) indeed reflect physical
behaviour of the NACA 64418 with droop nose mechanism. The wind tunnel tests were
executed in the Laboratory of Fluid Mechanics at the mechanical department of DTU. The
results and findings associated with wind tunnel experiments are posted in this chapter.
5.0.1
Limitations
A set of limitations related to the performed wind tunnel tests recorded before and during
tests are to be given attention when reviewing the final results achieved. These include:
• Blockage of wind tunnel test section due to obstruction of air flow caused by increasing
angle of attack for prototype wing. This was especially significant for higher Reynolds
number tests where blockage resulted in decrease in Reynolds number of up to 10%
• Friction in stepper motor used to adjust angle of attack for prototype wing
• Slack in droop nose mechanism due to mechanism imperfection. This causes judder
and oscillations of the droop nose mechanism (attempts to minimize this issue was
done)
• Friction of prototype wing edge with wind tunnel test section walls (this issue was
resolved by trimming wing edges)
• For negative angles of attack it was registered that the wing nose was buckled in a
downward motion due to aerodynamic force and mechanism
• Indications of insufficient number of pressure taps installed in prototype (32 taps in
wing and 32 in wake rake for wake flow capture)
5.1
Static Measurement Series
To test the droop nose mechanism, a series of static measurements were performed. I.e.
cycling through an angle of attack sequence while maintaining a fixed droop nose flex angle.
Pausing the stepper motor at a current angle of attack allows capture of pressure differential
distribution from the pressure taps.
The angle of attack sequence for the static measurements is as follows:

The sequence begins at -10 degrees angle of attack and then increases, so that the zero
lift angle may be determined, i.e. Cl(α) = 0. The stepper motor increases the angle of

information on aerodynamic hysteresis).
The actuation force delivered by the linear motor is controlled by an arbitrary offset value.

highest. This feature allows dynamic control of the actuation mechanism as adjustments of

5.1.1
Reflections on Static Wind Tunnel Results
The lift coefficient data obtained shows that for lower angles of attack, the unactuated wing
has higher lift coefficient than those of the actuated state. The value increases approximately
linearly to 9-10 degrees AOA, after which it decreases and then increases again. A possible
explanation of the cause of the short term increase in lift coefficient, is that vortices induced
by the soft stalling wing increase lift, as more and more of the suction side surface has
turbulent flow.
What is noticeable in the lift coefficient figures for Re = 2 · 105, 3 · 105, 4 · 105 and 5 · 105 in
Figures 5.1, 5.5, 5.15, respectively, is that there is a parallel shift in the lift coefficient curve
along angle of attack. That is, the lift coefficient achieved for the actuated state is equal to
that of the unactuated state at a higher angle of attack. This, however, only holds true until
a shifting point occurs near 10 degrees angle of attack where the highest momentaneous
lift coefficient is found for the actuated states, and the order is reversed. This behaviour is
indeed what was predicted by CFD simulations covered in Section 3.11.
Furthermore, the lift coefficient figures have patterns that show that all of the actuated
states achieve higher values of maximum lift coefficient in the non-stall regime (soft stall
happens approximately at 15 degrees angle of attack, and full stall at 18-20 degrees AOA)
than that of the unactuated state.
Regarding the drag coefficient, this value increases somewhat linearly from 0 to 10 degrees
angle of attack, although with a lower gradient between 5 and 10 degrees. For 10 degrees
AOA and above, the drag coefficient of the actuated states is again shifted along AOA, with
the unactuated state being the first setting to have an abrupt increase in drag coefficient
as function of AOA. The highest actuation states tend to have a sharper increase in drag
coefficient development, however.
The observations stated above agree with the lift- to drag coefficient ratios seen on Figures
5.3, 5.7 and 5.11 for Reynolds numbers 2 · 105, 3 · 105 and 4 · 105, respectively. Here, the
parallel shifting pattern is again noticed for increasing AOA.

5 Results & Findings
5.2.1
Reflections on Dynamic Measurement Series
The columns in Figures 5.20 and 5.21 show the responses of Cl vs. the oscillating offset
values for frequencies 1, 2 and 3 Hz, respectively. As Cl and the offset values are evaluated
simultaneously and that the offset oscillates in a sinusoidal motion, any differences in ob-
tained Cl values during the protracting and retracting motions will show as a ring shape in
the resultant plot. This is what is seen in Figure 5.21(b, c) for 12 degrees AOA, where lift
coefficient as function of offset value is observed to attain a ring shape. In the data analysis,
it was observed that the lowest of said values occur during the protracting phase, that is,
when offset value increases.
In other words, the phenomenon of momentary decrease in lift coefficient happens when the
state of actuation advances from minimally to maximally actuated. The ring shape is the
phenomenon of aerodynamic hysteresis loops, whose details are beyond the scope of this
thesis, but more information on the subject can be found in [25, 51].
Notice also how vertical bands are formed in all results figures for droop frequency of 1 Hz.
A possible explanation of this feature is the sampling frequency of the system that coincides
to some extent with that of the droop frequency.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
It is elaborated in this report how a hypothetical wind turbine blade with an ability to droop
its nose for aerodynamic gains was created. Preliminary analyses carried out to optimize
the mechanism include design, CFD, blade shell deformation analysis, and ultimately, a
prototype was constructed for wind tunnel testing.
The idea behind the droop nose mechanism rises from the fact that the transition between
laminar and turbulent flow around a body with installed morphing structure can be delayed
for increasing angles of attack, thereby maintaining high results of lift at low expense of
increased drag. This type of morphing structure offers aerodynamic efficiency by delaying
stall while having low penalties in mass and power, and exploits the blade internal space for
actuator mechanism placement as opposed to e.g. external slats.
In Section 3 it was covered how CFD- and FEM methods were used to examine the design.
CFD results of the airfoil NACA 64418 produced by simulations in Star-CCM+ and XFOIL
suggested that the droop nose design is feasible for achieving higher lift to drag ratio for
higher angles of attack, but specific angles of nose droop are necessary to accomplish this,
as the droop nose ability affects the Cl/CD as function of the AOA curve by shifting it right
for higher droop nose flex angles.
ANSYS Workbench was employed to carry out FEM analyses of different draft concepts
and to optimize the final design. Also, the tool was used to best position the bars and links
for optimal target deformation shape of blade nose. It was found here, that a sliding gap
(resulting in two pressure side panels that slide over one another upon actuation) on the
pressure side of the blade would result in good curvature of the nose, as the original shape
of the nose would rotate about a point on the suction side of the blade. Furthermore, the
actuation force required for deformation was found to be significantly lower for such a design.
Components for the most feasible design were constructed in the Mechanical Workshop at
DTU, and the wing was assembled according to CAD model.
The intention of building the prototype was to expose it to wind tunnel test to study the
effects of the design and mechanism. This was done in the Laboratory for Fluid Mechanics
at DTU. Static and Dynamic tests were conducted and are described in Chapter 5.
Static tests at Reynolds numbers 2·105, 3·105, 4·105 and 5·105 were done, and it was found
that at Reynolds number 5 · 105, buckling of the thinner shell on suction side occurred and
instabilities in actuation mechanism, hence it was decided not to proceed tests with higher
Reynolds numbers.
For the static tests achieved, experimental results showed that lift coefficient curves were
extended when the droop nose actuation system was activated. The increase in maximum lift
coefficient was for Re = 2·105 8%, for Re = 3·105 10% and for Re = 4·105 8%. Furthermore,

results showed that lift to drag coefficient ratios were reaching the same magnitude of that
of the unactuated state but parallel shifted 3 degrees AOA for Reynolds numbers 2- and
3 · 105 and 2 degrees AOA for Reynolds number 4 · 105.
The parallel shift was also observed for the drag coefficient, but that this would increase
more rapidly for increasing angle of attack.
It was gathered that the aerodynamic behaviour suggested by CFD analysis of the modelled
deflection geometry did not represent that of the actual morphing shapes constructed. The
geometry used for CFD simulations was based on the Solidworks flex feature which is
covered in Section 3.3. Geometry deformation using FEM analysis as in Section 3.5.2 would
assumingly offer a an improved depiction of the deformation for CFD studies.
6.1
Future Work & Improvement Proposals
Although the constructed blade with morphing capabilities has shown beneficial results,
there are parts of the design that can be improved. The morphing strategy used in this
project includes a sliding gap on the pressure side of the blade, and while this is experimen-
tally convenient, it might be unrealistic to implement this design in a modern wind turbine
due to durability, endurance and maintenance. However, it serves as a study in the field of a
series of problems that follow the growth of future wind turbines which morphing structures
perhaps are able to alleviate or overcome.
For the specific design developed in this project, the key improvements are
• Nose droop angle: The current angle of droop for the design is 10 degrees
• Redesign of internal components to avoid slack and other mechanical imperfections to
enhance droop mechanism
• Modify design that has durability for high Reynolds number wind tunnel tests
• Design mechanism without sliding gap on pressure side (see [3, 31])
• Research in upscaling of the proposed design e.g. actuation mechanism, skin material,
actuation power source
• Advanced geometry modelling of optimal actuation bars and links using e.g. topology
optimization
• Perform BEM analysis of the achieved design
