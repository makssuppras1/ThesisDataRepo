Summary (English)
The goal of this thesis is to determine wind pressures acting on external building
facades by Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) with the use of the open
source software OpenFOAM. A secondary objective is to generate a software tool
which can be used to calculate the wind pressures on actual building projects
with complex geometry.
In the ﬁrst part of this thesis, information of existing best practise and model-
ling approaches are presented. The gathered information concerning boundary
conditions and turbulence models are described.
In the second part, an investigation of how diﬀerent turbulence models and
settings in OpenFOAM inﬂuences the calculation of pressure coeﬃcients (Cp
values) on a simple rectangular geometry is performed. The results of the inves-
tigations are then compared with data from a wind tunnel experiment conducted
by Chand et al. (1998).
The development of the tool for determination of wind pressures is described
in the third part. The tool is used on a complex building geometry, Villa Gug,
supplied by Bjarke Ingels Group (BIG) and the results are presented in the last
part.
The ﬁndings of the study conclude that the tool can be used to assist decision
making in the early design phase. It will, however, require further validation
before the tool should be used for the ﬁnal design of building components.

Chapter 1
Introduction
When planning construction of a new building, it is important to take the wind
loads into consideration to avoid a structural collapse. The wind loads are most
often considered by using building codes such as the Eurocode (DS/EN:1991-1-
4). The norm contains guidance for calculation of wind loads on simple geomet-
rical shapes and is mainly based on data gathered through a extensive number
of wind tunnel experiments (Weerasuriya and Jayasinghe, 2014).
Building geometries which deviate from the simple geometries can be tested
in wind tunnels. However, this is often a costly and time consuming approach.
Therefore it is mostly used only for ﬁnal design and do not exploit the possibility
to inﬂuence the design of the building geometry. An alternative to the wind
tunnel experiments is Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). It allows coverage
of geometries outside the norm and can thereby provide a potentially cheaper
method to calculate wind loads.
CFD has been successfully applied in many diﬀerent areas, from hydraulics to
aerodynamics. The range of applications is profound and encompasses many
diﬀerent ﬂuid phenomena. (Fan, 2014b)
The use of CFD in the building industry has started to grow rapidly over the
past years because of the increased awareness of its potential, in some areas it
has even become a requirement. In the United Kingdom, wind studies are now a

common engineering practice and it has become mandatory documentation for
the plan authority (Xiang and Wang, 2007). Diﬀerent Danish companies have
already shown great interest in a cheaper alternative to wind tunnel experiments,
especially if it can be used early in the design phase (see Section 2.2).
The purpose of the existing wind load norm is to serve primarily as a design tool
at an early design stage in the absence of more sophisticated testing methods
such as wind tunnel experiments and CFD simulations. However, in contra-
diction to the purpose, the wind load norm are often used for ﬁnal design of
components. (Weerasuriya and Jayasinghe, 2014)
The Architectural Institute of Japan (AIJ) and European Cooperation in Science
and Technology (COST) have both published guidelines for determination of
pedestrian wind.
AIJ has made a guideline speciﬁcally for determination of
wind loads which is based on many ﬁndings from COST. (Franke et al., 2007;
Tamuraa et al., 2008)
It has previously been shown that CFD can replicate data from wind tunnel
experiments and full scale tests (Blocken, 2014; Oliveira and Younis, 2000).
However, one of the major challenges in regards to CFD is the computational
demand associated with the simulations. Therefore, if the computational re-
quirements are met, the use of CFD can potentially replace the need for wind
tunnel experiments. A great part of the theory for performance of CFD has
been extracted from wind tunnel experiments and has been essential for the
CFD to get to where it is today (Franke et al., 2007).
1.1
Project Motivation
The motivation of this thesis is the determination of wind pressures acting on
external building facades for simple as well as complex structures by CFD sim-
ulations. The main focus is the complex structures, since these are not covered
by any norm. Furthermore, the assumption is that if such wind pressures can
be determined it will lead to lower construction cost and decreased material use
which result in less environmental impact. It is desirable to be able to consider
more wind directions than the traditional norm and thereby increase safety for
the building as well as its occupants.

1.2 Project Objectives

1.2
Project Objectives
The primary objective of this project is to determine wind pressures acting on
external facades of complex building structures.
Secondary objective is to create a tool to determine such pressures. In order to
reach this objective, CFD simulations of a simple shaped isolated building will
be compared with a benchmark test (wind tunnel experiment). The tool will
thus automate the simulation process and thereby decrease the time spent on
setup for each new case. In CFD, the user inputs can greatly aﬀect the results
and the tool should therefore explain the user inputs in order to ensure correct
usage. The purpose of the tool is to provide the possibility to inﬂuence early
design choices when designing buildings.
1.3
Project Plan
The project is divided into ﬁve diﬀerent phases and reﬂects the objectives stated
in the previous section. The project plan is:
1. Collect information on existing knowledge in the literature regarding wind
pressures and CFD in OpenFOAM
2. Identify demand and need for determination of wind pressures by compa-
nies
3. Validate a simple isolated building model towards a benchmark test, in-
cluding:
• Request access to data for benchmark test
• Systematically investigate the inﬂuence of parameters in OpenFOAM
and compare simulation results to benchmark data
• Compare the ﬁnal simulated results with Eurocode
4. Develop tool for calculation of wind pressures
• Parametric setup
• Implement guides in tool
5. Apply the tool on a case study with a complex geometry (Villa Gug)
• Simulate wind pressures

1.4
Process and Programs
The open source software OpenFOAM (version 1612+) is chosen for this thesis
for a number of reasons.
The software is free which allows people to use it
without having to consider whether it is economically reasonable. Furthermore,
it allows access to all source code and the possibility to change it. Therefore
the code is accessible allowing all actions to be traced if the user has experience
with OpenFOAM.
OpenFOAM diﬀers from the commercialised CFD program Ansys Fluent by
not adding a limit to the number of cells which can be used in the student
version.
The limit is approximately half a million cells in Fluent and has a
signiﬁcant importance, since it can be impossible to perform grid independence
check which literature states is crucial for reliability of a simulation, explained
in Section 2.4.3 (Franke, 2006).
In order to capitalise the results obtained from the CFD simulations in Open-
FOAM the program Rhinoceros (Rhino) with the plug-in Grasshopper and the
plug-in Butterﬂy is used. These programs combined oﬀers an interface for the
OpenFOAM simulation and allows change in settings for each simulation.
Figure 1.1 shows a simple graphical illustration of the link between the diﬀerent
programs. The programs can send data back and forth, such that geometry
in Rhino can be used in an OpenFOAM simulation and the results can be
displayed back into Rhino. This work ﬂow provides an unique possibility for a
parametric design ﬂow, since the results can be used directly after the simulation
is complete. The results can be utilised by other plug-ins for Grasshopper, such
as Karamba1 to perform load combinations.
Before a tool can be used for determination of wind loads, an investigation of its
accuracy is a necessity. In order to validate the CFD simulations the accuracy
is investigated by the aforementioned simple geometry which is compared with
data from a wind tunnel experiment. The experiment is described in Section
3.1.
After the validation of the simple geometry has been performed the ﬁndings
will be implemented into a tool and then demonstrated on an actual building
project with a high complexity, Villa Gug.

Chapter 2
Literature Review and
Theory
This chapter provides a literature review focusing on how wind pressures acting
on buildings are calculated. It also contains theory regarding CFD in general
as well as speciﬁcally for the software OpenFOAM. Furthermore, a summary of
the most important ﬁndings are presented in Section 2.6.
2.1
Background
Wind pressures on building geometries are most often calculated by standards.
In Denmark the Eurocode ’DS/EN:1991-1-4:2007 Actions on structures’ is used.
The Eurocode is the ﬁrst international standard on wind actions and covers
several countries in Europe. It has been modiﬁed such that it is speciﬁc for each
country as a consequence of diﬀerent wind velocities. The Eurocode presents
typical geometrical building shapes, such as squares with a variety of roof angles,
cylinders and bridges. The Eurocode is based on wind tunnel experiments and it
is simpliﬁed in order to make it usable for a broader range of building geometries.
The intent is to make it easy to use and provide conservative wind loads. The
parts of the Eurocode that are relevant for this thesis will be explained in more
detail in section 2.5.
==PAGE:24==
8
Literature Review and Theory
During the recent years there has been an increase in buildings with unorthodox
shapes in architecture. When the wind pressures cannot be calculated by the
standard, conduction of wind tunnel experiments is often the obvious choice,
since years of validation have made these results trusted. During the last 50 years
the development within the ﬁeld of Computational Wind Engineering (CWE)
has undergone a signiﬁcant increase in the reliability of its results (Blocken,
2014). CWE uses Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software to replicate
the physical behaviour of the wind by Partial Diﬀerential Equations (PDE’s).
Previous studies have shown that CFD simulations can replicate the physical
behaviour of the wind in wind tunnels and even full scale experiments, if the
proper modelling techniques are applied. Many parameters can inﬂuence the
simulation results to diﬀerent extent. However, an increasing number of CFD
comparisons show good agreement between experimental and simulated data,
even for complex cases where also the nearby buildings have been considered.
(Antipin et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2011)
Studies on the accuracy of wind tunnels have been carried out. The results
are presented in a lecture concerning the use of wind tunnel experiments for
wind loads on structures by Geurts (n.d.). The closing remark states that the
experience has shown that a diﬀerence in values at approximately 20% are not
uncommon, although the same measurements are used and all relevant demands
are met (Geurts, n.d.). This is most relevant to be aware of when utilising a
wind tunnel experiment as a benchmark test for CFD simulations.
2.2
Determination of Wind Pressures in Compa-
nies
This section contains a summary of the most relevant information obtained
through interviews and mail correspondences with people working in the build-
ing industry. It outlines some of the methods used to determine wind pressures
acting on external building facades in actual building projects. Furthermore the
demand for a new tool is described.
Jens Christian Bennet who is a senior project leader at Rambøll and an ex-
pert in advanced ﬂow engineering states that they choose turbulence models
according to the intention of the study. Most often steady state simulations are
preferred over transient simulations because of cost for the costumer in terms of
computational complexity. The steady state provides a time averaged solution,
which means that only one speciﬁc time step is modelled whereas for a tran-
sient simulation the solution is time dependent and therefore contains more time

2.3
CFD Theory
In short terms CFD can be used to simulate the dynamic behaviour of a ﬂuid,
such as air, by computational technology.
There are diﬀerent ways to model the ﬂow around a building depending on the
application of the study. The methods have diﬀerent accuracy and the compu-
tational cost varies signiﬁcantly between the diﬀerent methods. The turbulence
model can be divided into two subcategories, steady state and transient solu-
tions.
For this study the Reynolds-Averaged Navier–Stokes equations (RANS) has been
chosen for the modelling of the turbulent ﬂow, since it has already been shown
that it can replicate averaged wind tunnel data (Montazeri and Blocken, 2013;
Tamuraa et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2011). The reason for this choice is also that
==PAGE:26==
10
Literature Review and Theory
the intention of this project is to determine wind pressures within a reasonable
time frame.
The RANS has been preferred over Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Direct Nu-
merical Simulation (DNS), since both require more computational power (mesh
quality and data). The LES model is more advanced than the RANS as it calcu-
lates the turbulence process and does not experience the closure problem for the
larger eddies (air vortices). However, the smaller eddies are still modelled. The
computational cost associated with LES is much greater than RANS, about
640 times greater compared to the standard turbulence k–ϵ model when wall
functions are applied. (Cheng et al., 2003)
DNS is the most complete form of CFD simulation, since it calculates the direct
solution of the Navier-Stoke equations for each control volume. Fluctuations
in a variety of sizes and frequencies of wind conditions allow the simulation
to precisely represent naturally occurring turbulence (Clannachan et al., 2010).
However in order for this type of simulation to capture a reliable result the mesh
must be smaller than the smallest turbulence eddies, which requires a very high
reﬁnement of the mesh. The computational demand of DNS is therefore much
higher than for LES and RANS simulations. In a PhD thesis by Knapp (2007)
it is concluded that DNS should be limited to small scale simulations with low
Reynolds numbers (laminar ﬂows).

2.4.1
Turbulence Models
There are a large variety of turbulence models, which contain diﬀerent advan-
tages and disadvantages. The default turbulence model in the software But-
terﬂy (for outdoor simulations) is the RNG-k-ϵ model. It was developed using
Re-Normalisation Group (RNG) methods by Yakhot et al. (1992). It is a modi-
ﬁcation of the standard k-ϵ model which is known for overestimating pressures,
especially at the windward facade. Another model is the buoyancy model k-ω
which includes the consideration of temperatures. Therefore it contains more
PDE’s than the standard k-ϵ model and requires more computational power.
The k-ω is often the preferred turbulence model for indoor simulation, where
low velocities can be strongly inﬂuenced by the temperature diﬀerences (Frana
et al., 2013).

There are a number of diﬀerent meshing strategies. Most used strategies have in
common that regions where either velocity, pressure or temperature is expected
to change rapidly or regions of speciﬁc interest is reﬁned. This is done for the
mesh to allow for accurate simulation of the physical behaviour of the ﬂuid.

The minimum grid resolution suggested by AIJ is approximately 1/10 of the
building scale within the region including the evaluation points around the target
building for determination of wind ﬂow around a building. There is no such mesh
recommendation when simulating wind loads. (Tominaga et al., 2008)
In most cases in CFD simulations the geometry investigated is simpliﬁed to
lower the time spent on both setup and computational power. However, studies
by Montazeri and Blocken (2013) has shown that an increase in detail such as
balconies have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the distribution of the pressure. At
one of the lower balcony locations the pressure coeﬃcient changes by a factor
of six. Therefore the details can play a signiﬁcant role and should be taken into
account.
For the expansion ratio COST recommends a maximum of 1.3 between two
consecutive cells. Even if this criteria is fulﬁlled it is desirable to conﬁrm that
the results do not change with diﬀerent grid layouts (Franke, 2006). As a result
of this a grid independence check should always be performed.
When simulating using wall functions the dimensionless wall distance y+ value
should be within an interval of 30-500. This yields the yp value, which is the
distance from the wall to the adjacent cell center (Blocken et al., 2007b). This
value is important for the modelling of boundary conditions for rough surfaces
and is explained in more detail in Section 2.4.4.

• y+ ranges 30-500 (when wall functions are used)
• Expansion ratio ≤1.3
• Perform grid independence check

3.1.1
Limitations of Validation
As aforementioned, the wind tunnel experiment takes measurements at three
vertical lines on the wind- and leeward facade. Therefore the data cannot con-
clude on the liability of the CFD results in other areas such as the roof or the
building sides. However, by including the leeward side, the accuracy of the tur-
bulence is investigated.
It also needs to be taken into consideration that the validation is based on a
scaled model which does not directly reﬂect a full-scale model, the Reynolds
number has to be taken into account along with other dimensionless parameters
which are used to describe the ﬂow. Two properties are worth mentioning with
respect to wind tunnel experiments. The Jensen number (Je), which describes
the ratio between building height (Zref) and roughness length (z0) and the Im-
mersion ratio (Im), which is the ratio between simulated boundary layer (zG)
and the building height (Koss, 2014).
Therefore the model validation is performed in reduced scale. However, a full-
scale CFD simulation is performed to show whether the scale has an impact on
the simulation result.

3.2.1
Numerical Modelling
The simulations have been conducted using the OpenFOAM 1612+ framework.
The simpleFoam solver, which is a steady state, incompressible and isothermal
solver for turbulent ﬂows is used. The turbulence model RNG-k-ϵ is used to re-
normalise the Navier-Stokes equations and thereby account for eﬀects of smaller
scales of motion than the standard k-ϵ model.
3.2.1.1
Turbulence Model
The choice of turbulence model is based on the objective of this project. The
objective is to ﬁt the pressure coeﬃcients on the building facade from the wind
tunnel experiment with an OpenFOAM CFD simulation. Based on the ﬁndings
in Section 2.4.1 the turbulence model RNG-k-ϵ has been chosen. As mentioned,
it is a steady state simulation which is good at reaching convergence and signif-
icantly less time consuming than transient simulations.

3.2.1.2
Solution Control
It is recommended by COST that the residuals should be reduced by four orders
of magnitude (Franke, 2006). The AIJ states that these values are largely de-
pendent on ﬂow conﬁguration and boundary conditions. Therefore it is better
to check the solution directly using diﬀerent convergence criteria such as the
aspect ratio and the stretching ratio of the grids which may be too large, the
relaxation coeﬃcient of the matrix solver which may be too small and the peri-
odic ﬂuctuations such as a vortex shedding which may occur (Tominaga et al.,
2008).
The COST recommended values for residual controls have been used. Notice
that in the study by Montazeri and Blocken (2013), the convergence was as-
sumed to be achieved when all scaled residuals have levelled oﬀto p < 1e-6,
U < 1e-5, k < 1e-4 and ϵ < 1e-4.
Furthermore the residual factors are kept at their default values, since there is
no problem with convergence. The default residuals in OpenFOAM are k = 0.7,
U = 0.7, ϵ = 0.7 and p = 0.3.

3.2.2.2
Mesh Settings
For mesh generation in OpenFOAM a background hex mesh, known as block
mesh or base mesh is created. Then the snappyHexMesh can be used to reﬁne
areas by splitting cells at feature edges and surfaces (reﬁne base mesh). The
unused cells can now be removed and the mesh can be snapped to the surfaces.
Finally layers can be added by the Add layers function to control the near
surface cell distance.
Before meshing the wanted mesh quality should be speciﬁed. OpenFOAM has
a mesh quality check implemented. If any of these checks fail then the mesh
will not be generated. These checks involve checking the maximum cell open-
ness, maximum aspect ratio, minimum and maximum face area and volume,
maximum and average mesh non-orthogonality and maximum skewness etc.
The recommended type of cells for the mesh are hexahedrals, which are used
by snappyHexMesh. The cell inﬂuences the simulation since the ﬁnite volume
method is used. Hexahedral shaped cells introduce smaller truncation errors
and display better iterative convergence. (Franke, 2006)

rect (location and size) if the base mesh is not reﬁned enough.

3.3.5
Velocity Independence Check
The Cp values are dimensionless and it is investigated whether the modelled
velocity has an inﬂuence on the Cp values. The hypothesis is that the averaged
Cp values using RANS simulation are not inﬂuenced by the velocity for a wide
range of velocities. This is investigated by simulating with diﬀerent inlet veloc-
ities. The velocities 1 m/s and 24 m/s at Zref are chosen yielding a Reynolds
numbers far beyond the 11,000 which is the lower limit for a ﬂow to be turbulent
instead of laminar.
Figure 3.19 clearly shows that the Cp values are not aﬀected by a change in the
inlet velocity for this case with sharp edges, neither when increasing or decreas-
ing the velocity within the tested velocity range. Using the method described
in Section 2.5 the peak velocity pressure (qpeak) from the Eurocode, can be cal-
culated for diﬀerent velocities and then multiplied with the Cp values obtained
from only one simulation. This means that one simulation can represent a wide
range of velocities.

suggested to modify Uref such that the simulated results are in better agreement
with the experimental data.

Partial Conclusion
The most important conclusions from the validation case are presented in the
list below as bullet points.
• Select a domain size large enough to avoid accelerated wind conditions,
this makes the building ratio calculations unnecessary
==PAGE:69==
3.5 Partial Conclusion
53
• Performing grid independence check is important and should not be ne-
glected
• The importance of y+ needs further investigations.
However it is not
necessary to fulﬁl the required range of 30-500 for accurate simulations of
pressure. It was found that even y+ values at 15,000 could provide good
results
• ks and Cs values are aﬀected by the mesh and should be set accordingly
• Choose the boundary conditions with care, since they inﬂuence the simu-
lated results greatly and play a signiﬁcant role on the convergence
• When meshing sharp edges without snap it is important to determine how
much the mesh may diﬀer from the original geometry
• The Cp values are velocity and scale factor independent for the Simple

• Measure the reference static pressure at a upstream distance of 1.8H from
the building
• For determination of Cp values the turbulent model k-ω with Equation
(3.8) provide adequate agreement with experimental data
It was found that the simulated result was sensitive to the mesh technique, the
boundary conditions, especially the TKE for the inlet (for the lower part of the
ABL). The implementation of the AIJ suggested TKE did not result in a better
agreement with experimental data than the one suggested by COST.
It has also been shown that the choice of turbulent model plays a crucial role for
the simulation. The roughness boundary conditions have less impact. However,
these are still important for accurate results and have to be modelled correctly
for the simulation to be able to run.

Partial Conclusion
The tool has shown to be capable of producing Cp values and converting these
into wind pressures by the use of Equation (4.3). The tool settings have shown to
be capable of producing a satisfying mesh for diﬀerent geometrical complexities.
However, it is again worth mentioning that the mesh generation requires special

results, since the locations of the test points do not match the location of the
geometry in Rhino. Moreover convergence is much more important than the
small inconvenience associated with visualisation.
The number of test points on the surfaces on investigation is a computational
bottleneck for the tool, since the LadyBug component genTestPts which gener-
ates the tests points is computationally demanding.

5.1.1
Considerations and Limitations
The most important assumption to mention is that the method proposed for
the Simple Case (from Chapter 3) is directly applicable onto a more complex
geometry. An essential consideration in regards to the case study is that no data
is available to compare and validate the simulations towards, contrary to the
Simple Case. Wind tunnel experiments could be performed to generate data for
comparison, however performance of such wind tunnels experiments was outside
the scope of this study. As for limitations the CFD simulations performed will
not be perfectly accurate, since the geometry of the building has been simpliﬁed
to a certain extent and the orography has been considered ﬂat. Furthermore,
it should be noted that the results generated provide pressures for design of
larger building components. This is because RANS is not applicable to smaller
building components as stated in Section 2.4 according to AIJ.

5.2.1
Terrain Category
The terrain category describes the roughness context around the building site.
The wind proﬁle is greatly inﬂuenced by the context and the correct choice of
terrain category is key to an applicable simulation. (AIJ, 2005)
Terrain category 3 in the Table 4.1 has been chosen for the Villa Gug simulation,
since the area around the building is country level with low vegetation. For
some wind directions the category could be considered within the category 4.
However, choosing category 3 for all wind directions is a conservative choice
(DS/EN:1991-1-4, 2007).

Mesh
In order for the mesh to represent the building geometry without any abnor-

minimum surface dimension for Villa Gug at the pent roofs.
The mesh is generated with snapping and with and without additional layers to
investigate the impact of adding additional layers on the simulation result. This
was investigated since selecting the most suitable settings for adding additional
layers perfectly around complex geometry such as Villa Gug is a very time
consuming process.
From Section 3.3.4 it was found that the importance of y+ values where not as
signiﬁcant as assumed and therefore the y+ values do not necessarily have to
be kept within the range of 30-500. However, the y+ values are set to aim for
around 200 for the add layer simulation, because of the required mesh reﬁnement
necessary to represent the Villa Gug geometry. This was done by the use of the
component developed for the Simple Case to control the y+ values, however this
component was modiﬁed and can be found in appendix B.2.
A grid independence check is performed to ensure that the simulation does not
change signiﬁcantly with a reﬁned mesh.
There are as mentioned diﬀerent techniques to be used. Grading (expansion
technique) is preferred in many previous studies (Montazeri and Blocken, 2013;
van Hooﬀand Blocken, 2010a) and is recommended by Franke (2006). However,
it was found in Section 3.2.2.2 that it had diﬃculties to convergence and a
problem with the automatic generation of roughness for the ground (Cs and ks
values which are based on yp). Therefore a simple meshing strategy is chosen
with reﬁnement around the building geometry.
The snap method is changed to explicit from implicit. The features are then
extracted from the geometry surface rather than extracting it from the stl ﬁle2.
The explicit method extracts the features by introducing a ﬁle surfaceFeature-
ExtractDict. The advantage of implicit is that it is fully automated and requires
less computational eﬀort. However, the explicit method oﬀers greater control
and therefore better results (if used correctly).
It is worth mentioning that the near wall pressure is corrected by using the
nNOnOrthogonalCorrectors in the solution.

To speedup the calculation time and lower the time to convergence the Gauss
linear-upwind is 1st order for the ﬁrst 100 iterations and is then increased to 2nd
order to increase the precision in the numerical calculations until convergence is
reached. This is proposed by Franke (2006) in the best practice guide for wind
around buildings. These settings have been set as default in the tool developed
for this project.
When simulating with a highly complex geometry it is diﬃcult to reach conver-
gence where all residuals (U, p, k and ω) are scaled oﬀto a magnitude of at
least 1e-4.
Running 3000 iterations give all residuals below 1e-4 except for the pressure (p),
which stabilises around 0.7e-3. The solution is very stable and only insigniﬁ-
cant changes occur after approximately 750 iterations.

Chapter 6
Discussion and Conclusion
This chapter contains the ﬁnal discussion and conclusion.
Furthermore, the
future work suggestions are outlined in Section 6.1.
The aim of this thesis was to determine wind pressures acting on external facades
of complex building geometries.
The development of a tool was included as
secondary objective. It has been shown that the tool is capable of simulating
Cp values by steady-state CFD simulations and converting these values into
wind pressures based on the peak velocity equations from the Eurocode.
The suggested CFD method was based on the k-ω turbulence model and Cp
values at
2
3H and involves some assumptions and uncertainties.
One of the
assumptions was that the method proposed in Section 3.4 is valid for other
geometries than the simple geometry. This assumption may be inaccurate since
only one wind direction was used for the development of the method, as a
consequence of limited access to experimental data.
Further comparisons to
experimental data for other wind directions and geometries can assist accessing
the legitimacy of the assumption.
Furthermore, the method to calculate Cp
values is based on the assumption that the wind tunnel experimental data is
correct, though previous studies have shown that they can deviate by 20%. The
experimental data has been very important, since the previous recommendations
by Franke et al. (2007) to use RNG-k-ϵ and COST inlet conditions produced
inaccurate pressures in OpenFOAM for the Simple Case. Additionally, it was
==PAGE:100==
84
Discussion and Conclusion
found that the suggested boundary conditions for the Turbulent Kinetic Energy
(TKE) by AIJ did not provide better agreement between the CFD simulation
and experimental data, than the ones suggested by COST.
The mesh was found to aﬀect the simulated results signiﬁcantly. The mesh needs
to be reﬁned such that the physical behaviour of the wind ﬂow can be accurately
simulated. Therefore the mesh should be inspected and a grid independence
check needs to be performed to ensure reliable results.
The Reference Static Pressure (RSP) was shown to have a great inﬂuence on the
simulated results. The peak velocity equations from the Eurocode are assumed
to provide conservative results, such that the gust factor is overestimated when
the reference high for the external pressure (ze) is used.
The tool has successfully been setup such that it can simulate the wind pressures
for any given building geometry by only requiring a few user inputs.
The development post-processing tools can visualise the results and provide
them as output. This allows the results to be utilised by other plugins, such
as load calculation tools.
The tool can be used in the early deign phase to
contribute to informed design decisions by providing an overview of the pressure
distribution on building geometries within a reasonable time frame. It is possible
to obtain a higher precision on the results but this increases the computational
time. The higher precision can be achieved by deactivating the wall functions
in the tool and ensuring that the y+ values are kept below 1.
The functionality of the tool for determination of wind pressures was demon-
strated on an actual building project, Villa Gug, with complex geometry. It
was found that having weather data for a construction location could poten-
tially decrease the wind pressures signiﬁcantly for certain directions. An inter-
esting ﬁnding was that calculating peak velocity based on weather data despite
of using the wind velocities speciﬁed in the Eurocode showed that the Eurocode
velocities are not always conservative.
The applicability of the tool is not only limited to determining wind pressures,
it could also be utilised to assist calculation of natural ventilation, since the Cp
values are normally used for this purpose.
6.1

In the future a validation of the tool for more wind directions and building geo-
metries would allow the usage of the tool in the ﬁnal design phase to determine
