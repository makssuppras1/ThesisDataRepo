Abstract
Reducing carbon emissions are a crucial goal nowadays. A hybrid power plant can be an alterna-
tive to fossil fuel. This focus of this thesis is on combining wind and solar power in combination
with battery storage. A wind and solar power based hybrid power plant is ﬁrst analysed, then
a wind, solar power and battery storage based hybrid power plant are analysed for three diﬀer-
ent locations, Denmark, the Netherlands and Spain. To help understand the results, weather
resources and market opportunities are analysed for the three locations. A mathematical model
is used to compute the optimal mix of technologies, which ensures the maximum net present
value. Besides net present value, wind and solar capacities and battery storage capacity, the
model also provides results for capacity factor, curtailed energy and internal rate of return.
The optimization model uses historical electricity market prices and simulated power time series
of wind and solar power based on weather data as input. The optimization model is applied to the
three diﬀerent locations, based on costs of diﬀerent technologies, grid capacity and interest rate.
A local sensitivity analysis is done to determine the uncertainty in the output of a optimization
model. Finally, a global sensitivity analysis is done to identify the most important parameters
for each output.
The conclusions are that Denmark is mainly driven by wind power with no solar power or battery
storage being installed. The Netherlands is mainly driven by wind power, mixed with solar
power. Battery storage starts to be implemented when the cost of battery components are set
to the projected costs for the mid evaluation for 2050. For Spain, solar power is the main energy
source, combined with a mix of wind power. Battery storage starts to be implemented when
the costs of battery components are set to the projected costs for the mid evaluation for 2030.
Overall the addition of battery storage reduces curtailment and increases the capacity factor
and NPV. Further, it is concluded that solar power and battery storage are strong together.
When the costs of battery components are reduced, a shift in technologies occur. The wind
power capacity decreases, and solar and battery capacities increases. Last it is concluded that
the interest rate is the input parameter that has the most signiﬁcant impact on most outputs.

Introduction
Due to environmental concerns, the European Union target of at least 20% renewable energy in
gross ﬁnal energy consumption by 2020 and 32% in 2030 [1]. Reducing carbon emissions are a
crucial goal nowadays, and it is one of the main factors for the energy transition. This is one of
the reasons why transmitting to renewable energy sources (RES), such as wind and solar, have
drastically expanded during the last years [2]. The levelized cost of energy (LCOE) for wind
power and solar PV have sharply reduced over the last few years, and it is expected to reduce
even further in the future [3]. This makes wind and solar power even more attractive, but there
can be consequences by increasing the installed capacity of RES. As individual RES are driven
by weather conditions and not by demand, individual RES challenges the power system due to
their variable and ﬂuctuating power output. A solution could be hybrid power plans (HPPs),
as beneﬁts of HPPs compared to individual wind or solar power plants are many. The beneﬁts
will be discussed in section 1.1.
1.1
Hybrid power plants
A hybrid power plant (HPP) is deﬁned as a combination between diﬀerent renewable technologies
(e.g. wind and solar) and energy storage. Compared to individual wind/ solar power plants,
HPPs have numerous beneﬁts. HPPs have the opportunity for better utilize of the electrical
infrastructures like cable, converters and transformers. The capacity factor for individual wind or
solar power plants are general low, thus the electrical infrastructures remain unused for a large
portion of the time [4]. By optimally utilize the electrical infrastructures, the infrastructure
investment costs of a HPP can be reduced. Further, combining wind and solar power provides
the possibility to increase the annual energy production, and the possibility of reducing LCOE.
Depending on the site and conﬁgurations, a mix between wind and solar power could result
in better use of land compared to individual wind or solar power plants [5]. If wind and solar
resources are negative correlated, the HPP provides a more balanced energy output, thus the
grid connection is better utilized. Further, a more balanced energy output reduces the variability
and power ﬂuctuation, which causes less ramping issues and reduces the instantaneous peaks,
thus results less stress on the grid [4]. The energy storage provides the opportunity to reduce
curtailment by storing a part of the excess energy. Energy storage also provides the opportunity
to store energy for times when electricity market prices are low or even negative, and sell then
market prices are high [4]. Additionally energy storage provides the possibility to operate as an
ancillary service.
1.2
Selection and Sizing of Technologies
The purpose of implementing a HPP depends on the stakeholder. The stakeholders may have
diﬀerent objectives for the design, such as energy balance, meeting renewable energy targets,
minimize the energy cost or something completely diﬀerent [6]. For this thesis, the objective
is to maximize the proﬁtability of hybrid power plants. The optimal mix of technologies and

size varies from site to site, depending on weather conditions, market prices, land cost and land
constraints [6]. The available literature on optimal sizing of a HPP is limited. On the other hand,
there are plenty of literature on hybrid renewable energy systems, especially on optimization
techniques for modeling of hybrid renewable energy systems. Various optimization techniques
are used to design and operate hybrid PV/wind energy system. The list below shows the various
optimization techniques that will be discussed in this section [7, 8, 9].
• Linear programming
• Iterative technique
• Probabilistic approach
• Open source and commercial software tools
1.2.1
Linear programming
R. Chedid and S. Rahman, 1997, presents a linear programming technique to determine the
optimal design of a hybrid wind-solar, to minimize the production cost of electricity [7]. The
model includes load requirement, controller design and even environmental concerns. The article
describes an operation policy for Grid-Linked System case. The operation policy for Grid-Linked
System case is based on the energy, that the renewable energy sources provides, must be used
ﬁrst, and the excess energy must be stored. In cases where the renewable energy sources cant
meet the demand, the energy stored in the batteries should be drawn. If that is not suﬃcient,
then diesel engines should supply the remaining part of the energy.
1.2.2
Iterative technique
Iterative technique is another simple technique used to solve the unit sizing. W. D. Kellogg
et al. 1997, presents an iterative technique to determine the optimal sizing of wind and solar
power and energy storage [8]. The paper is based on an experimental site in a remote area, and
determines the optimal mix of technologies and storage needed. The algorithm takes hourly
average wind, insolation, and power demand as input, and the objective function is minimizing
the total annual cost. The iterative procedure is taken to determine the number of turbines and
number of PV panels. First step is to select commercially available unit sizes for wind turbines,
PV panels, and battery storage. Then the number of turbines is kept constant while the number
of PV panels is increased until the system is balanced. The system is considered balanced then
the deviation of power has an average of zero over a given period of time. The previous steps
are repeated for a diﬀerent number of wind turbines. For the combinations that satisﬁes the
requirements, the total system annual cost are calculated, and the combination with the lowest
cost is chosen.

1.2.3
Probabilistic approach
S. H. Karak, 1999, published a paper which presents a general probabilistic model to design and
simulate the operation of an autonomous solar-wind energy conversion system [9]. The model
is composed on several diﬀerent wind turbines, solar modules, battery storage and load. The
model uses probability distributions of wind and solar power, additionally, energy ﬂuctuations
and hardware failure takes into consideration.
1.2.4
Open source and commercial software tools
Additionally open source and commercial software tools are available to solve the optimal mix of
technologies and their size. One optimization tool is the Renewable Energy Integration and Op-
timization (REopt) developed at NREL. REopt model considers various variable of technologies
such as solar PV, wind power, solar water heating, solar ventilation air preheating, biomass and
waste-to-energy, landﬁll gas, conventional generators, utility grid and energy storage. REopt is
formulated as a Mixed Integer Linear Program (MILP), and is eﬃcient to solve the optimally
size of the technologies, to minimize the site’s life cycle cost of energy. Further, REopt can pro-
vides strategies for the energy asset, if additional data such as electrical load proﬁles is available
[6].
Sunanda Sinha revived in 2014 19 software tools for hybrid system analysis [10]. Hybrid Op-
timization Model for Electric Renewables (HOMER) was found to be the most widely used
software tool out of the 19. HOMER performs quick prefeasibility, optimization and sensitivity
analysis for diﬀerent system conﬁguration. Note, REopt is not among the nineteen software
tools. HOMER is original developed at NREL, now it is a commercial software.
1.3
Utility scale battery storage
Utility scale battery storage could be a potential solution to solve some of the issues related to
renewable technologies. The sources of renewable technologies are driven by weather conditions
and not demand, e.g. time periods will occur where the supply from the renewable technologies
sources are not large enough to meet demand, or the supply is larger than the demand. A
combination of renewable technologies sources and battery storage could be a solution to the
problem. The addition of battery storage will be able to deliver a more stable and balanced
energy output, due to the battery storage’s unique capability to store energy, hold it and then
dispatch the energy at anytime [11]. Further, a grid scale energy storage could help with fre-
quency regulation, and increase the economic value by shifting energy, hence more attractive to
investors [12]. According to Dragan Stevanovic in 2018, it is impossible to switch to exclusively
renewable energy without energy storage. There are several ways to store energy, and one solu-
tion is thermal storage. Thermal storage is based on the idea to transform the surplus of energy
into heat, that will be stored and later convert it back to energy when there is a need for it
[13]. One of the advantages of thermal storage is that it generally has lower costs compared to
Li-ion based battery storage. The disadvantages of thermal storage is, that the transformation
3
==PAGE:13==
of heat back into electricity is at the expense of high losses of energy [13]. Another solution to
energy storage is Li-ion battery storage. A report publish by IEA in 2018 found out that Li-ion
battery storage accounted for nearly 90% of large-scale battery storage systems in 2016 [14]. The
advantages of a Li-ion grid scale energy storage system are their eﬃciency close to 99%, and
the capability to withstand thousands of cycles [12]. The disadvantage of Li-ion battery storage
is the high costs. The cost projection for Li-ion battery storage shows a rapidly decreasing of
cost over the next 30 years [15]. The study estimates the cost of a Li-ion battery storage could
decrease from 70% down to 20% of its original 2020 cost, making it much more proﬁtable.
1.4
Existing Hybrid Power Plants
As part of the prestudy for this thesis, the current market end technological development within
existing HPP’s was investigated, and there three existing HHP are presented and shows in ﬁgure

solar energy and Li-Ion battery
Parc Cynog is a wind and solar based hybrid power plant, located in south-west Wales. The
plant consists of 3.6MW wind capacity and 5MW solar capacity, and is developed by Vattenfall
as a project to gain experience of combining wind and solar power.
Kennedy Energy Park is a wind, solar and energy storage hybrid power plant located in Australia.
The hybrid power plant consist of 30 MW wind capacity, 20 MW of single axis tracking Solar
Panels, and 2 MW of Li-ion battery storage. Kennedy Energy Park aims to deliver a more
demand driven energy output [16].
Energy Park Haringvliet Zuid is a hybrid power plant located in the Netherlands, which combine
three technologies wind, solar and battery storage. The hybrid power plant is centred around
maximising the use of renewable energy, hence solving some the issues stand alone renewable

energy sources can have. The optimising synergies between the technologies enables little impact
on the environment as possible. The hybrid power plant consists of a wind capacity of 22 MW,
38 MW solar capacity and a 12 MW energy storage system. The hybrid power plant will produce
energy that corresponds to the annual consumption of 39000 households.
1.5
Research Questions
The focus of this thesis is to develop a mathematical model which optimizes the sizing of a hybrid
power plant. The model determines the optimal mix of technologies (wind, solar and battery
storage) based on weather inputs, installation costs, and grid capacity. The models objective is
to maximize NPV. A local and global sensitivity analysis will be made, in order to determine
how sensitive the model is. The research objective of the thesis can be formulated into:
The objective of this project is to develop optimal sizing and control methods to maximize the
proﬁtability of hybrid power plants combining wind and solar energy and energy storage solutions.

2.1.1
Land constraint
When optimizing a hybrid power plant, the land available to build on can be a constraining
factor. For this project, both wind turbines and solar panel take up space. For this report, it
is assumed that wind turbines and solar PV are separated at a distance, so they do not aﬀect
each other. Only onshore wind turbines are considered. In case land constraint become an issue,
there is the options to install the solar PV in between the rows of the turbines. The shadow
from the wind turbine tower and blades would impact the power output of the solar PV and
heat absorption from the solar PV panels will create turbulence, hence aﬀect the power output
of the wind turbines [17]. De Azevedo and Mohammed 2015, suggest a model to more accurately
estimate the yearly energy production of an hybrid power plant, considering wake eﬀects and the
shadows the turbines will cast on the solar PV panels [17]. Further it is assumed the electrical
infrastructures like converters and transformers are included land usage for wind turbines and
solar panel. The land required for wind turbines depends on the layout and the spacing between
the turbines.
The layout of a wind farm have been receiving increasing attention from the
scientiﬁc community [18]. This is due to the wake eﬀect turbines generates, and the layout of a

wind farm can reduce the wake eﬀect, thereby thereby optimize power production of the wind
farm. Recent studies indicate that an irregular layout of a wind farm results in a higher power
production compared to regular grids layout wind farms [18]. However, when calculating the
land required for the turbines, the layout of the turbines is assumed to be a regular grid. The
assumption allows for fast and easy calculation of land use. Regarding the spacing between the
turbines, Patel proposed that the beneﬁcial distance between wind turbines in prevailing wind
direction is 7 to 12 rotor diameters, and perpendicular to prevailing wind direction the distance
should be 3 to 5 rotor diameters [19]. The study was based on an empirical conclusion. For this
project, the distance between the wind turbines are assumed to be 7.5 rotor diameters in the
prevailing wind direction and 5 rotor diameters in the perpendicular to prevailing wind direction.
Further it is assumed that a single string contains 10 turbines. Note, these assumptions only
aﬀect the land use, and have no aﬀect the power production of the turbines. The land use for

2.2.2
Internal Rate of Return
Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is deﬁned as the interest rate that makes the NPV of a project
equal to zero. In other words, IRR is a metric used in capital budgeting to estimate the prof-
itability of a project. To determine the IRR, the same formula as NPV is used. For equation
2.15, NPV is set to zero and solved for the interest rate which is the IRR. If comparing two
projects with the same costs of investment, the project with the highest IRR would probably
be undertaken, as this would be the more proﬁtable project of the two. Generally, the higher a
project’s IRR is, the more proﬁtable would it be and the more desirable it is to undertake.

simulations, then ﬁtting a polynomial response surface to the outputs of the optimization model.
The Sobol indices can then be computed analytically from the response surface equation.
To perform uncertainty quantiﬁcation via polynomial chaos expansion, the Python software
toolbox Chaospy is used.
Chaospy is a numerical tool that specify to perform uncertainty
quantiﬁcation using polynomial chaos expansions [24]. To start, a polynomial chaos expansion
needs to be ﬁtted to the outputs.

3.1
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a simulation tool developed at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark, Department of Wind Energy and it simulates the normalized power
generation of both wind and solar power [27]. CorRES uses an unique combination of mete-
orological time series and stochastic simulation to generate normalized wind and solar power
productions on a hourly basis. To generate normalized power production of wind power in Cor-
RES, a onshore Vestas V112/3300 is used for modelling. Its rated power is 3300 kW and has a
rotor diameter of 112 m. The hub height is set to 100 m, and the wind farm consist of 20 tur-
bines. The weighted average onshore modern turbine size varies signiﬁcantly between countries.
In 2018 Norway was the country in Europe which installed the most powerful turbines. The
turbines had a average rating of 3.6 MW, while Lithuania and Greece were the countries which
installed the least powerful turbines with a average of 2.0 MW. The weighted average onshore
turbine size for newly installed turbines in Europe in 2018 was 2.7 MW [28]. The Vestas turbine
model V112/3300 is selected as it ﬁts in the range of newly installed onshore turbine size, as
well as being available in the CorRES tool. For all three locations, the same setup was used
to generate wind power generations. In addition, CorRES is also used to generate normalized
power production of solar power. According to Akif Karaﬁl et al. 2015, the optimum ﬁxed tilt
angle varies throughout the year. To optimize the power generations of the solar panel, the tilt
angle should be adjusted monthly or seasonally [29]. For simplicity, the tilt angle of the solar
panel is assumed ﬁxed and is not adjusted. Mark Z. Jacobson and Vijaysinh Jadhav provides
an estimate of the optimal tilt angles for ﬁxed tilt solar PV panels for all countries around the
world [30]. The optimal tilt angles used to model solar generation in CorRES, are based on
optimal tilt angle provided by Mark Z. Jacobson and Vijaysinh Jadhav. The optimal tilt angles
selected are the the optimal tilt angles nearest to the location of the hybrid power plant. For
Denmark, the Netherlands and Spain, the optimal tilt angles are 36◦, 34◦and 31◦, respectively.
G. S. Kinsey et al. 2019, analyzed the performance comparison of a ﬁxed-tilt and single-axis
tracker arrays. They found that the performance of a single-axis tracker arrays solar panels
generates 14% more energy compared to a ﬁxed tilt solar panels [31]. The study was based on
direct comparison of their performance of a 3 MW of single-axis tracker arrays and a 22 MW of
ﬁxed-tilt arrays.

Market opportunity
For this project the day ahead market price is used as the spot price, as actual spot prices are
not available. Day-ahead market prices refers to the electricity prices for the following day, while
spot price refers to the electricity price the hybrid power plant receives. Further it is assumed
that the hybrid power plant is acting as a price taker, and not price maker, meaning that the
energy generated by the hybrid power plant is not suﬃcient to aﬀect the day ahead prices. The
historical market data are provided by Nord Pool AS and Entsoe Transparency platform [33]
[34]. The day ahead market price depends on the demand, marginal cost of the diﬀerent energy
generations and transmissions capacity between areas [33]. The day ahead market price is found
so it maximizes social welfare. To get a better understanding how wind energy and day ahead
market prices are related in Denmark, the two data set are plotted in Figure 5, note that solar
power is not included. This should not aﬀect the relation of renewable energy sources and spot
prices, as solar power plant systems in 2015 in Denmark produced energy equivalent to 1.8 %
of the total Danish energy consumption [35], while the power generated by wind turbines in
Denmark in 2019 accounted for 45.8 % [36]. The relation is shown only for Western Denmark.
Data for the Netherlands and Spain are not shown as historical data of renewable energy provided
by Entsoe Transparency platform were not accessible.
Figure 5 show the correlation between generated wind energy and day ahead market prices for

The negative correlation values conﬁrms this. For the six years of data, the highest day ahead

Results

4.1
Wind and Solar based Hybrid Power Plant
In order to determine the optimal mix of technologies of a wind and solar based hybrid power
plant an iterative method is used. The method used to optimize mix of technologies of the
hybrid power plant is similar to the method suggested by W. D. Kellogg et al. [8] described
in section 1.2.2.
The iterative method is setup so the number of turbines is kept constant
while the number of solar PV panels is increased one step at the time. When two numbers
of solar PV panels have reached their maximum value, the number of turbines are increased
by one at a time. Then the number of solar panels is initially set to zero and increased step
by step. This continues until all conﬁgurations have been run through. An overplanting limit
of 300 % of the grid capacity is set as a limit, ensuring that the code does not run forever.
Later in the report, it is shown that installed capacities does not surpass a 300 % overplanting,
meaning a 300 % overplanting is suﬃcient to cover the range. The model developed in Python,
is based on maximizing the NPV. To determine the optimize mix of technologies that ensures
a minimization of NPV, NPV is calculated for each iterations. The NPV is computed based on
the equation 2.15. As battery storage is not included for the wind and solar based hybrid power
plant, the parameters regarding battery storage are set to 0. Each NPV is stored, and when all
conﬁgurations have been run though, the one which generates the highest NPV is selected.

Global sensitivity analysis
In the previous sections, a local sensitivity analysis was performed, which estimated how the
outputs of the model varies as one inputs parameter were changed at the time, while the other
inputs parameters were ﬁxed. Regarding the optimization model of the hybrid power plant, it is
likely that the input parameters interacts with each other. The interacting makes it unsuitable
to evaluate the impact of one input parameters, with other parameters being ﬁxed at a constant
value. To evaluate the impact each input parameters have on the outputs, a global sensitivity
analysis of the model is performed. A global sensitivity analysis is a method where all the input
parameters are varied simultaneously, in order to determine strength of inﬂuence each input has
on outputs.
In order to do the global sensitivity analysis, the inputs variables are limited to nominal invest-
ment cost of wind and solar power, grid capacity and interest rate. For this section, the land
constraint is not taken into account. Each of the input variables are assumed to be uniform
distributed within a range of values. The selected range of values for each input variable are
identical to the range of values used to perform the local sensitivity analysis. The ranges for the
inputs variables are listed in the Table 7. The ranges for the inputs variables are kept the same
for the three locations.

Wind, solar and battery storage based hybrid power plant
In this section wind, solar and battery storage based hybrid power plant is analysed. The addi-
tion of battery storage increases the complexity of the mathematical problem. The complexity
of the mathematical problem is increased due to the addition of a third technology, and that
battery storage allows for storing energy, which can be sold later then spot prices are higher.
When to sell it not easy to determine, as many factors are of importance. An iterative method
is no longer a decent method to determine the sizing of an wind, solar and battery storage
hybrid power plant. This is due to number of iteration that needs to be process is increased
signiﬁcant, thus making the computational time inappropriately long. Instead the commercial
software IBM ILOG CPLEX Optimization Studio is used [41]. CPLEX Optimizer provides a
high performance mathematical programming solvers for a mixed-integer linear optimization
problem. CPLEX solver is implemented in the python model.
As mentioned in a previous section, the battery is assumed to consist of two components, power
and energy. The power refers to the amount of power that can be charged/ discharged for a
given hour, while the energy refers to the energy capacity of the battery. In 2019 NREL publish
a paper that projects the cost for utility-scale lithium-ion battery systems. The projections
are based on 25 publications that consider utility-scale storage costs [15]. The projections are
showed in ﬁgure 23.

5
Discussion and Conclusion
Hybrid power plants may contribute to reach the European Unions goal of reducing CO2 emis-
sions from the energy sector. To analyse the advantages of an hybrid power plant, an optimiza-
tion model was used. The optimization model determines the optimal mix of technologies of
wind and solar power and battery storage, based on maximising NPV. The model uses historical
weather data, market prices, installation costs, interest rate, and grid capacity as inputs. To
determine how each parameter aﬀects the outputs, a local and global sensitivity analysis were
made for three locations, Denmark, the Netherlands and Spain. The locations were selected
based on their diversity wind and solar capacity factor and their variation of market prices.
First a wind and solar power based hybrid power plants were analysed. In general, Denmark is
mainly driven by wind power with no solar power being installed. This is due to the high capacity
factor for wind power, making wind power the preferred energy source. For the Netherlands,
wind power is also the main energy source, however with a mix of solar power and a small
overplanting occur. Similar to Denmark, the capacity factor for wind power is relatively high
compared to the capacity factor for solar power. For Spain, a signiﬁcant overplanting occurs
due to the high spot prices. Further solar power is the main energy source, combined with a
decent mix of wind power. The main energy source is the energy source with the lowest LCOE,
further the spot prices have a great signiﬁcance for overplanting ratio.
Second a wind, solar power and battery storage based hybrid power plants were analysed. The
addition of battery storage increased the complexity of the problem, hence the computational
time increased.
In order to reduced the computational time, the number of years of data
was reduced to 3 years. The reduction makes annual ﬂuctuations in weather conditions more
uncertain, further only using recent historical data ensures a more accurate estimate of spot
prices, as they seems to increase. The addition of battery storage had no signiﬁcant impact on
the results for Denmark, and similar results occur compared with the solution for the wind and
solar power based hybrid power plants. The results showed that there is no value of installing
battery storage. Due to the fact that wind energy often generates low or high energy production
for longer period of times, wind power and battery storage aren’t strong together. Longer periods
of either low or high energy production, are not suitable for battery storage, as the battery will
either be charged or discharged before the weather changes.
For the Netherlands, battery storage starts to be implemented when the cost of battery com-
ponents reaches the mid evaluation for 2050. In general, wind power is still the main energy
source, with a mix of solar power combined with a decent battery storage. As the cost of solar
power and battery components becomes cheaper, a shift in technologies occur. Solar power and
battery storage will outcompete wind power. As described in section 1.4, ﬁgure 1, Energy Park
Haringvliet Zuid hybrid power plant is a plant build in 2020 in the Netherlands. The hybrid
power plant consists of a wind capacity of 22 MW, 38 MW solar capacity and a 12 MW en-
ergy storage system. This is not consistent with the results provided by optimization model, as
56
==PAGE:66==
battery storage is implemented. The implementation of battery storage, indicates the costs of
battery components may have been estimated to high, or there are other parameters that are
not accounted for.
For Spain, battery storage starts to be implemented when the cost of battery components
reaches the mid projections for 2030. Solar power is still the main energy source, with a mix
of wind power and a huge battery storage. The battery size is able to cover the grid capacity
for 10 plus straight hours. It can be questioned, whether or not a battery this size will be
implemented, or smaller size will be selected to minimize the investment cost, and thereby
reduce the uncertainties.
When the cost of battery components are set to 200 dollars/kW for power components and 75
dollars/kWh for energy components, the share of battery storage becomes substantial, and start
to have a major impact on the results. The addition of battery storage reduces curtailment and
increases the capacity factor and NPV. The increase of the capacity factor is an important part
to achieve European Union targets of reducing carbon emissions. Further the increased revenue
makes hybrid power plant more attractive to investors.
The global sensitivity analysis shows that the interest rate was the parameter which has the
greatest impact on most outputs. The interest rate is estimated with great uncertainty, even a
small change can have a big impact on the results.
Note that this study is an academic analysis that shows the trend.
The results should be
interpreted with caution.

Future work
The optimization model gives an estimate of the optimal mix of technologies, which ensures
the maximum net present value. To obtain a more accurate solution, there are several things
that can be integrated. First it is suggested to integrate degradation of solar power and battery
storage. It is well documented that the eﬀectiveness of solar panels declines over time [45].
Further temperature is known to have a signiﬁcant impact on the performance and lifetime of
Li-ion batteries [46]. It will be interesting to see how much of an impact this has on the results.
Second would be to include more years of data, as a more profound data basis would give a
better foundation for the analysis. Yearly climate variability should be accounted for, so the
number of years of data used should be suﬃcient to denote deviations of climatic statistics on
an annual basis. Also, with climate change, how is the weather patterns expected to change for
the three chosen locations? E.g. in general it expected to have more extreme weather events
like draughts and rainfall?
Another thing what would be relevant to implement is the addition of new renewable energy
sources, such as hydro power and wave power. The addition of new renewable energy sources
may have potential for one more balanced energy output. Further, it would also be interesting to
include diﬀerent energy storage technologies. E.g. it could be interesting to implement thermal
energy storage.
Additionally it would be very relevant to include the options to buy energy. The battery storage
allows to buy and store energy when the price is low, to then sell it again when the price is high.
All of these suggestions should increase the complexity of the model, hence at the expense of
increased computational time.
One problem by optimizing by NPV is that the relative investment cost is not taken into ac-
count. In principle, there could be two conﬁgurations with two diﬀerent mix of technologies
that generates almost the same NPV. Is it worthwhile to go with the solution that generates a
1% higher NPV, at the expense of an extra investment cost of 200 MEUR? A better solutions
could probably be to optimize for a combination of both NPV and IRR.
Based on the assumption of perfect forecast, the day ahead market price is used as spot price.
Any uncertainty in weather forecast would results in a lower spot price. It would be interesting
to include the uncertainty in weather forecast to see how it aﬀects results. Further it would
be relevant to use projected electricity prices. For example, Germany is in the process of a
phaseout of nuclear power plants [47], causing an increase in electricity pricing [48]. It would
be interesting to implement projected electricity prices for 2030 and 2050 to see what impact it
has.
From an European Union perspective, it would be interesting to see what it would cost to reach
the European Union climate goals. The cost could be set in perspective to the cost of climate
change, e.g. cost of ﬂooding, lost crop yields, etc.
