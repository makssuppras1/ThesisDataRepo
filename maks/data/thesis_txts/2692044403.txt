Abstract
A growing interest in sustainable building materials has promoted and encouraged the use of cross laminated
timber (CLT) worldwide. With an increasing demand for light-weight and long-spanning timber structures,
serviceability requirements have become more important, and it is especially crucial to consider the dynamic
aspects as human footfall can be a common and signiﬁcant source of vibration.
In the present thesis, the vibration properties of CLT ﬂoors, subjected to human footfall, were examined by
laboratory tests and a ﬁnite element model. Seven different support conditions were tested in order to determine
their effects on the dynamic response of the ﬂoors. It was found that none of the examined test setups could
satisfy the comfort criteria proposed by the International Standard ISO 10137, even though the fundamental
frequency of the ﬂoor setups was found to be in the range of 11.4Hz to 13Hz.
A revision of the vibration subtask in Eurocode 5 is currently under preparation, and the ﬁnal draft was evaluated
in this paper. It was found that the design method of the current Eurocode 5 underestimated the acceleration
and is thus not applicable for CLT ﬂoors. The revision, however, has incorporated modern ﬂoor types e.g. CLT
and LVL ﬂoors, and the estimate of the dynamic response was proven to be a better approximation of the actual
behavior.
To predict the response of the ﬂoor elements, two different load models were considered in the ﬁnite element
model; a footfall force function and an effective impulse function. It was discovered that the classical footfall
function did not predict an accurate behavior of the transient ﬂoors, and despite the impulse load function vastly
overestimated the response, the vibration pattern followed the same tendency as the measured response.
Keywords: CLT ﬂoors; structural dynamics; comfort criteria; vibration criteria; laboratory experiment; damp-
ing; human footfall; signal processing; frequency weighting; time history analysis.

Introduction
The building industry is responsible for approximately 30% of the total CO2 emission in Denmark which in-
cludes the energy consumption of buildings, the building process, and the production of building materials [35].
This makes the building sector one of the main culprits related to global warming [35]. In recent years the
focus has primarily been on reducing the energy consumption during the building’s operation but an increase in
demand for certiﬁed buildings has resulted in a greater interest in sustainable building materials such as wood.
Cross laminated timber (CLT) is a solid wood material made of timber panels glued together in a cross-wise
composition. It is a relatively new and innovative construction material, developed in the 1990s, and has become
popular as the interest in sustainability have become a central priority [9]. CLT is suitable for multi-story
buildings due to its high strength compared to its low-density [31].
Serviceability requirements are often one of the more decisive factors when it comes to the design of CLT
structures due to the low-density of wood and wishes for longer spans [16]. Especially ﬂoor vibrations caused
by human excitation can be inﬂuential on the light-weight timber structures [16]. National and international
standards require that the dynamic aspects are considered to avoid uncomfortable vibrations. However, a design
method for CLT is not yet implemented in the standard Eurocode 5: Design of timber structures which is a
limiting factor in the spread of use [31]. The Eurocode 5 is currently under revision and a subtask regarding the
vibration design of timber structures including CLT is to be published.
Studies have shown that the vibration design methods for light-weight wood joisted ﬂoors are not applicable for
CLT-ﬂoors as the properties are not comparable. CLT has a mass of 30kg/m2-150kg/m2 which is signiﬁcantly
higher than a traditional wood joisted ﬂoor and still lighter than a heavy-weight ﬂoor of e.g. concrete with a
mass of above 200kg/m2 [13].

==PAGE:20==
1.1
Problem Statement
The aim of this master thesis is to investigate the vibration properties and response of CLT ﬂoors as the current
design codes do not include this type of ﬂoor. Different support conditions and their effects on the vibration
properties will be examined. The thesis is based on experimental work combined with theoretical knowledge
including ﬁnite element simulations.
The objective of this study is to examine ways of minimizing disturbing ﬂoor vibration caused by human-
induced vibrations and explore the concerns related to a new building material in the construction industry. It
is important to address the vibration concerns and eliminate unknown factors that suppress the spread of use of
CLT elements.
The present thesis will examine the following research questions:
• Is the current design code applicable for predictions of the vibration properties of CLT ﬂoors?
• How does the revised Eurocode 5 address vibration concerns and do the predictions comply with reality?
• What effects do support conditions have on the vibration properties?
• How should a FEM model be created to simulate the laboratory tests?
1.1.1
Delimitation
Structures can be excited by a multitude of dynamic loads, e.g. operating machinery and human motion such
as walking and rhythmical movement. Human footfall is a common source of vibration in buildings, and
especially for light-weight and long-spanning timber ﬂoors, the pedestrian body mass can be inﬂuential [16]
and the dynamic aspects should thus be considered. Without proper control, structural vibrations can affect
the comfort of occupants, disturb the operation of sensitive equipment, or in some cases damage structural and
non-structural members.
This thesis is limited to non-acoustic ﬂoor vibrations caused by vertical human motion and the effects experi-
enced by occupants. Vibration caused by e.g. operating machinery and the effects on sensitive equipment and
structural members will not be discussed.
The experimental part of the project is limited to laboratory testing of four one-way spanning CLT elements
sponsored by Proﬁle A/S. Due to the dimensions (0.62m x 6m) of the sponsored elements the beneﬁts from
the two-way spanning nature of CLT were not studied. Furthermore, only a single span was considered, as
continuous spans will often implicate unwanted ﬂanking transmissions. The authors have been impartial to any
preconceived notions and have not been inﬂuenced by the opinion of sponsors.

1.2
Literature Study
In this section, some relevant research will be reviewed with the purpose of investigating existing discoveries on
the subject of vibration properties of CLT. It was found that many research papers request further investigations
and experiments, and the amount of Danish experiments were very limited.
In 2010, a research project was presented by Hamm, Richter, and Winter [41]. The research included both
in-situ measurements of ﬂoors in real buildings and laboratory tests. In the research, the frequency, deﬂection,
acceleration, and velocity were measured and assigned to the subjective evaluation in order to develop improved
rules for design and construction. 38 ﬂoors of massive wood were tested in real buildings with heavy screed,
light screed, and some without any ﬂoor ﬁnish. In nearly all cases the measured frequencies were clearly greater
than the calculated ones, due to wrong assumptions for the calculations. The supports were assumed pinned
in the calculation, but were, in fact, acting as a torsional spring (semi-rigid connection) due to the walls in
the above stories. It was also found that non-bearing partition walls have a positive inﬂuence on the vibration
behaviour as the walls act stiffening. It was found that there was not sufﬁcient correlation between neither the
calculated nor the measured natural frequency and the subjective evaluation of the behaviour of the ﬂoors. The
research states that there is no doubt that the natural frequency is important, however, with a need for additional
criteria to prove the vibration behaviour. The research suggested a frequency criterion, a stiffness criterion, and
an additional examination of the acceleration if the frequency is less than the limit value.
A study by L. Hu and S. Gagnon [28] based on laboratory tests and subjective evaluations of CLT ﬂoors with
different ﬂoor thickness, spans, type of between-element joints, connections, and support conditions was pub-
lished in 2012. The study found that the measured damping ratio for bare CLT ﬂoors did not vary from one
assembly to another. It was quite constant with a value of around 1%. The paper states that it has been well-
recognised that determining the damping ratio accurately, and to a certain degree of reliability, is challenging as
it is controlled by ﬂoor construction details such as joints, connections, and support conditions. Non-structural
components such as partitions, ﬂooring, furniture, etc. also affect the damping.
Another study by K. Jarnero, A. Brandt, and A. Olesson [33] from 2015, found that damping of CLT ﬂoors
with and without light or heavy top ﬂooring varied between 2% and 4% which was depending on whether
the ﬂoor was supported on two or four sides. Floors supported on two sides revealed a damping ratio of 2%
independently of the type of top ﬂooring.
Existing design methods, such as the 1kN static deﬂection and the fundamental frequency, have proven accurate
predictions of CLT ﬂoor vibration performance [47]. However, these design methods generally assume simple
support conditions, which often deviates from the actual ﬂoor boundary conditions. Assuming simply supported
3
==PAGE:22==
conditions will simplify calculations, but also lead to conservative ﬂoor design. It has been widely recognized
that the in-situ boundary conditions signiﬁcantly affect the CLT ﬂoor performance [5].
A laboratory study from the National University of Ireland in 2018 investigated the inﬂuence of different screw
and bracket arrangements on the deﬂection and dynamic properties of CLT ﬂoors. The study found that the
addition of closely spaced brackets increased the fundamental frequency by up to 6% while reducing the static
point load deﬂection by up to 11%. Increasing screw ﬁxings only improved the frequency results marginally.
The inﬂuence of added mass was also measured and showed to reduce the natural frequencies by over 25% [10].
For satisfactory sound insulation, topping and suspended ceilings are often a necessity [9]. The effects of
various topping and ceiling details on vibration performance of CLT have been studied by L. Hu [37]. Adding
a ceiling system to the CLT ﬂoor meant a 22% increase in the ﬂoor mass which reduced the fundamental
frequencies signiﬁcantly. Furthermore, it was observed that the ceiling system did not increase the stiffness of
the ﬂoor system, as almost no changes in the measured 1.0 kN static deﬂection were detected after the ceiling
was installed. The modal damping ratio of the ﬂoor was signiﬁcantly increased from 1% to 3%, resulting in
improved vibration acceptability indicated by improved subjective rating. In the same study by L. Hu [37] it was
found that wood topping improved the stiffness of the ﬂoor, which was indicated by a reduced static deﬂection
of the ﬂoor with topping. The topping added 15% mass to the ﬂoor which reduced the ﬂoor frequencies.
However, the additional stiffness provided by the topping ultimately resulted in very little change in the natural
frequencies. The topping did not change the ﬂoor damping ratio, but the additional stiffness improved the
subjective rating of the ﬂoor.
Damping elastomers are often used in lightweight wooden constructions to ensure good sound insulation. A
study from 2012 by Å. Blomsvik and A. Brandt [5] investigated the effect on ﬂoor vibrations when using an
elastomer damping material (Sylomer®) in the joints. It was found that the average damping levels increased
from 1.2% to 2.1% when elastomers were added to the joints of the lightweight timber framework.
This present thesis will continue the investigation of the inﬂuence of support conditions on the vibration prop-
erties of CLT and address the correlation between frequency and perceived comfort.

Theory of structural dynamics
This chapter presents the general theory behind structural dynamics, load models, and signal processing. It
covers the behaviour of systems subjected to dynamic loads and the vibration properties such as frequency,
damping, and accelerations.
2.1

The simplest way to model a structure is with a single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) system, see ﬁgure 2.1. The
idealised system consists of a lumped mass m, a spring k, and a damper c. The spring and damper are considered
massless. The displacement of the SDOF system is only possible in one direction.

2.2
Multi-degree-of-freedom system
Structures in the real world are multi-degree-of-freedom (MDOF) systems, however, it is still important to know
the theory behind the single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) system, as an MDOF system can be represented by a
linear superposition of a number of SDOF characteristics [25].
An MDOF system is described as a system that requires multiple coordinates to explain the motions. The
number of degrees of freedom of a system is dependent on how many independent displacements that are
required to deﬁne the displacement positions of each mass relative to the original position [11].
The linear EOM for an MDOF system is an extension of the EOM of the SDOF system and is represented as:
M¨u + C ˙u + Ku = p(t)
(2.6)
where M is the mass matrix, C is the damping matrix, and K is the stiffness matrix. p(t) is the excitation
force applied to the system represented by a vector. The system consists of n ordinary differential equation that
determines the displacement u(t).
The dynamic response of an MDOF system can be analysed by modal analysis. The aim of a modal analysis is
to transform the differential equation into an uncoupled form of mode shape vectors to ﬁnd the solution of the
coupled equations [36]. The response can be determined individually for each vibration mode and subsequently,
the responses can be combined to calculate the total response of the structure. The modes of each vibration have
their own pattern of deformations which is called the mode shape. The modal responses can be treated as
individual SDOF systems of speciﬁc modes with vibrations properties, natural frequency, and damping [11].

2.5
Load model
The term dynamic can simply be deﬁned as time-varying. Dynamic loads are thus any load of which its mag-
nitude, direction, and/or position varies with time. In general, dynamic loads can be categorized as either har-
monic, periodic, transient, or impulsive dependent on their time function [1]. The simplest form of a dynamic
load is a harmonic load which is varying according to a sine function. Periodic loads have a time variation that
is repeated at regular intervals, e.i. periods. Human motion such as walking can be characterized as a periodic
load. Any periodic load can be represented as the sum of a series of simple harmonic components by means of a
Fourier analysis [42]. Transient loads exhibit an arbitrary time variation that is not repeated at regular intervals
10
==PAGE:29==
and can be caused by e.g. earthquakes, waves, or wind. Impulsive loads are also of a transient nature, but their
duration is very short. Impulse loads could be caused by e.g. a sudden collapse of a load-bearing member,
construction works, or impact such as a crash of a vehicle [1].
This thesis only considers the vertical human-induced loads such as walking and running as they are the most
common excitation of ﬂoor structures. Walking and running motions are complex and have been studied ex-
tensively. Walking or running motion can be characterised by the pacing rate, the forward speed, and the time
function of the loading [1]. Figure 2.4 shows an example of the load-time function of walking and running

2.8
Signal processing
The purpose of doing signal processing is to amplify the physical properties of a system from the quantity of
measured data [51]. The physical properties sought are e.g. the frequencies and mode shapes of a system,
which can be found by identifying the correlation function or the spectral densities. Both functions are based on
a Fourier transformation and thus containing the same information. The correlation function is a time domain
function and the spectral density is a frequency domain function [51].
Before the correlation function or the spectral density can be obtained, the signal should be detrended and/or
ﬁltered to remove noise from the signal. Different ﬁlter types are available e.g. high- and low bandpass ﬁlters
and FFT ﬁlter [51]. Due to excessive noise in the low frequencies from the direct current (DC) component in
a signal, a detrending can be made. By detrending a signal the offset from the DC is removed which will give
the signal a mean value of zero. One way to perform a detrending of a system with small errors is by dividing
the signal up into small segments and remove the mean from each segment. This can be done with a tapping
window e.g. the Hanning window [51].
It is preferred to identify the frequency of a system from the spectral density as it is a visual method to see if the
modal peaks are presented clearly. A Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) is a fast computational algorithm for a
Discrete Fourier Transformations (DFT) used to convert the recorded response data from the time domain to the
frequency domain [46]. The DFT method divides the time signal into sine and cosine waves, which is possible
since it applies to all non-linear functions that they can be represented as a sum of sine and cosine waves [45].
The Fourier transformation will then return the frequency of each sine wave as a plot in the frequency domain.
17
==PAGE:36==
The principle is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.8. The FFT downscales the DFT into smaller DFTs which allow a much
faster transformation [45].
Figure 2.8: Principe of fast fast Fourier transform [40]
The transformation of a time series using an FFT results in pairs of complex conjugated, including negative
frequencies which are not a physical phenomenon [51]. However, the function is symmetric, and thus the same
information can be obtained from the positive frequencies. The FFT aims to plot the response in the frequency
domain and represents the energy or power content of a signal at different frequencies. The energy related
to each frequency band can be plotted in a power spectrum. Since resonance occurs when the frequency of
excitation coincides with the natural frequency of the ﬂoor, peaks of the plot will identify the natural frequencies
of a system [45].
For random vibration, it is desired to use the power spectral density (PSD) to evaluate the response, because the
amplitude is normalized to the width of the frequency bin. By normalising the amplitude the result becomes
independent of the frequency bin width, making it possible to compare vibration levels of different signal lengths
[46]. For a signal of measured acceleration the unit is g2, and the PSD would therefore be expressed in the unit
of g2/Hz [52].
According to Parseval’s Theorem, energy is preserved when a signal is transformed from the time domain to
the frequency domain and thus the total energy in the time domain equals the total energy in the frequency
domain. As mentioned, the frequency is doubled-sided and symmetric, and thus the energy of the PSD should
be multiplied by two, to reach the same energy as in the time domain.

Human-induced vibrations
Human-induced vibrations are rarely a concern for the structural integrity but can often impair the serviceability
of a building [1]. According to DS/EN 1990 [17], the comfort of the users and function of the structure should
be considered to achieve a satisfactory vibration behaviour of buildings. Large velocities and accelerations are
known to cause disturbance and discomfort for occupants, and this chapter will present the acceptance criteria
related to human comfort. The comfort criteria are based on frequency-weighted values, which will also be
explained.
3.1
Frequency weighting
How vibrations affect health, comfort, perception, and motion sickness is dependent on the frequency content
and direction of the vibrations [22]. Vibrations in some frequencies will be perceived as more annoying than
others. To account for this, when evaluating the serviceability of a ﬂoor, the vibration should be frequency
weighted. The perception and the comfort criteria of vibrations are most often evaluated by acceleration in m/s2
or in dB. The frequency weighting curves for different directions given in dB are shown in ﬁgure 3.1. The
frequency weighting curve Wk is for motions in the z-direction, Wd is for motions in the x- and y-direction
while Wf is for the evaluation of the effects of motion sickness, see ﬁgure 3.2. Vertical ﬂoor vibrations, from
human walking, should be frequency weighted by Wk.
Figure 3.1 shows that the frequency at which humans are more sensitive to vibrations depends on the direction
of motion. It can be seen from the Wk curve that humans are more sensitive to vibrations in the z-direction in
the frequency range between 4Hz and 8Hz. The concept of frequency weighting is also well-known in the ﬁeld
of acoustic, where objective sound measurements are correlated to subjective human response [9].

3.2
Comfort criteria
Human response to vibration is very complex and the comfort level of structural vibrations is subjective and
inﬂuenced by multiple factors, such as body position, individual perception, situation, duration, the activity of
the affected person, social, and other environmental factors [1]. Generally, people tend to be more sensitive to
vibrations caused by others than those induced by one’s own activity. Continuous and intermittent vibrations
are often less tolerable than sudden impact vibrations of the same magnitude [1]. Human-induced vibrations
caused by walking e.g. in an ofﬁce can be considered continuous or intermittent, as the action is expected to
occur several times per day [32]. Different comfort criteria apply for different situations, for example, more
strict criteria apply for laboratories compared to ﬁtness centers and grandstands. Numerous publications and
codes of practice have attempted to create the most realistic criteria for evaluating the physiological effects of
vibrations [1]. In the following the comfort criteria from ISO 10137 [30], DS/EN 1995-1-1 [21], and its revision
of subtask 7 will be presented.
3.2.1
Comfort criteria in the current EC5
According to the current Eurocode DS/EN 1995-1-1 [21] (EC5), it must be ensured that actions on a member,
component or structure do not cause vibrations that impair the function of the structure or causes disturbance
and discomfort to the occupants. The vibration performance is to be evaluated based on measured or calculated
vibration magnitudes such as accelerations, velocities, and displacements. In order to avoid resonance, it is
recommended to have a sufﬁcient distance between the excitation frequency and the fundamental frequency of
the ﬂoor structure.

Cross laminated timber
Cross laminated timber, also referred to as CLT, originates from Austria where it was developed based on a
research request on how they could use the trees lining the Austrian valleys as a construction material instead
of scrapping ballast from the slopes and riverbeds to produce concrete [34]. The request was assigned to the
Austrian universities and CLT was developed as kreuzlagerholz in the early 1990s. The ﬁrst production of CLT
was made in 1995 [44]. At the beginning of the 2000s, the use of CLT in Europe increased signiﬁcantly, mainly
driven by the green movement of the building industry together with improved production efﬁciencies, product
approvals, and more knowledge of the material. Today CLT is used worldwide [27].
CLT is a massive wood element constructed by spruce slats of strength class C16 or C30 glued together in an odd
number of layers. Each layer is glued together cross-wise at an angle of 90 degrees, making it an orthogonally
layered material. The wood panels used for the elements have typically a thickness of 20-45mm and a width
ranging from 40mm to 300mm [44].
Figure 4.1: CLT composition [44]
27
==PAGE:46==
4.1
Production
CLT elements are produced with a new technology, which bonds the wood panels under high pressure with
adhesives approved for load-bearing timber elements. The individual boards are connected by a ﬁnger joint at
the end of each panel. For the production of CLT, the moisture content of the wood should be 12%, (+/- 2%)
in accordance with the European Technical Assessment (ETA), to avoid cracking of the surface and limit the
dimensional variation [34].
CLT elements are most often produced project-speciﬁc, where the elements are cut using state-of-art CNC tech-
nology, with pre-cut holes for windows, doors, etc. With CNC technology the shape and dimensions of the
elements are very ﬂexible within a limit of 16.5m x 3.5m [34], though the size is often restricted by transporta-
tion requirements.
4.2
Material properties
Wood as a material is an orthotropic material, which implies that the strength properties differ depending on
the angle to the ﬁbres. This also applies to CLT, which should be considered as an orthotropic plate material
with three perpendicular main directions each with different strength properties [29]. In general, wood is very
sensitive to moisture as the material is either shrinking or expanding depending on an increase or decrease in
the moisture content. CLT elements are, however, less affected by moisture, due to the cross-wise structure.
Another advantage of the cross-wise composition of a CLT element is that it levels out the variation in wood
and thus minimising the difference in strength properties [34].
The material properties of CLT depend on several parameters such as the grade of the wood panels, the type of
adhesives, and whether the wood panels are edge glued or not. The strength properties should be determined
by tests according to the product standard DS/EN 16351 Cross Laminated Timber [15], however, the standard
is not harmonised [31]. In Europe, all products, which are not included in a harmonised standard, can apply for
an ETA to obtain a CE-mark of a product. An ETA describes the technical speciﬁcations of a product and is
especially suitable for building materials with a high complexity [24].
The material proprieties, for CLT produced by Binderholz Brettsperrholz BBS, are based on the ETA issued
02.06.2017 [12] and technical data provided by Binderholz [4], is listed in table 4.1. The material properties

4.3
Vibration methods of CLT
The vibration of a ﬂoor structure depends primarily on the boundary conditions and material properties e.g.
support condition and stiffness of the system [13]. Existing methods for vibration control design of light- and
heavy-weight ﬂoors are not applicable for CLT due to the mass and natural frequency. Traditional light-weight
structures of wood joisted ﬂoors have a mass of approximately 20kg/m2 and a natural frequency above 15Hz,
where the mass of a concrete ﬂoor is approximately 200kg/m2 with a natural frequency below 8Hz. The mass
of a CLT ﬂoor varies from 50-150kg/m2 and has a natural frequency above 9Hz [13].

Finite element model
This chapter describes how a ﬁnite element model (FE-model) can be used to predict the dynamic response of
a structure. A description of how to manually model speciﬁc material properties using a stiffness matrix will
also be presented. An FE-model is created in Autodesk Robot Structural Analysis (RSA) to predict the static
deﬂection and fundamental frequency of elements. Furthermore, it will be explained how a time history analysis
can be used to simulate the load from a walking person.
5.1
Stiffness matrix for CLT
When modeling a structure in a ﬁnite element method (FEM) program, the boundary conditions and material
properties are important to deﬁne. Often the FEM programs have lots of predeﬁned materials which can be
assigned to the structure. However, the complex properties of CLT have not yet been implemented in many
of the commonly used FEM software applications. The properties of a material can be assigned manually
by a stiffness matrix. As mention in section 4, CLT is an orthotropic material which implies that the material
properties differ depending on the direction. In addition, CLT consist of laminated surfaces which also inﬂuence
on the stiffness. CLT is both a laminated and an orthotropic material, and therefore, the stiffness matrix will
vary depending on the composition of the CLT element [49].

Methods
The experimental test methods used for determining the vibrations properties will be described in this chapter.
The method follows the test procedures speciﬁed in the European standard DS/EN 16929 Test methods - Timber
ﬂoors - Determination of vibration [16]. To evaluate the vibration properties of the ﬂoor, four different tests
were performed; a deﬂection test, a free vibration test, and two different forced vibration tests. Additionally, a
random walking test was performed to simulate a more realistic situation.
6.1
Laboratory test facilities
The vibration tests were performed at the Technical University of Denmark at the Department of Civil En-
gineering. The tests were conducted in the concrete- and material laboratories in building 130. During the
test period, the laboratory was only used for the following vibration tests, to avoid disturbing vibrations from
external sources and huge ﬂuctuations in temperature and humidity.

Free vibration test
Free vibration tests were performed by introducing an impulse load to the slab, to make the ﬂoor vibrate freely

interfering with the ﬂoor. To keep the background noise to a minimum, the supporting structure should have a
stiffness that is considerably higher than the stiffness of the ﬂoor, which was ensured by using a steel frame for
the reference support S0. The test setup for the free decay test can be seen in ﬁgure 6.15. The load was applied
as an impulse load from an impact hammer at the geometrical centre of the ﬂoor.

the validity of the experiments, three consecutive tests were performed. It was controlled that the element had
stopped vibrating before a new test was commenced.

Forced vibration test

equipped with a loaded backpack to ensure a total weight of 70kg.
For the test excited by continues stepping, the stepping person was standing at mid-span of the ﬂoor. The
stepping person was exciting the structure at a frequency of 2Hz, which was ensured by a metronome and
recorded for 30s. Figure 6.16 shows the stepping at mid-span.

8.5.2
Weighting acceleration
The acceleration measurements were frequency weighted according to ISO 1631-1 [22] where the data was
ﬁltered by a high- and low-pass second-order Butterworth ﬁlter before it was frequency weighted as described
in section 3.1. The ﬁlters were applied to the sampled time history using the MATLAB code in Appendix A.6.
Figure 8.10 shows the difference between the unweighted- and the weighted acceleration for both a stepping-
and walking test. It can be observed for both tests that the disturbing noise was left out when the acceleration was
ﬁltered. In ﬁgure 8.10a, the acceleration from the unweighted stepping test shows large peaks indicating each
step, whereas the peaks were removed by the ﬁlter function in the weighted acceleration response. The weighted
acceleration from the walking test, seen on ﬁgure 8.10b, was slightly smaller compared to the unweighted
acceleration, however, peaks were not eliminated.

Discussion
In the present thesis, the vibration properties of four CLT elements have been investigated and throughout
this chapter, the results obtained from the experiments and the RSA model will be discussed and compared to
existing literature.
Through laboratory testing, it was found that the EC5 is not applicable in its current form. The vibration design
method in the EC5 is solely based on point load deﬂection and a unit impulse velocity requirement, which
was found to be inadequate when predicting the vibration performance. This study found a need for additional
veriﬁcation of acceleration or velocity, in order to properly estimate the performance level of a CLT ﬂoor, as
proposed in the revised rEC5. Before new vibration requirements are implemented, special attention should be
given to the vibration comfort of CLT ﬂoors.
Several studies have shown that support conditions have a crucial effect on the vibration properties, which were
tested in this study by evaluating seven different support conditions. The different test setups were made as an
attempt to simulate real structures based on the CLT producers guidance [43].
From the determination of the end restrain, a relationship between the end ﬁxity factor and the frequency
and deﬂection was seen which underlines that the vibration properties of ﬂoors are dependant on the support
conditions. However, it turned out that the deviation of the end ﬁxity of the different support conditions was
very small, and thus it has been difﬁcult to see a signiﬁcant difference between the tests. The small deviation
of the end ﬁxity could either be caused by too little variation of the support conditions or that the span length
dominates the effect of the end ﬁxity. A future study could investigate the relation between the effect of the end
restrain and the span length. The greatest effect on the vibration properties was seen at the ﬂoor supported on
the elastic intermediate layer. A signiﬁcantly lower frequency was observed, in addition to a higher damping
ratio and deﬂection, compared to the remaining test setups. The high deﬂection measured at setup S2 was
expected due to the Sylomer®layer, as the total deﬂection consisted of a contribution of both the compression
101
==PAGE:120==
of the elastic intermediate layer and the deﬂection of the ﬂoor. The high damping ratio is assumed to be caused
by the elastic nature of the intermediate layer where additional energy is dissipated, while the low frequency is
attributed to the change in stiffness of the support.
The results from the test setups, simulating the weight of an above structure, were not as expected. The de-
ﬂection and frequency were slightly improved for the test with 200kg as predicted but the measured damping
was signiﬁcantly lower compared to the test with only 100kg. There were no clear reasons for this, but some
uncertainties were related to the setup. The weights were carefully stacked on top of each other but might have
been vibrating during the tests due to their uneven shape. This could have been avoided by only using one
single weight element. Furthermore, higher loads could have been applied to see a more clear effect of adding
additional load to the supports. 200 kg corresponds to 3.17kN/m, which is considerably lower than the weight of
an above structure, and thus a lower degree of end ﬁxity was reached compared to a real structure. The support
condition with two screws followed the expected tendency. The screws improve the vibration properties of the
ﬂoor slightly compared to the simply supported condition but no difference was seen when additional 2 screws
were applied.
For further investigation of the relationship between support conditions and vibration properties, a more re-
strained support condition should be ensured to achieve a greater deviation between the results. Moreover,
in-situ tests could be performed to achieve realistic end ﬁxity factors which could be accounted for in the theo-
retical calculations. In this study, only bare CLT elements were investigated and the effects of a ﬂoor build-up
and/or suspended ceiling were not considered. It is reasonable to assume that the additional mass and stiffness
could change the vibration properties and improve the comfort of the ﬂoor.
The apparent bending stiffness of the four elements were tested and compared to theoretically values. It was
found that the mean Young’s modules and the moment of inertia calculated by the Gamma method gave the best
prediction of the actual stiffness of the ﬂoors. The density of the elements was measured and was found to be
slightly above the mean density of C24 timber. The experimental investigations of the study was limited to four
CLT elements and since timber is known to have a relatively large variation of its material properties, a larger
sample size should be investigated for more conclusive results.
In the current EC5, the damping ratio of timber ﬂoors is assumed to be ζ = 1% unless other values are proven to
be more appropriate. However, as stated in the background document in appendix A.9, a signiﬁcant number of
studies have found this value to be low compared to modern CLT ﬂoors. In the present study, the damping ratio
was found to vary between 0.4% and 1.30%, for the reference support and the test on the elastic intermediate
layer, respectively. The measured damping ratio was signiﬁcantly lower than the damping ratios proposed in
the rEC5, which is 2.5% for CLT ﬂoors without a ﬂoating ﬂoor. The reasons for this low damping in the test
102
==PAGE:121==
specimens have not been discovered but could relate to the width of the elements. With an element width of
0.62m the ﬂoor might act as a beam instead of a ﬂoor.
Even though a relationship between the support condition and the vibration properties was seen in the free
vibration tests, a similar relationship could not be found in the forced vibration tests. However, the stepping
test showed an expected decrease of acceleration when additional screws and weight were applied, which again
supports the thesis that boundary conditions can improve the ﬂoor performance. Less correlation between
the support conditions and rms acceleration in the walking test was found. Surprisingly, the weighted rms
acceleration of the walking test was increased by up to 48% compared to the stepping test when calculated
over 1s. In the light of this observation, the test procedure according to DS/EN 16929 [16] for determining
the acceleration of a ﬂoor, was unconservative. The same observation could not be veriﬁed by other literature
but could be due to the stepping person exciting and standing in the same position and thus absorbing some
vibrations. Another reason for the increased acceleration from walking could be caused by a difference in the
force function of stepping and walking. When walking, the step consists of a heel impact and a pushing to get a
forward propulsion, whereas when stepping in the same position, the vibrations are continuously interrupted by
the consecutive steps. It should be noted that the increase was found when only observing a 1s interval around
the peak acceleration and that comfort is also associated with the duration of the vibration.
In an attempt to simulate a more realistic scenario, a random walking test was conducted and it was observed
that the acceleration was signiﬁcantly larger in the random walking test compared to the regular walking test.
This could be explained by the difference between weight, pace, and footwear of the test participants from the
random and regular walking tests. It was found that the largest accelerations were not provoked by the heaviest
person, but by the person wearing high-heeled shoes. All other participants wore a form of sneaker shoes and
it is therefore assumed that the hard unabsorbent sole of the high-heeled shoes caused the larger vibrations.
Furthermore, the participant took shorter, and therefore more, steps across the ﬂoor.
Two different load models were created in RSA; a rhythmic crowd load of one person walking, as suggested
by DS/EN 1991-1-1 DK NA [19], and a series of effective impulses, suggested by Willford, Young and Field
[39]. The series of effective impulses gave a more accurate prediction of the actual transient ﬂoor response.
However, the load model idealises the reality as only one walking pace is considered. In reality, the force and
pace of the individual step would vary, causing a different response of the structure. It was found that the load
model suggested by the 1991-1-1 DK NA [19] could not give an accurate response of a transient ﬂoor which is
concerning and caution should be taken when evaluating the response of a ﬂoor using a time history analysis.

Conclusions
The aim of this study was to investigate the vibration properties of Cross Laminated Timber (CLT) and examine
ways of minimising disturbing ﬂoor vibrations caused by human motion.
Modern CLT ﬂoors are not speciﬁcally mentioned in the current design codes and thus it has been examined
whether the criteria are applicable for CLT. This study found that it is inaccurate to validate the ﬂoor vibrations
based solely on the vibration criteria from the Eurocode 5, seeing that the criteria were satisﬁed while the
comfort criteria, according to ISO 10137 [30], were exceeded. This inconsistency emphasises the need for
additional requirements as suggested by Hamm, Richter and Winter [41].
A revision of the vibration subtask 7 of the Eurocode 5 evaluates the comfort based on either an acceleration
or velocity criteria as supplement to the frequency and stiffness demands. It was concluded that additional re-
quirements were needed, as the frequency and stiffness demands were satisﬁed, while the additional demands,
ultimately, deemed the ﬂoor performance unacceptable. It was found that the method for estimating the accel-
eration and velocity were more accurate and also more conservative than the current code regulations.
From the laboratory experiments it was concluded that the support conditions had an effect on the vibration
properties, although not enough to satisfy the comfort criteria. It was found that the elastic intermediate layer
for acoustic insulation had the largest effect on the vibration properties where the fundamental frequency was
decreased and the damping ratio was increased. An unexpected low damping ratio ranging from 0.40% to
1.30% was found, which is signiﬁcantly lower than the 1% suggested by the current Eurocode 5 and the 2.5%
suggested by the revised Eurocode 5.
Furthermore, it was concluded that an ﬁnite element model could be created to estimate the deﬂection and
the fundamental frequency of the ﬂoor but special attention should be given to the excitation function when
performing a time history analysis of a transient ﬂoor. It was established that the load model suggested in
DS/EN 1991-1-1 DK NA [19] did not represent an accurate response of a high-frequency ﬂoor.
105
==PAGE:124==
In summation, based on the ﬁndings of this master thesis, the authors recommend using the mean Young’s
modulus and the Gamma method for calculation of the apparent bending stiffness. The authors advise against
using the current Eurocode 5 and the rhythmical crowd load function, suggested by the DS/EN 1991-1-1 DK NA
[19], for vibration control of a transient CLT ﬂoor. To acheive a more accurate and satisfactory prediction, it is
instead recommended to use additional acceleration and velocity requirements, and to use an effective impulse
load function.
