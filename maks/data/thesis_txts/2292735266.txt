PREFACE 
The present thesis was prepared at the Department of Electrical Engineering at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in partial fulfilment of the requirements for ac-
quiring the Master of Science in Engineering, MSc (Eng.) degree in Electrical Engineer-
ing with specialization in Electric Energy Systems. The MSc project was conducted in 
collaboration with Siemens Danmark A/S. The main scope of this thesis is an investiga-
tion of the optimal allocation of synchronous condensers in a low inertia power system 
which is inspired by the future Danish power system.

ABSTRACT 
The global climate change seen in the last decades has resulted in an expansion in 
power generation from renewable energy sources, where in particular Denmark has set 
very ambitious goals for the future power generation. The positive impact from this 
transition are clearly seen, however, this transition also entails new challenges for the 
power system operators, and one of these is low system inertia. Low inertia power sys-
tems entails lack of sufficient short-circuit power contribution.  
 A potential solution to this is the synchronous condenser, which is a large generator 
without a mechanical load, hence generating no active power, however offering many 
services to the system operators, like short-circuit power contribution. The present study 
investigates the ideal allocation of synchronous condenser in a low inertia power sys-
tem, which is inspired by the characteristics of the transmission system in the western 
part of Denmark. The ideal allocation is defined as the allocation that can provide the 
highest short-circuit contribution when a three-phase fault occurs, from a system per-
spective. Five candidate systems are modelled in Simulink, which contains two different 
types of synchronous condensers at two different locations and the fifth system has no 
synchronous condenser. The system dynamics are solved in Simulink and subsequently 
the short-circuit contributions are exported to MATLAB where the differential evolu-
tion algorithm was selected as the solution method to the optimization problem.  
 The results from this thesis show that synchronous condensers can play an important 
role in low inertia power systems, by ensuring the provision of short-circuit power to 
the system. Furthermore, it is found that the location of the synchronous condenser is 
the most vital parameter in these studies, as the same location was selected in all three 
case studies. The results also showed that the closer the synchronous condenser was to 
the fault, the higher contribution was provided, this finding is believed to be very inter-
esting as this means that future investigations could be conducted to find the most criti-
cal parts in a power system from a short-circuit contribution perspective. The type of 
synchronous condenser was also shown to have an effect on the results. However, the 
difference in contribution was found to be relatively insignificant,  when looking at the 
chosen type compared to the difference between locations, which again showed that the 
location of the synchronous condenser plays a vital part in future power system struc-
tures. Thus the study presented in this thesis shows the importance of having synchro-
nous condensers installed in a low inertia system, at least when investigating the prob-
lem from a short-circuit contribution perspective.

INTRODUCTION 
1.1 
Background 
The global climate change seen in the last decades has resulted in a transition in the 
power generation portfolio. The transition has led to an expansion in power generation 
from renewable energy sources. This development is expected to further increase in the 
coming years through global political initiatives. In particular, Denmark has set very 
ambitious goals for the future power generation [1]. This transition has an obvious posi-
tive impact on the environment as the power generation from fossil fuels is reduced as a 
consequence of the added renewables. However, the transition entails new challenges 
within the power system generation and transmission, where one challenge is to main-
tain the voltage within the acceptable limits. Furthermore, renewable energy sources are 
typically placed at long distances from the loads. Thus having difficulties in providing a 
sufficient amount of reactive power near the loads. Finally, the coupling of renewables 
by use of power electronics means a reduced amount of power system inertia, this leads 
to a low inertia system, which results in difficulties in providing a sufficient amount of 
short-circuit power. One solution to these challenges in the future power system is to 
implement synchronous condensers in the power system.  
A synchronous condenser is a synchronous machine running without a mechanical load, 
hence the shaft is spinning freely. By controlling the field excitation, a synchronous 
condenser can either be absorbing or generating reactive power. As such, the installation 
of synchronous condensers offers many benefits to a power system operator, e.g. 
steady- state and dynamic voltage control, reactive power control of dynamic loads and 
provision of short-circuit power.  
 
1.2 
Problem statement 
In extension to the above, the aim of this master project is to develop an algorithm that 
can optimally locate the ideal placement of synchronous condensers while taking the 
operators’ requirements into account. Such as, voltage security, operational efficiency 
and short-circuit power provision when the system experience a three-phase fault. The 
main tasks of the master project are as follows:

 Modelling of a power system, which has a high penetration level of renewable 
sources. This model is inspired by a future Danish power system and the simula-

 Apply evolutionary algorithms (EA’s) for identification of the optimum alloca-

1.3 
Approach 
As mentioned in the above, the study conducted in this thesis has three main objectives, 
these are motivated by future issues faced by transmission system operators in the pro-
vision of short-circuit contribution. The following describes the major contributions of 
the thesis: 
 
Modelling of power system:  
A low inertia power system is modelled in Simulink, primarily due to its ability of eas-
ily importing and exporting data with MATLAB, which was chosen at the software in 
which the algorithm should be modelled. All of the components in the power system are 
standard blocks from the Simulink library in which the original data are kept. In the cer-
tain cases where parameter values are changed an explanation to this follows. The syn-
chronous condenser selected is a synchronous generator where the active power output 
is adjusted to zero.  
Only one type of renewable energy generation technology is installed in the system, 
which is represented by the type-C (DFIG) wind turbine. This selection was motivated 
by two factors, namely, to limit the complexity of the modelled power system, and sec-
ondly since the DFIGs connection to the system makes it interesting from a short-circuit 
contribution as it provides inertia to the system. The wind farms are adopted from the 
power_wind_dfig  model in Simulink. The original data from this model is kept, besides 
from increasing the wind characteristics, hence adjusting the wind to increase at the ini-
tialization of the simulation.

Problem formulation & methodology:  
The study in this thesis finds the optimum allocation of a synchronous condenser in the 
context of maximizing the short-circuit power contribution from a system perspective. 
This entails that the scope of the maximization is to both determine the most attractive 
location and type of the synchronous condenser in a low inertia system, from the deter-
mination of the highest short-circuit contribution.  
Originally the study was conducted as a dynamic optimization problem, however the 
computation time was found to be too excessive, primarily due to the integration of the 
wind farms in Simulink. Therefore, the optimization problem was reassessed and a 
static optimization was selected. In this optimization the system dynamics are pre calcu-
lated in Simulink in prior to the optimization. This significantly lowered the computa-
tion time as the dynamics in the system is not evaluated each time the objective function 
is called. However, this simplification also have a negative impact as the system dynam-
ics in some way is lost from pre calculations. However, as none of the dynamic con-
straints or stability issues are investigated, the consequences of choosing the static opti-
mization is not found to effect the main findings of this thesis.  
 
Algorithm:  
The optimization algorithm is modelled in MATLAB, and the differential evolution al-
gorithm, which is a known branch of the evolutionary algorithms, is chosen as the solu-
tion method to the optimization problem. The differential evolution was chosen based 
on previous results in complex optimization, the algorithm is found to be robust and 
performing well.

POWER SYSTEM OVERVIEW 
The electric power system has been a vital part of society since Thomas Edison first in-
troduced it in 1882. However, it has evolved greatly since then and it is continuously 
developing towards the most sustainable solution is an ongoing project. Electric power 
systems can vary in structure and size, but all systems are governed by the same basic 
characteristics [2, p.5-6]: 
- 
Three-phase AC system operating at essentially constant voltage lev-
els. 
- 
Synchronous machines mainly produce the electric power. Prime mov-
ers of the system converts their primary source of energy into mechan-
ical energy, which is subsequently converted into electrical energy by 
synchronous generators.  
- 
The electric power is transmitted to consumers that are spread over a 
wide area.  
 
The fundamental elements for an electric power system is further elaborated in the fol-
lowing section.  
 
2.1 
Power System Structure 
The electric power system is commonly classified into three subsystems, namely: the 
transmission-, substransmission-, and distribution system.  
 The transmission system can be seen as the backbone of the power system as it operates 
on the highest voltage level of the system, normally above 230 kV. At this level all the 
major generating stations and load centers are connected. 
 The subtransmission system operates at voltage levels between 69 kV−138 kV, this 
system transmits power from the transmission- to distribution system, thus linking the 
high- and low voltage networks. Larger industrial consumers can be connected to the 
power system at this level.   
 The distribution system operates on the lowest voltage level, typically from 4 kV 
to 34.5 kV, hence the distribution system is the final stage in the steps of providing 
electrical power to the consumers, by the generators of the power system. [2, Ch.1.2] 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
Power System Overview 
16 
Power system control  
In addition to providing electrical energy, a power system should be designed to meet 
some fundamental requirements in order to be operated at the desired level e.g.: 
- 
The system must be capable of meeting a continually changing load 
for both active and reactive power. Electrical energy is not easily 
stored, therefore an adequate “spinning” reserve of active and reactive 
power should be controllable at all times.  
- 
The system should supply energy at minimum cost and with minimum 
environmental impact.  
- 
The quality of power must meet certain standards with regard to fol-
lowing factors: constant frequency, constant voltage and level of relia-
bility [2, Ch.1.3] 
 
To maintain a stable power system, the following must be controlled: Power balance, 
power transfer capabilities and loading capability. The power balance is seen as the bal-
ance between generation and consumption, hence keeping the frequency deviation 
within acceptable limits. Power transfer capabilities refers to the capacity of the compo-
nents in the system, and their capabilities regarding active and reactive power transfer. 
To maintain the above mentioned system properties, the operators of the power system 
have to contract certain services, known as ancillary services. Some of the most known 
ancillary services are:  
- 
Scheduled Power Generation 
- 
Inertial Response  
- 
Frequency Control  
- 
Voltage Control  
- 
Fault Current Contribution/Short-Circuit Contribution. 
 
Scheduled power generation means the planning of generation, hence a central power 
station can be scheduled for keeping a certain power level in certain hours, or con-
versely keep limited or no generation. 
The inertia of the system refers to the stored rotational energy in the generators and it 
reflects the degree at which rotating masses oppose changes to frequency. As such, iner-
tial response refers to the rate of change in frequency after a change in the balance be-
tween supply and demand.  
 Frequency control is a key service for keeping balance in the system, a mismatch be-
tween supply and demand of power is directly seen as a change in frequency throughout 
the whole system. If there is not sufficient generation of power, a decline in the fre-
quency is seen, and vice versa if there is excessive generation. Hence, there is a clear re-
lation between active power and frequency in a power system. This control is also re-
ferred to as load frequency control (LFC), which according to ENTSO-E definitions can 
==PAGE:19==
 
Power System Overview 
17 
be divided into three subcategories, namely; primary-, secondary-, and tertiary fre-
quency control [3]. The primary frequency control is an automatic service, stabilizing 
the frequency in a timeframe of seconds (normally 30 s) handling smaller disturbances. 
The secondary control relieves the primary control, and aims of restoring the frequency 
within 30 s to 15 min. For the most cases, the secondary control is an automatic service. 
Lastly, the tertiary control relieves the secondary control, it is a manual service that is 
handled by the Transmission System Operator (TSO), and it aims to restore the fre-
quency from 15 min up to several hours and handles larger disturbances.  
 Voltage control is another key ancillary service, with the objective to maintain optimal 
operation of the system by keeping the voltage within acceptable limits. Opposite fre-
quency, voltage deviations needs to be investigated on a more local level, as deviations 
can be found between different voltage levels and from its relations with reactive power 
transfer [2, p.254]. This relation means that the voltage is controlled by adjusting the 
amount of reactive power generated. If the voltage level is too high, there is excessive 
amount of reactive power and the opposite is the case if the voltage is below its limits.  
The voltage control can also be divided into two categories: Primary and secondary, 
where primary is automatically controlled reacting within milliseconds normally this are 
handled by voltage controllers close to the set point of the voltage deviation. Secondary 
control is acting in the range of seconds to a minute in bringing the voltage back within 
its limits, the objective is to minimize the reactive power flows and maximize reactive 
reserves wile restoring the voltage level in the region of the voltage deviation [2] [4]. 
 Fault current contribution refers to the amount of current and thus power, a system can 
provide in case of an unexpected fault in the system. The higher amount contribution 
leads to a stronger system, however the contribution should not be so high that it de-
stroys components in the event of a short-circuit [2, Ch.1] [5]. 
  Electric power systems have functioned rather well in the European countries for many 
years, experiencing only few blackouts. However, the chosen way of generating power 
has shown to have severe and critical consequences for the climate, consequently forc-
ing a transition towards renewable generation. 
 
2.2 
Environmental Consequences 
As previously mentioned, electrical power classically have been produced by large syn-
chronous machines that converts their primary source of energy, which in many cases 
are fossil fuels, into electrical energy. The production of electricity supplied from fossil 
fuels has however shown to have severe consequences for the environment, seen with 
ice melting at the poles, threating its wildlife and leading to increased sea levels glob-
ally, which consequently threatens smaller island nations all these factors resulting in 
global warming. These critical effects has resulted in several global political agree-
ments, which aims to change in the technology mix towards more renewable generation 
of electricity. 
==PAGE:20==

 Denmark is in many eyes seen as one of the first movers in this transition towards more 
renewable generation. Denmark has set some very ambitious climate goals where one is 
to have more than 35% of the final energy consumption in 2020 come from renewable 
generation and being totally independent of fossil fuels by 2050 [1] [6]. This transition 
into a greener and more sustainable power generation has some obvious positive im-
pacts on the environment, as the emission of greenhouse gasses is declining. However, 
this transition also entails new challenges for the power system operators in keeping the 
power system balanced.  
Some of these challenges are currently found in the Danish power system and some are 
expected to only become more severe in the future. Therefore, the Danish power system 
is seen as an inspiration for the analysis conducted in this thesis, in the following sub-
sections the characteristics of the Danish power system is elaborated.

The Current Danish Power System

Section 2.1. An interesting aspect about the Danish power system is the division of the 
transmission system into two separate synchronous systems. The western part of Den-
mark, called DK1, is synchronised with the European Union for the Coordination of the 
Transmission of Electricity (UCTE) and the eastern part, called DK2, which is synchro-
nised with Nordic Nordel area. In Figure.1, the different voltage levels in the Danish 
power system are shown.

2.3.2 
Ancillary Services/ Power System Control  
 
 
The Danish TSO ensures that the Danish power system is kept within acceptable limits 
and that the power is balanced at all time. In order to keep the balance in the power sys-
tem, Energinet.dk buys ancillary services from the main suppliers of electricity. As 
mentioned, Denmark is well interconnected with neighboring countries, consequently 
the requirements of ancillary services are regulated by: ENTSOE-E RG Continental Eu-
rope Operation Handbook-Policy, the Joint Nordic System Operation Agreement and by 
Energinet.dk’s regulations for grid connection [9]. 
As already mentioned, the Danish power system is divided into two different regions, 
DK1 and DK2. This splitting means that various ancillary services are provided depend-
ing on the region the analysis is performed in. As mentioned in Section 1.4, this thesis 
focusses on DK1, which entails that only the ancillary services performed in this region 
is further explained, though they are quite similar to the ancillary provision in DK2, In 
DK1 the following ancillary services are provided: 
- 
Primary reserves 
- 
Secondary reserves 
- 
Manual reserves 
- 
Short-circuit power, reactive reserves and voltage control.  
 
Primary reserve ensures that the balance between production and consumption is kept 
within acceptable frequency deviations (± 200 mHZ) around 50 Hz. It is an automatic 
control, like primary frequency control explained in Section 2.1.2 and has a similar time

As aforementioned, the overall goal for the Danish government is that Denmark in 2050

will have a large share of its generation from especially wind turbines. Furthermore, an 
expansion in generation from Photo Voltaic (PV) systems and transmitted power from

in new challenges for Energinet.dk towards: 
-

The goal for the Danish TSO is to guarantee a high level of security in supply, while 
still keeping it as efficient as possible. The concept of security of supply can be divided 
into two subsidiary concepts: system adequacy and system security.  
 System adequacy refers to the power systems ability of meeting the total power demand 
at all times.  
 System security refers to the power systems ability to deal with unexpected disturb-
ances: Short-circuits, loss of transmission lines or loss of generation. Energinet.dk has 
always ensured a high level of security of supply in Denmark, and this remains a top 
goal for the future power system. Central power stations have an important role to play 
in regards to keeping the security of supply high as these has key role in both system ad-
equacy and security [12]. Therefore, it is clear that Energinet.dk faces new challenges in 
keeping a high level of security of supply when the central power stations are being re-
placed by renewable sources. One challenge of renewable generation lies in the fact that 
most of the technologies are connected to the power system by power electronic tech-
nologies. This means the system losses the inertia generated from the conventional syn-
chronous generators and thus becomes a low inertia power system. The concept of low

2.4 
Low Inertia Power Systems 
In the previous section the structure of the Danish power system was described, includ-
ing both the current and the characteristics of a possible future system. As mentioned, 
the decarbonisation of the power system entails new problems for the system operators, 
one of these is low inertia in the power system. 
 Generally, system inertia can be defined as the total amount of kinetic energy stored in 
all spinning turbines and rotors in the system [14]. Conventional large power stations all 
convert mechanical energy to electrical energy by use of a synchronous generator, and 
thus provide a large amount of inertia to system. Low inertia power systems are there-
fore seen when these are being phased out, as seen in Denmark. 
 Many of the renewable technologies are coupled to the grid by use of power electronics 
and thus unable to deliver any inertia to the system, the same is accountable of the inter-
connections, as these primarily are HVDC connections, which again entails that no iner-
tia is provided to the system. At this point is should be noticed that the type-C (DFIG) 
wind turbine does provide some inertia to the power system, as the stator circuit of the  
induction machine is connected to the grid. However, the future might show the type-C 
being replace by the fully converter connected type-D turbine.

SYNCHRONOUS CONDENSER 
Researchers have suggested different solutions for keeping balance in future low inertia 
power systems [14].  One suggestion could be to install synchronous condensers in the 
transmission system. A synchronous condenser has the advantages of being able to ei-
ther produce or consume reactive power, hence keeping voltage stability, in accordance 
with the above description of voltage control. Furthermore, a synchronous condenser 
will provide inertia to the system, and thus increase the strength of the system, and fur-
ther provide short-circuit power when unexpected fault occurs.   
3.1 
Synchronous Condenser Fundamentals 
A Synchronous condenser (SC) is a well-known technology first introduced in 1930s. 
At that time the voltage control capabilities of SCs made them relatively common at the 
transmission level. It was however outcompeted in terms of reactive power compensa-
tion by the power electronics solutions. Such as Static Var Compensators (SVC) and 
STATCOM, these static reactive power devices has lower capital cost and maintenance 
level because these have no rotating parts [15]. However, the SC has experienced in-
creasing interest due to their multiple capabilities in keeping a stable power system in 
addition to reactive power compensation.  
 A SC is a synchronous machine running without a prime mover, hence the shaft is spin-
ning freely. The theory and modelling behind a SC is therefore the same as for a syn-
chronous machine, without the mechanical load. From this close relationship to a syn-
chronous machine, some research [16] suggest that the conventional power stations 
could be refurbished into being a synchronous condenser, thereby reducing initial capi-
tal cost. As stated, a synchronous condenser operates at close to zero active power as 
possible, it does however consume a small amount of active power from the system to 
cover loses. 
 The key element in a synchronous condenser is the ability of controlling the field exci-
tation, as the level of excitation determines if the synchronous condenser generates or 
consumes reactive power. Another important element is the voltage control, by use of a 
voltage regulator a synchronous condenser can automatically regulate the reactive 
power output to keep a constant terminal voltage [2]. Further elaborations on these fol-
lows in the upcoming subsections.

3.2 
Excitation System 
 
The main function of an excitation system is to provide direct current to the field wind-
ings in a synchronous machine. The capabilities of the excitation system can be consid-
ered from both a machine and system perspective. From a machine perspective, the goal 
of the excitation system is to provide and adjust the field current, so that the terminal 
voltage of the generator/condenser is maintained. Whereas, from a system perspective, 
the goal of the excitation system is to contribute to effective voltage control and thus en-
hancing the system stability. As mentioned, a synchronous condenser can either con-
sume or produce reactive power. This quality is controlled by the excitation system of 
the synchronous condenser, if overexcited hence leading power factor, it supplies reac-
tive power and if under-excited hence lagging power factor, it consumes reactive power 
from the system. 
 
Different types of excitation systems exists depending on the requirements for the 
power system, classically these are classified into three different categories [2, p.318]: 
- 
DC excitation  
- 
AC excitation  
- 
Static excitation  
As the DC excitation, by many, is seen superseded when compared to the latter two are 
the basic elements of this excitation system is left out of this thesis. Further elaboration 
on the AC and Static excitation follows next.   
3.2.1 
AC Excitation System  
This type of excitation system uses an AC alternator as source of the main generator ex-
citation power. As the generator field requires a DC current, the AC output of the exci-
tation system is rectified by either a stationary or a rotating solution [17]. These rectifi-
ers can further be either controlled or non-controlled, thus the AC excitation system 
may vary depending on the chosen rectifier solution.  
The stationary rectifier applies slip rings to generate the DC output, if a non-controlled 
solution is chosen, the regulator controls the AC exciter, which then controls the exciter 
output voltage.  If controlled rectifier (thyristors) are applied, the regulator directly con-
trols the DC output voltage of the exciter.  
The rotating rectifier solution is also known as the brushless excitation solution, because 
the need for slip rings and brushes are eliminated. Which simultaneously represents the 
main advantages of the brushless excitation system. These can be avoided by the apply-
ing rotating rectifiers as the DC output then can be directly feed to the main generator 
field. The brushless solution is found to be attractive for very large generators, as these 
are perceived to have issues with the brushes [2, p.322].

3.2.2 
Static Exciter: 
In a static excitation system all the components are stationary, which entails that the 
static rectifier supplies the excitation current directly through slip rings to the field of 
the main generator. The rectifiers in this type of system can also be either controlled or 
uncontrolled. Furthermore, the main generator supplies power to the rectifiers either 
through a step-down transformer or from the auxiliary windings in the generator. Three 
different forms of static excitation system are commonly known and used, namely:  
 
- 
Potential-source controlled-rectifier systems  
- 
Compound-source rectifier systems.  
- 
Compound-controlled rectifier systems.     
==PAGE:30==
 
Synchronous Condenser 
28 
 
  
In potential-source controlled-rectifier systems, the generator power is supplied to the 
excitation system through a transformer from the generator terminals or the stationary 
auxiliary bus. One limitation in this type of system lies in the fact that the maximum 
output voltage of the excitation is dependent on the input voltage. Hence, the excitation 
voltage is reduced during system-fault conditions as the generator terminal voltage is 
weakened. However, the high post fault field-forcing capability, its ease of maintenance 
and the fact that is quite inexpensive should be mentioned as advantages. Lastly, for 
generators connected to the extensive power system this system has been found to be 
operating well [2, p.323].  
 In compound-source rectifier systems, the power to the excitation system is delivered 
by utilization of both the voltage and current of the generator. This is achieved by a 
power potential transformer (PPT) and a saturable-current transformer (SCT). The regu-
lator controls the exciter output by controlling the saturation of the excitation trans-
former. When experiencing system-fault conditions, the current input enables the exciter 
to provide high field-forcing capability.  This type of system are found in General Elec-
tric SCT-PPT solution [2, p.324]. 
 In compound-controlled rectifier systems, the power to the excitation system is pro-
vided by utilizing the controlled rectifiers from the exciter output circuits and the com-
position of the current and voltage-derived sources within the stator of the main genera-
tor. This type of excitation system has a high initial-response with full “fault-on” forc-
ing capability [2, p. 325].

Advantages of Synchronous Condenser in Low Iner-

In this chapter, the basic characteristics of a SC have been explained, and in short, some 
of the key features mentioned. In this section, each of the future problems with low iner-
tia power systems versus the capabilities of SCs is elaborated.  
The first challenge mentioned is the lack of reactive power compensation for keeping 
the voltage within acceptable limits. This problem is handled very well by the SCs, by 
controlling the excitation field current, the SCs can either absorb or generate reactive 
power, and especially the fact that it can handle really low voltage dip, makes it superior 
to a lot of the static competitors of reactive power compensation. However, it should be 
mentioned, that some of the known wind turbine technologies offers some reactive 
power compensation. As mentioned, the inertia of the future system is expected to de-
crease since the connection of renewables is mostly performed by power electronics, ex-
cept for the type-C wind turbine. This challenge match the synchronous condenser well, 
as its basic characteristic are those of a synchronous machine, meaning it provides real 
rotational inertia to the system and thus keeps it less fragile towards frequency devia-
tions as shown in equation (2.1). A low inertia system also lacks short-circuit contribu-
tion, where a linear relationship can be made between the amount of inertia in the sys-
tem and the amount of short-circuit power in the system. This means the SC provides a 
significant amount of short-circuit power and thus increases the strength of the system.

Common Optimization Methods 
Optimization can from a mathematically point of view be seen as the discipline of find-
ing the maxima or minima of a function, where the function can be either constrained or 
unconstrained.  Fermat and Lagrange introduced the studies in mathematical optimiza-
tion, their method identified the optima by calculus-based formulas. One well-known 
approach is the Newton method of iterative methods for finding an optimum, which has 
shown great results especially in power flow studies [20]. In recent years, the methods 
of evolutionary algorithms (EA’s), which are based on biological evolution has shown 
great result for finding optima in highly complex problems [21]. The following sections 
explains the basic characteristics behind some of the known methods.

4.2.2 
Evolutionary Algorithms  
The application of EAs have become interesting in the recent decades due the promising 
results found when solving optimization problems of high complexity. 
EA algorithms are heuristic optimization algorithms based on the application of biologi-
cal evolution, hence EA algorithms have the ability to evolve when performing optimi-
zation problems. It follows the “survival of the fittest” principle, in [21 p.6] the three 
main characteristics for EA’s are: 
 Need of a population, this population is the initial solution and 
hence needs optimization. 
 Fitness of the individual elements of the population. For finding 
the global optimum solution will the EA prefer the fittest individ-
ual solution of the population.  
 Variation driven, the individuals will undergo a number of opera-
tions for finding the fittest value. 
As the EA algorithms are heuristic there is no guarantee of finding the optimum solution 
and at worst they take as long time as other algorithms. Most times, it will however find 
an acceptable solution of decent quality quite fast. An important goal when applying EA 
algorithms is to find the optimum mix between the characteristic of EAs; population 
size, fitness criteria and the key parameter in the operators, the chosen values for each 
of these characteristics is highly dependent on the problem investigating. In the upcom-
ing subsections, the basic concepts behind three different EA methods will be reviewed.  
 
 
4.2.2.1 
Genetic Algorithms  
The genetic algorithm (GA) is a classic EA method, it is a heuristic, sometimes called a 
metaheuristic algorithm that uses a randomly generated population space and uses selec-
tion and crossover, also known as recombination, as it main operators for the optimiza-
tion. In some cases, a mutation operator is also applied after the crossover operation.  
Each element of a population has two properties; the location (chromosome) and the 
==PAGE:40==
 
Optimization Problem 
38 
quality (fitness), the elements that has the best fitness value has a higher chance to be 
chosen and subsequently passed into the next generation through the crossover process. 
This process ensures that the new generation, mixed of two elements, has a better fitness 
than previously and hence the population become fitter through more generations until a 
stopping criteria or a max number of iterations is reached. Different crossover operators 
can be applied for finding the best fitness and is therefore seen as a main operator in the 
GA algorithm.   
The GA algorithm was originally proposed for binary representation, this could be seen 
as a limitation of the GA as the binary number cannot represent a real number  100% 
correct. The steps of a GA algorithm has the following structure: the first step is the ini-
tialization of a population, this is in general performed randomly. The next step is the 
selection, here the fitness of the individuals are found, so those with best fitness has a 
greater chance of being chosen for the crossover operation. As mentioned in the above, 
the reason for choosing those with the best fitness is speed of the optimization is thus 
increased. In the crossover process different operators can be applied e.g. 1-point, 2-
point and uniform crossover, the main thing to remember about the crossover operators, 
is that it makes a recombination between two of elements from the population and hence 
is the main operator of the GA algorithm. The mutation process, if applied, investigates 
all the bites of an element and mutate it with a probability, 𝑝𝑚 also known as the muta-
tion rate. In general the mutation rate is chosen to be quite low, less than 1%. One 
method to perform the mutation, is the bit-flip mutation, this simply changes the value 
of the bit to be mutated from 1 to 0 or 0 to1.  The above described steps; selection, 
crossover and mutation all forms one iteration/generation. This iteration process is per-
formed until and optimum result or maximum iteration is reached [21] [22]. 
4.2.2.2 
Particle Swarm Optimization  
The last optimization method investigated in this project is the PSO, this method was 
developed by Jamie Kennedy and Russell Eberhart in 1995 [23]. The main idea behind 
this method is to imitate a bird swarm’s ability to fly synchronously, change direction, 
scatter and then regroup. The PSO has roots in two methodologies, as the name states is 
has similarities with bird flocking, but more interestingly it also share common features 
with EA, in particularly the GA method. The PSO starts with an initialization of a popu-
lation or swarm, like GA and DE, the population consist of a number of particles, where 
each particle can be a vector of a size correlated to the dimension of the problem. Each 
particle has an initial knowledge about four attributes:  
 Current location 
 Historically personal best location 
 The global best location of any particle in the population 
 Current velocity  
==PAGE:41==
 
Optimization Problem 
39 
Where the velocity is a new parameter not seen in the two previous described methods, 
the velocity describes the movement of the particle. The PSO finds the optimum solu-
tion through updating iterations, again like GA and DE. It differs from the previous 
mentioned method, as the PSO has no evolution operators like crossover or mutation, 
instead each particle makes its own decision based on its own experience combined 
with the experience of the other particles in the population. In each iteration step the 
particles in the population then subsequently updates its velocity based on the previous 
velocity, its current position, the best personal position, the best global position in the 
population and some weighting parameter. This new velocity is then applied for the esti-
mation of a new position for the particle. The steps in the PSO algorithm is relative sim-
ple as only the velocity, position, best personal and global position is updated in each 
iteration. This search mechanism structure of the PSO is thus very comparable with the  
Differential Evolution algorithm, which will be described next. The PSO algorithm has 
its main advantages in this simple configuration, easy implementation and in [24] it is 
argued that it has shown successful results when applied to power system optimization.  
 
4.2.2.3 
Differential Evolution 
 
The DE algorithm was introduced by Storn and Price in 1995 [25] as a novel approach 
to numerical optimizations problems. Nowadays the DE algorithm is widely used for 
optimization problems as it has proven to be a very efficient and accurate optimization 
method. 
DE is like GA a heuristic, metaheuristic algorithm for solving complex optimization 
problems. Like the GA, DE has an initial population and needs a mutation, crossover 
and selection step, however it differs from the GA in one very important aspect, as the 
DE uses floating variables instead of binary variables (GA). Another main difference 
between DE and GA is found in the mutation operator, in the GA the mutation step is 
randomly selected and thus not the most important operator. In DE the mutation opera-
tor is very important, together with crossover and selection operators. The steps in the 
differential evolution follows the main characteristic of EA’s. First, an initial population 
is generated then mutation is performed, recombination and lastly the selection process 
is made. The steps will be explained in more detail in Section 5.3 (modelling of the al-
gorithm). However, as the mutation step is one of the main differences between DE and 
GA, the basics of this step is elaborated in the following. The mutation step size in the 
DE algorithm is not randomly chosen, DE uses direct information from the current pop-
ulation. From [25] [26]  it is known that the mutation operator can take different forms, 
however they all follow the same structure of choosing random elements from the popu-
lation. The mutation operator chosen in this project is:

4.3 
Methodology 
 
In this thesis, the EA’s are selected over the gradient-based techniques (Newton-
Raphson). The gradient-based is generally having difficulties when facing multi-objec-
tive optimization, constrained optimization, multimodal optimization and combinatorial 
optimization problems. In these optimization problems, the EAs are finding the opti-
mum much faster and more precisely than the gradient based [21].  In the present prob-
lem, it is expected that the short-circuit contribution from the candidate locations will 
not deviate to much from each other, hence the problem can to a certain extent be 
viewed as multimodal and hence the Newton-Raphson would present an inferior method 
[21, p.4].  
 Furthermore, the DE algorithm is selected to solve the optimization problem in (4.3), as 
it has proven superior to other methods in previous studies [28]. The GA is mainly lim-
ited by the fact that it can only handle binary problems, which is not the case in this pro-
ject as the flag representing the five candidates , 𝑋, is continuous. The PSO algorithm 
has shown good results and like the DE can handle continuous variables, however it 
could have a tendency to reach local minima/maxima in certain situations and thus the 
DE method is selected.  
 As mentioned previously in this chapter, the problem of finding the optimal location 
and type of SC that can maximize the contribution of short- circuit power from a system 
perspective is a maximization problem. The DE algorithm is selected as solution

candidate location which are exported into MATLAB and then the optimum solution is

 As previously mentioned in this chapter, the simulation in Simulink will be performed

SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS & DISCUSSION 
As aforementioned, a range of assumptions were made to conduct the chosen analysis. 
With these key assumptions a certain degree of uncertainty follows that could have an 
impact on the findings of this thesis. The key assumptions were described in the limita-
tion Section 1.4, and hence in order to validate the findings of this thesis the possible 
consequences of these limitations are highlighted in the following section.   
It was selected to only perform the analysis on the transmission system, this simplifica-
tion was chosen to limit the complexity of the modelled system and this limitation is not 
believed to have any severe consequences on the findings. However, as the location of 
the SC is found to have significant importance, an investigation on this matter could be 
of high interest. Furthermore, this is not such a strict assumption as most of the synchro-
nous condenser currently operating in power systems are installed at the transmission 
system levels [19]. 
 
To limit the complexity of the system only one renewable source was implemented.  
If the type-D wind turbine, HVDC connections and PV plants were to be added to the 
system would this only lower the ESCR of the systems and thus would this implementa-
tion only increase the need of short-circuit contribution. It should however be noted, 
that a certain amount of short-circuit contribution is required for new HVDC instal-
ments as well for newly installed wind farms which commonly are connected to the sys-
tem by HVDC. However, this contribution needs to be provided and again could SCs be 
applied for this. In [15] it is argued that SCs located near HVDC station is good for 
providing short-circuit power. 
There is also the argument of virtual inertia, the author of this thesis however has not 
been able to find studies were virtual inertia are used in short-circuit contribution. It is 
found argued towards inertial response, hence frequency deviations [44].  
Therefore it is only believed that an increase in system complexity from additional re-
newable generation would further highlight the results from this thesis, hence the need 
of SCs.  
The wind power penetration investigated are not reaching its maximum generation at 
fault occurrence, however this is not expected to have no significant impact on the re-
sults, as the amount of short-circuit power is based on the amount of rotational energy in 
the system and not the amount of produced power. Hence it is expected that higher wind 
==PAGE:94==

generation would have little effect and at least not add any new element to the key find-
ings. However, if additional capacity of  wind power were installed it is expected to 
have an positive impact on the short-circuit contribution, however little compared to the 
SCs contributions.  
If more central power station were implemented in the system e.g. biomass, it would be 
expected to increase the total provision of short-circuit power, as it would increase the 
total amount of kinetic energy and hence the level of inertia in the system. This can also 
be seen in the findings of this thesis, an increased number of central power station could 
actually result in reaching an contribution of short-circuit power that would be within 
the boundaries obtained from Energinet.dk and shown in Table 6-6, which only would

investigation in future projects.  
The limitations from selecting static optimization instead of dynamic is not expected to 
have had any major impact on the findings as also shown in the first case study where a

CONCLUSION 
 
The scope of the thesis was to find an ideal location and type of synchronous conden-
sers in a low inertia power system inspired by the characteristics of a future Danish 
power system.  
 Firstly, a low inertia power system was modeled in Simulink, the system was inspired 
by the characteristics of a future Danish power system, and had adapted some data from 
the IEEE 9-bus system. Five systems were made, each representing a different synchro-
nous condenser solution, i.e. two candidate locations each of which had two solutions of 
excitation system installed. The fifth candidate system had no synchronous condenser 
installed, besides from the above mentioned adjustment all the systems were identical. 
Each of the systems have a renewable penetration represented by 81 MW installed wind 
capacity, which corresponds to 75% of the demand in the performed simulations, in ac-
cordance with the Danish energy political goals. Only one synchronous generator was 
installed in the systems, hence highlighting the aspect of a low inertia system. 
 The Differential Evolution algorithm was selected to solve the problem of finding the 
optimum allocation of the SC, as it has previously shown great results in optimizing 
complex problems, which was found to be ideal when performing studies on power sys-
tem which are inherently nonlinear problems. It can perhaps be argued that as the opti-
mization problem was simplified by performing pre calculations on the system dynam-
ics and thus linearizing the optimization problem, selecting the DE as solutions method 
is a bit exaggerated.  
 However, the DE algorithm modelled in MATLAB was found to provide very solid re-
sults in all of the case studies investigated in this project, by keeping an acceptable com-
putation time without getting caught in any local maxima even though the difference be-
tween the candidate solutions in some cases were almost insignificant. Hence the opti-
mization problem could be seen as multimodal problems.  
 An objective function was developed in MATLAB, it was chosen that maximization of 
the short-circuit power contribution from a system perspective would be the objective, 
hence finding the synchronous condenser solution which provided the highest amount 
of short-circuit power at the fault location. This selection was made as short-circuit 
power contribution insufficiency could be a sever issue in future low inertia power sys-
tems. As mentioned the optimization method was switch from dynamic to static while 
performing initial test runs, due to the comprehensive computation time found in the dy-
namic optimization. However, it is believed that this simplification have had no or little 
effect on the results in this study. 
==PAGE:96==
 
Conclusion 
94 
  The results from this master thesis shows that synchronous condensers can play an im-
portant role in low inertia power systems, by ensuring the provision of short-circuit 
power for the system.   
It was found that the allocation of the SC is the most vital parameter in these studies, as 
the same location was selected in all three case studies, even though the candidate sys-
tems were identical beside from the location of the SC. The results also showed that the 
closer the SC was to the fault the higher contribution was provided, this finding is be-
lieved to be very interesting as this means future investigation could be conducted to 
find the most critical parts in a power system from a short-circuit contribution perspec-
tive.  
The type of excitation system installed in the SC was also shown to have an effect on 
the results, however both types of excitation systems were selected in the studies, 
though one was selected in two out of the three case studies. However, the difference in 
contribution is not as significant when looking at the chosen type of excitation system 
compared to the difference between locations, which again showed that the location of 
the SC plays a vital part in future power system structures.  
Besides, from the overall perspective of finding the ideal location by the application of 
DE an additional interesting element was found from this study. In the last case a key 
parameter was adjusted in order of maximizing the provision of short-circuit power, it 
should be mentioned that no studies was performed on the selected value, however the 
results from this adjustment was positive which from the authors perspective could re-
sult in studies on parameter optimization towards maximization of short-circuit power.  
 
 
 
 
8.1 
Future Work 
Several new studies can take origin from the findings in this thesis.  
 
One obvious study would be to adapt the modelled approach into the Danish Power sys-
tem, to see where the synchronous condenser actually is best allocated. Otherwise,  a 
system with higher similarities towards the Danish transmission system could be mod-
elled and thus a comparison with the obtained data from Energinet.dk could be per-
formed for validation of the data.  
 
The investigation could also be conducted on a lower voltage level, hence the distribu-
tion grid, as the Danish transmission is at moment very strong and the findings of this 
thesis shows that the closer the SC is located the contribution can expected. Thus an in-
vestigation on weak spots in the distribution grid could be interesting.  
 
==PAGE:97==
 
Conclusion 
95 
It would furthermore be interesting to conduct the dynamic optimization, in this ap-
proach some of the dynamic constraints could be added, which could allow for an inclu-
sion of the stability limits while still maximizing the short-circuit contribution.  
 
As mentioned, one very interesting project would be an investigation of parameter opti-
mization in a dynamic system, perhaps a simplified system. However, the advantages of 
dynamic optimization could really be found in this type of case studies, as all of the dy-
namics in the system could be accounted for while searching for the optimum value of 
some parameter, like the leakage impedance of the transformer, while optimizing the 
short-circuit contribution.

A.4 Objective Function: Master  
Here the objective function for the static optimization is shown. It differs from the dy-
namic as the five communications function with MATLAB are not called, instead are 
the results from these called as global variables in MATLAB.  
function y  = Master(X) 
% X is the search space, dimension 1, between 0-1 
% When the optimization is completed we round of X,  
% If X is zero has the left system provided the most short-circuit 
current 
% contribution. If 1, then the right side has provided the most. 
% N= number of contribution, hence fault locations = 4.  
%I start off with no constraints.  
  
% The function should be a maximization.  
  
global LFST RFST  LFAC RFAC NF 
p = 0; % Penalty factor 
m = 0; 
  
% Adding equality constraint 
if X(1)+X(2)+X(3)+X(4)+X(5)-1>0  
    X(2) = X(2)*p; 
    X(1) = X(1)*p; 
    X(3) = X(3)*p; 
    X(4) = X(4)*p; 
    X(5) = X(5)*p; 
end 
  
  
  
 m = 
X(1)*sum(LFST)+X(2)*sum(RFST)+X(3)*sum(LFAC)+X(4)*sum(RFAC)+X(5)*sum(N
F); 
  
y = -m; 
  
  
end

function [ V ] = Mut_de1(X,M,F,m ) 
  
R=randperm(M); % Returns a vector containin random integers from 1 to 
M.  
%making sure that r1 is not equal to r2 or r3 
    r1=R(1); 
    r2=R(2); 
    r3=R(3); 
    r4=R(4); 
     
 % Checking if r1,r2 ot r3=m 
    if r1==m  
       r1=R(4); 
    elseif r2==m  
       r2=R(4); 
    elseif r3==m 
       r3=R(4);                     
    end 
     
    % Generating a new trial vector 
    V=X(r1,:)+F*(X(r2,:)-X(r3,:)); 
  
end

                    %or initialize vector (X)  
                    if (R1<Cr || n==jrand) 
                        U(1,n)=V(1,n); 
                    else 
                        U(1,n)=X(m,n); 
                    end 
                end 
  
  
end
