Abstract
Integrating extraordinary optoelectronic properties of III┬¡V materials together with well established
and industrial compatible Si platform is a dream scenario for many future applications [1]. Epitaxial
growth of III┬¡Vs on Si, however, is not easy due to large lattice mismatch between Si and III┬¡V
semiconductors, see Fig 1.1. This project investigates InAs/InP selective area growth (SAG) with
droplet epitaxy (DE) on a structure prepared before with focused ion beam (FIB). Afterwards the
grown structures are investigated with with transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning
electron microscopy (SEM). The aim is to obtain 1550nm emission from InAs/InP structure which
fits into the low┬¡loss third spectral window of silica fiber [2]. Furthermore, in future, a possible
integration of the structure into silicon EDC cavity, see 1.3 ,as a few nanometer in diameter, active
material, for light┬¡matter enhancement [3]. During the project, using Ar, Xe, Ga ions it was possible
to obtain results close to expectations. Although, more optimization is still needed to be done.

Background and motivation
Monolithic integration of III┬¡V semiconductors into silicon (Si) is a dream scenario for many future
applications, as it can combine the desirable traits of these materials with the industrial compatibility
and prevalence of the Si platform. Epitaxial growth of III┬¡Vs on Si, however, is very challenging.
This project investigates how this can be achieved in nanoscale regions created using focused ion
beam (FIB) milling in Si wafers. It will involve cutting openings smaller than 50 nm in a mask on
Si using the FIB, followed by additional cleanroom processing and MOVPE growth of III┬¡Vs (e.g.
InAs/InP) in these regions. The grown structures will be investigated with transmission electron
microscopy in terms of their crystal structure, presence of defects, composition and strain.

The InAs/InP structure will be grown on Si, which is very challenging due o huge lattice mismatch,
see Fig 1.1. With its possibilities to emit light at 1550nm, which is third telecom window, became
a very interesting active material for devices such as lasers [holewa].

Monolithic Integration of III ┬¡V on silicon
Si is considered to be the most common semiconductor in micro and nanoelectronic industry[5]. II┬¡
I/V semiconductors are materials used mostly for optoelectronic devices due to their direct bandgap.
It allows improved photon emission in contrast to Si. This is why vast majority of devices such as
lasers, LEDs, detectors and sensors are based on III┬¡V materials. Moreover, high electron mobility
of this structures is an advantage when fabricating transistors. Both III┬¡V materials as well as Si

Integrating them together in one platform would combine their extraordinary properties giving a

1.2
Quantum Dots
In recent decades semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) have become a very hot research topic
[6]. It is due to their extraordinary quantum properties and thus many possible applications i.a. in
lasers, optical amplifiers, single photon sources (SPS) and many more. QDs are nanometric size
structures that can confine electrons and holes in three dimensions, what drastically changes their
density of states from continuous (bulk structure) to discrete levels┬¡ they are quantized, see Fig 1.2.
Once the carrier is excited in the QD, it can radiate spontaneously through the energy relaxation
process involving transitions between confined states. The resultant photon has the characteristic
very similar to that coming from an atom. Hence, QDs are often called artificial atoms. Contrary
to atoms, QDs have grate possibilities to shape their energy level structure and local environment
providing much more flexibility to the real┬¡world applications. By changing their size, their energy
gap can be tuned┬¡ increase in size results in decrease in energy gap [6].
This report investigates semiconductor InAs QDs, covered with InP layer, grown on Si. They are
expected to emit light in the near┬¡infrared spectral range around 1.55╬╝m wavelength (telecom┬¡
munication window). Therefore, these particular quantum dots are potentially interesting for the
optical gain material than can be utilized in optoelectronic devices used in telecommunication ap┬¡
plications. There are many approaches to create a QDs. The semiconductor InAs/InP qunatum

Methods
Due to fast development of nanotechnology and nanoscale devices throughout past decades there
is a constant need to improve either their fabrication and characterization techniques. The are
popular nanopaterning techniques such as photolithography or electron beam lithoghraphy (EBL),
though due to either their limitations in resolution or need to use resist they are not always sufficient
[7]. Moreover when using EBL it sometimes might has difficulty with penetration harder materials
without suffering from major distortion resulted from local charge buildup.
In the project, where high precision milling, material deposition, transmission electron microscope
(TEM) sample preparation, high resolution imaging takes place FIB seem to be most feasible to
the challenge. Combined with scanning electron microscope (SEM) it creates so called DualBeam
system which brings together their extraordinary advantages. Except patterning and imaging, the
structure for processing requires anisotropic wet etching and epitaxial MOVPE growth. All of the
methods, techniques and approaches are described in the chapter.

Focused Ion Beam
The instrument that has been used in the project is FEI Helios Nanolab 600, Fig 2.1. It consists of
vacuum system, stage, chamber, stage for handling samples, detectors, computer, gas injection
system (GIS) and ion/electron source. The ion and electron beam columns are localized 52Ôùªrela┬¡
tive to each other. In this particular microscope the liquid┬¡metal ion source (LMIS) is used as Ga+
ion source. Literature provides information that the LMIS could provide ion beam of 5nm diam┬¡
eter [8]. The typical LMIS consists of tungsten needle which is attached to reservoir with source
material inside. Most of the cases it is gallium ions. There are several reason why this particular
element is most popular for commercial uses, namely: low melting point 29.8Ôùª, mass is enough
to allow milling of heavier elements, if happen to be implanted into samples surface it is easy to
distinguish it from other elements with TEM investigatin.
FIB works under pressure, typically about 10ÔêÆ3 Pa. The resaon it to get rid of any air particles
which would contaminate the chamber and collide with ions or electrons created in the chamber.
It uses high energetic ions to perform high resolution direct milling on the structure, deposition of
material or even imaging is possible.

Ion beam generation
First step in order to generate focused ion beam is to heat up the reservoir to near evaporation
temperature, so the Ga+ melt will flow down to the tip (radius 2┬¡5um) of the tungsten needle being
drawn there by extractor┬¡ an electrode. The electric field (1010V /m) that is applied to the end of
the tip makes liquid Ga form a so called Taylor cone with diameter of 2┬¡5nm. The specific shape
is due to balance between surface tension of the tip and electrostatic forces. The tip of the cone
is so small that extraction voltage can successfully pull Ga from the needle and ionize it with field
evaporation. The field evaporation occurs when the potential barrier that prevents from evapora┬¡
tion is decreased due to presence of a field (here from extractor) and the only way to be overcome
is by evaporating atoms ionization on the surface of the field emitter. Once ionized they are accel┬¡
erated by applied potential towards down of the FIB column. Usually, the obtained current density
of ions extracted from LMIS system is 108A/cm2. Though, the operational current used during
experiments is on the order of 1pA┬¡3┬ÁA, depends on desired application. Using higher currents
might lead to decrease of source lifetime or energy spread of the beam. It is significant in order to
have a control over etching rate when milling. By adjusting voltage of the suppressor the extrac┬¡
tion current of ions will change. Meaning, the extraction current might be regulated only by using
suppressor without need of using extractor. Moreover, changing extractor voltage may influence
Taylor cone displacement resulting in beam drift on the sample surface.

Ion beam┬¡matter interactions
In order to fully use potential that FIB brings it is crucial to understand ion┬¡matter interaction. The in┬¡
teractions govern redeposition, deposition, milling, sputtering, image resolution and contrast mech┬¡
anism. A Fig2.2 presents schematic illustration of which interactions might take place after ion
approaching a matter.
Figure 2.2: Schematic diagram of sputtering process and ion┬¡matter interaction [9].
Milling a material away from a sample is a result of physical sputtering of target material [8]. It
occurs due to number of elastic collisions transferring a momentum from incident ion to atom in a
structure. An atom is being ejected from a surface when energy applied by incident ion exceeds
the binding energy of the target material. Moreover, some part of the ejected surface atoms might
be ionized becoming secondary ions (SI). Because SI are atoms knocked out from the sample
they carry information about chemical composition of the specimen. Inelastic scattering though
results in secondary electrons (SE) emission. SE are typically used for imaging since their number
is around 1000 times greater than SI for one incident ion.
Sputtering yield describes number of particles ejected from the target sample per incident ion. It
depends on angle of incidence, ratio between mass of incident ion and atoms in the surface and
the energy of the incident ion.
The incident atom interacts with sample until it loses its energy, due to electronic or nuclear energy
loss, and becomes implanted ion. The average depth at which ions stop is called projected range.
Energy loss of the ions happens because of elastic and and inelastic interactions. The first one
is when ion interacts with nucleus of atom whereas the inelastic one when ion interacts with elec┬¡
trons of atoms in the specimen. Sum of these two energies is the total energy loss. The elastic┬¡
==PAGE:14==
nuclear interactions can result in SI emission, dislocations, vacancies, sputtering, amorphization
etc. Whereas, inelastic┬¡ electronic interactions result mainly in SE emission.
Milling
Milling material away occurs upon ion beam hits the target material and knocks out the ion. It can be
controlled by adjusting number of parameters such as volume per dose, beam overlap, ion current,
acceleration energy, milling time etc. Things to avoid during milling are usually redeposition or
sample damage. The optimization process is all described in a results section.
Material deposition
Dual beam systems, FIBs and SEMs have that possibility to deposit a material on the surface when
a precursor gas is introduced to the structure. It is possible thank to gas injection systems (GIS)
which introduces gas to near the sampleÔÇÖs surface. There is a wide range of possible atoms to be
deposited such as Pt, C, W, gold, insulator material, depending to which application it will be. Pt
material is probably one of the most commonly deposited. Helios provides Pt and C. It can be ei┬¡
ther deposited with electron or ion beam. Usually it is used for TEM sample preparation, to deposit
protection layer of C+Pt. Though when depositing Pt with electron beam it has much more carbon
elements compared to when deposition is done with ion beam. Hence, for electrical purposes, ion
beam deposition is preferred═¥ however for sensitive samples, electron beam deposition is a very
useful alternative [8]. As a precursor gas for Pt deposition is used methylcyclopentadienyl platinum
trimethyl (CH3)3(CH3C5H4)Pt. For Carbon it is naphthalene C10H8.
Carbon deposition is mostly used for a protective capping layer for TEM sample preparation. Due
to its lower sputter rate than Pt, it offers more protection to the covered layer. The size of the
grains in the C deposited layer is smaller than Pt ones which reduces curtaining artifacts. More┬¡
over, because carbon has atomic number 6 (low), it is often advantageous when revealing other
low atomic number (light) elements at the sample surface during TEM analysis. Additionally, the
electron beam C deposition is rather slow compared to ion beam C deposition, or electron beam Pt
deposition [8]. Nevertheless, Pt layer due to faster deposition rate, in the project is used as often
as C.
The material deposition process starts with GIS directing a precursor gas towards specimen sur┬¡
face. The good precursor should have a sufficient sticking probability in order to stick to a sample.
Moreover, when bombarded with high energetic electron/ion beam, it should decompose faster
than it is sputtered away by the ion beam [8]. When the precursors are adsorbed to the surface the
deposition of electron beam or focused ion beam takes place. This electrons/ions energy results
in a chemical reaction involving the precursor. They will simply be decomposed into non┬¡volatile
products and removed from the surface.

2.1.3
Wet etching
The anisotropic etchants for a single┬¡crystal silicon has been known for many years now [10, 11].
It requires liquid etchants. They are used to exhibit (111) planes in (100) Si structure. In the project
two different etchants has been used┬¡ KOH 28% (potassium hydroxide) with tetramethylammo┬¡
nium hydroxide (TMAH) 2.5%. The idea was to compare their influence to the structure. Both
of them have different etching rate, selectivity, toxicity. The etch rate very strongly depends on
crystallographic orientation of the etched material, on temperature, on concentration. Tables with
exact numbers can be found in the paper by Holmes [10].
==PAGE:16==
KOH etching
KOH etching is one of the most popular ones when it comes to anisotropic Si (100) etching. It
is faster than TMAH, less toxic and causes less undercut to the structure [12]. The mechanism
behind Si atoms removal with KOH is well understood and described in literature. Depending on
crystallographic orientation etch rate will vary, it is a result of different atomic bonds formed at the
surface of Si wafer. When looking at Si (100) oriented, the surface atom has two back bonds which
connects it with another two underlying atoms. Therefore there are two more free bonds which may
react with OH radicals. Through the thermal excitation energy is applied to breaking bonds [13].
For a (111) oriented plane only one dangling bond exists for each superficial atom, thus making it
harder to be etched when comparing with a (100) oriented plane. Below are equations presenting
chemical reactions occur during the etching process for either (100) and (111) Si plane, see Fig

2.2
Characterization
Until a nearly hundred years ago, structures have been observed with optical microscopes [16].
Though, due to significant resolution limitation caused by wavelength of the light it is not sufficient
to observe nanostructures. Due to that a different than light excitation source has been used┬¡ such
as electrons and probe.
When the excitation source hits the sample, various types of interactions take place, resulting in
the emission of different signals. It looks similar to what happens in case of ion beam, but due to
much lower mass of electrons the interactions are not that strong. A scheme of possible interaction
between electrons and matter are presented in the Fig 2.7. All charged particles, ions and elec┬¡
trons, interact with matter in elastic and inelastic collisions, with either atomic electrons and nuclei.
Elastic collisions process is responsible for most of the energy loss of the particle whereas elastic
collisions are mostly responsible for changes in movement direction of the incident particle [17].
Most informative, when imaging, in case of SEM would be SE and backscattered electrons (BSE)
==PAGE:18==
[16]. This chapter shows advantages and possibilities available thank to extremely high resolution
microscopes. They can be divided into two groups, namely electron microscopes ┬¡ SEM, TEM and
force microscope┬¡ atomic force microscope (AFM).
2.2.1
Scanning Electron Microscope
SEM uses electron beam to create a image of the sample with magnification. Since it is integrated
into Dualbeam system with FIB, it also requires ultra high vacuum to work properly. The main
microscope parts are electron gun, magnetic lenses, objective aperture, detector, amplifier. Scat┬¡
tered electrons are main source of information. Electron detectors can provide two different types
of contrast. Namely, compositional, which is also called atomic number contrast, and topographic.
The detector of secondary electrons, EverhartÔÇôThornley detector (ETD), is the most popular type.
It is sensitive to either BSEs and SEs. The backscattered electrons are enough energetic to reach
the detector. However, low┬¡energy SEs need to be directed to the detector by the applied voltage
potential. Electrons are accelerated until they reach the detector and product light. After amplifica┬¡
tion the light (in a photomultiplier tube), produces an electrical signal, which will display the image.
Another detector used in this project is through┬¡the┬¡lens detector (TLD). Usually it was used in
mode when immersion lens is on and that let obtaining ultrahigh resolution.
Electron beam generation
The electron beam is generated at the very top of the column. The electrons are created by a
thermal emission source, meaning, heating a tungsten filament. The energy of the incident electron
beam chosen depending on what results one wants to obtain. A series of electromagnetic lenses
focus electrons into fine beam, so only well focused point reaches the target sample. The beam
can be focused to a final probe diameter as small as about 10├à [16].

Atomic Force Microscope
Atomic Force Microscope (AFM) is a type of scanning probe microscopy (SPM) with resolution of
fraction of a nanometer [19]. Here, force between tip and a sample is measured. It has a possibility
to analyze both┬¡ biological and non┬¡biological samples. In comparison to electron microscopes a
high vacuum is not required here.It has a possibility to image with very high resolution topology of
a sample it has been considered as a competitive method for other such as electron microscope
or X┬¡ray scattering. Another major advantage of the microscope is that it can image in 3D. AFM
consists of cantilever with a sharp tip at the end of it, which is usually made from silicon. Another
required parts are a piezo┬¡scanner which through feedback loop drives the cantilever, laser diode
and a detector which captures the signal. Any interactions that occur between tip and a surface
would band cantilever, which would change deflection of a laser beam, giving at the same time
information about sampleÔÇÖs topology. AFM is driven by two forces┬¡ repulsive Coulomb forces and
attractive┬¡ Van der Vaals forces, see Fig. 2.8. Static and dynamic modes can be distinguished.
In static, feedback loop control a tip┬¡sample force to be constant. Meaning, the bending of the
cantilever will be the same all over the sample. In dynamic, cantilever is excited to oscillate in
its resonance frequency. When the tip approaches the surface the frequency would be changed

which is combination on the two contact and non┬¡contact.

of the beam moving from pixel to pixel has been chosen to be 50%. It lead to openings sharper

Ar
The Ar ion beam is created from plasma. Ar beam has been used several times at the very begin┬¡
ning of the project, mostly for process of the beam diameter optimization. It has been compared
with Xe ion beam. After using same set of parameters the SEM images showed that openings
made with XE are better quality. They are deeper and their edges are sharper, see Fig 3.24. It
was a surprise, since Xe has higher mass = higher sputtering yield which, when same parameters
used, should create more redeposition and damage to the surface [nitrogen]. Nevertheless it has
been assumed that poor Ar performance was already due to technical problems. That problem
lead to project continuation with only Xe (plasma FIB) and Ga (FIB) ions.
Although, in the future, it would be interesting to continue investigation of that beam due to its in┬¡
ert nature (in comparison to Ga, but similarly to Xe), thus any chemical reactions should not take
place on the surface. Additionally its low sputtering yield might be very promising when it comes
to opening diameter in the SiN layer. At least better than Xe ions.

4
Summary and outlook
In conclusion, FIB┬¡assisted epitaxial growth method is very promising for the structure proposed
in this project. The whole process is faster and less complex than alternative EBL method. After
optimization and tests on three different ion beams, Ar, Xe┬¡ plasma FIB and Ga┬¡FIB, it was clear
that the best results in opening diameter size were possible with Ga ions and it was Ôê╝40nm. How┬¡
ever, there was an issue with this beam that after milling the sample it was not possible to etch
Si. Moreover, technical issues with Ar beam lead to situation where only on Xe was performed
growth. The epitaxial growth took place in MOVPE reactor, and the DE and SAG techniques has
been used. The PL spectra obtained from the structure clearly indicated presence of InP, though
there was no visible signal from InAs. That might have happened due to defect in the structure or
signal exceeding detection wavelength of used detector.
In order to find answer to no emission problem the TEM analysis is required. That will provide
us with information about chemical composition of the sample, any possible structure defects and
about the InAs size which is covered with InP layer. With that knowledge optimization of growth
receipt will be more straightforward.
Moreover, after TEM analysis it is known that Ga material is redeposited inside the openings, pre┬¡
venting from etching. To overcome that it is possible to evaporate the Ga material away by hitting
up the sample under pressure in MOVPE chamber. Another solution would be to use etchant,
which has been proposed in work by K.L. Chang for n┬¡GaP removal [gaetching]. It is to use aqua
regia solutions for etching away the Ga. Some other solution can be further FIB milling process
optimization, to minimize redeposition or heat and energy transfer of incident ions. Perhaps when
using lower energy or lower current, the redeposition or any processes that occur on the sample
will be more controllable.
In order to create smaller in diameter openings one of the propositions, next to milling parameters
optimization, is to use thinner SiN layer. That will reduce time needed to go through the mask,
which was confirmed in the Fig 3.23 to indeed influence the opening diameter. Shorter time of ions
exposure is equivalent to lower damage to the sample and possibility to decrease the opening
diameter.
