Abstract  
In recent years an increasing number of studies are focusing on microbiomes. This trend 
might indicate that being able to tell the content of individual microbiomes in a low cost, 
reliable manner will grow beyond scientific studies and niche applications into something that 
regular people can come into contact with. 
While whole metagenome sequencing can tell the near-complete contents of a microbiome, 
sequencing costs are still at a prohibitive level, which is keeping it from being used as a wide 
scale tool beyond scientific studies. With single primer pair solutions, such as 16S 
sequencing, the associated costs can be reduced to a lower level. Ultimately further 
developments might enable 16S sequencing to become a feasible tool in medical diagnostics.  
As more and more organisms are sequenced the richness of available genetic information 
increases. A richness visible in a number of increasingly large genetic databases that are 
publicly available and free to use. Combined with powerful bioinformatics tools this enables 
the development of an information driven approach to primer design. 
In an attempt at such an approach, this thesis constructed a new platform which has been 
named  Primer Finder, which can discover and evaluate potential primer targets in a database 
of sequences. Primer Finder also aims to estimate how well a specific read can be used to 
distinguish between different organisms. As such the platform seeks to optimize the capability 
to classify organisms present in a microbiome sample. 
Primer Finder works by dividing an input sequence database into genus clusters. Each cluster 
is analysed for information content and turned into a consensus sequence. By comparing the 
consensus sequences from the genus clusters the individual positions can be evaluated for 
their potential as part of a primer and part of a read. From these positions a number of 
potential primers are suggested. The potential primers are then evaluated for their 
performance in regards to the number of sequences that would theoretically bind to said 
primer. The found primers are scored based on the information content present in the 
theoretical reads that they would produce from the input database. This measure is calculated 
from the Shannon Index of cluster alignments. 
Ultimately the primers found by Primer Finder perform in a similar manner to a set of 
reference primers. Performance was based on the sheer number of sequences from the input 
database that would produce a theoretical read pair, and by the information content of the 
resulting read pairs. A similar score between the reference primers and the best primers 
suggested by the initial version of Primer Finder would indicate that the approach taken is 
valid. With further development and refinement of parameters, Primer Finder could become a 
valuable tool to suggest both universal and specialized primers for sequencing of specific 
genes or detection of specific organisms.

1. Introduction 
In recent years it has become clear that microbiomes can influence a person in a multitude of 
ways. From cancer, to diabetes, to neurodevelopmental disorders, microbiomes are becoming 
the modern holy grail of the medical sciences [1, 2, 3]. With potential links between 
microbiome composition and various illnesses, the identification of personal microbiomes 
could become a powerful tool in diagnostics.  
Being able to tell the composition of microbiomes has never been more relevant, and in all 
likelihood the need will only grow in the coming years.  
Historically organisms have been classified by their physical appearance [4]. Most people can 
tell the difference between a cat and a tree by looking at them. But when it comes to 
microbiomes visual identification is not feasible, both because many Bacteria are 
indistinguishable under the microscope, and because of the sheer numbers of individual 
organisms in any given sample. The development of methods to study genetics supplies a way 
to not only look at individual organisms with high resolution, but many organisms can also be 
considered at once. As such genetics answers both primary problems in identification of 
microbiomes. 
At the basic level the collection of gene/sequence data can happen in two ways: Untargeted or 
targeted. If the collection is untargeted the process will be set up to sequence as much 
different DNA/RNA as possible. It is said to capture the whole genome of all organisms in the 
sample, as such it is referred to as Whole MetaGenome Sequencing (WMGS). WMGS has the 
advantage that almost everything present in the sample will be represented in the resulting 
data to some degree. A challenge for WMGS is that DNA sequences are, for the most part, 
long. Genetic data contains a lot of information and is extensive to read. WMGS raw data can 
easily consist of Gigabytes of compressed text data. In comparison the text of this thesis is 
less than a Megabyte. Even after the sequence data has been collected, filtering through 
WMGS data is a time consuming task.  
In targeted collection the sequencing is aimed at hitting a specific number of regions. 
Targeted approaches give less data than WMGS, but the data will mainly come from regions 
with the desired information. Because of that the usefulness of targeted sequencing relies 
entirely on how the targeting is done.  
While development of sequencing technologies has stagnated some in recent years [5], the so 
called next generation sequencing methods are more than 10 years old, it is still a growing 
field. The costs associated with sequencing are dropping while the resulting data becomes 
more reliable. 
Over the years of continuous sequencing efforts more and more data has become available for 
analysis. This has given rise to a number of sequence-related bioinformatics tools, which in 
turn have increased the information that can be gained from sequence data. Some of these 
tools have made it possible to detect a multitude of organisms based on the reads produced 
from a single primer pair. An approach that greatly reduce the sequencing costs, which could, 
among other things, make it feasible to use microbiome sequencing as a diagnostics tool.  
==PAGE:8==
Page 2 of 45 
 
While sequencing with a single primer pair is cheaper and simpler in data processing, it is not 
without drawbacks. A serious pitfall is the selection of the primer pair itself. Since it is only 
possible to detect organisms that the primers bind to, the primers need to be designed to target 
all relevant organisms.  
 
1.1. 
Scope of the thesis  
This study will aim to develop an information driven platform that can be used to find a 
primer pair well suited for detecting microbiome bacteria based on a specific input set of 
sequences. The investigation will be purely in silico. This will be done by looking at the 16S 
rRNA gene and evaluating each individual position for its potential as part of a primer and as 
part of a read. The regions with the best primer and read potential will then be paired up and 
their combined potential will be evaluated. What specific genera from the library that the 
primers can potentially bind to, and thus theoretically detect in a real sample, will be part of 
the evaluation criteria, and notably the genera that do not bind will be part of the output. Thus 
part of the limitations will be known information.  
 
1.2. 
Primers 
As the fundamental focus of this thesis is primers, it is relevant to summarize them to clarify 
how they are viewed in the following work.  
A primer is a short sequence that facilitates DNA synthesis. The primer binds to a 
complementary region on the target DNA strand, which in turn allows a DNA polymerase to 
replicate the target strand from the 3’ end of the primer. DNA polymerase itself does not pose 
any requirements on the sequence of the primer, as such primers can be designed to target 
most regions on a known DNA strand. Synthetic primers are typically 15-30 nucleotides long. 
[6, 7, 8]  
 
1.3. 
16S rRNA based identification of bacteria 
While this thesis will aim at being independent of a specific gene as its input database, 
development and evaluation is performed on 16S data, as such it is important to understand 
what 16S is and why it has been chosen. 
16S rDNA is a gene that codes for a part of the 30S small ribosome subunit in prokaryotes. 
The Ribosome is a protein complex that catalyses translation of a RNA strand into a peptide 
sequence. Since translation is a necessary step in the production of all proteins, the Ribosome 
is found in all prokaryotes. Therefore the 16S rDNA gene is universally present in bacteria. 
The gene contains approximately 1600 base pairs varying slightly from species to species. 
In 1977 it was suggested that 16S could be used for phylogenetic studies of prokaryotes [9]. 
As 16S was sequenced from an increasing number of bacteria, it became apparent that the 
sequence of the gene had not mutated uniformly. That is, along the length of the gene 
==PAGE:9==
Page 3 of 45 
 
individual positions varied in how conserved they were between bacteria. In addition it was 
found that the conserved positions were roughly grouped together in conserved regions. As 
such 16S is referred to as having conserved regions and variable regions. Typically the 
variable regions are called V1-9.  
This structure of conserved regions interspersed by variable regions is well suited for 
sequencing as the conserved regions can be used as primer targets that provide reads going 
into a variable region. The use of 16S for identification of Bacteria was first suggested by 
Weisburg et. al in 1991 [6].Thus the conserved region primer will be able to bind to a large 
amount of organisms while the reads produced will have different sequences depending on the 
organism. With a list of reads that are distinguishable from one another each read can then be 
mapped against known sequences and if a read is uniquely mapped, that is mapped to a single 
organism, the classification of the organism that the read was mapped to can be assumed to be 
the correct classification. If the read region is not sufficiently variable multiple organisms can 
share the same sequence, meaning reads mapping to them would be ambiguous in identity.  
The prevalence of 16S means that all bacteria can theoretically be detected by sequencing this 
one gene. In addition the variable region and conserved region nature of the gene allows for 
identification of most bacteria from a simple primer pair [10], which reduces sequencing 
costs.  
16S has since become the go-to gene for identification of bacteria (barring WMGS), and has 
been studied extensively. One product of this is numerous databases of 16S genes covering 
sequences from thousands of bacterial species.  
To have an indication of what region of the 16S gene a specific primer targets, primers are 
often named for their starting position in an E. coli reference sequence [7]. 
 
1.4. 
Database  
When designing primers it is necessary to have reference sequences for each species that 
should be detected. This study will use the Arb-Silva small subunit 119 release [11]. The 
release contains 4,346,329 16S rRNA sequences, of these 80,619 have classifiers with seven 
capitalized levels. 56,042 different species are represented, with several having more than 
1,000 sequences (such as Bacillus subtilis with 5,426 sequences). On the genus level 3,955 
different genera are represented. Since the database does not have an even distribution of 
genera and species, any predictions directly based on the database would be biased towards 
the more common species. Thus it is necessary to pre-process the database to be able to detect 
rare species. 
 
1.5. 
Alignment 
An issue with sequence data is that two sequences are not immediately comparable. Whether 
or not two sequences are alike is not readily apparent, even with sequence summaries such as 
the G-C content. For this study it is necessary to be able to evaluate sequences in direct 
==PAGE:10==
Page 4 of 45 
 
relation to one another, as such it is relevant to look at the typically used method of 
comparison.  
Alignment techniques have been developed as a way to set up the individual sequences in a 
comparable structure. Overall alignment methods attempt to construct an m times n matrix in 
which m is the length of the alignment and n is the number of sequences to be aligned. The 
letters of each sequence are then placed along the length of the alignment in such a manner 
that as many positions of possible match with those of the other sequences. To allow for 
sequences of different lengths a position in the matrix can be empty, usually signified by a  
“-“. If one sequence contains more letters “in the middle” the other sequence can get empty 
positions in the corresponding spots (for example “flemenstag” and “fl------ag”), these are 
typically referred to as gaps or inserts. Gaps typically occur when a new sequence lacks 
something present in the majority, whereas inserts are the opposite.  
Performing an alignment of a number sequences is more complicated than simply forcing 
each position to either be a match or a gap. As a way to decide the best configuration 
alignments do feature a scoring system to determine their quality. In some alignment 
algorithms a position can be one of four cases: A match, a mismatch, a gap opening, or a gap 
extension. Each case has its own score. Typically a match will give a positive score while the 
other three each give a negative score. Gap opening and gap extension will always have a 
negative score and as such are often referred to as penalties rather than scores. 
The actual process of alignment depends on the particular implementation but a common 
denominator across implementations is that it is a computationally heavy process that is 
complicated to explain and ultimately irrelevant for the purpose of this study [12]. 
 
1.6. 
Illumina 
As this study is largely motivated by Illumina sequencing and the data it produces, it is 
relevant to understand how the method works and as such here follows an overview on how 
Illumina sequencing is typically performed [13]. 
Illumina is a biotechnology company that holds the rights to a sequencing method called 
Illumina Dye Sequencing. The method was invented by S Balasubramanian and D Klenerman 
of Cambridge University, who founded Solexa which was later bought by Illumina. For ease 
of reference their method is colloquially referred to simply as Illumina sequencing. 
 
1.6.1. Library preparation 
As input Illumina requires a sample of DNA or RNA, the extraction and purification process 
is irrelevant as long as the sample contains only DNA or RNA. 
After extraction and purification of the desired DNA/RNA sample, it is necessary to perform 
an amplification to get a sufficient signal. In cases such as this study wherein a specific set of 
==PAGE:11==
Page 5 of 45 
 
primers are used, amplification also serves as a way to extract the desired regions from the 
purified sample. 
In the standard application of Illumina the input sequences are first broken up into shorter 
fragments and each fragment is ligated with an adapter. Fragments without an adapter are 
washed away. Once a fragment has been ligated and is ready for amplification it is also 
referred to as an amplicon. For Illumina the length of the amplicon is typically up to 1600 
nucleotides, in this thesis 1600 will serve as the upper limit for theoretical amplicons. This 
number of fragment with specific ends is usually called a library. 
Since this study aims at a specific primer pair, the entire input sequences are not needed. 
Instead the purified sample is subjected to a Polymerase Chain Reaction - PCR with the 
desired primers first, and the resulting strands are then ligated with adapters in accordance to 
the standard application. 
 
1.6.2. Flowcell amplification 
The next step is to fix and amplify the library fragments to allow for sequencing. This is done 
with a Flowcell. This Flowcell is essentially a glass plate with millions of copies of two 
different adapters attached in close proximity. Each of these adapters is complimentary to one 
of the adapters ligated to the library fragments. Thus by adding the library to the Flowcell 
each individual fragment should bind to one adapter on the Flowcell. After the library is 
attached, non-binding fragments are washed away and amplification can begin. Since all the 
fragments are attached at one end, a regular PCR is not feasible. Instead first the bound 
fragments are replicated in extension of the Flowcell adapters (i.e. with the adapter supplying 
the primer, one amplification step is performed). Then the original library fragments are 
washed away. From here the process is called “Bridge Amplification”, named so because the 
fragments, now attached to the cell, bend down to build a “bridge” to a nearby adapter 
complementary to the adapter at the end of said fragment. Amplification is performed again 
with the other adapter, resulting in double stranded fragments attached to the Flowcell at both 
ends. These fragments are denatured resulting in a forward and reverse fragment pair of single 
stranded DNA or RNA. This process is repeated several thousand times until each original 
library fragment has made a little “forest” or cluster of identical forward and reverse 
fragments. 
The product of each of these fragments plus adapter is called an amplicon. The density of 
adapters on the Flowcell influences the allowed amplicon size since the total length of the 
amplicon determines the distance a bridge can span. If an amplicon is too short no bridging 
can take place, if it is too long cluster density has to be adjusted to prevent the clusters from 
growing into each other (blending together). It is imperative that clusters are physically 
separate on the cell since the signal is read based on region, thus if two clusters have blended 
together then the resulting signal will be fraught with errors. 
==PAGE:12==
Page 6 of 45 
 
1.6.3. Sequencing 
Once the Bridge Amplification is complete all the reverse strands are washed off leaving 
behind the forward strands. Complementary primers to the adapter are then added and the 
forward strand is then amplified with fluorescently marked nucleotides. In practice this is 
done by introducing polymerases once again and then a cycle of washing the Flowcell with 
the marked nucleotides, then detaching the marker. The fluorescent marker is “capping” the 
nucleotide at the 3’ end, ensuring that only one will attach per cycle. As the markers are 
detached their fluorescent signal is released. To distinguish between bases each has a marker 
with a unique wavelength. The sequence is read by recording how each cluster glows when 
bases are added. Once the entire forward strand has been read the product is washed off. As a 
control primer is added again to read the index 1 of the cluster. If the index gives the wrong 
signal, the cluster cannot be trusted. Primer is washed off again and the forward strand is 
allowed to bridge. As a second control, index 2 is checked and washed off. The reverse strand 
is then replicated through the last bridge amplification. The forward strand is washed off and 
the reverse strand is read in the same manner as the forward strand. The recorded sequence of 
a cluster is called a read. Each cluster on the Flowcell is read twice, once for the forward 
fragment and once for the reverse fragment (also referred to as sense and antisense). The 
resulting two reads are then inherently linked, a read pair. Such read pairs are often referred to 
as Paired End Reads.

four lines for each read.

3. Optional information line. Starts with a “+” and has no official requirements. 
4. Quality line. Contains a sequence of symbols of equal length as the sequence line, 
encoding the quality scores for each nucleotide in the sequence.

The quality score of a given position is based on how clear the signal was (how closely it 
matches the singular wavelength), the strength of the signal, and the quality of the preceding 
positions. If a position is incorrect, all following positions tend to be incorrect. Most 
significantly if an insert happens or a gap is created, the rest of read will be frame shifted one 
position. Because each cycle has a nonzero probability of not binding a new nucleotide to the 
extending fragments, thus creating a gap in the read sequence, an error will eventually occur. 
With the repeating cycles an increasing number of individual fragments of the cluster will be 
reporting an erroneous nucleotide, which gives an unclear signal. Thus a filter is used that 
requires a minimum difference in intensity between the highest and second-highest intensity 
wavelengths. As a consequence it is customary to have a threshold for how long a read can be 
before the sequence is untrustworthy, currently with the Illumina MiSeq platform about 250 
bp length reads are considered feasible [15]. Note that the amplification steps use a 
polymerase with error rates closer to those observed in the cell of an organism, which are not 
subject to this limitation. This allows for the primers to be spaced further apart than the read 
length. 
The paired read nature of modern Illumina sequencing is reflected in the output by having two 
fastq files. One file for the forward reads and one file for the reverse reads. The identifier line 
for each read contains a number signifying what cluster from the cell it belongs to. This 
number is shared for both read of a pair, a suffixed tag indicates whether the read is forward 
or reverse. In addition the reads tend to be ordered in the two files in a way such that read 
number x in both files belong to the same pair.  
Paired End Reads can be used for several purposes. If the amplicon is large the two reads will 
be non-redundant, yet with a spacer of known distance in between. This structure is valuable 
in whole genome sequencing as it supplies larger “building blocks” which facilitate assembly 
[16]. If the amplicon is small the two reads will overlap which supplies a measure of 
proofreading that gives more confidence in the quality of the reads, if a duplicate position is 
wrong, it must be identically wrong in both directions to cause actual errors. While a position 
with disagreements between the two reads would have issues with which base is the correct 
one, it would at least be clear that there is an issue. In contrast to positions that are only 
covered by either read, in which case an error can only be caught if the quality score is low. 
In this study it will be assumed that bases are correctly sequenced for the 250 bp length reads. 
Which means that reads can be evaluated individually for their predicted identification 
performance.

1.8. 
Melting temperature 
During PCR a number of factors influence what is produced. One such factor that can 
potentially lead to undesirable regions in the sample is when the primers have different 
melting temperatures, which enables off-target binding.  
In Watson-Crick base pairing, the A-T pairs bind with two hydrogen bonds whereas the G-C 
pairs bind with three hydrogen bonds. As a result of this disparity in bonds the sequence of a 
DNA strand influences its affinity for the complementary strand, the binding energy 
increases. High binding affinities leads to increased stability in the double stranded DNA 
complex. Thus it can be subjected to higher temperatures before the complex is denatured into 
the individual strands. The temperature at which a double stranded DNA complex will 
denature is referred to as the melting temperature. Ultimately this means that the melting 
temperature of a DNA complex can be calculated as a function of its sequence.

A big problem for amplification is when a primer binds in another location than the target. 
This process is called off-target binding, and can lead to fragments in the final product that are 
not desired. Off target binding can generally either happen when the primer is present in 
multiple copies on the sample strand, or the primer can bind with a number of mismatches. 
Since DNA has four different nucleotides each position has four different outcomes, the 
likelihood of a random sequence matching would thus be roughly 0.25lenght of the sequence. Thus it 
is much more likely for off-target binding occurring from mismatches than multiple copies. 
Mismatches lower the affinity of the complex and thus off-target binding can be limited by 
performing the amplification just below the melting temperature.

2. Method 
2.1. 
Overview 
Overall the Primer Finder platform operates in three steps: Subset preparation, Primer 
Discovery, and primer evaluation. 
Subset preparation is performed by running Libprep.pl a script that filters through the Silva 
database and extracts a number of genera for a subset.  Libprep.pl is called through a shell 
script that will make 100 subsets, each subset with a fixed number of genera depending on 
user input. For each subset a consensus sequence is made. In Illustration 1 this step is 
reflected in Database Preparation and Subets. 
Primer Discovery is then performed by running Profilegen.pl on each subset. Profilegen.pl 
works by going through the consensus sequences from the subset and marking the most 
conserved positions as potential primers. Discovery on each subset outputs a number of 
sequences. In effect Profilegen.pl is also called through a shell script to do each subset in 
parallel. Primersummary2.pl is then used to sum up the found primers across subsets, and the 
most frequently found are noted. 
The first step in primer evaluation is to run Primerreadscore.pl, which will take the list of the 
most common primers from Primersummary2.pl and check if they would theoretically 
produce a read pair in each of the subset consensus sequences. The Read Score step in 
Illustration 1, this leads into summing up the Read Scores for the primer pair by 
Primersummary3.pl in preparation for the coverage evaluation. 
The next step in evaluation is to check how the top primer pairs would theoretically bind to all 
the viable sequences in the Silva database. This is done through Bigreadmaker.pl by 
exhaustively checking if each primer pair binds, facilitated by a shell script to run it in parallel 
for each individual primer pair. Primersummary4.pl will then sum up the results of each stage 
and produce a single file with an overview of all the results for a particular number of genera. 
I.e. the results based on a single run of Libprep.pl.

2.2. 
Primer Finder steps  
2.2.1. Subset preparation 
As its input Primer Finder takes a database of aligned sequences. Primer Finder relies on 
position to position comparizons, gaps in the alignment can potentially mean a loss of position 
information which means that the comparizons becomes less accurate. How the position to 
position comparizons are performed will be elaborated on in the description of the primer 
discovery step in the next section. As such mismatches are much more tolerated than gaps. 
For this purpose the Silva database is subjected to alignment with high gap opening and 
extension penalties (15 and 2 respectively). Silva does make alignments of their releases, but 
for the 119 release their aligner had a leniency towards gaps which means that their result 
cannot be used for this platform.  
In this thesis the final alignments are performed using the command line version of Clustal2 
(ClustalW2) developed by Higgins et al. at the Conway institute, UCD [20]. 
To ensure that the sequences used are actually classified the identifier of each sequence is 
checked while splitting the sequences based on genus. If the identifier does not contain 
readable domain to species level information, the sequence is filtered out. To shorten the 
alignment process the sequences are split into their respective genus and each genus is aligned 
individually. For genera with many sequences in the Silva database 500 sequences are 
selected at random to be used for the genus alignment.  Each alignment is stored in a separate 
file for the genus. 
From the genus alignments a sequence profile is generated for each alignment. For this 
purpose each position of the alignment is iterated through and the information content is 
calculated by the Shannon Index. The information content of each position is stored as a 
sequence in a similar fashion to the quality scores of the fastq format. While calculating the 
information content the most frequent base for each position is noted as the consensus base. 
Thus the consensus sequence is generated to reflect the most common sequence. Note that 
positions with high information content will have more agreement between the consensus 
base and the genus sequences. This marks the end of the Database preparation box in 
Illustration 1.  
As the last step of subset preparation a number of genera are selected as the subset and their 
consensus sequences are aligned. The specific number of genera is a variable that depends on 
user input. In the end subset preparation outputs three files: one multiple alignment file of the 
consensus sequences, one file with the sequence of information content for each genus in the 
subset, and one file that is a list of the genera in the subset. It should be noted that the 
information content sequences match the length of the genus alignments, not the overall 
alignment. Subsetting is repeated 100 times, each iteration with the same number of genera 
chosen at random. Represented in Illustration 1 as the Subsets column. 
 
==PAGE:18==
Page 12 of 45 
 
2.2.2. Primer Discovery 
After the subsets are prepared, Primer discovery is performed individually on each of the 100 
subsets.  
The first step is to go through the training set alignment and generate a new profile of the 
genus consensus sequences. The process is the same as for the profiles generated during 
database preparation. With the addition that for each position the sum of information content 
in all the genera is added up. It should be noted that the information content for the training 
set sequences are loaded from the file that was generated during training set preparation. It is 
not possible to calculate based on the input to primer finder (information content is based on 
the original sequences in each cluster, which are only parsed to training set preparation).  
Once the overall consensus profile is generated the overall consensus sequence can be 
inspected for potential primers. For this purpose potential primers are referred to by their 
starting position and generated as a substring from that position and 15 bases forward. The 
potential of a primer starting from a specific position is estimated by checking whether or not 
it would theoretically bind to each individual genus consensus sequence in the subset. At this 
stage the focus is to get a quick evaluation of the position, thus the primer is only checked 
against the same position in the consensus sequences, not the entirety of each sequence. The 
primer is considered binding if it has one or fewer mismatches with the consensus sequence 
(in other words, up to one wobble position is allowed). The percentage of consensus 
sequences that theoretically binds to the primer is later referred to as bind fraction. Each 
consensus sequence is assigned a weight based on the information content in its primer 
region, this is expressed as its fraction of the maximum potential information content, thus if a 
consensus sequence is perfectly conserved at the position it would have weight 1. If any 
position in the middle of the consensus sequence is a gap, the primer matching around said 
gap would only match to the position on one side of the gap, the rest would be shifted one 
position to the side, preventing any binding. After checking all consensus sequences the 
position get a score based on the fraction of weighted consensus sequences that can 
theoretically bind to it. 
With all positions given a numerical score for their validity as a primer starting position, the 
potential forward and reverse reads for the highest scoring primer positions are evaluated. 
This is done by estimating how each individual position in the reads would contribute to a 
classification.  
For a read position to have any classification/predictive value, it must vary from genus to 
genus, but be conserved within a genus. A position is useless for classification if it is 
completely random under all circumstances, just like a position that is always constant yields 
no classification information either. The latter is important to note as it yields maximum 
information under the Shannon Index. Thus a read position should have maximum entropy in 
the new profile while the sum of information for all genera should be maximised. This 
measure will from here on be referred to as the Distinguish Power. In effect positions are 
evaluated by:

Primers are then paired by sorting the primer score list and iterating through the highest 
scoring primers one by one, considering the current primer position as the forward primer. For 
each forward position the primer list is iterated through again in respect to the reverse primer. 
If the reverse primer is at least 250 bp downstream of the forward primer, the pair is 
considered to produce a read pair of sufficient size. To get an estimate of whether or not the 
primer pair will perform similarly in a theoretical PCR setting, the melting temperature of 
both primers is calculated in accordance to equation 5. If there is a greater than three degree 
Kelvin difference in melting temperature the primers are considered incompatible and the pair 
is rejected, three was arbitrarily chosen as the threshold to have a restrictive estimate. As such 
each primer will act as the forward primer in a pair with another primer with which it is 
predicted to produce a read pair, these pairs are noted in a list. The primer list is sorted for the 
combined score of the selected primer starting positions.  
At this point each of the 100 subsets outputs a list of the primer pairs with the best theoretical 
coverage and matching temperatures. For each pair it is noted what the starting positions of 
the forward and reverse primers are. These lists are summed up and primer pairs are sorted 
based on how many runs featured said pair. This constitutes the Primer Discovery and Primer 
list boxes in Illustration 1. 
 
2.2.3. Primer evaluation  
To estimate how useful each primer pair would be for identification the Read Score of each 
primer is estimated. The Read Score is defined as the sum of Distinguish Power over all 
positions in the read. This is done by checking the list of primer pairs from primer discovery 
against the 100 subsets generated in subset preparation.  
While checking a primer pair against a subset the consensus sequence of said subset is first 
checked for the forward primer, if a binding position is found the following positions are 
checked for the reverse primer. Reverse primer checking continues until either a match is 
found or the distance exceeds the allowed amplicon size, in this case 1600 bp. Forward primer 
checking then continues from the found forward binding position (the entire sequence is 
checked for forward primer binding). While it would be faster to only check binding on the 
estimated positions that the primers were designed from, the process of alignment and 
consensus sequence generation tends to alter the sequence in ways that could make the 
primers incompatible. If the pair is found to match the consensus sequence, the theoretical 
Read Score is estimated by summing up the Distinguish Power of the positions in the 
simulated forward and reverse read (i.e. the 250 bp interval immediately following the 
Eq. 6 
==PAGE:20==
Page 14 of 45 
 
forward primer and the 250 bp immediately preceding the reverse primer). Any overlapping 
read positions are counted only once (in effect the overlapping positions are subtracted from 
the reverse read). If a primer pair does not match a consensus sequence it is assigned 0 as its 
Read Score. Note that this does not imply that a primer will be unable to produce reads from 
organisms that belong to the genus, just that the consensus sequence of said genus does not 
match the primer. 
For each primer pair the Read Scores across subsets are summed up and normalized to the 
theoretical maximum score. The theoretical maximum being the case in which the reads 
contains the full 500 positions, each position is completely conserved internally in each 
individual genus, and each position has an even distribution of bases between the consensus 
sequences. From this the average Read Score and standard deviation is calculated. Thus the 
primer pairs in the list from primer discovery are assigned a Read Score average and a 
standard deviation. 
For the final evaluation of the primers a larger test set consisting of all Silva sequences that 
were deemed viable while splitting up the genera is generated. 
Evaluation against the final test is performed by testing for each primer pair whether or not it 
would theoretically bind to each sequence in the set. Binding is tested in the same manner as 
described for Read Score estimation. Thus the best discovered primer pairs are tested against 
the database sequences directly. 
The final evaluation produces two outputs: First a summary file with the list of primer pairs 
tested and the coverage of each pair in four categories: total test set, sequences from Archaea, 
sequences from Bacteria, and sequences from Eukaryotes. Second is a detailed file of the 
coverage for each classification in each taxonomic level for each primer pair. The detailed file 
can be inspected to determine if a specific primer pair will be able to detect the organisms that 
could be present.  
 
2.3. 
Abandoned efforts 
To illustrate the efforts taken that did not make it into the final platform here will follow a 
description of the workflow related to said efforts. 
First off it was investigated what the current stance on primers was and it became apparent 
that their use and design was mostly taken for granted. For the most part it seemed like people 
simply picked some primers that had been used in previous studies without really specifying 
the reasoning behind it.  
The concept of treating individual positions as entities that could be evaluated independently 
of each other was decided upon early on as the most sensible approach to finding primers in 
an information driven fashion.  
Early on the major issue was how to include a way to identify the organisms that reads came 
from. It was investigated if individual positions in the 16S gene could be ranked on 
==PAGE:21==
Page 15 of 45 
 
information content or a similar measure. The highest scoring positions could then be used to 
construct a decision tree that would classify new reads based on the particular positions. The 
decision tree would be constructed by submitting the decision positions to a random forest 
evaluation. Validity could be estimated by leaf node purity and with a test set with positive or 
negative being the correct classification.  
The primary obstacle to this approach was that positions in real life sequences do not match 
1:1, and as seen in the final method, aligning the sequences globally in a manner that made 
the alignment usable for this thesis was unrealistic. If a query organism contained an insert in 
the read region before one of the identifier positions the entire read would be useless for 
identification. The idea was scrapped. 
To compensate for problems with a big alignment and to adjust for overrepresented organisms 
in the Silva database it was decided that input sequences should be clustered in a manner that 
could then form the basis for finding primers.  
Once it was decided to cluster the database sequences based on taxonomy it was investigated 
how each cluster could be verified to be homogenous in regards to the predictive 
performance. Taking a look at the Silva database it became apparent that not only was their 
complete alignment lenient towards gaps, a large number of the sequences seemed to be 
fragments with strange identifier lines. 
 At this point the platform used a training set – test set approach in which the Silva library was 
read through and a number of sequences with a valid identifier were extracted. The sequences 
were roughly clustered based on a desired taxonomic level in their identifier, more 
specifically genus. For each genus a file was made with the sequences belonging to said 
genus. Half the genus files were chosen at random to be used as the training set. For each 
training set genus an alignment was performed and the pairwise distances for the genus 
sequences were calculated. This was done in two steps because Clustal Omega was used for 
the alignment, which does not output distance matrices. 
From the pairwise distances each genus was subjected to hierarchical clustering and the 
resulting trees were iterated through to detect if any genus clusters should be split. The 
decision as to whether or not a cluster should be split was based on a distance threshold, if a 
join node happened at a distance greater than the threshold, the join would be rejected and the 
members of the two clusters would be assigned as cluster members. The distance threshold 
used was a user input. During this development phase both 0.03 and 0.05 sequence identity 
was used as the distance threshold. 
Ultimately it was decided that while it could possibly be used to filter out sequences wherein 
the taxonomic annotation and sequence similarity measures were not in concordance, the 
concept of improving on the Silva database was outside of the scope of this project and the 
clustering was scrapped in favour of simply trusting the Silva genus annotation.  
As the project progressed and the initial steps of Primer Finder started to work, the concept of 
classification was brought up again. The idea was to use the read regions from the training set 
sequences. Once a primer pair had been identified, the potential reads that would be generated 
==PAGE:22==
Page 16 of 45 
 
from sequencing an organism could be simulated by taking the positions following a site that 
could potentially bind the primer. In this approach the reads of the database sequences could 
then be simulated and indexed, giving a small annotated database of sequences that directly 
reflects how reads from sequencing the source organism would look. Once a biome had been 
sequenced with the primer pair, the resulting reads could then be classified by checking 
against the simulated database. The check would either be a direct comparison such as a hash 
lookup or through a small blast.  
In the end classification was dropped from the study mainly because the core aim of finding 
the best primers proved more time consuming than expected and classification was deemed a 
nice to have.

Evaluation Primer Finder 
To illustrate the behaviour of Distinguish Power and bind fraction along the length of the 
consensus sequence of a subset, the binding fractions (percentage of sequences that would 
theoretically bind) and the Distinguish Power of each position is plotted for a single subset.  
To test how the number of genera used for each subset influences the primer found by the 
method. Primer Finder was tested on subsets with 50 Bacterial genera, 200 Bacterial genera, 
500 Bacterial genera, 50 Archaea genera and five mixed genera. For each sample size the 15 
most frequently observed primers were further studied. 
To compare the Read Scores of the observed primers 1000 100-genera subsets were prepared 
and all the Read Scores of all the primers from the 50, 200, and 500 Bacterial sets were 
estimated against these 100-genera subsets. For reference the primers reported by Nossa et al 
were used [7]. 
The coverage of the observed primer pairs were tested against the large evaluation set in 
accordance to the primer evaluation step previously described. 
From the coverage and Read Score results four observed primer pairs and three of the Nossa 
pairs were selected for further analysis in regards to Read Scores and coverage. The Read 
Scores were investigated by testing them against the 50-, 100-, 200-, and 500-genera Bacterial 
subsets. The resulting Read Scores were plotted as a series of boxplots. 
For coverage the best pairs were tested against the primer evaluation set and each sequence is 
counted as either a hit or a miss for the Phylum it belongs to. From this a circular 
phylogenetic tree was made using the interactive Tree of Life (iToL) [21] to display and 
investigate how the distribution of hit and missed sequences is from a taxonomic viewpoint.   
After the taxonomic investigation has beed performed, it turned out that the identifiers

the 3955 genera. These will not be displayed in the tree, but can be seen as a Table in

As the aligner cannot throw away positions any inserts would be shown in the overall 
alignment. Primer Finder is set up to dismiss positions that are recognized as an insert from a 
few sequences, but such filtering is a threshold question without a clear yes or no answer. As 
such the particular settings that filtering should be performed at is a matter that requires some 
fine tuning to decide, a process that there simply has not been time for in this study. The 
problem with filtering is that every time something is filtered out some information is 
excluded from the system. Ultimately removing too many positions from the consensus 
sequence could result in important positions being disregarded. If a discovered primer target 
overlaps a position that has been discarded, the subsequent primer would have positions out of 
alignment from the true sequences and would be unable to bind.  
Another possibility is that it is a variable region that exists in a number of different versions, 
each of which is common enough to cause the aligner to overcome the inflated gap penalty. If 
that is the case the region would be more akin to how we look at alleles than a truly variable 
region. The fact that an allele-like relation between 16S sequences does not seem to have been

3.2.1. Primer positions 
If it is desired to annotate the found primers in relation to the E. coli reference sequence the 
primers could be mapped back against said sequence and their matching positions used as the 
official names. It is reasonable to assume that the best primer pair would theoretically bind to 
a region on the E. coli sequence simply because it is the gold standard and any pair that does 
not match would, barring special circumstances, have to be discarded for that reason alone.   
Regarding the position of primers from Tables 1-3 the standard deviation on primer position 
is between 50 and 100 for almost all bacterial primers. With primer starting positions being in 
a region at least 100 bases wide it becomes problematic to relate a particular primer to a 
specific variable region in the E. coli reference sequence, since such a region can span more 
than one variable region. This is why it would be necessary to map the primers back instead 
of taken the suggested position.  
Looking at Illustration 2 the distribution of high bind fraction positions does loosely match 
the conserved-variable-conserved region structure. From subset to subset the different peaks 
do shift a couple of positions in the same manner as the locations of the primers vary.  
 
3.2.2. Primer pairs with Read Scores at 0 
For the B500 primers it can be seen in Table 3 that five of the primer pairs have a Read Score 
at 0% while seven are at 0% < x < 10%, and all of them have a standard deviation that is at 
least equal to their average Read Score. For B500.2-4 their forward position is at around 1450 
while the reverse position is at approximately 1780, indicating that their missing Read Score 
might be a factor of the region being a gap in the same fashion as observed in Illustration 2. 
This explanation does stand in contrast to B500.7-8 each of which has roughly the same 
forward and reverse positions, but with Read Scores above zero. For each the Read Score is 
4% or lower while the standard deviation is 4% or greater, indicating that for the majority of 
subsets no read pair is predicted, but a few are with comparatively high Read Scores at 10-
12%.  
The contradictions between B500.2-4 and B500.7-8 could also arise from bugs in the code. 
An explanation that is substantiated by B500.2-4 being reported as having a read frequency of 
100% while having Read Scores and standard deviation at 0%. A similar behaviour is seen for 
B500.12-13 in relation to B500.11, B500.11 sharing its forward position with B500.12-13 and 
its reverse position with B500.2-4. 
Ultimately the 500-genera subsets do suffer from being a set of large alignments. The reverse 
positions tend to fall in the 1750-1820 range, which is around 200 positions beyond the 
“normal” length of the 16S gene. A tendency that can be seen expressed by the 200-genera 
subsets showing reverse positions around 1650-1700 and the 50-genera having a highest 
average reverse position of 1582. In essence, larger subsets lead to longer alignments, which 
in turn are caused by more gap regions that cause problems with the current implementation 
of primer finding and Read Score evaluation algorithms.

independently in all sample sizes, thus no pair has a bias over other pairs. For the Nossa 
primers all subsets are “new”, thus the Nossa primers are at an unfair disadvantage in 
comparison to the found primers and thus scores cannot be compared directly. These 
considerations notwithstanding, the tendencies observed for the Nossa primers and the 
observed primers respectively can still be discussed. 
 
3.4.1. How Read Score is influenced by sample size 
From Illustration 3 the width from the mean to the bottom quartile in most boxes means that 
in a large number of subsets no theoretical read pair is produced. For the most part the 
distance from the mean to the top quartile is less than three percent of the maximum 
theoretical Read Score. 
As discussed earlier in the consensus sequence example individual positions in the alignment 
are susceptible to alignment issues. While the positions used for primers should be highly 
conserved and thus much less subjected to insertions and deletions, it would only take one 
position that is inserted or deleted within the 26 non-terminal primer positions to count the 
primer pair as non-binding for that particular subset. Considering that the primers plotted each 
have more than 90% coverage for the raw sequences, it is reasonable to assume that they 
would not miss at least 25% of the time, especially since the subsets are consensus sequences 
which should further normalize towards the 90%.  
With primer binding being susceptible to alignment anomalies and primers binding to more 
than 90% of individual sequences, it is reasonable to assume that when a primer pair does not 
bind to a subset, it is most often because of alignment errors. 
Since Read Score is a measure of how well a theoretical read could be used to distinguish 
between reads from the same primer pair, the reads are only relevant when they are actually 
present. While it is relevant to note that a large portion of subsets do not produce a read, it is 
arguably only the subsets that produce reads that are relevant since those that do not produce 
reads likely do so due to the aforementioned alignment errors. Therefore in Illustration 4 the 
subsets with Read Scores at zero are omitted and it is thus showing only the results from 
positive reads. 
Looking at the nonzero plot it is apparent that Read Scores for the most part fall in the 30-
35% range, interestingly the selected Nossa primers have almost equal scores for the 50 and 
200-genera subsets, indicating that the Read Scores for the observed primers have not been 
overfitted. 
==PAGE:37==
Page 31 of 45 
 
The outlier primer pair is B50.1 with means about 10% lower than what is observed for the 
other primers, indicating that it would theoretically be comparatively poor at distinguishing 
between real reads from sequencing. This is unfortunate as it is also the pair with the highest 
coverage for Bacteria. This does stand in contrast to B50.5 and B50.7 which both have 
coverages at about 93,7% but Read Scores at twice what B50.1 has at around 33%.  
In regards to how sample size influences Read Scores each read pair should be viewed 
individually. For this the first loose trend is that the 50 and 100-genera subsets score about the 
same, with 100 trending slightly lower. The 200-genera subsets are always lower than the two 
prior, for the most part the upper quartile of 200 is below the lower quartile of 100. For B50.1 
a large upper limit is observed with scores above what is observed for the other subsets. For 
B50.3-7 roughly the same tendency is shown with boxes at essentially identical shapes and 
values. For Nossa nothing is observed, apparently the alignment artifacts associated with 
larger numbers of sequences introduce errors that make the Nossa primers incompatible. This 
might be caused by the fact that the Nossa primers are longer, and thus more susceptible to 
position errors. 
Another trend is that a number of sample sizes show a slight bimodal tendency, for the most 
part this is seen for the 100-genera subsets, which is likely enabled by the fact that 1000 
subsets have been run for this sample size in contrast to the 100 for the others. The higher 
number of dots increasing how large an outgroup can be without being part of a quartile. For 
the 200-genera subsets of B50.1, B50.7, and N2.1 similar outgroups are seen. This is likely 
because of the gap-anomaly effect discussed with the consensus example.

The presence of sequences with questionable identifiers amongst the sequences that have been 
marked as viable is a problem for the platform. When sequences are marked as the wrong 
genus they will tend to alter the consensus sequence of said genus from what would have truly 
been the consensus sequence. Misidentified sequences would thus push potentially all 
consensus sequences towards randomness, ultimately diluting the information of the system. 
The performance of any information driven method depends on the quality of the information 
that is put into the system. In this case Silva appears to have a significant problem with their 
identifiers. As such the results of this thesis are potentially severely hindered.  
During the early stages of the project some sequences where investigated and it seemed that 
filtering out sequences that did not fulfil the structure resembling taxonomic classification 
was sufficient to ensure a trustworthy set of sequences. As Silva is a large, well-known, 
supplier of 16S databases, it was assumed that the identifiers would make sense. 
It would appear that the identifier errors stems from allocating the wrong species information 
in the very last column. As such it could be something as simple as a coding error in an 
update that added sequences to the Silva database. 
The additional filter changed the number of phyla from 2133 to 1896 and it stands to reason 
that among the kept phyla a number of sequences were lost as well. As such it appears that 
enough sequences are at fault to influence the final result. Considering that a good primer pair 
can be made bad by a 2% decrease in coverage, which ultimately means that within the 
margin that improvements are observed. It would not take many wrong sequences to 
negatively influence the result.

3.5.1.  Primer Finder compared to Nossa 
Overall looking at the Nossa primers in comparison to the best primers found by Primer 
Finder It can be seen that the performance is largely alike. 
In Table 6 all pairs display large standard deviations, but the best Nossa primers show scores 
at 21% while the best found primers are at 25%, a higher trend, but the large standard 
deviations makes it ambiguous as to which set performs better. 
Overall the Nossa primers displays slightly lower coverages than the best primers observed 
through Primer Finder. The difference is small, going from 97.62% to 98.15% is only a 0.53% 
increase in coverage, but it is also a 20% decrease in missed sequences.

4. Conclusion and future perspectives  
The aim of this thesis was to develop a platform that could generate 16S primers information 
driven from a database of sequences. An aim that, assuming the evaluation tests performed 
are valid, has been fulfilled.  
It is observed that the primers found by Primer Finder in this thesis are comparable in 
performance to the Nossa reference primers within the confines of the evaluation tests. 
The high variation in both primer positions and read scores indicates that there is some work 
to be done in refining the platform to make the output more reliable and perfecting the read 
score estimation. While the evaluation steps performed in this thesis are not exhaustive, and 
should be seen as a proof of concept, the achieved results are promising for the viability of 
Primer Finder as a primer finding platform.  
There are currently plans at the Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Basic Metabolic 
Research to continue the development of Primer Finder with the intentions of using the 
resulting primers in future sequencing projects. The development would aim to make Primer 
Finder available to the research community as an open-source Perl package that predicts 
primer and their performance on a set of sequences representing copies of the same gene in a 
set of taxonomically classified organisms. The concrete application that the software is 
developed for is primer evaluation for 16S rRNA sequences from Bacteria and Archaea, the 
software and algorithms however, are sufficiently generalised to be used on any set of 
homologous sequences with known taxonomic annotation.
