The motivation for studding the theoretical knowledge of lean construction and last planner system 
was created based on course 42871 - Planning and control of building projects at the Technical 
University of Denmark, where a basic knowledge about Lean was obtained. And later in the light of 
experience as student assistance on a building site, where the conditions and the process were far

Summary 
 
This thesis deals with the changes in organizations during implementation of new systems, where 
the implementation of Last Planner System on construction projects is in focus. The theoretical 
method within Last Planner System and Takt Time Planning are presented, along with which steps 
are recommended to go through during processes of change and what is needed for the employees 
in the processes of change to accept and support it and become capable of contributing to the 
implementation. 
The ongoing construction of Building 202, a part of the Life Science & Bioengineering complex at 
the Technical University of Denmark is used as a case study, as in connection with the interior 
work, it is required to apply Last Planner System under the term Trimit, in the entire organization.  
The client, DTU Campus Service desires to become a Lean based client and the construction of 
building 202 is used as a pilot project and is the first step towards the desired goal.   
The foundation for a Lean based project was present as it was chosen to include Alan Mossman and 
Sven Bertelsen, both are acknowledged consultants within Lean Construction. They were 
responsible for the start-up phase consisting of numerous workshops, where the foundation for a 
successful execution was set through a thorough planning of the phase plan supported by a division 
for a Takt Plan. 
Due to several displacements and a not aligned implementation strategy the people who were in 
charge of leading and sustaining the Lean implementation were not competent at it. The execution 
met several obstacles due to the fact that the design and procurement was performed staggered, 
along with several structural problems, made the planned rhythm impossible to follow. This quickly 
resulted in a time delay, the tools within Trimit never managed to be used and the conducted plans 
were skipped and traditional production management was established.  
It was decided to replace the construction management. This was however not an action to promote 
the implementation of Trimit. Some parts of Trimit are still visible on the site and more tools are 
slowly entering, but not in form of a common strategy or an initiative from the top management.  
The experience which should be drawn from building 202 towards future projects and development 
of the Lean competence is that, tendering document should be more specific to get the right 
companies along, the procurement of the trades should happen simultaneously and during the 
design if possible. The specification of requirement must be formulated so it supports the Lean 
mindset. Lastly, a strong guiding coalition should be included along with a process manager who 
can maintain the Lean implementation until it is anchored in the organization.

2 Introduction 
 
Use of Lean Construction:  
The implementation of Lean in the construction industry has been in progress since 1999 in 
Denmark and is still ongoing (Bertelsen, 2003). Lean construction as a concept originated through 
the work that was performed at Stanford University in California in the early 1990s as an attempt to 
apply the modern Japanese production thinking in the construction process. Lean Construction is a 
completely new way of handling a building project, compared to the traditional way it is handled 
(Lean Construction – DK, 2015).  
The tools of Lean Construction are not used by the whole industry. It has been successfully 
implemented in several Danish companies but mostly widespread among contractors, and some 
companies using parts of the tools (Lean Construction – DK, 2015). The tendency is that the 
companies who possess competences of Lean tools only use these when it is possible or when it is 
required within the contractual framework. 
The Last Planner System, which is one of the most known Lean tools, is a short-term planning 
system that creates more predictable and reliable production, and will enable the project team to 
manage the process using pull logistics rather than the traditional push logistic. In this way the 
project’s actual state determines what will happen. 
Several companies have not achieved the desired results from Lean tools, while others have had 
more success. It has been proven that the tools themselves enable productive abilities in several 
situations. The difference must therefore lie in how the tools are used and not in the tools that are 
used (Bicheno, 2008). 
 
Organizational change in the construction industry:  
When making organizational and management changes in the construction industry, there is a 
challenge regarding the number of stakeholders and various people involved throughout a project. 
This means that during a change process, such as implementing Lean Construction, it is an 
important precondition that all main actors support the decision.  
Anchoring and retention starts at the top. Further down the chain it is impossible to maintain the 
changes in teams if staff turnover is greater than 30% per year, which is quite usual in the 
construction industry. This means that there is always a focus on shaping and understand the new 
process, and never to get a focus on performing (Bicheno, 2008). 
Around the world the construction sector delivers over half of projects late (= waste of time); many 
projects are over budget (= waste of money) and others are delivered with a reduced specification or 
quality (= waste of opportunity) in an effort to bring them in under budget. Finally, even though 
safety and health are improving, far too many employees and passers-by are killed and maimed by

Traditional methods of construction management based on use of project programs developed using

they manifest (Møller & Bottheim, 2015). Whereas Lean Construction says it above all is to create a

The client always has the ultimate responsibility for the building project, and is therefore the most 
suitable for contributing to the change processes to the new building culture. The client’s most 
important tool is to use the specification phase to formulate a strategic framework for respectively 
the concept phase and construction phase. (Bertelsen, et al., 2002) 
 
Case study:  
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has only just begun its journey toward being a Lean 
based client. The aim is eventually to be able to improve the safety level, be able to attract the best 
companies and at the same time obtain the lowest prices.  
The pilot project is the ongoing construction project of building 202, Life Science & 
Bioengineering at DTU, where the building client, DTU Campus Service (DTU CAS) has required 
application of Last Planner System and organized an implementation process under the term Trimit.  
Therefore, experts in Lean processes were collected and a long start-up period with workshops was 
selected to ensure a solid implementation. Beginning far too late in the process along with a number 
of obstacles during the execution led to the use of Trimit was stopped. Despite this, the use of some 
of the tools remained a part of the process.     
The brutal fact is that about 70% of all change initiatives fail (Beer & Nohria, 2011). Did the 
methods fail or did they not give the expected results, this information should not be hid in any 
ways. On the contrary, it is often the failed experiments in which the learning is greatest. The errors 
can give rise to test the theory: can it explain why it went wrong? In addition, studies describing 
what looked promising, but did not work in practice, may deter others from repeating the same 
mistakes (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). 
The case study of building 202 is studied by analyzing the content of the tool Trimit, the 
preconditions and the implementation which is performed; this is compared with change 
management to find the rote of the occurred issues and the current state. The relevance is lying in 
the learning aspect. If the use of Lean and Last Planner System should be developed it is important 
to look at both successful and unsuccessful cases. This construction project represents an interesting

3 Theory 
 
This section gives a review of the theoretical knowledge gathered from literature within this 
research area. The section is divided into subsections for each of the various research areas. The 
first section is a basic overview of Lean Construction and its origins in the production industry 
followed by a description of Last Planner System as a tool and how it is used. Then an introduction 
to the concept of Takt Time Planning is given. The final section focuses on organizational and 
implementation change and how this could be applied to overcoming the challenges within 
implementing Lean Construction.  
 
3.1 Lean Construction 
The point of this research is not to define Lean Construction (LC), but a short description of its 
origins is provided to give a basic overview.  
LC as a concept originated through the work that was performed at Stanford University in 
California in the early 1990s as an attempt to apply the principles of Lean Production transferred to 
the construction process, as well as a variety of tools designed specifically for the construction 
industry (Lean Construction – DK, 2015). One of the main inspiration sources was car manufacturer 
Toyota, which uses the production philosophy Lean production. LC is primarily a different way of 
thinking in terms of planning and management of construction projects compared to traditional 
production management (Lean Construction – DK, 2015). 
In 2004, Lean construction - DK was founded and adopted “Trimmet Byggeri” as the Danish 
version and further development of LC (Bertelsen, 2012).  
Denmark is one of the leading countries within LC. The concept is supplemented by the results 
from a number of Danish experiments with the development of a better and more efficient 
construction process. The aim of this tool is to promote construction processes that maximize 
customer value while minimizing waste, which provides an increased productivity (Lean 
Construction – DK, 2015). The key to the aim is not just a matter of know-how alone, but just as 
much to know-why (Bertelsen, 2012). 
The construction process is perceived as a complex and dynamic system, where the state of the 
system is only predictable a limited time forward. Such a process is on the edge of chaos and 
includes waste (Bertelsen, 2003). The reasons for the waste are many, but most of them can be 
reduced to being part of two: the preconditions for the work are not present, and construction is not 
organized in order to create an even flow of work. To create a building process with less waste, we 
must change the system itself, and create a smoother workflow. It is here LC enters (Bertelsen, 
2003).

LC use some principals inspired by the Japanese industry, which is:  
 First run: Is in a test production as early as possible before the actual production, it reveals 
inappropriateness of the project at a time where it is possible to correct them, and it shows 
the craftsmen what is required for their contribution to the process. 
 Single-piece flow: In the construction it means that the trades should work as closely 
together as possible and the flow must be kept. The errors that are not detected by those who 
made them, are often found in the next operation. And therefore it is beneficial to bring the 
trades/operations close to each other. 
 Stop-the-line: By pursuing the cause of any failure completely to its source and tackling its 
causes immediately. (Bertelsen, 2003) 
Problem solving must be conducted around the real reason. This is fundamentally the Lean 
philosophy. This means that problems should be solved at the root, rather than on the superficial or 
immediately obvious levels (Bicheno, 2008). To reach the core of the problem, it is necessary not to 
accept the first answer you get for the problem. But to use a questioning attitude, to always get in 
behind the answer. The underlying causes that created the problem to begin with must be found, and 
the solution is to take place on this level, to prevent the problem to reoccur again (Bicheno, 2008). 
The Lean technique called “5 whys” is used, the technique is simple - it requires that you ask why 
several times in succession. The inventor of the technique - Toyota - has experienced that you 
should ask five times in a row before the real cause of the problem is clear. (Bicheno, 2008) 
LC can benefit the whole construction process and there are theories about the use in both the 
design and the execution phase.  
 
3.1.1 Implementation of Lean Construction  
The tools of LC are not used by the whole construction industry. It has been successfully 
implemented in several Danish companies but mostly widespread among contractors, because the 
focus has for some years been on production at the construction site. The tendency is that the 
companies who possess competences of LC only use it when it is required in the framework (Lean 
Construction – DK, 2015). 
LC is a broad concept that targets the entire building process, and the use of LC is increasing in the 
construction industry at all levels. But there are still many challenges, many companies do not 
maintain the work with LC and forget to challenge the processes further (Arlbjøm, et al., 2008). 
Several companies have not achieved the desired results from LC tools, while others have had more 
success. It has been proven that the tools themselves enable productive abilities in several 
situations. The difference must therefore lie in how the tools are used, and not in the tools that are 
used (Bicheno, 2008).

The network around LC is still dynamic. Every year, new papers, new recognition and more people 
participate in the conferences. Some participate only once, but many have over the years remained 
in the network - captured by the inspiring and informal dialogue that takes place. And together they 
have within this network created a whole new understanding of the construction process (Bertelsen, 
2003). 
In the book Bedre Byggeprocesser, a number of clients, consultants and contractors tell of their way 
to re-think their part of the construction process and the results they have achieved. No one claims 
to have found the ultimate way. But they have all come up with something that works - and that is 
to make them more efficient and productive in the way they perform their tasks (Østergaard, 2009).  
The use of the methods within LC has repeatedly, both in Denmark and internationally, found that 
productivity increases substantially and there are examples of an immediate increase in the value-
added within time and economy.  At the same time, the number of errors and accidents significantly 
decreases and job satisfaction and communication are improved (Østergaard, 2009). 
The association Lean Construction - DK says that it is the individual companies and through their 
ideas and initiatives, that LC is developed (Østergaard, 2009). 
 
3.2 Last Planner System 
The tools within Last Planner System (LPS) are shortly described in order to accomplish a basis for 
comparing the theory behind the tool, with the tool used in practise. But the deeper understanding of 
LPS will not be provided in this study. 
LPS is a planning and management tool developed by Glenn Ballard and Greg Howell (Lean 
Construction Institute, 2015). The tool was created due to problems with construction plans often 
being made too generally and therefore resulting in the fact that plans cannot be held. It is a tool 
developed on the basis of the principles of LC on an assumption that the construction process is 
chaotic and therefore cannot be planned (Simonsen, 2007).  
The purpose of the LPS is to improve the predictability and reliability of time schedules that can be 
implemented in practice. The system creates a pull process, where the project is not controlled by 
the plans, but the actual situation on the construction site (Lean Construction - DK, 2009). Tasks are 
not launched because the plan says so, but because the situation on the ground says it is ready and it 
is the last part in the chain that regulates the process (Lean Construction - DK, 2009). 
The principle of the LPS is:  
 Plan in greater detail as you get closer to doing the work - As we get closer to the moment of 
execution of work the factor of uncertainty reduces drastically. 
 Produce plans collaboratively with those who will do the work. 
 Reveal and remove constraints on planned tasks as a team. 
==PAGE:20==

 Make reliable promises. 
 When promises are broken, find root causes and preventions—learn from those break-
downs. (Zimina & Pasquire, 2012) 
The result is better communication, overview and improved workflow, which in turn leads to better 
compliance time, better revenue, better security and greater job satisfaction (Lean Construction - 
DK, 2009).  
LPS is a management tool where the essential part is that the master Schedule with overall 
milestones is divided into three levels: Phase Plan, Look ahead Plan and Weekly plan, and these 
plans are concluded by an evaluation tool Percentage Plan Complete:  
Phase Plan:  
Phase Plan is the first plan which is developed, which takes you up to a specific milestone and is 
developed based on the master Schedule.  The purpose of the phase plan is reaching agreement on 
the upcoming activities and to find and discuss the process from the different subjects, such as in 
what order and how much time is needed for each activity, within each milestone (Bertelsen, 2003).  
In the phase planning each trade writhe their activities in the project on post-it labels, and put them 
on the wall in the order they believe them to be carried out. Along the way the trades plan the order 
and the placement of the labels. The planning is carried out from behind and focuses on the 
operations that should be done, before the given activity can be completed (Lean Construction - 
DK, 2009). 
The Phase plan must be performed by the people who have to perform the work. The participators 
must therefore be trade contractors and site foremen and the consulting engineers can 
advantageously be included in the process as questions often occur that they need to clarify (Lean 
Construction - DK, 2009). 
Look Ahead Plan:  
Based on the time schedule in the Phase Plan the Look ahead Plan contains the activities that fall 
within the next coming 4-8 weeks, which means it is a “rolling” work plan (Bertelsen, 2003). This 
is performed in meeting with the trade contractors/construction management and sometimes 
including the foremen by walking through upcoming activities and making sure any possible 
obstacles are removed (Lean Construction - DK, 2009). The idea is that proactive problem-solving 
should replace reactive fire fighting on site (Zimina & Pasquire, 2012). This is carried out by 
analyzing whether the seven streams are present.

3.3 Takt Time Planning 
The use of Takt time to plan construction work is rooted in Lean where the production rate is set to 
match the demand rate. Takt time is defined as: the unit of time within which a product must be 
produced (supply rate) in order to match the rate at which that product is needed (demand rate) 
(Frandson, et al., 2015). 
Takt Time Planning (TTP) and the LPS work well together because LPS provides the control 
mechanism and stability of the production system and TTP provides the means of a continuous 
workflow for trade activities (Frandson, et al., 2014). Said in other words, TTP expands the LPS by 
adding more continuous work and flow of the work for the LPS to control to, and the LPS provides 
the ability to control and adapt the planning where continuous flow is not possible. (Frandson, et al., 
2014). TTP increases the urgency for make ready analysis because failure to do so will immediately 
affect several trades (Frandson, et al., 2014).   
According to Frandson, et al. (2015) TTP consists of a six step process, as shown in  
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Six steps in TTP 
The objective is to produce a production plan that gives a balanced work flow for an area or to a 
milestone within the time allotted. Each trade must complete their work in each zone within a 
specific amount of time, namely the Takt time (Frandson, et al., 2015). Each trade will proceed 
through a sequence of zones, where the Takt time is the same in each zones, this means that the 
trade task durations typically will vary through the different zones. (Frandson, et al., 2015)  
The trades are expected to finish at or ahead of the Takt time, and can then work “off Takt” work on 
e.g. workable backlog, in buffer zones or prepare for the next Takt sequence. If crews are working 
quickly or id the task durations is low in a zone, less manpower is required to complete the task 
within the Takt time. (Frandson, et al., 2015). Is a Takt time not completed by a trade, the work may 
be performed immediately with overtime and increased manpower or completed during the 
following Takt time, as long as it does not interfere with the following trade. The work may be left 
out if the problem cannot be solved immediately e.g. missing design details. The reason for the miss 
should be researched to avoid that it is repeated in the future (Frandson, et al., 2015). 
To ensure the flow can be maintained it is important that every trade ensure that their sequences are 
feasible and that the Takt time is sufficient to perform the work in each zone. (Frandson, et al., 
2015).  
Common understanding is considered the eighth flow, added to Koskela's seven flows in 
construction. TTP provides the opportunity for the entire team, to develop a common understanding

3.4 Organizational change 
Organizational change is the movement of an organization away from its present state and toward 
some desired future state to increase its efficiency and effectiveness (Jones & George, 2013).  
In the last couple of years the number of concepts related to organizational change is increased 
(Kamp, et al., 2005), therefore, there are lots of theories within this topic. In this study some of the 
concepts and ideas are selected to be presented. Both knowledge from general organizational 
change concepts and concepts within Lean and LC is obtained.  
Evidence or proof of scientific sense whether a concept is working does not exist and can also be 
difficult to bring to light. The organization is not a laboratory isolated from the outside world, and 
therefore it is difficult to identify clearly the reasons for success or failure (Kamp, et al., 2005).  
The most often seen method is that the management chooses a concept and then implements it by, 
so to speak "follow the recipe,", however, this is not a viable solution. Most people who have 
experience of change management is aware that changing of any organization is a long lasting and 
often incomplete process that causes significant challenges for those leading the change, and for 
those involved or affected. This also applies when using standardized management tools (Kamp, et 
al., 2005). 
When a company has decided to develop an organization change, it is important to realize that this 
is not a single project, but that it requires a development that extends over a number of years. The 
development is in relation to a number of construction projects, where the methods are tested and in 
the intervening periods where actual outcomes and results processing take place (Bertelsen, et al., 
2002) 
To conduct the implementation of new forms of management is generally a challenge, because it 
breaks with the usual ways of working. Changes of culture constitute the most prominent barrier to 
the implementation of Lean. Other important barriers are related to education and training, retention 
and understanding of the Lean concept and specific practices including lack of basic understanding 
of the need for Lean (Arlbjøm, et al., 2008). It is a mental barrier which has to be moved if Lean 
should be introduced successfully. Employees must be happy using it, customers must be happy 
about it, and the owners should be happy about it. Only when we manage to get the three parties to 
be happy and satisfied, you have success with Lean; but it can succeed (Christensen, et al., 2010).  
A company is more on top of the situation when it is an internal pressure of change. Internal 
pressure for change is usually when the company's management or employees foresee a need for 
change in order to meet future events (Hildebrandt & Brandi, 2005). The client always has the

ultimate responsibility for the building project, and is therefore the most suitable for contributing to 
the change processes to the new building culture (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). But constructions are a

better to recognize that and relate the construction process to its true nature - where order is a

3.4.1 Structure of change 
Most literature about change in organizations emphasized the importance of structuring the process. 
Models which are often used in planning processes of change divide the process into manageable 
phases that follow one after another. These models are far from a complete picture of a change 
process, but they give a good indication of the process (Kamp, et al., 2005). 
To begin with, the targets/goal is defined; what you want to achieve. Subsequently it is planned how 
to reach the target. Then the plan is conducted, and finally the results are evaluated in relation to the 
objectives that were set up. There is a logical sequence in which each phase is a prerequisite for the 
other. In practice there may of course be a number of back loops in this process (Kamp, et al., 
2005). 
John P. Kotter delivers eight steps with an emphasis ranging from vision to culture and how they 
are seen as the keys to successful change of any magnitude in organizations. The processes of the 
eight steps are each associated with one fundamental error that undermines change work. Below is 
listed the Eight Step Process for creating major change, along with the eight mistakes that cause 
change leaders to fail (Kotter, 1999): 
Step 1: Establish a sense of urgency 
 Examining market and competitive realities 
 Identifying and discussing crises, potential crises, or major opportunities 
By far the most common error is to engage in a change without establishing a strong enough sense 
of urgency among managers and employees. This error is fatal because the changes never meet their 
targets when complacency is too high. They underestimate how difficult it is to move employees 
out of their convenience.  
Kotter suggests that 75 percent of a company’s management need to trust the change for it to be 
successful (Kotter, 2011).   
Step 2: Forming a powerful guiding coalition 
 
 Assembling a group with enough power to lead the change effort 
 Encouraging the group to work together as a team

It’s easy to underestimate the time and effort required to produce change, therefore it is important to 
have a strong guiding coalition. Individuals alone never have all the assets needed to overcome 
tradition and inertia.  
Step 3: Development of a vision and strategy 
 Creation of a vision that can manage the change process 
 Developing strategies for the realization of the vision 
A vision plays a central role in the process because they help guide, coordinate and inspire the 
actions of a large number of its members.  
A useful rule of thumb: Whenever you cannot describe the vision in five minutes or less and get a 
reaction that signifies both understanding and interest, you are in for trouble. 
Step 4: Communicate the vision 
 Using every vehicle possible to communicate the new vision and strategies 
 Teaching new behaviours by the example of the guiding coalition 
A great change is often impossible unless the majority of employees are willing to help, often to an 
extent that involves short-term sacrifice. But people will not make sacrifices if they do not perceive 
the potential benefits of change as appealing, and if they truly believe that change is possible. 
Without credible communication, the employees' hearts and minds never are captured by the idea.  
Step 5: Create conditions for action on a broad basis 
 Removing obstacles 
 Changing systems or structures that seriously undermine the change vision 
 Encourage risk taking and non-traditional ideas, activities and actions 
New initiatives often approach to nothing when employees - despite the acceptance of a new vision

obstacles it weakens the employees and undermine change.  
Step 6: Generate short-term wins 
 Planning for visible improvements in performance or 'victories' 
 Realization of these victories 
 Visible recognizing and rewarding employees involved in the improvements

and enjoy. Without short-term wins many give up or become active opponents. The commitment to 
produce short-term gains can help keep complacency down.

 Using increased credibility to change all systems, structures and policies that do not fit 
together and not in harmony with a vision for change 
 Hiring, promoting, and developing employees who can implement the vision  
 Reinvigorating the process with new projects, themes, and actors

3.4.3 People 
One of the factors that make building production special is that construction is project-oriented; 
each project leads to a single construction. Overall, it is harder to gain from a repeating effect, 
although, in each project, there will of course occur many repetitions. Furthermore, a new team is 
composed for each construction project, where the client composes a team of consultants and 
contractors. But it also applies to the construction site, where it is constantly new constellations of 
companies that will be responsible for the performance - this applies even down to the individual 
construction crew who might not even be consistent within the project. The many players and their 
relationships make the construction process complex (Arlbjøm, et al., 2008).  
In addition, the construction industry competition and tradition of the developer chooses its partners 
and suppliers for the lowest price, often for a collaboration marked by mistrust. The challenge for 
the construction industry is to break with the prevailing distrust tradition and take innovative steps 
towards new forms of cooperation characterized by trust and shared values. (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). 
The first step when you have to compose the team that can control a change is to find the right 
members, there must be enough experienced managers in the group to implement the change 
process. When managers do not really think that a change is needed, or that they do not believe that 
a strong team is required to manage the change, they do not take the necessary initiatives, even if 
they have the expertise or good advice (Kotter, 1999). It is essential to include the right people to 
obtain a successful transformation process. Collins (2001) showed that the leaders did not reach the 
targets by setting out a new direction, vision and a new strategy. The core is that the foundation is 
laid by getting the right people. The executives who succeeded with the transformations, they first 
got the right people on the bus (and the wrong people off the bus) and then figured out where to 
drive it.  
Secondly if you have the right people along, the problem of how to motivate and manage people

3.4.4 Education  
Even though education, training and communication are the key success criteria for implementation 
of Lean, these are often reduced in implementation processes, particularly if the agreed schedule of 
activities begins to slip (Arlbjøm, et al., 2008).  
In practice, there are many choices to make, choices that will be crucial for both the process 
direction and momentum. The choice of variant and consultant determines, in effect, what kind of a 
concept you get to work with (Kamp, et al., 2005). Development processes can usually benefit from 
associating an independent consultant to the change process (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). 
Within a learning perspective it is necessary to consider what prerequisites the different actor 
groups involved in the change process has, to acquire to new ways of thinking and practices. And 
what options they have to experiment and reflect (Kamp, et al., 2005). Implementation of Lean is 
==PAGE:32==
Planning Levels in LPS – Theory and Practice 
 
Page 24 of 108  
 
Michelle Mollerup 
 
not necessarily the same for all actors. Therefore, paying attention to the different types of recipient 
groups, their culture and values plays a key role in the communication of the Lean concept and the 
Lean activities and terminology should be articulated with the receiver for an optimal understanding 
and implementation of LC (Arlbjøm, et al., 2008).  
The tendency is that employees are expected to change habits that have been built up over years or 
decades, after a few days of training. Employees have learned some technical skills, but no social 
skills or attitudes that are necessary to make new arrangements work (Kotter, 1999).  
Motivation is a key prerequisite for learning processes can succeed. Therefore, the groups which are 
subject to change and therefore must participate in the learning process should be involved in the 
change project and feel ownership. Furthermore, they must also be involved in assessments of 
whether the process is realistic - time, resources and radicalism (Kamp, et al., 2005). This approach 
is comparable with the method in LPS where the planning of the work is performed by those to 
execute it.  
Employees receive training before they start their new tasks, but there is often no follow-up to help 
them with the problems they run into when doing these tasks (Kotter, 1999). And too often it is 
forgotten to inform about the basis when new participants join. And with the many mischievous 
problems it is thus a great risk that the solutions progress is lost (Bertelsen, 2003).  
There must in the organization be ensured that there are opportunities for what is learned is also 
maintained and used by creating the basis for a constant learning. (Bertelsen, 2003). The important 
thing is to maintain some strong key developments and to ensure that they have sufficient time and 
resources to sustain the process. This is necessary to avoid falling back to the usual standards and 
attitudes when everyday life begins (Bertelsen, et al., 2002).  
To ensure continual guidance, a process manager can be engaged throughout the project. The 
process manager represents the Lean expertise and has overall responsibility for maintaining Lean, 
by facilitating the process - but not by carrying out the process or be a part of the normal operations 
(Bicheno & Holweg, 2010) A process manager’s role is to make sure that the process does not stop, 
either as a result of conflicts, burnout or lack of resources by involving the actors in coaching/self-
diagnosis and to improve the actors ability within the concept (Jacobsen, 2005). Experience shows 
that the gain of efficiency exceeds the additional cost of this kind of leadership (Bertelsen, et al., 
2002). 
 
3.4.5 Evaluation 
Evaluation is a systematic collection of experience and data on completed task, followed by a 
systematic assessment of the results through, for example, a comparison with other similar data. It is 
difficult to make numbers comparable across construction projects, since there are no buildings 
which are the same (Bertelsen, et al., 2002).  
==PAGE:33==
Theory 
 
 
 
 
 
Page 25 of 108 
 
It is important to establish a system that continuously keeps the process under observation, and 
which makes it possible to improve the process week by week. The evaluation should be used as a 
tool to active learning and development, which is aimed at the greatest possible value to this 
construction while it is ongoing, for next time and for the continuous development and should be 
done as close as possible to the time where the work is performed; otherwise the evaluation only 
have moderate practical effect (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). 
The evaluation is likely to become distorted if there are any financial or other benefits to achieve a 
given outcome, so there should be a clear objective of the evaluation and that the actors, who 
perform the task, also see the point of doing it and accept the associated costs (Bertelsen, et al., 
2002). 
Evaluation of the work helps to give visibility to short-term gains. The role of the Short-term gains 
are to provide proof that the sacrifice is not vain, undermine opponents, strengthens the positive 
feedback, work ethic and motivation and gives progress (Hildebrandt & Brandi, 2005). A good 
short-term gain must contain the following three characteristics, it is visible, it is clear and it is 
clearly linked to the change initiative (Kotter, 1999).  
The client should in the concept stage ensure that there are clear follow-up systems for value based 
management and quality control of the change process (Bertelsen, et al., 2002), and it needs to be 
communicated, as it is important for the involved actors to know how the process is going and 
whether it is on the right track (Christensen, et al., 2010). 
 
3.4.6 Motivation 
To introduce Lean is partly to apply Lean principles and tools, but it is also to create conditions and 
frameworks which make the employees motivated in the process (Christensen, et al., 2010). 
Motivation is central to management because it explains why people behave the way they do in 
organizations, and the anchoring of implemented tools in an organization is related to the degree to 
which people are motivated to use them. In Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, the need to have a 
foundation of trust and support is described, but anchoring requires interest and involvement 
(Bicheno & Holweg, 2010).  
Managers try to motivate members of an organization to contribute inputs to achieve goals, through 
their behaviour, effort, and persistence; by ensuring that members of an organization obtain the 
outcomes they desire (Jones & George, 2013). 
People are motivated to work in organizations to obtain certain outcomes and the motivation can 
come from intrinsic or extrinsic sources: 
Intrinsically motivated behaviour is behaviour that is performed for its own sake; the source of 
motivation is to actually perform the behaviour, and motivation comes from doing the work itself. 
==PAGE:34==
Planning Levels in LPS – Theory and Practice 
 
Page 26 of 108  
 
Michelle Mollerup 
 
The motivation can come from e.g. autonomy, responsibility, a feeling of accomplishment, and the 
pleasure of doing interesting or enjoyable work. 
Extrinsically motivated behaviour is behaviour that is performed to acquire material or social 
rewards or to avoid punishment; the source of motivation comes from the consequences they 
receive as a result of their work behaviour, not the behaviour itself. The motivation can come from 
e.g. pay, job security, benefits, and vacation time (Jones & George, 2013). 
 
People can be intrinsically or extrinsically motivated or both at the same time and it is not the same 
things that motivate different people. (Jones & George, 2013). But according to Herzberg's 
motivation / hygiene theory, hygiene factors such as wages and working conditions can demotivate, 
but not motivate. Only motivators like recognition and personal satisfaction can actually motivate 
(Bicheno & Holweg, 2010). 
 
3.4.7 Vision 
When a leader starts to implement Lean, the visions should be so clear that the employees get a 
picture of how their workplace looks when Lean is adopted (Christensen, et al., 2010). 
The client’s most important tool is to use the specification phase to formulate a strategic framework 
for respectively the concept phase and construction phase (Bertelsen, et al., 2002), as an effective 
vision and supporting strategies can help to solve many problems during the implementation.  
The vision specifies the general course of the change; secondly it motivates the employees to take 
steps in the right direction, thirdly, it helps to coordinate different people's actions in a remarkably 
quick and efficient manner. It is extremely important to specify the course, because employees often 
disagree or confused of the direction or not sure whether significant change really is needed (Kotter, 
1999).  
Kotter describes that an effective vision at least includes six key characteristics (Kotter, 1999): 
 Imaginable: Provides a picture of how the future will look like 
 Desirable: Appealing to the long-term interests of employees, customers, shareholders and 
others who have an interest 
 Feasible: Contains realistic, achievable goals 
 Focused: Is clear enough to provide guidance when making decisions 
 Flexible: Is sufficiently general to allow individual initiative and alternative responsible.  
 Can be communicated: Is easy to communicate 
Unsuccessful attempts to communicate the visions are often attributed to either limited intellectual 
abilities among employees at lower levels or general human resistance to change and thereby also to 
accept information about change (Kotter, 1999). The development of a changing vision often 
==PAGE:35==
Theory 
 
 
 
 
 
Page 27 of 108 
 
requires that members of the governing coalition uses a few hundred hours of gathering 
information, digesting it, consider alternatives and finally make some decisions, and all others in the 
organization have to understand and accept the final vision in a fraction of the time (Kotter, 1999). 
 
3.4.8 Failing an attempt to change 
Change starts with an idea or an intention, and the outcome of a planned process of change will then 
be compared with the intentions. A change is measured against the intentions, and therefore they are 
crucial to whether the process of change is considered successful. If an attempt to implement more 
substantial changes fails, it is an assessment of the outcome of a process of change is not consistent 
with the intentions (Jacobsen, 2005). 
In too many cases the improvements have been disappointing, and have been followed by wasted 
resources and burned-out, scared or frustrated employees as a result (Kotter, 1999). Failing at an 
attempt to change can leave employees with the impression that things cannot be changed and this 
in a worse position than where you started. (Kotter, 1999) 
It is important to learn from the ongoing constructions, too often each building is perceived as an 
isolated event and not as part of a chain (Bertelsen, et al., 2002). The good effect to take from an 
earlier experiment of change in an organization is that you probably will be in a better position to do 
something similar in the future, because you both have acquired some skills and freed you of some 
of the redundant processes (Kotter, 1999).

4.1.1 Trimit as a tool 
The client on building 202 has Lean in focus through the whole organisation of the project, and the 
tools used are referred to as Trimit. Trimit is developed by Alan Mossman, Sven Bertelsen and 
Kjeld Schrøder, Trimit is a new kind of project management for DTU Campus Service’s execution 
of construction projects and is based on LPS. The first test of Trimit is preformed on building 202.  
The idea is to create a common system and culture where there occurs a common tone, language 
and behaviour that support cooperation and solidarity, the involved actors should build a common 
mindset (Bertelsen, 2015). The Trimit system is described and published in a leaflet, as shown in 
Annex A, and is the foundation for this section.  
The aim for Trimit builds on collaboration among all participants in the project, to strengthen the 
construction of building 202 at the following areas: 
==PAGE:39==
Empirical Study 
 
 
 
 
 
Page 31 of 108 
 
 Increase productivity and thereby the profitability for all 
 Strengthen security and healthy work environment at the construction site 
 Minimize the risk of errors, delays and cost overruns 
The vision of Trimit is reached by ensuring that the conditions are present, when the plan says they 
should be. The method to settle this is by using three kinds of plans to structure the work:   
Phase Plan (da: Procesplan) is agreed between the project managers for each phase or for every 3-4 
months. It determines the best order of tasks. 
Look Ahead Plan (da: Periodeplanen) is agreed every week between contractors and project 
managers. They look 4-5 weeks ahead and ensure that all conditions are met, or they get it before 
they are performed. 
Weekly Work Plan (da: Ugeplan) says what needs to happen in the coming week and is agreed 
between each crew every week. The foremen check up on the weekly plan and coordinate the day's 
work on a short standing meeting every morning, and align if required. On each floor a board 
meeting will be held every morning and comments can be put on the board. 
Through understanding the process as a flow it is possible to increase the productivity by 
collaboration between the different subjects. This means that reliability in everyday life is a 
keyword, and is achieved as followed: 
 We work together and trust each other 
 We make plans in collaboration, and the plans are made by the people who have to perform 
the task 
 We are preparing for forthcoming tasks with systematic planning that ensures all conditions 
are present, before we start to carry out a task 
 We monitor the reliability of our plans - not to criticize but to learn about where the 
problems arise 
 We are open to good ideas to enhance and optimize the flow of execution  
The reliability of the process is followed to learn from mistakes and improve the process while 
constructing.  
The intention is to build one floor or a room at a time. When a process is repeated or when a 
"milestone" is reached, time is used to evaluate and optimize the process of winning on time and 
resources next time.

Takt Time Planning: 
Work in construction flows when all trades take a similar amount of time to complete their work in 
each zone. But Alan Mossman wanted people to work in smaller zones than what was initially 
stated in appendix B; this is due to the fact that work flows more rapidly as the sizes of zones are 
reduced.  
The idea and gain from reducing the batch size by dividing the building into smaller zones was 
demonstrated by an example as followed: 
 
The building has 5 floors and it is assumed there are 5 trades.   
One floor at a time:  
4 weeks for each trade per floor 
 
 
One floor in 20 weeks  
 
 
All floors in 36 weeks 
4 zones per floor: 
1 week for each trade per zone 
 
 
One floor in 8 weeks  
 
 
All floors in 24 weeks 
 
 
As the example shows if one trade is working on a floor at a time, all five floors will be completed 
in 36 weeks. By reducing the batch size by dividing a floor into smaller zones of four, so the trade 
can work on the same floor at the same time, all five floors would be performed in 24 weeks, this 
means that there is a gain of 12 weeks or 3 months. This is illustrated by the figures in Annex D.

4.3.1 Effects of Trimit 
On a normal floor the benefit of the planning could be seen, as the plans were thoroughly prepared 
and the contractors had a good idea of the needed staffing and how much time to spend on each of 
the activities. The quality of the work carried out at building 202 is high and properly carried out,

focus from the start. 
Moreover, Claus adds that there is a culture of a very clean and organized building site, the 
craftsmen work for each other, for a common project and it is the most well-organized site he has 
seen.

The main effect of a Lean and clean construction site is that there are fewer accidents, due to the

information is gathered once a month and the trends are examined so proper actions can be 
introduced to minimize the accidents.

5 Analysis   
In this section the empirical data will be analysed by comparing with the presented theory. In this 
way it is aimed to define how the implementation of LPS on building 202 took place.  
As a Lean tool, it is known that if a process is not working, the reason is often on an earlier level 
than what we are looking at (Bertelsen, 2003), but is this also the case during implementation of 
Lean?   
This will be studied by a review of whether the structure of the change initiative of implementing 
Trimit on building 202 took place under appropriate conditions and are performed as theoretically 
recommended. Followed by the conditions for a person to be willing to change will be  worked 
through, which are role models, developing competency, understanding and acceptance, systems 
and structures, as illustrated in Figure 6.  
The discontinuity with Trimit will be examined and what the consequence of the failed 
implementation can bring. The degree of Trimit which is present on building 202 will be described 
compared to LPS and finally a closer look will be taken at the learning objects which are drawn

5.1 Structure of change 
In this section it will be verified why not all actors are capable of or interested in using the tools of 
Trimit or whether it is basically the manner in which Lean principles and vision is implemented that 
is the key to inconsistencies. First Kotter´s eight steps from section 3.4.1 are worked through 
regarding whether the step has succeeded or not.  
Step 1 of establish a sense of urgency was presented as a result of the tight main schedule for the 
whole life science complex, so to have a change of complete building 202 in time, several 
production teams were needed and LPS was a method to keep the productivity high. 
Step 2 of forming a powerful guiding coalition did not succeed as a consequence of replacement 
and lack of knowledge, understanding and training which is described further in section 5.1.1 and 
5.1.2. 
Step 3 is to develop a vision and strategy, which was done through the series of workshops and the 
developed system Trimit. The Trimit leaflet as seen in Annex A, clearly states the system, how to 
use it and what to obtain with it.     
Step 4 is to communicate the vision. This was done to a great extent in the beginning during the 
numerous workshops, and almost everyone was captured by the idea and could see potential 
==PAGE:78==

Page 70 of 108  
 
Michelle Mollerup 
 
benefits. But as the process went to execution, the communication stopped along with the guiding

Step 5 is about creating conditions for action on a broad basis, this is described further in section

Step 6 deals with generating short-term wins, this was supposed to happen by visualizing the

5.1.1 Role models 
 
One of the most important aspects before starting a Lean implementation is to believe in it. It is 
important to ensure that everyone in the value chain has enthusiasm and every link must believe in 
it and feel ownership. This is often not the case when the change is kicked off, therefore it is 
necessary to ensure that there are role models to follow and guide the affected employees in the 
right direction. This is also what Kotter it his 8 steps of change defines as forming a powerful 
guiding coalition.  
Compared to the type of Leadership from section 3.4.2, the type of consultant present on building 
202 consist of both the “hard” and “soft” type, in form of both external consultants and process 
consultant/manager.  
 
Consultants: 
At building 202 two highly recognized consultants within LC were introduced and they managed to 
get the participants to see the purpose of using LPS and TTP. The long start-up phase meant that the 
actors got to know the consultants well.     
All the actors have expressed great confidence by having both along, so they have been great role 
models during the learning phase, but did not continue to be connected to the project along with the 
execution.  
Process manager: 
To avoid that crises takes over and the risk of falling back into the old routines, it may be a good 
idea or necessary to have a process manager who controls the process and can provide guidance

actor performs the processes and provides feedback for further development.  
Rene Christiansen was put into position for this task; he had no previous experience in Lean.

no concern as he quickly showed faith and understanding of the system at the same time that he put

The responsibility for guiding the process was passed on to Kim S. W. Rasmussen from the

The consultants followed the Lean thinking where the actors have to perform the Lean

5.1.3 Understanding and acceptance  
Understanding: 
Several of the key figures in building 202 have expressed that they do not think that there is a major 
difference in what you normally do rather than how LPS plan, which according to Bertelsen (2015) 
is often the case when people learn about LPS, but the difference is found in their focus on 
operations instead of flow. 
Perhaps the LPS thinking already exist in the planning and execution of construction projects. 
Where there is looked forward to preparing future tasks and ensure that all prerequisites are present, 
but it is done by routine without an actual check list or "recipe". LPS has clearly established 
procedure, this ensures that assumptions are not easily overlooked and the flow becomes reliable. It 
can be compared to baking a cake without a recipe. If it is a cake you have baked many times, you 
may have the right ingredients and amounts, but it can easily happen that you forget an ingredient or 
the order of mixing the ingredients is reversed. 
There was training and planning in TTP during the workshops, but not in LPS and the Trimit 
system has not been introduced. It might have meant that the participants have not distinguished 
LPS and TTP and perceived it as one cannot run without the other, instead of thinking that LPS and 
TTP should complement each other, as described in section 3.3. This may have resulted in that 
when the Takt did not work, the participants thought that LPS was not possible to use neither. 
Ownership: 
It is important that the groups which are subject to change should be involved in the change project 
and feel ownership. The implementation structure of Trimit on building 202 is conducted as 
traditional project culture, based on orders. This is related to that which, in the theory section 3.4.2, 
is called “hard” leadership. This means that the initiative came from above and it is the top 
management who has decided to implement and develop this system, despite of the tendering 
method where all companies used resources on telling how they could contribute to the softer 
==PAGE:81==

was never communicated but just ignored.  
So the companies were not really a part of forming the system but only planning based on the 
system, which may be one of the reasons that people have not felt ownership. 
Furthermore the Trimit system was never introduced on the site which means that the feeling of 
ownership and the idea to have a common system to use and a sense of community around it on the 
site has not been present.     
Acceptance: 
To create an acceptance of a new system it can be necessary to create short-term goals or visualize 
the gains. In LPS the PPC is used for that purpose and it also creates a motivating effect as the 
actors week by week can see if things are succeeding.  
On building 202 the results for the PPC was not collected as the reliability on the planning was far 
from good and the management would not demotivate the actors. But instead of hiding the truth it 
should clearly have been stated what to begin with, despite the demotivating number to begin with, 
it would be changed to motivation when the numbers increased. This would have given proof of the 
functionality of the system and thereby create acceptance.  
It was not the theory which was lacking anything or the willingness from the actors, but the 
knowledge and therefore conflicting actions. The foundation for a Lean-based construction project 
was present but it was simply not executed due to the lack of a guiding coalition, which resulted in 
everything that had been agreed, was forgotten and people fell back into old habits. 
Resistance to change: 
The companies that should perform LPS gets no support and are frustrated that it is not proceeding 
as expected along with the numerous changes and ambiguities during the execution. This created 
mistrust towards whether what is planned and performed can also be remained. It affected the 
motivation and becomes a resistance to the change and a negative mindset that characterized the 
planning process and there occurred a need to see evidence that things are in order before people 
believe in it.

which they occur. 
Barriers in the project are; a construction management who could not / would not perform and their 
behaviour that goes in a completely different direction, a project that was not finished coordinated 
and did not have the right quality, continual changes in the project and when the process was 
planned, the actors had not enough knowledge of the project and they had different amount of

From the beginning it was not possible to anticipate that the implementation of Trimit on this site

has not been possible to reach the goal as attended. Perhaps some of the problems could have been 
foreseen, but other things came as a surprise, says Kjeld Schrøder. 
Acknowledging of obstacles against change:  
The use of Trimit never managed to get started, as everything was depending on the thoroughly

The project of building 202 was too loosely designed; there were too many ambiguities and lack of

was too tightly planned. When too many issues pile up, it is necessary to either increase manpower

more time and economy which was not available or devoted.

value, at the right time and within the given economy. All components must be present in order to 
have a smooth project. But in the absence of both the time and economy, two key components are 
missing and the value is not created. 
Replacement of obstacles against change: 
It is clearly the lack of competences and training within LPS which made it difficult for the 
participants to stay on track on the lean implementation. When the plans were not possible to 
follow, further planning and reorganizing was needed but no one felt they possess the competences 
to lead this new planning.  
The construction management stated that the occurred issues would result in an extension of the 
project, and to not fall more behind they went back to manage the construction in the traditional 
way they are familiar with. A while later it was decided to change the construction management; it

5.3 Trimit on the remaining part of Building 202 
The construction on building 202 is still ongoing. On the current state some of the tools within

It is very positive that it is attempted to adapt the tools that work on the project and that the tools are

To optimize the latter part of the construction they must be foresighted and prepared for the

5.4 Future work with Trimit 
The best way to implement changes on subsequent projects is not possible to determine in advance 
as it will vary from each project, as projects and the involved actors vary.  
As a public client it is not allowed to choose freely between the actors you want to work with, 
therefore you will on each project work with a new group of peoples and companies who do not 
know each other and their values. This is a great barriers to promote the effectiveness of lean for a 
public client, therefore lean is perhaps easier to implement in private firms. 
In this section some suggestions for where DTU CAS needs to develop their concept or do 
something differently next time are given. This is based on the learning objects which are gained 
from the building 202 along with the interviewee’s suggestions or demands if they should 
participate in such an initiative again.  
Tendering: 
The vision for DTU CAS to using Trimit is in the long term to attract the companies that are best at 
working with lean, providing the most well-conducted projects for the actors themselves, so they 
can make more money. When we do this, there will be fewer uncertainties and risks and we get 
cheaper prices, says Kjeld Schrøder.  
There is a great support from DTU CAS and especially from the headmaster at DTU, to work with 
LC. But Alan Mossman would like to see them state outside of the project that they want to use 
==PAGE:87==

Lean basis, so when the project goes out to tender the contractor will be told that production will be 
managed using LPS an equivalent process. 
Claus Møller Rasmussen agrees and says that next time there must be complete agreement that 
everyone wants this and more focus should be put into selling the solution during the tender phase. 
If they manage to sell the idea already in the tender, they would get better prices and get the 
companies along who are interested, which is optimal for all parties. 
Ideally, the designers and DTU CAS should be willing to integrate early contractor involvement so 
that the production process is designed as the building is being designed; this is also called Lean 
design. This will give a thoroughly prepared project which is more buildable and hereby, the 
amount of subsequent project clarifications diminished. But by using Lean design, contractors must 
be appointed on a fairly loose basis and it is difficult to bring the right people in earlier for clients in 
the public sector if they do not change the strategy of procurement.  
Requirements: 
Lean is not contractually controlled, but the Danish building culture is to a large extent, as the 
various contracts are measured on what is in their mutual contracts since it is what they are 
contractually required to provide. Therefore, it may be an idea to shape the specification of 
requirements in a way that the Lean thinking is emphasized and gets to dominate.  
There is a need for a further development of the specification of requirements. It should be 
described what skills you must possess, the obligation you engage to, and that a constant 
development is part of the process. There should be incorporated an obligating mechanism of the 
management within each trade, which deals with how to achieve a common goal, where they are 
required to cooperate to deliver a complete product.  
This allows formulating a harder and sharper Appendix B and must be described by establishing 
clear legal paragraphs and consequences, to support the work.  
Kjeld Schrøder says that an approach may be to focus on what DTU wants to promote, if it is 
wanted to promote safety an example is to require gloves as a security guard at the building site.  
Education: 
To ensure a high level of Lean in the future projects it should be ensured that there in every 
company are people who believes and understand lean. It can be done by requiring that the involved 
companies need people with knowledge of Lean or a selected persons within the companies who 
can receive additional training to be a role model for the given company. 
The use of which Lean tools can be used will vary from project to project, so it is necessary to make 
a strategy for the elements which will be used in the individual projects. Furthermore, the different 
companies may have different variants of how they use the Lean tools. Therefore it is beneficial if 
DTU provides training, to get the same understanding among all actors.

There has to be focus on both training and planning prior to the execution phase, but it is not 
necessary to have all knowledge from the start, but it should be required that all must participate in 
the training and planning sessions.  
To secure constant learning it should be stated to what extent further education is seen as part of the 
process. And a process manager should be hired to the organization; it should be a person with only 
the task of facilitation of the Lean project, and should not have any other conflicting positions or 
task on the project.   
Pilot projects: 
Many activities in a process of change have the character of being trials or experiments. However, it 
is rare that it is formulated as an experiment (Hildebrandt & Brandi, 2005). The project of building 
202 run as a Lean managed project is likewise identified as a pilot project, and that DTU is using 
this project to learning more about what to do and what not to do, in the future (Møller & Bottheim, 
2015). But it is not sensed that the there is put an effort into informing the actors of that or to give 
an extra effort to get LPS up and running again.  
How does this pilot project differ from any other project? According to the Danish dictionary a pilot 
project means the following: experimental plan; level of education; plan to develop the industry, 
etc., tentatively implemented in a small area before it is implemented in full extent.  
To learn how to use LC it is a good idea to start with a pilot project which is a smaller and simpler 
project with many repetitions so there is opportunity to test production principles and visible results 
can be seen.   
Several of the interviewees on building 202 feel such a pilot project is lacking where there is time to 
learn while building.  
The ideal would be to have a project which could be seen as an actual experiment where everyone is 
fully aware of what they are getting into. It would be an advantage if it was a government-
sponsored project and where there is no deadline or sanction-bearing termination, so there is time to 
stop when things are to be solved, so the flow is kept, also known as stop the line.

5.5 Perspectivation  
Generally, there is mentioned a number of things that are not completed as expected and have been 
inappropriate in this project. This is not saying that this project is much worse or with more errors 
than all other construction projects. 
One thing is certain, there is room for improvement in the way construction projects are being 
performed and the change of the building culture may as well happen now; there will not be a better 
or worse time to do this.  
There has in recent years been a huge development in the technology used in the building industry, 
where it has become much easier to communicate and monitor the project across the various trades, 
since everything is available electronically. But the optimization on how to carry out the projects, in 
relation to cooperation across the different trades has not followed the same development.  
Whether Lean is the right or only concept to use to improve the construction process, I am not 
aware of, as I do not have a deeper knowledge of other options, and most importantly I have no 
practical knowledge of using Lean tools.  
But I believe that Lean tools can be an integrated part of the Danish building culture, but it requires 
a focus on the culture, as it is the way people think about performing a construction project that 
needs to be changed. 
Development of Lean Construction: 
Arlbjøm, et al. (2008) states that it is a natural development that LC will be used in a greater extent 
in the future, because it is unlikely that companies can remain competitive without Lean. The 
requirement that you have to get it right every time will not disappear. This includes constant 
analysis and improvement, use of correct data, common sense and an acceptance of the need for a 
radical change. In addition, we will always be aware of waste, so there will always be a need for 
Lean. 
This development has Sven Bertelsen recognized, as there begins to emerge more companies who 
work with LC in Denmark, so he is optimistic and has a positive feeling that Lean will keep 
growing. He also believes that within a few years a major breakthrough will be seen, as it was seen 
in the US with a sudden development. However, more attention should be on involving learning 
from projects abroad that are more advanced than in Denmark.  
In addition, it is also about training enough people to have enough skills to implement Lean on a 
project. Rene Christiansen suggests to create an study line at DTU within Lean, at the level which 
can be seen on Stanford University, so more people are trained to have the right background as they 
graduate. Inspiration of skills and knowledge required to lead a lean transformation and curriculum 
suggestion for a development program, is given by Mossman (2015). 
Furthermore, several of the interviewees believe that there should be more focus of having pilot 
projects where it is possible to develop skills under appropriate conditions for everyone involved.

6 Conclusion  
In this thesis it is by theoretical and empirical study attempted to identify the problem statement. In 
the conclusion, the findings which will be presented is relating to whether the method of 
implementation of Trimit was carried out under appropriate conditions, and which experiences can 
be drawn from the implementation of a Lean tool on building 202 to future projects. 
 
6.1 Conditions for implementation of Trimit 
Implemented tools and use of them:  
The implemented system Trimit is based on and consists of the same values and planning method as 
LPS. Furthermore, Trimit is supplemented with the production tool TTP.  
During the start-up phase on the workshops, the time was assigned to get familiar with the project 
and planning, both the phase plan and TTP was performed according to the theory. It can be 
concluded that the tools which are developed are used as the theory describes during the workshops. 
Education of Trimit: 
To be able to complete a change process it is important to have a guiding coalition to maintain the 
change and to create still more support and development. The two consultancies were a good choice 
to include, as almost the whole organization had no experience and they managed to get the actors 
to believe in the concept on a theoretical basis.  
The start-up phase was too theoretical for some, due to the language barrier and the large span of 
different participants and ended up being wasted due to the large change in staffing.  
It is concluded that the greatest barrier towards Trimit was the lack of knowledge and experience 
within LPS among those who should lead it and the absence of introduction of Trimit on the site.      
Effects of Trimit: 
The effect of a new system is important to bring to light to maintain the motivation among 
participants and to visualize the efforts towards whether the implementation is worth it.   
The focus on a Lean site has given a very clean and organized site with good communication 
between the foremen; this has resulted in a low safety rate compared to the industry. The best was 5 
at building 202 against 33 for the industry; the number increased over time but has at all times been 
significantly lower than average.         
But the effect of Trimit and the performed planning was never measured and therefore it cannot be 
concluded whether the planning at the meetings gave any affect on the reliability or flow on the site. 
Instead the lack of visual effects has turned to a doubt and mistrust towards the management and the 
system.

Discontinuity with Trimit:  
The barrier against what was planned was the numerous changes and ambiguities in the project 
material as a result of the staggered design and procurement, along with the discovery of the non 
consistency throughout the building.  
The use of Trimit never managed to be implemented in the daily operations on the site, as the 
competence of those to implement and lead Trimit was not sufficient and their skills was never 
developed.   
It can also be concluded that when the construction management was replaced this was not an 
action to support the implementation of Trimit.  
Current and future situation on building 202: 
Some tools within LPS are used and some tools are on their way back on the site, but it is concluded 
that there is not a broad support or plan for what and how this implementation should take place.  
 
As long as the present plans are not aligned and the planning among the contractors is both changed 
constantly and only looks three weeks ahead, a reliable planning is not possible and the remaining 
part of constructing building 202 will not become more Lean.  
 
6.2 Learning objectives to future work with Trimit  
Next time DTU CAS is running a project with Trimit, they should be further in their own 
recognition of what they want. It should clearly be stated from the start which tools are supposed to 
be used to get the right companies and targeted offers. Then the implementation strategy should be 
fitted to the target group, their skills and knowledge of Lean. 
It can be concluded that staggered design and procurement of the different trades are not optimal 
when using LPS, and if the planning of the production process should be optimized, contractors 
must be included already in the design process.  
The process manager needs to be handled by a person who can assist a continuous implementation 
during issues or crises and should not have conflicting positions in the organization. Furthermore, it 
is important to ensure ongoing training and education when implementing LPS among people with 
limited experience within this.   
The contractual framework has a significant impact on the culture in the Danish construction 
industry, so it can be seen necessary to shape the requirements so they support the initiative.   
Despite the failure to manage building 202 with Trimit, the involved actors have developed a lot of 
competences during the workshops and have experienced which elements contain risk and 
obstacles. For DTU to achieve the goal of becoming a Lean based client, it is important to further 
develop and pass on all these experiences to future projects.

D. Example of Takt Time Planning 
 
The illustrations show the effect of reducing the batch size by dividing the building into smaller 
zones, which was presented at the workshops.   
 
There are 5 trades and the building consists of 5 floors. 
 
 
One trade can work at 1 floor at a time and 1 floor takes 4 weeks to complete for each trade, so the 
construction of all 5 floors takes 36 weeks.

work on the same floor at the same time. 1 floor still takes 4 weeks to complete but all 5 floors are 
constructed in 24 weeks. 
 
 
 
 
The flow which is created means that the time of construction is reduced, in this example the 
construction of the five floors is reduced by 12 month.
