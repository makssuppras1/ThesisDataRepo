Abstract
This Master’s thesis proposal is based on a research project called AEGIR, which is motivated by
the urgent need to address the impact of the maritime industry on global CO2 emissions. The
overall goal of this thesis is to develop a decision support framework to assess alternative marine
fuel to assist decision makers in the challenge of significantly reducing greenhouse emissions (GHG)
from international maritime transport in accordance with the ambitious targets established by the
International Maritime Organization (IMO). Fundamentally, the initiative addresses the IMO’s
mandate that greenhouse gas emissions from international shipping must be reduced by 50% by
2050 and completely phased out by 2100. At the same time, the Organization for Economic Co-
operation and Development (OECD) projects that global marine trade will expand thrice by 2050,
which emphasizes how urgent it is for low- and zero-emission technologies to be widely used in
maritime transport.
The innovative technology examined in the AEGIR project combines polymer exchange membrane
fuel cells (PEMFC), proton-conducting electrochemical membrane reactors (PCEMR), and solid
oxide fuel cells (SOFC) to effectively convert ammonia into electrical energy. This endeavor is a
result of the realization that ammonia has the potential to be a zero-emission fuel substitute when
produced using renewable energy, making it a particularly attractive option for large ocean-going
ships.
Compared to e.g.
hydrogen, ammonia has a higher energy content, and liquefaction is
simpler.
This Master’s thesis concentrates on the wider impact assessment of operational scenarios, as stated
in Task 5.3 in the AEGIR project. The Master Thesis’s goal is to create a decision support tool
using AHP for the calculation of criteria weights, and TOPSIS, SAW and VIKOR techniques for
alternatives ranking. It would allow to evaluate various operational scenarios reflecting a ship’s
actual performance and fuel consumption. The decision support tool using multi-criteria decision
analysis and economic assessment seeks to quantify the suggested initiative’s environmental, tech-
nical, and economic aspects, focusing on CO2 reductions, and compare this to other alternative
fuels identified as attractive for maritime propulsion. The evaluation encompasses a wide range of
KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) that are important for identifying the best-case scenario from a
commercial and policy standpoint. These KPIs will be identified through an initial literature review
and followingly assessed using the decision support tool.
A distinctive aspect of this initiative is its dedication to applying multi-criteria decision analysis
to integrate stakeholder perspectives.
Adopting a comprehensive approach guarantees that the
evaluation aligns with both technical feasibility and the wider concerns of stakeholders, thereby
increasing its strategic and pragmatic importance.
In conclusion, this Master’s thesis critically examines the AEGIR project’s innovative approach to
maritime propulsion. It compares it to other alternative fuels, emphasizing the development and ap-
plication of a decision- support tool to assess operational scenarios. The project makes a significant
contribution to the goal of developing sustainable and effective solutions for the maritime industry
by examining perspectives from stakeholders for the environment, the economy and technical and
social aspects.
KEYWORDS: PEMFC, SOFC, PCEMR, GHG, TOPSIS, VIKOR, SAW, stakeholders, ammonia,
hydrogen, multi-criteria decision analysis.

Introduction
1.1
Challenges in maritime transport
Previous technological advancements in shipping for the past centuries have both increased speed
and cut cost, making shipping more economically efficient. However, the current technology shift
distinguishes from previous shifts by being not only driven by economics and technology. Rather
the shift of today is driven by social, policy and regulatory factors which seek to solve the urge to
reduce emissions. (LR, 2023)
According to the International Maritime Organization (IMO), ships are responsible for 90% of in-
ternational transport, their Carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions accounting for approximately 2% of
the global total of such emissions. Therefore, the shipping sector contributes significantly to envi-
ronmental pollution. If the industry’s current accelerating trend of operation continues, Greenhouse
gases (GHG) emissions from ships are expected to increase by 130 % by 2050 compared to 2008 lev-
els while the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development OEC The Organisation for
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) forecasts that the international maritime trade
will triple by 2050. Over the past 10 years, there has been an increasing focus and need to enhance
the environmental and energy efficiency impact of maritime industry, although there appears to be
a delay in the shipping industry’s decarbonization. The International Maritime Organization IMO
has established a goal of lowering GHG emissions and improving the shipping industry’s carbon
footprint by 50-60% by the year 2050 and the complete phase-out of CO2 emissions by 2100 (IMO,
2020).
As a result, efforts have been made to find alternatives to reduce GHG emissions from shipping.
The IMO has established a framework for the sector’s decarbonization with short, medium and
long-term targets, being the wide-scale implementation of low- and zero-emission solutions, the
only way to reach these targets (IMO, 2020).
It is well known that reducing GHG emissions in the maritime sector will require efforts in finding
solutions to enable ships to run on greener fuels.
Alternative low-carbon fuels are receiving a
lot of attention and it is anticipated that they will play a big part in the shipping industry’s
decarbonization as over 95% of ships today are powered by ICE’s that use petroleum products such
as Heavy Fuel Oil (HFO) as their fuel. (MMMCZCS et al., 2023)
1.2
The AEGIR Project
This Master’s thesis is based on a research project called AEGIR, which proposes a new system
that could potentially help lowering the maritime sector’s GHG emissions by increasing energy
conversion efficiency. The AEGIR project was done in the collaboration of the Technical University
of Denmark, Ballard Power Systems Europe, SINTEF, CoorsTek Membrane Sciences, VARD and
VTT.
The innovative technology examined in the AEGIR project combines polymer exchange membrane
fuel cells Polymer Exchange Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC), proton-conducting electrochemical
membrane reactors Proton-exchange membrane fuel cell (PCEMR), and solid oxide fuel cells Solid

Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) to effectively convert ammonia into electrical energy. Figure 1 is a simplified
demonstration of how the SOFC, PCEMR and PEMFC work together in the AEGIR system. The
goal of the AEGIR project was to combine the three technologies to develop a highly efficient
maritime propulsion system which emits water from the electricity process, so the CO2 emissions
are significantly reduced and the NOx emissions are entirely avoided.
This Master’s thesis concentrates on a wider impact assessment of operational scenarios in the

1.3
Evaluating alternative propulsion systems and fuels
Evaluating the best potential alternative when it comes to maritime systems and fuels is a complex
task. There are many different aspects and perspectives that need to be taken into consideration.
For this reason, considering only the economic aspect is not enough; it is also necessary to include
technical, social and environmental aspects.
MCDA is a methodology used to evaluate various alternatives that have multiple socio-economic
perspectives and priorities, which makes it suitable for complex decision-making scenarios. The
alternatives are looked at from different criteria, and the stakeholder perspectives are used to eval-
uate weights. The weights are assigned to different criteria, and therefore recognising that not all
criteria have the same relevance. This requires a high level of expertise as the weights are subjective
and have a strong influence in the outcome of the assessment. In some cases decision-makers are
also forced to provide subjective expert opinion for the criteria scores, however when possible the
criteria is always preferably quantified objectively. (Barfod and Leleur, 2023)

1.4
The goal of the Master Thesis - Problem statement
The goal of the master thesis is to create and implement a decision support tool to evaluate how
the propulsion system designed in the AEGIR project scores compared to other possible alternative
fuels in ICE’s. The decision support tool is based on multi-criteria decision analysis and economic
assessment used to quantify environmental, economic, technical, and social criteria, leading to the
ranking of the different alternatives. The MCDA integrates different stakeholder perspectives to
capture both business and policy viewpoints. The different alternative fuels, criteria, stakeholders
and theoretical methods used will be identified through an initial literature review.
This can be summarized into the following problem statements:
1. How does the ammonia-based propulsion system developed in the AEGIR project compared to
current-, and alternative fuels in Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) propulsion system, which is
currently being used by over 95% of ships?
2. Which alternative fuels are feasible for future use in the maritime sector to ultimately decrease
greenhouse gas emissions?
To be able to answer this, first there is need to answer the following questions: (1) Which alternative
fuels are relevant for the assessment? (2) Which criteria are relevant for the assessment? (3) Which
stakeholders are relevant for this assessment? (4) Which methodologies are relevant for the MCDA
framework?
1.5
The assessment’s scope
The proposed scope is the result of a comprehensive literature search and a subjective evaluation
of the time and complexity limits for this master thesis.
The MCDA focuses on seven different alternatives. Five of the alternatives are different fuels for
ICE’s: The first being HFO which is used as a benchmark, followed by green LNG, green hydrogen,
green ammonia and green methanol. The ammonia-based AEGIR system makes up the last two
alternatives: One alternative is for the most efficient version of the system and one is for the least
efficient version of the system, based on the measurements from the AEGIR project data. The
criteria included in the MCDA and economic assessment are 15 in total and can be categorised into
Economic-, Environmental-, Technical- and Social criteria.

2.1
MCDA
Multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) refers to a set of techniques that assist individuals in mak-
ing decisions by considering their own preferences in cases where more than one conflicting criterion
exists.
This facilitates decision makers reaching a rational decision under uncertainty (Barfod,
2012).
This tool has been extensively used in many industries, but has proven extremely use-
ful in the maritime industry for evaluating different fuels based on specific criteria (Strantzali
et al., 2023a)(Hansson et al., 2020a).
Various frameworks such as Analytical Hierarchy Pro-
cess Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), Visekriterijumska optimizacija i KOmpromisno Resenje
VIKOR, Technique for Order Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution TOPSIS, Simple Additive
Weighting SAW, Elimination and Choice Translating Reality Elimination and Choice Translating
Reality (ELECTRE), Preference Ranking Organization Method for Enrichment and Evaluations
Preference Ranking Organization Method for Enrichment and Evaluations (PROMETHEE), Multi-
objective optimization on the basis of ratio analysis Multi-objective optimization on the basis of
ratio analysis (MULTIMOORA) , Complex Proportional Assessment Complex Proportional Assess-
ment (COPRAS) are currently widely used (Qu et al., 2018)(Rane et al., 2023a)(Zlaugotne et al.,
2020). Decision makers select these methods based on their properties and requirements to identify,
evaluate, and compare alternatives, ultimately providing a ranking based on preference(Rane et al.,
2023a)(Zlaugotne et al., 2020).
Analytical Hierarchy Process AHP is one of the most popular and widely employed multi criteria
methods developed by Tomas L. Saaty in the 1970s (Khazaii, 2016). AHP is primarily used to
determine the weights of decision criteria by comparing them with each other using a scale from 1
to 9, resulting in a binary matrix (Ramanathan, 2004). However, due to the context of ambiguity and
uncertainty present in some situations, fuzzy set theory, introduced by Zadeh, is employed to address
the fuzzy environment and enhance its performance by using a fuzzy linguistic scale (Liu et al.,
2020b)(Kim and Seo, 2019). For this reason, it is increasingly common for researchers to introduce
fuzzy theory with AHP and various multi-criteria analysis frameworks.
Fuzzy AHP, similar to
its non-fuzzy counterpart, relies on pairwise comparisons but demonstrates better performance in
situations of uncertainty. For example, some studies use fuzzy AHP to compare several fuels for
ships (Kim and Seo, 2019), while others combine fuzzy AHP to calculate criterion weights and
employ TOPSIS for alternatives ranking (Zlaugotne et al., 2020).
Fuzzy AHP offers numerous advantages over AHP, such as its capability to handle imprecise and
subjective criteria (Božić, 2023). The logic-based approach of fuzzy AHP enables decision makers
to evaluate multiple and sometimes conflicting criteria, and to assess the relative importance of
each criterion more realistically (Božić, 2023). Moreover, it allows decision makers to express their
judgments using fuzzy numbers or linguistic terms, reflecting their subjective beliefs or uncertainties
Page 16
==PAGE:18==
about comparisons. However, the Fuzzy AHP technique is more complex than the classical AHP
method, demanding greater expertise for effective utilization. Additionally, it involves the resolution
of a system of fuzzy linear equations to determine priority weights. Lastly, interpreting the results
of fuzzy AHP method can be more challenging compared to the classical AHP method, given that
the priority weights derived from the Fuzzy AHP method are often fuzzy and uncertain (Božić,
2023).
On one hand, TOPSIS is a widely used MCDM algorithm developed by Yong and Hwang (Alkhawlani,
2015). This algorithm identifies the perceived positive and negative ideal solutions based on the
range of attribute values provided for the alternatives (Alkhawlani, 2015)(Kaliszewski and Pod-
kopaev, 2016). The optimal solution is the one that has the shortest distance to the Positive ideal
solution (PIS) and the longest distance from the Negative ideal solution (NIS), with distances be-
ing measured in Euclidean terms. This method offer clear advantages such as its simplicity and
straightforward computational process (Karahalios, 2017).
Nevertheless, despite being a widely
used method (Zlaugotne et al., 2020), it falls short in deriving weights for decision criteria, allevi-
ating the requirement for pairwise comparisons, and maintain the consistency check of judgments
(Shih et al., 2007). Hence, it is common to see the application of a combined framework (TOPSIS
and AHP). In this scenario, AHP computes the weights of the chosen criteria in the study, while
TOPSIS ranks the different alternatives (Fu et al., 2023). Numerous articles combine both methods
(AHP-TOPSIS), such as (Karahalios, 2017) (Menon and Ravi, 2022)(Berdie et al., 2017) and it has
been proven to be a valuable decision support tool due to its adaptability, yielding a more accu-
rate result(Bucak et al., 2021)(Vakili et al., 2021). However, in cases where the decision maker’s
decisions are ambiguous and imprecise, it is desirable to change the approach and opt for a fuzzy
AHP-TOPSIS framework, widely utilized, for example, (Fu et al., 2023).
On the other hand, VIKOR is a technique that selects the best alternative by weighing the distance
from the ideal solution and overall performance, combining the ideas of outranking relations and
compromise programming (Rane et al., 2023b). This approach, introduced by Serafim Opricovic
in 1998, was specifically designed to tackle MCDM problems characterized by conflicting and non
commensurable criteria. It proves to be highly useful when decision makers seek to balance overall
performance against discrepancies among various alternatives (Rane et al., 2023b) (Opricovic and
Tzeng, 2007). There are numerous studies where this method has been employed, resulting in solid
results (Papathanasiou, 2021) (Wibawa et al., 2019) (Zhang et al., 2021), one of its key strengths lies
in its simple ranking procedure with a small number of steps. Unlike other techniques (TOPSIS and
SAW), a consistency check could be required. It focuses on maximizing group benefits to achieve
an acceptable compromise solution (Taherdoost and Madanchian, 2023).
However, it does come with some limitations, such as the challenge of determining the compromise
ranking order, particularly between the expected solution and the pessimistic one. As a result, small
changes in the criteria weights could potentially impact both the ranking and the outcomes of the
solution (Taherdoost and Madanchian, 2023). In order to improve the reliability and validity of the
VIKOR technique, several recommendations have been proposed. One such suggestion is to utilize
the parameter value v = 0.5, which is widely used and helps mitigate the aforementioned issue
(the explanation of the importance of this parameter can be found in the Methodology section).
Moreover, incorporating a combined AHP-VIKOR framework can effectively address imprecise in-
formation (Taherdoost and Madanchian, 2023) (Zhang et al., 2021) (Bakioglu and Atahan, 2021)
(Malakar and Rai, 2023).
Similar to the AHP-TOPSIS framework, the weights are determined
through the AHP method, while the alternatives are ranked using VIKOR.
Page 17
==PAGE:19==
Lastly, Simple Additive Weighting SAW, is one the oldest and most widely known MCDM methods
according to (Hwang and Yoon, 1981). This approach involves assigning weights to criteria based on
their importance, adding up the weighted scores for each option to determine the overall performance
and rank the alternatives, as highlighted by (Rane et al., 2023b). One of the key advantages of
SAW is its ability to balance criteria and maintain minimal complexity, thereby offering decision
makers an intuitive experience unlike other ranking methods. Additionally, SAW requires minimal
input from decision makers and assumes linear preferences, which may not always align with the
actual preferences of decision makers, resulting in a less refined outcome according to (Blagojević,
2019). There are numerous articles where the SAW method is combined with the AHP technique to
rank the alternatives and solid results have been demonstrated (Bachchhav et al., 2023) (Abrams
et al., 2018)(Ciardiello, 2023) (Shekhovtsov and Sałabun, 2020a).
Although TOPSIS, VIKOR and SAW share the common goal of ranking alternatives from worst to
best, the results of these techniques may vary, as highlighted by (Bachchhav et al., 2023) (Abrams
et al., 2018) (Ciardiello, 2023). Nevertheless, there is a possibility that they all yield similar results
(Opricovic and Tzeng, 2004).
On one hand, when considering similarities and differences between TOPSIS and SAW, both meth-
ods rely on the value function approach. However, while implementing the TOPSIS approach is
not particularly challenging, it does require more computational effort than SAW. Furthermore,
studies have demonstrated that the TOPSIS method suffers from the rank reversal phenomenon
(the alternatives’ order of preference changes when an alternative is added to or removed from the
decision problem) (García-Cascales and Lamata, 2012), while this issue does not impact the SAW
technique (Ciardiello, 2023). The author of this last mentioned study has carried out a thorough
analysis of the disparities between the SAW and TOPSIS methodologies. It is evident that the
outcomes obtained through the utilization of the Euclidean distance (the one has been used) in
the TOPSIS technique align closely with those of SAW. It is important to note that the distance
referred to as "Manhattan" is the one that best matches with SAW. Additionally, as it can be seen
in (Ciardiello, 2023), the rank agreement between TOPSIS and SAW decreases as the number of
criteria and alternatives increase.
On the other hand, both TOPSIS and VIKOR methods depend on an aggregating function that rep-
resents closeness to the optimal solution (Opricovic and Tzeng, 2004). Nevertheless, there are several
differences between these two techniques. To begin with, when it comes to eliminating decision-
making units and be able to compare all criteria together in the same matrix, TOPSIS approach
employs vector normalization, whereas the VIKOR method uses linear normalization (Taşabat and
Özkan, 2020) (Opricovic and Tzeng, 2004). The main difference appears in the aggregation ap-
proaches. The VIKOR method presents a consolidating function that signifies the deviation from
the ideal solution. This ranking index combines all the criteria, their relative significance, and a
equilibrium between overall and individual contentment. On the other hand, the TOPSIS method
introduces a ranking index that incorporates the distances from both the ideal and negative-ideal
points without taking into account their relative importance. Finally, the alternative with the high-
est ranking according to VIKOR is the one that is most similar to the ideal solution, while the
top-ranked alternative in TOPSIS is the best in terms of ranking index, although it may not be the
closest to ideal solution. Therefore, it is not uncommon that the ranking techniques provide differ-
ent rankings (Opricovic and Tzeng, 2004). This study (Shekhovtsov and Sałabun, 2020b) explains
the potential variations in rankings between TOPSIS and VIKOR, highlighting that the difference
between the two methods intensifies with an increase in the number of alternatives and criteria
Page 18
==PAGE:20==
considered.
Numerous studies have been proposed in the literature review to address MCDA problems. These
studies have focused various aspects, including the regulatory framework, the development of de-
cision support systems, and the application of different decision-making techniques such as AHP,
TOPSIS, SAW and VIKOR, among others. For instance, (Kim and Seo, 2019), a fuzzy AHP is em-
ployed to compare the performance of different marine fuels. Another study by (Rane et al., 2023a)
has introduced a combination of fuzzy AHP-TOPSIS for a decision-making problem in maritime

a sustainable supplier selection problem. Lastly, an alternative marine fuel selection problem has

It has been noted that numerous studies apply one of these three techniques along with AHP,
but always separately.
Therefore, a combined framework AHP-TOPSIS-VIKOR-SAW has been
proposed as a distinctive characteristic of this work. The utilization of these ranking methods is
also considered a form of sensitivity analysis due to the likelihood of producing varying rankings,

els in both ICE and Fuel cell (FC), such as hydrogen, ammonia and methanol. A total of nine
environmental, social, economic and technical criteria are evaluated, each given an equal weight.
The multi-dimensional decision-making framework proposed by the authors is based on pairwise
comparisons of the alternatives, which are scored on a qualitative scale from 1 to 4.
(Foretich et al., 2021) aims to guide decision makers and stakeholder for the transition to greener
marine fuel. The goal of this scoping study was to pinpoint and explain uncertainties and possibilities
for both short and long term transitions. The results should provide decision support on strategy
for alternative marine fuels and provide priorities and technical directions for relevant stakeholders.
The article is relevant as it assesses 12 different fuels, including all the alternative fuels analysed
in this master thesis. 20 different criteria where used in the assessment, which are categorized into
environmental, infrastructural, economic and technical. However some of the criteria could have
been classified as social as done in other research articles. To derive to the final performance score
to rank the alternative fuels, the metrics for each alternative fuel were assigned a qualitative score
of unfavorable, neutral, or favorable. The scores for each criterion where both quantitative when
possible, and if not possible, stakeholder perspectives were used to get a qualitative score.
(Yang et al., 2023) attempts to tackle the challenges of data uncertainty and multiple criteria in
decision support and assessment of alternative fuels. This article is relevant as it is a MCDA frame-

work with 13 different criteria under the categories environmental, social, economic and technical
relevant to the United Kingdom. The alternatives examined are e-fuel, solar-fuel, biofuel, e-biofuel,
which differentiates from the fuels examined in this master thesis. The MCDA framework which is
proposed in this article is based on a novel stakeholder participation to provide expert opinion. The
methodology is based on the AHP and the q-ROLPBM operator, which stands for q-rung orthopair
linguistic partition Bonferroni mean.
(Ren and Liang, 2017) assesses three out of the four alternative fuels used in this master thesis:
methanol, LNG and hydrogen. The goal of the article is to assist decision makers in the selection

(Maceiras et al., 2023) develops a framework with data from the Spanish Navy to guide decision

Alternative fuels
In this section discusses the thorough literature review which was performed on relevant research

2.3.1
HFO
Several research articles such as (Hansson et al., 2019) use HFO as a benchmark in comparison to
alternative fuels using a MCDA approach.
2.3.2
Ammonia
Ammonia, which has a chemical composition of Ammonia (NH3), is a common industrial chemi-
cal. It is essential for the world’s food security as it is mainly used for fertiliser production.(LR,
2023) Ammonia is the alternative fuel highlighted in the AEGIR project and it is considered to
be a possible alternative fuel having potential of being carbon-free and having zero emission if the
production uses renewable energy. The high production volume, the energy density and the ease
of liquefaction when comparing it to hydrogen for example makes it an interesting fuel alternative.
(Xing et al., 2021) (Prussi et al., 2021) (Hansson et al., 2020b) (DTU, 2023)
Today, ammonia is still commonly produced from fossil energy with no carbon capture and storage
and therefore it can emit a considerable amount of GHG. (DNV-GL, 2019) However there exists
two main methods to create cleaner sources of ammonia, depending on the production of hydrogen.
Sectors that currently use ammonia are being pressured to switch to ammonia sources that come
from renewable hydrogen by various decarbonisation efforts. The first of the two main hydrogen
production methods mentioned involves using carbon capture and storage for fossil energy. The
second involves extraction of hydrogen from water using electrolysis. In order for the ammonia to
be classified as green the electricity has to be from a renewable source in both the electrolysis and
in the process of combining hydrogen with nitrogen, which uses electricity to separate nitrogen from
the air. (LR, 2023)
With ammonia there are several safety issues. Ammonia is both flammable and explosive, meaning
that leaks can turn into flash evaporation. Ammonia is also highly toxic to humans and animals.
If ammonia is inhaled or ingested it can come with severe health issues and can even be lethal in
some cases. (LR, 2023) In spite of these safety concerns, ammonia has a long history of safe storage
and transport.(ABS, 2019)
In ICE’s ammonia has a lot of downsides such as the slim flammability limits, high auto-ignition
temperature, low flame speed and toxicity. However, theoretically ammonia as an alternative marine
fuel is technically feasibly. For ammonia to be technically and commercially feasible in a fuel cell
system there is a need for some advancements. Today the technical readiness level is relatively
low compared to conventional heavy fuel oils and the available infrastructure such as for bunkering
is limited. (DNV-GL, 2019) These limitations and challenges are still being dealt with, however
some problems that were supposed to be unworkable are being achieved, so that in 2025 the first
ammonia-powered ships begin to operate. (LR, 2023) Other technical factors worth considering is
that the energy density is much lower compared to the HFO used today. This means that around
Page 24
==PAGE:26==
2.4 times higher volume of ammonia is needed compared to conventional fuel oils, for the same
energy output. (ABS, 2019)
As ammonia used as a alternative marine fuel becomes more viable, fuel producers plan on scaling
the production of ammonia. According to forecasts the ammonia demand for marine fuel use in 2050
could be around 197 million tons per year. To put that into context, the current total production
of ammonia globally is around 180 million tonnes per year. (LR, 2023)
2.3.3
LNG
Although LNG is mainly composed of methane it is made from the combination of several gases.
Ethane, propane, butane and nitrogen are among the other gases which LNG commonly consists
of. Figure 3 demonstrates a typical LNG composition. (ABS, 2019)
Figure 3: Typical LNG composition. Source: (ABS, 2019)
LNG is evaluated as it has lower emissions when compared to the traditional HFO. (Osorio-Tejada
et al., 2017) When comparing typical LNG with HFO, Sulphur oxides (SOX) missions are reduced
by 95-98%, Nitrogen oxides (NOX) emission by 20-80% and CO2 by 14-25%, depending on the
engine type. However the technology to produce green LNG exists and production on a large-scale
is expected to begin in the coming years. To produce green LNG the electrolysis is required to be
done with renewable sources. (DNV, 2023a)
When considering safety, LNG is non toxic and it’s flammability range is considered safe as it gets
a score of 5 out of 5 from ABS, the same as HFO. (DNV-GL, 2019) (ABS, 2019)
Several research articles rank LNG highly when it comes to economic criteria. (Osorio-Tejada et al.,
2017) (Ren and Liang, 2017) (Hansson et al., 2019) However, the same as the other alternative fuels,
LNG has a lower energy density compared to conventional heavy fuel oils. To get the same energy
content with LNG as typical fuel oils around 1,8 of the volume is needed.(ABS, 2019)
LNG is already being used in ships today. (Ren and Liang, 2017) (Prussi et al., 2021) (Maceiras
et al., 2023) LNG is the alternative fuel that is furthest along when it comes to TRL and available
infrastructure such as bunkers, with most of the main marine bunkering centers already having
bunkers for LNG. LNG supply chains and infrastructure continue to develop as the adoption of

2.3.4
Methanol
Several research articles included methanol as an alternative marine fuel. (Xing et al., 2021) dis-
cusses the possibility of methanol being the most promising out of the carbon-neutral bio-fuels when
it comes to global shipping. Methanol has much potential in reducing CO2, NOX and SOX, the
Capital expenditures (CAPEX) and Operating expenditures (OPEX) are lower than many other
alternative fuels and the technology maturity is considerable. (Ren and Liang, 2017)
Methanol, also known as methyl alcohol, is a chemical compound with the chemical composition
CH3OH. (ABS, 2019) When compared to HFO, methanol has lower exhaust emissions with no
SOX and sulphate matter and also lower NOX missions. (ABS, 2019) (LR, 2024) Methanol is used
for various purposes. The main use cases are in industrial chemical and petroleum applications,
and to produce chemical derivatives that are used in production of products. Methanol is currently
most often produced from coal and natural gas, but it can be made from renewable sources such as
black liquor, agricultural waste or forest thinning and straight from CO2 that has been captured
(ABS, 2019) (DNV-GL, 2019) (LR, 2024). However today the total production of methanol is over
110 million tons per year, and as of 2021 only 0.2 million tons of the total production was green
methanol. (LR, 2024)
The main safety concerns that come with methanol is it’s toxicity and the risks of fire and explosions.
The toxicity can cause severe health issues to humans. Exposure to methanol can even be fatal.
The fire safety has to follow regulations as methanol is smokeless and burns with a clear blue flame
so leaks can be difficult to spot, especially in daylight. (ABS, 2019) (LR, 2024)

Methanol’s atmospherics pressure makes it less expensive to store when compared to other alterna-
tive fuels LNG, hydrogen and ammonia. (DNV-GL, 2019) However because of it’s energy density,
methanol needs around 2.4 times more volume for the same energy content compared to fuel oils
used today (ABS, 2019) The current technology readiness levels of methanol when it comes to
producing, delivering and combusting green methanol are relatively high. The readiness levels of
investment are on the other hand not as high with in the maritime sector. (LR, 2024) However as
seen on Figure 4 methanol powered ships account for around 8% of the number of ships on order
as of July 2023. (Xing et al., 2021)
2.3.5
Hydrogen
Hydrogen has been ranked as the most sustainable alternative fuel in several research article that
compare alternative marine fuels using MCDA methods, especially in environmental criteria as it
does not emit greenhouse gases when used. (Hansson et al., 2019) (Ren and Liang, 2017) The
challenges of using hydrogen as a maritime fuel include high energy usage for extraction and the
energy density being low. (Xing et al., 2021)
When hydrogen is produced from renewable energy it is the cleanest fuel of all the alternative fuels
analysed and has the ability of being a zero emission fuel. (DNV-GL, 2019) (ABS, 2019) Hydrogen
will however most likely mostly be made from natural gas without carbon capture and storage in
the near future, as the transition to production using renewable energy has not come far enough.
Although, the anticipated transition to renewable energy fits wll with future production of hydrogen.
(DNV-GL, 2019)
Regarding safety, hydrogen is a non-toxic chemical but it comes with safety concerns regarding it’s
flammability range. (DNV-GL, 2019) There are also safety challenges involved with containing the
hydrogen molecules safely. (ABS, 2019)
With the high cost of tanks and fuel cells and the low energy density that causes limited range,
hydrogen is currently limited to short-sea shipping and needs further technological development.
(DNV-GL, 2019) The energy density is much lower, even when liquefied, compared to the other
alternative fuels which means that hydrogen needs much larger volume. When compared to diesel
oil, hydrogen needs around 4.1 times the volume for the same amount of energy.(ABS, 2019)

2.5
Stakeholders
According to (McKinsey and Company, 2023), the most important factors that influence the adop-
tion of new fuels include the availability of these fuels, followed by their price, and regulatory
changes.
In this article (Hansson et al., 2020a)(Hansson et al., 2019), the study examines the
opinions of various stakeholders, including engine manufacturers, fuel suppliers, ship owners, and
authorities. According to fuel suppliers, the most important criteria are fuel cost, upcoming legisla-
tion, and reliability of fuel supply. However, engine manufacturers prioritize upcoming legislation,
health impacts, and reliability of fuel supply. Authorities emphasize GHG emissions, legislation,
and reliability of fuel supply. Ship owners prioritize safety, fuel cost, and reliability of fuel supply.
The conclusion drawn from this study is that the most important criteria are the reliability of fuel
supply, fuel cost, and upcoming legislation. Additionally, authorities express greater concern for
social factors, while ship owners prioritize the economic aspect. Both fuel suppliers and engine
manufacturers assign the most importance to technical criteria, whereas authorities emphasize en-
vironmental criteria. When considering the opinions of all decision-makers, the economic aspect
emerges as the most important, followed by social and environmental criteria. Finally, technical
criteria are deemed the least important.
There is another article that examines the preferences of stakeholders, including ship owners, fuel
suppliers, engine manufacturers, academics, banks, and the public (Strantzali et al., 2023a). The
most important criteria were CAPEX and OPEX under economic considerations, while GHG emis-
sions, regulations, and fuel availability were deemed most important among other factors.

As seen on the flowchart in Figure 2, this chapter uses the results of the literature review as a basis

3.1
Selection of alternatives
An MCDA has been suggested for determining the best fuel based on the chosen criteria in a context
of uncertainty and vagueness. Once the literature review is carries out, seven potential alternatives
(Green LNG, HFO, Green Ammonia, Green Methanol, Green Hydrogen, Green Ammonia FC 1,
and Green Ammonia FC 2) have been selected due to their significance for the shipping industry.
It is important to note that different scenarios have been compared, for example, HFO, Hydrogen,
LNG, and Methane have been considered to operate in an Internal Combustion Engine ICE, while
for Ammonia, three different cases have been considered. According to the AEGIR project, three
different fuel cells (SOFC, PCEMR, and Polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM)) are available to use
for the FC cases, and the objective in the AEGIR project is to determine the optimal sizing of these
components to produce 20 MW Alternating current (AC) for a ship. When operating with fuel cells,
two specific cases are considered. On one hand, the Direct current (DC) input power required by
the PCEMR has been provided by the SOFC part, meaning that the number of stacks used by PEM
is maximum (350), and therefore, the relative share of electrical power produced by PEM is 1. On
the other hand, it has been considered that the SOFC part can also generate electricity for the ship
besides providing power to the PCEMR part. In this way, the SOFC has incurred higher expenses
due to its large number of required stacks and has represented the majority of the electrical power
given to the ship. Lastly, as mentioned earlier, the last case consists of operating ammonia in an
ICE.
Considering the interest in determining the fuel for the upcoming shipping industry, sustainable
production techniques has been explored, with the exception of HFO which is used as a benchmark
to represent the current situation. As a result, e-LNG, e-Methanol, e-Ammonia, and e-Hydrogen
have all been analyzed.
It is necessary to introduce some important assumptions for this project:
• The efficiency for ICE has remained constant (35%) due to its similarity among them (Pulkrabek,
1997).
• The calorific value of each fuel has been taken into account in the calculation process.

4
Methodology
The integrated fuzzy MCDA (AHP, TOPSIS, VIKOR, and SAW) proposed is utilized to determine
the weights of 15 criteria as well as to rank the different alternatives according to their performance.
Initially, it has been decided to use the AHP method to calculate the weight of each criterion, given
its widespread use and popularity for solving this type of problems (Khazaii, 2016). As (Stofkova
et al., 2022) explains, the fundamental characteristic of the AHP technique lies in its representation
of the decision-making problem as a hierarchical structure. This structure is shown in the Figure
5 below, with the top level containing the evaluation objective, which in this case is the selection
of the best fuel for the shipping industry based on the mentioned criteria and emissions constraints
imposed by the IMO for 2050. Moreover, two additional levels can be identified: the second one
involves evaluating criteria, while the third level focuses on assessing alternatives (Stofkova et al.,
2022).
However, some alternative data are not precise values or expressed numerically. Therefore, a fuzzy
AHP approach will be employed to address these issues, taking into account the uncertainties
present in this industry (Ramanathan, 2004). As mentioned before, fuzzy AHP technique will be
used to determine the weight of each criterion. In contrast to the non-fuzzy AHP, where pairwise
comparisons are made on a 9-level scale (1 being equally important and 9 being extremely different
in importance) to derive their weights (Stofkova et al., 2022), the fuzzy AHP replaces exact numbers
by fuzzy numbers that represents linguistic expressions. These linguistic expressions consists of two
components, a set of elements x and an associated membership function u(x) (Klir and Yuan, 1995).
This approach allows for imprecise judgments by assigning membership degrees to precise numbers,
indicating the extent to which these numbers belong to an expression (Liu et al., 2020a).
Based on the literature review, there are three different approaches to implement a fuzzy Analytic
Hierarchy Process AHP: type-1, type-2, and intuitionistic.
The last two types, despite being
better at capturing imprecision, are disregarded due to their complexity and the inability to find
any comparative articles related to marine fuels using these methods. On the other hand, type-1
fuzzy sets, as highlighted in the analysis conducted by (Liu et al., 2020b), is a very well-known
method that, is used in more than 95% of all articles that employ fuzzy AHP. Triangular fuzzy
numbers (TFNs) and Trapezoidal fuzzy numbers (TraFNs) have been extensively studied within
the type-1 framework (Liu et al., 2020b). Among these, TFNs are preferred not only because they
are more popular but also because they simplify the calculation of criterion weights compared to
TraFNs.

The main goal of applying a defuzzification technique is to convert the fuzzy results into crisp values.

method has been utilized in this project due to its simplicity, involving only arithmetic addition

at sea. The data, which was limited to the AEGIR fuel cell system, also documented the
energy efficiency so from there it could be calculated how much inflow in MW is needed to
produce 10 MW of power for ICEs. The efficiency number used for all the alternative fuels in

5.3
Stakeholders’ preference
In order to determine the importance of each criterion, a thorough review of literature has been
carried out, taking into account the perspectives of all stakeholders participating in the decision-
making process. It is crucial to note that the weights assigned to the criteria will be computed
through the application of fuzzy AHP, a recommended approach when dealing with ambiguity and
imprecision. As such, a linguistic fuzzy scale will be employed, as outlined in previous points.
A decision-making matrix has been determined for each decision maker, and then, the opinions of
all have been aggregated into a single matrix giving the same weight to each stakeholder.
List of stakeholders that have been considered: ship-owners, engine manufacturers, fuel producers,
authorities and academia (neutral evaluators). The preferences of stakeholders could have a signif-
icant effect on the weighting of criteria. Government authorities are likely to prioritize social and
environmental criteria, while ship owners, engine manufacturers, and fuel providers may prioritize
technical and economic criteria. Therefore, the most viable fuel should perform reasonably well
across all criteria, with the primary goal being the reduction of GHG emissions mandated by the
IMO.

• Equally important (EQ) (1,1,1)

• Strongly Important (SI) (6,7,8)

To begin with the analysis, the potential reasons why TOPSIS and SAW rankings are more similar
could be due to the fact that both TOPSIS and SAW techniques rely on the same aggregation
approach. Nevertheless, the calculation method for TOPSIS requires more computational effort
than SAW, with TOPSIS being susceptible to the rank reversal phenomenon.
On the other hand, it can be noted that the TOPSIS and VIKOR rankings are not very alike.
Page 71
==PAGE:73==
Despite the fact that both methods depend on an aggregating function that represents closeness to
the optimal solution, TOPSIS uses vector normalization to eliminate decision-making units, whereas
VIKOR employs linear normalization.
However, the main difference, as explained in the literature review, lies in the approach to aggrega-
tion. The VIKOR technique aims to maximize group utility (S) while minimizing individual regret
of an "opponent" (R). On the other hand, TOPSIS employs an aggregation function for ranking
(C), where the highest score corresponds to the best ranking, whereas VIKOR works in the opposite
manner.
The TOPSIS method emphasizes the importance of selecting an alternative with the “shortest
distance” from the ideal solution and the “farthest distance” from the “negative-ideal” solution. It is
worth noting that while one alternative alternative may have a higher ranking compared to another,
the latter may be closer to the ideal solution. In this example, there would be a discrepancy between
TOPSIS and VIKOR methods.
The reason for this is that TOPSIS technique introduces two
“reference” points (distance from best and worst), but it does not consider the relative importance
of the distances from these points, which could potentially alter the ranking.
Looking at the ranking scores of VIKOR and TOPSIS: It is essential to consider that some of the
changes in the ranking are a result of very slight differences among the alternatives. For instance,
ammonia is ranked the lowest by VIKOR, and methanol is second to last. However, based on the
final scores, the difference is minimal and can be considered insignificant. By acknowledging this,
it could be observed that the rankings are more alike compared to those produced by TOPSIS and
SAW. In this scenario, methanol is identified as the least favorable, followed by ammonia as the
second least favorable in all rankings.
The biggest difference between VIKOR and TOPSIS is due to LNG and Ammonia FC 1. On one
hand, upon analyzing the scores of TOPSIS, the disparity of LNG does not appear to be significant.
The highest-ranked alternative according to TOPSIS has a score of 0.607, while the lowest-ranked
alternative has a score of 0.441, with LNG scoring 0.526. From this, we can infer that despite being
in the 5th position, LNG performs comparably to the top alternatives (Ammonia FC 1: 0.607,
Hydrogen: 0.580, HFO: 0.577, Ammonia FC 2: 0.533) rather than the alternatives with lower
scores. It can be argued that the ranking of LNG is unfair, despite being accurate according to the
TOPSIS technique. However, in the VIKOR ranking, LNG can secure the second position due to
the different scoring methodology. On the other hand, when considering the ranking variances for
Ammonia FC 1, it can be observed that there are slight discrepancies in rankings within TOPSIS.
According to TOPSIS, Ammonia FC 1 is positioned as the top choice. Nevertheless, the disparity
between the third option (HFO) and the first (Ammonia FC 1) is approximately 0.03.
In regards to the disparities between TOPSIS and SAW, it is evident that they concur on the
rankings for LNG, ammonia, and methanol, which are the lowest-ranked alternatives.
Despite
the similarities in rankings, the SAW ranking exhibits a significant difference between the 1st and
2nd positions (HFO and Ammonia FC 1, respectively) based on their preference. Conversely, the
TOPSIS ranking displays minimal variances.

Discussion
In this section further discussion were made on on the results of this thesis and the assumptions
made through out the assessment. Additionally recommendation on future works were made.
7.1
Final rankings
To start the discussion of the results, as can be seen in section 5.5, the best alternative for both
VIKOR and SAW is HFO, while for VIKOR it is Ammonia FC 1. Additionally, the Table 57 clearly
demonstrates a consensus among various approaches that both ammonia and methanol are the least
favorable options.
According to TOPSIS, Ammonia FC 1 is the best alternative but this is due to the different approach
of this technique as we explained in previous sections. The formula to calculate the score of each
alternative is the distance from worst divided by the sum of the distance from best and worst.
In this case, Ammonia FC 1 has the shortest distance from ideal solution but one of the largest
distances from the worst alternative. Even in this situation, it was ranked as the best alternative.
According to SAW, a comparable occurrence arises when we contrast the ranking with VIKOR. The
criteria "Current regulation" and "Fuel production" are the two criteria that are the determinants
for Ammonia FC 1 to have worse performance compared to HFO. Additionally, the economic aspect
also plays a substantial role in influencing this disparity.
As explained in the previously mentioned section, the rankings provided by the different methods
are more similar than they might appear at first glance. A small difference in scores is producing
significant changes in the rankings, as is the case with LNG in the TOPSIS ranking. Additionally,
the small difference between HFO and Ammonia FC 1 can be noted. Given the large number of
criteria and alternatives, it is very difficult to obtain consistent results across different methods,
especially when they are based on different principles and the steps to be performed vary. More
about these differences in rankings can be read in the Literature Review and Final Rankings sections.

see the average rankings of the three methods. In Table 57 the average rankings of each alternative

7.2
Sensitivity analysis
Upon examining the initial four scenarios outlined in the sensitivity analysis, focusing solely on
criteria related to one aspect (economic, social, technical, or environmental), it is evident that the
rankings remain quite consistent regardless of the diverse methodologies employed. This can be
attributed to the utilization of a smaller subset, resulting in a more cohesive behavior from both
methods due to the increased homogeneity within the group. The differences in the algorithms and
the sensitivity to the weighting and normalization of the criteria become more pronounced when the
number of criteria increases, especially if they are from different aspects, causing the alternatives
to behave very differently.
Furthermore, as seen in scenarios 1-4 the rankings between the three methods, VIKOR, TOPSIS and
SAW are identical for when only environmental criteria are taken into account and almost identical
when only technical criteria are taken into account. The results between the three methods start to
vary a little bit more for when only social criteria are considered. For case 1 when only economic
criteria are taken into account the rankings start to vary much more between the three methods
and so we can draw the conclusion that the economic criteria have the most effect on the variation
in the rankings between the three methods in the final results. Figures 8-11 for cases 6-9 gave a
better picture for truly how sensitive the weights are when systematically increasing and decreasing
the weights for each criteria category. The results of scenarios 6-9 imply that the weights are highly
sensitive.
7.3
Improvement suggestions and possible future research
In terms of recommendations and improvements, the authors have realised that the number of
criteria used in this thesis might be excessive, given the differences in the results of the three
techniques employed. Comparing the number of criteria included in this work with the articles
referenced in the literature review, there is a significant difference. Additionally, the authors believe
that there may have been overemphasizes on the significance of the HFO alternative, as it performs
the best in three of the four aspects. As a suggestion, it would have been prudent to incorporate
additional criteria that consider future implications because this is possibly where HFO performs
the worst compared to the other alternatives.
Regarding the three techniques employed, in VIKOR technique, the two determinant criteria are
"Current regulation" and "Fuel production". Due to these two factors, HFO performs best and
Ammonia FC 1 and 2 have a worse performance compared to HFO. Therefore these criteria and
their weights should be given careful consideration and future research could re-evaluate this as it
has significant effect on the results.
Regarding the selection of technical criteria: Recall that the criteria fuel production measures the
technical readiness levels of all production and processing of the fuels.
Initially it was decided
that the criteria available infrastructure would estimate the necessary infrastructure from after
the fuel production until the fuel would be inside the storage tanks onboard ships. This would
Page 85
==PAGE:87==
include distribution network, storage facilities, bunkering infrastructure and so on. However due
to limitation in available data, the data used in the criteria available resources only assesses the
infrastructure in the resource extraction and fuel production. This is a limitation which future
research could improve on, by assessing the whole infrastructure as the authors set out to do in the
beginning.
Another assumption the authors made was that both the current fuel cost and the future fuel cost
were important so they each had it’s own criteria. This leads to the combined weight for the fuel
cost being by far the highest weight with the second highest weight. In hindsight it may have been
more accurate to combine these two fuel cost categories into one to reduce the weights for fuel cost.
To capture the technical criteria commercial effects, it was decided to calculate the energy output of
each alternative assuming each alternative had the same tank size. This criteria attempts to capture
the effects on how often ships had to bunker and it would also effect the routes. As discussed another
way to capture the commercial effects would be to estimate the difference in available cargo space
for the different sizes of storage tanks of each alternative. This is something that future research
could attempt to do and possibly there would be some differences in the results.
Based on the results of this thesis the Ammonia FC 1 averages the highest and second highest score
for environmental and economic criteria, respectively. For both social and technical criteria, it ranks
in 4th or 5th place for all the MCDA methods. Therefore, future work needs to drive development
of the social and technical aspects. Of course some of those criteria are not possible to improve
such as ammonia’s toxicity levels. Other criteria can improve with future development such as the
technology criteria fuel production, available infrastructure and adaptability and the social criteria
current regulation. Even though the Ammonia FC 1 is second for economic criteria, it can be seen
in the scores that it is still far behind HFO in all economic criteria. Future work in the industry
will therefore also include improving the economic conditions for ammonia as a marine fuel in the
fuel cell system.

8
Conclusion
In section 1.4 the authors set out to answer how the ammonia-based propulsion system developed
in the AEGIR project compares to current-, and alternative fuels in Internal Combustion Engine
(ICE) propulsion system.
The Ammonia FC 1 averages a ranking of 2,33 in the three MCDA methods, second only to HFO
which averages a ranking of 1,67. In TOPSIS the Ammonia FC 1, being in first place, even outranks
HFO in second place. The conclusion is therefore that the Ammonia FC 1 outperforms all other
alternative fuel scenarios, however future work in the industry involves improving the social and
technical aspects for ammonia, were other alternative fuels score higher and to improve economic
conditions to rank closer to HFO.
As the Ammonia FC 1 is the most attractive alternative out of all the alternative fuels considered
in this thesis the authors recommend further development of the AEGIR system.
Additionally the authors set out to answer which alternative fuels are feasible for future use in the
maritime sector to ultimately decrease GHG. As it can be seen in the Table 57, without taking
into account the alternative Ammonia FC 1, that hydrogen is the most viable alternative fuel.
As demonstrated in section 6, it was shown that Hydrogen does not perform well when only the
economic aspect is considered, being the worst alternative after LNG. However, the industry and
organizations like the IMO are strategically positioning hydrogen as a potential fuel for the future
in order to address the pressing need to reduce GHG. Therefore, a price reduction is expected due
to more favorable regulations and increased production. The authors believe it is also important
to remember that the decision support framework proposed in this thesis has few criteria related
to future performance, where both hydrogen and Ammonia FC 1 are favored due to potential cost
reductions. Not only that, but significant improvements in technical and social aspects are also
expected.
