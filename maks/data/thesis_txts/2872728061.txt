Abstract
Dynamic measurements offer a way to capture the sound field within a room using a moving
microphone or microphone array. This approach has the potential to enable efficient spatial
sampling of the sound field, thereby accelerating the reconstruction process within a region
of interest. In this thesis, we explore the feasibility of reconstructing the sound field within
a limited frequency range from a dynamic measurement using a handheld microphone. Both
simulations and a previous master’s thesis have shown that such reconstruction is indeed pos-
sible. This is further confirmed through experimental validation, and preliminary results are
presented for large-scale reconstructions using measurements from a large-aperture setup.

Introduction
In acoustic engineering, the room impulse response (RIR) is a key descriptor of how sound
propagates from one or more sources to a receiver within a space. It is essential for analyzing,
understanding, and controlling room acoustics, and finds wide application in areas such as vir-
tual acoustics, architectural acoustics and loudspeaker system calibration.
The RIR inside an enclosure is a function of space, time and source position. As such, the
acquisition of spatially sampled room impulse responses is generally a demanding task. From
a purely experimental point of view, in order to obtain a spatial representation of the sound
field on a uniform grid, one would need to fulfill the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem which
states that the maximum distance between two measuring position should be d = λmin
2 , where
λmin is the wavelength associated with the highest frequency one would want to capture. For
instance at 17 kHz, d ≈10 cm which means that approximately 106 measurements would be
required to capture the sound field in a 1 m3 volume.
In recent years, in order to circumvent this limitation, we saw the rise of sound field recon-
struction technics. Sound field reconstruction is the process of estimating or recreating the
spatial and temporal characteristics of a sound field based on measurements taken at a limited
number of points in space. It involves using physical knowledge about sound propagation in
the enclosure and is often combined with a compressive sensing framework, which assumes the
sparsity of an underlying component of the system. Such physical knowledge includes using a
set a function basis that is solution to the wave equation or the fact that the RIR possesses
a sparse structure in the time domain. For instance in [2, 27], under the assumption that the
source is sufficiently far from the measurement area, the sound field can be decomposed using a
plane wave expansion. In [7], the structure of the RIR is segmented in order to decompose and
model each part using different models. A combination of spherical expansion for the direct
sound and first few reflections is used while the latter part of the RIR is modeled using a plane
wave expansion whose coefficients are estimated using a kernel method. Although the number
of measurements in the previously mentioned works is quite reduced, the process of measuring
the RIR needed for the reconstructions is still time consuming.
Dynamic measurement methods on the other hand, allow to capture the sound field along a sam-
pled trajectory using a constantly moving microphone or array. In theory, this method could be
an alternative to static recording of the sound field. These methods require a precise tracking of
the moving measurement equipment at the same sampling rate as the recorded sound field. Such
tracking can be achieved using a tracking device, for instance optically or using ultrasounds.
In [15], the tracking of a moving microphone was performed using perfect sequences and dis-
tributed loudspeakers and does not rely on an external tracking device. Different methods were
implemented in order to perform a dynamic measurement. In [9], the RIRs are reconstructed
from a circular trajectory with a non uniform rotation speed. The pressure measured by the
microphone is interpreted as a spatial-temporal sampling problem and interpolated using La-
grange polynomials. In a later paper [10], this framework is extended to multiple sound sources.

For more complex trajectories, in [16], a measurement strategy to reconstruct the sound field
on a uniform grid is presented, as well as error metrics based on the trajectory that is used to
sample the sound field. The authors later presented a compressed sensing framework for the
problem in [14]. Resolutions of the problem using spherical harmonics are presented in [13]
using a compressed sensing framework and in [3] using Bayesian estimation. The later paper
also includes the effect of the directionality of the microphone. Finally in [19], the problem is
approached in a state-space problem perspective using Kalman filters and transition matrices
in the time domain.
It should be noted that all the mentioned papers use numerical simulations. As a matter of fact,
in the literature there is little or close to none experimental validation of the dynamic sampling
methods. Recently in [26], an experimental measurement and reconstruction is performed. The
moving microphone is handled using a robotic arm while the reconstruction is based on a plane
wave expansion in the frequency domain.
The objective of this master thesis is, in the continuation of a previous master thesis [4], to build
a compressed sensing framework to solve the dynamic measurement problem, as well as to vali-
date said framework using simulations and by performing a handheld microphone measurement.
As presented in [24], the coloration of the room and most of the subjective perception is included
in the direct part and first few reflections. In [17], the authors pointed out that is should be
possible to substitute the late part of the binaural RIR with a common generated tail without
loss of directional information. Therefore, our efforts will be focused on the reconstruction of
the direct sound and first few reflections. In addition, we will focus on solving the problem in
the low frequency range (up to 500 Hz). This will help reduce the size of the problem as well as
limit the errors coming from an imperfect tracking of the microphone and effect of an operator
in the sound field while performing a measurement. Finally, a time domain approach is used
contrary to some of the previous work. This choice is motivated by the fact that some room
acoustics phenomenon (such as echos or slapback effects) are more easily captured and identi-
fied from a time domain perspective. Additionally, the moving microphone sampling problem is
rooted in the time domain. Some of the previous models [4, 13, 3] rely on the STFT to combine
the frequency domain estimation with the time domain measurement.
First a static sampling method is formulated using a time domain plane wave and spherical wave
representation, similar to [7] and [2]. Based on the static sampling method, a dynamic sampling
method is presented and implemented. Validation of these models are conducted using RIRs
generated from an image source method (ISM) [1] simulation. Finally, a dynamic measurement
is performed using a handheld microphone in order to evaluate the accuracy of the dynamic
sampling method experimentally.

Theory
This chapter outlines the physical principles, calculations and assumptions required to solve the
moving microphone sampling problem. Some fundamentals of acoustics and room acoustics are
presented in sections 2.1 and 2.2 to understand what a moving microphone measures, as well as
to prepare the ground work for the sampling schemes in section 2.4. In section 2.3, the concept
of perfect sequences and MLS signals is briefly explained, as they are used in the simulations
and experiments later on. Finally, in 2.5, the tools necessary to solve the inverse problems of
2.4 are outlined.

2.2
Room acoustics
In room acoustics, a common assumption is to consider that a room is a linear and time invariant
system (or LTI system for short). A LTI system can be fully described by its impulse response
h which is defined as the answer of the system to a dirac function δ.
In fact, if one excites a LTI system of impulse response h with a given sequence x, then the
response of the system y is given by
y(t) = h(t) ∗x(t),
(2.8)
where ∗is the convolution operation.
Equation (2.8) can also be interpreted as the frequency response function (FRF) in the frequency
domain by taking its Fourier transform
¯y(ω) = ¯h(ω) · ¯y(ω),
(2.9)
where ¯h (resp. ¯y, ¯x) is the Fourier transform of h (resp. y, x).
Note that the definition of h using x(t) = δ(t) holds as δ is the identity function for the ∗
operation and its Fourier transform is a constant.
In the case of room acoustics, the room impulse response (RIR) is a function of the position of
the source rs, receiver r and time t and is sometimes referred to as the plenacoustic function.
It can be seen as a time series composed of the direct sound, first order reflections, and late
reflections.

reflections inside the enclosure), it is worth mentioning the modal and statistical theories of

2.4
Sampling schemes
Let us assume that we are interested in estimating the RIR h inside a region of interest (ROI) Ω
from measurements inside a measurement volume Ωmeas ⊂Ω. Ωcould represent the enclosure
as a whole or some specific volumes of interest inside the room. It is assumed that the presence
of a measurement device in Ωmeas does not disturb the sound field and that there are no sound
sources or obstacles in Ωmeas.
Additionally, for the sake of simplicity, we consider that the sound field we are measuring comes
from a unique sound source inside the enclosure. Note, however, that the methods described in
the subsequent sections can be adapted to the case of a multichannel excitation of a room.
In the next sections 2.4.1 and 2.4.2, two sampling schemes to recover the sound field inside the
ROI using a plane wave basis are presented. A spherical wave basis can also be used and the
most important steps are described in A.2. A sampling scheme involving a combination of both
bases is presented later in 3.2.2.

2.4.2
Dynamic sampling scheme
In the previous section we assumed that nr static measurements of length nh were taken in
Ωmeas. It is possible to adapt the previous scheme to represent a dynamic measurement in
which a single microphone is moving inside Ωmeas with a sampled trajectory r(n) of length nr.
To do so, the sound field in the enclosure is generated using a sequence made of R repetitions of
ψ. By doing so, the sound field inside the room becomes nh-periodic and the recorded pressure
p can be reinterpreted as a spatial-temporal sampling problem [9]
p(r(n), n) = p(r(n), n mod nh).
(2.25)

2.5
Estimation problem
In Equations (2.23) and (2.26), we have access to the left terms p and m through measurements
and the goal is to estimate the coefficients in a of the plane wave expansion that would recon-
struct the sound field best. The underlying physics (here the wave propagation) is encoded in
the matrices T and U. This can be referred to as an inverse problem. In our case, the problems
are ill-posed, which means that there are multiple suitable sets of coefficients a that fulfill the
previous equations. Therefore the choice of estimation method will differ in which solution is
prioritized. In the case of matrices T and U, their structure and underlying physics make it so
that we expect the set of coefficients a to be sparse.

2.5.4
Cross-validation and hyperparameter tuning
In Equations (2.29) and (2.31), we introduced the regularization parameter λ. This scalar can
be related to the amount of noise in the observation vector y, depending on the assumed prior
distribution in a Bayesian interpretation of these equations [25]. However, selecting the optimal
value of λ that yields good accuracy on both training and unseen data can be challenging. One
common approach is to treat λ as a hyperparameter and determine it using cross-validation
(CV). CV involves training the model on different subsets of the data, allowing it to be validated
on unseen data and helping to mitigate overfitting. The most widely used form, known as k-fold
CV, proceeds as follows (see Figure 2.1):
1. Split the dataset into k equal-sized folds.
2. For each iteration, use one fold as validation data and the remaining k−1 folds as training
data.
3. Train the model on the training folds and evaluate it on the validation fold.
4. Average the performance metrics across all k folds to assess the model’s generalization
ability.

Simulation framework
In this chapter, we evaluate the sampling schemes derived in 2.4. For the first experiment in

3.3
Dynamic procedure
In this section the dynamic sampling scheme is evaluated. The pressure m measured by the
microphone is generated as described in 3.1 from the RIRs along the sampled trajectory defined
in Equation (3.1). Only the relevant samples are kept as in Equation (2.25). It should be noted
that in practice for lengthy measurements, directly computing the matrix T used to get the
dynamic sensing matrix U is not recommended due to memory overflow during construction.
Indeed, for each additional measurement point, the matrix T needs an additional nh rows. An
alternative is to build U sequentially:
==PAGE:27==
CHAPTER 3. SIMULATION FRAMEWORK
20
1. Build T for just one receiver position r(n), which gives a matrix with nh rows only.
2. Extract the row (n mod nh) of T and store it as the n-th row of U.
3. Increment the time step from n to n + 1.
4. Repeat the previous steps.
A more efficient procedure could be implemented by only calculating the row needed at each
iteration of the previous procedure. However, since the code to build T is already quite efficient,
we keep this procedure for the rest of the thesis. We will first evaluate the PW only model,
followed by the SW+PW model. We then discuss tracking imperfection and trajectory choice
from a PW only simulation. A convergence study is conducted in the next section 3.3.3, giving
us some valuable insight on the compromise between measurement length and accuracy. This
leads us to present a divide and conquer strategy with the aim of performing efficient sound
field reconstruction over larger spaces.

3.3.3
Convergence study
In the previous sections, we fixed the number of recorded samples to 40 × nh in order to com-
pare with the static reconstructions performed in [4] and in the previous section 3.2. However
this leaves the question of whether or not this number of recorded samples is optimal for the
reconstruction. Using the same Lissajous trajectory described in Equation (3.1), we plot the
distribution of the NMSE on the grid across the interpolation and extrapolation points as a
function of the number of samples in m expressed as a number of repetitions R of the exci-
tation signal. From Figure 3.8, there is no major improvement in the reconstructions after
20 repetitions of the MLS. This is reflected in the MAC values in Figure 3.9 as well, where
the curves overlap almost perfectly for R > 10. A reason to this might be the choice of the
trajectory. Since the Lissajous curve is periodic, we should capture the spatial information of
the sound field in one period or so. Going through multiple period would give similar spatial

Furthermore, we would like to provide some limitations of the divide and conquer strategy for
more practical reasons.
First, although this approach reduces the size of the domains and the number of wave compo-
nents, each subdomain Ωi
meas still requires a sufficient number of data points to enable accurate
reconstruction. This typically requires scanning entire areas rather than simply passing the mi-
crophone through, as insufficient spatial sampling may lead to poor individual reconstructions.
However, it is important to note that the sampling problem can be interpreted as a spatio-
temporal sampling task. As long as the ordering of the sequence is preserved, any recorded
pressure signal can be fragmented and reorganized. This provides the operator with a high
degree of flexibility in how to formulate the inverse problem. For example, in the construction
of m, one could transition from p(r(1), 1) to p(r(nh + 2), 2) instead of following the standard
order p(r(2), 2), thereby enabling the extraction of more spatial characteristics from a lengthy
and less structured measurement.
Second, the estimation and interpretation of the sound field (for instance identifying specific
reflections) becomes more challenging due to the fragmented nature of the problem. One must
inspect the coefficients of each individual reconstruction, which complicates the analysis.
Finally, since each sound field is fitted independently within its respective Ωi
meas, any lack of
overlap between domains means that information from adjacent regions is not shared, preventing
the model from leveraging potentially useful spatial correlations of the sound field.

3.4
Discussion
The simulations provided valuable insights on the moving microphone problem and the strengths
and weaknesses of the proposed models.
First, during the static procedure 3.2, we showed that the proposed PW only model performs
worse than the benchmark in the interpolation task, though it shows improved performance for
extrapolation. In contrast, the SW+PW model can achieve reconstruction quality comparable
to the benchmark, but only if the spherical wavefront is accurately fitted. This provides valu-
able insight into how and when to apply the proposed models. In reverberant environments,
frequency domain approaches are advantageous due to their ability to exploit the sparsity of
the FRF at low frequencies. Our proposed model on the other hand, may require more conven-
tional methods, such as Tikhonov regularization, for accurate field fitting in such conditions.
The inverse is also true: in dry (non-reverberant) environments, where the benchmark benefits
from simple regularization, our model can take advantage of sparse promoting algorithm to
leverage the finite number of reflections, yielding improved reconstructions. It is worth noting
that frequency domain approaches are supported by robust mathematical frameworks, such as
Vekua theory from complex analysis, which provide guarantees on convergence and reconstruc-
tion quality [20]. Nonetheless, prior work [4, 13] have demonstrated that these methods face
significant challenges when applied to the moving microphone problem. The main strength
of frequency domain techniques lies in their decomposition of the static problem into separate
bandwidths, offering flexibility in estimation. However, in practice, ideal band-limited filtering
is unachievable. As a result, inverse problems have been solved holistically, similarly to our
proposed models, which can degrade reconstruction performance.
Second, results from dynamic simulations indicate that our proposed models achieve recon-
struction with accuracy comparable to their static counterparts, provided the same number of
fitting samples is used and that these samples encode sufficiently well the spatial variation of
the sound field. This contrasts with findings from [4], where improved reconstructions were
observed with increased measurement durations. As discussed in Section 3.3.3, this discrepancy
may be explained by a saturation effect, where additional samples become no longer meaningful
to the reconstructions. This saturation is highly trajectory dependent and warrants further
investigation. However, in low SNR scenarios, using more fitting samples may still be benefi-
cial. Unlike in static measurements, where temporal averaging can mitigate noise (e.g., from
ventilation), dynamic measurements lack this luxury. To address this, one could either increase
the number of fitting samples, as proposed in [13], or estimate the sound field multiple times
using different trajectories within the same Ωmeas, followed by averaging.
As a consequence of these insights, we proposed a divide and conquer strategy for our models,
aimed at reconstructing the sound field over large measurement apertures with minimal com-
putational cost. The results are promising: reconstructions closely resemble the reference field,
even for relatively strict conditions of only using a one minute measurement over an aperture
of three meters.

Experimental framework
In this chapter, we perform handheld measurements of the sound field in a room and we evaluate
the reconstruction based on a number of static verification measurements in the vicinity of the
measurement area Ωmeas. The general workflow is described as
1. Perform a dynamic measurement of trajectory r(n).
2. Build the associated dynamic sensing matrix U and solve the inverse problem.
3. Build the sensing matrix T at a given verification location rv and use the coefficients
estimated in 2. to get an estimation of the RIR at rv.
In 4.2, we perform a measurement with similar measurement aperture as described in 3.1. 99
static measurements are used to draw statistics on the calculated reconstruction.
In the second experiment in section 4.3, we try to implement the described divide and conquer
strategy as in 3.3.4 from a lengthy measurement.
4.1
Protocol
The dynamic measurements are performed in the virtual acoustics and sound field control room
at DTU. As the measurement is performed handheld, a homemade two axes gimbal is used
to stabilize the microphone so that it always points towards the ceiling (see Figure 4.1). The
tracking of the microphone is performed using an ultrasonic tracking device from Marvelmind,
which has a precision of ±2 cm according to the provider. It uses four static beacons and one
mobile beacon fixed on the stabilizer 23 cm below the microphone position. The static beacons
are placed on the ground of the room, and one of them serves as the origin of the coordinate
system. To generate the ultrasonic pulses required for tracking, the static beacons emit clicks
that fall within the audible range. However, no corruption from these clicks was observed, as
the SNR is high. Additionally, the audio recordings were reviewed, and no audible clicks were
detected. The coordinates from the tracked microphone are corrected in post processing to
give a direct reference frame with positive coordinates in the measurement area. The room is
excited using a Dynaudio BM6 loudspeaker, which has a lower cutoff frequency of 43 Hz. Con-
sequently, the visualization of frequency-related variables is limited to the range of 50 to 512 Hz.

samples when piloted with an analog trigger. Therefore the first 107 samples are discarded in

Equation (2.25), the first 4 repetitions of the MLS are also discarded as to remove any transient
effects.
The trajectory is updated at a rate of approximately 10 Hz. Because a trajectory sampled at
the same rate as the audio signal is required according to Equation (2.25), the recording of the
trajectory is upsampled using a 1D interpolation on each coordinates (x, y, z) after applying a
Savitzky–Golay filter of window length 11 and order 2 to remove any recorded outliers. We
acknowledge that this is not ideal, as we are smoothing the recorded trajectory. However, in
the first place we expect to move the microphone quite slowly as to limit the Doppler effect
and tracking errors. Since the aim of the measurement is to reconstruct the sound field for a
relatively small frequency range, this should not impact the reconstruction much.
The generated and recorded RIR are post processed the same way as described in 3.1 i.e time
gating them to only conserve the direct sound and first few reflections.
Finally, as to validate the performed reconstructions, static measurements taken using a stand
are used. The reference RIRs are extracted from 10 seconds measurements using the same MLS
signal that is used as excitation signal. Once deconvolved they are averaged over 20 repetitions.

4.4
Discussion
The measurements provided valuable insights into the moving microphone problem and also
revealed several limitations of both the modeling approach and the measurement protocol.
First, we would like to emphasize that the main bottleneck encountered during the measure-
ments was the tracking of the microphone. In the case of the small aperture setup, we are
reasonably confident in the quality of the tracking, as we selected an area of the room where
the tracking system performs optimally. However, for the larger aperture configuration, the
measurement was significantly constrained due to unreliable tracking in certain areas of the
room, even though our original goal was to reconstruct the sound field throughout the entire
space. This issue should be thoroughly addressed and improved before attempting longer or
more complex measurement campaigns.
That said, it remains difficult to determine whether the decline in reconstruction quality is solely
due to inaccuracies in the tracking or the upsampling of the microphone trajectory. While the
NMSE increased notably, the values for the MAC remained excellent and aligned well with
expectations based on simulations. This suggests that the frequency response functions are still
consistent in terms of phase alignment and overall structure, although discrepancies remain in
the magnitude response.
At the same time, the reconstructions obtained still appear reasonable. They do not show signs
of extreme overfitting or divergence, such as those observed in the worst cases from the bench-
mark extrapolations. Based on this, one might conclude that although the reconstructions are
not ideal, they likely capture the low frequency behavior of the room to some extent, particu-
larly within the frequency range between the Schroeder frequency and approximately 300 hertz.
Within this range, even relatively large errors in the tracking may not significantly degrade the
reconstruction quality.
Finally, the experiments also revealed that dynamic measurements are more challenging to plan
than traditional static measurement campaigns. While dynamic approaches offer the advantage
of significantly reducing acquisition time, they require considerably more preparation and careful
consideration. As previously discussed, the choice of trajectory plays a critical role, making
the planning of both temporal and spatial coverage essential for reliable results. Moreover,
dynamic measurements may be more susceptible to errors.
For example, unintended noise
such as the operator’s footsteps can contaminate the recordings, potentially compromising data
quality. They also lack direct ways to compensate for external noise or low source power where
traditionally repetitions and averaging can be conducted.
==PAGE:52==
Conclusion
This thesis focused on the development of a time domain dynamic model for reconstructing
the sound field in the context of the moving microphone problem. While much of the existing
literature relies primarily on simulations, experimental investigations are beginning to emerge.
A recent proof of concept using a robotic arm was presented in [4, 26]. The primary contribu-
tion of this thesis lies in demonstrating dynamic reconstructions from a handheld measurement
conducted over a larger aperture than in previous work.
To achieve this, the proposed models were first evaluated within a simulation framework. Com-
parisons were made with a static benchmark, which resulted in somewhat lower reconstruction
quality under identical conditions. Nevertheless, the results remained encouraging. Notably,
the dynamic models demonstrated a reconstruction quality comparable to that of their static
counterparts when applied under similar conditions. One of the ideas explored in this thesis is
that, beyond a certain point, adding more samples to the fitting does not necessarily improve
reconstruction, assuming that the measurement region Ωmeas remains constant. This insight
led to the formulation of a divide and conquer strategy, inspired by state of the art numerical
methods, to enable the reconstruction of large sound fields from a minimal number of samples at
relatively low computational cost. A simulation using a larger aperture confirmed the viability
of this approach, achieving reconstruction quality consistent with earlier simulations. These
results emphasize that the trajectory is crucial to the reconstruction process, as it determines
the spatial variation in the sound field that the model tries to recover.
The experimental component of the thesis confirmed the feasibility of performing dynamic
sound field reconstruction using a handheld microphone. Results obtained over a small aperture
demonstrated good reconstruction performance, even with short acquisition times. However,
for the larger aperture case, we were unable to draw definitive conclusions due to limitations
in the tracking system. While the reconstructions still appear reasonable, capturing features
such as a clear spherical wavefront and possible ground reflection, additional measurements
are needed to conclusively validate the application of the divide and conquer strategy under
real-world conditions.

Outlook
We present here some perspectives and potential refinements for future work on the subject of
the moving microphone.
First and foremost, it is important to acknowledge that the proposed models come with their
own limitations. In essence, they are quite similar to the image source method, and therefore
share many of its known shortcomings. For example, we do not expect these models to perform
well in highly reverberant environments or at low frequencies. Just as different approaches are
used in room acoustics depending on the frequency range, such as wave-based solvers for low
frequencies and ray tracing methods for higher ones, it is conceivable that hybrid methods could
be employed. In this scenario, the sound field reconstruction in the low frequency range could
be addressed using frequency domain approaches, while higher frequencies might be more effec-
tively handled with time domain techniques. Consequently, we recommend further investigation
into the frequency domain method presented in [4], with the aim of exploring reconstruction
in rooms that exhibit strong modal behavior. For instance, following the approach in [2], the
regularization of the problem could be extended by employing a matrix instead of a single scalar
parameter, offering a promising direction for refinement with minimal efforts as the model al-
ready proved its viability.
Furthermore, we believe that the divide and conquer strategy introduced in this thesis could be
applied to other modeling approaches. The concept is straightforward yet powerful, and serves
as the foundation of many successful numerical methods such as the finite element method.
This work has demonstrated that it is possible to circumvent the limitations involved in re-
constructing large sound fields by breaking the problem down into smaller, more manageable
subfields. The core idea of this method is somewhat brute force, and it does not fully exploit
the extensive knowledge of room acoustics developed over the years. Additionally, not all the
measured data are utilized during the estimation process, as previously discussed. Nevertheless,
this method has the potential to enable rapid sound field computation once the subproblems
are individually solved. For our models, the current bottleneck lies in the matrix construction
stage, which consumes more than half of the total computation time. By contrast, solving the
inverse problem itself is relatively straightforward and efficient.
Generalizing the models for higher frequencies reconstruction might pose some limitations. The
comments we provide here are quite general and should apply to the majority of the current
approach on the subject. First, there are limitations related to computational requirements, as
capturing high frequency behavior would necessitate a much finer spatial sampling of the sound
field. Second, from a practical standpoint, the higher the frequency range targeted by the re-
construction, the more one must account for the influence of the microphone operator on the
sound field, be it a person or a contraption. At these frequencies, even small disturbances due
to scattering and absorption can affect the measurements. Finally, the tracking system must
operate at the same rate as the audio recording, which implies the need for higher precision
and faster update rates. These two requirements often present a tradeoff when performing real
time position acquisition. While this issue can be partially mitigated through post processing,

for instance by ignoring the real time update to get high precision tracking and then realign the
tracking with the audio samples, it remains a key challenge for future implementations.
At present, there exists a wide range of methods for dynamic sound field reconstruction, several
of which have been mentioned in the introduction of this thesis. The main limitation now lies in
the availability of reliable measurement data to validate and benchmark these models. We there-
fore strongly encourage further measurement campaigns. In particular, improved methods for
microphone tracking should be explored, as the tracking system used in this study proved to be
inconsistent at times and represented one of the main obstacles to expanding the research. One
promising solution would be to implement sensor fusion, combining multiple tracking methods
to produce accurate and high update rate positional data for the moving microphone.
