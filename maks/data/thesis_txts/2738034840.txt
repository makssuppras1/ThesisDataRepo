Abstract 
This study investigates the application of membrane aerated biofilm reactors (MABRs) 
utilizing methanotrophic bacteria (MOBs) for the removal of organic micropollutants (OMPs) 
and nutrients from synthetic anaerobic digestate. The experiment was conducted using two 
identical MABRs, focusing on the degradation of sulfamethoxazole, 1H-benzotriazole, and 
atenolol, as well as the analysis of ammonia and carbon dioxide dynamics. 
The results demonstrated significant removal efficiencies for the selected OMPs, with 
sulfamethoxazole exhibiting removal rates between 62% and 85% in Reactor 1 and 75% to 
78% in Reactor 2. 1H-Benzotriazole removal ranged from 54.5% to 58% in Reactor 1 and 
41% to 61% in Reactor 2, while atenolol removal varied between 57% and 68.5% in Reactor 
1 and 27% to 40.4% in Reactor 2.  
Ammonia removal analysis revealed that the first reactor achieved a removal rate of 12.25% 
at 2.21 mg/day, while the second reactor managed 8.29% at 1.46 mg/day. The detection of 
nitrite and nitrate indicated simultaneous nitrification alongside ammonia assimilation by 
MOBs. Total Organic Carbon (TOC) and Total Nitrogen (TN) analyses indicated stable 
nitrogen concentrations in the effluent, with TOC levels significantly lower than TN, 
underscoring the efficiency of methane removal by MOBs. 
This study confirms the potential of MABRs utilizing MOBs for effective biodegradation of 
OMPs and nutrient removal in wastewater treatment. Potential applications of this technology 
extend to industrial and municipal wastewater treatment, resource recovery contributing to 
sustainable water management and pollution control.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Background 
Global environmental sustainability is one of the most serious problems of our modern 
society. The rapid industrialization, urbanization and population growth have exacerbated the 
pressure on natural resources and on the ecosystems. In the fight to preserve the environment 
and to protect human health, waste management and especially wastewater treatment, plays a 
very crucial role. If the wastewater is treated inefficiently, it can cause serious environmental 
issues, such as water pollution, loss of biodiversity and adverse effects on human health. 
Furthermore, if the treatment is planned poorly it can lead to the loss of important resources 
that could be recovered and utilized again, like water and nutrients. In addition to being a lost 
chance for resource recovery, this puts more pressure on the environment by raising the need 
for chemical fertilizers and freshwater (United Nations Environment Programme, 2017). 
In order to address these challenges, the European Union has enacted a collection of strict 
rules, laws and regulations called the Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive (91/271/EEC), 
which compels member states to construct and implement adequate treatment systems for 
municipal wastewater. The objective of this directive is to minimize pollution, safeguard the 
quality of water, and ensure safe release of treated wastewater into the environment 
(European Commission, 1991). Despite these efforts, the rising complexity and amount of 
wastewater present substantial challenges to conventional treatment technologies. This means 
that more efficient and long-term solutions and technologies are needed. 
Recently, the European Union has revised this directive to address emerging concerns. The 
new directive focuses on the removal of micropollutants. It aims to enhance the quality of 
effluents from urban wastewater treatment plants (UWWTPs) across the European Union. In 
addition, it introduces higher standards for wastewater treatment, and it emphasizes the 
reduction of micropollutants and the improvement of wastewater quality (Euronews, 2023).  
1.2 Anaerobic Digestion 
An important part of every wastewater treatment facility is anaerobic digestion (AD). 
Through AD, organic wastes are treated, and biogas is produced. Biogas can be considered a 
form of renewable energy, and also reduces the volume of waste. A very important by-
product of AD is the anaerobic digestate. The anaerobic digestate is one of the main focuses 
in this project.  
 The processes that take place in anaerobic digestion and lead to the production of biogas and 
of the digestate are listed and explained below: 
The first stage of AD is hydrolysis. During this stage, hydrolytic bacteria convert intricate 
organic polymers (proteins, lipids, etc.) into more simple and soluble molecules (sugars, fatty 
acids, etc.) (Appels et al., 2008). This stage allows organic matter to be exposed to 
degradation. The second stage of AD is acidogenesis, which uses the products of the first 
stage and metabolizes them with the use of acidogenic bacteria. Here, carbon dioxide (CO2), 
volatile fatty acids (VFAs), hydrogen and alcohols are produced. It should be noted that 
during this stage there is a pH drop (Appels et al., 2008). Continuing with the same motif, the

products of acidogenesis are used in the third stage of AD, acetogenesis. In this stage, 
acidogenic bacteria convert the VFAs and the alcohols into acetic acid, hydrogen and CO2. 
Finally, in the fourth stage of AD, methanogenesis occurs. During this process, methanogenic 
archaea produce methane (CH4) and water by using the acetic acid, hydrogen and CO2 from 
the previous stage. These methanogens are very sensitive, and they operate under very 
specific conditions. The pH needs to be maintained between 6,5 and 7,5 to maximize the 
production of methane (Appels et al., 2008). It is worth noting that the biogas that is 
produced contains both methane (55%-65%) and carbon dioxide (35%-45%) (Angelidaki & 
Batstone, 2010). 
1.3 Importance of Anaerobic Digestate Treatment 
As mentioned earlier, the anaerobic digestate will play a crucial role in this project. The 
digestate, which is a by-product of AD, contains valuable nutrients but if it is left untreated it 
can cause severe environmental and health issues. The digestate is rich in nutrients like 
nitrogen and phosphorus, but if their concentrations are too high, they can lead to problems 
like eutrophication, since the digestate can also be used as a fertilizer (Sharifi et al., 2019). In 
addition, the anaerobic digestate consists of methane which is a potent greenhouse gas and 
contributes to climate change. But if the CH4 is utilized as a renewable energy source then the 
environmental impact is decreased (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). Finally, the anaerobic 
digestate contains organic micropollutants (OMPs). These substances can harm both human 
health and the environment because of their durability and toxicity (Kalita et al., 2024). These 
environmental problems highlight how crucial it is to create cutting-edge treatment methods 
in order to successfully handle newly emerging contaminants.  
1.4 Organic Micropollutants 
A broad category of synthetic organic compounds, known as organic micropollutants 
(OMPs), includes industrial chemicals, pesticides, personal care items, and pharmaceuticals. 
Even though their concentrations in wastewater are relatively low, the OMPs can constitute a 
serious threat to the ecosystems and public health. As mentioned above, this happens due to 
toxicity, durability and due to their potential for bioaccumulation (Kalita et al., 2024). OMPs 
in most cases enter the ecosystems through domestic wastewater and through agricultural 
runoff. But the major entry point is wastewater treatment plants. The treatment processes that 
are used are incapable of completely removing them from the wastewater.  In that way the 
OMPs escape into the environment, they circulate though the surface waters (e.g. via 
irrigation) and they accumulate in aqueous ecosystems eventually coming in contact with 
humans. In addition, OMPs can bioaccumulate to plants and be a part of the food chain 
(Kalita et al., 2024, Mortensen et al., 2023).  In a recent study, Nanusha et al. (2023) 
identified 121 micropollutants from a variety of sources, including industry and agriculture, 
highlighting the broad presence of OMPs in Danish groundwater. This emphasizes how 
important it is to have better treatment and monitoring systems in place to deal with the 
OMPs in water sources. 
According to Peake et al., 2016 many physical and chemical systems have been used to 
remove the OMPs from wastewater. Unfortunately, these are very expensive. So, a new way 
of seeing things is to introduce the biological component. Biofilms in particular are favored 
because of a number of important benefits. 
 
Microbial populations adhere to surfaces and create biofilms. These biofilms can offer a

stable and effective way to break down contaminants. Through mechanisms including 
cometabolism and biodegradation, the bacteria in biofilms are able to metabolize a variety of 
pollutants, including OMPs. This method utilizes microbial activities that occur naturally to 
remove pollutants in a sustainable and cost-effective way. The capacity of biofilms to sustain 
microbial communities that are able to survive in different environmental circumstances and 
pollution loads is one of their main advantages. Because of their increased diversity, biofilms 
are better equipped to degrade complex wastewater streams (Rittmann, 2018). 
1.5 Methane oxidizing bacteria (MOBs) 
Methane oxidizing bacteria (methanotrophs) are microorganisms that feed only on methane. 
They use it as their only carbon source. Basically, they use oxygen to oxidize the methane, 
and as a byproduct they produce carbon dioxide. These bacteria are extremely useful in 
mitigating methane emissions. They can be found in areas where both methane and oxygen 
are present. Most common places would be wetlands and landfills. There are two main types 
of methanotrophic bacteria, Type I and Type II. The classification is based on the two 
different metabolic pathways that these bacteria use. Type I are gammaproteobacteria and 
they use the ribulose monophosphate pathway. On the other hand, Type II are 
alphaproteobacterial and they use the serine cycle (Su, et al., 2021).   
 
 
Figure 1: Metabolic pathways of Type I and Type II methanotrophs (Khider et al., 2021). 
 
Studies have shown that the methanotrophs are able to biodegrade OMPs. This happens 
because these bacteria possess an enzyme called methane mono-oxygenase (MMO). Here 
there is another difference between the Type I and Type II bacteria. Type I have the 
particulate methane mono-oxygenase (pMMO) enzyme, and Type II have the soluble 
methane mono -oxygenase (sMMO) (Smith & Doe, 2024, Murrell, 2010). All methanotrophs 
have the ability to synthesize pMMO, especially when copper levels are sufficient. However, 
==PAGE:9==
Master Thesis

only some methanotrophs, particularly Type II methanotrophs, can produce sMMO under low 
copper conditions.  
The applications for methanotrophic bacteria are many. But the most interesting one is their 
use in bioremediation. Especially the Type I bacteria play a crucial role. They share many 
similarities with ammonia mono-oxygenase enzyme in ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AMO). 
These bacteria have been effective in degrading OMPs (Su, et al., 2021, Tang, et al., 2017, 
Tang, et al., 2021). These ammonia oxidizing bacteria have been tried in many different 
reactor setups including membrane bioreactors (MBRs) or biofilm reactors, such as moving 
bed biofilm reactors (MBBRs) or membrane aerated bioreactors (MABRs). In these setups 
the bacteria were able to remove high amounts of OMPs when compared to conventional 
activated sludge (Tang, et al., 2017, Tang, et al., 2021).  
If methanotrophs are used instead of the ammonia oxidizing bacteria in a reactor setup, 
multiple good things can happen. First of all, the bacteria will be oxidizing methane, which is 
a greenhouse gas, and it is more potent than CO2 (approximately 28 times more) at retaining 
heat in the atmosphere. Secondly, the methane oxidizing bacteria (MOBs) can remove 
nitrogen and phosphorus from the wastewater due to their metabolic processes. For example, 
they can assimilate ammonia and nitrite. Also, the biomass that these bacteria produce is rich 
in nutrients, which means that it can also be used as animal feed (Tsapekos, et al., 2019, 
Goonesekera, 2020, Valverde-Pérez, et al., 2020). Basically, the MOBs can degrade OMPs, 
remove nutrients, remove methane and produce biomass that can be used as feedstock. They 
can be a great example of circular economy. 
 
1.6 Membrane Aerated Biofilm Reactors 
Unfortunately, methane has low solubility, which means that supplying it in open air reactors 
is very difficult. However, we can utilize membrane contractors to alleviate the problem of 
methane's gas-liquid mass transfer. These membranes' extensive surface area will improve the 
mass transfer (Pen, et al., 2014). In this project we will use two replicate MABRs to 
determine the ability of methane oxidizing bacteria to remove nutrients (nitrogen, 
phosphorus), remove methane, and degrade OMPs. As media we will use a synthetic 
digestate and we will see if a MABR can be used as the next step of anaerobic digestion, 
utilizing both the digestate and the biogas produced.

2 Materials and Methods 
 
2.1.1 Inoculum  
Before starting the experiment, two MOB cultures were chosen to be the inoculum. We chose 
methanotrophs enriched from the Herlev hospital wastewater plant. These cultures were 
grown in media of dilute nitrate mineral salt (dNMS) with potassium nitrate (Sigma Aldrich, 
Germany) as nitrogen source. The recipe of this media was adapted from Whittenbury, et al., 
1970 and it had a concentration of 25 mg NO3 /L (appendix 1.1). The cultures were growing 
in that media from October 2023 to March 2024. Then we took the biomass, and we 
transferred it in dilute ammonia mineral salt (dAMS) media which was again adapted from 
Whittenbury, et al., 1970. Two enrichments 9were made in the new media. The enrichments 
were made in 250 mL serum bottles closed with rubber stoppers. First, the enrichments were 
activated with dNMSA, adding 67 mL MOB and 33 mL media. The bottles were then flushed 
with N2 gas (Alphagaz™ 1 Nitrogen, Air Liquide) for 5 minutes. Afterwards 50 mL of 
headspace were removed and 40 and 60 mL of CH4 and O2 were injected, respectively to 
achiee a molar ratio of 40:60, respectively. The serum bottles were placed in a dark room at 
25◦C for three days on a shaking table set at approximately 100 rpm. After a week the second 
set of enrichments were made. Before taking the MOBs and putting them in the two MABRs 
their optical density (OD)at 600 nm was ranging from 0,8 and 0,9.  
2.1.2 Cultivation media 
In this study we created a synthetic anaerobic digestate to mimic an actual anaerobic 
digestate. The base of our synthetic digestate was the dAMS media, which has 25 mg NH4+-N 
/ L. After preparing the media we spiked it with NH4Cl with the goal to reach a concentration 
of 100 mg/L, which is high enough to be found at an actual anaerobic digestate, but not so 
high to inhibit the MOBs (Nyerges & Stein, 2009). Unfortunately, because of a calculation 
error we achieved a concentration of 42mg/L on average. 
 
2.1.3 Membrane Aerated Biofilm Reactor setup and operation 
The experiments were carried out with two MABRs. The reactors were made of acrylic plates 
and in between there were silicone rubber sheet gaskets to prevent leakages. Both of the 
reactors had a total volume of 280 ml. The reactor chamber had a volume of 200 ml and the 
gas side of the reactor had a volume of 80 ml. Both of the MABRs housed the same flat sheet 
membrane (Fluence Corporation, NY). The surface area of the hydrophobic, but gas 
permeable membrane was 0.01 m2.  The acrylic casings were kept together with 8mm pins 
that were tightened manually with bolts.  
In the beginning of the experiment the reactors were run in batch mode. We inoculated them 
with the MOBs we mentioned earlier. Then we added just the dAMS media, and we added 
OMPs. In the gas side of the reactors, we supplied methane and oxygen with a molar ratio of 
40:60. We used two Watson Marlow peristaltic pumps which were calibrated prior to the 
experiment. The O2 was being pumped with a flowrate of 0,6311 ml/min (0.9015 mg/min) 
and the CH4 with a flowrate of 0,4633 ml/min (0.3322 mg/min). The gas flowrates were 
adapted from Mortensen et al., 2023, who had similar setup. While in batch mode the liquor

was recirculated with the use of a peristaltic pump (Longer Pump) at a flow rate of 260 
ml/min. The batch experiment lasted for a month, until the production of biofilm was visible.  
Then we transitioned the reactors in a continuous flow through mode. The hydraulic retention 
time (HRT) of the reactors was 12 hours and the influent flowrate was 0.3 ml/min. In this 
phase of the experiment, we used the synthetic digestate we had made earlier. We made sure 
that the initial pH of the digestate was 6.8 and we also spiked it with OMPs. Specifically, the 
OMPs were added to reach 2 μg⋅L-1, as typically found in wastewaters (Verlicchi et al., 2012) 
but also within quantification limits for the analysis (Tang et al., 2021). During this phase of 
the experiment, two gas flow meters upstream and downstream to the air flow-through 
chamber were used for controlling the pressure on the gas side of the membrane and air flow 
rate, which were set to 5 kPa (gauge) and 100 mL min−1, respectively. Instead of pumping 
oxygen and methane in the gas side of the reactors, we used compressed air. In addition, 
methane was supplied to the influent bottle, so that the methane would dissolve in the 
synthetic digestate, mimicking real liquid fraction of anaerobic digestate. That was phase 1 of 
the project. In phase 2, the methane was swapped with a gas bag containing a mixture of 
methane and carbon dioxide with molar ratio of 60:40. This was done to mimic the 
atmosphere of an anaerobic digestor. Before changing the media every week, the media 
bottles were flushed with nitrogen for 10 minutes. In the last phase of the experiment (phase 
3) we connected the gasbags with the same gas mixture to two Watson Marlow pumps. The 
pumps were connected with the influent bottles in two different positions. One position was 
in the bottom of the bottle and the other was on the top at headspace. Through the pump the 
gas mixture was being moved in a continuous loop in and out of the 5L bottles and flushed 
with a diffuser, so mass transfer was enhanced. In all phases the media bottles were sealed 
with a rubber stopper so that there was  no interaction between the media and the 
environment. All of the tubes used in this experiment Norprene (Norprene®, IDEX, 
Germany) to minimize gas transfer between our system and the surrounding environment.

2.2 Analytical methods 
 
2.2.1 Nitrogen, Phosphorus and Total Organic Carbon 
To determine the ammonia, nitrogen oxides and phosphate concentrations both in the influent 
and effluent of the two reactors, we used a continuous-flow auto-analyzer (SKALAR San++, 
Netherlands). Samples were taken from both ends of the reactors. Then the samples were 
filtered through a 0.2 μm filter (Econo filter, nylon, 25 mm, 0.2 μm, Agilent Technologies, 
USA). 1 ml of the filtered sample was put in test tubes and diluted to a factor of ten with 
distilled water. For the Total Organic Carbon (TOC) (multi N/C series, analytic jena, 
Germany) analysis we took samples only from the effluent of the reactors and we followed 
the exact same procedure.  
2.2.2 Methane, Carbon Dioxide, temperature, pH, Oxygen 
The dissolved oxygen (DO) and temperature in both reactor systems were measured 
continuously with a sensor (FDO 925, WTW, Germany) which was placed in the 
recirculation line of each reactor.  The DO sensor was connected to digital multimeter (3430 
Multimeter, WTW, Germany) which was receiving the measurements every one minute. The 
pH of both influent and effluent was measured offline with a digital pH meter (FiveEasy F20, 
Mettler Toledo, USA). To determine the dissolved CH4 and CO2 we took 3 ml samples from 
influent and effluent, and we injected them in 6 mL vacuum vials (Exetainer®, UK). Then we 
added 1-2 drops of phosphoric acid to stop any bioactivity. After collecting the samples, 
nitrogen was added to equalize the pressure. The vacuum vials were stored upside down at 
5◦C until they were analyzed within the same week. All CH4 and CO2 analyses were carried 
out by gas chromatography (Trace 1310 Gas Chromatograph, Thermo Scientific) using a HP-
Plot/Q column (Length 15 m, diameter 0.32 mm, film 20 μm, Agilent Technologies, USA). 
To calibrate the machine gas mixtures of known compositions were used. The compositions 
that we used for analyzing the MABR samples were: 1) 1,02% CH4, 0,76% CO2, 2) 5% CO2, 
5%CH4, and 3) 40% CH4, 30% CO2. To check the molar ratio of the gasbags we used the 
previous gas mixtures, plus 100% CH4. The conversion from gas concentrations to dissolved 
gases was calculated using Henrys law with constants at 25◦C. 
2.2.3. Batch phase Nitrogen 
During the batch phase of the reactors, liquor was sampled to estimate the nitrogen removal 
and to check if it was time to change the media in the reactors. To do that we used Total 
Nitrogen analysis (Laton Total Nitrogen, Hach, USA). We prepared the samples according to 
the instructions of the test kit, we put the samples first in a digester (HT200S, Hach, USA) 
and then in a spectrophotometer with RFID technology (DR3900, Hach, USA).

2.2.5 Biofilm Detachment 
At the end of the experiment, to measure if the biofilm had detached, we analyzed the 
effluent of both reactors by conducting a Total Suspended Solids (TSS) analysis. The TSS 
measurement was done by using 47 mm glass fiber filters (ADVANTEC®, Toyo Roshi 
Kaisha, Ltd.) with pore size 0.5 μm. The filters were rinsed with distilled water and then they 
were placed in the oven at 105◦C for two hours. After drying in the oven, the filters were 
weighed. Then we placed the filters on a Kitasato flask with a funnel and a holder. A vacuum 
pump was attached to it. Then we filtered our samples through the filter. The filters were then 
placed in the same oven to dry again for two hours. Afterwards we took them out and 
weighed them one final time to measure the TSS. Whenever we moved the dry filters, we 
first placed them in a silica filled desiccator to avoid humidification.

During the batch experiment some samples were taken to determine the concentrations of 
nitrogen and methane in the reactor liquor. The TN analysis showed that the first reactor was 
removing nitrogen with a rate of 2,99 mg/L/day and the second one at a rate of 3,06 
mg/L/day. Also, the first reactor had a concentration of methane 41,9 mg/L and the second 
one had 27,31 mg/L. The difference makes sense because the biofilm on the second reactor 
grew faster than the biofilm on the first. The MOBs were growing faster; therefore, they were 
consuming more methane. Since the methane flowrate was 0.3322 mg/min in both reactors, 
we can calculate the methane consumption. Reactor 1 showcased a removal percentage of 
98.3% and reactor 2 a percentage of 98.9%.  
 
3.2 Continuous mode results 
 
3.2.1 Dissolved CH4 and dissolved CO2 
In phase 1 of the experiment, we supplied directly the media with methane. The methane was 
dissolved in the media and then the MOBs in the reactors were able to use it. The methane 
was supplied to the reactors at the same rate as in the batch mode. In phase 2 a gasbag with a 
mixture of gases was introduced to the system. Equilibrium was achieved and the gases 
(methane and carbon dioxide) were interacting with the media. The gas ratio was 60% 
methane to 40% carbon dioxide, mimicking the atmosphere of an anerobic digestor. Finally, 
in phase 3 pumps were connected to the bottles and they were circulating the gas mixture 
across the media. During the last two phases we collected a sample of the headspace in the 
bottles, and we analyzed it with gas chromatography. The results showed us that the 
headspace was comprised of approximately 70% methane and 30% carbon dioxide. 
According to literature, the solubility of methane in water is significantly lower than the 
solubility of methane. This can also be proven by comparing their Henrys law constants 
(Chemistry LibreTexts).  
We monitored the influent and the effluent concentrations of our gases, and we concluded 
that the average methane concentration over the duration of the continuous experiment was 
approximately 34,58 ± 3.8 mg/L in reactor 1 and 20,51 ± 3.5 mg/L in reactor 2. The average 
concentrations in the effluent were 3,19 ± 0.8 mg/L and 4,3 ± 1.2 mg/L respectively. This 
means that the first reactor consumed 90,75% of the supplied methane, with a removal rate of 
13,558 mg/day and the second reactor consumed 79% methane at a rate of 7 mg/day. Phase 3 
showed the best removal rates of all the phases. Also, the second reactor, even though in the 
batch mode was working more efficiently, lost part of its performance over time. On the other 
hand, the MOBs in the first reactor took longer time to grow but they worked better over 
time.  The influent and effluent concentrations of methane are shown in the graphs that 
follow.

The SKALAR analysis of the nutrients in the influent and effluent of the two MABRs gave 
us important information on the way the bacteria were behaving thought the duration of the 
experiment. The SKALAR data showed us the concentrations of ammonia and phosphate. 
The phosphate concentration was always higher than the detection limit. This happened in 
every sample ever taken in both of the reactors. According to Goonesekera, 2020, not every 
type of methanotrophs is good at removing phosphate. Also, type II MOBs are considered to 
perform better at low phosphate concentrations. This is because these MOBs are equipped 
with enzymes that liberate phosphorus from organic compounds (Nijman et al., 2022). 
The ammonia concentrations in the MABRs showed us that the MOBs were successfully 
removing ammonia over time. The first reactor removed approximately 12,25% of ammonia 
with a rate of 2.21 mg/day and the second reactor removed 8.29% with a rate of 1.46 mg/day. 
The nitrogen removals were not that high when compared to literature (Mortensen et al., 
2023). In the last days of the experiment the second reactor experienced an increase of 
nitrogen in the effluent. The concentrations were not extremely high, averaging out at 
approximately 4,86 % increase. This might have happened because the second biofilm 
(visually) did not grow as much as the first, and the high concentration of ammonia in the 
influent might had inhibited the operation of the MOBs with a result of accumulating 
ammonia in the reactor.  
Differentiating between nitrification-induced ammonia oxidation and ammonia assimilation 
by MOBs is critical. The term "ammonia assimilation" describes how MOBs absorb and 
incorporate ammonia into biomass, thereby eliminating it from the water. In the beginning of 
phase 1 small concentrations of nitrite and nitrate were detected, so it appears that 
nitrification was taking place concurrently with ammonia absorption because nitrite and 
nitrate were found in the reactors. The aerobic conditions kept in the MABRs, which support 
the activity of both MOBs and nitrifying bacteria, are probably the cause of this simultaneous 
process. Nitrifying bacteria may have been able to oxidize the excess ammonia in the second 
reactor due to the concentration of ammonia there and the less successful biofilm growth. 
This would have led to detectable quantities of nitrite and nitrate in the effluent.

3.2.5 Oxygen 
During the experiment, the oxygen inside both systems was continuously measured. In the 
batch phase reactor 1 stabilized at an oxygen concentration of 1,8 mg/L and reactor 2 at 1,63 
mg/L approximately. Continuing the experiment and entering phase 1 of the continuous mode 
the DO concentration starts to decrease. By the time we reached phase 2 the DO 
concentration had reached and stabilized at the concentration of 0.45 ± 0.03 mg/L in the first 
reactor and 0.4 ± 0.03 mg/L in the second. 
To calculate the OTE we first calculated the OLR and then the OTR. The OLR equals 4320 
gCOD/m2/day. For the calculation of the OTR we needed to also find the yield, 0.25. 
gCOD/gCH4 (Goonesekera et al., 2022). Then by using the equations we mentioned in the 
previous chapter, we calculated the OTR for the two reactors. In the first reactor the OTR was 
5 gCOD/m2/day and in the second one was 2 gCOD/m2/day. The efficiency in the first reactor 
was 0,12% and on the second reactor 0,06%. These percentages indicate we feed too much 
oxygen through the gas phase, so the efficiency of the reactor is low. Even if they were 
removing all the influent pollutants, our experiment would still show low OTE. Intermittent 
aeration could be used to increase OTE. So, less methane and ammonia are being removed by 
the bacteria.  
3.2.6 TSS some small detachment from the biofilm. 
To determine the detachment of the MOBs from the biofilm, samples of the effluent were 
taken in the last days of the experiment. These samples were used for TSS analysis. The first 
reactor had a TSS concentration of 50.25 mg/day and the second one had 43,75 mg/day. 
These concentrations are relatively low and they indicate that there was not great detachment 
from the biofilm.  
3.2.7 Organic Micropollutant Degradation 
The biodegradability that we expected by the methanotrophs, was assumed to be similar to 
the biodegradability found for AOB. This assumption was because AMO and pMMO share 
many similarities. and the overlapping secondary substrate range of AMO compared to both 
pMMO and sMMO (Su, et al., 2021).  
For some OMPs the performance of the MABRs was inconclusive. The removal rates that we 
calculated were negative. But, out of the twelve OMPS that were tested, sulfamethaxole, 1H-

percentage of sulfamethaxole ranged between 62% and 85% in the first reactor, and between 
75% and 78% in the second one. 1H- benztriazoles removal ranged between 54,5% and 58%

3.2.8.1 Degradation of Atenolol 
 
While researching biological treatment methods, like membrane bioreactors (MBR) and 
submerged attached biofilters (SABF), we found literature that shows atenolol removal 
efficiency of up to 95% when operating under ideal circumstances (Rezaei et al., 2022). 
However, depending on the circumstances and the microbial communities present, atenolol 
removal in conventional wastewater treatment plants fluctuates between 40% and 70% (Falås 
et al., 2012; Gabet-Giraud et al., 2010). According to Elad et al., 2023, heterotrophic bacteria 
played a crucial role in removing atenolol from his MABRs. In some cases, they even 
measured a total removal of this OMP, and also, they concluded that intermittent aeration can 
increase the removal of atenolol. 
 
There are many factors that can affect the efficacy of atenolol removal. Most prominent 
factors are the hydraulic retention time (HRT), sludge retention time (SRT), and the presence 
of nitrifying bacteria. Higher clearance rates are typically attained by systems with longer 
HRTs and tailored microbial populations (Gabet-Giraud et al., 2010). 
 
In our experiment the atenolol removal efficiencies (57% to 68.5%) of the first reactor are not 
as high as those reported in optimized biological systems, but they are in line with the top 
range of what is generally observed in conventional wastewater treatment plants. This implies 
that the reactor sustains the right microbial community and sufficient oxygen supply to 
promote atenolol breakdown. The lower removal rates (27% to 40.4%) observed in the 
second reactor point to possible problems such as inadequate oxygen transfer, poor microbial 
activity, or unstable operations. These elements may prevent atenolol from degrading as 
effectively. 
 
3.8.2.2 Degradation of Sulfamethoxazole 
 
According to Elad et al., 2023 in their experiment they measured partial degradation of 
sulfamethoxazole. The removal of this OMP was unaffected by their intermittent aeration 
strategy. As stated earlier the heterotrophic bacteria played again a crucial role in the 
removal. In other sources, ammonia-oxidizing bacteria (AOB) were able to enhance the 
degradation of sulfamethoxazole in nitrifying activated sludge systems. Their removal rates 
frequently were up to 90%. The breakdown of this OMP is accelerated by the presence of 
ammonia and the activity of AOB. Through the action of ammonia monooxygenase (AMO), 
AOB can breakdown a variety of organic contaminants, including sulfamethoxazole 
(Kassotaki et al., 2016). In addition, hydraulic retention time (HRT) and the particular 
microbial community structure will affect the removal of sulfamethoxazole. Longer HRTs 
can lead to increased removal rates. 
In both of our reactors the removal efficiencies fall within the usual range described in the 
literature. The first reactor showed a larger range of removals and this could be a sign of 
operational unpredictability or variations in our microbial community. The reliable 
performance of the second reactor points to stable operating conditions that promote efficient 
sulfamethoxazole decomposition. 
 
 
 
 
3.8.2.3 Degradation of 1H-Benzotriazole 
==PAGE:24==

According to studies, 1H-benzotriazole removal efficiency in a variety of biological treatment 
systems, such as hybrid moving bed biofilm reactors (MBBR) and sequencing batch reactors 
(SBR), range from 50% to 90% (Struk-Sokołowska et al.). According to the same study, the 
removal efficiency in SBR systems demonstrated that 1H-benzotriazole can be efficiently 
degraded under aerobic conditions, with efficiencies over 80%. This was achieved at effluent 
concentrations not exceeding 200 μg/L. In addition, it is worth to note that since ammonia-
oxidizing bacteria (AOB) aid in the breakdown of 1H-benzotriazole, nitrifying conditions 
greatly accelerate this compound's breakdown (Trejo-Castillo R et al., 2021). 
 
In the project of Mortensen et al., 2023 the biodegradation of 1H-benzotriazole and 
sulfamethoxazole by methanotrophs was demonstrated by the hMBfR. The removal of these 
OMPs showcase that good correlations between CH4 oxidation rates and these specific 
compounds exist.   
 
Particularly for systems with increased nitrification, the removal rates found in our study are 
in good agreement with those documented in the literature. The more reliable performance of 
the first reactor points to stable operating conditions that support efficient degradation of 1H-
benzotriazole. Performance variations in the second reactor could have happened because 
there were differences in the microbial composition, oxygen transfer efficiency, or biofilm 
growth. Also, the removal efficiency can be greatly increased by ensuring that the ideal 
circumstances for MOB activity are met, such as sufficient oxygen supply and suitable 
ammonia concentrations. 
 
3.9 Future work 
 
Design and implement more efficient reactor configurations that maximize surface area for 
biofilm growth and optimize the flow. Enhanced reactor designs can lead to better contact 
between wastewater and microbial communities, improving treatment efficiency 
 
Conduct comprehensive microbial community analysis using advanced sequencing 
techniques to identify the specific strains of methanotrophs and nitrifying bacteria present in 
the biofilm. Understanding the microbial diversity and their specific roles can help in 
optimizing reactor conditions for enhanced OMP degradation and nutrient removal. 
 
Explore new methods to enhance oxygen transfer efficiency in MABRs, such as the use of 
advanced membrane materials. Improved oxygen availability could boost microbial activity 
and overall reactor performance. 
 
 
4 Conclusion 
 
This study explored the efficacy of methanotrophs in membrane aerated biofilm reactors 
(MABRs) for the removal of organic micropollutants (OMPs), nitrogen, and phosphorus from 
synthetic anaerobic digestate. The experiment was conducted in two identical MABRs, 
focusing on the removal efficiencies of sulfamethoxazole, 1H-benzotriazole, and atenolol, 
alongside the analysis of ammonia and carbon dioxide dynamics. 
==PAGE:25==
Master Thesis                                                                                                                          
Theodoros Theodoridis 
 
25 
 
The results demonstrated significant removal rates for the selected OMPs. Sulfamethoxazole 
exhibited removal efficiencies ranging between 62% and 85% in Reactor 1 and between 75% 
and 78% in Reactor 2. These rates are consistent with previous findings, suggesting that 
methanotrophs effectively contribute to the degradation process. The presence of ammonia 
and the activity of MOBs were crucial in achieving these high removal rates. In addition, 1H-
benzotriazole removal efficiencies varied from 54.5% to 58% in Reactor 1 and from 41% to 
61% in Reactor 2. These results align with literature indicating that nitrifying conditions can 
enhance the breakdown of this compound, with the help of MOBs. The differences in 
removal rates between the reactors could be attributed to variations in microbial community 
structure, biofilm thickness, and oxygen transfer efficiency. Furthermore, atenolol removal 
was observed to be between 57% and 68.5% in Reactor 1 and between 27% and 40.4% in 
Reactor 2. These findings are consistent with the upper range of removal efficiencies reported 
in conventional wastewater treatment plants. The lower removal rates in Reactor 2 may be 
due to inadequate oxygen transfer, poor microbial activity, or operational instabilities.  
The ammonia removal analysis revealed that the first reactor removed approximately 12.25% 
of ammonia at a rate of 2.21 mg/day, while the second reactor removed 8.29% at a rate of 
1.46 mg/day. The detection of nitrite and nitrate indicated that both ammonia assimilation by 
methanotrophs and nitrification processes were occurring simultaneously. The relatively 
lower nitrogen removal rates, compared to literature, suggest that further optimization is 
needed. 
The carbon dioxide results showed unexpected reduction rates, possibly due to anaerobic 
methane oxidation or the escape of CO2 through the gas phase. This highlights the complexity 
of microbial interactions within the biofilm and the necessity for further investigation into the 
specific pathways and microorganisms involved. In addition, the Total Organic Carbon 
(TOC) and Total Nitrogen (TN) analyses indicated stable nitrogen concentrations in the 
effluent, with TOC levels significantly lower than TN. This disparity shows the efficiency of 
methane removal by the methanotrophic bacteria, despite the excess nitrogen in the system. 
Overall, this study confirms the potential of MABRs utilizing methanotrophs for the 
biodegradation of OMPs and nutrient removal in wastewater treatment. The findings suggest 
that optimizing microbial community structure, oxygen transfer, and operational parameters 
can significantly enhance the performance of such systems. Future research should focus on 
identifying specific microbial strains and their interactions, as well as refining reactor 
configurations to improve overall treatment efficiency.
