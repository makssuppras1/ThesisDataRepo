Abstract
For a green future as expected, suggestions are presented to cope with the
intermittency caused by the seasonality of renewable energies. Thus, different
ways of storing renewable energy as storing hydrogen, methane, ammonia and
the implementation of the so-called "biomass-battery" are analysed, focusing on
the cost and overall efficiency of these processes. The objective is to find out the
best way to store a large energy demand of 50 GW in four summer months, using
the surplus energy, and use it to cover four winter months.
A techno-economic assessment is carried out over a time horizon of 20 years,
starting in 2030. As a basis for the analysis, the equipment required for each of
the routes, with their corresponding efficiencies and costs, are investigated.
All processes aim to move from electricity or biomass to electricity again. In
the cases of hydrogen, methane and ammonia, electricity is converted to hydro-
gen by electrolysis cells. Once the hydrogen is obtained, the corresponding fuel
is produced and stored, which in the case of hydrogen is stored directly in un-
derground storage. In the case of methane, it is produced from hydrogen and
carbon dioxide in a catalytic process and then stored in an underground storage.
Ammonia is then produced by combining hydrogen and nitrogen in the presence
of a suitable catalyst and stored in tanks at -33 °C. In the case of the biomass
battery, the biomass is fed into an anaerobic digester to produce biogas, which is
then upgraded to biomethane and stored in an underground storage. Finally, all
the products obtained, hydrogen, ammonia and methane, are used as fuels in fuel
cells to produce electricity.
The levelized cost of storage is calculated for all the above-mentioned path-
ways without taking into account the costs of electricity and biomass, as these
are very country-specific.
It is concluded firstly that all technologies analysed from a generic concept as
in this work, are competitive and must be placed in a specific context to analyse
them in more detail. Then, if only this analysis is considered, the most cost-
effective technology is the biomass battery including the solid oxide fuel cell, with
a levelized cost of 0,27 e/kWh and an overall efficiency of 38%.

Introduction
2.1
Motivation
One of society’s current aspirations is to run entirely on renewable energy
and to be carbon neutral. Running entirely on renewable energy is important
for creating a more sustainable and resilient future, while also addressing some of
the most pressing environmental, social, and economic challenges facing the world
today. There are several reasons why this is so important and some of the more
relevant are environmental, economic and public health benefits. The burning
of fossil fuels is associated with a range of public health problems, including
respiratory illnesses, heart disease, and premature death. There are also economic
opportunities as this is a new industry with a lot of potential to create new jobs
and economic improvements, particularly in regions with abundant renewable
energy resources.
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), in 2020, renewable en-
ergy (including hydropower, wind, solar, geothermal, and bioenergy) accounted
for 29% of the world’s electricity production. This is a significant increase from
21% in 2014 [2]. Still, renewable electricity needs to expand faster to reach the
milestones in the Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario, where the renewable share
of generation needs to be more than 60% by 2030. Annual generation has to in-
crease at an average rate of over 12% during 2022-2030, which is twice the average
of 2019-2021 [2].
Europe installed 19 GW of new wind capacity in 2022, this is 4% more than
in 2021. The EU-27 installed 16 GW, 40% more than in 2021, but still below
what the EU should build to meet its 2030 climate and energy targets [3]. Europe
is expected to install 129 GW of new wind farms during the period 2023-2027,
with the EU-27 installing 98 GW of them [3]. The EU is expected to build 20
GW of new wind farms per year during the period 2023-2027 [3]. The EU would
need to build more than 30 GW per year of new wind power on average to meet
its 2030 targets [3].

2.2
Background
There are two crucial concepts that are going to be explain to get the needed
context for this project. The first one is the importance of energy carriers for the
future and the second one is the importance of long duration storage in a world
intended to be run on renewable energies [5].
An energy carrier is a substance or form of energy that enables the storage,
transport, and distribution of energy from its source to where it is needed for
consumption or use. Energy carriers play a crucial role in our energy systems
as they facilitate the conversion, distribution, and utilization of different forms
of energy [5]. They enable the transport of energy over long distances, making
it possible to utilize energy sources that may be geographically distant from the
end users. This is especially important for renewable energy sources, which are
often located in specific areas (e.g., solar power in sunny regions or wind power
in windy areas) [5].
Overall, energy carriers can take various forms, including electricity, hydrogen,
natural gas, liquid fuels (such as gasoline or diesel), and even biomass. The choice
of energy carrier depends on factors such as the type of energy source, end-use
application, storage requirements, infrastructure availability, and environmental
considerations [5].
Long-duration energy storage is important in achieving a more sustainable and
resilient energy system. There are several aspect that these type of technologies
would help to overcome. One important aspect is the intermittency of renewable
energy sources. Renewable energy sources such as solar and wind are intermittent,
which means that they do not provide a constant supply of energy [30]. Long-
duration energy storage can help to mitigate the intermittency of these sources
by storing excess energy produced during times of high supply and releasing it
during times of low supply [30]. Also, increasing energy security and resilience by
providing a reliable source of energy during times of high demand, grid instability,
or disruptions in the energy supply chain. In addition, seasonal energy storage can
help to lower energy costs by reducing the need for expensive peak power plants
and enabling energy arbitrage. This is a practice that allows to take advantage
of the price difference between two different seasons. It would imply the use of
storage to charge energy at a low price in summer, when the offer is high and
discharge in winter when the demand and the price is higher [4].
Furthermore, long duration storage technologies are key for supporting the
growth of the renewable energy industry and reducing the greenhouse gas emis-

sions, by enabling a greater integration of renewable sources into the energy sys-
tem, providing a more stable and predictable source of energy and reducing re-

There are several large scale storage technologies and each of them present
different advantages and disadvantages. It is important to take into considera-
tion the specific application and requirements to understand which one is more
suitable. Below are some of the methods to store energy on a large scale.
• Underground storage: Both methane and hydrogen can be storage in un-
derground reservoirs. Underground storage offer benefits such as high stor-
age capacity, long duration storage, and relatively low energy losses during
storage. These facilities can be operated in a flexible manner, allowing for
injection and withdrawal according to demand fluctuations. This method
of storage is currently the most cost-effective and widely used large-scale
storage technology [7].
• Hydrogen tanks: As to compressed hydrogen storage, large-scale hydrogen
storage tanks can be used to store hydrogen at high pressures. These tanks
can be used to store hydrogen for industrial or transportation applications,
but they require a significant amount of space and can be expensive [7].
• Power-to-gas: Power-to-gas technology converts excess renewable energy,
such as wind or solar power, into hydrogen or methane gas through elec-
trolysis. The gas can then be stored and used as a fuel source for different
applications. Power-to-gas technology can help to balance the intermittency
of renewable energy sources and provide a means of large-scale hydrogen
storage [8].
• Power-to-liquid: Hydrogen can be stored as ammonia, which has a high
energy density and can be easily transported. The hydrogen can be extracted
from the ammonia when needed and used for industrial or transportation
applications [9].

3.1
Hydrogen
Hydrogen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas, it is non-toxic and highly
combustible. Under ordinary conditions, hydrogen gas is an aggregation of di-
atomic molecules H2. Hydrogen name derived from Greek words meaning "maker
of water" as it burns with oxygen to form water. Even though, it makes up only
about 0.14% on the Earth’s crust by weight, it represents about 75% of all matter
in the universe. It has a boiling point of -252.77°C and a density of 0.0837 kg/m3
under atmospheric conditions [11].

3.1.2
Uses of Hydrogen
Currently, hydrogen is mainly used in oil refining, ammonia production, methanol
production and steel production. These industries rely heavily on fossil fuels for
hydrogen production. The future growth of hydrogen demand in these indus-
tries will depend on the development of demand for downstream products such
as refined fuels, fertilizers and building materials.
With demand for ammonia and methanol expected to grow in the short to
medium term, the expansion of new production capacity presents an opportunity
to expand low-emission hydrogen pathways.
In the long term, there is great potential for increased demand for hydrogen
in the steel and high temperature heat sectors. Overcoming technical challenges
and reducing costs are major obstacles. While it may be technically possible to
produce all primary steel with hydrogen, it would require large amounts of low-
carbon electricity, equivalent to about 10% of the world’s electricity production.
This also depends on cheap electricity prices and political support.

3.2.4
Comparison of the water electrolysis technologies
Input
For all three electrolysis technologies, the input is electricity and water. For
SOEC, PEMEC and AEC a highwater purity is desired. The water quality can
be determined by the conductivity of the water used, the conductivity should be
in the order of 1 µS/cm, 0.2 µS/cm and 5 µS/cm, respectively [27]. SOEC uses
steam rather than water and thus is a consumer of heat, rather than a producer
of heat. If the hydrogen produced is to be used for synthetic fuel, then the waste
heat of these synthesis processes (e.g. Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, Haber-Bosch
process) could be used to produce steam for further use in SOEC electrolysis [27].
Output
For all three electrolysis technologies, the output is hydrogen, oxygen and
excess heat from the process.
For SOEC, the recoverable excess heat will be
used to produce steam that can then be used as input for further use in SOEC
electrolysis [27]. Oxygen is a by-product, which can be used in various industries,
such as paper and pulp production, glass manufacturing, water oxygenation, fish
farming, steel and metal industry, medical care industry, food, manufacturing, oxy
fuel Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS), thermal gasification, and many more.
The excess heat may be used for district heating in case of PEMEC and AEC
[27].

3.3
Fuel Cells
A fuel cell uses the chemical energy of hydrogen or other fuels to cleanly
and efficiently produce electricity. If hydrogen is the fuel, the only products are
electricity, water, and heat [12]. Fuel cells are unique in terms of the variety of
their potential applications; they can use a wide range of fuels and feedstocks and
can provide power for systems as large as a utility power station and as small as
a laptop computer [12].
Fuel cells work like batteries, but they do not run down or need recharging.
They produce electricity and heat as long as fuel is supplied. A fuel cell consists
of two electrodes, a negative electrode (or anode) and a positive electrode (or
cathode) [12]. A fuel, such as hydrogen, is fed to the anode, and air is fed to the
cathode [12]. In a hydrogen fuel cell, a catalyst at the anode separates hydrogen
molecules into protons and electrons, which take different paths to the cathode
[12]. The electrons go through an external circuit, creating a flow of electricity.
The protons migrate through the electrolyte to the cathode, where they unite
with oxygen and the electrons to produce water and heat [12].
Fuel cells are highly efficient, typically reaching fuel to electricity efficiency
of 60 percent [13]. Fuel cells also generate heat which, if captured, can increase
overall energy efficiency to more than 90 percent [12]. The heat produced by
fuel cells can generate additional electricity through a turbine, provide heating
directly to nearby buildings or facilities, and even cooling with the addition of an
absorption chiller [13].
Fuel cells are classified primarily by the kind of electrolyte they employ. This
classification determines the kind of electro-chemical reactions that take place in
the cell, the kind of catalysts required, the temperature range in which the cell
operates, the fuel required, and other factors. These characteristics, in turn, affect
the applications for which these cells are most suitable [14].
3.3.1
Polymer electrolyte membrane
Polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) fuel cells also called proton exchange
membrane fuel cells deliver high power density and offer the advantages of low
weight and volume compared with other fuel cells [14]. PEM fuel cells use a solid
polymer as an electrolyte and porous carbon electrodes containing a platinum
or platinum alloy catalyst. They need only hydrogen, oxygen from the air, and
water to operate. They are typically fueled with pure hydrogen supplied from
storage tanks or reformers [14]. PEM fuel cells operate at relatively low tempera-
11
==PAGE:21==
Master Thesis
tures, around 80 °C. Low-temperature operation allows them to start quickly (less
warm-up time) and results in less wear on system components, resulting in better
durability. However, it requires that a noble metal catalyst (typically platinum)
be used to separate the hydrogen’s electrons and protons, adding to system cost.
The platinum catalyst is also extremely sensitive to carbon monoxide poisoning,
making it necessary to employ an additional reactor to reduce carbon monoxide
in the fuel gas if the hydrogen is derived from a hydrocarbon fuel. This reactor
also adds cost [14].
3.3.2
Solid oxide fuel cells
Solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs) use a hard, non-porous ceramic compound
as the electrolyte. SOFCs are around 60% efficient at converting fuel to elec-
tricity. In applications designed to capture and utilize the system’s waste heat
(co-generation), overall fuel use efficiencies could top 85%. SOFCs operate at very
high temperatures as high as 1,000°C [14]. High-temperature operation removes
the need for precious-metal catalyst, thereby reducing cost. It also allows SOFCs
to reform fuels internally, which enables the use of a variety of fuels and reduces
the cost associated with adding a reformer to the system [14]. SOFCs are also the
most sulfur-resistant fuel cell type, they can tolerate several orders of magnitude
more sulfur than other cell types can. In addition, they are not poisoned by car-
bon monoxide, which can even be used as fuel. This property allows SOFCs to
use natural gas, biogas, and gases made from coal[14]. High-temperature opera-
tion has disadvantages, it results in a slow startup and requires significant thermal
shielding to retain heat and protect personnel, which may be acceptable for utility
applications but not for transportation [14].
Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFC) is the most common types of fuel cells for
stationary applications. In the other hand, Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM)
Fuel Cells are mainly used in vehicles [15].
The primary fuels that can be directly utilized within fuel cell stacks today
include natural gas, biogas, and hydrogen depending upon the fuel cell type [16].
PEMFC operate most effectively on high-purity hydrogen. While SOFCs can in
principle operate on hydrogen, commercial systems are designed to operate on
natural gas (due to the wide availability) with reformation internal to the fuel cell
given the availability of high quality heat [16]. As society reduces its dependency
on natural gas, all fuel cell types are available to operate directly on zero-carbon
renewable hydrogen [16].

Methane is an odorless, colorless, flammable gas, it is abundant in the nature
and is product of certain human activities [23]. Methane is lighter than air with
a relative density of 0.554 compare with air. It burns with air, forming carbon

Operation range
The operation range of a green ammonia plant can be divided into:
• Shut down - Cold standby is when the plant is shut down and cooled to
ambient temperature. Cold shut down should generally only be used for
maintenance, because the cooling and reheating could cause catalyst crunch-
ing and this will reduce the catalyst lifetime [27].
• Hot standby is an operation mode where there is no production but almost
all units are kept at normal operation condition (i.e. at normal operation
temperature and pressure) to enable a fast ramp up in capacity [27].
• 30-100% operation. A conventional ammonia plant usually has an operating
capacity of 70-100%. However, these plants can normally handle loads down
to 30% without mayor changes [27].
• 20-35% operation, this can usually be achieved by additional CAPEX for
equipment that can handle larger capacity ranges [27].
• 0 - 20% operation, for operation at lower capacity than 20% a significant
increase in CAPEX can be expected [27].
Depending on the requirements of the fuel different scenarios of operation
flexibility should be utilized. For this specific analysis a design NH3 plant with
large operation range and additional NH3 storage would be considered, to take
advantage of the periods of low price of power.

3.6
Biomass-battery
Biomass refers to organic matter, such as plants, wood, agricultural waste,
and other organic materials, that can be used as a source of energy. Biomass is
a renewable energy source because the organic matter used to produce it can be
regrown or replenished. Biomass contains energy first derived from the sun. The
energy from these organisms can be transformed into usable energy through direct
and indirect means. Biomass can be burned to create heat (direct), converted into
electricity (direct), or processed into biofuel (indirect).
3.6.1
Biogas production
Biogas originates from biogenic material and is a type of biofuels, typically
referred to a gas produced by bacteria fermentation of organic material under
anaerobic condition (in the absence of oxygen) [25]. Biogas is about 20% lighter
than air and has an ignition temperature in the range of 50°C to 750°C [25]. It is
odorless and colorless gas that burns with clear blue flame. The calorific value of
1m3 is about 22 MJ if burns with 57% efficiency. The composition of gas varies
with raw material used [25].
Biogas can be produced from biomass through different processes, like pyrol-
ysis, gasification, etc, in this specific analysis a process called anaerobic digestion
is selected. Anaerobic digestion is a simple technology widely use for processing
the biodegradable, organic waste for the biogas production. Animal manure (cow
dung) is used as inoculum, pretreatment of substrate [25]. The thermophilic con-
ditions improved the biogas yield by approximately 92%. The fermentation of

organic waste involves biological and chemical and the process is equally benefi-
cial in waste management. During the process, large organic polymers that make
up biomass are broken down into smaller molecules by chemicals and microor-
ganisms [25]. Upon completion of the anaerobic digestion process, the biomass is
converted into biogas, (methane, carbon dioxide and traces of other contaminant
gases), as well as liquid digestate (nutrient rich fertilizer) [25].
Here are the fundamental steps involved in producing biogas from biomass
through anaerobic digestion:
• Feedstock preparation: The first step in producing biogas is to prepare the
feedstock. The feedstock can include a variety of organic materials, such as
agricultural waste, food waste, and sewage sludge. The feedstock is typi-
cally ground or shredded to increase its surface area and make it easier for
microorganisms to digest.
• Anaerobic digestion: The prepared feedstock is then added to an anaerobic
digester, which is a sealed container where the anaerobic digestion process
takes place. The digester is typically heated to maintain a constant tem-
perature of around 35-40 °C, which is optimal for the microorganisms that
break down the organic matter [25]. As the organic matter is broken down,
biogas is produced as a byproduct. Biogas is typically composed of methane
(50-75%) and carbon dioxide (25-50%), along with trace amounts of other
gases like hydrogen sulfide and nitrogen [25].
• Biogas collection: The biogas is collected from the anaerobic digester and
purified to remove impurities like hydrogen sulfide and moisture. The purified
biogas can then be used as a source of renewable energy.
• Digestate management: The leftover material after the anaerobic digestion
process is called digestate. Digestate is rich in nutrients and can be used as
a fertilizer for crops.
Biogas can be converted into biomethane through a purification process at
an upgrading facility. Biomethane has similar properties to conventional natu-
ral gas [27]. The input for upgrading facilities is raw biogas from an anaerobic
digester, which typically contains 50-75% methane (CH4) and 25-50% carbon
dioxide (CO2), plus a minor content of hydrogen (H), nitrogen (N), oxygen (O),
hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and ammonia (NH3) [27]. The composition of the biogas
varies based on the specific mix of the input. During upgrading a stream of CO2

5.2
Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis is a technique used to evaluate the impact of changes in
input variables on the output or outcome of a model or system. This helps to
37
==PAGE:47==
Master Thesis
understand the sensitivity of the output to variations in the input parameters and
provides insight into the robustness and reliability of the model.
One-factor-at-a-time sensitivity analysis (OFAT) is going to be used in this
case as it is a simple and commonly used method to assess the sensitivity of a
model to variations in one input variable at a time while holding other variables
constant. This implies systematically changing the value of a single input parame-
ter, leaving all other parameters unchanged, and observing the resulting variation
in output.
5.2.1
Technology specific sensitivity analysis
LCOS is calculated using several input parameters. In this section, the impact
on the LCOS of some input parameters is analyzed. The main input variables
that affect the output or outcome of the model have been identify as the CAPEX,
OPEX, WACC and the fuel cell and electrolyzer efficiencies.
In the different
sensitivity analysis the variables are varied by ± 20 % for each case.
A change in CAPEX has a strong influence on LCOS because its share is high,
more than 60% for all the cases. An analysis of CAPEX allows to understand the
sensitivity of the cost to changes in capital expenses. It is important to analyse this
effect in order to be able to make different decisions mainly related to investment.
As this is a general analysis, many aspects and costs of the initial investment
have their origin in the specific business case, e.g. land costs, logistics associated
with equipment, etc. At the same time, many of the equipment involved in this
analysis are under development or maturing, there is uncertainty associated with
the technologies and their potential.
Decision-makers can evaluate the financial impact of various CAPEX assump-
tions and assess the resilience of investment projects under different cost scenarios.
It is therefore important to see to what extent the overall analysis would be af-
fected if these costs are changed.

Discussion
As mentioned above, although the levelized cost of storage differ, they might
not sufficiently different to make a decision based simply on this aspect. This in
turn is very important for any analysis, because even though the numbers express
something, it is necessary to analyse the environment in which each project will
be carried out. The same project can have very diverse outcomes if it is executed
in different countries with different cultures and environments.
For this analysis, the aim is to make a general calculation and to specifically
compare costs and technologies.
For this particular case, taking into account
the overall efficiency and the calculated costs, it could be said that the best way
to store energy and cover the seasonal demand, in terms of cost, would be the
biomass battery. This is because it is the least expensive way to store energy,
with a LCOS of 0,30 e/kWh for the PEM case and 0,27 e/kWh for the SOC
case with and efficiency of 25% and 38% respectively. In addition, is one of the
technologies that is least affected by variations in the fuel costs. However, it is
a technology that is highly dependent on the availability of biomass, i.e. if this
project is to be implemented in a context where biomass is not sufficient, it would
have to include extra costs for importing this fuel, taking into account that not
all types of biomass are transportable across borders.
Some countries with a large supply of biomass such as the United States,
China or Brazil due to their large areas of agricultural and forestry land, as well
as a developed infrastructure for biomass production and processing, could ben-
efit from projects such as the current ones. In 2020, domestic supply of biomass
is 57,5 EJ globally. 86% of the domestic supply is from solid biomass sources
including wood chips, wood pellets and traditional biomass sources. Liquid bio-
fuels accounted for 7%, municipal and industrial waste sectors accounted for 2 -
3% followed by biogas at 2% [40]. Europe accounted for 64% of all energy from
municipal waste. Europe is also the leader in biogas supply accounting for more
than half of the global biogas supply. Americas (mainly USA and Brazil) had sig-
nificant supply of liquid biofuels accounting for 70% of the global supply [40]. At
the same time, it is important to mention that these sites have the advantage that
existing infrastructure can be used to further reduce costs. In the following graph
it is possible to see how this project would be relevant for regions as America and
Asia, but not for Oceania.

6
Conclusion
In conclusion, the choice of energy storage and conversion technology depends
on various factors such as overall efficiency, cost, resource availability, existing
infrastructure, and specific local considerations. While the levelised cost of storage
is an important factor, it is not the sole determinant in decision-making, and the
broader context should be taken into account.
Based on the analysis conducted, it is determined that the biomass battery is
the most cost-effective way to store energy and meet seasonal demand. It has the
lowest levelized cost of storage (LCOS) at 0.27 e/kWh for the SOC case, with an
efficiency of 38%. However, its feasibility depends on the availability of biomass
resources and the potential costs associated with importing biomass.
On the other hand, when considering overall efficiency, the analysis suggests
that the best scenario is the use of green hydrogen with SOC technology. Although
it has a higher cost of 0.48 e/kWh, it boasts an efficiency of 47%, the highest
among all the technologies studied. Green hydrogen has a high energy density and
can be used in various sectors, such as heavy transport, industry, fuel cells, and
heating in residential buildings. In addition, hydrogen offers potential applications
in various fields due to its high energy density and versatility.
This offers a
promising option for energy storage, especially in energy-intensive applications
and as a raw material in the chemical industry. Global demand for hydrogen is
expected to grow, potentially leading to cost savings and economies of scale.
Other energy storage options such as green ammonia is found to be more ex-
pensive than other routes, and its lower energy density and larger storage volumes
limit its viability as an energy carrier. However, regions with significant ammo-
nia production, such as East Asia and Europe, may find value in subdividing
ammonia production for various applications and energy generation.
Lastly, methane is considered less efficient than hydrogen as an energy carrier,
but its well-established infrastructure for natural gas makes it easier and more
cost-effective to integrate into the energy system. Methane can be converted into
hydrogen or syngas, providing flexibility to adapt to changing energy demands.
Regions with high natural gas consumption, like the US, are suggested as suitable
for methane-based energy storage projects.
Overall, each energy carrier has its own strengths and weaknesses, and the
choice depends on the specific needs and characteristics of the project. When
evaluating different energy storage options, it is important to consider factors
such as cost, efficiency, infrastructure, resource availability, and safety.
