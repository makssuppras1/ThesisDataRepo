Abstract
The following thesis addresses the design of a fault detection, isolation, and reconВӯ
figuration (FDIR)Вӯsystem to detect and mitigate valveВӯmalfunction in a simulated GEA
MultiВӯstage Spray Dryer. During the operation of the spray drying system, undesired
oscillations were observed by GEA on numerous occasions. The root cause is a valve
malfunction in one of the spray dryerвҖҷs hot air supplies. By completing an exhaustive
survey of current literature, the nature of the malfunction is revealed to most likely be
due to a stiction phenomenon. It causes oscillations due to excessive friction within the
control valve which is sought to be detected and mitigated. The final detection system
consists of a Kalman filter which produces a fault signal used for oscillation detection. If
oscillations are detected, the magnitude of stiction is estimated using the span of the OP
signal to calculate the stiction parameter S. Should S be above a certain threshold, a
compensator is activated to obtain better closedВӯloop performance. The above system
proved to obtain decent performance. The detection system made it possible to detect
stiction down to a magnitude of S=0.3%. The estimation system proved to be accurate
down to an SВӯvalue of 0.5 with the highest relative error being 3%. The compensator
performs close to nominal operation during stiction. During nominal operation an IAE
of 760.6 is achieved while an IAEВӯvalue of 772.84 is obtained during compensation for
heavy stiction. The final FDIR system proved to be robust to falseВӯpositives after miВӯ
nor adjustments, and compensates for heavy stiction (S=5) within 1 hour of simulated
stiction. In conclusion, a working FDIR system has successfully been implemented in a
simulated spray dryer.

Introduction
Spray drying is a widely used unit operation in the chemical industry. It dries liquid soВӯ
lutions into powders which produces a lighter and more manageable product.[1] The
process is applied in the production of pharmaceuticals, paints, ceramics, and fertilizВӯ
ers.[2][3][4] It is, however, mostly used in the food industry for the drying of food products
such as dairy.[5][2][6]
A typical current generation spray drying process (Figure 1.1) starts at the spray nozzle
located at the top of a heating chamber. The nozzle disperses the liquid feed into the
heating chamber: here the liquid is dried to a powder by a heating gas. The gas flows
coВӯcurrently with the droplets from the top of the chamber down to a static fluidized bed
at the bottom. Here the powder is collected, and the fluidized bed dries the powder
further using a second heating gas which enters from the bottom of the fluidized bed.
The powder is then heated until it exits the spray dryer by an overflow mechanism.
The heating gases exits at the top of the spray drying unit. Some spray drying units
are expanded with several vibrating fluidВӯbeds following the static fluidВӯbed, where the
drying is finalized.[1]
Figure 1.1: Simplified illustration of spray drying system. 1: Feed stream into nozzle, 2:
Heating air, 3: Static fluidВӯbed, 4: Heating chamber, 5: Exhaust, 6: Product stream
The production capacity of such a spray dryer can vary from 1 kg/h to 10 ton/h depending
on its size. Some of the largest spray dryers found in the dairy industry produce milk
powder at a rate of 7.5 ton/h with an energy expenditure of 8 MW.[1] Considering skim

milk powderвҖҷs current market value of 2,889 USD/ton[7], a large spray dryer produces
products worth 21,667 USD/h. Having a spray drying unit with such a large production,
a single fault can lead to huge economic losses. A fault may lead to poor performance
or an eventual shut down of the entire production.
1.1
The GEA case
Several fault scenarios have been observed during the operation of a GEA Niro MultiВӯ
stage Spray Dryer size 20. The spray dryer is a fourВӯstage spray dryer system with two
vibrating fluidВӯbeds following the static fluidВӯbed. The system is controlled by a model

1.2
The Stiction Phenomena
Stiction is caused by increased static friction inside a control valve. This makes it necВӯ
essary to apply more external force to the valve when transitioning from a stationary
state to a moving state. When the static friction is overcome, potential energy is reВӯ
leased causing a jump in valve position. This is known as stickВӯandВӯslip behavior and it
drastically changes the way a valve is assumed to behave. [10].
Figure 1.4: Assumed valve dynamics assuming ideal behavior (left) and stiction behavВӯ
ior (right).
Ideally, the valve position (MV) should be equal to the commanded valve position by the
controller, also known as the actuator signal (OP). However, during stiction, this is no
longer the case (see Figure 1.4). The MV becomes a nonlinear function of OP and will
change behavior depending on OPвҖҷs direction. Following the arrow from the bottom left
to the top right corner of the stiction plot, OP must first overcome the friction before it
is possible to move MV. This results in a slipВӯjump as the potential energy is released,
leading to the vertical jumps in MV. Consequently, the system is only controllable in the
moving phase after the jump. If a lower MV is required, OP changes direction and must
overcome stiction in this direction as well, as shown from the topВӯright to the bottomВӯleft
corner. If the setВӯpoint of MV is located in the middle of the slipВӯjumps then the setВӯpoint
will not be obtainable and the controller will make the MV jump back and forth. This
behavior is what causes the oscillations [11][10].
In the GEA case, the malfunctioning control valve is located between the PI controller
and the heat exchanger of Ts actuator loop (see Figure 1.5). GEA requests a method
of detecting and mitigating this stiction phenomenon to obtain better performance and
minimize economic loses.

1.3
Fault detection, isolation and reconfiguration
By implementing the notion of fault detection, isolation, and reconfiguration (FDIR),
these goals might be obtainable. Inseok Hwang (2010) defines FDIR as вҖҳвҖҰ a conВӯ
trol methodology that ensures continual safe or acceptable operation of a system when
a fault occurs through fault detection and isolation, and controller reconfiguration in reВӯ
sponse to the specific faultвҖҷ[12]. It is obtained by applying the concept of redundancy.
This concept requires that redundancy is present in the system, and is obtainable in two
ways: by either exploiting hardware redundancies or analytical redundancies. HardВӯ
ware redundancies are the implementation of duplicative components. During a fault,
the redundant component is used and the process can continue as expected. The cost
of redundant hardware is expensive, thus analytical redundancies are usually preferred.
The analytical redundancy approach uses a mathematical model of the system, data,
and estimation techniques to evaluate the fault. It is more complex and challenging
compared to using hardware redundancies. One needs to ensure that the approach is
robust towards model uncertainties, disturbances, and noise[12].
6
==PAGE:18==
Figure 1.6: General FDIR scheme based on a figure from [12]
The analytical redundancy approach (see Figure 1.6) starts by generating residuals usВӯ
ing measurements and inputs. Residuals are variables equal to 0 under noВӯfault conВӯ
ditions but nonВӯzero during faulty conditions. Simultaneously, they are insensitive to
noise, model uncertainties, and disturbances. They are continually fed to a decision
block which continuously analyzes the residuals to determine if a fault has occurred. If
a set of nonВӯzero residuals enters the decision block, the block will attempt to isolate
the fault based on the pattern of nonВӯzero residuals in the set. When the exact fault
has been isolated, a suitable mitigation strategy is chosen in the reconfiguration block.
The reconfiguration changes the model structure to compensate for the isolated fault by
maintaining performance as close to nominal operational conditions as possible. This
flow of information forms the general idea behind analytical modelВӯbased FDIR.[12]

2.1
A detailed description of the stiction phenomena
One of the root causes of poor control performance in the chemical industry is oscillaВӯ
tions. This usually originates from linear causes such as aggressive controller tuning
and controller interactions[13]. In other cases, it is due to nonВӯlinear structural faults
such as valve malfunctions. These malfunctions include excessive hysteresis, backВӯ
lash, deadband, but in most cases stiction[14].
A typical pneumatic control valve consists of two major componentsНҫ the actuation unit
and the valve body housing (see Figure 2.1). The valve body contains packing, a seat
ring, and a plug attached to a valve stem. The spacing between the valve plug and the
seat ring determines the fluid flow through the valve body and is changed by adjustВӯ
ing the position of the valve stem. The other end of the valve stem is connected to a
diaphragm in the actuation unit which moves the stem based on the air pressure comВӯ
manded by the actuator signal. The packing creates friction which ensures the stem is
kept in the desired position and prevents leaks from the valve body. However, during
operation, this friction force may increase and cause problems when the stem has been
temporarily stationary. Thus an increased force on the diaphragm is required to overВӯ
come the static friction force which is followed by a slip of the stem. This is known as
stick and slip behavior[10].

2.3
The data driven detection methods
Detection of stiction in literature has primarily been centred around dataВӯdriven techВӯ
niques. ModelВӯbased techniques require detailed process knowledge, which in most
cases is unobtainable. Data driven techniques can be directly applied to either the
measured process variable or the error signal[17]. Dambros et al [18][19] separate
current dataВӯdriven methods into single time series oscillation detection (STSOD) and
plantВӯwide oscillation detection. The main focus of this thesis is on a singleВӯinput singleВӯ
output actuator loop. Therefore, the application of STSOD is the most relevant deВӯ
tection method. Dambros et al[18] divided the STSOD into four categories: the timeВӯ
domain methods, the ACFВӯbased methods, the frequencyВӯdomain methods, and the
decompositionВӯbased methods. The following is a brief overview of the most wellВӯknown
methods within each category.
TimeВӯdomain methods have the advantage of not requiring any data transformations
besides appropriate filtering. This makes the data directly applicable in the detection
methods. The first and most wellВӯknown automatic detection method was proposed by
HГӨgglund (1995) [20]. The method calculates the magnitude of the integral absolute erВӯ
ror (IAE) between zeroВӯcrossings in a oscillating signal. If it crosses a chosen threshold,
a counter is increased. If the counter reaches another chosen threshold limit, oscillaВӯ
tions are detected. The method was further developed by N. Thornhill and HГӨgglund
(1997) [21]. They incorporated the time instants at which the IAE crossed the threshold.
The distance between time instants is then used to assess oscillation regularity. If the
distances between the time instants do not follow an exponential distribution, a regular
oscillation is detected. The downsides to both methods are: requiring knowledge of the
controller timeВӯconstant and noise sensitivity. ACFВӯbased methods are better at attenВӯ
uating the heavy noise present in industrial applications. Additionally, the frequency of
the ACF is the same as the time domain signal[18]. This is an advantage compared to
the time domain methods. Miao et al [22] introduced the first ACFВӯbased method. They
evaluated the oscillation decay of the ACFВӯfunction and compared it to a threshold.
The aim was to determine whether the oscillations were present or not. N. Thornhill
[23] applied a method similar to their time domain method to the ACFВӯdomain. They
identified the distribution of the distance between zeroВӯcrossings and compared it to an
exponential distribution. If it diverged significantly from the exponential distribution, the
oscillations were assumed regular and were detected.
FrequencyВӯdomain methods are the simplest approach for identifying oscillations in a
signal. Oscillations can easily be found by identifying peaks in a spectrum visually .
11
==PAGE:23==
However, automating such a method has proved to be difficult [18]. Babuska et al [24]
proposed an automated method. Here the ratio between the maximum amplitude and
the minimum amplitude in the power spectral density was compared to a threshold. If
the ratio crossed the threshold, oscillations were detected. Zhang et al [25] did someВӯ
thing similar, where the amplitude in the discrete Fourier transform was compared to a
threshold. The technique can detect multiple oscillations compared to other works.
The last category is the decomposition methods. These methods are used for strongly
coupled systems where oscillation can propagate throughout the entire system. This
can lead to multiple oscillations in a signal. The decomposition methods seek to isolate
these oscillations into modes and apply detection to each mode [18]. This is the most
researched area within dataВӯdriven detection. Srinivasan et al [26] were the first to apply
such a method. One of the most recent and wellВӯregarded applications is by Aftab et al
[27].
The beforementioned methods all have their advantages and disadvantages. Yet, it has
been a general trend in literature that most authors are not considering using the methВӯ
ods online but instead use them as offline procedures. To automate the procedures, it is
therefore necessary to implement moving windows. Methods for designing the window
size is therefore lacking, and only a few authors such as HГӨgglund [20] and Miao et al
[22] have briefly mentioned the design of optimal windows sizes. This is not a problem
with modelВӯbased methods.
ModelВӯbased detection methods
The detection method of this thesis distinguishes itself from the previously mentioned
methods by being modelВӯbased. Only three methods have been developed: by StenВӯ
man et al (2003)[28], Srinivasan et al (2005b)[29], and Chitralekha et al (2009)[10].
Stenman et al used multiВӯmodel mode estimation, where a singleВӯparameter stiction
model was activated by a binary modeВӯparameter. Using plant data, the optimal seВӯ
quence of modeВӯparameters was estimated using an optimization approach. Stiction
was then detected based on that sequence. Srinivasan et al use the same singleВӯ
parameter model, where the parameter d was estimated through a parameterization
procedure. These singleВӯparameter models have since then proven to be too inaccuВӯ
rate. Chriralekha et al instead attempted to use an unknown input estimator along with
an optimization procedure, to automatically estimate the stiction parameters based on
the wellВӯknown ChoudhuryВӯmodel[30]. This method is fully automated. However, the
optimization procedure is not wellВӯdescribed and noise seems to have a significant imВӯ
pact on the stiction quantification procedure.
2.4
The openly discussed topic of stiction quantification
Stiction quantification is still openly discussed in the literature and the technology is not
as developed as stiction detection. The goal is to quantify the amount of stiction using
one or several parameters. The main issue in stiction quantification is the fact that most
industrial plants have not yet implemented smart pneumatic valves that measures the
actual valve position. Having this measurement would make it possible to estimate the
12
==PAGE:24==
fault by plotting the MVOP diagram directly and use fitting procedures to estimate S [30].
As stated previously, it is not possible to measure MV in most industrial plants. This
sparked several articles regarding stiction quantification. The simplest method is based
on PVВӯOP diagrams [30]. Since the actual valve position MV is unavailable, the meaВӯ
sured process variable (PV) is used instead. This often results in elliptical patterns due
to the filtering caused by process dynamics (see Figure 2.2).
Figure 2.2: The cВӯfuzzy method (left) and elliptical fitting (right). From [30]
Choudhury et al (2005) [30] measured stiction by defining it as the broadest distance
between two clusters around the mean of the PV value. This method is called the fuzzy
cВӯmeans technique. A second proposal by Choudhury et al was to fit an ellipse in the
leastВӯsquare sense and estimate the stiction. The definition of the stiction derived in the
above procedures is called apparent stiction as it is purely a relative estimate of stiction
based on PV data. The data is filtered by process dynamics, making it inaccurate [17].
This resulted in several articles trying to estimate the parameters of the dataВӯdriven
models using alternative methods. The most common method applied in literature is a
mix of blackВӯbox modeling and grid search procedures applied to a Hammerstein model.
Hammerstein models are blockВӯstructured models where the controllerВӯsignal first interВӯ
acts with a nonВӯlinear element (stiction), followed by linear elements (process dynamics)
(see Figure 2.3)[31]. The general procedure is to first create a grid for the parameters
of the nonВӯlinear model (such as S and J in the KanoВӯmodel). Valve stiction outputs
are then generated from all possible combinations of the parameters using measured
controller signals and the chosen stiction model. The output of the stiction model is
then fed to the linear model generating outputs[17]. The model fit can be evaluated by
comparing the generated outputs with the measured process data. The aim is to deterВӯ
mine the best combination of the S and J parameters to estimate the amount stiction.

The Hammerstein method is generally assumed to be the most accurate quantification

due to long processing times, and finding the exact initial condition for the stiction model

2.5
Progress within stiction compensation
When stiction is detected, and has a significant effect on the closedВӯloop performance, a
compensation procedure should initialize and keep performance at an acceptable level.
Several compensation procedures have been suggested in the literature. They all work
by adding a term to the output of the PI controller, such that
OPcomp(t) = uc(t) + uk(t)
(2.2)
Where uc is the output of the controller, uk is the added term and OPcomp is the comВӯ
pensated actuator signal.
The first compensator was developed by HГӨgglund et al (2002) [32]. It was named the
Knocker method and it reduced output variability by implementing uk as a pulse generВӯ
ator. The procedure adds short pulses to the control signal with constant amplitude and
duration. This moves the valve from its stuck position, reducing the oscillations. But the
many valve reversals, caused by the pulse generator, will over time wear out the valve,
thus decreasing its longevity. This leads to more frequent shutdowns of the plant.
The twoВӯmove method by Srinivasan et al(2008) [33] was developed to address the
aggressive valve movements cause by HГӨgglund et alвҖҷs knocker method [32]. Here,
the added signal uk ensures that the compensator overcomes valve stiction by using
two predefined movements above and below the MV setВӯpoint. It ensures that the valve
14
==PAGE:26==
position converges towards the desired valve position. In turn, it requires the exact
value of the stiction parameter S. Additionally, white noise and disturbances can not be
present in the plant. This makes the method impractical for industrial use.
Xiang et alвҖҷs work from 2009[34] tried to improve the knocker method. Instead of using
a pulse generator, they added a constant value in the direction of the controller signal. It
was appropriately named the constant reinforcement method. It improved the excessive
amount of valve reversals present in the knocker method. However, the number of valve
reversals were still significant.
Following this fundamental work, several other authors attempted to improve the original
knocker method and the twoВӯmove method. For example: the improved knocker method
by Arumugam et al (2014) [35] and the four move method of Bacci de Capaci et al (2016)
[36]. They all perform slightly better than the older methods, though they still suffer from
aggressive valve movements and/or the need for specific plant parameters.
In one of the latest works within stiction compensation, by Nahid et al (2019)[37][38],
a comparison between their method with all other current procedures was performed.
Their compensator obtained better performance in all cases. The method is very similar
to the knocker method. They reduce the aggressiveness of their controller by directly
dividing the incoming controller signal with a constant. This has shown great promise,
as it effectively removed all oscillations while also effectively handling disturbances.
However, a significant amount of tuning is required for the beforeВӯmentioned constant
but also the pulse generator.

Q1 is the dynamics of the spray drying process. The residence time of the powder is
between 5 Вӯ15 seconds and the residence time of air in the SD is 15Вӯ30 seconds.
S1 is the shared slow dynamic of the spray dryer and static fluidВӯbed. It is caused by
changes in drying conditions, which affect the powder layer in the static fluidВӯbed. The
slow replacement of powders, exposed to new drying conditions in the static fluidВӯbed,
causes slow dynamics to be visible in the measurements. The residence time is rather
high, around 10Вӯ40 minutes, since the capacity of the static fluidВӯbed is higher than the
inflow.
Q2 describes the quick simultaneous heating of the particles in the static fluidВӯbed. Due
to the wellВӯmixed nature of the fluidВӯbed, most particles are enveloped by air and are
rapidly heated. The temperature of the powder layer Tp is measured by a sensor. It
has sufficiently quick dynamics so that the sensor dynamics can be disregarded in the
model.

3.3
The model predictive controller
The MPC will only be briefly explained, as it is not a necessary theory for solving the
problem statement. A simpler controller will be implemented to mimic its dynamics. The
function of the MPC is to use the measured process variables from the sprayВӯdrying
plant, calculate the optimal inputs, and send them to the actuators. The linear MPC
implemented in the GEA sprayВӯdryers are thoroughly explained by Lars Norbert (2016).
The general structure of the MPC with a realВӯtime optimization layer is shown in Figure
3.5.
Figure 3.5: Control structure of MPC with RTO layer
It consists of a filter, a realВӯtime optimization layer, and a regulator. The filter used in
Lars Norbert (2016) is a Kalman filter. The filter uses the measured process variables
from the spray dryer to estimate the current states of the system. The estimated states
are fed to the realВӯtime optimization layer. It calculates the most profitable set point by
23
==PAGE:35==
minimizing an economic objective function. The setВӯpoints and the estimated states are
then fed to the regulator. It solves another optimization problem for the most optimal
inputs for a chosen time horizon. As the interest of this thesis is only situated in the Ts
actuator loop, tuning the MPC is not necessary. Instead, a PI controller will imitate the
MPC controller. Hereby, a simpler cascade controller is formed and tuned to obtain the
same settling time as the MPC from Tp,rto вҶ’Tp of 40 minutes. Tp,rto being the optimal
setВӯpoint calculated by the RTOВӯlayer. Tuning the controller in Simulink, a proportional
gain of Kc = 0.2275 and an integral gain of Ki = 0.0012 ensures a similar settling time.
The PIВӯcontroller will mimic the MPC and generate the input setВӯpoints for the actuator
loops.

3.4.2
Stiction model
Kano et alвҖҷs model [11] (see Figure 3.8) describes the stiction phenomenon. The dashed
line (l0) is the states where the elastic force of the spring and the air pressure is in
balance. The controller output and valve position will ideally follow this line, when no
friction is present in the system. This ideal system will be disturbed when stiction affects
it. Stiction is created by a combination of static friction (fS) and dynamical friction (fD).
At rest, the valve must overcome the static friction to move (A вҶ’B). At B, the static
friction is overcome and the friction force changes from static to dynamic friction. This
results in a slipВӯjump caused by released potential energy (B вҶ’C). After the slipВӯjump
the valve can move freely in one direction (C вҶ’D) along the line of lr . However, if OP
stops in the middle of the movement without changing direction (C вҶ’E), it must again
overcome the difference between the static and dynamic friction , J=fS вҲ’fD. J is the
slipВӯjump parameter and is also equal to the distance of the slipВӯjump in MV. When the
OP eventually changes direction, it must overcome the offset caused by the dynamical
friction in addition to the static friction to change the direction of MV. This gives a total
deadВӯband parameter of, S=fS + fD. When this is overcome the system will move from
(G вҶ’A), along lr. This cycle will be followed whenever MV is required to change
position.
Figure 3.8: Illustration of the Kano model. From [11]

below (see Figure 3.9),
вҖў fD > 0 : Stiction with undershoot or pure deadband

вҖў fD < 0 : Stiction with overshoot.

needs to be overcome [40].

In this chapter, a modelВӯbased detection system will be designed using the process

4.1

The fault representation in the system model is used to make the residuals insensitive
to any other inputs but the modeled faults. It is achieved by creating what is known as
analytical redundancy relations. For a more inВӯdepth explanation, we refer to Venkata et
al (2003)[42] for a chemical engineering context and Blanke et al (2016) [43] for a more
general engineering context. The GEA case is significantly simpler, as only one fault is
present in the system. Its representation can be estimated using appropriate observers.
The fault representation is obtained by noting that OP=MV under ideal circumstances
but not equal to MV during stiction. Stiction makes MV a nonВӯlinear function of OP. By
adding another input to describe the nonВӯlinearity, MV can be formulated as a linear
combination of OP and the fault representation, fadd, yielding,

The Kalman filter is an optimal observer, as it reduces the error estimate covariance.
However, this sort of design relies on the covariance of both the measurement noise and
the process noise. The general intuition behind tuning the Kalman filter is to choose
Q appropriately to reflect the users вҖҷtrustвҖҷ in the measurements and/or model. If the
variance of Q is higher than R, then more weight will be put on the measurements.
The opposite reflects more trust in the model. In the case of the stiction observer, it is
generally a good idea to trust the model when estimating the Ts. Reversely, trusting the
measurements is better when estimating fadd. This results in a filter where the innovation
(y(t) вҲ’ЛҶy(t)) will mostly be correlated to the fadd.
The QВӯmatrix values are chosen based on several simulations with measurement noise.
During the simulations, the value of the Q2,2 and the stiction parameters are varied
to find the best values for Q. In the simulations, Ts is measured using additive white
Gaussian noise with a variance 0.002 with a sample time of Tsample = 10s (based on
data provided by GEA). The measured Ts and the calculated OP is passed through the
Kalman filter to estimate fadd. This is then added to the calculated OP to obtain the
estimated MVВӯvalues. This results in the simulation shown in Figure 4.4 through 4.9.
The figures show the estimated fadd, the estimated MV, and the estimated MVOP plot
in blue with the simulated values superimposed in red. Two stiction cases are used
for estimation and three different Q2,2Вӯvalues have been applied. When designing Q, it
is important that the estimated fadd and MV are tracking the actual values, while also

4.3
Oscillation detection
Within FDIR, it is common to implement threshold methods for detection. These methВӯ

residual is nonВӯzero. An example of such a method is the generalized likelihood ratio
test as described in Mogens Blanke et al [43]. This method uses hypothesis testing
to evaluate if the mean of the residual has changed magnitude from its expected zero
mean. With the estimated fadd signal being oscillatory around 0 during stiction, such
methods are not compatible for stiction detection. Alternative methods must be impleВӯ
mented, such as the dataВӯdriven detection methods (see Chapter 2). Based on the fact
that the faddВӯsignal will have the following characteristics during stiction:
вҖў Regular (nonВӯchanging frequency and amplitude) during steadyВӯstate
вҖў Have a stationary mean during steadyВӯstate

If the user wishes only to determine S the same performance is obtainable by purely

data window. This gives the same result as the more complicated methods above. Since
bounding the vertical edges is the same as finding the distance between the maximum

this simpler and faster method will be used throughout the rest of the thesis. The relative
error will be the same as for the bounding case, so no further analysis is required.
5.1.3
Further improvement to the method
The major downside of the method is that it can not be applied to all stiction issues.
It is specific to the simulated sprayВӯdrying system. The assumption, that the MVOP is
always rectangular, is only applicable to systems with very conservative control. If the

6.1
Assembling the FDIR system
The entire system is modelled in Simulink (see Figure 6.1),
Figure 6.1: Simulation structure of the entire system
The figure shows the general structure of the Simulink model. For the actual Simulink
models look at Appendix A.2. The process model is used to generate the measureВӯ

In Simulink, the data window is saved and loaded using a memory block structure. The

It decides, based on the value of S, whether the compensator shall be activated or not.
If S is above a threshold, the binary output of the decision function will be 1 and the

6.2
Closed loop simulations
Using the described closedВӯloop system, three simulation cases without stiction are first
completed. This is performed in order to make sure false detection will not happen durВӯ
ing nominal operation, setВӯpoint change, and with an additive output disturbance. The
simulations will be run in a time span of 20000 seconds, at steadyВӯstate, and with a
sampling time of 10 seconds. Each Ts,m and Tp,m sample will be exposed to additive
white gaussian noise with a variance of 0.002 and 0.03, respectively. The Kalman filter
will use the previously described Q matrix (see Chapter 4), the oscillation detector is
implemented with h=0.5, and data window of size W=720. The decision function reВӯ
quires an SВӯestimate of 1 or above before compensation is activated. The compensator
is tuned exactly as described in Chapter 5.
Figure 6.2 shows a normal steadyВӯstate simulation without falseВӯpositive detection. FigВӯ

detection. The last test is a step disturbance of 1 at 10000s (see Figure 6.4). Here, a
falseВӯpositive is detected and the compensator is activated. Looking at the estimated
fadd signal, only a single oscillation is visible. This indicates that the detector may be too
sensitive. By increasing h to 0.6, the false positive detection is removed and the system
acts as expected (see Figure 6.5). This illustrates the difficulty of tuning the oscillation
detector. Increasing h will make the detector less sensitive to disturbances. However,
the minimal amount of stiction that can be detected will also increase. Instead of inВӯ
creasing h, another approach would be to only activate the mitigation system when D=1
for a prolonged time period. This will make sure that sporadic falseВӯpositives will not
activate the compensation system. It would be a suitable solution, since instantaneous
correction of stiction is not necessary.
Next comes the simulations with different amount of stiction. The simulations are identiВӯ
cal to the steadyВӯstate simulation above. In these simulations, stiction is added at 7500
s. Figures 6.6, 6.7 and 6.8 show the application of the FDIR system in three differВӯ
ent cases of stiction with [S=5, J=5], [S=3, J=3] and [S=1, J=1]. In the cases using
[S=5, J=5] and [S=3, J=3], the system works exactly as expected. Within an hour, the
oscillation is detected and the stiction has been mitigated by the compensator. Using
[S=1, J=1], the system takes close to 1.5Вӯ2 hours before compensation is initiated, even

the S estimate being below the decision functionвҖҷs S threshold of 1 during this time. At
13000 s, the estimate is slightly higher than 1 and the compensator is activated. When
stiction of S=0.75 is applied, compensatory mechanisms will not be activated due to the
decision function (see Figure 6.9).

7
Discussion of results and future work
With the complete FDIRВӯsystem designed, it is clear that the algorithm has the potential
in solving the problem of stiction. However, it is not perfect, and the following is a disВӯ
cussion regarding possible improvements, limitations, and the possible complications
of implementing the FDIRВӯsystem in the real spray dryer.
7.1
Comparing modelВӯbased methods to the current state of
the art
The question that arises is: Do modelВӯbased methods result in costВӯeffective FDIR of
stiction problems? In literature, this question is mundane. It is widely accepted that
modelВӯbased FDIRВӯsystems for stiction are inferior to the wide range of dataВӯdriven
methods available. The dataВӯdriven methods in literature work by using oscillation deВӯ
tection directly on the available signals. Thus, making the Kalman filter obsolete so that
no economic investment in model identification and tuning is required.
Yet, a major downside of using the dataВӯdriven methods is the difficulty of isolating osВӯ
cillations in plantВӯwide systems. Some literature on the subject is available [18], but
most methods are based on machine learning and require a significant amount of data
to design. As seen in Chapter 6, modelВӯbased methods can both isolate the fault ,and
if applied to all valveВӯdependent systems, can isolate exactly which valve is causing
the plantВӯwide oscillations. This makes it possible to activate compensation earlier and
regain close to the nominal performance.
Most of the dataВӯdriven compensators also perform worse than the modelВӯbased comВӯ
pensator designed in this thesis. The dataВӯdriven compensators usually result in an
excessive amount of valve reversals. They affect the durability of the valves, leading
to earlier shutВӯdowns[37]. The compensator in this thesis will, at most, add a valveВӯ
reversal every 5 minutes during setВӯpoint change or disturbances and obtain nominal
performance during steadyВӯstate.
7.2
Necessary improvements to the modelВӯbased method
Nominal performance is only ensured for the modelВӯbased method, if accurate S values
are estimated. Since the estimated S is used to calculate the amplitude of the pulse
applied by the compensator, a better estimate would reduce the energy added to the
valve. The quick method used in this thesis is optimal for rectangular MVOP plots.
However, the other valveВӯdependent processes in the spray dryer might not have as
conservative PIВӯcontrollers. If the FDIR scheme was to be added to an actuator with
an aggressive controller, the estimated S would be significantly larger than the actual S
value (see Chapter 5). This leads to undesirable enlarged pulses and increased valve
movement, as they can impact performance.
82
==PAGE:94==
To obtain a better estimates of S, it will be necessary to develop a new method for sticВӯ
tion estimation. If a method could be developed for fitting either a trapezoidal shape to
the data, or cluster the edges of the data, then a more accurate S estimation could be
obtained in realВӯtime. If this is not possible, more accurate methods (cf. Hammerstein
procedure, Chapter 2) can be implemented. Even so, this procedure can not be automВӯ
atized, meaning that the mitigation part of the system (quantification and compensation
step) will be performed offline. Since it was made clear by GEA that a realВӯtime FDIR is
not necessary, as stiction is not a fault that needs immediate attention, a combination of
an online detection system with an offline mitigation system could be a possible solution.
Before the FDIR system can be implemented, it will also be necessary to tune the osВӯ
cillation detector further. As seen in Chapter 6, disturbances within the actuator loop
are likely to trigger falseВӯpositives if the detector is not appropriately tuned. Therefore,
it is required to either create an analytical method for W and h determination, or tune
the detector by training it on appropriate dataВӯsets. This would hopefully lead to a better
detection performance.
The final step before the method can be implemented would be experimental validaВӯ
tion. This has not been possible in this project but it will be necessary, as the simulation
environment used in this thesis is likely to differ significantly from the industrial environВӯ
ment. By evaluating the experiments, it will be possible to improve the system where it
is deemed necessary.
7.2.1
Application of FDIR for valveВӯmalfunctions in future systems
When the beforeВӯmentioned issues have been solved, the system should be ready for
implementation. In general, the method can be used for any valveВӯdependent system,
as long as accurate heat exchanger models are available. The ideal scenario would be
to implement the method in all the heat exchanger systems present in the spray dryer.
This makes it possible to isolate exactly which valve suffers from stiction during plantВӯ
wide oscillations. Since all the necessary signals are available, the implementation
will be purely digital. Therefore, the main expense for implementing the system will
be model identification of the heat exchanger and the following tuning of detector and
compensator.
Contrary, this can also prove to be a significant expense. To finally evaluate if a modelВӯ
based method would benefit GEA, more analytical work is required. A more inВӯdepth
analysis of the available plantВӯwide dataВӯdriven methods should be performed.
For
example, a costВӯbenefit analysis to determine the method obtaining the best possible
profit.

8
Conclusion
The outcome of this thesis is a potential modelВӯbased FDIR system that shows great
promise in solving stiction related valve malfunctions. Using a combination of modelВӯ
based methods and a single statistical method, the FDIR is implemented successfully
in a simulated closedВӯloop system and compensates for most cases of stiction. The
modelВӯbased detector proved to be able to detect most cases of stiction. By applying
the detection system, it was possible to detect stiction down to [S=0.3, J=0.3] before
falseВӯnegatives were obtained. Improved detection can be achieved by further tuning
the detection parameters Q, h and W. However, this requires further research as the deВӯ
sign of h and W parameters is still a topic that remains untreated in literature. This is also
the case for stiction estimation. Accurate stiction estimation is currently only possible
using offВӯline procedures. An attempt to design an accurate allВӯencompassing method
detecting stiction in realВӯtime was not possible in this thesis. As only the stiction paramВӯ
eter S is found to be necessary for modelВӯbased compensation, an alternative method
is used. It can, however, only be applied to the specific spray drying system. With a
working estimation method, a modelВӯbased compensator is designed, operating close
to the same performance as the nominal controller. The nominal controller obtained
an of IAE=760 during steadyВӯstate conditions for 2 hours while the compensated sysВӯ
tem obtained an IAE=772.84. Unfortunately, it suffers from additional valveВӯreversals
and a larger settling time. Apart from tuning the oscillation detector and implementing a
more general stiction estimation method, work remains to be done. The system needs
to be experimentally validated as well as being compared with current available staВӯ
tistical methods. Additionally, a costВӯbenefit analysis has to be performed to evaluate
whether the modelВӯbased system is economically profitable compared to other available
dataВӯdriven methods.

A.3
Unknown input observer
Using this observer it is possible to generate residuals from all the sensors in the system
and make the residuals unaffected by modelled disturbances. This makes it an interВӯ
esting method, as there are disturbances present in the system such as Cin and Yamb.
Given a system
Лҷx = Ax + Bu + Exd + Ff
(A.1)
y = Cx + Du + Eyd
(A.2)
and a UIO with the structure
Лҷz = Azz + TBu + Ky,
K = K1 + K2
(A.3)
ЛҶx = z + Hy
(A.4)
where Az, T, K and H are to be appropriately selected to obtain a residual Лңx. Where Лңx
is defined as
Лңx вүЎx вҲ’ЛҶx
(A.5)
Then,
ЛҷЛңx = Лҷx вҲ’ЛҷЛҶx = Ax + Bu + Ed + Ff вҲ’(Azz + TBu + Ky + H Лҷy)
= Ax + Bu + Ed + Ff вҲ’(Azz + TBu + K1y + K2y
+ H(C(Ax + Bu + Exd + Ff) + D Лҷu + Ey Лҷd))
= Ax + Bu + Ed + Ff вҲ’(Azz + TBu + K1(Cx + Du + Eyd) + K2y
+ H(C(Ax + Bu + Exd + Ff) + D Лҷu + Ey Лҷd))
= (A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)x + (B вҲ’TB вҲ’K1D вҲ’HCB)u + (Ex вҲ’K1Ey вҲ’HCEx)d
+ (F вҲ’HCF)f вҲ’(Azz + K2y + D Лҷu + Ey Лҷd)
= (A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)(Лңx + z + Hy) + (B вҲ’TB вҲ’K1D вҲ’HCB)u
+ (Ex вҲ’K1Ey вҲ’HCEx)d + (F вҲ’HCF)f вҲ’(Azz + K2y + D Лҷu + Ey Лҷd)
= (A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)Лңx + (A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA вҲ’Az)z + ((A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)H вҲ’K2)y
+ (B вҲ’TB вҲ’K1D вҲ’HCB)u + (Ex вҲ’K1Ey вҲ’HCEx)d + (F вҲ’HCF)f вҲ’HD Лҷu вҲ’HEy Лҷd

==PAGE:113==
In order to make the above residual purely sensitive to initial conditions and fault the
following conditions must hold,
A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA вҲ’Az = 0
(A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)H вҲ’K2 = 0
B вҲ’TB вҲ’K1D вҲ’HCB = 0
Ex вҲ’K1Ey вҲ’HCEx = 0
HD = 0
HEy = 0
Which would result in the residual dynamics,
ЛҷЛңx = (A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCA)Лңx + (F вҲ’HCF)f
(A.6)
The resulting residual can then be analyzed by measuring the outputs such that,
r = C(Лңx) = Cx вҲ’CЛҶx = y вҲ’CЛҶx
(A.7)
Where ЛҶx is evaluated using the observer dynamics with the Az, T, K and H matrices
determined such that the above conditions hold. However this is difficult since H has to
be nonВӯzero. This means we need to redesign the output y so D and Ey is zero. This
makes it possible to neglect the derivatives and simplify the above conditions. In the
spray dryer system the D matrix is already zero so this only leaves the Ey matrix. By
modifying the output with the null space of Ey,N, and inserting D as a zero matrix, a
new augmented output (yn) is formed,
yn = Ny = NCx + NDu + NEyd = Cnx
(A.8)
This simplifies the above conditions to,
A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCnA вҲ’Az = 0
(A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCnA)H вҲ’K2 = 0
B вҲ’TB вҲ’HCnB = 0
Ex вҲ’HCnEx = 0

H = Ex(CnEx)вҖ 
Az = A вҲ’K1C вҲ’HCnA
K2 = AzH

Conditioned that,
вҖў rank(CnE) = rank(E)
