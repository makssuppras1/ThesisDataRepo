Abstract
Aquaculture requires effective and long-term strategy to meet the global food demand,
population growth and climate change. Principles and criteria formalised in “standards”
have become tools for addressing such issues. Principles have the potential to minimize
prices and energy usage, assess trade competitiveness, provide access to new markets,
and provide traceability and transparency.
The study’s main goal was to provide comprehensive insights into the carp farming indus-
try and to provide the ASC with the first data that could be used for carp metric setting
in the ASC Farm Standard. The thesis will investigate whether carp farming certification
is possible and what steps need to be taken to include carp in the ASC Farm Standard.
The research compared monoculture and polyculture practices as well as carp growth pa-
rameters. The principles of ASC were discussed, as well as recommendations provided.
Evaluations of nitrogen and phosphorus were made, as well as nutrient inputs and out-
puts were assessed. For the species, feed conversion and survival rates were determined
and presented. In addition, the recommendation of fish meal and fish oil was discussed.
Since the parameters mentioned in this paragraph are covered in the thesis, ASC could
possibly use those to implement the carp standard. However, the missing points such as
inadequate data for water quality, drugs used in farming, in-situ measurements and cost-
benefit analysis, were discussed in the thesis and could possibly be addressed in detail in
the future work.
Furthermore, the ASC accreditation process is a promising system for addressing poten-
tial environmental and social challenges and could lead the carp aquaculture industry to
long-term growth.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Aquaculture is playing an increasingly important role in providing freshwater and sea-
water products for a constantly growing population. While marine and inland fisheries
have exceeded their maximum sustainable yields and to recover it’s loss is very diﬀicult.
In recent decades, demand fulfillment for fish products that has been affected by over-
exploitation, has been restored by fish farming. Aquaculture is a solution that helps to
restore fish stocks in natural waters. Moreover, the significantly growing sector has many
benefits such as employment in urban and especially rural areas, increased exports and
business opportunities.
Furthermore, adverse conditions such as climate, land prices, rapidly spreading diseases,
water scarcity and lack of good water quality require urgent solutions. Despite the fact
that solutions to these problems have been found, aquatic animal welfare and food safety
issues have been identified in fish farming. Moreover, animal health, ecological and socio-
economic aspects have not been certified for all farming species.
This thesis aims to discuss the possibility of certification as a tool to promote more sustain-
able farming practises in collaboration with Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC). So
far, ASC have covered more than 17 species including bivalves, shrimp and different fin-
fish species such as tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus), pangasius (Pangasius), salmon (Salmo
salar) and trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) etc. A species such as carp has not been listed in
ASC farm standards. Although, the relevance of this species is crucial in many Asian and
European countries. In Asia, carp farming exceeds the total production of other farmed
species such as salmon, trout and tuna (Thunnini). However, the excessive carp farming
may affect organic matter and nutrient biomass in the water. Poor management of farms
can lead to change of abiotic properties of water.
1.1
Objectives
The main objective of the study is to provide detailed insights of the carp farming sec-
tor and provide the ASC with first data that can potentially be used for metric setting of
carp in the ASC Farm Standard. The thesis will evaluate if certification of carp farming
is feasible and the steps need to be taken in order to incorporate carp into the ASC Farm
Standard. The overall objective of this thesis is divided into 8 more specific objectives:
1. To describe relevant Cyprinidae carp family while identifying their habitat and feed-
ing niche.
2. Analyze current market for the species.

1.2
Aquaculture Stewardship Council

2.2.1
Historical background
Fish farming had been practiced in the world for several millennia before Christ. It is a
witness to ancient civilizations: Chinese, Egyptian and Roman scriptures, hieroglyphics
and drawings. The beginning of fish farming was based on primary knowledge and expe-
rience. At that time, practical experiments in fish farming were carried out separately and
solely in different parts of the world, based on observations of nature and the application
of these observations in practice.
The Cyprinidae is the largest freshwater fish family in the world consisting more than 1700
species. The culture of Cyprinids can be traced back to 460 BCE in China in the first study
Treatise on Pisciculture by Fan Li, which showed early common carp aquaculture practises
including breeding, pond characteristics and fish stocking density. Nonetheless, in other
contents, carp farming was not intensively practised until recent centuries. The most im-
portant carp species originated in China: silver carp (Hypophthalmichthys molitrix), grass
carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella) and bighead carp (Hypophthalmichthys nobilis), and the so-
called Indian major carps, which involves catla (Catla catla), rohu (Labeo rohita). mrigal
(Cirrhinus mrigala) as reported by Horvath et al. (2002). Their origins belongs to China.
The seventh species is the common carp (Cyprinus carpio), which is native to Eastern Eu-
rope.
Nowadays, Cyprinidae populations are extensively distributed in almost all countries of
the world. Mostly in Asia and many European countries. Fish are found in most fresh-
water rivers, lakes and ponds. Moreover, species also lives in waters with salinity lower
than 5 ppt, for instance in Baltic Sea and Gulf of Bothnia, Winfield (2001).
There are three main systems of carp culture in the world:
1. European system – main species is common carp;
2. Chinese system – generally the Chinese carps are cultivated together: silver carp,
grass carp and common carp. Additionally the system can compose of: bighead
carp, mud carp (Cirrhinus molitorella) and black carp (Mylopharyngodon piceus);
3. Indian system – Indian carps (rohu, catla, mrigal (Cirrhinus cirrhosus)) are cultured
together with Chinese carps.

Importance of common carp
The common carp is one of the most important species in many Asian and in some Euro-

In 2018, China produced 3 million tons of this species. After China, Indonesia is the coun-

is followed by Myanmar, Vietnam and Bangladesh with production ranging from 90000

Figure 2.9: Major common carp-producing countries (except China) and their production

Stocking density of carp has a significant impact on nutrients and decomposition of or-
ganic matter in the water column. Density of fish affects the habitat of carp. In polycul-
ture practises, water quality, natural food, fish welfare and fish growth success are greatly
altered when density exceeds 1000 kg per hectare. This density can be doubled by supple-
mentary feed. In addition, carp diets are replaced by less preferred foods and other food
niches that may be favourable to other species if there is not enough favourable food in
the water column. These conditions allow fish to grow and survive in both monoculture
and polyculture systems as per Rahman (2015).
Nitrogen and phosphorus levels of aquaculture pond waters often fall within the inter-
val where both nitrogen and phosphorus can be limiting, but the number of potentially
phosphorus-limited freshwater systems is much greater than that of potentially nitrogen-
limited waters. Carp is a bottom-feeding fish that accelerates sedimentation, this process
releases phosphorus from sediments. Soluble phosphorus is a limiting factor for phyto-
plankton production. This enhances the production of fish. For instance, tilapia and rohu,
both planktivorous fish, have shown better growth rates in ponds with common carp.

2.2.5
Fish farm
Carp can be produced in extensive natural food and supplementary feed based monocul-
ture and polyculture production systems, in water ponds. Intensive monocultural prac-
tises based on artificial feed can be carried out in cages, irrigation ponds, raceways and
recirculating systems. However, most practises are conducted in earthen water ponds.
Ponds require a much larger land area than cages and require higher water flow rates pro-
vided by rivers or, in some cases, municipal water supplies.
Fish farming activities and site selection are determined by environmental (climate, ter-
rain, water resources and soil) and socio-economic (local situation, degree of urbaniza-
tion, transportation and labor supply etc.) factors. These are group of factors which are
important for both the operation and construction of new ponds and fish farms as per
Lazard and Dabbadie (2009).
Important considerations
According to a study by Beveridge and Phillips (1990), climate is particularly significant
factor in fish farming. Climate zone determines the length of the vegetation period in the
pond. Climate data for a specific area can be obtained from meteorological services. Cli-
matic conditions includes: air and water temperature, number of sunny days, wind speed,
precipitation and evaporation. After evaluating these factors, farmers can determine the
average duration of the growing season and the feeding intensity. Wind direction and
strength are related to water gusts in the pond. The height of the waves determines the
formation of embankments and the pond level of flooding.
The main task in choosing the location of pond management is the rational use of the
terrain conditions, as this must ensure the required depth of the ponds and the most cost-
effective way of water supply, e.g. the use of gravitational pressure or the possibility of
self-flow to supply the ponds with water. Water is usually drawn from the river and in
some cases from the municipal water supply. Some conflicts might happen due to the
fact of competition of land space that been used previously for other purposes as per Bev-
eridge and Phillips (1990).

In Europe, most ponds are shallow, have lush vegetation, are eutrophic and have a muddy
bottom. From an ecological point of view, ponds resemble natural wetlands. Growth is
irregular in Europe and some parts of China due to the temperate and continental climate.
Temperature fluctuations change the feeding behaviour and metabolism. There are active
and passive feeding phases (no or very little feeding). The active phase is between May
and September, when the water temperature is above 12 to 14 °C. At the time of passive
feeding there is no growth and fish generally tend to lose body weight. The fish are able
to survive the winter because they keep their metabolism at the highest level. Due to tem-
perature differences, it usually takes three growing seasons (three years) for the fish to
reach market size 1 - 1.5 kg, in case of Hungary 2 - 3 kg in the third year of production as
stated by Horvath et al. (2002).
According to the length of the commercial production period, pond farms are divided
into:
• One summer old - from fry to fingerling;
• Two summer old - from fingerling to grower;
• Three summer old - from grower to table fish.

According to the degree of production intensity, pond aquaculture farms are divided into
extensive, semi-intensive and intensive aquaculture farms:
• Extensive fish farming is pond farming in which species are bred exclusively on the
natural food basis of the pond. Rice-fish practises are also considered as extensive.
Both rice and fish are cultivated in the same ecosystem and both benefit from this,
establishing a mutual relationship;
• Semi-intensive. Fish is fed regularly with additional feed (natural cereals and legumes
or artificial); partial fertilization (organic or inorganic) of the ponds is also carried
out.
• Intensive farming is the largest commercial product of production in the form of full
feeding of the fish with extruded feed and regular pond fertilization. These systems
provides high fish yields.

The existence of natural food sources is crucial in the development of early stages of carp
and other aquatic cultured animals, since it’s the only source of food. Green algae, di-
atoms, crustaceans, insects, and decomposition products are all natural food sources. In-
creasing natural water source production, such as carbon, nitrogen, and mineral salts, aids
plankton success and thus increases natural productivity and fish growth. As claimed by
Rahman (2019) plankton productivity is supported by the cultivation of various types of
aquatic species at their nutrient levels, such as algae, tilapia, carp, and grass carp.
When fish density rises, the demand for nutrients rises as well, and natural productivity
cannot satisfy the demand. Artificial feeding is then used to supplement natural feeding,
resulting in improved productivity per unit area. Increasing the natural yield of farmed
ponds while adding nutrients is more cost-effective. However, in most cases, improving
the quality of supplementary feeding is suﬀicient to achieve high fish growth rates as
stated by Rahman (2019).
Intensification measures that is prominent in pond aquaculture: pond fertilization which
is used to increase the natural productivity of the ponds, pond liming aims to neutralize
the acid reaction of the soil and disinfect the pond bottom, water level lowering that aims
to increase the water temperature (because the vegetation catches the sun’s rays). The
water aeration aims to maintain optimal oxygen levels to promote the nutrition for the
carp as reported by Virbickas (2000).

2.4.3
Carp farming in Europe
According to European Commission (2018), in the Middle Ages pond techniques begin to
take effect as Christian society expanded. For ethical reasons, derived from the Christian
faith, the monasteries demanded a meatless source of food during fasting days. Monks
were the initiators of the introduction of fish farming in Europe. At that time, carp were
kept in monoculture and the largest fish were used for broodstock. These practises spread
rapidly in Germany and the former Czechoslovakia. Furthermore, the first selective breed-
ing of mirror carps (Cyprinus carpio carpio) was carried out in Central Europe.
Subsequently, traditional fish farming in ponds was expanded to semi-intensive systems
in the 19th century, resulting in improved farming facilities, which contributed to the
spread of carp in the waters, as per European Commission (2018).
The greater part of the carp production is obtained from unselected strains. It is attested
that in Europe there are 30 - 35 strains for domesticated carp. In China, these strains are
kept, while the Indonesian carp strains have not yet been scientifically studied.
Over time, pisciculture has been limited due to high stocking densities, low nutritional
value, feed and disease outbreaks. For this reason, in the last decades, scientists started
research and develop of nutrient-rich protein feeds, especially in Western European coun-
tries where the growth rate of carps increased exponentially. The drawback of this de-
velopment was that production costs had risen. However, in Central Europe the cost
remained unaffected, as the carp was feeding on natural food. Carp aquaculture had a
relatively low value and market was affected in Central Europe by fate traditions. More-
over, the market contribution of this product is gradually shrinking, due to increase in
supply of new competitive aquatic products, as per European Commission (2018).
One of the largest carp producing countries in the EU are Poland and Czech Republic
(they both produce approximately 18 mil. kg of carp annually). In these countries carp
has a long tradition of consumption and high requirements for product quality, giving

• Diseases - water is an excellent medium for the transmission of infectious agents.
Diseases can spread rapidly through susceptible populations. In some cases, sev-

diseases of carps are Spring Viraemia of Carp (SVC), Grass Carp Hemorrhagic dis-

Currently, the most dangerous disease is Koi Herpes Virus Disease (KHVL). Carp
of any age, particularly fry, are susceptible to the disease. One of the diﬀiculties is
that infected carp continue to carry the virus. At water temperatures of 17 - 26 °C,
the disease develops most quickly. However, in colder water, the disease usually
progresses to chronic forms and mortality decreases. When infected, fish lose their
appetite, become apathetic, and their movement coordination is impaired, among
other symptoms. Fish gathers at the surface and “swallow” the air because their
breathing is disrupted. The signs can be seen on swollen gills, and the dead parts
usually lighten.

moldy, dirty gray fungus grow on injured areas. It is most common in ponds with
poor water quality. Fish become susceptible to fungal disease when they are stressed
or their skin is damaged.

• Escapes - escaped fish have a great impact on biodiversity. For example, carp is

carps (silver and grass carp) in the Great Lakes could spell disaster for the region,
affecting the environment and the economy, which relies heavily on tourism. Sci-

In summary, the effect of fish escapes lead to 3 main problems:
1. Benthic foraging disturbance of natural habitats by clearing vegetation;
2. Risk of spreading pathogens in the water;
3. Displacement or interbreeding with wild species and risk of genetic. modifica-

2.9
Water management
Water quality is one of the most important factors to consider when choosing a site for
building ponds, and it is important to inspect existing ponds at appropriate intervals. Wa-
ter intended for production must be tested in an approved laboratory, which confirms the
water quality by means of a laboratory test report. The complete water data analysis has
to be compared with technological parameters. The procedure and method of laboratory
analysis of the water is regulated by the laws of each state according to the purpose of
water consumption. In order to evaluate ponds and maintain them it is necessary to have
knowledge of water properties.
2.9.1
Water quality parameters
Parameters affecting fish growth and welfare are oxygen, temperature, dissolved oxy-
gen, carbon dioxide, salinity, pH, pollutants and organic compounds. These parameters
should be monitored regularly and especially when problems develop in the pond.
Dissolved oxygen
Dissolved oxygen (DO) is a vital parameter for the growth potential of fish. Low oxygen
levels can kill fish. There is a correlation between oxygen consumption and feeding rate.
The DO demand depends on the metabolic rate, size and density of the fish. For example,
smaller fish consume more oxygen than larger fish due to their higher metabolism. In
addition, the amount of dissolved oxygen increases with decreasing temperature.
The main source of oxygen in the water is the process of photosynthesis, which pro-
duces almost 100% of the total oxygen produced by vegetation in the water body. DO
fluctuates during the day, at night oxygen concentration starts to fall due to respiration
of aquatic animals, phytoplankton and by organisms in the mud, from Kanownik and
Policht-Latawiec (2015). Thereafter, in the morning oxygen might decrease to lethal con-
centrations for fish, because of this issue aeration might be necessary. Another way for
oxygen to enter water is the atmosphere. Oxygen is also used for self-purification of water
by oxidizing excess organic and inorganic compounds. Species as a carp shows high tol-
erance for oxygen fluctuations. They can grow adequately at low oxygen concentrations
of 3 - 4 mg/l. However, the lethal concentrations are in range of 0.3 - 0.5 mg/l as stated in
Horvath et al. (2002).
Good management practises can ensure that suﬀicient oxygen is available for fish devel-
opment. Aerators or oxygenation devices increase the amount of oxygen in the water. In
the summer this allows for more intensive feeding, and in the winter it helps keep fish
from dying.

Temperature
Water temperature is one of the most important environmental factors influencing the
growth rate of fish. Lower temperatures, which can be tolerated by fish, result in lower
metabolic and oxygen consumption rates. At higher temperature ranges, fish activity and
oxygen consumption increases, resulting in higher productivity of carbon dioxide and
ammonia. In the textbook by Dyglys (2019) optimal temperature for carp growth is 23 -
28 °C, with a minimum value of 15 °C and maximum of 33 °C.
Carbon dioxide
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is a by-product of fish respiration and is directly related to the
amount of oxygen consumed by fish and the presence of plankton. The high concentra-
tions of these two factors lead to an accumulation of carbon dioxide during summer nights.
As stated by Woynarovich et al. (2011), carp can regularly tolerate (CO2) concentrations of
5 to 10 mg/l on summer mornings. However, fish can survive at concentrations of almost
60 mg/l dissolved carbon dioxide. Exceeding this limit can lead to death, loss of appetite
and increased susceptibility to disease. On the other hand, persistent problems are un-
likely to occur because photosynthesis during the period consumes the carbon dioxide
produced during the night, Woynarovich et al. (2011). In absence of fry of carp present
the volume of mud in pond constantly increases. In essence, high carbon dioxide levels
indicate that the pond is polluted with organic matter. In this case, the ponds are either
limed or aerated, which reduces the feeding intensity of the fish.
pH
The pH value is the parameter that defines whether water is acidic, alkaline or neutral.
Depending on its condition, it affects the plants and water animals in the pond in differ-
ent ways. The pH value ranges from 0 to 14, with pH 7 indicating that the water is neutral.
Values above 7 indicate that the water is becoming alkaline, and values below 7 indicate
acidity. Pond water with values from 6.5 to 8.5 is suitable for fish farming. pH values fluc-
tuate during the day. At sunrise the pH is lowest, during the photosynthesis processes
carbon dioxide is removed, which leads to an increase in pH. At sunset photosynthesis
stops, but respiration continues until the next day, during which time carbon dioxide is
produced - pH decrease, Lucas and Southgate (2013). Optimal pH concentration is be-
tween 7.0 to 8.5 as shown in the Table 2.9.

content depends on the water pH. The more acidic the medium, the more H2S in the wa-
ter. At a pH of 8, hydrogen sulfide is practically absent in water. In the event of odor
of H2S contaminated pond bottom area must be drained and the pond should be replen-

Nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) are biogenic elements. Insuﬀicient amounts of nitro-

toxic.
Nitrate is formed as a result of the complete oxidation of ammonia and enters the ponds
with the wastewater and atmospheric precipitation. The production of phytoplankton, is
limited mainly by concentrations of ammonium, nitrate and phosphate.

determines the degree of eutrophication of the water and the significant quantities dis-

larger quantities than those produced by the fertilizer and chemical industries combined.

2.10
Monoculture and polyculture systems
In India and China, polyculture systems are dominant unlike in European countries, where
monoculture widely established. In this section, the monoculture and polyculture produc-
tion systems of carp are briefly described and their impacts on the environment presented.
2.10.1
Monoculture
Monoculture is an aquaculture method for the production of a single species or breed
at a time. Monoculture is generally practised for high quality, market-oriented fish. It
is easy to operate and control. However, the monoculture method of fish farming has
some negative characteristics compared to polyculture. For example, it has a higher risk
of spreading diseases, because the fish grow at very low oxygen levels. The pond space
in the water column and the natural productivity are not used fully.
In Europe, extensive monoculture practises are widespread and cultivation takes place
in ponds of between 1 and several hundred hectares. Due to the temperate conditions
and the size of the saleable fish, the traditional farming method requires three growing
seasons to obtain 1 - 1.5 kg in Western and Central Europe, and two growing seasons to
obtain 0.5 - 0.8 kg of saleable fish in Eastern Europe. The key species in this method is
the common carp with other cyprinids (tench (Tinca tinca), roach (Rutilus rutilus) and
rudd (Scardinius erythrophthalmus)) and piscivorous fish (pike and catfish) as a relatively
higher market value, as per Kestemont (1995).
2.10.2
Polyculture
This method allows the growth of several species of fish in the same pond, choosing the
appropriate stocking density and age group of the fish, which helps to fully utilise the
natural feeding base of the pond. The selection of pond fish in polyculture is based on the
compatibility of species that have different feeding habits. To be able to use the pond at
its limits, the species must be planktivorous and omnivorous. Which are surface/column,
bottom feeding species, respectively. Generally, due to species ability to optimize pond
sources, the growth and yield tends to be higher in polyculture than in monoculture farm-
ing practises.
In Eastern Europe, the leading fish species are the mix of three Chinese carps (bighead
carp, silver carp and grass carp) and the common carp are most common in polyculture an
the other fish account more than 10%: tench (6 to 7%), pike (0.9 - 1.7%), salmon about 4%
and others (1.2 - 4.8%) as mentioned by Safran (2009). By increasing the diversity of carp
species, it can be diﬀicult to control the pond system. In this case the Kestemont (1995)
recommends to use 3-4 species. For example, Cyprinus carpio is an omnivorous species
that feeds on benthos and detritus, Ctenopharyngodon idella feeds on macrophytes and
Hypophthalmichthy smolitrix prefers phytoplankton, both species are surface feeders and
Aristichthys nobilisfeeds on zooplankton, which means that the ecological niche is in the
middle of the water, Kestemont (1995). In this way pond ecological feeding niche is opti-
mized. Essentially, the silver carp feeds on unicellular algae, which makes the flesh very
productive, and it is also able to absorb small particles of artificial food. They can be kept
in higher densities than other carps. Grass carp feed on vegetation together with artificial
food for carp. The bighead carp competes with the carp for zooplankton. This fish filters
organisms from the plankton and simultaneously ingests artificial food. Feeding niche
are presented in the Table 2.10.

Discussion
The specific growth rate (SGR %/day): of carp in three age groups was different. The
SGR for one, two and three summer old fish in monoculture was 3.0 ± 0.6, 1.4 ± 0.1 and
1.0 ± 0.1, respectively. For carp in polyculture it was 2.9 ± 0.1, 1.7 ± 0.1 and 1.0 ± 0.1,
respectively. There was no significant difference between the two systems. In summary,
SGR decreases from approximately 3.0 to 1.0 %/day as fish age in monoculture and poly-
culture systems.
Enache et al. (2011) conducted an experiment of carp in a recirculating aquaculture sys-
tem. As a result, it was found that SGR results are influenced by stocking density. The
stocking density in RAS was much greater than in pond aquaculture. Two summer old
fish stocked at 64 kg/m3 had an SGR of 1.24, and where the stocking density was half
that, the SGR was 1.45. According to Enache et al. (2011) common carp in recirculating
aquaculture system FCR was 1.31 and 1.09, respectively such stocking densities. In exper-
iment fish were fed with high protein content of 35 % which led to greater feed conversion
performance. Feed composition consisted of fish flour, pea protein, soy, flour, rapeseed,
wheat, vegetable oil, fish oil, minerals among with vitamins.
Farmers who can cultivate the common carp culture can also produce the three main Chi-
nese carps: grass carp, bighead carp, and silver carp, according to past experience. Carp
larvae can be raised in tanks based on the information gathered, but studies have shown
that mortality rates are much higher in tanks than in ponds, and this farming practice is
more expensive. The reason for the larvae’s mortality appears to be that their liver en-
zymes aren’t completely functioning, which means that artificial food can’t be digested
properly. As a result, most farms operates in the ponds where the natural ecosystem pro-
vides food for the stocked larvae and fry. However, even though small fish farms have a
great track record in terms of management, they have gaps in data collection, surveillance,
and handling.
In appendix, SGR evaluation was done in more details for common carp, grass carp and
silver carp, but was not included in the main report. SGR evaluation only for C.carpio in
the Table A.2. Furthermore, SGR results for common carp, grass carp and silver carp is
listed in the Table A.2 and Table A.3.

were 33.5 ± 3.5, 75.0 ± 6.0 and 86.4 ± 4.4. There was no significant difference between

Initial and final weight (g): The results show that the mean initial weight (g) for both
systems of stocked fish in monoculture and polyculture was 1.0 g for a one summer old

614.6 ± 38.3 and 2741.0 ± 144.0. This means that carp raised in polyculture grow faster in
this type of system, mainly due to the fact that fish in these systems are able to use food
more eﬀiciently than in monoculture.
Yield (kg/ha): in earthen ponds, the yield for monoculture reached 562.0 ± 115.8 for one

fish in polyculture the fish yield was much higher. Furthermore, as stated by Horvath

contrary the author had included more cases. Additionally, the difference from reference
is 20 years, which could also influence the variations. As indicated by Gyalog et al. (2017),
the average gross carp yield in Hungarian ponds was 620 kg/ha from 44 farms. However,
the yield varied from about 500 to 1800 kg/ha in polyculture.

Feed conversion ratio for two summer old carp. Lower FCR values imply higher eﬀi-

standing practise of aquaculture ponds in Lithuania, but other countries such as Poland
also agree that their FCR is 1.3 when pelleted feed is used in monoculture. However, the
use of pellets is an expensive practise, remarkably not many countries in Europe practise
this type of aquaculture.
Moreover, farmers claim that they achieve a similar biomass increase with the feed and
supplementary feed. According to them breeding carp under the same conditions but
using supplementary feed - wheat- increases FCR by more than 50%. The FCR in LT case
1 was 2.8 and in PO 3.0. The least effective FCR in monoculture was in DE case 1 with the
result of 3.2 when using triticale as feed, but when only 5% of it is used as pelleted feed,
the FCR decreases to 3.1. This can be explained by the fact that pelleted feed contains a
larger amount of protein. Therefore, when the amount of protein in the feed increases, the
FCR becomes smaller. In the Czech Republic, the feed conversion ratio was 2.5. Could be
because the nutrients were better utilized by the fish than in other monoculture systems.
In the Jaszkiseri farm in Hungary, an FCR value of 3.0 was achieved, which means that to
obtain 1020 kg/ha biomass of fish, 3100 kg/ha of wheat were used. Comparing this farm
with the Polish one, where the FCR value was also 3, it can be seen that the farmer used
three times less feed and consequently achieved three times lower yield. However, it is a
personal decision what yield one wants to achieve and if it complies with regulations.
In polyculture systems, FCR was strongly influenced by the amount of natural food in
the system. The results showed that systems with a greater amount of natural food and a
lower amount of food had a significantly lower FCR, as in HU case 1 (3.8) and HU case 3
(72) of FCR being 1.4, the other results can be found in Figure 4.4.
The highest FCR was in the systems with lower percentage of natural food. This means
that for feed conversion ratio evaluation, the proportion of given feed and natural food
in the ponds would need to be known for all farms. The proportions of natural food are
presented and discussed in more details in Section 4.3.1.
For one summer old fish, the FCR was not determined since the majority of the fish are
farmed in extensive systems (natural food based systems).
From a standard perspective, FCR is one of the parameters included in ASC standardis-
ation. Essentially, ASC has implemented the FCR requirement based on estimate of first
quartile, which was obtained from the production data for other fish species. The first
quartile for carp is also available from the reported analysis. This is presented in Table 5.3.

of the feed was pelleted, FCR decreased to 2.4. This decrease is due to the higher protein

Fertilizers assessment: In this analysis, farmers tended to use dry organic fertilizer be-
cause it is easier to store. A nutrient evaluation of different types of fertilizers was done
to see how much nitrogen, phosphorus, effluent they released into the pond environment.
There was too little information about fertilization in one summer old fish pond. In this
case, the assessment was done for the other two age groups. It is relevant for farmers to
know which fertilisers provide the best nutrient availability. In most cases, the choice of
fertiliser depends on cost and availability. However, to maximize their natural food chain,
they could use analysis to determine which and how much nutrient is excreted. Organic
matter (animal manure, household waste, plant by-products, etc.) and nutrients such
as potassium, phosphorus, nitrogen they affect the consumption of oxygen, which leads
to excessive growth of algae and bacteria and pathogenic growth of organisms. This as-
sessment could be included in the ASC standard, as it is in tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus)
and pangasius (Pangasius). In the ASC standard for pangasius and tilapia, it was agreed
to set the requirements using the estimate of first quartile of all available data. This can
also be done for common carp. The results of the analysis show that monoculture farms
use significantly less manure compared to polyculture farms. Farmers reported that in
monoculture the amount of manure for all cases was 2 t/ha/y. It shows that farms has
similar fertilizing approaches. Chicken manure was estimated to have the highest effluent
excretion. Potassium was estimated but not included in the analysis because it is not yet
considered one of the pollutants regulated by the ASC.
The effluent originated from the manure was highest in DE case 1 and 2 where the effluent
of N, P, K was 18.0, 6.4 and 6.0 kg/ha/y, respectively. Sheep manure was used only on the
farm HU Jaszkiseri, with effluents of 11.8 kg/ha/y for nitrogen, 3.6 kg/ha/y phosphorus
and 3.4 kg/ha/y potassium. For Lithuania case 1 and 2 it was evaluated that effluents of
nutrients was 9.0, 2.4 and 3.6 kg/ha/y, respectively. In Polish carp farming, cattle manure

Principles from Aquaculture Stewardship Council
As discussed, aquaculture certification is a way to certify the process of producing aqua-
culture products and to ensure that the process is sustainable and that it is done fairly.
In aquaculture certification, Aquaculture Stewardship Council plays an important role in
validating the certification. Their goal is to ensure that the production facility is well man-
aged and that the aquatic animals are managed as well. By implementing the certificate,
they ensure that the process is environmentally friendly without harming the environ-
ment. It ensures that fish meal and fish oil products are present in the feed in appropriate
amounts without harming the ecosystem. Additionally, it ensures that the product is safe
for consumption. ASC aims to achieve this by promoting standards for environmental and
social best practice in aquaculture and rewarding responsible farming practices through
standard setting and certification.
Principles from Aquaculture Stewardship Council that applicable also for carp: Criteri-
ons were added from Tilapia standard that complies with carp and has been investigated
in the report. Thus, tilapia are a different species of fish than carp. Tilapia are cichlids,
whereas carp are cyprinids. Both fish are omnivorous, and if released into prosperous
waters, they may cause problems by reproducing rapidly and displacing native species.
Carp and tilapia have been effectively farmed for centuries and are important commercial
species.

Criterion 2.3 The effects of eutrophication
Rationale – The diurnal dissolved oxygen fluctuation is a realistic parameter for reducing
the effects of eutrophication on a specific water body, that can be set for carp.
81
==PAGE:94==
In response to the rate of photosynthesis and respiration, oxygen levels in water fluctuate
over a 24-hour period. Primary productivity grows as nutrients are introduced to a water
body. During daylight hours, this rise allows more oxygen to be released into the water
body as a byproduct of photosynthesis. Around the same time, oxygen is absorbed by
primary producers and other aquatic life forms when they respire during the day.
Photosynthesis stops in the absence of light, but respiration continues. As a result, oxy-
gen is absorbed during the night, resulting in a decline in dissolved oxygen. The more
primary producers there are, the more oxygen they consume. The difference between
peak daytime oxygen levels and decreased oxygen levels during the night can thus be
used to express the effects of eutrophication. To preserve fish health and productivity,
aquaculture operations must reduce unnecessary variations between day-time and night-
time dissolved oxygen levels.
Oxygen levels in carp varied from 2 mg/l to 12 mg/l throughout the season, according
to associates. To determine the oxygen requirement, more research is needed. However,
the oxygen requirement for hatcheries should not be less than 70%, and for pond farming
should not be less than 50%, according to Lucas and Southgate (2013).
Criterion 2.5 Receiving water monitoring
Rationale – It is important to consider the environmental effects of carp aquaculture,
when water bodies are used directly for carp farming and to collect water quality sam-
ples from farms.
Nutrient loading for carp aquaculture must be considered. Monitoring the quality of re-
ceiving water is one of the keys in aquaculture practises. It demonstrates that farmers are
aware of the complexities of the receiving water where farms discharge and, theoretically,
where they receive water for their farming operations. According to Aquaculture Stew-
ardship Council (2019), criterion 2.5 for tilapia of receiving water quality, measurements
must be completed and validated and the record requirement must be met months prior
to the audit. Therefore, the same requirement could be followed for carp aquaculture.

Criterion 5.1 Use of wild fish for feed (fish meal and fish oil)
ASC pays special attention to the use of fish oil and fish meal. As demand of fish meal
and fish oil increases in both aquaculture, chicken, pig and cow production, it is impor-
tant that the use of fish for feed does not impact on food security and human health.
All investigated farms except LT case 2 and DE case 2 were not using fish meal or fish oil
having feed. However, pelleted feed was used in two farms containing these substances.
It is noteworthy, that the farmers did not know the percentage of fish meal and fish oil
in the feed as it is not listed in the feed data sheet as feed producers tend to conceal feed
composition.
According to Huntington and Hasan (2009), herbivorous/omnivorous fish have lower
protein requirements (i.e., 20 percent), which comes from either plant or animal sources.
This group includes grass carp, common carp, other cyprinids, tilapias, and catfish, all of
which require about 5% fish meal in their diets. According to technical paper by Hunting-
ton and Hasan (2009), cyprinids require 1 - 2% fish oil in diet. Potentially, 5% can be set
as a limit for fish meal for ASC standard and 1 - 2% as a threshold for fish oil in carp feed.

Conclusion
The research aim was to identify if certification of carp farming is possible and what steps
need to be taken to include carp in the farm standard for Aquaculture Stewardship Coun-
cil (ASC). Based on a quantitative and qualitative analysis of carp farming, it can be con-
cluded that social and environmental factors need to be considered when implementing
such standard. The results indicate that growth parameters of fish and specific farm prac-
tises can define performance of those factors.
There are a few problems that come with poorly handled carp farming. The carp is a very
adaptable fish, and if escapes are not closely managed and controlled, they can cause
problems. If water quality is not adequately managed, there is also a risk of water con-
tamination, which increases the risk of disease. The introduction of ASC certification for
this fish species could potentially aim to mitigate these problems.
Different regions often use different eco-labels, each with their own standards and audit-
ing procedures. Eco-labels for seafood presumably make it easy for consumers who care
about sustainability to find and buy suitable products. Yet Zander et al. (2018) argues that
consumers have little understanding of the different eco-labels currently on the market.
Consumers have been shown to have diﬀiculty identifying truly sustainable and respon-
sible companies, weakening the claims of those that are certified to a high standard and
giving a competitive advantage to those that have an eco-label but may certify a lower
level of sustainability. The interest in standardisation is that the carp farming sector in
Eastern Europe is more interested in certification at the national level, as this is the mar-
ket for them (Christmas tradition). Moreover, products with national label have higher
market acceptance in case of carp. Potentially, The ASC carp standard will be a novel ad-
dition to the food industry, and it will be important to see how customers respond to it.
In the study, growth parameters of carp are presented and mono- and polyculture prac-
tises are compared. ASC principles are discussed and suggestions are presented. Nitro-
gen and phosphorus evaluations are performed and suggestions for carp species are given.
Inputs and discharge of nutrients are evaluated. Specifically feed conversion and survival
rates are determined for these species.
The thesis concludes by recommending the ASC certification program and potentially
carp standard as a viable scheme for addressing potential problems in terms of environ-
mental and social issues, as well as guiding the carp aquaculture industry toward sustain-
able growth. The effects of making such standard requires further investigation outside

A.1
Data from China
China is currently the leading producer of carp in the world. However, it was challenging
to collect information from this country as it is very diverse and breeding practises can
be very different. The data was received from Lianyungang, Jiangsu Province, China.
The main farmed carp species in Lianyungang, Jiangsu Province are common carp and
grass carp in a polyculture system (both species together in one pond). In a polyculture
pond, the most common carp species is whiteleg Shrimp (Litopenaeus vannamei). The
composition of a carp polyculture pond (/666m2) is - 400 to 600 common carp + 80 to 100
grass carp + 50 silver carp + 80 flat-head grey carp + 30000 to 100000 whiteleg shrimp.
The most valuable aquaculture product in a polyculture system is whiteleg shrimp. In
many cases in China extruded feed is used. Feeding is done - twice a day and the actual
amount depends on the temperature and ranges from 1%-4% of the total biomass in the
pond. In most ponds the single feed is used according to the species composition. For
example, if the main breeding species in pond A is carp, it is fed a feed designed for carp;
the main breeding species in pond B is grass carp, then it is fed a grass carp feed. In the
breeding process carp and grass carp has common diseases hemorrhagic disease , and gill
rot disease.
