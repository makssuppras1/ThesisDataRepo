Abstract
The increasing integration of wearables and mobile applications have enabled continu­
ous health monitoring of autonomic nervous system (ANS) activity through non­invasive
measures such as heart rate variability (HRV). This thesis proposes a digital twin frame­
work that integrates sensor­derived HRV features with contextual information to provide
a more comprehensive readiness­for­activity assessment.
To enable this framework, a 14­day study was conducted using the DTU CARP Studies
App and the Polar Verity Sense sensor to collect passive physiological data and active
survey responses on mood, stress, sleep quality, fatigue, and recovery. HRV features
were computed from collected measurements, and data processing techniques such as
rolling windows and synthetic data generation were used to enhance and capture recovery
trends.
Through Machine Learning approaches, this work defined HRV­based nervous system
profiles and predicted a composite readiness score from HRV metrics and contextual
features. A Support Vector Regressor achieved the best performance and outperformed
HRV­only baselines. A proof­of­concept mobile application was developed to present
these predictions in an interpretable manner, where users can visualize and simulate
readiness scores, and receive actionable feedback for health improvement.
This thesis provides a replicable pipeline, from data collection to application deployment,
that enables future development of an adaptive digital twin system for health and perfor­
mance monitoring.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, the gap between medical­ and consumer­grade health monitoring, par­
ticularly after the COVID­19 pandemic, has been shortening. Sensors capable of captur­
ing vital physiological measurements like electrocardiograms (ECG), electroencephalo­
grams (EEG), photoplethysmography (PPG), and oxygen saturation (SpO2), are now in­
tegrated into consumer wearables. Smartwatches, chest straps, and even headphones
have evolved into powerful health technology, enabling continuous and remote monitoring
of physiological signals in real­world environments.
Among the available measurement modalities, PPG has emerged as the most practical
and consumer­ready by offering a non­invasive and cost­effective means to estimate car­
diovascular metrics such as heart rate and heart rate variability (HRV). PPG­based HRV
estimation has been widely adopted by commercial devices such as Fitbit, WHOOP, Oura,
and Garmin, allowing millions of users to track recovery, readiness, and stress in their daily
lives.
Heart Rate Variability (HRV), a measure of the variation in time between consecutive
heartbeats, offers a non­invasive window into the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) func­
tion, reflecting the relationship between sympathetic and parasympathetic activity. Its
value lies in its ability to correlate stress, fatigue and recovery levels. However, the inter­
pretation of HRV is not straightforward. Stress itself is a complex and context­dependent
factor: short­term stress can be beneficial, while prolonged or excessive stress may have
serious health effects. Adding to this complexity, HRV metrics are influenced by numer­
ous physiological, lifestyle, and environmental factors, making isolated measurements
prone to misinterpretation.
Despite the advances of wearable sensors and HRV analysis, most consumer­grade tools
still rely on single HRV metric interpretations with minimal contextual information. This
creates an opportunity for integrating HRV with contextual information, such as mood,
sleep quality, and fatigue levels, to provide richer and more individualized insights. This
thesis explores this opportunity by combining wearable sensing and self­reported con­
textual data to model ANS fluctuations through a context­aware, HRV­based digital twin
framework.
1.2
Related work
HRV analysis has long been recognized as a reliable, non­invasive method for assessing
the ANS activity. An optimal level of HRV is associated with health and self­regulatory ca­
pacity and adaptability, whereas deviations, either to lower or higher values, can indicate
fatigue, overtraining, or certain cardiovascular risks [1].
Over 15 distinct HRV metrics exist, each shaped by different influences:
• Physiological: age, sex, circadian rythm, ethnicity
• Lifestyle: alcohol and caffein intake, sleep habits, physical activity
• Environmental: temperature, altitude, noise

Because these factors interact in complex ways, interpreting HRV in isolation can lead to
misinterpretations. For example, identical HRV values may signal recovery in one individ­
ual but fatigue in another [2]. Research has increasingly emphasized the importance of
combining passive data (e.g., HRV features) with active data (e.g., self­reported stress,
mood, or sleep quality), to provide both an objective insight into physiological states and
crucial context that sensors cannot capture [3].
The application of HRV has been used most extensively to predict cardiac health and
disease progression, although it has become a valuable tool in sports science to moni­
tor training adaptation, recovery, and risk of overtraining. HRV­guided training has been
shown to improve athletic performance and reduce injury rates by personalizing workout
loads to match an individual’s physiological state [4]. Existing tools are already able to
provide personalized recommendations to users based on daily captured HRV data [3, 5].
Kubios HRV [5] is an application widely recognized in both academic and clinical contexts
for offering in­depth HRV analysis by computing commonly used time, frequency and non­
linear domain parameters. However, it focuses on standalone physiological analysis and
does not integrate contextual information.
HRV4Training [3] app provides an easy and proven methodology for measuring morning
HRV through a smartphone camera, and is able to complement these HRV readings with
manually reported contextual inputs such as sleep quality, training intensity, and men­
tal state to provide training readiness scores. While more user­friendly, it applies static
interpretation rules and does not adapt dynamically over time.
These tools illustrate the advances of HRV­based monitoring but also highlight an impor­
tant gap: the lack of adaptive, context­aware systems that can continuously refine their
recommendations in response to an evolving physiological state.
1.3
Problem statement
This project aims to address this gap by developing an AI­driven framework that integrates
extensive HRV metrics with contextual factors to create a digital twin mobile health app
of the nervous system response. By leveraging machine learning techniques with an
optimized dataset, this project can enable a more dynamic and personalized assessment
of HRV patterns.
The core research questions guiding this work are:
• RQ1: What are the key contextual factors that must be integrated to enhance the
reliability of HRV analysis?
• RQ2: How can a self­reported passive and active data collection mechanism be
implemented to provide a more comprehensive HRV analysis?
• RQ3: How can an AI­powered digital twin app improve the personalization of HRV­
based readiness recommendations?
The hypothesis is that an AI­driven HRV analysis system, enriched with contextual infor­
mation, can provide more accurate and individualized insights into nervous system re­
sponses, possibly leading to improved training optimization, recovery tracking, and long­
term health outcomes.

Goals

• Develop a comprehensive dataset that integrates HRV metrics with contextual fac­

Theoretical framework
2.1
Autonomic nervous system (ANS) response and HRV
analysis
2.1.1
The autonomic nervous system
The autonomic nervous system (ANS) is a crucial part of human physiology that regulates
involuntary body functions such as blood pressure, respiration, digestion, bladder activity,
and heart rate [6]. It plays a critical role in maintaining internal physiological stability.
The system is divided into two main branches: the sympathetic and the parasympathetic
nervous system, which work in opposite ways to balance physiological responses. The
sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for action by initiating the ”fight or flight”
response through elevation of heart rate and blood pressure while redirecting blood flow
away from the digestive system toward the muscles. In contrast, the parasympathetic
nervous system promotes the ”rest and digest” state by slowing the heart rate, enhancing
digestion, and conserving energy.
A central function of the ANS is to support homeostasis, a state of balance among all the
body systems needed to survive and function correctly [7], by balancing and adapting the
different systems and functions to physical, emotional and environmental changes.
2.1.2
Heart rate variability (HRV)
Heart rate variability (HRV) refers to the variation in time intervals between successive
heartbeats, typically measured in milliseconds. These intervals, typically known as RR
intervals, are controlled by the ANS response, which continuously adjusts blood flow and
heart rate in response to internal and external stimuli. HRV captures this beat­to­beat
modulation and reflects the balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous
activity [8].
Unlike a heart rate average, HRV allows researchers to quantify the responsiveness of the
autonomic nervous system. Higher HRV is generally associated with better physiological
adaptability and greater vagal tone, while lower HRV has been linked to stress, fatigue,
chronic disease, and reduced parasympathetic response.
Measuring HRV
HRV is not a directly observable signal but is instead derived from precise heartbeat
events. Two primary sensing methods are currently used in HRV assessment: elec­
trocardiography (ECG) [3, 9, 10, 11, 12] and photoplethysmography (PPG), each with
distinct characteristics and trade­offs.
ECG is a method that [13] records the heart’s electrical activity using electrodes placed
on the skin, producing a characteristic waveform consisting of the P wave, QRS complex,
and T wave. HRV is derived from the detection of R­peaks (the sharpest, most easily
identifiable component of the QRS complex), allowing for accurate computation of RR
intervals (Figure 2.1).

Due to its high temporal resolution and clinical validation, ECG is widely used in medical
diagnostics and research. However, its use in mobile and wearable applications is limited
by the need for multiple electrodes placement and signal preprocessing to remove noise
from movement or skin contact variation.
PPG has emerged as a practical alternative for HRV monitoring in wearable and mobile
health technologies. PPG uses an optical sensor (typically green or infrared light) to de­
tect blood volume changes in peripheral tissue. As the heart pumps blood through the
circulatory system, the amount of light absorbed or reflected by the tissue varies. These
variations generate a pulse waveform from which peak­to­peak intervals (PPI) can be
extracted to estimate HRV (Figure 2.2).
Figure 2.2: Beat­to­beat analysis from R­R intervals (RRI) and peak­to­peak intervals
(PPI) [15]
Compared to ECG, PPG offers significant advantages in terms of usability, cost, and inte­
gration into consumer devices. It requires fewer components and less expertise for use,
making it ideal for continuous monitoring in real­world scenarios. However, PPG is more
sensitive to motion and changes in blood pressure, temperature, and in ambient light,
which can reduce signal quality, especially during intense physical activity or poor sensor
contact [16].

Factors influencing HRV
HRV is influenced by a wide variety of physiological, behavioral, and environmental factors
[19]. For example:
• Physiological: Age, gender, body weight, and circadian rhythms significantly affect
baseline HRV levels.
• Lifestyle and behavior: Exercise, alcohol intake, caffeine, smoking, and hydration.
• Environmental: Noise, temperature, altitude, and posture at the time of measure­
ment can introduce variability.
• Psychological: Stress, anxiety, and emotional state are closely tied to parasym­
pathetic withdrawal and reduced HRV.
These influences highlight the importance of contextual awareness when interpreting
HRV. A low HRV reading after a night of poor sleep or stressful events may not reflect
pathology, while a high HRV during illness or cardiovascular dysfunction may be mislead­
ing. This underscores the rationale for integrating self­reported context with physiological
signals [2], an approach central to this thesis.
HRV as a marker for ANS
The ANS’s ability to regulate the heart rhythm, including the interplay between the sym­
pathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems, is reflected in HRV [20]. Higher levels
of HRV, particularly those associated with parasympathetic­dominated indices, are com­
monly regarded as indicators of good health because they reflect the autonomic nervous
system’s flexibility and adaptability. In contrast, low HRV may be associated with car­
diovascular disease, psychological stress, anxiety, depression, and other problems. [21,
22]. Because of these properties, HRV has been used to assess ANS responses in a
variety of contexts, including stress monitoring, exercise physiology, and mental health,
as a non­invasive quantitative measure of autonomic function.
For example, psychological or physiological stress causes typical changes in HRV through
the ANS. Under acute stress, sympathetic nervous system activity increases while parasym­
pathetic (vagal) activity decreases, resulting in lower HRV. It has been shown in studies
that stress caused significant shifts in HRV measures, most notably a decrease in high­
frequency (HF) power (a vagal­associated metric) and an increase in low­frequency (LF)
power, reflecting decreased parasympathetic output and relative sympathetic predomi­
nance [23, 24].

2.1.3
Applications in training and recovery
HRV is a non­invasive method of measuring physiological responses to physical exertion.
Research consistently supports its utility for assessing training­induced stress and recov­
ery, especially in athletic populations. Changes in HRV reflect underlying ANS activity and
can help guide training intensity, manage fatigue, and personalize recovery strategies to
avoid overtraining or non­functional overreaching [25].
For example, Plews et al. (2013) demonstrated that daily HRV measurements, like RMSSD,
can effectively track training adaptation in elite endurance athletes. Elevated HRV values
were associated with positive adaptation and improved performance, while persistent re­
ductions indicated fatigue or overtraining [4]. Similarly, Buchheit (2014) reviewed practical
applications of HRV in team sports and concluded that morning HRV assessments could
be used to tailor training loads on an individual basis [26]. This individualized approach
allows better recovery management and reduces the risk of injury or maladaptation.
The decision to focus on morning, resting HRV measurements in this thesis is grounded
in recommendations from Buchheit (2014), who emphasized that short­duration record­
ings, such as 5­minute morning sessions, are among the most practical and sensitive
methods for monitoring parasympathetic activity in both acute and chronic contexts [26].
These measurements are easy to implement and less affected by confounding factors
like physical activity or stress later in the day.
Accordingly, participants in the underlying study collected daily morning HRV data over

such as 7­day rolling averages of RMSSD, for detecting meaningful trends in recovery

2.3
Machine Learning in ANS
Machine learning (ML) models for ANS analysis typically rely on features extracted from
physiological signals that reflect autonomic activity.
One common signal is the heart
rhythm (ECG or pulse), from which HRV metrics are derived. A broad range of HRV fea­
tures can be computed, including time­domain features [30], frequency­domain features
[31], and nonlinear features [32].
After feature extraction, a variety of ML algorithms have been applied to classify or predict
states from ANS data. Commonly used models include both conventional “shallow” ML
classifiers and more recent deep learning approaches:
• Support Vector Machines – SVMs are popular for HRV­based classification due to
their effectiveness on high­dimensional feature sets and limited data. Studies report
SVM achieving high accuracy in distinguishing stress vs. non­stress states [30, 31].
For example, an SVM with an RBF kernel reached ≈83% accuracy classifying driver
stress using time­domain and nonlinear HRV features [31].
• Decision Trees and Random Forests – Tree­based models are common due to their
interpretability and ability to handle mixed features. Random Forest ensembles in
particular have achieved robust performance. In a driver stress study with multiple
biosignals like ECG and respiration, a Random Forest classifier reached about 94%
accuracy in distinguishing high vs. low stress driving periods [30].
• Deep Neural Networks – Modern deep learning architectures, such as convolu­
tional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) including long
short­term memory (LSTM) units, have been increasingly applied to ANS analysis
Digital Twin Mobile Health Application for Nervous System Response Using Integrative HRV Analysis
11
==PAGE:19==
as larger datasets become available. LSTMs are a specialised form of RNN de­
signed to learn long­range temporal dependencies while mitigating vanishing gradi­
ent problems. CNNs are effective at automatically extracting spatial and frequency
patterns from raw ECG or PPG signals, while LSTMs capture temporal dependen­
cies in HRV sequences [24, 33]. Hybrid CNN–LSTM models have also been used
for multimodal fusion, e.g., combining HRV with electrodermal activity or respiration,
achieving classification accuracies above 90% in stress detection tasks [24].
In reality, the size of the dataset and the requirement for interpretability frequently influ­
ence the model selection. Due to their easy interpretability and robust performance on
sparse data, traditional models like SVM and RF are still frequently used [30]. As data
volumes increase, different deep learning models could become more popular because
it should enable models to recognize complex patterns and may be able to detect minute
variations in HRV or combined signals that manual features or shallow models might over­
look.
Deep neural networks can learn directly from raw or minimally processed physiological
signals, reducing the need for handcrafted features. Rastgoo et al. [24] achieved ≈92.8%
accuracy in driver stress detection with a CNN–LSTM model combining HRV and contex­
tual data. Similarly, Coutts et al. [33] used LSTMs on wearable HRV time series to predict
stress, anxiety, and depression with up to 83% accuracy. While effective for continuous
monitoring, such models require larger datasets and are generally less interpretable than
shallower approaches.
Key challenges in machine learning for ANS applications include motion artifacts, sensor
drift, and missing data in wearable signals [34]. Models often struggle to generalize across
populations, highlighting the need for personalization [35]. Deep models offer high accu­
racy but limited interpretability [36], and small labeled datasets for certain states constrain
purely data­driven approaches, motivating feature engineering and transfer learning [37].

4.3
Feature engineering: rolling HRV features
Transforming raw physiological data (i.e., PPI and HR signals) into HRV features is a
fundamental part of our data handling efforts as they allow to capture meaningful patterns
of the ANS. Additionally, the application of rolling windows to HRV features can provide
more interpretability over time, uncovering trends and underlying patterns that may lead
to offering a better personalization.
HRV feature computation
In this study, features are computed from the pulse­to­pulse interval (PPI) recordings ob­
tained from the Polar Verity Sense monitor by using the hrv­analysis Python library [49].
These raw PPI series are first cleaned and filtered by removing outliers outside a real­
istic physiological range, where the lower limit is set to 300ms, which corresponds to a
heart rate of 200bpm, and an upper limit of 2700ms, which corresponds to a heart rate
of 22bpm. This is followed by removing ectopic beats (irregular heartbeats often known
as early, skipped or extra heartbeats [51]) using the Malik method, designed to identify
and remove/interpolate these ectopic intervals. Finally, linear interpolation is conducted
to account for missing values.
Similarly, heart rate values are computed from the PPI values and then filtered within a
range of 30 to 220 bpm to ensure plausible values.
Finally, once this data is cleaned and filtered, three categories of HRV features are com­
puted:
• Time­domain features: RMSSD, SDNN, SDSD, mean NNI, median NNI, pNN50
Digital Twin Mobile Health Application for Nervous System Response Using Integrative HRV Analysis
21
==PAGE:29==
• Frequency­domain features: LF and HF power, LF/HF ratio, total power
• Non­linear features: SD1, SD1, SD2/SD1 ratio
Rolling window features
To support a more comprehensive analysis, rolling window features are computed from
the base HRV features. The rolling window method is a commonly used technique in time
series analysis that computes statistics over a sliding interval to account for variations
over time and smooth data, uncovering underlying patterns and trends.
To capture these trends and variability of HRV features, rolling statistics are calculated
using different window sizes as supported by prior HRV research:
• 1­day window: reflects daily variations and day­to­day recovery patterns [4]
• 3­days window: reflects short­term variability to guide decisions in recovery rou­
tines [12]
• 7­days window: reflects long term adaptation and highlights underlying patterns
[26]
4.4
Synthetic HRV and contextual data generation
Given the small number of participants and the limited number of daily observations, a
synthetic data generation procedure was conducted to expand the dataset and explore
the effect of its size in model development. The generation of synthetic data in healthcare
has emerged in the latest years as a solution to address, among several challenges, the
need for training artificial intelligence algorithms with sufficient sample size and statistical
power [52].
While not intended to reflect realistic physiological states, this process was designed to
preserve the statistical properties and contextual structure of the existing observations.
The resulting samples can be used to understand how increased data availability could
improve the model generalization.
As reviewed by Pezoulas et al. (2024), current approaches for synthetic data gener­
ation are categorized into statistical­based methods, probabilistic­based methods, and
machine­learning based methods. Since the scope of this project is not to develop a full
pipeline for synthetic data generation, the statistical­based method of multivariate normal
distribution was selected due to its simplicity and easy integration into our data processing
pipeline.

Machine Learning pipeline
This chapter describes the design and implementation of the machine learning pipeline,
which transforms physiological and contextual data into personalized readiness scores.
Two main data sources are used for this project: an external HRV dataset used to define
nervous system profiles, and the study dataset previously described in Chapter 3.
As shown in Figure 5.1, the process begins by doing feature engineering and clustering
on the external dataset to define distinct physiological profiles. A classification model is
then trained on this external dataset to assign users into these clusters, which is then
used to cluster participants from the study. In parellel, a composite readiness score for
participants in the study is computed by combining rolling HRV metrics with contextual
data, resulting in a daily measure of physiological recovery state.
Considering this readiness score as the target feature, three progressive feature sets
(e.g., HRV­only, HRV and morning features, HRV and morning/evening features) are used
to train a readiness prediction model, and statistically compared among each other. Fi­
nally, the resulting readiness scores are mapped into cluster­specific readiness zones,
enabling interpretation for distinct profiles.
With the integration of cluster­based profiles and predictive readiness modeling, this pipeline
allows evaluating the contribution of contextual data.

5.1
Nervous system profiles: participants’ cluster
classification
Clustering was performed on an external dataset comprised of multiple heart rate record­
ings per participant, with the number of sessions varying across individuals. The data
consisted of RR intervals, meaning the time durations between successive R­wave peaks.
The dataset was collected as part of a prior study in which participants underwent heart
rate measurements during a float tank treatment. Recordings began while participants
were standing before entering the tank and ended under the same standing condition
after the session.
To ensure uniform representation and avoid biasing the clustering process toward par­
ticipants with more sessions, it was necessary to aggregate the data. For this purpose,
each participant’s features were averaged across all valid sessions, resulting in a single
observation per individual. This approach preserves the general physiological profile of
each participant while enabling consistent, participant­level clustering. Aggregating in this
manner also mitigates the risk of overfitting to session­specific fluctuations and ensures
that the clustering reflects stable, trait­like differences in autonomic function rather than
transient state­level variability.
To classify participants into distinct HRV­based subgroups, a multi­step clustering ap­
proach was employed. First, resting HRV features were extracted from the complete
RR­interval recordings for each session, encompassing the full duration of data collection
rather than a selected segment. This ensured that all available information from the mea­
surement period was incorporated into the analysis. Feature extraction was performed
using the hrv­analysis library, covering both time­ and frequency­domain metrics. The
dataset was cleaned and preprocessed by correcting inconsistent entries, encoding cat­
egorical variables, and removing outliers or short recordings. Continuous variables were
then standardized. Clustering was conducted on both the processed dataset and a prin­
cipal component (PC) dataset that captured over 90% of the variance, to allow for com­
parison. Several clustering algorithms were evaluated, including K­Means, DBSCAN, and
Agglomerative Clustering, with K­Means selected for its balance of interpretability and sta­
bility. The number of clusters was determined using multiple metrics: the Elbow Method,
Calinski­Harabasz Index, Davies­Bouldin Index, and Silhouette Scores. Since both the
processed and PC datasets yielded nearly identical clustering metrics, the processed
dataset was used for further clustering and analysis due to its greater interpretability.
Cluster validation included PCA visualization and comparison of HRV profiles to estab­
lished population norms from literature (Barak et al., 2010; Nunan et al., 2010), enabling
a physiological interpretation of each group’s characteristics.
5.2
Readiness score
A composite readiness score was developed to quantify an individual’s physiological re­
covery status based on both HRV metrics and subjective contextual inputs. The goal is
to capture daily fluctuations in autonomic function and overall well­being, providing de­
tails about fatigue, stress, and recovery. Higher scores indicate a more favorable and
recovered physiological state.
In addition to this work, readiness scores are frequently used in applied research and
consumer health platforms. In order to help with training and recovery choices, current
commercial wearables combine HRV, resting heart rate, sleep quality, and recent activity
load to calculate daily readiness or recovery scores [54]. To support follow­up and care
Digital Twin Mobile Health Application for Nervous System Response Using Integrative HRV Analysis
25
==PAGE:33==
decisions, multi­metric scores that combine HRV, resting heart rate, sleep efficiency, ac­
tivity load, and symptom ratings are monitored longitudinally (day­to­day or week­to­week)
in clinical and rehabilitation settings [55, 56]. This allows for the detection of deviations
from baseline and a measurement of recovery.
1­day rolling averages of key HRV metrics are used to reduce daily noise and empha­
size sustained physiological trends. These include time­domain (RMSSD, SDNN) and
frequency­domain (LF, HF, LF/HF ratio) features.
Subjective data from morning and evening surveys are incorporated to enrich the physi­
ological profile with psychological and behavioral context. Morning inputs include mood,
perceived stress, and sleep quality, while evening responses (shifted to represent the
previous day) cover mood, fatigue, stress, and recovery perception.
The score is computed in three main steps:
1. Feature standardization: Physiological and contextual features are converted to
z­scores to account for differences in scale and distribution. For HRV metrics, this
standardization (z­score calculation) is performed using reference population means
and standard deviations from published literature (Barak et al., 2010; Nunan et al.,
2010).
2. Normalization: Standardized values are passed through a sigmoid transformation
to map them into a [0,1] range. This ensures that each feature contributes propor­
tionally to the final score.
3. Aggregation: The normalized feature values are averaged to produce a single ag­
gregate readiness score for each day.
By combining physiological and contextual inputs, the readiness score provides a holistic
view of an individual’s daily state, supporting more personalized feedback and decision­
making.
5.2.1
Cluster­based readiness score zones
For potential users to better understand their daily readiness scores, participants were
grouped into clusters based on their baseline HRV measurements. This stratification
enabled the derivation of cluster­specific readiness zones, accounting for inter­individual
variability in autonomic tone and response patterns.
For each cluster, the mean and standard deviation of physiological daily readiness scores
were calculated. These statistics served as the basis for defining personalized thresholds
using z­scores within clusters. Each score was then classified into one of four readiness
zones according to its deviation from the cluster mean:
• Under­recovered: z < −1
• Mild fatigue: −1 ≤z < 0
• Recovered: 0 ≤z < 1
• Very recovered: z ≥1

5.5
Evaluation methodology
5.5.1
Cross­validation
Model evaluation was carried out using a participant­level, time­aware train­test split strat­
egy. Specifically, for each deployment (participant), the first 90% of observations were
used for training, and the remaining 10% for testing. This approach preserves temporal
order within individuals and mimics a realistic deployment scenario in which predictions
are made on future data.
For internal validation during hyperparameter optimization, a 5­fold TimeSeriesSplit
cross­validation was applied on the training set.
5.5.2
Statistical tests
To determine whether observed performance differences between models were statisti­
cally significant, paired two­tailed t­tests were conducted. For each test, model prediction
errors (squared residuals) were computed, and pairwise comparisons were performed
between model pairs (e.g., RF vs. SVR, Dummy vs. XGB).
The Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) was used as the primary evaluation metric due to
its interpretability and sensitivity to large errors. In addition to reporting absolute RMSE
values, relative improvements over the baseline model were also assessed.

Digital Twin Mobile Health application
This chapter presents the design methodology behind the digital twin mobile health ap­
plication as a proof­of­concept (PoC) to visualize and communicate the outputs of the
readiness model. Instead of implementing a fully functional system, the goal was to cre­
ate a user­focused prototype that demonstrates how insights derived from physiological
and contextual data, such as HRV, can be made interpretable and actionable for users in
alignment with two core functions of a digital twin: state estimation and predictive simu­
lation.
6.1
User­centered design approach
The application was developed using a User­Centered Design (UCD) methodology [58],
which emphasizes in understanding and aligning with user needs.
User insights were derived from the HRV and contextual data collected in the study de­
scribed in Chapter 4, in which two key user needs were identified based on the readiness
model detailed in Chapter 5:
• The need to receive an interpretable daily summary of physiological readiness with
actionable recommendations.
• The desire to explore how changes in lifestyle or behavior (e.g., sleep quality, stress,
workout intensity) could influence physiological state.
6.2
Design principles: usability heuristics
To ensure clarity, simplicity, and coherence in the user interface, the design of the ap­
plication was guided by a subset of Nielsen’s Ten Usability Heuristics for User Interface
Design [59], a widely accepted set of principles in user interface design:
• Visibility of system status: Each screen immediately displays the key information
(readiness score or simulation result), ensuring users are aware of the system’s
output without requiring too many additional steps.
• Match between the system and the real world: The design should speak the
users’ language by using phrases and concepts familiar to the user.
• Aesthetic and minimalist design: Each screen is focused on a single task, reduc­
ing cognitive load and visual complexity. Only the most relevant inputs and outputs
are presented.
• User control and freedom: In the simulation screen, users can freely adjust pa­
rameters and explore multiple scenarios without forcing them onto a single course
of actions.

refer to the high­level requirements for smart personalized healthcare systems outlined by

addressed through the application design.

Discussion
8.1
Study experiment
The 14­day study addresses the second research question of this thesis: How can a self­
reported passive and active data collection mechanism be implemented to provide a more
comprehensive HRV analysis?
Using the CARP infrastructure:
The study successfully demonstrated the feasibility of
collecting physiological and contextual data in a real­world setting using mobile sensing
and self­reporting. One of the main strengths of this methodology was the integration of
the CARP Studies app, which allowed for seamless and offline deployment of the study
protocol to participants’ mobile devices. This approach ensured that participants could fol­
low the experiment independently without the need for constant supervision, and enabled
structured data collection through multiple settings, including daily HRV measurements,
contextual surveys, and baseline tests.
However, while the app is robust in its design, several synchronicity issues occurred within
the CARP infrastructure, impacting reliability and data collection. For instance, due to
unknown bugs in the connection and authentication between the Studies app and the
CARP Web Services, a portion of the collected data was never uploaded, and due to the
protocol configuration, it was impossible to retrieve it in any other way.
Use of the Polar Verity Sense:
The integration of wearable sensors into the study pro­
vided access to physiological measurements, making them one of the most important
components of this thesis. The Polar Verity Sense was selected for its compatibility with
the CARP framework and its ability to capture raw PPI signals necessary for HRV com­
putation when worn correctly.
However, using a wearable sensor always introduces noise in some way due to sensitivity
to motion artifacts and misplacement, and it is a common problem acknowledged in the
mobile health field. Although techniques such as ectopic beat removal and interpolation
were applied, recordings taken during movement or improper sensor positioning were
still prone to errors. These limitations suggest a trade­off between accessibility and data
reliability in consumer­grade wearables.
Study protocol design and structure:
Reflecting on the study protocol, the structure
of daily tasks was designed to achieve data richness while taking participants’ time into
consideration. Collecting daily surveys along with HRV measurements at different points
throughout the day, allowed for a comprehensive view of physiological responses and
subjective reflections.
However, since the experiment relied on self­reporting and unsupervised measurements,
variability across how participants completed the tasks could not be controlled. For in­
stance, it was impossible to control wether a morning HRV was taken immediately upon
waking or after some activity. This affects the interpretability of the data and highlights
the challenge of balancing self­reporting health technologies with controlled experiments.

54

Reflection on the study experiment:
Overall, the methodology adopted for this study
demonstrated the benefits of digital phenotyping through third­party tools by enabling rich
data collection with minimal supervision, enabling the data analysis process that will be
discussed in the next section. The technical limitations and the unpredictability of self­
reported experiments are lessons that serve future iterations of mobile health studies and
emphasize the importance of designing a robust and scalable study protocol.
8.2
Data processing and analysis
This section represents the bridge between the first two research questions, by providing
a comprehensive HRV analysis that serves as the foundation for developing HRV and
contextual­based readiness models.
HRV metrics:
The HRV metrics collected across different contexts aligned with ex­
pected physiological patterns. As summarized in Table 7.2, parasympathetic indicators
such as RMSSD, SDNN, SD1, and SD2 were highest in morning and pre­workout record­
ings, reflecting a resting or recovered state. During workouts, these metrics decreased
considerably, shifting towards a stronger sympathetic system response as observed through
an increase in the LF/HF ratio. After­workout measurements indicated an autonomic re­
covery by slowly reversing towards a parasympathetic dominance. This response is con­
sistent with known ANS responses to physical stress and recovery [4], providing confi­
dence in the validity of the contexts and the data collected. An important consideration
when dealing with data processing of physiological signals, specially HRV, is to align the
selected metrics with the type of measurement and temporal analysis done, as not all
metrics have the same recommended measurement range (i.e., measurements can be
ultra­short­term, short­term or twenty four hour terms) [1]. In this case, metrics like DFA
and Total Power are considered twenty­four­hour term measurements, which doesn’t
align to the short­term measurements used across this study.
Use of rolling windows:
The addition of different rolling windows provided additional
insight into temporal dynamics of ANS responses. A 1­day window highlighted daily vari­
ability that appeared to align closely with daily ratings. If RMSSD dropped for a specific
day, the reported morning mood also dropped. This 1­day window is useful for analyzing
the relationships between how a participant feels and how close this matches their body’s
response, providing significant insight into how well a person adjusts and responds to
tasks like working out [4]. A 3­day window introduced enough smoothing that revealed
short­term trends of recovery, which are particularly useful when guiding decisions in en­
durance training [12]. For instance, if a person has had high intensity workouts for several
days in a row, this can highlight how well the body recovers and what kind of adjustments
can be made into the weekly routine. Finally, the 7­day window allowed for a longer­
term trend analysis, capturing gradual recovery patterns that would not be visible through
daily or short­term trends. This window is particularly useful to uncover declines in health,
sickness, or preparation for high­intensity events [26] (e.g., running or cycling races). As
the daily observations continue to grow and are not restricted to a 14­day period, more
window sizes can be considered to analyze even longer­term trends that could uncover
a whole new set of patterns such as chronological sickness or diseases.
Synthetic data generation:
The generation of synthetic HRV and contextual samples
using multivariate modeling and conditional sampling proved to be useful. The correlation
structure and distributions of the synthetic data closely aligns to the real observations and
Digital Twin Mobile Health Application for Nervous System Response Using Integrative HRV Analysis
55
==PAGE:63==
effectively retain plausible relationships between both HRV and contextual features. This
step effectively serves as a tool to simulate an increase in sample size and examine the
robustness of the model to a larger dataset [52].
However, it is an important limitation and reminder that these synthetic samples are not
physiologically or biologically grounded, and may not capture all plausible responses
given that it is set to be statistically similar to a small sample of data. Other methods
for synthetic data generation, such as machine learning methods, can be considered to
account for this variability and effectively capture more complex patterns within the data.
Reflection on data processing and analysis:
The results demonstrate that physiolog­
ical patterns can be observed through structured daily recordings. These patterns, espe­
cially when enriched with rolling trends and contextual information, present a promising
baseline for the development of readiness models. In this step, it is always relevant to
conduct a thorough feature engineering process, carefully considering the required and
applicable features, how to clean the data according to the type, and how to account for
missing data.
8.3
Nervous system profiling via clusters and readiness
zones
The four identified clusters represent distinct autonomic profiles, differentiated by rela­
tive levels of parasympathetic and sympathetic activity. Clusters characterised by higher
RMSSD, SDNN, and HFnu alongside lower resting heart rate and LF/HF ratio reflect more
favourable autonomic states, whereas the inverse pattern indicates reduced autonomic
fitness.
Readiness zones complement this trait­level classification by capturing short­term devia­
tions from an individual’s baseline. Integrating both perspectives allows for profiling that
distinguishes between long­term autonomic capacity and day­to­day physiological state.
This dual view supports clearer interpretation of HRV patterns, enabling differentiation be­
tween participants with similar acute states but differing baseline profiles, and vice versa.
8.4
Machine Learning pipeline
The machine learning pipeline developed for this thesis was designed to model day­to­day
variation in physiological readiness based on both HRV­derived features and contextual
survey data. This addresses the third research question of this thesis: How can an AI­
powered digital twin app improve the personalization of HRV­based readiness recommen­
dations?. Several core components were involved, including data preprocessing, feature
engineering, model selection, hyperparameter optimization, and statistical evaluation.
8.4.1
Hyperparameter optimization
As mentioned in Section 7.4.1, the Optuna framework was used to find the optimal hy­
perparameter combinations for each model. This framework was picked due to its high
modularity, ease of use, and dynamic search spaces. Furthermore, this library allows for
efficient search of hyperparameter spaces, compared to a randomized or standard grid
search, which can suffer from inefficiency in high­dimensional spaces and an inability to
prioritize promising regions of the search space.
The hyperparameter ranges or search spaces were chosen based on a trial and error
approach, meaning that each model was trained to see if for example a parameter was
Digital Twin Mobile Health Application for Nervous System Response Using Integrative HRV Analysis
56
==PAGE:64==
consistently on the extremes of its range, in which case the space was expanded. In ad­
dition, parameters like learning rate for XGBoost or MLP could be even smaller, however
the trade­off would be significantly increased training times. In all cases, hyperparameter
optimization led to noticeable improvements over default configurations.
8.4.2
Rationale for Model Selection
The models evaluated in this thesis were selected to represent a diverse range of algo­
rithmic paradigms to apply for predictive modelling of physiological and behavioural data.
The Dummy regressor served as a baseline for quantifying the added value of learned
models. Random Forest was included as a robust, non­parametric ensemble of decision
trees capable of capturing non­linear interactions with relatively low risk of overfitting. Sup­
port Vector Regression was chosen for its ability to model smooth, continuous functions
through flexible kernel mappings. Extreme Gradient Boosting was selected as a powerful
gradient­boosted tree method with proven performance on structured tabular datasets.
Finally, the Multi­Layer Perceptron was incorporated as a representative neural network–
based approach, capable of learning complex non­linear mappings between inputs and
outputs. Together, these models provide a broad methodological spectrum, enabling a
balanced comparison of ensemble methods, kernel methods, gradient boosting, and deep
learning for readiness score prediction.
8.4.3
Feature set and model combinations
The evaluation across multiple feature configurations revealed that both contextual and
physiological features contributed meaningfully to predictive accuracy. Notably, adding
morning and evening survey responses (mood, stress, recovery, fatigue) improved model
performance beyond physiological features alone, highlighting the relevance of subjective
context in readiness estimation. The best results were obtained when using the full feature
set (rolling HRV + all context) for most configurations. This was the expected behavior as
the readiness score itself was calculated using both the HRV and survey features.
Paired t­tests (Figure 7.12) between the original and synthetic datasets showed that all
models performed significantly better on the synthetic dataset, which lead to the notion of
training on this dataset and testing on the original. In addition, all models trained on the
synthetic data performed almost identically when compared to the baseline, which meant
that there was no clear choice as to which model to proceed with.
8.4.4
Final Mixed Model
Statistical testing using paired t­tests showed that differences between model perfor­
mances were in some cases significant (e.g., Random Forest vs. SVR), but not always so.
This may reflect the relatively small sample size or inherent variability in participant data.
Still, the improvements of all models over the dummy baseline support the hypothesis that
readiness is predictable from the chosen inputs.
In the mixed dataset setting, SVR achieved the lowest overall RMSE, closely followed by
MLP and XGBoost, with no statistically significant differences between these top three
models (p > 0.1 for all pairwise comparisons). MLP’s competitive performance highlights
the potential of neural network–based models to capture complex, non­linear interactions
in HRV and contextual data, while XGBoost’s strength lies in its gradient­boosted tree
framework, which is particularly effective for structured tabular datasets.
Despite these similarities in performance, SVR was ultimately chosen for the final train­
ing/testing analysis due to its relative simplicity, lower computational requirements, and

ease of interpretability compared to MLP and XGBoost. The superior performance of SVR
compared to tree­based models like Random Forest may stem from its ability to model
smooth, continuous functions, which is a good match for the relatively stable, gradual dy­
namics of physiological readiness signals. Nonetheless, while SVR outperformed other
models on average, it may still fail to capture abrupt physiological changes or outlier days

Furthermore, predictions at the individual level, visualized for selected participants (Figure

8.5
Digital Twin Mobile Health app
This proof­of­concept mobile application addresses the third research question of the the­
sis: How can an AI­powered digital twin app improve the personalization of HRV­based
readiness recommendations?
While the app presents a variety of functionalities, from data collection to scenario sim­
ulation, the personalization of predicted HRV­based readiness is present in the newly
included features: the Readiness Score and the What­If Simulation.
The Readiness Score screen allows users to interpret their physiological state not in iso­
lation, but within the context of a readiness zone shared by others with similar patterns.
Rather than encouraging users to chase a higher score, the interface promotes reflection
on behavior leading to recovery.
Suggestions are currently predefined for each zone, but this component offers rich poten­
tial for future development. Feedback could be expanded using expert­generated advice
or even personalized through large language models trained on physiological data and
health behavior.
On the other hand, the What­If Simulation screen adds an interactive feature that most
existing HRV­guided health tools lack. By allowing users to explore how changes in sleep,
stress, or training intensity might affect readiness, the app encourages deeper engage­
ment and builds self­awareness around lifestyle decisions.
The ANS is one of the most complex systems in the human body, and understanding even
a small portion of its behavior requires extensive research and expertise. This makes it
more important to recognize that most users require simple and explainable insights to
assess their physiological state and develop personalized habits. The additional features
implemented demonstrate how predictive and simulated insights can be meaningfully pre­
sented to users by aligning with usability heuristics [59] through a user­centric approach
and showing only the necessary information in an easily explainable manner.
Through the digital twin requirements [60], the app illustrates a clear path towards digital
twin systems in personalized health by considering an end­to­end flow. Built on top of the
DTU CARP Studies App infrastructure, the prototype is embedded in a robust and scal­
able ecosystem, which allows for future integration of real­time model inference without
requiring major architectural changes, paving the way towards a truly responsive digital

twin application capable of collecting data, connecting to wearable devices, computing
data­driven predictions, and scenario simulation.
Due to time constraints and the limited scope of the PoC, no usability testing or validation
with users was conducted. As a result, while the PoC adheres to design principles, it
remains untested with end users. This gap limits the ability to draw conclusions about
how effectively the application addresses key user needs. Additionally, using the DTU
CARP Studies app as a baseline introduces a dependency on an external architecture
which is not yet proven to support digital twin requirements. This could result in scalability
issues and a complete redesign on the required architecture. Despite these limitations,
the app successfully achieves its purpose as a PoC: to explore how a digital twin can be
represented through a mobile health app. It sets the baseline for future extensions that
could result in a full end­to­end implementation.

Conclusion
The increasing integration of consumer wearables with mobile health applications has
created an opportunity to monitor ANS activity in daily life. HRV is key for understanding
ANS activity, but its isolated interpretation is limited due to its sensitivity to physiological,
lifestyle, and environmental factors, calling for a need to move from an HRV­only analysis
towards a more adaptive and context­enriched approach.
This thesis was guided by three research questions. The first focused on how and which
contextual factors could be integrated with HRV to enrich interpretation. Through the
design of structured surveys along the study carried out for this thesis, it demonstrated that
contextual variables (e.g., mood, fatigue and perceived recovery levels) add meaningful
interpretation to an individual’s physiological state.
The second research question focused on how a self­reported passive and active data col­
lection mechanism could be implemented to provide a richer physiological analysis. The
14­day study, using the DTU CARP Studies App and the Polar Verity Sense, confirmed
the feasibility of implementing such a mechanism through unsupervised data collection
in diverse contexts (e.g., morning, evening, workouts). Challenges related to synchro­
nization, wearable placement, and participant adherence highlighted the importance of
ensuring a robust software infrastructure and a more controlled environment for future
deployments.
The third research question addressed the potential of an AI­powered digital twin to im­
prove HRV­based readiness recommendations. The machine learning models developed,
particularly the SVR model, successfully predicted daily readiness scores using both HRV
metrics and contextual data. The higher performance seen when using a context­enriched
feature set over HRV­only validated the hypothesis that context provides not only richer
but more accurate insights into the ANS state. Additionally, the PoC mHealth application
communicated how these models could be meaningfully shown to users through inter­
pretable readiness zones and simulations, laying the foundation for future real­time digital
twin applications.
From data collection to readiness visualization, this thesis provides a replicable and scal­
able framework for future health applications that aim towards a more proactive physio­
logical analysis.
9.1
Future work and recommendations
Several opportunities remain for future work that can be founded in the work done through­
out this thesis:
• Data collection scaling and advanced modeling: Conduct longer­term studies
with larger and more diverse populations to assess generalization through a variety
of demographics, fitness levels, and health conditions. This also enables a possible
implementation of more advanced modeling techniques, such as recurrent neural
networks, to effectively extract deeper patterns in ANS state.
• Machine learning pipeline: Future models could implement mechanisms for un­
certainty estimation and anomaly detection to handle abrupt physiological changes.
Personalization strategies, for example transfer learning or cluster­wise models,

==PAGE:68==
could improve generalization, while interpretability tools such as SHAP or GAMs
can enhance transparency.
• Mobile application development: Integrate real­time backend capabilities to pro­
vide instant feedback and readiness scores. Explore not only the DTU CARP frame­
work but also other digital phenotyping tools that can provide a richer user experi­
ence. Additionally, expand readiness zone­based suggestions with implementation
of Retrieval­Augmented Generation that integrates Large­Language Models with ex­
ternal sources (e.g., advices and suggestions from literature), to provide explainable
and interpretable suggestions to users.
• Ethical considerations: as the framework continues to expand, it is important to
revise ethical considerations for the data collection methods, and ensure a proper
study setup that considers all relevant regulations. The same applies to the model­
ing and application deployment if this was to become a real implemented system,
calling for a need on uncovering and understanding AI regulations.
By pursuing these recommendations, future work can move this proof­of­concept applica­
tion towards a complete digital twin system to monitor and improve readiness, recovery,
and overall well­being.

==PAGE:83==
The increasing integration of wearables and mobile applications have enabled continuous health
monitoring of autonomic nervous system (ANS) activity through non­invasive measures such as
heart rate variability (HRV). This thesis proposes a digital twin framework that integrates sensor­
derived HRV features with contextual information to provide a more comprehensive readiness­for­
activity assessment.
To enable this framework, a 14­day study was conducted using the DTU CARP Studies App and
the Polar Verity Sense sensor to collect passive physiological data and active survey responses on
mood, stress, sleep quality, fatigue, and recovery. HRV features were computed from collected mea­
surements, and data processing techniques such as rolling windows and synthetic data generation
were used to enhance and capture recovery trends.
Through Machine Learning approaches, this work defined HRV­based nervous system profiles and
predicted a composite readiness score from HRV metrics and contextual features. A Support Vec­
tor Regressor achieved the best performance and outperformed HRV­only baselines. A proof­of­
concept mobile application was developed to present these predictions in an interpretable manner,
where users can visualize and simulate readiness scores, and receive actionable feedback for health
improvement.
This thesis provides a replicable pipeline, from data collection to application deployment, that enables
future development of an adaptive digital twin system for health and performance monitoring.
