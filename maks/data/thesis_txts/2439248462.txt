V. 
Abstract 
Using macroalgae as bio-feedstock for the bio-based plastic industry is a developing process that 
possesses inherent comparative advantages with regard to its environmental impacts compared to 
using other bio-feedstock sources. Thus, research effort is considered necessary to improve its 
competitiveness and resolve the technical challenges that it is currently facing. Within this context, 
this thesis aims at improving existing knowledge on ecotoxicity impacts from metals found in 
macroalgae tissue to microbes used within bio-reactors for their fermentation. 
The basis for a novel impact pathway within LCA methodologies is provided according to a fate-
exposure-effect approach. Namely: microbial biota within a bio-reactor is exposed to metal that is 
bioaccumulated by macroalgae tissue from background sea water, which derives into potential 
ecotoxicity effects. This pathway can be further studied to incorporate microbial ecotoxicity in bio-
reactors within current LCA practices. 
Under this approach, two data sets have been analysed to evaluate levels of pollution of metals in 
macroalgae feedstocks against their potential ecotoxicological effects in biochemicals producing 
microbes: one concerning metal uptake by macroalgae, including measurements on algae tissue and 
background sea water; and a second one including metal ecotoxicity measurements on relevant 
microbial species used in biochemicals production processes. As a result of this analysis, it is 
concluded that there is basis for including microbial ecotoxicity from macroalgae feedstock as a 
relevant criterion within decision-making in the bio-based plastic industry. Moreover, input for the 
industry is obtained as direct recommendations and score tables that can be used under case-specific 
scenarios for macroalgae and microbial species selection. 
The conducted project should be regarded as a first iteration to the problem. Further work is required 
in order to refine the outcome of research and maximise input recommendations for the bio-based 
plastic industry.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Background 
1.1.1. A growing demand for bio-based plastics 
A global human population greater than ever at the beginning of XXI century and with no signs to 
halt its growth (UN 2015) has led to a situation where the natural environment, and everything that 
comes with it, is under great pressure. The rising of several well-known worldwide environmental 
problems such as Climate Change, an unprecedented biodiversity loss or the depletion of natural 
resources, to mention a few examples, is shaping our times. In fact, according to the Planetary 
Boundaries1 (SRC 2015), the Earth has exceeded its carrying capacity, beyond uncertainty, already 
with regards to biodiversity loss and the alteration of the nitrogen cycle. As a response to such a high 
impact situation, a variety of alternatives to conventional practices are being explored in an attempt 
to face today’s environmental challenges. Aiming to move from fossil-based to bio-based production 
of chemicals used in various plastics applications is one of the most well-known initiatives in this 
direction. 
Bioplastics is a broad term that comprises plastics that are either bio-based or biodegradable, or both 
at the same time. Bio-based plastics are those that are manufactured from a renewable source of 
biomass, e.g. sugar cane or corn; whereas biodegradability is a property of substances upon which 
they can decompose under set conditions at a reasonable pace (Bioplastics 2018) 
Climate Action as stated on the 17 Sustainable Development Goals proposed by the United Nations 
(UN 2018) is one of the starting points behind the rationale that policy-makers around the world are 
using to justify their promotion of bio-based plastics. However, these are foremost driven by raising 
oil prices and a strategic national need for security of supply (Hettenhaus 2006, Chiarakorn et al. 
2014). Additionally, the European Union (EU) argues its need to further develop its bio-economy, 
which “[...] encompasses the production of renewable biological resources and the conversion of 
these resources and waste streams into value-added products, such as food, feed, bio-based products 
and bioenergy” (CoR 2012, p.6). The reasons behind this positioning are its potential to play a leading 
role in the emerging high-tech and high-value industry sectors that it comprises, with all of the 
strategic advantage and economic profit that it would lead to (Ronzon et al. 2017). As a result, 
European policy plans, such as the EU Circular Economy Action Plan (CORDIS 2017) or Europe 
2020 (Ronzon et al. 2017) among many others, define bioplastics as a key sector to be developed.  
Intensive policy-making and public funding within the EU has led to major industry advances of bio-
based plastics with regard to end-product technical properties. This, in turn, makes them increasingly 
attractive as alternatives to fossil-based plastics, consequently resulting on a market share growth of 
more than 20% on a yearly basis (CEBR 2015).

Overall, it can be seen how governmental actions aim at improving the economic viability of bio-
based plastics by means of technological advancement and public funding. This approach, however, 
lacks of relation to the main reason that lays behind the promotion of bio-based plastics, which is 
after all a political positioning favouring more sustainable products, and can thus deviate. In order to 
correct this situation, the EU is developing an Eco-label strategy for bio-based products. Under this 
strategy, bio-based products are requested to provide Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) evidence 
that they perform better than conventional alternatives in case-specific environmentally relevant 
categories in order to earn Eco-labels (Dammer 2016). Bio-based plastics are not, however, part of 
the range of products that have already been addressed under this approach (EC 2017), and currently 
only certifications accounting for the bio-based carbon content share of the end product are available. 
This situation is arguably a consequence of the fact that there is still a very uneven distribution of 
standardised practices around the world (Bioplastics 2016).  
Still, LCIA as a tool to assess the environmental performance of bio-based plastics and to compare it 
to conventional practices is already an established methodology from which a significant amount of 
literature is available (Hottle et al. 2013). Research consistently shows that bio-based options might 
indeed perform better than their counterparts when analysing Global Warming Potential (GWP) or 
depletion of fossil resources, for instance. This advantage, however, comes as a trade-off with greater 
environmental costs in other typical areas of study in LCIA assessments, such as ocean acidification, 
eutrophication of the marine environment or human toxicity (Hottle et al. 2013). Main issues of 
concern with regard to the environmental impact of bio-based plastics are mostly linked to the 
production of bio-feedstock (Hottle et al. 2013), and include2:  
• Pressure over freshwater resources (Buschmann et al.). 
• Green-House Gas (GHG) emissions from change of land use that might even harness the 
claimed carbon neutrality of the end-product (Fargione et al. 2008). 
• A strong need for pesticides (Tolinski 1970) and fertilisers (Buschmann et al.). 
• Resource competition for arable land with the food industry (Zhuang and Herrgard 2014). 
Hence, bio-based plastics are seen as an attractive emerging sector with promising sustainability 
connotations by relevant decision-makers; yet there is room for substantial improvement on their 
overall environmental performance and how it is assessed. A strong point of debate remains, which 
type of feedstock should be preferably chosen over the others in each context, as several important 
aspects need to be considered. That includes how ideal a type of feedstock is for bio-refining purposes 
against how much it interferes with other production systems, how large its impacts are in the 
environment, or how well developed related technologies are. 
1.1.2. Macroalgae as an emerging feedstock for bio-based plastics 
Algae are a photosynthetic division of aquatic organisms that do not follow a single evolutionary 
path, and hence there is a high degree of variability within the range of characteristics that define 
them. Algae are mainly differentiated from aquatic plants, such as seagrasses or mangroves, because 
they do not have a clearly differentiated structure (e.g. roots, leaves, stems). Algae can be further

subdivided into microalgae and macroalgae, depending on whether they are single cells or 
multicellular organisms, which have more complex leafy or blade shapes and are generally visible to 
the naked eye3 (Barsanti and Gualtieri 2014). Macroalgae4 include algae from 4 different phyla i.e. 
Rhodophyta (or red algae), Ochrophyta (or brown algae), Chlorophyta (or green algae), and the less 
common Cyanophyta (or blue-green algae) which are all easily differentiated due to their respective 
pigmentations from where their common names come from (Diaz-Pulido and McCook 2008).  
Over the recent years, there has been a growing interest on macroalgae as feedstock for bio-refining 
processes, including, but not limited to, the production of bio-based plastics (Jung et al. 2013). Several 
advantages have been identified when comparing macroalgae to terrestrial bio-feedstocks: 
• Due to a higher photosynthetic efficiency, aquatic autotrophic biomass is up to 4 times more 
intrinsically productive than any terrestrial autotrophic biomass (Aresta et al. 2005). 
Moreover, growth is fast and continuous, without being affected by seasonal variations 
(Buschmann et al. 2017). 
• In addition, macroalgae farming does not exert pressure over freshwater resources and arable 
land availability. Consequently, it is not in competition with terrestrial food production 
systems relying on freshwater and arable land, such as crops or cattle (Zabochnicka-Swiatek 
2010). 
• Different algae species can grow in different areas, and algae as a group are ubiquitous to 
most of the coastal water ecosystems worldwide. Thus, security of supply for nations is 
extremely high  (Barsanti and Gualtieri 2014). 
• Using macroalgae as bio-feedstock also eradicates the need for fertilisers required in the 
production of other bio-feedstocks, as seas are much richer in nutrients than arable land 
(Buschmann et al. 2017). Moreover, pesticides are rarely used (Kraan 2013). 
• Furthermore, macroalgae farming is being explored as a mechanism of bio-removal of 
nutrients and potentially harmful substances that could contribute to solve sea water 
eutrophication and marine ecotoxicity problems (Michalak and Chojnacka 2009) (Suutari et 
al. 2017). 
Microalgae can compare to macroalgae in all of the above stated advantages over terrestrial bio-
feedstock and, in fact, most of research on algae as bio-feedstock for bio-refining purposes up to date 
has focused on microalgae (Yun et al. 2014). However, they are much more difficult to harvest and 
dewater (Rajendran et al. 2012), which has a considerable impact on its associated costs. Therefore,

Prior to discussing disadvantages when comparing macroalgae to other types of bio-feedstock for 
bio-based plastic production, it is deemed necessary to provide a few notions on how bio-feedstocks 
are processed within the bio-based plastics industry. It should be noted at this point, though, that it is 
not the intention of this thesis to provide a detailed and exhaustive technical description of these 
processes: 
• Bio-feedstocks, regardless of their composition or origin, are meant to provide available 
sugars which can be further processed into end-products by different mechanisms, mainly 
biological, chemical or thermochemical (Taylor et al. 2015). This study only considers the 
biological mechanisms, which are the most common by volume of production (Taylor et al. 
2015). 
• Biological processing of sugars into bio-based plastics is done via fermentation by certain 
bacterial and fungal species. The specificities of each single pathway into their respective 
resulting plastics are not included within this report; however, a highly relevant publication 
describing best practices up to date was executed by Straathof (2013). 
• How readily available sugars are within a type of bio-feedstock is one of the main drivers 
characterising its suitability to produce bio-based plastics, as this determines how much pre-
treatment is needed (Taylor et al. 2015). 
• Macroalgae feedstock does not have a well-established methodology for pre-treatment before 
sugar fermentation, as it is not yet used at an industrial scale and related research is ongoing. 
Still, literature indicates that there is need for washing, drying and milling (Adams et al. 2015), 
and also pre-treatment steps aiming at the decomposition of cellulose, hemi-cellulose and 
other types of complex carbohydrates present in macroalgae into readily available sugars 
(Taylor et al. 2015). The decomposition of complex carbohydrate from macroalgae bio-
feedstock is still being studied, yet literature indicates that main pathways would include 
either hydrolysis or further microbial fermentation (Jung et al. 2013). 
This said, disadvantages to the usage of macroalgae as bio-feedstock for bio-based plastic production 
compared to other sources are:  
• Firstly, the uncertainties and lack of technical development that are inherent to any novel idea: 
bio-based plastics are still an emerging sector, which implies uncertainty with regard to its 
prospective market share (CEBR 2015). Also, in particular for macroalgae, they are not yet 
used at an industrial scale in this sector. Industrial macroalgae farming exists, mainly for 
human or cattle food consumption, and also for biofuels production. Still, its overall 
production volume is five orders of magnitude lower than that of terrestrial crops (Buschmann 
et al. 2017). This difference of magnitude shows how macroalgae as a bio-feedstock is not 
only small in terms of relative output volume with regard to the bio-based plastic industry but 
also in relation to any other sector where they are used. 
• Secondly, the composition of macroalgae has a much lower fraction of cellulose and 
hemicellulose available compared to other bio-feedstocks, including food crops, paper pulp, 
agricultural residues and forestry. The fraction of cellulose and hemicellulose in a bio-
feedstock source is regarded as a measure of the likely bio-available sugars for fermentation.

Arguably, macroalgae as an emerging feedstock for bio-based plastic production is indeed worth 
additional research and technology optimization efforts.

1.1.4. Identifying a knowledge gap 
Potential toxicity of chemical substances to living organisms and the effects that they can cause to 
human beings and ecosystems has been a widely studied topic over the last decades. Such is the 
concern about human toxicity and ecotoxicity effects of chemicals that in the present day entire public 
institutions such as ECHA are solely dedicated to ensuring its safe usage (ECHA 2018). 
With regard to ecotoxicity, chemical pollution can be harmful at different ecosystem levels. 
Accordingly, empirical data describing ecotoxicological effects is generally available for a range of 
living organisms throughout the trophic chain, including microorganisms. As a result, there is ample 
literature showing that microorganisms can indeed be sensitive to the harmful effects of chemical 
pollution (Zare et al. 2015, Ezemaduka et al. 2018). 
On the other side, chemical pollution can bio-accumulate in the biota. In fact, there is also plenty of 
literature dedicated to monitor concentrations of chemical pollutants throughout the different levels 
of the trophic chain. In particular, several sources can be listed where chemical bio-accumulation in 
macroalgae is reported (Montone et al. 2001, Seisuma et al. 2011, Bonanno and Di Martino 2016). 
By linking the above provided sources, it can be derived that microbes used in fermentation processes 
within bio-reactors can be harmed by chemicals that accumulate in algae tissue, when macroalgae is 
used as feedstock for bio-based plastic production. 
This entire impact pathway6 needs to be modelled if these impacts should be considered in evaluating 
biochemicals production in a life cycle perspective but, in fact, existing literature reveals that the 
mentioned impact pathway is to the present date completely missing in LCIA (Chen et al. 2016, van 
Oirschot et al. 2017).  
Acknowledging that LCA as a methodology is constantly being updated, a reason that can be provided 
as an explanation to this particular knowledge gap is that, in general ecotoxicity, overall impact of 
chemicals on the environment is studied, whereas for the bio-refining industry this approach needs to 
be adapted to cover single microorganism and macroalgae species. 
Nevertheless, this missing impact pathway is a priori considered to be relevant because it can affect 
environmental performance of macroalgae as feedstock for bio-refining processes, basically because 
of two different, arguably opposed to each other, reasons:  
• On one hand, removal of pollutants from the aquatic environment occurs when macroalgae 
are harvested, which undoubtedly will have a positive impact on the media where pollution is 
being removed. 
• On the other, microorganisms used in the bio-refining processes involved will be exposed to 
these pollutants, consequently suffering from adverse effects.  
The impacts of this second aspect can transcend the environmental dimension inherent to LCIA 
purposes. Adverse effects in the bio-refining microbiota might be translated into process efficiency

1.2. Aim and Objectives 
Taking into consideration the context introduced within the previous section, a specific research 
question for this project is drawn as:  
“What are the potential impacts that toxic substances found in macroalgae can have on 
microorganisms involved in their bio-refining for bio-based plastic production?” 
The main goal of the present thesis is therefore to explore how chemical substances present in 
macroalgae can contribute to ecotoxicological impacts on microorganisms applied in bio-based 
plastic production processes as a potentially relevant impact pathway to be considered in Life Cycle 
Assessment. In order to achieve this main goal, three specific objectives have been defined: 
• To assess contamination levels of chemical pollutants in macroalgae used as feedstock for 
bio-based plastic production, with focus on metals as a well-known environmental 
contaminants group; 
• To explore the potential ecotoxicological impact of contaminated macroalgae on 
microorganisms used in biochemical production processes; and 
• To provide initial recommendations on the performance of different macroalgae-
microorganism combinations for biochemicals production, as well as to identify further 
research needs to include ecotoxicity of microorganisms used for biochemicals production as 
an impact pathway into Life Cycle Assessment. 
Addressing these specific objectives can contribute to optimizing the assessment of the environmental 
performance of bio-based plastic applications using macroalgae as feedstock, and simultaneously can 
help to optimize the selection of macroalgae and microorganism species in the biochemicals 
production processing in support of increasing its economic viability.

2. Methodology 
The work that has been performed within this thesis is structured as follows. First, a data collection 
step provides experimental data from previous research on metal pollution levels in macroalgae and 
ecotoxicological effects of metal pollution in microorganisms. The data from these two sets is then 
analysed, mainly to identify trends, discuss some aspects with regard to its variability and explore the 
limitations of the data sets compared to the data that would have ideally been gathered. Following to 
this first data analysis, the data sets are integrated within the fate-exposure-effect chain model 
described in USEtox, which serves as a basis for the thesis and is explained in detail under this section. 
Finally, heat maps are built with the resulting output from implementing the gathered data sets on the 
given chain model. These heat maps, along with impact scores from the fate-exposure-effect model, 
set the basis for recommendations to the bio-based plastic industry as defined within the goals of this 
thesis. 
The methodology section now goes through each and every of the steps mentioned on this 
introductory paragraph. 
2.1. Data collection 
Two main data sets have been gathered, which directly align with the first two specific objectives that 
have been stated under the previous section: 
•  Data set on metal pollution levels in macroalgae tissue, including background metal 
concentration measurements in sea water. 
• Data set on ecotoxicological effects of metal pollution in microbial organisms used in 
biochemical processes within the bio-based plastic industry. 
It should be highlighted that no laboratory experimentation was carried out during this project. 
Instead, data collection is focused on available data from previous measurements. Therefore, data 
acceptability criteria have been established to analyse the reliability of the sources where data points 
were gathered from: sources have been considered acceptable only when coming from peer-reviewed 
data base search engine Scopus, or from well-known public institutions or international bodies, such 
as the European ECHA. In the particular case of ECHA, it has a reliability score for the sources that 
it incorporates. Consequently, sources within ECHA database that were given a score of 3 (not 
reliable) or 4 (not assignable) were excluded from the final data sets. 
Specificities for each data set are further discussed within this section; however, no other filters have 
been applied systematically, such as time or space bonded restrictions to discard sources which are 
deemed to be too old or from non-representative locations.

2.1.1.1. 
Metal content in macroalgae tissue 
Metal content in macroalgae tissue is measured as a concentration, and expressed as mg/kg dry 
weight. In the exceptional cases where the original data has been found as concentration in wet 
weight, a factor of 0.2 is applied, acknowledging that dry matter is on average 20% of the wet  
Macroalgae as a potential type of feedstock for bio-based plastic production is, ultimately, the 
research field in which this thesis is developed. Therefore, only sources regarding measurements in 
tissue from macroalgae individuals are considered relevant.  
Earlier during this research, the possibility of using data from other trophic level species with some 
adjustment factors was considered. This is a common practice within ecotoxicity studies concerning 
bio-accumulation patterns within an ecosystem. However, this practice was later disregarded as the 
data pool for macroalgae species with regard to metal traces content was found to be sufficient. By 
doing so, the data set is limited to measurements that are closer to what actually is being studied. 
On another matter, macroalgae are ubiquitous in both freshwater and saltwater bodies. However, only 
macroalgae from saltwater bodies are considered within the scope of this thesis, as metal speciation 
can vary highly between the two types of waterbodies. In addition, focus is placed on saltwater species 
in an attempt to increase the potential impact of this thesis, as saltwater bodies are dominant 
worldwide.  
Finally, it is again highlighted that there is no industrial facility that yet utilized macroalgae as 
feedstock for bio-based plastic production. In consequence, the list of selected macroalgae species is 
not narrowed down to a few, but, instead, any saltwater macroalgae species on which data is found is 
considered relevant for this thesis. 
2.1.1.2. 
Metal content in background sea water 
Metal content in background sea water is measured as a concentration, and expressed as mg/L. 
Apart from metal content data in macroalgae tissue, which is more directly related to the goal of 
assessing pollution levels in macroalgae, metal concentration measurements in background sea water 
are also considered relevant. Chemical pollution uptake by the biota has generally been linked to 
background concentrations and bio-availability. 
There is no yet an established classification of the different fractions of trace metals in sea water 
through academic literature. This is so because a classification that is purely based on size does not 
reflect the nature of the particles present in sea water containing metal, yet a classification based on 
the nature of these particles risks not being exhaustive (Haack 2013). 
For this thesis, a classification proposed by Dong et al. (2015) is used. This classification is based on 
the nature of each fraction of metal content, and it is chosen because some understanding of the 
differences between fractions is needed to understand the rationale behind this work. 
Within the chosen classification, the total metal content in sea water is understood as every mole of 
a given metal element, regardless of its oxidation state, found on sea water. This total metal content 
can be further subdivided into three fractions: 
• Suspended Particulate Matter-bonded (SPM-bonded) metal, which is particulate, and can be 
filtered out of a water sample by means of a conventional 0.45 µm filter. 
==PAGE:21==
Analysing toxicity for bio-chemical producing organisms 
August, 2018 
 
 
 
 
 
Raül López i Losada 
 
21 
• Truly dissolved metal, which corresponds to metal ions and molecules smaller than 1nm. 
• Dissolved Organic Carbon-bonded (DOC-bonded) metal, which is mostly colloidal (i.e. sizes 
ranging from 1µm to 1nm). Its lowest molecular fraction weight, though, is still smaller than 
1nm. Consequently, a fraction of the DOC-bonded metal is in the same size ranges that what 
is defined as truly dissolved metal. Thus, DOC-bonded metal and truly dissolved metal 
cannot be completely split apart by means of physical filtration methods. 
Making this differentiation is meaningful, as there is scientific consensus showing that only free ions 
and smaller molecules are indeed bioavailable for the biota to uptake (Dong et al. 2015), whereas the 
rest of the metal is already fixed to other compartments of the considered system. Thus, taking into 
account total metal concentrations when considering uptake of trace metals by macroalgae would be 
adding unnecessary noise, as only truly dissolved metal is actually meaningful for this purpose. And 
on top of that, ratios between total and truly dissolved metal vary depending on local conditions. 
This said, results from modelling simulations performed by Dong et al. (2015) and their comparison 
with previous work show that DOC-bonded metal is found in a lower order of magnitude than truly 
dissolved metal and SPM-bonded metal. For this reason, this work differentiates only between 
dissolved metal and total metal concentrations in sea water. This simplification is, indeed, very 
helpful: as pointed out on the above classification, SPM-bonded metal can be separated by means of 
conventional physical filtration, which is a commonly established practice for metal content 
measuring. Thus, simplification is used as a means to relate existing literature to the data that would 
ideally be needed for this work. 
A question that can rise following this thread is why should metal uptake by macroalgae be analysed 
as a function of metal content in sea water, instead of other relevant compartments such as sediment 
or soil. This choice is made because of the non-differentiated nature of macroalgae. In sea water 
plants, metal uptake could be expected to happen mostly through its roots, and thus mostly in relation 
to metal content in soil. Macroalgae, on the other hand, do not have differentiated working parts and 
thus it is easier to relate its metal uptake to the compartment that surrounds them the most, which is 
sea water. This is, however, a choice that needs to be made to limit the scope of the study to a 
reasonable length. It is acknowledged, though, that there are as well other studies which are trying to 
establish relations between metal content in macroalgae and in sediment, for instance (Gubelit et al. 
2016).

2.3. Integration of data under fate-exposure-effect approach proposed 
by USEtox 
In LCIA methodology, substance-specific characterisation factors are used to compare the impact 
that each substance has under a specific impact category. By doing so, those impacts become 
comparable and can be added up into total impacts for a certain product or activity. USEtox is a model 
aiming at providing substance-specific characterisation factors for human toxicity and freshwater 
ecotoxicity categories, based on scientific consensus over best application practices (USEtox 2018). 
For freshwater ecotoxicity impacts, the USEtox model proposes an approach based on a combination 
of fate, exposure and effect stages, which in the end results in an overall ecotoxicity characterisation 
factor for each substance (Rosenbaum et al. 2008). This approach has been widely used for 
characterising general toxicity impacts to freshwater ecosystems. On contrast, this project focuses on 
certain types or organisms and individual species, for which reason this method needs to be adapted 
to be able to cover the mentioned goals. A description on how these three stages are conceived under 
USEtox and how they are adapted to this project follows: 
2.3.1. Environmental fate of metals 
Fate, or more precisely environmental fate of chemicals, is a scientific term that relates to the 
distribution that substances reach when released into the environment. As a common practice in 
USEtox and other LCIA ecotoxicity characterization models, the environment is divided into different 
compartments according to its characteristics: biota, sediment or freshwater, to list a few examples. 
A first approximation to the environmental fate of a substance is obtained when implementing its 
inherent physic-chemical properties in fairly simple models such as Mackay’s (Mackay 2001). 
However, chemical fate is a complex field of study, for which modelling can be as refined and 
compartmentation as case-specific as required.  
With regard to LCA, chemical fate is generally studied at a low resolution level. The holistic approach 
of this methodology comes as a trade-off to the level of detail achieved. The location of the emission 
sources is often unknown, which makes detailed spatial analysis unrealistic, or directly not 
meaningful. Hence, models relying mostly on physic-chemical properties of given substances are 
preferred. 
Relating to the goals of this project, it is relevant to focus on the fate of trace metals in salt-water

2.3.2. Ecological exposure via accumulation in macroalgae 
Ecological exposure addresses the question of how much of the potentially toxic substance is actually 
interacting with the studied organisms. This step is meant as a linking bridge between fate and effect.  
Its format can vary a lot from case to case, as it depends on both the nature of the studied organisms 
and the mechanisms involved for the substance to show ecotoxicological effects upon it.

macroalgae are used as feedstock. Subsequently, relevant microbial species within the bio-reactor are 
used to ferment the sugars present in feedstock. Therefore, exposure relates to trace metal 
concentrations within the bio-reactor, where they can affect microbial fermentation processes. Trace 
metal levels within the bio-reactor differ from metal concentrations as directly measured from 
macroalgae tissue because:

2.3.3. Ecotoxicological effects on bio-refining microorganisms 
The effect step is meant to assess the adverse consequences that an organism experiences because of 
the toxic substance to which it is exposed, at the relevant concentrations11. 
In order to be able to quantify effects of substances in an ecotoxicity experiment, first thing to do is 
to select an objectively measurable property of the organism that can be used as a proxy of the 
malfunctioning of the organism. This property is called the endpoint of the experiment. 
Endpoints are generally divided into chronic and acute. The first relate to non-lethal effects over the 
lifetime of the organism such as reduced fertility, and the second, to a more or less imminent death 
following the administration of the substance.

Once an endpoint is chosen, an ecotoxicity experiment commonly focuses on obtaining a toxicity 
curve. Toxicity curves, also called dose-effect curves, establish a relation between a percentage of 
the effect that has been achieved (from a total maximum) for each concentration that has been tested 
in the relevant organisms.  
EC50s, which are the type of data points gathered under the data set on ecotoxicological effects of 
metal pollution on relevant microbes, are particularly well-known points within these curves. They 
relate to the concentration of the pollutant at which 50% of the exposed organisms show a particular 
effect. Preference for this point instead of others such as EC10 or No-Effect Concentrations is 
explicitly reported by experts on ecotoxicity working within the LCA framework (Rosenbaum et al. 
2008). 
EC50s data is then treated under the approach applied by Rosenbaum et al. (2008) in order to provide 
harmonisation that lays a basis for comparison. In order to ease the reading and avoid repetition, this 
approach is further detailed while applying it into the collected data set under the results section. 
Main outputs from this step to be discussed are interspecies variability, effects of considering different 
endpoints and a hierarchical ranking of metal ions according to its ecotoxicological effects to the 
studied species.

As earlier introduced, data analysis started by subdividing the data set on metal pollution according 
to the measured fraction of metal in background sea water. It has also been pointed out that this thesis 
differentiates between data points where total content of metal in sea water is analysed from those 
where only the dissolved fraction is measured. This classification is not as straightforward as it might 
seem. 
When reviewing literature, it was most of the times not completely clear whether measurements 
related to total or dissolved metal concentration, as authors would refer to expressions such as "metal 
content in sea water". Additionally, the methodology sections of the articles where reviewed to be 
able to tell both fractions apart depending on how filtration was applied. This was also difficult: 
sometimes filtration was a very small step within the methods that it was hardly mentioned, and other 
times unconventional methods of preservation were used that made it difficult to identify which metal 
fraction was being kept. An overall concern around the correctness of the data classification process 
led to the perception that expert reassurance was needed. Therefore, communication by email was 
established with the correspondent authors to check which fraction of metals they had indeed 
measured. 
Six articles were split four and two into dissolved or total metal according to own judgement on how 
the authors were proceeding. After four of them replied, the articles were split again. This time, five 
of them corresponding to total metal and only one to the dissolved fraction. Articles by Encina-
Montoya et al. (2017) and Wahbeh et al. (1985) where then reviewed again and, with the expertise 
gained from the other author’s replies, they were reassigned as well.

3.2. Assessing metal ecotoxicity to chemical-producing organisms 
As introduced within the methodology section, metal ecotoxicity is assessed by analysing EC50 data 
points for relevant microbial species (and related species from the same genii) and metal ions.  
Data points for this section have been found to be mostly related to sources which test the suitability 
of particular microbial species to act as bio-indicators of metal concentrations in samples. This is so 
because of the need to develop easy, reliable and fast experiments to determine metal concentrations 
in water samples in situ. 
Data corresponding to several different endpoints has been found. No selection criterion has been 
applied to discriminate endpoints and, instead, all of them have been accepted. These endpoints, 
which can be worded differently from source to source, have been classified under aggregated 
categories according to their nature. An overview of this process is presented in Figure 10. 
It can be observed how data variability is generally under two orders of magnitude when considering 
individual metal species. Also, endpoints related to microbial respiration effects tend to show higher 
EC50s, or said in a different way: microbial respiration shows lower sensitivity to metal toxicity.  
However, the variety of endpoints makes difficult its usage for a direct comparison between values: 
for instance, the ranges of EC50s per each metal makes it imposible to rank them according to their 
toxicity effects. This fact sets the basis that justifies their further processing under the approach 
proposed by (Rosenbaum et al. 2008)to obtain comparable data.

• Ecotoxicity assays are often very time consuming, which make them expensive and 
therefore relatively scarce. 
• Single species are not the main focus of ecotoxicity assays, which instead try to obtain 
the magnitude of the impact that a certain pollutant would have when released in the 
environment at relevant concentrations. This means that a few selected species from 
different trophic levels are generally assumed to be a good proxy for the whole 
ecosystem. Therefore, ecotoxicologists do not aim at producing data which has a wide 
range with regard to the number of species tested. This approach is, to a certain extent, 
also due to the ethical implications of ecotoxicity testing on living beings. 
• The microbial level of ecosystems is usually overlooked, when compared to other 
trophic levels, as emphasis on ecotoxicity is commonly placed on bioaccumulation 
and bio-magnification issues, which worsen on higher trophic levels. Also, there is a 
trend on ecosystem ecotoxicity assays where microbial flora is tested as a single entity, 
thus providing data that is not differentiated per single species, which is of no use 
under the approach followed under this project. 
This said, a comprehensive literature review performed by Ansari et al. (2004) reported that the heavy 
metals of greatest concern in aquatic ecosystems were Cu, Zn, Cd, Hg and Pb, all of them covered 
under the gathered data set. This indicates, at least with regard to metal ions, the existance of selection 
criteria when performing toxicity experimentation. Thus, data gaps might not be so relevant if it 
happens that the range of available data is biased towards focusing on the really problematic ions.

Overall, the data set provides data which is not directly comparable (different endpoints) and which 
does not offer any clear trends. The approach introduced by Rosenbaum et al. (2008) is therefore

explanation on how this approach is applied, along with the results derived from it at each step. 
Firstly, the different nature of endpoints is acknowledged by grouping them into two categories: 
chronic and acute ecotoxicity. Endpoints are harmonised by a *0,5 multiplying factor when acute. 
Then, a mean EC50 for each species is obtained by calculating the geometric mean over data points 
from its different endpoints.  
Table 11 provides an overview of these results, where mean EC50s have been obtained for the 
different species studied, taking into account the different endpoints as found in the original sources. 
Also, results for this table have been converted into logarithmic scale, so that a heat map can be built 
that emphasises differences in the scale of the EC50 values across the different species for each metal 
ion.  
As a general rule, a geometric mean is used instead of other types of means to give more weight to 
more sensitive species or data points.

3.3. Evaluating potential impact to the bio-plastic industry given 
ranges of pollution and ecotoxicological effects 
Following a fate-exposure-effect pathway both fate (i.e. metal uptake and concentration in 
macroalgae) and effect (i.e. ecotoxicity of metal ions towards microorganisms) have been studied in 
the previous sections. Exposure has been defined during the methodology section as that step in which 
macroalgae tissue containing metal traces gets in contact with the fermenting microorganisms in a 
reactor within a bio-refining facility. The exposure step is only accounted for by means of a factor 
considering the dilution ratio of macroalgae tissue within the bio-reactor. 
Figure 11 illustrates the results of applying a fate-exposure-effect pathway in order to evaluate the 
potential ecotoxicity of metal elements in macroalgae to microbes used in their fermentation. Both 
data sets are taken into consideration. Concentrations in macroalgae tissue have been converted to 
exposure concentration in bio-reactor (and referred to in the plot as “algae mix”). A normal 
distribution of data points from minimum and maximums measured has been assumed in order to 
draw percentiles. Several important points can be extracted from Figure 11: 
• Metal elements are plotted in the order of maximum concentrations found in 
macroalgae tissue. Out of the 24 metals studied in this project, no experimental 
ecotoxicity data on relevant microbial species has been found for the first nine most 
frequent. Some of them, such as Fe and Ca are generally considered to be essential 
metals (i.e. necessary for organisms), whereas others such as Na, Mg and K are 
extremely ubiquitous in the sea water media. The fact that ecotoxicity data has not 
been found on them could easily be explained from a selective approach to studying 
ecotoxicity in order to optimise resources, meaning that it is highly unlikely that these 
pose a threat to organisms, unless their concentrations are exceptionally high. 
• In all the cases where EC50 data is available both for microorganisms and macroalgae, 
the former are in the scale of one order of magnitude lower than the latter, except in 
the case of Cd, where both EC50s are similar. This is relevant because it shows how 
there is a potential range of concentration of metals within which ecotoxicological 
effects on macroalgae are much lower than on the microbial species considered for its 
fermentation. Therefore, it is realistic to picture a scenario where macroalgae can be 
farmed regularly but where its fermentation appears to be problematic or directly 
unachievable. 
• Following on the previous point, all of the EC50 values for macroalgae are orders of 
magnitude higher than maximum concentrations found in water. In contrast, with the 
given assumptions, several metals are found in maximum concentrations which are 
very close or over their respective EC50s, namely: Cr, Cu and Zn. Moreover, Pb, As 
and Ni show EC50s that are still at a distance of only one order of magnitude from

4. Discussion 
Within this section, results from this thesis are reflected upon with regard to its further implications 
and limitations. Discussion is structured around the different topics considered to be relevant. 
Namely: the bio-based plastic industry and their potential to use macroalgae as feedstock for 
production, the applicability of the fate-exposure-effect approach explored within this thesis and its 
further development under LCA methodologies, the limitations of the approach and the gathered data, 
and recommendations for future research.  
4.1. Bio-based plastic production from macroalgae feedstock 
Figure 11 provides an overview of implementing a fate-exposure-approach model to relate metal 
content in macroalgae tissue to toxic effects in microbes meant to ferment their sugars. From it, it can 
be extracted how ranges of pollution that reach the bio-refining reactor under maximum cap 
assumptions are similar in magnitude to concentrations that have been found to be harmful to the 
microbial organisms on whose wellbeing depends the proper functioning of the facility. Therefore, 
this scenario does indeed justify paying attention to metal pollution introduced within bio-reactors by 
macroalgae feedstock. 
With regard to metal uptake by macroalgae, two main points were covered: 
• First, to provide ranges of metal concentrations in macroalgae tissue, as a key step to link 
actual levels of metal pollution potential in macroalgae tissue to potential effects in relevant 
microbes. 
• Secondly, to explore variability of the data set due to several factors. Namely, metal 
accumulation with regard to background concentrations and inter-species variability have 
been studied. Prediction models based solely on background concentrations have been found 
to not provide good predictions on neither metal concentration nor metal accumulation in 
macroalgae tissue. As an alternative which is not explored under this thesis, knowledge gained 
on interspecies variability could potentially be coupled to local measurements in macroalgae 
specimens when needed, so that estimated ranges of metal accumulation could still potentially 
be derived16.  
Overall, drivers of metal uptake by macroalgae have been identified and contrasted with literature, 
and can be listed as: metal speciation (as much as it influences the dissolved fraction in background 
sea water), macroalgae interspecies variability and location-specific influences (as reported by 
literature: pH or temperature, for instance). 
The data set on ecotoxicologic effects of metals in fermenting organisms has been used to derive 
statistically robust data based on the model proposed by Rosenbaum et al. (2008). A ranking of metals 
according to their ecotoxicity potential towards relevant microbes has been provided. Additionally, 
microbial genii have been ranked as well according to their sensitivity to metal toxicity.

With these tools, macroalgae–microbial genii combinations are compared with regards to their

On the microbial side, it is noticeable how the fungal genus performs much better than its bacterial 
counterparts. This fact, at least from an ecotoxicological perspective, can be seen as a very 
straightforward recommendation to aim at fungal rather than bacterial fermentation processes within 
the bio-based plastic industry.  
On the macroalgae side, a few genii can be identified as performing better than the others, yet the 
genus Caulerpa outstands above all. These results should be handled with care, as they only comprise 
metal ecotoxicity, while other environmental impacts have not been reflected upon. Actually, 
Caulerpa is considered to be a very problematic invasive species over the Mediterranean due to its 
fast growth and lack of predators (Portela 2017), which could lead to an undesired loss of biodiversity 
where active farming of this genus occurs. In fact, biodiversity loss has already been pointed out as a 
main negative impact of seaweed farming, especially with regard to non-native species (Loureiro et 
al. 2015). Still, evaluating environmental impacts others than the ecotoxicological ones is outside of 
the scope of this thesis. It is also noticeable how green algae as a group has been observed to perform 
generally better than the rest. This fact contrasts with Figure 1, where green macroalgae species are 
shown to not be representative at all as a global market share within macroalgae production. 
Other than providing input for macroalgae and microbial species selection, the results from this thesis 
can be further explored as input for decision-making when designing other aspects of bio-based 
plastic production from macroalgae feedstock. For instance, with regard to including, or not, pre-
treatment steps to reduce the amount of pollution accumulated in macroalgae tissue, or performing 
strain selection to obtain microbes which are resistant to a particular pollutant. 
Lastly, macroalgae farming for bio-remediation purposes should be done in awareness of the inverse 
correlation between BCF and metal background concentrations in sea water, as this can be a main 
factor affecting its efficiency.  
Overall, this thesis has set a basis upon which ecotoxicological effects of metal accumulation in 
macroalgae feedstock for bio-refinign purposes are justified to be a relevant issue of concern. Still, 
the potential of this area of research has only been realised and derived implications are on a very 
early phase. 
4.2. LCA practices and applicability of the approach 
The magnitude of the ecotoxicological impact that metal as accumulated in macroalgae tissue used 
as feedstock for bio-based plastic production might have on fermenting microorganisms has been 
evaluated. A fate-exposure-approach model chain proposed by USEtox has set the basis for such 
analysis. After assessing the magnitude of this impact to be meaningful, the possibility to expand the 
LCA framework under these premises has arisen: LCA methodologies in relation to bio-based plastic

The approach described within this thesis serves as a basis to include the mentioned ecotoxicological 
impact within the LCA framework. In order to do so, a full impact pathway covering in detail fate, 
exposure and effect steps should be described. There are two main missing links to be addressed in 
this direction: 
• The exposure phase has been only roughly sketched as a first iteration to approach the 
problem. Now that the results of this thesis have revealed levels of metal pollution that are 
estimated to be problematic to the microbial colonies used in bio-refining processes, there is 
a valid reason to further describe this step. Metal speciation models under bio-reactor 
conditions can be used for this purpose. 
• The fate of metal pollution has not been described upstream from macroalgae tissue to the 
emission source. This is a requirement in order to be able to provide characterisation factors 
as explained within the methodology section, as otherwise the emission source is not 
connected to its impact. 
With regard to LCA as a tool to assess environmental impacts of products and systems, this project 
sets the basis for a fundamental question, as current practices do not cover by any means harm which 
is done to living microorganisms within industrial reactors used for manufacturing processes. This is 
indeed a very relevant issue on what is it that we seek to protect from the environment which has not 
been considered to be part of the scope of this thesis. 
4.3. Limitations of the approach and the data set 
It has been previously pointed out how there is a mismatch between the data that is measured when 
analysing metal content in sea water samples (i.e. total metal content), and the data that would be the 
most desirable for ecotoxicity studies (i.e. the bioavailable fraction, mostly linked to the dissolved 
fraction of metal). Even more importantly, the fact that there is yet no generalised denomination of 
the different fractions of metal content in sea water hampers the transmission of experimental results 
through the scientific community. These two factors have had an impact on the insight obtained with 
regard to metal uptake by macroalgae from bioavailable metal concentration in sea water.  
A general limitation that is acknowledged within ecotoxicity studies for which no satisfactory solution 
has been found so far is the interrelation that different pollutants might have with each other (Ansari 
et al. 2004). In this case: whether different metal elements might have relations of synergy, 
antagonism or neutrality with regard to their ecotoxicological effects on the studied microbes. 
Currently, LCA practices do not account for this phenomena, and might well be, indeed, an open 
criticism point in favour of other approaches that emphasise the use of ecotoxicity studies with real 
scenario mixes of pollutants. 
With regard to data on microbial ecotoxicity, it is noticeable how gathered ecotoxicity measurements 
are mostly limited to those metal elements considered as the most problematic to the aquatic 
environment, as given in literature (Ansari et al. 2004), and also circumscribed to the oxidation states 
that are most likely, by at least one order of magnitude, to be found in sea water (MBARI 2017). This 
fact is an indication that research efforts have already been directed towards the most pressing issues. 
Subsequently, available data, even if limited to a handful of metal ions, might be fairly representative 
of the ecotoxicological impact of metal elements in microbial life. The lack of experimental data with 
regard to most of the selected microbial species, on the other hand, is seen as a relevant data gap

4.4. Recommendations for future research 
A first recommendation that should be considered for future research is the need to develop consensus 
on denominations with regard to metal speciation in water. Also, matching experimental observation 
to the requirements of the ecotoxicologic scientific community is a pressing issue to be addressed. 
With this thesis, the potential ecotoxicity effects of metals in macroalgae to fermenting microbes have 
been estimated to occur at meaningful magnitudes. Consequently, further research work is advised to 
be performed in order to gain a deeper insight on the problem and refine the outcomes explored within 
this thesis while working on describing an impact pathway to be incorporated to LCA methodologies. 
In this direction, future research would follow by modelling metal speciation within the bio-reactor. 
This could be regarded as a second iteration step in providing further refinement to the explored 
approach, while in addition could work as a safe check that ecotoxicity to fermenting microorganisms 
from macroalgae feedstock is indeed a relevant research topic. This step should presumably be done 
by chemical complexation modelling in water under bio-reactor conditions. 
Then, further work could be done in order to establish the impact pathway and incorporate it for LCA 
studies on the bio-based plastic industry. In this direction, it would be a main goal to obtain 
characterisation factors as a result from combining fate, exposure and effect factors. In order to do 
so, the fate step from emission source to macroalgae tissue would need to be detailed.  
Chemical pollution comprises both metal elements and organic chemicals. In fact, there is nowadays 
much awareness with regard to the pollution of organic chemicals, in particular for some such as 
those classified as Persistent Organic Pollutants. As a next step, the fate-exposure-effect approach as 
described within this thesis could be expanded to cover also organic chemicals. In fact, some 
exploratory work has been performed in this direction, which has risen several important questions 
that remain to be answered: 
• In contrast to metal pollution, where ecotoxicological effects can be directly related to metal 
ions and their oxidation states, organic chemicals possess more complex structures. Once they 
start degrading, it might be that the resulting metabolites have an even higher ecotoxicity 
potential than its parent molecules depending on the mechanisms involved. The behaviour of 
organic pollutants under fermenting bio-reactor conditions remains unknown, and places an 
important uncertainty within the pathway.  
• In order to deal with the huge amount of data that would mean covering organic chemicals 
(i.e. 100.000+ compared to a few metal elements) there is indeed need for standardisation 
processes that allow for a certain level of automation in data treatment and results analysis 
and implementation. 
Additionally, the approach explored in this thesis could be implemented within the bio-based plastic 
industry for all kinds of bio-feedstock. This, indeed, would be a meaningful improvement of the LCA 
framework, as it would allow for a more comprehensive comparison between different bio-feedstock 
sources.

5. Conclusions 
In a global scenario of increasing awareness with regard to the human footprint in the environment, 
alternatives to conventional practices, such as bioplastics, are gaining the interest of public bodies, 
researchers and the general audience. Macroalgae as bio-feedstock for bio-based plastic production 
has not yet been developed at an industrial scale; even so, it has been made evident how it presents a 
series of inherent advantages in comparison to other sources with regard to their environmental 
impact. Research effort is therefore being placed in order to sort out its technical challenges and 
increase its competitiveness. 
This thesis aimed at exploring how metals as present in macroalgae can contribute to ecotoxicological 
impacts on microorganisms applied in bio-based plastic production processes as a potentially relevant 
impact pathway to be considered in Life Cycle Assessment.  
Within this context, an approach based on a fate-exposure-effect model proposed by USEtox has been 
implemented. Two data sets have been gathered for this purpose: one concerning metal uptake by 
macroalgae, including measurements on algae tissue and background sea water; and a second one 
including metal ecotoxicity measurements in relevant microbial species. 
Subsequently, metal content in macroalgae as a potential feedstock for the bio-based plastic industry 
has been evaluated: ranges of metal content have been provided, and its variability, which has been 
attributed mainly to background concentrations, macroalgae inter-species variability and local 
ambient factors, has been discussed. Additionally, metal toxicity to relevant microbial species has 
been explored and, as a result, metal ions have been ranked according to its ecotoxicity potential 
towards microorganisms. Also, microbial species and genii have been ranked with regard to their 
sensitivity towards metal toxicity, as a first approximation to deriving recommendations for the bio-
based plastic industry based on toxicity criteria. Finally, metal content in macroalgae tissue and 
ecotoxicologic potential of metal ions to relevant microbes have been combined, leading to rankings 
on macroalgae-microbial genii combinations with regard to its suitability for bio-based plastic 
production based on ecotoxicity criteria. The resulting rankings provide the basis for inputs for 
recommendations to the bio-based plastic industry on metal ecotoxicity to microbes when using 
macroalgae as feedstock. This overview of the project allows to verify that the specific goals of this 
thesis have been achieved. Namely:  assessing metal pollution levels in macroalgae as feedstock for 
bio-based plastic production, exploring potential ecotoxicological impacts of contaminated 
macroalgae feedstock on fermenting microbes and providing initial recommendations on macroalgae 
as feedstock for bio-based plastic production with regard to ecotoxicity criteria. 
Overall, this thesis has provided solid indications that the potential ecotoxicologic impact of metal 
accumulated in macroalgae to fermenting organisms can occur at an scale that is meaningful. With 
regard to LCA practices, this provides a good starting point on which an impact pathway that is 
currently not accounted for can be further developed. In order to do so, research needs have been 
identified. In case that this impact pathway is explored, it could be used as a basis to incorporate 
biochemical ecotoxicity to microorganisms within LCA methodologies, as a means to optimise the 
assessment of its related environmental impacts. 
To conclude, it is deemed necessary to remark once again the exploratory nature of this project, which 
should be regarded as a starting point to approach the problem. Once proven that there is indeed 
enough basis to consider the concerned issue legitimate with regard to its scale, further work is 
required in the suggested directions in order to refine the outcome of research to maximise input
