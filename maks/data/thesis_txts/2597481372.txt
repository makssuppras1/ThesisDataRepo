Abstract
The goal of this project is to study the design of a retaining wall based on the European Building
Standards and its seismic response is assessed. To evaluate the performance of the wall two
different finite element analyses were performed using PLAXIS 2D: pseudostatic analysis and
non-linear dynamic analyses. The retaining wall was subjected in eight seismic motions, which
were scaled in the design peak ground acceleration (PGA) of the wall and in two higher PGA
levels. The effect of the earthquakes and the retaining wall on the soil motion was examined.
The response of the wall is evaluated under seismic loads.
The performance-based design
methodology was implemented by defining damage criteria in terms of displacements, bending
moments, and anchor force to determine the serviceability of the structure when subjected to
strong earthquakes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the last decades, the continuous growth of population and the increasing urbanization ne-
cessitate new civil constructions and probably an expansion of the existing one, such as the
transport infrastructures and underground structures. Numerous urban areas are located in
seismic prone zones, which makes the construction process and the long-term serviceability
of the structures a significant research topic worldwide. History has shown that the econom-
ical development and the prosperity of societies are substantially affected by the devastating
damages caused by major earthquakes.
Retaining structures are significant infrastructural elements and they need to be designed to
withstand during earthquakes and mitigate the seismic risks. Thus, it is of great importance to
gain insight regarding their seismic behavior. The performance of the retaining structures un-
der seismic loads has been proven through experiments and observations, in reality, to depend
profoundly on the presence of both water and loose cohesionless soil. This was demonstrated
during Kobe earthquake in 1995 in Japan during which 90% of the harbor facilities were dam-
aged or destroyed entirely [17]. The magnitude of the Kobe earthquake was Ms = 7.2 and
the quaywalls on the port were designed with a seismic coefficient ranging from 0.1-0.18g. On
the contrary during Parnitha earthquake in 1991 in Athens, a concrete multi-anchored wall
in Kerameikos station exhibited exceptional behaviour [13]. Although, the magnitude of the
Parnitha (Ms = 5.9) earthquake was lower than Kobe earthquake, it is interesting that this
particular retaining system had not been designed to sustain any seismic load, since it was
meant to be only a temporary retaining wall. Thus, the response of a retaining structure is a
rather complex soil-structure interaction problem.
The typical design guidelines propose a simplified analysis for the seismic design of a retain-
ing wall, which is a force balanced method and has two main limitations. The first limitation is
that it does not provide an insight regarding the behavior of the soil when the limit equilibrium
is exceeded, while the second one is the high cost when the structure is designed to resist high
but rarely seismic forces. To minimize the effect of these limitations and utilizing the knowl-
edge obtained from the failure of retaining structures over the years, a new Performance-based
design methodology was developed. This methodology takes into consideration that the behav-
ior of a structure under seismic loads depends on the stress state and the deformations of the
soil and the structure. Moreover, some residual displacements are acceptable as long as there
is no loss of the serviceability of the structure. An important aspect of the aforementioned
methodology is that different levels of seismic motions can be considered and combined with
different degrees of damages.
Based on the previous remarks, the objective of this thesis comprises the seismic response of
an anchored retaining wall designed based on the guidelines of European Building Standards
(EC8).
Prior to elaboration on the scope of the thesis a theoretical background and the
European standards regarding the design of retaining structures are described in Chapter 2.

CHAPTER 2
Retaining walls
Earth retaining walls are defined as the structures which retain ground at a slope steeper than
it would ultimately be selected on the absence of structure. The retained ground comprises of
soil, rock or backfill and water. All the types of walls and supporting systems at which stresses
are imposed by the retained material, constitute retaining structures.
These types of structures are commonly used in seismically active areas and possess a
principal role in structures such as ports and harbors, transportation systems, lifelines and
other constructed facilities. Permanent deformations, which are either negligible or significantly
damaged, have been caused on the retaining structures by earthquakes. In some cases, collapse
of the retaining structures has been observed during earthquakes leading to destructive physical
and economical aftermath.
In this chapter, the theoretical background of the embedded anchored walls is recalled
regarding the evaluation of its behavior with different seismic analysis methods and the process
of the design of the wall according to EC8.
2.1
Types of retaining walls
In geotechnical engineering, one of the oldest challenges is the retaining soil from excavations
and underground structures. The combination of the fundamental principles of soil mechanics
with a variety of materials has led to rational design of retaining structures which are success-
fully used until these days. Materials such as metallic, polymer and geotextile reinforcement
have been used to elaborate plenty of innovative types of earth retaining walls. In terms of
their relative mass, flexibility and anchorage conditions, the retaining walls can be categorized
as gravity walls, cantilever walls, braced walls and embedded walls restrained against lateral
movement by anchors.
The complexity of the preliminary design of a retaining wall is due to the various types
of retaining structures and the necessity to retain a backfill and/or the loads transmitted
from adjacent structures. The selection of the most suitable type of retaining wall is based
on numerous factors, such as the structure geometry, the limitations imposed from the soil
type and the ground water table conditions, the knowledge and the availability of the different
construction methodologies. In the present study, the embedded walls restrained with ground
anchors will be studied.
The most common applications of anchored walls are highway and marine structures, deep
excavations and slope stabilization. These types of retaining systems consist of the retaining
wall and the ground anchor. The retaining wall can be either discrete wall elements or con-
tinuous wall elements. The former are elements that are continuous throughout their length,
are spanned by lagging along the excavation, include driven or drilled-in piles and can be
constructed in most of the soil types. The latter are elements that are continuous throughout

their length and width, can be either sheet piles that are connected with joints (timber, steel
and precast concrete sheetpiles) or cast-in-situ concrete diaphragm walls.
Anchored wall support relies on the shear and bending stiffness of the wall element, the
passive resistance from the soil below the excavation and the lateral resistance of the ground
anchors. It has been reported that most of the damages encountered in anchored walls are due
to the deficient design of anchors or damage of the anchors during construction.
2.2
Failure mechanisms of anchored retaining walls
One important aspect of the design of retaining structures is to define the failure of the wall.
Under static conditions, the forces acting on the retaining walls derive from the mass of the wall,
the soil and the anchored systems. Equilibrium of these forces is required in order to design
properly a retaining wall, on condition that the induced shear stresses will not exceed the shear
strength of the soil. However, during an earthquake the equilibrium may be not satisfied due
to the inertial forces and the variation of the soil strength, resulting in permanent deformations
of the wall. The failure mechanism such as tilting, sliding and bending, is different for each
type of retaining structure.
A range of potential failure conditions can be identified for the anchored retaining walls,
which should be considered in their design procedure. Considering the different elements form
the retaining system, the failure mechanism can be associated with the soil, the wall and the
ground anchor.
Failure of the anchor involves failure of the steel tendon, which is caused
when the applied load is higher than the structural capacity of the tendon. Another failure
condition is the failure of the ground-grout bond and it is developed if the skin friction between
the bonded length of the anchor and the soil is exceeded resulting in the pullout of the anchor.
Failure of grout-tendon bond may also be induced and provoke failure of the anchor. This type
of failure depends on the adhesion, the friction and the mechanical interlock of the tendon
and the grout body of the anchor [18]. In Figure 2.1, the aforementioned potential failure
mechanism are illustrated

The seismic analysis of retaining structures has a significant impact on the design of the

• Simplified dynamic analysis: ”Possible to evaluate extent of displacements/stress/ductil-

2.2.2
Simplified dynamic analysis
The simplified dynamic analysis is an approach to predict the permanent displacements induced
by ground motions in retaining structures, such as gravity walls and sheetpile walls. In this
analysis, the Newmark sliding block analysis and a failure mode is assumed. A key assumption
of this method is that the structure or the soil mass under seismic excitation is assumed to
be a rigid block and it will start to slide on its base when the yield (critical) acceleration is
exceeded. This procedure is equivalent to that of a block resting on a slope which will start to
move when the driving forces overcome the resisting forces [20].
The ground motion input for this analysis is generally described by acceleration time his-
tories that are applied at the ground level. The acceleration time histories are computed by
performing site response analysis on the recorded seismic motion at the bedrock. Moreover,
the critical acceleration is estimated from the limit equilibrium of the retaining system and
the soil. The residual displacements are obtained by double integration of the parts of the
acceleration time history that exceed the critical acceleration, a procedure that is presented in
Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.6: Newmark integration procedure, adopted from [20]
The residual displacements that are estimated by the Newmark method expect from the
assumption that the system soil-wall behaves as a rigid-perfectly plastic body, is that no relative
displacement occurs between soil and wall when the seismic load is below or equal to the critical
acceleration and the inertia forces of the soil-wall system and the internal forces of the wall
increase with the applied accelerations. On the contrary, when the applied acceleration is higher

than the critical acceleration, permanent displacements develop on the wall, but internal forces
remain stable and equal to the value that was reached for acceleration equal to the critical
[19]. Another assumption made in the Newmark method and which can not be disregarded in
the estimation of the permanent displacements of the wall is the neglect of the dynamic pore
pressure [20].
The Newmark method can be used with simple modifications to estimate and evaluate
the residual displacements of retaining structures at the end of the shaking event, but to the
authors’ knowledge, most of the research work relates to cantilever diaphragm walls. Recently,
Cattoni et al.
have proposed a Generalized Newmark Method to estimate the permanent
displacements of diaphragm walls which are propped at the crest [11]. The reliability of this
method is based on the accurate estimation of the critical acceleration and the corresponding
failure mechanism.
2.2.3
Dynamic analysis
Dynamic analysis is appropriate for the evaluation of the final design of the structure and
commonly is performed by using the Finite Element Method (FEM) and Finite Difference
Methods (FDM). According to PIANC [26], the dynamic analysis offers numerous modeling
possibilities, allowing to idealize the structure by a linear or non-linear model and the soil by
an equivalent linear or by an effective stress model, depending on the stress-strain dependency
under seismic loading. The effects of the earthquakes are expressed by a set of time histories
of seismic motion.
In the analysis of underground structures, an important feature is the interaction between
the soil and the structure, especially in sites characterized by soft materials. This aspect of soil-
structure interaction is accounted for in the dynamic analysis by including a coupled response
of the soil and the structure. A coupled analysis means that the dynamic response of both the
soil and the structure are considered in the same model.
It is admissible that the response of the soil-structure system is affected significantly by
the applied seismic motion and for that reason, many design codes suggest that the dynamic
analysis should be performed by using multiple ground motions. Moreover, it is significant
that before any dynamic analysis on the retaining system, the calibration and validation of the
numerical model should be performed. This can be done by verifying the model either with
experimental data or theoretical solutions.
A common approach for the solution of the dynamic response problem is the numerical
integration of the dynamic equilibrium equations in small time steps. For each time step, the
stress-strain relationship from the previous step is provided, in order to have more accurate soil
properties as an input at the beginning and as an output at the end of that time step. Numerous
techniques have been developed for numerical integration, but the most commonly used are
the explicit and implicit integration schemes. The former can be formulated relative easily
but is not as robust and its reliability depends on the size of the time step. On the contrary,
the latter is more complicated only to provide more accurate and stable solutions. One of the
most commonly used integration schemes is the Newmark method, which includes a family
of single-step integration schemes developed for both blast and seismic loading. Newmark’s
method has been widely used for the past decades in the dynamic analysis of structures and
has been modified and developed by several researchers. The standard form of Newmark’s

2.3.1
Earthquake provisions according to European Building Code
According to Eurocode 8 part 5 (EN 1998-5:2004) [4], earth retaining structures must be
designed to be serviceable during and after an earthquake event, without detrimental structural
damages. Residual displacements, due to sliding and tilting, the latter caused by irreversible
deformations of the foundation soil, may be acceptable to a degree that they do not violate
functional or aesthetic requirements. The safety of a retaining structure may be assessed by any
established method that considers the structural and soil dynamics and is based on experience
and observations. In the analysis the following aspects should be taken into consideration:
• The non-linear behaviour of the soil and its dynamic interaction with the retaining struc-
ture.
• The inertial effects of the retaining system correlated with the masses of the soil, the
structure and any other gravity load.

of the wall.
• The compatibility between the deformations of the soil, the wall and any type of tieback

damage criteria and no limitations on displacements values are given.

2.3.3
Performance-Based design and damage criteria
The principle goal of the performance-based design is to overcome the limitations that occur
from the conventional seismic design. The difference between the two design methods is that
the design of the latter method is based on a specified seismic force that the structure must
resist without any information regarding the response of the structure under higher induced
seismic forces.
On the contrary, the design according to the former method accounts for
multiple intensities of seismic motions combined with different accepted levels of performance.
The higher the important class of the structure, the higher the demand for performance grade
and thus higher the degree of complexity of the performed seismic analysis. The response of
the structure is evaluated by comparing the results of the seismic analysis with the level of the
damage criteria. A detailed flowchart of the performance-based design procedure is provided
in PIANC [26] and is presented in Figure 2.8.

thus the level of the design ground motions and the acceptable damage degree. It should be
mentioned here that the categorization of all the aforementioned parameters referred to port
structures.
2.3.3.2
Damage criteria in performance-based design
An important aspect of the performance-based design is to establish the extent of the damages
that is acceptable in engineering terms, such as displacements, stress and strain limit states.
The parameters to identify the damage criteria for each type retaining structure are based
on the potential failure mechanisms that can be developed during the shaking event. The
established damage criteria correspond to operational damages in port structures and for quay
walls that no human life, hazardous materials or cranes are threatened or considered during a
seismic event.
PIANC [26] have proposed different qualitative and quantitative parameters for specifying
the damage criteria for various quay walls, such as gravity quay walls, sheet pile quay walls,
pile-supported wharves, cellular quay walls, quay walls with cranes and breakwaters. Focusing
on the sheet pile walls, the parameters for specifying the damage criteria are:
Displacements:
• Horizontal and differential displacements, settlements and tilting of the sheet pile wall
• Settlements and tilting of the apron
• Differential settlement, ground surface cracking and pullout displacement of the anchor
Stresses:
• Stresses in the sheet pile wall above and below the mudline
• Stresses in the tie-rod including the joints with the sheet pile wall and the anchor
• Stresses in the anchor
The displacements of the different structural components of the sheet pile wall are quantified
only for damage Degree I, while for the rest there is no guideline. On the contrary the stresses
may not be quantified, but they are characterized in terms of elasticity and plasticity.

2.3.3.3
Evaluation of structure in performance-based design
The last step in the performance-based design is the selection of suitable seismic analysis.
Different types of analyses can be used depending on the design phase (preliminary or final) and
the performance grade of the structure. The results obtained from the analyses are compared
with the established damage criteria to evaluate the seismic response of the structure. In case
the damage criteria are not fulfilled, then it is necessary to redesign the structure and perform
the appropriate seismic analyses again.
In general, this process is an iterative procedure
intended to decrease occupational accidents and lead to more cost-effective designs.

3.6.2
Damping ratio and dynamic boundary conditions
The next step for correct calibration of the model is the damping ratio and the boundary con-
ditions. Setting the appropriate boundary conditions is important because the soil behaviour
on the boundaries needs to be simulated realistically and any effect from the boundary and
the reflected seismic motions will alter the response of structure. For that purpose, the dy-
namic boundaries were evaluated by performing site response and comparing the results with
the model including the retaining wall. The parameter that is used for the comparison is the
maximum acceleration along the thickness of the soil layer in different points along the length
of the model.
Before reporting and compare the effect of the chosen dynamic boundaries, some important
features of the dynamic model need to be specified. These features are the damping ratio of
the soil material, the mesh of the model and the set up of the dynamic calculation, which
includes the time interval and the load steps that should be applied.
To begin with, a Rayleigh damping was decided to be applied in the soil material. Although,
the HSsmall soil model includes a hysteretic material damping to describe more accurately the
behaviour of the soil at small strains, it was observed that spurious oscillations were developed
during the vertical propagation of the seismic motions. In order to avoid those oscillations, a
Rayleigh damping equal to ξ = 5% was introduced into the model. Although this amount of
damping may be high in correlation to the hysteretic material damping, it was noticed that
lower amount of Rayleigh damping was still resulted in spurious oscillations.
Moreover, the Rayleigh coefficients need be determined. To calibrate the aforementioned

A time history analysis in PLAXIS 2D is simulated by setting up a dynamic calculation

since it defines the total duration of that phase and should be equal to the duration of the
motion. Last the load steps is necessary to be determined and in general can be taken equal
to the number of data that have been recorded for the ground motion.

3.6.3
Selection of the ground motions
To perform dynamic analysis and to assess the response of the retaining wall, eight ground
motions were selected. There are different parameters that can describe an earthquake motion
such as the earthquake magnitude (Mw), the peak ground acceleration (PGA), the epicentral
distance and the soil type at the location of the recorded accelerograms. All of these parameters
have a direct impact on the response of the retaining system and different criteria can be used
to select the ground motions in order to evaluate the response of the structure.
In this study, all the earthquake motions were recorded in soil type C, since it was assumed
that the retaining wall is meant to be constructed in that soil type. Furthermore, the higher
the magnitude of the motion the higher the probability to excite the structure. Thus, the
selected ground motion have a magnitude higher than 6 and can be described as strong ground
motions. Another, factor that was taken into consideration was the duration of the earthquakes.
Shorter and longer ground motions were selected, with their duration to range between 19sec
and 61sec.
The last but most important factor for selecting the ground motions was their frequency
content. It is well known that the response of a structure can be analysed by using the response
spectrum, based on the assumption that the retaining wall behaves as a single degree of freedom
system (SDOF). This is a reasonable assumption for the majority of the structures and for
==PAGE:49==
3.6 Time history analysis
37
the soil, considering that the first dynamic mode is the one that dominates the response of
the system, and thus the first eigenfrequency in combination with the response spectrum can
be used to assess the performance of the retaining system. However, the estimation of the
fundamental frequency of an anchored retaining wall is complicated and it was decided that
the predominant frequency of the soil will be used as a reference. As it was already mentioned,
the predominant frequency of the soil is f = 3.14Hz and its predominant period is T = 0.32sec.
Therefore, the selected earthquakes have a predominant period close to the predominant period
of the soil, but also cover a wider range in order to obtain a more clear idea regarding the
behaviour of the retaining system.

A better understanding of the effects of the seismic motion on the soil motion can be
obtained possibly by evaluating the soil motion on the ground surface, by relating the site
response analysis with the 2D analysis. For this reason, the Topography Amplification Factor
(TAF) is used. This factor is defined as the ratio of the maximum acceleration at the ground
surface of the 2D model and the maximum acceleration at the surface obtained from the site
response analysis. In Figure 4.14 the horizontal profile of these factors behind the retaining
wall are illustrated for a peak ground acceleration of 0.24g.
It can be seen that the soil motion is indeed influenced by the presence of the wall. In
the vicinity of the wall, Spitak motion presents the most prominent effect, while Landers the
least. For two of the earthquakes, namely Borrego and Landers an attenuation of the TAF
is observed close to the wall. Moreover, it can be seen that for Borrego motion, the TAF
obtain almost a constant value for a quite big length along the ground surface. For the three
earthquakes that have a predominant period close to the elastic fundamental period of the soil,
the irregularities in the TAF are more intense especially for a length of 30m of the wall, as it
can be seen from the first graph. On the contrary, the deviation of the TAF becomes smoother
for the rest of the seismic motions. It should be mentioned that for a distance of around 40m
from the wall and further the values of TAF are close to one and thus the effect of the presence
of the retaining wall in the soil motion is minimized.

CHAPTER 5
Performance based design
The earthquakes are incidents in which their peak ground acceleration can not be predicted.
Although an anchored retaining wall is designed based on European Building Standards and
the seismic zones which are established for each country, the uncertainty of the seismic per-
formance of the structure under stronger seismic motions than it has been designed demands
the knowledge of the damage levels. Evaluating the response of the structure under different
PGA levels, the performance-based methodology can be linked to the design of the structure
according to European standards.
In the performance-based design, it is essential to identify the acceptable limit of dam-
ages in engineering terms, that is displacements, limit stress state and structural capacity
and ductility of the structure. Regarding the residual displacements on top of the anchored
bulkheads, five degrees of damage are distinguished according to Kitajima and Uwake [24].
These damage degrees can be linked to the damage criteria that were defined by PIANC [26]
and are presented in Table 5.1. As can be seen, PIANC [26] has defined one damage degree
for residual displacements lower than 0.1m, combining the cases of no damage and negligible
damage. Thus, the degrees of damages that are used for the performance-based design are four
and in terms of structural and operational effects the Degree I corresponds to ’no damage’ or
negligible damage to the wall itself and noticeable damages to adjacent structures and Degree
II to ’noticeable damage to the wall’. Degree III and IV are associated with ’preserved the
general shape of anchored sheetpile, but significantly damaged’ and ’complete destruction and
unrecognizable shape of the wall’, respectively.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this chapter the evaluation of the research results is conducted for both pseudostatic and
non-linear dynamic analysis.
6.1
Pseudostatic analysis
In section 4.1 the results of the pseudostatic analysis were presented. A significant observation
regarding the pseudostatic analysis was that the arching induced in the wall around the location
of the ground anchor causes the reduction of the pressures below that point and results in an
underestimation of the lateral pressures acting against the wall in comparison to the analytical
calculations. This is because the Mononobe-Okabe solution can not capture the concentration
of stresses around the point of the support, as the pressure is proportional to the depth without
account for the stresses concentrated because of the ground anchor.
Moreover, the earth
pressures are not proportional to the depth as it is considered from the M-O method. This
might be attributed to the fact that the inertial forces are applied to the whole mesh and thus
it accounts for the non-linear behavior of the soil and the stress-strain relationship in the soil
mass. On the contrary, in the M-O method, the induced stresses caused by the inertial forces
are estimated through a total active thrust that acts on the active soil wedge. The reduced
stresses behind the wall between the dredge line and the anchor level results to lower bending
moment than the design bending moment.
Moreover, by increasing the horizontal acceleration, the driving force behind the wall is
increasing and the resistance force in front of the wall decreasing, resulting in an increase of
the maximum bending moment of the wall and a lowering of the point of contraflexure along
the wall. Although it is not depicted clearly in Figure 4.3, it was observed that as the value of
the horizontal acceleration increases, the deformed shape of the wall approaches those of the
free earth support.
Evaluating now the displacements in the top of the wall and taking into consideration
the damage criteria for sheet-pile wharves that were proposed by Uwabe [24] and are pre-
sented in Table 5.1, the retaining wall exceeds Degree I when PGA=0.15g and Degree II when
PGA=0.24g. In the former situation, the structural damages are controlled and there is little
or no loss of serviceability, while in the latter the induced damages are extensive and the re-
taining system is out of service for a long duration. For accelerations higher than the critical
(PGA=0.29g) the retaining collapses. Furthermore, the structural capacity of the retaining
wall in terms of bending moments is not exceeded for all the applied accelerations resulting in
a satisfying structural design of the wall. In contrast, a failure pullout capacity of the anchor
occurred at the critical acceleration and thus an evaluation of the design is required.

6.2
Non-linear dynamic analysis
The non-linear dynamic analyses consist of site response analyses and 2D analyses. The purpose
of the site response analyses was to first gain knowledge regarding the behavior of the soil under
different seismic events and then evaluate how the response of the soil motion is affected by the
presence of the retaining wall and the frequency content of the seismic motion. For that reason,
the normalized maximum acceleration with the base maximum acceleration along the depth of
the soil deposit was computed for the site response analyses and along the depth behind the
wall for the 2D analyses. In the site response analyses, the amplification of the acceleration
is more regular in comparison to the results obtained from the 2D analyses. The latter has a
more irregular shape and it was noticed that the rate of acceleration amplification increases
mostly around and below the point of the embedment depth. This might be attributed to the
increase of the strains in the bottom of the embedment depth which results in degradation
of the soil stiffness and amplifies in higher rate the acceleration. It is important to mention
that for both types of non-linear dynamic analyses the rate of the acceleration amplification is
decreasing when the applied PGA level is increased. This is because stronger motion causes a
higher degradation of the soil stiffness and a higher increase of the damping ratio, which leads
to higher dissipation of energy and an increase of the acceleration at a slower rate.
A parameter that can be obtained from the site response analyses is the soil factor S as
is defined in EC8. This soil factor is the amplification factor between the bottom of the soil
deposit and the ground surface and according to EC8 is depending mainly on the mean shear
wave velocity of a 30m soil layer. The design of the examined retaining wall was based on this
soil factor. From the site response analyses, it was estimated that the amplification is ranging
between 1.24 and 1.98, with an average value of 1.58. Compare to the soil factor S=1.15 this
is a significant difference which could lead to erroneous results.
Moreover, EC8 part 5 [4]
suggests than in the case of a retaining wall more than 10m high, a refined value of α can be
defined from the site response analysis. For the soil type of this study and taking the average
of the peak horizontal acceleration along the soil deposit, it was estimated that α = 0.279g.
Compare to the value of 0.24g that the Greek National Annex [27] defines to be used for a
seismic zone II, it can be said that by utilizing the more refined value the embedment depth
of the retaining wall would be higher. Thus, a site response analysis and an evaluation of the
soil behavior may be performed before the design of the retaining structure.
Since the normalized maximum acceleration with the base maximum acceleration along the
wall provided quite ragged results and no specific conclusion on how the frequency content of
the seismic motion affects the soil motion, the topography amplification ratio was used. As was
already mentioned this factor is the ratio of the maximum acceleration on the ground surface
obtained from the 2D analyses divided by the corresponding of the site response analyses. It
was noticed that the soil motion is affected noticeably from earthquakes with a predominant
period close to the elastic fundamental period of the soil and least from earthquakes with a
higher predominant period. Moreover, an interesting observation was the effect of the PGA
level. An increase of the PGA level seems to affect the soil motion for a longer distance from
the wall.
Having evaluated the effect of both retaining wall and frequency content on the soil motion,
the seismic response of the retaining structure is assessed.
As was mentioned the seismic
motions were scaled in three different PGA levels. For PGA=0.24g and PGA=0.4g the non-
linear dynamic analyses seem to capture the arching around the location of the ground anchor
and because of this the effective stresses are higher on the ground surface compare to the
==PAGE:82==

M-O method in which only the stresses from the surcharge loads are taken into account and
not the non-linear behavior of the soil. Due to the arching, the effective stresses are reduced
in the unsupported section of the wall. Another remark is that for higher PGA levels M-O
method overestimates even more the effective stresses compare to both pseudostatic and non-
linear dynamic analysis and this is due to the fact that the active earth coefficient increases for
increase peak ground acceleration as it is illustrated in Figure 4.23. The contrary applies to the
passive effective stresses, as the M-O method underestimates all the more the passive stresses
compare to the finite element analyses since the passive earth coefficient reduces by increasing
the applied acceleration. Significant is to be mentioned that utilizing the M-O method the
stresses for the specific soil type can not be computed for peak ground acceleration higher
than 0.46g, while the non-linear dynamic analyses can provide the corresponding effective
stresses.
Increase of the effective stresses acting behind the wall caused by an increase of the peak
ground acceleration results in mobilization of the active limit conditions of the soil.
As a
consequence, passive resistance is fully mobilized until a new equilibrium is reached.
The
increase in the stresses both behind and in front of the wall results in an increase of bending
moments. At the end of the earthquakes, the residual displacements resemble flexible wall
deformation. However, the deflection of the wall for some earthquakes scaled to PGA=0.7g
indicates passive failure.
Specifically, the deformed shape during Tabas earthquake scaled
to PGA=0.7g indicates that there is failure of the wall due to insufficient passive resistance,
resulting in higher displacements in the bottom of the wall and lower in the upper part. This
is also justified and the reduction of the maximum bending moment as it is noticed in Figure
5.2. Interesting presents the deformed shape of the wall during the Managua earthquake scaled
also in the highest PGA level. Notice that slightly higher displacement occurred in the bottom,
which also indicates that the passive resistance is not sufficient resulting in a relatively small
increase of the anchor force and the bending moment. Furthermore, the quite small increase
of maximum bending moment in relation to the high rise of the anchor force for Landers
earthquake is justified by the considerable increment of the horizontal displacements of the
wall. Similar is the trend also for Northridge earthquake.
An important observation is that the earthquakes with a predominant period close to the
predominant period of the soil do not result in the maximum residual displacements in the
wall. The induced displacements depend on the steep fluctuations in the acceleration, which
cause higher inelastic deformations.
Comparing now the pseudostatic and the non-linear analyses only for the design peak
ground acceleration (PGA=0.24g), it is noticed that pseudostatic results are significantly higher
in terms of horizontal displacements.
This can be attributed to the fact that the applied
forces in the wall involve the horizontal seismic coefficient kh, through the earth pressure
coefficient, which consists of the peak ground acceleration and the soil factor. As has already
been mentioned, the soil factor for soil Type C is S=1.15 and by utilizing it in the estimation
of the earth pressure coefficient, it increases the active and decreases of the passive earth
coefficients and thus the stresses and forces. Therefore, it can be said that the magnitude of
the displacements is considerably influenced by the contact stresses behind and in front of the
wall.
Last, the deviatoric strains depict that a failure wedge starts to form from the bottom
of the retaining wall and expands upwards and near the connection point of the unbonded
and bonded length of the ground anchor. The induced strains along the bonded length of
the anchor create the risk of making the anchor less effective for higher PGA levels and fully

mobilize the skin friction between soil and anchor resulting in possible failure of the anchor.
Moreover, the deviatoric strains present a concentration in front of the wall and at the bottom
of the wall indicating that the embedment depth is not sufficient. Based on these remarks, it
may be prudent to consider a failure wedge from the bottom of the wall and utilize the refined
values of α, which defined from the site response analyses, to estimate a higher embedment

6.3
Performance-based design analysis
In the last part of this project a performance-based design was performed to assess the seismic
behavior of the designed retaining wall based on the damage criteria introduced by Uwabe
[24] and PIANC [26]. The performance-based design was performed by evaluating the residual
displacement on top of the wall, the maximum bending moment, and the anchor force.
Regarding the displacements, the aforementioned damage criteria were utilized and it can
be deduced that earthquakes of a maximum 0.5g can be permitted in case of a repairable
anchored retaining wall. Shaking events with higher amplitudes will induce severe damages
or even collapse of the structure. The bending moment on the wall was compared with the
design bending moment to evaluate its structural design. Based on the performance-based
design analysis it is valid to assume that even for really strong ground motions, the design
of the retaining wall in terms of bending moments is adequate. Moreover, the anchor pullout
capacity was used to assess the performance of the ground anchor and it is concluded that the
design of the ground anchor is sufficient for PGA levels to range between 0.24g and 0.5g.
As an overall conclusion, the anchored retaining wall exhibited satisfactory performance in
terms of displacements and capacity of the structural elements up to a PGA level of 0.5g. This
peak ground acceleration can be considered as the limit over which it might be expected to
moderate or substantial damages in the structure, depending on the characteristics of the
earthquake.
However, the performance-based design provides only an idea of the seismic
performance limits of the anchored retaining wall and it should be acknowledged that the
quantification of the damage degrees involves some speculations. The reason for that is that
the importance grade of the structure is not taken into consideration despite the fact that it is a
significant parameter. Furthermore, it should be emphasized that the quantitative assessment
developed in this section is based upon the characteristics of the studied anchored retaining
wall only and does not reflect the seismic performance of similar retaining walls.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and future work
In this project, the seismic response of an anchored retaining wall was evaluated. The soil
conditions and the specifications of the retaining wall are adopted from the benchmark exam-
ple and the design of the wall follows the European guidelines. Two finite element analyses
were carried out in PLAXIS 2D in this study, a pseudostatic analysis and non-linear dynamic
analyses. The non-linear analyses were site response and 2D analyses. Initially, the models
were calibrated in terms of dimensions and boundary conditions, in order to avoid as much as
possible erroneous results.
To start with, it is of high importance to perform site response analysis prior to the design
of a retaining wall, in order to evaluate the behaviour of the soil under seismic loads and assess
the different parameters that the design guidelines propose to be used, such as the soil factor
and the value α for determine the design ground acceleration. Furthermore, the active earth
pressures computed with M-O method are exceeded slightly the pressures computed by finite
element analyses for moderate earthquakes, but for stronger seismic motions the difference is
more intense. In contrast, the passive earth pressures are significantly higher for M-O method
compare to the finite elements analyses for both moderate and strong motions. Moreover, the
dynamic interaction between soil, retaining wall and ground anchor is captured adequately
with the finite element methods.
The pseudostatic analysis seems to be more conservative in comparison to the non-linear
dynamic analyses inducing significantly higher displacements.
However, it is advisable to
be used for the preliminary stages of the design of the retaining wall. Although non-linear
dynamic analysis is more time consuming, it provides a detailed insight of the non-linear soil
behaviour subjected to seismic motions and captures more realistically the seismic response
of the structure mainly because recorded earthquakes can be utilised. Also, the performance
of the retaining wall in strong ground motions can be assessed by the performance-based
methodology. Regarding now the design of the retaining wall, it was proven to be adequately
designed and able to sustain stronger ground motion.
The seismic design of an anchored retaining wall is a demanding process and several param-
eters need to be taken into account. Although, the seismic analyses that were performed during
this project is sufficient to provide significant informations regarding its seismic performance,
factors such as the wall, the ground anchor and the soil type could be further investigated.
Regarding the wall, the type of the material and the flexibility is suggested to be further
investigated. Moreover, different dimensions and inclination of the ground anchor would be
examined. Finally, the soil type is one of the most important parameter that influence the
seismic response of a retaining structure, thus cohesive soils or multi-layer soil profiles should
be assessed.

B.1.2 Water pressures
The estimation of the seismic loads acting on the retaining wall and computed by the
Mononobe-Okabe method are limited to dry backfill. Although, the majority of the retaining
walls are designed with a drainage system in order to avoid build-up of the water in the backfill,
this is not possible for all the retaining walls depending on their location. For instance, retaining
walls located to waterfront areas.
The presence of the water has a significant role in defining the forces on the retaining wall
during the shaking event. Dynamic pressures in front of the wall are exerted because of the
water outborad of the water. Also, the dynamic pressures are affected by the the presence of
the water within the backfill. Thus, it is important to take into consideration the effects from
the water in the seismic design of the retaining walls.
B.1.2.1 Backfill water pressure
The water in the backfill of a retaining wall can alter the seismic loads acting on the wall
by:
• by changing the inertial forces within the backfill
• by inducing hydrodynamic pressures within the backfill
• by developing excess pore water pressure due to cyclic straining of the retaining soil
In saturated soils the inertial forces depend on the relative movement between the soil par-
ticles and the water and the surrounding water.
In case of a soil with small permeability
(κ ≤10−3cm/sec, porewater and soil move simultaneously during an earthquake and thus the

APPENDIX C
Software
PLAXIS 2D is a two-dimensional finite element software used to analyse a various types of
geotechnical problems concerning deformation, stability and groundwater flow. A significant
aspect of this program for the present study is the ability to perform non-linear dynamic
analysis. Analysis of earthquake motions can be applied and their influence on the soil and on
underground and adjacent structures can be studied. These types of analysis do not include
liquefaction.
In the following paragraphs, the relevant material regarding the modelling of the case study
will be described. The topics are the constitutive soil model, the structural elements and the
dynamic parameters.
C.1 Soil material
PLAXIS software includes a numerous material models, which are used to represent the
stress-strain behaviour of soils with different degrees of accuracy depending on the purpose of
the task. The material model that will be implemented in the present study is the Hardening
Soil model with small-strain stiffness (HSSmall). For greater clarity, more simple models will
shortly be described before elaborating the main features of HSSmall model. For this part,
the main reference is the PLAXIS 2D Material Models Manual [8] and for more detailed
information than presented in this section, one should refer to the manual.
C.1.1 Linear Elastic model (LE)
The Linear Elastic model is the most simple model in PLAXIS 2D and is based on Hooke’s
law of isotropic elasticity and it considers that the material is linear elastic. This means that
no plasticity and no limitations of the strength of the material is considered. However, the
behaviour of the soil is non-linear and irreversible and this model can not capture its essential
features. On the contrary, this model can be used to model stiff structures in the soil such as
concrete elements or bedrock layers. The main input parameters of this model are the Young’s
modulus E (kN/m2) and the Poisson’s ratio v (-).
C.1.2 Mohr-Coulomb model (MC)
The Mohr-Coulomb model or the Linear Elastic Perfectly Plastic model is considered as
an enhanced model of the Linear Elastic model, as a distinction between the linear elastic and
the perfectly plastic part is made. In the former the material behaves according to Hooke’s
law, while in the latter is based on the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion and irreversible strains
develop. The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion is characterized by a fixed yield surface, which is
defined by the cohesion c and the friction angle ϕ of the soil, is not affected by plastic straining
and evaluates if plasticity occurs or not.
==PAGE:95==
C Software
83
C.1.3 Hardening Soil model (HS)
The Hardening Soil model is a more sophisticated model than the Mohr-Coulomb in cap-
turing the behaviour of the soil. This model considers the increase of the soil stiffness with the
pressure and a yield surface that expands due to plastic straining (hardening). But in PLAXIS
2D this expansion is confined by the Mohr-Coulomb yield criterion which constitutes the ul-
timate yield surface. The hardening of the soil can be either shear or compression hardening.
The first is adopted when the soil is subjected to primary deviatoric loading, in which irre-
versible strains develop and the soil stiffness decreases, while in the second irreversible strains
occur under oedometer and isotropic loading.

the stress dependency of the soil stiffness and this is depicted by introducing three different

C.1.4 Dynamic boundary conditions
A significant aspect in the dynamic analysis is the dynamic boundary conditions. The ap-
propriate boundaries should be chosen in order to absorb incoming waves and avoid reflections
of these waves back into the model and to simulate the free-field conditions which account for
the propagation of the waves into the far field. In PLAXIS 2D, the available dynamic boundary
conditions are:
• xmin boundary: None (Standard fixities), Free-field, Tied degrees of freedom
• xmax boundary: None (Standard fixities), Free-field, Tied degrees of freedom
• ymin boundary: None (Standard fixities), Viscous, Compliant base
• ymax boundary: None (Standard fixities), Viscous
The free-field boundary conditions can be applied only in the lateral boundaries and simulates
the wave propagation into the far field.
The free-field motion is reproduced by applying
equivalent normal and shear forces and the reflected waves from the structures on the domain
are absorbed by two dashpots which are added in the lateral nodes. Moreover, these dynamic
boundaries require user-defined interface elements along the length of the lateral boundaries.
The interfaces should be placed inside the model, otherwise the free-field boundaries will be
disregarded.
The tied degree of freedom boundaries are available only for the lateral boundaries and
characterized by the connection of nodes in the same elevation which will experience the same
vertical and horizontal displacements. These boundaries are suitable for site response analysis
of a one dimensional soil column.
The viscous boundaries, applicable in the ymin and ymax boundaries, simulates viscous
dampers along the boundaries that absorb the energy from the incoming waves by providing
a resistant force in the normal and tangential direction. This boundary option is considered
appropriate in cases that the dynamic source is inside the mesh.
==PAGE:99==
C Software
87
Last, the compliant base boundary can be applied only in the ymin boundary and simulates
the continuation of waves into the deep soil with the least possible reflection at the bottom
boundary.
In the applied signal on this boundary only upward propagating waves should
be regarded. Also, user-defined interface elements should be applied along the length of the
boundary.
