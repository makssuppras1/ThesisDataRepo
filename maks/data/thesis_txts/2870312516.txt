Abstract
The inspection and maintenance of photovoltaic (PV) panels in the field are critical for
ensuring long­term performance and efficiency. Conventional electroluminescence (EL)
imaging methods, while effective, require electrical contact and are often impractical for
large­scale field inspections. This thesis presents the development of a novel diagnostic
imaging system that explores the feasibility of using photoluminescence (PL) imaging to
enable contactless inspection of PV panels with the same level of detail as conventional
electroluminescence (
 
 
EL) imaging.
A prototype system is developed consisting of an infrared (IR) LED line light source and
a short­wave infrared (SWIR) camera mounted on a custom­built gantry scanner. The
gantry enables automated scanning of PV modules, capturing luminescence emissions
that reveal defects such as cracks and degradation.
This thesis details the design, implementation, and validation of the system, including me­
chanical construction, motor control, IR illumination, and image processing techniques.
Testing and evaluation compare the proposed system with existing inspection methods,
assessing its feasibility for real­world deployment.
The findings suggest that PL imaging, combined with automated scanning, offers a promis­
ing approach for scalable, contactless PV diagnostics in the field. The insights gained
from this prototype serves as a foundation for future development of a field­deployable,
autonomous PL inspection system.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Solar photovoltaics is rapidly developing, and is the fastest­growing renewable energy
source. In 2024, the total global energy production passed 2 TW [1], and it is one of the
main pillars of the green transition.
From factory, solar cells are inspected before shipment, ensuring defects are not present.
But already during transport and installation, cracks and other damage might occur, which,
given the harsh environmental conditions of PV plants, might develop over time. This
damage can have a disproportionate effect on the total power output of a string, and so
replacing them is crucial [2]. Furthermore, this results in a significant economic impact
on the profitability of photovoltaic (
 
 
PV) plants and can even cause safety risks in normal
plant operation [3].
The damage is not always visible to the naked eye, and with tens of thousands of panels
in modern
 
 
PV plants, inspection methods are needed to accomplish this goal that are
mostly automated, accurate, and has a large throughput.
Current inspection of photovoltaic panels in field for defects presents a number of prob­
lems. These stem from both the limitations of state­of­the­art inspection technology and
from the ambient light exposure and non­ideal inspection conditions present in field.
Contemporary
 
 
PV inspection with
 
 
EL is too slow [4, 5], and contact and handling pose
a risk of damage to the very panels being inspected for damage. Other conventional
inspection methods pose their own problems. Thermography imaging provides limited
information on the nature of the damage inspected, visual inspection has proven to be
inadequate for electrical inspection and
 
 
UV fluorescence imaging degrades over time and
is unsuitable for common UAV­type inspection.
Diagnostic luminescence imaging is one of the most accurate inspection methods cur­
rently on the market, but scaling it to larger
 
 
PV sites has proven to be difficult. Either the
modules have to be electrically biased for
 
 
EL, leading to a difficult and slow process with
the aforementioned risk of contact/handling damage, or in the case of photoluminescence
(
 
 
PL) imaging, measurements have to be performed during the nighttime due to interfer­
ence from solar radiation. A new method of contactless, fast and accurate imaging is
needed.
1.2
Problem Statement
Previously,
 
 
PL line scan inspection with LED excitation only exists in lab or in few exper­
imental field setups, and methods with laser excitation and whole panel irradiation is the
main focus of contemporary research. To address the in the thesis background and add
to current state­of­the­art, a prototype for a novel system of
 
 
PV module inspection is de­
veloped. It is unclear whether line excitation of
 
 
PV panels in field is viable using infrared
(
 
 
IR)
 
 
LEDs as a light source, so this work will explore the feasibility this, and evaluate ac­
cordingly. Furthermore, the prototype will integrate this line light into a line scanning robot
using a short­wave infrared (
 
 
SWIR) camera and explore different scanning methods.

Project Objectives
The project can be split into distinct objectives.
• Development of a
 
 
LED
 
 
IR line light source, including drivers.
• Assembling a
 
 
SWIR camera system for capturing
 
 
PL from scans.
• Building a gantry robot scanner with parallel linear stages for control
• Evaluating the scanning system and comparing with contemporary inspection meth­
ods
These bring us to pose several research questions for this thesis:
­ Can photoluminescence be utilized for the inspection of
 
 
PV panels in the field?
­ Is using IR LEDs feasible for this purpose?
­ How should the panels be scanned for optimal imaging?
­ What lessons can be learned from a prototype for use in a commercial scanning
system?
1.4
Project Limitations
While the project aims to build a complete system for scanning a
 
 
PV panel using infrared
light as excitation and scope its capabilities, this study is subject to a number of limitations.
Common to many of these is a conscious choice of making a prototype scanner that does
not necessarily fulfil the objectives completely due to time constraints.
Full­scale testing was limited to a few test sessions, and only a select few panels were
scanned. This obviously puts a bias in the results, since corners in panel degradation,
panel type and conditions were not fully explored. This also expands to the limited outdoor
testing done in section 4.2.7.
The robustness of the mechanical drive system is questionable at best, due to the self­
developed 3D­printed belt drive system that might misalign or skip steps. This leads
to a slight drift in the alignment and absolute coordinates of the scanning X and Y axes
which impacts image repeatability. To address these mechanical issues, more design time
would have been needed. Furthermore, further development of the scanner depends on
mending these errors.
While the project was aimed towards comparing
 
 
IR and laser excitation of
 
 
PV panels in
linear scans, time limitations led to implementation of just
 
 
IR scanning. Comparison was
done between previous research and the method used in this thesis, but direct comparison
would offer more insights.
Further compromising rigidity of the results of the study is biases in image acquisition
and processing, where parameters like optimal exposure and frame rate were estimated
from visual experience. Adjustment values for the image stitching were also based on
estimates of drift, and image downconversion necessarily loses some data. This can
impact the accuracy of the final results.
A comparison between EL and PL in the results as a ratio of the maximum emitted inten­
sities would have supported the results and made for a more robust conclusion. This was
not prioritized due to the aforementioned time constraint.

Theory and state-of-the-art
In order to solve the inspection problem at hand, a multidisciplinary approach needs a
thorough understanding of the disciplines involved. This includes the physics behind the
photoluminescence phenomenon, camera sensor technology, motor control and electron­
ics.
2.1
Photovoltaic module degradation and failure modes
Ideally, a photovoltaic panel would maintain its rated power during the entire lifetime of the
panel. However, real life is rarely ideal. Factors such as environment, handling, module
type and normal wear contribute to degradation, leading to a gradual reduction of the
maximum power of the module. This section explores these degradation modes along
with methods used inspect
 
 
PV panels for these.
Over a 25+ year lifespan, PV plants are expected to operate in changing, often harsh
environmental conditions, delivering a constant power output. Research from the US
National Renewable Energy Laboratory (2018) estimates a 0.5% median degradation rate
of solar cells yearly [6]. The degradation means a significant median 12.5% reduction in
power generation over the rated lifespan.
To understand why this occurs, it is essential to examine the specific mechanisms that
impact the power generation of a
 
 
PV module during its lifetime.
What constitutes failure in a
 
 
PV panel?
Zafirovska (2018)[4] defines a failure in its simplest form as ”...when the power output of
a module irreversibly decreases or when a safety problem is created.”. In other words,
failure occurs when the the
 
 
PV module can no longer perform its function at full capacity,
and where replacement of the module might be applicable. Local environment damage
such as shading and soiling and damage with a negligible impact on power generation
are in some cases considered failures, but literature disagrees on this point [7, 8].
Degradation
Even in the absence of critical failure, degradation can still impact power generation. The
modes of degradation vary widely with some ­ such as light induced degradation (LID)
­ accounted for by the manufacturers guarantees and other resulting from unpredictable
environmental stressors. Factors like humidity, temperature and mechanical stress con­
tribute to these losses.
Based on a review by Köntges et. al (2014)[7], Meribout (2023) [8] and Zafirovska (2019)
[4], common modes of degradation are shown in table 2.1, along with potential impact on
power loss.

2.2
Luminescence of solar cell modules - theory and principles
On contact with the surface of a solar cell, a photon is absorbed by the semiconducting
material of the cell. Carriers are excited from the valence band to the conduction band,
generating an electron hole­pair. In normal operation, this pair is extracted via an electric
circuit as an electric current when aa potential is applied across the cell [4]. This is the
fundamental principle behind photovoltaic cells, although specific implementations vary
6

depending on type.
However, when there is no carrier extraction ­ and to an extent even when there is ­ the
electron hole­pair undergo recombination, largely radiative [10]. When it recombines, an
electron moves back from the conductive band to the valence band, emitting a photon
with a wavelength corresponding to the energy of the band gap. A visualization of this
band gap can be seen in figure 2.1

This photoluminescent effect in solar cells is largely similar to the operation of

Once generated, the photon will escape the cell. This quantity is measured as emitted
luminescence flux, Φ, which can be measured by an image sensor, provided that it is
sensitive to the wavelength emitted. This effect, known as photoluminescence, forms the
basis for the diagnostic imaging methods used to assess PV module degradation in this
thesis.

In itself, an automated machining system is nothing but a series of sensors and actuators,

dynamic load rating ­ corresponding to a 4050N load before deformation of balls in the
bearing ­ way beyond what the 2 axes can reach with their respective sub­10kg weight
and relatively slow speeds. This margin ensures a long component lifetime.
To drive the axes along the length of the gantry, a number of options are possible. Tradi­
tional belt drives work by having a toothed pulley pull along a strung belt. This is simple
to design, but presents a number of drawbacks.
In gear­drive­design, backlash is an inevitable quantity. Whenever a set of mated gear
teeth meshes, there has to be some amount of clearance for the gears not to get stuck.
This holds true for belt­and­pinion drives also, where sudden stops or reversal of the driv­
ing pinion leads to unwanted movement.
In the case of the
 
 
PL scanner, imaging and subsequent image keying requires non­shaky
movement, and backlash is thus undesirable. To avoid this, a more advanced drive ap­
proach is needed. On each axis, a motor drive has been designed, loosely inspired by

The motors are driven using DM542T drivers. These are 2­phase stepper motor drivers
designed for high­current stepper motors, 24­48V and up to 4.5A drive current, accepting
5V/24V input signals for the steps and drive [39]. In this project, they are configured for 4x
microstepping, since the 200 steps/revolution that the NEMA 23 motors drive at induce a
large resonance with every step.
From figure 2.16, it can be inferred that microstepping leads to less resonance, directly
translating into better imaging and lower wear on the components. It does, however,
lower the maximum torque and speed, but this is only a problem if the system has a
heavy load on an axis or needs to run particularly fast. The microstepping is set to 800
steps/revolution, using a set of 8­bit DIP switches on the drivers themselves. The same
switches also configure the current supplied to the stepper motors. This is set to 2.03A
RMS (2.84A peak), and another switch on the side sets the accepted input voltage to
5V. The switch configuration for this can be seen in figure 3.12, and a table of possible
configurations can be found in the datasheet [39].

3.4.2
Supply and driver
The datasheet for the Luminus IR LEDs [41] specifies a max rated current of 1.5A (contin­
uous) at 3V nominal. This gives a power rating for each LED of 5W. Thermal calculations
show that the LEDs should have no issue dissipating this power, at a theoretical 17°C
temperature rise at 5W supplied power. These can be found in the appendix F.2. Ther­
mal reflection in the lens and interface with the board has not been examined, and will
have an unknown influence on the temperature rise. The full thermal characteristics of
the LEDs will be explored and verified in section 4.1.1.
The LEDs are wired in 4 parallel strands of 10 boards each. Powering these close to the
max rated current would require a 6A 30V nominal supply, with voltage overhead up to
34V (see datasheet [41]).
Cleverly, setting the nominal voltage supply of the system below 50V allows the electrical
system to be considered safe (specified by the EU Low­voltage directive [42]), but parallel
LEDs pose a problem. Manufacturing differences between different batches of the same
types of LED result in rather large variances in forward conductance [43]. This means the
same forward voltage on LEDs might produce drastically different forward currents ­ and
figure 3.21 shows just that.
Figure 3.21: Relative forward voltage vs. forward current of Luminus SST­10­IRD­810nm.
Relative to nominal 3V, from datasheet [41]
Here we see that a voltage difference between 3V and 3.5V corresponds to more than
1A difference in forward current! This leads to brightness differences between strands,
which is strictly unwanted in our application. More critically though, it can lead to forward
current above the rated maximum, potentially burning out LEDs in that chain.
To prevent this, a simple yet effective current limiting circuit is added to each chain, con­
sisting of an LM317­HV voltage regulator and a sensing power resistor rated for the power

Camera calibration
Cameras, especially those imaging in the infrared spectrum, are noisy. When performing
camera initialization, it is important to adjust and calibrate, and a series of these calibra­

Most important of these is the non­uniformity calibration (
 
 
NUC). In essence, this is minor

environment around it changes. It causes striped artifacts which makes the output images
very unreliable, and needs to be performed whenever a change of camera settings occur.
In software, this is implemented at every scan.
Image sensors tend to exhibit ”bad pixels”, where the value does not reflect the incident
light. A pixel map is included in the FLI itself, and enabling this allows the any pixels that
are malfunctioning to be filtered out of the resulting image [45]. The image edges exhibit
significant vignetting, or darkening.
This can be filtered in software if it is mild using a so called flat­field correction. Due to
time constraints, this was not prioritized in this project.

3.5.4
Image enhancement
Images captured in 16bit format often have data represented in a very narrow range.
This makes it difficult to visualize when compressing to normal 8­bit images ­ and the
data captured with the C­RED3 is no exception. Images have to be contrast processed
in order to see the
 
 
PL captured, and several ways are implemented in this project. A
simple, yet effective method to accomplish larger contrast in output images is using a
linear histogram stretching algorithm 11 Essentially, a given low percentage of the image
is mapped to 0, a high percentile to 255 and the rest is stretched linearly between. This
does result in some data loss, and the percentile values have to be set manually, but very
good results are achieved for the purpose of
 
 
PL.
Figure 3.32: Histogram strething in action. From [48]
Also implemented in the source code for this project is CLAHE, or Contrast Limited Adap­
tive Histogram Equalization, a type of histogram stretching. This is a less lossy contrast
enhancement, which mitigates over­amplification by redistributing values over the clip limit
to all histogram bins in the image. More detail can be found in the OpenCV documenta­
tion: https://docs.opencv.org/4.x/d5/daf/tutorial_py_histogram_equalization.html.

5
Results and discussion
The goal of this project is to examine the feasibility of using photoluminescence in the
field with
 
 
IR
 
 
LEDs as a light source. While the prototype setup is not field­ready itself,
it provides insight into the research questions outlined in this thesis while it serves as a
test platform for further investigation in its own right.
5.1
Can photoluminescence be utilized for the inspection of PV
panels in the field?
Results from section 4.2.7 indicate that PL imaging using an IR LED bar is not viable
under direct sunlight without modulation, as the emitted photoluminescence signal is too
weak for reliable extraction in ambient noise from the sun. However, these tests were per­
formed at only ∼25% of the maximum rated LED current, and further testing is required to
assess the potential of higher power excitation. Despite these limitations, the
 
 
PL imaging
performed with the scanning system proved effective in low light conditions. This confirms
that night­time
 
 
PL is feasible with the current setup, even without modifications.
Furthermore, the system is specifically designed to accommodate a form­fitting light­
blocking cover, which could enable daytime operation under controlled irradiance levels.
The findings suggest the irradiance inside this would have to be below 50W/m2. Recom­
mendations for improving field feasibility, and answering the research question fully:
• Modulation of the
 
 
IR
 
 
LED source, which might enable daytime PL extraction.
• Higher LED output power, since the LEDs support up to 6A supply current.
5.2
Is using IR LEDs feasible for this purpose?
If the daylight limitations discussed above can be mitigated, IR LEDs provide an adequate
excitation source, offering a both scaleable and safe operation with details comparable to
EL images captured in laboratory settings. Comparing with the work in Benatto (2021)[29],
this offers a suitable alternative to laser excitation with lower complexity and higher safety.
However, comparison between
 
 
EL and
 
 
PL output is not quantified directly in this thesis.
5.3
How should the panels be scanned for optimal imaging?
The examinations of the the different scan methods in section 4.2.5 indicate that a paral­
lel axis scan along both longitude and latitude provides a robust result, though limitations
like vignetting and camera FOV must be addressed for full usability. Both of these are
solvable with a new lens system and flat­field correction of the image.
A line scan enables simultaneous extraction of both
 
 
PL and
 
 
EL imaging from the same
image set, significantly enhancing the information depth of each scan. Results confirm
that a continuous scan with axes fixed with respect to each other may be preferable to
the multi­stepped scanning approach due to simplicity and good variable control.
If vibration is present in the camera movement, the fixed camera approach might provide
slightly better image quality, but the emission characteristics of PV panels vary with angle,
which might prove a challenge for accurate PL in the stepped approach. This would have
to be tested.

5.4
Key lessons for future system development
Besides the results from the above, key points include:
­ Structural robustness. The system must be rigid, adjustable and able to withstand envi­
ronmental factors outside.
­ Adaptive imaging Exposure control, clipping and calibration are key to good imaging as
seen in section 4.2.3. Ad hoc methods are shown to be unreliable.
­ Optimized scanning methods. Continuous scanning with a fixed camera is effective and
yields stable images, as long as no vibration is present in the system.
­ Ease of use. Since one of main tenets of the background for this project is scaleability,
ease of use and some degree of autonomy is important.

6
Conclusion and further work
In this project, a scanner capable of providing quality photoluminescence diagnostic imag­
ing of single PV modules is successfully developed. It is proved that photoluminescence
diagnostic imaging with this system provides images with detail comparable to
 
 
EL images
in low­light environments.
Scans can be acquired in different direction, and camera variables can be set in software
and controlled with relative ease.
It is noted that the system is not capable of outdoor inspection as it stands.
After demonstrating that the system function is feasible, and being able to use it as a re­
search platform in photoluminescence, it has become clear that much work is still needed
before a field­inspecting system using PL is ready for operation:
6.1
Future work
Future work for the system includes both work on the prototype as a
 
 
PL research platform,
but also work that must be done to create a ready­for­field inspection system. This can
be split into categories of upgrading the system as­is, upgrading the methodology and
software for imaging and control and finally, the work that has to be researched before a
field­ready scanning system can be set up.
Pressing Prototype Upgrades
First, system upgrades that can readily be implemented into the system as­is and are
important for gaining full function of its scanning capabilities.
• Improved cabling and energy chains. A serious hindrance to consistent scans is
the wiring. As a prototype, cable lay­out was not of priority, and new cables and
guidance systems such as ”energy chains” is imperative before robust functionality.
• Waterproofing of the system. The electronics are vulnerable to the environment,
and while changing the cabling, cable glands can be installed to provide a closed
cabinet with weather resistance. This is important for outdoor function
• Portability. Since the system should be tested outside, it would make for easier
operation if wheels were mounted on the frame and the electronics cabinet was
mounted directly on the frame.
•
 
 
IR
 
 
LED driver upgrade. As discussed in previous sections, there are significant
thermal limitations of the LED limiting circuit. Upgrading this ­ either with resistors
capable of better power dissipation or a 3rd party solution ­ and implementing soft­
ware control for the LED power provides new opportunities for imaging.
• Sun­cover of the machine.
Since daylight has a large effect on the photolumi­
nescene image acquisition, a custom made suncover should be added to the ma­
chine. Luckily, the machine has been designed such that all motor components are
inside, and with the square frame, a such modification should be trivial.
• Further development of the drive system. Testing has proven the belt­drive system
to need much maintenance, and this should be developed to minimize this.
• Flat­field correction and lens­reevaluation. Implement proper flat­field correction to
avoid vignetting at image edges. If this proves too difficult with the current lens
Carl Emil Elling, 2025
67
==PAGE:80==
setup, change of the lens to something better suited for IR imaging would provide
an improvement.
Software and imaging improvements
Other proposed future work is related to software and imaging:
• GUI implementation. As the control script is for a prototype, it is not especially
user­friendly, and to maximize usability, the library functions in lib and PLRobot.py
should implemented as a GUI.
• Modulating IR LED current and adding a background removal algorithm. Outdoor
imaging using the system proved difficult, and to mitigate that, light modulation is
suggested. This should be done with a combination of the new IR LED driver sug­
gested above and new software.
• Easier camera setting workflow.. Camera settings like exposure, framerate and
calibrations that follow have a difficult current workflow. A better workflow or even
automation of these settings is important for further useability.
• Better stitching algorithms. Rather un­robust, the geometric stitch relies on mechan­
ical constraints that might change. Stitching algorithms should be able to account
for this ­ either by implementing sensors on the gantry like linear encoders on rails,
or advanced image processing features to make small adjusments to the stitching.
Towards a future Field-deployable system
For development of an actual field­operating prototype additional challenges have to be
solved:
• Robust movement in field. Possibly the largest obstacle to a field deployment, a
system for attaching and moving the system precisely in field has to be developed.
Inspiration from both DaySy and PLatypus is interesting here, since they both work
on frame­clamping driving methods with working examples.
• Further autonomy. Since the background for the project is to allow inspection with­
out contact, a workflow that is at least as efficient as current EL images should be
implemented. Exploring the use of autonomous systems here is suggested to ac­
complish this.
• Other camera setups. For pricing, it should be explored whether multiple lower­
resolution (and lower cost) cameras can replace the current FLI C­RED 3.
• Comparison with laser PL. Attachment of a laser system might provide a higher
efficiency system. This would mean exploring the PL area of a laser induced lumi­
nescence scan and comparing it directly with the system performance as­is. This
will
• Better EL stitching. A unique feature of the line scanning system is the electrolumi­
nescence the line light induces in the cell. With better stitching algorithms, possibly
mixed with modulation, this system could output both EL and PL diagnostic imaging,
providing more information than other state­of­the­art robotic scan systems. The EL
capabilities would have to be explored further.
