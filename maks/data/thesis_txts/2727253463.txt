Abstract
To ensure the longevity and reliability of offshore wind turbines, geophysical engineers
employ robust design practices, including materials selection, load calculations & fatigue
analysis. Critical for fatigue analysis is cyclic lateral loading from wind and waves causing
non-linear hysteric and ratcheting behaviour. To theoretically model the soil response, a
thermodynamical theoretic macro scale incremental framework denoted the Hyperplastic-
ity Accelerated Ratcheting Model (HARM) is in use.
Contrary to the incremental framework, this thesis addresses a new alternative continuous
and fully analytical approach for HARM. The main finding is the proof of a slow-fast
dynamical system within the equivalent continuous HARM model. Incorporating
geometrical singular perturbation theory, we conclude that the many unloading-reloading
cycles exploited in loading curves align with gain-loss of hyperbolicity for corresponding
critical manifolds. The analytical results serve as an alternative for the otherwise stiff ordi-
nary differential equations solvers, addressing long-term ratcheting and expected hysteresis
from soil responses.
The thesis initiates by examining the Burridge-Knopoff model, a different yet related mass-
spring system that also addresses friction. Through a comprehensive GSPT analysis, we
shed light on the fundamental question of the existence of frictional travelling waves. Close
to the continuum limit of the lattice-type system with spinodal friction force, we present
proposals of excitable dynamic solutions in a 4-D (x, y, z, w)-space and by a numerical
study of the layer problem conclude that at least a single travelling wave solution is likely.

Introduction
Orsted and University of Oxford have together scoped a series of projects to produce the
optimal monopile foundations for offshore wind turbines. Through this collaboration, an
unprecedented understanding of cyclic loading has been reached across soil profiles [1].
The fundamental theoretic work concerning cyclic loading follows from the Hyperplastic-
ity Accelerated Ratcheting Model, which is a parameter-heavy, incremental and rather
complicated soil response framework [2]. At its core, HARM is a 0-D macro model of
a non-linear unloading-reloading relation between strain and stress capturing the soil re-
sponses exhibiting hysteric behaviour and further the critical phenomenon of ratcheting [3].
While offshore wind turbine foundations have grown larger to optimize the benefits of
higher wind yields & reliable availability [4], HARM in collaboration with loading re-
sponse frameworks has reduced the weight of a single foundation by up to 500 tons of
steel and 5 meters of depth [1]. Observing the loading curves that HARM is utilized for,
the unloading-reloading cycles elucidate slow-fast dynamics [3]. This result motivates the
study of HARM from an analytical perspective by utilizing techniques from geometrical
singular perturbation theory (GSPT).
This thesis represents the first-ever exploration, motivation and analysis of the slow-fast
dynamics within the mass-spring system of HARM. The research work is comprehensive
as it entails establishing an algebraic-differential set of equations, deviating from the tra-
ditional incremental framework.
In addition to the study of HARM, we delve into the related Burridge-Knopoff lattice dif-
ferential equation, originally developed to model non-linear effects in earthquake faults [5].
The extensively studied model has primarily been proposed in the case of steady-state
velocity-weakening friction [6]. In alignment with the recent research paper [7], we ap-
proach veloctiy-strenghting friction following spinodal friction laws. With excitable regimes
following from the proposed set-up, we use GSPT to provide results on the existence of a
numerically observed friction wave for a large chain of blocks at the continuum limit.
In the conclusion we align on similarities between the two friction models HARM and
Burridge-Knopoff. We address how progress in the Burridge-Knopoff area may be relevant
for prospering GSPT use in HARM. The structure of the thesis is organized into five
main chapters. In chapter 1 we introduce the mathematical framework of GSPT in order
to state Fenichel’s theorems and briefly highlight where dynamics can break Fenichel’s
theory and how to corporate in such situations. Chapter 2 is dedicated to the Burridge-
Knopoff model. We propose two scenarios of travelling solutions for the perturbation of
a single block in the complete system.
The part is completed identifying that one of
the scenarios is likely to form the needed homoclinic connection, while the other can be
neglected from a GSPT perspective. Following results on the Burridge-Knopoff model, the
remaining chapters 3 to 5 concern the HARM study. Chapter 3 establishes the theoretical
thermodynamic framework of HARM and identifies its continuous equivalent version. With
the new algebraic-differential equations in hand, we in chapter 4 identify a small parameter
and show the existence of a slow-fast dynamical system for 0-D kinematic hardening models.
We delve between the extended layers of complexity added per internal plastic surface and
its chosen spring, sliders and dashpot parameters. The preliminary chapter 5 examines
applications of GSPT in loading tools, specifically for analytical results on ratcheting.

Geometric Singular
Perturbation Theory
Geometric singular perturbation theory is a specific approach within the realm of singular
perturbation theory. Some common techniques employed in singular perturbation theory
include: boundary layer analysis, matched asymptotic expansions and the widespread use
of multiple time scale methods [8,9]. This is only a snippet of utilized methods, for example
when dealing with fast oscillations or rapidly varying solutions one can apply the specific
averaging method with the aim of collecting an averaged equation for slow dynamics [9].
In a nutshell, singular perturbation theory aims to systematically construct an approxima-
tion of solutions to problems that are otherwise intractable. What the mentioned methods
above all have in common is that they rely on the existence of a relative small parameter
in the system. This is a common scenario in real-life applications and a big reason why
singular perturbation theory is heavily studied & a cornerstone of applied mathematics [8].
Contrary to studies of regular perturbation problems, we are in singular perturbation
problems concerned with nonuniformity in our system and thereby in the solutions. So
what is nonuniformity and how can it occur for example in systems of differential equations?
To give an indication of nonuniformity, let us try to solve:
ϵx3 −3x + 1 = 0
(1.1)
for 0 < ϵ ≪1. Indeed, eq. (1.1) is a cubic equation and therefore we expect 3 solutions. We
note that the equation is singular in the sense that, if we approximate by setting ϵ = 0 the
equation reduces to being linear. Hence, we can not immediately provide an approximation
of all 3 roots. To solve for all 3 roots in eq. (1.1) there are multiple approaches. One can
insert the trivial root with ϵ = 0 and use the Implicit Function Theorem [10]. Otherwise,
the solutions can be found by asymptotic expansions from an initial guess of x (see Ap-
pendix A). The two non-trivial solutions are going towards plus/minus infinity for ϵ →0!
The phenomenon of nonuniformity is extended to more difficult cases such as in ODEs and
PDEs, where it is often located at the highest order differential terms [8]. In geometrical
singular perturbation theory we also try to understand systems’ nonuniformity. Here by
introducing, identifying & analyzing geometric structures known as slow manifolds [11].
In the shortest chapter of this thesis, we intend to present the fundamentals of geometrical
singular perturbation theory (GSPT). The introduction departures in section 1.1 by defin-
ing the structures of differential systems with multiple time scales or specifically slow-fast
dynamical systems. We continue in section 1.2 building theorems, corollary and definitions
in order to eventually state Fenichel’s theorems and provide an approximation technique
for slow manifolds. The chapter is completed by a short section 1.3 on use cases.

1.1
Slow-Fast Dynamical Systems
Geometrical singular perturbation theory is built upon multiple time scales. If only two
time scales are present in a given system, the term fast-slow system is commonly used.
But at least locally, we can for general multiple time scale systems write the systems as:
x′ = dx
dτ = ϵf(x, y, ϵ)
y′ = dy
dτ = g(x, y, ϵ)
(1.2)
with x ∈Rn, y ∈Rm, 0 < ϵ ≪1 and with the conditioning that f : Rn ×Rm ×[0, ϵ0) 7→Rn
and g : Rn × Rm × [0, ϵ0) 7→Rm are at least piecewise smooth. Upon defining the theory
we assume that both f, g ∈Cr, r < ∞are sufficiently smooth.
We have defined the small parameter 0 < ϵ ≪1. Say we were to approximate the system
by setting ϵ = 0. It immediately follows that the system contains nonuniformity - the
fundamental form of the system changes. ϵ is viewed as a measure of separation between
the two time scales. The smaller ϵ, the more separation. Finally, that eq. (1.3) contains
multiple time scales is elucidated taking a general point for the system. If g(·) ̸= 0, then
we expect dynamics in variables x to be slower than for variables in y. Hence, x and y are
called the slow and fast variables respectively.
τ is in eq. (1.2) known as the fast time. Let us perform an appropriate rescaling identifying
the relation t = ϵτ. Then the system can be written with respect to the slow time t as:
˙x = dx
dt = f(x, y, ϵ)
ϵ ˙y = ϵdy
dt = g(x, y, ϵ)
(1.3)
The dynamical systems (1.2) and (1.3) are noted respectively fast and slow corresponding
to the time scale they are viewed from. They are equivalent for ϵ ̸= 0 but when we enter
nonuniformity at the (ϵ = 0)-limit for both systems, they are fundamentally different. In
particular, the fast system is reduced to a set of m differential equations with x fixed:
x′ = 0
y′ = g(x, y, 0)
(1.4)
The now regular problem is appropriately denoted the layer problem. One can imagine
that fixing x variables is like introducing layers of dimension n in the combined n + m
dimensional system. Solving eq. (1.4) somehow defines dynamics on the layers. It is of-
ten called the fast subsystem, although one needs to be a bit careful with the interpretation.
On the other hand, the reduced problem is defined as the (ϵ = 0)-limit for the slow system:
˙x = f(x, y, 0)
0 = g(x, y, 0)
(1.5)
Again, eq. (1.5) is a regular problem consisting of n differential equations and m analytical
equations. In the reduced problem we have dynamics in x, while y need to satisfy the
analytical equations. The flow of the reduced problem is likewise called the slow flow.

1.2
Theoretical Framework for GSPT Analysis
The theoretical framework for GSPT analysis initiates from work of Fenichel [12–14] and
have turned systems on the form eq. (1.2) to be desirable. The literature on GSPT is
rather limited. Citeable work is primarily found in review papers as [15,16] or in the book
by Kuehn on the topic [11]. Here, we only introduce parts of GSPT to be used in this thesis.
Fenichel’s work is built around studies of an important set:
S = {(x, y) ∈Rn+m | g(x, y, 0) = 0}
(1.6)
It is indeed the set of equilibrium points for the layer problem, eq. (1.4), and the analytical
constraint y must satisfy in eq. (1.5).
In general, the set S defines an n dimensional
sub-manifold due to the set being defined by n + m unknowns and m equations.
Definition 1.2.1. S from eq. (1.6) is given the name the critical set and if its a manifold,
the critical manifold.
As indicated in the definition, one needs to be a bit careful about stating S directly as a
critical manifold, as it might not even be a manifold. It is sufficient to show that if the
Jacobian Dy(g(x, y, 0)) ∈Rm×(n+m) for any y has full rank, then S is a critical manifold.
The theorem is stated as theorem 12 in [10] and utilizes the implicit function theorem,
theorem 1 in [10]. It follows that at least locally S can be written as the graph:
S = {(x, y) ∈Rn+m | y = h0(x), x ∈N}
(1.7)
where N is a subset of Rn. If g(x, y, 0) is smooth we can extend the results above to state
that h0(x) is also smooth. With the identified local graph one can substitute y = h0(x) in
the reduced problem and obtain differential equations dependent on x.
For future use let us continue by defining invariant and local invariant sets:
Definition 1.2.2. A set M is invariant with respect to the flow from eq. (1.2) if no tra-
jectory can leave M. A set M is locally invariant with respect to the flow from eq. (1.2) if
it has a neighbourhood V so that no trajectory can leave M without also leaving V .
Notice, the simplest invariant and locally invariant set is an equilibrium point. Indeed, S
is per construction an invariant set for the layer problem but the invariance is likely not to
be extended for the complete problem eq. (1.2). We are within the next pages aiming at
showing existence of at least local invariant sets for eq. (1.2). Further, we aim to determine
a way of approximation such sets.

To determine stability of points at S we can not use classic hyperbolicity studies from local
dynamical system theory [15]. Instead, we introduce normally hyperbolicity:
Definition 1.2.3. A point (x, y) ∈S is normally hyperbolic if matrix A evaluated at the
point has exactly n eigenvalues with zero real part. Define the submanifold S0 ∈S. The
submanifold S0 is called a normally hyperbolic critical manifold if all (x, y) ∈S0 is normally
hyperbolic.
Remark, if more than n eigenvalues have real value 0 on parts of the critical manifold, we
denote such parts as non-normally hyperbolic.
Definition 1.2.4. Let S0 be a normally hyperbolic critical manifold. We define
• S0 as an attracting critical manifold, if the remaining m eigenvalues of matrix A all
have negative real part.
• S0 as a repelling critical manifold, if the remaining m eigenvalues of matrix A all
have positive real part.
• S0 as a critical manifold of saddle-type, if the remaining m eigenvalues of matrix A
have neither all positive nor negative real part.
Evaluating the m remaining eigenvalues determining stability of S0 with respect to the fast
variables can be simplified to obtain the m eigenvalues of Dy(g(x, y, 0)). This is due to the
n eigenvectors with corresponding eigenvalue 0 spanning the tangent space of S. It follows
directly that S0 being normally hyperbolic is equivalent to Dy(g(x, y, 0)) having full rank.
Hence, when S0 is normally hyperbolic we can write S0 at least locally as a graph on the
form eq. (1.7). In the following definitions and theorems, it will be important that S0 is
compact [10]. Hence, we naturally extend the definition of our local graph for S0 to be:
S0 = {(x, y) ∈Rn+m | y = h0(x), x ∈N}
(1.12)
where N now is some compact domain, N ∈Rn.

1.2.2
Fenichel’s Theorems
We are now ready to state the main theorems of this chapter.
Theorem 1.2.1 (Fenichel’s Theorem, Part 1, [17]). Suppose S0 is a compact normally
hyperbolic submanifold (possibly with boundary) of the critical manifold S of the system:
˙x = f(x, y, ϵ)
ϵ ˙y = g(x, y, ϵ)
where f, g ∈Cr, r ≤∞. Then for ϵ > 0 sufficiently small the following holds:
(F1)
There exist a locally invariant manifold Sϵ diffeomorphic to S0. Sϵ is invariant
besides at boundary points of S0 where points can leave Sϵ.
(F2)
Sϵ has a Hausdorff distance O(ϵ) from S0.
(F3)
The flow on Sϵ converges to the slow flow as ϵ →0.
(F4)
Sϵ ∈Cr.
(F5)
Sϵ is normally hyperbolic and has the same stability properties with respect to the
fast variables as S0.
(F6)
For a fixed ϵ > 0, Sϵ contrary to S0 is usually not unique. All manifolds satisfying
(F1)-(F5) lie at a Hausdorff distance O(e−K/ϵ) from each other for K > 0, K =
O(1). The order of Hausdorff distance implies that a potential Taylor expansion of
Sϵ is equivalent for all Sϵ and thereby unique.
Sϵ is in literature defined as the slow manifold. All asymptotic notation refers to ϵ →0.

1.2.3
Breakdown of Fenichel’s Theory
There are multiple reasons to how Fenichel’s theory can break. Noncompact critical man-
ifolds are not covered within the theory and also sub-parts of the critical manifold, where
normally hyperbolicity is not the case, can not be studied [15]. The latter due to the fact
that the implicit function theorem fails. Non-normally hyperbolicity is for example the
case at singular or even regular fold points [10]. For smaller dimensional systems one can
try to divide the studied critical manifold into compact smaller regions of normally hy-
perbolicity. Then Fenichel’s theorems provide slow manifold parts of potentially different
stability that can be connected to give an approximation of system dynamics.
For more complex systems (often experienced at higher dimensions) and for a better un-
derstanding of the exact transition near for example a fold point, more advanced GSPT
methods are needed. The blow-up method is a technique to study such degenerate points.
The idea is to stretch a specific region of interest. The blow-up method has more general
applications than GSPT and was pioneered by Dumortier and Roussarie in [19].
An-
other application of the general blow-up method is for example defining boundary layers
in the approach of matched asymptotics for general singular ODEs or PDEs. For matched
asymptotics, the method is referred to as a stretching transformation [8]. To understand
the blow-up method from a GSPT perspective the reader is referred to [15,20].

1.3
Known Applications of GSPT
Geometrical singular perturbation theory has found applications in neurology [21], chem-
ical reaction networks [22] but also in weather forecasting and control theory [23,24] etc.
Specifically, there are use cases in quantum mechanics, e.g. solving Schrödingers eigenvalue
problem close to the classical limit which is otherwise computationally heavy and math-
ematically non-trivial [25]. In this thesis, we study another use case of GSPT in physics,
namely mechanical systems with friction. Yet, there are no universal friction models [26].
Instead, there are different modelling approaches to account for friction assumptions. To
briefly motivate multiple time scales for frictional depended mechanics let us look at a
single slider component: In the context of static friction, a slider is activated when the ap-
plied force exceeds the maximum static friction force. Before activation, the slider remains
at rest. Once the slider is activated, it is in motion and experiences kinetic friction. If the
applied force is reduced, the kinetic friction opposes the motion and gradually decelerates
the slider until it comes to a stop. One can suspect that displacement for an object in such
system is occurring on multiple time scales dependent on slider activation. We make use
of GSPT in advanced frictional dependent mechanical systems through chapters 2 to 5.

Travelling Waves in a Truncated
Burridge-Knopoff Model
The Burridge-Knopoff (BK) model is a nonlinear model of the interaction between two
solids in contact, one pulled with constant velocity and the other fixed. By discretizing
the pulled solid into blocks of minor masses, the BK model is formally describing a chain
of pulled blocks connected by springs [5,7]. Each block in the chain is further attached by
additional springs to the fixed surface subject to a determined friction force. The dynam-
ics modelled are highly dependent on the selected friction laws, often chosen as non-linear
and non-monotonic inferred from microscopic studies [27]. The fundamental work in this
chapter will be based on either general friction laws or specifically spinodal friction forces.
Additionally, we focus on the BK model close to the continuum limit targeting the phe-
nomenon of travelling shock waves for a large number of blocks. As the BK model es-
sentially is a non-linear lattice differential equation, we can look at a continuum limit by
forcing the lattice spacing towards 0. Let kc denote the individual spring forces connecting
the chain, while kp is the spring forces between the discretized masses and the fixed solid.
Then for the studied physical system, illustrated in fig. 2.1, the continuum limit is found
letting k →∞, where k denotes the relation k = kc/kp [7].
The chapter is organised as follows. In section 2.1 a truncated version of an excitable
BK model close to the continuum limit is derived from a Taylor expansion of the spatial
coordinates. In order to approach the truncated BK model we rewrite the system into
an appropriate first-order form in section 2.2. In section 2.3 we set ϵ = 0 for respectively
the fast and slow system, initialising a study of the 4-dimensional dynamics based on
respectively the layer - and the reduced problem. The chapter is closed with sections on
travelling wave solutions from an analytical and numerical approach, section 2.4 & 2.5.

In this section, we cover the illustrated fast jumps proposed in fig. 2.6 and fig. 2.7. In
order to determine whether they are obtainable we propose the use of Melnikov’s theory.
We start by fixing a relevant x-values and study dynamics for the layer problem:

z′ = w

When modelling the fast jumps, we also fix a cleverly chosen value of y to match dynamics
from respectively the attractive and repelling parts. On the (z, w)-plane we return some
Melnikov distance function based on an appropriate metric. The objective is to determine
whether we can set this distance to zero and conclude that there exists a heteroclinic orbit
of the layer problem for the specific x-value.

2.4.4
Conclusion of Analytical Existence of Travelling Wave Solutions
With c2 = 0.476, close to the numerically found c2 ≈0.472, we were not able to complete
a series of heteroclinic orbits for the layer system eq. (2.27). Hence, we can not from the
above study prove the existence of a travelling wave solution for the complete system fol-
lowing from a single homoclinic orbit with 0 < ϵ ≪1.
That we are capable of performing a robust jump from Sl to the equilibrium at Sr for
c2 ≈0.472 is an interesting finding. It seems that selecting c2 ≈0.472 and thereby ˆc ≈2587
for the complete BK-model does have dynamical implications for solutions curves. How
they exactly look is still uncertain. One can also imagine system dynamics where Sl is
not reached. Maybe, we can construct a smaller travelling wave simply by the robustly
observed jump from Sr to Sm and an unstudied jump from Sm to the equilibrium at
x = −0.801? This we leave as an open question.
Another observation from section 2.3.3 is that c2 is a sensitive parameter. It was found
numerically to be 0.472 for a system of 20000 blocks, but how will changes in the selected
parameter value c2 change potential dynamics? We have previously looked primarily at
c2 ∈[0.4, 0.5] but what if we widen our parameter interval? The study of a widened inter-
val for c2 is initiated in section 2.4.5.
As a final consideration, Morales states in [7]:
After a very long transient, the fast oscillations near the rear shock disappear, which leads
to the shock-wave profiles displayed in the right column of Fig. 7 (in [7]). Given the long
time scale of simulations, it is not clear whether this slow drift towards a non-oscillatory
shock is a dynamical property of the BK model or is due to numerical integration errors.
The above statement can imply that the size of ϵ changes as a function of selected block n.
For blocks far away from the initially perturbed one, ϵ will become smaller. Hence, from
a GSPT perspective, it seems that the traveling wave structure (found for large n) in the
right column of Fig. 7 in [7] is more likely to be captured as a homoclinic connection from
a GSPT analysis, since ϵ there is sufficiently small. Therefore, we conclude that it is more
likely to obtain complete travelling waves solutions on the form fig. 2.7 than fig. 2.6.
2.4.5
Preliminary Proposal of Future Work
With the conclusions and open questions from section 2.4.4, we initiate a brief study on
a widened parameter interval of c2. In the same study, we look closely at what is exactly
happening for c2 ≈0.472 and determine if the behaviour is unique around c2 ≈0.472.
Finally, we in this section aim to be more careful and complete in our study of Sr to Sl as
this jump is part of the most likely existing homoclinic connection for the BK-model.
Looking again at Sl to Sr for a wider interval c2 ∈[0, 1] we see that for specific c2-values,
especially smaller, the numerical solutions can be lacking or at least non-trivial to interpret.
This result might be due to the influence on linearizations close to the nearby fold point.
One could use blow-up methods to extend studies of smaller c2-values, but in general
we only find one parameter value where the heteroclinic connection can occur, namely
c2 = 0.476. In fig. 2.12 we see how the blue point traverses the red curve from below at
exactly c = 0.476 illustrating the robust connection.

3.1.1
Defining the Lateral Loading Problem
It is the same subjects, that provide energy from a wind farm to our grid, which also
cause the lateral loading problem. Hence, in a simplified world we would have that the
better condition to extract energy from wind turbines, the bigger is the loading problem.
This correlation is exactly why the loading problem is of interest. The better we are at
solving it, the more we can relax constraints on foundation designs and extract more energy.
A monopile is impacted by a couple of different loads divided into two main categories:
Operational and environmental loads. The lateral loading problem is defined as the un-
derstanding of soil responses to horizontal loads. Lateral loading can represent up to 60%
of the vertical load [34] and is primarily caused by contribution from environmental loads
such as wind and waves. Wind and wave loads are dependent on the local environmental
conditions and their point of attack on the pile. For example, the wind loads can be divided
into steady aerodynamic forces and the more tricky stochastic aerodynamic forces follow-
ing from turbulent wind structures [34]. The amount of each type is highly dependent on

3.1.2
Possible Modelling Approaches of Pile Loading
There are a couple of current design practices when setting up a modelling approach for
monopiles’ response to loading. In fig. 3.4 we have illustrated the 3 most applicable and
used. We briefly touch upon all 3 itemized below.
3-D Finite Element
The most complex and computationally heavy approach to model
the idealised lateral loading of a pile is FEA (Finite Element Anal-
ysis) [4]. For example, this is the only current design practice with
the potential of modelling drag and front-line pressure as distribu-
tions, see fig. 3.3a. So in essence, the 3D FEA allows for detailed
modelling of the entire soil continuum and in addition predicts
a realistic response of the foundation. The requirements for 3D
FEA are heavy: One needs a robust and appropriate constitutive
model and correct soil inputs. Finally, there are also numerical
considerations. 3D FEA for stiff systems with cyclic loading is
for long-term loading analysis very computationally heavy.
To
perform such analysis on each turbine at a site would require ex-
tended computational work [37], especially if the results are used
within an ’optimization of design’ engine.
Winkler-type Approach
The Winkler-type approach is a 1-D model reducing the pile to a
beam [34,38]. As seen in fig. 3.4, one locates a series of indepen-
dent springs along the pile which supplements the pile head move-
ment with useful information of the response along the monopile.
The downscale in dimensions provides a heavy reduction in com-
putational time compared with 3-D FEA and it can be relatively
easily extended to multi-axial loading. With the reduction from
3-D modelling to 1-D you lose accuracy, although it has been
observed that changes to pile dimensions do not require new cali-
brated soil parameters [4]. It is within the Winkler-type approach
that p-y models are developed. For p-y models, the spring soil re-
action p at a given depth is described by a non-linear monotonic
increasing function of the lateral displacement y, see [39,40].

The simplest approach is macro models where we solely locate a

3.1.3
Introduction to HARM as a Macro Model & Potential
Extensions to Greater Dimensions
In this thesis, we will primarily study macro models, more specifically we target to study
the Hyperplastic Accelerated Racheting Model (HARM) framework developed in collab-
oration between Orsted and Oxford University. Even though HARM is in its framework
a macro model, it was developed due to questionings from DONG Energy (Now Orsted)
about the capability of 1-D p-y curves capabilities [39]. Specifically, with respect to oper-
ational wind turbines with a greater outer diameter. It appears that classical p-y curves
are under-predicting the foundation stiffness [34]. Under-estimation of the stiffness is con-
servative, therefore leading to large amount of steel used per foundation. In 2013 DONG
Energy thoroughly enumerated a list of technical issues using 1-D Winkler p-y curves [34].
Two major points from the list were that cyclic loading is poorly addressed through de-
graded pseudo-static p-y curves and that the original p-y formulations do not focus on the
small-strain response experienced during operational conditions. Both points are heavily
addressed and were the main objectives developing the constitutive model relying on the
HARM framework [34]. So how to interpret the HARM framework?
Let us first present the theoretical considerations upon which the framework is built. The
model is formulated using a hyperplasticity approach [35]. In section 3.2 and 3.3 we show
how the hyperplastisticy framework mathematically aligns with the Laws of Thermody-
namics. Further, it can be reduced to only two scalar functions for respectively energy
and dissipation. This result, showed in section 3.2, emphasises how concise the mathemat-
ical framework can become. The until now considered loading and displacement (H, u)
relationship on macro scale, introduced by the HARM framework, reduces to a stress and
strain (σ, ϵ) relation. Besides changing the terminology, the two relationships consist of
different units. While lateral loading H is measured in [N] and displacement µ in [m],
stress σ is now measured in [N/m] while the strain ϵ is dimensionless. Hence, we no longer
have units of length which is also why geophysicists call macro-scale models for 0-D models.
Since the HARM framework is a macro model, σ and ϵ should be understood as global
stress and global strain. For example, ϵ can be understood as the integrated soil reaction
distribution. Hence, as shown in fig. 3.4 we can allow our results to be directly integrated
into structural analyses without considering the distributed load down the pile. As dis-
cussed in section 3.1.2 the structural analyses might then not be super advanced. To take
the structural analyses to the next level and include depth of the pile (1-D modelling),
one can within the HARM framework extend it to a p-y similar model. This result was
shown in [41]. One can also imagine the framework being the basis for the development
of relevant constitutive models for 3D FEA calculations [34]. A final note on the HARM
framework as a macro model: With the 0-D model, HARM is stated more generally and
can be reused for other friction-driven systems [35].

3.2.1
Thermodynamic Terminology
In the application of thermodynamic principles to constitutive models for solids, two ap-
proaches are possible.
First, the models may be developed within a framework which
embodies the Laws of Thermodynamics. Secondly, models may be derived using arbitrary
procedures where thermodynamic principles are applied to the models post hoc. In HARM
the first approach is adopted. The approach used here is based on what is often termed
generalised thermodynamics. It places strong emphasis on the use of internal variables to
describe the past history of the material. The First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics
are enforced in the formulation described below so that any model defined within this
framework will automatically obey these laws.

3.4.1
Loading Curve - Restriction to One Global Variable Known
First, how to define ϵ as a function of time? We simply resolve this by stating that the
global strain ϵ must be a known smooth function in the given time interval. Hence, the
entire objective of the kinematic hardening modelling is then to solve for global stress as
a function of known global strain.
If we have had the opposite objective of solving for global strain as a function of global
stress we could infer a Legendre transformation and utilize Gibbs free energy instead of
Helmholtz free energy. This would be equivalent to isolating ϵ in eq. (3.26). With this
objective, it would then be a requirement that σ must be a known smooth function in the
time interval of interest.
We are left with the general requirement that either global strain ϵ or stress σ should be
known as function of time for the HARM framework. This requirement is classic when
modelling soil responses and leaves no major concern. For example, stress as a function of
time can be predicted from expected lateral loads. For simplicity, we will (before stating
otherwise) continue utilizing Helmholtz free energy and thereby solving the problem of
stress, σ, being the dependent variable as a function of known strain, ϵ.

4.4
Summarising GSPT in Kinematic Harderning Models
The horizontal loading problem can be modelled based on thermodynamically inspired
equations [35]. The equations are fundamental to introduce kinematic hardening models
with the property of being introduced both incrementally and continuously. Kinematic
hardening models can be used as a stand-alone but are otherwise the core of the HARM
framework. In this chapter, we have for the first time shown the existence of a slow fast
dynamical system with a relevant critical manifold in kinematic hardening models. It arises
by studying the model with global strain known and periodic as a function of stress. The
requirements for the slow fast dynamics to occur align with the real-world Kaolin data set.
The Kaolin data set was chosen randomly and is a quite general data set [46], hence there
exist many real-world scenarios where the slow fast dynamical system and GSPT analysis
can be utilized.
For a single plasticity surface, we have provided the one-dimensional critical manifold and
utilized Fenichel’s theorems to state also the leading order slow manifold. Extending to
multiple plasticity surfaces we obtain two different slow fast dynamical systems following
from an assumption of whether or not the surface spring constants are dependent on the
main spring constant H0. The two different slow fast dynamical systems have respectively
a critical manifold of dimension Ns and dimension 1, but the dynamics are similar. This
could be studied even further with the introduction of an interior layer.
We find that an initial perturbation is damped if Hi ̸= 0 for any plasticity surface i.
Hence, for studies of the long-term dynamics, one can set all initial stresses to 0. With this
knowledge, we have provided results trying to reduce the dimensional size modelling stress.
We conclude that by carefully selecting Hi, i > 0, a dimensional reduction is possible.

5.2.5
Connection Between the Regular and Inverse Slow Fast Systems
We will complete the section on the inverse slow-fast system by comparing it with the
regular slow-fast system. As they are defined from equivalent problems with respectively
strain and stress as an independent variable, we suspect some useful relations. Since we
have only defined the inverse slow-fast system for a single plasticity surface, we will restrict
ourselves to such reduced scenarios.
In the inverse problem the ratcheting αr and also internal strain α1 is constant at what
we suspect to be the slow manifolds. In the regular problem α1 and αr only changed at
the slow manifold. From the simulations of the regular problem we saw that decreasing δ
leads to more time spent at the slow manifold. While for the inverse problem decreasing δ
lead to less time spent on the assumable slow manifold, see fig. 5.8.

The physical understanding of the above is that activating and deactivating the slider com-

The contrary is the situation for stress, which remains rather constant in time when the
slider is activated. Now when the slider is no longer activated, we see close to immediate
jumps in stress while strain remains constant.
From the analysis above it follows that the two differently defined small parameters δ
actually have the same meaning for the entire loading problem. A small value of δ implies
that nearly at all times the slider is activated, while larger δ values imply significant
moments where the slider is not activated and the amount of ratcheting is relaxed. So
what is the exact global definition of δ? Well, δ is in essence a measure of how fast stress
from loading curves will reach the coefficient K1 where the slider is activated. For small δ-

definitions found from respectively the regular and inverse problem. Below, the physical

Limitations & Uncertainties of GSPT in the HARM
Framework
Chapter 5 shows perspectives of GSPT use in wider applications of the HARM framework.
Generally, the slow fast dynamical system demonstrated in this thesis does seem to have
extension possibilities to include ratcheting and a study of the inverse problem. However,
in this section we state a couple of reached limitations and concerns for future works.
5.3.1
Observed Limitations
Steep Complexity Increases
The major experience in chapter 5 has been the steep
complexity increase simply by adding ratcheting to try
completing the HARM framework.
Although most of
the work is preliminary, it is clear that the systems with
ratcheting require even more careful and technical GSPT
analysis. For example when H1 ̸= 0 in eq. (5.14) and
eq. (5.15) where the fast dynamics also become of inter-
est as the location of the normally hyperbolic slow mani-
folds changes during the fast jumps, see figs. 5.5 and 5.6.
There exist multiple extensions of HARM including e.g.
variable ratcheting rate [2]. With R as a function of time,
the complexity will increase significantly.
Normally Hyperbolic Parts
Although the inverse problem has only been studied very
preliminary, the lack of normally hyperbolic compact
parts of S0 make the use of GSPT limited. An alter-
native is to look at a parallel implementation contrary
to the studied serial kinematic hardening model. The
parallel model is invoked for the inverse problem [2].
5.3.2
Concerns for Further Studies
Inclusion of Stochasticity
GSPT is in general defined as fully deterministic, hence
not looking at the impact of stochastic effects.
Many
modern optimization engines are built upon stochastic
effects. Hence, GSPT will not be able to provide a con-
fidence interval of ratcheting. The lack of stochasticity
does not differ from the classical HARM framework.
Extension to Soil Layers
Finally, the major question is the extensibility of slow-
fast dynamics and GSPT analysis when increasing di-
mensions from the currently studied 0D macro models
to for example 1-D Winkler-type approaches. How many
continuity assumptions and conditions do we need to re-
quire for applications of GSPT in higher dimensions?
Let us look at the 0-D kinematic hardening model for the
regular problem without ratcheting. The addition of soil
layers will demand such a system for each layer, where
the systems are connected to their neighbouring layers
through individual layer strain functions ϵlayer [41].

5.4
Potential of GSPT Analysis in Loading Problem
Related Tools
In the previous section, we stated a couple of concerns and limitations on GSPT in loading
problems. Before finishing off this thesis, we also attempt to answer the opposite ques-
tion: How can the finding of slow fast dynamical systems in kinematic hardening models,
specifically HARM, be applied in loading problem related tools?
As mentioned, it is a general problem with long-term cyclic loading that it computationally
becomes impracticable to model every cycle individually - there could be millions. Hence,
providing estimates of for example ratcheting one would need to accelerate the model.
In [2] they propose accelerated modelling by a new scaling factor Rfac determined after the
cyclic reduction factor. This is clearly only an appropriate approach if the shape of the
underlying unload-reload loop is not modified as ratcheting proceeds. Otherwise, it is a
significant approximation.
If we can extend the analytical approach following from GSPT to higher dimensional mod-
els, we can for differently shaped input functions of either strain, ϵ, or stress σ utilize
our knowledge of the attractive parts of the critical manifold to at least provide a non-
conservative upper bound of for example ratcheting along the pile. This analytical bound
is found sufficiently faster and can prove more accurate than current solutions of the cyclic
loading problem.
In a foundation design optimization engine, one could apply such (close to immediately
found) bound in the wind farm industry to test many foundation designs for also long-term
cyclic loading. Afterwards, the incremental set-up can be used to validate the best found
design. The combined set-up will significantly decrease the amount of time spent within
the optimization phase corresponding to potentially better general foundation designs.
Another approach for GSPT in loading problem tools is to incorporate an expectation of
dynamics in current numerical solvers to avoid very stiff problems such that the numerical
solver does not have to deal with numbers that are of the same order as machine pre-
cision numbers. One must then glue fast and slow dynamics together. In conclusion, if
we are capable of extending GSPT analysis to higher dimensions of loading approaches,
then GSPT can bridge the gap between the mathematical HARM structure and related
numerical solvers to handle stiffness of the HARM model framework.

Summary & Conclusion
The research conducted in this study departs from two advanced frictional-dependent me-
chanical systems, respectively the Burridge-Knopoff model and the HARM framework.
The starting point from the two related models was very different, having already estab-
lished differential equations for Burridge-Knopoff. In what follows, the main conclusions
of each of the two models are stated:
• For the Burridge-Knopoff model, a truncated version is studied at the continuum
limit under the ansatz of travelling wave solutions. In chapter 2 we propose different
constructions of such travelling wave solutions with fast jumps close to the located
fold points.
The jumps occur between different parts of the normally-hyperbolic
subparts of the critical manifold forming potentially a homoclinic connection for the
equilibrium point of the full system. From the numerical analysis, an appropriate
parameter c2 ≈0.472 is found such that with scaling c = c2ϵ, travelling waves
solutions on the form fig. 2.7 are likely.
It is worth noting that while there are
numerical indications of a rear shock created by Sm as illustrated in fig. 2.6, the
analytical and numerical attempts to establish a heteroclinic map from Sm to Sl in
the layer system have not been successful.
• To reach the point of studying the HARM framework utilizing GSPT, a new equiva-
lent continuous version is constructed through chapter 3. The work is initiated by an
understanding of the thermodynamic assumptions and laws for elastic-viscoplastic
models by Byrne & Houlsby [2]. The chapter culminates with eq. (3.31) where a new
set of algebraic-differential equations for 0-D kinematic hardening models is proposed.
In chapter 4 it is shown that the unloading-reloading cycles following from the dif-
ferential equations in eq. (3.31) is in agreement with gain-loss of hyperbolicity for
obtained critical manifolds. The chapter is rounded off by trying to interpret the
implications of a multi-surface scenario on slow-fast dynamical system. It is con-
cluded that assumptions on the spring constants Hi, i > 0, are critical for system
structure. If Hi, i > 0 are selected appropriately, it is possible to provide a great
1-D piece-wise approximation of internal stress dynamics. The approximation im-
plies a heavy reduction in model size, as the many internal strains are reduced to one.
Finally, in chapter 5 the use of GSPT is extended to the complete HARM framework
including ratcheting. In the regular loading problem, highly accurate approximations
of ratcheting are produced using GSPT. Contrary, for the constructed inverse problem
GSPT does not provide further information for the system. Instead, we construct
an analytical bound for the experienced ratcheting. The chapter is completed with
sections on future use of GSPT in loading problem tools, sections 5.3 and 5.4.
The thesis serves as a precursor for the fundamental achievement of bridging the gap
between the mathematical loading problem structure & related numerical solvers to handle
stiffness. Completing the gap is incremental for future loading tools, as it provides fast and
accurate measures of hysteric & ratcheting behaviour experienced from cyclic loading. As
highlighted in section 5.4, next step is to extend the 0-D HARM macro model such that
it provides displacements along the pile. Here, the two components of the master thesis
collide, as a first approach would be to concatenate the ’chain of blocks’ discretization from
the Burridge-Knopoff with the HARM macro model. The combined framework may lead to
use of already-known results from Burridge-Knopoff studies within the loading problem.
