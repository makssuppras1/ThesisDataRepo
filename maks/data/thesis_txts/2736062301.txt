Abstract
In this thesis we bound the advantage of an adversary in the extractable Quantum Random Oracle
Model, playing the Find Failing Plaintexts - No Key game against Kyber. In the game, which was
introduced in [HHM22], an adversary, with no knowledge of the public or secret key, is tasked with
finding a message that causes a decryption failure.
Along the way, we update the computation of the expected failure rate over the keys. We also
compute a bound on the variance in failure rates over the keys, which, to the best of our knowledge,
has not been computed before. Additionally, we present a new upper bound on the success rate
of an adversary searching an extractable quantum random oracle for a Gaussian variable. This
last result is a general one, and may be of use in other security games.

Preliminaries
When quantum computers reach their full potential, many of the encryption schemes we use today
will become vulnerable to adversaries with access to a quantum computer. In late 2017 NIST1
issued a call for proposals to new quantum-secure schemes. At the time of writing, four systems
have been selected to replace our current public key encryption schemes, one of which is Kyber.
A scheme is considered quantum-secure if we can show that it is IND-CCA secure in the Quantum
Random Oracle Model (QROM). The new encryption schemes are Key Encapsulation Mechanisms
(KEMs). KEMs are built by applying the Fujisaki-Okamoto (FO) transformation to a Public-Key
Encryption scheme (PKE). Generally a KEM can be shown to be quantum-secure if we can show
that the underlying PKE has certain notions of security which are strengthened through the Fujisaki-
Okamoto transformation.
The underlying PKE of Kyber has a quality that complicates the security proof: decryption failures
may occur. A decryption failure is when an encrypted message does not decrypt back to the original
message m;
m ̸= Decryptsk(Encryptpk(m, ρ))
where (pk, sk) is the public and private key respectively and ρ is a randomness seed used for en-
cryption.
The original FO transformation was not applicable to PKEs that exhibit such behaviour but sub-
sequent works ([HHK17]) have given us variants on the FO transformation that can be applied to
imperfectly correct PKEs.
In [HHM22] Hövelmanns et alii introduced two new security games: the Find Failing Plaintexts -
No Key (FFP-NK) game and the Find Failing Plaintexts - Non Generically (FFP-NG), which allows
closer analysis of the effect decryption failures have on the security of a scheme. Our focus is
on FFP-NK, in which an adversary is given neither the private nor public key and is tasked with
finding a plaintext that fails in decryption. The game is played against a de-randomised version of
the underlying PKE.
If we can satisfyingly bound the advantage of an adversary playing the FFP-NK game against
Kyber, we can show that Kyber is IND-CCA secure. [HHM22] provides an upper bound on the
advantage of the FFP-NK adversary. The bound contains two statistical parameters of the PKE; the
expected failure rate when keys are chosen independently of the message, δik, and the variance
in failure rate, σ2
ik.
The aim of this thesis is to compute the two statistical parameters for Kyber by applying the general
results obtained in [HHM22].
In the rest of this chapter we present our problem statement, introduce central definitions, give an
overview of the context of the project, and present our contributions. We end the chapter with a
project outline.

1.3
KEMs and the Fujisaki-Okamoto Transformation
We do not need much information about the Fujisaki-Okamoto transformation, but FFP-NK is
played against the de-randomised PKEG scheme. I.e. the ”middle” step of the FO transforma-
tion. Therefore, we provide a quick recap of FO transformation here.
The purpose of a KEM is to allow two parties to exchange a symmetric encryption key, K, which
they can use in further communication. A KEM consists of three algorithms; a key generation al-
gorithm, an encapsulation algorithm, and a decapsulation algorithm.
KEMs are built by applying the FO transformation (or a variant thereof) to a PKE. The three al-
gorithms of a KEM call the key generation, encryption, and decryption algorithms of the PKE as
subroutines.
The original FO transformation does not allow correctness errors in the PKE, but Hofheinz et alii
introduced a solution in [HHK17]; when dealing with an imperfectly correct PKE, we can obtain a

simplified KyberG also applies to the true KyberG.
The two main take-aways from this section are that KyberG returns ⊥when a decryption failure
occurs and, since we replaced ρ with G(m), the probability of a decryption failure in KyberG is now
dependent on the choice of m.
Now we can introduce the FFP-NK game.
4.2
FFP-NK
In FFP-NK an adversary A is playing against the de-randomised KyberG and her goal is to identify a
failing plaintext. The game is played in the extractable Quantum Random Oracle Model (eQROMf).
In this model, the adversary has access to an extractable Quantum Random Oracle (eQROf),
which is defined relative to a function f. In the case of FFP-NK, that function is the encryption
function. Before elaborating on that, we have a look at the game:

The game starts with the drawing of a random key pair, (pk, sk). To win the game, the adversary,
A, must find a message m that fails with respect to the key pair. More precisely, she needs to find
a pre-image of a ciphertext. Suppose she has access to a list of ciphertexts and is looking for a
plaintext that fails. She cannot simply decrypt ciphertexts until one fails, because if it does, the
decryption in KyberG is ⊥. So no plaintext is revealed. She must start with the ciphertext, extract

Decryption Failures in Kyber

the pre-image that made it, and then encrypt and decrypt that pre-image.
The adversary A has access to an extractable Compressed Oracle simulator, eCO (an advanced
type of eQROf). The simulator, when queried with a ciphertext, returns a pre-image. The eCO
can be queried multiple times but the adversary can only decrypt one pre-image after the simulator
has finished.
After the simulator outputs an m, A encrypts the message m to c, and decrypts c to obtain a
message m′. The adversary wins iff the returned pre-image fails in decryption, which is when
m′ ̸= m. In that case, DecryptG returns ⊥. If a decryption failure occurs, the return value is 1,
otherwise it is 0.
The game is simple except for the eCO functionality which merits an introduction.
4.3
The Extractable Compressed Oracle, eCO
eCO stands for extractable Compressed Oracle (a type of quantum random oracle). The eCO is a
simulator which acts as a black-box oracle and is equipped with an internal ”database”.
The eCO has two interfaces; the extraction interface, eCO.Ef, and the random oracle interface,
eCO.RO. The extraction interface is defined relative to a function f, which, in FFP-NK, is the
encryption algorithm of the PKE, Encrypt. Encrypt takes two inputs; a message m and a hash of
the message G(m). The RO interface simulates hash function G from Algorithm 7, so an oracle
value RO(m) is G(m) = ρ.
The adversary can query both interfaces.
Figure 4.1: Graphic of the queries to each interface. Curved lines indicate quantum queries. In this
illustration, it appears as though the two interfaces are disconnected. That is not actually the case
but for our purposes a simplified representation will do. In this graphic, we have made the query
responses from Ef classical. That is not necessarily the case: Ef can return a super-positional
state of m which can then be measured to obtain a classical value for m.
The eCO’s internal database initially holds an entry for each RO-query m.
One query in the
database has an input and an output register, call them X and Y . The initial state of a query
is |⊥⟩(meaning ”no answer” or ”empty”).
In broad terms, the compressed oracle technique allows us some access to these internal quantum
states (the query states) without disturbing the general state of the database. There are two opera-
tors that can be applied to the query registers. One is the oracle unitary, OXY . When applied to the
initial query state, it puts the state into a uniform superposition |+⟩, meaning the input register X
”holds” all possible m values, and the output register holds all possible output values RO(m). The
other operator is the measurement operator Mt which partially collapses the state of a query reg-
ister, to a point where it contains only those values (m, RO(m)) for which Encrypt(m, RO(m)) = c
is true (c being a ciphertext).
When the extraction interface is queried with a ciphertext c, it tries to extract a state of (m, RO(m))
from the database, such that Encrypt(m, RO(m)) = c. It can only find such a state, if the RO has
previously been queried with one. If it cannot find one, it outputs ⊥.

is also large. Because of the error margin, j will sometimes be a little larger than i, sometimes a
little smaller. So when we put all values of i through steps 2 and 3, the value of i −j distributes
evenly on the interval.4

ρwc, and used them to bound the variance by assuming that every ρ would produce the failure rate

imb(ρa) < imb(ρb) =⇒δik,a ≤δik,b

probability distribution of the imbalance measures and paired it with known failure rates to obtain
a slightly tighter bound.

a simple Markov with a cap (section 7.3). This is the value we present as our final result.
We did obtain a smaller estimate in subsection 7.4.1, using two tail bounds for vectors and the

8.4.2
Is it Gaussians All the Way Down?
We have seen the χη distributions and the Z′ distribution. They are discrete, so technically it is not
correct to call them Gaussians. They are binomials. But they do have the same shape.
What does the failure rate distribution look like? In applying the Chernoff bound, we have assumed
that it is Gaussian. Is that a fair assumption?
The elements of the distribution are failure rates, and each failure rate stems from a Z′
ρ-distribution.
The failure rate is the ”weight” in the tail of the Z′
ρ-distribution. As discussed in chapter 7, the failure
rate of one ρ is high when the tails are heavy, i.e. when the variance is big. The variance is big when
the coefficients in the encryption components are large. The coefficients are sampled from the χη
distributions. Because the χη distributions are centred binomials, the Gaussianity is inherited into
the Z′
ρ-distribution and from there into the failure rate distribution.
Technically the failure rate distribution is also discrete. However, the distribution is so large, and
tightly concentrated, that is seems reasonable to view it as continuous and describe it simply as a
Gaussian. So yes, it appears to be Gaussians all the way down.

Randomness and Sampling Functions
Kyber uses randomness for key generation and for encryption. The encryption randomness seed ρ

function (XOF’s).

sampled variables in the encryption algorithm.

G : B∗→B32 × B32
G(x) := SHA3-512(x) = (a, b)
