Abstract
Integration of renewables is changing the electricity system and markets in a number of dif-
ferent ways. Addition of bulk electricity storage may solve many of the issues but currently
known technologies appear to have a limited expansion potential mainly due to economical
constraints.
This study deals with the combined technical and economical assessment of a new thermal
storage of electricity concept based on the idea of storing heat in a packed bed of rocks
heated up by air at a high temperature. Electricity is converted to heat by means of an
electric heater or a air heat pump, whereas the opposite can be achieved with steam or air
cycles.
The aim of this work was to highlight technical challenges by evaluating round-trip eﬃciency
in design conditions and modelling the temperature proﬁle in the storage unit. Also, the
optimal behaviour in the future electricity market was modelled in the electricity system
model Balmorel evaluating the business case for revamping applications and the system
eﬀect of optimal investments in the technology.
It was found that the studied technology can achieve round-trip storage eﬃciencies of at
least 40% with high dependency on component performance. The storage thermal model
indicated that the steady state exergetic performance of the packed bed can be above
90% even without any improvement strategy. Modifying the temperature gradient should
improve the operational range and keep eﬃciency close to 100%. A suggestion on how to
do that by at the same time generating energy for district heating was provided as well.
The economic analysis showed that thermal storage would perform similarly to a peaker
plant in the electricity market, hence with low generation capacity factor. A revamping
application of electricity-symmetric thermal storage in the Danish energy system may be
able to repay its annualized ﬁxed costs from 2030. A great deal of the success was due to
capacity credit, i.e. the ability of the storage unit to provide reliable capacity when demand
and supply were unbalanced.
Also, a third of the revenues came from district heating
production. An electricity-asymmetric unit showed potential for higher revenues and even
lower investment cost if used to revamp combined cycle gas turbine plants.
The introduction of thermal storage of electricity in the energy system was found to decrease
system costs, more so when integrated with oﬀshore wind. It improved the business case
for renewable energy technologies and penalised mainly gas-ﬁred generators.
Overall, thermal storage of electricity may be a viable investment option for the future
energy system but its success possibilities seem to be strongly linked with achieving a low
investment cost.

Chapter 1.
Introduction
1 Introduction
In this chapter a general introduction to the topic of electricity storage is given.
Motivation for the need of storage is provided by presenting several potential beneﬁts.
The currently known storage technologies are brieﬂy presented and their main
features are named. In this context thermal energy storage and speciﬁcally Henrik
Stiesdal’s concept are introduced.
Following, motivation and aims for this thesis are presented and the approach that
will be followed is illustrated.
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Rationale for Energy Storage
The current European electricity system is facing major challenges. More and more focus is
put on reaching low-carbon energy production by means of incentives towards installation
of renewable energy plants and implementation of energy savings.
Renewable energy sources (RES) are often ﬂuctuating and uncontrollable. As a result they
can put strain on the electricity system in terms of grid and market issues.
As for the former, problems can derive from extreme (positive or negative) power ramps
that can result from extensive integration of wind or solar power. Power output can vary
with high frequency thus forcing diﬀerent strategies for dispatching than usual [1]. These
cause conventional generators to quickly modify their power output with negative eﬀects
on performance, emissions and equipment lifetime. Additionally forecast errors can lead to
unplanned lack or excess of power. Finally, RES generation can cause congestions due to
inadequate grid development [2]. These phenomena contribute in increasing the cost borne
1
==PAGE:30==
1.1.
Background
by TSO’s (Transmission System Operators) and DSO’s (Distribution System Operators) to
run the electricity grid properly and guarantee power quality and reliability to consumers [3].
On the market side, low-carbon energy sources such as wind and solar can have a strong
eﬀect on prices in the day-ahead market. Due to their low marginal production cost when
deploying extensive amounts of energy they cause price to drop. This is called merit order
eﬀect. In addition, depending on the subsidy scheme to which generators are allowed to
access, they might have an incentive in bidding at a negative price in the spot market 1.
As an example, historical data from [4] shows that in the period 2012-2015 the German
spot market price EEX went negative in 316 occasions (frequency of 1%) mainly because of
high wind production. That causes also losses in revenues for wind producers themselves,
which are mathematically accounted by the diﬀerence between the average price and the
wind production-weighted average price, i.e. the downlift.
These issues could be mitigated by additional ﬂexibility in the power system and market,
e.g. with demand response management, highly ﬂexible generation or energy storage. These
alternatives would have profoundly diﬀerent eﬀects on society if implemented.
Demand response management would require that each consumer fundamentally change
the way they behave with respect to electricity consumption. Today demand is basically
inelastic, i.e. it has a very shallow response on price changes. Implementing demand re-
sponse would mean having consumers to react to price development. The potential system
beneﬁt is great but transaction costs may be prohibitive due to the substantial behavioural
changes required.
Conventional generators have already started to improve their assets to become more and
more ﬂexible. Products like GE’s virtual power plant [5] go in this direction. Nevertheless
it may be not socially optimal to have producers bear all the costs related to increasing
system ﬂexibility.
Energy storage would be a valid alternative but ownership plays an important role. As will
be discussed further, substantial deployment of energy storage owned by proﬁt-maximizing
producers has eﬀects on prices that in turn decrease potential for additional investment in
storage. Also, current technologies appear to have excessive capital cost to be successful in
a market without support schemes.
Energy storage technologies can be employed both for capacity and energy purposes, i.e.
they can provide regulation and grid balancing services, reserve capacity but also stabilise
spot market prices if employed on a large scale.
A report for the US Department of Energy [6] by Sandia National Laboratories indicates
17 possible diﬀerent applications of energy storage in the power system. They are grouped
between applications in:
• Electricity supply - main application is arbitrage2, i.e. buying energy in low-price
times and selling in high-price hours, thus having a stabilizing eﬀect on the spot price.
Social welfare is improved by the usage of low price energy and avoidance of too high

price. Storage units can also substitute additional capacity of, for instance, peaking
plants adding to security of supply.
• Ancillary services - Storage units can provide load following services in a ﬂexi-
ble way. When charging, up-regulation can be applied by decreasing consumption
and down-regulation by increasing it (if possible).
Conversely when discharging
up-regulation can be done by increasing power generation (if possible) and down-

a lower wear cost due to output variability.
• Grid system - Energy accumulation in speciﬁc key areas can support transmission
by relieving congestions, with avoided cost for T&D (Transmission and Distribution)
systems. In addition it can be used as a cheaper alternative to grid investments. This
is already being done in some EU countries, e.g. Italy [8] where a large battery has
been installed on the line connecting the continent with Sicily in order to improve
utilization of the wind resources in the region.
• Utility - Smaller, utility-scale storage can provide value in terms of demand manage-
ment, i.e. adapting consumption to price trends. Also electric service reliability and
power quality can be improved whenever the grid connection was not satisfactory.
• Renewables integration - Energy storage technologies allow renewable production
time-shift thus reducing price drops and stabilizing price. They can also be used for
capacity ﬁrming, i.e. adding reliability and controllability to renewable production,
thus improving grid integration by decreasing ﬂuctuation and eﬀects of forecast errors.
• Distributed energy storage - All beneﬁts illustrated above can be seen on a smaller
scale when employing storage on a distributed level. Also, diﬀerent speciﬁc beneﬁts
can stem from particular situations depending on the location and the characteristics
of the system in the area.
Not all energy storage types are suitable for all applications because of diﬀerent inertia,
response time, availability or technology-speciﬁc constraints. This work will focus on the
electricity supply aspects, i.e. storage in the day ahead market.
1.1.2
Energy Storage Technologies
Several energy storage technologies are available at present, with very diﬀerent scales, ap-
plication and characteristics.
Technology features in terms of being able to store energy and generate electricity are ex-
pressed in terms of four parameters, i.e. charging capacity, discharging capacity, charging
hours and discharging hours.
Charging capacity is the rated power on the charging side, i.e. the amount of power that
the storage unit is able to consume at full load. Similarly the discharging capacity is the
rated power on the generation side. The ratio of these two parameters (with consumption
capacity on the denominator) is called capacity ratio. It may be optimized to maximize
possible earnings. The optimal value depends on the market characteristics, i.e. on the fre-
quency of low-price times or price spikes, peak to baseload average price ratio and others.
3
==PAGE:32==
1.1.
Background
Storage charging hours express the energy content of the storage by quantifying the num-
ber of hours it would take to completely charge the storage at the rated charging capacity.
Same deﬁnition is given for discharging hours but with relation to the discharging capacity.
It follows that whenever the charge/discharge capacity ratio is one then charging and dis-
charging hours are equal if losses from the storage are neglected. If the capacity ratio is not
one than the economically optimal number of charging (or discharging) hours will depend
on the capacity ratio itself.
A ﬁrst distinction among technologies should be done on the general basis of application
[9]:
• Power quality - technologies that are able to accumulate energy for seconds to
minutes. Main usage is for frequency regulation.
• Bridging power - technologies with storage capacity up to one hour. Often used for
contingency reserves or ramping.
• Energy Management - technologies with storage capacities up to several hours.
Biggest eﬀects are on load shifting, capacity ﬁrming and T&D investment deferral.
According to [10] six diﬀerent categories of storage technologies can be found:
1. Solid state batteries - Electro-chemical storage of energy. They include electro-
chemical capacitors, Li-ION, Ni-Cd and Na-S batteries. The ﬁrst technology is reli-
able and has a long lifetime. It is particularly suitable for discharge times of less than
one second. Conventional batteries like Li-ION’s have and advantage in the fact that
the technology is mature and developed while cost is steadily decreasing while still
quite high.
2. Flow batteries - Energy is stored in an electrolyte solution, instead of in the electrode
material as in conventional batteries. They oﬀer the advantage of fast and simple
rechargeability by replacing the electrolyte solution but are still in the development
phase.
3. Flywheels - Devices that store rotational energy with a very fast response time.
They have a very long lifetime and are suitable for load following and power quality
and reliability services.
4. Compressed air storage (CAES) So far one of the most studied storage technolo-
gies capable of energy management. Energy is stored in the form of pressurised air
by compressing it and keeping it in an underground cavern. To produce electricity,
air is then expanded through a turbine. In the traditional scheme compression heat
(due to ineﬃciency of the compressors) is lost and has therefore to be replaced when
expanding, usually by burning natural gas to heat up the air at the turbine inlet. In
the adiabatic version compression heat is recuperated and stored to then be used to
heat the expanding air. Both the applications have the disadvantage of the need of
particular geo-morphological conditions since a natural underground cavern is needed.
5. Thermal energy storage (TES) - Energy is stored in the form of heat. This cate-
gory has a great variability in applications and sizes, from energy storage in buildings
to bulk electricity storage. As for the latter, the design principle is to use electricity to
4

generate heat and store the thermal energy, which is much easier and cheaper to ac-
cumulate than electricity directly. When discharging, a heat engine is able to convert
the stored heat to electricity. TES may be cheaper than other competing technologies
but shows limited eﬃciency due to thermodynamic limitations.
6. Pumped hydro - The most widespread electricity storage technology.
Installed
capacity in the whole world in 2012 was 104 GW [11], of which 44 GW in Europe.
Pumped hydro storage exploits a height diﬀerence between two water basins. Water
is pumped to the upper basin when charging and stored as potential energy. When
discharging, water is run through a turbine and the lower basin is reﬁlled.
The present work will focus on large scale energy managing applications. In this ﬁeld the
competing technologies are, today, CAES, TES and pumped hydro. Batteries are currently
not competitive due to limited lifetime and higher costs.
Among the three highlighted technologies, thermal energy storage has the least locational
requirements and could therefore be the most attractive if economically competitive.
1.1.2.1
ETES and HT-TES
Siemens Wind Power has developed in its R&D department a thermal storage of electric-
ity concept called ETES (Electro-Thermal Energy Storage). It is based on a solid storage
material (built of rocks, sand or ash), a charging cycle constituted of an air heat pump,
possibly substituted by an electric heater, and a discharge cycle based on a conventional
Rankine cycle, likely of an already existing plant to be renewed. The idea was based on a
patent from 1992 illustrating a possible way to store wind energy [12].
Henrik Stiesdal, former CTO of Siemens Wind Power, has come up with his own version of
the technology after leaving Siemens in 2014, which he named HT-TES (High Temperature
Thermal Energy Storage). It has the same charging possibilities of ETES, but the dis-
charge cycle is based on air. This allows a more compact system of a smaller scale. Another
possible conﬁguration is a combination of the two i.e. by using the HT-TES as a topping
cycle and a conventional Rankine cycle as the bottoming cycle. Heat is given at the highest
temperature level to the modiﬁed Brayton-Joule cycle and the waste heat of this is used to
generate additional power in the Rankine cycle below. In this case the bottoming cycle can
be an already existing one.
These two technologies will be more thoroughly described in Section 2.2.2.
1.2
Project description
1.2.1
Motivation
Potential for electricity storage investment is currently limited, at least in the northern Eu-
ropean system, due to the fact that ﬂuctuation in prices is low and so-far-developed storage
technologies are too capital intensive. Thermal storage of electricity has the possibility to

have a lower investment cost than the other storage technologies and presents fewer geo-
graphical constraints. At the same time it achieves lower eﬃciencies and components such
as the packed bed unit can lead to decreased performance if not properly designed and
operated. Therefore a detailed study that highlights possibilities and challenges is needed.
When dealing with this type of technology it seems important to keep an eye both on the
technical and on the economic side at the same time, as one strongly interferes with the
other. This is the philosophy of this work.
1.2.2
Aim
The aim of this project is to study thermal energy storage from both a technical and an
economical perspective. Speciﬁcally the studied technologies are ETES and HT-TES. This
work should be able to give a broad picture of what are the important technical features and
what could make a successful business case for the technology. It will highlight what the
speciﬁc techo-economical characteristics are and which are the most important challenges.
The expected outcomes of the project are:
1. An estimate of the round-trip storage eﬃciency of both technologies. Additionally
key issues to achieve satisfactory eﬃciency will be highlighted in order to be able to
point out crucial factors to possible stakeholders, constructors or investors.
2. A study of the transient behaviour of the storage unit with the aim to give an instru-
ment able to predict the performance under a given schedule.

Approach
The above-mentioned aims will be met by building speciﬁc models to simulate the technol-

The round-trip storage eﬃciency will be calculated by building a thermal design model

2 Analysis framework
This chapter provides the general framework for the analysis. Recommendations for
the study of economic potential of energy storage are taken from scientiﬁc literature.
Additionally the general concept of thermal storage of electricity is explained and a
review of the performance levels of technologies of this type is presented. Further the
concept of ETES or HT-TES is illustrated and the basic technical and economical
features are given.
2.1
Economics of energy storage
Even if possible advantages of energy storage are numerous, their economic estimation in
system perspective remains complicated due to the scale of the possible eﬀects. In this
perspective it is important to diﬀerentiate between an application and a beneﬁt. According
to [14] an application is a speciﬁc technological use of energy storage, as those illustrated in
Section 1.1.1. A beneﬁt can be seen as the outcome of a application, i.e. is related to money
(additional revenues, avoided costs) or improved system reliability, security and adequacy.
Academic literature has mostly focused on single applications, e.g. by estimating system
beneﬁts of bulk energy storage performing only arbitrage in the day-ahead market and dis-
regarding other possible applications, since combination of more applications results in a
more complicated assessment.
Two clearly separate levels can be identiﬁed: (i) A system perspective that evaluates the
eﬀect of storage for the energy system or for society as a whole and (ii) a private perspective,
that concentrates on the value for the storage owner. In the ﬁrst case, an energy system
simulation tool is needed to assess the eﬀect of the technology. In the second case, a sim-
pler optimization instrument can be used, as it is not necessary to understand the mutual
interaction between the technology and the rest of the system.

2.1.
Economics of energy storage
2.1.1
Owner beneﬁts, price taking models
Studies like [15] by Sioshansi et al. and [16] by Walawalkar et al. focused on the private
perspective, evaluating possible beneﬁts from arbitrage in [15] and from both arbitrage and
regulation in [16]. They both used a linear programming optimization technique thanks to
which the optimal dispatch of a price-taking storage unit could be found and its maximum
revenue calculated.
Sioshansi et al. analysed the eﬀect of diﬀerent sizes of the storage by varying the discharging
hours and found that the marginal value of an additional discharging hour sharply dropped
around 8 h for a storage unit with 70% round-trip eﬃciency. They also pointed out that
uncertainty in future prices did not play an important role in the analysed system, since
the storage unit was able to obtain 85% of the maximum revenues even when using a two-
week backcasting strategy1. It should be noted that this result is strongly related to the
type of system and may change signiﬁcantly when applied in a renewable-intensive context.
Finally authors show that deploying large-scale energy storage has a ﬂattening eﬀect on
energy prices that decreases the potential for additional investment.
Walawalkar et al. showed that in New York State Electricity Market there was potential for
storage units basing their revenues on arbitrage and reserve, speciﬁcally Na-S batteries for
the former and ﬂywheels for the latter. They also pointed out that achieving high eﬃciency
might give a better business case than decreasing investment cost. Again, this ﬁnding is
true in the context of the study and may not be considered general.
While the illustrated studies have the strength of being based on historical, accurate data,
they do not allow to include the eﬀects of the changes in prices that can come from storage
capacity installation. These can only be accounted in full scale energy models, i.e. consid-
ering storage as part of the energy system.
2.1.2
System perspective
As pointed out in Section 1.1.1 energy storage can be beneﬁcial to the electricity system
in terms of increasing its ﬂexibility therefore capability of integrating renewable energy
sources.
High level documents such as [6] and [14] provide the analysis framework and beneﬁt quan-
tiﬁcation guidelines from a general, technology-independent perspective. Following these,
in [17] a number of SMO (Storage Market Opportunities) are evaluated for the US electric-
ity market. Authors found that the most attractive ones concern T&D (transmission and
distribution) beneﬁts, for which up to 90 $/kW/y can be saved, and power cost volatility
reduction. Authors also came to the conclusion that an environmental credit of at least
0.1 $/kWh is needed to make storage (combined with renewable generation) economically
attractive for emission reduction purposes.
Other studies use energy system simulation tools to assess the social beneﬁts of energy
storage, but tend to focus on speciﬁc technologies and lose in generality due to the need for
implementing technology-speciﬁc constraints.

2.1.2.1
The Balmorel Model
In order to assess the performance of thermal storage technologies in the European Energy
system the Balmorel Energy Model [21] has been used. It is a model that was born as a
modelling tool for the power and combined heat and power sectors in the Baltic regions.
It was originally developed as a cooperation between several organizations in the period
1999-2001 [13].
Since then its development has been taking contributions by a great number of users, e.g.
energy consulting companies like Ea Energy Analyses and COWI, universities like DTU
and the RisøNational Laboratory for Sustainable Energy, utility companies like Hofor and
organizations such as Dansk Energi. Several users have also given their own contribution
to the model. It is now extended to most of Europe. For the development of this thesis the
Dansk Energi version has been used, which includes the countries represented in Figure 2.1.
Balmorel is a dynamic partial economic equilibrium model developed in GAMS [22] as a
linear optimization problem. The objective function to be minimized is the total system
cost constrained by an hourly balance of production and consumption therefore simulat-
ing the day-ahead electricity (and heat) market. Demand is generally considered inelastic,
which is why the tool is deﬁned as a partial equilibrium model.
Balmorel is a simulation tool with a hourly resolution, capable of changing time aggrega-
tion and running for several years and scenarios as speciﬁed by the user. It allows time

2.2.1
Basic thermodynamics
As anticipated in Section 1.1.2 the concept of thermal energy storage of electricity consists
in transforming electricity to heat by means of a heat pump or high temperature electric
heater, storing the heat and then re-transforming it to electricity by means of a heat engine
whenever needed. Generally speaking, the charging and discharging cycles can either be
diﬀerent or utilize the same equipment that must then be reversible.
The performance of a storage unit can be eﬀectively measured in most cases by the round-
trip storage eﬃciency, deﬁned as the ratio between the energy generated and the energy
consumed. The round-trip storage eﬃciency for a TES (Thermal Energy Storage) then
depends on the charging performance coeﬃcient, the discharging eﬃciency and the eﬀec-
tiveness of the heat transfer to and from the storage.

ing. According to the second law of thermodynamics no real system operating between the
same temperature levels can have a higher eﬃciency, so considering a Carnot cycle can help
understanding the upper limit of performance.

The charging system
Both cycles can be equipped with the same type of charging
system. It can be constituted of a high temperature electric heater or by a gas heat pump.
For the latter, a gas similar to air must be used since heat pumps using normal refrigerants
cannot reach high temperatures after the compression. The system would be constituted
of a compressor, a hot heat exchanger connected to the storage, an expander and a cold
heat exchanger. The working ﬂuid is compressed and heated up by the compressor until
the desired temperature. Then it is cooled down by giving thermal energy to the storage in
the hot heat exchanger. Consequently it is expanded in a turbine that allows to recuperate
useful work and increase eﬃciency. The temperature after expansion would be well below
ambient and therefore cold can be produced. The ﬂuid is then brought back to the starting
temperature in the ﬁnal cold heat exchanger. The system is represented in Figure 2.3 in an
ETES conﬁguration.
Such a heat pump would allow to have a COP greater than 1 if properly designed and cou-
pled with the storage and discharging units. Nevertheless it poses some challenges: (i) to
reach high temperatures several compression stages would be needed, (ii) the cold temper-
ature reached after the expansion would cause ice formation if moist air was used, therefore
the cycle must be closed and employ either perfectly dry air or a similar gas (Nitrogen or
Argon), (iii) performance depends on temperature levels.

Electricity asymmetric unit
This technology also oﬀer the advantage of the possibil-
ity of installing diﬀerent capacities on the charging and discharging sides, since they are
fundamentally separate systems. For instance, it could be possible to build a unit with a
capacity ratio higher than unity if that was shown to be economically beneﬁcial.
In this study comparisons will be made between an electricity-symmetric unit, i.e. with
the same charging and discharging capacities, and an electricity-asymmetric unit or heat-
symmetric, i.e. with the capacity ratio equal to the reciprocate of the round-trip eﬃciency.
For a unit with 40% discharging eﬃciency, in fact, 1 MWh of electricity can be generated by
discharging 2.5 MWh of thermal energy from the storage. If the charging COP is 100% (e.g.
with an electric heater charging system) 1 MWh of electricity corresponds to 1 MWh of
thermal energy. Hence to make a heat symmetric unit an electricity consumption capacity
of 2.5 MW would be needed. The same eﬀect would be obtained with a charging COP of
250% and consumption capacity of 1 MW.

2.2.2.1
Thermal storage unit: conﬁguration and challenges
Both ETES and HT-TES technologies are based on solid storage materials, i.e. crushed
basalt rock and coal ash or sand in a conﬁguration similar to a packed bed. High temper-
ature thermal energy storage currently sees most applications in the ﬁeld of solar thermal
technologies to allow storing of solar energy and improve dispatchability. [38] provides a
comprehensive review of the diﬀerent thermal storage technologies and materials pointing
out advantages and disadvantages. Solid thermal storage, usually in form of packed bed,
provides a cheap alternative but can have problems related to use that will be illustrated
later in this section.
The general layout of the storage for the analysed technologies is described by Stiesdal in
his patents [40] and [41]. Some speciﬁc modiﬁcations have been done to the basic concept
of a packed bed of rocks to improve eﬃciency.
The ﬁrst is based on the idea of reversing the ﬂow of air in the storage when switching from
charging to discharging, somehow employing a counter-current principle. In this way the
hot side of the storage, i.e. the inlet section of air when charging, is also the outlet section
when discharging. As a consequence the heat transfer is eﬃcient (being comparable to a
counter-current heat exchanger) and the obtainable ﬂuid outlet temperature is closer to the
temperature of the storage mean on the outlet side, thus increasing eﬃciency. By placing
both connections with the storage at the same side it is possible to shorten feeding or return
pipes and therefore reduce losses.
Such a unit oﬀers the advantage that the storage mean is very cheap, suﬃciently dense not
to have excessive storage volume and easy to handle and store with no need for pressuriza-
tion. On the other hand, three main issues are found when using solid storage materials.
The ﬁrst is that the storage temperature evolves with time and the heat transfer with the
working ﬂuid decreases as the storage is loaded (or unloaded) [39]. The decreasing heat
transfer is due to the fact that as the storage is ﬁlled up with thermal energy, an expanding
part of the device is heated up to a temperature close to the one of the heat transfer ﬂuid,
thus decreasing the mean temperature diﬀerence between the storage material and the ﬂuid
across the unit. Secondly, after several incomplete charging and discharging cycles the spa-
cial temperature gradient will progressively ﬂatten (Figure 2.6).

The temperature gradient in a solid thermal storage unit is deﬁned thermocline. If the area

so that quality of energy that is released by the storage decreases together with eﬃciency of
discharge. The ﬂatter the thermocline, the sooner the eﬀect of outlet temperature variation
will be seen.
Finally, when the storage is neither charged nor discharged, the internal temperature gradi-
ent will cause conduction through the storage material and convection in the ﬂuid remaining
in the storage depending on the orientation. This will cause mixing losses and may decrease
hot-end temperature. These cause the operational zone of the unit to decrease. The user
would be forced to operate the storage below its full capacity and limiting the amount of
incomplete cycles performed thus worsening the business case.
To limit these inconvenient phenomena Stiesdal developed several solutions in terms of de-
sign of the storage unit.
In [42] it is suggested that the storage unit should be longitudinally developed, i.e. that
the length of the unit parallel to the heat transfer ﬂuid direction should be suﬃciently
long compared to the inlet velocity. This allows the hot and cold end temperatures of the
storage to be almost constant at every loading level.
The logic behind this is that the
length of the area presenting the temperature gradient depends on ﬂow velocity and ther-
mal characteristics of the storage mean, but not on storage length. Also, the capacity of
the storage is proportional to the volume, i.e. to the length if cross-sectional area is kept
constant. Therefore the longer the unit, the lower the charging level at which a decrease
in the outlet temperature is seen, thus the larger the operative region at maximum eﬃciency.
The described design feature, though, does not prevent the ﬂattening of the proﬁle when
performing multiple incomplete cycles. The decrease in the temperature gradient can also
happen because of conduction losses in the storage material which would tend to equalize
the hot and cold end temperatures. In order to avoid this the storage can be internally
subdivided into independent sections insulated from each other thus decreasing internal
conduction losses.
The internal insulation oﬀers multiple opportunities for eﬃcient operation as well. Firstly,
by equipping such a unit with several parallel connections as showed in Figure 2.7a it is
possible to adapt the size of the storage to the actual working conditions. That is to say,
if the owner decides to charge (discharge) the storage with a small quantity of energy it is
possible to use only the sections closer to the inlet/outlet side. Consequently the unused
sections would remain at the previous temperature level allowing for the highest possible
heat transfer in the future.
Secondly, having diﬀerent insulated sections it is possible to correct the temperature spacial
gradient as described in [43]. In this patent the storage unit is equipped with alternative
parallel feed-in pipes that allow to insert a hot (or cold) ﬂuid in diﬀerent positions along
the length of the storage instead of from the inlet section. Consequently the temperature
proﬁle can be restored and rendered steeper.

2.2.3
Economic features of thermal storage of electricity
The main feature of ETES and HT-TES is the use of a solid material as thermal storage
mean. This is the strongest point of this technology from an economic perspective, as it
gives two advantages over other types of storage:
• The storage material is inexpensive and easy to handle;
• The storage unit can stand very high temperatures, making it possible to integrate
with already existing conventional power plants.
The investment cost of storage has been estimated by [44] around 500 e/MWhth without
considering infrastructure cost.

2.2.4
Literature review
The following review is divided in two parts. The ﬁrst focuses on studies related to charging
and discharging cycles. Here a history of the evolution of the concept of thermal energy
storage is presented. The second part deals with studies on packed bed thermal storage
units.
2.2.4.1
Thermodynamic cycles
Thermal storage of electricity was ﬁrst proposed in 1924 by German engineer Marguerre
[46]. He studied an electricity-driven axial steam compressor coupled with a storage unit
based on pressurized hot water, where energy was cumulated in form of both heat and
pressure.
The concept was then revisited in the 70’s after the oil crisis by Cahn, who patented the
idea of a thermal storage coupled with a reversible heat pump [47], [48]. Such a device
worked transferring heat between two hot and two cold storages.
When charging, water from a hot water storage was ﬂashed and therefore cooled down in a
cascade multiple pressure system. The condensate was stored in the cold storage, whereas
the steam generated in the ﬂashing process was compressed. Heat coming from the com-
pression was then exchanged with a low vapor pressure (LVP) thermal energy retention
material ﬂowing from a cold to a hot storage. When discharging, the hot LVP material was
used to generate steam expanded through the turbine.
More recently Desrues et al. [49] and White et al. [50] analysed the concept of thermal stor-
age of electricity using direct and inverse Brayton-Joule cycles for charging and discharging.
[49] considered a system with storage units at diﬀerent pressures and designed as regenera-
tors, i.e. made of refractory materials alternatively storing or releasing thermal energy. [50]
considered instead the possibility of having a packed-bed thermal storage.
They performed a general thermodynamic analysis to point out the most important ineﬃ-
ciencies in such a technology. [49] concludes that expected performances are comparable to
the ones of other bulk electricity storage systems (CAES, PHS) but face minor geograph-
ical constraints. Eﬃciency trends report that for a storage temperature of 600◦C 4 and a
polytropic eﬃciency of 90%5 eﬃciency is above 70%.
Results in [50] showed that the power and energy density of a unit were increasing with
the temperature ratio, i.e the ratio between the hot and cold storage temperatures. On the
other hand eﬃciency improved when decreasing the ratio, leading to the need of a dedicated
optimization. Achieved eﬃciency was of 40% when considering an average component isen-
tropic eﬃciency of 80% and an isentropic temperature ratio of 1.7.
Authors also point out that due to the peculiarities of the solid storage mean, the irre-
versibilities related to the heat transfer with the storage strongly depend on the way the
unit is operated.

2.2.
Thermal storage of electricity
Thermal energy storage has also been developed by corporate research. ABB has demon-
strated interest publishing several patents and articles [52], [53], [54].
In [52] the apparatus needed for a thermal storage of electricity application is described.
The possibility of a solid-state storage is investigated to avoid design diﬃculties coming from
phase changing or pressurized liquid storage materials. Possible charging devices include
a simple high temperature electric heater included in the storage or a more complex and
eﬃcient heat pump. An example of a discharge cycle is given, by releasing the previously-
stored heat to a Stirling engine. Authors found possible round-trip eﬃciency values in the
order of 43%.
In [53] this concept is further investigated by focusing on a device in which charging and
discharging systems are made of the same, reversible components. In such a system it is
important to minimize the temperature diﬀerences between the thermal storage mean and
the working ﬂuid in order to minimize losses. If the proﬁles of the storage and working
ﬂuids are not well matched then signiﬁcant losses are obtained in the energy production.
For this reason authors proposed to use a ﬂuid in transcritical conditions to have a better
temperature match.
This line of thought brought the authors to decide for carbon dioxide as the working ﬂuid
in a system with water as storage ﬂuid, as presented in [55]. In this paper the choice of
CO2 is discussed, arguing that it represents a good alternative being the ﬂuid with the
lowest critical pressure and that there is a good temperature match with water, a cheap
and eﬀective storage mean. In addition, the possibility of having an ice storage as heat sink
of the cycle is pointed out, with advantages with regard to eﬃciency and non-dependence
from ambient condition, although an additional cooling system is needed to remove heat
coming from irreversibilities.
Estimates for round-trip eﬃciency are of 51% for an hypothetical 1 MW pilot plant and
65% for a commercial 50 MW unit. Cost estimates are also provided around 4’500 USD/kW
for the former and down to 1’500 USD/kW for the latter.
In [54] an additional modiﬁcation to the system is proposed by adding an intermediate
temperature hot storage tank to add the possibility of ﬂow rate variation on the storage
mean and approximate even better the temperature curve of the hot storage to avoid losses.
Further in [56] a regenerative heat exchanger is added to increase cooling of the working
ﬂuid at the output of the hot storage tank and pre-heat the working ﬂuid at the output of
the cold storage when charging, thus increasing the coeﬃcient of performance.
The best design possibilities for the above system were investigated in [57] with a thermo-
economic optimization, looking for optimal trade-oﬀs between thermodynamic eﬃciency
and investment cost. Authors performed a two-level optimization where the intensive pa-
rameters were ﬁrst optimized through a genetic algorithm, then optimal mass ﬂows were
calculated subject to restrictions imposed by Pinch Analysis cascade calculations. The opti-
mal heat exchanger network was found as well. As a result authors found that superheating
before the CO2 heat pump, two independent systems of hot water storage tanks above
and below the ambient temperature and air cooling at the heat pump side maximized the
system performance at the minimum costs. The design parameters that had the biggest
inﬂuence on cost and performance were found to be the cycle pressures together with the
turbomachinery eﬃciency.

2.2.4.2
Packed bed thermal storage
A review of studies focusing on packed bed thermal storage is presented in [59]. A wide
number of studies has been performed, most focusing on eﬀects of design on performance
and only few applying cost-eﬀectiveness valuations. The large majority of packed bed ther-
mal storage applications has been developed for solar thermal systems such as thermal
collectors or CSP and none have considered the possibility of direct electricity storage.
The theory of thermal transients in packed bed thermal storages was developed by Schu-
mann back in 1929 [60]. It was then developed by several authors that studied correlations
between ﬂow variables and the volumetric heat transfer coeﬃcient between air and the solid
storage means. A useful table with a synthesis of all the correlations found in scientiﬁc lit-
erature is available in [59]. Among these, the one developed by Coutier and Farber [61] is
the most widely adopted when dealing with rock packed beds. They also developed a set
of two diﬀerential equations based on the Schumann model that allowed to ﬁnd the time
development of the thermocline. They experimentally veriﬁed their correlation showing a
very good match with empirical data. A modiﬁcation of these equations was used in this
thesis for the storage model. Authors of [61] also provided an useful method for the design
and dimensioning of the storage unit.
The work by Coutier and Farber was the base of the studies of H¨anchen et al. [39]. They
applied a number of consecutive cycles and evaluated the cycling performance of the storage
unit. They found that the unit reached a steady state after about 20 consecutive full cycles
and that eﬃciencies could stay well above 90% after convergence was reached. This study
was taken as the base for the storage module thermal evaluation of Section 4. It is in fact
interesting to see how the unit would perform over a certain amount of time and not only
in one single cycle.
Crandall et al. [62] developed a strategy to enhance thermal stratiﬁcation in the storage.
The solution is similar to Stiesdal’s idea of an internally divided and insulated unit and
it involves several separated storage modules. The system is adapted to a solar thermal

In this chapter the thermodynamic analysis of diﬀerent design alternatives for

the expected round-trip eﬃciency and evaluate eﬀects of layout modiﬁcations on

Results showed that the optimal conﬁgurations of both ETES and HT-TES can

3.1
Modelling methods
The aim of this Chapter is to calculate an estimate for round-trip storage eﬃciency for
ETES and HT-TES. For each of the two versions, three alternatives have been generated.
These are for ETES (i) simpliﬁed base Rankine cycle, (ii) two-pressure-levels with reheat
cycle, (iii) regenerative cycle with steam extraction. The alternatives for HT-TES are (i)
two-stage inter-cooled Brayton-Joule open cycle, (ii) three-stage intercooled Brayton-Joule
open cycle, (iii) regenerative two-stage inter-cooled Brayton-Joule open cycle.
Both the optimal conﬁguration considering the heat pump cycle and the electric heater
charging system were investigated and round-trip eﬃciency calculated for each possibility
together with charging and discharging thermal and exergetic eﬃciency.

3.1.
Modelling methods
Focus was put on pointing out diﬀerences between the alternatives in the optimal conﬁgu-
ration, i.e. with values maximizing the round-trip eﬃciency.
The most important decision variable was the cold-end temperature of the storage which
provides the connection between the design of the charging and discharging cycles. When
cold-end storage temperature increases, though, losses to the environment are increased and
volumetric energy density is decreased in the storage unit. This causes a less eﬃcient oper-
ation and a higher investment cost for the same energy content of the storage. At the same
time, as was shown in Section 2.1, the cost for thermal storage of electricity is driven by
the charging and discharging cycles cost. Therefore a decrease in energy density would not
be excessively problematic. Also, losses to the environment can be decreased by increasing
insulation which also has a relatively low cost compared to the total. For these reasons it
has been considered that the decision on the storage conditions would be dictated by the
need to reach the highest eﬃciency without considering cost.
All the models presented in this section were coded in EES [64] and developed as steady-
state, control volume based thermodynamic models.
In each of them the charging and
discharging cycles were modelled independently and the connection between the two was
represented by the storage hot and cold end temperatures.
The structure of the model was developed as follows: ﬁrst the storage temperatures were
set, then the charging cycle was modeled and the heat that charged to the storage was
calculated once the electric charging capacity was deﬁned. The heat taken from the storage
by the discharge cycle was set to be equal to the energy charged which set the size of the
discharging cycle. Once the discharging eﬃciency was calculated the round-trip storage
eﬃciency was computed as the ratio between the power generated by the discharging cycle
and the one consumed by the charging one.
Optimization was performed with the built-in EES tool comparing the solutions with the
diﬀerent optimization methods provided in order to verify the convergence to the same (or
very similar) optimal conﬁgurations.
3.1.1
General assumptions
In the design models the following assumptions were made:
1. Perfectly insulated storage unit, i.e. no energy lost to the ambient through the enve-
lope and heat charged to the storage equal to the heat discharged from it;
2. Storage unit divided in several sections, each and every one perfectly insulated from
the others, i.e. no internal conduction loss;
3. Constant hot and cold end temperatures in the storage;
4. Fixed isentropic eﬃciencies for compressors, expanders and turbines ;
5. Heat exchangers modelled with pinch point temperature diﬀerence except for regen-
erative ones for which eﬀectiveness is deﬁned;
6. Atmospheric pressure in the compressor inlet for the heat pump cycle;

7. Thermodynamic coordinates of inlet conditions for the heat pump compressor ﬁxed
at 20◦C and 1 atm;
8. No pressure or heat losses in the heat exchagers;
9. Constant unitary COP of electric heater system;
Assumptions 1 and 2 were necessary to develop a time-independent design model. The
reason is that if the storage was not perfectly insulated both internally and externally some
energy would be lost to the ambient or through conduction between high and low tem-
perature zones in the storage. The amount of energy lost would depend on the temporal
distance between the charging and discharging cycles, so time-independence could not be
obtained.
Regarding assumption 4, a choice had to be made between representing the performance
of the machinery with isentropic eﬃciency or polytropic eﬃciency. The former has been
chosen because of its simplicity of utilization. Additionally scientiﬁc literature stresses that
polytropic eﬃciency should be used when evaluating comparisons of the same component
between diﬀerent working conditions given that this parameter is not dependent on pressure
ratio. In this case the compared systems are diﬀerent in conﬁguration and it is possible to
imagine that diﬀerent components could be used that achieve the desired performance.
The reason behind assumption 5 is that the heat transfer to and from the storage does not
happen in a conventional heat exchanger but through direct contact of the working ﬂuid
with the storage material. Scientiﬁc literature shows that it is very limited at the two ends
of the storage when this is in an intermediate charging state. In this case the temperature
diﬀerence includes also intermediate heat exchangers such as from the charging ﬂuid to the
storage loop. In regenerative heat exchangers is not always straightforward to understand
whether the PPTD will be located at the hot or at the cold side. Hence eﬀectiveness was
used.
Assumption 7 was made because, being the charging cycle closed, there is the possibility
of pressurizing it and therefore increasing volumetric energy density. Nevertheless as com-
pressor inlet pressure increases, so outlet pressure has to do in order to reach the desired
temperature to feed in the storage. Consequently there would be a need to pressurize the
charging heat exchager which would cause an important increase in investment.
Assumptions on exergy analysis
When performing the exergetic analysis a distinction
was made between irreversibilities due to machinery and to system conﬁguration. In order
to do so the exergy destruction in each component due to the conﬁguration were calculated
as the normal destruction minus the one calculated with unitary isentropic eﬃciency for
the turbomachinery (ideal cycle).
This choice allowed to show the improvement potential with more eﬃcient components.
No value was considered in the hot and cold streams produced by the cycles, i.e. electricity
was considered the only product of the storage unit. In other words, no distinction was
made between exergy loss and exergy destruction. Usually the share of exergy fuel that is
not present in the product is referred to as exergy destruction if it is due to irreversibility
of the component, whereas as loss if it is not utilized from the speciﬁc application. For
instance in a cooler the exergy destruction is related to the temperature match of the ﬂows
and pressure losses, whereas the loss is the exergy of the cooling ﬂuid that is discharged

3.1.3.2
Two-pressure-levels cycle
One of the main reasons for ineﬃciency in the discharging cycle is the temperature proﬁle
mismatch in the HRSG. One of the possible ways to improve this is to adopt a two-pressure-
levels heat exchanger. The working principle is that after the ﬁrst evaporation the ﬂuid is
collected in a drum in saturated conditions. Saturated liquid is further compressed, re-
evaporated, superheated and expanded through a ﬁrst high pressure turbine stage. Then
it is mixed with the lower pressure steam coming from the drum and both are reheated
and expanded through the low pressure turbine stage. The corresponding T-S diagram is
visualized in Figure 3.1. This way the integral average temperature diﬀerence between the
proﬁles can be reduced and eﬃciency improved.
To model this the base model illustrated above has been modiﬁed. A low pressure level has
been added together with an additional pumping stage from the low to the top pressure.
The additional pressure level adds two degrees of freedom to the problem. These can be
chosen to be the pressure value and the fraction of total mass that is expanded through the
low pressure turbine stage.
Both variables are found by setting the pinch point temperature diﬀerence at the two evap-
oration temperatures equal to the minimum ∆T together with the temperature diﬀerence
at the maximum temperature. This was veriﬁed to ensure that the temperature proﬁle of
air in the HRSG and steam are as close as possible decreasing irreversibilities.

3.1.3.3
Regenerative simple cycle
In this modiﬁcation of the simple cycle the presence of a regenerative feedwater preheater
was considered. Steam is taken from the turbine at a speciﬁc pressure and with a certain
mass ﬂow and mixed isobarically with the ﬂuid at the outlet of the condenser after it has
undergone a ﬁrst pumping stage. The conditions in the preheater were ﬁxed to saturation
at the extraction pressure, hence the extracted mass ﬂow fraction and pressure are linked.
Consequently the optimization variables were two, i.e the cold-end temperature and the
extraction pressure (or mass ﬂow fraction).
The pumping stage had therefore to be split in two parts, one before the preheater where
the ﬂuid is brought from the condensation pressure to the extraction pressure and one after
the preheater where the ﬂuid is taken to the maximum cycle pressure.
Work coming from the turbine, pumping power consumption and exergetic eﬃciencies had
to be redeﬁned accordingly.

3.3.
Discussion
3.3
Discussion
This chapter had the aim of estimating the round-trip storage eﬃciency of diﬀerent alterna-
tives pointing out diﬀerences in the optimal conﬁguration and potential for improvement.
All the results were obtained with 85% isentropic eﬃciency of turbines and compressors.
This number is not at the top end of the scale, meaning that more eﬃcient components are
commercially available. As isentropic eﬃciency is the parameter to which the model is most
sensitive it is fair to say that the ﬁgures obtained for round-trip eﬃciency are conservative.
A source of ineﬃciency that the models neglect, though, is the pressure loss in heat ex-
changers. The eﬀect of this is nevertheless accounted in the uncertainty assessment where
a possible pressure loss of 0.1 bar is included. The previous Section contains the shares of
uncertainty related to the diﬀerent parameters and it can be seen that, with the exception of
the regenerative HT-TES cycle, the pressure loss is not responsible for a very large portion
of the uncertainty on the result. The main reason why it has a higher importance in the
mentioned case is that a high pressure loss in the regenerative heat exchanger would mean
the need for more compression work and also less pressure diﬀerential for the expansion
with negative eﬀects on the work produced.
Regarding the uncertainty assessment it should be reminded that the total uncertainty
ﬁgure on round-trip eﬃciency illustrated have been calculated with assumed parameter
uncertainty. A more precise calculation would need a technical analysis to ﬁnd the real
uncertainty intervals for the parameters.
When comparing the eﬃciency results with previous studies such as [55], [73], [57] and [49]
it can be seen that ETES and HT-TES are on the low end of the eﬃciency range. Nev-
ertheless they can oﬀer advantages compared to the other thermal storage types found in
scientiﬁc literature which concentrate on CO2 systems.
As for ETES, the main advantage is on the cost side and in the possibility of revamping.
The much decreased investment cost plus the ability of re-using power plants that are not
in operation or to be decommissioned can make it a more attractive investment than other
types of storage.
As for HT-TES, it is a simple and compact system that uses already existing and tried-
and-tested equipment which can be scaled to a high capacity (10 to several hundred MW).
This is not true for the CO2 systems for which equipment is either of small scale or not
yet commercially available and still on the research stage. Plus those options have a much
more complicated layout with several storage tanks that increase investment cost.
The value of eﬃciency found for the optimal conﬁgurations is also close to the one calculated
by [50] for a unit with 80% isentropic eﬃciency.
One additional consideration can be done about the method for this analysis. These conclu-
sions have pointed out the best, i.e. the most eﬃcient alternative for ETES and HT-TES.
This does not mean that these options are also the best ones from an economic point of
view. In fact the eﬃciency gain from the two-pressure level HRSG may not be worth the
expense. Same discussion can be made for the heat pump charging system. In fact even if
it can give an important contribution in the case of a discharging steam cycle it may not be
worth installing it due to the high investment cost. This could be checked by performing a
detailed cost-beneﬁt analysis.

4 Thermal storage unit
This chapter deals with general dimensioning and modelling of the thermal storage
unit, with focus on the temperature proﬁle development in operation. The thermal
model is based on the work by Hanchen et al. [39] to which some modiﬁcations have
been applied in order to include the special features invented by Stiesdal.
Thermocline development was studied in charge and discharge for an electricity-
symmetric and electricity-asymmetric unit. It was found that the operational range
in which the storage was able to keep the top temperature at the design value was
about 63% of the nominal energy content. Eﬀect of design parameters such as speciﬁc
air mass ﬂow, void fraction, number of insulated sections were studied. Results show
that air ﬂux does not importantly aﬀect the proﬁle development but has an large
inﬂuence on pressure losses. The trade-oﬀbetween cost of volume and life-cycle cost
of the air circulating fan was analysed ﬁnding the optimal range for the storage void
fraction. Internal insulation was found to allow the unit to keep a better temperature
proﬁle when not operating.
The cycling performance of the two units was analysed when applying a repeated
weekly schedule. It was shown that both units reach a convergence value of exergetic
eﬃciency higher than 90%. The starting condition of the storage did not inﬂuence
the convergence value and the number of cycles required. Finally, a suggestion for
proﬁle improvement was made that could allow to integrate heat production for
district heating. The eﬀect on the thermocline is shown compared to a non-restored
proﬁle.
The aim of this chapter is to study the dynamics of the packed bed thermal storage unit.
First of all a dimensioning was performed and eﬀect of a change in selected parameters was
studied with the model. The procedure for the dimensioning is similar to the one suggested
in [61]. Then, a full charge-discharge cycle was analysed to understand at what charging
level a decline in hot end temperature was experienced. Finally the performance of a unit
with a repeated schedule was analysed by means of exergetic eﬃciency.
For the analysis of the transient behaviour a quite detailed model was developed. It is in
fact critical to have an understanding of the dynamics of the temperature inside the unit
because that can inﬂuence the eﬃciency of discharge. Also, an improper scheduling can

4.1.1
Thermocline simulation
The heat transfer model was based on the following assumptions:
1. The model is mono-dimensional and follows the axis of a cylindrical storage unit;
2. Two phases are considered, i.e. the air ﬂowing in the unit and the storage material;
3. The heat transfer ﬂuid is assumed to be heated outside the storage unit itself;
4. The storage material is uniformly packed and thermal properties are constant;
5. Radiative heat transfer is neglected;
6. Conduction in the ﬂuid phase is neglected being considered signiﬁcantly smaller than
convection;
7. Buoyancy eﬀects are neglected;
8. Intraparticle conduction is negligible (Bi«1 1).
Two models were developed, one for charging or discharging operation and one for steady
operation (no charging neither discharging happen). The models are based on the applica-
tion of energy conservation equations for both the ﬂuid and the solid storage material.
The storage was longitudinally divided in several control volumes. The number of these was
tested and it was ﬁnally chosen to use . That allowed fast calculation time but at the same
time the simulated proﬁle was virtually identical to the ones obtained with higher number
of control volumes.

ﬂuid phase, the only inlet heat ﬂow is convection from the previous volume, whereas outlet
ﬂows are convection to the next control volume and heat transfer to the solid. Conduction
was considered negligible with respect to convection so no other heat ﬂow was included. As

4.2.2.1
Eﬀect of mass ﬂux
Figure 4.6 represents the temperature proﬁle for diﬀerent values of the mass ﬂux G. It
should be noted that since the mass ﬂow rate was ﬁxed (because rated power and temper-
atures were ﬁxed) then a change in G (i.e. speciﬁc mass ﬂow rate, expressed in kg/m2s)
caused a change in the required ﬂow area and length of the storage. Hence the x axis of
Figure 4.6 is expressed as a percentage of the total length.
The tested values of G were 4 times more and 4 times less the base assumption (which was
the value used in [39]). Notably Figure 4.6 shows that the inﬂuence of the parameter on the
proﬁle is small. Nevertheless a lower speciﬁc mass ﬂow rate caused a slightly ﬂatter proﬁle
and a lower gradient. Increasing G above 0.2, instead, resulted in no detectable change in
the proﬁle. The same trend was found when reversing the ﬂow and discharging the storage.
An important variable on which the speciﬁc mass ﬂow had instead a large inﬂuence was
the pressure loss across the storage and hence the fan power needed to circulate the air.
With the base hypothesis of G = 0.2 kg/m2s the pressure loss was limited, i.e. 0.11 bar
in charging and 0.52 bar in discharging mode. Nevertheless the volumetric air ﬂow was
substantial, hence the required fan power was of 5.1 MW in charging mode and 81.5 MW in
discharging. This was found to be excessive as in discharging the power needed to circulate
the air in the storage was as much as 40% of the rated power output.
The volumetric ﬂow was ﬁxed by the charging/discharging power, so the only way to reduce
the fan consumption was to further decrease pressure losses. This was done by decreasing
the length of the storage, which was obtained by decreasing G (volume and mass ﬂow were
ﬁxed hence to decrease length area had to be increased, which was obtained by decreasing
G).
With a value of 0.05 kg/m2s the required fan power was decreased to 266 kW in charging
and 2.3 MW in discharging mode, which was now almost negligible compared to the total
electrical power (200 MW).

Eﬀect of void fraction
The void fraction ξ was set to 40% in the base calculation (again after [39]). The void

pressure loss across the device (see Equation (4.14)). As seen from Equation (4.3) the vol-

Increased void fraction had the eﬀect of decreasing the overall density and heat capacity
of the storage, hence for the same energy quantity a bigger volume was required. Since
the speciﬁc mass ﬂow rate was ﬁxed at 0.05 kg/m2s the cross-ﬂow area was ﬁxed as well.
Therefore increasing void fraction meant increasing the length of the storage unit. At the
same time the pressure loss across the unit decreased, so that a trade-oﬀwas established
between the size of the storage and the power of the fans required to circulate the air, as
represented in Figure 4.9.
Results show that the curve is ﬂat close to the optimum and that low total cost values are
obtained for void fractions between 0.3 and 0.5. The optimal value was 0.37.

4.2.4
Proﬁle restoration
In the previous paragraph the cycling performance of a symmetric and an asymmetric unit
was assessed with a schedule that did not pay any attention to the proﬁle development
inside the storage.
In order to keep the eﬃciency as high as possible the user would need to prevent the top-end
temperature from decreasing. To do so, it is necessary to keep the thermocline as vertical
as possible. One possibility to do that is to restore the proﬁle by exploiting the internal
division of the storage unit and locally decrease or increase the temperature so that the
shape of the proﬁle changes for the next cycle.
The localized charge method has been illustrated by Stiesdal in [43]. An alternative way
would be instead to discharge the storage completely for sections with a temperature lower
than a certain set level. The energy obtained in this fashion may be used to produce district
heating hot water if the cold-end storage temperature is high enough (indicatively above
100◦C ).
A comparison between the methods is shown in Figure 4.12. In the example the starting
condition is the same (black straight line). First the storage undergoes six consecutive hours
of discharge followed by six hours of charge. The restoration technique is applied before the
discharging/charging take place.
Results show that both methods can increase the temperature gradient of the thermocline
with respect to the normal proﬁle case. Consequently the eﬀect should be that of increasing
the operational range for the storage unit in both cases. Nevertheless a diﬀerence is seen on
the hotter side of the storage. While the discharging method created a proﬁle with the same
shape of the starting one, the temperature spike of the charging method caused a change
in the shape of the temperature proﬁle. This presents a lower temperature zone that would
cause a hot-end temperature decrease when discharging further.

4.3
Model validity
The ﬁndings of this chapter are dependent on the reliability of the thermal model of the
storage.
Probably the most uncertain outcome is the possibility of actually achieving the needed heat
transfer between air and rocks even at very low air speeds. The heat transfer coeﬃcients
calculated with the correlation from [61] may be in fact diﬃcult to achieve in reality when
working with such low speciﬁc mass ﬂows or may present a diﬀerent dependency.
The model also neglects buoyancy and radiative heat transfer. Nevertheless, it is based on
[39] and authors claim that their method has a very good adherence to experimental data.
While this is a positive note for the expected accuracy of the present work, the analysed
thermal storage has a completely diﬀerent scale than the one in [39] so the validity of the
model may be hindered. The only way to accurately establish that would be with exper-
imental analysis or by testing the results with a more accurate model including buoyancy
and radiative heat exchange.

4.4.
Conclusions
4.4
Conclusions
This chapter has dealt with the simulation of the thermocline development in the thermal
storage unit by means of a heat transfer model. A general dimensioning has been performed
studying the eﬀect of some design parameters, in a procedure similar to what outlined by
[61].
Due to the high volumetric ﬂow rate of air it was found that it is important to keep an
extremely low pressure loss across the unit. This because (i) the required fan power can
be excessive and (ii) a high pressure loss would require pressurization of the whole air loop
increasing investment cost.
It was pointed out that the speciﬁc air mass ﬂow has a low inﬂuence on the proﬁle develop-
ment along the longitudinal axis of the storage. Nevertheless a low value of this parameter
was chosen to keep the pressure losses low.
Another parameter that inﬂuences the length of the storage and hence the pressure loss is
the void fraction. This parameter is the ratio of the air ﬂow area to the total cross-sectional
area. By estimating the lifetime cost of a circulation fan the trade-oﬀbetween fan costs
and volume costs has been shown and the optimal found in the range of 0.35 to 0.4 for a
symmetric unit.
The full charge/discharge analysis has shown that about 63% of the nominal energy content
of the storage can be discharged without the hot-end temperature to decrease. That leads
to the conclusion that a signiﬁcant over-dimensioning of the unit needs to be accounted to
be able to operate the unit eﬃciently if no technique is adopted to improve the thermocline
gradient. For a unit with a nominal capacity of 48 discharging hours the actual energy
content obtainable with constant hot end temperature becomes about 30 hours (without
proﬁle restoration).
Further, a comparison between the cycling exergetic eﬃciency of an electricity-symmetric
and electricity-asymmetric unit has been performed when applying an hypothetical weekly
schedule. The results showed that both unit reached a convergence value when applying
the same schedule. The convergence value was above 90% even when not adapting the
operation to the conditions of the thermocline in the storage. The simulation also showed
that this performance level does not depend on the starting condition of the storage unit.
Finally, two ways of controlling the shape of the thermocline have been discussed, one based
on localized charging and one on localized discharging. Both achieved an increased in the
temperature gradient, but the charging option had the eﬀect of changing the shape of the
curve towards the hot end. The problem in this case was that the disturbance caused by
the localized increase in temperature was carried to the rest of the storage in the following
charge/discharge cycle. This may be avoided by fully charging the sections of the storage
before the one where restoration takes place, i.e. bringing them all to the top temperature.
The problem with such a technique would be that the eﬃciency of charging (i.e. the energy
retained by the storage relative to the one fed into it) would progressively decrease as the
temperature increases, while it is important to be able to charge the storage as fast as
possible in order to exploit electricity when it is cheap.
The discharging method, instead, can be put in operation when the unit is not operating in

the electricity market and hence has less strict time constraints. This may also yield energy
for direct district heating water production where possible.
It can therefore be concluded that the discharging method seems to be more attractive if
heat production for district heating is a possibility.

Chapter 5.
Economic analysis
5 Economic analysis
The aim of this chapter is to assess the economic success possibilities of thermal
storage of electricity.
This was done by modelling the behaviour of a unit in the
electricity market in the energy system model Balmorel.
A base scenario built on [74] and [75] was created and results showed that, under the
speciﬁc assumptions, a brownﬁeld application of electricity-symmetric ETES could
have a successful business case in the future Danish energy system and repay its
yearly ﬁxed costs from 2030. The behaviour of the unit was that of a peaker, with a
low generation capacity factor and a signiﬁcant share of proﬁts coming from capacity
credit. The importance of heat revenues was assessed ﬁnding that around a third of
the proﬁts was related to heat sales which may contribute substantially to hedge the
risk coming from the electricity market.
Symmetric and asymmetric units were compared and it was found that the asymmetric
unit could have a better business case especially when used to revamp existing CCGT
plants.
An analysis was performed on other factors inﬂuencing the business case such as
storage capacity in the system, spread between peak and base load prices, grid tariﬀs.
Also, the robustness of the proﬁtability result was assessed with a sensitivity analysis.
Finally, the optimal future investments in ETES were studied. Results showed that
the unit may be chosen for investment instead of new natural gas capacity and that
it decreased overall system costs. System beneﬁts were shown to be mainly related to
improved business case for renewable producers.
Current electricity systems do not show potential for additional penetration of energy stor-
age. In fact demand stagnation and RES production have contributed to decreasing the
electricity price especially in the Nordic countries. Nevertheless RES integration is not in
an advanced stage yet, so price ﬂuctuation is not enough to make storage attractive. In the
next years the picture is likely to change completely as more and more renewable capacity
increases ﬂuctuation and uncertainty of production, which accompanied by decommission-
ing of conventional plants will decrease system reliability and adequacy.
To assess the economic potential of a storage technology a simulation tool is needed that can
provide insights in the dynamics of the future energy system. Since energy storage exploits
diﬀerences in hourly prices it is important that the tool has at least a hourly resolution.
75
==PAGE:104==
5.1.
Methods
At the same time it must be able to include economic features of the various technologies
and model competition between them. The Balmorel energy system model meets all these
requirements.
Since Balmorel is a linear optimization tool, it was necessary to develop and test a way
of modelling such a unit in a linear problem. A quick tool where these constraints could
be tested was therefore developed. The tool consisted in a price-taker model that would
not attempt to simulate the whole energy system but rather one speciﬁc technology under
historical prices. For the sake of brevity this part has been omitted from the present report
as it does not add anything to the analysis.
The following analysis concentrates on the study of the business case of brownﬁeld ETES,
i.e. revamping of an existing steam plant with the storage technology using an electric
heater charging system. This choice was done because that represents the cheapest hence
most attractive option.
5.1
Methods
ETES was modelled in Balmorel by adding technology-speciﬁc constraints described in 5.1.2.
Two fundamentally diﬀerent approaches were taken: (i) ﬁxed capacity and (ii) investment
runs.
Fixed capacity runs are a set of simulations performed with a ﬁxed 1 MW capacity to
evaluate the value of ETES per MW. Units were placed in Copenhagen and ˚Arhus. Two
speciﬁc locations were chosen in order to be able to place the units in the corresponding
areas in Balmorel and have a realistic heat system representation.
In order to understand the most important revenue drivers a series of cases were compared
after the base scenario to assess the importance of several factors. These include eﬃciency,
heat production, capacity ratio, fuel prices, grid tariﬀs.
Investment runs are a set of simulations in which initial ETES capacity is zero and the
model is allowed to autonomously invest in several technologies ﬁnding the optimal capac-
ity from a system perspective. In this case possibility of investment in thermal storage of
electricity was in Denmark, Germany and UK. These countries were the same analysed in
the price-taker model and were chosen for the fact that they have very diﬀerent markets but
have ambitious renewable energy policies in common. UK represents a high price market,
Denmark a low price market with an eﬀective district heating system and Germany the
largest European market with extensive renewable integration.
It should be underlined that, being that Balmorel models perfect competition, a beneﬁt
to the system would never be experienced when adding a technology with an unsuccessful
business case. Hence if a certain type of production plant is installed it is because it can
both decrease the system costs and make a proﬁt. No capacity market was modelled.
For these runs the system value of ETES was calculated by comparing the yearly costs for
the above mentioned countries with a base simulation where no thermal electricity storage
was allowed. The simulations are described in detail in Section 5.1.5.

Economic analysis
In all simulations revenues have been computed as the simple product between price and
production both for heat and electricity. By subtracting to these the costs related to elec-
tricity purchase, operation and maintenance costs and taxes, the proﬁts based on variable
components were calculated (i.e. gross proﬁt). The technology was considered to have a
successful business case if these were higher than the sum of the annualized investment cost
and ﬁxed annual operation and maintenance costs. The former is calculated by multiplying
the investment cost for the annualization factor deﬁned as in Equation (4.16). A lifetime
of 20 years and a discount rate of 8% were assumed for the calculation.
The net sum of all the above mentioned components represents the annual proﬁtability
of the unit.
The analysis the assessment concentrated on the following indicators:
1. Yearly gross proﬁts - yearly sum of revenues from power and heat minus all the
variable costs, i.e. electricity purchasing cost, variable O&M costs and taxes. Also
referred to as yearly value;
2. Proﬁtability - net sum of the yearly proﬁt, annualized investment and ﬁxed operation
and maintenance costs. If positive it indicates successful business case ;
3. Average buying price - purchasing cost of electricity divided by total consumption.
Gives information on at which price the unit buys on average;
4. Average selling price - revenues from electricity divided by total production. Gives
information on at which price the unit sells on average;
5. Charging and discharging capacity factors - They represent the fraction of the
year for which the unit is charging or discharging electricity at full power. They are
calculated as consumed or produced energy divided by rated capacity. In other words
a unit with a 10% discharging capacity factor is producing the energy equivalent to
876 hours of full-power production.
Accuracy of results for electricity revenues depends mainly on the ability of the model to
simulate historical prices.
The interested reader can observe a comparison between the
historical and simulated 2015 prices for Germany as an example in Figure B.9.
As for the heat revenues, being that in reality the heat system is not based on a compet-
itive market no heat price can be deﬁned. The hypothesis behind the analysis is that the
owner of a thermal storage unit could potentially stipulate contracts with the local district
heating company oﬀering to substitute the most expensive generator whenever possible.
In that case he could be remunerated with a payment that is lower or equal the marginal
production cost of that generator, which is the heat price as Balmorel output.

Balmorel set up
The model was run on 13 weeks instead of 52 due to computational time constraints. The
tool allows to choose the simulated weeks, which have been veriﬁed to not have excessively
unbalanced uncontrollable generation or consumption. The output is available with hourly
deﬁnition on the 13 weeks but values are scaled so that the whole year can be simulated

and each time slot in the model represents 4 non-consecutive hours. Simulated years were
2015, 2020, 2025, 2030 and 2035 but the analysis of ETES focused on 2020-2035.
The most important features that have been used are listed below with a brief description.
It should be reminded that this does not want to be a complete overview of Balmorel, but
just a highlight of the peculiarities that may inﬂuence ﬁnal results the most. The interested
reader can refer to [13] to have a more detailed description of the tool.
1. Part-load operation and start-up of thermal units were modelled with relaxed MIP
implementation. This feature allowed to model the decrease in eﬃciency of thermal
units when producing below 100% load and an improved representation of hourly
variation in prices which is important when evaluating storage units.
2. Investments in renewable energy capacity such as wind and solar were exogenously
modelled. In other words capacity for these technologies was set for each year at the
beginning of the model run depending on the chosen scenario. Also nuclear capacity
was assumed to be controlled by political decision and therefore exogenously modelled.
Thermal plants and thermal energy storage investments were instead endogenously
modelled, as explained below.
3. Investments in new dispatchable technologies were allowed and endogenously mod-
elled. This is handled in the model by including a list of units in which the model
may invest if convenient and of areas in which each unit may be installed. All char-
acteristics (both technical and economic) of investment possibilities are externally
given. New units are modelled exactly like already existing ones but have a separate
production variable and separate constraints. The upper limit of the new production
variable, unlike for already existing unit, is also a variable to which an annualized
investment cost is associated in the objective function of the model. Therefore if the
system beneﬁts coming from a new unit are higher than the annualized investment cost
the model will invest in it choosing the optimal capacity. A much similar modelling
approach is used with CHP plants but with the inclusion of the new heat production
variable.
4. Non-dispatchable technologies such as wind and solar power are modelled with ﬁxed
production. Capacity and full load hours were set in the model and production was
scaled with historical proﬁles.
5. Reservoir hydropower is modelled by inputting a historical inﬂow proﬁle. Total pro-
duction is constrained to meet a value calculated from input data in terms of capacity
and full load hours. Part of this production is modelled as uncontrollable to mimic
real behaviour due to minimum outﬂow requirements.
6. Availability of interconnection between Germany and Jutland, Germany and Sweden
and Jutland and Sweden is modelled as a function of renewable production in the
areas, obtained by ﬁtting historical data.
Other interconnections are modelled by
giving the historical average of hourly availability in the day-ahead market for 2015.
7. Curtailment of wind and solar power were enabled and no cost was associated to them.

5.1.3.5
Self-cannibalization eﬀect
The self-cannibalization eﬀect for storage is somewhat similar to the price pressure or down-
lift on wind power producers. As the installed storage capacity in the system increases there
will be appreciable eﬀects on the electricity price. Peak prices will lower due to additional
production and low prices will increase due to additional consumption from storage units.
Storage revenues are driven exactly by the spread between peak and base prices, hence the
eﬀect of the storage is to reduce its own revenues if not acting strategically. In this Section
the eﬀect of increased size in the modelled storage units has been assessed. In addition
to the base case, where production capacity of each unit was ﬁxed at 1 MW, cases with
capacities of 10, 100 and 1000 MW have been investigated. Units were always installed
both in Eastern and Western Denmark. Simulations have been run in year 2035 which was
the highest-proﬁt year.

Figure 5.13 illustrates sensitivity ratios of the yearly proﬁtability of an electricity-symmetric
unit in 2030 for selected assumptions. They are in descending absolute value order but re-
ported with the correct sign. It should be reminded that a negative SR means that the
variable, i.e. in this case the yearly average proﬁtability, decreases when the parameter
increases.
The assumption to which variable is most sensitive is the one on wind capacity. This was
somewhat expected as inexpensive renewable energy is fundamental for the business case
of the storage unit. The result indicates that if the wind capacity prediction used for the
modelling was wrong by 10% then the proﬁtability of the unit in 2030 would be 48% oﬀ. An
increase in wind capacity would cause an improvement in proﬁts due to the higher amount
of cheap electricity available in the market.
In second place are assumptions related to charging and discharging eﬃciency. They have
an equal SR around 3.4 which means that an increase of 10% in eﬃciency (it does not mat-
ter whether it is charging or discharging) yields an increase of about 34% in proﬁtability in
2030 if the other parameters are not varied. SR’s are obviously positive in both cases as a
more eﬃcient unit will always have a better business case when cost is ﬁxed.
The third most important assumption is the one regarding total eﬃciency, i.e. the ability
of the discharging cycle to convert energy drawn from the storage. Sensitivity ratio for this
parameter is above 2.2.
Fourth place is for electricity demand, which shows a SR of -1.84. It is negative because an
increase in electricity demand would cause low prices to increase more than high ones with
thus a worse business case for storage.
Investment cost, for which SR is close to -1,5, follows in ﬁfth place. The fact that the SR
is lower for the investment than for eﬃciency suggests that the ﬁrst step to improve the

5.5
Discussion
A critical element for the success of thermal storage seems to be investment cost. The au-
thor has made an eﬀort to conﬁrm the estimates but he has only partially succeeded. The
Danish company SAN Electro Heat [82] was contacted for an estimation of investment cost
for a high temperature electric heating element. They have estimated the cost of heating
elements plus a mid-to-low voltage transformer and controls around 83 e/kWC which is
not far from the 75 e/kWC estimated by [44] for heater and infrastructure. The company
is currently looking into lower-cost conﬁgurations and has stated that it can be possible to
get closer to the estimate.
On the discharging side the main cost for an electricity-symmetric unit is related to the
substitution of the boiler with the HRSG. A literature survey provided some cost correla-
tions but these required speciﬁc technical data and were mainly used for thermo-economic
optimization. Unfortunately no real estimate from manufacturers was obtained to validate
the estimate from [44].
As for the cost of storage, it is not possible to get a conﬁrmation since the unit has some
special design features and diﬀers from other packed bed thermal storages built so far.
Other uncertainties stem from the ability of the model to simulate electricity prices. As
seen in the 2015 benchmark in Figure B.9 the model tends to overestimate low prices and
underestimate high ones, therefore the performance of storage is underestimated.
Underestimation of the potential also comes from the eﬃciency of the unit which, as said
in Section 3.2.1, is conservative.
Three issues would contribute in the opposite direction, i.e. decreasing the real potential
with respect to the modelled one.
The ﬁrst is the fact that thermal electricity storage will most likely ﬁnd itself to compete
against other ﬂexibility-providing alternatives such as ﬂexible demand, hydrogen technolo-
gies, smart electric cars and similar. This would hinder the business case for thermal storage.
The second issue is the fact that Balmorel is based on perfect forecast, so the result of the
analysis represents the maximum obtainable revenue. In reality the value of storage will
depend on the adopted strategy and by on quality of the forecasts used to develop that
strategy. In order to quantify the proﬁts in such a case a stochastic analysis should be
performed.
Thirdly, taxes and tariﬀs need to be accounted. In this analysis ETES has been considered
to be taxed as an electric boiler, i.e. on the heat output, and no grid tariﬀhas been added
in the base case. The eﬀect of the grid tariﬀseems to be substantial but not enough to
hinder the ability of ETES to repay its ﬁxed costs. No PSO taxation has been considered,
but in the price taker analysis it was found that this tariﬀwould have had a detrimental
eﬀect on the business case for thermal storage of electricity. In fact, in the third quarter
of 2016 the Danish PSO will be of 20.3 øre/kWh, i.e. 27.3 e/MWh which would add to
the purchasing cost. One could argue that not accounting PSO is a fair assumption, as
the storage unit would help RES integration and thus should be exempted from paying the
PSO. On the other hand this is a political decision and is out of the scope of this thesis.
As seen in Section 5.2.1 the success of ETES is for a large part due to capacity credit. It
comes from the market hitting the price ceiling in several hours due to lack of generation

5.6
Conclusions
This section has dealt with the estimation of the economic potential for a brownﬁeld ETES
application. The technology was modelled in Balmorel for the period 2020-2035 and speciﬁc
constraints were developed and tested in a price-taker model.
The aim of the ﬁrst part of this chapter was to understand key drivers for the business case
of thermal energy storage in a country like Denmark. This was achieved generating several
scenarios with an exogenously-imposed 1 MW capacity and studying the proﬁtability per
unit of installed generation capacity. Additionally, a sensitivity analysis on the result for
the breakthrough year has been performed.
Findings indicate that under the base case assumptions described in Section 5.1 a com-
bined heat and power electricity-symmetric storage unit would have an almost exponential
increase in proﬁts from 2020 (18 e/kW/y) to 2035 (184 e/kW/y). It appears that a brown-
ﬁeld application of electricity-symmetric ETES might be able to repay its yearly ﬁxed costs
by 2030. In this year, in fact, proﬁts were about 31 e/kW above the sum of yearly operation
and maintenance costs and annualized investment.
It was also found that the unit was participating in the market with the role of a peaker, i.e.
by operating in a few high price hours. Revenue distribution showed that a large share of
the realised revenue came from extreme prices, i.e. the unit was able to get a capacity credit
for providing reliable capacity in low generation/high demand critical hours. Nevertheless
these prices are subject to a high degree of uncertainty, reason for which the business case
would pose a quite high risk unless capacity markets are put into place.
The importance of diﬀerent revenue drivers was assessed with scenario analysis.
The business case of an electricity-asymmetric unit was studied. Results showed that yearly
proﬁts increased signiﬁcantly. The reason for this improved performance can be found in
the fact that a unit with a larger charging capacity can buy cheaper energy on average.

5.6.
Conclusions
Additionally an electricity-asymmetric unit can be designed to have symmetrical conditions
in the thermal storage, which can simplify design and operation. Electricity-asymmetric
ETES appeared to be particularly suitable for revamping existing CCGT plants, in which
case the system could come close to repaying its ﬁxed costs already in 2025 under the spe-
ciﬁc assumptions.
Heat revenues seemed to constitute a sizeable part of the yearly proﬁts. They were most
important in the period 2020-2030. After that the decommissioning of conventional power
plants, the addition of RES capacity and the shift towards a more ﬂexible but expensive
gas-based generation caused an important increase in the potential revenues from electricity,
while at the same time heat production cost decreased due to the shift towards biomass.
Four scenarios were built to study the eﬀect of fuel and quota prices on the business case
for ETES. It was found that there is a linear relation between marginal cost ratio of natural
gas and coal and proﬁts. More speciﬁcally, coal price was controlling proﬁts until 2030 and
substituted by gas price later.
Self-cannibalization eﬀect was found to be an important factor when increasing the capacity
of storage. It appears that a electricity-asymmetric unit is less aﬀected by this.
The eﬀect of gird tariﬀs was found signiﬁcant but did not seem to be enough to hinder the
ability of the technology to repay its ﬁxed costs.
A second part of the economic assessment looked at the optimal ETES investments in Den-
mark, Germany and UK and at how ETES competed with other production technology in
the future energy system.
It was found that the model chose to invest in thermal storage of electricity instead of
ﬂexible, small scale and low-eﬃciency open cycle gas turbines. Also, oﬀshore wind capacity
was not found beneﬁcial unless ETES was available. Thus it can be concluded that ETES
seems to improve the business case for wind power.
When looking at the eﬀect of storage on system costs in Denmark, UK and Germany it was
found that investments in ETES decreased annual system costs signiﬁcantly and even more
when accompanied by investments in oﬀshore wind. Most of this advantage came from
improved business case for RES producers. Consumers were found to be aﬀected positively
when storage was together with oﬀshore wind.
All in all, the technology of thermal storage of electricity seems to be a promising alternative
for the future energy system.
The possibility of revamping existing plants and turning
them into ﬂexible storage units is promising for several reasons. First, it seems to be worth
the investment if the assumptions are valid, meaning that such units are able to repay
the revamping cost in their lifetime. Additionally, a producer may have an even larger
incentive in investing in ETES if it oﬀers the possibility to extend the economic lifetime
of his assets. Transforming power stations with ETES could be a valuable alternative to
biomass conversion given the decreased liability related to uncertainty in biomass prices.
The possibility of thermal storage to participate in the heat market as well seems to be
favourable, as it can increase revenues and decrease uncertainty in earnings.
The unit
was also shown to favour investments in additional renewable capacity and help the green
transition decreasing the expected system costs.
It is nevertheless important not to confuse the role of this technology in the energy system:
it functions as a ﬂexible peaker and hence has few operational hours. The eﬀect is twofold:
it causes a need to keep the investment cost as low as possible and it increases uncertainty
related to revenues.

6 Conclusions
This chapter presents conclusions for this study. The author tries to assess whether
the ﬁndings have matched the initial aims of the project and highlights the most im-
portant ones.
The aims of this work were to give an indication of opportunities and challenges related to
Henrik Stiesdal’s version of thermal storage of electricity. This is a concept that employs a
packed bed thermal storage unit to accumulate heat and uses a air heat pump or an electric
heater for charging and steam or air cycles for discharging. It is peculiar in the fact that
most of the components are well known and commercially available while their inclusion in
a single organic system is original and has not been tried before.
The analysis has been conducted both from a techno-economic perspective. Both sides of
the coin had to be kept into consideration due to the nature of this technology and the
competitive conditions of the electricity market. Challenges are present in designing and
operating the system, especially related to the development of the temperature proﬁle in the
storage. At the same time the market puts storage in a diﬃcult situation in which revenue
potential is currently limited and great attention has to be put in designing a system with
the lowest possible cost.
In respect, the following points have been achieved with the analysis:
• Estimating the round-trip storage eﬃciency for steam (ETES) and air (HT-TES)
cycles
• Studying the potential of the air heat pump charging compared to the electric heater
• Understanding temperature dynamics in the storage, for both a symmetric and an
asymmetric unit
• Studying behaviour of the thermocline when applying a repetitive schedule
• Proposing an alternative method for controlling the temperature proﬁle that can give
advantages in district heating production

==PAGE:140==
• Studying behaviour of a unit in the current and future electricity market
• Studying economic feasibility of brownﬁeld ETES
• Evaluating business case drivers: heat, spread between fuel prices, capacity ratio,
extreme prices, self-cannibalization eﬀect, grid tariﬀs
• Studying social-optimal electricity-symmetric ETES investments, also in combination
with oﬀshore wind
• Understanding eﬀect on system costs and business cases of the diﬀerent players.
Overall it seems that the possible eﬃciency of ETES or HT-TES is in the range of 40%
(up to 50% considering an ETES heat pump system). This is mostly dependent on perfor-
mance of components, especially turbines or expanders. The eﬃciency level is lower than
other thermal storage options, such as CO2 low-temperature systems such as the ones in as
[55], [73], [57] and [49], but presents the advantage of employing conventional, commercially
available components and working ﬂuids. Also, the investment cost should be signiﬁcantly
lower.
Both the steam and the air version seem to be able to reach this eﬃciency level, albeit
with diﬀerent values of the storage temperatures. The round-trip eﬃciency value found can
be considered a conservative estimate as it was calculated with relatively low performance
components.
The thermocline development in the storage appeared quite robust and not very much in-
ﬂuenced by the design parameters. When applying a repeated schedule the unit reached a
steady state exergetic eﬃciency in the range of 90%. Controlling the temperature proﬁle
to keep it as vertical as possible should allow to keep this number even higher and close
to 100%. In this regard the option of discharging selected sections of the storage seems
to perform better than the section charge and allows also to generate energy for district
heating directly from the storage.
From the economical viewpoint the main ﬁndings can be summarized as below:
• Thermal electricity storage behaves as a peaker in the electricity system and shows
low generation capacity factors;
• A large share of proﬁts depends on capacity credit, which can be aﬀected by a large
uncertainty;
• Heat revenues seem to be a consistent part of the proﬁtability and oﬀer the possibility
of hedging risk from the electricity market. The unit should be designed considering
also a heat-only option (bypass of discharging cycle);
• An electricity-symmetric ETES unit employed to revamp a conventional steam power
plant seems to be able to repay its annualised costs by 2030;
• High round-trip eﬃciency of an electricity storage unit does not justify a large invest-
ment cost. Cheaper and less eﬃcient units may be more successful;

• An electricity-asymmetric unit seems to have a better business case. It can earn higher

investment cost low and does not seem to pose excessive technological challenges. With the
appropriate operation of its storage unit a satisfying eﬃciency can be achieved and if that
is veriﬁed it looks like thermal storage of electricity might be successful in the future energy
system as a power plant revamping option.

Future perspectives
7 Future perspectives
In this chapter possible future works in the framework of this project are suggested,
starting from weaknesses in the used approach and taking into consideration the ﬁnd-
ings of this study.
This thesis had a broad aim and general objectives. As speciﬁed multiple times, its role
should be the one of describing this type of thermal storage of electricity and highlighting
opportunities and challenges. As such, the level of speciﬁcity has on purpose been kept
low. Future works could be based on the ﬁndings in this report to face speciﬁc challenges
highlighted in the previous chapters. The author believes that it is of capital importance
that the combined techno-economical perspective is kept in future studies related to this
technology.
A case-speciﬁc techno-economic valuation would be the natural continuation of this work.
For instance, a speciﬁc plant that is currently not operating could be selected and the design
of the revamping with thermal storage could be performed with the given real constraints
of the system.
In order to have an accurate analysis, ﬁrst of all a validation of the investment cost as-
sumptions should be performed to thoroughly assess the robustness of the feasibility result.
Component manufacturers should be contacted to estimate the cost of installing a thermal
storage unit similar to the one suggested by Henrik Stiesdal and of the substitution of the
boiler with an HRSG (if necessary). At the same time a detailed technical analysis should
be performed in which the eﬀect of each possible layout modiﬁcation to achieve higher eﬃ-
ciency is analysed both in terms of improved performance and costs. In any case eﬃciency
gains or losses coming from the revamping would be case speciﬁc and call for a dedicated
techo-economic assessment. This was impossible in this study for time constraints.
Secondly, the storage thermal model should be improved and validated experimentally.
Once this is done, the thermal model and the economic one could be integrated, creating a
single tool able to decide how to optimally operate the thermal storage unit considering the
proﬁle degradation if no restoration is applied. It would be interesting to ﬁnd out whether

ket as often as desired and to what degree deterioration should be allowed to be able to
produce/consume when needed. The operational model may be integrated with strategic
behaviour. Also, the behaviour may change the optimal design of the unit, i.e. optimal
number of storage hours and geometrical features of the packed bed.
Additional studies may be performed to assess the eﬀect of uncertainty in renewable gener-

stochastic optimization of strategy. This tool should be extremely valuable to the owner of

A.2.1
Eﬃciency trends for ETES
The eﬀect storage hot and cold end temperatures was assessed varying the latter between
80◦C and 250◦C for three diﬀerent values of the former, i.e. 400◦C, 500◦C and 600◦C. No
higher temperature was considered due to the fact that a conventional sub-critical Rankine
cycle has a upper temperature bound around 550◦C .
Figure A.13 illustrates the various eﬃciency trends. It is possible to see the diﬀerence in
behaviour when the pinch point is at the top temperature (from 165◦C above) and when it
is at the evaporation temperature level.
Figure A.13: On the left: round-trip storage eﬃciency when including a heat pump and
thermal eﬃciency of discharging cycle. On the right: Charging and discharging exergetic
eﬃciencies. Trends are expressed as a function of cold-end storage temperature for three
diﬀerent values of hot-end temperature.
It can be noticed that the higher the hot-end storage temperature the higher the exergetic
eﬃciencies, including charging, discharging and total.
For charging this happens because losses in the expander signiﬁcantly decrease as the hot-
end temperature increases. On the production side losses in the turbine increase as the
temperature increases but an opposite trend is seen in the HRSG. As the cold-end tem-
perature increases, in fact, the pinch point temperature diﬀerence stays constant and the
diﬀerence in the temperature proﬁles of the storage and the production cycle becomes less
important.
The round-trip storage eﬃciency has a maximum around 165◦C , i.e. the temperature at
which both pinch point temperature diﬀerences are close to the minimum 15◦C .
This
behaviour somehow follows the discharging exergetic eﬃciency whereas the charging one
is almost constant until a certain temperature and then decreases, more sharply for lower

hod-end storage temperatures.
For T < 165◦C the pinch point is located at the evaporation temperature, therefore when
decreasing the cold-end temperature the evaporation temperature (or pressure) has to adapt
accordingly. As the cold-end temperature decreases from 165◦C so does the evaporation
temperature. Consequently the heat of evaporation increases and the obtained steam tem-
perature at the outlet of the HRSG decreases as shown in Figure A.14. As a result the
Figure A.14: Temperature proﬁles of steam and ﬂuid coming from the storage for diﬀerent
values of the cold-end temperature
two proﬁles become more and more distant, hence irreversibilities from the heat exchange
increase.
When instead the temperature is above 165◦C and the pinch point is located at the hot-end
temperature the temperature proﬁle of the steam does not change as the hot-end tem-
perature increases.
This because the evaporation pressure is the same as the top cycle
pressure. Hence the thermal eﬃciency of the production side stays constant. Nevertheless
the discharging exergetic eﬃciency decreases because the distance between the two proﬁles
increases thus increasing losses from the HRSG.
As for the charging cycle it is not inﬂuenced by the HRSG pinch point position, therefore
the derivative of the curve is continuous across the analysed temperature interval. Exergetic
eﬃciency basically follows the trend of the biggest loss component, i.e. the expander. As the
cold-end temperature increases losses in the expander become higher and higher (because
the exergy content at the expander inlet increases) and eﬃciency decreases.

Eﬃciency trends for HT-TES
Similarly to what was done in the previous Section, here the eﬃciency trend is investigated
for the air-based cycle HT-TES. The cold-end storage temperature was varied between
200◦C and 400◦C whereas three values were tested for the hot end temperature, i.e. 800◦C
900◦C and 1000◦C .
Figure A.15 illustrates the most interesting trends. Looking at the round-trip eﬃciency of
Figure A.15: On the left: discharging thermal eﬃciency and charging COP; on the right:
optimal LP pressure ratio and round-trip storage eﬃciency of the system with heat pump;
curves are as a function of cold-end storage temperature and for diﬀerent values of hot-end
storage temperature.
the cycle including heat pump charging (graph on the right, red curves) it can be seen that
it presents a maximum for a certain cold-end temperature. This maximum moves to higher
values and temperatures the warmer the hot end of the storage. If moving the attention to
the discharging thermal eﬃciency, which is also equal to the round-trip storage eﬃciency
of the electric heater charging version (left graph, blue curves) a similar behaviour can be
seen but with higher optimal temperatures. The reason for this is the trend of the charging
COP. The curve of COP as a function of cold-end temperature becomes less steep as the
hot-end temperature increases.
The optimal LP pressure ratio is ever-decreasing with increasing storage temperature. That
means that as cold-end temperature increases it appears that it is more and more conve-
nient to decrease inter-refrigeration. This is a reasonable result. In fact a higher storage
temperature means the need for a higher after-compression temperature in the cycle to get
close to the level of the hot air coming from the storage. In this case cooling of the ﬂuid
is causing the need for more compression work to reach the desired temperature. It is also
decreasing irreversibility in compression, but as cold-end storage temperature increases this
eﬀect becomes less and less important and cooling is reduced.
The trend discussed above is reversed when considering a regenerative cycle, as more re-
generation (hence higher eﬃciency) is achieved if the after compression temperature is low
because more of the exhaust energy from the turbine can be recuperated.

A.2.3
Eﬃciency and pressure ratio relationship in regenera-
tive Brayton-Joule cycles
When dealing with a regenerative cycle an eﬃciency improvement will be obtained by
increasing the internal heat recuperation. In practice the lower the overall pressure ratio the
higher the regeneration hence the eﬃciency (when assuming constant isentropic eﬃciency
of turbine and compressor), but the lower the speciﬁc work and the higher the mass ﬂow
needed to achieve the same power. If regeneration happens then the temperature after
compression has to be lower (since heat is given from the regeneration) and hence the total
pressure ratio.
With the decrease in pressure ratio the speciﬁc work, i.e. the amount of work that can be
obtained with 1 kg/s of mass ﬂow, decreases whereas the thermal eﬃciency increases as
shown in Figure A.16 (obtained by considering equal pressure ratios for the LP and HP
compression stages).
Figure A.16: Discharge thermal eﬃciency (left axis), speciﬁc work and cold-end storage
temperature as a function of total pressure ratio.
For a pressure ratio of 2, for instance, the system is able to obtain a 54.3% thermal eﬃ-
ciency but the obtained speciﬁc work is more than three times less than what would be
obtained with a pressure ratio of 20. The speciﬁc work can be increased by pressurizing the
system but this causes additional investments. The optimal trade-oﬀbetween eﬃciency,
pressurization and overall volume of the plant should be found.
In a regenerative system the lower the post compression temperature, the higher the amount
of heat coming from the turbine exhaust that can be utilized hence the higher the eﬃciency.
For this reason the inter-refrigeration gives a larger advantage in this conﬁguration com-
pared to the base one.

A.2.4
Comparison of exergetic eﬃciency of components
This Appendix shows a comparison between the exergetic eﬃciencies of selected compo-
nents in the diﬀerent alternatives.
It should be noted that heat exchangers with ambient, i.e. the condenser in the steam
cycles and the inter-coolers in the air cycles, have been left out of the comparison since their
exergy product is considered zero (see assumptions is Chapter 3). One could argue that
a more proper modelling might have considered the component itself deﬁning its exergetic
eﬃciency and then accounting separately for the losses. This would have allowed to evaluate
the performance of the component itself and separate the losses due to the component and
those due to non-utilization of the exergy ﬂow to the environment.
It has been chosen not to follow this path because that would have required to model the
ambient side as well, i.e.
incoming and outcoming temperature or ﬂow.
That was not
considered part of the scope of this analysis which is focused on exergetic eﬃciency of the
cycle as a direct measure of round-trip storage eﬃciency. Consequently each non-electricity
ﬂow have been disregarded and considered as a loss.
Figure A.17 illustrates the comparison for ETES whereas Figure A.18 for HT-TES.
It can be seen that exergetic eﬃciency does not change signiﬁcantly for the components
of the heat pump system. In fact the exergetic eﬃciency of this cycle has been shown to
have a low dependency on the cold-end temperature, which is the only variable aﬀecting
the charging cycle that changes among the alternatives.
It also be noted how passing from a single-pressure to a double-pressure HRSG causes an
improvement in the exergetic eﬃciency of this component. The turbine is also positively
aﬀected.
The preheater presents a low exergetic eﬃciency compared to the other components but
this has been shown to have a non signiﬁcant impact on overall eﬃciency.
As for the HT-TES cycle it appears that exergetic eﬃciency of compression stages increases
with the pressure level, i.e. the HP stage has a performance that is higher or equal the one
of the LP stage. Additionally the exergetic eﬃciency of the discharging HEX is quite high
given the fact that proﬁles are virtually parallel.

B.1
Handling of recursive constraints in GAMS
The mathematical model shown in Section 5.1.2 illustrates that all variables have three
time dimensions, i.e. hour, week and year. Weeks and timesteps are ordered sets in GAMS,
meaning that the following set can be found by adding 1 to the previous. For instance:
LEVEL(Y,S,T)=LEVEL(Y,S,T+1)
makes sure that the level at the previous timestep is equal to the one at the following
timestep. This speciﬁc code, though, does not link the last timestep to anything as it fails
to ﬁnd a suitable object. Therefore GAMS provides a speciﬁc option:
LEVEL(Y,S,T)=LEVEL(Y,S,T++1)
In this case the last timestep of the season is linked to the ﬁrst one in a circular fashion. This
functionality is nevertheless not suitable for the speciﬁc application as we are interested in
linking the last timestep of a season with the ﬁrst one in the following season. Therefore
the problem has been solved by doubling the storage balance constraint. One is applied to
all the timesteps except for the last one using the GAMS code below:
$ not CARD(T)=ORD(T)
Note that the term $ indicates a condition in GAMS. CARD calculates the dimension
(cardinality) of a set and ORD gives the index of the current set element. So the code line
is equivalent to asking GAMS to apply the constraint whenever the time instant is not the
last one.
The other constraint is applied only to the last timestep and links it to the ﬁrst in the next
season.
B.2
Modelling of seasonal storage in Balmorel
Balmorel allows to decrease the number of weeks assessed in the simulation and at the same
time increase the actual duration of each time instant by means of the factor IHOURSINST.
143
==PAGE:172==
B.2.
Modelling of seasonal storage in Balmorel
This indicates the number of real hours represented by each time instant in the model. It
is simply calculated as the ratio between the total number of weeks in a year (52) and the
number of simulated weeks.
This factor can be accounted for in only one equation in the unit model, i.e. in the balance
equation (5.2) as a multiplying factor for the energy ﬂows to and from the storage. For
instance, when simulating 13 weeks with time aggregation each hour actually represents
4 hours. Hence an electricity consumption of 1 MW can be accounted as 4 MWh when
entering the storage. If the factor is not accounted then the model would simulate a storage
of a size IHOURSINST times larger than reality. On the other hand adding it means not
simulating the actual weekly dynamics of the storage properly.
The possible alternative is to perform a restriction in the simulated number of weeks, keep
factor value of 1 and pretend that the simulated weeks are repeated. Basically this consists
in applying the factor to revenues, expenses, consumption and production but after the
simulation is completed. This allows to properly model both the size of the storage and the
hourly schedule. Errors in the modelling are generated when there is a signiﬁcant amount
of energy transfer between seasons, because that makes so that it is not possible to pretend
the week is repeated. To be clear, if for a selected week the storage starts empty and ends
at 50% charge the following week should start at 50%. Instead, in the hypothesis above, the
simulated week is repeated and the starting level is again empty thus generating a modelling
error.
If the amount of seasonal storage is not signiﬁcant then this represents the best way to de-
crease computational time for a Balmorel simulation. Since in the price taker pre-analysis
it was shown that the unit is not performing much storage across weeks, this was the tech-
nique adopted for the Balmorel simulations. It is important to point out that if the aim
of the simulation was to model seasonal storage, i.e. the shifting of production between
summer and winter seasons then the scaling factor should be accounted.
