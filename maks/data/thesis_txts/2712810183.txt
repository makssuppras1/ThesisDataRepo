Abstract 
 
The rising world population is pressing on global food security, and many regions are experiencing high 
rates of malnutrition. Crop production is suffering from climate change, pests and diseases which can 
gravely affect yield. Biocontrol microorganisms, specifically plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria, have 
demonstrated great potential in supporting plant growth and health in a variety of ways, therefore they 
represent a key asset in sustainable agriculture practices. This work investigates the development of a soil 
bacterial community in wheat for plant growth promotion. Two experimental approaches were taken to 
this end. Firstly, a large bacterial community was analyzed after six rounds of rhizosphere re-inoculation, 
by selection of the microbiome from fastest and slowest-growing plants. Amplicon sequencing was 
employed to reveal the microbiome composition resulting from the continued selection of this plant 
parameter. The second approach consisted in testing smaller inoculated bacterial communities, to 
evaluate their impact on wheat growth. 
Analysis of 16S amplicons suggested that the microbiome which was developed in fast-selected versus 
slow-selected lineages was, in fact, significantly different. Similarly, rhizosphere communities generally 
diverged from the bulk soil microbiome. A few bacterial taxa were identified, which appeared to enhance 
these differences. However, it could not be demonstrated whether the repeated selection of this plant 
parameter determined the development of a specific soil microbiome in a reproducible way. Moreover, 
it was not possible to describe clear relationships between smaller inoculated communities and wheat 
growth. Overall, these approaches proved to be promising starting points of investigation to be applied in 
biocontrol studies.

Introduction 
 
Background  
“Zero hunger” is the name of goal number 2 of the UN Sustainable Development goals, created to 
recognize and respond to humanity’s most pressing issues (UN, 2015). In fact, as the world population 
rises, it is becoming increasingly crucial to tackle the lack of food and proper nutrition affecting millions 
of people worldwide. The pandemic has exacerbated the already grave condition of malnutrition and food 
insecurity, with the UN reporting 720-811 million people being undernourished in 2020 (UN). About one 
third of the world population does not have enough food or regular access to a healthy, balanced diet 
(UN). Agriculture plays a fundamental role in food security, by providing the crops for human 
consumption, as well as for animal feed. Therefore, it is imperative that agricultural practices are 
conducted sustainably and efficiently to produce enough food for our growing population. However, great 
losses of products and cultivations are suffered as a result of diseases, pests and climate change, which 
can gravely affect food security in certain areas (Oerke, 2006; Lobell et al., 2011). While actions are being 
taken to tackle climate change, improper agricultural practices are regularly being employed against 
diseases and pests threatening our cultivations (Hassaan and El Nemr, 2020). Chemical pesticides, 
fungicides and fertilizers are administered abundantly across the world to boost plant growth and prevent 
loss of harvest due to plant pathogens. Despite this, they cause grave pollution of the environment and 
affect human health (Sabarwal et al., 2018). Humans can get exposed to toxic chemicals in multiple ways, 
from field workers to chemical transporters, to food sellers and consumers, and develop various health 
disorders, including cancer (Sabarwal et al., 2018; Thongprakaisang et al., 2013). The environment is also 
affected by the overuse of pesticides, as these chemicals accumulate and spread through the earth and 
water (Hassaan and El Nemr, 2020), as depicted in Figure 1. Importantly, they can cause resistance in the 
pathogens and unwanted organisms that they target, as well as other organisms populating the natural 
ecosystems outside of fields. This is a problem because it renders the chemicals ineffective and introduces 
resistance into the environment in a way that is hard to monitor and control.

A promising set of alternatives to the use of agrochemicals consists in the application of biocontrol 
microorganisms, capable of promoting the health and growth of plants through various natural 
mechanisms (Crecchio et al., 2018). The main sphere of action of biocontrol microorganisms is the 
rhizosphere, which is the thin layer of soil surrounding the roots. In the rhizosphere, bacteria, fungi, and 
other microorganisms interact with each other and with plant roots in ways that may be beneficial or 
detrimental to plant health (Crecchio et al., 2018). The roots of plants secrete substances termed root 
exudates that can attract and be used by soil microorganisms, which in turn can strengthen their 
attachment to the roots and positively affect the plant itself (Jones et al., 2009). Plant growth promoting 
rhizobacteria (PGPR) are such bacteria that can improve plant growth through an array of processes 
(Crecchio et al., 2018) (Figure 2). One of the best understood processes is the improvement of the 
bioavailability of nutrients which plants acquire from the soil. For example, nitrogen-fixing bacteria living 
in a symbiosis with plants in root nodules can convert molecular N2 to bioavailable nitrogen for plants 
(McInnes and Haq, 2007). While siderophores, iron-chelating organic compounds produced by certain 
microorganisms, aid the acquisition of iron by plants (Lemanceausoils et al., 2009; Grobelak and Hiller, 
2017). Biocontrol microorganisms can also enhance the plants’ own ability to take up nutrients, for 
example by affecting their transport systems or by inducing the upregulation of specific genes (Kechid et 
al., 2013; Nacry et al., 2013). Some can even produce or modulate plant phytohormones which promote 
faster plant growth (Arora et al., 2013). The mentioned dynamics are examples of direct mechanisms of 
plant promotion by rhizobacteria, which are often described as biostimulation. PGPR can additionally 
support plants by indirect mechanisms, for example by competing with pathogenic organisms for space 
and nutrients, or by producing antibiotic substances and lytic enzymes that can directly act on unwanted 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
pathogens in the rhizosphere, such as pathogenic fungi (Mazurier et al., 2009). One additional mechanism 
consists in the enhancement of the plant’s own defence system, stimulated by certain biocontrol bacteria. 
This action, termed induced systemic resistance (ISR), lets the plant fight against a broad range of 
pathogens, and appears to involve all plant tissues, so that the incidence and severity of plant diseases 
can be reduced (Annapurna et al., 2013). 
 
Figure 2: Examples of biocontrol and biostimulation actions by rhizobacteria in a plant root system. PGPR can form beneficial 
interactions with plants by populating their roots. They can support their nutrient acquisition (phosphorus mobilization, nitrogen 
fixation, siderophore production) and boost their growth (phytohormone modulation). Additionally, PGPR can outcompete other 
soil microorganisms by occupying root space, as well as by producing lytic enzymes and antibiotics against pathogenic fungi or 
bacteria. Finally they may enhance the plant’s own defence system. Figure made on Biorender. 
 
Thanks to their potential in sustainable plant promotion, biocontrol microorganisms are constantly being 
studied and tested to understand the mechanisms by which they interact with plants, and to maximize 
their positive effect on the growth and health of crops. The identified microorganisms can be administered 
on the seeds, in the field, or on produce to help guarantee healthy plants and secure, stable products, 
without resorting to harmful chemicals (Theresa and Radhakrishnan, 2021).

Main classes of PGPR 
While studies on bacteria’s biocontrol properties are spanning progressively more bacterial groups, a few 
genera are especially interesting to be investigated by researchers. Here, some of these groups are 
introduced, along with examples of plant-promoting actions which they are most known for. Moreover, 
these genera are highly represented in the complete bacterial community used for wheat inoculation in 
this project. 
A major class of biocontrol bacteria is the Bacillus group (Islam et al., 2019). Bacilli are gram-positive 
bacteria which produce spores that are able to withstand the harsh conditions of hostile environments 
(Losick, 2020). Many Bacillus species, such as B. subtilis are natural soil-dwellers and are therefore present 
in the rhizosphere of plants. A key factor that makes these bacteria adapted to living in conjunction with 
plant roots is their efficient production of the biofilm matrix. While biofilm production is common in 
various different bacterial groups, the Bacillus group is often taken as an example to describe the 
functionalities of the biofilm matrix. A biofilm is a bacterial community which is encased in an extracellular 
matrix, mainly composed of exopolysaccharides and proteins (Hall-Stoodley et al., 2004). The matrix helps 
cells stick together and form aggregates that can adhere to surfaces, including the root surface 
(Beauregard et al., 2013). The biofilm matrix provides protection to the bacterial cells and allows the 
colony to retain nutrients and valuable enzymes while diffusing toxic compounds (Romeo, 2008). The 
growth of B. subtilis biofilm on plant roots has been found to be stimulated by the compound L-malic acid 
present in the root exudates themselves (Rudrappa et al., 2008), exemplifying a direct mechanism of 
recruitment by the plant to the bacterium. Bacillus species have been extensively studied and tested for 
biocontrol applications. An important mechanism of action is based on the production of cyclic 
lipopeptides (CLPs), including the families of surfactins, fengycins and iturins, some of which have 
antimicrobial activity. Fengycins can inhibit filamentous fungi such as A. flavus (Farzaneh et al., 2016), 
while the main action of surfactins is the promotion of colonization of plant surfaces by aiding biofilm 
formation and cell movement, besides having antimicrobial properties as well (Lopez et al., 2009; Kearns, 
2010; Cawoy et al., 2015). The production of surfactin-like molecules is not unique to Bacillus species, and 
is thought to be a distinctive feature of many soil-dwelling bacteria, due to its useful properties in the 
rhizosphere environment. Three B. subtilis isolates are among the 87 strains making up the whole bacterial 
community inoculated in this project. 
Another genus which has been extensively studied for biocontrol applications is Pseudomonas. 
Recognized Pseudomonas species for plant growth-promotion include P. fluorescens, P. putida and P. 
chlororaphis, which can produce antifungal compounds that are effective against fungal pathogens such 
as Pyricularia oryzae and Fusarium graminearum (Chen et al., 2018; Chuen et al., 2021; Mazurier et al., 
2009). Products containing these species are therefore sold as antifungal products which can be 
administered to a wide variety of crop plants (Theresa and Radhakrishnan, 2021). Chen et al. described a 
novel antifungal property of P. chlororaphis subsp. piscium against F. graminearum in wheat (Figure 3). 
==PAGE:10==

P. fluorescens isolates have additionally been found to help plants with taking up phosphate. The gluconic 
acid they produce aids phosphate solubilization, which translates into efficient plant promotion, as 
demonstrated in pea (Pisum sativum) plants (Oteino et al., 2015). Five P. fluorescens isolates are among 
the 23 Pseudomonas strains included in the bacterial community used in this study. 
The genus Stenotrophomonas has been studied for its biocontrol properties (Park et al., 2005). 
Stenotrophomonas are found ubiquitously, but they are especially adapted to living in conjunction with 
plants (Ryan et al., 2009). They have been found in a variety of plants’ rhizosphere, including wheat. The 
mode of plant growth promotion by the members of this genus spans several mechanisms. The most 
important are growth hormone production, nitrogen fixation, and sulphur oxidation (Park et al., 2005).   
S. maltophylia is one of the best characterized biocontrol species of this genus. It was tested to produce 
a significant increase in wheat growth under salt stress, as well as stimulation of plant resistance to 
pathogens (Singh and Jha, 2017). However, it is also a recognized human pathogen which can cause severe 
infections, so its field applications may be limited (Tunger et al., 2007). S. rhizophila, on the other hand, is 
not harmful to humans, while it also has valuable plant growth-promoting potential (Maurer et al., 2013). 
For instance, it increases germination rate and shoot and root growth in wheat and many other crops, 
including tomato and sweet pepper, in highly-salinated soils (Berg et al., 2013). S. rhizophila and other 
Stenotrophomonas species also produce volatile organic compounds with antifungal activity, such as β-
phenylethanol and dodecanal, which strongly inhibit soil-borne pathogenic fungi like Rhizoctonia solani 
(Kai et al., 2007). Ten S. rhizophila isolates and one S. maltophylia isolate are among the strains making 
up this study’s bacterial community.

Biocontrol community studies 
The soil houses a multitude of bacteria, such as the genera described above. In addition to these, fungi, 
archaea, and small animals, coexist and interact with each other, as well as with plant roots (Soni et al., 
2017). Such a collection of organisms inhabiting a defined space is termed microbiome or microbiota, and 
it is specific to the kind of environment it occupies. Microbiomes inhabit a variety of different niches in 
the gut and the mouth of animals, or on plant roots and leaves (Bauer et al., 2018; Santos and Olivares, 
2021). In the rhizosphere, bacteria such as Proteobacteria, Firmicutes and Bacteroides groups, and fungi 
like Ascomycota and Basidiomycota colonize the root-soil interface and form symbiotic, mutual, and 
competitive interactions between them and with plants (Trivedi et al., 2020). While these participants can 
single-handedly promote beneficial or deleterious effects in the rhizosphere, they typically become 
involved in a dynamic system of microorganisms, forming a microbiome which can promote or hinder the 
health of plants (Santos and Olivares, 2021).  
In order to assess the potential effect on plants of specific rhizobacteria, in vitro and in vivo experiments 
are to be conducted (Cordovez et al., 2021). In vitro approaches include studying the growth and 
properties of the strain on agar plates, genomic and metabolite analyses, and biofilm formation assays. 
Analysing the genome and transcriptome of the bacterium can give insight on its function, such as any 
antimicrobial compounds it may produce which may be relevant for biocontrol (Chen et al., 2018). 
Moreover, characterizing the strain’s biofilm formation ability is done to infer the root colonization ability 
of the bacterium. However, a complete assessment of the strain for biocontrol typically includes an in vivo 
experiment of plant cultivation, otherwise called “in planta”, which involves the inoculation of the single 
bacterial strain in question (Djebaili et al., 2021). This approach allows the observation of aspects such as 
plant growth parameters and the extent of root colonization related to the specific bacterial strain 
involved. In certain in vivo experiments a pathogen such as a pathogenic fungus may be inoculated along 
with the bacterial strain to observe the strain’s ability to suppress its growth. For example, Chen et al., 
inoculated the F. graminearum fungus on wheat heads to analyze the biocontrol action of their isolate P. 
piscium (Chen et al., 2018). 
The described experiments are fundamental in the characterization of a biocontrol strain and rely on a 
simplified system which basically includes the plant and the inoculated rhizobacterial strain. To be able to 
gather accurate information from this kind of experiments, many variables of the system are neutralized, 
for example the soil and the plant seeds may be sterilized before use, and the operations are usually 
carried out in a controlled, sterile environment (Wolf and Skipper, 2018). This way the complexity of such 
a system is radically reduced compared to an actual field setting, where the natural soil is populated with 
an extremely intricate microbiome, and the plants are exposed to all kinds of external stimuli and other 
microbes outside of the soil (Ciarmiello et al., 2014; Nadarajah, 2017). While certain cultivation aspects 
are easily determined in the laboratory with the help of a growth chamber, such as light exposition time 
and temperature, other variables known to affect plant growth are often hard to control in experiments 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
(Gonzalez et al., 2021). Such variables include soil type, plant genotype, presence of endophyte bacteria 
and fungi inside the plant seeds (Chapelle et al., 2020; Cordovez et al., 2021) and, importantly, the 
microbiome already present in the soil. In fact, upon sowing in the field, the seeds enter a pre-existing soil 
environment which is populated with a complex microbiome (Gadhave et al., 2018). Given the intricate 
relationships bacteria develop between them and with plant roots, the presence of a natively established 
microbiome in the soil could impact the effect that the single studied strain have on plant growth in ways 
that are not easily predictable (Chang and Turner, 2019). Considering such limitations of experiments 
conducted on just selected microbes, other approaches seek to provide a better model of the complexity 
of a field cultivation. 
Different experiments are designed to take into account the formation and potential biocontrol action of 
a whole bacterial community, instead of focusing on a single strain. Such experiments may involve the 
development and testing of a synthetic bacterial community, which is a collection of different strains that 
is purposely assembled by the researcher to assess its biocontrol potential in cultivation (Schmitz et al., 
2022). The underlying observation behind this approach is that some biocontrol bacteria have been found 
to have a heightened plant-promotion action when inoculated together with other strains. Schmitz et al. 
developed a small synthetic community which was able to support growth and salt tolerance of tomato 
plants in high salinity conditions. It was composed of Massilia, Enterobacter, Ensifer, Bacillus, and 
Streptomyces isolates. The community produced significantly better plant growth, than each isolate did 
on its own (Schmitz et al., 2022). A bacterial community may be created based on information on the 
single biocontrol strains, or based on a bacterial community naturally present in the soil. In the 
aforementioned study, the synthetic community was the result of rhizosphere sampling of the legume 
plant Indigofera argentea, which is adapted to high salinity conditions in the desert. It is possible to isolate 
a bacterial community from the soil through simple microbiological techniques, such as plating of a 
rhizosphere sample dilution on agar plates. The culturable strains present in the sample may grow and be 
isolated from the plates, while any strains that are not easily culturable in the lab due to specific growth 
requirements would not appear through this method (Alain and Querellou, 2009). However, a more 
comprehensive outlook on the composition of a natural rhizosphere microbiome can be obtained by a 
16S rRNA gene sequencing analysis, which eases the identification of all bacteria present, culturable or 
not (Kim et al., 2011). Performing experiments with the inoculation of a bacterial community enables 
researchers to test a set of dynamic interactions arising in the community between its members and with 
the plant, and assess the effect of the community on plant fitness and growth (Trivedi et al., 2020). 
Biocontrol studies on small synthetic communities can yield important information on the single 
interactions between the involved bacterial species, so that such an assembly can be treated as a model 
community for other experiments. 
 
==PAGE:13==

An alternative approach to studying a simple synthetic community is to analyze a larger community 
composed of bacterial strains that are naturally present in the plant’s soil environment (McClure et al., 
2020). This can be done by cultivating the plant in natural soil sampled from the field, or by first isolating 
a high number of different bacterial strains from the plant’s rhizosphere and reinoculating the community 
into sterile soil in cultivation, providing more control over the inoculated microbiome. In the methods just 
described, the inoculated bacteria are native to the soil environment of the plant in question and are 
potentially relevant to its well-being in the field. Moreover, such experiments involving a larger 
community can be done to better resemble the complexity of natural rhizosphere microbiome (Schmitz 
et al., 2022; Cordovez et al., 2021).  
As relatively recent development in biocontrol studies involves the successive cultivation of a crop, to 
analyze the development and patterns of its microbiome. In fact, plants in the field get introduced into 
non-sterile soil, inhabited by a microbiome which has been shaped by continued plant growth cycles 
(Hendriks et al., 2015). Cordovez et al. demonstrated that the microbiome of tomato plants became 
progressively more diversified between different tomato plant genotypes upon successive cultivation, 
while it also became significantly different from the initial bulk soil used (Cordovez et al., 2021). While it 
is not clear how determinant plant genotype is in microbiome selection, other environmental factors in 
plant cultivation have been found to be significant in shaping the composition of plant microbiomes, as 
Morella et al. observed on tomato phyllosphere (Morella et al., 2020). Therefore, successive plant 
cultivation experiments can unveil a deeper level of understanding of biocontrol interactions, highly 
relevant in agricultural settings. 
 
16S gene sequencing 
When studying microbiomes it is crucial to be able to identify a large number of microorganisms quickly 
and effectively. Being able to gather such information on the microbes populating different niches gives 
researchers a more complete outlook on the studied microbiome, and can spark further questions on the 
relationship between the microorganisms and their environment (Franzosa et al., 2015). To characterize 
the bacterial variety present in a sample, performing 16S sequencing is usually the go-to approach taken. 
The 16S rRNA gene of bacteria encodes the rRNA component of the 30S subunit of ribosomes. The 
peculiarity of this gene is that its sequence is very conserved among closely related species (Wang et al., 
2015). Therefore, amplicon sequencing of the 16S gene is an effective way to identify the bacteria in a 
sample, taxonomically. The gene is amplified through a PCR reaction where specific primers are used to 
define the target sequence of the 16S gene (Kim et al. 2011). Nine hypervariable regions are found in the 
16S gene, which is approximately 1600 bp long in total. These regions have varying degrees of 
conservation, where some evolve faster than others. Therefore, more conservative regions tend to be 
indicative of higher taxonomic levels, whereas more variable regions can be targeted to distinguish 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
different genera and species. Specifically, the V2 and V3, or V3 and V4 regions of the gene are often chosen 
to be amplified, producing shorter amplicons of high taxonomic significance (Kim et al., 2011; Bukin et al., 
2019). 16S rRNA gene sequencing can often not achieve species specificity (Konstantinidis and Tiedje, 
2007). Still, it is an effective way to gain insight into the overall composition of bacterial microbiomes. 
 
The described methods are valuable tools to study what can be achieved with plant-promoting bacteria. 
The main application of biocontrol bacterial formulations is to be administered in crop cultivation. In fact, 
major crops that are cultivated across the world can be subject to diseases, and yield losses of various 
nature. Wheat is no exception, so it is of fundamental to explore the potential of PGPR communities to 
be employed in the cultivation of this important crop. For this reason, wheat is the focus of this study.  
 
Challenges in wheat cultivation 
Wheat (Triticum aestivum) is one of the most important crops cultivated worldwide, along with maize and 
rice (Wrigley, 2018). Wheat’s ancient wild varieties were first cultivated 10’000 years ago by hunter-
gatherers, and the cultivation of this crop contributed enormously to the establishment and evolution of 
human civilizations, by providing a stable food source; in fact, it could be grown on a large scale, and it 
could be easily stored for a long time (Mergoum et al., 2009). Since then, technological advances in wheat 
cultivation, harvesting, and processing of wheat kernels have increased productivity and have expanded 
this crop across the world. More recently, purposeful genetic breeding has produced more high-yielding 
varieties, at the expense of many traditional varieties, which are at risk of disappearance (Feldman and 
Levy, 2015). Since the so-called Green Revolution in the 1960s, breeding has focused on few, crucial 
variables, with grain yield being the most important. Yield can be measured by kernel weight per spike, 
and it is influenced by other factors which are also the subject of breeding efforts, such as resistance to 
lodging, the bending of the stem to ground level, which makes harvest difficult (Mergoum et al., 2009). 
Breeding has contributed to a radical increase in the yield of wheat cultivars since the 1960s. Other factors 
that were the subject of breeding are frost and drought resistance, resistance to pests and diseases, and 
grain nutritional quality (Feldman and Levy, 2015). Nowadays, more than 700 million tonnes of wheat are 
produced globally every year, and while the main producer countries are China, India, USA, and Russia, 
wheat products are consumed across the world (FAO). About two thirds of it are processed into foods, 
20% into feed, while the remaining portion is destined for other uses, including production of biofuel 
(Grote et al., 2020).  
Unfortunately, grave wheat losses are suffered globally due to various reasons. One of the main agents 
putting wheat production at risk are pests and pathogens which affect wheat plants and harvested seeds. 
They can provoke diseases in the plants, which can affect the yield of the cultivations, and decrease the 
quality of the harvest. This phenomenon also reflects negatively on the economy of the agricultural sector, 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
which relies on consistent crop harvesting. According to a recent estimation, wheat losses due to pests 
and pathogens correspond to 21.5% of total yield losses (Savary et al., 2019). Some of the main pathogens 
responsible are fungi, for example Pyrenophora tritici-repentis causing tan spot on the leaves, Fusarium 
like F. graminearum causing head blight, Puccinia graminis causing stem rust, and Magnaporthe grisea 
and M. oryzae causing wheat blast (Periyannan, 2017; He et al., 2022). Fungal and bacterial pathogens 
can determine yearly global losses of about 10.2 Mt of wheat (Oerke, 2006). Nematodes and insects like 
aphids can become wheat pests as well, contributing with other animals to wheat losses of 7.9 Mt every 
year (Oerke, 2006). To combat pests and pathogens in an effective way, chemical formulations have been 
developed that can inhibit these organisms and prevent damage and disease arising in the crops. Similarly, 
unwanted weeds are often the target of agricultural practices, where herbicides are applied to remove 
unwanted plants from the field. In fact, herbicides have been found to be very effective at preventing 
yield losses (Oerke, 2006). However, such chemical pesticides and herbicides can become integrated in 
the environment, and get released into water systems. When these toxic chemicals become incorporated 
into the food chain, higher predators including humans become susceptible to biomagnification, which is 
the accumulation of higher levels of chemicals in the tissues (Drouillard, 2008). 
Besides losses due to pests and pathogen, wheat yield is also affected by climate change. An increase in 
temperature, combined with drought, has been proven to shorten the production season of this crop 
(Hvlacova et al., 2018). In particular these conditions lead to a decrease in shoot and grain mass, a 
decrease in sugar content in the grains, and affect the ability of the plant to efficiently take up water and 
to photosynthesize, decreasing its CO2 assimilation rate (Hlavacova et al., 2018; Shah and Paulsen, 2003). 
When droughts last long periods of time, regions with pre-existing food insecurity can experience famine, 
water crisis, and worsening of agriculture-based communities’ livelihoods and economy. Wheat and maize 
production has been found to suffer significantly from the current climate trends, with as much as 5.5% 
of global net losses compared to potential yields in more stable climatic conditions (Lobell et al., 2011). In 
fact, it is the wide variability of our climate that can work against the recent improvements in yield 
obtained through breeding and technological advances (Daryanto et al., 2016; Lobell et al., 2011). 
Given the described challenges affecting wheat production, it has become of major importance to find 
effective ways of ensuring good yield and plant health in sustainable ways, without harmful pollutants, 
and that are environmentally conscious and scientifically sound. Like other crops, wheat too can be the 
subject of biocontrol applications which can improve the growth and health of wheat plants. Such 
research on potential wheat growth-promoting microorganisms is tightly connected to the understanding 
of the wheat microbiome, and the interactions of this microbiome with specific PGPR.

Aim of study 
 
The aim of this study was to investigate the relationship between plant growth-promotion in wheat and 
its soil microbiome architecture. 
Specifically, a large rhizosphere bacterial community was characterized after successive plant cultivation. 
Plants were repeatedly selected for fast and slow growth to prompt a corresponding selection of 
rhizosphere components. Amplicon sequencing was employed to determine the relative abundance of 
the microbiome components, with an interest in relating specific bacterial groups to plant growth 
parameters. Moreover, smaller bacterial communities were tested in cultivation, to assess their effect on 
various plant parameters in a more direct way.

Materials and Methods 
 
Two different cultivation approaches were taken in this project to investigate the stability of bacterial 
communities in the rhizosphere of wheat, and their potential wheat plant-promoting action.  
The first and main approach will be referred to as “successive cultivation”. Wheat plants were inoculated 
at the very beginning with a large bacterial community composed of 87 strains isolated from the wheat 
rhizosphere. The plants were further cultivated in succession across 6 cycles, where the root microbiome

be referred to as “random communities” and consisted in a single – not successive – cultivation of wheat 
inoculated with smaller randomly-assembled communities of bacteria. The two approaches are depicted

Media 
Liquid 2% LB medium (Lennox-Roth) was used for O/N cultures of all strains employed in the experiments. 
0.5% Hoagland’s solution (MP biomedicals) was administered to soil pellets before cultivation. Lastly, PDA 
(Roth), M9 (Appendix A), Pseudomonas selection medium (Millipore), and LB (Lennox-Roth) media were 
used as substrate with the addition of 1.5% agar (Roth) for bacteria plating. All media were sterilized by 
autoclaving at 121°C before use.

Seed germination and soil sterilization 
For both cultivation approaches, seed germination and soil sterilization were performed in the same way. 
Dry, compressed soil pellets (Grow it - Gardman) were soaked with 0.5% Hoagland’s solution, until 
expanded and fully hydrated. For sterilization, the pellets were autoclaved one time after soaking, then 
again two days later, before planting. Seeds were surface-sterilized in 2% sodium hypochlorite (Cl2) and 
vortexed for 20 min. The tubes were shaken on a vortexer for 20 min, after which the 2% sodium 
hypochlorite solution was extracted. The seeds were washed 5 times by adding MilliQ, vortexing for 10 s, 
and removing the waste liquid. The sterilized seeds were germinated in petri dishes on a piece of filter 
paper soaked with MilliQ. After 2 days of germination the seeds were ready for planting (Figure 5).

petri dishes. On the same day, the soil pellets were soaked with Hoagland’s solution and sterilized by autoclaving. The pellets were 
autoclaved again on Day 3 before planting the seeds after 2 days of germination. Figure made on Biorender. 
 
Successive cultivation 
The successive cultivation experiment lasted 6 weeks and consisted of 6 plant cultivation cycles.  
Cycle 1 included 20 plants, all of which were inoculated with the complete mix of bacteria, while from 
cycle 2 onwards, plants were subdivided into 4 series of 5 plant replicates each. Two series of plants were 
selected for slow growth, while the other two series were selected for fast growth. At the end of each 
cycle, the slowest- or fastest-growing plant was selected from each series, based on daily measurements 
of stem length. The root microbiome extracted from the selected plant of a series was used to inoculate 
a series of plants in the next cycle (Figure 6). All cycles included a series of 5 control replicates without 
added microbiome. Wheat cultivation was carried out in a plant chamber with 16 h of light time at 24°C, 
and 8 h of darkness at 20°C. Finally, the microbiome of all plants of the 6th cycle was determined by 16S 
amplicon sequencing, to be able to compare the bacterial communities established in the rhizosphere of 
slow- versus fast-growing plants.

The cultivation setup 
For each cultivation cycle, a total of 25 plants were grown in 5 cm square plastic pots filled with one 
sterilized soil pellet each. Seed planting was done by placing each germinated seed into a separate pellet 
and covering with soil. Right after planting, the seeds were watered with 10 mL of sterilized tap water. In 
each cycle, 5 of the 25 planted seeds were kept on a separate tray as controls. The remaining 20 plants 
were subdivided into two trays. From cycle 2 onwards the two trays were kept separate to distinguish 
slow growth selection from fast growth selection. The tray setup is described in Figure 7. Tray one 
contained plants that were being selected for fast-growth, while tray 2 contained plants that were being 
selected for slow growth. To keep track of each plant in the system, a plant is designated by whether it 
pertains to the fast or slow tray, the left or right series in the tray, and its replicate letter. For example, 
plant 1-L-C is the third plant replicate of the left (L) series of tray 1.

Cultivation and Inoculation 
All 20 plants of the first cycle were inoculated with the complete mix of 87 bacteria. All bacterial strains 
are listed in Appendix B. Strains A1 to G12 were isolated from the wheat rhizosphere, and had been 
previously identified by 16S V3-V4 region amplicon sequencing. Overnight cultures of each of the 87 
bacteria were grown in LB medium, shaking for 3 days at 28°C. Based on previous OD measurements of 
each strain, the cultures were diluted by slightly different factors to equilibrate all to about OD600=0.1. 
Finally, they were mixed together. 1mL of the complete mix was inoculated into each plant after 2 days 
of plant growth in soil, by pipetting close to the soil where the stem originates. Stem length was measured 
every day from the day of inoculation to identify the plants growing the fastest and slowest. The resulting 
slope of growth, as well as the final net increase in height were considered to select the plants. At the end 
of the first cycle, the two fastest-growing plants and the two slowest-growing plants were selected to 
have their microbiome reinoculated into the second cycle of plants. A schematic representation of a cycle 
is depicted in Figure 8.  
==PAGE:21==

The bacterial extraction from the selected plants was done following the procedure explained in Cordovez 
et al., (2021) with modifications. Plant roots were freed from the surrounding soil and cut to be inserted 
into a 50mL falcon tube containing 10 mL of MgSO4 (Roth) buffer. The tubes were vortexed at high speed 
for 1 min, then sonicated in a water bath sonicator for 2 min, and finally vortexed again for 30 s. The 
solution was filtered through 2 layers of sterile Miracloth (Millipore), into another falcon tube. 1 mL of the 
solution was used for reinoculation of each plant of a series of the new cycle.  
 
DNA extraction and V3-V4 PCR amplification 
At the end of the 6th cycle, bacteria were extracted from the roots of all plants following the procedure 
explained in Cordovez et al. (2021) until the sonication step. DNA was extracted from the liquid solution 
containing root bacteria, as well as from the bulk soil of each plant using the DNeasy Power Soil Pro Kit 
(QIAGEN) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. A control was included by performing DNA extraction 
on MilliQ water. 
A PCR reaction was done on the DNA extracted from the plant roots and bulk soil to amplify the V3 and 
V4 region of the 16S gene of bacteria present in the samples. The reaction mixture was 8.9 μL DNAse free 
water, 12.5 μL TEMPase Hot Start 2X Master Mix, 0.8 μL forward and reverse primers, 0.2 μL DNA 
template.

16S gene data analysis 
The data analysis on 16S amplicon sequences was done in collaboration with PhD. student Xinming Xu 
(BIE group). The sequences were processed on QIIME 2 (Bolyen et al., 2018), where they were 
demultiplexed, then barcodes and primers sequences were removed. The DADA2 implementation was 
used to filter, denoise, and merge the data to achieve better sequence quality (Callahan et al., 2016). The 
refined sequences were assigned taxonomically with pre-trained classifiers based on the SILVA database 
of 16S data (Quast et al., 2013). 
The resulting AVS present in each sample were analyzed with the R package ampvis2 (Andersen et al., 
2018) to visualize their relative abundances, as well as the diversity within and between the bacterial 
communities of different plant series. 
 
Single isolate identification 
At the end of the successive cultivation experiment, samples of microbiomes extracted from the 
rhizosphere of each plant in the 6th cycle were plated on various solid media plates, as an additional way 
to inspect the variety of strains arising in each plant series. Specifically, the rhizosphere samples were 
plated without dilution on PDA agar, M9 agar, Pseudomonas selection agar,  LB agar, and LB agar added 
with erythromycin and lincomycin. For each plant series, including the control plants series, 6 isolates 
were picked from the plates, so that each plant contributed with at least one isolate to the collection. The 
selection was done taking care to choose colonies that had different phenotypes and came from different 
media plates to get a good array of diverse isolates, making up a total collection of 30. Colony-PCR was 
performed on the isolates using forward primers to the 16S gene, as described in Appendix D, whereafter 
the isolates were sequenced by Sanger, and identified taxonomically on NCBI BLAST. 
 
Random communities 
This experiment consisted in a single plant cultivation with the inoculation of a small, randomly-selected 
bacterial community. To gather information on potential correlations between certain soil bacteria and 
faster or slower plant growth, as well as plant health, wheat plants were inoculated with a simpler 
community compared to the complete mix of all strains. The experiment was conducted twice and lasted 
approximately 20 days. Each time, 8 random communities were tested on 5 plant replicates each. 5 
control replicates were included as well, for a total of 45 plants.

Planting and inoculation 
Seeds were germinated and planted in falcon tubes which were filled with 25 mL of soil gathered from 
the sterilized pellets. They were administered with 4 mL of water. All strains were grown in LB medium 
for 3 days, whereafter they were equilibrated to about OD600=0.1 by differential dilutions. Random 
subgroups of the total 88 strains were generated with an online tool, each comprising approximately 11 
strains. The communities were assembled by mixing the cultures accordingly, and each random 
community was used to inoculate 5 plant replicates on the 3rd day after planting. Starting from the day of 
inoculation, the height of each plant was recorded every other day by measuring with a ruler from the 
base of the stem in cm and mm (Figure 9).  
The stem height was recorded for two weeks, at the end of which, the fresh stem weight was measured 
for each plant, as well as the root dry weight. Furthermore, any visible differences in the appearance of 
the plants’ leaves were recorded, such as spotting and dry leaf tips.

Results 
 
Successive cultivation 
The successive cultivation experiment was performed to study the effect of successive rhizosphere re-
inoculations across 6 cycles of wheat cultivation. After each cycle, the microbiomes of the fastest-growing 
and slowest-growing plants were inoculated into the plants of the following cycle. Plant parameters were 
monitored throughout the cultivation, and particularly at the end of the last cycle, to obtain a more 
complete outlook on plant growth in addition to their microbiome. 
Stem length and growth recordings 
The stem length of each plant in the successive cultivation experiment was recorded every day, starting 
from the day of inoculation. The initial stem length of a plant on the day of inoculation was subtracted 
from every following measurement, so as to normalize for any differences in growth occurred before 
inoculation. At the end of each cycle, the normalized measurements were plotted against time to visualize 
the growth pattern of all plants in a series. It was not feasible to calculate the growth rate of each plant, 
due to the irregular shapes of their growth curves, so the selection of the fastest or slowest plant in the 
series was done according to other parameters. These included highest total growth, growth consistency 
in time and final slope shape. The graphs showing plant growth in cycle 4 are displayed in Figure 10 as an 
example. All four plant series are shown, distinguished by whether they were selected for fast or slow 
growth, and left or right side. Days are numbered starting from the day of inoculation, which is Day 0.

From Day 0 onwards, the daily net height increments are plotted. Generally, the growth curve of most 
plants consisted of a relatively steady growth phase during the first 4-5 days after inoculation, while 
around the 6th day most plants experienced a decrease in apparent growth rate, with a smaller height 
increase per day, compared to days 1-5. Between day 5 and day 7, the shape of the curve became more 
variable between different plants, where some plants kept growing similarly to the first days after 
inoculation, while others started showing a slower growth rate. Exceptions to this trend can be found 
exemplified in replicates 1-L-D and 1-R-E in cycle 4.  
When selecting for fast growth, a steeper slope in days 1-5 was prioritized, as well as the maintenance of 
steady fast growth in days 6-7. As an example, in cycle 4, in the left series selected for fast growth, 
replicate 1-L-C was chosen because, despite its slope in days 1-5 being similar to other replicates, it 
showed continued fast growth in days 6-7, while other replicates like 1-L-E slowed down. In the right 
series, replicate 1-R-B was chosen because its slope during days 1-5 was among the highest in the series, 
and this growth was maintained until day 6, whereas replicate 1-R-A started slowing down already on day 
5. On the other hand, when selecting for slow growth, a flatter slope was prioritized, as well as an earlier

start of decrease in daily growth increments. For example, in the left series of cycle 4, replicate 2-L-B was 
selected for slow growth because its slope appeared slightly flatter than other replicates, while in the right 
series, replicate 2-R-B was selected for the same reason, and specifically for a sharper decrease in growth 
between days 6-7 compared to other replicates. 
In general, however, plants grew quite similarly within each series, as well as across series. The growth 
curves often overlapped and were very similar in shape. As is noticeable in the graphs on cycle 4, the total 
net height increase at the end of the cycle was most often between 10 and 14 cm, both in series selected 
for fast and slow growth.

16S amplicon sequencing analysis 
The 16S V3-V4 region of bacteria extracted from bulk soil and rhizosphere of plants at the end of the 6th 
cycle was sequenced. The resulting bacterial communities were characterized through various analytical 
parameters, in order to gather information on any differences in diversity and composition of fast-
selected, slow-selected, and control plants, as well as between bulk soil samples and rhizosphere samples. 
To study the degree of taxonomic diversity present within bulk soil and rhizosphere samples, the alpha 
diversity of bulk and rhizosphere samples was determined. The alpha diversity refers to the species 
diversity found within the bacterial community extracted from the bulk soil or the rhizosphere of a wheat 
plant. Figure 13 shows the measure of alpha diversity in control, fast-selected, and slow-selected plants, 
by distinguishing between bulk samples and rhizosphere samples. It is observed that control plants’ 
microbiomes have a lower degree of alpha diversity, which is very similar between bulk and rhizosphere 
samples. Interestingly, the rhizosphere microbiomes found in fast-selected plants appear to have a higher 
alpha diversity than in bulk soil samples. The same is not the case for slow-selected plants, where bulk soil 
and rhizosphere bacterial samples do not have a significantly different degree of alpha diversity. It must 
be noted that both left and right series have been plotted together for fast-selected and slow-selected 
plants, therefore it is not clear whether the left and right series are significantly different.

Random communities 
This experiment was performed to test the impact of the inoculation of smaller bacterial communities on 
wheat plants. Therefore, it is meant to complement the findings the successive cultivation experiment. 
The experiment involved inoculation of random sub-groups of the complete strain collection, each 
comprising 10-11 strains. It was carried out only twice, due to time restrictions. The two trials will be 
identified as Trial A and Trial B from here onwards. In each trial, the complete mix of bacteria was 
randomly subdivided into 8 subgroups, each of which was inoculated into 5 plant replicates. Therefore, 
each trial included subgroups numbered 1 to 8, and a control series of 5 replicates with no inoculation. 
The stem length of all plants was recorded every other day for 2 weeks, and finally the stem fresh weight 
and root dry weight were measured for each plant. 
The growth pattern of the plants in this experiment largely resembled what was observed for the 
successive cultivation experiment. Consequently, the first parameter of comparison between plant series

Discussion 
 
Scientific principle 
The motivation behind this study was to identify any collection of different bacteria, which could form a 
stable microbiome able to promote wheat growth. Therefore, it was fundamentally important to choose 
a consistent method of assessing plant growth or well-being, by identifying a plant parameter to monitor 
in cultivation. Wheat is known to be vulnerable to a variety of diseases and pathogens which affect the 
plant in ways that are often visible, such as yellow spots and wilting leaves. Other factors can affect wheat 
growth and health as well, such as mineral nutrients present in the soil, moisture content, temperature, 
and light exposure. However, these variables are typically studied with respect to their effect on wheat 
yield, which is naturally the most desirable feature in agriculture. Yield can only be measured directly at 
the time of harvest or in the later stages of cultivation, by counting the number of seeds per head or spike 
(Mergoum et al., 2009). Another measure of yield, or rather of seed quality, is the thousand-kernel weight, 
which is the weight in grams of exactly 1000 randomly-sampled kernels (Wang et al., 2012). Generally, 
some wheat varieties might produce a higher number of smaller kernels per head, while others might 
produce a lower number of bigger kernels per head. The limitation of these parameters is that they can 
only be observed towards the end of wheat cultivation when the spikes develop. In this project, it was not 
possible to grow the plants until the formation of spikes, due to time restrictions and to the very nature 
of the experiment which involved multiple cultivations in succession. Therefore, a different parameter 
was needed in order to evaluate plant health and potential yield, which could be measured at a much 
earlier stage in cultivation. Still, there is very limited information on any such parameters, and seedlings 
growth patterns are poorly defined. In the absence of a defined parameter, the variable to be monitored 
was chosen based on observations of a trial wheat cultivation. Since no plants showed any visible 
differences in the color or shape of their leaves and stem, the parameter which was chosen was stem 
length, meaning the height of the plant from the base of the stem to the tip of the tallest leaf. The 
advantage of this measure, compared to root or stem weight, for example, is that it allows monitoring of 
the plant in time without killing it. The significance of stem length as a measure of plant well-being is not 
straight-forward. Specifically, it is not clear whether a plant which grows faster in its seedling stage will 
give rise to a better or worse yield at harvest. For this reason, in this project a link may be drawn between 
‘stem growth-based plant selection’ and certain features of the plant microbiome, but it cannot be 
concluded whether the overall microbial composition of fast-selected plants is more beneficial to yield or 
plant health than the one of slow-selected plants. In fact, the growth rate of wheat plants has been studied 
to affect yield in a multitude of ways. A plant which grows too fast could experience stress and not develop 
properly; additionally, wheat varieties that are taller are more subject to lodging, which affects yield 
negatively (Mergoum et al., 2009). For this reason, shorter varieties are sometimes preferred. It must be 
stressed that the growth period studied in this project corresponds to the very first stages in wheat plant 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
growth, so, with the limited information available, it is not possible to know how the growth rate of wheat 
seedlings reflects on the rest of the growth period of the plant, or on its yield. 
Even though stem length can be an appropriate variable to monitor, it is apparent that selection of 
microbiomes of fast or slow growing plants did not give rise to the expected difference in stem growth 
between fast and slow-selected series. In general, plants grew very similarly across cycles and series, and 
did not display any other significant visual differences, such as leaf color. Having observed this, it was 
hypothesized that the growth conditions of the wheat plants in the experiments were already favourable 
enough, such that no substantial improvements to their health could come from their relationship with 
rhizobacteria. In other words, the plants might not have needed to form specific interactions with their 
soil microbiome, since their growth conditions were already too optimal. Instead, introducing a selection 
pressure in the form of a specific sub-optimal condition, could make the plant more dependent on its 
microbiome, prompting it to form tighter relationship with beneficial bacteria. This approach would result 
in a microbiome that is more directly related to the plant’s needs. With such a setup, the same parameter, 
stem length, or a different parameter could be recorded as appropriate, linking it back to the microbiome 
composition in a more direct way. 
 
Observations on cultivation 
The cultivation setup itself can be the subject of various considerations that are relevant for the 
reproducibility of the experiment. The first, and most apparent throughout the cultivations, is the 
presence of fungi growing on the surface of the soil. This was observed for the first time in a first trial cycle 
of the successive cultivation experiment, on several plants placed on separate trays. Since fungi were not 
the subject of the study, the successive cultivation experiment was started again, to try to eliminate this 
variable from the setup. However, these molds arose again, relatively early on in the experiment, so it 
became apparent that they would be involved in the cultivation setup throughout the cycles. It is plausible 
that they were endophytic fungi which were present inside the seed and spread to the soil. Endophytic 
fungi are in fact commonly inherited vertically across plant generations through the seeds (Hardoim et al. 
2015). Therefore, the fungi observed in cultivation may have firstly come from inside the seeds, and 
secondly fungal spores may easily have been carried over to the next cycle through the rhizosphere re-
inoculation process. To support this, the fungal growth did not seem to appear on other kinds of plants 
present in the plant chamber at the same time, while it did appear on the soil of wheat plants in the 
random communities approach as well. To mitigate and prevent the growth of fungi, it was taken care not 
to overwater the plants or otherwise increase the humidity in the plant chamber, which seemed to be 
effective. Despite this, mold was observed on the soil of several plants in every cycle. It was not possible 
to identify the fungus, or its effect on wheat. Its presence constituted a variable that was potentially 
relevant to monitor, in regards to plant-microbiome interactions. In the span of this project, this variable 
was not considered, due to the lack of information available on the fungus and limited experiments and 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
cultivations that could be carried out. Upon considerations, it was suggested to apply an antifungal 
compound to prevent growth of the fungus completely. However, it could not be studied how the addition 
of an antifungal would impact the inoculated soil bacteria, any endophytes present in the plant, or the 
plant itself. In fact, such fungi with tight interactions with plants could even be beneficial to plant health. 
Therefore, no antifungal was prescribed in cultivation. 
Another factor which proved to be difficult to keep in balance was the need for waterings. In the 
successive cultivation experiment, plants were watered every 2-3 days according to the apparent dryness 
of the soil. To mitigate any differences in waterings, the plants were watered by pouring sterilized tap 
water into the bottom of the tray and letting the soil in the pots soak it up. An additional reason for not 
watering from the top of the soil was to minimize the risk of altering the textural composition of the soil 
or causing any displacement of bacteria towards the bottom of the pellet. However, it was unclear 
whether bottom watering was effective at providing homogeneous moisture to the soil. Moreover, any 
excess moisture quickly led to fungi growing on the soil, so for this reason, it was decided to water more 
often by smaller amounts. Importantly, plants in the growth chamber seemed to dry up faster depending 
on their location in the chamber. Despite consistently displacing the trays, some plants appeared to need 
water more often than others. This was especially the case in the random communities experiment. 
Therefore, plants were occasionally watered individually to prevent drought stress. Of course, uneven 
moisture was a variable that might have affected the microbiome of plants, by posing stress on the plants 
and on the microbiome components. However, the present cultivation setup did not account for this 
variable. 
Lastly, while most steps in cultivation were performed sterilly, including germination, planting, DNA 
extraction, and inoculation, others could not be. Plants had to be transferred once to and from the plant 
chamber in a non-sterile way, and the plant chamber environment itself is not meant to be sterile, despite 
being closed most of the time. Moreover, the plants’ stem length was measured outside the growth 
chamber, though with a sterilized ruler. Due to the described conditions, the air-borne contamination 
could not be prevented in all steps of the experiment. However, it was considered to be a minor variable, 
since a small amount of contamination from the air would likely not affect the rhizosphere microbiome of 
the wheat plants in a fundamental way, especially considering the high abundance of inoculated 
community in comparison. Nevertheless, the possibility of such contamination could be taken into 
account by introducing an additional series of control replicates consisting of sterilized pellets treated and 
handled in the same way, but without growing a plant in them. Any bacteria found in such pellets at the 
end of the experiment would be considered contamination, which could be accounted for, by comparison 
with the microbiomes found in the rhizosphere and bulk samples of wheat plants. 
 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
Strain growth imbalances 
The 87 bacterial strains which were inoculated at the start of the successive cultivation experiment were 
all grown overnight in LB medium and subsequently tested for their OD. While the majority of strains grew 
to a similar OD, some appeared to grow to a lesser extent. It is important to note that comparing the OD 
of different strains should not be considered an infallible way of comparing their growth. In fact, it is not 
known the extent to which other components of the bacterial culture, apart from live cells, like biofilm 
components, might impact the OD measurement in different strains. However, considering the high 
number of strains employed in this study, equilibrating the OD was deemed to be an adequate mode of 
comparison to attempt to homogenize the initial cell concentration of strains before inoculation into soil. 
Despite this initial normalization for OD at the start of the 1st cycle, it is possible that any differences in 
growth pattern of strains might have impacted their performance in the soil, such as their ability to 
compete with other strains to acquire space on the roots, and thereby their stability in the rhizosphere 
and their nutrient acquisition from the roots. 
 
Successive cultivation 
The successive cultivation experiment was targeted at developing the wheat microbiome according to the 
repeated selection of a plant parameter across cultivation cycles. The key questions posed by this study 
are, firstly, whether the continued selection of a plant parameter was reflected in a significant difference 
in that parameter at the end of the experiment; and secondly, whether the successive reinoculation of  
rhizosphere microbiomes of the selected plants resulted in a specific bacterial distribution and 
composition in the last cycle. 
The wheat plants were selected according to their apparent growth pattern, to identify plant replicates 
which grew the fastest and the slowest. It was thereby hypothesized that the consistent selection of 
faster-growing plants could result in faster growth of the plants in the last cycle, and opposite for slow-
growth selection. Achieving this result would be very valuable, since the intended application of this kind 
of studies is the improvement of plant yield in agriculture, where plant-growth promotion is a crucial 
aspect. In this work, it appears that the plant series selected for fast growth through successive 
reinoculations did not produce plants which grew faster than the ones resulting from slow-growth 
selection. In fact, the opposite is true for the right slow-selected series, which grew to a significantly larger 
extent than one of the fast-selected series, as well as the control series. Therefore, it is not clear whether 
plant selection had a corresponding effect on the measured wheat growth. A higher number of plant 
replicates may be necessary to better evaluate this. 
To address the second question, it may first be relevant to observe that the alpha diversity of fast-selected 
series was higher than in both the slow-selected series and the control, especially for rhizosphere samples. 
Lower alpha diversity in the rhizosphere may be related to disease conditions in plants, as described by 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
Wei et al. in cotton (Wei et al., 2021). In the combined alpha-diversity of bulk and rhizosphere samples, it 
is likely that the bulk samples, which were shown to be more homogeneous between series, may have 
inhibited any large differences present between the rhizosphere samples, making the left and right series 
of fast-selected plants, as well as slow-selected plants, more similar between each other. As it appeared 
from the NMDS plot of bulk versus rhizosphere samples, there was no fundamental difference between 
the two loci, bulk and rhizosphere, in terms of their bacterial composition, across series. However, a 
statistical analysis revealed that the locus variable, i.e. whether the sample came from bulk soil or 
rhizosphere, was indeed significant in determining the sample’s microbiome composition. Therefore, it 
would be appropriate to investigate further what factors determine such differences, and the extent to 
which certain bacterial groups contribute to this effect. While the most abundant bacterial taxa, such as 
Paenibacillus, were very similar between bulk and rhizosphere samples, a few differences are worth 
noting. Firstly, Rahnella, which is highly abundant in the bulk samples of all series, as well as in most 
rhizosphere sample series, is lacking in the rhizosphere of slow-selected plants. Rahnella aquatilis was 
among the inoculated strains isolated from wheat, but it could not be determined whether it may 
additionally be a wheat endophyte. Thus, it would be necessary to conduct a deeper analysis on the 
relationship between Rahnella and wheat to understand what prompts this bacterium to become 
associated with its roots. Conversely, Pantoea was firmly established in the bulk and rhizosphere of all 
series, apart from the bulk soil of fast-selected series. Therefore, it may be the case that fast-selected 
plants can secure stronger interactions with Pantoea, determining the low abundance of this bacterium 
in the bulk soil. Moreover, Pantoea is known to be a wheat endophyte, which may support the occurrence 
of a high rhizosphere abundance compared to bulk soil (Díaz Herrera et al., 2016). Bacillus seems to be 
equally established in the bulk and rhizosphere of fast-selected series, while it is relatively lacking in the 
rhizosphere of slow-selected plants. It is possible that slow-selected plants did not recruit this bacterium 
to the roots as much as other bacterial groups. The relatively low abundance of Bacillus in general might 
reflect the fact that only three B. subtilis isolates were inoculated at the beginning. The same reasoning 
may be made on the Rhizobium group. The lack of these two groups in the control plants suggests that 
their relative abundance in the other series is a more direct reflection of inoculation and subsequent plant 
interaction. Many Pseudomonas isolates were among the inoculated strains, which may have led to expect 
a high relative abundance of this group in the samples. Strikingly, however, it did not get established in 
the rhizosphere nor in bulk soil remotely as much as other groups did. Considering that the inoculated 
strains were actually isolated from wheat rhizosphere, it seems quite contradicting to report a low 
abundance of Pseudomonas across all series. It is possible that other dynamics and interactions within the 
microbiome resulted in this discrepancy, therefore it would be very interesting to perform tests on 
bacteria-bacteria interactions, as well as plant root attachment assays, to delve deeper into the 
characterization of such an important biocontrol group. 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
A higher relative abundance of Pullulanibacillus and Saccharimonadales distinguish the bulk soil of slow-
selected and fast-selected series, respectively, which may represent a significant factor differentiating the 
bulk microbiome of the two selection lineages. On the other hand, Rahnella and Nocardioides are 
particularly established in the rhizosphere samples of fast-selected series compared to slow-selected 
ones, which may be a key differentiating factor between these series in the rhizosphere. In fact, while bulk 
microbiomes appear to be dissimilar between fast-selected and slow-selected series, as shown on an 
NMDS plot, the rhizosphere communities seem to diverge even more. This may be connected to the 
higher alpha diversity present specifically in the rhizosphere of fast-selected plants. The rhizosphere is the 
area of the soil where microorganisms interact the most with plant roots. Therefore, there may be a more 
effective selection of bacteria in the rhizosphere, resulting in a stronger differentiation between fast-
selected and slow-selected plants in this locus. The fast versus slow parameter was in fact found to be 
significant at determining the composition of the microbiomes of the two selection lineages. 
It was not possible to determine whether the left and right series of fast- and slow-selected plants were 
significantly different between each other. However, they displayed a few discrepancies in relative 
abundance of taxa. This was especially observed between the two slow-selected series, both in bulk soil 
and rhizosphere. Here, several taxa have a relative abundance that is different between left and right side 
by a factor of up to 6. Examples are Paenibacillus, Nocardioides and Microbacteriaceae in bulk soil; while 
in the rhizosphere Curtobacterium, Pseudomonas, and Herbaspirillum represent the most striking 
difference in relative abundance between the left and right series. Instead, the two fast-selected series 
display smaller disparities in relative abundance of each taxa, up to a factor of 2, with the exception of 
Pantoea in bulk soil. To sum up, the same selection method on two plant series may have given rise to 
significantly different microbiomes, especially for slow-selection. However, it is not clear how selection 
actually affected the composition of the microbiome. Therefore, differences may have arisen 
unpredictably, especially since the selection pressure on the plants was likely not strong enough. More 
cultivation experiments on separate sets of plant replicates should be done to understand whether 
selection of the same parameter consistently results in a similar microbiome. Alternatively, it is possible 
that multiple different community architectures may be associated with the same recorded plant 
parameters, so that a different bacterial distribution can arise every time the same experiment is carried 
out. 
Single isolate selection and sequencing indicated that a few bacterial taxa may be much more dominant 
than others across all plant series. These are Pantoea, Rahnella, Pseudomonas, and Paenibacillus. 
Interestingly, these groups appeared in control plants, both in single isolate identification and in 16S 
amplicon analysis. Therefore, it seems that their presence is particularly tied to the plant, more than a 
result of inoculation. Upon further research, these groups were identified as wheat endophytes, with the 
exception of Rahnella (Tamosiune et al., 2017; Díaz Herrera et al., 2016; Ahlawat et al., 2022). Endophytes 
have been found to be transferred vertically across plant generations through the seed. However, the 
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
extent of this carry-over has been studied to be largely variable in different seed samples (Robinson et al., 
2016). Robinson et al. found that majority of the endophytic bacterial load of wheat seeds is carried in the 
seed coat, crease, and endosperm, while the embryo does not house many endophytes. This imbalance 
is possibly due to restrictions posed by the wheat anatomy itself, which partly prevents endophytes from 
reaching the tissues where kernels form (Robinson et al., 2016). Consequently, sterilizing the surface of 
the seeds before germination and planting may have prevented the proliferation of a considerable portion 
of endophytes which could have populated the rhizosphere. Nevertheless, it is apparent that these 
endophytic groups got firmly established in the microbiome studied in this experiment. 
An alternative explanation to the high abundance of strains which are known to be endophytes in wheat 
may lie in the experimental process itself, specifically in performing successive reinoculation across cycles. 
By inoculating the plants of the next cycle with the rhizosphere microbiome of the former cycle, it is likely 
that any endophytes present were carried over to the next plant through inoculation, where they would 
be added to any pre-existing endophytes inside the seed of plants of the next cycle. In this way, 
endophytes might have accumulated throughout the successive cultivation, in a more intensive way 
compared to other non-endophytic strains. Therefore the high relative abundance of some strains may 
be the result of this phenomenon, and not simply a reflection of their ability to be more dominant in the 
microbiome. An analysis of the seed microbiome of wheat should be done in order to identify any 
endophytic strains which are prevalent inside the seeds. With this information, it would be possible to 
attribute a higher relative abundance of a bacterial group to just better stability in the rhizosphere, or 
rather to a combination of stability and accumulation. 
 
Random communities 
The total stem growth of plant series inoculated with different subgroups, as well as the control series, 
was generally homogeneous. This is in accordance with the observations from the successive cultivation 
approach, where plants largely grew similarly across series. Therefore, no direct relationship can be drawn 
between the different bacterial subgroups and the growth pattern of the plant. 
Instead, upon examination of the stem over root weight ratio, there appears to be more variation 
between plant series, compared to the total stem weight measure. While it may be expected that plants 
of the same species develop their stem and roots similarly, this might not be the case when they are 
inoculated with different rhizobacteria, which may promote or hinder plant growth in different ways. 
Therefore, the ratio of stem and root weight could indicate whether the inoculated bacterial community 
might favor the growth of the stem, reporting a higher ratio, or favor the development of the root system 
over the stem, with a lower ratio. Though, the significance of a more or less developed root or stem could 
not be studied in the span of this work. More information should be gathered on these parameters to 
evaluate what is most desirable for wheat growth-promotion. 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
Considering the various factors in this experiment which may have affected plants during cultivation, such 
as uneven drought stress and irregular growth time, it is not yet reasonable to associate certain subgroups 
with a different plant-promoting property. The experiment should be carried out more times with the 
introduction of a stress factor. This stressor may stimulate the plant to interact more with its bacterial 
community, highlighting any differences in plant growth-promotion potential between bacterial groups. 
 
Future perspectives  
One key issue with the cultivation may have affected the final results by a larger extent than others. That 
is, the lack of a selection pressure strong enough to effectively drive the coupled selection of plants and 
their soil microbiome. Therefore, the experiment could be repeated with the introduction of a specific 
condition which can pose stress on the wheat plants. One such condition may be higher salinity in the soil. 
Plants need to be equipped with response mechanisms to salt stress in order to survive in arid and semi-
arid environments (Bharti et al., 2013). When the plant itself is not adapted to dealing with these stressors, 
it may seek to be supported by its soil microbiome. Singh and Prabhat demonstrated that S. maltophilia 
can promote wheat growth in high salinity conditions, as tested on several wheat parameters: shoot and 
root length, plant fresh weight and dry weight, and chlorophyll content (Singh and Jha, 2017). Monitoring 
some of these plant parameters would not be compatible with successive cultivation and reinoculations, 
since they involve killing or disrupting the plants. Nevertheless, increasing salinity may be effective for 
selecting a microbiome which can in fact promote plant growth by supporting wheat stress response. An 
alternative stress factor to introduce in wheat cultivation is drought. Climate change is causing more 
intense and longer drought periods in many regions in the world, which gravely affects food security in 
these areas (Lesk et al., 2016). A variety of rhizobacteria, including Pseudomonas, Bacillus, and 
Achromobacter species have been found to support plant growth and its yield for several crops under 
drought stress, by testing plant parameters like root and shoot weight, and seed yield (Bhattacharyya et 
al., 2021). Si et al. were able to obtain a bacterial community with drought-enriched bacterial components 
on wheat roots, by imposing drought stress in cultivation. Actinobacteria, specifically Streptomyces 
became enriched in the study (Si et al., 2021). Cultivating wheat in drought conditions would be done by 
regulating scarcer waterings. However, it should be tested what watering pattern is most appropriate, 
and what plant parameters to monitor to measure the effect of water scarcity on the plants. 
Introducing a stress factor in cultivation may strengthen the link between plant growth-promotion and 
the rhizosphere microbiome. When this is the case, it is expected that certain bacterial taxa would become 
especially significant in determining the community composition of the soil microbiome. It would be very 
insightful to identify these key bacterial groups as an additional step in data analysis. This is typically 
visualized as a NMDS or PcoA plot showing the extent of dissimilarity between samples, complemented 
with vectors representing important taxa which are most determinant towards the composition of the 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
clusters of samples on the map. Thereafter, further cultivation tests may be done with the single 
inoculation of the selected bacteria, to assess their effect on wheat plants in a more direct way. 
Additionally, the interactions between key bacterial groups should be investigated more in depth, to 
understand their dynamics, and study whether they could constitute a functional community in the 
absence of the wheat plant. 
Lastly, it would be interesting to analyze the microbial composition of all series in the intermediate cycles, 
from cycle 1 to cycle 5. In this way, it may be possible to follow the development of the microbiome across 
plant generations, and evaluate the changes in relative abundance of the various bacterial groups across 
re-inoculation cycles, as was done by Cordovez et al. in tomato (Cordovez et al., 2021). Such analysis would 
help to better determine the stability of the developing microbiome.

Conclusion 
 
In this work, the development of a wheat growth-promoting microbiome was studied.  
An experiment involving six successive wheat cultivations and rhizosphere re-inoculations, revealed the 
stability of different bacterial taxa in the rhizosphere and bulk soil. In particular, it was shown that 
continued selection of fast-growing versus slow-growing plants was determinant in producing different 
microbiomes. According to their relative abundance, especially in the rhizosphere, a few bacterial groups 
appeared to distinguish fast-selected plants over slow-selected ones. These are Rahnella, Bacillus, 
Rhizobium, and Nocardioides. However, it could not be determined just how significant these groups are 
in differentiating the microbiome of fast- and slow-selected plants. When comparing the two parallel lines 
of slow-selection, their microbiomes seem to have developed considerably differently between each 
other. In particular, the relative abundance of bacterial groups including Curtobacterium, Pseudomonas, 
and Herbaspirillum especially differentiates the rhizosphere microbiome of the two slow-selected series. 
Few bacterial taxa were generally highly abundant in all plants, including Pantoea, and Paenibacillus. Their 
establishment may be favored, since they are known wheat endophytes which may be present inside the 
seed. It cannot be concluded whether a specific microbiome has determined a more beneficial effect on 
plant growth. Moreover, there was no consistent effect of plant selection on stem growth itself. 
Furthermore, smaller bacterial communities were tested in wheat cultivation, by measuring various plant 
parameters. No definitive evaluations could be made on which communities may promote better wheat 
growth, also due to irregular factors in cultivation which may have affected plant growth. 
Few key modifications to the experiment could enhance its scientific value. Most importantly, the 
introduction of a selection pressure would better stimulate the plant to interact with specific bacteria. 
Overall, the described cultivation approaches represent promising techniques to investigate the 
development of plant growth-promoting bacterial communities.

Appendix D 
 
Protocol for Colony-PCR: 
The following buffers were used:  
- 
Buffer 1: 25 mM NaOH, 0.2 mM EDTA at pH 12. 
- 
Buffer 2: 40 mM Tris-HCl at pH 7.5 
 
1. 30 μL of O/N culture were added in PCR tubes with 50 μL of Buffer 1. 
2. Tubes were boiled at 95°C for 30min, then cooled down. 
3. The tubes were centrifuged to separate cell debris from nucleic acid. 
4. The supernatant was extracted to be used as DNA template. 
The reaction mixture was 8.9 μL DNAse free water, 12.5 μL TEMPase Hot Start 2X Master Mix, 0.8 μL 
forward primer, 0.2 μL DNA template. The forward primer used is: 5’-CCTACGGGNGGCWGCAG-3’. The 
PCR program was: 95°C denaturation 15 min, 30x cycles of 95°C denaturation 30s, 62°C annealing 30 s, 
72°C extension 30s, followed by 5 min extension at 72°C and cooling to 4°C.
