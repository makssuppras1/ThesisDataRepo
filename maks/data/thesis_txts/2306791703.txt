Abstract 
 
This report is the outcome of the author’s thesis project “Sustainable design with respect to LCA 
using parametric design and BIM tools” which was part of the STED program and in collaboration 
with vandkunsten architectural studio. Aim of this project was to find ways of integrating LCA 
better in the design process through the use of BIM. The projects particularly focused on how to 
render LCA a design tool which is not only used in the end of the design for assessing the 
environmental impacts, but during all the design stages in order to provide instant feedback on 
design choices. More specifically the use of integrated dynamic models was investigated using 
Revit as a design tool and Dynamo as a Visual Programming Language.  
The main dynamic model that was developed consists of Revit, Dynamo and an external LCI 
database maintained in an excel sheet. The dynamic model’s main advantage is the constant link 
between the Revit material library and the LCI database, avoiding the materials matching every 
time a new LCA has to be performed. The dynamic model produces results in a material level 
which means that analyses can be done also in construction type level and construction category 
level giving the designers more ways to perform hotspot analyses. The results are exported in an 
excel sheet where charts are produced automatically for any life cycle stage, impact category, 
material, construction type or construction category. The results are also communicated visually 
in the BIM model where the elements are color-coded according to their impacts per m2 or pcs.  
Moreover, an additional dynamic model was developed for use in the pre-conceptual stage of the 
design when a geometrical model is not yet established. This model is based on an excel sheet 
where the known preliminary data are added and Dynamo which then performs a rough 
estimation of the environmental impacts. 
The two dynamic models were tested in real projects provided by vandkunsten studio and also 
compared with other commercial LCA tools. The case studies showed that the main dynamic 
model gives seems to give valid results and in a quite less time compared to the commercial tools, 
but further investigation and improvements need to be done. The pre-conceptual stage model 
did not seem to give an accurate estimation of the final impacts but it can still be used for 
comparison purposes, while further improvements could render it more accurate.

1 Introduction 
The Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry has many times been criticized for 
lack of control and management of carbon emissions (Wong et al. 2013) while recent studies have 
shown that buildings are responsible for 30-40% of the energy consumption in Europe (Zabalza 
Bribián et al. 2009) and 40-50% of the greenhouse gas produced globally (Abd Rashid & Yusoff 
2015). Increased awareness on the fact has led to the conduction of studies on how to reduce the 
environmental impacts of buildings. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) which is one of the methods that 
have been developed and used to quantify the environmental impacts of a process or product 
(European Commission -- Joint Research Centre -- Institute for Environment and Sustainability 
2010) is used in the building sector since 1990 in order to assess the environmental impacts of 
buildings (Ortiz et al. 2009). On the other hand, the building industry continuously aims for cost 
reduction and simultaneous increase of the projects’ quality (Tao & Tam 2013). Therefore 
methods that can lead to more sustainable design of buildings while assisting in keeping the total 
budget low are of high interest in the last few years.  
Various tools and methods have been developed till today that can assess a building’s 
environmental impacts (Liu et al. 2015) but none is yet produced to assist the architects and 
engineers to make the right choices in the early design stage of a building. Considering that the 
early stage of a building design is the stage when environmental matters are best arranged and 
given the fact that making good design decisions at the initial stage plays an important role in 
reducing a project’s cost and completion time, the development of such a tool is considered 
necessary. The emerge of Building Information Modeling (BIM) concept, the wide use of it in the 
AEC industry and the benefits of its use in respects with the information enrichment and visual 
representation of a building (Eastman 2011), dictates that such a tool should also be able to be 
integrated and interact with the most popular BIM viewers in the AEC industry. 
This project aims to develop integrated dynamic models (Negendahl 2015) that will enable the 
design team to assess the environmental impacts of their design from the early stages of the 
design through the end of it. The developed models are built to function as design tools for the 
design team and offer useful feedback regarding the environmental impacts. 
The main dynamic model developed in this project consists of Revit (Autodesk 2016), Dynamo 
(Autodesk 2015) and an external LCI database. The results produced in this dynamic model are in 
a material level and exported in an excel sheet enabling the analysts perform hotspot analyses in 
a material level, construction type level or construction category level.

2 Life Cycle Assessment in construction 
Knowledge around LCA is considered granted for the readers of this report and thus a very brief 
overview is given in the methodology itself. This chapter is focusing on how LCA is used in the 
construction industry and on identifying this project’s research question. 
LCA is a standardized methodology for assessing the environmental impacts of a product or 
system, that considers its entire life cycle, from raw materials extraction, through product 
manufacturing, to the use of the product and the end of life treatment and disposal (International 
Organization for Standardization 2006). An illustration of the stages and flows considered in LCA 
is shown in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: LCA flows and stages. 
The conduction of an LCA consists of four stages, the Goal and Scope Definition, the Inventory 
Analysis, the Impact Assessment and the Interpretation (International Organization for 
Standardization 2006). In the Goal and scope Definition, the intended application is made clear as 
well as the reasons for carrying the study and the system’s boundaries and the functional unit. 
The Life Cycle Inventory Analysis is the stage where all data about the inputs and outputs of the 
system are collected. In the Life Cycle Impact Assessment, the potential environmental impacts 
are calculated and are associated with specific impact categories. In the Life Cycle Interpretation, 
the results are analyzed and evaluated in order to be consistent with the defined goal and scope. 
After the interpretation, the results should be able to lead to useful conclusions and provide 
feedback. 
Just like for a lot of products, LCA is also used for assessing the environmental impacts of buildings. 
Its use might not be well-spread around the industry yet, but it is the main tool used to declare 
the low environmental impacts of a building. Its use is mandatory when it comes to sustainability 
certification systems one of which is analyzed in Paragraph 2.1. 
2.1 Sustainability rating systems (DGNB) 
The ongoing raising of interest in sustainability in buildings and other constructions led to the 
creation of sustainability certification organizations target of which is the sustainability

assessment of construction and the issue of a sustainability certifications according to the

The most popular among these rating systems are BREEAM(Building Research Establishment 
2015), LEED(U.S. Green Building Council 2016) and DGNB(German Sustainable Building Council 
(DGNB) 2016). BREEAM which was developed by the Building Research Establishment in the UK 
in 1990 is the oldest established method for sustainability assessment of buildings. LEED was 
developed by the US Green Building Council in 1994 while DGNB was established in 2007 by the 
homonymous organization in Germany. 
For the purpose of this project only the DGNB system will be further analyzed since it is considered 
the most popular among the three systems in Denmark. Its approach to LCA will be especially 
analyzed since it is of high interest in this specific report. 
The DGNB system is a certification system for buildings and urban districts in respect to specific 
criteria related to sustainability. Such criteria can be related to the key aspects of ecology, 
economy, socio-cultural aspects, technology, process work flows and site (German Sustainable 
Building Council (DGNB) 2016) (Figure 2).  
 
Figure 2: Key aspects of the DGNB Certification Assessment (German Sustainable Building Council (DGNB) 2016) 
The DGNB system was developed by the German Sustainable Building Council (DGNB) which is 
part of the World Green Building Council’s network (WorldGBC 2016). Aim of the World Green 
Building Council is to support and organize the individual Green Building Councils in each country 
and connect them “to a network of knowledge, inspiration and practical support” (WorldGBC 
2016). This way, more and more Green Building Councils around the world have adopted the 
DGNB system in order to evaluate the sustainability of buildings in the respective countries. The 
Green Building Council Denmark (Green Building Council Denmark 2016) has adapted the DGNB 
system and promotes it in Denmark. 
The DGNB system leads to award of the DGNB certificate in bronze, silver, gold and platinum 
according to the level that the criteria are met. Furthermore, a pre-certification option is available

2.1.1 LCA data 
As far as the data used for performing the LCA Analysis is concerned, each country has set its own 
restrictions. Specifically for Denmark, the Green Building Council dictates that these data should 
be verified LCA data from Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs)(EPD International AB 
2014)(International Organization for Standardization 2015) of specific products. In the cases that 
these data are not available for some materials, then the generic data from databases should be 
used. In this case ESUCO and Oekobaudat databases are allowed. The ESUCO database is based 
on European averages for various construction products and building related data. Oekobaudat is 
the German national database and it is based on German data. The data sources should be used 
according to the following priority sequence(Danish Green Building Council 2014), 
 
Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) 
 
ESUCO database 
 
Oekobau.dat database 
Other than these, other specific datasets can also be used if they are first approved according to 
EN ISO 14025 and EN 15804. 
As for the life cycle stages that these data have to include, are the material extraction, 
transportation and manufacturing for the production stage, the material replacements and the 
energy use for the use stage and the recycling, disposal or reuse of materials for the end-of-life 
stage. 
2.1.2 Building elements to be assessed 
The building elements that have to be taken under consideration in the LCA are: 
1. Foundation 
2. Exterior walls 
3. Interior walls 
4. Stairs, ramps, balconies, ceilings 
5. Roofs 
6. Heating, cooling and ventilation (excl. pipes, wires, ducts and other installations) 
7. Other building installations 
8. Other equipment with significant energy consumption in the use phase 
 
2.1.3 LCA Results Evaluation 
To evaluate the environmental impacts of a building, the Danish Green Building Council has set a 
ruleset with which a comparison is performed between the examined building’s impacts and 
those of a predefined reference building. The comparison finally defines the points that the 
examined building earns in each category which are then weighted with the weighting factors to 
be summed to the total DGNB system score. This evaluation system has some slight differences 
depending on if the building is an office building or a residential building. In this paragraph the 
system for evaluating office buildings will be described.

The results for each impact category are compared to the reference building’s ones and a score is 
a assigned for each impact category after linear interpolation according to the table in Figure 4

Then, the score of each impact category is multiplied with the weighting factor as presented in

The same process is followed for two standard life expectancies of the building, 50 and 80 years 
(50 and 120 years for residential buildings). The 50 years results will in the end count for the 70% 
of the final results, while the 80 years results will count for the rest 30 %. Adding the weighted 
score per impact category for these two life cycles, the total score in 100 points scale is finally 
calculated. This score will count for the 35% of the Environmental Criteria area, which counts for 
22.5 % of the very total DGNB Certification score.

2.1.4 LCA impacts calculation 
The Danish Green Building Council has developed a spreadsheet in which all the LCAs of the 
assessed buildings should be performed. The ESUCO and Oekobau.dat databases are already 
included in the spreadsheet. The user has to input all the building components quantities and link 
them with an existing material from the databases. The quantities have of course to be input in 
the same unit as the database’s material to be linked (m, m2, m2, items). Also, each material’s life 
expectancy is input manually for the number of replacements to be calculated. 
 
2.2 Current LCA methods 
The recognition of LCA as a factor of sustainability assessment of buildings has led to the 
increasing use of it in the construction industry. The way it is usually performed though differs 
from LCAs performed for other products. The fact that buildings are not industrialized products 
in their majority makes a detailed LCA hard and time consuming. For this reason, in most of the 
cases these assessments are based on a material database that contains various generic materials’ 
impacts per specific quantity unit. These impacts are then multiplied with the quantities of the 
construction’s materials to give the total impacts of the construction. An illustration of this 
principle is shown in Figure 5.

For this reason, more sophisticated and expensive tools like GaBi (thinkstep 2016) and SimaPro 
(PRÉ Consultants 2016) are not widely used in the construction industry. Instead, more simplified 
tools that follow the above mentioned principle with the database and the simple multiplication

DGNB provides its own excel based tool for performing the LCA of the studied building. In this

perform simple and fast LCAs of buildings. A more sophisticated tool though which is based in the 
same principle is LCAbyg (Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut Aalborg Universitet 2016). LCAbyg 
works as a standalone software where the practitioner adds the building’s various material 
quantities and links them with existing or new datasets. 
2.3 Problem definition 
Although the whole process of assessing the environmental impacts of buildings is simplified as 
described in Paragraph 2.2, LCA is still not widely used in the construction industry except for the 
cases where a sustainability certification is needed or requested in the building program. 
Moreover, the whole process of performing a building’s LCA is considered time consuming, 
preventing the design teams using it more than once in the end of the design as a way to assess 
the impacts of their final design. 
The aim of this project is to develop a tool which will render LCA a design tool used during the 
whole design process. The project will focus not only in creating a tool that will be able to assess 
a building’s environmental impacts, but will also be an analysis tool which will help the design 
teams make the right decisions and help them minimize their design’s impacts. 
To achieve this, the use of Building Information Modelling is going to be examined as a concept 
and the integration of LCA to it. By achieving the integration of LCA in BIM, the data stored in the 
BIM model can easily be used while the BIM environment’s visualization techniques will be taken 
advantage of.

3 LCA and Building Information Modelling 
Having made clear the importance of Life Cycle Assessment of construction projects, it is also 
necessary to make this process as simple and fast as possible in order to render LCA a decision 
making tool during the design process.  
The emerge of Building Information Modeling (BIM) concept, the wide use of it in the AEC industry 
and the benefits of its use in respects with the information enrichment and visual representation 
of a building (Eastman 2011), dictates that BIM models could be the source of all relevant 
information needed for a project’s LCA.  
The integration of projects’ LCA studies in BIM will not only make LCA analysis faster but by using 
the graphical interface of BIM tools the results from the LCA analysis will be communicated better 
among the different engineering disciplines and architects. This better communication of results 
could lead in more sustainable solutions for the studied buildings and projects (Jensen & Nielsen 
2011). 
In this chapter, the integration of LCA to BIM is studied by reviewing some state of the art research 
on the topic, reviewing existing applications and by proposing a new concept for performing LCAs 
in a BIM environment. 
3.1 BIM 
Building Information Modelling (BIM) is a term used for the concept of creating a model of a 
construction project where relevant information can be stored. Such information are obviously 
geometrical information which are graphically represented in the various BIM software but other 
information as well (e.g. location, structural properties, thermal properties, etc.). 
The use of BIM has the potential of improving the information flow between the stakeholders and 
thus achieving better collaboration between them, higher quality and performance of the final 
design as well as avoidance of errors and challenges during the construction or use phase. 
These benefits of BIM make it a valuable tool for achieving more sustainable designs of 
construction projects. Considering the three main pillars of sustainability, cost, social impacts and 
environmental impacts, BIM models can offer information and facilitate processes that maximize 
the final level of sustainability (Alvarez Anton & Diaz 2014). More specifically, the higher quality 
of information reduces the total cost by avoiding errors and repetitive work. Furthermore, the 
BIM model offers the opportunity to more easily assess various social aspects such as working and 
living condition, daylight etc. that previously where hard to calculate or assess. Finally regarding 
the environmental aspects, it is a common practice to use the same BIM model to perform energy 
simulations the results of which are used for optimizing the building’s geometry and systems in 
respect to energy consumption. 
The integration of LCA though is not yet achieved in a satisfactory level. Since LCA is a relatively 
new process in the construction sector, not enough effort has been put in integrating it the design 
process. There has been some research and attempts in using BIM to perform LCAs which will be 
presented in the following paragraphs.

3.2 Literature Review 
The idea of integrating LCA to BIM is not new and is a topic that many researchers have worked 
with. Some of the work that has been made is in a conceptual level meaning how this integration 
could be done and some is suggesting more specific ways of doing this. In this chapter, an 
overview of LCA integration in BIM research is given as well as an overview of the “Integrated 
Dynamic Models” concept.  
3.2.1 LCA and BIM 
Alvarez Anton and Diaz proposed in 2014 two approaches for BIM and LCA integration(Alvarez 
Anton & Diaz 2014). In the first approach, it was suggested to get the construction data from the 
BIM model and use them in an LCA software which will combine these data with the data saved 
in a life cycle inventory and then calculate the environmental impacts for various impact 
categories (Figure 6). 
 
Figure 6: First suggested approach.(Alvarez Anton & Diaz 2014) 
The main advantages of this approach are described as followed: 
 
No data re-entry in the LCA software. 
 
Easy to highlight hotspots and thus enhance environmental performance. 
 
Quick and fast assessments which renders the model a decision-making tool. 
Their second approach includes the environmental impacts in the BIM object’s properties. This 
approach combined with another publication of theirs (Alvarez Anton et al. 2014) suggests to 
embed the environmental information in the BIM objects. In this case, it would be easier to 
employ LCA in the early design stage since the LCA information will already be stored in the 
objects.

The main advantage of this approach is that all necessary information is already stored in the 
objects. On the other hand, this means that the architects or engineers have to input this 
information in the objects’ properties since there is not be an automatic way for these to be 
calculated and filled in. 
A more specific proposal was suggested in 2015 by Y. Shin and K. Cho. Their proposal is a 
worksheet structured application (Figure 8), which takes under consideration the manufacturing 
of the building materials, their transportation to the construction site, their assembly process, 
their maintenance during the life cycle, the energy consumption during the operation of the

3.2.2 Integrated Dynamic Models 
While in Paragraph 3.2.1 specific concepts and ideas were described on how LCA can be integrated 
in a BIM environment, in this paragraph more generic concepts are studied of how a Building 
Performance Simulation (BPS) process like LCA can be integrated in a geometrical design process. 
Designing according to Building Performance feedback is a common strategy that comes from the 
advent of computational modelling. Considering a building’s environmental impacts as a building 
performance metric, it is very relevant to find an optimum method for integrating its simulation 
in the design process from the early stage.

In 2015 Kristoffer Negendahl examined the integration of Building Performance Simulations in 
the early design stage and distinguished three sub-domains of integration: the human domain, 
the tool domain, and the model domain (Negendahl 2015) as shown in Figure 9. 
 
 
Figure 9: The three domains of integration. A) Human domain, B) Tool domain, C) Model domain. (Negendahl 2015) 
 The “Human domain” has to do with the relationship between the architect(s) and the 
engineer(s) of the project and is divided in three types: 
a) The engineer as an assistant to the architect. 
b) The hybrid practitioner acting both as engineer and architect. 
c) The engineer as a partner to the architect. 
In the first case where the engineer acts as an assistant to the architect (Figure 10), the 
information is transferred from the architect to the engineer who then gives feedback back to the 
architect concerning their own often narrow field of expertise. In this traditional approach of 
collaboration, the engineer and the architect use their own tools meaning that the engineer is the 
only responsible for any simulation. This kind of arrangement contains a high risk of model’s 
divergence and can lead to errors and misunderstanding during the early design phase (Klitgaard 
et al. 2006). 
 
Figure 10: The engineer acting as assistant to the architect approach.(Negendahl 2015) 
In the “hybrid practitioner” case (Figure 11), the practitioner has adequate skills both in 
architecture and engineering and can ensure a satisfactory results in both perspectives. Kilian in 
2006  specified the amount of expertise of the hybrid practitioner to be enough to allow him 
accomplish their tasks. There could be cases though that the expertise for a specific task or 
simulation like LCA or energy could be embedded in a computational process. In this case the

embedded expertise does not ensure sufficient knowledge of the practitioner on the field 
(Negendahl 2015). 
 
Figure 11: The hybrid practitioner approach.(Negendahl 2015) 
In the “engineer as partner” (Figure 12) approach which is actually an Integrated Design Process 
(IDP), a team of specialist is formed where each participant has an influence in the design process 
according to their area of expertise. 
 
Figure 12: The enginner as partner to the architect approach.(Negendahl 2015) 
 
As for the “Model domain”, this concerns the model interactions between the design tool and the 
BPS tool. Negendahl describes three types of model integration in the early design 
stages(Negendahl 2015) (Figure 14):  
a) The combined model method 
b) The central model method 
c) The distributed model method 
The combined model (Figure 13), is based on the concept that there is a common model which is 
used for both the design and the simulation in the same tool. This provides the advantage of 
ultimate tool integration, but restricts the user to the features of the specific tool (Hensen 2004). 
 
Figure 13: Combined model approach. (Negendahl 2015) 
==PAGE:32==

Figure 14: Model integration methods. Combined model method (left), Central model method (middle), Distributed 
model method (right). (Negendahl 2015) 
The “Central model” method (Figure 15) is based on the concept that a central BIM model exists 
which can be used in various BPS applications. Most commonly the BIM model is exported in IFC 
(buildingSMART 2016) or gbXML (Green Building XML schema 2016) file format and then imported 
in the BPS application. Although these formats can contain all the necessary information for the 
simulations, the central model has to be prepared with this information interchange in mind, in 
order fulfill the data completeness (Plume & Mitchell 2007). Finally, although the data transfer 
between the calculation model and the geometry model is bidirectional in this method it is very 
rare for a calculation model to provide feedback or changes back to the geometry model after the 
simulation. 
 
Figure 15: The Central model approach for model integration. (Negendahl 2015) 
The “Distributed model” method (Figure 16) is based on the concept that there is not a central 
data schema like in the case of the central model but data are created in all relevant tools and can 
be transferred from one to another. To enhance this data transfer the concept of middleware tool 
is introduced which convert the file format between the different tools and prepare and modify 
the data in a way that they will properly be read from the other tools.

Figure 16: The distributed model method. (Negendahl 2015) 
In the case that the middleware consists of a Visual Programming Language (VPL), the distributed 
model is then transformed to an Integrated Dynamic Model (Figure 17). An integrated dynamic 
model is actually a combined model where the design tool is dynamically coupled with a visual 
programming language which in turn is coupled with a Building Performance Simulation tool. 
 
Figure 17: Integrated Dynamic Model. (Negendahl 2015) 
Most common examples of VPLs are Grasshopper (Grasshopper 2016), Dynamo (Autodesk 2015) 
and GenerativeComponents (Bentley 2016a). These VPLs are coupled bidirectionally with design 
tools like Rhino (Robert McNeel & Associates 2016), Revit (Autodesk 2016) and Micro Station 
(Bentley 2016b). Although VPLs can sometimes be considered as design tools because of their 
ability to create and modify geometric forms, their main feature is that they can handle non-
geometrical data and allow the users to develop their own script classifying them in a different 
tool category. An advantage of VPLs compared to formats like IFC for transferring data is that VPLs 
offer higher flexibility and facilitate data transfer across any content and object relationship. 
Formats like IFC prescribe object relationships through attribute data (Negendahl 2015).  
Now, answering the question of what is the best way for coupling geometric models and building 
calculation models, Negendahl suggests that integrated dynamic models “provide better support 
in terms of BPS tool diversity, flexibility of feedback and multidisciplinary collaboration during the 
early design stages” (Negendahl 2015). VPLs offer better integration of the design model to the 
BPS environment while they allow for very fast feedback. This combined with the ability of VPLs 
to apply optimization algorithms offers the practitioner a method for evaluating multiple solutions 
the analysis of which takes under considerations multiple parameters. 
However, it is pointed that the integrated dynamic models cannot offer the convenience of the 
standardized formats (IFC, gbXML) for transferring data. This means that every integrated

dynamic model has its own unique interoperability challenges when the design tool needs to be 
coupled with another BPS tool.  
3.3 Existing Methods/Implementation (tally) 
Maybe the only method currently used that integrates LCA in a BIM environment is the Revit add-
in “tally” (KT Innovations et al. 2015). Tally which is a joint development program from KT 
Innovations and Autodesk, is an application that calculates the environmental impacts of a 
building using its Revit model for extracting information about the materials’ quantities. Being an 
add-in for Revit, classifies tally in the combined model according to Paragraph 3.2.2 in regards to 
model integration. In this case, Revit is the main tool and tally the sub-tool which performs LCA 
analyses and uses the same model as the main tool. 
Opening as an add-in through Revit, tally does not need to manually input any new data after 
changes are made in the Revit model. Due to this functionality, tally is fast and simple enough to 
be considered as a decision making tool during the design process. There are still some features 
that are causing inaccuracies and demand more time to be spent than ideally, though. These 
features are going to be explained below together with an overview of the application. 
The basic concept in which tally works is that the user manually matches the Revit materials with 
materials from its impact database. After the material matching, the calculations are performed 
and a report is exported in both pdf and excel format (Figure 18)

The first thing that the user is called to do is to define which elements tally will make the 
assessment for. Thereafter, the material matching is done by the user. The Revit model is

tally database itself. The main drawback of the database tally uses, is that the majority of the 
datasets are from the United States which means that they are not valid for construction projects 
in Europe and more specifically in Denmark since the processes performed for the manufacturing 
of the same product can differ a lot in different countries. Moreover, the application does not 
allow to add any new materials in its database. Although the database is quite detailed it is not 
possible to include every material and eventually the user will come up with the situation where 
they will have to assign a material which is not the same as the real one but as close as possible.

example would be the lack of drawing of metal studs or a wooden skeleton to support the 
insulation layer in a wall. It is a common practice for these constructions not to be included in the 
Revit model. If there was the ability to add more materials in the database, this issue could be 
solved by creating a new dataset which would be equivalent with the compound structure of 
insulation and its support. 
After the material matching, the application performs the calculations and exports two reports in

to improve their total impacts.

and thus has a high cost for using it. Although the cost is lower than the cost of more sophisticated, 
detailed and multi-purpose LCA programs, it is still high enough, rendering it difficult to acquire 
especially for small and medium size practices. 
 
3.4 Proposed Methods/Implementation 
Taking under consideration the existing literature and methods for integrating LCA into BIM, in 
this paragraph a new method for integrating LCA in BIM is proposed. This method is proposed 
because it is considered to solve problems and challenges that are faced in the existing methods. 
The method consists of two different integrated dynamic models and one more simple VPL 
application. Each model-application is applied in a different stage during the design stage 
according to the available data and the needed feedback. 
In 2014, Negendahl pointed the fact that the building performance analyses are most often 
conducted in the late design stages (Negendahl 2014). By describing the transition from heuristic 
knowledge into the use of procedural precise calculation models based on a previous study by 
Shaviv et al. (1992) he pointed the periods during the design process where model collaboration 
was in its peak and described the level of integrated dynamic model control for the various 
practitioners working on an integrated dynamic model as described in Paragraph 3.2.2. Although 
the described process (Figure 21) was for an integrated dynamic model for energy simulation, it 
can also describe a similar dynamic model for the integration of LCA analysis.    
==PAGE:38==
LCA and Building Information Modelling 
38 
 
 
Figure 21: Model transition from heuristic knowledge to procedural precise BPS models. Ideal transition (Shaviv et al. 
1992) (up). Proposed transition (Negendahl 2014) (down). (Negendahl 2014) 
The practitioners working in the described design process are the designer, the programmer and 
the analyst. The designer is the responsible for the geometric model, the programmer is 
responsible for scripting in the VPL and the analyst is the responsible for the BPS tool and the 
analysis of the results. 
As shown in Figure 21, there are three peak points during the design process where the 
collaboration between all three practitioners is needed. These points are in the very beginning of 
the pre-conceptual stage, the beginning of the conceptual stage and the beginning of the detailed 
stage of the design. In each case, what causes these peak points is the increase of the amount of 
information that are known. For this specific example of energy simulation, the model simulation 
model starts as a mono-zone model while in some point where more information are acquired 
the model consists of more than one zones and in the detailed stage a CFD simulation is possible 
to be performed. 
Respectively, a similar model could be applied for LCA simulations. In the pre-conceptual stage 
the information that are known in the practitioners are mainly numbers about the size of the 
building, a general idea of what kind of constructions will be used and an approximate shape of 
the building. In the conceptual stage, the designer starts applying more specific constructions to 
form the building shape and needs feedback on the material selection and the thicknesses. Finally 
in the detailed stage, the specific constructions and their materials are decided. The building has 
taken its final form and there is a need for a detailed LCA analysis which will help the designer 
reduce the environmental impacts even more. 
Having these in mind the following tool-set is proposed for LCA integration in the design process 
from its early stage to the end of the detailed stage: 
a) Pre-conceptual stage tool 
b) Conceptual stage integrated dynamic model 
c) Full LCA integrated dynamic model

To develop these tools and models, Revit (Autodesk 2016) is going to be used as a design tool, 
Dynamo (Autodesk 2015) as VPL and an external LCI database as a basis for the calculations. The 
whole concept is described in the following paragraphs. 
Revit will be the program where the building’s BIM model will be created and maintained and it 
is chosen because of its extensive use from architects and engineers around the world and 
especially in Denmark. The VPL Dynamo is an open source programming language which is used 
and opens as an add-in in Revit. The advantages of Dynamo are that it is open source, simple to 
use and is actually part of Revit since it opens through the application. The material LCI database 
is suggested to be a Microsoft Excel Sheet (Microsoft 2016) since this makes it possible to directly 
be read from Dynamo. 
3.4.1 Pre-conceptual stage tool 
The amount of information that are known in the pre-conceptual stage of the design process is 
usually limited to the demands of the owner which can be represented by simple generic numbers 
like the gross area of the building and its height. Knowing these parameters an abstract building 
form can be shaped that can represent the building in its very early design stage. These known 
numbers can be used to calculate other parameters like the quantities of the walls, the floors or 
even the structural system. Using all these known and calculated values an early LCA analysis can 
be performed. 
This means that a geometric model is not needed for this tool to work. The tool will be able to 
calculate the geometrical quantities by only using a few parameters that are manually input by 
the user. Although the concept could quite easily be implemented in an excel sheet, the use of 
dynamo is proposed for this in order for the tool to be part of a complete Dynamo LCA package 
that will also consist of the integrated dynamic models that are described in the next two 
paragraphs. 
The basic concept of this tool (Figure 22) is that the user will define all relevant parameters in an 
excel sheet which will be read by dynamo. Dynamo will then calculate the impacts according to 
these inputs and extract the results in the same excel sheet. In order to make the tool as simple 
as possible, the user will be restricted regarding the range of some parameters like the kind of 
wall construction. In this particular example the user will have to choose from a predefined list of 
constructions the impacts of which are stored in a construction database in the same excel file.

Although this specific tool is running on Dynamo it cannot be classified as one of the models 
described in Paragraph 3.2.2 since in the stage that it is used, a geometric model does not yet

for the very early stage of the design process. 
The tool is developed to mainly give an approximate estimation of the environmental impacts and 
let the practitioners experiment through changing the various construction types. This way some 
basic decisions can be taken in this stage like the main material for each construction type or the 
complexity of the building shape. After this stage, the designer will have defined a more specific 
building shape and will have to decide on more specific construction types and materials. 
3.4.2 Conceptual stage integrated dynamic model 
The conceptual stage is the stage where a general building shape has to be formed and more 
details need to be decided regarding the materials to be used. A general idea of what are the 
construction types and their materials exists from the pre-conceptual stage and it is in this stage 
that the practitioner has to choose more specific constructions and dimensions for each material.  
In terms of LCA, in this stage, the practitioner needs to select the materials and the constructions 
that will minimize the total impacts of the building. Therefore, the most common action in this 
stage is the comparison between different constructions and the selection of their layers’ 
thickness. 
For this stage, an integrated dynamic model is proposed (Figure 23) which will have as main 
functionality the easy changing of the various layers’ thickness and the instant calculation of the 
environmental impacts according to these changes. This way the designer will get instant 
feedback on the impacts of their changes and be able to do many iterations until they reach a 
desired result.

In this integrated dynamic model, all the constructions’ geometric data are read from the BIM 
model which in this case is a Revit (Autodesk 2016) model and transferred to VPL Dynamo 
(Autodesk 2015). In the same time Dynamo reads a material LCI database which is stored in an 
excel sheet format. In this point the practitioner will have the opportunity to change the various 
materials’ thicknesses in order to check how the environmental impacts are changing. After 
changing the thickness, the calculation is performed in the VPL environment and the results are 
exported back in an excel sheet and/or visualized in the BIM model. 
Originally, this integrated dynamic model was intended to use some predefined Revit family types. 
This feature was adopted in order to facilitate the “layer thickness changing” function since VPL 
Dynamo is unable to automatically add equal amount of nodes as the layers of all compound 
structure family types. The initial concept was to build a Dynamo script which would contain all 
the necessary nodes to modify all the types’ layers. Although this feature looked like a constrain 
in the model’s functionality, this was not considered as a problem since in this stage, the 
practitioner would normally work with some predefined Revit family types and would just check 
how the different thicknesses would affect the total environmental impacts. 
Later in this project though and after the main dynamic model (Paragraph 3.4.3) was completed, 
a new feature was suggested in order to increase the functionality of this early stage dynamic 
model. More specifically, for the functionality of modifying the thicknesses, instead of sliders in 
the VPL environment, another way was suggested. This way involves the connection of VPL with 
Microsoft Excel (Microsoft 2016) and the basic idea is to export all the Revit family types, their 
layers and thicknesses in excel where the practitioner modifies them and then VPL reads them 
back and applies any changes occurred according to the excel sheet. This process is explained 
more in detail later in this report in Chapter 6. Adding this functionality to the detailed LCA

integrated dynamic model that is explained in Paragraph 3.4.3, a new dynamic model was made

In this case as well as in the detailed integrated dynamic model (Paragraph 3.4.3) and the final

3.4.3 Full LCA integrated dynamic model 
In this model the connection of Revit (Autodesk 2016) is suggested with an external material LCI 
database and the Visual Programming Language (VPL) Dynamo (Autodesk 2015) similarly to the 
conceptual stage dynamic model. The LCI database will actually be the source where VPL will get 
the environmental impacts of the used materials from. The database will consist of all common 
construction materials and their environmental impacts per a specific functional unit such as kg 
or m3. 
The basic concept of the proposed model is that there will be a permanent link between the Revit 
materials and the Database materials resulting to zero input from the user every time they want 
to perform an LCA. To achieve this permanent link, a unique material ID is employed which will 
be the same in both Revit material library and the LCI database. Such an ID can be shown in 
column A in Figure 24. If this ID is saved as a Revit material parameter as well, then the permanent 
link is achieved.  
 
Figure 24: Material LCI database.(Flux et al. 2015)

5.1 LCA specific questions 
 
1. When is the first time in the design process that you perform an LCA? Why? 
It is usually just before the detailed design starts. This is also the point of the first delivery when 
we are talking about a competition. The reason that we perform the first LCA in that point and not 
earlier is that it is then when we have all the relevant data like the geometry, the materials etc. 
2. When do you perform again an LCA after that? Why? 
In general there is not a specific rule that we follow regarding LCA. It always depends on the project 
and the program that we have to follow. Most of the times LCA analysis is not required from the 
clients and it is performed only as a supplementary feature to the design. For the case that LCA is 
required, it is performed many times during the design process in order to monitor and minimize 
==PAGE:48==
Vandkunsten’s case. 
48 
 
the environmental impacts. It could be said that in these cases it is performed 3-4 times during the 
detailed design stage. 
3. How do you perform LCAs regarding the toolset that you use? 
We most often use the DGNB spreadsheet tool because it is a kind of an industry standard. Using 
this tool, the results can be compared with other buildings’ results that have been created with 
the same tool. Furthermore, for inner use mainly we use a custom made excel sheet that is using 
the oekobaudat database exactly as the DGNB tool. In both cases, the concept followed is similar. 
The different constructions and their quantities are manually imported to the tool and the 
materials’ quantities and lifetimes are specified. The environmental impacts are then calculated 
for 50 and 120 years building lifetime.  
4. Do you use LCA as a design tool? What kind of feedback do you take after an LCA of your 
design? 
We mainly use LCA as a design tool. We perform LCAs to get feedback that will help us minimize 
the environmental impacts of our design. The most common reason we perform LCAs is to compare 
various solutions for the same construction. For example to find which wall type has the lowest 
impacts or how the impacts are changed after a change in the geometry of the construction or a 
replacement of a material. 
5. How would you describe the office’s average employee’s knowledge in LCA? 
Since LCA is not yet very well-known and used among the construction industry, the majority of 
our employees are just familiar with the term of LCA. They know what it is but do not know how 
to perform an LCA. A small portion of the employees however are able to use the DGNB tool and 
perform an LCA. The analysis of the results though is done only by Jan (Jan Schipull Kauschen) who 
then suggests any improvements and changes to the design team in order to minimize the 
environmental impacts. 
6. What is the main feedback you would like an LCA tool to give? 
The most important feature an LCA application should have is the comparison between 
alternatives of the same construction. Not necessarily between some predefined constructions, 
but between all similar constructions existing in a project. We need a hotspot analysis that will let 
us more easily identify what we need to change in order to minimize the total impacts. An example 
of this would be to present the results not only per material or per construction category (e.g. 
walls, floors etc.) but creating and presenting results from a very detailed level which is the 
materials and continuing with construction types (e.g. the different types of walls) and 
construction categories. This way we can then choose the level in which we do the comparison. It 
can be the materials, the types of the same construction or the constructions. 
Moreover, an uncertainty analysis would be very useful. Different inputs in an LCA analysis have 
different uncertainty levels. It would be nice to know in the end how valid our results are and which 
material is causing higher deviation on the results. 
Another useful feature would be the representation of the impacts against the time. This means 
that we do not only need to know the total impacts in the end of the life cycle of the building but

Vandkunsten’s case. 
49 
 
we want to see a representation of how these impacts are increased or decreased during the 
building’s lifetime. This would help us more visually understand the impacts related with the use 
and maintenance stage and the end-of-life stage. 
7. How much time do you need to perform an LCA analysis? 
It depends on the kind of analysis we are doing and the level of detail. For example analyses where 
we compare different construction types in order to choose one takes a few hours. If we need to 
do a full LCA analysis of a building this takes a lot of hours and maybe even more than a day. 
8. What is the most time consuming part of the process that you are currently following to 
perform an LCA analysis? 
The most time consuming part is the quantity take-off which is then used as input to the DGNB or 
custom LCA sheet. Although Revit offers the possibility of doing a quantity take-off easily by using 
a schedule, it doesn’t include the generic models. This is a problematic feature for us since in many 
cases we use generic models for creating more complex geometries. To face this, a material take-
off is then needed which is converted in the same structure as the schedule in order to be imported 
to the LCA sheets. Moreover an equally time consuming part is building the LCI inventory which 
actually means the matching of the materials with the datasets from the database. 
9. What is your biggest concern in the current method you use for performing LCA analyses? 
Our biggest concern is the quality of the database we use. In both cases, DGNB and custom excel 
sheet, we use the oekobaudat database. Although it is one of the most valid, detailed and 
recognized databases in Europe, we have found that there are a lot of errors in many of its 
datasets. Another common problem is the inconsistency among the datasets regarding the life 
cycle stages that are included in the calculated impacts of each material. Another thing that we 
are concerned about this database is the datasets of all timber materials or materials that partially 
contain wood. Due to the method with which the impacts are calculated, it is allowed for wood to 
be considered that it is burnt in the end-of-life and thus substitute other fossil fuels that are used 
for energy production. The impacts that are avoided by this action are then subtracted from the 
total impacts of wood causing negative impacts in the GWP impact category. 
Other than the database itself, we would say that the with the current way we perform the 
quantity take-off in order to import it to the LCA sheets is easy to do mistakes that will of course 
affect the final results. It would be better if the quantity take-off could somehow be automated. 
10. Would you consider yourselves (vandkunsten) as LCA specialists? 
We would say that our office is among the architectural offices in Denmark with the best 
knowledge in the field of LCA and the highest level of integration of it in the design process.

6.2.1 LCI database structure 
The first feature of the database to be defined is the quantity unit per which the environmental 
impacts will be stated (functional unit). This is a variable that depends not only from the 
developer’s random choice but also from Revit’s restrictions and the final users’ way of using 
Revit. The selection of the materials’ functional unit is crucial and had to be defined for the very 
beginning since the whole Dynamo script would be built according to this. 
An overview of the two most popular open source databases, Oekobau (Bundesministerium für 
Umwelt Naturschutz Bau und Reaktorsicherheit (BMUB) 2015) and Quartz (Flux et al. 2015) 
showed that the most common functional unit of construction materials’ life cycle impacts is kg. 
It was initially thought that this might be the best option until some challenges came up. 
The main challenge was that the quantity take-off in Revit is performed in units of volume and 
area. This means that the density of each material should be stated as well in order to “translate” 
the volume and area quantities into mass. Although, a density section exists as a parameter in 
Revit materials this is not useful for the cases where the material quantity is measured with area 
units. This is the case in the cases of membranes since Revit does not allow membrane materials 
to be modelled with thickness other than zero. This led to the thought of creating a new material 
parameter where the density will be specified in mass units per area units (kg/m2). Although this 
seemed like a valid solution to the problem it came up with some more challenges. 
The main challenge of this option is the fact that the new density (kg/m2) parameter is a project 
parameter and cannot be stored as a Revit material property in the Revit material library. This 
combined with the fact that there is higher risk of creating errors due to the need of specifying 
the density by the user led to the conclusion that the materials’ functional unit should be the 
same as the quantity’s take-off unit.  
As stated above, the Revit’s quantity take-off units are m3 and m2. This way all the materials’ 
impacts should be stored in the database as impacts per m3 or m2. The materials that are modelled 
in Revit as membranes are stored with their impacts per m2 while all the rest as impacts per m3. 
As shown in Appendix B: Revit quantity take-off examples, in some cases a quantity take-off in 
other units than m2 or m3 would be preferable (m). To achieve this the Revit Family Types should 
include the respective parameters to allow that, since Revit cannot see any other quantities than 
area and volume on its own. 
The fact that there will be two possible functional units for each material makes necessary this 
information to be mentioned in the database. This will help the user to match the Revit materials 
with the database materials in the first set up of the model but also check every time this might 
be necessary. Furthermore, as it will be described in Paragraph 6.4, a pre-check tool in dynamo 
will check if the material match has been done correctly in respect to the functional unit. An 
example of this pre-check process is that the tool will check if the membrane materials in Revit 
are linked with database materials whose functional unit is m2.  
==PAGE:57==
Development

Given the fact that Quartz and a large portion of Oekobau databases give their datasets in 
kilograms, the conversion to m2 or m3 has to done before the datasets are added in the proposed 
database. This means that for checking purposes the materials’ density and their density unit 
(kg/m2 or kg/m3) has to be included in the database as well.  
For the same data validation purposes two more fields are considered necessary. These are the 
origin of each dataset and the date or version of the original dataset it was taken from. The 
processes that are used for the manufacturing or the disposal or recycling of a material are 
changing in a high pace making the update of their calculated life cycle impacts important. Having 
the latest dataset for each material in the database ensures the highest accuracy of the final total 
results. 
As for the impacts themselves, it is important that they are stored per life cycle stage. This way 
the final results will be possible to be calculated per life cycle stage as well. The life cycle stages 
in a building’s lifetime are the construction, the use stage and the end-of life. As per construction 
for the purpose of this project it is meant only the manufacturing of the construction materials. 
The use stage includes the energy consumption and the number of replacements of the various 
materials. The end-of-life includes the recycling or disposal of all the construction materials during 
the life cycle of the building. 
To make possible the division of the impacts per life cycle stage the materials’ are divided in 
impacts from the manufacturing of a functional unit and impacts for the end-of-life of a functional 
unit. As for the use stage, the number of replacements of materials is calculated in the Dynamo 
script automatically as described in Paragraph 6.3. This calculation requires data about the 
lifetime of materials. These data are stored in the database. Due to the fact that the same material 
can have different lifetimes according to each position in the building, three different lifetimes for 
each material are defined. One for the case that the material is placed on the interior of the 
building, one for the case that the material is in contact with the interior and one for the case that 
the material is placed in the core of an element. As for the impacts from the energy consumption 
during the building’s life cycle, as described in Paragraph 6.3 the user specifies the energy 
consumption per m2 per year for three different energy sources. The impacts of these energy 
sources per kWh are also included in the database as materials. 
Another basic feature of the database is the impact categories for which the materials’ impacts 
are defined. Usually, in the construction industry the impact categories that are mainly taken 
under consideration are the Global Warming Potential (GWP), Ozone Layer Depletion (ODP), 
Photochemical Ozone Creation Potential (POCP), Acidification Potential (AP) and Eutrophication 
Potential (EP). For this model it was decided to include more impact categories in order to make 
it possible for even more detailed and holistic results. More specifically, the impact categories 
used in the Oekobau database are used. These are explained later in Paragraph 6.2.2.

6.2.3 Quartz 
The Quartz database (Flux et al. 2015) is an environmental impact database for common 
construction materials which is the result of the homonymous quartz project. Quartz project is 
supported by FLUX, Google, HBM and thinkstep. The database contains generic datasets for 
construction materials and can be downloaded as an XLSX or JSON file. 
The quartz database’s structure is closer to the proposed LCI database than the oekobaudat one. 
The database provides with the materials’ environmental impacts for the following impact 
categories: Acidification Potential (AP), Eutrophication Potential (EP), Global Warming Potential 
(GWP), Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP), Smog Potential (SMOG), Primary Energy (PE). The 
environmental impacts are also divided into the manufacturing and end-of-life stage. 
The functional unit of the materials’ impacts is “kg” for all datasets which increases its consistency. 
To use the datasets though a conversion needs to be done to impacts per m3 or m2. The database’s 
drawback in this case is the fact that the materials’ density is not included in the datasets. To 
convert the impacts, the density from a different source has to be used rendering the final 
datasets less accurate since the density of the original material is not necessarily the same. 
Although, the quartz database is very similar to the proposed database and despite the fact that 
it is very easy and simple to acquire all its data and transform their structure, there is a parameter 
that renders the database less attractive for the purpose of this project. This parameter is the 
geographic origin of the datasets. The datasets that form the database are datasets that are based 
in processes that take place in the USA. The same material has different environmental impacts 
when manufactured in different countries. This has to do with processes that take place during 
the manufacturing and/or the end-of-life of the material and some common examples would be 
the different ways of producing energy which is used during the manufacturing of the material or 
different ways of treatment of the material in the end-of-life. 
For this reason, it was decided not to include any datasets from the quartz database in the 
proposed database, since the developed model is intended to mainly be used at least initially by 
Danish practices. On the other hand, these datasets would be relevant in cases where projects 
placed in the USA were to be assessed.  
In Figure 35, the correspondence between the quartz and the proposed data is shown. It can be 
noticed that there not absolute consistency between the two databases. Quartz database 
includes impacts for fewer impact categories while not all the ones are the same as the proposed 
database’s. This means that datasets from oekobaudat and quartz databases when used in the 
same time by the model, they reduce the level of detail of the final results. This due the fact that 
the materials of which the data were taken from the quartz database will inevitably not be 
considered in the impact categories of POCP, ADPE, PENRE, PERE and RSF since their impacts are 
not contained in the quartz daabase. Additionally, the data for the impact categories of SMOG 
and PE that are contained in the quartz database cannot be used as well since there is no 
respective field in the proposed database.  
Besides that, the quartz database could be used with no problem when the proposed database 
contains data that come only from the quartz database or when all the other datasets follow the 
quartz datasets in respect to the amount of data. For example, the SMOG and PE data could be

6.3 Detailed stage Integrated Dynamic Model 
The purpose of this Dynamic Model is to perform detailed calculations of the environmental 
impacts for all the stages of the building’s life cycle. As shown in Paragraph 3.4.3, the model 
consists of the design tool Revit where the BIM model is built, the VPL Dynamo and an external 
database (Figure 31) which is linked through a unique ID with the Revit materials. This model is 
described in this paragraph. First the datasets linking is described and the main dynamo script 
explanations follows. Finally the ways that the results are communicated is presented. 
6.3.1 Datasets and Revit materials linking 
As shown in Figure 25, the initial thought was to add the datasets’ unique ID as a material 
parameter in Revit materials. Although this concept was working fine in the beginning and such a 
parameter seemed logical to be created as a material parameter it had to be reconsidered later 
in the process. The drawback of this concept was that this parameter can be created only as a 
project parameter and thus cannot follow the Revit materials when these are stored in the 
material library. This means that the material linking had to be performed every time a new Revit 
project was created.  
To solve this issue it was decided to and the ID in one of the built-in parameters of the materials. 
This way the ID follows the revit material even when this is stored in the material library. Loading 
the material library in a Revit template or a Revit project the matching is already there and there 
is no need for any more manual work. 
 
Figure 36: Comment parameter of Revit materials.

Such a parameter is among others the comments section in the material’s identity asset. For the 
case of this project the comments sections was used exclusively for the material ID, but in order 
to allow the user to also add comments a convention can be used in order to allow both. For 
example the ID could be written first and then the comments with a special character in between 
which allow Dynamo to distinguish them when the comments parameter is read. 
6.3.2 BIM7AA encoding system 
From the first step of the project and after the feedback taken from the interview with tegnestuen 
vandkunsten (Chapter 5) it was decided that the results to be created should be in very high level 
of detail. This practically means that the practitioner should be able to see and analyze the impacts 
in many ways, from total impacts per impacts category to impacts per material per family type 
per construction category and per life cycle stage. The idea behind this concept was that for every 
Revit element from which a material take-off is gotten there is always a value for the family type 
of the element and of course a value for the construction category of this family type. 
Furthermore, since the database provides the impacts per life cycle stage and per impact category 
the total impacts can be calculated in the same way. For all these parameters, the impacts were 
easy to be grouped except for the construction category (walls, floor, etc.). The reason is that 
there are more than one ways to model an element in Revit. For example a wall can be modelled 
in Revit by simply using the “wall modelling” option or it can be modelled as a generic model that 
is not classified in a construction category. 
To allow Dynamo “understand” the category of all elements even if these are generic models, a 
classification system was introduced that would be applied in the family type’s name. A simple 
but adequate classification functionality for the purpose of this project was identified in the 
BIM7AA encoding system (BIM7AA 2015a). This system is based on the international classification 
system SfB. Its application is based on assigning a “Type Code” in the BIM objects’ name. In the 
case of Revit this means that each family type’s name starts with this type code that can be found 
in the encoding system’s catalogue (BIM7AA 2015b). An example of the BIM7AA encoding system 
to an object would for example be “312_Openable_1200x1200”. The type code 312 indicates that 
the family type is an exterior window. After the type code, an underscore follows to separate the 
type code from the rest of the name. This underscore also facilitates the dynamo script in 
extracting the BIM7AA code from the family types’ name.

tool, but as the BPS tool itself. Subsequently, the Dynamo script (Figure 37) contains coding for 
both reading-gathering data from the design tool (Revit) and the database and performing the 
calculations. The Dynamo script can be “divided” in parts-steps that undertake different

6.3.3.1 
Material Take-Off 
For the material take-off it, the “Element.Materials” clockwork package node (Dieckmann 2014) 
was initially used. The node needs a list elements and gives a list with the materials of each 
element and their respective surface area and volume. To provide the list of elements a custom 
node was built that collects all relevant element and creates a list of them (Figure 38). The 
elements that are gathered include all the elements that are needed by the DGNB certification 
(Paragraph 2.1). 
 
Figure 38: Custom node that collects all DGNB relevant elements. 
==PAGE:67==

Although the script was developed in an extensive point using the “Element.Materials” node, a 
major drawback was detected. When the Dynamo script was first tested in a real project, unlike 
the smaller simple projects tested before, it could not process the huge amount of elements and 
eventually crashed together with Revit. The reason behind this behavior was that the material 
takeoff was organized per element, which means more than 5-7 thousand lists (equal to the 
elements) containing 1-5 indices that stand for the materials, areas and volumes. When the 
Dynamo had to process these lists to filter and create new data it was estimated that several 
million of calculations-loops had to be performed. 
To surpass this challenge a custom node was built which performs the material take-off per family 
type instead per element. This has as a result to reduce the size of the lists from a few thousands 
to a few tenths. After this point, the calculations were performed much faster and without causing 
any performance related problems in Dynamo and Revit. 
 
Other than the area and the volume of the materials, the nodes also provides a list with the family 
type’s name which is later used to extract the BIM7AA code and the number of indices of each 
family type which later accompanies the results. The node is not taking under consideration the 
elements that no materials have been applied and family types that have BIM7AA code greater 
than 49. The reason for the BIM7AA restriction is that the codes that are greater than 49 
correspond to objects that are relevant with the LCA calculation (fixtures, furniture etc.). Although 
the relevant elements are collected in the previous node (LCA.DGNB.Elements), this second filter 
is placed in this node in order to exclude the generic models that passed that node but now are 
detected as irrelevant because of their type code. 
==PAGE:68==
Development 
68 
 
After the material take-off is performed, it is processed in order to modify some values in a way 
that will be useable from the rest of the script. More specifically, this process involves only the 
glazing materials. The main idea is that for all the materials their volumes are used in order to 
calculate the total impacts except for the membrane materials that practically have no volume 
and thus their area is used. The case of glazing though is one more exception and the reason has 
to do with the way the glazing is usually modelled in Revit. It is a common practice the glazing to 
be modelled as one thick pane as shown in Figure 39 instead of the detailed geometry that 
includes all the panes (usually 2 or 3) and the air or other gas plenums in between. This practice 
is very reasonable when it comes to level of detail needed in the geometry, but causes a problem 
in the material takeoff since the whole glazing unit is counted as a glass material regardless the 
percentage of it covered by just air or other gases. For example, a 4-16-4-16-4 glazing unit that is 
modelled as one single layer in Revit would give a 267% larger quantity of glass than in reality. 
 
Figure 39: Common window glazing modeling in Revit window families. 
To keep the functionality of modelling the glazing units as one layer but also solve the quantity 
take-off issue it was decided to use the layer’s area instead and provide the glazing’s impacts in 
impacts per m2 in the database. The area that Revit calculates though is not the layer’s area but 
the total area of all 6 surfaces of the rectangular parallelepiped. Since there is not a way to 
calculate the face’s area the total area is divided by two and thus can represent the face area due 
to the very small thickness of the parallelepiped in relation to the other two dimensions. 
The only precise way for the glazing’s are would be all the families that consist partly from glazing 
to report through a special parameter the glazing area. This option was abandoned because it 
demanded the modification of all the window and door Revit families of the cases that were 
provided by tegnestuen vandkunsten and mainly because the simple approximate way is a way 
that can work with all projects regardless the existence or not of that special glazing area 
parameter. 
A similar challenge was faced with the caser of the window frames since the real geometry of an 
aluminum or PVC frame is much more complicated than a solid parallelepiped. In this case though, 
the issue was solved by creating equivalent datasets in the database. Instead of linking the Revit 
==PAGE:69==
Development 
69 
 
material that is used in the PVC frame with a PVC dataset, it is linked with a custom made dataset 
that consists of 33% PVC and 67% air. 
Such custom made datasets are also a good solution for more similar situations. An insulation 
layer of a wall is a characteristic example. Although these layers consists of both insulation and 
their supporting system (studs) which most often is made of wood or aluminum, the studs are not 
modelled in Revit. By creating a dataset that for example comes from 20% wood and 80% 
insulation can minimize the errors and thus include the wooden studs in the calculation. 
6.3.3.2 
Database reading and materials matching 
With the material take-off completed, the materials’ datasets from the database have to be read 
in order for their impacts to be calculated. As mentioned in Paragraph 6.2.1, the database is 
maintained in a spreadsheet format and thus can easily be read by Dynamo using the respective 
core node. After reading the database, the data are sorted in lists that correspond to the sheet’s 
columns. 
The next step is was to direct each of these lists to the appropriate nodes of the script where they 
are needed. For example direct the lists containing the manufacturing data to the part where the 
manufacturing impacts are calculated and the same for the end-of-life data. This would work for 
the case that the database will always have the same structure. 
It was thought though, the database will might need to change eventually regarding the order of 
the columns. If something like this is done then the respective changes have to be done also in 
the Dynamo script. To avoid this and make the database’s structure more flexible, a new sheet 
was introduced in the same file with the database. This sheet is actually acting as a translator 
since it is there where the user specifies the column’s letter for each data category (GWP, ODP, 
ID, dataset name etc) and then lets the Dynamo script understand where to find the data it needs.

6.3.3.3 
Manufacturing and End-of-Life LCI 
Until this stage the material quantities have been calculated, the database is read and the 
materials’ corresponding datasets have been found. What needs to be performed now is to 
calculate the impacts of each material for each family type, each life cycle stage and each impact 
category given in the database. Since the datasets are organized in a way to give the materials’ 
impacts in such detail, what needs to be done is to multiply the impacts per functional unit that 
are gotten from the database with the materials’ quantities. 
The materials’ quantities though can be area or volume regarding the material and where in the 
element this is placed. As said above the membrane materials and the glazing units are included 
in the database with their impacts per m2. For this reason, a python script (Figure 42) was 
developed that reads the functional unit of the materials’ datasets and this way decides if the 
impacts will be multiplied with the materials’ area or volume.

what is the functional unit of their respective dataset. If the functional unit is m2, then the total 
impacts are calculated by multiplying the unitary impacts with the material’s area. Else, if the 
functional unit is m3, the material’s volume is used instead.

cause errors and miscalculations. The same applies for the situations where a material’s

these kind of errors and to enable the model run normally, the impacts of the materials in such

6.3.3.4 
Materials’ Life time Estimation and Maintenance LCI 
To calculate the impacts from the maintenance stage, the material quantities to be replaced need 
to be estimated. To achieve this, the lifetime of each material has to be estimated first. The 
lifetime of a material is not always the same. It depends on the climatic conditions and of course 
the use of the space which is surrounded by the element it contains it. Subsequently, the same 
material will endure fewer years if it is in the façade and exposed to the exterior than if it is placed 
==PAGE:73==
Development 
73 
 
in the core of an exterior wall. In the same way, a gypsum board for example will endure less if it 
is placed in a kindergarten wall than in an apartment. 
To allow for this dynamic change of the materials’ lifetime, three new columns where introduced 
in the database as shown in Figure 44. These three column contain the life expectancy of the 
materials for different places in the construction. Column AA contains the life expectancy of the 
materials that are located in the exterior side of exterior elements (exterior walls, roofs, etc.). 
Column AB represents the lifetime of materials that are located in the core of exterior element. 
Finally, column AC contains the lifetime of the materials as if these are placed in the interior side 
of exterior elements or in any side of interior elements. 
 
Figure 44: Life expectancy of materials as these are contained in the database. 
To decide which of the three lifetimes is the appropriate one in each case, two parameters need 
to be known. These are the location of the element in which the material is placed and the location 
of the material in the element itself. 
For the Elements’ location in the building, Revit uses a parameter called “function” which is a 
family type parameter and is filled in by the modeler who among others can select if the family 
type is an exterior or interior object. This parameter though is not available in all the kind of Revit 
families. It is available in walls and floors but not in roofs, windows, doors and structural elements. 
The case of roof was solved by assuming that a roof will always be an exterior element and that 
otherwise it would be modelled as floor. For the cases of doors and windows, the BIM7AA code 
is used since it is different on whether these are interior or exterior. Finally, for the structural 
elements none of the above options was possible since BIM7AA does not provide separate codes 
for these elements according to their function. Although, in their majority, structural elements of 
a building can be considered interior elements a more detailed solution was given. A “function” 
parameter was manually added in the family types so that this one will be then read by Dynamo.  
Regarding the material’s location in the element itself, Revit can provide the relevant data by 
using some Revit API functions (Autodesk 2014). This of course applies for all constructions that 
consist of multiple layers like walls, floors etc. Of course the quality of the information that Revit 
can give is only as good as the information that the modeler inputs. In Figure 45, the Revit window 
in which the layers of a construction are edited. As shown, among the layers there are also two 
layers with zero thickness that represent the core boundaries. Thus, all layers placed between 
these two layers are considered core layers. All layers placed above the core are the layers that 
are in the exterior side of the wall, while the ones below the core are the interior layers.

6.3.3.5 
Paints LCI 
The paints are treated differently because of the different way they are modelled in Revit. Paint 
information can be added in Revit with two ways. The first one which could be said is the most 
detailed and proper one is by applying a paint materials in certain surfaces of the 3d model as 
these are recognized by Revit. These paints cannot be applied in a family type but instead have to 
be applied in all elements one by one which makes the process very time consuming. These paint 
materials have an area parameter which can then be read by Dynamo which in turn can calculate 
their impacts in the same way as all other materials. The second way is to integrate the visual 
effect of the paints in the materials that are to be applied. For example a red wooden panel would 
be represented by a wooden material the appearance asset of which is red. This way is much 
faster since the paint is applied in the family types and thus there is no need to apply it in every 
single element in Revit. 
This second practice though has the drawback that an area cannot be calculated since the paint 
is not a separate entity and thus has no parameters like area. To get this information the paint 
information a material name convention is introduced. With this name convention, the material 
names will is able to carry information on if the material is painted or not. If the material is 
painted, the area will then be equal to the area of the material.

For this project and according to the needs of vandkunsten studio, all the paints are classified to 
one of three different paints. These three paints consist of a generic exterior paint used for all 
exterior surfaces, one interior generic paint used for all interior surfaces and a lacquer paint used 
for wooden surfaces like frames and doors. Subsequently, the two parameters of what paint is 
applied in each surface is the location of the element (exterior or interior) and if the material is 
wooden or not. 
The function of the elements is already sorted in a list which is used to estimate the materials’ 
lifetime. The information of whether the material is wood or not and whether it is painted or not 
are extracted from the material’s name. Python’s possibilities in handling with text allows to get 
the needed information by following a very specific name convention. 
This name convention (Figure 47) dictates that a material’s name will consist of text values that 
correspond to some of the material’s parameters. Some of these parameters are mandatory to 
include and some others are optional. The name starts always with a prefix that specifies that his 
material follows the name convention. Second comes a mandatory value of the material class that 
can be wood, metal, stone, concrete etc. Then some optional parameters are specified like the 
specific material type which specifies what kind of wood or metal this material is, the material 
function which shows in what kind of elements is this material used, color information which can 
be filled with “painted”, “colored” or “natural”, the color and the glossiness. All these values 
connected with underscores give the final name of the material to be used.

From this name a python script node in Dynamo can read the material class which is located 
always after the first underscore from the left. Also it searches for the word “Painted” to see if 
the material is painted or not. 
Using this convention it would be possible to include even more paint datasets and make the 
paints’ impacts calculation even more accurate. By reading the material class, Dynamo could 
classify the paint in more specified datasets according to the material that the paint is applied 
to. 
Of course this process of renaming the materials in order to contain all the paint information 
needed is done only once. The updated materials are then stored in the Revit material library 
and are available for any new project. To allow the faster renaming of the materials of existing 
projects a special Dynamo script was developed that exports all the Revit materials in an Excel 
sheet where the parameters are filled as shown in Figure 47 and then the new material names 
are imported back to Dynamo which applies them to the Revit materials. This script though will 
not be further analyzed since it is outside the scope of this project. 
6.3.3.6 
Energy LCI 
In the use stage, other than the replacements of the materials, the energy consumption is also a 
big contributor in the stage’s impacts. In fact, energy in the use stage is considerable part of the 
==PAGE:77==
Development 
77 
 
total life cycle impacts of a building. Although, this project focuses mainly in the impacts caused 
by the construction, maintenance and end-of-life of a building, the energy consumption during 
the use stage is also integrated in the model for a holistic study of the impacts to be available. 
The energy delivery to the building is assumed to be in three possible ways, electricity, district 
heating and natural gas. The concept for calculating the impacts of the energy consumption is the 
same as in the case of construction materials. The different energy sources are linked with their

6.4 Pre-check dynamic model 
The accuracy of the produced results by the LCA dynamic model depends on the accuracy of the 
datasets and the accuracy of the information of the Revit model. To make sure that all needed 
information are included in the Revit model, a new dynamic model was built that checks the BIM 
model before the LCA model is run. This dynamic model performs 8 different checks before it 
approves that the BIM model is ready to be used to the LCA dynamic model. Of course the LCA 
dynamic model is built in a way that it surpasses and ignores any potential errors so that it gives 
a final result. This pre-check model’s purpose is only to enhance the accuracy of the LCA model’s 
results by informing the modeler what is not modelled correctly. The checks performed by this 
model are explained in Paragraph 6.4.1. 
6.4.1 Dynamo Script 
The Dynamo script in this case is until some point identical with the one of the LCA dynamic model. 
The database is read, the materials and their properties are gotten and linked with the datasets 
==PAGE:84==
Development 
84 
 
as described in Paragraphs 6.3.3.1 and 6.3.3.2. After this point, instead of calculating the impacts, 
a qualitative check of the information is done according to the data that gathered. 
6.4.1.1 
BIM7AA code missing 
In this check, the BIM7AA code is extracted from the family types’ names. The first characters of 
the types’ names are gotten until the first underscore since this is the name convention followed 
for the family types. To check if these strings correspond to BIM7AA codes there is not a direct 
way except for hardcoding all the BIM7AA codes and comparing the strings with them. This 
strategy though was considered unnecessary and making the script heavier. Instead, these strings 
are just checked if they consist entirely of numerical characters. In case they do, it is assumed that 
they correspond to the BIM7AA code since there is no other reason to start a type’s name with 
numerical characters separated with an underscores with the rest of the text. 
The types the name of which does not start with a BIM7AA code are gathered in a list and then 
all the instances of these types are found. 
6.4.1.2 
Revit materials without IDs 
In this check all the materials’ comment field is gotten. The comment parameter is where the ID 
that links them with the database’s datasets is stored. If a material’s comment parameter is 
empty, then all elements that use this material are gathered. 
6.4.1.3 
Invalid ID 
In this check, the material IDs that are read for the previous check, are checked if they correspond 
to a database dataset. If not, all the elements that contain this material are gathered. 
6.4.1.4 
Datasets with impacts per m2 
As described in Paragraph 6.3.3.2, only the membrane materials and the glazing materials can be 
matched with datasets the impacts of which are measures per m2. All the others are matched with 
datasets with m3.  To check if this rule is followed, Dynamo script checks all the material links’ 
functional unit. If the unit is m2 it checks if the volume of the material is equal to zero. If it is, it 
means that the material is a membrane material and thus the link is correct. If not, it then checks 
if the material is a glazing material. Since there is not a way to check if a material is used for a 
glazing in Revit, the check is performed indirectly by checking if the dataset corresponds to a 
glazing material. This is done by checking the dataset’s ID. It was decided for this purpose that all 
glazing datasets’ IDs will start with the letter G. 
During this check, all elements that use materials that mistakenly are linked to datasets with 
impacts per m2 are collected. 
6.4.1.5 
Wrong dataset unit 
This check is quite similar with the previous one but checks all materials and not only the ones 
linked with m2 datasets. What this check simply does is to check if the unit of the linked dataset 
is m3 or m2. Since all materials’ quantities are gotten in these units, any other unit in the database 
is wrong for this purpose and should not be used. 
6.4.1.6 
Columns’ and Beams’ function 
This check is performed to ensure that all columns and beams have a function value that 
corresponds to the type being used inside or outside the building. As described in Paragraph 
==PAGE:85==
Development 
85 
 
6.3.3.4, function is not a built-in parameter and thus has to be created when the family is 
modelled. 
6.4.1.7 
Compound constructions with missing materials 
In this check all compound elements are checked if all their layers contain a material. This check 
cannot be performed to non-compound elements due to the different API function the materials 
are gotten from. 
6.4.1.8 
Constructions without any materials 
Although it cannot be found if a part of the non-compound elements has not assigned material, 
it can be said if the elements have no materials at all. This check finds all elements that are 
empty of materials.

to get the feedback and correct any errors found. For each check-step, the elements that are

By changing the slider, a preplanned error occurs that blocks all the script’s functionalities except 
for the one that the user has chosen. As shown in Figure 68, between the part for changing the

7 Main dynamic model’s case studies 
 
After the proposed model was developed, it was time to test it in real projects and detect any 
potential malfunctions or failures. The more cases studied the more robust the model would 
become since after any error detection the model was fixed 
Clearly it would be more beneficial to test the model in both existing and new projects. The 
existing projects case studies would give feedback of what should be avoided in terms of 
modelling practice while the new projects case studies would give feedback if the proposed model 
works as planned in terms of integration in the design process and providing useful feedback 
regarding the life cycle impacts of the architects’ choices. Due to lack of time after the model was 
finished though new projects were not used as case studies.   
Testing the model in existing projects came with various challenges that are not relevant in the 
case of new projects. These challenges had to do mainly with the lack of classification system, lack 
of material naming system and the way Revit models were modelled. An example of the latter 
one is the extensive use of generic models as mentioned in Chapter 6 as well. 
The projects that were studied are the “Danmarksgrunden” and the “Havnevigen” designed and 
provided by the “vandkunsten” architectural studio. The life expectancy of the buildings was set 
to 50 years in all cases. Due to lack of time the lifetime of all the materials in the database was 
not set to realistic numbers. Since the lowest lifetime set was 50 years for all materials, in these 
case studies, the use stage is excluded since in any case the impacts from the replacements would 
be equal to zero and the impacts from the energy consumption only complement the total 
assessment.

area is equal to 11.000 m2. The project consists of five buildings that all together offer 72 
apartments.

7.1.1.2 
Useful feedback 
Running this project some errors were detected in the dynamo script. Some of them were minor 
while others caused significant distortion of the final results.  
The most major one had to do with getting the layers and their respective materials from the 
compound constructions. As described in Paragraph 6.3, the life expectancy of the materials is 
==PAGE:103==
Main dynamic model’s case studies 
103 
 
calculated automatically according to the materials’ position in the element and the element’s 
position in the building. To get the information of the materials’ position, the 
“GetCompoundStructure()” Revit API function is used together with “GetMaterialId()” and 
“IsCoreLayer()” (Autodesk 2014). The combination of these functions results in a list of materials 
for each element that correspond to the construction’s layers. This means that if the user has not 
defined a layer’s material in Revit the dynamo script will still create in item in the list but with 
nothing(null) as its content. 
This creation of empty items in the list of materials’ position causes inconsistency with the list of 
materials per type which is created by the “GetMaterialIds()” function. This function works in a 
different way and thus ignores all the layers that do not have any assigned materials. This in 
consistency of the list lengths and the existence of “null” items made the calculation of the 
materials’ life time impossible. 
To address this problem, a new “Check-group” was added in the “Pre-Check” tool where the 
compound constructions are checked for empty layers. In these cases, the user is notified as 
described in Paragraph 6.4. 
Moreover an error was found in the calculation of the impacts per unit. The reason was that since 
the “count” output was added afterwards in the “LCA.GetTypeMaterials” the count list was 
created for all the Revit types without filtering them according to their SfB code. The error was 
simply fixed by passing the count list through the BIM7AA filtering sub-node of the 
“LCA.GetTypeMaterials” node (Paragraph 6.3). 
Furthermore, a minor error was detected in the color coding part of the dynamo script. The color 
sequence started from red to blue and thus the elements with the highest impacts were colored 
blue while the elements with the lowest impacts were colored red. The error was fixed by 
changing the color sequence in the “LCA.ColorSet” node (Paragraph 6.3). 
Finally, an observation was made regarding the way the compound constructions were modelled 
in Revit. It was noticed that most of these family types were not made properly regarding the 
layers’ position. In most of the cases, all the layers were placed in the core of the element. 
Although this did not cause any problems in this case, it could cause small mistakes in the 
calculation of the various layers’ lifetime.

8 Models’ evaluation 
In this chapter the combined dynamic model is evaluated in terms of accuracy of results and time 
needed for a full LCA analysis. This is done by performing the assessment with the developed 
dynamic model, LCAbyg and tally. LCAbyg is chosen because it is the most popular LCA tool for 
assessing buildings in Denmark and its results will be used mainly for assessing the accuracy of 
the developed model. As for tally, it is known that it uses an american database and a different 
LCA method than oekobau and thus a deviation is expected between its results and the results of 
the dynamic model. It is chosen though mainly for comparing the efficiency of the two methods 
in terms of time needed for the assessment of the same building.  
Other than the detailed LCA dynamic model the pre-conceptual one is evaluated as well. This is 
done by simply filling the parameter values as these are known from a finished project and 
checking how close the estimated impacts are to the calculated ones from the detailed dynamic 
model. 
The case which is used for the evaluation is the Danmarksgrunden project provided by tegnestuen 
vandkunsten. In all cases the materials’ lifetimes were set to be larger than the building’s one 
while the energy impacts were not included in the calculation. This way only the manufacturing 
and end-of-life impacts were calculated and compared to each other. To make the analysis of the 
results easier, only the GWP impacts are compared although all the impact categories are 
calculated. 
Before performing the assessments with the above mentioned tools a preliminary evaluation was 
performed as explained in the following paragraph. 
8.1 Simple evaluation of the calculation core of the Detailed LCA dynamic model 
This evaluation step was performed only for making sure that the parts performing the 
calculations in the Dynamo script are correct and no errors occur. Although the calculations are 
simple this step was considered important in order to allow excluding at least some parts of the 
Dynamo script in the case errors are found later and the script needs to be checked. 
To check the calculation part a new database was created only for this purpose. This database 
was identical with the one used normally regarding the structure. The difference is that all impacts 
of materials were set to 1 for both manufacturing and end-of-life stage. This way, the results 
produced by the dynamic model should be equal to the quantities of the Revit materials since the 
results are produced in the material level.

8.5.1 Detailed dynamic model – LCAbyg 
As expected, the impacts calculated in the detailed dynamic model and LCAbyg are quite close 
since the datasets used in both cases are equivalent. The deviation of 4% though is still something 
that worth discussing. Since the datasets used are equivalent, the deviation is caused most 
probably by the quantities used each time and maybe errors during the typing of the various 
values in LCAbyg. Since in LCAbyg, the area of each material was added as its quantity, for the 
compound constructions the element’s area was used for all layers for saving time during the data 
input. On the contrary, since the dynamic model gets all the quantities directly from Revit it uses 
more accurate numbers for each layer’s quantity. The difference in the area of each layer has to 
do with the connection of the various elements (Figure 29 and Appendix B: Revit quantity take-
off examples). On the other hand, it should also be pointed here that there is also this uncertainty 
in the material take-off from Revit as described in Paragraph 6.1. 
Another reason for this deviation could be the simplifications made for the doors and windows 
when these were added in LCAbyg. In order to make the process faster, it was assumed that all 
door panels have a thickenss of 5 cm while all windows frames have a section of 80x50 mm. Any 
muntins separating the glazing panes were not taken into consideration. 
As for the time needed for each assessment, it was from the beginning expected that the dynamic 
model would be much faster since this is also one of the reasons it was developed. The dynamic 
model produced all results in less than one minute while LCAbyg needed more than 15-20 hours.

Although, the manual entry of datasets in order to render the results comparable consumed a 
significant amount of time, LCAbyg still demands a considerable amount of time to set up the

9 Discussion 
After the performed case studies and the evaluation of developed dynamic models, it appears 
that these models could potentially be useful for engineers and architects who try to monitor and 
minimize the environmental impacts of their design in all stages, from the pre-conceptual to the 
end of the detailed one. Nevertheless, there are some factors that can affect the validity of the 
results of the developed dynamic models. These factors together with future development and 
improvement potentials are analyzed in this chapter.  
9.1 Validity 
Although the evaluation part of this report showed that the developed models serve the purpose 
they were built for, there are some points that can affect the final results they produce. In this 
point it needs to be pointed that these models are not autonomous tools and the quality of their 
results depend mostly in the input data that are provided to them.  
9.1.1 Main dynamic model 
For the main dynamic model the factors that affect the final result’s quality are distinguished to 
human factor and Revit factor. The human factor has to do with all data that is created and 
provided from the practitioner. The Revit factor has to do with all restrictions and weaknesses 
coming from Revit. 
Although the dynamic model was developed in a way to minimize the human factor, it is still 
considered to be the main factor that can cause inaccuracies and errors. The most obvious part 
of the process that involves the human factor is the building of the BIM model. The dynamic model 
is developed to calculate the impacts of the geometry of the BIM model. Thus, the precision of 
the BIM model’s geometry is strongly related with the accuracy of the final results. Although the 
model can work well with simplified and generic materials the results it generates are the same 
simplified and generic. This issue is not that simple though. The quantity take-off and the way it 
is performed can affect the total impacts in a very high extend. More detailed take-offs most 
probably lead to higher impacts giving the practitioner no motivation for creating a detailed 
quantity take-off. Although, as described in Paragraph 2.1, DGNB defines the elements needed 
for an LCA performance there should be more specific guidelines about the level of detail for each 
construction type separately. As far as the use of the BIM model for quantity take-off is concerned, 
this assurance of level of detail could be achieved by the development and establishment of an 
LOD (level of development) (BIMForum 2015), relevant for LCA. Similarly with other cases like 
energy analysis or structural analysis, the LOD standard for LCA will specify the information each 
element should include in the BIM model. Thus, by demanding a specific LOD level for all building 
LCAs it could be assured that the quantity take-off is satisfactory. 
Besides the BIM model’s geometry and its level of detail, other aspects where errors can be 
caused by the human factor are the database and the materials’ matching with the database’s 
datasets. Although these two occur only once when the dynamic model is set up, it is still pointed 
that they can cause errors. Since there is currently not a standardized LCI database for 
construction materials, all data have to be modified and structured in a way that the dynamic 
model can read. During this data transformation, errors might occur that if not noticed will be 
transferred to the final results of the model. The existence of a standardized database would solve 
==PAGE:116==
Discussion 
116 
 
this issue since the dynamic model could be modified to read that database instead and exclude 
this way the human factor. An example of such a database could be said that is the Quartz 
database which is presented in Paragraph 6.2.3.  
As for the Revit factor, it is mainly meant the ability of Revit to perform accurate material take-
offs. As it was pointed in Paragraph 6.1, there seems to be a small error in Revit’s material take-
off which is detected to its ability to determine the various elements’ joins and how their layers 
are connected. This issue was detected during the 3-week special project held by the author 
before this project and was intended to be investigated further. Due to the emerge of more 
important issues though, this was not possible. Although the error is not considered significant, it 
should be quantified in order to accompany the final results as a deviation value.  
9.1.2 Pre-conceptual dynamic model 
The pre-conceptual stage dynamic model was developed for estimating the final impacts of a 
building based on some loose parameters and for comparing different material strategies. The 
evaluation of the model showed that regarding the estimation, it can only perform a rough 
estimation of the final impacts. Although the results produced by this model were not expected 
to be very close to the final results, the level of estimation depends exclusively on the data 
provided by the user. The better estimation of the various geometrical values needed, the more 
precise the estimation will be. To render the model more precise, further work needs to be done 
(9.2.2). Regarding the comparison purpose of this model, it is considered that the model still can 
be useful, since the comparison will be performed between results of the model itself and not 
with results coming from other applications or methods. 
9.2 Future development 
The developed models have shown that they have a lot of potential to be used in reality. Some 
improvements would provide them with more functionalities, increase their accuracy and render 
them more useful. Furthermore, these developed models could be the basis for further work in 
the field of LCA and its integration in BIM.  
9.2.1 Main dynamic model improvements 
A series of improvements would make the main dynamic model even better in many aspects. Such 
improvements include the color coding capabilities, the energy consumption impacts and some 
additions like uncertainty analysis. 
The color coding as currently is performed uses relative values for assigning colors. This means 
that the same colors represent different values in each assessment since they depend on the 
maximum and the minimum impact existing in that particular iteration. It would be beneficial if 
the practitioner could also be able to select the color coding to be with absolute values meaning 
that the minimum and maximum values for each impact category would be steady for every 
assessment and according to these all the elements would be colored. Although this is not a hard 
thing to implement in terms of programming, these benchmarks have to be first established. 
Using these benchmarks as the bases for color coding it would be easier for the practitioner to 
visualize how low or high the impacts are per element. 
As for the energy related impacts the way these are calculated now are by manually entering the 
energy consumption in the Dynamo script. It would be a more holistic solution if the energy 
==PAGE:117==
Discussion 
117 
 
analysis could be somehow be part of the dynamic model. Although a full energy analysis 
simulation could need a lot of time to perform, linking a more simplified energy tool could give 
more instant feedback on the energy related impacts. 
The sensitivity and uncertainty analysis functionality was something that was also mentioned 
during the interview with vandkunsten in Chapter 5. This functionality would help to more easily 
detect the materials that are responsible for the largest portion of the impacts and define the 
uncertainty of the final results.  
Finally, a small modification is proposed that would make the model run even faster. Regarding 
the functionality of changing thickness, it would be beneficial in terms of time if the impacts were 
calculated without actually changing the thicknesses in the BIM model. There could be a final step 
where the practitioner approves that wants the imported from excel thicknesses to be applied in 
the BIM model. This way there could be many iterations until the impacts minimized and then the 
thicknesses are changed only once. 
9.2.2 Pre-conceptual stage dynamic model improvements 
Regarding the pre-conceptual stage dynamic model, it is stated again that its purpose is 
exclusively for a rough estimation of the impacts based on some rough estimations on the 
geometry and the materials’ selection. The evaluation of the model in Paragraph 8.5.3 showed 
that the estimated results where far different (-65%) from the detailed ones. Although this does 
not overcome the model’s ability to be used for estimation and comparison purposes in the pre-
conceptual stage, it clearly shows that there is need for improvement. The enrichment of the 
database and the enhancement of the construction’s formation in the input data sheet would 
render the model more detailed and thus more accurate. 
On the other hand, the observation that a simplified model cannot estimate closely the final 
impacts questions the overall need for such a tool in this stage. The case study of 
Danmarksgrunden showed that it is hard to simplify the detailed material choices to some 
predefined construction types. In the same way when in a new project the materials are not 
known and the construction types are chosen from a predefined list, it is quite unlikable that the 
material changes will not affect the final impacts in a high extend. Moreover, the option of 
enriching the model’s database and thus the available options for constructions selection might 
not be relevant for this stage of the design process. Since in this stage a geometry model is not 
yet set up it might be hard to make decisions regarding the various elements’ materials. Thus, 
even when such decisions are made, it is possible that changes will be done which will affect the 
final impacts. To conclude, two are the main questions about the pre-conceptual stage model. 
The one is if there is really a need for such a tool in this design stage and what changes could 
render it more accurate. 
 
9.2.3 Life Cycle Cost 
A similar approach with the one followed in this project could be used for performing Life Cycle 
Costing. If cost could be considered as just one more impact category, then the appropriate 
modification in the database could render the dynamic model capable of performing some kind 
==PAGE:118==
Discussion 
118 
 
of LCC. Although the cost cannot be directly related to the materials used in a building, it could at 
least be used as a rough estimation or for comparison purposes. 
On the other hand with a modification in the whole way the BIM model is connected with the 
database, an accurate LCC could be performed. The key for such a model would be the quantity 
take-off transition from the material level to an element level. In the same way, the database 
would consist of costs of the various constructions and not materials. 
Similarly to the material library used in this project, a Revit family type library would be used 
where all the family types would be linked with a dataset in the database. Of course this concept 
involves more manual work since unlike the materials the family types that are used in a project 
could easily not be possible to be linked with a dataset just because of a layer’s different thickness 
or a different material use. 
9.2.4 Connection with LCAbyg 
The most interesting and promising thought for future development though could may be the 
development of a new dynamic model that would include LCAbyg. LCAbyg is the most recognized 
software in Denmark for performing LCA for buildings. As found in the Danmarksgrunden case 
study (Paragraph 8.5.1) its main drawback is the time needed to set up the inventory. This 
drawback could be surpassed if there was an importing functionality. Since the data needed in 
LCAbyg follow a very specific structure, an XML file could be the ideal option for importing these 
data. This XML file the structure of which would be known and very specific could be produced in 
Dynamo by getting all relevant data from the BIM model. Ideally this process could decrease the 
time needed now in LCAbyg in just a few minutes. 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:119==
Conclusion 
119 
 
10 Conclusion 
The outcome of this project shows that the use of LCA integrated dynamic models could be useful 
during the design process of a building.  
The case studies of the main dynamic model developed in this project showed that such a model 
could facilitate the assessment of a building from the early stages of design and provide useful 
feedback in a short amount of time. The level of detail of the produced results facilitates the 
further analysis by the practitioner. The results can be interpreted in material, construction type 
and construction category level, helping the practitioner perform a hotspot analysis, detect 
materials and constructions causing higher impacts and perform the necessary changes. The 
virtual representation of the results directly in the BIM environment by color-coding of the 
building elements is also a feature that is proven helpful in terms of quickly detecting the hotspots 
in the construction type level.  
As for the pre-conceptual stage model, it is pointed out that further development needs to be 
done until it can be able to provide more accurate estimations. As also discussed in Chapter 9, the 
need of such a tool in this early stage of design is questioned since more detailed BIM models are 
built more and more even in the early stages. The developed model as it is presented in this 
report, could still be used for comparison purposes and for a very rough estimation of the 
building’s impacts.  
Regarding the complexity, the use of dynamic models for LCA is considered not more complicated 
than the use of conventional simplified LCA tools. The complexity in this case has mainly to do 
with the use of the VPL language. The practitioner has to have some basic knowledge in VPL 
Dynamo while at least one practitioner in the design team should be able to verify the final results, 
maintain the dynamic model and be in charge of updating the LCI database. On the other hand, 
the assessments are performed much faster in the dynamic model while LCA knowledge is not a 
prerequisite for a practitioner to perform them. This, combined with the fact that the inventory 
is automatically updated when changes occur in the geometrical model, actually enables the 
production of more results with a lot of iterations in considerably less time and leaves the LCA 
specialist of the design team more time to only analyze the results instead of creating them and 
then analyze them. In the same time the modeler can use the time saved for more iterations or 
even other tasks more relevant to their discipline. 
To conclude with, this project showed that the use of LCA integrated dynamic models can help 
towards the integration of LCA in the design process and the design of more sustainable 
constructions in terms of environmental impacts. The developed dynamic models with some 
further development and improvements could be used by design teams while they could be the 
basis for development of new more stable and precise LCA dynamic models.

Appendix A: Main Dynamic Tool Guidelines 
This appendix contains some basic and quick guidelines of how to set-up the main dynamic model 
that was developed in this project and how to set-up the studied Revit model to enable it to run 
properly in the dynamic model. The guidelines are given in form of steps as followed: 
1. Software installation 
The software used for the model’s development are Revit 2016, Dynamo 0.9.0 and 
Microsoft Office Excel 2013. Although newer versions of Revit and Excel are not expected 
to cause any problems, the same cannot be ensured for the case of Dynamo where some 
built-in nodes might need to be replaced with new ones. 
2. Dynamo set-up 
The dynamic model was entirely built with built-in nodes and custom nodes written in 
python. In order for Dynamo to read the custom nodes, their definitions have to be 
imported. The definitions are uploaded in CampusNet as Appendix C: Dynamo Definitions 
together with this report. The file path containing the definitions has to be defined in 
Dynamo on the “Manage Node and Package Paths” in settings. 
3. Revit model set-up 
To prepare the Revit model to work with the dynamic model, two things have to be done, 
the BIM7AA coding and the material matching with the database’s dataset. The BIM7AA 
has to be applied in the beginning of the family types’ name separated with an underscore 
(_) from the rest of the name. The dataset’s ID that correspond to each material is applied 
in the material’s “Comments” section. For both cases assisting Dynamo scripts are 
provided (Appendix D: Relevant files) to facilitate the process. 
 
4. Revit model pre-check 
The Pre-check step is optional but is recommended for ensuring the validity of the final 
results. For this step, the Pre-Check Dynamo script has to be run (Appendix D: Relevant 
files). The script will locate and color all element that miss materials, ID links or have any 
other errors as explained in Paragraph 6.4. 
 
5. Dynamo script input data 
Before running the dynamic model certain information have to be given in the Dynamo 
script. All parts that need inputs from the user are painted purple. These include the 
database’s file path, the results’ file path, the building life expectancy, the color-coding 
settings, the energy consumption, the total building area, the energy sources IDs and the 
paint IDs from the database. Some of these of course are specified only once since they 
are not expected to change in every assessment. 
 
6. Changing impact categories order

When changing the order of impact categories in the database, the translator sheet has 
to be updated as well. 
 
7. Adding new impact categories in the database 
If new impact categories are added in the database, the respective new cells have to be

in the “Read Database” group of the Dynamo script has to be updated. An example of
