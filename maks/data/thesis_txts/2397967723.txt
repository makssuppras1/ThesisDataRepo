Abstract
This thesis strives to investigate the behaviour of piston ring materials in dry slid-
ing conditions against a laser hardened cylinder liner material and a base cylinder
liner material. This is done via experiments made on a block on ring test rig.
The thermal expansion is determined via ANSYS which makes it possible to de-
termine the wear amount over time through distance probes. The wear track on
the surfaces is investigated after the experiments in order to determine how the
wear behaves, this is done via microscope pictures and SEM images.

Introduction
This thesis aims to investigate the tribological behaviour of diﬀerent piston ring
materials in sliding contact with diﬀerent cylinder liner surfaces. Piston rings are
of big importance, as they among other things seal the combustion chamber, thus
it is desired to reduce the wear of the piston ring and cylinder liner as wear leads
to a loss of eﬃciency. In order to investigate the tribological behaviour, MAN
Diesel & Turbo have produced a cylinder liner material with a laser hardening
surface. The idea of hardening the surface of the cylinder liner is to reduce the
wear, as a harder material leads to less wear according to Archards wear model.
The laser hardened cylinder liner material is tested in regards to diﬀerent piston
ring materials. The piston ring materials tested appear in diﬀerent hardnesses
and coatings. The desire is to investigate how these piston ring materials behave
with the new cylinder liner surface, in a dry contact situation. The dry contact
situation is chosen, as it provokes a scuﬃng situation, which basically is a lag of
lubrication in a sliding contact. Therefore the sliding condition tests the scuﬃng
resistance of the piston ring and cylinder liner materials.
The test components are investigated on a block on ring test rig, where it is possible
to yield the wear amount, friction force and temperatures.
2.1
Problem
By using a block on ring test rig it is possible to extract diﬀerent parameters
which describe the behaviour of the components in contact. It is desired to use
the achieved empirical data to investigate the behaviour of diﬀerent piston ring ma-
terial in regards to a laser hardened cylinder liner surface. In order to accomplish
this purpose, two main problems are identiﬁed:
• Ranking of the materials in question
• Construction of an empirical equation for the wear
2.2
Test setup
Test segments
The object is, as described earlier, to investigate the performance of piston ring
segments and wheels. On ﬁgure(1) a ring segment and a wheel is shown. The
wheels have no variation in geometry and only diﬀer in material properties, the
outer diameter of the wheel is 98mm with a width of 30mm. Two types of wheels
are investigated, a cast iron wheel and a laser hardened wheel. The ring segments
have a general geometry of 10x5x8mm, where the area of contact is 10x5mm. Five
diﬀerent ring segments are investigated, a cast iron ring and two diﬀerent cermet
coatings (CM1 and CM2) in two versions a ground(S) and an unground(U).

The CM1S is the cermet coating grounded ring segment. The base material is

to resist scuﬃng. The diﬀerence of the cermet coatings is the composition of
the powder and the adding process. The CM1S initial powder consist of a
mixture of two powders, a nickel-chromium and chromium carbides powder
and a molybdenum powder. The powder is applied by atmospheric plasma
spraying. The CM1 coating has a microhardness of minimum 500mHV and

Test rig
The piston ring and wheel are investigated using a block on ring test rig, the
main purpose of this experiment is to receive the necessary parameters, which
then can be used for data processing. The wanted data from the experiments are
the wear, the friction force and the temperature, which this experiment comprises.
On ﬁgure(2) the experiment setup is shown. The experiment works by applying
mass on sled 13 or the counter sled 12, both mounted on the bridge, by doing
so a pressure is applied on the piston ring segment mounted on 6. The piston
ring is in contact with the test wheel 5 rotating on the shaft 4. As mentioned
the purpose is to measure the wear, the friction force and the temperature. The
temperature is measured at 6 where a thermocouple is mounted through a hole in
the piston ring segment. The hole in the piston ring is 0.5 mm from the contact
point, and it is thereby assumed to measure the temperature in the contact area.
The thermocouple is measuring within a tolerance of ±2.2 degrees celcius.
A load cell is mounted at 3 and used to measure the friction force. The load cell
is a HBM 0.5 kN load cell.
At 6 two distance probes are mounted, these are used to ﬁnd the wear, as the
travelling distance towards a steel plate placed beneath the probe, is a measure of
how much material has worn of the piston ring and wheel while running.
Over 11, pointing at the wheel surface, a infrared temperature sensor is placed.
The purpose of this sensor is to measure the surface temperature of the wheel.
The sensor operates within a accuracy of ±1 percent.

3.1
Friction
When dry friction is considered three main laws apply for most sliding conditions[1]:
• The friction force is proportional to the normal load.
• The friction is independent of the contact area.
• The friction force is independent of the sliding velocity.
The ﬁrst two laws are accepted as being true when metals are in contact, whereas
the last law has to be used with care. When sliding begins the friction is generally
higher than the friction needed to preserve the sliding. Nevertheless when sliding
is preserved, the friction is independent of the velocity. Bowden and Tabor came
up with an expression for the adhesive term of the friction:

3.2
Wear
In general wear can be described within 5 diﬀerent types of material loss, abra-
sion, adhesion, fatigue, erosion and corrosion [1]. In this section the abrasion and
adhesion wear will be described as this is the main focus throughout the report.
Wear is a complex phenomena as many parameters contribute to the material loss,
such as sliding speed, applied load, lubrication and the environments condition [2].
Furthermore the friction in the contact point have an inﬂuence of the wear rate,
but it is diﬃcult to predict whether low friction will cause an increase or decrease
in the wear rate [2], thus a theoretical model of a wear case have to be implemented
through the parameters that were found experimentally. The material of the parts
in contact is of importance, as the material in contact is mainly cast iron, which
contains graphite. This will aﬀect the coeﬃcient of friction as the graphite acts as
a solid lubricant which might lead to an inﬂuence of the wear rate.
Adhesive wear
When two solid materials are sliding relative to each other in dry conditions,
asperities will interact and cold weld. When these welding asperities separate due
to the sliding, the material will become worn oﬀ, giving a loss of material. The
adhesive wear situation is shown in ﬁgure (3) it can be seen that after the welding
is formed, the material will break of from the softer material, or the material with
the lowest yield strength [1].
Figure 3: Adhesive wear during sliding [3]
The main theory for adhesive wear was establish by Bowden and Tabor, this basic
theory was further developed by Archard [4], and a basic equation for adhesive
wear was developed. The main laws of adhesive wear are [1]:
• The material removed is directly proportional to the sliding distance.
• The material removed is directly proportional to the load applied.

The phenomenon scuﬃng is related to adhesive wear, as lack of lubricant in the
surface leads to micro welding of the asperity tops. When the shearing force then
act on these welded tops a separation of the asperity tops occur and a loss of
material is achieved. This happens locally thus the local temperature increases
rapidly during scuﬃng.

Previous work
Most of the attempts to establish a theoretical model of wear is based on the Ar-
chard’s wear equations. The models are either based on adhesive wear, or abrasive
wear depending on the wear situation. Some attempts are to implement a model
10
==PAGE:11==
that will predict the wear for the coupling wear behaviour made between two wear
models.
The abrasive wear is either produced by sharp asperities cutting the material, or
produced by debris stuck in between the two materials in contact. When choosing
an abrasive wear model, this has to be taken into consideration, as this change
how the abrasion wear takes place.
Chen Yunxia, Gong Wenjun , Kang Rui Coupling behavior between adhe-
sive and abrasive wear mechanism of aero-hydraulic spool valves (2006)
This approach of making a wear model is based on archard’s wear model. The
idea is to establish a model which couples the abrasive wear and the adhesive wear
in lubricated conditions. Due to the assumption of dry contact condition in the
adhesive wear model, a fractional ﬁlm deﬂect index is implemented, which then
describes the lubrication in the contact area. The normal force is replaced with
an asperity load which is determined by a probability distribution function due to
the unknown asperities in contact.
The abrasive wear model used is a three body model, which describes the wear due
to particle wear between the contact surfaces. The abrasive wear coeﬃcient is the
unknown parameter in this model, and is determined by splitting the coeﬃcient
in six diﬀerent coeﬃcients describing the parameters aﬀecting the abrasive wear.
These models are coupled in order to make a dynamic wear model describing both
abrasive and adhesive wear.
Simon C. Tung, Yong Huang
Modeling of AbrasiveWear in a Piston Ring
and Engine Cylinder Bore System (2004)
The purpose of this paper is to make an three body abrasive wear model for a
piston ring-cylinder liner system in steady state condition. This model is based on
Archard’s wear model, further developed by Rabinowicz which takes hardness of
both materials into account.
In order to describe the normal load, it is assumed that the normal load is sup-
ported by the particles uniformly in the contact area. Giving a model which takes
into account the sliding length during a time interval, number of particles in con-
tact, the normal force supported by the particles in contact and the hardness of
both materials. This is further developed by adding the surface roughness as a co-
eﬃcient and the oil degradation, which has to be found experimental. This model
is used to establish a numerical model for the prediction of wear.
Nicolas Fillot, Ivan Iordanoﬀ, Yves Berthier Simulation of Wear Through
Mass Balance in a Dry Contact (2005)
The wear model establish in this paper strive to make a three body abrasive wear
model based on mass equilibrium. In order to develop this model the wear process
is seen as a mass stuck inside the contact surfaces, a ﬂow of this mass ejecting
the particles out of the contact area and a recirculation ﬂow which will lead some
of the ejected particles back into the contact area. From numerical simulations a
wear behaviour of the diﬀerent ﬂows is produced
Pardeep Sharmaa, Dinesh Khandujaa, Satpal Sharmab Dry sliding wear
investigation of Al6082/Gr metal matrix composites by response surface method-
ology (2015)
11
==PAGE:12==
A wear model for dry sliding is establish in this paper by applying a response sur-
face mythodology method. The main idea of the method is to choose the varying
parameters, in this example, load, sliding speed, sliding length and reinforcement
was chosen. The design parameters is then run with 5 diﬀerent values given a large
matrix of design parameter input and a wear output. This method then yields a
wear equation with the desired design parameters.

Temperature sensor
As explained earlier an infrared temperature sensor is mounted over the bridge,
measuring the surface temperature of the wheel. The sensors output will change
with diﬀerent target materials as the emissivity ratio diﬀers from the material
properties in contact. This emissivity ratio is an input when calibrating the sen-
sor. As the emissivity ratio is unknown, another approach of calibrating the sensor
has been carried out. The thermocouple is assumed to measure the right tempera-
ture, as it exactly measures 100 degrees Celsius in boiling water and correct room
temperature. In order to calibrate the infrared sensor an experiment is carried
out by heating up the wheel by the friction power applied in the contact area.
The motor is stopped and the thermocouple is mounted at the target point of the
infrared sensor, which is known by a laser beam from the infrared sensor pointing
at the target point. The thermocouple then measures the exact temperature in the
contact point over time, which can be used to calculate a regression for converting
the infrared temperature. The thermocouple has a response time when measuring
the temperature which means it does not measure the exact temperature when
mounted on the wheel, but needs time to reach the exact temperature. This is
due to the diﬀerence in start temperature as the thermocouple starts at a lower
temperature than the heated wheel. The process of the experiment is shown on
ﬁgure(10), in the beginning of the test the thermocouple is mounted in the contact
area, where the temperature reaches 100 degrees. After 50 seconds the motor is
stopped and the thermocouple is mounted on the wheel. When the thermocouple
reaches a steady temperature the data for the calibration begins, which means
everything before a steady temperature is not considered. On ﬁgure (11a) the
temperature measurement of the two sensors in the contact point on the surface

5.2
Initial tests
Before starting the experiments, an initial test is made, this is done in order to
make ensure that the experiment produces usable measurements. As the wear is
wanted it is important that it somehow can be extract from the experiments, this
can be done by using the distance transducers. Another way of getting a wear
measurement, is to weight the piston ring before and after the experiments, the
disadvantage of this approach is that one has to clean the piston ring thoroughly,
as dust particles or debris attached to the piston ring will lead to incorrect mea-
surements. As for the wheel one can measure the surface roughness, thus knowing
the wear within the tolerance of the roughness measurement tool. The easiest
method is using the installed distance transducers and this method also have the
huge advantage of being able to measure the wear as a function of the time.

5.4.2
Simulation of thermal expansion
A Finite element method analyse of the thermal expansion is made to achieve the
expansion over time, hence determine the wear over time. First the FEM analyse
is veriﬁed by setting up the simple cases derived experimentally.
When these
results are within reasonable percentage of error, the model is extended to cover
the desired experiments. The FEM analyse is carried out in ANSYS Workbench
as this tool is easy to apply, especially for import of CAD models, set up mesh and
extracting results. The general model used to simulate the thermal deformation is
shown on ﬁgure(22)

First an approach for solving the cases has to be decided, there are several ways
of establishing the desired thermal expansion model. Common for all the methods
is that one has to make a correlation between a thermal and structural analyse in
a transient simulation as the deformation over time is needed. The three methods
listed below has been considered.
1. The Piston ring and wheel are in frictional contact in a structural analysis,
by applying a rotation and a normal load on the piston ring, the temperature
of the parts can be determined and hence the deformation.
2. The temperatures in the contact area and in the wheel surface are known
from the experiments, by applying these temperatures in a thermal analy-
sis, the temperature throughout the parts (not only at the surface) can be
determined and the deformation can be found in a structural analyse.
3. As the applied frictional power is known, it can be used in a thermal analyse
to determine the temperature for the parts, then the deformation can be
found in a structural analyse
Method 1 is the most realistic method, it is based on using an element type in
which couples the thermal and structural analysis, here Element type SOLID226
is a good ﬁt. By using the Keyopt and rmodif it is possible to enable heat dis-
sipation in a structural analysis. Unfortunately problems occur when it comes to
applying a rotational velocity while the model is in frictional contact. This is due
to the mesh which somehow has to be ﬂexible as the notes in the contact area will
be stretched. Even though this method seems promising, to much time was used
trying to implement a rotational velocity, thus the method was not used.
Method 2 takes advantage of the knows temperature found in the experiments.
During a transient thermal analyse in ANSYS workbench it is possible to apply
the temperature over time in the contact surface and at the wheel surface. The
thermal analyse then determines the temperature throughout all the parts over
time, this output is then transferred to a structural analysis which determines the
deformation over time.

6
Experimental analysis and ranking of materi-
als
The experiments are carried out on the block on ring test rig described earlier.
When it comes to the ranking of materials it is important to keep the experiment
conditions constant and make sure that these do not deviate from experiment to
experiment. If the conditions are not kept constant the diﬀerent experiments can
not be compared hence the ranking of the materials is not possible.
Diﬀerent
experiments have been carried out, with both an applied load program of step
load and of constant load. Further a motor and gearbox change has been done,
which is explained later in this section. All of these actions aﬀect the experimental
conditions, hence one has to be careful when comparing the experiments. All of
the experiments have some conditions in common, the piston ring segment is thor-
oughly cleaned with acetone and the mass is measured. This mass of the piston
ring segment is used as a measurement of the wear. Further all other components
are cleaned with acetone in order to eliminate impurities such as old lubrication
or debris from old tests which will disturb the results. After the experiment the
piston ring is again cleaned thoroughly with acetone and the mass is measured
hence a measurement of piston ring segment wear is known.
In order to achieve reasonable results, the noise which appears on the result curves
is smoothed, by applying a moving average with a window of 30. Before applying
the moving average the curves are averaged over each second, this is done in order
to apply the thermal deformation compensation and when the results from equal
experiments has to be assembled.

This increase in friction is then followed by a quick decrease and then a slow
increase towards a peak in the friction coeﬃcient. This is the basic behaviour
before the peak for all the tests except the CM1U and the CI specimens, which
have a quick increase to the peak value. On ﬁgure(43) the ﬁrst stage of the friction
behaviour for the test specimens are shown. From ﬁgure(43b) the CI and CM1U
ring segments are shown, within the ﬁrst 10 minutes the peak for these segments
is reached. While the rest of the specimens have a quick decrease in friction as
shown on ﬁgure(43a) before a slow increase towards the peak. The initial increase
in the coeﬃcient of friction is due to the high friction force needed to begin sliding.
A metal to metal contact might also inﬂuence the coeﬃcient of friction in the start
up, before degradation of the oxide layer begins[9]. The oxide layer is degraded
in diﬀerent wear rates. The quick increase to the peak for the CI and CM1U ring
segments suggests that the oxide layer is worn oﬀquickly. This leads to a metal
to metal contact given a steady coeﬃcient of friction for a while, followed by a
slow decrease in friction. For the test specimens shown in ﬁgure(43a) the decrease
initiates the oxide degradation. The behaviour of the degradation is diﬀerent for
all the test specimens, in general the CMS ring segments use a longer time to
reach a peak level. Further the peak value for the CM1 rings are higher than the
CM2 segments and they terminate at a higher value. The diﬀerence between the
CMU and CMS is the roughness which is lager for the CMU rings. As the only
deviation of the material is the roughness, the friction between the two materials
should behave more or less the same. On ﬁgure(44) the ground and unground
material are compared, the behaviour of the ground and unground material is
clearly not the same. On ﬁgure(44a) the CM2 ring segments are shown, it is seen
that the ground material has a slow increase until the steady friction coeﬃcient is
reached. The unground material has a quicker increase reaching a peak in friction,
followed by a slow decrease until a steady value is reached. As explained earlier the
only diﬀerence lies in the roughness of the materials, the deviation must then be
found in the asperities in contact. As the rougher surface has a higher coeﬃcient

angle which is larger, leaving a frictional ploughing term larger than the one for the

Conclusion
The friction force suggests that the diﬀerent ring segments behave diﬀerently. The
degradation of the oxide layer seems to appear at diﬀerent wear rates. The laser
hardened wheel provides a lower coeﬃcient of friction throughout the experiments
compared to the base material. The degradation of the oxide layer for the laser
hardened wheel occurs during the whole experiment, which might indicate that the
oxide layer on the laser hardened wheel is never worn completely. The graphites
lubricating eﬀect in the contact surface is more evident when base material is
in contact with base material.
It is observed that when a coated ring is used
the source of the graphite is from the base material wheel. Whereas the laser
hardened wheel has a low amount of graphite reaching the surface. When it comes
to the wear, the unground material perform better. Yet the wear on the counter
part is larger when using a unground coating. The wear observed on the laser
hardened wheel surface is close to nothing, where the main wear appears on the
ring segment. It can be concluded that the laser hardened wheel perform better
in a scuﬃng environment.

6.5.3
Metallurgical conclusion
The wear situation for the diﬀerent test has been establish, both adhesive and
abrasive wear has been observed. Further oxide traces on the surface is present
which suggests oxidation wear on every test specimen. The graphite traces on the
surface was much more clear when base material was in contact with base material.
The most dominant wear situation was adhesive wear probably due to the lack of
oxide layer which leads to a metal to metal contact provoking scuﬃng. Scuﬃng
was found as newly born martensite when looking at the base material of the
ring segments in the constant load case. The most severe adhesive wear situation
was found when base material was in contact with base material, thus the coated
rings accomplished a more scuﬃng resistance sliding, it should be noticed that
scuﬃng was found on every base material wheel, yet the base material of ring
segments seem to cause a more severe scuﬃng situation. The abrasive wear was
more noticeable when using the unground material as the rougher surface tend to
cut through the counter material. The graphite structure in the base material rings
was observed at the surface, thus the base material of the rings was behaving as
expected with graphite lubricating the surface during the experiments as suggests
in the earlier section. When comparing the diﬀerent coating materials it was seen
that the damage on the surface was reduced when the CM2 was used, this is
due to the higher hardness of the material. Further comparing the ground and
unground material, a small amount of diﬀerence in the surfaces was shown, yet
59
==PAGE:60==
the unground material appeared more damaged. When the material is ground the
surface is more smooth leaving a larger asperity area in the contact surface. The
asperity tops which are removed when grinding seems to leave a more noticeable
wear on the surface. Yet it does not show anything about the amount of wear,
even though the damage on the unground material seems more, the wear amount
of the ring segment was less. As shown earlier the amount of wear of the unground
ring segments is less, but looking at the counter part, the wear amount is larger.
This suggests that the wear debris leaves more damage on the counter part when
the ring segment is unground. Comparing the wheels it was clear to see that when
using laser hardened wheels the damage on surface was reduced drastically. All the
wheels and ring segment pictures not shown in this section are listed in appendix.
7
Future Work
The most important work which should be done in the future is producing a lot
more experiments, especially for the laser hardened wheel. By producing more
experiments error made throughout the individually experiments will decrease as
more experiments will cover the averaging.
Further the laser hardened wheel
should be run against diﬀerent coated ring segments, hence a larger comparison
basis will establish how the laser hardened wheel will behave. The work in the
future should deal with a lubricated contact, which will determine how the laser
hardened wheel will perform at an overall level. Moreover the ring segments be-
haviour when it comes to a lubricated surface might be completely diﬀerent, as
the rougher surface might penetrate the lubricating layer faster. This gives a com-
pletely diﬀerent wear situation, which might indicate that the ground material
behaves better when scuﬃng occurs. By doing more experiments and during more
varying experiments where lubrication is also taken into consideration , a proper
ranking of the materials can be done. This includes wear maps, which leaves a
great illustration on how the materials behave under diﬀerent load situation, like
varying sliding velocities, applied loads, and environment.
The construction of a empirical equation, is not made in this thesis, as the amount
of variation in the experiments were inadequate. As usable experiments were pro-
duced with ﬁxed running conditions, the varying parameter would be limited to
the hardness of the material. As explained earlier, more varying experiments will
lead to a possibility of using a response surface methodology, therefor a lot more
experiments are yet to be done.
8
Conclusion
A thermal expansion of the components in the test set up was carried out in AN-
SYS, this lead to a possibility of using the distance probes mounted on the test
rig. A lot of vibration was noticed throughout the ﬁrst experiments, therefore a
investigation of the placement of the load sleds in order to minimize the vibrations
was carried out. This lead to a situation in which a small amount of vibrations
was noticed.
The laser hardened wheel has been tested against ring segments, and a clear dif-
60
==PAGE:61==
ference in wear amount, coeﬃcient of friction and temperature is observed. The
coeﬃcient of friction of the laser hardened wheel is less throughout the experi-
ments compared to base material wheels and the temperature is less and more
stable, this suggests that the laser hardened wheel performs better in a scuﬃng
situation. The wear amount of the laser hardened wheel is less compared to the
base material. This again suggests less scuﬃng has taken place. Even though the
graphite was not as active when it comes to lubricating the surface, a less wear
was observed. This was due to the harder surface, which kept the oxide layer, and
minimizing the metal to metal contact. This was also noticed while investigating
the surface of the laser hardened wheel, where a mild wear situation was noticed
with a low amount of adhesion compared to base material wheels. The oxide layer
of the surface was noticeable on the laser hardened wheel, which suggests that
oxidation wear has taken place. As the amount of oxide on the surface was larger
it indicated that the oxide layer was not degraded completely which was the case
for the base material wheel.
Comparing the coated ring segments, it was noticed that in general adhesive wear
was dominating the wear situation, yet more abrasive wear was noticed for the
unground material, which was due to the rougher surface. Due to the larger sur-
face in the contact area more wear was noticed on the ground material, leading
to more adhesion and more scuﬃng. In general the CM2 ring segments performed
better than the CM1 ring segments.
