Abstract  
In order to decrease the sintering temperature to (600-900°C) of solid oxide electrolysis 
cells, different electrode materials have been studied. The electrode material Cerium–
Gadolinium oxide (CGO) have been sintered and examined on supports made of steel. 
Sintering additives such as lithium carbonate (Li2CO3) and bismuth oxide (Bi2O3) have 
been used in order to improve the structure densification at low temperature (below 1000 
°C). Besides, the ramping rate was increased from 1°C/min to 3°C/min, 5 °C/min, 10 
°C/min and 15 °C/min in order to avoid evaporating the sintering additives and thereby 
achieving a denser structure of the electrode.  
Furthermore, sintering in reducing atmosphere with 5% H2 in N2 through a water bubbler 
was done with two different sintering profiles at temperatures of 650°C and 700°C, 
allowing for improved CGO densification as well as diffusion of cations through the lattice 
due to the increased oxygen vacancies concentration. The sintering profile with 650°C 
showed more promising results compared to 700°C. Moreover, an improved electrode 
suspension with smaller CGO particles was successfully made in order to obtain a 
homogeneous particle size, increased porosity, while maintaining the sintering activity at 
these low temperatures.  
The steel supports of the type 430SS, 441SS, Crofer 22H and Crofer 22 Apu foils, and 316 
meshes, were coated with Yttrium using electrolytic deposition (ELD). Hot pressing was 
carried out at pressures around 0.4 and 0.8 MPa for the steel plates (441SS and Crofer 22 
H) during sintering, and this method yielded a denser structure of the electrode, but 
delamination between the deposited layer and the steel remains a challenge.    
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) 
were used to investigate the corrosion scale thickness, the adhesion to the substrate and 
the porosity of the deposited layer.  
The overall results were improvements of the electrode structure densification at low 
sintering temperature, while simultaneously maintaining electrode porosity. However, 
some issues still remain, such as delamination and crack formation in the electrode 
structure. These difficulties can likely be overcome with more experiments, e.g. different 
sintering profiles and conditions can be tested.

1. Introduction  
The solid oxide cell (SOC) is an energy conversion device that electrochemically 
exchanges chemical energy of a fuel e.g. hydrogen or methane directly to electricity 
without any combustion [1]. 
There are two operating modes of the solid oxide cell, one is as a solid oxide fuel cells 
(SOFC) and the other is as a solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC). They share the same 
solid oxide membrane that is an oxide ion, O2-, conductor.  
 
Figure 1: Operating principle of a solid oxide cell [1]. 
Figure 1 demonstrates a classic SOFC. SOFC fundamentally contains two porous 
electrodes, which is parted by a dense oxide ion conducting electrolyte. Molecules of 
oxygen gas which is on the positive electrode cathode side react with incoming electrons 
that comes from the external circuit in order to create oxygen ions that wander through the 
electrolyte to the negative electrode anode. 
On the anode side, oxide ions reacts with H2 to create H2O and/or CO2, releasing 
electrons that flow from the anode side to cathode side through the external circuit in order 
to produce electricity [1]. Thus, the overall reaction is [2]: 
𝐻2 + 𝑂2 ↔𝐻2𝑂 
At the anode side, the reaction is described as [2]: 
2𝐻2 + 2𝑂2−→2𝐻2𝑂+ 4𝑒− 
At the cathode side, the reaction is described as [2]: 
𝑂2 + 4𝑒−→2𝑂2− 
Similar reactions can occur for CO and CO2.

2. Theory  
A Solid oxide cell is a multi-layered metal-ceramic (cermet) device. It features a gastight 
oxide ion conducting electrolyte sandwiched between two electrodes. The electrodes must 
be porous for reactant/product gasses to enter/leave the reaction sites. The electrode must 
also percolate oxide ions and electrons as these species participate in the electrochemical 
reactions. During operation the cell voltage is around 1 V. In order to reach higher 
voltages, the cells are serially connected by inserting metal interconnect plates between 
the cells. In this way a solid oxide stack is formed.  
The anode supported cell is typically 30-40 microns thick and has a support layer of 
approximately 200 microns thickness. The support layer is required to handle the cell and 
avoid that it breaks during stack assembly. 
In a typical planar SOC, the electrolyte is made of 8 mol% yttria-stabilized zirconia (8YSZ) 
and deposited on a tape-casted NiO/8YSZ electrode layer on top of a NiO/3YSZ support 
layer [3]. 
These layers can be either shaped by the organic binder before the firing to make a non-
sintered ceramic electrode layer which is called the green material/state, or a consolidated 
pre-sintered NiO/YSZ substrate. After this step, the cell requires sintering at high 
temperatures in order to achieve a dense and consolidated YSZ electrolyte layer [3]. 
 
At this current time and based on the latest knowledge within this field, the following types 
of supported cells are stated:  
1.  Electrolyte supported SOC cells - (ESC) 
This type of supported cell is well-known by working at high temperature in the range of 
(850-1000°C). The electrolyte needs to have a thickness of >100 µm, in order to ensure 
the mechanical stability of the electrolyte and have enough mechanical strength. 
The main challenge of this type is the high cost of stack materials and system components 
capable of withstanding the high operating temperature, and the low performance because 
of the thick electrolyte [4]. 
 
2. Anode supported cell - (ASC) 
In contrast to ESC, this type of supported cell allows much lower operating temperatures in 
the range of (650-800°C). The mechanical strength of the cell depends on the anode part 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
which consists of a nickel electrolyte material cermet composite and an electrolyte film of 
<20 µm thickness. The main issue with ASC is the formation of nickel oxide in the anode 
caused by the loss of fuel in case of uncontrolled shut-down due to a technical failure. If 
this is the case, the nickel oxide will cause a volume expansion which causes the whole 
cell to crack [4].       
 
3. Cathode supported cell - (CSC) 
Cathode supported cells are very challenging to manufacture because of the high thermal 
expansion coefficient of most of the material used for the cathode.  
The high thermal expansion coefficient leads to cracks and failure and the challenge of 
maintaining sufficient porosity of the cathode at electrolyte firing temperatures [4].   
2.1 Theory of sintering  
Sintering is described as densification of ceramic compact. Pores removing between the 
starting particles combined with growth together and strong bonding between the particles.   
In order to a sintering process can happens, following conditions must be encountered:  
1. A mechanism for material support  
2. A source of energy to activate and sustain this material support    
The mechanism for material support transport is diffusion and viscous flow, where the 
source of energy is heat [5].  
Sintering process happens in stages according to the classification in physical changes 
when the particles bond together and the porosity disappears.  
The stages of sintering are [5]: 
1. 1st stage, initial stage: Rearrangement and neck formation  
2. 2nd stage, intermediate stage: Neck growth, grain growth, high shrinkage and pore 
phase continuous 
3. 3rd stage, final stage: Much grain growth, discontinuous pore phase and grain 
boundary pores removed   
The 1st stage means the rearrangement of particles and early neck formation between 
individually particles. This is done to increase the number of points of contacts of the 
particles. Bonding between the particles happens when the material transport takes place 
and where the surface energy is maximum.

2.2 Solid Oxide Cell 
SOC operates at high temperature (700–1000°C) which makes it more efficient and 
increases fuel flexibility. However, high temperature operation has negative effects on the 
chemical stability, durability and long-term reliability as well as the high cost of operation. 
This is one of the reasons for reducing the sintering temperature below 700°C has become 
interesting in recent years. This may improve the cost of the SOC technology, where 
cheaper materials can be used as well as new production methods can be applied to the 
stack [6].   
The catalytic activity of the electrode and the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte, which 
decreases with temperature, are currently unacceptable at lower temperatures. For that 
reason, developing a new electrolyte is necessary in order to facilitate high ion 
conductivities at low temperature [6].  
Electrolytes for SOCs are usually based on yttrium-doped zirconia (YSZ). Nickel (Ni) is 
used as the anode in the form of a cermet with YSZ [7]. Ni-YSZ can potentially be replaced 
by CGO when operating at low temperature.   
As an alternative for Ni, as this material has issues with agglomeration, carbon formation 
and oxidation to NiO and impurity intolerance, the single perovskite strontium iron 
molybdenum oxide, Sr0.99Fe0.75Mo0.25O3-δ (SFM) could potentially be used.  
CGO and perovskite structure oxides have also been investigated for been used in SOC 
operations. Following plot shows the ionic conductivity as a function of temperature for 
common SOC electrolyte material.  
 
Figure 5: Conductivity as a function of temperature for YSZ, CGO and LSMG [1]

Even though YSZ shows the lowest ionic conductivity, it has been confirmed to provide 
long term stability up to 80.000 hours at 1000°C [1], and this makes YSZ as the only 
material that is often used in these operations.  
As the figure shows, the ionic conductivity for CGO is increasing at lower temperature 
compared to YSZ, e.g. as we aimed for operating at temperature range of 500-650°C. 
Thus, the CGO materials are candidates for cell operation at low temperatures as it 
provide a higher ionic conductivity than YSZ [1].  
Replacing Ni-YSZ with CGO-based electrode will introduce some issues, such as 
worsening the mechanical stability, but this can be solved by using a steel support. 
As reported elsewhere ([1] and [8]) , CGO has 4-5 times higher conductivity at low 
temperatures (400-700) °C, but on the other hand it lacks sufficient electronic conductivity 
in reducing atmospheres to act as an electrode backbone. Another material is needed to 
supplement CGO and the poor electronic conductivity, (SFM), has a relatively high 
electronic conductivity, measured in air to be between 15-22 S/cm in the temperature 
range of 500-800°C, while in reducing conditions have been stated to be 13 S/cm and 33 
S/cm. For that reason, this material, could potentially be utilized as an electron percolating 
network [9].  
In latest years, SFM has attracted the attention to be an alternative new electrode material 
for SOC operations due to the excellent performance and it has also a thermal expansion 
coefficient (TEC) of 11·10-6K-1 which is very close to that of CGO that is 12·10-6K-1  [9] [10].   
Since the ceria-based materials are very difficult to densify below 1200°C, using sintering 
aids to reduce the sintering temperature may be necessary. Adding lithium carbonate 
(Li2CO3) and bismuth oxide (Bi2O3) as sintering aids has been found to be effective for 
reducing the densification temperature [8], [6].  
Following figure 6 and 7 shows how the sintering additives form a thin layer on the surface 
of the CGO grains which helps the atomic diffusion rate between grain boundaries. This 
mechanism lowers the energy required for structure densification which results in lower 
sintering temperature.

2.3 Operating at lower vs. higher temperature  
Operating at lower temperature enables the use of more materials which leads to cheaper 
fabrication. Especially the fact that different types of steel can be used is advantageous.  
Lower temperature operation offers a number of other advantages such as quicker start up 
and shut down, reduced corrosion rate of metallic components, better durability as well as 
the advantage of significantly simpler balance-of-plant requirements.  
Operating at low temperature means that it is easier to use materials together that have a 
mismatch in (TEC) without lowering the mechanical integrity of the electrode during 
temperature transients. As it mentioned earlier, in low-temperature solid oxide cells (LT-
SOCs) operations low cost ferritic (low TEC) stainless steel alloys can be used. These 
steels also owe their corrosion resistance to the formation of chromia (Cr) at the surface. 
Yet, at low temperatures Cr vaporization is not as extreme as for high temperature SOCs.  
Ferritic steel has the benefit of high strength, machinability, closely matched TEC with the 
electrolyte material where, in this case using e.g. 430 or 316 steel meshes is due to the 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
TEC that is close to CGO. TEC for CGO, 430 steel and 316 steel are respectively 12·10-
6K-1, 11-12·10-6K-1 and 8·10-6K-1at 800°C [11] [12]. 
Based on conductivity measurements of Y-coated 316 and 430 steel made by my 
supervisors, these steel types have a low ohmic resistance of 0.02-0.05 Ω cm^2 over the 
course of 1000 h at 650 °C. Assuming a linear degradation the ohmic resistance after 
40.000 h is however too high, approximately 0.4 Ω cm^2 (see appendix A). Therefore, it is 
likely that a lower operating temperature is required (500-600 °C) if these relatively 
inexpensive steel types are to be used.   
316 and 430 steel are more interesting to be used than e.g. Crofer steel due to the costs 
which is less expensive and the both types are available as steel plates as well.  
But, as it mentioned earlier, operating at lower temperature leads to decreased catalytic 
activity of the electrode and the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte, which decreases with 
temperature. That is one of reasons of developing a new electrolyte and electrode is 
necessary. 
However, high temperature operation means that almost all materials of the stack must be 
ceramic because using steel in this case leads to a higher steel oxidation, and tubular 
design are generally used in this case, which results in low volumetric power density [11]. 
A better design could be planar designs, which offer numerous potential advantages, as 
well as simpler and less expensive manufacturing processes and higher power densities, 
than tubular design [1]. 
2.4 Electrolytic deposition (ELD)  
In order to minimize corrosion of the steel support because corrosion will lead to loss of 
electronic percolation and loss of contact between steel and electrode, coating of the steel 
support may be necessary and thus this problem may be avoided by applying a thin oxide 
layer. This can be done by using the ELD method. Section 2.6 describes more about how 
the ELD coating helps the steel support from corrosion.   
There are several basic mechanisms of ELD. One of these mechanisms is cathodic 
electrolytic deposition that has essential advantages (see section 2.7) and can be used for 
deposition of various oxide materials. 
Cathodic reactions are used to generate OH-groups and increase the pH at the electrode. 
Metal ions that are stable in the bulk of solutions at low pH, are hydrolyzed by electro-
generated base at the electrode surface to form colloidal particles [13].

2.5 Motivation  
To further develop the solid oxide cell technology and make it more cost-effective, lower 
sintering temperature (600-900°C) of the fuel cell is desired, while still retaining the high 
efficiency of the device.       
The main problems with high temperature operations are corrosion of the metal, durability, 
high operational cost and time consumption.  
To reduce the high cost and high operating temperature, diverse material should be 
investigated. Since CGO is a mixed ionic and electronic conductor, it can be used as both 
an electrolyte and an electrode. It has mostly attracted attention as an electrolyte, but in 
this project we focus on using the material as an electrode. LT-SOCs based on CGO is 
interesting to investigate.    
Using metal instead of ceramics as the supported layer is another important part of the 
study and using sintering additives indicates that the operating temperature can be 
decreased to below 1000°C [14], [15].  
The main challenges of using metal as the supported layer is the tendency of the steel to 
oxidize when sintering at high temperature. Different methods should be examined by 
sintering at lower temperature without destroying the electrode.    
The electrode must consist of ceramic material deposited on a steel plate and/or mesh.  
The type of electrode that can be used in this case is SFM and CGO (SFM-CGO). This 
type of ceramic material electrode in combination with a flexible steel mesh can possibly 
attain high flexibility which could lead to new solid oxide cell designs.     
Applying force (hot-pressing) during the sintering process could be interesting to 
investigate, where the sintering temperature might be decreased and a better structure 
densification can be achieved [16]. 
Another part that is important to consider is sintering at low temperature and in reducing 
atmosphere where CGO densification can be improved [17].

2.6 Goal & Plan for the project 
The aim of this study is to develop an electrode that can be sintered on a support made of 
steel. This process requires minimizing corrosion of the steel support, which can be done 
by coating of the steel support and sintering of the electrode at temperature below 1000°C. 
The steel supports will be coated with Yttrium using electrolytic deposition (ELD).  
Yttrium together with the steel will form a Y-Cr scale which has relatively high electronic 
conductivity, which may also be able to stop the oxidation of the steel.  
In order to enable sintering of CGO and SFM at low temperature, four strategies will be 
attempted in this project; 1) addition of sintering additives, Bi2O3 and Li2CO3, 2) high 
ramping rate of 3-10°C/min, 3) applying force during the sintering process, and 4) sintering 
in reducing atmosphere. 
When the solutions for ELD are made, ball milling will be used to disperse the solid 
particles and avoid agglomeration. Once the electrode suspension is made, it will be 
deposited on Y-coated steel substrates.  
In order to lower the sintering temperature and achieve a better structure densification, the 
green solid loading of the electrode should be maximized. This can be done using 
mechanical pressing before sintering (cold pressing) [16], [18], but an alternative method is 
to press simultaneously with the sintering process, which is called hot pressing.  
Hot pressing enables substantial reduction of the compression force, which solves the 
issue of using unrealistically high force during cold pressing when scaling the cell footprint 
up. Thus, hot pressing scales better. Hot pressing will be conducted at pressures around 
0.4 and 0.8 MPa for each of the samples.  
Substrates with electrodes will be sintered in air at 900°C. Sintering in reducing 
atmosphere will be done at temperature of 650°C and 700°C with 5% of hydrogen. As gas 
access is required through the electrode and support layer, some manufactures use laser 
drilling for making holes (Ceres Power, see section 2.7).  
However, lase drilling is expensive. Using steel meshes as will be attempted in this project, 
one can avoid laser drilling.  
Finally, the sintering behavior will be investigated by using two types of microscopes such 
as tabletop TM3000 and Carl-Zeiss Merlin.

2.7 State of the art  
As mentioned earlier, S.M. Jamil et al. [8], CGO lacks in electronic conductivity in reducing 
atmospheres dissimilar to SFM which have relatively high electronic conductivity, Skafte et 
al. [9]. Thus, a combination of CGO and SFM should be examined.  
However, using CGO leads to some challenges. CGO is typically fired at 1500°C in order 
to achieve the required density. As a result of this, the combination of CGO and the metal 
as support layer is mismatched due to the high temperature which leads to oxidation of the 
steel and the different TEC of the metal support and CGO.  
As a solution for this, the sintering process could be done in reducing atmosphere, where 
the oxidation of the steel can either be avoided or decreased [4]. 
 
Z. He et al. [17] observed that, sintering of CGO in a reducing atmosphere indicates that 
densification of CGO is significantly enhanced at lower temperatures. Furthermore, the 
improvement on densification take place in the early-stage sintering of CGO during 
reduced oxygen partial pressure, as it offers a promising way for low temperature 
densification of CGO. 
This enhanced low-temperature densification of CGO in reducing atmosphere might be 
related to the increased concentration of oxygen vacancies, which enables the diffusion of 
cations through the lattice. 
        
In the paper S. M. Jamil et al. [8] the use of sintering aids and their benefits were 
described and showed. Using the sintering aids such as Bi2O3 and Li2CO3 helps to 
increase the activity on the surface of CGO. The additives will adsorb onto the grain 
surface of CGO to form a thin layer which helps atomic diffusion rate between the grain 
boundaries. This mechanism lowers the energy that required for the structure

The interest in ELD for fuel cell applications stems from the need to decrease the

The Ceres Power patent [16], offers facts on the preparation of SOFC operating at low 
temperatures with metal anode supported cell. The SOFC cell that Ceres patent describes 
uses a ferritic stainless steel foil, which is made partly porous in its central area in order to 
permit fuel access to the anode, as its mechanical support. The main layer of the cell which 
consists of anode, electrolyte and cathode, are all deposited on top of the substrate foil as 
thick films. 
Furthermore, Ceres Power SOFC cell allows operating at lower temperatures in the range 
of 450°C-650°C and often the operation temperatures are in the range of 500°C-620°C. 
This operation is done by using CGO, which have intrinsically higher oxygen ion 
conductivity than zirconia-based materials.    
Ceres patent recommends that, the green density may be improved by applying 
mechanical pressing such as cold/hot pressing during sintering. An application of a 
pressure of 50-500 MPa for 0-5 min was showed an improved green density.

3.1.2 Particle size distribution PSD 
Particle size distribution PSD was measured using LS 13320 Laser Diffraction Particle 
Size Analyzer which is manufactured by Beckman Coulter, where a little amount of the 
solution/sample was taken out by using a transfer pipette or glass spatula and mix it with a 
little amount of ethanol. Then, the sample was poured in the instrument and PSD was 
measured by using the computer where the corresponding software is connected.   
 
This instrument analyzes the size of the particles by the light scattering. When light 
illuminates a particle that is different from that of the medium, depending on the 
wavelength of the light and the optical properties of the particle, the light will be scattered 
in a unique way. In general, the scattered light intensity of a particle is a function of the 
following variables: particle dimension, particle refractive index, medium refractive index, 
light wavelength, polarization, and scattering angle [20].     
This technique of determination of particle size distribution requires following conditions: 
1.  Depending on the type of analyzed particle, an optical model needs to be chosen 
The optical model called Fraunhofer was used in this project.  
2. Particle numbers added to the instrument must not saturates the detecting system 
This can be controlled by checking directly the amounts of sample that is added to the 
instrument where a color bar shown on the computer, turn into green when the 
concentration reaches the optimum value.       
3.1.3 Microstructural investigation 
The samples were investigated by using two types of microscopes such as tabletop 
TM3000 and Carl-Zeiss Merlin.  
TM3000 was used for the quick investigations of the material structure and the contact 
between the electrode and steel support layer.  
 
Carl-Zeiss Merlin involves a field emission gun and 3 types of detectors called 1) in-lens 
detector, 2) secondary electrons detector (HE-SE2) and 3) backscattered detectors (AsB).  
The in-lens detector was used for high-resolution images, HE-SE2 detector was used for 
morphology studies and AsB was used for mass contrast analysis.  
The SEM images were mostly achieved by using the HE-SE2 detector, where the 
accelerating voltage was at 3-15 kV with a working distance of 7-10 mm.       
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
The samples were prepared by a laboratory technician where they were cut in half and 
casted in epoxy. Then the samples were carbon coated and was brought to the test holder 
belonging to the microscope. Once the sample was in the sample holder, a carbon tape 
was put on to connect the sample with the holder.  
Before placing the samples in microscope chamber, the chamber mode should be 
switched from evacuation to air and then back to evacuation when the sample was placed. 
Once the sample was found and focused with the help of software connected with 
microscope and computer, the investigation got started.      
 
The purpose of using Carl-Zeiss Merlin was to take high-resolution scanning electron 
microscopy (SEM) images as well as making energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) 
where it measured the material composition of the sample. 
SEM is a familiar scientific technique which is used to detect and characterize the structure 
of a material. This technique uses focused electron beam to scan the surface of a sample 
identifying signals from electron and sample interface. The electrons generates by an 
electron gun which is located at the top of the microscope, hereby the electrons are 
focused on the sample by applying an accelerating voltage [18].  
The development of the image depends on collecting the different signals that are 
scattered as a consequence of the high energy beam interacting with the sample.  
The backscattered electron increases when the atomic number of the sample increases.  
The secondary electrons are formed when the electrons beam hits the sample, or when 
the backscattered electrons interact to atoms that come out from the sample [21] [18].

3.4 Test of steel support  
Four different steel types were tested in terms of corrosion (430SS, 441SS, Crofer 22 Apu 
and Crofer 22 H). This part of the experiment was done by cutting 10 samples in 1cm2 of 
each steel type followed by Yttrium coating using ELD.

chemical composition of the corrosion scale, the substrate and the deposited layer.  
As will be shown and discussed later, the best two steel types were hereby chosen to be 
441SS and Crofer 22 H, and these were used for further work in the project. 
Steel meshes 316SS/316L has been used as well in this experiment. The reason for 
choosing this steel type, was because it was not possible to get 441SS or Crofer 22H in

3.6 Hot-pressing procedure 
The hot pressing on the samples was done while sintering. Two different pressures of 0.4 
Mpa and 0.8 Mpa were tested. The purpose of hot pressing was to make a more dense 
structure and investigate if the adhesion between the electrode and the steel support is 
improved.  
The hot pressing was done by placing a load consisting of ceramics on the samples while 
sintering slowly to 600°C with a ramp rate of 1°C/min and then up to 900°C with a ramp 
rate of 10°C/min and cooling down with 1°C/min. The reason for the slowly sintering and 
cooling was to give the electrode more time to extent without forming cracks and 
delamination between the electrode and the steel support.

help to improve the sintering of the electrode.

was added to the suspension and was placed on the ball mill for 7 days where the particle 
size distribution PSD was measured almost every day using LS 13320 Laser Diffraction

the focus of the improvement, we decided to continue with a PSD of SFM of 3.176 µm and

5. Conclusion 
As this project work was about development of ceria based electrodes for low temperature 
solid oxide electrolysis cells, different electrode suspension were made and deposited on 
metal supported layer.  
Several sintering profile at lower temperature (<1000°C) were examined where different 
ramping rate of 3-10°C/min were carried out. Furthermore, hot pressing while sintering at 
two different pressures were investigated. 
The electrode suspension which was based on SFM-CGO-(sintering additives) was 
painted on several metal supported layer such as 430SS, 441SS, Crofer 22 H and Crofer 
22 Apu.  
The other electrode suspension which called the improved suspension in this work was 
based on only SFM-CGO and this was painted on 316 steel meshes.   
Sintering in air at 900°C showed that the steel type of 441SS and Crofer 22 H have the 
best performance regarding the interface between the steel support and the electrode due 
to the structure densification and the attachment of the electrode to the steel support. 
For that reason, these steel types were chosen to be sintered at 900°C in air with different 
rampe rates as mentioned earlier. But the SEM images showed that the steel type of 
441SS has the tendency to develop delamination between the electrode and the steel 
supported layer.   
Hot pressing was done on both steel types but unfortunately it has not helped to minimize 
or avoid the delamination, but on the other hand a better structure of the electrode was 
achieved. 
Thus, an improved electrode suspension regarding to electrode particle size was made 
and painted on 316SS steel meshes and sintered at different temperatures in reducing 
atmosphere. The results showed that a sintering profile at 650°C gives a better electrode 
structure compared to the one with 700°C.  
From the results achieved in this project, it can be concluded that using the 316SS steel 
meshes could likely fulfill the goal of the project as it provided better outcomes in terms of 
structure densification of the electrode.  
 
Furthermore, the SFM-CGO electrode suspension appears to be a good choice compared 
to electrode suspension of CGO alone, due to the higher porosity.  
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
Regarding the four strategies (sintering aids, high ramping rate, hot-pressing and reducing 
atmosphere) the following conclusion can be derived: 
1. Sintering aids: steel type of 316 steel meshes cannot be used together with 
sintering aids in the electrode suspension and if the sintering temperature is 900°C 
in air, because it will lead to extreme corrosion of the steel.  
2. High ramping rate: as it showed earlier, the delamination of the steel could be 
because of the high ramping rate. For detailed explanation, see section 4.2.  
3. Hot-pressing: it does provide a more flat surface of the electrode, but otherwise it 
does not appear to be a promising method to be used.  
4. Reducing atmosphere: sintering in reducing atmosphere decreased the oxidation of 
the steel (see SEM images from section 4.4). However, this section refers to the 
steel meshes, but sintering the steel plates in reducing atmosphere could also lead 
to less oxidation. Moreover, sintering in reducing conditions led to improved 
sintering of the CGO, while the SFM helped maintain sufficient porosity.  
  
The overall conclusion is that, the project work provided the possibility of further working 
with the steel type of 316SS/430SS at sintering temperatures of 650-700°C in reducing 
atmosphere with a ramp rate of 1°C/min.  
 
Despite the encouraging results discussed above, much work remains to be done. Here 
follows some suggested experiments to further the understanding and increase the 
likelihood of success for the overall project.

5.1 Outlook  
Future work that can be done to achieve the main purpose of the project: 
1. Other sintering profiles could also be interesting to examine, such as sintering in N2 
at 750-1000°C, where N2 can be less reducing which might solve the problem with 
of too low porosity.  
2. The issue with delamination could be solved by cooling the sintering process with a 
slower ramping rate somewhere between 1-3 °C/min.  
a.  Or try to preheat the coated steels at high temperature (800°C) before sintering 
to form an oxide layer that can avoid the delamination. The oxide layer can act 
as a sort of buffer layer between the steel and the electrode.  
b. Increasing the solid loading as well as increasing the binder content in the 
electrode suspension could also be attempted. 
3. Try to burn off the organics at 500°C in air and immediately change to H2/N2 or N2 
and increase to the sintering temperature. 
4. Also, while cooling, since the particles are stressed due to the difference in thermal 
expansion coefficients and due to the fact that they are not kept in place by the 
organics anymore, the cooling rate could be lowered. 
5. Another thing could be interesting to do, is to have the steel integrated in the 
electrode as it provides: 
a. A high conductivity level in the electrode 
b. Improvement of electrode strength, which can be done by the steel welded onto 
a thick steel mesh, then the structure of the steel mesh will be smooth at the 
inside layer and rough at the outside layer.  
6. More testing is required to determine the exact increase in ohmic resistance over 
time of Y-coating and steel combination.
