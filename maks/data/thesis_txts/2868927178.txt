Abstract
This study explores the use of snow as a construction material through a comprehensive literature
review and a series of experimental tests. The literature review examines the mechanical properties
and applications of snow, providing insight into its potential for structural use.
The study presents a snow production method for indoor, temperature dependent snow produc-
tion in a small room with limited cooling capacity. While the method introduced in this study is
effective for producing snow, its limitations include low production capacity and the inability to
precisely control temperature and humidity, which affected the consistency of snow quality.
A series of experimental tests on snow were conducted. Geotechnical tests were adapted to assess
snow hardness, shear strength, and shrinkage, as well as to investigate how these properties develop
over time. The results indicate that both snow hardness and shear strength increase over time.
Additionally, this study discusses the complexities and limitations inherent in applying geotechni-
cal testing methods to snow.
A series of beam tests were conducted to investigate the structural behavior of snow. The study
demonstrates that simple beam tests can effectively determine the viscoelastic properties of snow.
Strain on the beam surface was measured using fiber optic technology and by manual deformation
measurements. The recorded deformations were then used in a beam model to calculate strain and
analyze the viscoelastic response of snow under load. In this case, a Burgers model was applied to
calculate the short and long term viscoelastic properties, providing insights into the time depen-
dent mechanical behavior of snow.
Furthermore, this study examines the effects of aluminum reinforcement on the load bearing capac-
ity of snow beams. The results indicate that reinforced beams exhibited greater resistance to long
term deformation under static loads but, no significant improvement under incremental loading or
failure tests.
Due to variations in snow quality and challenges in accurately measuring strain within the re-
inforced sections, it remains inconclusive whether aluminum reinforcement consistently enhances
structural performance. Further research is needed to refine reinforcement strategies.

Introduction
Snow construction has long been a crucial practice in colder regions, serving a variety of purposes
throughout history. In contemporary engineering, the use of snow has expanded to include modern
projects like temporary winter shelters and constructions for tourism, leveraging its unique prop-
erties such as the ability to form diverse structural shapes and its transient nature, which allows
for seasonal usage without long term landscape alteration [1].
Despite these advantages, constructing with snow presents significant challenges. Its properties
vary greatly with changes in temperature, moisture content, and compaction methods, making it
a non-homogeneous material that is difficult to standardize. The mechanical properties of snow,
such as load-bearing capacity and viscoelastic properties, are crucial yet challenging to predict
accurately due to these variations.
This study aims to enrich knowledge of snow as a construction material, with the expectation that
new insights will lead to more dependable and creative building techniques. Figure 1 illustrates
the structured process of the study. The initial phases, consisting of a literature review and snow
production, establish a foundational basis for conducting the subsequent experimental tests. This is
followed by data collection, which is then analyzed to derive results, culminating in a comprehensive
understanding of the thesis’s objectives.
Figure 1: Flowchart of process of the thesis.
The objectives of this thesis are threefold, each designed to contribute to the knowledge and ex-
pand the possibilities of using snow as a construction material.
First, the theisis conducts a comprehensive literature review to gather knowledge on the use of
snow in various construction contexts, including snow roads, airfields, and general construction
projects. By employing a systematic approach, this review aims to provide a broad overview of
snow as a construction material, exploring how it can be utilized effectively and how its material
properties can be determined and optimized.
Second, the objective is to establish a snow production facility at the Technical University of Den-
mark, DTU. This is the first time snow production has been conducted at DTU. By optimizing
snow production using the available facilities, DTU will enhance its capabilities for conducting more
1
==PAGE:7==
extensive snow studies. This initiative will also serve as a model for other institutions with limited
snow production resources, thereby broadening the opportunities for comprehensive snow research.
Third, the thesis seeks to augment the existing knowledge of snow as a construction material
through experimental testing on its structural capacity. Specifically, the interaction between snow
and aluminum is examined to determine if aluminum can serve as an effective reinforcement ma-
terial.
Simple test methods, traditionally applied to soil, are adapted to assess the physical and mechan-
ical properties of snow. In addition, a series of beam tests will investigate whether aluminum
can improve the structural integrity of snow beams. The use of fiber optic technology to measure
strain introduces an innovative technique for evaluating the viscoelastic properties of snow over
time. This data will be compared with results from traditional manual measurement techniques.
By utilizing both a beam model and a Burgers model, the study aims to thoroughly characterize
the viscoelastic properties of snow, integrating both fiber optic and manual measurements to gain
a comprehensive understanding of these properties.
To summarize, the experimental tests aim to determine whether simple geotechnical tests can be
adapted for snow, if fiber optic technology can accurately measure snow’s viscoelastic properties,
and whether aluminum can improve the structural capabilities of snow constructions. The findings
from this research will contribute to the further development of snow as a viable construction
material.

2.2
Fundamentals of snow
This section delves into the essential characteristics and formation processes of snow, which are
pivotal for its application in various engineering and environmental contexts. It explores the condi-
6
==PAGE:12==
tions necessary for snow formation, including temperature thresholds and atmospheric conditions,
and details the different types of snow based on their physical properties and formation mechanisms.
2.2.1
Formation of snow
Snow is defined as precipitation in the form of ice crystals that form in the atmosphere when the
temperature is below 0◦C [2]. Snow formation requires three key conditions: Temperatures below
0°C, adequate air humidity, and the presence of particles that act as freezing nuclei to facilitate
the formation of ice crystals.
Snow forms when water evaporates from oceans and lakes, rises into the atmosphere, and condenses
into water droplets upon encountering cold clouds. These droplets gather around microscopic par-
ticles with a similar size to dust or sand, which act as freezing nuclei. If the cloud temperature is
below 0°C, ice crystals begin to form around these particles. The ice crystals then grow through
two processes: Deposition, where water molecules from the droplets transfer to the ice crystals, and
riming, where the crystals collide with supercooled water droplets that freeze onto them. These
processes determine the shape and growth rate of the falling snow crystals [2].
Snow crystal morphology is heavily influenced by both temperature and supersaturation. Super-
saturation refers to the condition where the air contains more water vapor than it can normally
hold at a specific temperature and pressure, relative to its equilibrium point. In this state, the air
is ”supersaturated” with respect to ice, which provides ideal conditions for snow crystal growth.
Higher supersaturation levels mean there is an excess of water vapor available, which encourages
rapid condensation onto the freezing nuclei, leading to faster growth of ice crystals. Under con-
ditions of high supersaturation, snow crystals can develop intricate shapes, such as dendrites or
complex branching patterns, because of the abundant moisture that supports elaborate crystal
growth. Temperature dictates whether the crystals form into plate-like, columnar, or dendritic
shapes, while supersaturation influences the complexity of these structures [29].
Figure 5 illustrates the relationship between temperature, supersaturation, and snow crystal mor-
phology. It shows that at certain temperature ranges, different types of snow crystal shapes are
more likely to form, depending on the level of available water vapor or supersaturation in the
atmosphere. At low temperatures and low supersaturation, simple shapes like columns and plates
tend to form, as there is minimal water vapor available to support complex growth. As supersat-
uration increases, a variety of shapes emerge, with more branching and complexity visible in the
crystals. The figure demonstrates how increased water vapor availability, even at relatively low
temperatures, allows for the formation of more elaborate structures. At high levels of supersatu-
ration, dendritic structures, which are highly branched and intricate, are most common. This is
particularly evident at colder temperatures, where the excess vapor condenses rapidly onto the ice,
producing complex patterns. Overall, the figure highlights how specific atmospheric conditions
drive snow crystal growth patterns, illustrating that both temperature and supersaturation are
crucial factors in determining snow crystal geometry [39].

The process of snow formation can be categorized into natural snow and machine made snow. Each
type has distinct characteristics and formation processes.
Natural snow
Natural snow is characterized by its intricate crystal structures, which are shaped by the complex
interplay of atmospheric conditions during its formation. Figure 6 illustrates various types of snow
crystal morphologies observed in natural snowfalls, highlighting the diversity in crystal shapes that
result from different atmospheric conditions. The formation of each type is influenced by factors
like temperature, humidity, and supersaturation, as discussed in the chapter.
Natural snow is inherently more complex and non-homogeneous compared to machine made snow,
due to the diverse atmospheric conditions that influence its formation and the resulting variability
in crystal structure, grain size, and density [2].
Machine made Snow
Machine made snow is produced by snow machines. Snowmaking involves spraying water and
compressed air into cold air. Water is pumped into the machine. Inside the machine, water is
atomized into tiny droplets using high-pressure nozzles that break the water into fine mist-like
particles. The fine water droplets are then blown into the air below 0◦C, where they rapidly freeze
and form small ice crystals.
Colder temperatures improve the efficiency of snow production and the quality of the snow pro-
duced.
Low humidity levels are preferable as they enhance the cooling and freezing of water
droplets.
Machine made snow has a more uniform grain size and density than natural snow, which varies
widely due to the complex atmospheric conditions involved in its formation. This uniformity in
machine made snow provides higher density, typically between 200 and 400 kg/m³ initially, de-
pending on the temperature, humidity, and type of snowmaking equipment used. Its increased
density and reduced variability make machine made snow suitable for construction applications,
as it can offer better load-bearing capacity and stability when compacted [6].

2.2.2
Snow metamorphism
Metamorphism describes the evolution of snow’s physical properties over time, influenced by en-
vironmental factors such as temperature, pressure, and humidity. This process can be categorized
into three distinct processes described below.
Densification
The first metamorphic process of snow, known as densification or compaction, involves the increase
in density of the snow due to the rearrangement and settling of snow grains under the influence of
gravity and overlying pressure. This process reduces the amount of air space between the grains,
leading to a denser snow [17].
Sintering
Sintering is the process where snow grains bond together over time, increasing the mechanical
strength of the snow.
This bond formation primarily occurs through the movement of water
molecules across grain boundaries, driven by differences in vapor pressure. At low temperature
gradients, the snow grains weld together at their contact points. This bonding occurs because, at a
microscopic level, the vapor pressure is lower over the surfaces where grains meet, compared to the
surfaces of the grains themselves. As a result, water molecules migrate toward these lower pressure
areas through vapor diffusion, creating necks or bonds between grains that gradually grow stronger
over time. This movement of water molecules is an application of equilibrium thermodynamics,
where molecules naturally move from areas of higher energy to areas of lower energy to achieve a
more stable configuration.
This process is influenced by temperature, time, and the initial porosity of the snow. When growth
rates are low, as is often the case in cold environments, this process proceeds slowly and steadily,
10
==PAGE:16==
enhancing the structural integrity of the snow. However, at higher growth rates, particularly when
the surface of the snow grains is highly supersaturated, the equilibrium principles governing vapor
diffusion become less dominant. In these conditions, the shapes and bonding of ice crystals are
less stable, and bonds between grains are less likely to form or may be eliminated if faster growing
grains consume the smaller, more slowly growing ones.
In other words, in warmer conditions,
larger, weaker bonds form, creating a more unstable structure, while in colder conditions, the
bonds are smaller and grow more slowly but ultimately provide greater strength. This behavior
explains why snow subjected to varying temperature conditions can develop weaker layers, which
can be prone to failure [40].
Recrystallization
Recrystallization is a metamorphic process where snow grains undergo structural changes over
time, leading to increased density and mechanical strength within the snowpack. Driven by tem-
perature fluctuations and pressure, this process involves the transformation of smaller snow grains
into larger, more stable crystals, typically under sustained low temperatures and mechanical pres-
sure. As fresh snow accumulates, the weight of the overlying layers compresses the lower layers,
reducing porosity and causing grains to deform, merge, and refreeze into denser, more cohesive
structures. This enhances the mechanical strength of the snowpack, making it more resistant to
deformation and failure under load [17].
Classification of metabolic state
Snow can be classified into four distinct types based on the stages of the above mentioned processes
it has undergone: (i) Fresh snow, (ii) granular snow, (iii) semi bonded snow and (iv) sintered snow.
Over time, fresh snow can transform into other forms such as granular or sintered snow. In the
context of snow as a construction material, the classification and transformation of snow into dif-
ferent types are crucial because each type has distinct physical and mechanical properties that
affect its stability, strength, and load-bearing capacity. Fresh snow is generally light and fluffy,
with low density and poor cohesion, making it unsuitable for construction as it lacks structural in-
tegrity. However, over time, through processes like compaction, sintering, and temperature driven
metamorphism, fresh snow can densify and transform into granular or sintered snow, both of which
offer better stability and load-bearing potential [30, 9].
2.3
Physical and mechanical properties of snow
This chapter provides an in-depth exploration of the physical properties of snow, which are essential
for predicting and managing its behavior in structural and construction contexts. The mechanical
stability, load-bearing capacity, and adaptability of snow as a construction material depend on
properties like density, porosity, hardness, grain size, and shape. Understanding these attributes
helps evaluate snow’s suitability for applications such as roads, runways, and other structural bases,
where stability and resistance to deformation are critical.

Over time, snow becomes denser due to compaction under its own weight and settling. This process

The initial density of machine made snow typically varies between 200 and 400 kg/m³, depending

The load-bearing capacity of snow surfaces is significantly influenced by the density of the snow.
Higher density results in greater compressive and shear strength [1, 12, 34].

Snow hardness refers to the resistance of snow to penetration or deformation, which is an important
factor in assessing snow stability. Harder snow can bear heavier loads, making it more suitable for

Grain size
Grain size has a significant effect on snow’s behavior as a construction material, particularly in
terms of its strength, density, and cohesion.
In terms of density, snow with smaller grains can be compacted more easily, resulting in higher
density and consequently greater compressive strength. This is important for construction appli-
cations where snow needs to support loads. Larger grained snow is typically less dense due to more
air trapped between the grains, making it weaker and more prone to deformation [17].
The rate of sintering in snow is more rapid for smaller grains compared to larger grains. This
is because smaller grains have a higher surface curvature, which increases the driving force for
sintering. As a result, diffusion of material between the grains occurs more efficiently, leading to
faster bond formation and greater overall strength in the snow. This characteristic makes fine-

Mechanical properties of snow
Compressive strength
Compressive strength is the capacity of a material to withstand compressive loads. Snow is char-
acterized by its significant compressibility, much of which is irreversible. This irreversible com-
pressibility sets snow apart from ice and most other engineering materials [26].
Snow’s compressive strength is dependent on its physical properties which are constantly changing.
This dynamic nature makes it challenging to accurately predict the behavior of snow. Continuous
monitoring and advanced modeling are required to address these challenges [12, 34].
Unconfined Compressive Strength (UCS) is a measure of a material’s ability to withstand com-
pressive forces without any lateral confinement.

Tensile strength
As described in the previous section, the compressive strength of snow can be relatively high, when
compacted. Snow is more vulnerable to tensile stress.
Tensile strength in snow is often determined by direct uniaxial tension tests. According to Mel-
lor, uniaxial stress tests in tension are considered one of the most straightforward methods for
determining strength in snow. These tests provide valuable data for understanding the mechanical
16
==PAGE:22==
properties under tension, especially when performed at high strain rates. Tensile strength data,
like other mechanical properties of snow, show a strong dependence on factors such as density,
strain rate and temperature.
At lower densities, tensile strength tends to be lower but increases significantly with rising density.
Additionally, failure under tensile loading tends to occur through brittle mechanisms when tested
under rapid loading conditions, which characterizes the high rate brittle regime.
The relationship between the density and tensile strength of snow indicates that as snow becomes
denser, its tensile strength generally increases. This increase in strength is due to stronger bonds
forming between the snow grains as they are packed more tightly. Specifically, high-density snow
shows a more pronounced and predictable exponential increase in tensile strength. Conversely, low
density snow has a more variable tensile strength and the correlation between density and strength
is less consistent. Factors such as grain structure, temperature, and snow type also significantly
influence the tensile strength in low density snow. This illustrates that while denser snow typically
exhibits higher tensile strength, the strength of low density snow can be less predictable and more
influenced by additional variables.
Temperature plays a significant role in snow’s tensile strength. At warmer temperatures, near 0°C,
snow grains become more mobile, and the bonds between them weaken, leading to a decrease in
tensile strength. In contrast, colder temperatures can increase tensile strength to some degree as
the snow becomes more rigid [26, 12].
Shear strength
One of the most crucial aspects of snow strength is its shear strength, which reflects the material’s
resistance to deformation under applied shear forces. Shear strength is particularly important in
the design of structures that interact with snow, such as avalanche control measures and vehicles
moving over snow.
Strength properties of snow are determined by a variety of factors, including density, temperature,
and snow microstructure. As snow is a highly variable material, its mechanical strength can change
significantly based on these factors.

Viscoelastic properties
Viscoelastic behavior of snow is nonlinear, meaning its response to stress is not constant and varies
with factors like density, temperature, and the duration of applied stress.
Viscosity in snow governs the creep behavior under sustained stress, where snow deforms slowly
over time. Snow’s creep behavior is highly nonlinear and sensitive to changes in density and stress
levels. As snow deforms under sustained stress, it undergoes primary creep and secondary creep
[26].
Primary creep in snow is the initial phase of deformation under sustained stress, characterized
by a decreasing rate of strain. During this stage, the snow adjusts to the applied stress, and the
deformation rate gradually slows down as the snow begins to stabilize.
Secondary creep, also known as steady-state creep, is the phase following primary creep where the
material deforms at a constant rate over time under constant stress. This phase is often the longest
and is characterized by a linear increase in strain over time.

2.4
Applications
This section explores the diverse applications of snow in construction and infrastructure. It delves
into the practical aspects of using snow as a construction material, discussing various structural
forms such as snow foundations, snow roads, and snow runways. This section examines how these
structures are built, maintained, and utilized in cold regions, highlighting both the engineering
challenges and innovative solutions employed to harness snow’s unique properties.
2.4.1
Snow structures
Snow structures are constructions made primarily from compacted snow, often reinforced with
materials like ice, wood, or steel to enhance stability and longevity. These structures are primarily
built in cold climates where snow is abundant and temperatures remain below freezing.
The construction of snow structures often have an arc shape, like can be seen in Figure 16. The
arch shape is highly effective in construction because of its ability to distribute weight evenly across
the structure. When a load is applied to an arch, the force is transferred down along the curve
and into the supports (or abutments) on either side. This reduces tension and allows the arch to
bear significant loads while maintaining stability. These shapes are achieved through the use of
moulds or inflatable forms, which guide the shape of the snow or ice structure as it is packed and
compacted. A mould can be seen in Figure 17.

One of the primary challenges in snow construction is the ephemeral nature of the material. Snow
and ice structures must be carefully designed to handle the temperature fluctuations and environ-
mental stresses that come with their temporary nature [7].

Reinforcement in snow constructions
Snow as a bearing element is inherently weak in tension and bending, which makes it prone to
failure under load. To overcome these weaknesses, reinforcement can be applied to improve its
structural performance. By combining snow with materials like wood, steel, or ice, the snow’s
compressive strength can be paired with the tensile strength of the snow. This composite system
23
==PAGE:29==
helps distribute stresses more effectively, enhancing the snow’s load-bearing capacity and durabil-
ity in constructions like snow domes, igloos, or artistic snow structures.
Ice has been widely used as a reinforcement material in snow construction due to its ability to
increase compressive strength, especially in Arctic and Antarctic regions. Ice can be combined
with snow to create robust structures, such as temporary snow construction, where solid layers
of ice serve as foundational or bearing elements to stabilize the snow and enhance load-bearing
capacity.
A common construction technique involves compacting snow and slush into structural forms, which
are then solidified as the water in the slush freezes. This freezing process strengthens the material
by bonding the snow particles together, resulting in a stable and durable structure. This method is
frequently used in snow construction projects, where the frozen slush serves to reinforce the overall
stability and strength of the formed elements.
However, ice reinforcement has limitations. It requires consistently cold temperatures to maintain
strength, and it is less effective in handling tensile forces compared to materials like wood or steel.
Additionally, ice can become brittle over time, especially in fluctuating temperatures, leading to
potential cracks and structural weakness [7].
Wood has also been used to reinforce snow. Reinforcing snow beams with wood greatly enhances
their structural integrity and load-bearing capabilities. Snow alone is brittle and prone to failure
under bending stresses, but adding wood, known for its tensile strength, creates a composite struc-
ture that better resists deformation and failure.
This method has been successfully employed in large scale snow constructions such as the Icehotel
in Jukkasj¨arvi, Sweden. In this project, wood is used to reinforce snow arches and load-bearing
elements within the structure. The wooden framework distributes the stresses more evenly across
the snow, reducing the risk of cracking or collapse [3].

2.4.2
Snow foundations
Snow has been increasingly used as a foundation material, particularly in polar regions. However,
snow exhibits a time dependent behavior known as creep, where it slowly deforms under constant
loading. Understanding the creep behavior is crucial for ensuring the longevity and stability of
structures built on snow.
The creep of snow can be modeled using rheological models, which simulate snow’s viscoelastic
behavior. A commonly used model is the Burgers model, which accounts for both the elastic and
viscous components of snow deformation. This model is extended to three dimensions to better
predict the creep behavior under varying loads and environmental conditions [21].
2.4.3
Snow roads
Snow roads are crucial in the Arctic and other cold regions where traditional asphalt or concrete
roads are impractical due to the extreme cold and snow cover. Snow roads are constructed using
compacted snow and are maintained by ensuring proper density and structural integrity.
Similarly, snow runways play an essential role in these regions, particularly in supporting air trans-
port where permanent airstrips are not feasible. Snow runways are built by compacting snow to
create a smooth and stable surface capable of supporting the weight of aircraft during takeoff and
landing.
In its natural state, snow is typically unable to support most wheeled vehicles. Therefore, it is
necessary to modify the snow’s properties.
The most crucial modifications are disaggregation,
compaction, and layering [17].
25
==PAGE:31==
Disaggregation is the process of breaking up the natural structure of snow to create a more uni-
form and dense configuration. This process helps in reducing porosity and increasing the strength
and stability of the snow, making it suitable for construction purposes such as roads and runways.
Disaggregation is typically achieved through mechanical means, such as milling or using specialized
equipment. Sintering starts immediately after the disaggregated snow is deposited [17].
Increasing the compaction of snow significantly enhances its density, which in turn improves its
load-bearing capacity by reducing the presence of air pockets. Compacted snow forms a more
cohesive structure that can better withstand the stresses imposed by vehicles and other loads.
Constructing snow roads in layers, with each layer carefully compacted, results in a much stronger
and more resilient road. Layering not only increases the overall strength and stability of the snow
road but also ensures a more uniform distribution of loads across the surface. This method mini-
mizes the risk of localized stress failures by providing each successive layer with a solid foundation,
thus enhancing the road’s ability to support heavy loads.
By compacting each layer individually, the road achieves optimal density throughout, leading to
a more consistently dense and stable structure compared to attempts at compacting a thick layer
all at once. This approach is particularly effective in maintaining road strength under varying
environmental conditions, such as temperature fluctuations and changes in snow quality, which are
common in cold regions [10].
Temperature affects snow roads because snow and ice become softer and weaker as temperatures
rise. When the temperature increases, snow loses density and its ability to support heavy loads
decreases. This softening causes the snow to compact and deform under the weight of vehicles,
leading to rutting and surface instability.
As a result, maintaining snow roads becomes more
difficult during warmer periods, requiring constant monitoring and compaction to preserve their
strength and usability [10].
McMurdo Station’s snow roads are an excellent example of how temperature impacts transportation
infrastructure in polar regions. The station’s road system extends for approximately 32 kilometers
and connects the base to airfields.
At McMurdo Station, snow roads and runways experience
significant wear and tear during the warmer months, where temperatures may rise above freezing.
During these periods, the load-bearing capacity of the snow decreases due to softening, which leads
to surface instability. The strength and hardness of snow decrease as it melts, which compromises
the roads’ ability to support heavy transport vehicles, leading to rutting, increased maintenance
requirements, and, in some cases, road closure. To mitigate these effects, McMurdo uses snow
processing, age hardening, specialized vehicles, and regular maintenance to ensure road usability
[10].
The performance and longevity of snow roads are heavily influenced by the vehicles that travel on
them. The weight, speed, and type of vehicle can all affect the snow’s compaction, strength, and
wear.
The weight of a vehicle is one of the most significant factors affecting snow roads. Heavier vehi-
cles exert higher pressure on the snow surface, causing increased compaction and, in many cases,
26
==PAGE:32==
deformation.
Different tire types affect the degree of surface disturbance and rutting. High flotation tires gen-
erally distribute the load more evenly, causing less damage [10, 15].
2.4.4
Reinforcement in snow roads and runways
Geocells, also known as Cellular Confinement Systems (CCS), have been widely studied and ap-
plied in various geotechnical and civil engineering projects, particularly for soil reinforcement and
stabilization. The use of geocells in snow road reinforcement is an emerging area of interest, where
the technology’s ability to confine and stabilize material could offer significant advantages.
Geocells work by creating a three-dimensional cellular structure that confines the infill material,
whether it’s snow, soil or aggregate. This confinement enhances the load bearing capacity of the
surface and distributes loads more evenly, which is crucial for maintaining the integrity of snow
roads under heavy traffic [11].
When filled with compacted snow or ice, geocells can increase the stiffness and shear strength of
the snow road. This can help prevent issues like rutting and differential settlement, which are
common problems in snow roads under repeated loading conditions.
Geocells are typically made from high density polyethylene (HDPE) or other durable polymers
that can withstand extreme cold temperatures. This makes them suitable for use in snow and ice
environments.
In practical applications, geocells can be deployed on the surface of the snow road or within the
snow layers. They are then filled with compacted snow or other materials to create a reinforced,
stable surface. This approach has proven effective with other types of soil materials, suggesting
that it holds promise for application in snow as well [10, 15]. Figure 21 shows geocells being filled
with snow.
Another way to reinforce snow roads is to use compacted snow blocks, shown in Figure 22. Snow
can be compressed into highdensity blocks using a technique called hot pressing, which involves
compacting snow at high pressures while it’s near its melting point. The resulting snow blocks are
much stronger and can be used as paving materials for roads or runways. These blocks are highly
resistant to melting and deformation [15].
2.4.5
Snow runways
Snow runways, primarily used in polar regions and other extreme cold environments, serve as vi-
tal infrastructure for aircraft operations where conventional paved surfaces are impractical. The
construction of snow runways involves processing and compacting snow to achieve the necessary
strength to support aircraft of varying weights.
The snow pavement must support the wheel loads of aircraft without excessive deformation or

failure. Load bearing capacity is influenced by the snow’s density, compaction, and age hardening

2.4.6
Snow loads on structures
Properties of snow loads
Understanding the properties of snow as a load on buildings is critical since snow has unique
characteristics that differentiate it from other types of loads. Snow load is calculated from depth
and density and changes over time with new snowfall, melting, and compaction, making them
dynamic rather than static loads. Maximum snow depth and density do not necessarily occur at
the same time. This introduces complexities in designing for snow loads, as the weight can increase
or decrease unexpectedly due to temperature variations, freezing, and thawing processes [22, 23].
28
==PAGE:34==
Snow density also varies according to geographic region, altitude, and climate, with fresh snow
having a lower density and compacted or wet snow having a much higher density. Additionally,
snow has a tendency to accumulate unevenly due to wind, further complicating structural load
calculations [34]. Engineers must consider snow redistribution, roof slope, and the presence of
parapets or other roof obstructions that may influence load concentration.
Calculations of snow loads
To calculate snow loads on structures, several factors need to be taken into account. The basic
equation typically used in most standards for calculating snow load is:
S = µs · Ct · Ce · Sg
(7)
Where µs is the snow load shape factor, accounting for the roof’s geometry (e.g., flat, sloped, or
curved roofs); Ct is the thermal factor, accounting for heat loss from the building that can affect
snow accumulation; Ce is the exposure factor, which considers the building’s surroundings (e.g.,
exposure to wind or surrounding trees/buildings that might shelter it); and Sg is the ground snow
load, derived from snow load maps and meteorological data for a given location.
The shape of a roof plays a significant role in determining how snow accumulates. Flat roofs are
particularly vulnerable to heavy snow loads because snow can accumulate and become compacted
over time. Sloped roofs are generally more effective in shedding snow, but if the slope is too shal-
low, snow can still accumulate, leading to significant loads. Curved or complex roofs may require
special considerations to account for snow drifting and accumulation in valleys or at transitions
between slopes [24].
The thermal factor is crucial in snow load calculations, especially in regions where buildings are
heated. Heat loss through the roof can cause snow to melt, potentially reducing the overall snow
load. However, melted snow can also refreeze, forming ice, which is often denser and more danger-
ous than snow [23]. Insulated roofs with minimal heat loss are less likely to cause melting, so they
may be subject to higher snow loads.
Snow load zones are defined by meteorological data that outline the expected ground snow loads for
different regions. Standards such as those published by the ISO and local building codes provide
snow load maps that builders and engineers must reference during the design process [24]. These
maps take into account the historical record of snowfalls, average annual snow depth, and extreme
snow events. These values are then modified by exposure, roof shape, and thermal conditions to
calculate the final design snow load for a building.
The impact of climate change
The impact of climate change should be considered when calculating snow load zones. Higher
temperatures and more frequent precipitation can reduce overall snow cover while increasing its
density and weight. Additionally, higher temperatures cause more frequent thawing and refreez-
ing, creating ice layers within the snowpack or on surfaces, which can increase loads and structural
stress. These dynamic changes can add complexity to snow load predictions and require engineers
29
==PAGE:35==
to incorporate more flexible design parameters [23].
Climate change increases the likelihood of extreme weather events. Although total snowfall may
decrease in some regions, the frequency of heavy snowfalls is expected to rise, leading to more
frequent extreme snow load events. This results in more variable and unpredictable snow loads,
necessitating careful consideration in building design and maintenance to ensure structural safety
and resilience.
Buildings in regions where snow is a regular occurrence may need to be over-
engineered to accommodate potential future changes in snow accumulation patterns.
Regular
maintenance, including snow removal and the monitoring of ice buildup, will become even more
critical as climate patterns shift [23].
2.4.7
Snow production
Produced snow is superior for construction due to its consistent and controllable properties, such as
density and grain size, which enhance structural integrity and load-bearing capacity, as highlighted
in Section 2.4 of the thesis. This section describes the most common methods of snow production,
emphasizing the environmental factors that significantly impact these processes.
Temperature dependent snow production
Temperature dependent snow production relies on specific atmospheric conditions, primarily tem-
peratures below 0°C, to facilitate natural snow crystallization. This method is significantly influ-
enced by fluctuations in ambient temperature and humidity.
The dry bulb temperature, commonly referred to as air temperature, measures ambient atmo-
spheric temperature without considering moisture. The wet bulb temperature is crucial in snow
production as it represents the lowest temperature achievable through the evaporation of water
into the air at constant pressure. It provides a comprehensive measure of an environment’s cooling
potential by combining the effects of air temperature and humidity. Snow production is feasible
when the wet bulb temperature is below freezing, facilitating the efficient transformation of wa-
ter into fine, durable snow particles. Notably, snow can also be produced at temperatures above
freezing if the humidity is sufficiently low, enhancing evaporation and cooling. The quality of snow
depends on achieving a low wet bulb temperature, ensuring that water droplets can freeze quickly
and completely.
Temperature dependent snow production can be implemented both indoors and outdoors, with
indoor settings offering more controlled conditions for temperature and humidity. Effective indoor
snow production requires dehumidification processes, typically based on removing moisture from
the air by cooling it down below its dew point with a cooling coil, causing water vapor to condense
and thus reducing moisture content. The air is then reheated to an appropriate temperature, main-
taining a low relative humidity suitable for indoor environments. Dehumidification processes are
crucial for indoor temperature dependent snow production to ensure the snow quality and control
the snow’s mechanical properties [43].
Building on these principles, sophisticated snow making machines designed for laboratory settings
have significantly advanced the controlled production of artificial snow. An exemplary model is the
30
==PAGE:36==
Lumi apparatus, which operates within a cold room where temperatures are meticulously regulated
down to -25°C. This apparatus integrates several critical components that work synergistically to
emulate natural snow formation. The core elements consist of two tangential blowers directing
cold laboratory air over water baths maintained between +30°C and +40°C. This configuration
effectively humidifies the air, subsequently directing it into a snow-growing chamber. Here, snow
crystals commence formation on a polymercoated steel grid. Periodic vibrations induced by motors
shake the grid to deposit the formed snow into a harvesting box, facilitating continuous operation.
Encasing this setup is an aluminum frame with fine mesh fabric, optimized to retain moisture
within the system while minimizing the impact of external humidity.
This meticulous control of environmental variables ensures the production of snow with consistent
and replicable characteristics, which is essential for reliable research and testing in the fields of
snow science and engineering [44].
Temperature independent snow production
As global temperatures rise, the reliability of traditional snow making, which depends on subfreez-
ing ambient temperatures, is compromised. Temperature independent snow production becomes
essential for adapting to climate change and supporting snow dependent activities in regions where
temperatures frequently exceed 0°C.
One effective method for temperature independent snow production involves flake ice systems.
These systems operate by cooling water on the surface of a refrigerated drum or cylinder, rapidly
freezing it into a thin layer of ice. As the drum rotates, a mechanical scraper or blade removes
the ice in the form of flakes, which are thin and dry. The efficiency of these systems is driven by a
transcritical CO2 refrigeration cycle, which enhances heat recovery and optimizes thermodynamic
performance. This continuous ice production method not only ensures that the ice is subcooled
and dry, suitable for immediate use in snow production but also produces small, manageable ice
flakes that cool quickly and are easy to distribute. This technology is ideal for creating artificial
snow in varied temperature conditions and is especially valuable where temperature dependent
snow production is not feasible [45].

The literature review has detailed various methods for utilizing snow as a construction material,
from examining its basic properties to its applications in building construction and transportation
infrastructure. This comprehensive overview has compiled a broad range of knowledge, spotlight-
ing both traditional methods and innovative techniques for using snow.
There are many opportunities for innovative uses of snow, particularly in sustainable construc-
tion and temporary structures. Based on the literature review, the study will delve deeper into
understanding snow’s structural properties and explore whether its load-bearing capacity can be

During the test, two failure modes occurred: Normal failure and irregular failure. Both failure
40
==PAGE:46==
modes are known in shear box testing with snow.
Normal failure, seen in Figure 35, typically occurs under conditions where the applied normal
stress is sufficient to compact the snow and maintain its structural integrity during shear. This
mode of failure is associated with the cutting shear mechanism, where the snow behaves similarly
to other granular materials. The failure develops along a well defined shear plane within the snow,
typically following the direction of maximum shear stress. This is a typical response of the snow
when the intergranular bonds are strong enough to resist initial shearing but eventually give way
under continued stress.
Irregular failure, seen in Figure 36, occurs when the thickness of the snow layer being sheared
is insufficient or when the applied normal stress is relatively low, which does not allow the snow
structure to fully stabilize under the shear forces. This type of failure often happens due to inade-
quate confinement of the snow, leading to a lack of uniform pressure distribution across the shear
plane. As a result, the snow may fail in an uneven manner, exhibiting characteristics more akin
to tearing or crumbling, rather than a clean shear. This can also be triggered by high horizontal
forces compared to the normal forces, making the snow behave in an unstable and unpredictable
manner.

Results and discussion
4.1
Snow production
The method presented in this study, for indoor temperature dependent snow production, is suffi-
cient to produce snow under the circumstances presented in Section 3.1. The challenges experienced
with snow production highlight the importance of controlling factors such as humidity and tem-
perature.
The maximum snow production capacity of the SNOWPRO snow machine is 0.023 m3/min [50].
However, taking into account the necessary time for the room to cool down between snow produc-
tion intervals, the effective capacity observed with this method was approximately 8.5·10−5 m3/min,
representing about 0.4% of the stated maximum capacity.
Factors contributing to the decreased production capacity, beyond the limited operational time of
the machine, include spillage, as it was not possible to collect all the snow produced within the
room. Additionally, the snow closest to the machine was more like ice because the water droplets
had not turned into snow due to too short a traveling distance, and the temperature and humidity
were far from optimal most of the time.
In this experiment, the inability to measure or control humidity made it challenging to accurately
assess the quality of the produced snow. The extent to which temperature fluctuations and high
humidity levels influenced snow quality remains unclear. However, these factors likely introduced
variability in the snow properties, complicating the comparison of results from compaction tests,
such as beam tests with and without reinforcement. It cannot be ruled out that the snow used in
reinforced beams was produced under different humidity conditions, potentially resulting in differ-
ent physical properties that significantly affected the outcomes.
To optimize snow production for future experiments, a cooling apparatus with sufficient capacity
to counteract the heating effects caused by water and air from the snow machine, is necessary.
Additionally, methods to remove humidity from the air, should be considered to ensure stable and
consistent snow production.

4.3.4
Comparison of methods to define elastic modulus
In this study, four methods for defining the elastic modulus of snow are presented. The short and
long term incremental loading test and the static loading test all estimated the elastic modulus of
the same snow beams, but gave different results.
The values calculated with Burgers model, gave different results, compared to the results from
the long term incremental loading. The results indicate that the elastic moduli, obtained from
the incremental loading, are more reflective of the short term elastic modulus, rather than the
long term modulus. This observation highlights a key limitation of the that method, it does not
adequately account for the complex, non-linear, time dependent, viscoelastic properties of snow.
Figure 110 shows the difference between values obtained with different methods.
Figure 110: Comparison of elastic moduli obtained
by different methods.
A possible explanation for the significant difference is the age of the beams at the time of testing.
The static loading test was conducted 40- 60 days after the snow beams were produced, and the
Burgess model accounts for both the instantaneous elastic and the time dependent viscoelastic
responses during that period. Meanwhile, the incremental loading test took place 60 days post-
production and represents the elastic modulus of snow beams that have hardened for 60 days.
Therefore, the results may suggest that the elastic modulus has increased over time, aligning with
findings from the literature review which indicate that snow’s elastic properties tend to increase
over time due to sintering. To validate this theory, it would be interesting to conduct static loading
tests with samples of different age with same method.
The method of short term incremental loading and unloading did provide values lower than those
for static loading and long term incremental loading conducted on same beams. Possibly, the elas-
tic modulus increased over time, as the short term incremental test estimates the elastic modulus
after 40 days of hardening, and the long term incremental test after 60 days. Another possible
explenation is that the Bernoulli-Winkler model underestimated the elastic modulus as the model
85
==PAGE:91==
does not fit well with the maximum strain.
The shear box test estimated the elastic modulus of fresh snow, of lower density compared to the
other tests, making comparisons to other tests conducted challenging.
The elastic modulus and viscosity values obtained with all four methods to calculate elastic modulus
of snow, were compared to the data discussed in Section 2.3.2 of the literature review. The data
from the literature review were obtained using different test methods and on snow with varying
properties and temperatures.
However, the fact that the values from the experimental tests,
conducted in this study, fall within the same range indicates that the method used in this study
can be used to estimate the elastic modulus and viscosity of snow. Figure 111 shows elstic moduli
obtained from all four tests compared with values from literature.
Figure 111: Elastic modulus compared to values from litera-
ture [26].
The elastic modulus calculated with the shear box test was significantly lower than the elastic
modulus obtained from the beam tests. The values derived from these different methods cannot
be directly compared because the shear box test measures the elastic modulus of fresh snow, with
average density of 276 kg/m3, whereas the beam test measures the elastic modulus of snow that
has been stored for 40 days, with a density around 500 kg/m3. Considering the differences in
the age and density of the snow samples, it is logical that the results from the shear box test are
substantially lower. The values obtained are significantly lower than those reported in the liter-
ature. However, the literature values are derived from static creep tests on snow over extended
periods[26], which differ fundamentally from the shear box tests on fresh snow conducted in this
study, making direct comparisons inappropriate. In summary, the shear box test yields realistic
measurements of the elastic modulus, particularly when considering the age of the snow samples
tested. Therefore, it makes sense that these values are lower than those obtained from other tests
or reported in the literature.
The values from the short term incremental loading test are significantly lower than the values
from the literature, even though the snow had hardened for 40 days, indicating that a Bernoulli-
Winkler model is possibly not a good match with the measured strain, and that the elastic modulus
is underestimated in the model.
When the results from the static loading test are observed, it can be seen that the short term

literature, indicating a consistent response in the initial resistance to deformation under load. This
consistency suggests that the short term behavior of snow can be reliably predicted based on ex-

prolonged stress, the material exhibits more deformation than typically expected.

the reinforcement is less effective or not effective at all. Given that aluminum is a slippery ma-
terial, the poor adhesion between the snow and aluminum could explain the ineffectiveness of the
reinforcement. Additionally, the reinforcement creates a hole in the middle of the beam, which
could also negatively impact the beam’s load-bearing capacity. The position of the reinforcement
is in the natural axis, that calud also explain why the reinforcement is not effective.
Compared to the pre-test beam, which was loaded after 10 days of hardening and broke under a
40 kg load, the beams tested after 60 to 65 days demonstrated significantly higher load-bearing
capasity. This is consistent with literature suggesting that due to sintering, the strength of the
beams increases over time.
This experiment provided some insight into the maximum load-bearing capacity of snow beams
after 60 to 65 days. However, the large span in maximum bearing capacity, ranging from 65 kg to
205 kg, highlights significant variability in the factors influencing the results.
For future research, it would be interesting to explore whether improving the adhesion between
aluminum and snow could enhance the effectiveness of the reinforcement. Additionally, positioning
the reinforcement lower in the beam, rather than in the neutral axis, might also yield different
results.

5
Conclusion
The first aim of this study was to conduct a systematic literature review. The findings revealed a
limited amount of research on snow as a construction material, underscoring the inherent challenges
associated with its use. Specifically, it is difficult to determine the properties of snow, as they vary
with time and temperature. Moreover, the time dependent properties of snow as a construction
material have been poorly researched and are difficult to predict. Consequently, the results of
the literature review led to the decision to conduct experimental tests to assess the viscoelastic
properties of snow over time and to explore whether reinforcing snow could enhance its structural
properties.
The second aim of this project was to establish a snow production facility at DTU, capable of
producing high-quality snow. By optimizing a snow production setup, this was achievable with
a production capacity of 0.005 m3/hour. The production capacity was lower than anticipated,
due to challenges in maintaining stable room temperature and humidity levels. This instability
in temperature and humidity also led to variations in snow quality.
Although it was possible
to maintain the wet bulb temperature within quality limits, the snow quality varied both across
different production days and within different areas of the room during production. This inconsis-
tency made it difficult to obtain homogeneous samples for testing and comparison. Consequently,
isolating the effects of snow quality from other factors affecting snow properties, such as density,
age, and reinforcement, became problematic. This complexity introduced significant challenges in
interpreting the results of the experimental tests of the study.
The third aim of the study was to conduct a series of experimental tests, reflecting the time de-
pendent properties of snow and trying to determine if the structural properties of snow could be
improved with aluminum reinforcement.
Geotechnical tests provided insights into how hardness, shear strength, and shrinkage of snow
evolve over time. However, these methods also highlighted limitations, demonstrating that snow is
a more complex material than soil, due to its changing properties dependent on multiple factors.
The cone penetration test offered a consistent method to measure penetration resistance on a small
sample, revealing that hardness increased over a 60 day testing period, from 1 MPa to 28 MPa.
The shear box setup used in this study had significant limitations. The setup’s horizontal force was
restricted to 25 kg, limiting the testing scope to snow samples with a density of about 300 kg/m3
and within a 1 hour time frame. Samples, that were more compacted or older than 1 hour could
not be tested. Within this limited scope the results showed that shear strength increased linearly
during one hour. The shear box was used to calculate Shear modulus and Elastic modulus, giving
results on elastic modulus from 0.22 MPa to 0.27 MPa.
The sample used for linear shrinkage lost about 98% of its mass over a 60-day period. The test
setup has significant limitations when compared to soil testing, as snow is a more complex material.
Shrinkage in snow is influenced by many factors beyond water loss from the material. Furthermore,
91
==PAGE:97==
the shrinkage observed in the sample was significantly greater than that in larger samples, kept
under the same conditions. Due to these inconsistencies, this method is not recommended for
accurately observing shrinkage in snow.
Fiber optics were used to measure strain in snow beams. The strain data was used to determine
the stiffness modulus of the snow with a Bernoulli-Winkler model, and to calculate the elastic
modulus, showing that the average elastic modulus varies from 4.8 MPa to 1.9 MPa. the values
obtained with this method were significantly lower than other elastic modulus values observed on
the same beam with other methods, indicating that the model underestimates the elastic modulus
and is not an accurate way to estimate the elastic modulus of snow.
Fiber optic technology proved effective for measuring strain over short periods. However, when
used to determine strain over extended periods, its was limited to just four days.
To estimate if aluminum could improve the structural properties of snow, a series of beam tests
were conducted. By fitting strain data from a 20-day period to the Burgers model, the results show
that the average short-term elastic modulus, EK, is 80 MPa for unreinforced beams and 120 MPa
for reinforced beams. Furthermore, the results show that the long-term elastic modulus, EM, is 5.5
MPa for unreinforced beams and 16.8 MPa for reinforced beams. The results show that there is a
statistical difference in the long-term elastic modulus for beams with and without reinforcement.
Conversely, the elastic modulus, obtained by incrementally loading beams over a period of 5 days,
showed no difference between beams with and without reinforcement. The average elastic modulus
from those tests varied between 188 MPa and 215 MPa.
The maximum load-bearing capacity of the beams after 60 to 65 days ranged from 65 kg to 200
kg, marking a significant increase from 40 kg after 10 days. These beams without reinforcement
showed higher load-bearing capacity.
Given the large variations in snow quality during production and differences in density and other
properties of the beam samples, this study cannot conclusively determine that the aluminum re-
inforcement enhances the structural capacity of the snow beams. Variations in results could be
attributed to differing qualities of snow.
92
==PAGE:98==
6
Perspective
The results of this study did not provide a clear indication of whether aluminum reinforcement
enhances the structural properties of snow. While some trends suggest a potential influence, vari-
ations in snow quality, density, and testing conditions introduce significant uncertainties.
In relation to the production process, standardizing the snow production method to reduce vari-
ability in snow quality and conducting beam tests on more homogeneous specimens would yield
clearer results on the effects of the reinforcement.
The study demonstrated that a beam test, utilizing deformation measurements, and a beam model
to estimate strain over time, can provide a reliable assessment of the viscoelastic properties of
snow. In the study, the deformation of beams is observed over a period of 20 days. The beams
exhibit consistent deformation throughout this period. For future research, it would be interesting
to observe the beams over a longer period to examine how the deformation and strain rate evolve
over a longer time period.
For future research, it would be valuable to further investigate how fiber optic technology can be
implemented. In this experiment, the data was usable for strain measurement within a limited time
frame. However, incorporating improved technologies, such as more robust fiber, better adhesives
like epoxy, or enhanced data processing methods could provide more accurate and reliable strain
measurements over extended periods.
Furthermore, fiber optic technology has significant potential for measuring strain directly on the
reinforcement within the beam. This would provide more detailed insights into the interaction
between the aluminum reinforcement and the surrounding snow, helping to determine whether
reinforcement effectively improves the structural properties of snow.
