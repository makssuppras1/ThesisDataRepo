Executive 
summary 
 
The purpose of the project “Design for disruptive innovation & user-
development” was to solve the problem facing student 
entrepreneurs and DTU [Technical University of Denmark] when it 
comes to lack of idea validation. To solve the problem, an idea 
validation process was designed to encourage students to test 
projects for startup potential in a fast, cheap and fun way. 
The project was divided into four phases following the “Double 
Diamond” methodology. Four phases were derived from this: 
Discover, Define, Develop and Deliver. In the Discovery phase, the 
cause, effect were explored through an extensive survey, designed 
to better understand the entrepreneurial mindset of DTU and 
validate our hypothesis. Data from the survey and material from 
the theory was analyzed in the Define phase in order to specify the 
requirements of a tool that will help students validate ideas and 
encourage them to experiment with startups. 
During the Development phase, the Idea HackPack took its form 
and was further developed through high user involvement and with 
the help of criticism from industry experts. In the define phase, the 
Idea HackPack was “locked” and reflected upon.

1.1 Project description 
1.1.0 Problem 
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) is very ambitious regarding the amount of student 
startups. “Entrepreneurship should be a part of DTU DNA,” says Senior Vice President of DTU 
Marianne Thellersen. There were a total of 1,000 new Danish startups in 2014; this number 
has increased 12% annually since 2009. This proves the ongoing trend of entrepreneurship in 
Denmark. Of the more than 11,500 students at DTU, only 21 student startups were established 
in 2014.1 This number could imply that DTU is not on par with the national rise in 
entrepreneurship.  
With the implementation of Fremdriftsreformen that requires all students to study full time 
and limits the possibility for temporary leave, odds are that even fewer students than 
previously will choose to engage in entrepreneurship, despite this being a wish of DTU. This 
paradox could leave the students in a state of limbo in which they face hard choices such as 
dropping out of school to startup businesses2. Research shows that 67% of students are 
considering pursuing an entrepreneurial career.3 The average student at DTU creates many 
projects during his/her time at DTU; however, only a fraction will become businesses. 
DTU’s Senior Vice President Marianne Thellersen has a wish for DTU to be the leading 
university when it comes to innovation and to develop entrepreneurs who will create new 
workplaces in Denmark. It is about time for DTU to tackle this problem and catch up with 
these ambitions. (For the full interview with Marianne Thellersen, see appendix Dev-1) 
We intend to find a solution to this problem. 
1.1.1 Hypothesis 
Our hypothesis is that students find the process of validating their ideas’ business potential 
complicated, time-consuming and expensive. 
The assumption is that a platform consisting of tangible LEAN-inspired methods – using 
ideas on how tech students can test, develop and measure early concepts on the market – 
can motivate and create a new wave of student startups at DTU. Developing this platform, 
testing the methods and measuring the effects would verify or reject our hypothesis. Should 
this succeed, it is assumed that the outcome might help bridge the gap between the 
educational system requirements and the ambition to create more student startups. 
1.1.2 Research question 
Based on the problem described above, our research question is as follows: 
 
Can you create a tangible tool that will validate ideas quickly and cheaply in a context 
suitable for DTU students?

1.1.4 Background 
A lot of the inspiration for this project is derived from the new movements that are rising 
globally but which have strong ties to the entrepreneurial environment in Silicon Valley and 
the ever-innovative magnetic force that seems to be uniting bright minds in “the Valley”. 
Having spent the past six years at DTU, creating and developing ideas, new entrepreneurship 
would have been used by us a lot earlier because it proves to be such a useful tool for the 
entrepreneurial engineer. We see a contradiction in the general perception of the course of 
action applied to startups from DTU as opposed to those from the movement of “new 
entrepreneurship”. Most prominent is perhaps the lack of customer development.  
This project is about finding out what DTU students need in order to validate their ideas fast 
and cheap. The aim is to design a process, based on “new entrepreneurship”, that will 
facilitate idea validation and make it fun and simple. A criteria for success would be 
implementing a solution into the DTU startup ecosystem.

1.2 Literature 
 
1.2.0 Main literature 
Our initial inspiration for this project has its roots in the movement we have defined as new 
entrepreneurship. The authors of this movement's main literature are mostly people with 
some sort of tie to Silicon Valley. One of the main contributors is serial entrepreneur and 
Stanford professor Steve Blank who is teaching what he learned himself about starting up 
and customer development in particular. Steve is questioning the mentality and also the 
instruction given at institutions such as DTU. Another contributor is serial entrepreneur and 
former student of Steve Blank, Eric Ries, who is the author of “The Lean Startup” and founder 
of the lean startup movement. Google employee Alberto Savoia has coined the term 
pretotyping, which in his own words is the art and science of faking it before making it. A 
common factor for all of them is that they rely on experiments and quick iteration cycles using 
a minimum of resources to validate and refine their ideas and products. This is something 
that aligns very well with our hypothesis about the possibility to make a quick, easy and cheap 
start on your student startup. Besides these main contributors, we have an array of industry 
experts and professors cited throughout the report. 
1.2.1 Literature contribution 
The main focus of this thesis is on developing a product. Therefore the contribution to the 
main literature will be more of a reflection of theory in a practical, applied setting within a 
certain context: the student environment at DTU.  
1.3 Methodology 
 
The following chapter contains documentation and explanation of our actions during the 
discovery phase of the project. 
Our project will follow an engineering/scientific method using these four steps: 
● 
Observing and describing a phenomenon; 
● 
Formulating a causal hypothesis to explain the phenomenon; 
● 
Using the hypothesis to predict and explain observations; 
● 
Measuring prediction performance based on experimental tests. 
 
The intention is to link together field study and theory to ultimately merge the findings of both 
into a tangible product that is designed for the users and based on field research, but also 
supported by relevant theory.

1.4.0 Small batches 
Every milestone report delivered to Balder Onarheim throughout the project, will be a partial 
summary, a “small batch” of what will be in the final report, which is to be delivered on June 
26th, 2015. Small-batch delivery is a known concept often used in LEAN manufacturing 
designed by Toyota. The concept is in contrast to mass manufacturing practices in which 
large batches are produced before delivery. It has been scientifically proven to be faster to 
produce and deliver units in small batches, and simple experiments will show this.5 In our 
case, delivering our report in small batches will benefit us in different ways. It will allow the 
detection of mistakes and wrong decision-making earlier in the process (the “fail fast” 
concept). 
 
It will allow us to iterate on our report more often. 
 
 
The end quality of the report will be better as the usual motivation-draining final two 
months of report writing are spread out over the entire project run time. 
 
 
Contact with our adviser will be more effective. By using the andon analogy6 our adviser 
can detect quality problems at an early stage and stop our workflow so that the problems 
can be fixed. This also means we will have fewer quality control problems in the later 
iterations, which will reduce the attention required from our advisor.  
 
 
Our learning goals will be validated throughout the whole process. 
 
Our final report and end product will be better in every detail because of increased efficiency, 
due to better focus gained from feedback during the 5-month project period and early 
discovery of any quality problems.

2.0 Introduction 
 
The Discover chapter consists of two main parts. The first half addresses topics within the 
subject area of new entrepreneurship theory – customer development, LEAN startup and 
Pretotyping. The second half is a study of entrepreneurship at DTU and includes student 
surveys, mapping of services and the current application of the theory. The research thereby 
covers both a study of relevant theory to apply, and also the users and the context in which 
the product will be introduced. 
2.1 New Entrepreneurship (NE) 
 
The term New Entrepreneurship is still in flux, meaning that no one has really coined the term 
or decided what it specifically covers. The term is used in this project is to distinguish 
ourselves from “old entrepreneurship” (OE).  
 
NE relies on experimentation and user feedback rather than planning and intuition.  
 
NE is iterative and agile in the search for repeatable and scalable business, whereas OE 
uses stage gate and waterfall methods.  
 
This results in an early and routinely validation process for NE and has the side effect of 
potentially limiting resources wasted by a product that users don’t want.  
 
OE has proven time and again that it is capable of producing very profitable outcomes. 
However, the risk of failing as well as the big investment made before the idea is validated. 
Therefore the tools of NE possess great appeal for entrepreneurs having limited time and 
money such as DTU students. 
 
2.1.0 Start-ups and validation defined 
Eric Ries defines startups as “A human institution designed to create new products and 
services under conditions of extreme uncertainty.”8  Steve Blank explains it as “A temporary 
organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model” The definitions 
are many and varying; however, these quotes seem to be most in line with our own 
interpretation. The search and the uncertainty is important to remember because they 
support the idea that you have to continuously build, learn and measure throughout the 
process.  
A validated startup idea is a broad definition and varies depending on industry. Within tech-
startups, Steve Blank9 agrees that a validated idea has demonstrated value and market 
acceptance. Demonstrating value refers to if the idea adds any value to a specific user-group. 
If there is a space on the market for the idea, or if the idea is creating its own new market, 
there is a market-acceptance.

2.1.1 Customer development 
Customer development, invented by Stanford professor and serial entrepreneur Steve Blank, 
is one of the cornerstones of this movement. It revolves around discovering and validating 
the right market for your idea; building the appropriate product features that solve customers’ 
needs; testing the correct model and tactics for acquiring and converting customers; and 
deploying the right organization and resources to scale the business. It is the opposite of 
waterfall product development where you choose a set of assumptions about your 
customers’ needs and problems, but do not validate until the product is in the hands of 
customers, if they buy it. 
 
The benefits of using the theory of customer development actively in the IDHP are distinct 
due to the fact that this approach allows users of IDHP to link an identified group of users to 
a problem and a product. Moreover it encourages the users to do it at a very early stage which 
can potentially prevent a lot of wasted time. 
 
 
Figure above is showing how Steve Blank depicts the symbiosis between the iteration 
that goes on between customer discovery and validation. The two first columns of 
customer development will be the only theory on this subject that we will apply to this 
thesis due to the fact that we focus solely on search and validation.

2.1.2 Pretotyping methods explained 
Pretotyping tools play a big part in the foundation of the NE way to approach startups. 
Pretotyping is a way to test an idea quickly and inexpensively by creating extremely simplified, 
mock-up or virtual versions of that product to help validate the premise “Do the users want 
the product?” Minimum viable products are essentially a functional pretotype. They are, as 
the name implies, products that just barely contain the absolute minimum of functions which 
make them usable by customers and thereby to customer tests. 
  
Many pretotype tools are by definition nothing new; however, newly developed web-based 
tools and platforms such as Google Analytics and Kickstarter are reshaping their possibilities 
and the way to use them. Recently, pretotypes are used in a much more analytical way in 
which the lessons learned from experiments are used in a much more structured manner in 
order to learn about user segment and product popularity. Practical applied pretotyping is 
very beneficial in this project since the users of IDHP have very limited time and money. This 
makes a tangible field tool such as pretotyping very attractive since it delivers fast and cheap 
idea verification. 
 
To view our entire collection of test methods, see appendix Disc-4. 
 
2.1.3 LEAN startup 
LEAN startup applies the LEAN methodology developed by car manufacturer Toyota in Japan 
to the process of starting up a business. It is a tool that essentially focuses on going from 
customer validation to proof of business. 
 
LEAN startup provides a scientific approach to creating and managing startups. Key 
elements of this movement are agility, reduced waste and fast iterations. It is in many ways 
a child of customer development. LEAN startup has often preached mantras like Release and 
Repair which is the opposite of many products that are perfected and even produced before 
ever being introduced to customers.  
The LEAN startup theory is the direct inspiration for our Idea HackPack build-measure-learn 
approach along with the decision to either pivot or persevere based on actionable data.   
The Build phase refers to creating something to present to. It could be anything from a 
pretotype to a powerpoint, the important thing is that you are able to measure your feedback 
and learn from it. 
 
2.1.4 Metrics 
Metrics are important for assessing how you are doing and if you are doing the right thing. It 
does not matter if a million people visit your website if only 10 of them buy your product. 
 
This specific method is called initial level of interest where a conversion rate calculates the 
amount of people who were offered the opportunity to take positive action and the people 
who actually took action. Action can be clicking a purchase button, subscribing to emails, etc. 
With these two numbers a conversion rate is achieved and you can make your very first 
assessment regarding the possible success of your idea. Metrics are all about testing 
hypotheses. Another useful metric tool is a simple survey setup like this: 
 
How would you feel if you could no longer use Idea HackPack for idea validation? 
1. Very disappointed 
2. Somewhat disappointed 
3. Not disappointed 
4. N/A – I no longer use Idea HackPack for idea validation

2.2 Research on entrepreneurship @ DTU 
 
2.2.0 Mapping of resources 
It was necessary to understand the situation of students at DTU and what their options are 
regarding support to become entrepreneurs. The different services/hubs offered by DTU and 
by the Copenhagen area for students were mapped. What was especially interesting was 
what sort of support they offered and what they expected each student to bring to the table. 
The services were color-coded according to the different kinds of services. 
Furthermore, all services available both at DTU and elsewhere to students of DTU were listed 
and placed in a diagram with the X axis going from “Thinking” to “Doing” and the Y axis from 
“Idea” to “Settled Business”. This was a way of clustering the different services. From this 
clustering process we were able to make an interesting conclusion. We found that there was 
a large corner of the chart that was unclustered where idea meets action. This could mean 
that there is no apparent institution that can help you in the critical initial phase of testing and 
validating your idea. 
2.2.1 Startup hubs 
During this research we found amongst other things a comprehensive collection of different 
start-up communities that had all been very short-lived. Their latest Facebook posts were 
years old and no users seemed engaged in any discussions on their webpage. One could ask 
the question, “Why so many would try to make a startup hub instead of just joining and thereby 
strengthening an already existing one?” Silicon Valley guru Paul Graham has several 
suggestions for the recipe of a successful start-up hub.11 A crucial one is number and 
traction. A lot of people have to be involved and contribute for the hub to create value. When 
well-meaning people attempt to create a new hub, they are at the same time cannibalizing 
other hubs.

2.3.0 Strategy

Act as an elevator pitch for our master’s thesis project. It should explain our thesis in a



2.3.4 Challenges within entrepreneurship @ DTU 
In our survey it was seen that 74% of students without a startup thought that time was the 
biggest hurdle to consider when  testing whether their idea had business potential or not. 
When briefly analyzing the entrepreneurship theory that DTU students are subjected to, this 
seems reasonable. The courses judge the students based on a business plan and the 
conducted market analysis. For a student with limited time, expertise in technology and not 
business, this may be perceived as confusing and overwhelming. Assessing cost structure, 
revenue streams and threat of entry from competitors might discourage the student from 
even testing their idea. One might argue that a lot of the theory taught by professors at DTU 
is more relevant for already-existing companies with defined core values rather than the DTU 
student start-upper. Stanford Professor Steve Blank argues this. Old entrepreneurship tells 
people to “go research and write a business plan, you will then know everything and you can 
go run your business.” This has been the way to conduct entrepreneurship for the past 50 
years. In 1908 Harvard created the MBA. It was created out of the need of competent men to 
run the already existing big companies.13 However, no tools for starting new businesses were 
created in the process. All tools used in the MBA curriculum in the first chaotic year or two of 
an early stage venture are useless. Startups have been treated as just smaller versions of a 
larger company. The biggest difference, Steve Blank says, is the fact that big companies 
execute, but startups are searching for something to execute. “We used to believe that if you 
did an operation plan + financial model, you could forecast the future and just hire people to 
execute your plan. We now know that no business plan survives first contact with 
customers.”14  
Startups are very unpredictable series of events that you cannot fit into a 5-year plan. This 
seems to be an insight that has not reached the campus of DTU. In the course entitled 
Knowledge-Based Entrepreneurship a long and detailed business plan is expected from the 
students before they even have the chance to expose their idea to customers. It seems that 
only one course (Innovation in Product Development) offers lectures about entrepreneurship 
in a context that is more applicable for students with ideas. However, the prefessor gives you 
a DTU-patented technology and still makes you focus on writing a business plan rather than 
validating whether the idea/patent is even viable. 
We believe that there is a gap between what is taught at DTU and what the optimum process 
in real life is. When going through our survey answers for students with a startup, the vast 
majority answered “gut feeling” when asked how they knew their idea was worth 
implementing. When comparing that to the estimate of 90% of startups that fail, our 
assumption is that the initial step of customer validation is vastly overlooked. This initial step 
will answer many questions and is a much quicker way of evaluating market fit.

2.3.5 Waterfall product development 
Waterfall product development is a very common process for developing product and making 
them into a business with strong ties to old entrepreneurship. Waterfall methods assume you 
know the customer’s problems and needs from day one. In a big company with existing 
products, existing sales channels and existing customers, this makes good sense; but in a 
startup, usually all you have is an idea.  
The problem is that working from a waterfall method makes you assume that you know every 
detail of the customers’ problems and needs and therefore you are able to assume every 
product feature of your product. This will make you invest a lot of time and money into a 
product offering features you don’t even know are wanted or not.  
The table above from 2014 shows that lack of customers is by far the greatest cause why 
startups fail. The waterfall development method, explained on the graphic below, does not 
take customer validation into account because the product meets the end user before it’s in 
the store. Build-test-measure iterations and small batches can help eliminate this problem 
and thereby decreasing the risk of failing.

2.4 Discover-phase conclusion 
To sum up and conclude on the conducted research: New entrepreneurship is not taught and 
practiced at DTU. Entrepreneurship professor John Hebøll who manages the biggest 
entrepreneurial course at DTU is still telling start-uppers to write out a 5-year business plan 
when they get an idea. The resources and services available for DTU students were mapped 
and an interesting gap was found for the initial phase of idea testing and validation – it was 
almost completely neglected. 
 
The first part of our hypothesis – that students find it very time consuming, expensive and 
complicated to start a startup – has been confirmed as true. Furthermore, a new insight is 
that the missing customer development phase is being replaced with gut feeling and a “just 
do it” attitude. Eric Ries states that when you “just do it” and then see what happens, all you 
succeed in is seeing what happens. Our landing page ideahackpack.com had a fair amount 
of traction when taking into account the selective promoting of our project. Our mission with 
the Idea Hackpack was to analyze the crowd, have them fill out our survey and maybe get 
some “first-mover” fans. If have 1,000 likes on Facebook was desired, this could probably 
also have managed that, but it would have been seen as a perfect example of a vanity metric. 
Better 100 lovers than 1,000 likes. On the other hand, it was hard to catch the attention of the 
more “hard” engineer programs at DTU. 
2.5 Reflection 
 
The subject was chosen not only as the result of a wish to dig into an obvious problem at 
DTU, but also because of personal interest in the subject. In the last few years a movement 
of start-ups have appeared, that builds on Steve Blanks’ and Eric Ries’ theories. Their books 
have been studied thoroughly and many of the given theories have been tried in real life over 
the last 2-3 years. “Spinning out” our master thesis as an experiment with the primary goal of 
learning and not “surviving” (bootstrapping) has enabled us to risk more and go all-out with 
new entrepreneurship methods. The main difference between a real-world startup and our 
masters’ thesis has been the work pace. Reading secondary literature and the documentation 
process has been time-consuming, and this has meant that our detail resolution has not been 
as high at milestone 1 as was hoped. We tried to reach out to as many stakeholders as 
possible. Many of these persons were dead ends, while some other stakeholders we did not 
initially expect much from ended up providing us with a lot of valuable information. As 
mentioned, it has been frustrating and time-consuming to discover the many options for the 
entrepreneurial student. The sad story is that many of the offerings are a top-down 
constructed, confusing and incoherent collection of old-fashioned entrepreneurship 
strategies which is close to impossible to navigate within the ecosystem (like CIEL’s beautiful 
but scary roadmap). Our hope is still that the Idea HackPack can disrupt this static scene 
which is sucking the entrepreneurial spirit out of the many potential game-changers at DTU.

4.1.3 The pre-Hackpack “hackpack” 
It was realized that there had to be a pre-hackpack process. A process where you would do 
very simple tasks before even considering to apply your idea to the IDHP. Simple tasks 
include: 
“Did you Google it?” A simple but very useful thing to do – maybe you were not the first 
person to think of this idea. 
“Asking your mom” (or friends) by sharing and using open source on a macro level is super 
useful. Just articulating and describing your newly born idea will make you evaluate its 
general principles fairly quickly. However be aware that the feedback you get from friends 
and family is usually very biased and a great deal more positive than from the rest of the 
world. 
“Pinocchio.” If possible, do an initial Pinocchio. For example, if you have come up with the 
idea for an app, pretend you have the app on your phone and use it for a week and see how 
you feel about it. 3D print a “dead” product and pretend it has the features of your product. If 
you yourself will not use the product, no one will. 
“Substitutes.” How do people currently solve the problem your idea is designed to make more 
manageable? If they don’t do anything to resolve a need or problem, odds are that the pain 
the problem is causing is not great enough. 
“Runway.” How much time are you willing to spend on validating your idea? It is nice to have 
a preconceived notion about the amount of time you will invest in the project before you start 
out. 
 
The gain from this initial pre-HackPack phase is a big leap towards knowing whether to 
pursue your idea further. In many cases, the lessons might be enough to already kill the idea. 
An example was the inception of a baby smartwatch. It should monitor stats such as heart 
rate and temperature on newborns to relieve anxious new parents. However, after a quick 
search on Google it was clear that others had come up with this idea and several companies 
were already established and had traction. Since our idea did not have a unique feature 
distinguishing us from the others, it was decided to kill the idea.

4.1.4 Features 
The features step is implemented so that users of the IDHP can think about their idea’s 
functions and features. 
 
As described earlier, ideas have very different characters depending on how developed they 
are. Rapid ideas usually have one specific feature while the more developed ideas suffer from 
too many features. The mission with the feature section is to boil down the idea to one unique 
feature that is unique for your idea. The unique feature has to offer something which no-one 
else offers. 
 
The reason for doing this is to avoid launching a non-innovative red ocean product. Serial 
entrepreneur and author Seth Godin argues the importance of the “purple cow” which is the 
unique product feature that will distinguish your product from all others.21

4.1.5 Problem 
To focus attention and clarify the purpose of the product, users write down a main problem 
that the chosen unique feature solves. What is important to note is that initially, idea owners 
are assuming that their users have these problems, but they won’t know until the first test. 
Therefore, it is wise to think about several problems with different aspects so that a possible 
other main problem can be chosen. Problems are either functional or obstacles.22  A 
functional problem typically means the current solution does not solve the problem well 
enough or has a negative consequence (ex: bike helmets protect my head, but make me look 
ugly). Obstacles are preventing the function from even being carried out (no bike helmet will 
fit my head because I wear a turban). The intent is for users of the IDHP to consider this 
distinction when formulating the questions. By being very specific to what problem is solved, 
the ideator is able to narrow down the value proposition.

4.1.6 Minimal Viable User-Group 
The purpose of this section is to find a specific, tight customer cluster, which is experiencing

you should target one customer segment at the time and use that as a base for targeting the 
next. By starting with the early adopter, you launch using the segment that will love your 
product the most and build from there. This links up very well with our LEAN approach of 
initially only using resources on a small user group to apply agile learning.

4.1.7 Action Data Plan 
This box is important and has a key function on the IDHP board. Before starting each test, 
you have to decide on a percentage value which your test must equal in order to validate. This 
means that if you are not able to live up to these preconceived criteria of success during your 
rounds, your idea should essentially be killed. These numbers are derived partly from 
scientific research, back-of-the-envelope calculations and the theory behind creating a 
monopoly in a niche market24. By noting these down before testing, you are much less likely 
to start fooling yourself afterwards regarding your criteria of success. It will be harder to lie 
to yourself. 
The tests carried out during the IDHP process should get the idea in first degree contact with 
its users. However, it is very hard to evaluate the “goodness” of the idea without some 
predetermined value sets to compare with. This is the basis for our thoughts behind killing 
your idea. It is much harder to revise the success of your idea after a test if you have no 
preconceived notion about what percentage you wanted to achieve. This is why it’s not only 
important to stick to your action data plan, but also design your tests in a way that lets you 
gather some form of quantitative data. 
 
Action data plan for test 1 
Test 1 is about finding out how many users experience your problem. By narrowing down the 
minimum viable user group to the most concentrated unit possible, you should have a group 
of users that should almost all have your problem. This means you should set your success 
metric high, perhaps around 90%. 
 
Action data plan for test 2 
Test 2 is about finding out how many users want your idea. This success metric we base on 
the 40% rule popularized by Sean Ellis after examining many startups. It states that if 40% of 
your customers consider your product as “must have,” then you have accomplished a 
product/market fit.25 Again, it is a rule of thumb and users of the IDHP should customize to 
their own project accordingly, but we recommend approximately 40%. 
Our definition for users who want the idea, is that they actually want the idea, by taking some 
sort of action. 
 
Action data plan for test 3 
Test 3 is about finding out how many users are willing to pay for your product. This success 
metric is very individual. Keep in mind this test is only conducted on a minimum viable user 
group who should all have the problem and around half should want the product. This is why 
our recommendation is setting your success metric to about 20%. 
 
If the idea owner went out and immediately tested if the specified minimal viable user group 
wanted to buy the idea, and afterwards failed, the idea owner will have no clue as to what 
would be wrong with the idea. By testing the riskiest assumption first, if people have the 
problem, it enables the idea owner to trim his/her idea incrementally.

If we start by looking at the relation between data quality and time/test-phases.  
The test methods included in test phase 1 all have a nature of participatory design and 
evaluation. This data represents a mix of what the users “say” and to some degree what they 
do. Verbal evidence can be very inaccurate and many factors should be taken into account 
when analyzing people's “opinions”. The data quality within test phase 1 has “low quality” 
when it comes to quantifiable data, while the tests in the other two test phases have a higher 
degree of hard data/evidence linked to them. 
 
In test phase 1 the uncertainty connected with the idea is relatively high. The idea is yet 
untested and there are a lot of factors from the building phase that can be invalid. As the idea 
makes it through the different iterations, it converges towards a validated idea and the 
uncertainty level decreases. 
 
 
The effort required to execute the different test methods also varies. Interviewing only takes 
minutes, while setting up a successful crowd funding campaign takes weeks. This fact is also 
linked with how the test methods are divided into the specific test phases. 
 
Because of this high risk of wasting a lot of effort on work that might prove unfruitful, it is 
unwise to start testing using high-effort methods instead of starting out with the faster and 
less demanding ones. 
 
It is always recommended that the idea owner chooses testing methods, which require 
minimum effort to execute, so they can test earlier and refine later. All the tests are listed in 
the Idea HackPack 101 and the more advanced pretotypes are explained later in “practical 
applied pretotyping”.

4.1.9 Learning 
The learning box is where you gather your data and compare it to the action data plan that 
you formulated earlier. The key function for the learning stage is reflecting on -and evaluating 
the results from your field expeditions and deciding whether to pivot or persevere.  
 
The research showed that students have a tendency to fall in love with their ideas and develop 
a strong urge to see them through. This affects decision-making during an IDHP process. If 
you completed the action data plan and test phase without having reached the desired 
outcomes, it is much harder to look at the numbers and tell yourself that despite pivots you 
were unable to succeed but that your idea still good.

4.2.1 Pretotype – experiments 
Pretotyping has become a buzzword lately in product development circles and is receiving 
many praises. However, it is one thing to understand the power of testing your product or 
reaching your users at a very early stage; it is quite another thing to actually be able to do it. 
All of the IDHP pretotype methods were tried and tested using our own experiments. We did 
this to learn more about how they could be applied in a student idea-related context and 
benefit the IDHP. Two prototype experiments (Mechanical Turk and MVP)  are featured in the 
following section while the complete prototype experiment catalog can be seen in Appendix 
Dev-13. 
 
Mechanical Turk 
 
For the mechanical Turk we collaborated with a group of students who needed help testing 
their idea. They had found a technology (conducting ink) that could be used to make invisible 
switches. To test the value and obtain user feedback, a mechanical Turk was carried out. 
Users were led into a room where a clear sticker was placed on a roof-to-ceiling window. The 
real switch was placed just on the other side of a pillar right next to the window. The user 
would touch the sticker while the test facilitator would cautiously flip the real switch with his 
far side hand. Since the room was illuminated with fluorescent lighting, the user did not notice 
the lack of response. Fluorescent lights are always a bit slow to turn on.

personal interest in the Idea HackPack in order to validate his idea in a structured manner. 
His profile and an example of a feedback session can be seen in appendix Dev-12. We had 
multiple feedback sessions with him in order to get “black hat” critiques of Idea HackPack. 
His critique point were mostly on the use-experience of the IDHP, the back-end management 
of ideahackpack.com and inspiration to use cases. 
 
4.4 Development phase conclusion 
 
In the development phase the last part of our hypothesis was validated. It was possible to 
develop a product that would help validate students’ business ideas cheaply and quickly. This 
had many positive side effects such as getting the engineer out of the building and in front of 
their customers at a very early stage of development. The goal of developing a process that 
would help kill bad ideas and improve good ones was achieved. In the true LEAN spirit, the 
risk of wasting much time and money was reduced. The chosen theory of LEAN, pretotyping 
and customer development applied to the development phase output (IDHP). It was punished, 
vandalized and put to the test during the experiments and user sessions. On the basis of this 
information it was decided that the chosen theory was indeed applicable and usable for idea 
validation.

4.5 Reflections on the development phase 
In order to develop the Idea HackPack the theories preached (LEAN, customer-development, 
pretotyping etc.) were also practiced in this phase. The first versions of the IDHP we 
presented for our test-panel were very rough and poor looking. It was a part of the release-&-
repair strategy where we would conduct incremental and rapid development through user-
feedback. We found out that being “embarrassed” takes practice and is a big hurdle for many 
in order to release products early. By being visible and loud, important stakeholders have 
approached us and offered their support, both economy- and knowledge-wise. This has been 
a revelation on the benefits of keeping projects “open”.   
The first week of developing the IDHP-structure could be better documented. Fast pace and 
traction was some of the reasons causing the lack of good photo-documentation. White-
boards were plastered with post-it’s containing potential elements of the toolkit. These 
elements would be shifted around, deleted and added as different cases were applied. The 
radical changes decreased after 3 weeks, and the closer the deadline came the more detail-
oriented the development was. Few days before the deadline, we decided to “lock” the design 
and seek the industry-professional’s feedback.  
In the development-phase, we planned to roll out four “real-life” experiments in order to test 
pretotypes and the IDHP-process. Only two of the real-life experiments were tested, as the 
Venture-Cup cases proved to be more efficient when pressure testing the IDHP. The VC-cases 
simply represented the student-ideas from all faculties better.   
Idea HackPack had very different nature depending on the idea being validated. E.g. the 
amount of time used to validate/invalidate the ideas in the development phase varied from 
10 minutes to 10 days. 
Besides the lack of documentation, a few more things could have been better. It was rather 
fast decided upon a gamified process, as the benefits of this seemed very clear. We could 
although have been more divergent in the beginning of the development-phase in order to 
cover the solution-field better within gamification.

5.0 Final design 
 
The final design of Idea HackPack consisted of a physical process board. From user sessions 
we received positive feedback on the Idea HackPack being a lay-flat, tangible and inviting 
“board game”. The following benefits were found in the board-game media. 
 
Unboxing and assembling the Idea HackPack seemed to encourage a more playful state 
of mind. 
 
It invited to better interaction between team members, as they stood in front of each other 
instead of facing a poster wall. 
 
It helped make the Idea HackPack a “plug n’ play” process attractive to students who 
normally would find entrepreneurship unappealing and complicated. 
 
A feeling of achievement seemed to be voiced by users when seeing their obvious 
progress on the board. 
 
The behavioral pattern of users was easier to influence to push them a bit away from what 
they were primed with already. 
 
The Idea HackPack communication was kept in an informal language and the tacky tagline 
“free the unicorns” together with the cartoon icons gave the Idea HackPack a humorous 
angle.  
In version 7 (final version) all the elements from version 6 were retained. A “pre-hack” was 
added, which the user must complete before proceeding to the wheel. This pre-hack consists 
of elements from the “pre-Hackpack hackpack” described in the development phase. 
“Functions” was also changed to “Features”. The reason was that “feature” relates better to 
actual value propositions of the idea for the end user, where “functions” were pointing more 
towards describing the technological advantage (not necessarily helping the user). The UX 
was generally updated, for example with descriptive text in the “test” element, and space for 
Post-its in the “learning” section and guiding arrows.

5.1 Idea compatibility 
 
From the experiments in the Development-phase we eventually began to see a pattern of 
which ideas were most compatible to the IDHP. These can be read in the following sections. 
 
5.1.0 Idea maturity 
Ideas have a wide variety of maturity when they are chosen to base a startup upon. It is a 
known fact that many tech people can work on an idea in their mind for years, detailing 
everything carefully. When the timing is right the idea will surface in the real world with 
complete blueprints and proof of concept. These ideas are perfectly applicable, but the idea 
owner will without a doubt have a harder time invalidating their idea. This can be illustrated 
with the curve below from Alberto Savoia’s “Pretotype It”. The curve shows the relation 
between time spent on an idea and the compulsion to keep going at all cost. 
Another case is the “instant ideas”. These ideas are very rough and shared with others at an 
early stage. In many cases, early-stage instant ideas have fewer functions and have a 
tendency to be more disruptive. Examples of instant ideas could come from our Idea Slot 
Machine28 that we used to come up with random instant ideas for our workshop the 30/4 
(appendix Dev-4). The instant ideas have a higher chance of being invalidated at an earlier 
stage as the idea owner has a minimum of personal affection for the idea. 
 
Summing up: ideas of all maturity stages can and should be applied to the Idea HackPack. 
 
5.1.1 Ideas that work 
From the ideas we have applied to the Idea HackPack (appendices Def-2, Def-3) we know that 
most human-centered startup ideas will work, no matter if the idea is targeting a billion 
users29 or merely a few hundred. There are however ideas which will fit better than others. 
Ideas which are disruptive or discontinuous, and therefore force a change of behavior in the 
customer will in our experience fit perfectly, “spot on”. An idea which on the other hand is 
making an existing product cheaper or in another color is in the grey zone of applicable ideas 
and will usually fail at an early stage.

5.1.2 Ideas that might not work 
Ethically sensitive ideas 
The pretotype used for tests has been called by its own inventor, “the act of faking it before 
making it.” For many ideas this is perfect. However, if you are working for example with 
medical technology, one might argue that putting a fake product for sale to treat an illness is 
crossing an ethical line. You can revise many of the methods to give you important lessons 
about the problem and your user group, but pretotyping has to be used more cautiously. 
Research ideas 
Some students came with ideas that were more related to research projects than potential 
startups. This was the case with some of the students, which came from DTU Space. Their 
ideas had a nature of being “big picture” solutions or very theoretical and it was therefore 
extremely difficult to discover both end user and problem that it solved. A real world example 
is nanotechnology. It was invented in the 1980s but did not find a commercial application 
until decades later. Since at its birth it did not solve a specific problem, a user group would 
be hard to find, thus IDHP would be of no use. 
Non-problem solving product ideas 
Many ideas that have success on e.g. Kickstarter do not solve any specific problem. An 
example could be the floating light bulb, which is a campaign on Kickstarter that at the 
moment of this writing has over $500,000 in funding. The floating light bulb is what we qualify 
as a nonfunctional product idea since it does not solve a problem. The nonfunctional ideas 
usually have an “artsy” character and value propositions such as “pretty” or “good looking”.

5.2 Further work 
 
5.2.0 General improvement 
We do not foresee any radical changes within the process of Idea HackPack, but small 
incremental adjustments are inevitable as more and more idea cases are tested.  
The Idea HackPack concept is still young, and because of this it is still relatively untested, 
even though more than 50 people have run through the product. When validated ideas spin 
out they should in the future be featured on IdeaHackPack.com as a form for validation mark. 
Having settled companies test and use the method actively within their product development 
could also be a great validation stamp to feature. 
 
5.2.1 Pocket version 
Many of the subscribers were on “solo ventures” when it came to their startup experiment. 
For these students a “pocket-version” of the Idea HackPack would be appropriate. It could be 
implemented as a printable A3 version, without dedicated space for social 
brainstorming/Post-its. 
 
5.2.2 Digitized version 
A digitized version/learning tool would be a good solution for entrepreneurs outside the 
Danish entrepreneurial/tech ecosystem. It could be implemented as an app or a simple web-
based tool. The programming skills are not present within the current team so a back-end 
developer would be needed for this (approximate cost $30,000). 
 
5.2.3 Business planning 
Spinning out Idea HackPack as a startup was not the project’s intention. Major interest from 
stakeholders, and the fact that we touched a “dry spot” within the startup ecosystem, has 
shown that there was business potential within the project. 
 
Designing the business model for the project has only briefly been touched upon; the 
following models seemed relevant: 
● 
A “freemium” business model. This meant that we offer a free downloadable version 
from our site and a “premium” physical version consisting of process board, booklet, 
brainstorming tools and box. 
● 
Acquired. Another possibility was to sell the concept to game manufacturers as game 
tools30 specialized in gaming knowledge and distributing games to institutions. 
● 
License. DTU has shown great interest in the project. A third option could be to 
license/rent the Idea HackPack project to institutes in return for facilitating 
workshops, keeping board games updated, or tweaking the project to fit specific 
knowledge fields as “DTU Food”.

5.3 Challenges 
 
Through user involvement, we highlighted different mental challenges that the Idea HackPack 
must take into account. 
 
5.3.0 Unlearning Classic Entrepreneurship 
Many courses that are available or mandatory to start-up interested students follow the 
curriculum of classic entrepreneurship and the students are graded according to how well 
they have mastered these skills. The IDHP follows the radically different theories of new 
entrepreneurship (See section 2.1 “New Entrepreneurship”), and therefore the skills taught in 
DTU’s entrepreneurial courses are contradictory to the ones preached in the Idea HackPack 
process. 
 
5.3.1 Practicing embarrassment 
During our testing, we concluded that many engineers have a detail-oriented and perfectionist 
nature. This skill set is very useful in some aspects of running a start-up, but in an early idea-
validation process the engineer has to perform imperfect, fast and deliver unfinished 
solutions that lead to a feeling of embarrassment. No-one likes to be embarrassed, but it is a 
natural and nearly essential feeling connected to a “perfect” MVP. The perfectionist and anti-
embarrassment impulses could be closely connected to the fear of failing as we discovered 
in our survey in the discovery phase. Teaching the students to embrace failure is a much 
bigger task than what we can grasp in this thesis. 
 
5.3.2 Stubbornness 
Engineers have a tendency to treat their ideas like overprotective moms treat their babies. 
This is done by sweeping away criticism or any dangers that might kill the idea.  
“People are motivated to overcome personal obstacles through public urging by their closest 
friends” (The Lean Entrepreneur, p. 181). As Eric Ries wrote, the idea owner often only shares 
his/her idea to a very select group of “yes sayers” instead of seeking so-called “black hats”.  
We make the IDHP user set up criterias for success (Action data plan phase) prior to 
pretotype testing. If a person has an unrealistic evaluation of the idea value, there should be 
a rude awakening when the data is gathered. The setup of the IDHP is so that if you are not 
able to reach the success criterias in the action data plan, then you can pivot and pivot again 
but shortly after the options will be exhausted and the function tree will advise you to kill idea.  
Eliminating self-delusional idea creators is impossible, but IDHP is giving them a very hard 
time.

5.4 Reflections on the project 
5.4.0 Theory and literature 
In retrospect, the main theory applied matched our needs to a very satisfactory level. It 
offered great solutions to our problems and offered a good connection between theory and 
applied practice which fit the nature of the project. Furthermore, the merging of LEAN startup, 
Customer Development and pretotyping was a strong and relevant combination. Pretotyping 
methods and a LEAN-based action data plan supported each other perfectly. 
 
5.4.1 Method 
We stuck to our initial strategy as outlined below: 
 
● 
Observing and describing a phenomenon; 
● 
Formulating a causal hypothesis to explain the phenomenon; 
● 
Using the hypothesis to predict and explain observations; 
● 
Measuring prediction performance based on experimental tests. 
 
This structure served our needs well and acted as good guidelines to the scientific approach 
of our thesis. 
 
The strategy of launching fast, iterating often and constantly being exposed to users worked 
well. We developed the basis of user feedback from tests and workshops, our own 
experiments and existing Venture Cup cases. Our ideas for the board based on brainstorming 
were quickly put to the test in real situations, which meant that all the elements of IDHP have 
been tested and approved. This felt very effective since no extensive work was wasted on 
something we did not know would work.   
 
5.4.2 Experiments 
The experiments conducted during the development phase taught us a lot about idea 
validation and applying pretotyping into a structured context. It is not as easy as the 
description of the various methods makes it sound. If you plan to get data, you don’t just 
make a mechanical Turk or a fake door. At least, not if you plan to obtain reliable data from 
it. The quantifiable data that you receive is also questionable and very dependent on the 
quality of your pretotypes. Reaching your Minimum Viable User Group (MVUG) can be tricky. 
It was concluded that being part of the MVUG yourself or having relations with them could be 
a big advantage. This is due to the fact that reaching your MVUG and exposing them to your 
test can be hard. The experiments proved very valuable when evaluating the value of 
pretotypes as an idea validation tool and helped shape much of the IDHP board. 
 
 
==PAGE:97==
96 
 
5.4.3 Testing pretotypes 
Working with all the different pretotypes provided good insights into the amount of work 
required by the various pretos and what lessons they were able to provide. Afterwards we 
could see how the pretotypes could be divided into two groups: product testing and market 
testing. Pinocchio's and minimum viable products were great at testing the product part of 
an idea, either on yourself or on a potential user. Fake door, provincial or one-night-stand are 
best at testing the market, whether people want it, who wants it and how to sell it.

5.4.4 Validation of Idea HackPack 
Applying IDHP to IDHP would be a way of validating – or invalidating – the idea of a gamified 
process for testing ideas fast and cheap. What would be important are the questions, how 
many people have the problem (of finding it hard to know if their idea is good), how many 
want to use IDHP, and how many are willing to pay. 
From our survey in the discovery phase, it would be safe to say that of the entrepreneurial 
students at least 80% have the problem. 
  
Students who subscribed the Idea HackPack newsletter during the development period 
received a downloadable version of the Idea HackPack. 90% of the emails sent out were 
opened and the file downloaded.31 This showed a relatively high rate of “returning customers”, 
and what was more interesting was to experience how they could apply the Idea HackPack 
to their specific specialization (Food, Space, Design & Innovation, Management, etc.).32 
 
Regarding how many people would be willing to pay for IDHP, this test is a bit off center. We 
would not expect any students to pay for the IDHP; however, we have had a good handful of 
requests to host workshops for a decent fee. To sum up, the IDHP seems validated as a good 
idea. A metric yet to evaluate would be what Alberto Savoia calls “ongoing level of interest”. 
If students will keep using IDHP, we will consider it a triumph. 
 
6. Conclusion 
When setting forth with this project, we wanted to try to prove a hypothesis. The first part of 
this process was proven already after the discovery phase. We could with quantifiable data 
prove that students found it time-consuming, expensive and hard to validate ideas for 
business potential. Our research had proven that “new entrepreneurship” was still not very 
present at DTU. Students at DTU were not exposed to the concept of idea validation 
throughout their academic career even though the amount of entrepreneurial courses had 
increased the last few years. Resources and services available for DTU students were 
mapped in the discovery phase and it was concluded that methods of idea testing and 
validation was almost completely neglected – even though it is one of the single most 
important factors in a modern startup. 
The second part of the hypothesis, “We can design a process that will make it cheap and fast 
to validate ideas,” was the hard part. After developing, doing real-life experiments, workshops 
and talking to students, professors and entrepreneurs, we believe we have accomplished that 
goal.  
Idea HackPack was designed to validate early concepts and ideas using theory from the New 
Entrepreneurship movement including customer development, pretotyping and LEAN 
startup. By invalidating ideas without market need, validated ideas in the Idea HackPack have 
potentially up to a 42% higher chance of succeeding.  
Major challenges were found during the development phase. The theory used in Idea 
HackPack was new to most students and the thought of meeting real people and not fully 
developing their product before getting feedback was very disruptive.  
The Idea HackPack was exposed to its own validation principles as they were discovered. 
Getting feedback on rough versions of the Idea HackPack from the test panel, industry 
experts and students helped converge towards a functional, tagible and commendable final 
product.  
We believe that the learning goals defined in the project description were fully met and 
practically applied during the whole project period.

6.0 Limitations 
The evaluation, validation and quality assessment of IDHP has been based on feedback from 
four perspectives: students (users); professors with relevant competencies; people adept 
within the field of startups; and our own practical experiments. All of these have indicated 
that the structured validation process of IDHP is a useful tool. However, a limiting factor in 
the process has been linking a good idea to a good business. In defense of IDHP it is only a 
tool for idea validation. Therefore it does not take into account important subjects such as 
revenue streams, cost structure, market size estimations, etc. In other words, referring to the 
startup definition discussed in the discovery chapter, IDHP will not tell you if you have a 
scalable and repeatable business. It will make sure you avoid the biggest reason for startup 
failure – which is no market need. The IDHP cannot ensure success, but vastly reduces risk 
and waste. Nonetheless, due to the limited resources including time, no successful startup 
has been created based on an initial IDHP process.  
 
6.1 Implications 
Since IDHP is disrupting the traditional entrepreneurship at DTU the hope is that it will create 
several implications and on different levels. First and foremost is that our engagement and 
feedback from professors at DTU, along with the fact that DTU’s SVP Marianne Thellersen is 
pushing for every department on DTU implementing entrepreneurship into the student 
curriculum. This could open up possibilities for IDHP to become an integrated part of theory 
taught at DTU. Several courses and summer schools have already inquired about IDHP 
workshops. Furthermore, since this tool was created specifically with students in mind, the 
hope is that the appeal of IDHP could create a “bottom up” effect in which entrepreneurial 
students will pick up IDHP and start using it on their own inclination.
