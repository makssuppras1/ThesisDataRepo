Abstract
Understanding the relationship between subjective and objective measurements of snor­
ing in children is crucial for early detection and management of sleep­disordered breathing
(SDB). Although snoring is often viewed as a less severe form of SDB than obstructive
sleep apnea (OSA), it should not be dismissed as harmless. Research indicates that ha­
bitual snoring can negatively impact children’s health. This study evaluates the predictive
validity of snoring­related questions from parental questionnaires compared to objective
polysomnography (PSG) measurements. A community­based cohort of 107 Icelandic
children underwent one night of PSG to measure objective snoring. Parents completed
questionnaires containing specific snoring­related questions. The results indicate that
these snoring­related questions from parental questionnaires demonstrated high speci­
ficity but moderate to low sensitivity in predicting objective PSG measurements. Sensitiv­
ity measures the proportion of true positive cases (children who snore) correctly identified
by the specific question, while specificity measures the proportion of true negative cases
(children who do not snore) correctly identified. Significant weak correlations were found
between subjective parental reports and objective snoring measures. The objective snor­
ing ≥2% of the night was identified as optimal threshold for defining objective snorers.
Question 4 (”Does your child snore loudly?”) showed the best balance between sensitiv­
ity (38.9%) and specificity (91.0%) at the 2% objective snoring threshold. The Positive
Predictive Value (PPV) ranged from 35.7% to 66.7%, indicating a moderate chance that
identified snorers truly snored. The Negative Predictive Value (NPV) ranged from 75.6%
to 90.2%, indicating a high chance that identified non­snorers truly did not snore. These
findings suggest that while snoring­related questions in parental questionnaires effectively
rule out non­snorers, they may overestimate the prevalence of snoring in children. This
study provides valuable insights into which questions predict snoring most accurately,
emphasizing the need for a specific questionnaire about snoring to improve the screening
and diagnosis. Combining subjective reports with objective measurements is essential
for accurate diagnosis and management of snoring in children.
Keywords: sleep­disordered breathing (SDB), snoring, obstructive sleep apnea (OSA),
children, polysomnography (PSG), parental questionnaires, sensitivity, specificity, positive
predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV)

Introduction
Sleep is crucial for both physical and mental development in children. Insufficient sleep
or poor sleep quality can significantly impact long­term health outcomes and behaviors
extending into adulthood. These sleep problems have been linked to various adverse
health outcomes, including poor cognitive and cardio­metabolic health, obesity, diabetes,
and depression [1]. Notably, children’s sleep­disordered breathing (SDB), ranging from
habitual snoring to obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), are becoming more recognized as sig­
nificant contributors to various health problems [2].
However, diagnosing SDB and understanding the relationship between subjective parental
reports and objective measures of snoring in children remain challenging, with most re­
search relying solely on parental reports [3]. Undiagnosed sleep problems can lead to
adverse effects on behaviour, cognitive abilities, and quality of life [4].
Early detection and management of SDB are essential to prevent long­term consequences
and improve children’s overall health, academic performance, and well­being [4, 5, 6, 7].
The primary aim of this study was to bridge the gap in the existing literature by evaluating
the predictive validity of specific snoring­related questions from parental questionnaires
compared to objective polysomnography (PSG) measurements in assessing snoring in
children. The analyses delved deeper into specific questions about snoring to enhance
diagnostic approaches and deepen insights into childhood snoring, particularly regard­
ing the use of subjective measures. Sensitivity and specificity were obtained to evaluate
the performance and reliability of the subjective measures. Additionally, positive predic­
tive value (PPV) and negative predictive value (NPV) were calculated to understand the
likelihood that children identified as snorers or non­snorers by the questionnaires were
accurately classified. Furthermore, the correlation between a few objective parameters
and snoring­related questions from the questionnaires was analyzed to understand their
relationship.
Both objective (PSG) and subjective (parental questionnaires) measurements were ob­
tained from a previous study conducted in the research Sleep Revolution at Reykjavík
University.

1.1
Research Question
The primary focus of this study was to evaluate the predictive validity of specific snoring­
related questions from parental questionnaires in relation to objective PSG data. The
research question for this study was:
How well do specific snoring­related questions from parental questionnaires predict the
presence of snoring in children compared to the objective measurements provided by
PSG?
This question encapsulated the main goal of the study. By examining the correlation
between subjective parental responses to snoring­related questions and objective PSG
data, this study aimed to clarify the effectiveness of these specific snoring­related ques­
tions in identifying the presence of snoring in children. Specifically, the aim was to assess
the sensitivity, specificity, PPV and NPV of the snoring­related questions in predicting
objective PSG measurements.
1.2
Hypotheses
The hypotheses proposed for this study were as follows:
Sensitivity, Specificity, Positive Predictive Value (PPV), and Negative Predictive
Value (NPV) Hypothesis: The snoring­related questions in the parental questionnaires
would demonstrate adequate sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and NPV in predicting objective
PSG measurements.
This hypothesis was based on the expectation that the questions could accurately distin­
guish between children who snore and those who do not, as verified by PSG data, and
that they would reliably predict both the presence and absence of snoring.
Correlation Hypothesis: The presence and severity of snoring reported subjectively by
parents would show a statistically significant positive correlation with the objective PSG
measurement of snoring in children.
This hypothesis assumed that parental reports of snoring would align with the objective
measurements, reflecting a reliable relationship between subjective and objective assess­
ments of snoring.
These hypotheses were based on the common use of parental questionnaires in clinical
and research settings to assess children’s sleep patterns, including snoring. Given the
widespread use of these questionnaires, it was expected that the objective PSG data and

Background
This chapter outlines the key concepts of sleep, the clinical aspects of Sleep­Disordered
Breathing (SDB), and snoring, providing a basis for understanding their relevance to chil­
dren’s health.
2.1
Sleep
Sleep is essential for both mental and physical well­being, particularly in children. Lack
of sleep has been linked to poor cognitive and cardio­metabolic health outcomes, mak­
ing children’s sleep a significant area of interest. Many childhood lifestyle behaviors and
disorders carry over into adulthood, highlighting the importance of addressing sleep prob­
lems early [1]. One of the main health challenges today is short sleep duration and sleep
disorders [8]. Sleep disorders in children are prevalent, affecting 20–30% of health con­
ditions reported by pediatric healthcare providers [9, 10]. However, this statistic might
underestimate the true prevalence, as sleep disorders in children are frequently undiag­
nosed and untreated [11].
There are two primary sleep stages: non­rapid eye movement (NREM) and rapid eye
movement (REM) sleep. Early in the night, sleep transitions from lighter stages (NREM
stage 1 [N1]) to deeper stages (slow­wave sleep [SWS]), with REM sleep occurring more
frequently in the later part of the night. In children, studies have shown that as they age,
their sleep efficiency, percentage of SWS, and REM sleep decrease, while the percent­
age of lighter NREM stage 2 (N2) sleep increases [12].
Research by Zhu et al. (2014) found that children with primary snoring did not exhibit sig­
nificant adverse influences on normal sleep architecture in prepubertal school­aged chil­
dren. However, pubertal adolescents with primary snoring showed increased N1 sleep
and wake after sleep onset (WASO), indicating increased sleep fragmentation compared
to non­snorers [12].
Numerous studies have connected sleep problems with negative health outcomes such
as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)­like symptoms, poor academic perfor­
mance, emotional dysregulation, and reduced cognitive performance [4, 5, 6, 7]. The
consequences of sleep deprivation are more severe for children compared to adults due
to its impact on both physical and mental development, which can have lifelong implica­

tions [13]. The central nervous system is most commonly affected, though other systems
can also be impacted [13]. Given the crucial role of sleep in children’s overall health and

Habitual snoring in children has been shown to have significant negative impacts, ad­
versely affecting both cognitive development and behavior problems [14, 15].
2.2
Sleep disordered breathing (SDB)
One of the most common sleep problems in children is SDB, which is only one of sev­
eral factors that can affect children‘s sleep quality [16]. SDB is characterised by recurrent
episodes of complete or partial upper airway obstruction that occur during sleep. It is
a broad term used to describe a spectrum of breathing problems during sleep, ranging
from primary snoring to the most severe type, obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). Respiratory
symptoms that interfere with sleep architecture, such as gasping, snoring, and breathing
pauses, are characteristics of SDB [17].
Snoring is essentially the primary symptom of SDB and indicates the presence of in­
creased upper airway resistance [18]. Children with SDB experience symptoms such as
sleepiness, loud snoring during night, gasping or choking, arousals, restless legs, and in­
creased breathing effort [19]. Another common symptom is experiencing nocturnal sweat­
ing and mouth breathing [20]. Sleep disorders such as SDB might contribute to symptoms
of ADHD and can influence daytime neurobehavioral functioning [21, 22].
2.2.1
Prevalence of SDB
The accurate prevalence of SDB in children varies widely between studies, ranging from
1.2 to 33% [3, 9, 23, 24, 25]. Most studies reported that the prevalence of OSA in chil­
dren ranges from 1­5% [3, 26] and prevalence of habitual snoring in children ranges from
1.5% to 34.5%, indicating considerable variation between different studies [3, 26, 27, 28].
The wide range of SDB in children may be attributed to the broad spectrum of condi­
tions it encompasses, ranging from primary snoring to OSA, and the subjective nature
of symptoms, diagnostic challenges, and age­related differences influencing prevalence
estimates. This could be due to the lack of universally accepted, precise definition of snor­
ing. Hence, the prevalence may vary depending on differing interpretations of the term
across different cultures [3]. Generally, habitual snoring is defined as snoring at least
three nights per week without showing symptoms of apneas or hypopneas. However, the

definition of habitual snoring varies widely between research, ranging from snoring 1­2
times per week to snoring every night [3].
The prevalence of habitual snoring varies depending on age groups. In infancy, the occur­
rence of habitual snoring is relatively low compared to older children and adults. Studies
report a prevalence of between 5.0% and 14% in children under one year of age [29, 30,
31]. At toddlers age, habitual snoring becomes more prevalent, affecting approximately
10–20% of children aged 18–24 months [31, 32]. In school­aged children, the prevalence
ranges from 5–12% [27, 28, 33].
Fiz et al. found that individuals with non­apneic snoring were 3.2 times more likely to de­
velop OSA compared to non­snorers [34]. Additionally, research has shown that snoring
for three months or more is a significant predictor of OSA. Therefore, it is recommended
that children who snore for longer than three months consult a sleep professional and
potentially undergo a PSG [35]. Diagnosing snoring in children as early as possible and
starting treatment is crucial to prevent negative health impacts. This emphasis on timely
diagnosis and intervention is essential given the potential health implications snoring may
have, especially during childhood.
Various measuring methods are used across studies; some utilize objective measures
such as PSG or home sleep apnea testing. Others rely on subjective measures such as
parental­ or self­reports [18, 36, 37]. The significant variability can additionally be due
the diverse clinical cut­off points for SDB in children. For OSA, these cut­off points range
widely between studies from an apnea­hypopnea index (AHI) ≥0.5 to AHI ≥10 [3, 26]. In
the literature, the PSG metric that most frequently describes the severity of SDB is the
AHI [38].
The AHI represents the number of apnea and hypopneas per hour of sleep [39]. Diag­
nosing OSA in children relies on patient history, the presence of an AHI ≥1, and other
respiratory irregularities during PSG, this is in accordance of the International Classifica­
tion of Sleep Disorders third edition [40]. Several studies utilize the AHI to categorize the
severity of OSA into different levels, including mild (AHI 1–4.9), moderate (AHI 5–9.9),
or severe (AHI > 10) [41]. In children without OSA, including those who snore, typically
have an AHI of < 1. Upper airway obstruction, such as snoring, can be overlooked when
using adult criteria or focusing only on the AHI. Therefore it is crucial to find an appropriate
criteria for children [42].

2.2.2
Risk factors
Habitual snoring results from a complex interplay of genetic and environmental factors. In
infants and preschoolers, SDB prevalence is linked to being a boy, lower socioeconomic
status, African American ethnicity, and factors like chronic rhinitis, regurgitation, and to­
bacco smoke exposure [43, 44, 45, 46, 47]. In addition, snoring is linked to asthma in
parents, disturbed sleep, and parent concerns about their child’s breathing. Breastfeed­
ing has been associated with a lower likelihood of habitual snoring in children [48]. A
family history of snoring further heightens the risk [43, 28].
The risk factors for SDB can vary depending on the age of the child. In infancy and early
childhood, craniofacial abnormalities and prematurity may pose risks for primary snor­
ing and OSA. Enlarged adenoids and tonsils are the common cause of SDB in toddlers
and school­age children. However, during adolescence, obesity becomes a major risk
factor. Additionally, upper airway neuromotor tone and reflexes during sleep vary with
age and are increased in normal infants and children compared to adults, perhaps as a
compensatory response for their relatively narrow airway [49].
2.2.3
Health Implications of SDB
The correlation between SDB and various health implications like cognitive impairment,
growth problems, obesity, and cardiovascular diseases is well­established among clin­
icians and researchers. Timely therapy can potentially prevent many of these negative
consequences, highlighting the importance of early recognition and treatment [50, 51, 38].
SDB is linked to childhood obesity, with hormonal changes during sleep potentially con­
tributing to increased hunger and energy intake, further exacerbating the risk of obesity
[51]. Additionally, SDB is associated with various heart­related issues, including high
blood pressure, coronary artery disease, and pulmonary hypertension [52], especially in
children with complex disorders like Down syndrome or neuromuscular disease [38]. Ha­
bitual snoring alone has been linked to hypertension in childhood, with blood pressure
levels increasing across the severity range of SDB [53, 54].
Although snoring is often viewed as a less severe form of SDB, it should not be dis­
missed as harmless.
Research indicates that habitual snoring can negatively impact
child’s health, including elevated blood pressure, behavioral issues, and neurocognitive
disorders [27, 54, 55]. However, these studies on the impacts of snoring are primarily
based on heterogeneous parental questionnaires—not objective measurements [56].
Children who habitually snore have been reported to exhibit lower intelligence quotient

school performance tends to be poorer [27, 57]. Long­term studies have shown that chil­
dren with a history of snoring during early childhood or those who required adenotonsillec­
tomy for snoring are more likely to have lower academic performance [59]. Furthermore,
habitually snoring children often display more hyperactive, inattentive, and externalizing

health implications.
To identify which children have benign snoring and which need treatment, the necessary
clinical cut­off values for objective snore percentage, snore loudness and frequency, and
AHI are required. Children who snore are more likely to experience fragmented sleep,
which may have an impact on their cognitive development [60, 58]. The effects of OSA­
caused sleep fragmentation and habitual snoring can cause symptoms that mimic ADHD
symptoms [61, 62].
Determining if children are generally getting good sleep quality is really important. Few
studies have been done on children who have mild to moderate SDB; the majority of in­
vestigations are on children with moderate to severe SDB. Research indicates that most
children with mild OSA or habitual snoring don’t get treatment, yet these children still have
the same cognitive and behavioral impairments as children with more severe symptoms
[63]. According to a recent meta­analysis by Menzies et al. (2022), children with primary
snoring may occasionally exhibit more cognitive impairments than children with OSA [64].
Therefore, it is crucial to diagnose and get treatment for children across the entire spec­
trum of SDB severity.

Snoring
2.3.1
Definition of snoring
Snoring is characterized by loud upper­airway breathing sounds during sleep. This flut­
tering noise results from high­frequency vibrations of the soft palate, pharyngeal walls,
tongue, and epiglottis, indicating partial upper airway obstruction. If there is no indication
of apnea, hypopnea, improper gas exchange, or frequent awakenings from sleep, the
snoring is considered primary [18]. Snoring is a respiratory noise occurring during both

snoring is quite common, and nearly all children snore from time to time, particularly during
respiratory infections. However, habitual snoring in children, defined as snoring occurring
three times a week or more, may indicate a more serious underlying condition [38]. There
is still no standard cut­off definition for how much a child needs to snore to be considered
a snorer.

2.4
Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)
OSA is described as a pause in airflow lasting more than 10 seconds, typically requir­
ing an arousal to resume normal breathing [66]. Arousals are distinct brain wave events
detected across different frequency bands recorded by an electroencephalogram (EEG)
[67]. OSA events are further categorized into obstructive apnea, hypopnea, and central
apnea, with all events typically associated with a decrease in blood oxygen saturation or
an arousal [68].
The most common symptom of OSA is snoring, with 90­95% of patients exhibiting snoring
[69]. Loud snoring is also the primary indicator of OSA, and can frequently pose chal­
lenges for parents, leading to disruptions in their own sleep patterns and overall sleep
quality [70]. Children who initially solely experience primary snoring may be at risk of de­
veloping OSA in the future, particularly as they age or gain weight [3].

Indoor environment
2.5.1
Impact on sleep quality
Several factors can significantly affect sleep quality. The indoor environment’s cardinal
parameters, including light, noise, temperature, and indoor air quality, are important de­
terminants of overall sleep quality.
Studies show that lighting influences biological rhythms, with excessive nighttime light
hindering sleep initiation and maintenance [72]. Daytime lighting also impacts biological
rhythms [73].
Noise disrupts sleep patterns, with sensitivity varying by sleep stage and individual dif­
ferences. Significant correlations exist between noise disturbances and sleep outcomes,
highlighting the importance of noise level satisfaction for sleep quality [74].
Temperature is another important factor, with even slight variations affecting sleep quality.
The World Health Organization (WHO) advises maintaining bedroom temperatures at a
minimum of 18°C, while CIBSE recommends 17–19°C in winter and 23–25°C in summer
[75].
Indoor air quality, including levels of CO2 and particulate matter (PM2.5), impacts sleep
quality due to inadequate ventilation. Research indicates a link between poor indoor air
quality and sleep disturbances, suggesting the need for improved ventilation in bedrooms
[76].

2.5.2
Impact on snoring
It is important to recognize that these factors do not only influence overall sleep quality
but may also play a significant role in the occurrence and severity of snoring [78, 79].
In a study by Sánchez et al. (2019), the association between air pollution and SDB in
children was examined. The findings revealed that lower temperatures were associated
with snoring in children more than half of the time. However, this relationship remains
controversial, as different studies in adults have reported conflicting results regarding the
impact of temperature on SDB symptoms [80]. Seasonal variations, including relative hu­
midity, have been proposed as potential factors influencing the effects of air pollution and
temperature on SDB. Only humidity emerged as a significant predictor of PSQ scores in
multivariate models, aligning with findings from other studies in adults. Humidity levels
may affect the nasal and pharyngeal mucosa, potentially influencing SDB outcomes. It is
hypothesized that humidity alterations could predispose to inflammation in the upper air­
way mucosa, leading to nocturnal respiratory symptoms such as snoring and wheezing
[80].
Kheirandish­Gozal et al. (2014) investigated the correlation between children’s habitual
snoring and exposure to air pollutants, including PM10, NO2, SO2, CO, and O3 in Iran.
Among the 4322 children aged 6–12 years, those residing in areas with heightened levels
of pollutant exposure rather exhibited habitual snoring. This association persisted even
after adjusting for other contributing factors such as family history of snoring, wheezing,
respiratory issues, and parental smoking habits [81].
Revisiting the study by Liao et al. (2021), which investigated the impact of bedroom ven­
tilation, CO2 levels were compared between open and closed window conditions. With
open windows, CO2 levels averaged approximately 600 ppm, whereas closed windows
resulted in levels around 1600 ppm. The findings revealed that insufficient ventilation,
indicated by higher CO2 levels, was associated with increased instances of snoring and
awakenings during sleep [77].
In the present thesis, the indoor environment was not considered as a factor. However,
it is important to recognize that environmental factors such as ventilation could contribute
to why children snore.

This chapter reviews existing research on diagnosing and treating SDB in children, high­

3.1
Diagnosis of SDB
Diagnosing SDB involves various diagnostic tools. A number of structured questionnaires
combining symptoms and clinical findings have been developed to assess the risk of ha­
bitual snoring and OSA. Examples of these findings include observed apneas, frequent
awakenings, and daytime sleepiness. However, the gold standard for diagnosing primary
snoring and OSA continues to be PSG, both in children and adults [82].
Questionnaires are often utilized as initial screening tools to assess the likelihood of SDB
and to determine whether further diagnostic evaluation, such as PSG, is necessary. While
questionnaires collect symptom information and clinical history, they may not always pro­
vide definitive diagnoses or relate to objective findings on PSG. Therefore, their efficacy in
replacing PSG requires careful evaluation to ensure accurate diagnosis and appropriate
treatment of SDB. Additionally, there are other less frequently utilized methods.
This section provides an overview of the diagnostic tools employed in clinical and research
settings to evaluate SDB in children. Each diagnostic approach offers unique advantages
and challenges. Understanding these diagnostic methods is essential for accurately iden­
tifying and managing SDB in children, ultimately promoting better health outcomes and
quality of life.
3.1.1
Polysomnography (PSG)
PSG is a comprehensive method for collecting physiological data during sleep, utilizing
various measurements to evaluate sleep disturbances. A polysomnogram identifies ob­
structive and mixed apneas, hypopneas, episodes of snoring, and hypoventilation during
sleep. The parameters monitored during PSG typically include brain activity, eye move­
ments, muscle activity, and heart activity recorded via electroencephalography (EEG),
electrooculography (EOG), electromyography (EMG), and electrocardiography (ECG), re­
spectively.
Additionally, blood oxygen saturation is assessed using a pulse oximeter,
snoring is most often detected via a microphone, airflow is monitored through a nasal
cannula, and respiratory movements via respiratory inductance belts on the thorax and

guide the visual assessment of sleep stages and the scoring of respiratory events and

Measuring snoring
Currently, there are no European standards for how to measure snoring. However, the
American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) has indicated three equivalent methods
for snoring measurement [82]. These methods include the use of an acoustic sensor
(such as a microphone), to measure snoring sound; a nasal pressure transducer (com­
monly a cannula), to measure snoring vibration; and a piezoelectric sensor, to measure
vibration. Furthermore, there have been recent efforts to assess snoring through smart­
phones and dedicated applications. According to a study by Arnardottir et al. (2016), the
best standardized measurement is suggested to be audio­based via an acoustic sensor
and should incorporate guidelines specifying the range of decibels (dB) and frequencies
of snore events. These guidelines should also address technical specification concern­

Sleep stage analysis
The AASM has issued a comprehensive manual to guide users in performing PSG studies,
scoring sleep stages and other events, and interpreting PSG results [86]. During PSG
monitoring, EEG and other sensors are utilized to categorize sleep into various distinct
stages. They define five sleep stages: Wake, REM, and NREM sleep with stages 1, 2, and
3 (i.e. N1, N2, and N3). The EEG signals are processed to determine the different sleep
stages using frequency band analysis. The EEG frequencies are typically categorized into
five activities: slow wave activity (0.5 – 2.0 Hz) with a peak­to­peak amplitude greater than
75 µV, Delta (0 – 3.99 Hz), Theta (4 – 7.99 Hz), Alpha (8 – 13 Hz), and Beta (≥13 Hz). The
EOG helps in identifying REM sleep through the detection of rapid eye movements. EMG
signals, particularly from the chin and legs, help in detecting muscle tone and movement,
aiding in the identification of arousals and periodic limb movements. ECG is crucial for
monitoring heart rate variability, which can be indicative of autonomic nervous system
activity during sleep [87]. The EEG features and hypnogram of sleep stages can be seen
in Figure 3.2.
The characteristics of sleep stages are as follows:
• Wake: Dominated by alpha activity in occipital regions (for those with eyes closed),
along with eye blinks and rapid eye movements. Normal or high chin muscle tone
is also observed.
• N1: Low­amplitude mixed­frequency EEG (theta dominant), slow eye movements,
and vertex sharp waves.
• N2: Identified by the presence of K­complexes or sleep spindles. K­complexes are
sharp negative deflections followed by positive components, while sleep spindles
are sinusoidal wave trains in the 11 – 16 Hz range.
• N3: Characterized by slow­wave EEG and delta activity in the frontal regions.
• REM: Noted for rapid eye movements, low chin EMG muscle tone, and low­amplitude
mixed­frequency EEG without K­complexes or sleep spindles. Sawtooth waves,
which are sharp triangular EEG patterns, are also typical.

3.1.2
Questionnaires
In both research and clinical environments, the assessment for SDB often involves relying
on parental reports of their children’s snoring. Consequently, this subjective factor plays
a major role in the physician’s decision to suggest a PSG or use it as the basis for tonsil­
lectomy without PSG. Nevertheless, self­reporting is usually viewed with skepticism. The
accuracy of parental reports regarding snoring in early childhood has not been thoroughly
examined nor taken into consideration for potential confounding factors generated from
the population [18]. Previous studies have demonstrated an association between race
and socioeconomic status with both increased prevalence and severity of SDB, highlight­
ing the importance of considering sociodemographic factors in assessing pediatric SDB
[44]. Pediatric SDB is believed to be not identified and treated adequately [63]. There

reports, and understanding how parental report varies by social determinants can inform
the contributors to under­diagnosis, especially in minority populations [44].
PSG is the gold standard for diagnosing SDB. However, due to the time, cost and ex­
pertise, there is a lack of resources for such studies. The availability of multiple validated
questionnaire measured to screen SDB and associated symptoms has benefited adult re­
search [88]. However, there are fewer published questionnaires that are tailored to eval­
uating SDB in children [88]. It is imperative to ascertain the reliability of parent­reported
questionnaires, which serve as a more accessible diagnostic alternative than PSG and
are an important screening tool [89]. Many questionnaires have been employed in an
effort to detect SDB in children. However, due to the lack of a universally accepted ob­
jective definition of snoring, parental reports of snoring have generally not been validated
against an external gold standard. Limited data exist on the sensitivity and specificity
of reporting snoring or other specific symptoms associated with OSA as diagnosed with
PSG. Nonetheless, Lumeng and Chervin’s study demonstrated that a positive response
to at least one­third of 22 SDB­related questions yielded a sensitivity of 0.85 and speci­
ficity of 0.87 for the report of snoring or other specific symptoms for diagnosing OSA on
PSG [3]. Additionally, their meta­analysis showed that there is significant variability in the
definition of apneas and the diagnosis process for both OSA and snoring. Of 48 studies,
more than 70% diagnosed OSA only with questionnaires [3].
The Montgomery study assessed the predictive validity of parental reports of snoring and
other sleep­related behaviors by comparing these reports with objective findings from
overnight PSG. This study involved two nonclinical samples: at­risk preschoolers and an
older group reflective of the general community. The study included 122 preschoolers
and 172 children aged 5 to 7 years, along with their parents, who participated in both
subjective­report and objective­recording portions of the study [18]. Compared to the
presence of snoring observed on PSG, parental reports of frequent snoring were found
to be highly sensitive and specific for both age groups. Furthermore, predictive ability
improved at all but the lowest level of severity of SDB when a parental­report score based
on multiple measures of child behavior was used, compared to parental reports of snor­
ing alone. However, the profiles of predictive child behaviors differed between the two
age groups, as did their sensitivity and specificity at their high ranges of parental report.
These findings underscore the importance of considering age, risk status, and the pur­

snoring or SDB in children [18].
However, in the case of OSA and snoring, as well as the capacity to evaluate severity and

SDB using subjective measures alone [90]. If treatment is not received, children with SDB
are likely to continue having abnormal breathing for a number of years. A significant pro­
portion of children who habitually snore may have their symptom spontaneously resolve
over time, while a small percentage of children who don’t snore frequently at first might
eventually start snoring regularly. For instance, a study by Ali and colleagues found that
50% of children who snored ’most nights’ at ages 4 to 5 no longer did so by ages 6 to 7.
Conversely, 12 (2.9%) of children who never snored at the first time point were reported

snored habitually in the third grade continued to do so a year later, while Chervin and
colleagues reported that 44.0% of children who snored habitually at baseline continued
to do so four years later, with 4.3% of children who did not snore habitually at baseline
beginning to snore habitually by follow­up [3].

which has a negative impact on specificity and sensitivity [26]. In a study by Goodwin
et al. (2005), which focused on children aged 6 to 11 years, the accuracy of predicting

sensitivity (29.5%) [89].
Seven questionnaires were evaluated in terms of sensitivity, accuracy, and feasibility for

Sleep Questionnaire (PSQ), the Sleep Clinical Record (SCR), the I’m Sleepy Question­

3.1.3
Other diagnostic methods
Given the expense and limited accessibility of PSG, there is a growing need to explore
alternative methods to screen for SDB [88]. Other diagnostic approaches are being ex­
plored. These include ambulatory PSG, home sleep apnea testing, pulse transit time
(PTT), and nocturnal pulse oximetry [94, 95, 96, 97].
Ambulatory PSG, conducted at home, monitors the same variables as full PSG, but of­
fers lower cost and more habitual sleep environment. A study comparing ambulatory PSG
(type II) with traditional PSG revealed comparable diagnostic capabilities, with similar out­
comes in clinical parameters and treatment follow­up. This supports ambulatory PSG as
a promising alternative for improving accessibility to care for individuals with OSA [96].
3.2
Treatment of SDB
It is crucial to provide children with effective treatment options for SDB, including snoring
and OSA, in order to enhance their quality of life and lower any associated health risks.

The treatments can vary from conservative approaches like watchful waiting and active
follow­up to more invasive interventions such as surgery [98].
Tonsillectomy has been the preferred treatment for children with adenotonsillar hypertro­
phy and SDB symptoms. Various surgical techniques are employed for tonsil excision,
with tonsillectomy generally leading to improved PSG results and symptom relief [99].
However, recurrence of symptoms after surgery is common.
Other surgical interventions, such as septal surgery or craniofacial surgery, may be neces­
sary in children with specific anatomical abnormalities contributing to SDB [100]. Nonsur­
gical options include anti­inflammatory medications like intranasal steroids and leukotriene
receptor antagonists, which show promise in reducing symptoms such as snoring. Contin­
uous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy and bilevel PAP therapy delivered through
a mask are effective but may pose challenges in compliance in children [41].
Oral appliances and orthodontic treatments like rapid maxillary expansion are alternative
options to increase the oropharyngeal space. Weight loss interventions may be con­
sidered, especially in obese children with mild lymphadenoid hypertrophy, as obesity can
exacerbate SDB symptoms. However, treatment approaches are often empirically driven,
and further research is needed to develop more validated diagnostic tools and refine treat­
ment strategies for pediatric SDB [100].
3.3
Summary of the Literature Review
This literature review has examined the diagnostic tools and treatment methods for as­
sessing and managing SDB in children. The gold standard for diagnosing these conditions
remains PSG, which provides comprehensive data on objective sleep parameters. How­
ever, PSG is resource­intensive and not always accessible, leading to the widespread use
of parental questionnaires as a more convenient screening tool, despite concerns about
their accuracy.
The review highlighted significant variability in the sensitivity and specificity of parental
reports when compared to objective PSG findings. While parental questionnaires can
be effective for initial screening, they tend to overestimate the prevalence of snoring and
underreport conditions like OSA, especially in diverse populations. This variability un­
derscores the limitations of relying solely on subjective measures for accurate diagnosis.
Despite the widespread use of these questionnaires, there is a notable lack of compre­
hensive studies comparing their effectiveness directly to PSG, especially in the context
of snoring in children. This gap in the literature suggests a need for further research to

better understand their predictive validity.
The current state of knowledge suggests that while there is no perfect diagnostic tool,
a combination of subjective questionnaires and objective PSG measurements offers the
most reliable approach to identifying and managing snoring in children. The findings also
indicate a need for further refinement of diagnostic questionnaires to improve their pre­
dictive validity. Early and accurate diagnosis is crucial, given the potential health risks
associated with untreated SDB in children.
In conclusion, while significant progress has been made in understanding and diagnosing
pediatric snoring, challenges remain in knowing how well specific snoring­related ques­
tions predict snoring in children, which was the primary aim of this thesis.

study in 2019. Data collection took place at the Icelandic Children’s Hospital in Reykjavik
between March and November 2019, with each visit lasting around two hours. Parents
or other guardians answered two online questionnaires, PSQ and OSA­18, shortly before
the visit.
At the onset of the visit, height and weight measurements were taken of the children.

Each participant had their parent(s) with them during the visit. The parent(s) filled out a
written parental consent. Additionally, either parent was asked about previous diagnoses
and medications of the child (not included in this report).
The main component of the data collection involved data from PSG, which is the gold stan­
dard for diagnosing SDB. PSG records multiple physiological parameters during sleep.
4.2.1
PSG Measurement Process
Instrumentation
The PSG device used in this study was the Nox A1, developed by Nox Medical in Reyk­
javik, Iceland. This device is designed for home use, allowing children to sleep in their nat­
ural environment rather than in a sleep laboratory. The Nox A1 is a small and lightweight
PSG device that provides reliable, high­quality signals. It is designed for an easy hookup
experience and ensures patient comfort during sleep studies. The PSG device included
the following sensors:
• EEG (Electroencephalography): To monitor brain activity and sleep stages.
• EOG (Electrooculography): To detect eye movements, crucial for identifying REM
sleep.
• Finger Probe Oximeter: To measure oxygen saturation levels in the blood.
• Microphone for Snore Analysis: To detect and analyze snoring sounds.
• EDA (Electrodermal Activity): To measure skin conductance as an indicator of
autonomic nervous system activity.
• Accelerometry: For position and movement detection during sleep.
• Chin and Leg EMG (Electromyography): To measure muscle activity, particularly
to identify arousals and periodic limb movements.
• ECG (Electrocardiography): To track heart rate and rhythm during sleep.
• Nasal and Mouth Pressure Transducers: To measure airflow and detect obstruc­
tions.
• Thorax and Abdomen Respiratory Inductance Plethysmography (RIP) Belts:
To monitor respiratory effort by measuring chest and abdominal movements.
• Additional Nox A1 Device: To evaluate the validity of a novel frontal EEG setup,
with the mouth pressure transducer attached to the second device due to limited

pressure channels.
Process
At the end of the visit, a sleep technologist attached the PSG device to the children,
which they were to sleep with the following night at home. The home setting was chosen
to increase comfort and replicate typical sleeping conditions. The next day, parents were
asked to complete a brief questionnaire regarding the child’s sleep quality and comfort
level during the night of the sleep study.

Why Only One Night of PSG Data?
The PSG study was conducted over a single night to align with standard practices in pedi­
atric sleep research. Conducting the sleep study in the child’s home over one night allows
the child to remain in a familiar environment. While multiple nights of data might provide
more comprehensive information, research has shown that a single night of PSG is gen­
erally sufficient for accurately diagnosing sleep disorders like OSA in children. However, it
is important to note that using multiple nights of PSG could capture the night­to­night vari­
ability in sleep patterns, potentially offering a more detailed picture of the child’s snoring
patterns. Additionally, conducting multiple nights of PSG can be costly and resource­
intensive. By limiting the study to one night, we balance the need for accurate data col­
lection with practical considerations such as cost, time, and minimizing disruption to the
child’s routine.

PSG Device Challenges
Wearing a PSG device can present challenges, particularly for children who may find it
unfamiliar and uncomfortable. The sensation of being attached to various sensors can be
unsettling, and the child might take some time to adjust to the device. This initial discomfort
may impact the child’s ability to fall asleep and maintain a typical sleep pattern, potentially
leading to a night that does not fully represent their usual sleep quality. However, the Nox
A1 is designed with comfort in mind to minimize disruption.
Manual Scoring of Data
An expert sleep technologists manually scored snore events by listening to all audible
parts of the recording using Noxturnal software version 5.1.3.20388 (Nox Medical, Reyk­
javik, Iceland). The scoring encompassed both inspiratory and expiratory snores while
excluding non­snore sounds such as coughing, groaning, high­pitched squeaks, duvet
noise, and large breathing sounds without vibration. The manual scoring process fol­
lowed the most recent guidelines recommended by the AASM. From the snoring report,
the average volume of the snore events was calculated in dB.
4.3
Measurements
4.3.1
Subjective Measures
The subjective measures in this study consist of two questionnaires answered by the par­
ents: the PSQ and the OSA­18. The results obtained from the snoring­related questions
in these questionnaires were examined and compared to the results of the objective mea­
sures. The questionnaires are presented in the Appendix in Figures A.1 and A.2.
PAEDIATRIC SLEEP QUESTIONNAIRE
The PSQ is used to screen children for SBD. It measures hyperactive behavior, tiredness,
snoring, and inattention. It also establishes whether a child has witnessed apneas or
snoring. In the PSQ the parent(s) were asked to circle the numbers of the questions
which applied to their child. More than eight positive responses may indicate a problem
with SDB.
QUALITY OF LIFE SURVEY (OSA­18)
The OSA­18 is a tool used to assess the health­related quality of life of children with sleep
disorders by focusing on functional restrictions, physical issues, and emotional discomfort
caused by the condition. In the OSA­18, parents were asked how often their child expe­
rienced different symptoms. The child received a score from this questionnaire, where
0­60 indicated a small impact on health­related quality of life, 60­80 indicated a moderate
Snoring in Children: A Comparison of Subjective and Objective Measures

==PAGE:40==
impact, and a score of more than 80 indicated a severe impact. The total score ranges
from 18 to 126, with higher scores indicating poorer conditions.
These are the five snoring­related questions from the questionnaires (A.2) that were an­
alyzed:
• Question 1: Does your child ever snore? (This study)
• Question 2: Does your child snore more than half the time they sleep? (PSQ)
• Question 3: Does your child always snore? (PSQ)
• Question 4: Does your child snore loudly? (PSQ)
• Question 5: How often has your child had loud snoring during the past 4 weeks?
(OSA­18)
Focusing on Snore­Related Questions
The study focused on specific snoring­related questions from the PSQ and OSA­18 ques­
tionnaires to evaluate their predictive validity against objective PSG measurements. While
the OSA­18 and PSQ are developed to detect symptoms and impacts of severe SDB, such
as OSA, they also include relevant questions about snoring. By narrowing the analysis
to these questions, the study aims to provide a clearer assessment of parental reports
on snoring, ensuring that the findings remain focused by excluding unrelated symptoms.
This targeted approach also supports the future development of a snoring­specific ques­
tionnaire.
4.3.2
Objective Measures
The PSG device collected data on several key parameters used in this study:
• AHI: The AHI represents the number of apneas (complete pauses in breathing) and
hypopneas (partial reductions in breathing) per hour of sleep. These events are
typically identified by detecting reductions in airflow and respiratory effort [39].
• Objective Snore Percentage: The percentage of total sleep time during which
snoring is detected, using the microphone.
• Snore Volume: Measured in dB, representing the average volume of snore events
during the night.
• Total Sleep Time (TST): TST is the total amount of time a person spends asleep
during the PSG recording. It is calculated by summing up the duration of all sleep
26
Snoring in Children: A Comparison of Subjective and Objective Measures
==PAGE:41==
stages, which are identified through EEG, EOG, and EMG signals [87].
• Sleep Efficiency (SE): SE is the ratio of total sleep time to the total time spent in
bed, expressed as a percentage.
• Slow­Wave Sleep (SWS): SWS, also known as deep sleep or NREM stage 3 sleep,
is the phase of sleep characterized by slow brain waves (delta waves). It is crucial for
physical restoration and memory consolidation, identified through EEG recordings
[87].
• REM Sleep: REM sleep is the sleep stage characterized by rapid eye movements,
vivid dreams, and increased brain activity. It is essential for emotional regulation
and memory processing. REM sleep is detected through a combination of EEG,
EOG, and EMG signals [87].
• Arousal Index: The arousal index measures the number of arousals (brief awak­
enings) per hour of sleep. A high arousal index indicates frequent sleep disruptions,
which can lead to fragmented sleep and reduced sleep quality. Arousals are iden­
tified through changes in EEG patterns [102].
4.4
Statistical Analysis
Demographic characteristics and PSG parameters were obtained for the entire sample,
which was divided into two groups (two years prior to the study): the reported snoring and
the control group. First, t­test analysis were conducted to compare continuous variables
between the two groups, while Fisher’s exact test was used for categorical variables.
A chi­square analysis was conducted to examine the relationship between reported snor­
ing two years prior, snoring­related questions in the current study and objective snoring.
The chi­square test for independence was employed to determine which question should
be used to define the subjective group in relation to the objective data. With five potential
questions about snoring, the chi­square test compared each question’s association with
the objective data. Additionally, the chi­square test was employed to evaluate which ob­
jective snoring percentage threshold (1%, 2%, or 3%) best corresponds with the snoring­
related questions.
Sensitivity and specificity analysis were performed for different objective snoring thresh­
olds. These analysis were also conducted for non­significant questions to determine if
the chi­square analysis missed any associations. Given the absence of a standardized
Snoring in Children: A Comparison of Subjective and Objective Measures
27
==PAGE:42==
definition for identifying a child as a snorer based on objective snoring, these analysis
were conducted to determine the optimal cutoff percentage (1%, 2%, or 3%) for objective
snoring.
The subjects were also categorized into two groups: one based on objective snoring
percentage (objective snoring group) and another based on the parental report question
considered the best indicator of snoring (subjective snoring group). The aim was to com­
pare these groups to determine the alignment between objective measurements obtained
through PSG and subjective reports provided by parents. This comparison was conducted
using the objective snoring threshold and the parental report question that showed the
best results in predicting snoring. Descriptive statistics, including mean values for de­
mographic characteristics and PSG parameters, were computed for both groups. T­tests
and Fisher’s exact tests were used for comparing continuous and categorical variables,
respectively.
Correlation analysis was employed to assess the predictive value of parental reports on
PSG and to explore the relationship between objective and subjective measures of snor­
ing. The Pearson correlation coefficient was used for Questions 1­4, which have binary
responses, with parameters normalized to allow the use of Pearson correlation. For Ques­
tion 5, which has ordinal responses, the Spearman correlation coefficient was applied, as
Spearman correlation is appropriate for ordinal data. Table 4.1 shows the interpretation
of the absolute value of the correlation coefficient utilized throughout the study.
A significance level of p<0.05 was used to determine statistical significance. Data were
square­root transformed when needed to normalize the distribution for statistical analysis.
The statistical analysis were performed using MATLAB, R, and Excel.

6
Discussion
This chapter examines the results of the thesis, discussing key findings, limitations, strengths,
and directions for future study.
6.1
Summary of results
This study evaluated the predictive validity of specific snoring­related questions from
parental questionnaires in relation to objective PSG data. The findings partially support
the sensitivity and specificity hypothesis and fully support the correlation hypothesis. The
snoring­related questions from parental questionnaires demonstrated high specificity but
moderate to low sensitivity in predicting objective PSG measurements. Significant weak
correlations between subjective parental reports and objective snoring measures indicate
that parental questionnaires can be a useful tool for identifying children who snore, though
they may not capture all cases.
The results identified the objective snoring ≥2% of the night threshold as optimal for defin­
ing objective snorers when compared to the snoring­related questions. Question 4 (”Does
your child snore loudly?”) showed the best balance between sensitivity (38.9%) and speci­
ficity (91.0%) at the 2% objective snoring threshold. Positive Predictive Values (PPV)
ranged from 35.7% to 66.7%, indicating the need for additional objective confirmation for
accurate diagnosis. Negative Predictive Values (NPV) ranged from 75.6% to 90.2%, high­
lighting the effectiveness of subjective questions in ruling out non­snorers. The correlation
analysis revealed several significant weak correlations between the snoring­related ques­
tions and the objective parameters, with Question 4 showing the strongest correlation for
objective snoring (%).
6.2
Interpretation of Results
6.2.1
The sample
Given the limited research in this area, the results of this study provide valuable insights
into the relationship between subjective and objective measurements of snoring in chil­
dren. The lack of significant differences between the reported snoring and control groups
in terms of demographic characteristics suggests that the groups were well­matched. The
trend towards a significant difference in objective snoring percentage is promising, indi­
Snoring in Children: A Comparison of Subjective and Objective Measures
51
==PAGE:66==
cating that the reported snoring group may have a higher percentage of objective snorers
compared to the control group. This aligns with the expectation that parents can correctly
identify, at least partially, snoring in their children, as demonstrated in this research.
6.2.2
Longitudinal Consistency in Parental Reports
The chi­square analyses reveal associations between parental reports from two years
prior and current snoring­related questions, particularly Question 4 (”Snore loudly?”), high­
lighting a consistency in parental observations over time. However, there was no signifi­
cant association between two years prior reported snoring and current objective snoring
measures. This may indicate that snoring patterns can change over time due to various
factors such as growth, medical interventions, or changes in health status.
6.2.3
Optimal Threshold for Objective Snoring
From the chi­square analysis, the association between snoring­related questions and ob­
jective parameters varied, with Question 4 showing the strongest correlation across multi­
ple objective measures. The results suggest that defining subjects as objective snorers at
a threshold of ≥2% of the night is optimal. This threshold also demonstrated good sensi­
tivity and specificity. Given the absence of a gold standard for defining objective snoring,
this provides a practical cut­off percentage for objective snoring.
6.2.4
Sensitivity, Specificity, PPV, and NPV Analysis
The sensitivity and specificity analysis provide critical insights into the effectiveness of
various snoring­related questions in identifying children who objectively snore. Among
the questions analyzed, Question 4 (”Snore loudly?”) was particularly effective at the 2%
objective snoring threshold, balancing sensitivity and specificity effectively. This further
supports the selection of the 2% threshold as optimal. The high specificity indicates that
this question is particularly effective in correctly identifying children who do not snore,
which is important for minimizing false positives and avoiding unnecessary interventions.
This threshold was determined to be the most efficient, providing a sensitivity of 38.9%
and a specificity of 91.0%. Therefore, the 2% threshold (objective snoring ≥2% of the
night) is a robust criteria for clinical screening tools. The findings indicate improved speci­
ficity compared to sensitivity for subjective questionnaires in predicting objective snoring,
suggesting that relying on questions alone will underestimate the prevalence of objective
snoring.
Comparing these findings to previous research, Montgomery­Downs et al. reported a sen­
sitivity of 64% and a specificity of 57% in preschoolers, and a sensitivity and specificity of
52
Snoring in Children: A Comparison of Subjective and Objective Measures
==PAGE:67==
73% in first­graders. This study shows higher specificity but lower sensitivity, suggesting
that Question 4 (”Snore loudly?”) is better at identifying non­snorers, it is less effective at
identifying all snorers. This discrepancy may be due to differences in the study popula­
tions and methodologies [18].
Similarly, a study by Phoebe et al. found higher specificity at 82% but lower sensitivity
at 30%. Their study included more non­white children from urban neighborhoods and
children up to 12 years of age. While Montgomery­Downs et al. assessed snoring in an
in­laboratory PSG, both this study and the Phoebe et al. study used quantitative mea­
sures of snoring sounds recorded in the child’s home environment [44].
The PPV values across different thresholds and questions ranged from 35.7% to 66.7%.
This variation highlights that while subjective reports are useful, they are not entirely re­
liable for identifying true snorers. This moderate PPV suggests that additional objective
confirmation is necessary to accurately diagnose snoring in children reported to snore.
The highest PPV was observed for the question ”Does your child snore more than half
the time?” at the 2% and 3% thresholds, suggesting that this question might be partic­
ularly useful in identifying true snorers. The NPV values ranged from 75.6% to 90.2%,
suggesting that the subjective questions are quite effective in ruling out non­snorers. This
high NPV is clinically significant as it helps in identifying children who do not need further
diagnostic evaluation, thereby reducing unnecessary testing. The highest NPV was ob­
served for the question ”Does your child snore loudly?” at the 3% threshold, emphasizing
its effectiveness in ruling out children who do not snore.
6.2.5
Comparison of Objective and Subjective Snoring Groups
The comparison between the objective snoring group (snoring ≥2% of the time) and the
subjective snoring group (children reported to snore loudly) revealed several significant
differences in average snoring volume, the proportion of children with objective snoring ≥
2%, and REM sleep percentage. The discrepancy in average snoring volume may be at­
tributed to the inclusion of non­snorers in the subjective group, as parents might perceive
occasional loud breathing or other nighttime noises as snoring.
7 out of 14 children (the PPV) classified as subjective snorers also snored objectively, sug­
gesting that while parental reports can be indicative, they are not entirely reliable. Ideally,
all the subjective snorers being snorers would indicate accurate parental predictions.
The significantly higher REM sleep percentage in the subjective snoring group is an in­
teresting finding. This could indicate differences in sleep architecture between children

identified as snorers by their parents and those confirmed by objective measures. This
aligns with existing knowledge that SDB is one of several factors affecting children’s sleep
quality [16]. However, further research is needed to explore the underlying reasons for this
difference and its implications for understanding the impact of snoring on sleep quality.
6.2.6
Correlation Analysis
The correlation analysis revealed several significant weak correlation between the snoring­
related questions and the objective parameters. Among these, Question 4 (”Snore loudly?”)
demonstrated the strongest correlation for objective snoring (%). This question showed
significant correlations with all three objective measures: AHI, Objective snore (%), and
Average Snoring Volume (dB). All the questions except question 1 showed some weak
correlation with the objective snoring (%) parameter.
These findings suggest that parental reports of loud snoring are aligned with objective
measures of snoring severity, supporting the use of loud snoring as a key question in
screening tools for identifying children at risk for SDB. This is consistent with existing stud­
ies, that have reported weak correlations between PSG and questionnaires in the context
of OSA and snoring, underscoring the difficulty in evaluating severity and distinguishing
between the two conditions based solely on subjective reports [90].
6.2.7
Handling ”Don’t Know” Responses
In this study, ”don’t know” responses were excluded from the calculations of sensitivity,
specificity, and correlation analyses. This decision was made to ensure that the analy­
sis focused on definitive responses, either responding ”yes” or ”no” to the presence of
snoring. However, it is important to consider the potential implications of excluding these
responses. See Table A.1 in the Appendix for the number of participants who selected
”don’t know” for various questions. These non­responses could introduce bias, as the
reasons for selecting ”don’t know” might be systematic rather than random.
6.3
Strengths
A key strength of this study is the sample selection, drawn from a community­based cohort
rather than solely from clinically referred children. This approach provides a representa­
tive sample of the general population, offering valuable insights into the sleep patterns
and potential prevalence of SDB among children who may not typically receive medical
evaluation. By examining a broader cohort, this study contributes valuable information
on whether children in the general population are obtaining sufficient quality sleep and if
54

==PAGE:69==
they need treatment for SDB. Additionally, the dataset is extensive, providing a rich source
of information on various aspects of the children’s health and well­being. Moreover, the
objective nature of the PSG, complemented by manually reviewed audio recordings for
snore analysis, significantly enhances the reliability and accuracy of the findings.
6.4
Limitations
This study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results.
Firstly, the PSG measurements were only conducted for one night, which may capture an
atypical sleep pattern for the child. Ideally, the PSG measurements should have spanned
multiple nights to account for night­to­night variability in snoring and OSA events.
Secondly, the reported snoring group and the control group were invited to the study based
on a question about snoring and apneas asked two years prior to the objective measure­
ments. However, this same question was not asked again in the current study; instead,
other snore­related questions were used, which showed some associations. This ap­
proach raises the possibility of misclassification, as children’s snoring patterns may have
changed over the two years, potentially affecting the consistency and comparability of the
data over time.
Thirdly, when dividing the sample into groups, the groups tended to be rather small. The
children in the study were generally healthy, and not many of them were objective snor­
ers, resulting in a small sample size for both the objective and subjective snoring groups.
This limited sample size may affect the accuracy of the findings.
Additionally, environmental factors influencing snoring in children were not considered in
this research. Future studies should include these factors to provide a more comprehen­
sive understanding of snoring in children.
Finally, the exclusion of ”don’t know” responses from the calculations of sensitivity, speci­
ficity, PPV, NPV and correlation analyses is another limitation. Excluding these responses
might underestimate or overestimate the true sensitivity and specificity of caregiver re­
ports. The potential implication of not having this information is that it could introduce
bias into the results, as the reasons for selecting ”don’t know” might be systematic rather
than random. For instance, parents who are unsure about their child’s snoring behavior
might have specific characteristics or face particular challenges that are not captured in
the analysis. The extent of this problem depends on the proportion of ”don’t know” re­
sponses; if a significant number of participants chose this option, it could substantially
impact the accuracy of the study’s findings.

6.5
Implications of the study
The findings of this study have several practical implications for children, parents, medical
professionals, and researchers. The identified 2% threshold, snoring ≥2% of the night,
along with the predictive capability of Question 4 (”Does your child snore loudly?”), pro­
vides healthcare providers with a practical criterion for timely and accurate diagnosis. This
threshold was found to be optimal in this study, supporting its use in clinical settings. The
study’s results showed that at the 2% threshold, Question 4 had a sensitivity of 38.9%
and a specificity of 91.0%, indicating its utility in clinical screening tools.
The prevalence of snoring, found to be 21.5% in this study, and potential OSA in chil­
dren underscores the need for raising awareness about sleep health in children. This
highlights the importance of educating parents about the impacts of SDB on children’s
health. Increased awareness can lead to better parental recognition and reporting of
snoring symptoms, facilitating earlier intervention.
The results also suggest the importance of developing standardized screening protocols
for snoring in children within primary care settings. Developing guidelines based on these
findings can ensure early identification and necessary diagnostic assessments for at­risk
children. These protocols would help streamline the diagnostic process and potentially
improve the early detection of SDB in children.
Moreover, these findings can inform future research aimed at refining diagnostic methods
and enhancing the overall management of snoring in children. The study’s results, par­
ticularly the correlations found between subjective reports and objective measures, can
guide the development of more effective screening questionnaires for snoring.
6.6
Future study
Future research should aim to improve the sensitivity of subjective questions without com­
promising specificity. This could involve refining the questions further, incorporating ad­
ditional questions that capture different aspects of snoring.
Research should involve PSG measurements over multiple nights to capture night­to­
night variability in snoring and OSA events. This approach would provide a more compre­
hensive understanding of sleep patterns and help validate the findings from single­night
studies.
Larger sample sizes for both objective and subjective snorers are needed to validate these

Conclusion
This chapter encapsulates the key findings of the study, providing a concise summary of
the research outcomes.
This study aimed to evaluate how well specific snoring­related questions from parental
questionnaires predict the presence of snoring in children, as compared to objective mea­
surements obtained through PSG.
The key finding of the study was that the snoring­related questions effectively identify
non­snorers, due to their high specificity (the ability to correctly identify those who do not
snore) and high NPV (the likelihood that a child identified as a non­snorer truly does not
snore). However, these questions were less effective at identifying snorers, as indicated
by their low sensitivity (the ability of the questions to correctly identify those who snore)
and moderate PPV values, which reflects the likelihood that children identified as snor­
ers by the questions actually do snore. This suggests that while parental questionnaires
are useful for ruling out non­snorers, they may underestimate the prevalence of objective
snoring.
The results also revealed a weak but significant correlation between some of the snoring­
related questions—particularly Question 4 (”Snore loudly?”)—and objective measures
such as AHI, objective snore percentage, and average snoring volume.
Regarding the gold standard for snoring measurement, this study reaffirms that PSG re­
mains the most reliable and comprehensive tool for objectively diagnosing snoring in chil­
dren. While parental questionnaires offer a convenient and accessible screening method,
they should not be solely relied upon for accurate diagnosis. Instead, they are more ef­
fective when used in conjunction with PSG to provide a fuller picture.
The hypothesis that parental reports would demonstrate adequate sensitivity, specificity,
PPV and NPV were partially supported. While specificity and PPV were high, sensitivity
was notably low, and NPV was moderate. The hypothesis that there would be a sta­
tistically significant positive correlation between parental reports of snoring severity and
objective snoring measurements was supported, although the correlation was weak. This
finding highlights the limited, but present, alignment between subjective and objective as­
sessments of snoring.
Strengths of this study include its community­based sample, which provides a more repre­

sentative view of children’s sleep patterns beyond a clinical population, and the extensive
dataset used, which enhances the reliability of the findings. However, the study also has
limitations, such as the use of a single­night PSG, small sample sizes for certain groups,

The findings have practical implications, particularly in the development of standardized
screening protocols for snoring in children within primary care settings. They also high­
light the importance of raising awareness about the impacts of SDB on children’s health.
Future research should focus on improving the sensitivity of subjective questions, consid­
ering environmental factors, and employing multi­night PSG measurements to validate
these findings further.
In conclusion, while parental questionnaires are useful for excluding non­snorers, they are
less effective at identifying true snorers. Future research and improved screening tools
are needed to enhance the accuracy of snoring diagnosis in children.
