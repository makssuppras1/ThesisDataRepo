ABSTRACT
Abstract
Sound textures, such as “rain” and “ﬁre”, are comprised of many similar superimposed acoustic events.
Imposing time-averaged statistics of real textures, measured through a model of the auditory sys-
tem, onto a noise seed provides a useful means of probing the auditory system’s representation of
such signals in a systematic way. Normal-hearing (NH) listeners’ ability to identify sound textures
increases as the number of statistic classes used in the synthesis stage increases. Discrimination tasks
have also shown that normal-hearing listeners tend to use the ﬁne temporal detail where possible,
but will instead switch to a time-averaged statistical representation when discriminating textures of
longer duration. This study extends the existing research to investigate how hearing-impaired (HI)
listeners with sensorineural hearing loss perform in the same sound texture identiﬁcation and dis-
crimination tasks with stimuli ampliﬁed to compensate for their loss of audbility. In the ﬁrst study,
identiﬁcation scores of HI listeners increased as the number of statistic classes used in the synthesis
stage increased as with NH listeners, but a signiﬁcant reduction in overall identiﬁcation accuracy
was observed. In the second study, texture discrimination performance was measured across four lis-
tener groups categorised by age (young/old) and hearing loss (NH/HI). Discrimination performance
reduced modestly with hearing loss in a duration-independent manner, while a signiﬁcant duration-
dependent eﬀect of age was found. Compared to young listeners, older listeners showed reduced
performance speciﬁcally for short-duration sounds, regardless of their hearing status. A model was
proposed which predicts texture discrimination performance across all listener groups through clas-
siﬁcation of duration-dependent and independent temporal deﬁcits. The model may provide viable
diagnostic tools for age- and hearing-related impairments. In the third study, variability of statistics
was measured through systematically impaired models of the auditory periphery to gather insight
into the internal representation of textures in impaired listeners.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background
To say that the study of the auditory system is a challenge is an understatement. Given the vast
array of transformations that take place - from acoustic vibrations in the outer ear, to mechanical
movement in the middle ear, ﬂuid compression in the cochlea, and ﬁnally to synaptic action potentials
in the auditory nerve - modelling already the early stages of the auditory system becomes extremely
complex. While the peripheral (outer-middle-inner ear) and, partially, brainstem and central stages
(retro-cochlear) have been studied and modelled, the later stages remain to be so in such detail.
Physiological methods such as Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR) provide an objective means of
measuring how speciﬁc stages of the auditory system respond to acoustic stimuli. However, given
the noisy nature of signals in the brain, objective measurements of the brain’s response to acoustic
stimuli alone becomes a great challenge both practically and technically. Thus, it is often necessary
to rely on perceptual tasks: a ﬁeld commonly referred to as psychoacoustics.
Psychoacoustics has revealed much about the early stages of the auditory system, typically making
use of some combination of pure tones and narrow/broadband noise to systematically analyse the
system’s response as a function of (e.g.) frequency. This has proved invaluable to conﬁdently propose
models such as the cochlear [1] and modulation [2] ﬁlter banks, which have also been validated
physiologically (e.g. tuning curves). These models work by “splitting” the acoustic stimulus into
smaller and smaller “pieces”, in terms of acoustic and modulation frequencies, and time. Thus, the
system can be considered to be composed of many branches, like the descent through a family tree.
Each time we move through a layer, a greater number of possible interactions between each branch
arises; interactions which occur in parallel and yield slower responses proportional to the depth of
the layer.
Study of the later stages of the auditory system using stimuli such as pure tones fail to expose these
complex interactions. Instead, it has been proposed that sound textures oﬀer a more useful set of
statistical characteristics, with which we can explore these later stages. In addition, it also assumed
that the auditory system has evolved to process natural stimuli (of which textures are a subset)
eﬃciently, and thus deemed ecologically valid stimuli with which to probe the system.
A texture is a temporally homogeneous signal. Textures as sensory stimuli were ﬁrst introduced to
the study of visual perception in 1962, by Julesz [3]. It was proposed that textures are represented
within the system by a set of statistics whose properties are shaped by the earlier stages of the
sensory function in question - for example, Portilla and Simoncelli developed a model based on
statistical representations of visual textures [4]. The idea is that textures are represented by their
statistics, such as marginal moments of, and correlations between, the early processing stages and
oﬀer an eﬃcient encoding of the stimulus. As a result, probing sensory systems with texture stimuli
can help reveal more about the individual processing stages that make up the sensory system in
question.

INTRODUCTION
1.2
Project Aims
Much of the work in sound texture perception has been conducted on how Normal-Hearing (NH)
listeners and healthy auditory models respond to sound textures [5, 6, 7, 8]. However, little research
exists on how an impaired system responds. The aims of this project are to investigate the eﬀect of
Hearing Loss (HL) on sound texture perception.
Hearing Impaired (HI) listeners typically report a host of diﬃculties in tasks such as speech recog-
nition [9, 10], sound source separation [11, 12], and pitch perception [13, 14]: all of which contribute
to the more general phenomenon known as the “cocktail party” problem [15]. Hearing loss is typ-
ically characterised by reduced cochlear compression [16], broader acoustic ﬁlters [17, 18] and/or
loss of temporal acuity [19, 18], based on both data collected via psychophysical experiments and
physiological observations. This understanding of the early stages of auditory processing has led to
the development of useful models [1, 20, 21] with which the auditory system can be further explored
and novel compensation strategies (e.g. [22]) can be developed. Furthermore, typical stimuli used to
probe the system and models include pure tones and noise (broadband or band-passed) as they oﬀer
systematic means of evaluating the system’s response to a change at the input [23]. Sound textures
also oﬀer a systematic and ecologically valid means of probing the system. Given that a texture’s
statistics are shaped by the early stages of auditory processing, and that changes in the statistical
representation induces perceptual changes, then impairments to those stages are assumed to alter
the statistical representation and thus also their perceived quality. It is therefore interesting to ask
the question: how do HI listeners respond to sound textures?
Answers to this question are hoped to oﬀer further insights into how an impaired system is really
impaired, and lead to more accurate models of impaired auditory systems, with which improved
compensation strategies can be developed.
This study has been conducted through three avenues of research:
1. Experiment 1: Sound texture identiﬁcation in hearing impaired listeners (section 3).
2. Experiment 2: Sound texture discrimination in hearing impaired listeners (section 4).
3. Experiment 3: Analysis of statistic variability in models of hearing impairment (section 5).
Texture identiﬁcation performance was measured on impaired listeners in order to provide evidence
that statistical representations of sound textures within an impaired system are perceptually relevant.
Speciﬁcally, this study expands on the existing research that studied only healthy listeners, and aims
to provide evidence that synthetic textures oﬀer a valid means of probing impaired auditory systems.
Discrimination tasks were conducted to provide evidence as to whether Hearing Impaired (HI) listen-
ers make use of time-averaged statistics in perception and draw empirical comparisons with previous
studies which measured discrimination performance of young, healthy listeners. It is possible to col-
lect this evidence by varying the excerpt duration of synthetic textures from short to long, which
in turn varies the statistical variability of the excerpt from high to low. This duration-dependent
property was then used to explore HI listeners’ ability to utilise time-averaged statistics through

Sound Textures in Hearing Research
2.1
Textures in sound
In regard to sound, textures are commonly found in nature, whereby many similar acoustic events
occur sequentially - examples include rain, wind and ﬁre.
Textures lack the complex temporal
ﬂuctuations, as found in speech or music, and have been used to investigate mid-level stages of the
auditory system due to their simplicity, and ecological validity.
Furthermore, it has been shown that textures may be synthesised by:
1. Measuring time-averaged short-term statistics of real-world texture recordings at the output
of a model representing the early stages of auditory perception.
2. Imposing those time-averaged statistics back on to a “seed” (in the case of sound textures, this
is typically white Gaussian noise).
If the hypothesis that texture perception relies on time-averaged statistics is true, then manipulating
a noise signal to match the long-term statistics of a real-world texture should yield a perceptually
faithful synthetic version. McDermott and Simoncelli [5] investigated just this in 2011, relying on
three key models representing the early stages of auditory perception, ﬁg. 2.1.

The three layers of the model represent the key stages of early auditory processing: (i) frequency
selectivity of the cochlea, (ii) half-wave rectiﬁcation and compressive non-linearities of the hair cells
and (iii) a modulation ﬁlter bank. For each layer, two statistical measures were measured: marginal
moments and pairwise correlations. The former corresponds to basic statistical measures of a single
acoustic/modulation channel: mean, variance, skew and kurtosis, while the latter describes correla-
tions across acoustic/modulation channels. It was found that synthetic textures, whose long-term
statistics measured across these stages matched those of a real-world texture, could be accurately

identiﬁed, indicating that the peripheral auditory stages play a key role in shaping the perception
of signals such as textures.
A further study by McDermott et al. in 2013 [6] investigated whether the auditory system makes
use of the time-averaged statistics in texture perception. Here it was found that discrimination
performance between two diﬀerent textures improved with excerpt duration, while discrimination
between two diﬀerent excerpts of the same texture worsened, indicating that time-averaged statistics
are indeed utilised by the auditory system when an eﬃcient statistical representation can be formed.
In addition, a mode of auditory representation was proposed which aimed to explain the auditory
system’s apparent transition from use of short-term cues to long-term cues. Short textures, whose
inherent variability is high oﬀer little in the way of time-averaged cues: instead the auditory system
makes use of the ﬁne structure of the signal through feature encoding, which operates on a short-term
basis. As the texture duration increases, the statistical representation converges and the long-term
cues dominate instead.
2.2
Statistics for representing sound textures
The auditory system can be modelled as a series of cascaded modules, each of which decomposes the
input acoustic stimulus into more discrete representations. Naturally, the further along the system
we move, the greater the number of decompositions, leading to a high-dimensional space within
which any number of statistical measures may be drawn. The following section details the statistics
which are of particular salience for sound texture perception.
The Marginal moments represent statistics within individual envelopes, as investigated by [24, 25].
Figure 2.2 illustrates how certain textures possess diﬀerent marginalmoments.
Figure 2.2 indicates that, while the means of all cochlear envelopes have a similar amplitude, their
variance and symmetry is quite diﬀerent.
It is precisely these diﬀerences that are captured by
marginal moments, for both acoustic and modulation channels. Perceptually, low variance within
the acoustic channels may manifest as sparsity: infrequent but powerful bursts of sounds for certain
acoustic frequencies. For the modulation channels, the variance is of particular importance, and
describes how often the signal ﬂuctuates at each modulation frequency, serving as a cue for detecting
sounds which modulate rapidly or slowly. In addition, acoustic frequencies can ﬂuctuate at diﬀerent
rates: consider, for example, the contrast between the hum of crickets on a summer’s evening with
the ocean’s tide. These properties are captured by correlations between acoustic and modulation
channels, ﬁg. 2.3.
Figure 2.3 shows how modulation power is distributed across acoustic frequencies for three diﬀer-
ent sound textures. Modulation power and bandwidth measured for insects increase with acoustic
frequency, while waves possess powerful modulations with a ﬁxed bandwidth across all acoustic fre-
quencies. A stream, on the other hand, has moderate modulation power distributed evenly across all
acoustic and modulation frequencies. It is already apparent from just three examples that the statis-
tics formed across the early auditory stages result in highly unique statistical proﬁles, suggesting

2.3
Sound texture identiﬁcation in normal-hearing listeners
Sound texture identiﬁcation tasks aim to provide evidence that statistics imposed on a noise seed
are perceptually relevant. The hypothesis is that, as the statistics of the synthetic texture closer
match those of the real-world equivalent, identiﬁcation performance should improve. McDermott
and Simoncelli [5] investigated sound texture identiﬁcation in ten young (average age = 22.2 years)
self-reported NH listeners.

real-world texture as measured through a model of the subcortical auditory system. The number of
statistics imposed in the synthesis stage ranged from power only up to all marginals and correlations,

2.4
Sound texture discrimination in normal-hearing listeners
Increasing the number of statistics used in the synthesis stage improves the realism with respect
to the real-world recording. As a result, synthetic textures synthesised using a higher number of
statistics are more easily identiﬁed than those synthesised using fewer statistics. While this provides
evidence that textures may be realistically synthesised using time-averaged statistics, it does not
provide empirical evidence that listeners really do make use of those statistics when processing
textures.
In addition, given the high temporal resolution of the auditory system in combination with humans’
short- and long-term memory capacities, a more abstract representation of complex sounds such as
textures is likely derived, else the storage requirements become prohibitive [6].
To determine whether listeners use time-averaged statistics, even in the presence of ﬁne temporal
detail, and whether use of these statistics are speciﬁc to certain types of stimuli, McDermott et al.
[6] conducted a series of discrimination tasks using NH listeners.
The tasks exploited the fact that time-averaged statistics of a particular texture converge as the
excerpt length increases. Conversely, short excerpts taken from the same texture will vary consider-
ably, meaning the ﬁne temporal detail of that particular excerpt will be a more salient discrimination
cue than the time-averaged statistics.
From this, two discrimination tasks were designed: (1) texture discrimination, in which listeners
were tasked with discriminating between two diﬀerent textures and (2) exemplar discrimination,
in which listeners had to discriminate between two diﬀerent excerpts of the same texture.
The
independent variable in both tasks was excerpt duration, ranging from 40 to 2500-ms. The results
are shown in ﬁg. 2.7.
The results indicate that as excerpt duration increases, the auditory system uses time-averaged
statistics for discrimination of textures, while for brief excerpts, the ﬁne structure of the stimulus are
dominant. This implies that the auditory system is adaptive, making use of whichever representation

performance for sparse sounds was at the same level or improved with excerpt duration. To rule out
the possibility that statistic variability of sparse sounds might actually increase with excerpt duration
(thus providing more salient discrimination cues at longer durations), the statistical variability was
computed for all stimuli at short (40-ms) and long (2500-ms) excerpt durations.
While sparse
stimuli had greater variability overall, both sparse and dense stimuli showed the same trend: reduced
statistical variability at longer excerpt durations. Thus, an alternative explanation was proposed
for the observed improvement. Two concurrent encoding mechanisms take place in the auditory
system: (i) the temporal structure is encoded as a sequence of features, and simultaneously (ii)
a more compact running time-averaged statistic representation is encoded.
Since sparse sounds
are encoded with few features and places little demand on memory capacity, the ﬁne temporal
structure is available even at longer excerpt durations. In addition, the statistics of sparse stimuli
do not converge, meaning there is no beneﬁt for the auditory system to switch to a time-averaged
representation. Conversely, the statistics of dense stimuli converge as duration increases providing
more salient perceptual cues; in addition, at longer durations, the feature encoding of dense stimuli
results in a larger number of features and places greater demand on memory. As a result, it is
proposed that the auditory system gradually “switches” from feature representations to time-averaged
statistical representations for dense stimuli, such as sound textures.
A later study in 2017 by McWalter and Dau [8] investigated exemplar discrimination performance
of NH listeners for sound textures synthesised using an auditory model that included second-order
modulations. The results indicated that second-order amplitude modulation rates up to around
16-Hz may be accessible by the auditory system, and that sound textures synthesised including
second-order modulation statistics were preferred over textures that did not.
2.5
Analysis of statistic variability and excerpt duration
The inherent variability of a signal increases as signal duration - or length of analysis window -
decreases. This property is the basis of texture and exemplar discrimination tasks. McDermott et
al. [6] measured the statistic variability as a function excerpt duration through a model of normal
hearing using stimuli that ranged from sparse (single-source speech) to dense (115-speaker babble),
and in all cases the variability of each statistic class decreased with increasing excerpt duration,
ﬁg. 2.9.
The variability of textures (dense sounds) measured across a model of normal hearing decreased
with excerpt duration, and provides supporting evidence that time-averaged statistics are used by
the auditory system: as duration increases, the statistic representation of a texture converges to a
unique value oﬀering more salient cues when required to discriminate between two diﬀerent textures.

been shown to impair the listener’s ability to follow the Temporal Fine Structure (TFS) of the signal

discriminate between short sounds whose cues rely on the same acoustic properties that HI listeners
traditionally struggle with.

Experiment 1: Sound Texture Identiﬁcation
3.1
Rationale
Sound texture identiﬁcation tasks provide evidence that the time-averaged statistics measured across
stages of the auditory periphery are perceptually relevant. A previous study [5] with NH listen-
ers showed that synthetic textures whose long-term statistics better match those of the real-world
equivalent are easier to identify. The sensitivity of HI listeners to sound textures and their ability to
identify and discriminate textures has not yet been studied. Here, the performance of HI listeners
will be investigated and compared to that of NH listeners.

3.2
Methods
The methodology was almost identical to that of “Experiment 1: Texture Identiﬁcation” in [5, p. 930],
the key diﬀerence being the use of HI listeners. All other factors were kept as similar to the original
investigation as was practically possible. Hearing impaired listeners were asked to identify sound
textures (either synthetic or real): in each trial, a single stimulus was presented to the listener, along
with ﬁve labels, one of which must be chosen. One label was correct, the other four were incorrect
and randomly selected from the stimulus pool. Below, details on speciﬁc elements of the experiment
will be provided.

The textures were categorised into ﬁve texture classes: animals, environment, mechanical, people
and water. Stimuli within each class were highly similar in their statistical properties, and hence
each class was referred to as a Confusion group: stimuli within each group were more likely to be
confused with each other. For a full list of each texture and their corresponding confusion group,
see table A.1.
All tasks were conducted with MATLAB 2017a on a Macbook Pro 2017 model. Stimulus presentation
level was calibrated to 70 dB SPL using a G.R.A.S IEC 60318-1 Ear Simulator and a Norsonic Nor139
sound level meter. Playback was via a Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 USB audio interface (48-kHz, 24-bit)
and Sennheiser HD650 headphones.
An isolated sound booth (customised IAC 1200-A medical
room) was used, with the fan oﬀ. All listeners conducted the tests without a hearing aid but were
provided with audibility compensation. This was done using a CAMEQ ﬁlter which brings as much
of the stimulus’ spectrum above threshold as possible by equalising the average speciﬁc loudness of
the stimulus across the frequency range 500 - 4000-Hz [29].
3.2.3
Procedure
Each listener completed the identiﬁcation task in one session, with rests. Before beginning the task
listeners were questioned to ensure they fully understood the task, which consisted of 864 trials (96
textures × 9 conditions).
A 1-interval 5-alternative-forced-choice (1I-5AFC) task was used with a method of constant stimuli.
Listeners were presented with a stimulus and ﬁve text labels, each with a texture name on it.
Only one label corresponded to the presented texture, and the remaining four labels were randomly
selected from each of the other four confusion groups. The listener’s task was to identify the label
that corresponded to the texture presented to them.
3.2.4
Listeners
Listeners were selected based on the following search criteria:
• hearing loss within N2 - N5 category [30], and
• symmetrical hearing loss (± 10 dB)
The category was chosen to ensure that some degree of HI was present, but not so much that
audibility may be a concern. Symmetrical hearing impairment was desirable as signals were presented
diotically over headphones.
In total, eleven listeners participated (ten male, one female, average age 71), all fulﬁlling the criteria
listed above. All listeners were native Danish speakers.
The pure tone audiograms for all listeners are given in appendix A.1.2. The average audiogram for
each of the three categories along with standard audiogram thresholds [30] are shown in ﬁg. 4.3.
All listeners’ individual audiograms can be found in ﬁg. A.1.

3.4
Discussion
3.4.1
Eﬀect of imposed statistics on synthetic texture identiﬁcation performance
Results from the identiﬁcation tasks indicate that HI listeners are able to perceive synthetic sound
textures, and beneﬁt from increasing the number of statistics imposed during the synthesis process.
The overall performance of HI listeners was, however, lower than NH listeners: for nearly all statis-
tic groups, NH listeners performed signiﬁcantly better than HI listeners. Two suggestions for this
observation include (i) HI listeners form less salient statistical representations, perhaps due to im-
pairments in their peripheral auditory processing stages and (ii) higher cognitive stages involved in
the perception of auditory stimuli are less capable of utlilising the statistical representations. Given
that the statistical representation of textures are shaped by early auditory processes, impairments
to those processes are assumed to also change the statistic representation of textures. However,
assuming these diﬀerences do exist, it is not clear if they are inherently less salient or simply diﬀer-
ent: i.e. it is not clear if changes in the statistical representations result in an impaired perceptual
representation.
Furthermore, if less salient representations do exist, one might also expect that HI listeners would
learn to adapt to such diﬀerences. Neural adaptation is a well-studied phenomenon found in all
sensory systems, whereby a neuron’s response to a stimulus varies over time to enhance or suppress
some property of the signal [31]. In audition, for example, neurons in cat auditory A1 cortices have
been shown to adapt in response to the novelty of a stimulus’ frequency [32, 33], an adaptation
which has shown to change if the statistics of a stimulus’ properties vary over time [34]. Neurons
adapt their response functions to (e.g.) enhance the representation of onsets and oﬀsets [35, 36] and
eﬃciently encode intensities that occur most often [37]: these are examples of short-term stimulus-
driven adaptations.
In addition, long-term homeostatic adaptation has been observed to (e.g.)
recover from impaired localisation of sounds spatialised with non-personalised head-related transfer
functions [38].
It is therefore reasonable to expect that an impaired auditory system is capable of adapting (short- or
long-term) to changes in statistical representations of sound textures which arise due to impairments
in the physiology of the auditory periphery. One method of testing this hypothesis would be to
conduct texture identiﬁcation tasks on NH listeners using textures synthesised through an impaired
model and tracking performance over time.
Diﬀerences in performance between NH and HI listeners was relatively small for statistic groups
1 to 5.
This suggests that for both NH and HI listeners, statistical representations of textures
within individual stages of the auditory periphery are perceptually dominant and readily accessible.
Speciﬁcally, for both listener groups, performance increased signiﬁcantly when cochlear correlations
(statistic group 3) and modulation power (statistic group 4) were included, and an even greater
increase in performance occurred when both statistics were included.
The performance gap between NH and HI listeners increased, however, as the number of imposed
statistics increased, indicating that NH listeners continued to beneﬁt from these additional statistics,
29
==PAGE:42==

while HI listeners did so but to a lesser degree. One might suspect that the higher-order statistics
are less salient within an impaired system perhaps because any impairments to individual stages
of auditory processes accumulate. For example, considering the cascaded auditory model shown in
ﬁg. 2.1, a HI listener may have impairments aﬀecting some auditory ﬁlters and some modulation
ﬁlters: the overall impact on the statistical representation within those individual stages of the
model would be relatively small, while correlations which involve both stages would be aﬀected to
a greater degree due to accumulation of those impairments. This could be tested, for example, by
analysing the statistics through a model of normal hearing and hearing impairment (e.g. broader
acoustic ﬁlters), and then comparing the diﬀerences between the models for each statistic. If the
hypothesis is true, greater diﬀerences could be expected for the correlation statistics than for the
marginals.
In general, increases in performance due to correlation statistics (statistic groups 6 to 8) were modest.
For NH listeners, signiﬁcance was reached only between statistic groups 5 and 8, indicating that
correlation statistics, C1 or C2, alone are perceptually less salient, however, inclusion of both brings
beneﬁt. In contrast, HI listeners performed equally well across statistic groups 6 to 8, indicating
a lack of sensitivity to these speciﬁc correlations. For both listener groups, performance for the
original texture was highest. While this is perhaps no surprise, this does highlight an important
ﬁnding applicable to both listener groups: the analysis/synthesis process failed to account for some
other perceptually relevant cues.
Other statistics that could be included for example, were investigated in a 2017 study by McWal-
ter and Dau [8]. Textures were synthesised with an auditory model which included two cascaded
modulation ﬁlter banks designed to capture second-order modulations, believed to be important for
the perception of rhythm. The results were based on data collected from young NH listeners, and
indicated that the second-order modulation statistics brought a modest but signiﬁcant beneﬁt over
inclusion of ﬁrst-order correlations only.
On the other hand, while it has been shown that listeners are better able to identify synthetic
textures whose statistics closer match those of the real-world equivalent, it is possible that short-
term cues still play a perceptual role, and are necessary to be captured in the analysis/synthesis
process in order to close the performance gap between synthetic and real-world textures.
Nonetheless, while there are clearly perceptual diﬀerences between synthetic and real-world textures
under the current synthesis process, it is also important to note that HI listeners’ identiﬁcation per-
formance of real-world textures was signiﬁcantly worse than NH listeners’ and indicates an inherent
performance deﬁciency not attributable to the analysis/synthesis process, nor to audibility.
3.4.2
Texture identiﬁcation performance across confusion groups
Analysis of HI listeners’ performance across confusion groups indicated that water and environment
textures were identiﬁed correctly and incorrectly most often, respectively. The confusion matrix in
ﬁg. 3.4 indicates high confusion rates with water textures and it was assumed that this was not

EXPERIMENT 1: SOUND TEXTURE IDENTIFICATION
simply a “default” response that arose from uncertainty, since listeners were explicitly told during
the pre-experiment brieﬁng that, if uncertain, they should randomly select one of the labels. Thus,
assuming listeners adhered to these instructions, incorrect responses should be randomly distributed
across all confusion groups. That was not the case, implying that listeners actually were confusing
non-water textures with water.
One suggestion is related to the inherent statistical properties of water textures, which are highly
similar to white Gaussian noise - thus, any texture (be it animal, environment, mechanical or people)
is more likely to be confused with the white Gaussian noise seed with which all textures were
synthesised. If true, confusion rates should therefore be even higher for low-order synthetic textures
in which less shaping of the seed’s statistics has taken place. This hypothesis was conﬁrmed in
the post-hoc analysis which showed that confusion rates in the low-order statistics subset were
signiﬁcantly higher for water, while no signiﬁcance was reached for the high-order subset.
The data required to perform the same analysis on NH listeners was not available and so this
conclusion can only be conﬁdently applied to HI listeners. Therefore, if reliable inferences are to
be made about synthetic texture perception in HI listeners, it is recommended that textures are
synthesised using the statistics corresponding to statistic group 6, or higher, to avoid confusion
rates from biasing the data. Addressing the low performance levels for environment textures, it
is suggested to be due to the relatively narrow diversity of textures within this group. Referring
to table A.1, within the environment confusion group, there are ten textures in total, of which
ﬁve are variations of “ﬁre” and four are variations of “wind”.
Diﬃculty identifying one of these
texture classes would likely have a greater impact on the overall identiﬁcation performance of the
environment confusion group. In contrast, within (e.g.) the animals group a more diverse range of
textures was used meaning a diﬃculty in identifying, say, “bees” would not have such an eﬀect on
the animal identiﬁcation performance overall.
3.4.3
Eﬀect of hearing thresholds on synthetic texture identiﬁcation performance
The analysis of identiﬁcation performance across three HI categories revealed no signiﬁcant eﬀect of
listeners’ hearing thresholds. The three categories performed remarkably similar across all statistic
groups, as well as confusion groups and indicates that the overall variability of the data collected in
this study was low. Regardless of hearing thresholds, listeners indicated highly similar sensitivity to
statistic groups, with particular salience observed for cochlear correlations and modulation power.
It is apparent, therefore, that these speciﬁc statistics are perceptually relevant to auditory systems
in general. Although signiﬁcance was not reached, overall mean performance did decline as hearing
thresholds increased: under the assumption that a correlation exists but was failed to be observed,
one might suspect that the range of PTAs within the selected listeners was insuﬃcient to expose this
correlation to signiﬁcance. However, given that listeners were provided with sensitivity compensa-
tion, greater sensitivity loss would require further compensation and risk artefacts such as digital
clipping. Instead, it is suggested that increasing the sample size within each of the HI categories

used in this study would provide suﬃcient data to reliably conclude whether PTA and identiﬁcation
performance are signiﬁcantly correlated or not.
As a closing remark, the key observation from this study was that all listeners, regardless of hearing
thresholds, were able to beneﬁt from imposed statistics in a similar way but to varying degrees. Fur-
thermore, an inherent deﬁcit in HI listeners’ ability to identify sound textures exists. Together, with
the fact that listeners were compensated for sensitivity, this indicates that sound texture perception
is aﬀected to some degree by suprathreshold hearing abilities.

Experiment 2: Sound Texture Discrimination
4.1
Rationale
Sound texture discrimination tasks provide evidence that the time-averaged statistics formed across
stages of the auditory periphery are used by the auditory system in perception. A previous study [6]
with NH listeners showed that, as excerpt duration increases, discrimination performance between
two diﬀerent textures improves. Conversely, discrimination performance between two excerpts of
the same texture increases as excerpt duration decreases.
The observations are correlated with
the fact that time-averaged statistics of textures converge at long durations, oﬀering more salient
discrimination cues, while access to highly variable, short-term cues are necessary at short durations.
In this experiment, the discrimination performance of young and old listeners, with and without
sensorineural hearing loss, will be investigated. It is hypothesised that age- and hearing-related
impairments may aﬀect the temporal acuity of listeners required to discriminate textures, and that
these factors may aﬀect performance in diﬀerent ways.

4.2
Methods
The following section details the method used in the discrimination tasks. Great eﬀort was made
to ensure exact replication of the same tasks conducted in [6], and any elements which were not
replicated are made explicit in the text.
A 3-interval, 2-alternative-forced-choice (3I-2AFC) task was used with a method of constant stimuli.
Listeners were presented with three intervals, A-Ref-B. The task was to indicate the interval, A or
B, which contained the odd-one-out. In the case of texture discrimination, one of the intervals, A or
B, is a diﬀerent excerpt but taken from the same texture as the Reference, while the other interval
is from a diﬀerent texture entirely. The task measures listeners’ ability to hear diﬀerences between
diﬀerent textures. In exemplar discrimination, three intervals are again presented to the listener,
however this time all three belong to the same texture, whereby one interval, A or B, is physically
identical to the Reference, while the other is a diﬀerent excerpt of the same texture. This task tests
listeners’ ability to detect diﬀerences within a single texture.
All stimuli were separated by a ﬁxed inter-stimlus interval (ISI) of 400-ms.

4.4
Discussion
The results presented in section 4.3 provide evidence that texture discrimination performance is,
in general, not signiﬁcantly aﬀected by age or hearing loss, with performance approaching ceiling
values in all cases. When grouped by age, some performance diﬀerences were observed between
durations 40 and 209-ms, however for durations 478 to 2500-ms, performance between the two age
groups was remarkably similar. This indicates a modest eﬀect of age only at shorter durations.
When grouped by hearing, HI listeners showed very similar performance to NH listeners, but with
an overall negative oﬀset at all excerpt durations. The data supports the hypothesis that time-
averaged statistics are used by the auditory system, and that they are highly robust to age-related
and sensorineural auditory impairments.
In contrast, exemplar discrimination performance was aﬀected signiﬁcantly by age. Listeners under
ﬁfty years performed well and approached ceiling values at the shortest durations indicating accessi-
ble short-term cues. However, listeners over ﬁfty years performed signiﬁcantly worse, in particular at
short durations. A large divergence in performance was found at short excerpt durations indicating
a duration-dependent deﬁcit in older listeners. The eﬀect of hearing loss was modest but rather
consistent across all durations.
However, in contrast to texture discrimination, the HI listeners
performed better than NH listeners.
The subsequent sections will discuss the performance of listeners in both age and hearing groups,
and a model proposed that predicts performance of listeners.
4.4.1
Eﬀect of hearing loss on texture discrimination
The eﬀect of hearing loss on texture discrimination, while insigniﬁcant, did result in lower mean
performance consistently across excerpt durations.
Results from texture identiﬁcation tasks, as
discussed in section 3.4.1, indicated a similar ﬁnding: HI listeners beneﬁted from textures’ statistics
in a similar way to NH listeners, though to a consistently lesser degree.
It was suggested that
less salient statistical representations of textures are formed in an impaired auditory system. Poorer
texture discrimination performance implies that the auditory system is less able to converge textures’
statistics to their long-term average, resulting in greater statistical variability, and therefore less
salient between-texture discrimination cues. The lower performance observed in both HI listeners’
identiﬁcation and discrimination tasks may therefore be linked to a common phenomenon: greater
variability in the statistical representations.
The textures used in the discrimination tasks were synthesised using the highest-order statistic group
(i.e. group 8 in the identiﬁcation tasks). Identiﬁcation performance for this statistic group was
signiﬁcantly lower for HI listeners than NH listeners, while diﬀerences in discrimination performance
did not reach signiﬁcance. However the chance level of the identiﬁcation tasks was much lower at
20%, meaning it is hard to say if diﬀerences failed to reach signiﬁcance in discrimination because
the task was relatively easy.
Nonetheless, the data implies that texture identiﬁcation is a reliable predictor for texture discrim-
48
==PAGE:61==
4

ination, a task trend observed in other psychophysical practices [39]. Together, these observations
provide further evidence that statistical representations of sound textures are robust to impairments
within the auditory system, leading to similar trends in performance, but with overall negative, and
relatively consistent, oﬀsets.
One natural question that arises from this theory is: why should statistical variability in an impaired
auditory system increase, rather than decrease or even remain the same? One reason may be related
to poorer temporal resolution in HI listeners. Many studies have attempted to measure the auditory
system’s temporal resolution, and while there is no consensus in the literature, many studies have
found an eﬀect of cochlear hearing loss. Earlier studies based their hypotheses and methods on
the theory that the auditory system integrates over long durations, for example [40, 41], and found
that HI listeners had poorer temporal resolution than NH listeners. However, the “multiple looks”
theory proposed by Viemeister and Wakeﬁed in 1991 changed the perspective by suggesting that,
as opposed to long-duration integration, the auditory system is able to observe, store and integrate
many shorter auditory “looks” of the signal [42].
Therefore, the longer the signal duration, the
greater the number of “looks” the listener hears which provides more accurate information about the
signal. Speciﬁcally, a listener’s total detectability (assuming contiguous, non-overlapping analysis
windows), d′, could be deﬁned mathematically as the sum of the detectability within each “look” the
listener has access to:

4.4.2
Eﬀect of hearing loss on exemplar discrimination
The eﬀect of hearing loss on exemplar discrimination performance failed to reach signiﬁcance, though
an overall negative oﬀset in mean performance was again observed consistently across excerpt dura-
tions. This is the same trend observed in texture discrimination, with one key diﬀerence: this time
HI listeners performed better than NH listeners. While this may appear paradoxical, the reasoning
given previously is applicable to this observation too, as explained in the following, and illustrated
in ﬁg. 4.14.
A HI listener (under the multiple looks model assumption) has poorer duration-independent tem-
poral resolution. This in turn corresponds to a listener who observes fewer “looks” at the signal per
unit time, than a NH listener. For a given excerpt duration, a HI listener will form a more variable
statistical representation than a NH listener since their time-averaged representation is derived from
fewer “looks”.
With respect to texture discrimination in which the listener is presented with three unique excerpts
(two of the same texture, one diﬀerent), low variability will aid the listener by converging the two
excerpts of the same texture to very similar representations, while the odd-one-out converges to
a diﬀerent representation. Conversely, high variability would cause the two excerpts of the same
texture to converge to less similar representations and result in greater chance of confusion.
In exemplar discrimination, the listener is presented with three excerpts all taken from the same
texture: two identical excerpts and one diﬀerent. In this case low variability (NH listeners) would
result in weaker discrimination cues as all three excerpts would converge to the same representation
of the texture. Conversely, high variability (HI listeners) would hinder the rate of convergence, and
provide more salient discrimination cues.

Eﬀect of age on texture discrimination
The results collected in this study indicate that age may have an eﬀect on texture discrimination,
however the eﬀect may also be confounded with hearing loss.

performed remarkably similar. Interestingly too, diﬀerences were negligible at durations where high
performance is expected, indicating that the long-term cues are robust to such deﬁcits.
When grouping the data by age, no signiﬁcant diﬀerences were found with remarkably similar average
performance between young and old listeners for durations 478, 1093 and 2500-ms: however, as

4.4.4
Eﬀect of age on exemplar discrimination
The largest and most signiﬁcant diﬀerences were found between age groups’ exemplar discrimina-
tion performance. The older listeners performed poorly compared to NH listeners, in particular at
durations shorter than 478-ms and, at these durations, actually matched very closely that of their
own texture discrimination performance. It seems, therefore, that older listeners cannot access the
necessary cues required for good exemplar discrimination performance at short durations and, fur-
thermore, that their performance is reduced to be as comparably good as their texture discrimination
performance.
The duration-dependent eﬀect of age on exemplar discrimination is shown clearly in ﬁg. 4.11(c).
The shape of the curve is also highly similar to that observed for texture discrimination performance
diﬀerences, but with a much steeper slope that plateaus at around 8-9%. This indicates that, in
general, age-related deﬁcits are duration-dependent, but are more clearly exposed in tasks which
require salient access to the ﬁne structure of the signal.
Temporal Envelope (TE) and Temporal Fine Structure (TFS) information becomes more unique at
short durations. Many studies have investigated the eﬀect of both age and hearing loss on temporal-
related tasks. For example, in [44], the eﬀects of aging and hearing loss were studied independently
through a large battery of listening tasks and found that aging without hearing loss resulted in
signiﬁcantly greater Diﬀerence limens for duration discrimination. Older listeners have been found
to have higher Temporal Modulation Transfer Function (TMTF) thresholds both above and below
the transition frequency [45]. Sensitivity to the TFS was also found to be signiﬁcantly aﬀected by
age despite no correlation with audiometric thresholds or frequency selectivity [46], although a later
study did ﬁnd a correlation when the sounds were presented in noise [47]. Another study found
that young NH listeners were more sensitive to TE and TFS information than old NH listeners [48].
Unfortunately, many of these studies were unable to disentangle the confounded eﬀects of hearing
loss and age, making meaningful conclusions diﬃcult.
One explanation for age-related hearing diﬃculties is deaﬀerentation: a permanent loss of auditory
nerve ﬁbres which can contribute to speech-in-noise and temporal processing deﬁcits [49], found
in older listeners with normal audiometric thresholds [50]. Furthermore, deaﬀerentation has been
successfully modelled through stochastic undersampling [51], and duration detection thresholds of
NH listeners increased when measured with “deaﬀerented” stimuli, indicating temporal-processing
deﬁcits. Yet more interesting and relevant to this study was that the thresholds were duration-
dependent, elevated only for stimuli less than 200-ms: divergence of exemplar discrimination per-

4.4.5
General discussion: eﬀect of age and hearing loss
Before attempting to unravel the eﬀects of age and hearing loss, it is important to understand ﬁrst
what is happening in a young NH listener: why does the auditory system transition from using
short-term cues (such as TE and TFS) to time-averaged statistics? McDermott et al. [6] proposed
two modes of auditory representation: (i) encoding of short-term sequential features (higher memory
demand) and (ii) time-averaged summary statistics (lower memory demand), both of which occur
simultaneously, with the selected mode depending on stimulus type (sparse/dense) as well as duration
(due to memory capacity). Restricting the discussion to dense stimuli (such as textures) whose long-
term statistics converge, the model can account for the data measured from young NH listeners,
ﬁg. 4.15.
As shown throughout this study, performance of HI and/or elderly listeners diﬀer quite signiﬁcantly.
One could therefore ask if it is possible to predict how a HI and/or elderly listener will perform?
The analysis presented and discussed in this study suggests the answer is yes. The eﬀect of age was
a duration-dependent deﬁcit, and the eﬀect of hearing a duration-independent deﬁcit. Furthermore,
even young NH listeners’ performance for short exemplars was not quite at the same levels as long
texture durations: this indicates a general deﬁcit for short excerpts, applicable to all listeners.
Based on these key observations, a model is proposed as follows: (1) Three weighting functions
that represent no impairment, age and hearing loss are deﬁned. The shape of the functions are
derived from the analysis discussed previously, and combinations of functions are achieved through
multiplication (i.e. HI+50 is derived by multiplying the “age” function with the “hearing” function).
(2) A sensitivity weighting function is applied to each of the combined deﬁcit functions to represent
internal noise in the system and accounts for the slight reduction in performance observed at short
exemplars, even in young NH listeners. Combination of deﬁcit and sensitivity curves yields a total
deﬁcit weighting function. (3) Reference performance curves represent the theoretical performance
of an ideal observer who is able to achieve performance levels of 100% in both exemplar and texture
discrimination.
This theoretical listener has no “noise” in the system, and is able to perfectly
encode all features at short excerpt durations and perfectly converge time-averaged statistics at long
durations. The resulting performance curves for each task are therefore mirror images of each other.

5.2.2
Stimulus groups
The same texture pairs as used in section 4, and listed in table A.4 were used. Statistic variability
was computed as the average across for all left- and right-column textures separately.
5.2.3
Statistic classes
The seven classes of statistics measured represent auditory processing from the cochlea to the thala-
mus. They can be divided in two subclasses: envelope and modulation ﬁlters. The former represents
the output of envelope extraction that results from peripheral ﬁltering and cochlear compression.
Subsequently, the cochlear envelopes are then subjected to modulation-sensitive ﬁlters which allows
for detection of slow variations in the acoustic signal. Ultimately, four statistics were measured
across the cochlear envelopes (mean, variance, skew and auto-correlations), and three across the
modulation ﬁlters (power, across-audio-frequency correlations (C1) and across-modulation-frequency
correlations (C2)).
5.2.4
Excerpt durations
The envelopes and subbands of the input stimulus were ﬁrst computed. Subsequently each excerpt
duration (40, 91, 209, 478, 1093 and 2500-ms) was windowed eight times using non-overlapping
rectangular windows to increase the sample size per excerpt duration, yielding eight unique samples
per excerpt duration. The statistics within each of these samples were then measured and averaged.
The total variability across all statistics was computed by ﬁrst normalising the variability of each
statistic class by its mean (necessary, since each statistic class has its own units), and then averaging
across all statistic classes. A detailed derivation of these computations as well as a pseudocode block
diagram are given in appendix A.3.1 and ﬁg. A.11, respectively.

IMPAIRMENT
5.4
Discussion
Measurements of statistic variability across models of normal hearing and seven hearing impairments
indicated that the early peripheral auditory stages have an eﬀect on the statistical representation of
sound textures. A number of model of hearing impairment were used, not to simulate physiologically
accurate representations of HI listeners, but rather to force the model to deviate away from a healthy
model.
Total variability oﬀers a snapshot of how the statistics are represented in the auditory system, and
furthermore oﬀer a useful insight into how these representations might aﬀect discrimination tasks.
As discussed in section 4.4, it was proposed that HI listeners might form statistical representations
with greater variability (i.e. less able to converge to a long-term value). The results from this section
provided no concrete evidence to strengthen or weaken this argument. It is clear that, for example,
total loss of compression in the auditory model resulted in signiﬁcantly greater variability, and could
be an avenue for further investigation by altering the degree to which compression is lost. This could
be done by varying the exponent of the compression in the model. On the other hand, impairments
involving the auditory and/or modulation ﬁlters had much less eﬀect. Systematic impairment of
one stage of the auditory periphery was conducted to test if systematic changes in the variability
were observed at the output. This was found to be true, whereby broadening of the auditory ﬁlters
resulted in systematic shifts in variability across all statistic classes and excerpt durations. However,
the direction of the shift (increase of decrease) was dependent on the statistic class. It is perhaps
not surprising that correlation statistics (envelope correlations and modulation C1 and C2) showed
an increase since model Biii is comprised of the fewest ﬁlters (4× less), each of greatest bandwidth
(4× broader), meaning the correlation between the nearest neighbouring ﬁlters must also increase.
Furthermore, measurements of the total statistic variability indicated that model Biii resulted in
greater average variability than the Bii or the healthy model. This implies that correlation statistics
(the only statistic classes to show an increase in variability with broadening of the auditory ﬁlters)
are driving the total increase in variability.
In general, systematic impairment of the auditory ﬁlters did not provide suﬃcient evidence to con-
clude that auditory impairments lead to greater variability per se. However, it is clear than im-
pairments to the model do give rise to changes in statistical variability, which surely do manifest as
discrimination cues.
It is further stressed that the impairments imposed on these models are not presumed to simulate
a real HI listener. Instead, typical physiological impairments, such as loss of frequency selectivity,
that have been modelled with great success in engineering applications, have been applied and their
eﬀects studied. Naturally, a real HI listener will not have the physiological manifestation of broader
acoustic alone: in addition, loss of cochlear compression and poorer temporal resolution would also
be present to some degree.
To better understand the eﬀect of auditory impairments on statistic variability a larger analysis
including systematic changes in all auditory processes and their interactions would be highly bene-

OVERALL DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
6
Overall Discussion and Conclusions
The aim of this study was to explore sound texture perception in hearing impaired (HI) listeners.
Sound textures oﬀer an ecologically valid and systematic means of probing the auditory system.
However, little research has been conducted with HI listeners.
6.1
Summary of main ﬁndings
6.1.1
Experiment 1: Sound texture identiﬁcation
The results from experiment 1 indicated that HI listeners with audibility compensation beneﬁt
perceptually from the statistics formed across the peripheral auditory stages in a similar way to
normal-hearing (NH) listeners, though to an overall lesser degree. In addition, an inherent deﬁcit in
identiﬁcation of real-world textures was observed indicating that the perception of auditory statistics
operates at a suprathreshold level, whereby it is not clear whether this is due to hearing status or
age. Finally, the results provided evidence that synthetic textures are a viable means of probing not
only NH, but also HI listeners.
6.1.2
Experiment 2: Sound texture discrimination
In experiment 2, texture discrimination experiments were conducted to explore how four listener
groups responded to textures of varying length.
The results showed that listeners with hearing
loss, regardless of age, performed similarly to NH listeners across all durations, but with an overall
negative performance oﬀset. In addition, elderly listeners were much less able to discriminate short
duration sounds where access to the temporal detail is crucial, while performance at longer durations,
where time-averaged cues dominate, was unaﬀected.
The key conclusion was that hearing loss,
regardless of age, may manifest as a duration-independent temporal deﬁcit, while age, regardless of
hearing status, may manifest as a duration-dependent temporal deﬁcit. A model was proposed to
account for these deﬁcits and to oﬀer a new avenue of research that may aid in future diagnostic
tools.
6.1.3
Experiment 3: Analysis of statistic variability in models of hearing impairment
In the third and ﬁnal experiment, the internal statistical representation of textures in auditory
models that were systematically impaired was explored and compared to a model of normal hearing.
It was found that impairments to the peripheral auditory stages, such as loss of compression or
broader auditory ﬁlters, aﬀect the statistic variability, whereby the eﬀect depended on which stage
of auditory processing was impaired. Furthermore, systematic impairments to the model (2x and
4x broader auditory ﬁlters) resulted in systematic shifts in statistic variability. More work is needed
to relate these ﬁndings to the data presented and discussed in experiments 1 and 2.

OVERALL DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
6.2
Limitations of the study
This study has some limitations. Firstly, the listener group used in experiment 1 represented listeners
of old age (average 71 years) and hearing loss. Given the results analysed and discussed in experiment
2, it is important to state that the results from experiment 1 may infer confounded eﬀects of both
age and hearing loss.
Secondly, the age distributions of the two older groups in experiment 2 were not statistically matched.
Age groups “old” and “young” were broadly deﬁned as above or below ﬁfty years of age; within-group
eﬀects can not be eliminated without matched age groups. Eﬀort was made to match the groups as
best as possible, but access to HI and NH listeners was limited.
Thirdly, the sample size of the HI-50 group was small. While the data from this group already align
well with the observations based on the other listener groups (of larger sample sizes), more listeners
within this listener group must be measured.
Finally, the age distribution of HI-50 listeners was much larger than the other groups. The within-
group eﬀect on performance correlated well with the proposed theory (age aﬀects sound texture
perception). However, to more accurately measure the eﬀect of age itself, a smaller distribution
would be required.
6.3
Perspectives
1. This study investigated the perception of naturalistic stimuli in HI listeners.
2. The ﬁndings of this study provided evidence that listeners with hearing loss and of older age
struggle to process textures of short duration. Further work is now required to explore deeper
what this means and its consequences to processing speech stimuli.
3. Hearing impaired listeners identiﬁed real-world textures signiﬁcantly worse than NH listeners.
The cause of this inherent deﬁciency is not clear, and may be due to less salient cues formed
in the auditory periphery, or to high-level cognitive impairments that are unable to utilise the
statistics in perception. In addition, results from the discrimination tasks in this study indi-
cated that age aﬀects sound texture perception, and therefore the sound texture identiﬁcation
results of elderly HI listeners may in fact show confounded eﬀects of both age and hearing loss.
4. Listeners in this study were compensated for audibility. It would be interesting to also study
of the eﬀects of “aided” hearing: testing the eﬀects of diﬀerent types of compensation strategies
on texture perception.
5. Traditional measures of the auditory system’s temporal resolution include gap detection, tem-
poral integration and modulation detection and discrimination. Correlates of these measures
with listeners’ texture discrimination performance would help to shine more light on the un-
derlying deﬁcits observed in this study.

==PAGE:79==

OVERALL DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
6. It has been suggested in this study that the diﬀerences in performance between NH and HI
listeners could be due to diﬀerences in statistical variability. In addition, it has also been
suggested that this could be explored by measuring the variability of sound textures through
models of hearing impairment. An alternative, and arguably more ecologically valid approach,
would be to manipulate the variability of sound textures and measure performance of young
NH listeners to these stimuli. If the performance of young NH listeners in response to the
manipulated stimuli approach those of elderly and/or HI listeners, this would demonstrate
better understanding of how changes in auditory processes aﬀect texture perception.
7. All experiments related to sound texture perception have been conducted in quiet. It would
be interesting to also investigate listeners’ ability to identify and discriminate sound textures
in a noisy acoustic environment.
