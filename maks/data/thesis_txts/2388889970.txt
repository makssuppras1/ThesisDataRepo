Abstract
The present thesis describes the work carried out to obtain the master
degree in Earth and Space Physics and Engineering, Space Research subpro-
gramme, at Denmark’s Technical University. The purpose of this project is
to inquire whether cosmic rays, and energetic protons in particular, had a
role in the disappearance of the organic molecules on Mars’ surface.
To do so, three amino acids, two biological and one not, have been mixed
with the Martian soil simulant “JSC-Mars-1” and exposed to a 32MeV pro-
ton beam. This beam was generated with a cyclotron Scanditronix MC32-NI
at the Copenhagen University Hospital “Rigshospitalet”.
The samples generated in this way have been compared to control ones
through infra-red spectroscopy, measurement possible with the Attenuated
Total Reﬂectance machine (ATR) at DTU Chemistry.

Preface
Project rationale and history
The topic for this thesis was ﬁrst proposed by Morten Bo Madsen (Niels
Bohr Institute), and consisted in observing the damage cosmic rays could
do onto particular organic materials in order to deepen the knowledge about
organics on Mars. The latter have notoriously been key parameters in inquir-
ing the rocky planet’s evolution, and therefore useful to understand Earth
better, too.
Organics on Mars have been especially elusive: each mission has never de-
tected the expected amount, for which few theories have been proposed to
explain the processes with which they could have eluded our sensors or sim-
ply have been consumed way before.
The presence of cosmic rays is a ﬁrst in this ﬁeld, since the majority of works
on organics involves UV light induced damage only. In fact, to this date
little to none empirical work has been done with cosmic rays, but there are
plenty of guidance papers about simulations and general results that await
conﬁrmation.
Given the very ambitious goal, the project was restricted to ﬁt a master’s
thesis on Ten Kate’s advice. The ﬁnal form of this project is to observe and
quantify, if any, the nuclear and chemical alterations that energetic protons
(which are the main component of cosmic rays) induce in amino acids, in
order to understand whether the cosmic rays could have had a signiﬁcant
role in the disappearance of Martian organics.
With Madsen’s unfortunate withdrawal from the project in the early
stage, there was need to ﬁnd a ﬁgure of support. Carsten Søgaard (Niels

Chapter 1
Theory
The fourth rocky planet of our solar system, Mars, has an atmosphere which
is widely diﬀerent from the one we experience on Earth: with a mean tem-
perature of 63◦C, it is mostly composed of carbon dioxide, and its pressure
is about 200 times lower than ours.
This condition is thought to have been occurred after Mars lost its dipole
magnetic ﬁeld, allowing the solar wind to strip down its atmosphere molecule
by molecule.
The combination of these two facts also exposed Mars’ surface to a big vari-
ety of radiation from space: the so called Galactic Rays.
Galactic Rays are mostly from neighbour stars, whose supernovae ejected
atoms in every direction. Pulsars, Quasars and other stars launch powerful
electromagnetic radiation in space, generated by collisions and explosions
that have no rival in nature.
On earth, life is protected from most of these hazards. On the other hand,
Mars cannot boast the same. Any organic material is exposed to ionizing
radiation, high energy radiation that strips electrons from the atoms and
triggers chemical reactions that do not usually happen.
Here, we take a look at what happens with the particle that composes the
major part of the cosmic rays: the energetic protons.

1.3
Nuclear decays
Because this project involves the bombardment of a target with a proton
beam, it is expected that such projectiles will destabilize the nuclei of its
atomic species. In this section will be treated the likely nuclear reactions
that can arise from a surplus of protons, neutrons, or a simple excess of en-
ergy within the nucleus. Unless speciﬁed, the theory exhibited in this section
makes reference to the textbook “Particles and Nuclei”[2] 6th edition.
Only a particular number of protons and neutrons allow each nucleus to
be stable. The lightest nuclei possess such ratio of 1, but the relative number
of neutrons increases with the atomic mass (ﬁgure 1.3). When there is a
deviation from this proportion, a speciﬁc type of nuclear reaction will bring
the ratio back to stable values.
Nuclear reactions obey the conservation of charge, energy, angular momen-
tum, and number of leptons and baryons.
The nuclear reactions expected in this project after the irradiation will
be among the following ones.

Chapter 2
Instrumentation
2.1
The proton beam
The machine that made this project possible was the cyclotron Scanditronix
MC32-NI at the Copenhagen University Hospital “Rigshospitalet” (Den-
mark): great merit goes to the cyclotron’s chief physicist Holger Jan Jensen
to have allowed this project to happen, and for its personal involvement in
the irradiations.
The protons that will constitute the beam are accelerated in this gar-
gantuan machine’s two dees, the D-shaped plates that will allow the ions
to spiral out and be extracted. In this machine, the two dees are actually
shaped like quarters of a circle[10], allowing the particles to travel outside
them for longer, thus increasing the acceleration (see below).
Figure 2.1: particles’ path within the dees[9]
The circular motion of the ions is achieved by introducing a very intense
8
==PAGE:16==
magnetic ﬁeld of 1.6T[10] perpendicular to the dees’ plane, so that the par-
ticles could experience a centripetal acceleration towards ⃗v × ⃗B due to the
Lorentz force.
The pure speed increase on the other hand is driven by the electric ﬁeld
that the particles are subject to when outside the dees. The voltage used to
achieve so is 35kV[10], inverted with a frequency of 24MHz[10] to make sure
that the acceleration matches the particles’ velocity at each exit from a dee.
The resulting beam currents range from nanoAmperes to a hundred mi-
croAmperes, and its nominal energies between 16 and 32 megaelectron-
Volts[10].
The beam is composed by vast majority of hydrogen nuclei, plus a small
fraction of slower neutrons.
With a current of about 1.5 nA and 31MeV
energy, the beam’s proﬁle was measured in the PhD thesis “Development
of CdZnTe detector systems for space applications” [7], and it resembled a
two-dimensional Gaussian distribution.
Accounting for neutrons’ inﬂuence on the target would have been very chal-
lenging, because of the limited knowledge about the portion of the beam
being composed of neutrons and their energy spectrum.
The neutral charge of the neutrons here comes handy. Excluding potential
resonating phenomena of the neutron cross-section with the soil compounds’
atoms, the probability of interaction between the two is, according to a study
published in 1956, very small: “The integrated cross section [...] (of 12C) from
18 to 30 MeV is [...] 100 mb-MeV”[8].
Considering the very little collisions neutrons could have had in a target 1mm
thick, and their relative low presence in the beam, it was chosen to disregard
them from further calculations in this work.
2.2
The Attenuated Total Reﬂection machine
(ATR)
The Chemical Department of DTU, in particular Renè Wugt Larsen and
Dmytro Mihrin, kindly made available the Attenuated Total Reﬂection ma-
chine (ATR) to be used as a measuring instrument for this project.
The ATR machine mainly consists in a vacuum pump, the measuring di-
amond crystal, and an infra-red emitter with its detector. The chamber that
contains the 1mm2 diamond surface can be brought to pressures of a few
hectoPascals, so that the inﬂuence of atmospheric gasses and water vapour
in the measure can be minimized.
The instrument has a spectral region of (45000 ÷ 10)cm−1[12], but only the

The instrument functions by shining an infra-red beam at 45◦from the
normal vector in the diamond surface as shown in ﬁgure 2.3. When the light
reaches the diamond-sample interface it gets reﬂected, but not before pen-
etrating for 1.66µm[12] into the sample in the form of an evanescent wave.
This depth depends by the relative diﬀerence of the sample’s optical refrac-
tive index and the diamond’s (2.40)[12]. The wave that peers beyond the
interface interacts with the sample, and gets absorbed at the characteristic
wavelengths of the materials. This shortage is ﬁnally measured after the wave
leaves the crystal, and converted in the infra-red spectrum of the sample.
Each peak in the spectrum corresponds to a singe chemical bond or a par-
ticular molecular conﬁguration; some of these correspondences are shown
in “Guide for infrared spectroscopy”[13] by the machine producer company
Bruker.
Several methodological and practical adjustments had to be made to the
project’s plan in order to use this exceptional instrument without taking any
risks for its delicate components. In fact, some of the ATR’s characteristics
proposed challenging situations, in which the solution for an issue generated
a second scenario to avoid. Most notably, the machine required the samples
to be as ﬁne as possible to to provide a reading that reﬂected the compo-
nent’s proportions correctly, but this made the sample so thin that it would
have been sucked away when vacuum was applied to the measure chamber.
Retaining some micrograms of dust inside the instrument’s conducts not ad-
visable per-se, and it is strictly forbidden by law whenever that material is
even slightly radioactive.

Chapter 3
Compounds choice
When it is tasked to investigate the relation between cosmic rays and the
absence of organic compounds on Mars, the straightforward approach is to
replicate the same interaction between the two. Not knowing which phenom-
ena brings to their destruction (or alteration), it is of most importance to
replicate the very same conditions on Mars at the best of one’s capabilities.
For this reason, the ﬁrst draft of this project involved the presence of Mar-
tian atmospheric gasses and soil at the moment of irradiation of the organic
compounds.
Unfortunately, most of the smallest details had to be left behind, since they
would overcomplicate the project and stretch it past the time assigned.
Priority was given to the compounds that would have interacted the most
with the organics: the components of Mars’ soil. Atmospheric gasses and
alike were removed due to the lower interaction with the (hypothetical) or-
ganic compounds, and because of the challenge they presented in containing
them along all the diﬀerent phases of the project.
3.1
The Martian soil simulant
In the past decade, research that concerned practical involvement of Martian
soil made use mostly of ﬁve samples[14]: the Atacama Desert[15][16] and Are-
quipa organic Mars soil simulants, the chemical simulants Mojave Desert[15]
and JSC-Mars-1[17], or the magnetic simulant Salten Skov dust analogue.
The preferred kind of simulant for this project has been of course the
chemical one, since a key factor is to replicate the combined eﬀects of high
energetic proton exposure and the following possible chemical alterations.
The relative abundance of the various chemical species in the simulant is not
a top priority, provided they contain the least amount of alien molecules to

3.3
Optimal amino acid-to-soil ratio
Since chemical reactions could be involved in the future calculations, it makes
sense to express the amino acid-to-soil ratio as a molar percentage. This
choice was actually driven mostly by the practical reason that the molar
weights can be calculated precisely from literature data, whereas mass ratio
could have not. This is because the latter is dependent from the dust’s den-
sity, which is computed from its non-constant volume.
The ﬁrst parameter that inﬂuenced the choice for a molar ratio has been
ATR’s sensitivity: the easier the machine could detect the organics, the
better.
On the other hand, a counterbalancing factor was the need to have the
maximum relative amount of soil. This is because the ideal scenario (where
the only chemical reactions happening are the ones between amino acids and
soil) is met when each organic molecule is surrounded exclusively by JSC-
Mars-1 molecules.
Combining the two aforementioned restrains, the ﬁnal verbalisation of the
goal of this section will then be to ﬁnd the smallest molar ratio above which
the ATR readings do not improve signiﬁcantly.
To do so, the ATR machine has analysed three mixtures of respectively 5%,
10%, and 30% alanine-to-JSC molar percentages.
Figure 3.3 exhibits the most characteristic interval of alanine’s spectrum.
It can be seen that the 30% molar ratio curve (in pink) meets the condi-
tion above: the diﬀerence between the green curve (10%) and the pink one
is quite small, when compared to the diﬀerence between the green and the
blue (5%). This means that any percentage higher than 30% would increase
the amplitude by an amount that can be sacriﬁced to have as much soil as
possible. 30% was therefore chosen as optimal molar percentage.
A second information that can be extracted by this trial is the smallest ratio
that the technique can measure: a 5% molar diﬀerence already has shorter
(but broader) peaks than the background noise. It is still recognizable, but it
would be diﬃcult to spot whenever one does not know what spectrum proﬁle

Chapter 4
Casing design
In order to create the most eﬀective casings that will contain the soil com-
pounds, a list of requirements had to be met. Some constraints were more
pressing than others, so it was more appropriate to deﬁne a priority list (top
being highest). This means that every choice made to meet each requirement
did not worsen signiﬁcantly the fulﬁlling of the ones above it.
Shape requirements:
1. Be the most airtight possible.
This is because of three reasons. Most importantly, after the irradi-
ation, it is imperative that no active dust spills out of the casings.
Secondly, mostly valid during the irradiation, it is preferred that the
amino acids only come in contact with molecules that are abundant
on Mars, not with earthen atmospheric gasses. Lastly, to keep the soil
deprived of its water content.
2. Be the thinnest achievable in front and back of the soil, according to
the beam path.
An excessive amount of material in front of the soil would decrease
the particles’ velocity so that the energy deposition would shift to its
maximum, the Bragg peak. Also, the more material around the soil,
the more diﬃcult it is to disperse the heat generated by such deposited
energy.
3. Be able to ﬁt in a socket of the target support (ﬁgure 4.1).
4. Have the same symmetry of the beam, i.e. radial from its mean posi-
tion.
This will just simplify the calculations for the proton ﬂuencies later.
21
==PAGE:29==
Material requirements:
I Be aﬀordable.
II Be conductive.
The target support that is placed in front of the beam is connected to
an ammeter, so that it is possible to measure the current extracted, plus
relieve the target from any electric charge accumulated in the process.
III Have little to none chemical interaction with the soil compounds.
Similarly to the airtightness, it is best to avoid unnecessary sources of
impurities and chemical reactions that do not happen on Mars. One
simple way to overcome this problem is to have the compounds in
contact with a material that is already abundant in the Martian soil
and atmosphere.
IV Have the least thermal resistivity.
The energy deposited by the beam on the target will be converted in
heat. High temperatures must be avoided to prevent heat degradation
of the organics or any state changes at all.
V Contain the least amount of atomic species that would produce long-life
isotopes and high energy radiation after being exposed to the proton
beam.
Despite some of the bottom requirements may sound very important for
safety reasons, they have been moved to their respective place because it was
possible to modify the beam parameters so that they would not be source of
any hazards.
4.1
The material
The most suitable candidate for what concerns the material has been found
in the aluminium alloy EN AW-5754. Along its well known metallic charac-
teristics that fulﬁl the respective requirements, special attention must be paid
for the constrain n.III. Aluminium oxide (Al2O3) is quickly formed where
its pure metal counterpart comes in contact with the atmospheric oxygen; it
forms a passivating layer of a dozen nanometers thick. This fact is a double
advantage, since it doesn’t allow the raw (and much more active) metal to
get in contact with the compounds, and because the oxide is already present
in the Martian soil simulant JSC-Mars-1 and on Mars itself.

Thermal test
Rigshospitalet’s cyclotron is very seldom used at the energies and current
extraction settings that this project was aiming for. Therefore, before com-
mitting resources and time to the complete irradiation of the targets set, it
was decided to have a test run. Such preliminary irradiation would have been
used to check that the casing design was appropriate, and that the temper-
ature of the soil would not compromise the organic compounds (table 3.3).
In case the design 1.11 would have been generating too much heat because
too thick, a second modiﬁed casing was tested as well. In this variation, the
1mm back plate was substituted with a 25µm iron foil held in by the same
1mm back plate, but hollow with a 16mm hole in the middle (design 1.12,
ﬁgure 5.1).
5.1
Resistor simulation
Following the same logic of the paragraph above, it was decided to have a
quick simulation to mimic the power dispersed by the casings.
A simple and eﬀective way to do so is to put in contact a (ﬁlled) casing
with a resistor, and to apply a voltage V so that the power dissipated from
the resistor R would be P = V 2R−1. Such electrical quantities would be
tuned to match the heat experienced by the target under the beam ﬂuence.
The simulation set-up is in many ways far from the scenario of the ir-
radiation, but it will ﬁt the purpose to have an approximate value of the
targets’ temperatures whenever the simulation will be allowed to reach an
equilibrium state. This is because most of the diﬀerences between the two
set-ups are reﬂected in the time that takes the soil to reach equilibrium.
To mention one, the heat in the simulation is transferred from the interface
30
==PAGE:38==
resistor-casing, while in reality it would be generated (almost) evenly within
the target by the energy deposition of the protons.
Because of this diﬀerence, a scenario that will not be reproduced by the sim-
ulation (nor measurable in the irradiation test itself) is the one where the
poor thermal conductivity of the soil will prohibit the latter to disperse heat
quick enough, making the soil hotter than the surrounding material (where
the probe is), and possibly exceeding one of the melting points for a short
time. Unfortunately, there isn’t a solution for a better temperature measure
that wouldn’t either compromise the sealing or include expensive equipment,
i.e. a thermal imaging camera.
The beam energy of the thermal test was ﬁrstly planned to be 17MeV .

energy deposition curve in the target and, by integrating such curve, calculate
the power dissipated P (equation 5.1).

5.3
Nuclear activity study
After about 20 minutes from the irradiation that took place May 17th
2017,
the exposed material measured over 2mSV/h at 10cm distance. Such dose
is not only dangerous for humans to work with, but also not allowed to be
transported, and let alone possible to go in a state-of-the-art measurement
equipment such as the diamond ATR machine.
It was clear that in order to proceed forward, it was needed to lower the beam
parameters to a point where the materials would have an activity comparable

7.2
Common Assumptions
The two methods for calculating the current spatial distribution share the
following assumptions.
1. The beam is not signiﬁcantly altered when it passes through the hollow
regions of the collimators.
2. Since the vertical and horizontal beam spans are very similar, it is
assumed that it is possible to describe the beam spatial distribution
through a bi-dimensional, monovariate Gaussian distribution N 2(0, σ2).
This symmetry translates to the distribution having the same variance
for both y and z. The axis x : y = 0, z = 0 is deﬁned by the average
beam velocity vector, and the yz plane is where the distribution lays.
3. The targets and the collimators were speciﬁcally manufactured to be
symmetric respect to the mean beam position: it is assumed that such
position did not vary signiﬁcantly, and that the time-dependent beam

irregularities would cancel themselves out when integrated over the
exposure time.
7.3
Method 1: Using past measurements
In the PhD thesis “Development of CdZnTe detector systems for space ap-
plications” [7] the proton beam proﬁle was characterized and measured with
dedicated measurement. The beam proﬁle was measured on the yz plane by
moving a Faraday Cup in a zig-zag motion on such plane. In that work the
exit energy of the beam was accurately measured to be (31.3 ± 0.5)MeV ,
comparable to the nominal energy of (32 ± 2)MeV used in this project. The

concern in using this method, in fact there is no certain relation between the

charged eﬀects and other electronic charge phenomena alike.

7.5
Dose equivalent reached
Whatever the results the IR spectroscopies will yield, it is relevant to know
the dose accumulated by the samples.
The knowledge about the proton total ﬂuencies acquired in section 7.3 (table
7.2) allows us to make a comparison between the damage the samples sus-
tained from the proton beam, and the one they would have been subject to
on Mars’ surface. We will introduce the quantity Teq, which stands for the
equivalent time that the compounds would require to reach the same dose
of the experiment, but on Mars. Teq will appear later with the measure unit
kiloanni [ka], where anni is latin for years, thus thousands of years.
The measurements obtained from about 300 days by the Radiation Assess-
ment Detector (RAD) on the Mars Science Laboratory’s Curiosity were used
to achieve such comparison. Its data has already been elaborated by Has-
sler in his research article [26] to get to the equivalent dose at ground level:
(0.64 ± 0.12)mSv/day.
In said article, it is stated that outside Mars’ atmosphere, protons from Solar
Energetic Particles (SEP) have a quality factor of about 1. This value would
match the experiment’s one whenever table 1.1 is used to compute the latter,
56
==PAGE:64==
in fact it is possible to show through TRIM simulations that 32MeV protons,
after losing energy in about 3cm of air and 50µm of aluminium, have a LET
in water of about 0.2 eV/Å.
However, the average quality factor of the radiation on Mars’ surface is
higher: 3.05 ± 0.30 [26]. This is due the loss of energy that particles ex-
perience passing through the atmosphere, and after they do, their energy
is low enough to have a signiﬁcant impact on their energy deposition. In
fact, particles can reach the ground only whenever their energy is more than
about 150MeV, but this value is of course inﬂuenced by the total mass of
the medium the particles traverse. For example, this means that the irra-
diative ﬂux has an inverse relationship with the daily changes of Martian
atmospheric pressure. Galactic Cosmic Rays have a second phenomena that
anti-correlates with their ﬂux, driven by the solar wind and Coronal Mass
Ejections.

Control samples
As the scientiﬁc method dictates, when researching for the eﬀects of a phe-
nomenon, one must be certain that the results are due to it and it alone.
To do so, an additional set of samples is often created with the purpose of
undergoing through each single process experienced by the main set, with
the exception of the researched phenomenon.
In this project the above statement translates in having four additional sam-
ples, one for each amino acid mixture plus one with regolith dust only, which
will not get exposed to the beam in order to be able to state that any change
observed was the product of the sole proton damage. This way, Sample G,
H, I, and J were created as well (ﬁgure 8.1).
The two sets of samples were carried together everywhere, except to the
cyclotron room. That said, what was left to accomplish the Controls’ purpose
was to be exposed to the same temperature that samples C÷F reached in
their irradiation. Design 2.0 didn’t allow for the presence of a probe socket,
therefore once again the software TRIM was used to understand the heat
generated in the samples.
The same logic used in the “resistor simulation” section (5.1) was applied
here, with the diﬀerence that this time the most signiﬁcative temperature
was the one reached by the aluminium: the soil socket was so small that its
content would have been brought to the same temperature of the surrounding
material in no time.
The expected energy deposition for 32MeV protons in the ﬁrst 1.05mm of
aluminium is about 0.4eV/Å, meaning a total loss of ∆E = 4.2MeV . This
time it will not be appropriate to consider the total current because of the
collimators, therefore we’ll only consider the heat produced by the beam that
freely passes through their 5mm central opening.

As previously mentioned across the previous chapters, a few precautions have
been taken in order to utilize the ATR machine.
Among them, the only one which impacts the IR spectroscopy is the method
used to prevent the dust from going airborne: the paraﬁlm used introduced
some peaks that may be present in every measure. As can be seen in ﬁg-
ure 9.1, the most prominent ones are at 2910cm−1, 2850cm−1, 1470cm−1,
and 720cm−1.
The blue spectrum is from the ﬁlm alone, the bordeaux
from soil “taped” down with the ﬁlm, and the green one from soil with the
ﬁlm wrapped around the pin. It is appreciable how in the green spectrum
the paraﬁlm bands are barely noticeable, but the peaks at the paraﬁlm’s
wavenumbers must still be accounted for in any computed results.
When treating the characterizations, it is important to keep in mind that
the limits of these measures are set by two facts: the inconstant background
which changes every time the vacuum chamber is open (and slower with
time as well), and the dependence of the peak height from the measured
compound’s abundance. The latter may appear to be a feasible character-
istic to exploit, but that’s not the case since the sample is heterogeneous
and there is no guarantee that the material in contact with the diamond will
reﬂect the overall composition ratio.
In the pages that follow will be illustrated the various spectra measured.
In order to detect changes in the chemical composition of the amino acids
the spectrum of the irradiated samples was subtracted with the spectrum of
their respective control sample. The trial version of the software Essential
FTIR was used to sum nd scale the spectra; each subtraction was performed
with a weighting factor in order to account for the diﬀerent composition ra-
tios that were mentioned earlier.

Chapter 10
Conclusions
After numerous restrictions and modiﬁcations, the experiment has been suc-
cessfully carried out: three mixtures of Martian soil simulant and amino acids
plus one of bare soil have been irradiated with the maximum amount of dose
that was feasible to apply through the proton beam in use.
Such compounds have then been characterized with infra-red spectroscopy,
and their molecular proﬁles have been compared to their respective control
samples.
The results show that a proton shower under this experiment’s conditions
does not impact the chemical composition of the amino acids in radi-
cal ways. Minor molecular changes have been observed for alanine and 2-
aminoisobutyric acid, but only the latter is likely to have irradiative causes.
In case the shortage of 2-aminoisobutyric acid would be actually driven by
the proton interaction, there is the chance that any trace of that amino acid
would have been consumed since the dose applied is about 2.7·10−6 times the
dose that the Martian surface experienced since the period Mars is thought
to have lost its atmosphere.
This is unlikely to be the case, since chemicals of the same family usually
react in similar ways.
Furthermore, the gamma spectroscopy did not show any sign of nuclear ac-
tivity from carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and oxygen, not directly nor from
any of their possible reaction products.
Under the experiment’s conditions, the organics are overall stable.
Improvements & future work
69
==PAGE:77==
It should come as a surprise to know that one of the reasons why the
results are so stationary is because of the many restrictions that had to be
applied to the project. Despite the already great costs and instrumentation
the author has been allowed to make use of, the facilities and resources avail-
able were not suﬃcient to accomplish the full task.
Future work may be considered to increase the dose applied to the com-
pounds, as well as reproducing ambient conditions that better reﬂect the
Martian atmosphere. It is indeed known that chemical reactions in particu-
lar are widely dependent from pressure and temperature with no exceptions.
