Abstract
6G-class Internet-of-Things (IoT) deployments will depend on on-device intelligence yet
face strict privacy and networking constraints. This thesis investigates whether federated
learning (FL) with client-side differential privacy (DP) can deliver effective anomaly de-
tection under realistic network conditions. We design a configuration-driven framework
that integrates DP-SGD into a synchronous FedAvg loop and routes model exchanges
through ns-3 scenarios—Ethernet and Wi–Fi via socketed transport—and an idealized
short-range terahertz (THz) path adapted from TeraSim. The study targets tabular intru-
sion detection on N–BaIoT and MedBIoT using a compact multilayer perceptron to keep
payloads small and DP stable.
Methodologically, we validate the end-to-end stack with an MNIST-over-Wi–Fi baseline
and THz sanity checks, then execute two factorial blocks: (A) Network×Partition (IID vs.
non-IID, DP off) and (B) Partition×DP on THz (noise multipliers 0.6 and 1.0, clip= 1). Re-
sults show that privacy-preserving FL is feasible for these workloads: Ethernet and THz
provide the shortest time-to-target, while Wi–Fi is slower due to contention and rate vari-
ability. Enabling DP produces the expected privacy–utility–latency trade-off—a modest
penalty at 0.6 and a larger one at 1.0—amplified under non-IID partitions; importantly, DP
does not reorder the network-dependent wall-clock behavior observed without DP.
The framework artifacts runs with manifests, code hashes, and per-round telemetry (ac-
curacy, time, bytes), enabling reproducibility and post-hoc privacy accounting. Within
an honest-but-curious threat model, the findings translate abstract trade-offs into mea-
surable levers—noise, clipping, client selection, and link provisioning—for smart-city de-
ployments. We outline next steps toward stateful 6G transport, adversarial robustness,
authenticated execution, and edge testbed validation.

Introduction
With ubiquitous technologies continuously advancing and becoming more integrated into
today’s society [1], digitalisation efforts [2] by nations world wide work towards establish-
ing smart cities, to further their economic and digital competitiveness [3] [4]. In this world,
emerging technologies will play a vital role in a person’s daily life, and therefore, in or-
der to ensure that the safety and safeguarding of a citizen’s individual rights to privacy
is maintained, measures must be taken to ensure privacy and data protection. This is
further emphasized with the trend of emerging technologies moving towards gathering
more sensitive data, employing data-driven artificial intelligence (AI), and a parallel emer-
gence towards more efficient and larger data transmissions will also become available,
as advances towards 6G communication networks progress [5] [6].
Within the field of AI, there exist current available techniques that show promising results
in handling and processing sensitive data effectively. Studies related to a distributed ma-
chine learning technique known as federated learning, a technique commonly combined
with privacy preserving techniques, such as differential privacy [7], aim to meet this goal
of privacy, by paving the way for advances in emerging distributed AI approaches with pri-
vacy preservation integrated into the technique. With federated learning being a technique
deployed on distributed networked applications, by evaluating its performance within the
conditions of emerging 6G networks, a framework can be established that provides valu-
able insights into the technical aspects and legislative requirements, to meet the needs
for privacy concerns. By studying this area, a greater understanding on what role privacy
in these advancements will have in future directions and challenges to come.
1.1
Motivation and Context
This thesis proposes a framework for privacy-preserving federated anomaly detection tai-
lored to resource-constrained IoT devices. The framework combines asynchronous Fed-
erated Averaging with client-side differential privacy (DP-SGD) and evaluates the learning
loop over realistic network conditions emulated in ns-3 (Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and THz/6G-class
links). The design is motivated by privacy-by-design principles (e.g., GDPR Art. 5/25),
while avoiding central collection of raw data. Using smart-city IoT as a running context,
we demonstrate the framework’s viability and derive practical design implications for 6G-
ready deployments.
1.2
Problem Statement and Scope
Future 6G IoT deployments will connect large numbers of resource-constrained devices
that continuously generate sensitive telemetry. Centralising such data to train intrusion
detectors is often infeasible due to privacy, governance, and bandwidth limits. Federated
learning (FL) mitigates this by training on devices, but two questions remain open for a
deployable, privacy-preserving intrusion-detection pipeline: (i) how to integrate privacy
mechanisms at the client without breaking the FL loop, and (ii) what utility and latency
costs those mechanisms impose once realistic networks are in the path. Concretely, this
thesis asks:
• RQ1: how can privacy-preserving techniques be integrated into a federated learn-
ing–based anomaly-detection framework for 6G IoT?
• RQ2: what trade-offs do such techniques impose between data confidentiality and
detection effectiveness?.

We state these formally in Chapter 4. This thesis fixes the two research questions: RQ1
asks how client-side differential privacy (DP-SGD) can be integrated end to end in an FL
anomaly-detection framework suitable for 6G IoT, RQ2 asks what trade-offs arise between
confidentiality and detection effectiveness under realistic network conditions.
Scope boundaries are explicit. We study tabular IoT intrusion detection on N–BaIoT and
MedBIoT using a compact supervised MLP. Privacy is provided by client-side DP-SGD
(clipping and Gaussian noise before transmission). The FL server runs asynchronous
FedAvg with polynomial staleness weighting. Model updates traverse ns–3 Ethernet and
Wi-Fi via socketed stacks, a short-range THz/6G case is realised as a stateless emulation
that returns deterministic service time per payload. The default experiment scale is 10
clients with 2 selected per round and 20 global rounds. We assume an honest-but-curious
aggregator, do not implement secure aggregation or multiparty computation, do not model
adversarial clients or mobility, and do not claim packet-true THz transport. Reproducibility
is a requirement: all runs are manifest-driven and archived with per-round telemetry.
1.3
Approach and Contribution
We take an empirical systems–ML approach. First, we implement a configuration-driven
framework that couples a lightweight, asynchronous FedAvg loop and client-side DP-
SGD (Opacus) with ns–3 network scenarios. Ethernet and Wi-Fi use socketed stacks so
each update experiences queueing and contention, the THz option is a parameterised,
stateless emulation that provides an optimistic short-range 6G bound. For Ethernet/Wi-Fi,
the payload is the serialized model update (delta or full weights) sent over ns–3 sockets,
so measured bytes and time reflect the actual payload and medium behaviour. For THz,
we use the same payload size to schedule a PHY-informed transfer duration (no packet-
level transport).
Second, we report response metrics that capture the trade-off of interest: accuracy per
round (when evaluation is enabled), time-to-0.90 (t90) using a cumulative-best envelope
for asynchronous runs, and bytes per round. We log DP accountant inputs (sampling
rate, steps) to allow optional ex-post (ε, δ) reporting, but we do not rely on it for the main
results.
Third, we execute two compact factorial blocks per dataset: Block A varies network
medium under IID vs non-IID with DP off. Block B fixes THz and varies DP level un-
der IID vs non-IID. Hyperparameters, packetisation, and cadence are held constant to
attribute effects to the intended factors.
Contributions.
• A reproducible FL + network framework that enforces client-side DP-SGD and mea-
sures utility, wall-time, and bytes with packet-level realism for Ethernet/Wi-Fi and an
optimistic THz bound.
• A declarative experiment design (manifests) that binds datasets, partitions, privacy,
and network parameters into auditable packs.
• An empirical characterisation of privacy–utility–latency on N–BaIoT and MedBIoT
across IID/non-IID and Ethernet/Wi-Fi/THz, we summarise time-to-target and com-
munication cost, and where computed, (ε, δ).
• Implementation notes for integrating a stateless THz path into ns–3 and for cluster
orchestration that stabilises timing (sequential arrays, thread caps, archived arte-
facts).

Background and Preliminaries
The following chapter aims to establish the theoretical and technical foundation needed
for the remainder of this thesis. The goal is to cover all key concepts for sufficient under-
standing over essential content covered. It includes a general background and a high-
level mention of related work to contextualize the study.
2.1
6G Networks and IoT Environments
6G is framed as the IMT-2030 era of mobile systems and is expected to extend and
generalize 5G capabilities along three axes: higher peak rates, tighter latency/reliability,
and native integration of intelligence across the radio, edge, and core [6, 5]. Proposals in-
clude using spectrum well above 100 GHz into the lower terahertz (THz) range with highly
directional links, large antenna arrays, and new propagation/beam management tech-
niques [8]. Peak throughputs in the terabit-per-second (Tbps) class and sub-millisecond
air-interface latencies are frequently cited targets for short-range links, with end-to-end
service performance depending on scheduling, transport, and edge compute placement
[6, 5, 8].
Architecturally, 6G roadmaps point to space–air–ground integrated networks, reconfig-
urable intelligent surfaces, and AI-native control planes for resource allocation and mobil-
ity [5, 6]. At THz, very high bandwidth coexists with fragile, alignment-sensitive channels,
beams are narrow, ranges are short, and blockage is critical, which motivates directional
MACs and careful link budgeting [8]. For research and prototyping, ns-3 provides packet-
level realism for sub-6/5G-class Ethernet and Wi-Fi paths, while THz can be studied with
TeraSim-style models to capture directional parameters and service times [9].
Massive IoT remains a first-class 6G use case. Densities on the order of 106 devices per
square kilometer and diverse QoS classes (mMTC and URLLC) are assumed to continue
and expand, spanning smart city sensing, industrial automation, and healthcare [6, 5]. In
this setting, on-device intelligence and edge offloading are emphasized to keep latency
bounded and data local, federated learning is a natural fit for training models without
centralizing raw telemetry [10, 11].
IoT environments impose tight constraints that shape system design. Many nodes have
limited compute and memory, rely on batteries or energy harvesting, and connect intermit-
tently over contention-prone media (e.g., dense Wi-Fi) alongside heterogeneous radios
and stacks. High device density magnifies medium access contention and queueing.
From a security and governance perspective, continuous telemetry can include sensitive
inferences (e.g., behavioral or biometric signals), so data minimization and privacy-by-
design are required [12, 13]. For learning systems this implies small models or compact
updates, communication-efficient training schedules, and privacy mechanisms that act
at the client boundary (e.g., client-side DP-SGD) so confidentiality does not depend on
transport.
In summary, the 6G/IoT context assumed here is: packet-level Ethernet/Wi-Fi realism
for general connectivity, an optimistic short-range THz bound for high-capacity backhaul,
and a learning stack that keeps computation light at the edge while enforcing privacy
before transmission. This motivates our use of ns-3 sockets for Ethernet/Wi-Fi and a
parameterized THz emulation, and it aligns with our choice of compact tabular models
and client-side differential privacy in later chapters [14, 9, 15, 16, 17, 18].

IoT operations and constraints in 6G environments
6G is positioned to extend the 5G family of capabilities with higher carrier frequencies,
tighter integration of edge computing, and service classes that combine extreme through-
put with low and bounded latency. In this setting, IoT deployments will span tiny, battery-
powered sensors, industrial controllers, and mobile platforms such as vehicles and drones.
Standards work (IMT-2030) and industry roadmaps emphasize massive device densities,
reliability for time-critical control, and pervasive intelligence at the network edge, with pri-
vacy and security treated as first-class requirements [6, 5, 12].
Despite network-side advances, individual IoT nodes remain resource constrained. Many
devices offer limited compute and memory, tight energy budgets, and intermittent connec-
tivity, which makes on-device analytics and security non-trivial and pushes computation
toward nearby edge servers when possible [1, 5]. At the system level, high device den-
sity and continuous sensing produce large data volumes, moving all raw data to the core
is neither economical nor privacy-preserving. Workloads also diverge: some applica-
tions tolerate sporadic low-rate traffic, while others require low-latency and high-reliability
streams (for example, control loops and safety functions), so the network must serve

heterogeneous QoS targets concurrently [6, 12]. Heterogeneity in hardware, operating
systems, radios, and protocol stacks further complicates interoperability and the uniform

These constraints motivate edge intelligence patterns in which models are trained or
adapted close to where data originate. Federated learning fits this trajectory by keeping

net, Wi-Fi, and an optimistic short-range THz emulation) make latency and contention
visible to the learning loop, reflecting the operational realities of 6G-class IoT [14, 9].
2.2
Deep-Learning Foundations
This section provides a concise primer on neural networks and deep learning fundamen-
tals. The purpose is to equip the reader by reviewing the essential principles of deep
learning that underpin federated learning and differential privacy mechanisms and imple-
mentations, further elaborated later in this thesis. Following the introduction to neural
network components, the typical training loop will have been outlined:
1. Forward propagation: input features are transformed layer-by-layer to produce a
prediction.
2. Loss computation: a task-specific loss function quantifies the discrepancy be-
tween prediction and ground truth.
3. Backpropagation: gradients of the loss with respect to each trainable parameter
are computed via the chain rule.
4. Optimisation step: an optimiser (e.g., stochastic gradient descent) updates the
weights and biases using these gradients.
Subsequent subsections will provide the mathematical rigor required to understand gra-
dient clipping and differential privacy mechanisms applied to this fundamental training
loop.
2.2.1
Fundamentals of Neural Networks
A neural network is a graph representation of a network of neurons. Inspired by biologi-
cal neuronal networks, by structure and function, these artificial neural networks (ANN),
also called multi-layer perceptrons (MLP), describe a type of computational system that
comprised of interconnecting nodes called ”neurons”/”perceptrons”, and are organized
in layers for processing data. This computational system is widely used as a machine
learning modeling technique, for recognizing patterns, predicting outcomes and solving
complex tasks, when used in application.
Model Parameters: Weights and Biases
The fundamental parameters that determine the neural networks ability predict outcomes
are weights w and biases b. The output of a neuron is the numeric value denoted as a,
is called the activation, and the connecting edges that connect these neurons are called
weights w, see figure 2.1 depicting the neuron’s structure.

Federated Learning Fundamentals
Federated learning (FL) trains a single global model across many clients while keeping
raw data on-device. A server maintains parameters θ, invites clients to perform local
optimisation on their private data, and updates θ using the returned client updates. This
reduces central data exposure and suits large-scale 6G–IoT deployments where data
are distributed, bandwidth is limited, and privacy is a concern. (Privacy is not automatic:
gradients can leak information; the differentially private mechanisms used later in this
thesis address that risk.)

Participation and scheduling (asynchronous)
Partial participation is the default (|St| ≪K). Clients are invited continuously; the server
does not wait for a cohort to finish. Scheduling can bias toward low-latency or high-loss
clients; in our experiments we use an asynchronous policy with polynomial staleness
compensation.
Learning-rate policies (client side)
Learning-rate schedules (constant, step decay, cosine with warmup, etc.) are local and
should depend on the client’s own step count, not wall-clock time, since clients progress
at different speeds.
Non-IID data and stability
Non-IID data can cause drift. Stabilisation options include fewer local epochs E, smaller
local η, proximal regularisation (FedProx), server learning-rate λ < 1, and staleness
weighting ω(s). In this work we control drift via E, η, λ, and ω(·).
Security by design (what FL does and does not provide)
FL keeps raw samples on device, reducing central exposure; however, updates can still
leak information. We therefore employ per-example gradient clipping and Gaussian noise
(DP-SGD) to achieve (ε, δ)-DP at the client boundary. Secure aggregation can further
protect updates in transit but is not required for the privacy guarantees we report.

2.4
Anomaly Detection in IoT Systems
Anomaly detection for IoT is the task of identifying departures from the statistical regulari-
ties of benign device behavior. In 6G style deployments, devices are numerous, resource
constrained, and often send encrypted payloads, so useful signals come from headers
and timing rather than deep payload inspection. This makes local feature construction
and communication efficient training central to any practical design.
In this thesis the problem is framed as binary classification on fixed length feature vectors
x ∈Rd built from header and timing telemetry (for example packet and byte counts, rates,
and simple interarrival statistics). Labels are binarized as benign versus malicious by
collapsing attack families on N-BaIoT and MedBIoT. Numeric features are standardized
by z score using statistics fit on the training fold only and applied unchanged at evaluation
so that scales are comparable without leaking test information.
The detector is a compact multilayer perceptron. Shallow, fully connected architectures
capture nonlinear interactions among aggregates at low computational cost and with small
parameter counts. This choice keeps inference feasible on constrained clients and keeps
model updates small, which matters when training is distributed and the network is in the
loop.
Training follows a federated regime that reflects the privacy and networking constraints of
IoT. Clients optimize locally and the server aggregates updates asynchronously using a
staleness aware variant of Federated Averaging. To enforce confidentiality at the source,
clients apply differentially private stochastic gradient descent with per sample clipping and
Gaussian noise before any update is transmitted. All model exchanges traverse an ns-3
path so that Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and a parameterized short range THz class option contribute
realistic latency and contention to end to end learning time.
Evaluation focuses on learning dynamics and communication cost rather than single shot
scores. We record accuracy once per global round together with bytes sent and received
and wall clock since the start. Summaries report final accuracy at a fixed round budget,
time to a target accuracy threshold using a cumulative best envelope for asynchronous
runs, area under the accuracy versus time curve, and average bytes per round. These
choices allow the reader to interpret the privacy, utility, and latency trade offs studied in
later chapters without assuming central access to raw telemetry.

2.6
The CIA model and its CIAAN expansion
The classic CIA triad frames information assurance around three goals: confidential-
ity (preventing unauthorized disclosure), integrity (preventing unauthorized modification),
and availability (ensuring timely access for authorized users). In practice, these are real-
ized with controls such as encryption and access control for confidentiality, hashing and
digital signatures for integrity, and redundancy and fault tolerance for availability [12, 19].
Many works extend the triad with authenticity and non-repudiation, yielding CIAAN. Au-
thenticity verifies that an entity or message is genuine (e.g., via authentication protocols
or certificates). Non-repudiation binds actions so that their origin cannot be credibly de-
nied (e.g., via digital signatures and audit logs) [12, 20]. Regulatory guidance in the EU
complements these pillars with privacy by design and by default [21, 22].
In this thesis, CIAAN serves as a framing device, not a full threat-model evaluation. Our
experiments directly measure confidentiality costs by enforcing client-side DP-SGD before
transmission, integrity and availability are observed insofar as clipped-and-noised training
converges under realistic network conditions, authenticity and non-repudiation are noted
as deployment concerns (e.g., mTLS and tamper-evident logs) but are not implemented
in the simulator.
2.6.1
Trade-offs Among Security Goals
Security goals often conflict or impose trade-offs - improving one can degrade another.
As the adage goes, ”there are no solutions, only trade-offs.”
Achieving an optimal balance among confidentiality, integrity, availability (and the ex-
tended goals) requires careful risk management. Some common trade-offs include:
Confidentiality vs. availability
Stronger confidentiality (tight access control, encryption, multi-factor auth, client-side pri-
vacy) can add latency or reduce fault tolerance, while maximizing availability (wide replica-
tion, simplified access) increases the attack surface. In time-critical domains this tension
is obvious: stronger privacy controls can delay legitimate access. looser controls improve
responsiveness but risk exposure. In our setting, client-side DP–SGD enforces confiden-
tiality before transmission and, as observed in the results, increases time–to–target at
higher noise levels (the privacy–latency cost), whereas disabling DP reduces that delay.

Authenticity vs. privacy
Authenticating devices and attributing updates require some notion of identity, but col-

2.6.2
Data Confidentiality and Privacy
Protecting sensitive telemetry is a first-order requirement in AIoT. Devices continually pro-
duce data that can reveal personal or operational details, and leakage can occur both in
transit and via model updates [24, 23]. In this thesis, confidentiality is enforced at the client
boundary: per-sample gradients are clipped and Gaussian noise is added via DP-SGD
before any update is serialized and sent over the emulator [15, 16, 18]. Only privatized
updates traverse ns-3, raw features never leave the device. We log the accountant in-
puts (clip norm, noise multiplier, sampling rate, steps) so that ε(δ) can be computed post
hoc and reported beside utility and latency. This design aligns with data-minimization
goals and reduces exposure to gradient inversion risks documented for vanilla FL [25,
26]. Transport encryption and secure aggregation are not implemented here, traffic termi-
nates inside the simulator and confidentiality is provided by local DP. Adding authenticated
channels or secure aggregation would further reduce exposure but introduces CPU and
byte overheads that should be measured against the same accuracy–time–bytes metrics
in future work [27, 28].
2.6.3
Integrity of Models and Data
Integrity failures in IoT appear at two layers: corrupted data streams (e.g., forged sensor
readings) and corrupted learning dynamics (e.g., poisoning or Byzantine clients). The
latter has been widely catalogued for FL [29, 30, 31]. Our prototype assumes an honest-
but-curious aggregator and does not implement robust aggregation or adversarial filtering,
poisoning resistance is therefore out of scope and listed as future work. In practice, in-
tegrity hardening would combine (i) device and update authentication, (ii) robust statistics
at the server (median, trimmed mean, Krum), and (iii) simple update-level checks (mag-
nitude, cosine similarity, or replay tests) before aggregation [30, 31]. Code and model
integrity on devices (secure boot, signed firmware) are orthogonal but necessary con-
trols, they were not simulated. The immediate contribution here is to quantify how privacy
(DP noise) and network conditions affect convergence timing under non-adversarial as-
sumptions, integrity defenses can be layered and evaluated with the same telemetry.
2.6.4
Availability and Resilience
Availability degrades under network contention, protocol overheads, and adversarial pres-
sure (e.g., DDoS on edge nodes or update spam that prevents convergence) [19]. Our
ns-3 results isolate the non-adversarial side: Ethernet and THz deliver shorter time-to-
target than Wi-Fi, DP noise increases wall-clock to a useful model in a predictable way
(Ch. 8). We do not simulate jamming, DDoS, or client churn beyond participation set-
tings, resilience to these events is future work. Practical mitigations include redundancy

With many heterogeneous nodes, identities must be verified and limited privileges en-

3.3.1
Taxonomy of Known Attacks
Depending on the threat model, the attack surface may vary and consequently influence
the type of attacks that may be considered in an analysis. With the landscape introduc-
ing adversaries that may be honest-but-curious actors that passively observe protocol-
compliant data in order and infer private patterns from received gradients, or malicious
clients that craft updates with the goal of probing for sensitive data. Researchers have
generally categorized three primary privacy attack types in FL under these models [35, 7,
29, 50].
3.3.2
Data Reconstruction (Gradient Inversion)
Data reconstruction attacks describe attacks where an adversary, even without direct
access to raw data, can attempt to reconstruct a client’s input from gathered model up-
dates (gradients). Prior work has demonstrated that captured shared gradients can re-
veal highly detailed sensitive data by exploiting optimization techniques [25, 26]. The
data reconstructed through gradient inversion showed that it was possible to recover im-
ages at high fidelity from the gradients of model parameters, breaking the intended pri-
vacy introduced by FL [26]. Similar attacks have also been replicated on deep networks,
and even when gradients are averaged over small mini-batches, reconstruction can re-
main feasible (though increasingly challenging as batch size grows) [25, 26, 51]. More
recent works have further improved effectiveness, including reconstructions beyond im-
ages (e.g., token-wise text recovery) and stronger attacks against modern architectures
[25, 51].
3.3.3
Membership Inference
Membership inference attacks aim to determine whether a specific data sample was a
part of a client’s private training set, such as a patient’s hospital data in model training
can be sensitive, e.g. the sample data reveals that a patient has cancer, and it comes
from this client [40, 7].
24
A Federated Learning Framework for Privacy-Preserving Anomaly Detection in 6G IoT Environments
==PAGE:33==
The data itself isn’t reconstructed, but the mere inclusion of a person’s data in model
training can be sensitive. In the study by [41], the authors found that a curious central
server in FL could correctly infer training set membership with great accuracy [40, 7, 29].
Even participating clients that have only access to the global model along with their own
updates could achieve high accuracy in inferring another client’s data membership [41].
Nasr underscores that even well-generalized deep models might leak significant informa-
tion about their training data. The repeated model update exchanges in FL can amplify
this leakage [41, 35, 29].
3.3.4
Property Inference
Beyond specific data points, an adversary might infer aggregate or secondary properties
of another client’s dataset that are not related to the main task. For instance, [42] show that
an adversarial participant can infer properties that hold only for a subset of another party’s
training data (independent of the task label), such as identifying when a particular person
first appears in photos used to train a binary gender classifier. More broadly, prior work
on model/attribute inference demonstrates that sensitive attributes can be inferred from
models or their updates even when those attributes are not targets of the learning task
[52]. Surveys corroborate that property/attribute inference in collaborative and federated
settings can reveal sensitive attributes (e.g., health status, demographics, writing style)
from gradients or model parameters, raising privacy concerns even without reconstructing
raw inputs [7, 29, 50, 35].
3.4
Defence Mechanisms
In response to these threats, a range of defense mechanisms have been developed in
both academia and industry. Each comes with trade-offs in accuracy, efficiency, or as-
sumptions. The following sections review current methods for privacy defense strategies
in FL.
3.4.1
Differential Privacy in FL
This is a mathematical framework that adds calibrated random noise to data or model
updates to provide probabilistic privacy guarantees [15, 16, 17].
Google researchers implemented DP-FL for Gboard’s next-word prediction models —
the first production FL deployment with a formal DP guarantee (reported using zCDP with
parameter ρ ≈0.81) [53, 54, 55], meaning the model training on ∼3 billion users’ data
has a quantifiable privacy loss budget.
Academic works have similarly integrated DP into FL protocols and reported that it ”makes
it possible to train models on private data distributed across users’ devices without harm-
ing privacy” [56].
Downside: DP inevitably adds noise, which can degrade model accuracy if the privacy
budget is strict. There is a privacy-utility trade-off, and finding the right balance (often via
techniques like large cohort sizes, secure aggregation, and careful tuning of noise) is an
active research area [28, 10].
3.4.2
Secure Aggregation
To prevent the central server (or anyone intercepting updates) from seeing the individual
client updates in plaintext, secure multiparty computation protocols have been devised
for FL. A prominent approach is secure aggregation (SecAgg) by Bonawitz et al. (2017)
[27], which allows the server to only obtain the sum of all client updates, but not any single
client’s update [27, 10, 20].

==PAGE:34==
In essence, clients cryptographically mask their updates (e.g. with one-time pads or ho-
momorphic encryption) and the masks cancel out when aggregated, revealing only the
aggregated model update. This means an honest-but-curious server cannot inspect any
one client’s gradient [27, 20].
Secure aggregation has been deployed in Google’s FL infrastructure to ensure even the
server cannot peek at a single phone’s update in e.g. the training of Google Assistant
models [57].
3.4.3
Trusted Execution and Homomorphic Encryption
An extension of the cryptographic approach is to perform the entire model update aggre-
gation (or even training) on encrypted data [10, 20].
Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) allows computation on ciphertexts — for exam-
ple, the server could aggregate encrypted gradients and even update an encrypted global
model, which only clients can decrypt. This provides strong privacy (the server learns
nothing about the values it’s summing) [10, 20]. However, current FHE techniques are
computationally heavy, and FHE in FL is typically limited to simple aggregation or small
models [10, 29, 20].
An alternative is using Trusted Execution Environments (TEE) like Intel SGX: the ag-
gregation (and even training) happens in a secure enclave on the server, which aims to
provide cryptographic isolation of raw data from the server’s operating system [10, 20].
Techniques like robust aggregation, outlier detection, and secure enclaves can help main-
tain integrity without fully exposing data [30, 31, 29, 20].
These approaches can be impractical for large-scale or cross-device FL due to perfor-
mance overheads and trust assumptions (e.g., reliance on hardware vendors), and TEEs
have had documented vulnerabilities [10, 20, 29].
3.4.4
Robust Aggregation / Poisoning Defences
Some defenses specifically tackle active attacks (like poisoning or malicious updates)
which, while more of a security issue, can indirectly help privacy by identifying anomalous
gradients [29, 20]. For example, outlier detection on updates or robust aggregation rules
(median, trimmed mean, etc.) can limit how much influence a single client’s update has
[30, 31].
This doesn’t prevent inference leakage per se, but can mitigate worst-case scenarios
where an attacker manipulates updates to extract information (as shown by Nasr et al.’s
active attack where a malicious client pushes the global model to magnify certain influ-
ences) [41].

emphasize that as data flows among producers, processors, and consumers across the
6G stack, sensitive information can be exposed at multiple stages absent strong safe-

could be mounted on models deployed at the edge that were trained on private IoT data,
echoing concerns from FL but now in a 6G context [52, 40, 41, 7, 29].

overhead, though these methods can be environment-dependent or probabilistic [59, 58].

IoT endpoints can conflict with power and cost constraints, IoT devices are often designed
to be low-cost and energy-efficient, so expecting them to run complex intrusion detection
or anti-malware might be unrealistic [13]. One mitigation in a 6G scenario is to offload
some security monitoring to edge servers or to use AI to detect anomalies in traffic patterns

and data minimization is crucial—collecting only what is needed for the service—which
is consistent with privacy-by-design guidance highlighted in 6G position and framework
documents [5, 6, 12].
3.5.8
Trade-offs in Federated Learning
Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning approach designed to address
data confidentiality concerns by keeping raw data on devices and exchanging only model
updates [10, 39]. In FL, multiple clients collaboratively train a global model under a coor-
dinating server, improving privacy by avoiding centralized raw data aggregation while still
enabling learning. This paradigm has been adopted in production settings (e.g., mobile
keyboard modeling) and combined with differential privacy to further bound disclosure risk
[54, 53]. By not sharing raw data, FL inherently reduces large-scale data exposure, but
it introduces new trade-offs among confidentiality, integrity, availability, and utility: com-
munication overhead and stragglers can slow training, privacy mechanisms (e.g., DP) can
reduce accuracy if budgets are strict, and robustness measures (e.g., secure aggregation,
TEEs) add complexity and cost [10, 20, 27].
Confidentiality/Privacy vs. Utility
Keeping data on devices preserves privacy, but it can reduce model performance ”utilitiy”
if not carefully managed. Clients may have limited data (affecting accuracy) or non-IID
(imbalanced) data that complicates training. Moreover, recent research shows that even
sharing model updates can leak information about the underlying data (through inference
attacks) [25, 26, 40]. Defense techniques like differential privacy (adding noise to updates)
or secure aggregation (encrypting updates so the server only sees an aggregate) improve
privacy at the cost of model accuracy or efficiency [28, 15, 27]. In fact, [28] argues

it is unlikely to simultaneously achieve excellent privacy, high model accuracy, and high

Integrity vs. Privacy
In a distributed setting, ensuring the integrity of the global model is challenging because
some clients or the server could be malicious. FL is vulnerable to poisoning attacks
- malicious clients can corrupt model updates to mis-train the model (compromising in-
tegrity) [29]. Detecting and preventing such tampering often requires analyzing updates
for anomalies. But overly aggressive integrity monitoring might involve inspecting client
updates in detail, potentially risking privacy if those updates reveal information about lo-
cal data. Thus, FL developers must find a balance between validating contributions for
integrity and preserving the confidentiality of those contributions. Techniques like robust
aggregation, outlier detection, and secure enclaves (Trusted Execution Environments)
can help maintain integrity without fully exposing data [30, 31, 20].
Availability vs Confidentiality
Federated learning systems also contend with availability issues. clients are often edge
devices (smartphones, IoT devices) that may drop out or have intermittent connectivity.
Ensuring the availability of the training service (e.g. that the global model still updates
even if some fraction of clients go offline) is critical [57]. Yet measures to improve avail-
ability (such as involving more clients per round or relaxing security checks to accept
updates faster) could introduce more privacy risk. For instance, allowing any available
client to join training improves fault tolerance but if those clients are not authenticated,
it could open doors to Sybil attacks (many fake clients degrading the model)—a hit to
integrity and authenticity. Conversely, requiring strong client authentication and vetting
improves security but might exclude or delay legitimate participants, affecting the time-
liness of model updates. Federation protocols thus juggle network efficiency, client
selection and secure update propogation to keep the system both available and secure.
Relatedly, large IoT fleets have historically been co-opted into botnets (e.g., Mirai/BASH-
LITE), threatening service availability via DDoS [37], securing endpoints and monitoring
traffic are recommended in 6G/IoT surveys [13, 58].
Authenticity vs Non-repudiation in FL
Authenticating each client update is a newer facet of federated learning security. In cross-
device FL (like mobile phones), devices may not have persistent identities, but the server
still needs to ensure updates come from genuine devices (not a malicious emulator). This
often relies on platform security (e.g., devices signing updates with keys or using secure
hardware) and production FL system mechanisms for client eligibility and attestation [57,
10]. In cross-silo FL (e.g., multiple hospitals collaborating on a model), authenticated
identities and provenance become important for accountability. For example, if a partic-
ipating hospital submits an anomalous update that poisons the model, having a crypto-
graphic record (digital signature) of that update supports non-repudiation. Ensuring au-
thenticity in FL typically involves secure authentication protocols and device attestation,
and some works explore blockchain-based designs. In blockchain-integrated FL, an im-
mutable ledger records contributions to provide verifiable history and non-repudiation [34,
33]. While this can improve trust (tamper-evident logs of who contributed what), it also
introduces overhead and may raise privacy concerns if contributions are linkable on-chain
[34, 33].

3.8
6G and Terahertz Communication for FL
6G networks promise orders-of-magnitude improvements in data rates and latency, partly
through using new spectrum like millimeter-wave and terahertz frequencies [12, 19, 11,
8]. Terahertz (THz) band (0.1–10 THz) communication is envisioned to support multi-
gigabit to terabit-per-second wireless links in short range (a few meters) [8, 48]. This
could be transformative for bandwidth-heavy applications such as FL model aggregation.
We review key characteristics of THz wireless and how they are modeled:
High Path Loss & Molecular Absorption: THz signals face extremely high free-space
path loss, and unique to this band, significant attenuation from atmospheric molecules
(water vapor, oxygen) that absorb energy at certain frequencies [8, 48]. The channel
thus tends to be frequency-selective with absorption “peaks” where almost no signal gets
through [36]. Effective communication requires either very short distances or selecting
frequencies with lower absorption.
Directional Antennas: To overcome path loss, THz systems use high-gain, highly direc-
tional antennas [8, 48]. Narrow beams (often pencil-like) concentrate energy but necessi-
tate precise alignment between transmitter and receiver. Node mobility or misalignment
can break the link, so beam steering/tracking is a big research topic. TeraSim models
directional antennas within its THz PHY/channel framework [36].
Propagation Range: In nanoscale THz networks (like on-chip or tiny IoT devices), range
might be centimeters, in macroscale (e.g., device-to-device in room), a few meters might
be practical at high data rate [8]. TeraSim provides a THz channel/PHY model suitable
for evaluating such short-range scenarios [36].
MAC and Transport Considerations: The extremely high data rates and short range
of THz links mean that traditional MAC protocols (like CSMA/CA for Wi-Fi) may be ineffi-
cient—directional links reduce interference but also require coordination for beam align-
ment.
Many THz studies and simulators, including TeraSim, emphasize the physical
channel and often assume a simplified MAC for single-link or TDMA-like access [36, 8].
This is a limitation: current models don’t fully capture medium-access contention in a
multi-device THz network. Similarly, transport protocols may need tweaks (e.g., shorter
packets or specialized error control for THz). Our use of TeraSim in FL will abstract away
detailed MAC, effectively simulating a best-case scenario of a stable, high-throughput
THz link without interference.
In summary, TeraSim is an open-source ns-3 extension for THz networking [36]. It im-
plements a THz channel model that accounts for molecular absorption (following estab-
lished THz propagation formulations) [36] and provides a playground to test higher-layer
protocols on top of it [36]. By using TeraSim, we treat the THz link as an idealized 6G up-
link/downlink for FL: very high bandwidth, minimal latency, but only viable at short range
(which is acceptable in, e.g., an in-home or on-premise scenario). Comparing this to

3.9
Differential Privacy in Practice for FL
Differential Privacy, while theoretically appealing, brings practical challenges in tuning
and interpretation. Local DP-SGD (which we implement via Opacus) requires configuring
a per-sample clipping norm and noise multiplier [15, 18]. Common practices from prior
work include:
Clipping Norm: A small clip (e.g. 1.0 or even 0.1) bounds the impact of any single
training example’s gradient [15]. If set too low, many gradients get flattened to the same
norm, hurting convergence, too high and it hardly provides privacy. A grid search over
clip values is often recommended in practical DP training workflows [18, 55].
Noise Multiplier: Determines the standard deviation of Gaussian noise added relative
to the clip. A higher noise multiplier yields stronger privacy (lower ε) but more accuracy
loss [15, 66]. In production on-device language modeling and FL settings, teams report
that tuning the noise/clip trade-off is critical for utility, and that moving to stricter user-level
DP (smaller ε) can reduce top-line accuracy unless compensated with additional training
or model changes [55, 54]. For simpler models on tabular data (like our IoT anomaly
detection MLP), the utility loss may be less severe at moderate noise – one of our goals
is to quantify this trade-off.
Privacy Accounting: Modern approaches use the Moments Accountant or RDP (Rényi
DP) to track ε across many gradient steps with subsampling [15, 16]. A nuance in FL is
that if only a fraction of clients participate each round, the effective sampling rate for DP
accounting is that fraction. Client-level DP formulations and accounting choices for feder-
ated settings are discussed in prior work [62, 17, 16]. In our experiments, we simulate a
fixed number of clients (e.g., 10 or 20) participating every round (synchronous FedAvg),
so the sampling probability per round is 1 (if all clients participate) or a fixed fraction for
partial participation.
Utility vs. Privacy on IID vs. non-IID: Prior studies have found that non-IID data makes
DP’s impact worse: if some clients’ data distributions are unique, noise can disproportion-
ately affect their contributions, causing higher variance in model updates. Techniques like
grouping or per-client adaptive noise have been explored, but in general there is a known
tension between data heterogeneity and privacy budget allocation [28, 10]. We will ob-
serve this in our non-IID vs IID partition experiments under DP.
By grounding our DP implementation in these practical insights, we ensure that our eval-
uation of DP-FL is aligned with known best practices. We will report not only final model

3.10
Federated Learning for IoT Anomaly Detection
IoT anomaly detection (particularly intrusion or malware detection in network traffic) is a
natural application for FL: many IoT devices (security cameras, sensors, etc.) generate
data that can indicate cyberattacks (e.g., a device infected by malware might produce
abnormal traffic patterns). Due to privacy and bandwidth constraints, it may be infeasible
to stream all this raw data to a central server, so learning a joint anomaly detection model
via FL is appealing [49, 10]. Two prominent IoT security datasets used in literature are
N-BaIoT and MedBIoT, which we also adopt.
N-BaIoT (Network-Based IoT Anomaly Detection Dataset): Released by Meidan et al.
(2018) [37], this dataset contains traffic logs from 9 different IoT devices (e.g., baby mon-
itor, thermostat) under normal operation and under attack from the Mirai or BASHLITE
botnets. It provides 115 derived features (packet  and flow level statistics), which are
sometimes reduced via PCA (e.g., to 23 principal components) in downstream studies
[37]. The original study trained a deep autoencoder on benign traffic from each device
and used reconstruction error to detect anomalies, reporting near-perfect detection on
several devices [37]. Subsequent works have treated N-BaIoT as a supervised classi-
fication problem (benign vs. attack) or an unsupervised anomaly detection problem. In
a federated setting, a common approach is to assign each physical device’s data to a
different client (since each IoT device naturally generates its own data) [49]. This means
the data is non-IID across clients, as each device has unique normal behavior. Despite
this heterogeneity, published FL experiments have reported performance comparable to
centralized baselines on N-BaIoT under reasonable settings [49]. The dataset’s limita-
tion is scale: only 9 clients max and fairly small data volume, which is why researchers
later sought larger-scale IoT datasets [38]. Unless otherwise stated, we use the full 115
engineered features from N-BaIoT (PCA variants discussed in Chapter 6).
MedBIoT: Introduced by Guerra-Manzanares et al. (2020) [38], MedBIoT is a more ex-
tensive IoT botnet dataset focusing on medical IoT scenarios. It contains traffic from a
testbed of 3 real devices and 80 virtual devices, under Mirai, BASHLITE, and Torii at-
tacks [38]. Unlike N-BaIoT, which already provided engineered features, MedBIoT may
require custom feature extraction from raw traffic. It is used to evaluate malware de-
tection methods needing more diversity in device types (including healthcare devices).
Fewer FL papers have explicitly used MedBIoT so far, but it serves as a good secondary
benchmark to test generalization—e.g., training a federated model on N-BaIoT devices
and testing on MedBIoT, or vice versa, to see if the model can detect similar botnets in
different environments.
Model architectures: For anomaly detection, two main deep learning approaches are
common: (1) Autoencoders (AE)—unsupervised models that learn to reconstruct benign
traffic and flag large reconstruction errors as anomalies [37], and (2) Classifiers (e.g.,
MLP or CNN)—trained on labeled data to classify benign vs. various attack types [49].
Autoencoders were used in the original N-BaIoT paper and many follow-ups, often achiev-
ing high AUC/F1 on several devices [37]. Classifiers (like a simple feed-forward network
on features) can also perform strongly given labeled attack data [49]. In our project, we
opt for a compact MLP classifier for each client (due to ease of federated averaging and
A Federated Learning Framework for Privacy-Preserving Anomaly Detection in 6G IoT Environments

straightforward DP integration). This choice is guided by prior work showing that even a
small neural network (2–3 hidden layers) can reach strong detection accuracy on these
feature-based IoT datasets [37, 49], and it keeps the model payload size small (important
for network simulation).
Non-IID Partitioning: A key consideration is how data is split across clients. The default
real-world split for N-BaIoT is device-wise: each client gets all data from one IoT device.
This is a form of label/distribution skew—each client sees both benign and attack traffic,
but only from its device’s perspective (which may have device-specific patterns). Alter-
natively, one could partition data by time or attack type. Research on FL has identified
that non-IID data can slow convergence and lower final accuracy [10, 28]. For example,
McMahan et al. (2017) simulated pathological non-IID by giving each client data from only
two classes of MNIST, noting significant accuracy drop compared to IID [39]. In intrusion
detection contexts, non-IID splits likewise tend to require more rounds to reach the same
accuracy as IID splits [10]. We will explore two extremes: IID, where we shuffle all data
and each client gets a random subset (an upper bound on performance, but unrealistic
if devices genuinely differ), and non-IID (by device), which reflects real deployment. By
comparing these, we can quantify the impact of data heterogeneity on both accuracy and
the efficacy of DP noise. Prior work suggests that some accuracy loss is expected with
non-IID, and that FedAvg might converge to a slightly lower peak accuracy or oscillate
more [10, 28]. Techniques like data sharing or personalized FL can mitigate that, but
those are beyond our current scope [10].
Scope note on non-IID mitigation.
Personalized FL and non-IID mitigations (e.g., Fed-
Prox, clustered FL, meta/personalized heads) are well-studied, we acknowledge their
benefits but do not employ them here to isolate the joint effects of (i) DP noise and (ii)
realistic network conditions on baseline FedAvg. We revisit these as future work [10, 28,
67, 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73].
Baseline Comparisons: In evaluating FL for IoT anomaly detection, literature commonly
uses the following baselines [10, 49]:
• Centralized Training: All data pooled at a server (if privacy and bandwidth were
no issue). This gives an upper-bound accuracy. For N-BaIoT, centralized models
commonly exceed 99% detection on test data in the original and follow-up studies
[37].
• Local Training: Each device trains its own model on its local data, and no FL occurs.
This usually underperforms because each device has limited data (e.g., one device
might not see all attack types). It’s a lower bound for utility but the most private (no
sharing at all) [10].
• Federated (No Privacy): Standard FedAvg with all clients, to measure the gap in-
troduced by just decentralization (often small if data isn’t too non-IID) [39, 10].
• Federated with DP: Our focus—measure the accuracy hit due to DP. Prior work
compares FL with and without DP, showing that DP can reduce accuracy depending
on ε and task complexity [15, 54, 66].
• (Optionally) Other Privacy/Security Baselines: Some studies include secure ag-
gregation (cryptographic, no accuracy cost) or robust aggregation (tolerance to at-
tacks). We note them but do not implement them here [27, 30, 31].
By aligning our experiments with these baselines, we ensure that our results can be con-

3.11
Evaluation Metrics in Prior Work
Evaluating privacy-preserving FL requires looking at multiple axes: model utility, privacy,
and efficiency. Different works emphasize different metrics:
Utility Metrics: Most common are classification accuracy, precision/recall, or F1-score
on a held-out test set. In anomaly detection (with class imbalance), F1-score or AUROC is
often preferred over plain accuracy. Many N-BaIoT papers report F1 > 0.98 for their best
models [37, 49]. We will use F1-score (or accuracy) to measure detection performance.
Privacy Metrics: The standard is to report the achieved (ε, δ) of differential privacy. Prior
work reports model accuracy under varying ε (e.g., ε = 1, 2, 4), showing degradation as
ε decreases [66, 54]. Some works also empirically evaluate attack success rates (e.g.,
how well a membership inference attack performs) to validate the privacy protection, but
such active evaluations are less common in FL due to complexity. In our case, we’ll stick
to theoretical DP guarantees (ε) since we trust the DP mechanism to provide worst-case
bounds [15, 16, 10].
Communication/Network Metrics: When studies consider efficiency, they report things
like time per round, total bytes sent/received, or convergence time to a target accuracy.
For instance, ns3-fl measured convergence time in seconds for Wi-Fi vs. Ethernet deploy-
ments [14], and systems/survey papers commonly track total traffic and communication
rounds during training [57, 10]. However, many FL papers (especially those focusing on
algorithms or privacy) assume an ideal network and omit these metrics. This is a gap we
address: by using ns-3, we will report latency per round and cumulative data transmitted
under each network scenario [14]. We believe our work is one of the few to jointly eval-
uate privacy, utility, and network cost in a single framework, as prior art typically covers
one or two of these dimensions but not all three simultaneously [14, 36, 12, 11, 19].

design, keeping raw data on devices and only sharing noised, aggregated updates is a
strong compliance story [21, 22]. As one industry analysis put it, “Embrace edge com-

3.13
IoT Anomaly Detection with FL: Opportunities and Gaps
The motivation for our research lies at the intersection of FL, privacy, and IoT security.
The IoT domain (smart cameras, appliances, health monitors, etc.) has produced sev-
eral datasets of network traffic aimed at developing anomaly-based intrusion detection
systems (IDS). Two prominent examples, as noted, are N-BaIoT [37] and MedBIoT [38].
These datasets consist of labeled network flows or packet statistics from IoT devices
under both normal operation and botnet attacks (like Mirai, which floods targets using
IoT devices). Traditional machine learning approaches to these datasets use centralized
training: gather all device data and train a classifier or anomaly detector (e.g., an autoen-
coder or one-class SVM) that can later flag suspicious activity.
However, centralized training on IoT data raises privacy and practicality concerns. The
network traffic may contain information about user behavior or device usage patterns (for
example, healthcare IoT devices may transmit sensitive data). Also, in realistic deploy-
ments, raw traffic data from many distributed IoT sensors might be too voluminous or sen-
sitive to collect in a single location. This naturally suggests a role for Federated Learning:
each IoT device (or local gateway) could keep its packet traces locally and only share
model updates. FL-based IDS training could allow collaborative learning of global attack
patterns without uploading raw data from hospitals, smart homes, etc., thereby preserving
privacy and reducing bandwidth usage [10].
Surprisingly, few prior works have applied FL to IoT anomaly detection, and none (to our
knowledge) have done so with differential privacy guarantees. Some recent studies have
started to explore federated deep learning for intrusion detection, but they often assume
a benign setting (no adversary trying to infer data from gradients) and typically consider
38
A Federated Learning Framework for Privacy-Preserving Anomaly Detection in 6G IoT Environments
==PAGE:47==
either IID data splits or simple network conditions [49]. Privacy-preserving FL for IDS
remains largely unexplored, which is problematic if we wish to deploy IDS in sensitive
environments (e.g., medical or industrial IoT) where data confidentiality is paramount.
Moreover, evaluating an FL IDS in an IoT context requires considering network aspects:
IoT devices often connect over wireless links that may be bandwidth-constrained (e.g.,
LPWAN, Wi-Fi) or intermittent. The added overhead of FL (communication rounds for
model updates) could impact the timeliness of detection if the network is slow. Conversely,
as alluded, a future THz-enabled IoT hub could mitigate this, but no one has quantified
this benefit in the context of federated IDS [14, 36].
Finally, an aspect rarely reported in prior art is the combined measurement of privacy,
model accuracy, and network cost. Papers in FL privacy usually emphasize privacy (ε
values) and accuracy on a test set, but ignore communication latency or data throughput.
Conversely, systems papers might measure training time or network traffic but not pro-
vide privacy guarantees. This siloed evaluation makes it hard to answer questions like:
“If we enforce a strong privacy budget (say ε < 1) for the IDS, how much does detection
accuracy drop, and how much longer (or more bandwidth) does training take under re-
alistic network conditions?” Such multidimensional trade-offs have been theorized (e.g.,
the privacy–utility–efficiency triad) but not demonstrated in an end-to-end experiment [28,
14].
Identified Gap: No prior work, to our knowledge, has jointly evaluated a differentially
private federated learning approach for IoT anomaly detection under realistic network
conditions. In particular, we did not find any study that combines all of the following: an
IoT botnet detection task (using data like N-BaIoT/MedBIoT), FL training with formal DP
guarantees, simulation of packet-level network effects (latency, loss, limited bandwidth),
and exploration of an idealized high-bandwidth scenario (such as a THz wireless link)
for comparison. Each of these components has been studied separately – but bringing
them together is novel. By doing so, we can uncover interactions (for example, how DP
noise and network latency compound their effects on model convergence) that would be
invisible in siloed analysis [37, 38, 14, 36, 28].
3.14
Scope and Limitations
Before proceeding, we clarify the scope and limitations of our work. First, our focus is on
privacy leakage through gradient updates and the countermeasure of DP. We do not ad-
dress active poisoning attacks or Byzantine robustness – i.e., we assume clients may be
curious but not maliciously altering their data to corrupt the model. Defenses like secure
aggregation or robust median aggregation are out of scope, though they could comple-
ment our approach in a production system. Second, our treatment of THz communica-
tion is idealized: akin to TeraSim’s early model, we assume a clear line-of-sight THz link
with static nodes (no mobility or beam alignment issues) to gauge an upper bound on
performance [36, 8]. Real-world THz networks would face shadowing and require beam-
tracking, which we do not simulate. Third, our experiments use the N-BaIoT and MedBIoT
datasets as representative IoT scenarios, while these are comprehensive, they cover pri-
marily botnet attacks on IoT devices. Thus, our findings may not directly generalize to
other types of IoT anomalies or datasets (though the methodology would apply). Finally,
we acknowledge that implementing full cryptographic secure aggregation or more com-
plex adversarial threat models could impact results – we deliberately set those aside to
isolate the effects of differential privacy and network conditions on federated anomaly
detection. These scope choices allow us to manage complexity and focus on the core
contributions, but they also mark avenues for future work to build on our foundation.

5
Methodology
This chapter specifies the methodological design used to answer the thesis aim and the
two research questions (RQ1 and RQ2). The framework adopts privacy preservation by
design: learning occurs on-device, per-sample gradients are clipped and noised using
DP-SGD before any update is sent, telemetry is minimal and reproducible, and the com-
munication path is exercised through ns–3 Ethernet/Wi–Fi sockets and a THz/6G-class
stateless emulation to surface latency and bandwidth constraints relevant to 6G IoT. The
objective is to make explicit the methods, tools, and design choices that underpin the
experiments and to justify them in light of the research questions.
5.1
Research Methodology Overview
This work follows an empirical systems–ML methodology. We quantify how network con-
ditions (Ethernet, Wi–Fi, and a short-range, high-rate THz link emulated in a stateless
manner), data heterogeneity (IID vs. non-IID), and differential privacy (DP) interact to af-
fect utility, latency, and communication cost for federated anomaly detection on tabular IoT
datasets. The design prioritises compact, reproducible experimentation over exhaustive
hyperparameter tuning so that observed effects can be attributed to the intended factors.
Concretely, we execute two factorial blocks per dataset (N–BaIoT and MedBIoT). Block A
varies the network medium under IID/non-IID partitions with DP disabled, Block B fixes
the medium to the THz stateless emulation and varies DP noise under IID/non-IID. All
runs use an asynchronous FedAvg server with a latency-aware selection policy and poly-
nomial staleness down-weighting (parameter α = 0.9). The THz path is realised as a
high-rate, short-range stateless emulation (rather than a socketed PHY/MAC) due to time
constraints on a full socket implementation, this preserves the intended effect on round
time while keeping the study tractable. Dataset partitions (IID and non-IID) are specified
in per-experiment config.json files and not hard-coded.
Assumptions.
To keep the matrix tractable, we assume: (i) at this scale (clients.total
= 10, clients.per_round = 2) the asynchronous server with polynomial staleness achieves
comparable utility to a synchronous baseline, (ii) the stateless THz emulation captures the
intended upper bound on latency/throughput effects for our payload sizes, and (iii) a com-
pact MLP suffices as a strong tabular IDS baseline under DP-SGD. These assumptions
are stated upfront and revisited in the discussion.

reproducible.
The primary components are Python 3.12 with PyTorch, Opacus, and NumPy/Pandas, C++20
for ns-3 and the THz module, and shell tooling bash and make with LSF job control. Ex-

5.1.3
Network Simulation Platform: ns-3
The ns-3 framework is used such that every model exchange goes through an explicit
link model, not an abstract bytes counter. Ethernet and Wi-Fi paths run through ns-3
socket stacks end to end. Transfers are packetized, queued, and carried over the config-
ured devices and channels. Contention, variable physical rates, and queueing delay are
therefore reflected in wall clock time without extra logic in the learning code.
For the short range 6G case a stateless parameterized terahertz emulator is used. A
full THz socket with beam training, a MAC, and rate control is out of scope. Instead, the
emulator maps a payload size and a small set of directional and rate parameters to a
deterministic service time and returns. No stateful sockets or queues are created. This
is an optimistic upper bound for high rate THz links and is sufficient when the goal is to
isolate communication effects rather than to model a full protocol stack.
Network settings live in the same JSON manifest as the federated run. At start up the
server reads the network block and passes the parameters to ns-3. Packet size and inter
packet interval are kept the same across media so that wall clock differences come from
the medium and not from batching. The payload on the wire is the flattened 32 bit tensor
of the model delta, so byte counts scale with model size. For each round ns-3 reports
bytes in both directions and the elapsed service time and the federated loop logs these
values next to accuracy.

5.1.5
Privacy and security libraries
We enforce confidentiality at the client with DP SGD implemented in Opacus. Before
transmitting an update each client clips per sample gradients to ℓ2 norm C = 1.0 and
adds Gaussian noise with multiplier σ ∈{0.6, 1.0}. The target is δ = 10−5. Clipping
and noising occur before serialization so the privacy guarantee does not depend on the
transport in ns-3.
Privacy loss is composed with the Opacus Renyi accountant under Poisson style subsam-
pling. For each run we record the inputs needed to recompute (ε, δ) ex post, namely C, σ,
the effective sampling rate q, the total number of private steps T, and δ. With shard size
nloc = 200 and batch size B = 128 the nominal rate per participation is q ≈B/nloc = 0.64.

Framework Design
This chapter presents the technical blueprint of the proposed framework for privacy -
preserving federated anomaly detection in 6G IoT environments. In contrast to Chap-
ter 5 (methods and procedures) and Chapter 7 (empirical execution), the aim is to specify
what the system is and how it operates: the software components and their interfaces,
the anomaly-detection model, the federated learning workflow (synchronous and asyn-
chronous), the integration of differential privacy, and the network communication substrate
that binds the learning loop to realistic link behaviour.
6.1
System architecture overview
The system uses a star topology with one central aggregator and N clients. Each client
holds local tabular features from N-BaIoT or MedBIoT, trains a small classifier, and sends
a private model delta. Privacy is applied on the client with DP SGD before any bytes are
sent. The aggregator applies Federated Averaging and redistributes the global model.
Both synchronous and asynchronous variants are implemented, but all experiments use
the asynchronous policy. Updates are integrated as they arrive and stale contributions
are down weighted, with the option to drop very stale updates.
All model traffic goes through ns-3 so the chosen medium affects wall clock time. Ethernet
and Wi-Fi use socket stacks at packet level, which exposes queueing, contention, and
variable physical rates. The THz or 6G class option is a parameterized stateless emulator
that maps each payload to a deterministic service time.
Codebase partitioning
• ns3-fl-network C++ and ns-3. Defines Ethernet and Wi-Fi scenarios with sock-
eted packet level models and a THz emulator derived from TeraSim. Exposes a
simple send and receive interface so the learning code does not depend on ns-3
internals.
• flsim Python. Owns orchestration and learning. run.py launches the aggregator
and clients, loads a JSON configuration, and runs the FedAvg loop in synchronous
or asynchronous mode. A small NetworkController switches between Ethernet or
Wi-Fi sockets and the stateless THz emulator. Client side DP uses Opacus, and
per round telemetry is logged as CSV.
To elaborate on how this works, a reference round in FL proceeds as follows. The server
broadcasts the current weights wt to K selected clients. Each client trains for one local
epoch (E = 1) and forms a model delta. The client applies DP SGD with per sample
clipping and Gaussian noise to this delta, then sends the serialized update through ns-3,
over sockets for Ethernet and Wi-Fi or via the stateless THz emulator. On receipt the
server applies the update. In our experiments the server is asynchronous and integrates
updates as they arrive with staleness weighting rather than waiting for a barrier.

The detector is a small supervised multilayer perceptron with 2 to 3 hidden layers, ReLU
activations, and a single sigmoid output. Training uses binary cross entropy. This choice
matches the tabular header and timing features used in the datasets and avoids heavier
sequence models.

6.7
Design evolution and rationale
We made the following deliberate choices to keep the study focused and to make privacy,
networking, and utility effects measurable and reproducible:
1. Minimal FL runner for timing attribution. Replacing a high level FL framework
with a lightweight flsim runner gave exact control over when bytes are emitted,
where DP noise is injected, and how ns-3 timing is measured. This eliminated mid-
dleware effects such as gRPC that could mask network induced latency.
2. THz via TeraSim under time bounded realism. Porting TeraSim provided THz
PHY fidelity. We model THz transfers with a stateless, PHY informed bulk schedule
with duration derived from rate and payload size. This captures the dominant effect,
namely directional ultra high throughput over short range, within project constraints.
A full THz MAC or transport is future work.
3. Asynchronous aggregation with small concurrency. The server accepts up-
dates on arrival with no strict K barrier, down weights stale deltas by ω(s), and
drops if s > τmax. We limit per broadcast concurrency so link and DP effects remain
visible without large scale contention artifacts.
4. Client side DP as the privacy boundary. Opacus DP SGD runs on device. Only
privatized updates traverse the network. This aligns with privacy by design and
keeps transport and channel assumptions orthogonal to confidentiality. Secure ag-
gregation and channel authentication are left as measurable extensions.
5. DOE friendly metrics and fixed configs. Fixed hyperparameters and declarative
manifests ensure runs are comparable across media Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and THz and
across DP settings, with per round logging of accuracy, wall clock, and bytes to
support the trade off analysis in later chapters.

7.1
Chapter Overview and RQ Alignment
This chapter specifies what we ran, under which conditions, and how outcomes were mea-

7.2
Experimental Setup
Federated algorithm All runs use asynchronous FedAvg. In each global round the server
invites K clients and integrates updates on arrival. Stale updates are down weighted by a
polynomial rule with α = 0.9. Defaults are clients.total= 10, clients.per_round= 2,
rounds= 20, local_epochs= 1, batch_size= 128. Early stopping is disabled.
• Privacy mechanism When enabled, clients train with DP SGD in Opacus. Per sam-

is added. We report (ε, δ) with a Renyi DP accountant at δ = 10−5.
• Data and partitions We use N-BaIoT and MedBIoT. IID partitions distribute labels
uniformly. Non IID partitions skew clients toward device identities or attack families.
Feature normalization and label encoding are fixed across runs.
• Model backbone Tabular runs use a compact MLP with about 103 to 104 parameters
to keep payloads small and clipping stable. The MNIST baseline uses a small CNN
or MLP only to check the end to end stack.
• Network emulation All communication traverses ns-3. Ethernet and Wi-Fi use sock-
eted transport with packet level effects. THz uses a TeraSim based parameterized
stateless transfer with fixed beamwidth, gains, and geometry that maps a payload to
a deterministic service time. Packetization and per round windows are harmonized
across media.
• Execution environment DTU LSF queue gpuv100 with 1 x NVIDIA V100, 4 CPU
cores, and 6 to 8 GiB RAM. CUDA 11.6. Numerical libraries are pinned to one thread
with OMP_NUM_THREADS=1, MKL_NUM_THREADS=1, and OPENBLAS_NUM_THREADS=1. Jobs
run sequentially. Each run archives the config, the git commit, environment versions,
and per round CSVs with accuracy, timestamps, and bytes.

9
Discussion
This chapter interprets the empirical findings with respect to the thesis aim of a privacy-
preserving federated anomaly-detection framework for 6G-class IoT and the two research
questions. Chapters 7 and 8 documented what was run and what was observed. Here the
focus is on what those observations mean, how they relate to the chosen system design,
and what they imply for deployment.
9.1
Key Findings and Interpretation
The centralised tabular baselines in Section 8.1 show that compact MLPs saturate quickly
on both datasets. This establishes a high utility ceiling with the chosen backbone and
preprocessing and makes it possible to attribute any degradation in the federated runs to
decentralisation, privacy, or networking rather than to model capacity.
The MNIST over Wi-Fi baseline in Section 8.1 confirms that the integrated learning loop
and the ns-3 path operate end-to-end as intended. The run reaches the ninety percent
threshold by round twenty and the wall-clock trajectory is consistent with asynchronous
integration and a packet-level Wi-Fi path [14]. This validates the orchestration so that
subsequent differences in tabular runs can be traced to dataset characteristics, link con-
ditions, and privacy settings.
The THz sanity checks in Section 8.1 indicate that the modeled THz link behaves as a
high-throughput pipe for the payload sizes used and that the ADAPT-style handshake
sustains goodput under higher offered load while the no-handshake baseline collapses
[76]. This aligns with later observations where THz often matches or slightly improves on
Ethernet in wall-clock for compact models when beams and geometry are fixed, and with
known characteristics of THz modeling in TeraSim and the THz channel [36, 9, 8].
Block A in Section 8.2 shows that network choice and statistical heterogeneity both shape
time to target. Ethernet gives the shortest wall-clock to a fixed accuracy. Wi-Fi is slower
due to contention and variable physical rates. The modeled THz path matches or sur-
passes Ethernet when beams are fixed and the offered load is moderate. Non-IID parti-
tions increase time to target and widen round-to-round variance across all media. These
patterns are visible in the cumulative-best accuracy traces and in the time-to-0.90 sum-
maries [14, 57, 10, 67].
Block B in Section 8.2 shows that client-side DP-SGD with clipping at one and noise mul-
tipliers of zero point six and one point zero produces the expected privacy–utility–latency
trade-off on a high-capacity link. The lower noise level increases time to target moderately
and reduces final accuracy slightly. The higher noise level accentuates both effects and
the penalties are larger under non-IID partitions. The relative network ordering in wall-
clock that appears in Block A remains stable when privacy is enabled, which indicates
that privacy costs add to but do not fundamentally reorder link effects for these compact
models [15, 16, 18, 28].
The execution controls in Section 8.4 support auditability. Runs are tagged by resolved
manifests and code hashes. Per-round telemetry includes time and bytes. This is suffi-
cient to reproduce the comparisons across network media, partition regimes, and privacy
levels and to tie outcomes back to concrete configurations [14].

75
==PAGE:84==
Taken together these results support the claim that privacy-preserving federated learning
for tabular IoT intrusion detection is feasible in a 6G-class setting. When models are
compact and links are provisioned to edge-class capacity the main levers are partition
skew and the privacy dial. Their costs are measurable and can be tuned through clipping
and noise, client selection, and network provisioning.
9.2
Addressing the Research Questions
For RQ1 the framework integrates client-side DP-SGD into FedAvg with traffic carried by
ns-3 scenarios for Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and a THz path adapted from TeraSim. Privacy is
enforced before transmission since each client clips and noises locally and only sends
privatized updates [15, 18, 16]. The approach fits compact tabular models and the asyn-
chronous cadence used throughout the study [39]. The MNIST baseline demonstrates
that the end-to-end stack functions as expected [14]. The THz sanity checks demon-
strate that the high-capacity path behaves predictably under the modeled conditions [36].
The factorial blocks show the combined learning and networking effects with and without
privacy on both datasets.
For RQ2 the experiments quantify confidentiality–effectiveness trade-offs. Enabling pri-
vacy with a noise multiplier of zero point six increases time to target moderately and re-
duces final utility slightly. Increasing the multiplier to one point zero strengthens both ef-
fects and the impact is larger under non-IID partitions. The ordering across links does not
change when privacy is enabled. On the modeled THz path the cost of privacy appears
primarily as extra wall-clock and a small utility drop rather than a qualitative change in
learning trajectory. Where privacy accountant values are computed the reported epsilon
at delta can be placed next to time and accuracy so the trade-offs are auditable [28, 54,
15]. The data recorded per run already contain the quantities needed for that reporting.
9.3
Unexpected Observations and Explanations
Early convergence in several federated runs is consistent with the strong signal visible
in the centralised baselines. To expose link and privacy effects over time early stopping
was disabled and a fixed number of rounds was used. The modeled THz path often
matches or slightly improves on Ethernet in wall-clock for the tested payload sizes and
fixed geometry. This is consistent with the THz sanity runs and with the fact that the
THz component is a high-throughput abstraction rather than a stateful transport [9, 75,
78]. The privacy penalty at a noise multiplier of zero point six is smaller than expected
on these tabular tasks which fits the combination of compact models, clipping at one,
and moderate batch sizes [18, 15]. The penalty grows under non-IID partitions, which is
expected when statistical heterogeneity and privacy noise interact.
Notes on asynchronous traces are relevant for interpretation. Accuracy is plotted against
wall-clock as a cumulative-best envelope. Smoothed lines are used only for visualiza-
tion. Pass or fail thresholds and time-to-0.90 are taken from the cumulative-best curve by
design.
9.4
Security Posture and CIAAN
The current implementation enforces confidentiality at the learning boundary. Clients ap-
ply per-sample clipping and add Gaussian noise locally. Raw data do not leave devices
so the transport choice does not affect the privacy guarantee. The logs record the inputs
needed for post-hoc privacy accounting [15, 16, 17]. The results show the expected cost
of privacy in time and in utility. A complete presentation should place the computed privacy
76
A Federated Learning Framework for Privacy-Preserving Anomaly Detection in 6G IoT Environments
==PAGE:85==
budget next to the corresponding accuracy and time for each configuration (cf. Literature
Review §§3.4.1–3.9).
Integrity is evaluated under benign clients. There are no poisoning, backdoor, model-
replacement, or Sybil scenarios in this study. The observed stability therefore reflects
benign learning under clipping and noise. Extending the same simulator with adversarial
clients and robust aggregation would allow integrity to be measured alongside utility and
timing while keeping the network and privacy settings realistic [30, 31, 50, 29].
Availability depends on the link. Ethernet remains the fastest path in wall-clock. Wi-Fi is
slower due to contention and rate variability. The modeled THz path can match or surpass
Ethernet when geometry is fixed and load is moderate. Privacy increases wall-clock but
does not change that ordering. A more complete analysis would add background traffic,
dropouts, and beam alignment dynamics with a stateful THz transport so that slowdowns
can be attributed to specific link events [14, 47].
Authenticity and non-repudiation are not implemented. Channels are not authenticated
and updates are not signed. The pipeline does provide scientific auditability through man-
ifests, code hashes, and per-round CSVs. Moving to operational accountability would re-
quire authenticated transport and a tamper-evident log that chains round summaries and
model digests and optionally records signed update receipts [27, 32, 34, 33].
9.5
Implications for Smart-City Deployment
City deployments benefit from enforcing privacy at the device boundary because transport
and topology then become orthogonal to confidentiality. The experiments show that com-
pact tabular detectors train reliably with client-side privacy on links that range from Wi-Fi
to Ethernet to a THz-class path. For these models the primary system levers are data het-
erogeneity and the privacy dial. Link choice changes how fast useful accuracy is realized
rather than whether it is attainable within the given budget of rounds. In practice, opera-
tors can provision links by cost and coverage and then tune the noise multiplier, clipping,
and participation to meet their service targets. Because heterogeneity widens time to
target even when bandwidth is ample, deployments should monitor it explicitly and report
how often and how quickly a chosen accuracy threshold is crossed under the selected pri-
vacy settings [5, 6, 12, 11]. Operational traceability is already in place through manifests
and per-round telemetry. Authenticity, secure aggregation, and a tamper-evident log re-
main to be added and then measured for timing and energy overheads, with policy-aware
reporting aligned to privacy-by-design principles [21, 22, 74].
9.6
Distributed Systems and Auditability
The framework is engineered for reproducibility.
Each run persists resolved configu-
rations, code commit identifiers, random seeds, and per-round telemetry for accuracy,
bytes, and time. This supports re-creation and cross-checks and ties outcomes back to
the exact training and networking settings. It does not yet provide cryptographic guaran-
tees about who contributed which update or whether logs were altered later. A minimal
next step is a hash-chained log of per-round summaries and model digests and optional
signed update receipts [34, 33, 32]. From a systems perspective the current simulator
uses a single central aggregator. Edge placement of aggregators and failure injection are
natural extensions and would let wall-clock, privacy, and robustness be studied together.
9.7
Qualitative and Quantitative Synthesis
From an engineering standpoint client-side DP-SGD integrates cleanly into asynchronous
FedAvg and keeps the privacy boundary on device. Across the experiments the link pri-

==PAGE:86==
marily affects when the model becomes useful rather than whether it becomes useful
within the fixed round budget. The guardrails used in execution, such as thread caps
and sequential arrays, were needed to make timing and byte measurements credible on
shared resources. Quantitatively, the most informative summaries pair time-to-0.90 with
the area under the accuracy-versus-time curve and, where counters exist, mean bytes per
round and bytes-to-target. On the privacy axis, contrasting runs with privacy off against
noise multipliers of zero point six and one point zero on the THz path shows a tunable
cost in time and a small to moderate change in utility that grows under non-IID partitions.
Where available, epsilon at a fixed delta should be listed next to each cell so that the
trade-offs are directly comparable [10, 28].
9.8
Future Work and Emerging Directions
9.8.1
Methodological directions
Future studies should compare centralized training, classic server-based FL, and decen-
tralized topologies because the literature shows distinct privacy, integrity, and availability
trade offs across these regimes [10, 35]. Cross-silo collaboration with stable links and gov-
ernance differs materially from cross-device IoT with intermittent connectivity and churn
[10]. Secure aggregation and MPC can hide individual updates from the server [27, 57,
20], while permissioned ledgers or hash-chained logs can add tamper evidence and non-
repudiation for provenance [34, 33]. These controls should be evaluated side by side
with accuracy and time to target, not in isolation. Policy and society matter in smart-city
contexts; consent, transparency, and fairness need to be discussed alongside technical
metrics [5, 6, 12, 21, 22].
9.8.2
Framework and tooling
Replace the idealized THz path with stateful 6G sockets that model queueing, beam align-
ment, mobility, and contention so link events are attributable in round timing [36, 8, 48].
Extend the ns-3 coupling used here to include background load and mobility as in network-
coupled FL work [14]. Broaden privacy beyond local DP-SGD to include central DP and
distributed DP, and add adaptive clipping so the clip norm follows gradient statistics rather
than remaining fixed [15, 16, 17, 18]. Move beyond compact MLPs to recurrent and con-
volutional models, transformers for tabular or sequences, and generative models where
appropriate, and exercise time series, images, and operational technology traces so con-
clusions generalize [10]. Each addition should ship with parameter sweeps and repro-
ducible manifests, following the ns3-fl practice of configuration-first evaluation [14].
9.8.3
Threat modeling and attackable evaluation
Make the simulator attackable. Add poisoned and backdoor clients, Byzantine and Sybil
behavior, and gradient inversion baselines so confidentiality and integrity trade offs are
measured rather than assumed [25, 26, 40, 41, 42, 30, 31]. On the network side, script
congestion and DDoS patterns to stress availability and relate failures to round timing
and throughput [59, 58, 12]. Evaluate robust aggregation, update filtering, and secure
aggregation under the same workloads and report accuracy, backdoor success, time to
target, bytes, and privacy accounting together.
9.8.4
Deployment at the edge
Validate the pipeline on Raspberry Pi class devices and similar gateways. Measure en-
ergy, thermal throttling, CPU contention, and battery effects for local DP-SGD, and com-
pare Opacus-style per-sample accounting to lighter client-side perturbations that may be
more practical on constrained hardware [18, 54, 55]. Quantify when local DP becomes
impractical and what is gained or lost by shifting to central or distributed DP. Reuse the

ns3-fl validation pattern that compared simulation to a Pi testbed to ground timings and
bytes in physical measurements [14, 47].
9.8.5
Privacy accounting and cryptography
Publish epsilon at a fixed delta next to utility and timing for every configuration and log
per-round sampling, steps, and clip distributions so privacy budgets are auditable [16,
17]. Add secure aggregation or masked sums so the server observes only aggregates
and measure wall-clock impact [27, 57]. Harden channels with mutual authentication and
maintain an append-only provenance log by hash-chaining round summaries and model
digests or using a permissioned ledger where appropriate [34, 33, 32, 12]. This turns
reproducibility into accountable operation.
9.8.6
Smart-city and health implications
As Internet of Senses applications emerge, features will correlate with biometric and be-
havioral signals and raise inference and retention risks [6, 5, 12]. Studies should relate
concrete privacy budgets to model utility and time to target and describe purpose limita-
tion and opt-out flows in operator-facing terms. The IDS setting used here on N-BaIoT
and MedBIoT motivates this, since botnet behaviors can implicate household or patient
contexts [37, 38]. Operators need policy-aware reporting that citizens and auditors can
verify [21, 22].
9.8.7
6G FL directions
Future 6G-class FL must account for extreme device density, fast directional beams, net-
work slicing, and split or hierarchical learning across edge tiers [5, 6, 11, 19]. Mobility-
aware schedulers and stateful THz transport will make timing effects attributable and help
design FL that remains stable at scale [36, 8, 48]. The literature indicates that commu-
nication remains a principal bottleneck in FL and that privacy, utility, and efficiency form
a constrained triad [10, 28]. The results here suggest that on compact models a high-
capacity link removes bandwidth as the primary limit while non-IID partitions and DP be-
come the governing levers; future 6G FL should therefore co-design network scheduling,
participation, and privacy budgets under realistic radio and mobility.

9.11
Conclusion
This thesis addressed privacy-preserving anomaly detection for 6G IoT by training models
collaboratively without centralising raw data. We designed and implemented a configuration-
driven framework that couples a compact supervised MLP with client-side DP-SGD and
routes federated updates through ns–3 network scenarios: Ethernet and Wi–Fi via sock-
eted transport, and a THz/6G-class link via a TeraSim-based, parameterised, stateless
transfer. Execution on DTU’s LSF cluster used sequential arrays and per-run artefacting
to support reproducibility.
Methodologically, we validated the end-to-end stack by reproducing the MNIST Wi–Fi
baseline over ns–3 (see Chapter 8), then evaluated two tabular intrusion datasets (N–
BaIoT, MedBIoT) in two compact experiment blocks: Block A (Network × Partition with DP
off) and Block B (Partition × DP on THz). Across networks, the federated MLP achieved
strong utility with realistic convergence trajectories, enabling client-side DP produced the
expected privacy–utility–latency trade-off: a modest penalty at noise_multiplier=0.6
and a larger one at 1.0, amplified under non-IID partitions. Network-wise, Ethernet and
the idealised THz path delivered the shortest time–to–target, while Wi–Fi was slower due
to contention and variable PHY rates. This relative ordering remained stable with DP
enabled.
Answers to the research questions.
RQ1 (integration). We demonstrated a practical
integration of client-side DP-SGD into an asynchronous FedAvg loop that communicates
over ns–3 (Ethernet/Wi–Fi/THz). Privacy is enforced before transmission: clients send
only clipped and noised updates. RQ2 (trade-offs). We quantified that DP noise intro-
duces measurable time and utility costs that are tunable by the noise level and clipping,
with stronger effects under non-IID. Network selection chiefly modulates how fast utility is
realised (wall-clock), not whether the same utility is ultimately reachable given sufficient
rounds.
Contributions.
C1. An ns–3-integrated FL framework for tabular IoT IDS that enforces client-side
DP-SGD and records per-round telemetry (utility, wall-clock, and where available,
bytes) under Ethernet, Wi–Fi (socketed), and THz/6G (TeraSim) conditions.
C2. A declarative design-of-experiments for FL+network+privacy, realised as con-
figuration packs (Block A and Block B) that separate what to run from how to or-
chestrate it, enabling fair comparisons across media, partitions, and DP settings.
C3. Empirical characterisation of privacy–utility–latency on N–BaIoT and MedBIoT:
calibrated DP noise yields predictable cost, non-IID exacerbates it, network differ-
ences manifest primarily in time–to–target.
C4. THz integration notes in ns–3: TeraSim was used under contrib/ and adapted
to the modern ns3 helper, THz transfers are modelled as parameterised stateless
durations, while Ethernet/Wi–Fi use real sockets.
In summary, privacy-preserving federated anomaly detection for 6G IoT is feasible with a
compact MLP plus client-side DP-SGD, and its operational costs can be measured and
tuned by balancing DP level, participation, and network provisioning. Within the honest-
but-curious threat model and the idealised THz abstraction studied here, the framework
turns abstract trade-offs into measurable engineering levers for smart-city deployments.

9.12
Outlook
The results point to three immediate extensions. First, replace the idealised THz trans-
fer with a stateful 6G socket so queueing, beam alignment, and mobility map to round
timing (cf. section8.1 and the Block A timing patterns in section8.2). Second, complete
the privacy story by reporting ε at fixed δ next to utility and time for every cell and by
evaluating adaptive clipping and alternative DP modes (local, central, distributed) under
the Block B design (section8.2 and section3.4.1). Third, move from benign to attackable
runs by adding poisoning and backdoor clients with robust or secure aggregation, so con-
fidentiality, integrity, and availability can be read together under realistic links (section3.3,
section3.4, section9.4). A small edge testbed will help calibrate the simulator and surface
practical costs for authenticated transport and secure aggregation.

Differential Privacy Fundamentals
Differential privacy provides a mathematical framework to quantify and control the privacy
guarantees of a data analysis system. It is defined in terms of a parameter called ϵ, which
represent the privacy budget or the level of privacy protection provided.
Pr[M(D) ∈S] ≤eϵ · Pr[M(D′) ∈S]
(A.1)
The probability distribution of the output remains nearly unchanged whether an individual’s
data is included or excluded. This means that whether your information is in the dataset
or not, an attacker cannot reliably infer anything about you. Another variation of equation
A.2 also found in the literature is the approximate differential privacy (ϵ, δ)-DP:
Pr[M(D) ∈S] ≤eϵ · Pr[M(D′) ∈S] + δ
(A.2)
This approximate differential privacy relaxes pure ϵ-DP by allowing a small probability of
failure δ. The change ensures that for a probability of ”at most” δ, ϵ-DP can be violated.
In other words, except for a probability of δ, the outcome distributions d and d′ are within
a factor eϵ. δ bounds the probability of such a privacy leak, a low probability event where
the likelihood ratio between Pr[M(D) ∈S] and Pr[M(D′) ∈S] is greater than eϵ. For
instance, let B be the set of bad outputs that violate the pure ϵ-DP ratio:

a large C requires adding more noise for privacy (since noise scale must grow with sen-
sitivity). Thus, C is a tunable hyperparameter that affects the privacy-utility trade-off: it
should be large enough to minimally affect model training, but not so large that it blows
up the required noise.

Synopsis
In this thesis, we test whether federated learning with client-side differential privacy can deliver ef-
fective IoT anomaly detection under realistic networks for 6G-class deployments. The framework
