Abstract
The objective of this work is to develop and test new and eﬀective ways of numerically solving
the high-order Boussinesq formulation, for the study of highly non-linear deep water waves in two
horizontal dimensions. For this purpose the high-order Boussinesq formulation of Madsen et al.
(2002) is used, which provides a formulation describing the ﬂuid domain in two steps. The ﬁrst
step from the sea bed to the still water datum, and the second step from the still water datum to
the free surface. This approach provides increased non-linear accuracy compared to the one-step
formulation, describing the ﬂuid domain directly from the sea bed to the free surface in one-step,
which is also provide in Madsen et al. (2002).
Two numerical models are developed providing a solution to the high-order Boussinesq formulation,
using respectively this one- and two-step formulation, referred to as the one- and two-step models
throughout this work. In the numerical models spacial dizcretisation is provided by a spectral
method, which is combined with a high-order explicit Runge-kutta time integration method. The
advantage of the spectral appraoch is that it oﬀers spectral accuracy.
Solving the Buossinesq formulation in two horizontal dimensions involves a solution to a lin-
ear system at each stage elevation. For this, a Krylov subspace iteration method is used, as a
direct solution method would be very challenging computationally. To achieve a relative eﬃcient
computation, using the Krylov subspace method, a matrix free environment is introduced. This
combined with the spectral discretisation in space, provides high accuracy and low storage making
the present numerical scheme very attractive for large demanding problems.
Both numerical models were systematically veriﬁed and then used to study highly non-linear
wave events. Validation of the fundamental linear and non-linear properties of the system of
equations conﬁrmed the correct implementation of both models. The models were successfully
tested against cases with steepness of up to H/L = 0.120 (waveheight divided by wavelength),
showing increased accuracy when using the two-step model, as expected. However, when testing
the models on highly non-linear waves with a steepness greater then H/L = 0.120, the two-step
model showed increased numerical instability. For this reason, the non-linear terms were examined
further by investigating steep highly non-linear crescent wave patterns, which conﬁrmed the correct
implementation of both models, but showed the same unstable behaviour of the two-step model
when highly non-linear waves where simulated. This numerical instability, most likely relates to

the increased numerical complexity of the two-step Boussinesq formulation.
Having established the correct implementation of both models, highly non-linear focused wave
events, which were spread both in frequency and direction, were studied. The one-step model
showed excellent match with experimental measurements, both for the free surface elevation and
the wave kinematics for all cases considered. The two-step model, on the other hand, required

for simulating and studying highly non-linear water waves in two horizontal directions. It also
establishes, that the one-step numerical solution seems most attractive, and that the expected
beneﬁt of improved non-linear accuracy, from the two-step numerical solution, when simulating
highly non-linear wave events, is not achieved due to numerical instabilities likely resulting from
the more complex formulation of this model (higher order derivatives).
The numerical models developed, as part of this thesis, allowed the studying of demanding
wave events, and the computational results combined with analysis given throughout this thesis
provide a good insight into the complicated physical phenomenons of non-linear wave events.

Chapter 1
Introduction
An ocean wave ﬁeld can be characterized by an inﬁnite number of wave components, with diﬀerent
frequencies and directions. An event, where a large number of these waves are focused in time
and space is believed to represent the evolution of extreme ocean waves in deep water. From an
engineering perspective these highly non-linear, near breaking wave events are of great importances
for design of oﬀshore structures and ships. These wave events are also often referred to as the cause
of severe damage to ships and oﬀshore structures. For these reasons eﬃcient and accurate methods
for predicting the elevation, kinematics and thereby the forces of such extreme wave events, are
very important for coastal and oﬀshore engineering (Fuhrman and Madsen 2006).
In the present study a high-order Boussinesq-type method/formulation is used to simulate and
study such highly non-linear water wave events.
The principle of the Boussinesq method, is that it incorporates the eﬀect of non-hydrostatic
pressure while eliminating the vertical co-ordinate, causing a signiﬁcant improvement in computa-
tional eﬃciency relative to a three-dimensional solution. This method was originally formulated by
the mathematician Boussinesq in Boussinesq (1872).
The numerical model developed, as a part of this thesis used to simulate water waves, is based on
the fully non-linear, high-order Boussinesq-type formulations of Madsen et al. (2002). This method
uses exact representations of the kinematic and dynamic free surface conditions, expressed in terms
of the horizontal and vertical velocities at the free surface.
In the present work however, the horizontal velocity variables, and thereby the free surface
boundary conditions, are re-formulated in terms of the velocity potential. This is done to decrease
the total number of unknowns and simplify the numerical implementation, which should lead to
increased computational eﬃciency. This re-formulation of the velocity variables, in terms of a
velocity potential, is inspired by Bingham et al. (2009) and Jamois et al. (2006).
The Boussinesq equations of Madsen et al. (2002) is the most accurate Boussinesq formulation
today, and was a major breakthrough in modern Boussinesq theory, capable of providing accurate
surface descriptions for roughly kh Æ 25 (k the wavenumber, h the depth), and accurate velocity
kinematics for roughly kh Æ 12 (Fuhrman 2004)

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The high accuracy unfortunately comes at the expense of a very complicated system of par-
tial diﬀerential equations (PDEs). The purpose of this thesis is to develop an eﬃcient solution in
two horizontal dimensions, on a ﬂat bottom for the high-order Boussinesq formulations of Madsen
et al. (2002), using a spectral method for solving the partial diﬀerential equations. A ﬁnite element
or ﬁnite diﬀerence method has been used extensively in previous works, for solving the Boussinesq
formulation. This work represents the ﬁrst adoption of a spectral method to the solution in two
horizontal dimensions of the Boussinesq formulation. The main advantage of a spectral method
is its high accuracy and low storage requirements, which should provide increased computational
eﬃciency compared to other methods. The numerical implementation of the present model is done
such that the solution to the PDEs from the Boussinesq formulation is completely matrix-free,
which further contributes to the low storage requirements of the spectral method. This makes the
present model very applicable for large demanding problems such as simulating highly non-linear
and irregular wave ﬁelds, where other models would require excessive storage demands.
One of the key objectives for the present work is to produce a solution to the Boussinesq formulation
describing the ﬂuid domain in two-steps. The ﬁrst step, going from the seabed to the still water
datum, and the second step from the still water datum to the free surface. This formulation was
introduced in the original work of Madsen et al. (2002) and used for a solution in one horizontal
dimension, showing increased non-linear accuracy compared to the one-step solution. In the present
work a numerical solution in two horizontal dimensions, using this two-step formulation, will be
presented and throughout this work referred to as the two-step model. As far as the author is
aware this has not been solved before. A numerical solution to the Boussinesq formulation, in two
horizontal dimensions, describing the ﬂuid domain in one step, directly from the sea bed to the
free surface, will also be presented, and throughout this work referred to as the one-step model.
The two-step model will most likely be less computational eﬃcient than the the one-step model,
but should provide increased non-linear accuracy as was shown by Madsen et al. (2002) in the one
horizontal dimension case. The two models will be extensively compared in reference to accuracy
but also in reference computational eﬃciency.
The numerical models will then serve the purpose of studying physical demanding problems,
involving highly non-linear focused wave events. The increased non-linear accuracy of the two-step
approach is indeed interesting for these steep, near breaking wave events, where the non-linearity is
more prominent. Up to the linear free surface both formulations are identical.
1.1
Scope of work
The work, done during this thesis, consists of the development of the two numerical models, capable
of modelling water waves using high-order Boussinesq equations, validating the numerical models by
comparing with know and analytical results, and lastly using the models to study very demanding
focusing wave events. The structure of the thesis is represented in terms of theses three main parts.

1.1. SCOPE OF WORK

In Chapter 2 the governing high-order Boussinesq equations, used in the numerical models, will be
presented along with the general assumptions and conditions required for these. Followed by this,

The validation is done,

disadvantageous of the two approaches.

model. These are very demanding cases, which are also used to determine the capability of the
models in reference to accuracy, stability and computational eﬃciency and to conclude which model,
is most attractive for simulating highly non-linear wave events.
The conclusion and recommendations for further work are given in Chapter 6.

governing equations required to satisfy the high-order Boussinesq formulation will be described.

Chapter 3
The Numerical Model
3.1
Introduction
The aim of this chapter is to describe the most fundamental features of the numerical model used
to solve the systems of high-order Boussinesq equations.
The governing Boussinesq equations outlined in section 2.3 is a non-linear time-dependent problem
involving a solution to a system of diﬀerential equations. Many diﬀerent numerical approaches
are available to do this. In the present work a spectral method is used to numerically solve the
diﬀerential equations, resulting from the Boussinesq formulation, using Fast Fourier Transformation
(FFT). The principle of FFT is that the solution of the diﬀerential equation is formulated as a
Fourier series with its coeﬃcients chosen in order to satisfy the diﬀerential equation as well as
possible. Fourier series are only appropriate with periodic boundaries, which for this reason is used
in the numerical model.
The Boussinesq formulation deﬁnes a set of Partial Diﬀerential Equations (PDEs). A solution
to this set of equations, using a spectral method, is most eﬃciently done by only applying the
spectral method to the spatial dependent variables, whereby the original set of PDEs will be
reduced to a set of Ordinary Diﬀerential Equations (ODEs) in time. The time dependence can
then be marched forward in time, using time integration methods (Boyd 2000). For this rea-
son, a more accurate deﬁnition of the method would be pseudo-spectral, because the spectral
method is not applied to the time dependence, which would be very computationally expensive com-
pared to time marching. In the present work a Runge-Kutta method is used for the time integration.
The high-order Boussinesq equations reduces, the three dimensional Laplace problem to a two
dimensional one, which is solved numerical on a grid of points distributed over the still water plane.
Evolving ÷ and ˜„ (eq. 2.10a,b) forward in time will require a solution to the system Ax = b to give
‚„ú, ‚wú at each grid point and thus deﬁne the entire three-dimensional solution. A Krylov subspace
iterative method called Generalized Minimal RESidual (GMRES) algorithm of Saad and Schultz
(1986), which is implemented within Matlab, is used for solving this linear system Ax = b.

3.3
Spectral diﬀerentiation
The key advantage of a spectral method is that it is global in the sense that it uses all available
function values to construct the necessary approximations. This is in contrast to methods such
as ﬁnite diﬀerence or ﬁnite element that use nearby grid points to approximate the function or
its derivatives and therefore become more local in nature. For problems with signiﬁcant spatial
variation these methods will require very ﬁne grids and signiﬁcant computational resources. In
comparison, the spectral methods give high accuracy with coarser grids and are therefore less
resource demanding. The improved function and derivative approximations, provided by the
global nature of the spectral methods, also result in more rapid convergence of the error, with
increasing grid points, than that of other methods. This rapid convergence is referred to as spectral
accuracy, meaning a coarser grid is suﬃcient to obtain the same level of accuracy as for the other
methods and thereby also lower storage is required for the spectral method (Hussaini and Zang 1987).
The principle idea of the spectral method used in the present work is that the spacial depen-
dent variables can be represented by Fourier series in the horizontal plane, assuming periodic
boundaries. Discrete Fourier transform (DFT) is a form of Fourier analysis that is applicable to
the uniformly-spaced samples of a continuous function. The discrete Fourier transform can be
computed by the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which then conveniently allows diﬀerentiation in
space using spectral diﬀerentiation (Trefethen 2000).
The process of spectral diﬀerentiation of a function f(x,y) is performed by multiplying its two
dimensional Fourier representation F(Kx, Ky) by the corresponding wavenumber coordinate raised
to a power equal to the order of diﬀerentiation (Trefethen 2000):

Though the validation phase, the Ode45 function within MatLab and the self implemented RK4

when simulating demanding cases, compared to MatLabs’s Ode45 function. However for the less
demanding cases there was no diﬀerence between the result of the two methods, except that
simulations were substantially faster when using the ode45 function within MatLab. For this reason

to the direct access of the calculated variables at each time-step, giving the ability to ﬁlter and
combat instabilities at every time step. This was not possible when using the ode45 function within
MatLab, where the time step was chosen automatically and access of the variables at each time
step was limited.
Filtering was as mentioned used to combat instabilities, caused by the non-linearity. The ﬁl-

with a speciﬁed number of points per wavelength or fewer, are set to zero, thereby cutting of the
highest wavenumbers. This eliminate instabilities caused by the non-linearities which presumably
results in the model breaking down. It is a ﬁne balance when using ﬁltering, because energy is

3.5
Spectral implementation
For solving the discrete linear system Ax = b for x presented in section 2.5 and 2.6 the fast iterative
matrix solver GMRES, within MatLab, is used as mentioned above. This solver uses the Krylov
subspace iterations method. The input in GMRES must be a square coeﬃcient matrix A, and the
right hand side (column) vector b must have length n, where A is a n-by-n matrix. Generation of
the coeﬃcient matrix A would for large problems require signiﬁcant storage capacity. To avoid this
a function is made which, for input x, returns the matrix vector product Ax, thus avoiding the
generation of A itself. This function conveniently replaces the matrix A in GMRES and thereby
makes the iteration process ’matrix free’. This approach is one of the elements that contribute to
the above mentioned speed and low storage requirements of the spectral method.
The key to the success of any Krylov subspace method used to solve linear systems lies in
eﬀective preconditioning. A good precondtioner must approximate A well, yet also be ”easy”
to solve. GMRES uses the preconditioner M to eﬀectively solve the system M ≠1Ax = M ≠1b.
Using the same analogy as for the coeﬃcient matrix A, a function is made which for an input
b returns (M ≠1b). This function replaces M in GMRES making also the preconditioning matrix free.
In the following the functions outlined in the above will be further described.

Introduction
In this chapter a validation of the present numerical Boussinesq model is performed. When devel-

4.3.4
Discussion
The results presented in Section 4.3 demonstrate the correct implementation of the non-linear terms
in both models, showing excellent agreement with the stream function solution of steepness up to
H/L = 0.120. As expected, increased non-linear accuracy is obtained when using the two-step model.
However when increasing the steepness and thereby the eﬀect of the non-linear terms the two-step
model becomes numerically unstable and eventually breaks down. An explanation for this behaviour
is most likely related to the added complexity of the Boussinesq equations used in the two-step
model. As was seen from equations 2.19 and 2.20 the governing equations of the two-step model
require solutions of up to tenth order derivatives. This is quite computationally challenging and
likely to be the reason for the numerical instabilities causing a break down when simulating waves
with a steepness greater than H/L = 0.120 - using the two-step model.
This numerical stability issue potentially limits the expected beneﬁt of increased non-linear accuracy
from the two-step Boussinesq formulation, when simulating steep highly non-linear and challenging
problems. In the following the non-linearity of the two models will be examined further by analysing
steep highly non-linear crescent water wave patterns.

In this chapter numerical simulations of diﬀerent test cases, designed to systematically validate
the correct implementation of the present high-order Boussinesq model, has been presented. The
diﬀerent test cases were simulated using both the one- and two-step model.
The ﬁrst test case involved linear waves in one horizontal dimension, and prefect results where
achieved using a discretization of 32 points per wavelength conﬁrming the range of surface accuracy
of roughly kh Æ 25 from Madsen et al. (2002), for both models.
The second validation case included similar simulations of non-linear waves using a stream function
solution for comparison. Very accurate results were presented of a steepness up to H/L = 0.120
demonstrating correct implementation of the non-linear terms in both models. The two-step model
giving slightly more accurate results compared to the one-step model. This conﬁrmed the ﬁndings of
Madsen et al. (2002) who proposed this two-step formulation showing increased non-linear accuracy
in one horizontal dimension. However this test case also illustrated the increased numerical insta-
bility of the two-step model compared with the one-step model, when simulating highly non-linear
waves with a steepness greater then H/L = 1.20. This numerical instability is most likely related
to the added complexity of the Boussinesq formulation used in the two-step model, requiring a
solution of up to tenth order derivatives, which is very demanding computationally. The one-step
model on the other hand was capable of simulating waves with a steepness of up to H/L = 0.135,
for at least on wave period, before numerical instabilities occurred due to the non-linear terms.
The ﬁnal validation case involved the three dimensional steep highly non-linear crescent wa-
ter wave patterns, which conﬁrmed the result of the previous test case. It was illustrated that both
models are capable of simulating these ,deep-water highly non-linear, wave patterns accurately,
conﬁrming the correct implementation. However the two step model showed the same unstable
behaviour when highly non-linear waves were simulated, requiring increased ﬁltering resulting
in hampering of the crescent wave development. This stability issue, presumably will limit the
expected beneﬁt of increased non-linear accuracy from the numerical solution of the two-step
formulation, when simulating steep highly non-linear problems.

two-step Boussinesq model and illustrates the capability and accuracy of the models. Madsen

5.1
Introduction
Focused wave ﬁelds are believed to represent the surface elevation of extreme ocean waves in deep
water. These highly non-linear, near breaking wave events are of great interest for coastal and
oﬀshore engineering (Fuhrman and Madsen 2006).
In this chapter the present numerical Boussinesq model is used to predict and study the ele-
vation and kinematics of such extreme wave events. Both the one- and two-step model are used for
the simulations of these extreme events, and the computed results are compared with experiments
to determine which model is most attractive.
To perform simulations involving focused wave ﬁelds the work of Johannessen and Swan (2001)
involving focusing wave events, that are spread both in frequency and direction, are considered.

5.5
Conclusion
In this chapter physical challenging cases involving highly non-linear focusing wave events, caused
by the focusing of irregular directional spread water waves, were considered. These extreme events
were based on the experiments of Johannessen and Swan (2001) and were simulated using both
the present one- and two-step models. Following Bateman et al. (2001) and Fuhrman and Madsen
(2006) the focus has been on the six cases from the Johannessen and Swan (2001) experiments
having the largest linear amplitude sum from the most narrow- and broad-banded spectrum covering
a large range of directional spreading. These cases represented very demanding cases which were
near their breaking limit.
In all cases considered, the results computed using the one-step model were very impressive.
The computed free surface elevation using the one-step model is very accurate for all six cases
comparing well with experiments but also with results from other models described in previous
works such as Bateman et al. (2001) and Fuhrman and Madsen (2006). The same applies for the
velocity kinematics which also compare well with experiments and with results from other models.
This establishes that the present one-step high-order Boussinesq model based on a spectral method
is capable of accurately simulating extreme wave events. The spectral method used, providing
spectral accuracy and low storage, maintain comparable computational speed compared to the
model used in Fuhrman and Madsen (2006) which uses a ﬁnite diﬀerence method in a lower level
Fortran code.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis set out to develop and test new options for improving the ability to simulate two and
especially three dimensional non-linear water wave events, with the hope of providing further insight
into the complicated physical processes involved.
This was pursued through the adoption of the high-order Boussinesq formulation of Madsen
et al. (2002), which was solved numerically in two horizontal dimensions using a (pseudo) spectral
method for solving the partial diﬀerential equations of the Boussinesq formulation on a ﬂat bed.
This method provided an elusive combination of high accuracy combined with low storage resulting
in relatively eﬃcient computations, which are all essential elements for large demanding physical
water wave problems. Especially the spectral implementation described in Chapter 3 providing a
matrix free environment for the solution to the Boussinesq formulation makes the present model
very applicable for large demanding problems. It is also important to mention, that this spectral
method also provided relative simple and straight forward numerical implementation using Fast
Fourier Transformation when calculating up to tenth order derivatives, which would have been
extremely complicated with other methods.
The method described in Bingham et al. (2009), providing a velocity potential formulation of the
Boussinesq equations instead of the velocity formulation, was also adopted, hence reducing the
number of unknowns. This formulation presumably reduces the work load compared to the velocity
formulation.
Furthermore a numerical solution in two horizontal dimensions, to the Boussinesq formulation of
Madsen et al. (2002), describing the ﬂuid domain in two steps was attempted. First step going
from the sea bed to the still water datum, and the second step from the still water datum to the
free surface. This formulation was also adapted from the original work of Madsen et al. (2002) who
presented a solution to this in one horizontal dimension, showing increased non-linear accuracy
compared to a solution describing the hole domain from the sea bed to the free surface in one step.
Throughout this thesis, it has been established that the high-order Boussinesq formulation of
Madsen et al. (2002), describing the ﬂuid domain in two steps, can indeed be solved and imple-
mented numerically in two horizontal dimensions using a (pseudo) spectral method. This is in its
self quite an accomplishment as there presumably is no previous solution to this problem, except for

the one dimensional solution presented in the ordinal work of Madsen et al. (2002). Furthermore a
numerical solution describing the hole domain in one step, using a (pseudo) spectral method, is
also successfully presented.
Despite the advantageous gained from using a spectral method, distinct diﬀerences in the numerical
stability of the two numerical solutions (models) were seen. Namely that the one step numerical
solution is much more stable when high non-linearity is combined with large depth or reﬁned grids.
This is an important conclusion, as many of the practical wave events of interest in this thesis
involve highly non-linear waves in deep water. Given that, the two-step numerical solution was also
substantially slower, it can be concluded that the one-step numerical solution seems most attractive
for simulating highly non-linear wave events in two horizontal dimensions.
This conclusion comes from a thorough validation of the two numerical solutions, described
in Chapter 4. It was through varies test cases of linear and non-linear wave simulations, both in one
and two horizontal dimensions, seen that the two-step numerical solution required increased ﬁltering
when highly non-linear waves where to be simulated. This numerical instability of the two-step
numerical solution is most likely related to the added complexity of the Boussinesq formulation,
which was presented in Chapter 2. In Chapter 2 it was seen that the governing equations, within the
two-step formulation, required solutions of up to tenth order derivatives, which is very demanding
computationally and thereby presumably limits the beneﬁt of increased non-linear accuracy from
the two-step Boussinesq formulation.
Having established that the one-step approach is most eﬃcient and stable numerically and after a
thorough validation procedure of the numerical implementation, focused wave events were consid-
ered in Chapter 5.
Six demanding cases from the Johannessen and Swan (2001) experiments, which were near their
breaking limit where considered. The computed result from the one-step numerical solution showed
excellent agreement with the experiments for both the free surface elevation and the velocity
kinematics, witch maintained comparable computational speed to the numerical model used by
Fuhrman and Madsen (2006). The same six cases where also considered with the two-step numerical
solution, as expected showing decreased accuracy due to the increased ﬁltering necessary to avoid
numerical instability.
This thesis demonstrates that the high-order Boussinesq approach of Madsen et al. (2002), using a
(pseudo) spectral method, is ideal for studying highly non-linear water waves and irregular seas
in two horizontal dimensions. The developed one-step model is capable of eﬃciently simulating
three dimensional highly non-linear waves in deep water, giving a good insight into the complicated
physical phenomenons of non-linear wave events.

Recommendations
In the work with this thesis, a number of ideas have emerged that would have been interesting to
pursue if time had allowed. Some of these ideas are provided below as recommendations for further

6.1. RECOMMENDATIONS

work and research.
This work demonstrated two-step model, in the present formulation, seems less attractive nu-
merically than the one-step model. An interesting idea would therefore be to replace the velocity
potential with the horizontal velocities, as in the originally Boussinesq formulation of Madsen et al.
(2002). The velocity formulation reduces the order of derivatives that must be taken compared to
velocity potential formulation. This may provide a more numerically stable two-step solution. This
implementation would of course increase the number of unknowns in the Boussinesq formulation
and therefore require more computational eﬀort, but if this on the other hand provides a more
numerically stable two-step solution this, would indeed be interesting and potentially deliver the
improved non-linear accuracy.
Another interesting idea would be to incorporate the calculation of pressure and force in the
numerical model giving the ability to predict the forces of extreme wave events, which are of
great interest for design of oﬀshore structure and ships. As the present model predicts the wave
kinematics very accurately and eﬃciently it would be straight forward to include force calculations
on slender bodies using the Morison equation.
