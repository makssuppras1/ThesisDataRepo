Abstract
The object of this Master’s Thesis is to ﬁnd the best performing Particle Filter
(localization ﬁlter) in environments where the measurements from a robot (the
local map) does not match completely to the a priori map (the global map),
resulting in a wrong estimate of the robot position.
Throughout the report, the theory of particle ﬁlter is explained, as well as the
diﬀerences between particle ﬁlter versions. Three ﬁlters are tested against each
other: The Monte Carlo Localization (MCL) ﬁlter, the KLD sampling MCL ﬁlter
[1] and an Optimal Particle Filter [3].
A laser scanner simulator is constructed in order to create a testing environment
to add obstacles for the localization algorithms.
Diﬀerent tests are carried out, including a comparation test of the laser scanner
simulator and the laser scanners on the robot in use and a performance test of
the particle ﬁlters in an obstacle free environment. Lastly a set of simulations
are performed, starting with a simulation with a few measurement obstacles and
ending with a very high amount of measurement obstacles.
After several simulations and tests, the conclusion is that the KLD sampling MCL
ﬁlter is the best particle ﬁlter for MiR100.

Introduction
Robots, the avatars of computers in our physical world, has come to stay. They
come in many shapes and sizes and for many diﬀerent purposes.
The most
widespread robot is the robotic arm. The popularity of the robotic arm comes
from its ability to work very accurately, for very long hours in controlled environ-
ments. The industrial sector in the western world uses robotics arms for repetitive
tasks like welding cars, pick & place tasks and other tedious work, that can be done
in a stationary position. Mobile robots are beginning to emerge in the industrial
sector as well as the commercial sector in the shape of warehouse robots, vacuum
cleaners and lawn mowers.
A company developing autonomous mobile robots for internal logistics in small
and medium-sized enterprises is the Danish robot company MiR (Mobile Indus-
trial Robots).

The main product from MiR is the MiR100, which is a diﬀerential drive robot
with four supporting caster wheels. The caster wheels in front are connected to
the driving wheels through a boogie suspension to maximize traction of the driving
wheels.
MiR100 has a wide range of sensors, including two laser scanners, stereo camera,
an IMU, wheel encoders and two sonar.
The laser scanners measure distances up to 30 meters in a 270◦angle. The laser
scanners are positioned diagonally on the corners of MiR100 giving a 360◦angle
coverage.
The stereo camera and the sonar sensors are positioned at the front to observe
objects invisible to the laser scanners (table tops, glass door/windows, etc.). The
wheel encoders and the IMU are used to calculate the odometry of MiR100.
The on board computer of MiR100 runs a Linux distribution from which all robot
behaviour is controlled. The Linux distrobution runs ROS (Robot Operating Sys-
tem), which is an open source operating system designed to provide a framework
for communication between devices. Both C/C++ and Python scripts are sup-
ported by ROS.

make that happen. It is assumed that some a priori knowledge is present in the
form of a map of the surroundings. According to [2, Introduction to Autonomous

The motion of MiR100 is subject to noise and even though the robot knows where
it is going the end position is never guaranteed. Therefore MiR100 must be able
to perceive the environment, in which it is in, making one or several measurements
of the local surroundings. This is the Perception step.
The Localization step takes the information retrieved in the Motion Control step
and the Perception step and ﬁnds the best position in the global map.
The scope of this thesis is mainly Localization with a touch of Perception.

Problem Formulation
At the moment MiR100 uses an open source library for localization called amcl.
Despite the name, the amcl does not refer to the Augmented Monte Carlo Localiza-
tion (AMCL) ﬁlter presented in [1, Probabilistic Robots] which addresses the "Kid-
napped Robot" issue. Instead the amcl library implements another version of the
particle ﬁlter in [1, Probabilistic Robots] called Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD)
sampling Monte Carlo Localization (MCL) ﬁlter which addresses the density of
the proposal distribution from the particle ﬁlter. The KLD sampling MCL ﬁlter
is derived from the basic particle ﬁlter called Monte Carlo Localization (MCL).
In the preliminary investigation of the issue, a publication from a Spanish group
came up: [3, Optimal Filtering for Non-parametric Observation Models: Appli-
cations to Localization and SLAM] by Blanco, González & Fernández-Madrigal.
This publication should provide an algorithm for an optimal version of the Particle
Filter.
The amcl library uses an a priori map, robot motion commands and measure-
ments of the surroundings to localize the position of the robot. Even though the
amcl library works well the localization sometime fails. The main reason for the
localization failure is when objects appear in the surroundings that are not in the
a priori map. Theses objects could be people, moved furniture, temporary stored
goods etc. The real world/map comparison fails when the the local map (from
the laser scanner measurements) does not resemble the a priori map. MIR wishes
to investigate if the Optimal Particle Filter (OPF) can help MiR100 to perform
better by minimizing the potential failure.
In the light of the above this Master Thesis investigates:

Chapter 1
Particle Filter
1.1
Introduction
This chapter present the basic theory of particle ﬁlters. Particle ﬁlters are part
of the family nonparametric ﬁlters which are distinguished by having the distri-
bution of the robot position as a set of samples or particles rather than having a
parametrized normal distribution like the Kalman ﬁlter family.
Some of the advantages of the particle ﬁlter are multi modality, the ability to rep-
resent arbitrary distributions and it allows for easy implementation.
The particle ﬁlter is derived from Bayes ﬁlters but unlike other Bayes derived ﬁl-
ters like the Grid localization ﬁlter, the past probability of the distribution is not
directly included in the algorithm due to degeneration of the probability weight.
Instead the particle ﬁlter uses a resampling method to avoid the importance weight
degeneration.
The MCL is the base of all the ﬁlters therefore it will be thoroughly explained.
The other particle ﬁlters are derived of the MCL so the diﬀerences of these will be
explained as well.
1.2
Monte Carlo Localization (MCL)
The most simple particle ﬁlter is the MCL. Much like conventional ﬁlters (KF,
EKF, etc.) it consist of a motion model update followed by a measurement update.

1.2.2
Measurement Model: Weighting the Particles
Each particle in the particle ﬁlter represent a hypothesis of where the robot is
located in the a priori map. To decide which particle matches the true robot
position, some kind of perception of the surroundings is needed, as well as an a
priori map. A measure of how correct the particle position is, can be extracted
from a comparation of the measurements and the map.
While a laser scanner is used for measurements, two or more methods exist to com-
pare the measurements to the map. The method discussed here is the Likelihood
ﬁeld method, which project the given sensor measurements to coordinate frame
of each particle. The likelihood of how correct each measurement, is compared to
the map is calculated and from this the weight of the particle is found.
Throughout this section the weighting of the particles from the likelihood ﬁeld
method is explained by using a small case. The case involves one set of sensor
measurements, a small map and an arbitrary particle set.

1.2.3
Resampling of the Particles
In the resampling step of the particle ﬁlter, the algorithm makes a distribution
from the importance weight of each particle. The importance weight is the prob-
ability of each individual particle, as found in the previous subsection. Using the
distribution of importance weights, the ﬁlter creates a new set of particles, where
each new particle is more likely to spawn at the position of a high valued particle
(an important particle) and less likely to spawn at the position of the low valued
particle.

(KLD)
The Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD) sampling MCL algorithm is based on the
MCL algorithm. The theory behind the algorithm will not be explained in depth

here, instead the practical understanding of the algorithm will be presented.
The main objective of the KLD sampling MCL is to adapt the number of parti-
cles in the particle set to an adequate size representing the current distribution.
During global localization, a lot of particles are needed to make the distribution
dense enough to ensure the convergence at the robot position. But after the par-
ticles have converged, the high amount of particles is not needed to represent the
distribution, since it only occupy a small space compared to the whole map.
So instead of keeping the same particle set size, the KLD sampling MCL algorithm
uses the density of the distribution to adjust the size of the the particle set. If the
particles are positioned close to each other, fewer particles are resampled.
This is done by dividing the map into a histogram of 3 dimensions (x, y, θ). In
the resampling step, when a particle has been resampled, the particle is put into
the according 3D histogram bin. The more particles put into diﬀerent bins, the
greater the particle set will become.
The ﬁnal amount of particles in the set is determined by a lot of diﬀerent param-
eters: The size of the bins (sx, sy, sθ) are parameters, as well as the two statistical
error bounds ϵ and δ. The statistical bounds work in such a way that there is a
probability of 1.0 −δ that the error between the two distributions is less than ϵ.

Subconclusion
The MCL ﬁlter contains all the basic features of the particle ﬁlter. The proposal
distribution is created by the randomness of the motion model combined with the
drawing of particles through the importance weight. The importance weights is
found by giving each particle a measure of their accuracy of position according
to the measurements. The measurement model depend on the sensor type, in the
case of MiR100, a likelihood ﬁeld model is used since this is normally used on laser
scanners.
The Low Variance Sampler algorithm was highlighted due to its property of en-
suring an evenly spread of particle selection and still keeping the consideration the
importance weights.
The KLD sampling MCL algorithm addresses the issue of excessive number of
particles for smaller distributions. The algorithm spawn an appropriate amount
of particles for a particular distribution. The adaptive behaviour of the algorithm
works well for global localization.

3.5
Adding Objects to Map Test
The way to add obstacles to the measurements is done by drawing diﬀerent objects
in a distinct color into the a priori map. The likelihood ﬁeld algorithm is set to
ignore objects in the distinct color and only regard the original colors of the a
priori map. The laser scanner simulator will able to see the objects and produce
measurements according to the objects.
A number of simulations will be executed, starting with a a priori map without
obstacles. The next simulation include 10 obstacles in the a priori map, the sim-
ulation after that include 20 obstacles and so forth. Obstacles are being added at
the beginning of a new simulation, until all three ﬁlters break down.
To identify the point of break down, a comparable numerical measure between
simulations is needed. The mean non-normalized importance weight of the par-
ticle set decreases when objects appear in the local map of the measurements.
Unfortunately, the mean non-normalized importance weight rely on the number of
used measurements from the laser scanners and the settings of the likelihood ﬁeld.
To account for this, the mean non-normalized importance weight is divided by the
theoretical maximum value of the mean non-normalized importance weight. This
theoretical maximum value can only occur if all measurements ﬁt the a priori map
at every position of the particles.
The importance weight of each particle depends on the output from the measure-
ment model. The output of the measurement model relies on the amount of used
measurements from the laser scanner and the settings of the likelihood ﬁeld. As
mentioned in section 1.2.2 only

Conclusion
The conclusion to the Problem Formulation is that the KLD sampling MCL ﬁlter
and the Optimal Particle Filter perform equally good, and slightly better than
the MCL ﬁlter. The Optimal Particle Filter has a slightly more narrow proposal
distribution than the KLD sampling MCL ﬁlter but in the simulations, it did not
make a diﬀerence to the mean squared errors of the proposed positions. The KLD
sampling MCL ﬁlter is currently implemented on the MiR100 and since the Op-
timal Particle Filter uses a lot more computational resources, the best ﬁlter for
MiR100 is the KLD sampling MCL ﬁlter.
Throughout this report, the mechanisms of the MCL ﬁlter, the KLD sampling
MCL ﬁlter and the Optimal Particle Filter have been investigated. The MCL ﬁl-
ter is the basic version of the particle ﬁlter with a ﬁxed number of particles. The
KLD sampling MCL adapt the size of the particle set to an appropriate amount
according to the size of the proposal distribution. The Optimal Particle Filter
oﬀers an optimal solution to the proposal distribution on the expense of computa-
tional resources. This knowledge was used to build the particle ﬁlters in order to
compare their performances with regard to localization with obstacles in the local
map.
A simulator for the laser scanner was made in order to provide an easy method
for the detecting of virtual objects for the measurement model.
The particle ﬁlters were tested, on both real runs on the MiR100 and in the simula-
tion scripts described in section 3.2. This test showed that laser scanner simulator
could substitute the MiR100 laser scanner in the further testing.
The optimal amount of auxiliary particles in the Optimal Particle Filter was found,
so the ﬁlter did not use unnecessary computational resources.

The three ﬁlters were tested in order to establish that they work as intended, fol-
lowed by several tests with obstacles included. The last set of simulations revealed
that the MCL ﬁlter was the ﬁrst to break down when a lot of obstacles was added.
Both the KLD sampling MCL and the Optimal Particle Filter performed slightly
better, though they were only marginally better than the MCL ﬁlter. This result
led to the main conclusion of this report.
Future Work
The research in this Master’s Thesis did not improve the Localization of MiR100,
but a suggestion for future work could be to implement an algorithm using the
mean normalized importance weight and the best normalized importance weight
(from section 3.5) to detect when the a priori map is not up to date. Another
usage of the two measures could be serving as an indicator to the particle ﬁlter
getting close to diverge.
