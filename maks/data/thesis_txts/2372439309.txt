Abstract
Gas fuel injection systems developed by the large-bore diesel engine producer MAN Diesel &
Turbo are complex arrangements of mechanical and hydraulic components that allow the intro-
duction of natural gas into the combustion chambers of an engine. Such systems are susceptible
to faults that develop at the control valves used to control the ﬂow of gas during an injection.
This thesis employs the methods of fault diagnosis to facilitate a hybrid of signal-based and
knowledge-based fault detection and isolation (FDI) techniques with the purpose of detecting
and isolating a selected number of faults that may arise in the gas injection system.
The control volume analysis approach is used to derive a mathematical model of the unsteady,
compressible ﬂuid behaviour demonstrated by the gas that ﬂows in the gas injection system.
The model is described by diﬀerential equations that take the form of an initial boundary value
problem (IBVP), which is implemented numerically by using the ﬁnite diﬀerence method. The
derived model simulates the propagation of faults onto the response of a pressure measurement
which, in the real world, is used to monitor the state of the gas injection system. Uncertainty is
imposed on the model parameters as well as in the magnitude of the faults in order to explore
their eﬀects on the uncertainty of the model output. A sensitivity analysis is performed that
explains how the output uncertainty can be apportioned to the diﬀerence sources of model
uncertainty, which provides a variable signiﬁcance ranking that demonstrates which uncertain
model components explain most of the output variance. This technique is used to locate samples
in the model output time series that carry fault signatures and are insensitive to parameter
variations. The fault diagnosis problem is reformulated as a classiﬁcation problem, in which a
feed forward neural network is used as a form of knowledge-based FDI technique in order to
isolate the selected fault cases. The network isolates between the diﬀerent faults by feeding a
sequence from the output time series.
A performance comparison is done between using the samples in the model time series that
carry fault signatures as an input to the neural network against using the entire time series as
the input. The results show that a signal-based sensitivity analysis on the output uncertainty
is a viable method to improve the performance of a classiﬁcation neural network.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction and motivation
MAN Diesel & Turbo SE, based in Augsburg, Germany, is a multinational company that pro-
duces large-bore diesel engines and turbomachinery for marine and stationary applications [1]
[3]. It designs two-stroke and four-stroke engines that have power outputs ranging from 450 kW
to 87 MW. In particular, MAN Diesel & Turbo SE develops dual fuel ship engines that use both
gas and liquid fuel as the reactant in their chemical combustion process[2]. The engines can
operate on a wide range of available natural gases as well as liquid fuels such as diesel, biofuel,
or heavy fuel oil. The engines can switch between liquid fuel mode and gas fuel mode without
retroﬁt requirements or other discontinuances. Such an engine provides several beneﬁts, such
as a broad power output range due to the potential variability of the heat of combustion of
the reactants, as well as the ﬂexibility of choosing between fuel sources with regards to their
availability or economic costs. Each reactant type is injected into a combustion chamber via
its own fuel injection system, i.e. a gas injection system is responsible for injecting gas and a
separate liquid fuel injection system is responsible for injecting liquid fuel.
MAN Diesel & Turbo SE faces the challenge of detecting and isolating faults that may
develop during the gas injection process. The faults are mainly defects at the control valves that
are used to control the ﬂow of gas. It is essential that the faults are detected and subsequently
treated as they are at risk of reducing the performance of the engine and potentially damaging
the equipment used in the gas injection system. Currently, faults are detected by determining
how well real time pressure measurements ﬁt against pressure data that represents a nominal
(fault absent) case of the gas injection system.
The measured pressure corresponds to the
pressure of gas that is being injected into a cylinder. Figure 1.1 displays an example of a single
set of pressure measurements and depicts the currently implemented fault detection method.
The time series exhibits a change in pressure as a function of the current angular position of
the engines rotating crank shaft. The sequence of data represents the measured pressure during
an engine cycle; upon completing a single revolution of the crank (i.e. a rotation of 360o), the
sample angle is reset to zero degrees and a new sequence is sampled for another revolution of
the crank. The time series is similar across engine cycles; that is, under fault-less conditions, the
sequence recorded during each cycle looks analogues to that of the curve in Figure 1.1. A fault
1

is determined to have emerged if the nominal reference curve (black curve) located between
sample angles 225o and 280o does not ﬁt the measurements at the same angle interval. The
current method of detecting faults has several limitations. Firstly, only a single measurement is
available to evaluate the condition of the gas injection system. This restraints the eﬀectiveness
and ﬂexibility of detecting faults as decisions are based solely on a single measurement and
cannot be veriﬁed through any other means. Secondly, the current method cannot determine
the cause for the discrepancy between the measurements and the nominal reference curve. It’s
incorrect to assume that fault events are the only reason for measurement misﬁts, since sudden
changes in the operation of the engine and its physical properties (e.g. sudden increase in engine
load, or a sudden change in the ambient temperature of the engine) can cause measurements to
deviate from the nominal reference curve. Thirdly, and most importantly, if a fault has deemed
to have emerged, it’s impossible to pinpoint the type of fault and its location in the gas injection
system. This is because the fault detection system only has knowledge of how the pressure
measurements look like for a fault less gas injection system and not for when a fault is present.
In other words, faults cannot be isolated since pressure data that represents the presence of fault
is not used to compare to the pressure measurements.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the curve ﬁt algorithm used in the current fault detection system. The black line
depicts the reference curve ﬁtted to the measurements.
The engine switches to operating in liquid fuel mode when a fault is detected, enabling the
engine to continue its operation and prevents the detected fault to cause any further problems on
the gas injection system. The sequences of pressure measurements that caused the curve ﬁtting
algorithm to deduce the presence of a fault is sent to MAN Diesel & Turbo SE and subsequently
examined by specialists. Upon completing their analysis, they send a reply back to the operators
of the engine with the possible cause of the fault and the means to ﬁxing it. This back and forth
communication is a time consuming process as operators may need to wait several days before
receiving a reply which can be problematic, for example in the case when ship using a duel fuel

1.2. State of the art
engine is out at sea and may only have limited reserves of diesel fuel.
MAN Diesel & Turbo SE wishes to improve their fault detection method so that the detected
faults can be isolated such that their type and location in the gas injection system are determined.
This would make it possible to inform operators immediately with a possible cause for the
detected abnormality. The fault can then be promptly treated, reducing the amount of time
that the engine spends in liquid fuel mode. The improved fault detection system should also take
into consideration the eﬀect of the variations in the parameters of the system, as they inclined
to cause uncertainties in the pressure measurements that can cause false alarms.
1.2
State of the art
One of the ways that the currently implemented fault detection method can be improved is by
ﬁtting the pressure measurements to reference curves that correspond to known fault cases. A
fault case is deﬁned as a circumstance in the gas injection system where a fault has emerged. The
fault reference curves would be obtained from previous pressure measurements that illustrate
the presence of a known fault. This would generate a number of reference curves equal to the
number of possible faults. In the event that the measurements do not ﬁt to the nominal reference
curve, a second comparison would be done with each fault reference curve. The fault is then
isolated if there is a match between the measurements and a particular fault reference curve.
The problem with this approach is that it does not take into account that the magnitude of a
fault and its subsequently eﬀect on the pressure measurements can vary. For example, a leaking
valve can cause varying forms in the pressure measurements depending on how much the valve
leaks. This variation in the measurements thus makes it diﬃcult to determine a fault reference
curve that correctly explains the presence of a fault.
Because of this, alternative fault detection and isolation (FDI) techniques are considered.
FDI techniques are broadly classiﬁed into three categories; model-based FDI, signal-based FDI
and knowledge-based FDI [11] [16] [18] . In the model-based FDI, a mathematical model of
the system is used to determine fault events on the system being monitored.
In a general
sense, the model predicts the sensor output behaviour of the system that is generated by its
inputs. Analitical Redundancy Relations (ARRs) are used to generate residuals that represent
the deviation of the model from the system it monitors. They begin to increase in magnitude
when a fault occurs in the system as it now deviates away from the nominal behaviour of the
model. With a suﬃcient amount of sensors, the generated residuals can be used to determine
which components in the system are faulty and thus isolate the faults. Signal-based FDI makes
use of the quantitative historical data of the system sensors. Signal processing approaches, such
as gradient analysis or frequency analysis, are used to extract features from the signal that
carry fault signatures which indicate the presence of a fault in the system [36]. For example, the
frequency components of a signal may change due to a fault. Quantitative similarities between
the faulty features and the sensor data would suggest the presence of a fault. The currently
implemented curve ﬁtting algorithm is an example of a signal-based FDI. The knowledge-based
FDI does not need any expert knowledge of the system or the behaviour of the output signals.
3

Instead it uses a large historic data set that has information of each fault case and the nominal
case. The data set is used to train machine learning classiﬁcation models that can, if a fault
occurs, detect and isolate a fault.
Artiﬁcial neural networks (ANN) are well-established in
fault detection and isolation, where both feed-forward and recurrent neural networks have been
utilized with success [41] [48] [47].
1.3
Research objectives
Because of there only being a single pressure measurement available to monitor the condition of
the gas injection system, it is impossible to implement model-based FDI techniques, based solely
on the fact that it would require more sensors. The viable methods left are signal-based and
knowledge-based FDI techniques. In this thesis, the goal is to implement a hybrid of signal-based
and knowledge-based FDI techniques in order to isolate a number of faults that can occur in the
gas injection system, whilst taking into account the eﬀect of variations in system parameters on
the pressure measurements. The faults are to be detected during the cycle in which they emerge.
The ﬁrst objective is to decide on which faults are to be considered, and subsequently develop a
numerical model that simulates the gas injection system with these faults. The model is deemed
as an accurate representation of the real world system if the simulation results are comparable to
real world pressure measurements. With such a model, it is possible to introduce an uncertainty
in the model parameters, as well as a variation in the magnitude of the investigated faults,
that mimics the uncertainty in the real world system. The second objective is to conduct a
signal-based analysis on the model which aims to explain the uncertainty in the model output
time series. A combination of an uncertainty and sensitivity analysis is done, with the purpose
of determining which model parameter uncertainties and fault magnitude uncertainties explain
the variance in the output. This allows for feature extraction of the output time series, which
would produce informative points in its sample angle domain that clearly indicate the presence
of a fault. Such an analysis makes it possible to obtain a set of selected sample angles where
the output is predominantly determined by the variations of faults and not by the variations of
parameters. The third and ﬁnal objective is to use neural networks as a form of a knowledge-
based FDI technique in order to isolate the selected fault cases. The aim is to show that feature
extraction of the output uncertainty (i.e. the set of fault sensitive sample angles obtained from
objective 2) is a viable way of improving the eﬀectiveness of the neural network in classifying
the faults.

2.1
Design of the gas injection system
The gas injection process that takes place inside the gas injection system is a series of recipro-
cating operations that occur during an engine cycle, which is deﬁned as a single revolution of
the engines crankshaft. One of these operations is gas injection, which is the introduction of gas
in the dual fuel engines by the means of a gas injector. It is a high-pressure injection that injects
gas directly into the combustion chamber. The gas injection system occupies only a fragment
of entire dual fuel engine. The system and the process it carries out can be readily understood
without diving too much into the details of the engine as a whole.
The injection and combustion of gas occurs in cylindrical combustion chambers, referred to
as cylinders. A dual fuel engine is composed of multiple of such cylinders, increasing its overall
power output as multiple consecutive injections can occur. Each cylinder is equipped with a
gas injection system that is separate from its liquid fuel injection system. No two cylinders
share an injection system. A technical drawing of a cylinder furnished with the interconnected
mechanical units that make the gas injection system is given in ﬁgure 2.1.
In essence, the
gas injection process describes the series of operations that enables the ﬂow of gas from the
accumulator and into the cylinder. Flow is controlled with two control valves, the window valve
and the gas injection valve.
The pipe in which gas ﬂows is the gas channel.

can be diﬃcult to understand and is thus subdivided into three separate modules in order to
explain it in an orderly fashion. The models are explored separately and their interaction with
one another is explained. The modules are the gas accumulator module, gas block module, and
cylinder module.

2.1.1
Gas accumulator module
A technical drawing of the gas accumulator module and the names of the components integral
to its function is given in ﬁgure 2.2. The primary function of the gas accumulator is the storage
of gas used in the gas injection process.
Figure 2.2: A technical drawing of the gas accumulator module along with the names of the components integral
to its function. It is responsible for the storage of gas used in injections.
The mechanical work produced by a combustion engine depends on the amount of reactant
injected into its cylinders. The more reactant that is injected, and subsequently combusted, the
greater the caloriﬁc value of the combustion process. A supply pump is the device that pumps
the reactant into the cylinder during an injection. The amount of reactant injected depends on
the speciﬁcations and performance of such a pump. In other words, the supply pump partially
determines the maximum caloriﬁc value of an injection. Signiﬁcant amounts of gas must be
injected in the dual fuel engines for them to produce large amounts of power. On its own, the
supply pump in the gas injection system is unable to meet this demand as it cannot generate
a large enough ﬂow rate. This problem is solved by including a gas accumulator between the
supply pump and the cylinder. The introduction of the accumulator means that the rate of ﬂow
is no longer determined by the supply pump but rather by the amount of available reactant gas
inside the accumulator as well as its pressure. The accumulator stores a suﬃcient amount of
gas that can be injected. The pump supplies the accumulator with gas between injections. Gas
ﬂows out of the accumulator and into the gas channel across the window valve.
The amount of gas present in the accumulator depends on the rate of gas entering and leaving
it. During an injection, gas leaves the accumulator through the window valve at a greater rate
than that generated by the pump. This causes changes to the density, and consequently the
pressure, of the gas contained by accumulator. More speciﬁcally, the pressure rapidly falls during
an injection and rises slowly afterwards. The volume of the accumulator is designed such that
the pressure drop is not too large so that the high ﬂow rate can be maintained. In the event
that gas should be expelled from the accumulator, the purge valve is opened and the supply
pump ﬁlls the accumulator with air. The purge valve is not studied in this project.

2.1.2
The gas block module
A technical drawing of the gas block module and the names of the components integral to its
function are given in ﬁgure 2.3. The primary function of the gas block is enabling the transport
of gas from the accumulator and injecting said gas into the cylinder. Gas ﬂows within the gas
channel, a narrow pipe located inside the gas block.
Figure 2.3: A technical drawing of the gas injection module along with the names of the components integral to
its operation. Its primary function is enabling the transport of gas from the accumulator and into the cylinder.
The gas injection process is controlled with the window valve (WV) and injection valve (IV).
It is a reciprocating process that occurs every engine cycle, which is deﬁned as a single revolution
of the engines crankshaft. It is a four step process:
• The window valve opens, allowing gas to ﬂow between the accumulator and the gas channel.
• The injection valve opens, allowing gas to ﬂow between the gas channel and the cylinder.
Gas is injected into the cylinder due to the pressure being less in the cylinder than in the
gas channel. The injection valve opens when the engine is at around its top dead center
(TDC) of the combustion cycle.
• The injection valve closes, prohibiting gas to ﬂow between the gas channel and the cylinder.
• The window valve closes, prohibiting the ﬂow of gas between the accumulator and the gas
channel.
The pressure of gas inside the gas channel is measured with the gas channel pressure sensor.
From here on, this measurement is referred to as the gas channel pressure. The sensor produces
an electrical signal that is sampled by an external computer. This signal is used by the currently
implemented fault detection system that evaluates the state of the gas injection system and
determines if any fault is present.
The signal is collected in batches, where a single batch
contains the measured gas channel pressure for a single engine cycle. The sampled cycle begins
at zero crank angle degrees (CAD) relative to the bottom dead center (BDC) of the combustion
cycle and ends at 359 CAD relative to BDC. In the event that gas should be expelled from the
accumulator, the window and blow oﬀvalves are open and the supply pump ﬁlls the channel
with air. The blow oﬀvalve is not studied in this project.

2.2
Dynamics of the gas injection system
The gas injection process is dynamic in which the thermodynamic and mechanical states of
the gas injection system vary with engine cycle. The opening and closing of the window and
injection valves causes changes in pressure, temperature, density, and ﬂow rate of gas within
the gas accumulator and the gas channel. Furthermore, pressure and volume of gas inside the
cylinder varies with piston stroke. Figure 2.5 displays an example of gas pressure time series in
the accumulator, gas channel, and the cylinder. Accumulator pressure time series is obtained by
simulating the gas injection model presented in Chapter 3, whilst the gas channel and cylinder
pressure time series are obtained from measurements. An example of the dynamic opening and
closing of the window and injection valves is provided. The ﬁgure shows how the pressure in the
accumulator, gas channel, and cylinder varies during a single engine cycle. The x-axis in each
sub-ﬁgure represents the sampled CAD relative to BDC. For accumulator pressure, the y-axis
represents the pressure in bars. For the gas channel pressure and cylinder pressure, the y-axis
represents sensor measurements in milliamps. For the gas channel pressure, the y-axis represents
the scaled gas channel pressure, where the data including and after sample angle 154o is scaled
so that the value at sample angle 154o is 13.56 mA. In the valve opening time series, the y-axis
represents the opening of each valve. A nominal cycle exhibits the following behaviour:
• Injections occur at around TDC and are brief, as indicated by the relative short time the
injection valve is open in Sub-ﬁgure 2.5d. The window valve remains open for a longer
time compared to the injection valve.
• Cylinder gas pressure rises and falls in a cyclic manner due to the reciprocating piston
causing changes in the volume of the cylinder (Sub-ﬁgure 2.5c).
• After the window and injection valves are closed, the pressure inside the channel increases
as the gas is heated up by the hot engine. This is visibly seen by the red curve in Sub-
ﬁgure 2.5b. The scaling/reset of the measurements causes a sudden increase in pressure
at sample angle 154o. The gas inside the accumulator is not heated nearly as much as the
gas in the channel because of its larger surface to volume ratio, and thus its pressure stays
constant (Sub-ﬁgure 2.5a).
• Gas channel pressure falls and accumulator pressure rises when the window valve opens.
This is because the heated gas in the gas channel ﬂows into the accumulator in order to
reach a thermal equilibrium with the much cooler gas encased by it. The pressure increase
in the accumulator occurs due to the one way valve preventing any further backﬂow.
• Gas channel pressure and accumulator pressure falls when the injection valve opens. Dur-
ing an injection, the pressure inside the accumulator falls as gas leaves it through the
window valve, which consequently causes a decrease in gas channel pressure. The injected
gas is combusted, and a pressure spike is observed in the cylinder pressure.
• Gas injection stops when the injection valve closes, and the injection pump resupplies the
accumulator with gas, causing the gas channel pressure and accumulator pressure to rise.

2.3
Fault scenarios
The four fault cases that are considered in this project are:
• Hanging Window Valve (HWV) faults and Inactive Window Valve (IIV) faults.
• Hanging Injection Valve (HIV) faults and Inactive Injection Valve (IIV) faults.
These faults occur at the window and injection valves located in the gas block.
A hanging
fault is when a valve doesn’t close properly, and an inactive fault is when a valve doesn’t open
properly. Each fault and their eﬀects on the behaviour of the system is studied independently,
i.e. no more than a single fault can be present in the gas injection system. The magnitude of a
fault can vary. For instance, an inactive valve opens in some cases only to half of its maximum
opening size, where as in other cases it may not open at all. The upper and lower bounds of
the magnitude a fault are 0 and 1, where a fault with a magnitude of 0 is non existent and
a fault with a magnitude of 1 is fully developed. As stated in the research objectives in the
introduction, the faults are to be detected during the cycle in which they emerge.

Chapter 3
Thermodynamic ﬂuid modeling
This chapter presents the methodology in which the gas injection system and its possible fault
cases are modeled. Important behaviours and assumptions of the system are stated on which
the modeling approach is then based on. Mass ﬂow balances allow for the modeling of the gas
accumulator, and the cylinder is modelled as a single-zone model. A system of initial boundary
value problems describing the unsteady ﬂow of a compressible ﬂuid in the gas channel are given.
The numerical implementation of the model is provided.
3.1
System behaviours and assumptions
In essence, the gas injection system and the process it observes can be described as the evolution
of the thermodynamic and ﬂuid properties of gas inside the accumulator, gas channel, and
cylinder due to the controlled opening and closing of the window and injection valves. The rate
and direction of ﬂow varies with respect to the engine cycle, as gas expands into the accumulator
and later on ﬂows from the accumulator and subsequently injected into the cylinder. The states
of the gas evolve with respect to time and vary along the length of the gas channel. Furthermore,
the system is susceptible to four types of faults. In order to derive a numerical model that will
simulate this system, it is vital to have an understanding of its behaviours and have a sensible
list of assumptions to simplify its dynamics. The key system behaviours are:
■The volume occupied by the cylinder varies accordingly to the reciprocating piston. Changes
in the volume causes changes to the thermodynamic properties of the gas it contains.
■The volume of the accumulator is constant.
■The thermodynamic properties of gas inside the accumulator varies due to gas entering
and leaving it through the window valve.
■The supply pump will resupply the accumulator with gas. A one way valve located between
the pump and the accumulator prevents back ﬂow into the pump in the event that the
pressure in the accumulator rises above the output pressure of the pump.
■The direction of gas ﬂow across the valves and inside the gas channel varies along the
engine cycle.
17
==PAGE:44==

■The thermodynamic properties, as well as the ﬂow velocity, varies along the length of the
gas channel.
■The pressure sensor is located in the gas channel. Its location is of importance since the
pressure of gas varies along the length of the channel.
■The measurements at and after sample angle 154o are scaled so that the value at sample
angle 154o is 13.56.
■The gas inside the channel heats up due to its surroundings.
■The magnitude of the opening of the valves varies.
■The window and injection valves are susceptible to faults.
The model assumptions are:
■The gas behaves as calorically perfect gas. This enables the use of the ideal gas law to
relate its thermodynamic properties, namely the pressure, temperature, and density, with
each other. Furthermore, its speciﬁc heat at constant pressure and constant volume are
not sensitive to changes in temperature [6].
■The gas channel is a cylindrical pipe (duct) and its diameter does not vary along its length.
The length of the channel is ﬁnite.
■Friction due to the walls of the channel is modeled as Moody type friction.
■The gas inside the channel behaves like a continuum, where all ﬂuid properties are uni-
formly varying functions of time and position [40].
■The gas in the system is methane.
■Flow of gas is unsteady and compressible.
■Because the gas is assumed to behave as an ideal gas, its ﬂow across a valve is modelled
as an isentropic process, which has been proven to be a good assumption (see [17] [14] [39]
[51]). This asserts that no heat is added to the ﬂow and no energy transformations occur
due to friction.
■Elevation changes along the gas channel are ignored.
■The temperature of gas inside the accumulator and cylinder does not vary with engine
cycle.
■Gas inside the accumulator is assumed to be uniformly distributed.
■The output pressure of the supply pump is constant.
■The surrounding temperature of the gas channel does not vary with time and is constant
throughout the gas block.

describe an initial boundary value problem (IBVP), a combination of an initial value problem
(IVP) and a boundary value problem (BVP). An IVP is a diﬀerential equation that describes
the evolution of a system with time given an initial condition. A BVP is a diﬀerential equation
that is satisﬁed on the domain of the solution given a set of domain boundary conditions. The
domain of these equations is the cylindrical gas channel encased in the gas block. The domain
is one dimensional, ranging along the streamline of the duct. Its boundaries are at the inlet and
the outlet, following and preceding the window and injection valves, respectively. The IBVP
thus describes the evolution in time of the density, velocity, and energy of the ﬂuid along its
streamline. Naturally, the solution to the problem diﬀers accordingly to its initial conditions
as well as its boundary conditions.In the remainder of this chapter, the IBVP described by
Eqs. (3.20), (3.21), and (3.22) will be denoted as the gass channel problem (GCP). The GCP
represents an advanced form of the Euler ﬂow equations [5] in that it takes into account the
viscosity of the ﬂuid and the resulting wall friction exerted on it as well as its thermal conductivity
and heating from the surroundings. Multiple solutions can be derived from the IBVP depending
on the set of assumptions applied on the problem. These include, but are not limited to, steady
state ﬂow (where the velocity of the ﬂow is constant with time), incompressible ﬂow (where
variations in pressure and temperature have little to no eﬀect on the density of the ﬂow), or any
a combination of predeﬁned thermodynamic processes (such as isothermal ﬂow, in which the
temperature is constant along the streamline). It is diﬃcult to know with certainty what type
of ﬂow occurs inside the gas channel. A safe assumption is that that the ﬂow is unsteady and
compressible.

Chapter 4
Simulation results
This chapter presents the results obtained from simulating the gas injection model presented in
3. The simulated gas channel pressure results are compared with real world measurements. This
includes evaluating the similarities in the nominal case as well as each fault case. An uncertainty
analysis is performed on the model in order to study the eﬀects of parameter and fault magnitude
uncertainty on the model output time-series. The analysis makes it possible to visualize of how
the dynamic form of the output time series transforms as the magnitude of the diﬀerent fault
cases increases. A sensitivity analysis is performed in order to distinguish between the diﬀerent
parameter and fault variances that cause the uncertainty in model simulations, as well as explore
the linear/nonlinear relationship between fault variance and output variance. The objective is to
obtain a set of sample angles in the time series that contain fault features which are to be used
to increase the performance of a FDI nerual network.
4.1
Model veriﬁcation and validation
As part of a good modeling practise, the prediction results obtained from simulating the gas
injection model are veriﬁed and validated. A model that is implemented incorrectly will produce
incorrect results and conclusions drawn from them will be unreliable [26], and thus verifying and
validating a model gives insights into whether it is a good representation of the system. The
American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) deﬁnition for veriﬁcation is "the process
of determining that a computational model accurately represents the underlying mathematical
model and its solution" [4], i.e. establish that the model and its system of equations are imple-
mented and solved correctly. Examples of the veriﬁcation tests that were conducted was steady
state ﬂow analysis, in which the window and injection valves are left open until a steady state
solution is obtained and subsequently compared to the solution of the compressible Bernoulli
equation, as well as check that the boundary conditions were implemented correctly by asserting
that the isentropic relation in Eq. (3.9) is met across each valve. The ASME deﬁnition for vali-
dation is "the process of determining the degree to which a model is an accurate representation of
the real world from the perspective of the intended uses of the model" [4], i.e. establish that the
model accurately simulates the real world system by, for example, comparing simulation results
and hypothetical data from the real world system. Although model veriﬁcation and validation

don’t guarantee that the model is a perfect representation of the real world system, they can
enhance the conﬁdence in the results it produces and the conclusions drawn from them. The
results from validating the gas injection model are presented in this section.
4.1.1
Validation of simulations results with real world measurements
The certiﬁability of the predictions from the gas injection model are assessed by evaluating the
simulated gas channel pressure to that of real world measurements. It’s stressed here that the
pressure time-series measured by the real world gas channel pressure sensor depends on a variety
of factors, such as the design speciﬁcations of the engine, its operating load, its revolutions per
minute, opening times of the window and injection valves, and even the ambient temperature
of the engine, to name a few. These properties vary from cycle to cycle, and also vary between
engines. This implies that the sequences of gas channel pressure data will never be exactly the
same. Nevertheless, the time-series will often look similar (that is, illustrate the same process
or phenomena) if the batches under comparison describe an engine operating in the same nom-
inal/fault case. More speciﬁcally, the batches will more often than not contain the dynamic
features present in the gas channel pressure time series seen in Figures 2.5b, 2.6b, 2.6d, 2.7b,
and 2.7d. This also applies when comparing simulated gas channel pressure to real world mea-
surements. That is, the two diﬀerent sets of data will not be exactly the same, but they should
illustrate the same dynamic process/features if they are obtained from the same nominal/fault
case. Therefore, model simulations will be deemed as suﬃciently accurate if they portray the
same dynamics observed in the real world measurements. Consequently, a knowledge based fault
detection and isolation scheme that classiﬁes between the diﬀerent fault events based on certain
features of the output produced by the model may thus also be applicable to that of the real
world system.
Method
When performing simulations, the following model constraints are applied:
• The domain of the gas channel is discretized by introducing a set of M = 21 nodes equally
spaced apart a distance dL = 0.1m.
• The location of modeled gas channel pressure sensor is set to be a distance of Lp = 0.3m
away from the inlet of the gas channel. This is equivalent to sampling the pressure at node
i = 3.
• The window valve opening and closing times are at 170 CAD relative to BDC and 200
CAD relative to BDC, respectively.
• The injection valve opening and closing times are at 180 CAD relative to BDC (equivelant
to TDC) and 185 CAD relative to BDC, respectively.
• The openness variable α(u, fHV , fIV ) of the window valve and injection valve is passed
through a low pass ﬁlter with the purpose of slowing down their opening and closing.

4.2
Uncertainty and sensitivity analysis
An uncertainty analysis is the study of how the uncertainty (otherwise known as variance) of
the model components of a mathematical model or system aﬀects the uncertainty of its outputs.
Model sensitivity analysis is complementary to uncertainty analysis and is the study of how
the output uncertainty can be apportioned to diﬀerent sources of model uncertainty [44]. The
classes of model uncertainty that are investigated with respect to the gas injection model are:
• Parameter uncertainty that represents incomplete knowledge about ﬁxed model parame-
ters (e.g. the pipe radius of the gas channel is not necessarily ﬁxed and may vary across
engines).
• Fault uncertainty that arises from the uncertainty of fault magnitudes for diﬀerent fault
events (e.g. a leaking valve may only be leaking partially or be completely open).
Sensitivity analysis can be viewed as an analysis of variance, in which the cause of the output
variance is quantitatively rooted back to the diﬀerent model uncertainties [25]. This approach
38
==PAGE:65==
4.2. Uncertainty and sensitivity analysis
provides a parameter and fault signiﬁcance ranking that explains which uncertain model com-
ponents explain most of the output variance. Furthermore, it is possible to explore the linearity
of the model uncertainties with respect to the output (e.g. are certain model variations only
noticeable if their magnitudes exceed a certain threshold). An uncertainty and sensitivity anal-
ysis is performed on the gas injection model in order to study the eﬀects of parameter and fault
uncertainties on its output uncertainty and quantitatively determine which model uncertainties
explain its variance. To be more speciﬁc, the motivations are:
M.1 Solely explore the eﬀects of fault uncertainty on the output uncertainty whilst not including
parameter uncertainty. This aids in the understanding of the eﬀect of the diﬀerent faults
on the output. The output uncertainty is displayed graphically showing its variance across
the sample angle domain.
M.2 Illustrate the transformation of the output time-series as the magnitude of a fault in-
creases. As an example, a hanging injection valve fault with a magnitude of 1 results in a
rapid decrease in gas channel pressure (evident in both model simulations and real world
measurements in Sub-ﬁgure 4.3c), but it’s unknown how the output time-series looks when
the fault magnitude is less than 1 or how large the magnitude must be before this distinct
feature in the time-series begins to surface.
M.3 Explore the linearity of between fault uncertainty and output uncertainty. Linearity would
imply that scaling the fault magnitude results in an equivalent scaling of the output. For
example, a linear hanging injection valve fault implies that increasing its fault magnitude
would cause a linearly proportioned increase in the pressure drop.
M.4 Analyze how fault uncertainty may be more prevalent in certain regions of the output
time-series. That is, diﬀerent faults may result in varying degrees of output uncertainty
along the sample angle domain.
M.5 Analyze the eﬀect of fault uncertainty in addition with parameter uncertainty, and compare
how the output uncertainty has changed.
M.6 Explore the eﬀect of parameter and fault uncertainty on the gradient of the output time
series.
M.7 Perform a parameter and fault sensitivity analysis to determine how the uncertainty of
the output and its gradient at diﬀerent sample angles can be apportioned to the diﬀerent
uncertainties.
The goal is to locate sample angles where the faults are most sensitive
compared to the parameters.

4.2. Uncertainty and sensitivity analysis
Fault uncertainty is speciﬁed by the upper and lower bounds of the magnitude of each fault
case. Each case uses the same bounds. Two sets of fault uncertainties are used:
• Fault uncertainty set 1: The upper and lower bounds of the magnitude of a fault are
speciﬁed as 0 and 1, respectively. This set contains the entire spectrum that a fault may
lie in (i.e. no fault present or fault fully developed)
• Fault uncertainty set 2: The upper and lower bounds of the magnitude of a fault are 0.95
and 1, respectively. This set contains the upper region of the fault spectrum, which is
where a fault is fully developed.
There are four reasons for using two sets of fault uncertainties:
1. Explore the eﬀect of the entire fault uncertainty spectrum on the output uncertainty, visu-
alizing how incremental increases in the magnitude of a fault causes incremental changes
in the output time-series.
2. Explore the eﬀect of small variances on fully developed faults on the output uncertainty. In
some cases, a fault only becomes noticeable when its magnitude exceeds a certain amount.
3. Study how parameter uncertainty aﬀects the output uncertainty when a fault is fully
developed. This makes it possible to illustrate the distribution of the output for a fully
developed fault.
4. Generate a time series training set that contains highly developed faults and a time series
test set that contains the entire spectrum of the faults. These two sets are used in reference
to the detection and isolation of the faults in Chapter 7.
The Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) method is used for sampling of the parameters and
faults[38].
This provides an uncorrelated, uniform distribution of the parameters and faults
between their selected upper and lower bounds, as opposed to random sampling where some
parameters/faults can have reoccurring values in the resulting sampled set. For the nominal

from the fault uncertainty set. For each fault case, N samples are selected from the model

5.1
Faults in a System
A fault is a defect or abnormal condition of a component in a system. When a fault occurs it
changes the behaviour of the system, so that the system does not satisfy the task it was designed
to do. A fault can cause a decrease in the system performance or even lead to system failure. By
using FDI and fault-tolerant control, faults can be prevented from causing loss of performance
or system failure. [8] [52]

Model-Based Approach
In the model-based approach, models that can describe both the nominal and the faulty response
of the system are utilized, i.e. using an over-constrained model. The goal is to use the model to
generate residuals, that are then used for FDI. Residuals are signals that are close to zero when
the system is operating normally, but when the residuals are above a certain threshold a fault
has occurred in the system [30]. The residuals are deﬁned as
|r(t)| > κ ≥0

|r(t)| < κ
for f = 0

where κ is a chosen threshold.
Figure 5.2 shows a block diagram of the model-based method. The FDI consists of two
blocks, the ﬁrst block residual generator takes in the input and outputs of the system and
generates residual signals. Most residuals are generated by using methods that obtain Analytical
Redundancy Relations (ARR) from the model. ARR are constraints of an over-constrained model
that are not needed to acquire the unknown variables of the system and therefore redundant.
These relations can then be used to generate residuals. There are multiple ways to ﬁnd ARR
and it mainly depends on the model, if the model is nonlinear or linear, deterministic or if a
structural model is used [8] (p. 173).
A deterministic continuous-variable model is of the form

vector, f(t) is the fault input vector and θ is the system parameters.
If the state vector is not available and the system cannot be represented with a linear
state-space representation, methods for ﬁnding analytical redundancy in nonlinear deterministic
system ([8], p. 218) can be used. When the system can be represented with a state-space model,
the ARR are found using methods both in the time-domain [8] (p. 226) and in the frequency-
domain [8] (p. 231). If a structural model of the system is available, the ARR can be found
using structural analysis, where a structure graph is used to analyze the system [8] (p. 119).

6.1
Structure of Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
An Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN) is a computational model from machine learning that is
used in: classiﬁcation using pattern recognition, image recognition, character recognition, time-
series prediction and many more. An ANN consist of processing elements (neurons) that are
highly connected to each other, where each connection between two neurons is weighted. A
neuron in an ANN is essentially an activation function (usually non-linear) that takes in a linear
combination of values from other neurons of the network and outputs an activation (the value
from the activation function).
6.1.1
Network Layers
ANNs are usually organized in three layers, the input layer, the hidden layer and output layer.
The ﬁrst layer, the input layer, is the layer that the input vector is fed straight into. The number
of neurons in the input layer are equal to the length of the input vector. There are no weights
between the input vector and the input layer and each neuron in the input layer is given an
activation equal to the corresponding element in the input vector.
The next layer is the hidden layer, it can contain one or several layers (the deﬁnition of
deep learning is an ANN with more the one hidden layer) that receive the weighted activations
from the input layer. Each neuron in the hidden layer (ﬁrst layer if there are more then one)
takes a diﬀerent linear combination of the activations from the input layer as an input, each
combination is transformed using the activation function and sent to the next layer.
The last layer of the network, the output layer, makes sure that the output of the network
corresponds to the output coordinate system. The activation functions in the output layer are

6.2.1
Gradient decent
Gradient decent is an iterative algorithm that ﬁnds parameters that minimize a cost of a multi-
variable function. It can therefore be used to minimize the cost of an ANNs cost function.
If the cost function is deﬁned and diﬀerential, then an initial weight is chosen and the partial
derivative of the cost function with respect to the weights is calculated. The weight vector is
then updated in the direction of the negative gradient of the cost function, i.e. towards the
minimum of the cost function. This is done iteratively until a minimum is reached or until the
gradient is smaller than a desired threshold. The gradient decent scheme is
Wk+1 = Wk −γg
(6.4)
where γ is the step size (the step size changes during each epoch), g is the gradient of the cost
function with respect to the weights and k is the current epoch (iteration).
An illustration of a gradient decent on an one-dimensional cost function (the cost function
is label J(W) instead of E(W), not be confused with the Jacobian matrix) is shown in ﬁgure
6.4. An initial weight is chosen, the gradient is found and the weight is updated, and after a
few iterations the minimum is reached.

6.2.3
Model validation
When training a machine learning model it is important to make sure that the performance of
the network is optimized and that the data is not overﬁtted. Overﬁtting is when a model is
ﬁtted so well to the training data that it starts to describe the noise or a random error in the
data instead of the behaviour of the system. Figure 6.5a shows a simple example of overﬁtting,
where the model does not follow the linear trend of the data, but has a completely diﬀerent
response that ﬁts the data perfectly instead of the system response.
(a) Overﬁtting
(b) Cross-validaiton
Figure 6.5: (a) is an example of overﬁtting, where the black line is the real data and the blue line is the overﬁtted
model (Ghiles, 2016 [19]). (b) is an example of a cross-validaiton process. The blue curve is the training error and
the red curve is the cross-validation error. While the training error continuous to decrease, the cross-validation
error, however, stops decreasing and starts to increase again after a certain point (Raphaël Cherrier, 2014 [12])
One way to compare and validate a model so that the performance of the model is suﬃcient
and the overﬁtting is minimized is to cross validate each model using the cross-validation method.
The cross-validation method partitions the data into two subsets, one for training and one for
testing and validating the model. Multiple models are created with diﬀerent complexity and
each model is trained using the training subset. Then all models are tested with the validation
subset and the model with the best performance is chosen. Figure 6.5b shows an example of
model validation using cross-validation. The complexity of the model is chosen using the global
minimum of the cross-validation error curve, because that is the model that performs best on
new data.
When an ANN is trained, the network is not made more complicated, i.e. the complexity of
the model is not altered. Instead the cross-validation is used to ﬁnd which epoch (iteration) of
the training the validation set has the best performance.

approaches are utilized, the angles of the signal that have important features, can be extracted

nonlinearities in the training data. The feature extraction in the new FDI changes the input

the hidden layer somewhere between the number of inputs neurons and number output neurons

the outer loop, shows (see ﬁgure C.1) that the number of neurons should be between 4 and 12
and therefore the number of neurons in the hidden layer was chosen to be 7.

8.1
Evaluation of the gas injection model
A considerable amount of the results obtained in this project are received through the use of
data produced from simulating the gas injection model. These results range from the comparison
between simulation results and real world measurements to the training and testing of the
FDI neural network. The conclusions drawn from these results therefore highly depend on the
performance of the model. Because of this, it is deemed as ﬁtting to start this discussion by
evaluating the model.
One of the contributing factors for the behaviour of the model are the simulation constraints
applied to it. These include the opening and closing times of the window and injection valves,
the hampering of the rate of their opening and closing through the use of a low pass ﬁlter,
the operating load of the engine, the revolutions per minute of the crankshaft, the number of
discretization nodes M applied on the domain of the gas channel, and the simulated location of
the gas channel pressure sensor. These variables had their values ﬁxed throughout the course of
this project. That is, the eﬀects of diﬀerent engine loads or valve opening times on the output
of the model were not investigated. It is therefore a possibility that results of this project, and
the conclusions derived from them, only apply for the selected simulation constraints and the
engine that they represent. An example is the sequence of sample angles where the inﬂuence of
the fault cases had a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the gradient of the output; it is very possible that they
may be diﬀerent for a another set of simulation constraints.
During the course of the project, it was highly considered to model varying simulation con-
straints as simulation uncertainties and include them in the uncertainty and sensitivity analysis.
This would have extended the analysis, as the eﬀect of the simulation constraints on the un-
certainty of the output would have been studied. A multiple linear regression could have been
done that included these constraints as independent variables, and a subsequent standardiza-
tion of the regression coeﬃcients could have been carried out to investigate their signiﬁcant on
the output. Thus a set of sample angles could have been selected that had low standardized
regression coeﬃcients for these simulation constraints. These sample angles would be the most
optimal inputs to use for the FDI neural network, as they represent the sample angles where
faults variations have the most eﬀect and parameter/simulation constraint variations have the

least eﬀect on the gradient of the output.
One of the challenges in this project was the modeling of the ﬂow of gas across the window
and the injection valves. The issue was that the large pressure diﬀerences across these valves,
often times over 100 bars, and that, in the real world, they are opened and closed quite rapidly.
The simulation would generate large time derivatives for the model states (especially the ﬂuid
velocity) when the valves opened, causing them to respond rapidly and grow signiﬁcantly in
size. Closing the valves in a swift fashion would cause the simulation to become unstable un-
less a signiﬁcant amount of discretizations nodes were implemented (at least M = 300, based
on experiments conducted on the model). This would make simulations take too much time.
In order to compensate for this problem, the opening and closing of the window and injection
valves was slowed down through the use of a low pass ﬁlter, slower than what happens in real-
ity. Unfortunately, this meant that the model behaviour deviated away from how a real engine
behaves, which questions the validity in the gas channel pressure it simulates. Despite these
complications, the gas injection model was able to simulate the same process observed in a real
engine. Comparison between simulated gas channel pressure and real world measurements for
the nominal case, the HWV fault case, the HIV fault case, and the IIV fault case (Figure 4.3
showed that the distinct features present in the measurements were also present in the simula-
tion results. Unfortunately, no measurements were available for the IWV fault case, however
MAN Diesel Turbo & SE conﬁrmed that the gas channel pressure was simulated accurately for
this case. Even though the gas injection model is not a perfect representation of the system it
simulates, it simulates the distinct characteristics present in the gas channel pressure that arise
from the development of the faults. Based on this fact, the results obtained from the uncertain-
ty/sensitivity analysis and from fault isolation using neural networks hold a certain degree of
validity in that the developed hybrid of signal-based and knowledge-based FDI technique may
also apply for diagnosing the real gas injection system.
8.2
Correlation between the sensitivity analysis and FDI neural
network performance
In the early phase of the project, a knowledge-based FDI approach using a feed-forward neural
network was designed. The goal was to create a FDI scheme that could not only detect the
selected faults but also isolate between them. A neural network was designed that used the
model output time series from when the window valve opens until the end of the cycle (i.e.
sample angle 170o to 360o) as the input, a hidden layer consisting of 40 neurons, and ﬁve output
neurons that corresponded to each case (i.e. nominal, HWV fault, IWV fault, HIV fault, and
IIV fault). It was evident that training the network was a time consuming process since the
value for 20,000 weights and biases had to be determined. Training would take up to two hours.
Once training of the network was completed, it could easily classify between the faults in the
region of the time series it was trained for.
Even though the part of the time-series that varies the most and is not sensitive to fault,
before the opening of the window valve, had been removed from the cycle. The network was
84
==PAGE:111==
8.2. Correlation between the sensitivity analysis and FDI neural network performance
very unstable, overﬁtted and sensitive to variations in fault magnitude (if it was 0.001 instead
of 0) or if a parameter in the system was changed. A neural network can handle large variation
in data, but has to be trained to handle the variance. A new neural network was trained using
data from a parameter varying simulation of the pressure in the gas channel. The parameters of
the system were varied using a latin hypercube sampling, where the maximum variance of each
value is estimated using expert knowledge. Training the network using the new simulation data
took about the same time as the old network, despite the variations in the data. A robustness
test where the entire range of the fault magnitude was varied including parameter variations,
shows that the network is able to classify between faults quite easily, even distinguishing between
faults at magnitudes that it had not been trained for. But the network has diﬃculty detecting
the HWV fault, the response is quite noisy and it even has a false negative in the range of
fault magnitude that it was trained for. The network had been trained using data with a large
variation, but the data was still overﬁtted.
The robustness of the FDI can be improved by using a hybrid FDI approach, where a
combination of the signal-based and the knowledge-based approach is utilized. A neural network
is still used to classify the faults, but only the angles of the signal that contain important features
are fed to the network. A sensitivity analysis is used to ﬁnd the sample angles of the output
time-series that are not sensitive to parameter variations, sensitive to faults in the system and
the development of the magnitude of the fault is as linear as possible. Twelve angles are extracted
from the output time-series to be used to train the new network. Because the input layer has 12
neurons, the hidden layer contains only 7 neurons, reducing the training time of the network to
a couple of seconds (from hours). A robustness test shows that the FDI is able to detect faults
for a larger range of fault magnitude than before, the noise in the HWV has been completely
removed and no false negatives are apparent. The network has some diﬃculties detecting the
HIV fault case when the fault magnitude is in the 0.2 to 0.6 range and misclassiﬁes it as the
HIV fault. This is because this region of the HIV fault has the same characteristics as the IIV
fault in the 0.95 - 1.0 range. It is also apparent that if angles that contain "bad" features, i.e.
sensitive to parameter variations, not sensitive to faults and development of the magnitude of
the faults is nonlinear, are chosen the FDI cannot distinguish between the faults.

9.1
Conclusion
This thesis proposed a hybrid of signal-based and knowledge-based FDI techniques for the
detection and isolation of four selected faults that can develop in the gas injection system of
the dual fuel engines that are designed by Man Diesel & Turbo SE. The developed scheme
had promising results, showing that the faults were isolatable. To begin with, the structural
design and important dynamic behaviours of the gas injection system was introduced.
The
type and location of each of the four faults, as well as their eﬀect on the systems gas pressure
measurement, was presented.
A mathematical model of the system was derived that would
simulate the propagation of the four faults onto the pressure measurement.
The ﬁrst research objective, i.e. developing the mathematical model, was met by illustrating
that the derived model accurately simulates the gas injection process. This was accomplished by
comparing the simulated gas pressure to that of real measurements. The comparison consisted
of matching certain characteristics that are present in the output due to the development of
the diﬀerent faults. Unfortunately, measurements for the inactive window valve fault were not
available for comparison.
An uncertainty in the parameters of the model, as well as in the
magnitude of the faults, was introduced with the intend of mimicking the uncertainty of the real
world system. The motivation was to explore the eﬀect of how the combination of parameter
and fault variations have an eﬀect on the uncertainty of the model output.
The second research objective, i.e. conducting a signal-based analysis on the model which
explains the uncertainty in its output, was met by performing an uncertainty and sensitivity
analysis. The intend was to explore the eﬀects of parameter and fault variations on the out-
put uncertainty. The transformation of the output with respect to increasing magnitudes of a
fault was illustrated, which aided in the understanding of the implications of the faults on the
simulated gas pressure. The analysis was extended to study the eﬀect of model uncertainty on
the uncertainty of the gradient of the output. Results showed that parameter uncertainty had
limited inﬂuence on the gradient uncertainty and that its variance was primarily determined by
the uncertainty of the faults. A linear regression model of the gradient was built that regarded
the uncertain parameters and faults as independent variables. The regression coeﬃcients were
standardized, which produced a signiﬁcance ranking of the regression variables that explained
87
==PAGE:114==
Chapter 9. Conclusion & Future Work
with uncertain model components explained most of the gradient uncertainty. Performing this
analysis over the sample angle domain provided a set of sample angles in the output time series
that were insensitive to parameter variations and instead carried fault signatures that suggested
the presence of a fault.
The third research objective, i.e. using neural networks as a form of knowledge-based FDI
technique to isolate the selected fault cases, was met by formulating the fault diagnosis problem
as a classiﬁcation problem. A feed forward network was proposed that would classify the diﬀerent
faults from the sequence of sample angles that contained the gradient of the model output. The
results showed that the locations of the output gradient along the sample angle domain used
in the input to the neural network had a noticeable eﬀect on the performance of the output.
The results indicated that the neural network performed much better when its input consisted of
twelve sample angles whose SRCs for the faults were high and SRCs for the parameters were low,
as opposed of using the entire sample angle domain. The results also showed it is desirable that
variations in the gradient of the output observes a linear relationship with increasing magnitudes
of the fault as opposed to a nonlinear relationship.
Based on the results in this study it can be concluded that using signal-based and knowledge-
based FDI techniques is a viable method of diagnosing the faults that can occur in the gas
injection system.
9.2
Future Work
The proposed signal-based and knowledge-based FDI technique has promising implications when
it comes to fault diagnosis of the gas injection system. The research can be extended to incor-
porate additional faults that can develop, such as leaks in the purge and blow oﬀvalves, located
at the gas accumulator and the gas channel, respectively. A sensitivity analysis could then be
done that would include the newly added faults as regression variables. The research can be
further extended by tweaking the simulation constraints imposed on the gas injection model and
were kept constant throughout the project. The simulation constraints could be modelled as
simulation uncertainties, stretching the uncertainty and sensitivity analysis as the eﬀect of the
simulation constraints on the uncertainty of the model output would then be studied.
In this project, the eﬀect of the four fault cases on the simulated gas channel output were con-
sidered separately. That is, only a single fault was permitted to be present during a simulation.
In reality, it is very possible for multiple faults to be simultaneously present in the gas injection
system. How a combination of these faults aﬀects the simulated gas channel pressure was not
explored. The research should be extended to assess the developed hybrid of signal-based and
knowledge-based FDI technique is still viable for when multiple faults can occur.
MAN Diesel & Turbo could use the developed FDI technique on their own mathematical
model of the gas injection system. It is highly likely that their model is a much more accurate
representation of the real system, as the model derived in this project incorporates several
assumptions that may deviate the simulations of the gas pressure measurements away from
reality. MAN Diesel & Turbo could thus apply an uncertainty on the parameters and faults of
88
==PAGE:115==
9.2. Future Work
their model, as well as an uncertainty on their simulation constraints, such as the opening and
closing times of the window and injection valves. Using the proposed Monte Carlo procedure,
the eﬀects of the model uncertainties on the gradient of the model output could then be studied.
Using the sensitivity analysis technique proposed in the project, the output uncertainty can be
apportioned to the diﬀerent sources of model uncertainty, and a sequence of sample angles where
the faults have a more signiﬁcant eﬀect on the output gradient rather than the parameters could
be obtained. Applying the proposed neural network classiﬁcation scheme would then allow MAN
Diesel & Turbo to ﬁnally isolate between the diﬀerent faults that may occur in the gas injection
system.
