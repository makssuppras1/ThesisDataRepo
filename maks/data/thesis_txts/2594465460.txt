Abstract 
With the deadline being only a decade away, there is an increasing pressure to accelerate progress 
towards achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Financial support plays an important 
role in this, and it is key to direct investments towards SDG-enabling activities. This is where the new 
Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds by SDG Impact aim to contribute. 
When companies adopt the PAS for SDG Bonds, it should be ensured that the standards framework is 
linked to their business strategy to contribute to corporate sustainability and the achievement of the 
SDGs. Research has found that this link between performance assessment frameworks and strategy is 
often limited. This thesis aims to inspire and support companies in integrating the PAS for SDG Bonds 
into their business strategy, enhancing their performance on corporate sustainability. In the context 
of this thesis sustainable business model (SBM) innovation is seen as a tool to achieve this integration, 
leading to the following research question: How can the Practice Assurance Standards for SDG Bonds 
guide the sustainable business model innovation process of companies?  
In this research, SBM innovation is defined as a process of transforming a business model into a more 
sustainable business model. To guide this process, this thesis identifies and highlights the principles of 
an SBM based on a review of relevant literature. The principles represent the following characteristics 
that an SBM should include: sustainability oriented, extended value creation, stakeholder integration, 
long-term perspective, and systemic thinking. The reviewed literature also highlights and explains the 
three stages in the process of SBM innovation: the sensing, seizing, and transforming stage. The 
literature review is followed by a qualitative content analysis based on coding which systematically 
maps the links between the PAS for SDG Bonds and the SBM principles. The result from this analysis is 
subsequently used in a case study to test its applicability, with the advisory company Ramboll as a case 
company. The case study analyses whether the PAS for SDG Bonds can guide the SBM innovation 
process through the mapping analysis, answering the main research question. 
The results of the mapping analysis demonstrate that the PAS for SDG Bonds can be aligned with the 
SBM through the identified principles. The consecutive case study analysis shows that the PAS for SDG 
Bonds can guide the SBM innovation process in the sensing and seizing stage, by indicating Ramboll 
what opportunities should be further investigated in the SBM of the company and how to address 
these opportunities. This finding indicates that this thesis contributes to a corporate perspective. 
The academic contribution of this thesis is that it addresses the research gap of integrating 
performance assessment frameworks into business strategy. It also contributes to SBM literature by 
exploring how the current business model can be evaluated and new opportunities for the SBM can 
be identified. In addition, the thesis benefits policymakers as it illustrates how companies can be 
guided towards an improved performance in corporate sustainability, which will support the 
policymakers, including governments to achieve the SDGs and their sustainable development targets. 
It could also give them insights on where their support could accelerate this process. 
For future research, it is recommended to extend this thesis to the transforming stage of SBM 
innovation. Furthermore, it would be interesting to analyze if the approach in this thesis could be 
applied to other performance assessment frameworks in addition to the PAS for SDG Bonds.

1 Introduction 
To conceptualize and facilitate working towards sustainable development, the United Nations adopted 
the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015 as a common global agenda until the year 2030 
(A/70/L.1, 2015). Achieving these SDGs would lead to a world that is socially fair, environmentally 
secure, economically prosperous, inclusive, and more predictable (Business & Sustainable 
Development Commission, 2017). National and international managed financing schemes are 
necessary to support the agenda of the SDGs (Inter-agency Task Force on Financing for Development, 
2020). 
However, achieving the SDGs will require a much higher amount of dedicated capital than currently 
available (Doumbia & Lauridsen, 2019). The United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 
(UNCTAD) has identified that an annual global investment of $5 to $7 trillion is needed to meet the 
SDGs (UNCTAD, 2014). While the United Nations Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) data 
shows that current investments are still around $1.3 trillion, a substantial financing gap is implied 
(Principles for Responsible Investment, 2019). To direct investment towards activities that contribute 
to the progress of the SDGs, SDG Impact (an initiative by the United Nations Development Programme 
(UNDP)) has developed the Practice Assurance Standards (PAS). SDG Impact mentions that “there is 
currently no defined standard that enables auditors to assure that an investor’s impact management 
practice is of sufficient quality to be considered SDG-enabling,” which is where the PAS aim to support. 
This is confirmed by Eccles and Klimenko (2019), who mention in their article that there is still a lack 
of comprehensive information reported directly by companies concerning their performance on 
corporate sustainability, and even less so on the SDGs. They identify this as one of the barriers holding 
back SDG-enabling investing. Furthermore, most existing performance assessment frameworks are not 
aimed at investors, and thus of little use to them (Eccles & Klimenko, 2019). 
The PAS consist of a set of transparent standards describing best practices, which can be used as a 
benchmark for the impact practices that are needed to achieve the SDGs. Users of the PAS can test 
their adherence to the standards, and have the opportunity to achieve qualified certification for their 
SDG-enabling investment practices (SDG Impact, 2020). SDG Impact is working on three sets of the PAS: 
for Private Equity, for Bonds, and for Enterprise. This thesis will be focusing on the PAS for SDG Bonds, 
as this is the most recently developed and most progressed set of PAS. 
Researchers have found that sustainability performance assessment frameworks, such as the PAS for 
SDG Bonds, need to be incorporated into strategic levels of the companies to enable contributing to 
corporate sustainability (Morioka et al., 2018b; Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; Thijssens et al., 2016). 
However, the link between performance assessment frameworks and business strategy is still often 
missing (Maas et al., 2016b; Morioka et al., 2018b; Remmer & Gilbert, 2019; World Business Council 
for Sustainable Development, 2014), identifying a research gap. While it is still fragmented and in the 
conceptualization stage in research, companies are nevertheless being pushed to transform their 
business strategy towards sustainable development and the SDGs (Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; 
Pieroni et al., 2019; Remmer & Gilbert, 2019). This indicates the increased pressing relevance of closing 
this identified research gap.  
With the new PAS coming into view, this thesis will aim at inspiring and supporting companies in 
integrating the PAS for SDG Bonds into their business strategy, enabling their performance towards 
corporate sustainability. The sustainable business model (SBM) is seen as a tool that can potentially 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
support this integration. The link between SBMs and the PAS for SDG Bonds will be explored, 
supporting companies in linking their business strategy and sustainability practices through SBM 
innovation. The main research question for this exploration is the following: 
How can the Practice Assurance Standards for SDG Bonds guide the sustainable business model 
innovation process of companies? 
This question will be answered through three sub-questions, namely: 
1. What does the development of the PAS for SDG Bonds entail for companies? 
a. What is the goal of the PAS for SDG Bonds? 
b. What is the relationship between the PAS for SDG Bonds and other reporting initiatives 
and standard systems available to companies?  
c. What information is required for companies to adopt the PAS for SDG Bonds? 
2. How can the PAS for SDG Bonds align with the SBM? 
a. What are the principles of the SBM? 
b. Which aspects of the PAS for SDG Bonds can be mapped to which of the SBM principles 
and what are the effects of this? 
c. How can the links between the PAS for SDG Bonds and SBM support a company in its 
business model innovation process? 
3. How can Ramboll utilize the identified method for alignment between PAS for SDG Bonds and 
SBMs to guide prospective clients within the sustainability bond market? 
a. What recommendations could Ramboll give the prospective client for the adoption of the 
PAS for SDG Bonds? 
b. How should Ramboll interpret the results of the PAS for SDG Bonds in the context of SBM 
innovation? 
c. How can Ramboll use these interpretations to guide the SBM innovation process of the 
prospective client? 
In the second chapter of this thesis, a literature review will be conducted. Various important concepts 
will be defined and clarified in the context of this thesis. There will also be a focus on SBMs and SBM 
innovation, looking into the research sub-question 2a. In the third chapter the PAS for SDG Bonds will 
be analyzed and explored, including the relationship with other standard systems and reporting 
initiatives and principles. This will aim at answering research sub-questions 1a and 1b. In Chapter 4 the 
methodology for this thesis will be addressed, and Chapter 5 will focus on the analysis and findings. 
This will include the mapping of the PAS for SDG Bonds to the SBM principles, answering research sub-
question 2b. It will also include the findings of the case study, referring to research sub-questions 1c, 
2c, and 3. The analysis and findings presented in Chapter will thereafter be discussed in Chapter 6. The 
thesis will be concluded in Chapter 7. This chapter will also include the limitations of this thesis and 
propose further research in the future.

2 Literature Review 
2.1 Sustainable development in the corporate context 
Sustainable development has been given various definitions depending on the discipline and the 
context. This diversity in definitions only tends to increase (Bolis et al., 2014). Bolis et al. (2014) aimed 
to tackle the various concepts of sustainable development, and mention in their research that the most 
well-known definition is that of the Brundtland report (World Commission on Environment and 
Development, 1987), which has been widely adopted by businesses: “Sustainable development is 
development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future 
generations to meet their own needs.”  
In the transition towards sustainable development, corporations and businesses play a big role. All 
corporate activities influence the society and environment, and thus contribute to sustainable 
development (Baumgartner & Rauter, 2017). To accelerate sustainable development, organizations 
should not only focus on economic development, but also on social and environmental development. 
The three pillars of economic, social, and environmental development are widely known as the Triple 
Bottom Line (TBL) (Elkington, 1997). Focusing on sustainable development for corporations would aim 
at reducing negative environmental and social impacts of corporate activities without compromising 
the economic performance of the corporation (Baumgartner & Rauter, 2017). Businesses are impacting 
the natural environment in various ways through their operations, supply chains, and use of products 
and services. Vice versa, the natural environment also has an impact on the businesses. When 
considering the social impact, the impact and dependence of a business on employees, customers, 
suppliers, retailers, and neighbors come into view. Their prosperity and well-being play a central role 
in the success of an organization. Thus the strong case for contribution to the reduction of negative 
environmental and social impacts becomes clear and needs to be effectively managed to ensure 
business continuity (World Economic Forum, 2020). 
Besides the interdependencies on social and environmental factors, there are many more benefits of 
incorporating sustainable development in an organization: it opens up new opportunities and 
efficiency gains, drives innovation, and enhances reputation (Business & Sustainable Development 
Commission, 2017). With this enhanced reputation, the corporation will attract and retain employees, 
consumers, and investors (Business & Sustainable Development Commission, 2017; Gomez-Bezares et 
al., 2017), with the latter playing an especially important role in this thesis that will be further 
elaborated upon in section 2.3. 
2.2 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 
To conceptualize and facilitate working towards sustainable development, the United Nations adopted 
the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015 as a common global agenda up to the year 2030 
(A/70/L.1, 2015). Achieving these SDGs would lead to a world that is socially fair, environmentally 
secure, economically prosperous, inclusive, and more predictable (Business & Sustainable 
Development Commission, 2017). Although these goals are not legally binding, they have been 
accepted and agreed upon by 193 UN members, indicating international policy coordination in terms 
of sustainable development (Raith & Siebold, 2018).  
Achieving the SDGs will depend on the involvement and collaborative efforts of all parties globally, 
including businesses (GRI & UN Global Compact, 2017). The private sector is to play an important role 
in delivering on the SDGs through their innovation, responsiveness, efficiency, and provision of skills 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
and resources (Scheyvens et al., 2016). Business activities can be seen as the main drivers of the global 
economy and sustainable development (Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020), and it is therefore necessary that 
companies advance progress on the SDGs for them to materialize (Business & Sustainable 
Development Commission, 2017). Thus, engaging businesses is key in achieving the SDGs and it is only 
logical that they played a significant role in designing the SDGs and have been involved in discussions 
from the start (Scheyvens et al., 2016).  
Companies will also benefit when adopting and incorporating the SDGs into their businesses. Similarly 
to a company focusing on sustainable development, the SDGs offer a compelling growth strategy 
(Business & Sustainable Development Commission, 2017). It could lead to the benefits of having lower 
financial volatility, higher sales growth, and higher chances of survival in the long-term (Schramade, 
2017). These benefits have lead enterprises to aim at incorporating the SDGs into their business 
strategies (Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020). Especially the impacts through investments are considered to 
be significant, as the SDGs play an important role in communications with investors and other 
important stakeholders and networks by providing a common language in progress on sustainable 
development (Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020; Schramade, 2017).  
2.3 Responsible investing 
Trends show that the market of investors seeking to direct funds towards businesses that are leading 
the way on corporate responsibility and sustainability and SDGs is increasing (Adams, 2017; Eccles & 
Klimenko, 2019; GRI & UN Global Compact, 2017; Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020; Principles for Responsible 
Investment, 2019). Companies that integrate the concept of sustainable development and the SDGs 
into their core business for the long-term gain several benefits that make them more attractive to 
investors. These benefits include that the companies are likely to experience lower financial risk due 
to stronger financial performance and shareholder value creation (Eccles & Klimenko, 2019; Gomez-
Bezares et al., 2017). They also tend to be more adaptive and better-positioned for future changes in 
the competitive as well as the regulatory environments, which lowers the chances of financial risk 
(Schramade, 2017). Furthermore, companies engaging in sustainable development also encourage 
financial stakeholders’ trust in them as they are likely to accumulate resources such as reputation, 
financing, clients, effective partners, and motivated employees (Ortiz-De-Mandojana & Bansal, 2016).  
It is therefore no surprise that companies are becoming more and more immersed in improving 
corporate environmental and social development issues (Clarke, 2019), leading to incorporating the 
SDGs into their business strategies and modifying their business models accordingly (Kawamoto & 
Kanie, 2020; Schramade, 2017).  
Not only will companies attract more environmentally and socially responsible investors once their 
performance on corporate sustainability increases, but it will also provide the resources to improve 
this performance by financially enabling and ensuring sustainability oriented activities. This will in turn 
improve the company’s performance on corporate sustainability, leading to the opportunity to attract 
even more investors. This process can be described as a positive feedback loop where the 
improvement of the business’ sustainability performance will lead to more sustainability-earmarked 
capital, which leads to environmentally and/or socially responsible business investments, which will 
lead to an even further improvement of the corporate sustainability performance and so on (Figure 1).

Responsible investments supporting sustainable development are often referred to as Environmental, 
Social and Governance (ESG) investments, which are defined by the Principles of Responsible 
Investment (PRI) as investments that aim to impact environmental, social and corporate governance 
issues (Principles for Responsible Investment, 2019). With the establishment of the SDGs, ESG investing 
is slowly expanding into SDG investments, giving it a wider range but more targeted themes identified 
by the SDGs (Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020).  
A relatively new and growing financial instrument within sustainable development investing is the use 
of bonds. Three main types of bonds can be identified: green bonds, social bonds, and sustainability 
bonds. Of the three main identified bond types, the green bonds were established first with the 
issuance of the first green bond in 2007 by the World Bank. They are the most common of the three 
types with the largest market by far. However, the most compelling market growth can be seen in the 
sustainability bonds with an increase from $10.3 billion in 2017 to $18 billion in 2018 (Environmental 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Finance 2019, 2019), resulting in a substantial growth of 75%. Even though $18 billion is still limited 
compared to the $175 billion issued for green bonds, it should be recognized that sustainability bonds 
have just recently been introduced in 2014 (Gaynor & Martín, 2019). Due to an increasing interest in 
responsible investing and the SDGs, the sustainability bond issues are forecasted to continue growing 
(Environmental Finance, 2020; Gaynor & Martín, 2019). 
As the market for these bonds is quite dynamic, there is not yet a global or legal understanding of what 
defines a green, social or sustainable bond (Doran & Tanner, 2020). Multiple standards for these bonds 
have been developed. However, they all remain voluntary, allowing an issuer to “self-label” their bond 
as green, social, or sustainable. Where green bonds merely focus on environmental impacts and social 
bonds on social impacts, sustainability bonds are a combination of both. It remains unclear whether 
this includes positively contributing to both social and green aspects, or merely contributing to one 
aspect without harming the other. The International Capital Market Association (ICMA) interprets 
sustainability bonds as the latter, stating that their Sustainability Bond Guidelines protect against green 
projects with potentially harmful social impacts, and the other way around for social projects (Clapp & 
Wuennenberg, 2019). Furthermore, many new types of bonds are entering the market, such as the 
SDG-enabling bonds, transitional bonds, blue bonds, or gender diversity bonds. 
The social, green, and sustainable bond market has been mostly dominated by European issuers over 
the past few years. The percentage of these issuers that are corporations has increased from 5.9% in 
2018 to 25% in 2019 in the sustainability bond market, and this trend is expected to continue 
(Environmental Finance, 2020).  
2.4 Corporate sustainability strategies and assessment 
In the previous sections, the benefits of integrating sustainable development and the SDGs into a 
company’s strategy were outlined, as such efforts can attract more financial opportunities and support 
improved financial long-term performance.  
To achieve such benefits, corporate sustainability strategies and management are of crucial 
importance (Schaltegger et al., 2012). Building upon the definition for sustainable development by the 
World Commission on Environment and Development (1987) mentioned in 2.1, a definition of 
corporate sustainability is given by Dyllick and Hockerts (2002) as: “Meeting the needs of a firm's direct 
and indirect stakeholders (such as shareholders, employees, clients, pressure groups, communities, 
etc.), without compromising its ability to meet the needs of future stakeholders as well.” A more holistic 
approach aligned with the TBL (Elkington, 1997) is: corporate sustainability strategies are challenged 
to contribute to the economic, social, and environmental interconnections and impacts while still 
addressing short, medium- and long-term demands (Baumgartner, 2014; Morioka et al., 2016).  
Performance assessment frameworks can support navigating corporate sustainability strategies by 
helping to better understand the current situation and the desired end state (Searcy, 2012). The 
frameworks support the achievement of the corporate sustainability goals and ultimately improve 
strategic decision-making (Adams, 2017; Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; Naidoo & Gasparatos, 2018; 
Remmer & Gilbert, 2019). To define performance assessment frameworks, the definition provided by 
Searcy (2012) is adopted: “a system of indicators that provides a corporation with information needed 
to help in the short- and long-term management, controlling, planning, and performance of the 
economic, environmental, and social activities undertaken by the corporation”.  
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Despite the supporting role performance assessment frameworks play in corporate sustainability 
strategies, several researchers have found that the link between the frameworks and strategy is often 
missing (Maas et al., 2016b; Morioka et al., 2018b; Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; Remmer & Gilbert, 
2019; World Business Council for Sustainable Development, 2014). Whereas the information provided 
by the performance assessment frameworks can identify issues that pose risks or business 
opportunities (Remmer & Gilbert, 2019; Searcy, 2012), assessment frameworks are mostly aimed at 
transparency purposes rather than performance improvement purposes (Maas et al., 2016b). 
In this thesis, the limited link between performance assessment frameworks and corporate 
sustainability strategies will be addressed. The business strategy of a company will be approached 
through the innovation and transformation of business models (Ciasullo et al., 2019; Dembek & York, 
2019; Evans et al., 2017; World Economic Forum, 2020). It will be analyzed to what extent the 
opportunities identified in the performance assessment framework can guide the innovation and 
transformation of business models. 
2.5 Sustainable business models (SBMs) 
Engagement of businesses that aim for sustainable development, and specifically the SDGs, calls for a 
more transformative way of thinking. New business models that align with not only economic but also 
social and environmental aims are expected to play an important role in this transition (Agarwal et al., 
2017).  
Schaltegger et al. (2016) investigated the relevance of business models for corporate performance 
generally, and corporate sustainability specifically. After taking into account existing literature on the 
subject, they came to the following definition of business models in terms of value: “A business model 
can be defined as a concept describing what value a company proposes to existing and potential 
customers (value proposition), how the business is organized to create the value (value creation), with 
which resources and infrastructure (value creation infrastructure), under which circumstances (value 
creation conditions), and how financial value is retained for the company (value capture).” Value 
proposition, value creation (and delivery), and value capture are often the three main pillars found in 
common business model frameworks (Dembek & York, 2019; Evans et al., 2017; Morioka et al., 2017).  
From literature it can be concluded that current business models are failing in promoting sustainability 
due to their lack of integrating social and environmental externalities (Schaltegger et al., 2016), their 
focus on short-term and revenue centered value (Morioka et al., 2017, 2016; World Economic Forum, 
2020) and limitations in multiple-stakeholder inclusion (Freudenreich et al., 2019). SBMs are aiming to 
address these limitations identified in current business models. In a literature study by Geissdoerfer et 
al. (2018b) multiple concepts and definitions in the field of SBMs are explored. Based on their analysis, 
they propose the following definition for SBMs: “business models that incorporate pro-active multi-
stakeholder management, the creation of monetary and non-monetary value for a broad range of 
stakeholders, and hold a long-term perspective.” 
This definition aligns with the four principles that Breuer et al. (2018) identify as issues that an SBM 
should address. These principles can be used to increase the possibility of designing SBM without 
predefining a distinct outcome. The four principles proposed by Breuer et al. (2018) include 
sustainability orientation, extended value creation, stakeholder integration, and systemic thinking. 
These principles can be linked to the identified shortcomings of current business models, and are 
backed by many other research papers on SBMs (Baldassarre et al., 2017; Bellucci et al., 2020; Dembek 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
& York, 2019; Evans et al., 2017; Geissdoerfer et al., 2018a; Morioka et al., 2018a, 2016; Schaltegger 
et al., 2012). These papers mention a long-term perspective as a major element as well, therefore it 
will be considered the fifth principle of SBM in this thesis. The next few paragraphs will expand on 
these five principles, and an overview of the principles is given in Table 1. 
Sustainability oriented. The sustainability orientation is seen as a key requirement for SBM 
development (Breuer et al., 2018a). A sustainability oriented company expresses its purpose, vision, 
and mission in terms of social, environmental, and economic outcomes (Stubbs & Cocklin, 2008). This 
aligns with the approach to sustainable development by Elkington (1997). As environmental and social 
issues gain relevance in the strategy of a company, these aspects should also be reflected in the 
business model, for example through the definition of value (Breuer et al., 2018a). 
Extended value creation. Just as in conventional business models, the three pillars of value proposition, 
value creation, and value capture are still relevant in SBMs (Dembek & York, 2019). However, when 
focusing more on sustainability oriented business models, the meaning of value is extended by 
including ecological and social aspects besides the economic aspect (Breuer et al., 2018a; Schaltegger 
et al., 2016). This leads to considering non-monetary aspects as opposed to solely the traditional 
monetary values (Breuer et al., 2018a; Morioka et al., 2017). Additional to this is the scope of 
uncaptured values. This scope assumes that by realizing the value that is either missed, destroyed, not 
internalized or not offered, despite an existing demand for it in the market, companies can achieve 
benefiting society while also gaining competitive advantage (Yang et al., 2017).  
Stakeholder integration. Freudenreich et al. (2019) and many others (Breuer et al., 2018a; 
Geissdoerfer et al., 2018b; Morioka et al., 2016; Remmer & Gilbert, 2019; Yang et al., 2017) also 
propose actively engaging stakeholders when exploring the value of a business. They find the value of 
stakeholders essential in the development and implementation of effective sustainability solutions, as 
their perceptions of what is valuable plays a key role in the actual operation of these solutions. This 
will cause a shift in not only producing what customers find valuable, but also what multiple 
stakeholders consider to be valuable from their perspective. Thus the challenge is formed to not only 
find ways to create benefits and value for the company itself, but also for its stakeholders, in both long-
term and short-term (Morioka et al., 2016). With this also comes the challenge of considering how 
value creation for one stakeholder might lead to value destruction for another (Dembek & York, 2019). 
Examples of other stakeholders than customers are business partners, financial stakeholders, societal 
stakeholders, employees (Freudenreich et al., 2019), and the natural environment (Morioka et al., 
2017).  
Long-term perspective. Sustainable strategizing that enables long-term positive environmental and 
social impacts instead of merely pursuing short-term impacts is increasingly important for ensuring a 
company’s continuity and growth. Without it, the business model is unlikely to move towards 
sustainability (Dembek & York, 2019). The long-term dimension of an SBM considers going beyond the 
timeframe for short-term strategic planning and considering future generations.  
Systemic thinking. To allow the sustainability orientation, stakeholder integration, extended value 
creation, and long-term perspectives, the SBM should include a systemic thinking approach (Breuer et 
al., 2018a, 2018b; Evans et al., 2017; Pieroni et al., 2019). This enables the inclusion of boundary-
spanning and interdependent activities in the SBM. While keeping the company itself central, it allows 
for the integration of sustainability and stakeholder perspectives when defining the extended and 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
long-term value creation (Breuer et al., 2018a). Boons and Lüdeke-Freund (2013) identify three levels 
of analysis in the systemic boundaries of SBMs. The first and most common of these is the 
organizational level, which focusses on the individual firm and its own value-adding activities. The 
second level is the inter-organizational level, focusing on the interrelationship with other stakeholders 
to co-create and share values. Spanning the boundaries even further is the societal level, which 
focusses on the interrelationship with other organizations to produce a shared societal value. Adopting 
the inter-organizational or even societal level in the SBM is recommended as it aligns best with the 
systemic thinking principle (Pieroni et al., 2019).

2.6 Sustainable business model innovation (SBM innovation) 
Companies can use SBM innovation to transform their current business model into a more sustainable 
business model that includes the aforementioned SBM principles (Yang et al., 2017). It will allow for 
systematically integrating corporate sustainability in their strategizing process and continuously adapt 
their business strategies to changing environments and stakeholder expectations (Dembek & York, 
2019; Morioka et al., 2018a). Furthermore, it will support facing the current sustainability challenges 
(Bocken & Geradts, 2019) and achieve the potential and opportunities that sustainability strategies 
have to offer (Pieroni et al., 2019; Schaltegger et al., 2012).  
As defined by Geissdoerfer et al. (2018b), SBM innovation is “the conceptualization and 
implementation of sustainable business models”, which includes the development and acquisition of 
new business models, the diversification into additional business models, or the transformation from 
one business model to another. The transformation can affect the entire business model, the value 
proposition, creation, and capture elements and the interrelations between these elements 
(Geissdoerfer et al., 2018b). Voelpel et al., (2004) even go a bit further in this notion to suggest that 
business model reinvention is needed, which is based on disruptive innovation instead of incremental 
change or continuous improvement. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
Business model innovations are seen to be a key tool to implement sustainable solutions into 
organizations due to the useful capabilities in analyzing, structuring, planning, and communicating that 
the concept offers (Geissdoerfer et al., 2018a). The extent to which the SBM innovation will have an 
impact on societal, environmental, and economic issues, depends on the levels of ambition of the 
decision-maker (Pieroni et al., 2019).  
Within the SBM innovation process, three stages can be identified. These stages are sensing, seizing 
and transforming (Bocken & Geradts, 2019; Pieroni et al., 2019; Teece, 2018). Sensing is the stage in 
which opportunities are assessed and identified. Integrating stakeholders tends to play an important 
role in this. Mobilizing resources to address these opportunities and (re)designing and refining the 
business model refers to the seizing stage. Finally implementing the business model and realigning the 
structure and culture of the company forms the transforming stage. 
2.7 Sustainable business model (SBM) innovation and the Practice Assurance 
Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds 
The scope of this thesis within the business model innovation stages will be focused on the sensing 
and seizing stage. Sustainability performance evaluations such as reporting and measurement 
frameworks can guide the sensing stage by giving insights to the impact on sustainability issues (Adams, 
2017; Morioka et al., 2018a, 2016; Rosati & Faria, 2019a; Temmes, 2019). By implementing the PAS for 
SDG Bonds, the company’s practices will be compared to the standard best practices, identifying 
opportunities for the company in economic, environmental, and social issues. This relates to the 
sensing state.  
The next step would be to advance towards the seizing stage by acting upon the opportunities 
identified by the PAS for SDG Bonds and integrate these results into the SBM of the company. 
Researchers have found that sustainability performance assessment, such as the PAS for SDG Bonds,  
needs to be incorporated into the strategic levels of a company in order to contribute to corporate 
sustainability (Morioka et al., 2018b; Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; Thijssens et al., 2016). However, 
the practice of executing this has been identified as a research gap in various sustainability 
performance assessment frameworks (Maas et al., 2016b). This thesis aims to support the progress 
towards the seizing and sensing stage by mapping the requirements PAS for SDG Bonds to the five SBM 
principles that were defined in section 2.5 (Table 1). In this way, the thesis aims to contribute to 
improving the corporate sustainability performance of a company and feeding the earlier analyzed 
investment/corporate sustainability feedback loop (Figure 3). The transforming stage of the SBM 
innovation process has not been taken into the scope of this thesis due to time constraints.

3 Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds 
The measuring, assessing and reporting of impacts on sustainability issues is crucial for a company’s 
journey towards sustainable development, which in its turn has a positive effect on the responsible 
investment opportunities (Adams, 2017; Büyükozkan & Karabulut, 2018; Escrig-Olmedo et al., 2017; 
GRI & UN Global Compact, 2017; Kawamoto & Kanie, 2020; Principles for Responsible Investment, 
2019). Sustainability performance assessment can function as a communication tool to clarify a 
company’s or investor’s level of dedication and direction towards achieving the SDGs and contributing 
to sustainable development issues (Eccles & Klimenko, 2019; Schramade, 2017). This is where the PAS 
for SDG Bonds has its foundation: the aim of the PAS for SDG Bonds is to guide investors looking to 
direct their capital towards SDG-enabling activities and companies. The other way around, it will also 
support companies aiming to attract investors that are looking to direct their capital towards SDG-
enabling activities. The PAS for SDG Bonds is a new sustainability performance assessment framework 
for best practices in the SDG investment field. They are a set of standards that have been developed 
by SDG Impact, which is an initiative of the UNDP, in collaboration with the Impact Management 
Project, which is a network of organizations working to harmonize impact measurement and reporting 
initiatives.  
This facilitating role of the PAS for SDG Bonds is represented in Figure 4 by the arrow between the PAS 
for SDG Bonds and the two-way arrow, which represents the communication and relationship between 
corporate sustainability and responsible investing.

Current sustainability performance assessment frameworks are not aimed at investors (Eccles & 
Klimenko, 2019), not standardized (Business & Sustainable Development Commission, 2017; Evans et 
al., 2017) and the alignment with the SDGs is still underrepresented (Rosati & Faria, 2019b; Schramade, 
2017). The PAS for SDG Bonds aims to contribute to resolving these limitations by defining practice 
standards that enable the assurance of SDG-enabling activities for investors and investees. This will

encourage more enterprises and investors to start or accelerate their SDG-impact activities (SDG

“They bring logic, clarity, and transparency to understanding, measuring, and reporting on the nature 
and depth of SDG impacts of investments. They describe and encourage best practice, promote better 
decision making, and improve accountability about the impacts of investments,” (SDG Impact, 2020).  
The standards describe best practices, which can be used by companies and investors to benchmark 
their SDG impact practices. Companies and investors involved in SDG-enabling investment practices 
can test their adherence to these standards, and have the opportunity of achieving qualifying 
certification (SDG Impact, 2020).  
Besides the PAS for SDG Bonds, two other sets are being developed: the PAS for Private Equity and PAS 
for Enterprise. The PAS for Private Equity was the first consultation draft that was published in 
September 2019 by SDG Impact. While writing this thesis, the PAS for SDG Bonds has just been released, 
and the PAS for Enterprise is yet to be developed. For this thesis, it has been decided to work with the 
PAS for SDG Bonds, as it is the most progressed version.  
In the next paragraphs, the PAS for SDG Bonds will be discussed in more detail. Firstly, the 
characteristics of the PAS for SDG Bonds will be highlighted. Then the structure of the framework will 
be analyzed and the information, existing frameworks, and the process required to adopt the PAS for 
SDG Bonds will be explored. 
3.1 PAS for SDG Bonds characteristics 
In this thesis the PAS for SDG Bonds will be analyzed from a non-financial corporate issuer perspective, 
scoping the focus on corporate bonds. The PAS for SDG Bonds benefit issuers by providing guidance 
for good impact practice, providing assurance, facilitating benchmarking, providing competitively 
neutral and accessible standards, and supporting trust-building between issuers and investors (SDG 
Impact, 2020). When comparing the PAS for SDG Bonds to other well-known bond principles and 
standards, the main differences that emerge are that the PAS for SDG Bonds builds on many existing 
frameworks and takes a harmonizing approach. The PAS for SDG Bonds have a more holistic view of 
what SDG enabling activities should be financed and take into account that this should be aligned with 
the issuer's strategy and have a large focus on the impact that the issuer and its SDG enabling activities 
has on stakeholders. These characteristics have been identified by a comparative analysis summarized 
in Table 2. 
3.1.1 PAS for SDG Bonds aligned with other frameworks 
The PAS for SDG Bonds is designed to facilitate the adoption and implementation of principles and 
frameworks already in place (SDG Impact, 2019). The developers of the PAS for SDG Bonds 
acknowledge that the field of responsible investing is developing dynamically, and with it many tools 
and frameworks to support it. To address the concern of interoperability, the PAS for SDG Bonds are 
based on and work alongside many other frameworks and standards. This will simplify the 
implementation of the PAS for SDG Bonds, as it will serve as an extension to the common existing 
frameworks that companies have already adopted (SDG Impact, 2020). The PAS for SDG Bonds applies 
to defined Bond Programs issued under the International Capital Market Association (ICMA) 
Green/Social Bond Principles, the Climate Bonds Initiative (CBI), the Proposed EU Green Bond Standard, 
and SDG-linked bonds.

3.1.1.1 
ICMA Green and Social Bond Principles and Sustainable Bond Guidance 
The International Capital Market Association (ICMA) is a non-profit membership association that aims 
to promote debt security markets essential to fund sustainable economic growth and development. 
They do this by, among other things, having developed Green and Social Bond Principles (the Principles) 
serving as a broad and general leading framework globally for issuance of green, social, and 
sustainability bonds. Besides the Principles, the ICMA also set guidelines for Sustainable Bonds, which 
includes adhering to the Principles defined for both Green and Social Bonds. Sustainability Bonds 
should be aligned with the four main components of these Principles, which are the following:  
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
1. Use of Proceeds. The proceeds of the bond should be allocated to projects that contribute to 
both social and environmental objectives. The Principles list several categories in which these 
objectives should be placed to be deemed as an eligible project. Examples of categories for 
Green Bonds are renewable energy and pollution prevention and control. Examples of Social 
Bonds are affordable housing and food security. 
2. Process for Project Evaluation and Selection. The issuer should clearly communicate the 
following aspects: environmental and social objectives; the process by which the issuer 
determines how the projects fit within the identified eligible Green Projects and Social Projects 
categories; the eligibility criteria or any other process applied to identify and manage the 
environmental and social risks associated with the projects.  
3. Management of Proceeds. The net proceeds of the bond and the project to which they are 
allocated should be tracked by the issuer. A high level of transparency is encouraged, and the 
use of a third-party auditor is recommended for verification of the tracking. 
4. Reporting. The issuer should report at least annually on the projects to which the bond 
proceeds have been allocated, as well as a brief description of the projects and the amounts 
allocated, and their expected impact. 
Compared to the PAS for SDG Bonds the ICMA principles are very general and broad and provide only 
high-level guidance (Paparian, 2019). This gives the issuer a lot of freedom in deciding what projects 
are qualified as green or social, which could result in issues such as greenwashing (Doran & Tanner, 
2020; Katori, 2018). However, many certification frameworks follow the ICMA Principles, as they 
outline general criteria and requirements that define and guide the issuance of Green and Social Bonds. 
(Climate Bonds Initiative, 2019b). 
3.1.1.2 
CBI Standard 
The Climate Bonds Initiative is a not-for-profit organization, that promotes investment in projects and 
assets necessary for a rapid transition to a low carbon and climate-resilient economy. They do this by 
among others having developed the CBI Standard, which could lead to certification if the issuer meets 
the requirements of the CBI Standard which is assessed by an external assessor. Whereas the CBI 
Standards builds on the ICMA Principles, they include eligibility criteria for projects that can be used 
for the bonds through a sector-specific taxonomy. The CBI Standard is only focused on projects that 
support environmental goals, and specifically climate goals, and do not take into account any social 
impacts the projects might have (Climate Bonds Initiative, 2019a). 
3.1.1.3 
Proposed EU Green Bond Standard 
Whereas the ICMA Principles and CBI Standards were launched in respectively 2014 and 2010, the 
Proposed EU Green Bond Standard (EU GBS) has just recently been introduced in 2019. It is developed 
by the Technical Expert Group on sustainable finance (TEG), which is a team set up by the European 
Commission. The final version of the proposal was launched in March 2020, and the EU GBS are 
therefore not yet in use. The EU GBS is linked to the EU Taxonomy Regulation, which has been 
developed simultaneously. For a project to be eligible for the bond, it must substantially contribute to 
one of the six environmental objectives determined under the EU Taxonomy Regulation and not 
significantly harm any of the other environmental objectives (Technical expert group on sustainable 
finance, 2020a). Furthermore, it must be carried out in compliance with minimum safeguards. These 
safeguards are defined as procedures implemented by the issuer to ensure alignment with 
international standards, such as the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and the UN Guiding 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
Principles on Business and Human Rights (Technical expert group on sustainable finance, 2020b). Thus, 
compared to the CBI Standard, the EU GBS align more with sustainable development issues and take a 
more holistic approach, even if the focus is on Green Bonds.  
3.1.2 SDG impact focus 
As the materiality of the impact of SDGs is different in any context, for example, country- or sector-
specific, SDG Impact does not see it practical to develop a taxonomy to define eligible projects. 
However, by using the Impact Management Project (IMP) framework for classifying the impact of an 
investment and applying it to the SDGs, the Issuer can evaluate the expected (positive, negative, 
intended and unintended) material impact(s) on stakeholders. This framework identifies five 
dimensions of impact: what, who, how much, enterprise contribution, and risk. Within these 
dimensions, there are several data categories, resulting in a total of 15 data categories. The specific 
SDG targets impacted are considered in the what category. 
3.1.3 Holistic view 
Whereas the previously mentioned principles and standards have developed their own taxonomies to 
identify projects as eligible, the PAS for SDG Bonds have not and instead will build on those already 
existing and on the IMP framework. However, to ensure that a bond issued under the ICMA, CBI or EU 
GBS covers the social aspect of sustainability issues besides the green aspect, the PAS for SDG Bonds 
take into account overarching frameworks that define the organization’s responsible business 
practices, such as the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, the OECD Guidelines for 
Multinational Enterprises, the Ten Principles of the UN Global Compact, the UN Working Group report 
on human rights due diligence, and the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business 
Conduct. 
Furthermore, the PAS for SDG Bonds require a credible link between the issuer’s corporate strategy 
(or the sustainability component of its corporate strategy) and its SDG Bond Program. By this, the PAS 
for SDG Bonds addresses the identified gap in various existing performance assessment frameworks 
that are often missing the link to business strategy. It supports creating value for the whole corporation 
and its stakeholders and ensures that the corporation’s actions to not counter the positive SDG Impacts 
achieved through their SDG Bond Program. 
3.2 PAS for SDG Bonds structure

for an SDG Bond Program. The SDG Bond Program is the documentation for setting out the complete 
arrangements for issuing one or more SDG Bond(s). “[It] includes every facet of intentions, strategies, 
organization, and operations. Includes all promises and parameters in any proposal that claims SDG-
enabling features. Includes also all regulatory, market, and Stakeholder communication” (SDG Impact, 
2020). 
The six standards can be divided into four sections: Strategic Intent and Impact Goal Setting, Impact 
Measurement and Management Approach, Transparency and Comparability, and Context and

3.3 Adopting the PAS for SDG Bonds 
As mentioned before, the PAS for SDG Bonds will be analyzed from a corporate issuer perspective. A 
corporate issuer can have various reasons for adopting the PAS for SDG Bonds, for example wanting 
to benchmark or check the adherence of their current SDG Bond Program to the PAS for SDG Bonds, 
or determine the readiness of their SDG Bond Program to be certified. As the focus of this thesis is not 
on creating an SDG Bond Program, but on using the PAS for SDG Bonds to identify practices that will 
guide the business model innovation process, the focus will be on benchmarking an existing SDG Bond 
Program to the PAS for SDG Bonds. Reviewing strengths and possible gaps in an SDG Bond Program’s 
adherence to the PAS for SDG Bonds will create insights on which practices should be improved to 
enhance their corporate sustainability. 
To adopt the PAS for SDG Bonds, it is recommended to assess the SDG Bond Program with every 
practice indicator using the guidance on evidencing and the suggested resources. For several practice 
indicators, the guidance offers multiple ways of evidencing and points towards multiple different 
resources. The assessor must consider the specifics of the context, for example the sector or 
geographical location of the issuer and its SDG Bond Program, to decide what evidencing is fit for the 
practice indicator.  
Some frameworks are essential in the assessment and must be included by the issuer. The issuer must 
have a defined SDG Bond Program, or something similar, that sets out the complete arrangements for 
issuing one or more SDG bonds. It is the SDG Bond Program that will be assessed with the PAS for SDG 
Bonds, making it essential for the process. The program should include the SDG impact intentions, 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
strategic impact goals, impact management practices, governance, and its eligibility criteria for 
qualifying activities.  
One of the crucial frameworks used in the PAS for SDG Bonds is the IMP framework for classifying the 
impact of an investment, which has been introduced in section 3.1.2. This framework is used to 
evaluate the potential qualifying projects on their expected impacts on stakeholders, considering 
specific SDG targets impacted. Once the projects have been assessed by this framework, the overall 
impact can be determined by the ‘ABC Impact Classification’. This clarifies whether a project is ‘Acting 
to Avoid Harm’, ‘Benefiting Stakeholders’, or ‘Contributing to Solutions’ in the context of the SDGs.  
Whereas the PAS for SDG Bonds are aligned with many overarching frameworks for responsible 
business practices, two of these are specifically mentioned in the practice indicators. These are the UN 
Guiding Principles for Business and Human Rights and the Ten Principles of the UN Global Compact. 
These principles ensure that the issuer not only focusses on the integrity of their SDG Bond Program, 
but also adhere to responsible business practice in general.

4 Methodology 
This thesis is divided into three stages. The first stage focusses on the literature review, in which 
multiple concepts are explored that play a significant role in this study. This stage also includes an 
extensive description of the PAS for SDG Bonds. The second stage provides an analysis in which the 
PAS for SDG Bonds is mapped to the SBM principles. In the third stage, this mapping analysis is applied 
in a case study.  
4.1 Stage one: literature review and Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds 
In the first stage of this study, many different concepts are introduced. These described concepts 
contribute to gaining a better understanding of the PAS for SDG Bonds and SBM innovation. The 
chapters of the various concepts are structured in such a way that they build on the previous concepts 
and the link between them becomes apparent. To support this, a conceptual model (see Figure 4) of 
the concepts has been designed that shows how they are interrelated and how they are placed in the 
context of this thesis. This model is revisited each time a concept is added to it, reflecting how they 
build on each other. 
The described concepts have been identified as crucial in this thesis by working back from the PAS for 
SDG Bonds and SBM innovation, which are the two main topics of this research. In the process of 
researching these concepts, multiple other concepts were often mentioned and identified as crucial. 
These identified concepts were then further researched which led to encountering more frequently 
occurring concepts. In this manner, the various concepts were identified. 
Furthermore, the first stage of this thesis focusses on research sub-questions 1a, 1b, and 2a, repeated 
below: 
1. What does the development of the PAS for SDG Bonds entail for companies? 
a. What is the goal of the PAS for SDG Bonds? 
b. What is the relationship between the PAS for SDG Bonds and other reporting initiatives 
and standard systems available to companies?  
2. How can the PAS for SDG Bonds align with the SBM? 
a. What are the principles of the SBM? 
Research sub-questions 1a and 1b on the PAS for SDG Bonds are primarily answered in Chapter 3, and 
the research sub-question 2a is answered through the literature review in Chapter 2 and specifically 
section 2.5 (Table 1). 
4.2 Stage two: mapping of the Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds to 
the Sustainable Business Model (SBM) principles 
In order to answer the second question of the second research sub-question concerning the alignment 
of the PAS for SDG Bonds and the SBM, the second stage focusses on a qualitative analysis of the links 
between the PAS for SDG Bonds and the SBM. The research sub-question is:  
2. How can the PAS for SDG Bonds align with the SBM? 
b. Which aspects of the PAS for SDG Bonds can be mapped to which of the SBM principles 
and what are the effects of this? 
To answer this research sub-question the method of qualitative content analysis will be used. It is a 
method often used for text analysis (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005; Kuckartz, 2019), and is based mainly on 
the systematic approach of coding (Kuckartz, 2019). Coding generally is used to break up and segment 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
the data into simpler, general categories. This can then be used to formulate new levels of 
interpretation (Coffey & Atkinson, 1996).  
4.2.1 The coding process 
According to Tesch (1990), coding consists of tagging text segments with information about the 
category of the organizing system into which it belongs, or several categories if the segment is relevant 
to more than one. These categories of the organizing system can come from a variety of sources, such 
as theoretical frameworks or ideas (Coffey & Atkinson, 1996). Categories derived from theories or 
literature are known as concept-driven, or deductive developed categories (Kuckartz, 2019). As the 
goal of this research is to explore how the PAS for SDG Bonds can guide the SBM innovation process, 
the PAS for SDG Bonds should be mapped to the SBM. By doing this, the results of the assessment with 
the PAS for SDG Bonds could be used to analyze the SBM, and thereby how the SBM innovation process 
could be improved even further. Therefore, in this thesis, the five identified principles of an SBM 
(section 2.5, Table 1) represent the categories into which the PAS for SDG Bonds practice indicators 
will be organized. 
To begin the coding process, keywords and concepts by which the text segments can be tagged and 
assigned to categories, are derived from the research literature (Coffey & Atkinson, 1996). The 
research literature used for this analysis is given in section 2.5, in which the five principles of SBM are 
described. The first set of keywords was identified in the description of the principles and can be seen 
as the “starting list”. These keywords were then searched for in the PAS for SDG Bonds practice 
indicators. In this way, the practice indicators were mapped to one or multiple SBM principles. As the 
analysis progresses, these keywords and concepts can be expanded, changed, or were scrapped as the 
ideas develop through repeated interactions with the data (Coffey & Atkinson, 1996). This iterative 
process is described in the following paragraphs and visualized in a flow chart in Figure 5. 
Besides only searching for the keyword itself in the text, variations were also used. It was checked 
whether the keyword could be formulated differently in the practice indicators. An example of this is 
that in the practice indicators ‘long term’ was used, instead of ‘long-term’ as in the principle’s definition. 
Also, parts of words were used, such as ‘strateg-‘, as this would lead to both strategy and strategic. 
Furthermore, synonyms were also used. For example, ‘benefit’, which is a synonym for ‘value’. During 
the search, when it became more apparent which words were used in specific contexts, the keywords 
list expanded to include these. An example is the keyword ‘future’, which was often used in the 
practice indicators in the long-term context. The final set of keywords are shown in Table 5.

4.2.2 Validation of the coding process and results 
This thesis recognizes two possible limitations in the process of the qualitative analysis. These include 
subjectivity in the identification of keywords, and the possible misinterpretation of the PAS for SDG 
Bonds practice indicators. To limit this risk and to seek validation of the analysis, the keywords and 
results of the coding process have been discussed with the developer of the PAS for SDG Bonds through 
a ‘validation interview’, or also known as a ‘member check’ (Birt et al., 2016; Buchbinder, 2010). As 
defined by (Buchbinder, 2010), a validation interview is a dialogue between interviewee and 
interviewer intended to confirm, substantiate, verify, or correct researchers’ findings. By receiving 
feedback on the process and results of the analysis, researchers are given the opportunity to reflect 
on their own biases and other sources of misinterpretation (Koelsch, 2013).  
The approach to the validation interview is typically to present the interviewee with the results of the 
analysis followed by an interview that focuses on confirmation, modification, and verification of 
interpretation (Birt et al., 2016; Buchbinder, 2010; Koelsch, 2013). This method is also applied to this 
thesis. The developer of the PAS for SDG Bonds (from now on ‘the Developer’) was presented with the 
keywords used for the coding process, and a summary of the results of the analysis. Afterward, a semi-
structured interview was conducted to discuss the keywords with the Developer. Specifically, a semi-
structured interview has been chosen as a method, as its flexibility enhances reciprocity between the 
interviewer and the Developer, enabling the interviewer to improvise follow-up questions based on 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
participant´s responses and allowing the Developer space for her own individual verbal expressions 
(Kallio et al., 2016). These aspects are believed to prove useful in an interview that aims at receiving 
feedback on the analysis and its results.  
4.3 Stage three: case study 
In order to test the mapping analysis conducted in stage two, a case study is performed. A case study 
is useful in an analysis where a theory is to be examined in its real-life context (Yin, 1981). A single-
case study will be performed, as it can be used to test a theory (Yin, 1981). Applied to this thesis, the 
theory is the mapping of the PAS for SDG Bonds to the SBM, and the real-life context is where the PAS 
for SDG Bonds and the mapping analysis are adopted by a company that is an issuer in the bond market. 
This will be done from the perspective of the company Ramboll, which is an engineering, architecture 
and consultancy company founded in Denmark in 1945, and now present in 35 countries (Ramboll, 
n.d.-b). Ramboll offers the service of advising clients at every stage of the project life: from strategy 
development and planning to detailed design and execution. While Ramboll offers various services in 
different markets, this thesis is focused on its Management Consulting services. This service is focused 
on assisting companies in their management and strategic practices, which can be combined with 
knowledge from the various sectors Ramboll specializes in. 
In the specific case study of this thesis, it will be researched whether Ramboll can apply the PAS for 
SDG Bonds to guide the SBM innovation process of a prospective client that is active as an issuer in the 
sustainability bond market. Specifically, the case study will contribute to answering research sub-
question 1c, 2c and the third research question of this thesis, which are: 
1. What does the development of the PAS for SDG Bonds entail for companies? 
c. What information is required for companies to adopt the PAS for SDG Bonds? 
2. How can the PAS for SDG Bonds align with the SBM? 
c. How can the links between the PAS for SDG Bonds and SBM support a company in its 
business model innovation process? 
3. How can Ramboll utilize the identified method for alignment between PAS for SDG Bonds and 
SBMs to guide prospective clients within the sustainability bond market? 
a. What recommendations could Ramboll give the prospective client for the adoption of 
the PAS for SDG Bonds? 
b. How should Ramboll interpret the results of the PAS for SDG Bonds in the context of 
SBM innovation? 
c. How can Ramboll use these interpretations to guide the SBM innovation process of 
the prospective client? 
Ramboll’s perspective fits well within the scope of this thesis and in this case study as it is a company 
that advises its clients in their strategy development. They are a strong supporter of the SDGs and aim 
to impact the public agenda and the sustainable development of society through their projects 
(Ramboll, n.d.-a).  
The PAS for SDG Bonds will be applied to one of Ramboll’s prospective clients. This prospective client 
(from now on “the Company”) has been chosen according to the requirements that they are a) an

In the following sections the most important findings of the analysis results shown in Table 7 will be 
highlighted for each of the six standards. 
5.1.1 1st standard 
The first standard shows to be linked to all five SBM principles, but especially to the sustainability 
oriented and systemic thinking principle.  
The strong link with sustainability orientation can be explained by the fact that this standard falls into 
the section of “strategic intent and impact goal setting”. It focusses on how the issuer engages 
strategically with the SDGs, and how the SDGs are impacted. This aligns with the definition of the 
sustainability oriented principle in section 2.5: “a sustainability oriented company expresses its 
purpose, vision and mission in terms of social, environmental and economic outcomes.” The strategic 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
aspect is referred to in the mentioning of the purpose, vision, and mission, and the focus on SDGs can 
be aligned to the social, environmental, and economic outcomes.  
The fact that the systemic thinking principle is strongly represented in the first standard, could be 
because it focusses on the context of corporate strategy, stakeholder interests, and the SDGs. Systemic 
thinking is needed to take these different contexts into account. 
5.1.2 2nd standard 
What is noticeable in the analysis of the second standard, is that all principles are represented. The 
sustainability orientation principle is linked to this standard the most. This could be explained by the 
fact that the second standard “covers how the Issuer embeds impact measurement and management 
for its SDG Bond Program into its business practices” (SDG Impact, 2020). This aligns with the 
description given for the sustainability orientated principle. 
The stakeholder integration principle is the second strongest represented principle in this standard. 
Also, compare to the other standards, the stakeholder integration principle is linked most to this 
standard. This indicates that stakeholders should play an active role in the impact management and 
measurement of the SDG Bond Program and its qualifying activities. This is supported by the 
description given for the stakeholder integration in the literature review. The description emphasizes 
the key role stakeholders should play in the development and implementation of effective 
sustainability solutions. 
5.1.3 3rd standard 
The third standard addresses all SBM principles besides the stakeholder integration. Stakeholders are 
mentioned in several of the practice indicators, but this is limited to considering the impacts on them, 
and not the actual engagement of them in the process.  
Furthermore, it is observed that practice indicator 3.2 is linked to four out of five SBM principles. This 
practice indicator is focused on conducting an impact assessment of the potential qualifying activities 
to determine their suitability for the SDG Bond Program. This is a crucial practice indicator as it 
evaluates the impact of the qualifying activities that will be financed by the bonds. The relation to so 
many SBM principles can be explained by the fact that many aspects are to be considered in the 
evaluation of the impact of qualifying activities.  
It is also noticeable that one of the SBM principles that is addressed most often, is sustainability 
orientation. This could be due to the fact that the third standard focusses on the link between the 
company’s SDG impact intentions and its strategic impact goals, and the assessment of the  SDG 
enabling activities: “Guided by its SDG impact intentions and strategic impact goals, the Issuer selects 
and undertakes ex ante impact assessments (including measuring expected impact) of potential 
qualifying activities for its SDG Bond Program.”  
This standard is also the standard that has the most linkages with the long-term perspective. This 
principle is addressed in practice indicators 3.2, 3.4, 3.5, and 3.6. Practice indicator 3.2 is focused on 
the inclusion of due diligence and evaluation of future expected impact performance in the assessment 
of qualifying activities, whereas practice indicator 3.4 emphasized the importance of establishing 
credible baselines to measure future impact performance. Practice indicator 3.5 mentions that the 
issuer sets realistic targets that will be tested and assessed over the term of the bond and/or life of 
the qualifying activities. In practice indicator 3.6 the focus is on periodically reviewing the criteria for 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
the qualifying activities. The strong link to the long-term perspective could be due to the strong focus 
this standard has on impact assessment. As mentioned in section 2.5, when addressing environmental 
and social impacts a long-term perspective plays an important role. 
5.1.4 4th standard 
The fourth standard of the PAS for SDG Bonds concerns the systematic measuring and managing of the 
ongoing impact performance of its SDG Bond Program and associated qualifying activities. This 
standard is equally linked to the stakeholder integration, the sustainability oriented, and the systemic 
thinking principle.  
The strong link to the stakeholder integration principle can be explained by the fact that the active 
engagement and participation of stakeholders is important in the measuring and managing process to 
fully understand the overall ongoing impact of the qualifying activities financed by the SDG bonds, as 
also mentioned for the second standard. 
The presence of the systemic thinking aspects can be explained by the importance of practicing 
systemic thinking while monitoring and evaluating the impact. The company should consider the 
impacts on other stakeholders and in the right context, to truly assess the impact of their SDG Bond 
Program and qualifying activities. 
The sustainability oriented principle is represented in indicator 4.1 and 4.3. They both address the fact 
that the issuer should not only consider the expected and intended positive impact, but also the 
unexpected, unintended, and negative impacts, to ensure a holistic approach to sustainable 
development. 
The principles concerning extended value creation and long-term perspective are not addressed in this 
standard. A long-term perspective might be expected due to the emphasis on measuring and managing 
impact performance during the course of the SDG Bond Program and its qualifying activities. However, 
this is not the case as the practice indicators for this standard are focused on how to measure and 
manage the impact in the current and ongoing timeframe, and not how to take future perspectives 
into account. 
5.1.5 5th standard 
The fifth standard is categorized under transparency and comparability. What is noticeable is that it is 
linked to all SBM principles. This could be because this standard is aimed at the disclosure and 
reporting aspect of the PAS for SDG Bonds, which should ideally cover all decisions and aspects from 
the other standards. As the other standards together address all five principles, it is logical that they 
are all represented in this standard.  
5.1.6 6th standard 
The section for the sixth standard is context and governance. It assesses how the issuer demonstrates 
that its responsible business practices are consistent with the Standards. The reference to the 
responsible business practices explains why both practice indicators for this standard are linked to the 
sustainability oriented principle.  
Besides being linked to the sustainability oriented principle, practice indicator 6.1 also links to the 
systemic thinking principle. This could be explained by the focus on context in this practice indicator: 
“The Issuer’s governance processes provide the appropriate context for its SDG Bond Program to 
operate in” (SDG Impact, 2020). 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
Practice indicator 6.2 is linked to the stakeholder integration and long-term perspective principles 
besides the sustainability oriented principle. This practice indicator focusses on the governing body or 
board of the issuer. The board or governing body is usually the entity within a company that is 
responsible for considering the future and direction of a company, explaining the link of this practice 
indicator to the long-term perspective principle. Furthermore, the board or governing body is often 
responsible for overseeing the policies and practices engaged with stakeholders, which clarifies the 
link this practice indicator has to the stakeholder integration principle. 
5.1.7 Validation interview 
As mentioned in 4.2.2, the above results have also been discussed with one of the developers (‘the 
Developer’) of the PAS for SDG Bonds by means of an interview to seek validation of the results. In this 
interview further clarification of practice indicators was given where needed and additional findings 
were introduced. The interview has been transcribed with the online program Otter.ai. The interview 
was held on the 10th of April 2020, and the transcription can be found in Appendix 1.1. 
During the call with the Developer it became apparent that the practice indicators addressing the 
mindset and resources of the issuer also show a link to the sustainability orientation principle. As the 
Developer said, “because how sustainability oriented are you [the issuer] if you don't have resources, 
abilities, and giving them sufficient authority in the organization to make decisions?”. About the 
mindset she mentions: “Are you more interested in actually trying to ensure a sustainable outcome? 
Or are you interested in trying to give the impression that you've achieved sustainable outcomes? 
There's a bit of a difference between the two.” This new finding influenced the initial results of this 
analysis as it caused for more practice indicators to be linked to the sustainability oriented principle. 
The Developer also clarified the content and significance of the embedded existing frameworks in the 
PAS for SDG Bonds. An example is the OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct, 
which is included in many of the practice indicators. The Developer pointed out that meaningful 
stakeholder engagement is an important aspect of this framework. This indicates that all practice 
indicators that include this OECD framework should not only be linked to the sustainability oriented 
principle, but also to the stakeholder integration principle.

The Company in this case study will not be named due to privacy reasons. However, basic background

2032 and to achieve net-zero emissions by 2040. 
The Company first entered the Green Bond Market in 2017, when they published their Green Bonds

replaced their Green Bonds Framework from 2017 with the Green Finance Framework and issued a 
total of six green bonds. The Green Finance Framework is aligned with the ICMA Green Bond Principles

The main changes in the Green Finance Framework compared to the Green Bonds Framework is that 
other green financial products than just green bonds are included. Furthermore, the eligible project 
categories have been reduced from offshore wind projects, bioenergy projects and energy storage, 
smart grid, and other energy solutions, to offshore wind projects only. They have narrowed the scope 
to offshore wind projects for it to align with the core focus of their company and their main growth 
platform. 
5.2.1 Applying the Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds 
When applying the PAS for SDG Bonds to the Company, each practice indicator is assessed as ‘No 
information’, ‘Not yet observed’, ‘Developing’, and ‘Developed’ to indicate the performance of the 
Company on the practice. The last three categories are proposed as the categories to assess by in the 
PAS for SDG Bonds, and ‘No information’ was added to the categories in this thesis as not all 
information required to assess the practice indicators was available. ‘No information’ means that the 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
Company’s performance on this practice indicator is unknown as the required evidence was not found. 
‘Not yet observed’ indicates that the Company is not in any way addressing the practice indicator 
where it should be included. An example of this is that the targets should be specified for each specific 
qualifying activity, which was not the case in the Company’s SDG Bond Program. ‘Developing’ means 
that the Company has recognized the practice indicator, but is not performing on all aspects of it, or 
only to a certain extent. An example of this is when the Company does not include negative unintended 
impacts in the SDG Bond Program, while it is included in the Sustainability report. ‘Developed’ indicates 
that the Company is performing well on all aspects of the practice indicator and is providing all the 
evidence required.

6 Discussion 
The analysis and findings presented in Chapter 5 lead to several points of discussion. Firstly, the 
analysis that maps the PAS for SDG Bonds to the SBM principles will be discussed. Thereafter, the 
results from the case study will be discussed. This includes the application of the PAS for SDG Bonds 
to the Company and the mapping analysis that follows.  
6.1 Mapping the Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds to the sustainable 
business model (SBM) principles 
The first analysis conducted in this thesis was aimed to answer the second research sub-question: 
“How can the PAS for SDG Bonds align with the SBM?”. More specifically the mapping analysis is aimed 
at answering research sub-question 2b, whereas 2c is mainly addressed in the case study phase of this 
thesis. Research sub-question 2a is answered in the literature review in section 2.5 (Table 1), where 
the five principles of an SBM are identified.  
To answer research sub-question 2b, the alignment of the PAS for SDG Bonds and the SBM is explored 
by mapping the practice indicators of the PAS for SDG Bonds to the five identified SBM principles. A 
result of this mapping analysis is that the PAS for SDG Bonds aligns with all SBM principles, some 
principles to a higher extent than others. The central findings will be discussed in the following 
paragraphs. 
What becomes apparent when studying the results is that the sustainability oriented principle of the 
SBM plays a significant alignment role with the PAS for SDG Bonds, as most of the practice indicators 
are linked to this principle. Three different reasons can be found for these strong links and are listed 
as the following: 
• 
The PAS has a strong focus on the SDGs. The PAS for SDG Bonds have a strong focus on the 
SDGs, as they assess the bonds that are linked to the SDGs and aimed to enable the SDGs. This 
results in a large amount of the practice indicators referring to the SDGs. Often these practice 
indicators are linked to the sustainability oriented principles because when the issuer focusses 
on the SDGs (such as setting SDG impact intentions and SDG impact goals), this shows that the 
issuer is sustainability oriented. 
• 
Clear link to business strategy. Another often-occurring link between the practice indicators 
and the sustainability oriented principle is when the practice indicator includes the practice of 
linking an issuers SDG Bond Program to its strategy. The practice indicators referring to the 
linking of strategy are mapped to the sustainability oriented principle as it shows that the 
issuer has embedded a sustainability oriented approach in its strategy as well, and not only in 
its SDG Bond Program. This could even be more present in the PAS for Enterprise, as the focus 
in this set of PAS for SDG Bonds is merely the SDG Bonds. To check whether this is the case a 
similar analysis could be conducted for the PAS for Enterprise in further research. 
• 
Addressing resources and mindset. When a practice indicator mentions the resources needed 
to facilitate the best practices for SDG bonds, the practice indicator is linked to the 
sustainability oriented principle. It indicates that a company is dedicated to allocating the 
necessary resources to sustainability practices. The same goes for the mindset of a company. 
This shows that the company is not only interested in giving the impression that it achieves 
sustainable outcomes, but that it ensures the sustainable outcome.  
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
Another central finding is that the extended value creation principle is addressed the least by the 
standards in the PAS for SDG Bonds. The extended value creation was mostly only linked by the ABC 
framework that the PAS for SDG Bonds uses as impact classification. This link is made because the ABC 
framework classifies impacts as ‘acting to avoid harm that detracts from achieving the SDGs’, ‘benefit 
stakeholders in relation to the SDGs’, and ‘contribute to solutions towards achieving the SDGs’. 
Avoiding harm indicates that value is not destroyed, where benefiting stakeholders and contributing 
to solutions show facilitating value creation or creating the value themselves. It seems that the 
extended value creation is not mentioned as explicitly as the other principles, but that it is still very 
much embedded through those principles. The Developer mentioned in the validation interview that 
“organizations that take these [the principles] seriously and really embed it in their organizations, see 
these as a core part of how they create their value. So, you know, there is that element,[…] it's more 
subtle. It's not something that's actually stated in here.” 
6.2 Applying the Practice Assurance Standards (PAS) for SDG Bonds 
The first step of the case study was to assess the Company’s sustainability performance on bonds with 
the PAS for SDG Bonds. This assessment was executed as a component of this thesis. However, it is 
more likely that in real life the assessor will be someone within the Company if the Company wishes 
to gain insights on best practices and its own performance, or an external accreditor that is authorized 
to award the Company with a certification. Assuming that the external accreditor is specialized in 
applying the PAS for SDG Bonds, this sub-chapter will focus on the perspective of the Company when 
applying the PAS for SDG Bonds.  
From the application process of the PAS for SDG Bonds to the Company, several recommendations can 
be given to simplify the process, answering to the first research sub-question of this thesis: “What does 
the development of the PAS for SDG Bonds entail for companies?”, and specifically addressing 1c, 
which refers to the information required for applying the PAS for SDG Bonds. Research sub-question 
1a which refers to the defined goal of the PAS for SDG Bonds has been addressed in Chapter 3. Namely, 
the PAS for SDG Bonds will encourage more enterprises and investors to start or accelerate their SDG-
impact activities. They aim to direct investment towards activities that contribute to the progress of 
the SDGs.   
Research sub-question 1b is also addressed in Chapter 3 by giving an overview of other common 
existing bond frameworks and how they compare to the PAS for SDG Bonds. In the overview (Table 2) 
it can be seen that the PAS for SDG Bonds has many characteristics similar to the other common 
frameworks, showing that they were based on and work alongside them. The PAS for SDG Bonds are 
not meant to replace other bond frameworks but will serve as an extension to the common existing 
frameworks. The PAS for SDG Bonds take a more holistic approach by taking in all TBL aspects of 
sustainability. Furthermore, it integrates the impact on SDGs, which is an aspect that is still 
underrepresented in common existing frameworks (Rosati & Faria, 2019b; Schramade, 2017). 
Firstly, it is advised to have a clear overview of the different actors that are involved in the different 
related aspects. This will be useful when needing more information on a subject, as it will be clear 
whom to contact for this information. This would most importantly include the person responsible and 
accountable for overseeing the SDG Bond Program’s impact measurement and management practices, 
also known as the Impact Manager in the PAS for SDG Bonds terminology. A significant amount of 
practice indicators guidance notes mention an interview with the Impact Manager would support the 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
evidencing of the assessment. It is recommended to ensure beforehand that this person has the 
dedicated time and devotion to support the process of applying the PAS for SDG Bonds. 
Furthermore, it is recommended that the assessor collects all necessary documentation concerning 
the bond program and sustainability strategy and performance of the Company. This could include the 
Sustainability report, Annual report, ESG reports, the Bond Program, and all policies regarding 
responsible business and environmental practices. By gathering these documents beforehand, the 
process of finding the necessary information will be more efficient. Furthermore, it would also be 
useful to gather external documents such as second opinions or assessments of the Company’s Bond 
Program or the Company’s sustainability performance by external accreditors. These documents can 
be used to support the assessment for each practice indicator by using it to compare it with their own 
assessment. An example from this case is that there is a second opinion on the Company’s Green 
Finance Framework provided by an external assessor. This document has been used to check if no 
information was overlooked in the PAS for SDG Bonds assessment that was used for the Second 
Opinion assessment.  
Lastly, there are three frameworks that the Company should comply with in order to score well on all 
practice indicators. These are the UN Guiding Principles for Business and Human Rights, the Ten 
Principles of the UN Global Compact, and the IMP framework for classifying the impact of an 
investment. This last framework includes the Five Dimensions of Impact and the ABC Impact 
Classification. By ensuring that these frameworks have been applied and are embedded in the 
Company and their SDG Bond Program, there is a higher chance that more practice indicators will be 
assessed as ‘Developed’ from the start.

6.3.1 Improving performance and implementing best practices 
The Company can decide to use the results of the PAS for SDG Bonds assessment to improve its 
performance of the SDG Bond Program, and to implement the best practices that are included in the 
PAS. They have the option of undertaking the improvement process themselves, but can also decide 
to hire an advisory company, such as Ramboll, to support the achievement of the best practices.  
==PAGE:48==

First, it is noticeable from the analysis in section 5.2.1 that many practice indicators linked to the 
sustainability oriented and systemic thinking principles are often assessed as ‘Developing’. This 
possibly indicates an opportunity for the Company to make slight changes in order to achieve the 
‘Developed’ level for these indicators. Examples of this are practice indicators 3.2, 5.1, and 5.2, where 
the Company should apply the ABC Impact Classification and Five Dimensions of Impact frameworks 
developed by the IMP in order to achieve the ‘Developed’ level. These practice indicators are also the 
three practice indicators that are linked to the most SBM principles, namely four out of five. Thus, 
performing well on these practice indicators could lead to the biggest improvement in the overall 
performance on the SBM principles. It is therefore highly recommended to apply the aforementioned 
IMP frameworks. 
Furthermore, the Company scores a high amount of ‘No information’ for the practice indicators linked 
to the stakeholder integration principle. Ramboll can also support the Company by investigating the 
performance of the Company on this principle further and gain clarification, by for example conducting 
interviews and requesting internal documents regarding this principle. If this additional information 
shows that the practice indicators of this principle are indeed ‘Developing’ or ‘Developed’, it could lead 
to the advice that the Company should more specifically mention their practices concerning this 
principle in the publicly available documentation to enhance transparency. If the additional 
information shows that the practice indicators concerning the stakeholder integration principle are 
‘Not yet observed’ by the Company, Ramboll can guide the company in its stakeholder engagement 
process.  
Lastly, the results indicate that the Company performs poorly on the long-term perspective principle. 
This is mainly caused by practice indicators that involve the measuring of the SDG impacts of the 
qualifying activities. Whereas the Company takes an extensive and detailed approach to the measuring 
of impacts on each SDG in its overall sustainability and ESG report, it fails to do so for each specific 
qualifying activity of the bond program. Measuring is crucial when considering the impact of the 
qualifying activities in the long-term. Ramboll can support the Company in this by investigating what 
SDGs are impacted, both positive and negative and expected and unexpected, what stakeholders are 
involved in this, and how the impacts should be measured and to which baseline. 
When considering the poor performance on the long-term perspective principle, it should be 
considered that this could be because there are no practice indicators where information was missing 
for this principle, whereas other principles do have practice indicators assessed as such. Possibly these 
indicators for other principles would also be assessed as ‘Not yet observed’ if the data was available 
and the indicator is nevertheless not observed, resulting in a different ranking of the performance on 
principles where the long-term perspective principle does not score the lowest. 
Implementing best practices and improving the performance of the SDG Bond Program will result in 
an overall better score on the PAS for SDG Bonds. As the PAS for SDG Bonds enables and directs 
responsible investing, a better score will attract investors seeking investors looking to direct their 
capital towards SDG-enabling activities and companies. This is especially relevant now that trends 
show that the market of these responsible investors is growing. Improving on the score of the PAS for 
SDG Bonds will enhance the facilitating role that the PAS offer. This effect is shown in Figure 8 at the

6.3.2 Guidance of the Sustainable Business Model (SBM) innovation process  
After having assessed their SDG bonds performance with the PAS for SDG Bonds, the Company can 
decide to use the assessment results to support the guidance of their SBM innovation process. The 
goal of this research is to explore whether the combination of the results of this assessment and the 
results of the mapping analysis can guide the process of innovating the Company’s business model 
towards sustainability.   
As discussed in section 2.7, this thesis focusses on the sensing and seizing stage of the SBM innovation 
process. In the sensing stage, the opportunities to innovate the business model towards sustainability 
are identified. In this case study, the results of the PAS for SDG Bonds assessment in combination with 
the mapping analysis show how the Company performs on each SBM principle. The performance level 
indicates opportunities. For example, a low performance on the practice indicators linked to a specific 
principle identifies the opportunity of improving this principle and thus possibly innovating the 
business model towards sustainability. The seizing stage refers to the integration of the opportunities 
in the SBM by mobilizing resources to address the identified opportunities.  
One noticeable result from the analysis is that a lot of the practice indicators linked to the stakeholder 
integration principle are assessed as ‘No information’.  As discussed before, this could indicate 
limitations due to the data, because if more data would have been available the results would give a 
better representation of the performance of the Company on the stakeholder integration principle. 
However, it could also indicate that that the Company does not have the evidencing because the 
practice indicators linked to stakeholder integration have not yet been observed, thus scoring poorly 
on this SBM principle. If this is the case, an interesting approach as an advisor would be to analyze 
whether this level of performance is reflected in its overall business strategy or only the bonds 
framework, and if so, how it can be improved.  
Furthermore, it is noticeable that the results show that the Company does not score well on the long-
term perspective principle. Here it would also be interesting to analyze whether this low score on the 
long-term perspective principle could be indicating a deficiency not only in their bond framework, but 
also in their overall strategy. It could imply that the long-term perspective principle is not only lacking 
in their bond framework, but also in their business model. Ramboll can then investigate how the 
Company can improve the long-term perspective principle in their SDG Bonds Program and in their 
business model. 
These discussed results can indicate the possible opportunities to support the sensing stage of SBM 
innovation. In this case, the opportunities include improving the performance on the stakeholder 
integration principle and the long-term perspective principle. However, as the PAS focus on the SDG 
Bonds, it remains unclear whether these opportunities also apply to the Company’s SBM and overall 
strategy. To determine whether the identified opportunities for the PAS of SDG Bonds also apply to 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
the SBM, Ramboll could further investigate the performance on the SBM principles outside of the SDG 
Bond Program. 
Concerning the seizing stage, these opportunities can be acted upon by improving the performance on 
the practice indicators linked to these principles. However, the practice indicators mostly demonstrate 
best practices related to the SDG Bond Program, and do not necessarily address the Company’s SBM. 
Therefore, to use the PAS for SDG Bonds to guide the SBM innovation process, Ramboll should 
investigate whether the opportunities for the SDG Bond Program are also applicable to the SBM. If so, 
it should be explored whether the practice indicators linked to these opportunities for the SDG Bond 
Program will also affect the SBM, or whether this is limited to the SDG Bond Program. 
By using the results of the assessment with the PAS for SDG Bonds to guide the sensing and seizing 
stage of SBM innovation, Ramboll can support the Company in their SBM innovation process. 
Innovating their business model towards sustainability will allow for systematically integrating 
corporate sustainability in their strategizing process and continuously adapt their business strategies 
to changing environments and stakeholder expectations. Furthermore, it will support facing the 
current sustainability challenges and achieve the potential and opportunities that sustainability 
strategies have to offer. This includes long-term results in which the Company will experience lower 
financial risk due to stronger financial performance and shareholder value creation, making them more 
attractive to investors. This fuels even more improvement in the corporate sustainability performance 
of the Company. These developments and how the mapping analysis and Ramboll can affect them are

7.1 Corporate perspective 
Finding an answer to the main research question can be approached from two corporate perspectives: 
that of Ramboll and that of the Company. Ramboll’s perspective represents a consultant perspective 
that advises its clients in their strategy development. The Company perspective is that of the client 
being advised by the consultant.  
The analysis in this thesis demonstrates that the results of an assessment with the PAS for SDG Bonds 
can support Ramboll in guiding the SBM innovation process of the Company. It is especially useful in 
the sensing and seizing stage of the SBM innovation process where it supports identifying 
opportunities, and ways to act upon these opportunities. For example, the analysis might show that 
the company is not performing well on practice indicators linked to the long-term perspective principle. 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
This could indicate the opportunity of improving on these practice indicators in order to improve on 
the long-term perspective principle.  
However, because the PAS are focused only SDG Bond Programs, and not the overall strategy of the 
company, Ramboll should investigate whether the opportunities for the SDG Bond Program are also 
applicable to the SBM. For example, practice indicators linked to the long-term perspective principle 
might only support the improvement of the SDG Bond Program. It should be explored whether it will 
also address the opportunities for the SBM. In this way, this thesis supports the guiding of the SBM 
innovation process by indicating the opportunities that Ramboll should investigate. 
From the perspective of the Company, the assessment with the PAS for SDG Bonds indicate what 
improvements the Company should make to obtain a better score on all practice indicators. A more 
complete and improved assessment gives Ramboll the opportunity to gain more insights from the 
results and to guide the SBM innovation process even better. This is beneficial to the Company, as an 
improved SBM can lead to an improved corporate sustainability performance and thus targeting more 
responsible investors.  
7.2 Policymaker perspective 
Besides corporations, policymakers can also benefit from this thesis. As mentioned in the literature 
review, businesses play an important role in achieving the SDGs. By linking the PAS for SDG Bonds to 
the SBM, businesses will not only be able to apply the framework, but also embed it in their core 
strategy. This thesis supports that companies not only use the PAS as an assessment framework for 
their SDG Bonds, but also to act upon the results and incorporate them into their business. This will 
improve the overall performance of companies on corporate sustainability, and thus on the sustainable 
development and the SDGs (Figure 9). This will support the policymakers, such as governments or 
international organizations, in achieving the SDGs and their sustainable development targets.  
Besides achieving the SDGs and overall sustainable development through improved corporate 
sustainability, the mapping of the PAS for SDG Bonds to the SBM principles can also support the 
decision-making process. The assessment indicates how companies are currently performing on both 
the PAS for SDG Bonds and possibly also the SBM principles, which provides information on where and 
how the policymaker can support. Examples of this could be facilitating or influencing the SDG Bond 
market by means of subsidies or taxes.  
A policymaker that was especially impacted by this analysis is SDG Impact, an initiative by the UNDP. 
While writing this thesis, there were several moments of contact and communication with the person 
responsible for developing the PAS. This allowed providing feedback for the PAS, after having analyzed 
it closely and having applied it in the case study. The approach on how to link the PAS for SDG Bonds 
to the SBM principles reflects how the standards are aligned with corporate strategy, and what a 
possible approach could be if performing the same analysis for the PAS for Private Equity and the PAS 
for Enterprise. 
7.3 Academic perspective 
Figure 9 shows how this contribution is placed in the context of this thesis. The contribution to the 
academic perspective lies in the approach to the focus of this thesis, which is the mapping of the 
performance assessment framework (the PAS for SDG Bonds) to a strategic tool (the SBM). Existing 
literature has shown that the link to business strategy is often missing in performance assessment 
frameworks (Maas et al., 2016b, 2016a). This thesis explores how a performance assessment 
==PAGE:54==
54 
 
framework can be incorporated into the strategic level of a company, contributing to closing this 
identified research gap. It sets an example of how to approach linking strategy and performance 
assessment frameworks, and it would be interesting to investigate whether this approach would also 
apply to other assessment frameworks. Another research gap this thesis addresses is the lack of 
empirical studies on performance assessment frameworks (Büyükozkan & Karabulut, 2018). There are 
limited case studies performed where performance assessment frameworks are treated as a decision 
support tool for businesses. Performing such a study can transform the understanding of the literature 
(Büyükozkan & Karabulut, 2018).  
Besides the performance measurement literature, this thesis also contributes to the corporate 
sustainability literature. Researchers have found that sustainability performance assessment needs to 
be incorporated into strategic levels of the companies to enable contributing to corporate 
sustainability (Morioka et al., 2018b; Morioka & de Carvalho, 2016; Thijssens et al., 2016). This thesis 
contributes to this field of literature by exploring whether a sustainability performance assessment 
framework, namely the PAS for SDG Bonds, can be incorporated into the strategic levels of companies 
through SBM innovation. 
Furthermore, this thesis also supports sustainable business model literature. By conducting a literature 
review and building upon previous research, it identifies five principles that are characteristic for an 
SBM (Table 1). Four of these principles are mainly based on a research by Breuer et al. (2018a), and 
backed by many other researchers (Baldassarre et al., 2017; Bellucci et al., 2020; Dembek & York, 2019; 
Evans et al., 2017; Geissdoerfer et al., 2018a; Morioka et al., 2018a, 2016; Schaltegger et al., 2012). 
Many of these researchers also mentioned the long-term perspective as a typical characteristic of an 
SBM, leading to expanding the list of four principles developed by Breuer et al. (2018a) to five principles 
in this thesis. Besides the SBM principles, this thesis also supports the assessment of the sustainability 
performance of business models. This is done by assessing a company with the PAS for SDG Bonds, 
which is thereafter linked to the SBM principles. This results in an assessment that indicates how a 
company performs on the SBM principles, which in turn indicates how the current business model is 
performing and what opportunities for improvement and innovation of the business model are. 
Assessment of the sustainability performance of a business model has been identified as a research 
gap in existing SBM literature (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2017; Rauter et al., 2019; Schoormann et al., 2018). 
Pieroni et al. (2019) mention in their paper that to advance the SBM innovation literature, future 
research should be addressing sustainability oriented business model innovation as an ongoing process. 
Many approaches in this literature field address SBM innovation as an individual and single-stage 
process. This thesis takes this approach by adopting the three stages of the SBM innovation 
process: ’sensing’, ’seizing’, and ’transforming’ (Bocken & Geradts, 2019; Pieroni et al., 2019; Teece, 
2018). Furthermore, Pieroni et al. (2019) also emphasize the need for more case studies in the field of 
SBM innovation literature to move the approaches from a theoretical stage to an empirical phase, 
giving insights on SBM innovation approaches in a different context. In this thesis, a case study has 
been applied as a research method, thus contributing to the SBM innovation literature. 
7.4 Limitations and further research 
When considering the outcome of this thesis, several limitations should be taken into account. Firstly, 
the analysis has only been conducted for the PAS for SDG Bonds. However, two more sets of PAS are 
under development, namely the PAS for Private Equity and the PAS for Enterprise. It would be 
interesting to analyze whether the approach in this thesis is also applicable to these sets of PAS, or 
==PAGE:55==
55 
 
whether it only applies to the PAS for SDG Bonds. As all three PAS will have a similar structure and are 
based on similar frameworks, it is probable that the approach should apply to the PAS for Private Equity 
and the PAS for Enterprise. However, there is a possibility that the outcomes will be different. For 
example, the PAS for Enterprise might be linked to the sustainability oriented principle more often, as 
it will have a bigger focus on the Company’s strategy, mission, and vision. In future research, it would 
be interesting to analyze whether the approach used in this thesis is also applicable to the PAS for 
Private Equity and the PAS for Enterprise. 
Furthermore, it should be considered that a Consultation Draft version of the PAS for SDG Bonds was 
applied in this thesis, as the final version is not fully developed yet. This implies that the PAS for SDG 
Bonds are still subject to change, which will also have an impact on the outcomes. Therefore, it would 
be interesting to analyze whether the approach in this thesis is still relevant once the final version is 
published. 
Concerning SBM innovation, the scope of this thesis is limited to the sensing and seizing stages of the 
SBM innovation process. The effect of the PAS for SDG Bonds on the transforming stage is not 
addressed. It is suggested that in future research the link between the PAS for SDG Bonds and the 
transforming stage of the SBM innovation process is analyzed. The transforming stage includes 
implementing the business model and realigning the structure and culture of the company. This stage 
has not been taken into this thesis due to time constraints. Analyzing how the approach in this thesis 
could be continued to the transforming stage of the SBM innovation process would give interesting 
insights on how the identified opportunities in the sensing and seizing stage can be translated to the 
business model. An example of such research could be to define what specific opportunities relate to 
the value proposition, value creation and delivery, or value capture of a company. In this way, new 
business model concepts can be developed and implemented.  
In this thesis, the PAS for SDG Bonds is applied to a company within the energy production industry. It 
has not been analyzed whether the industry sector of the company has a large impact on the outcome 
of the analysis. It would be interesting to research whether choosing a company in another sector 
would affect the results and if so, in what way. A possibility is that this company might be in an industry 
sector that Ramboll is not specialized in, which could lead to hiring another advisory company. The 
results will then not be analyzed from Ramboll’s perspective, but from the perspective of another 
advisory company that might take different approaches. However, as the PAS for SDG focusses mainly 
on the SDG Bonds and not on the company’s overall strategy, it is expected that the impact of a 
company from a different sector will not be substantial. The analysis will most likely still result in 
indicating opportunities for the SDG bond program, which could also form opportunities for the 
sustainable business model. How these opportunities are implemented will probably differ per 
company and sector, however, this is not in the scope of this thesis as it refers to the transforming 
stage of the sustainable business model innovation process. 
Lastly, it should be noted that the data used for the PAS for SDG Bonds included data made publicly 
available by the Company, and no internal documentation or interviews as recommended in the PAS. 
This limited the accuracy and completeness of the results of the PAS for SDG Bonds assessment for the 
Company and may have also affected the results of this thesis. Also, it is unclear in the PAS for SDG 
Bonds whether all practice indicators are equally important. It is therefore complicated to assess 
whether the Company is performing ‘better’ or ‘worse’ on specific practice indicators and as a result 
on the SBM principles. It would be interesting to investigate whether all practice indicators and 
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
subsequently the standards are of equal importance, and if not, how it would influence the outcome 
of the mapping method utilized in this thesis.
