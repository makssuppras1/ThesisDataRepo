Abstract
This work proposes an ebike control algorithm that can mitigate the ever­changing distur­
bances occurring during a bike ride. Model Reference Adaptive Control (MRAC) algorithm
is chosen as a suitable tool to tackle this task.
Mathematical model of the bike system is developed to calculate obedient and reference
velocity during perfect road conditions based on the cyclist power input. Plant model
i.e., physical ebike is represented as a system with ever­changing disturbances such as
wind, slope gradient, etc. Adaptive controller within MRAC compares the plant velocity
with reference velocity and computes the control command that is executed by the ebike
motor. This results in matching the ebike velocity with the desired reference value.
To prove the efficacy of the solution, simulation of the algorithm is performed. To expose
the controller to the real­life data, Physical Testing Platform is developed. Experiments
are carried out in the indoor as well as outdoor environment.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Ebikes are becoming more popular for people in all ages which causes the growth of the
ebike market in recent years [1]. In contrary to the common belief, ebikes can promote
the active lifestyle and encourage people to use ebikes for commuting instead of sitting
in a car or public transport [2]. However, after decades of ebike existence on the market,
the riding experience is still far from perfect. The motivation for this work is to improve
ebike riding experience so it makes the rides feel more natural and safe.
1.1.1
Ebike impact and growth
Ebikes sales between 2010­2016 grew by 284 % and in 2017 the ebike sales make 8.7 %
of all bike sold in the EU [3]. The light electric vehicles industry is a fast growing sector
in recent years and it is no longer limited to ebikes [4]. Bike industry is changing with the
development of e.g. cargo bikes capable of carrying extra load besides the rider, or serial
hybrid ebikes where there is no mechanical connection between the pedals and the wheel
­ see Figure 1.1. EU sees the growth of this market and is trying to adapt the laws that can
regulate this e­moblity sector [5]. An assistive control algorithm needs to be developed in
order for those ebike types to feel equally intuitive to operate as the conventional bikes.
Figure 1.1: Different types of unconventional electric bikes. Cargo [6], serial hybrid [7]
and hydrofoil [8] water bike.
Ebike dangers
According to Allianz [9] the risk of fatalities on an ebike is 3x higher than on a conventional
bicycle. Even though the EU is putting speed and power restrictions which limit their speed
to 25 km/h, the accidents still occur. One of the reason for decreased ebike safety might
be the imperfect ebike ride feeling.
1.2
Scope and hypothesis
The goal of this work is to develop the ultimate ebike assistive control algorithm. The
assistive algorithm should feel natural for the rider as he would ride the conventional bike
and yet still feel the assist from the motor on the ebike.
In this work, in order to create the control algorithm algorithm, the ebike perfect and still
natural riding experience hypothesis is stated. The perfect ebike ride is when the rider
does not feel the ever changing road conditions. The wind, road inclination and surface
condition etc. should not affect the rider’s experience of riding. The assistive system
should be designed in the way such that it mitigates these disturbances so the ebike ride

also provide the further assistance that can be felt as a light wind breeze from the back.
To see the perfect conditions definition, see Section 3.2.4.
1.3
Thesis structure
Firstly to achieve the goal, research on the current solutions is made in Chapter 2. Then
the introduction into the bike and ebike properties and concepts for riding experience are
described in Chapter 3.
Chapter 4 consists of simulation of ebike dynamics with Newton’s law, including equations
and model in Simulink. Main purpose of this chapter is to derive the obedient velocity in
perfect riding conditions and prepare the system to apply the Model Reference Adaptive
Control (MRAC) algorithm.
In the next Chapter 5, the chosen control algorithm is elaborated, including necessary
theory behind MRAC alongside with its Simulink model for ebike use case. In the same
Chapter 5 the efficacy of simulated control algorithm is presented.
In order to make this controller robust not only in simulation, physical testing platform is
developed, described and validated in Chapter 6. The tests from simulation, the lab setup
with an adjustable resistance trainer and outdoor rides are described in Chapter 7.
The conclusions and future work ideas can be found in Chapter 8.

2
State of the art
Before deciding on the ebike assistive control algorithm which provides a natural riding
experience, state of the art research is performed. First two sections are about the riding
differences among different bike types and human muscle types. Last section of this
chapter is the main research that is conducted ­ ebike control algorithms.
2.1
Riding mechanics
Some efforts are made to study bike stability [10] and stability differences depending on
bike frame shapes [11] and research the behaviour when steering [12]. From those papers
it can be concluded, that different types of frames, tyres, surface conditions and rider’s
reaction time, center of mass position influence steering behaviour at different speeds.
The steering behaviour is probably one of many riding mechanics that may vary when
taking electric bike into consideration. The ebikes usually operates at different accelera­
tion profiles (hence the velocities) than conventional bikes. User might not correctly adjust
his steering behaviour to the occurring situations on the ride. One may assume this can
be dangerous to the rider and people around.
2.2

2.2.1

is treating specifically cycling as a use case. The model includes maximum isometric
force for fully rested person MV C, parameter of recovery of an individual Rf, fatigue
constant of an individual kf. The equation below describes the optimal isometric force

Researchers try to fuse the fatigue models into control algorithms. Bergmann et al. [14]
developed a control system for a ride planning that would ensure the rider is not fatigued
at the end of the ride. It takes the battery discharge element into consideration.
Due to the fact fatigue is not the same for all people and the tests need to be carried out
for each rider individually, this approach is not developed in this work.
Safety
Safety and comfort aspect of a control solution was considered in Hsu et al. work [15].
They focus on the acceleration as an important factor in those criteria. Finding the univer­
sal factors of ebike riding safety is a difficult task on its own and there next to no papers
are found in this area of research.
2.3
Ebike control
Here, all ebike control strategies and techniques from literature review are presented.
Most of the articles described in this section validated their algorithms through simulation.
Hence it is difficult to evaluate how a given approach will behave in a real life scenario.
If the article has an physical experiments part, it is highlighted below. Some approaches
[16] are not focusing on the control algorithm, but on delivering the whole system instead.

This type of papers are not considered because of the availability of ebike conversion
kits that easily can transform conventional bike to electric. The goal is to find the ultimate
ebike control algorithm that is safe and comfortable to the user in all conditions. Moreover
the desired control algorithm should follow the hypothesis stated before in Section 1.2.
2.3.1
Conventional control methods
Roy Chaoming et al. [15] are dedicating a part of their research to make a real­life ex­
periment comparing the Constant­assisted power (CAP) and Proportional­assisted power
(PAP) methods, proving the PAP superiority. However, PAP is widely recognized as not
ideal solution for ebike control [17]. This makes the conventional control methods not
sufficient in terms of the riding performance.
Constant­assisted power (CAP)
Constant­assisted power is based on the same level of assist no matter if cyclist is ped­
alling or not [18]. This solution requires the throttle button/lever, so riding with CAP re­
sembles motorcycle experience, rather than ebike. In modern ebikes, this solution is no
longer used since its many drawbacks regarding the user interaction with the system and
safety.
Proportional­assisted power (PAP)
Proportional­assisted power is probably the most common approach within the ebike con­
trol thanks to its simplicity [15]. The motor power output is proportional by a constant f to
the cyclist power which results in the total power:
Ptotal = Pm + Pcyc = Pcyc · f + Pcyc
(2.2)
There are variations of this control model, depending on the available sensors on the
ebike:
• PAP based on the cadence only. The power of the motor is proportional to the
cadence of the rider. Results in a low assist level, when is is expected to have more
assistance, because the bike gear ratio is high (high torque) and the cadence is low.
Moreover, there is an awkward feeling of high assist level when there is only high
cadence on low gear ratio, with human torque close to 0 Nm.
• PAP based on the torque. There are no drawbacks of this variant of PAP in mid mo­
tors designs, as the cadence can be omitted ­ described in Section 3.2.2. However,
the drawbacks of PAP control in general are still existing. In hub motors designs,
torque proportional control still makes more sense than the cadence only propor­
tional control.
• Full PAP with power as an input ­ both torque and cadence, as in Equation 2.2.
This is the most common option for higher end ebikes, as both torque and cadence
sensors are necessary. This variant is especially useful in starting scenario.
2.3.2
Velocity­based control
There are many articles that treat maintaining the bike velocity constant as a main criteria
of a successful ebike contorller [19, 20, 21, 22, 23, 15, 24, 25, 26, 27]. Yet, keeping
the same velocity will never be the goal of riding the bike. In this work, this common
methodology will not be explored for numerous of reasons mentioned below.
The cyclist does not have a velocity as a main criteria when riding. It is the power, that
he can regulate by changing the gears. As a consequence, the bike velocity is a result
of the power put into the ebike subtracted by the resistances occurring at that moment.
However, with ever­changing road conditions and disturbances, the velocity is influenced.
Mentioned papers only focus on keeping the velocity constant, which in reality would be
very dangerous. Designing the regulator to keep the constant velocity is not safe.

2.3.3
Torque­based motor control
Even though, some algorithms may seem like the torque control was used, still velocity
was utilized as a base. Chen et al. [28] proposed proportional and assisted torque motor
control, but the cyclist torque was estimated by comparing the current speed with the
desired speed.
Gromba [29] used torque motor control method by manually setting the desired motor
torque and observing the change in speed.
Thejasree et al. [30] proposed three modes of the assist, with different current limiters.
There are attempts to focus on the rider’s torque and design the controller accordingly
[31].
2.3.4
Sensor­less design
This section of state of the art mostly considers compact ebike motor applications with
no sensors integrated due to limited budget. This solutions are gaining its popularity on
crowdfunding [32], but never go into mass production. There are quite a few papers
released with novel approaches for a better ride feeling, which will be presented in this
section.
In Niki et al. work [33], the rider’s torque together with running friction was expressed
as disturbance torque with disturbance observer. They simulated and made experiments
that proved rider feels as if he ran on the flat road all the time, thanks to compensation of
real running friction.
Similar approach was shown by Deyi et al. [34]. They also focused on compensating the
slope gradient, however wind and rolling resistance was omitted. Others used Kalman
filter to estimane the road slope [35].
Kai et al. [36] developed an adaptive model which takes into consideration not only the
slope but also a bike body load prediction by disturbance observer. By knowing the torque
generated by the motor and the speed, the controller can detect the road slopes and other
disturbances. By comparing the perfect model response with the real response, the power
of the motor can be adjusted accordingly. Plant was normalized by disturbance observer
[37]. In the results section of this approach, the counter intuitive situation was observed
where the bike obtained more speed on 10 % slopes than on the declined ones with −10
% slope. However, the change in bike body load resulted in correct response.
In all those aforementioned approaches they do not take all of the disturbances that are
happening during the ride such as surface friction, wind or bike malfunctions. These phe­
nomena are difficult to predict with sensor­less designs. However, the solution to this
problem might not be putting more sensors where in the ebike market every cost matters.
Moreover, as the air drag resistance can make up to 90 % of all resistances the cyclist
has to overcome [38], the approaches [35, 34, 33] which omitted the air drag resistance
cannot be trustworthy.
In principle, the rider does not care if the wind or the slope value is predicted correctly
by his ebike. According to the hypothesis stated in Section 1.2, a controller’s task is to
compensate the effort to reach the top of the hill and adjust the assist level in such a
way that the uphill ride is perceived as riding the on a flat surface. Obtaining the value
of the disturbances is not a primiary task in this use case, hence all those sensorless
approaches in this section might not be suitable to match the hypothesis.

2.3.5
Machine learning approach
Hsu et al. [15] used a model of comfort and safety of the rider defined by certain ranges
of velocities at a given time. This desired velocity is set a reward function in Reinforced
Learning (RL) to optimize the power assist. Authors concluded ever changing human
pedal power output due to fatigue and road conditions is still a problem to correctly esti­
mate the riders demand for desired motor support.
Hatada et al. [26] proposed a cadence estimation algorithm in order to decrease the fluc­
tuation of the velocity occuring when riding. However this effect seems to be an integrated
part of biking experience and removing it could make bike riding more artificial.
Liu et al. [27] proposed fuzzy logic controller treating pedalling power, bike velocity and
gradient of the road as input. Using Q­learning allowed for tuning the parameters as the
trip goes. The simplification of treating the air drag and friction as constant is made. They
build an experimental platform to verify the simulation and results are promising in regards
to keeping acceleration profiles consistent. The tests are carried out already at the point
where bike is moving, not considering the starting scenario.
There is a significant concern using Machine Learning (ML) approach in human life­
dependant application. Treating the control algortihm logic as a black box that gives com­
mands to accelerate the ebike can be dangerous to the user and the surroundings. Even
with good statistical proof of proving the ML algorithm to work in a predictable way in 99.9
%, there is still a small chance to deviate from the rule.
The logic of the control algorithm should to be fully predictable in all scenarios.
Moreover in most of those solutions slope or wind sensor is necessary. This increases
the bike cost, hence the feasibility at larger production scale.
2.3.6
Model Reference Adaptive Control
In contrary to the conventional and Machine Learning based approaches that were found
in relation to ebike control algorithm, Model Reference Adaptive Control (MRAC) is not
found as explored in the bike related papers. However, Model Reference Adaptive Con­
trol [39, 40, 41] is commonly used in aircrafts [42, 43] to e.g. compensate wing rocking
[44]. The learning mechanism is built into the controller in the form of adaptive law, which
is proven to always converge to desired value ­ see Section 5.1.2. In spite of the gain
scheduling method, the gains are adjusted real time [45] in MRAC. This allows the con­
troller to react correctly even in the situations which are not pre­designed before.

System description
Before developing the contorl algorithm that would enhance the riding experience, the
exploration of the system is made. The understanding of bike mechanics, important prop­
erties and also ebike concepts is essential to deliver the control algorithm that feels like
riding the ebike with a gentle backwind breeze without any unwanted disturbances.
3.1
Bike overview
In Figure 3.1 common bike parts are mentioned. Parts indicated in purple e.g. bottom
bracket, crank arm etc., can be referred as a drivetrain. The force from both pedals is
transferred to the bottom bracket (Figure 3.2), which interferes with the chain by a chain­
ring. Applying the force on the pedals, which are at a certain distance (0.165­0.175 m)
from axis of rotation, results in torque generation. Rotational velocity of the axis (how
fast the rider move the pedal) is called cadence. The torque combined with cadence is
represented by power. Chain transfers the power to the rear wheel that moves the bike
forward.

3.2
Cycling variables
After a mechanical overview of the bike parts, introduction to cycling variables is made.
Firstly, the input variables coming from the cyclist are described. Afterwards, the resis­
tances counteracting the cyclist power are explained.
3.2.1
Power of the rider
The definition of power in the context of biking is the product of the torque applied by
a cyclist Tcyc in Nm and the rotational speed ω in rad/sec of the crankarm at which the
torque is generated. In the bike nomenclature, ω is in rpm unit and is called cadence.
Power of the rider is described by the equation below:
P = Tcyc · ωcyc
(3.1)
Most of the professional riders have a power meter on their bikes. By knowing their power
expenses at given time, they can estimate how much energy they can spend before get­
ting too fatigued. There are many metrics derived from power tests that can optimize the
performance by optimal energy distribution [48].
Competitive cyclists focus on the power they generate. As the bike velocity may fluc­
tuate because of the terrain, wind or other disturbances, it is the power that should be
kept at the certain constant level to make the ride efficient.
Adjusting the bike gears to achieve certain power, not velocity should be the concept to
follow not only by the professionals but also amateurs riders as well.
However, the same amount of power can be achieved by increasing one of two com­
ponents ­ torque or cadence. In order to design the ebike motor algorithm, it is important
to understand the power equation with its nuances specific to biking. The torque and
cadence are correlated to each other ­ as the rider increases cadence, the torque de­
creases. To maintain the same torque while bike speed increases, switching to a higher
gear is necessary.
People have specific muscle fibers composition that can vary across all individuals. This
results in different preferred cadence or/and torque [38]. Some people with more slow
twitch fibers prefer higher torque and hence lower cadence. Individuals with less slow
twitch and more fast twitch fibers prefer the opposite. Both preferences still results in the
same power output.
The relation of torque and cadence can be described by the Figure 3.5 below, taken from
Douglas et al. research [38]. In Figure 3.5 the optimal power generated by the rider for
high intensity ride is at approximately 120 rpm for healthy adults without relevant power­
training. The optimal point can lean towards higher torque and lower cadence (force
based) or the opposite (velocity based), depending on the person’s fibers build.

Cadence
Cadence is already described in previous sections, especially in Section 3.2.1. As torque

its range that all bike users are expected to fit into. The summary of expected cadence

Slope gradient is expressed in percentage by division of rise and run of the slope. The

• 2 % gradient for no length limit,
• 4 % gradient for maximum 4 km,
• 5 % gradient for maximum 2 km,

Road type
There are many possible road surfaces that a bike can encounter: gravel, asphalt, forest
road, pavement brick etc. Each type results in different riding experience due to their
surface elasticity, smoothness and rolling friction which causes the change in amortization
and acceleration. According to Copenhagen Kommune [60], asphalt is the surface of
choice.
Air resistance
Air drag plays a substantial role compared to all other resistances. On a flat road it can
make up to 90 % of all resistances that a cyclist has to overcome ­ more details provided
in Section 4.2.2.
Part of air resistance is not only drag that a cyclist creates while riding at certain velocity,
but also wind velocity. Head wind increases the resistance even more, making the ride
more exhausting.
As the air resistance plays such a significant role, the bike designers of frames, clothes
and accessories are trying to find the ways to minimize the drag coefficient and total bike
frontal area through wind tunnel experiments and CFD simulations [61].

perceived as the perfect conditions, this section is created. As all of the resistances are
defined in the previous sections, the perfect riding conditions can be derived based on
the values of resistances. The perfect riding conditions are defined as follows:
• Slope gradient value is 0%.
• No head wind: wind velocity value is equal to 0 m/s.
• Cyclist is riding on the asphalt road.

Ebike differences
In this section, the main differences between the ebikes are mentioned.
3.3.1
Motor placement
Hub motors, mid motors or other non standard designs [32] are the main types of motors
that an ebike can have and each has different placement on vehicle. All of the locations
have their advantages and disadvantages described below.
Mid motors can damage the cassette and chain because of the slack in the freewheel
in the rear hub [62]. Moreover, to place the mid motor, the bike frame has to be designed
specifically for the chosen motor. The advantage of mid motors is the possible high torque
generation thanks to their gearbox system. While EU limits the ebike power to 250 W, high
torque can be very useful while starting or riding on a steep hill.
Hub motors have the advantage of direct power transfer to the wheel. They do not cause
extra strain on the chain or other parts. They can fit most of the bike frames on the market
as well. The main disadvantage of this solution is limited gear ratio that can be developed
due to its dimensional constraints.
Currently on the ebike market both types of motor designs are popular and each sec­
tor is coming up with better solutions each year [63, 64].

The EU introduced set of rules for electric bikes. Here is a short summary of the most

3.5
Safety
There were many attempts to include safety as a measurable factor in research papers
related to grading the motor control [27, 24]. The main observations about safety are sum­
marized in the list below. More in depth analysis is reported in the following subsections.
• Assist cannot work when cyclist’s torque or cadence is equal to 0.
• Acceleration must not exceed 5 m/s2 during start and 1.5 m/s2 during constant
speed riding. To visualize acceleration, the visualization tool [65] is used.
• Sudden motor cutoff at 25 km/h should be replaced with gradual decrease of assis­
tive level in order to achieve smooth transition.
• During the first 0.5 s of the movement, assist power factor has to be scaled down
and then gradually reach 100%.
3.5.1
Start scenario
Usually, starting the bike is the most difficult part the ebike motor control algorithm has to
face ­ it is when the torque from the rider and acceleration reaches its peak. The rider
would like to get to the desired speed quickly, but also have full control of this process.
This is why the motor control in this scenario has two counteracting aspects:
• Provide sufficient support for to biker with his peak efforts.
• Do not overshoot with the support ­ too much assistance will lead to loosing the
control of the bike and dangerous situations.
To compare the starting and normal constant speed scenario, the Table 3.1 is made. To
get total power (Pcyc = Tcycωcyc), the peak torque is divided by 2, to get the average of
sinusoidal wave signal. The power in constant speed scenario is matching the functional
threshold point FTP (the highest power a cyclist can maintain in a quasi­steady state
without fatiguing for approximately 1 h) [48] of average commuters ­ lower limit, and non­
professional riders ­ higher limit.
The acceleration optimal bandwidth in the starting scenario is equal to reaching the speed
of 25 km/h in 15 s and 2 s. In constant speed scenario, it is assumed that variations of
1­3 km/h during 5 s can occur. All of these values are presented in Table 3.1.

gerous situation, especially if additionally boosted with a motor. This is why response time
is identified as a important factor to research. The answer is not obvious, as the lower
response time of the ebike motor controller does not necessarily mean the safer solution,

3.5.2
Human response and bike response time
Response time highly depends on the context. In 1968 article [66], 100 ms is described
as instant, and 1 s or less is labelled as fast enough to feel that a person is interacting
with an object. However, with technology and internet development, the criteria changed.
According to Carroll [67], in online gaming the target response time (data stream latency)
is below 50 ms, where 300 ms makes the game unplayable. This shows the latency of the
motor control system should be below in range of 50­100 ms in order to feel the smooth
interaction with an ebike described as instantaneous.
However, this does not mean that the assist can be triggered full power in under 300 ms.
Amplifying cyclist’s torque in such short notice is a recipe for accidents.
The power of the assist should be limited in first 1 s of the movement and then linearly
expanded to full capacity until 5 s of continuous riding is reached. These are assumptions
that need to be verified on the physical ebike setup.

4.1
Main focus of the model
In order to develop any control algorithm, bike dynamic model has to be simulated. The
goal of this ebike simulation is to obtain the bike velocity given the power of the cyclist
and the disturbances on the way.
The resistance disturbances described in Section 3.2.4 such as slope gradient and wind
can be adjusted to simulate the bike ride in perfect riding conditions to derive the obe­
dient velocity necessary for the development of the chosen Model Reference Adaptive
Control algorithm. The disturbances can also represent the ever changing environment
that negatively influence the bike velocity and experience.
4.1.1
Simplifications & out of the scope parameters
The developed simulation does not fully reflect the real life ride. Here some simplifications
of the model are listed:
• Power from the battery to the motor is always the same ­ no discharging effect
• No outliers coming from the sensors.
• There is no delay coming from electronics (sensors, controllers etc.).
• Continuous datastream from the sensors is avalible.
• Assistive power delivery can happen instantly.
Following phenomena are discarded from the model:
• Battery usage and load.
• In­depth motor simulation i.e. motor windings behavior, temperature effects, gear­
box backlash.
• Rider’s kinematic model.
• Fatigue of the rider.
• Bike dynamics while taking sharp turns.

MRAC Architecture for Ebike Application ­ Control
Design Overview
Figure 5.1 represent an overview of the chosen control algorithm and its interaction with
the system. The components on this overview are divided into three groups:
• MRAC: these components are related to the chosen ebike control algorithm itself.
36

• Environment: These components are provided from external sources. Either they
are measured by sensors and known such as cyclist torque, or their values remain
unknown to the controller such as disturbances.
• Plant: represent a physical ebike system where both Environment and MRAC is
acting on.
Below, each component of proposed ebike algorithm is described.
Cyclist
Cyclist is modelled by his ability to generate torque over time with certain cadence ­ see
Section 4.2.1. This information is sufficient to provide the thrust force to the system.
Obedient Model
Obedient Model is the virtual bike system that reacts to the applied torque by responding
with a velocity. Obedient Model is a bike model driven in perfect conditions, without distur­
bances. The bike system is developed in Section 4.3. Here, there is no motor assistance.
This part of the algorithm is responsible for calculating the velocity control signal c = vob
of the bike that would occur if there are perfect conditions on the road and with certain
total mass of the system. This is the velocity that the reference model is based on and
obey with a different time constant and dynamics, hence the name obedient velocity.
Reference Model
The Reference Model describes how the Plant should behave when the control signal
from Obedient Model is provided. In this case, the Reference Model has more inertia
than the Obedient Model. As an input it takes an obedient velocity vob and transforms it
in such a way the bumps caused by torque spikes are smoothed, which in reality should
limit the catapult effect and other unwanted reactions from the ebike controller. Then, the
reference velocity is compared with plant real velocity and through the MRAC adaptive
law algorithm, the adjustment is made such that the error between plant and reference
velocity is minimized.
Plant Model
The Plant is the physical bike system that reacts to the applied torque by responding with
a velocity. It this aspect, it is the same as the Obedient Model. However, the difference
is that here the road conditions are not perfect as in the Obedient Model ­ in the Plant
Model the conditions are unpredictable, because plant is in the real world, while Obedient
(and Reference) Model is just virtual. In the Obedient Model, the slope gradient and wind
are equal to 0, here in the Plant they are unknown variables, modelled as Disturbances.
The Disturbances are the reason the Plant Model outputs different velocity than obedient
velocity and reference velocity. To match the plant velocity to the reference velocity, the
adaptive controller is developed.
Disturbances Model
To act on the Plant Model, the Disturbances Model is developed. It simulates the slope
gradient and wind speed that are transferred into the Plant Model as unknown resistance
forces.
Adaptive Law
To counteract the disturbances, the Adaptive Law is introduced. This is the essential
part of MRAC, that outputs the assistance level dependant on the ever­changing road
conditions. As the constant values of the system such as total mass or wheel diameter
can be compensated through nominal controller, it is the adaptive controller that makes
this solution work in every conditions. It compensates the error between the plant and

reference velocity by providing a control command. Adaptive Law is furtherly explained
in subsequent sections and also in the Methodology Section 5.1.2.
Motor and Motor Controller
This is a actuator part that is executing the adaptive controller command onto the plant.
As they are mounted on the ebike, they are perceived as a part of the plant. They execute
adaptive command and provide the additional torque directly to the plant’s wheel.

Torque
Torque voltage is obtained every time the sensor voltage changes. In order to derive the
obedient velocity, the voltage is multiplied by a constant ratio that converts the voltage to
torque values. The torque data frequency is not constant, but it is only utilized every time
the main algorithm MRAC() function thread is executed, which happens every 0.1 s.
Cadence
The ideal solution for obtaining the cadence would be by getting the cadence sensor.
However due to limited resources, another way has to be found. One way of doing it
is deriving cadence from torque data, but as this is not the main focus of the work, the
cadence is chosen to be a constant at 60 rpm. There are many motivations behind this
decision.
Firstly, cadence uncertainty is very high in the starting scenarios. To have an initial guess
about the cadence based on torque data, at least full crank rotation has to be made. This
is not sufficient in the starting scenario, so might as well use constant cadence.
Moreover, the cadence for a city commuter ranges from about 30 rpm at the start scenario
to 90 rpm in constant speed scenario ­ see Table 3.1. Hence, the power of the rider is
overestimated at the start and underestimated later on. Usually the most energy and
fatigue is exerted at the start, power overestimation may be desirable. However, the best
solution would be having a reliable cadence sensor in the first place.
6.2.3
Control algorithm
The separate thread is created to invoke all most recent values from the sensors and feed
them into the the control algorithm. The algorithm outputs the current command that is
sent to the motor controller. This thread is computed every 0.1 s. At the end of this thread,
all values of interest are printed out into the terminal, including the timestamp. The values
of interest can be changed, but for testing purposes, they are selected as follows:
• Timestamp which is the time elapsed from the start of the ride.
• Torque from the rider putting force on the pedals.
• Obedient velocity is the computed velocity from Newton’s law bike equation at this
timestamp and with rider’s torque. The coefficients of this equations such as drag,
friction, wind speed and slope gradient are equal to the ride in perfect conditions.
• Reference velocity derived from the obedient velocity.
• Plant velocity is the actual velocity of the physical testing platform. It is different
from reference or obedient velocity due to the fact that the rides are never in
perfect conditions.
• Adaptive law command is computed from MRAC control algorithm. It compares
the plant with reference velocity and computes the control command.
• Current command is sent to the motor controller. In theory this should be equal to
adaptive law command, but during the physical tests, some limiters are introduced
for better performance.

from the simulation are already described in Section 5.4, hence they will not be revisited.
In this chapter, the results from rides on the trainer as well as outdoor rides are presented.
7.1
Tests methodology
The main goal of the tests is to see how well the control algorithm fulfill hypothesis of the
natural riding experience. The hypothesis stated good and natural ebike riding experience
is when the rider does not feel the influence of the disturbances. This mean that while
applying the same constant torque profile, the cyclist will still maintain his velocity even
if the resistances increases of decreases. The sudden changes in resistances should
be compensated with the motor assistance. The main criteria to test is the bike (plant)
velocity compared to reference velocity.
As a side note, it is worth to mention the tests cannot be properly carried out without
the same input data. Physical Testing Platform counts on the human to deliver the input
power, hence the direct comparison of the experiments is difficult as the input is not 100%
reproducible.
7.1.1
Performance indices
The algorithm’s performance indices are mentioned below. Velocity error: average ve­
locity difference between plant and reference velocity should be kept low, preferably below
1.4 m/s (5 km/h) even with changed resistance. Moreover, the velocity overshoot error
is examined, as it may lead to dangerous situations on the road.
Acceleration: the criteria of safety defined in Section 3.5 are revisited. Most of the other
safety considerations are already solved through software limiters, so here to assessment
of the controller behaviour is mainly from the acceleration profiles standpoint. The accel­
eration optimal maximum range is 3.5 m/s2 and 0.15 m/s2 in starting and constant speed
scenario respectively. As the edge case previously defined, the acceleration cannot ex­
ceed 5 m/s2 and 1.5 m/s2 in the respective scenarios.
Motor current command signal: a helpful performance index is a signal coming from the
motor current command. The smoother it is during the time cyclist’s torque is applied, the
less jerk is can be felt by the user. Also, hearing the motor to turn on and off frequently
might not be desirable experience for the rider. This is why this signal is captured and
evaluated.
7.1.2
Test scenarios
First scenario is comparing the influence of road resistances on the controller output. The
way to obtain the consistent and controllable resistance is through stationary riding on the
trainer. See Section 7.2.1 for the results.
Another scenario is getting the bike on the real ride. See Section 7.3 for evaluation of the
dynamic ride in the outdoor environment. The purpose is to examine the efficacy of the
controller when different gears are set by the cyclist and the ride feeling when the ’normal’
biking is introduced, instead of the trainer.

MRAC efficacy
Finally, after analyzing the reference velocity and torque curves similarity, the Model Ref­

Figure 7.4 shows the results of the motor current response. During the ride with higher
resistance, the adaptive controller outputs the motor current values that are higher than
during lower resistance by 0.5 A on average (see Table 7.1). The difference could be
even more significant, if the current limiter is turned off ­ from 30 s the current command
saturates at 5 A at the highest resistance level. As the velocity error between plant and
reference is kept on the same level in both resistance scenarios, the plant velocity stays
constant, even though the resistance increases.
The motor current command signal shape has a similar shape as the torque data. It might
be a good sign, however more stable signal could potentially increase the user comfort.
During the ride the motor jerk is not noticeable by the cyclist, as the input torque generates
the change in velocity as well.
The velocity error hovers around 1 m/s (3.6 km/h) and is not exceeding 2 m/s. The
error is a positive value in most cases, which means the control algorithm hardly ever
overshoots. This can be beneficial to mitigate the catapult effect. The delay of the motor
response does not allow to adjust the plant speed at every pedal stroke, hence the error
resembles the torque signal shape.

8
Conclusions & Future Work
8.1
Conclusions
In this work, the Model Reference Adaptive Control algorithm for the ebike use case has
been developed. Making the simulation to validate the concept and also developing the
Physical Testing Platform has been a great combination to assess the efficacy of the
solution. Here are the main conclusions that are derived from this work:
• The approach taken in quantifying the results from the experiments proved the al­
gorithm to work satisfactory.
• Using Model Reference Adaptive Control approach in ebike use case is proven to
bring good results in the scenario where the disturbances are unknown.
• There is no need to derive accurate value of disturbances, but only to compensate
their negative effect. MRAC is a suitable algorithm to achieve that.
• Having reliable data acquisition is important for the algorithm to succeed.
• Results from the simulation are different from the real life data, as the cyclist input
is more unpredictable and cannot be approximated with the sinusoidal wave. More­
over, the system is probably more complicated than simple Newton’s law equations.
• It is important to develop the simulation in order to describe the system and experi­
ment with MRAC implementation.
• After the simulation’s results were promising, putting less focus on modelling and
MRAC simulation was proven as a right approach. Meaningful results came from
the development of the physical testing platform.
• Having repeatable results from the physical experiments is difficult, as humans are
not able to produce the same power output all the time. In that case, simulation is
useful.
8.2
Future Work
To have the ultimate ebike control algorithm, there are still many areas that can be devel­
oped. The importance of the physical reliable testing platform is undeniable.
Future work is focused around the Physical System development. It is believed the real
bike rides are the way to asses the control algorithm efficacy.
8.2.1
Control algorithm development
Derive Plant and Obedient Model based on real data
Having a physical environment where there are perfect conditions would be helpful to
derive most accurate obedient model for the specific ebike. If the environment is fully
controllable with e.g. wind speed, slope gradient and friction coefficient, plant model with
certain disturbances can be derived. This way, the Obedient Models will be individually
developed based on the unique properties of each ebike.
Improve the MRAC algorithm
Nominal controller as LQR or a tuned PID can be applied instead of simple feedback and
feedforward constant gain. More advanced MRAC algorithms and tweaks can be applied.

==PAGE:82==
Moreover, the current implementation is a basic state of the art MRAC, there are papers
with novel approaches that could be worth trying on the physical system.
Improve the MRAC software implementation
MRAC implementation could be improved from the software perspective. Current python
threading implementation might not be the most efficient way.
Introduce a better driver­less mode
For tuning the controller, it could be worth to approach it from the perspective where there
is no cyclist pedalling. Making the system follow the desired velocity curve perfectly would
be a great addition to this work.
Compare parameters of the current algorithm
Experimenting with different mass of the plant, MRAC learning rate, and plant and refer­
ence model parameters could provide even better results. This approach requires a lot
more structured testing. With the stable platform as it is now, it can be performed.
Develop new algorithms
The way that the software is constructed allows to quickly implement new control algo­
rithms. Comparing the efficacy of the algorithms from State of The Art Chapter 2 could
be a direction of further research.
8.2.2
Study the Physical system
Define perfect ebike riding experience
In this work some measures were taken into consideration to derive perfect riding experi­
ence. However, without initially having the Physical Testing Platform, it was very abstract
definition. With all the data that is now available on this Platform, the perfect riding expe­
rience definition should be more feasible.
Delays
Delay of the signal in the ebike can play a big role in the overall solution. Knowing what
are the sources of delays, what is a response time of the motor etc. can provide with
additional information how to improve the Platform.
Compare performance of components
In this ebike platform, the components such as motor controller, sensors, were selected
mainly by the availability and price criteria. It is certain, that better hardware and software
components exist on the market, hence would be interesting to see the comparison of
their performance on this ebike application. Some ideas are presented below.
• Compare the motor contorllers’ performance.
• Mount few e.g. cadence, torque, velocity sensors at the same time and compare
their output during the same ride.
• Study the influence of different motors on the overall riding experience.
Derive cadence from torque data
Interesting subject of research could be using statistical tools or Deep Learning for ca­
dence derivation from torque data.
8.2.3
Improve the Physical Testing Platform
Mechanical improvements
The ebike is still made for use mainly in the labolatory environment by a skilled user. In
order to make the Platform more user­friendly, accessible in any weather conditions me­
chanical adjustments have to be performed. Important feature of this Platform to maintain
is the ease of hardware expansion. By using standard connectors, it should be easy to
mount e.g. new sensor. Another aspect to improve is the Ingress Protection ­ the electrical
components of the ebike should be sealed for safer city rides.

New velocity sensor
Some steps were taken in order to improve the velocity readings. Currently, the Hall
sensor inside the motor is triggered too often to be recognized robustly by data acquisition
Phidget device. New external hall sensor is being developed with 12 magnets around the
bike wheel. This decreases the frequency of triggering the hall sensor, so Phidget can
read the incoming data correctly. This is just a permanent solution, as the best would be
still using the hall sensor inside the motor. There are other ideas to improve the velocity
readings:
• High speed data acquisition device to handle Hall signal from the motor.
• Improve existing Hall + slow phidget solution by making digital motor hall signal to
analog with F2V funciton.
• Buy ebike ABS front wheel system from Bosh and make modifications on it.
Velocity curves without the controller
In order to have a better understanding of a natural riding experience, a raw bike (not
ebike) rides has to be captured. With current setup it is not feasible as the motor Hall
sensor data is not active when the motor is not powered. A better way has to be discovered
to achieve this goal.
New torque sensor with cadence
In this work the outsourced torque sensor is used. As the main expertise of the company
(Sensitivus gauge) collaborating with this thesis is torque sensors, it would be interesting
to see Sensitivus’ torque sensor on the Physical Testing Platform. Having full documen­
tation of the sensor and control over the data stream would increase the robustness of
the solution.
More sensors for detecting the disturbances
To have a better understanding of disturbances, new sensors should be mounted on the
bike. Accelerometer for detecting the slope gradient and pressure sensors would be the
first options to expand the platform.
Miniaturize the hardware
Depending on the future goals for this Platform, it can be beneficial to make the hardware
as small as possible, while still maintaining its functionality. This may involve designing
custom motor controller with the sensors’ data acquisition on board or finding new off the
shelf components.
8.2.4
Simulation improvements
In this work, simulation is only used to validate the implementation of MRAC is correct.
The author of this thesis chose to focus mainly on the physical system, realizing that
making a reliable simulation might be a demanding task, especially without having the
physical reference data. Once the Physical System is developed, the simulation could be
improved.
Simulation derived from Physical System
The simulation was a minor part of this thesis compared to the Physical Testing Platform
development. All the simulations found in the State of The Art Chapter 2 were very similar
to each other, and they have almost no similarity with actual physical system. Deriving
the simulation’s equations straight from the Physical System can improve the simulation
accuracy.
Data quantization
Continuous time series data is different from the data coming every 0.1 s. The simulation
can be changed to compute the algorithm only every 0.1 s to match the physical system
behaviour.
