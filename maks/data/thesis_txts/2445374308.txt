Abstract 
 
The project LivingLab is a prototype house, covered by a semi-outdoor space, and it was originally planned to be 
part of the Solar Decathlon competition for 2019 (SDE19). Initial designs of the project were made by the DTU 
Team which eventually withdrawn from the competition. However, the house is expected to be built in DTU and 
monitored for several months in order to evaluate its performance and investigate the 3rd room concept. The 
concept of the 3rd room, can be characterized by three distinctive areas: The core building, the semi-outdoor 
space surrounding the core building and the shell that constitutes the construction, separating the semi-outdoor 
space from the ambient environment. 
 
The thesis is based on the initial architectural designs made by the DTU Team for the contest. The focus was the 
validation of models with semi-outdoor space in IDA ICE, investigation of the impact of the 3rd room concept on 
the core house, as well as investigation of various HVAC strategies. The software used for energy analysis and the 
indoor climate of the LivingLab, was IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE 4.8).  
 
The results from the validation of the models with the semi-outdoor zone were not very accurate (up to 10% 
depending the investigation) but reasonable enough so the models could be considered trustworthy for the 
purpose of the thesis. Linear losses from thermal bridges on the core house found to be miscalculated, as the 
software have them linked with the ambient temperature despite the fact that they are connected with the semi-
outdoor zone. No benefits can be seen and as a result, space heating consumption is presented always higher 
than it should. Daylight measurements and solar heat gains from direct and diffuse radiation were also found 
unrealistically high. The solar heat gains were higher mostly during summer (1-10% depending in the intensity of 
the radiation) affecting mostly the cooling energy consumption. The 3rd room concept can reduce the impact from 
the infiltration parameter, on the space heating consumption of the reference core house, up to 14% with 
marginal effect on cooling consumption. Infiltration airflows through the shell found particularly low. Age of air 
the semi-outdoor space was unrealistically high and moisture content was also constantly high leading to 1470 
hours of condensation per year. A virtual AHU was designed to ensure a minimum recirculation of air. 
 
The total space heating consumption of the core house was calculated at 1869 kWh/year. The 3rd room concept 
was estimated to decrease it by 374 - 432 kwh/year (about 21-28%) depending on how much natural ventilation 
for cooling was used. The implementation of a ground loop technology as described in this thesis, would further 
decrease the consumption by around 208 kWh/year (-14%) to a final 1264 kWh/year. Glazings with better 
performance on the shell could decrease it further by around 6%. 
 
The total space cooling consumption of the core house was calculated at 427 kWh/year. The 3rd room concept 
was estimated to increase it by 562 kWh/year (57%) to a total of 989 kWh/year. The implementation of a ground 
loop technology would decrease it by 191 kWh/year (-19%) at a total of 796 kWh/per year. Glazings with better 
performance on the shell could decrease the space cooling consumption further by around 30%. 
 
The greenhouse effect that increases the temperature inside the 3rd room, is only observed during daytime with 
solar radiation and lasts until the sun sets in the evening. During these hours of the day the demand for heating 
is already very low or nonexistent. Thus, not a significant amount of energy for space heating is saved. Overall 
The 3rd room concept have a positive impact on the space heating consumption during winter (up to 20-30%) 
and can potentially reduce the cooling consumption of a building during summer, if proper shading techniques 
are used together with open facades for natural ventilation. Increased temperatures can be observed mostly 
during sun hours and especially for low ambient temperatures with high direct solar radiation. Climates that fit 
to the above description are most likely to benefit from the 3rd room concept.

Introduction 
 
In the modern developed world people spend most of their time indoors. According to The National Human 
Activity Pattern Survey (NHAPS), Americans spend 86.9% of time indoors, of which 68.7% in a residence plus 
another 5.5% inside a vehicle [1]. Indoor thermal comfort and air quality receive increasing attention from the 
international scientific community, as they play a significant part in their mental, emotional and general state of 
health. However, increased thermal comfort and air quality is linked with higher energy demand. Therefore, 
several guidelines and standards were developed over the years to ensure thermal satisfaction and maintain 
minimum levels or air quality and reduce adverse health impacts from air pollution while restrict the energy 
consumption in buildings. 
 
Energy use in buildings accounts for about 30% of global final energy consumption [2]. More than half of a 
building’s energy demand depends on the architectural design of the building envelope. Is the part of a building 
that forms a primary thermal barrier with the environment and plays a key role in determining levels of natural 
lighting, comfort, and how much energy is required to heat and cool a building. The design of its structure and 
façade, the orientation towards the sun, the availability of daylight and the choice of glazing and insulation, all 
work together with the building services, HVAC systems and control strategies to produce a great overall 
environment for its occupants.  
 
Several concepts were developed over the years trying to reduce energy consumption in buildings while maintain 
a high level of indoor comfort and air quality. The Passive Solar concept originates from the 1970s and it aim was 
to minimize the energy needs of a building by utilizing the solar gains. Passive solar design takes advantage of a 
building’s site, climate, and materials to reduce heating and cooling loads through the use of solar energy. 
Typically, a passive building collects solar energy through glazed facades and direct it into the building. This energy 
can be used immediately or stored for later use. The passive solar design reduces the space heating consumption, 
but the buildings require high levels of thermal mass for this purpose and can easily suffer from overheating issues 
during the summer.

During the last years the energy requirements for buildings defined by national and international standards,

ventilation and shading techniques can potentially improve the previous overheating problems, with positive 
impact on the cooling consumption.

The semi-outdoor space is heated-up naturally during daytime, allowing the air temperature to raise higher than 
the ambient and closer to the comfort zone of occupants. Even though they are unheated spaces, they can provide 
additional living space when the natural conditions make them comfortable to the occupants. Research on indoor 
thermal comfort shows that people are more tolerant in naturally ventilated buildings and are willing to accept 
conditions that do not entirely comply with the usual standards [4]. Any building that is covered with a semi-
outdoor space, will rise two main concerns. What are the indoor climate conditions inside the semi-outdoor space 
and how can these conditions affect the building and its occupants? An improved method for prediction of thermal 
comfort and indoor air quality is needed as well as detailed physical and mathematical models to describe these 
interactions. Air temperature, solar heat gains, moisture content and CO2 concentration levels, are some of the 
parameters that must be also investigated.  
 
The Solar decathlon competition 
The Solar Decathlon is an international competition created by the U.S. Department of Energy in which universities 
from all over the world meet to design, build and operate a grid-connected, energetically self-sufficient house 
using solar energy as the primary renewable energy source, equipped with the technologies that permit maximum 
energy efficiency. During the final phase of the competition university teams assemble their houses, open to the 
general public, while undergoing the ten contests of the competition [5]. 
 
The DTU participated in the competition with the house FOLD during 2012 in Madrid and again during 2014 in 
Versailles with the house EMBRACE. The original plan was to participate again during 2019 in Budapest with the 
house LivingLab. After the competition, the project was intended to be used for future research on buildings with 
semi-outdoor space, for the university. That was also the case for the houses FOLD and EMBRACE which were 
transported back in Denmark for research. However, the DTU 
team, later decided to withdraw from the competition but 
continue working on the project. The Living lab was the focus on 
this thesis and most of the work was done before the withdrawal. 
As a result, the relevant competition rules were followed, and 
they are frequently mentioned in the text. According to SDE 2019 
rules, the proposed design has to fulfill the Building Code of the 
country of origin, the one of the host country and the Solar 
Decathlon Europe Building Code. However, the Hungarian

architectural design, as the DTU Team was eventually decided to

for the work in the present thesis will be mentioned beforehand. 
 
Description of the simulation software used in the thesis 
The simulation software used for the investigation of energy analysis and the indoor comfort of the various 
concepts, was IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE 4.8). It is a whole-year detailed and dynamic multi-zone 
simulation application, for study of thermal indoor climate as well as the energy consumption of the entire 
building. It is developed by the Swedish company EQUA and is commonly used in the northern European countries. 
Apart from its popularity in Denmark, IDA ICE was chosen, as it doesn't work as a black box, which means that 
every underlying equation can be browsed, and every variable can be logged. For a single zone, this means that 
temperatures, heat fluxes, CO2-levels, control signals and other variables can be inspected by the critical user. 
Most of its available features were used during the simulations. Natural and demand-controlled ventilation, 
heating/cooling systems, ventilated facades and windows, daylight calculations and more, were utilized to inspect 
different strategies to minimize energy consumption and achieve superior indoor comfort, according to the Danish 
standards.

Establishment of the architectural plan of the core house  
According to SDE19 regulations, rule No.6 (Project Extents) include requirements about the architectural plan and 
relevant information about the available construction area on site for each team. The “Measurable area” (internal 
floor heated area) must not exceed 70.0 m2 for one story house. Walls, columns, stairs shaft, and storage or 
technical element built from floor to ceiling, are excluded. Interior surfaces of walls define the building’s thermal 
envelope form the measurable area perimeter. All primary living areas shall be located within the measurable 
area. The total architectural footprint cannot exceed 150.0 m2 and includes the entire area within the defined 
building perimeter (including the house and the site components) as well as active systems simulating urban 
systems set outside of the Measurable Area [5].

1.2 
The building envelope of the core house  
After the floor plan was modified for IDA ICE, the design model was created according to the Danish building 
regulations and recommendations. The Building Regulations 2018 (BR18) are in effect in Denmark as of January 
1st, 2018 with a transitional period lasted until July 1st, 2018. [7] The energy requirements are largely the same as 
in BR15 [8]. The Building Regulations operate with six different categories of construction projects that differ in 
relation to the energy requirements [9]. The current project is placed in the first category that consists of “New 
buildings” so the relevant energy consumption requirements came for Chapter 11 (sections §250-266). 
 
In relation to new buildings, there are four primary levels of requirements that when combined, ensure that 
buildings have a low energy demand, are well-insulated and are problem-free when it comes to moisture. Neither 
of the four requirement levels can achieve this alone [9]. 
 
Requirements for New buildings (BR18): 
i. Energy performance framework (§ 259-266) 
ii. Requirements for airtightness (§ 263) 
iii. Requirements for design transmission loss (§ 264) 
iv. General minimum requirements for the building envelope (§ 257-258 + Annex 2, table 1) 
The relevant sections of BR18 were used to design the reference model of the core house. All later investigations 
with the implemented semi-outdoor space, will be conducted on this reference model. 
1.2.1 Core house constructions and thermal bridges 
The shell construction of the semi-outdoor space is the outer envelope, with aim to protect the core house from 
various weather conditions like snow, rain or wind. Even though the shell is exposed to the outdoor air 
temperature, its main purpose is not thermal insulation. Instead, the building envelope of the core house is the 
actual thermal envelope of the building, providing thermal insulation and the one that has to comply with the 
Danish regulations. [6]

should be insulated such that the heat losses through them do not exceed the values in Appendix 2, Table 1 in

1.2.2 Windows   
Daylight performance is also evaluated by the competition, under contest 8: Comfort conditions together with 
temperature, humidity, CO2, formaldehyde concentration and acoustic Performance. For the natural lighting level 
measurements, photometers will be located in the living room. Light intensity of the area will be measured 
according to the spectral levels defined by the organization. All available points are earned by keeping the Daylight 
Factor, above 4% during measurement periods (cloudy sky). Reduced points are earned linearly if the ratio is 
between 2,5% and 4%. The measurement point height is 0.9m and the minimum distance to a window is 2m. 
 
The daylight factor (DF) is defined as the ratio of the internal illuminance at a point in a building to the unshaded, 
external horizontal illuminance under a CIE overcast sky. For buildings in Denmark and an outside illuminance of 
14200 lux under an overcast sky, the corresponding daylight factor requirement becomes 300 Lux / 14200 Lux = 
2.1% minimum. Daylight factor considers building geometry, light reflected from external obstructions, surface 
properties (color, diffuseness, specularity, transmittance, reflectances) and light losses through glazings. Daylight 
factor calculations do not include any movable shading devices as they are not needed under the worst case 
overcast sky condition.  
 
The competition requirements are very high regarding natural daylight, and a daylight factor above 4% for a 
distance of minimum 2 m from the windows, can be considered as quite high value to achieve. During the previous 
DTU participation in SD14 in Versailles with the house EMBRACE (Figure 6, right), the performance on the 
subcontest for the daylight measurements, was relatively poor. The measured daylight factor ranged from 1.3% 
to 6.1% with an average of 2.7%. The DTU Team only received 2 points out of 15 for this sub-contest [11] 
 
To ensure that the daylight performance on the competition will be high enough this time, the initial design 
included two large windows (4 x 2.2m) placed in the living room facing east and west. However, the distance 
between them was more than 8m, which made it impossible to achieve DF higher than 3% in the middle of the

1.4 
Reference energy systems 
Heating and cooling units with PI temperature control 
 
The individual heating and cooling units in all rooms are ideal devices. They heat and cool the zone when no 
detailed information about an actual room unit is available, or this amount of detail is unmotivated. The two 
devices are separate and can act individually, meaning that heating and cooling can happen simultaneously if the 
system is not properly configured. They are not hydronic devices, have no given physical location on any room 
surface and they are not connected to the plant of the building. The default capacity of the ideal heater/cooler 
(calculated automatically), is large enough to safely be able to heat/cool the zone under all conditions [14].  
 
A Proportional-Integral controller (PI) is used to keep the room air (or operative) temperature at the heating 
setpoint. The PI controller uses the setpoint, given as an input by the user, and an integrator term that accounts 
for the “history” of the difference between measured value and set-point, leading to a more precise control.  
 
The Primary system (Plant) 
 
In the default configuration, the plant consists of two main components with their schedules and controls: 
• 
The chiller (1) and a schedule (2) for its operation  
• 
The boiler (3) and a schedule (6) for its operation  
The boiler also include a linear segment temperature controller (4) for hot water supply temperature and a 
schedule (5) for night setback operation (Figure 8). 
 
 
Figure 8. The Primary system (Plant), [from IDA ICE manual] [14] 
The boiler converts purchased energy, e.g. gas, electricity or district heat, to warm water with given temperature 
and pressure for circulation through water-based heat exchangers in the building. It also uses energy for 
production of domestic hot water and pumping. The chiller uses electrical power to produce chilled water at two 
different constant temperatures (but with the same pressure). One supplies the AHU while the other supplies the 
zones [14] 
 
The configuration of the systems, schedules and controls remained unaltered during all investigations, since the 
AHU and the ideal heaters/coolers were not connected with the Plant. Hydronic devices and distribution of 
domestic hot water which are also connected with the Plant, were not part of this thesis.

This chapter investigate HVAC strategies for the core house described in Part I. The core house is the core building 
of the LivingLab, designed for the solar decathlon competition SDE19. The semi-outdoor space and the 3rd room

The simulations were performed using the software IDA ICE 4.8 with the CFD extension beta 13. Any important 
parameters of the systems involved in the simulations, that affect or define the presented results, will be 
introduced beforehand. All building models consist of a central reference air handling unit (AHU) and a reference 
single primary system (Plant). Any change on these systems will be mentioned in detail. 
 
In this chapter the heating and cooling setpoints in all zones are 20°C and 25°C respectively. There is no natural 
ventilation, with the windows being always closed. All internal doors are always closed have a leak area of 0.01 
m2 for air exchange between zones.  
 
Information on parameters regarding indoor air quality (IAQ) in IDA ICE  
 
Humans produce carbon dioxide (CO2) proportional to their metabolic rate. In terms of quantity it is the most 
important human bioeffluent but at the low concentrations that typically occur indoors, CO2 is usually harmless 
and not perceived by humans. Still is a good indicator of the concentration of other human bioeffluents being that 
can be perceived as a nuisance. The ambient air contains some amount of CO2 and for the simulations it was at 
400 ppm. 
 
The operative temperature is the average of the air temperature and a radiation temperature at a certain point. 
The occupant’s position on the zone floor in IDA ICE is indicated by a chair icon which is the measuring point for 
operative temperature. All occupants were placed in the  middle of the room, at 0.6 m above the floor (sitting). 
When the Climate zone model fidelity in IDA ICE has been selected, operative temperature will depend on the 
position too., in six directions, are also available using Climate model. Since the climate model is limited for zones 
with rectangular shape, the position of the occupants will not be examined and directed operative temperatures 
will be not used. 
 
Air age is a measure of how long an average air molecule has spent in the building. If a zone is ventilated by outside 
air only and is in steady state, this number is 1/(air changes per hour). The age of air measure takes account also 
of air that has aged in neighboring zones. 
 
Balanced ventilation 
 
All solutions presented in this thesis have balanced ventilation system. Buildings should be airtight in order to 
minimize exfiltration or infiltration and avoid draughts. Exfiltration can cause high heat losses during heating 
season and increase the space cooling consumption during summer. Exfiltration can also cause other problems. 
During winter, the indoor air usually has a higher moisture content than the outside air. As the air leave the 
building’s construction as exfiltration, its temperature drops, and it condenses, leading to many kind of problems 
like mold growth, bended wooden floors, health problems, etc. Excess infiltration on the other hand could lead to 
cool draughts during winter that can cause thermal discomfort and could have considerable impact on the space 
heating consumption. Therefore, it was decided that all proposed strategies must demonstrate balanced 
ventilation.

According to BR18 section 443, in residential rooms as well as throughout the house as a whole, supply of outdoor

kitchen should be adjustable to minimum 20 l/s and in the bathroom adjustable to minimum 15 l/s. [7] 
 
The European standard DS/EN 15251:2007 was extensively used for the design of the ventilation system. This 
standard refers to indoor environmental input parameters for design and assessment of energy performance of 
buildings addressing indoor air quality, thermal environment, lighting and acoustics. [16] 
 
According to DS/EN 15251, Annex B.2 about recommended design ventilation rates in residential buildings, there 
are three main criteria: 
 
• 
Exhaust of pollutions in wet rooms to remove local pollutions (bathrooms, kitchen, toilets) 
• 
General ventilation of all rooms in the dwelling.  
• 
Fresh air criteria in the main rooms (bedrooms and living rooms) 
 
The supply air to wet rooms may be the transferred air from the main rooms. Requiring general ventilation in all 
rooms is also allowing some transfer from main rooms to wet rooms via corridors. Requiring fresh air in the main 
rooms add emphasize on the minimum supply air per bedroom and living room. This addition allows for a better 
indoor air quality (IAQ). These three criteria are important to take into consideration when designing ventilation 
strategies in a dwelling [16].

Ventilation strategies for the core house 
As mentioned earlier, the BR18 regulations state that in residential rooms as well as throughout the house as a

system should provide supply of air into to residential rooms and exhaust in bathrooms, toilet kitchen and scullery. 
 
The chosen ventilation control in IDA ICE was the “VAV schedule”. This type of ventilation is controlled by 
schedules that are used to define how air flows vary with time. Each room has its own daily schedule which is 
divided into two states (ON/OFF) whether there are occupants in the room or not. In other words, there is either 
airflow if occupants are present, or no airflow if the occupants are not in the room. The individual schedules will 
be explained during each ventilation strategy investigation. 
 
“Significant energy savings can be achieved by operating heating, ventilation and air conditioning controllers using 
a feedback from sensor-based occupancy detection methods” according to (Theis et al. 2017) [18]. Since the 
occupant behavior is not unpredicted here, the occupancy detection method can be assumed as ideal.  
 
The 24h day was divided into two parts: 
• 
Nighttime 23:00 – 06:00 (all 4 occupants are in bedrooms sleeping)  
• 
Daytime    06:00 – 23:00 (all 4 occupants are awake, not in bedrooms).  
Working hours during weekdays will not be included yet. The house is assumed to be occupied during these hours. 
 
There will be two strategies investigated here: 
1. The first complies with the regulation of BR18 regarding supply of air in bedrooms, where the main criteria 
is the supply of fresh air to improve the indoor air quality. The air is then transferred to the adjacent rooms 
through door leaks and will be evacuated from the wet rooms (kitchen and bathroom).  
2. The second strategy involve an alternative approach where the air is extracted from the bedrooms and wet 
rooms to directly remove pollutants from the residence. The fresh air is provided from the other main rooms.

Another approach could have the polluted air extracted from the rooms where pollution is produced, without

2.4 
The Air Handling Unit for the core house 
 
For new houses with tightly constructed envelopes, balanced ventilation with passive heat recovery can be 
appropriate in some climates [12]. With heat recovery, filtered outdoor air and exhaust stale air from the house, 
pass through a heat exchanger at nearly equal air flows, where heat (and sometimes moisture) is transferred 
between the airstreams. This usually reduces the energy required later to condition the air in the house by 60 to 
80% [21]. According to BR18 section § 432, ventilation systems with injection and evacuation must be installed 
with a heat recovery solution with minimum efficiency is required at 80%. At this point it was decided to follow 
the Building class 2020 which requires heat recovery and a dry temperature efficiency of minimum 85 per cent 
(BR18, § 483). 
 
Regardless of which strategy is chosen in terms of supply and exhaust of air in the rooms, the air delivered, is 
expected to have a temperature that will not create discomfort to the occupants. With heat recovery the air 
suppled in the house is previously conditioned and the supply air temperature less likely to cause local discomfort. 
However, in cold climates when the ambient temperature is very low, frosting issues on the heat exchanger can 
still occur, limiting its heat recovery potential [22]. This normally leads to supply of relatively low air temperatures 
(compared with the indoor air temperatures), increasing the space heating consumption and the chance for local 
discomfort. 
 
To investigate the heat recovery performance and different strategies regarding the control of supply air 
temperature to the core house, a custom air handling unit, was designed. The custom AHU provided two different 
approaches on how the temperature of the supplied air can automatically be controlled: 
a) Constant supply air temperature control  
This method provides a predefined fixed setpoint of the air temperature for the heat exchanger 
b) Return air temperature control 
This method monitors the temperature of the extract air from the house, and depending on the strategy 
used, it will calculate the setpoint of the air temperature for the heat exchanger

The air-to-air heat exchanger (1) is defined by an effectiveness (%) and a maximum capacity parameter for the

outgoing air (to outdoors) may not fall below 1°C, to protect from frost. The heat-exchanger will utilize the return 
air stream both to heat and to cool the supply air. However, the cases where there is potential for cold recovery 
in Denmark’s climate are limited [22].

2.4.1 Constant supply air temperature control method and its limitations. (CSAT) 
This method of supply air temperature control attempts to adjust the supply air temperature according to the 
setpoint on the top left box in the AHU schematic (5), which in this case is a constant value of 20°C. In IDA ICE, the 
supply air temperature in the air handling unit, is measured in the supply fan after the heat from motor and drive 
losses being deposited into the air. The return air is measured in the heat exchanger inlet. 
 
A particular week in March (12-18/3/18) was chosen for this investigation for multiple reasons. Despite the 
subzero ambient temperatures during this week the weather conditions fluctuate from clear to overcast sky, 
affecting the indoor temperatures.  
 
The Figure 21 (left graph) shows the actual supply air temperature entering the house (orange line), the return air 
temperature leaving the house (red line), and the outside air temperature (blue line). The right graph display the 
direct solar radiation during these days. 
 
 
Figure 21. Air temperatures measured in the AHU in °C (left), Direct solar radiation in W/m2 (right) 
During Thursday and Friday, in the middle of the week, there is no significant solar radiation, thus no solar heat 
gains (Figure 21). For these days, in the house the average air temperature is around 20°C. When this air is 
extracted from the house, it enters the heat exchanger to exchange heat with the incoming air stream from 
outdoors (Figure 22). The ambient air temperature on the intake of the heat exchanger is around 0C and the 
temperature difference between the two air streams (supply and return) is around 20°C. Since the heat exchanger 
has an efficiency of 85%, the supply air temperature is finally around 17°C.  
 
However, the constant temperature setpoint in the AHU (requested temperature) is 20°C, which means that the 
desired temperature cannot be fulfilled. As long as there is no extra heat coming from solar radiation, the heating 
==PAGE:33==
32 | P a g e  
 
units will provide the remaining heat and then try to maintain the temperature in the house at 20°C (heating 
setpoints in the zones). 
 
The limitations of the constant supply air temperature method 
 
For the first 3 days of the week (Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday), the solar radiation is high and the solar heat gains 
increased. As a result, the temperature inside the house increases up to 25°C during daytime. To avoid 
overheating, the cooling units try to maintain the cooling setpoint at 25°C. Consequently, the return air 
temperature in the AHU is around 25°C too. Since the heat recovery potential is more than enough, the requested 
supply air temperature in the AHU (20°C) is easily achieved during sun hours, (straight horizontal parts of the 
orange line). 
 
During these hours, the supply air temperature (entering the house) is 
at the requested 20°C while return air temperature (leaving the house) 
is around 25°C. No additional heating energy is used from the local 
heating units during these hours. However, because the temperatures in 
the house are around the cooling setpoint of 25°C, cooling energy is used 
to avoid overheating. The requested supply air temperature at 20°C is 
inconveniently high during these hours and lower supply air 
temperatures could be more beneficial especially with such low outdoor 
temperatures. It is apparent at this point that this method is not 
efficient. 
This situation could be avoided if there was a temperature sensor at the 
exhaust air intake from the house, monitoring the temperature. The sensor could send a signal to the heat 
exchanger, to bypass some amount of the incoming air from outside, directly to the house (or some amount of 
exhaust air directly outdoors). This strategy could decrease the supply air temperatures, providing natural cooling. 
 
In general, with the constant supply air temperature control, heating through the AHU is provided even when it is 
not needed. This method doesn’t monitor what is the air temperature inside the house. The rooms where there 
is supply of air, can possibly receive air which is hotter than needed, contributing more to overheating. Controlling 
the supply air temperature with a “live” temperature feedback from the house, is needed.

2.4.4 How heat recovery ventilation is limited by frost protection in IDA ICE 
As mentioned before, the potential for heat recovery is very much dependent on the outdoor and indoor 
temperatures. Days with very low or high outside temperature, can reduce the potential of heat or cold recovery 
respectively. The heat exchanger is not only limited by the temperature difference between outside air and indoor 
air. It has also a maximum efficiency of 85% with a frost protection that will not allow the temperature on the 
outgoing air (toward outdoors) to drop below 1°C. That means the desired supply air temperature will not always 
be achieved during very cold or warm days.  
 
The left graph in Figure 28 refers to the heating season and shows the air temperatures in the AHU with the CSAT 
method described earlier. The temperature drops (vertical downward spikes) presented on the supply side 
(orange line), can be translated as hours with potential discomfort to the occupants, due to cool draughts. They 
are correlated with very low outside temperatures recorded mostly on December and March.

On the right graph, one particular day was chosen to display how the supply air temperature levels are halted by 
the frost protection in the heat exchanger. The focus should be on the exhaust air temperature, leaving the heat 
exchanger (brown dashed line). As mentioned before, the heat exchanger used for the simulations has unlimited 
airflow capacity at efficiency 85%.  
 
However, if the heat exchanger uses the entire airflow capacity at sub-zero ambient temperatures, it could 
possibly warm the supply air to comfortable levels but at the same time would also cool the exhaust air below 
0°C. This will lead to the appearance of condensation in the exhaust as it cools below the dew point temperature. 
A bypass damper, routes some of the supply air around the heat exchanger instead of through it, to decrease the 
amount of heat recovery. This will avoid frost formation that would damage the heat exchanger, but the result 
can be uncomfortably cold supply air temperatures. Acceptable supply air temperatures are usually higher than 
16°C and lower air temperatures could cause local discomfort to the occupants [12]. However, the heat exchanger 
can often supply air with considerably lower temperatures as seen in Figure 28.  
 
Further increase of the supply air temperature using heating coils 
One common strategy to increase the supply air temperature after the heat exchanger, in order to avoid cool 
draughts, is using a heating coil. The heating coil should be used, when the temperature of the supply air after 
the heat exchanger (towards the house) is low. The heating coil should be used only during these times to avoid 
cool draughts. Then, the individual room heating units will further increase the temperature to the desired levels 
according to the zone heating setpoints. Additional pre-heating of the air with the heating coil should not be 
used by setting a high supply air temperature in the AHU. Using the heating coil excessively can have multiple 
negative impacts 
Exergy perspective: The hot water through the heating coil needs high flow rate to deliver heating to the supply 
air stream. From an exergy perspective it can be considered wasteful to use a high temperature source for low 
temperature heating. On the other hand, water-based systems like floor heating, use a much larger heating 
surface allowing to use considerably lower supply water temperatures. [23]. 
 
Zone control: Suppling all zones (the ones where there is injection of air) simultaneously, with high air 
temperature, removes control over individual rooms, offered by thermostats and dedicated heating units. 
Occupants are more likely to be satisfied with the thermal conditions in living rooms and bathrooms, for increased 
air temperatures. On the other hand, a high degree of satisfaction in bedrooms is linked with lower air 
temperatures. As a result, oversupply of heat to bedrooms during winter, leads to extensive opening of the 
window for natural ventilation, which leads to an increased space heating demand [24]

2.5 
Heat recovery ventilation with closed-loop ground heat exchange 
A better alternative to the use of heating coils, to further increase the supply air temperature from the AHU, is 
the use of the ground as heat source. The ground is warmer than the sub-zero outdoor air and it could be more 
efficient to use it as a preheater (than heating coils), as long as installation cost is not high, and it doesn’t cost too 
much in pumping power. 
 
However, the ground temperature would never be high enough to be able to preheat the air after the heat 
exchanger. It would be possible though to preheat the outside air, before it enters the heat exchanger. The ground 
temperature reacts slower than the ambient air temperature and as a result, is higher than the outdoors during 
winter and lower during summer. Thus, the ground can be used to pre-heat the incoming air to the AHU during 
winter and pre-cool the air during summer. The ground act as a heat source during winter and heat sink during 
summer. Figure 29, shows ground temperatures in relation with outdoor air temperatures in Denmark. 
 
 
Figure 29 
There are two types of ground heat exchange systems. The first is an open system where the air, is passing through 
a pipe in the ground before it enters the building and supply the AHU (Figure 30, left sketch). The ground will 
transfer some of its heat to the air that travels towards the AHU. By the time it will reach the heat exchanger, the 
air temperature will be higher than it was outside. [25] 
 
The second type is a closed system where the outdoor air will not pass through the ground. Instead it is led through 
an air-liquid heat exchanger, before it enters the AHU. The liquid is a glycol-water mixture that is flowing through 
a tube. The tube can be several meters long (50-100m) and it is horizontally installed in the ground where the 
liquid exchanges heat with the ground. The tube can be installed underneath the house before the construction, 
or around it if the house is already in place or in case of renovations (Figure 30, right sketch). 
 
==PAGE:40==
39 | P a g e  
 
 
Figure 30. Ground loop with Open system (left), closed system (right) 
The closed-loop ground heat exchange is preferred, because it is easier to install, and less prone to damage 
because of natural settling of the ground or digging into the ground after installation. The closed-loop system also 
avoids potential microbial growth problems than can occur with the open system. Contrarily to the air pipes in 
the open system, the tube is having a small diameter (around 20mm) and needs less area as it can be installed in 
a meandering pattern. [26]  
 
The custom AHU in IDA ICE, was modified further to 
simulate a ground loop, with the above behavior. A 
heating coil, a cooling coil, a boiler and a chiller were 
used to represent the ground-sourced liquid-air heat 
exchanger, instead of an actual ground-loop model.  
 
The coils are connected with the outdoor supply air 
stream, before it reaches the heat exchanger. When the 
supply air from outside, passes through them a heat 
transfer occurs between the air and the liquid in the 
coils, similar to a liquid-air heat exchanger.  
 
A boiler and a chiller (Figure 31), provide the heating 
and cooling coils, with hot or cold water respectively.  
 
The boiler can only replace the ground as an energy source and the chiller can only replace the ground as an 
energy sink, which is why both of them are needed. 
 
The average ground temperature for each month was provided as input to the coils to control the temperature of 
the air. In both coils, capacity control is achieved by adapting the air side temperature effectiveness up to the 
given maximum level. The necessary water flow is calculated, and the water temperature is reduced. There is no 
bypass on the liquid side; control is achieved by restricting the water flow.

 Part III (Validation of models with semi-outdoor space in IDA ICE) 
 
In order to verify the validity of the models covered with a semi-outdoor space, different parameters were 
investigated. The zone model fidelity, geometries of the zones, heat transfer between zones, solar radiation 
through two transparent layers, natural ventilation of the semi outdoor space, airflows between zones, infiltration 
and thermal bridges, were examined thoroughly. Each parameter was checked individually using clone models 
with and without the semi outdoor space.  
 
3.1 
Zone model fidelity 
There are two different “zone fidelity models” that can be used in IDA ICE. The energy model and the climate 
model. That means the degree of accuracy for the mathematical models of the zones can be chosen as needed. If 
the Climate model is selected, a very detailed physical model of the building and its components will be simulated. 
For example, the possibility for a vertical temperature gradient. If instead, the Energy model is selected, a simpler 
physical model is used. It has a more conventional degree of accuracy based on a mean radiant temperature. 
Consequently, the calculation time will be somewhat shorter, all else being equal. 
 
There are certain limitations for both models. The energy model does not calculate directed operative 
temperature. On the other hand, the climate model is currently restricted to zones with rectangular geometry.  
 
Both zone models use the same description of the building. All the component models in and around the zone 
such as windows, radiators, controllers, leaks, terminals, etc. are common for the simplified and detailed zone 
models. Since the semi-outdoor space and the living room have complex geometry, the use of the Energy model 
was the only option. 
 
3.2 
Design process of an outdoor space in IDA ICE 
In IDA ICE it is possible to import geometries from other software like SketchUp or Revit. However, the complicated 
geometry of the semi-outdoor space can create several problems during imports and the simulations were not 
always successful. For this reason, the models were created directly in IDA ICE and not imported from other 
software, to avoid any errors during import.  
 
The building’s boundary layer with the environment, is characterized 
as “building body” in IDA ICE. Every construction or object in contact 
with the building body, is considered as external and in direct 
interaction with the environment. The external constructions appear 
in a dark olive color while internal constructions in white.  
 
The building body defines the inner floor heated area and the 
thickness of walls roof floor windows and doors, expand outside of

3.3 
Thermal bridges configuration when implementing a semi outdoor zone in IDA ICE 
In IDA ICE, in order to include heat losses from the thermal bridges of the core house in the simulations, a 
dedicated window is provided in the “Building level”, that includes all coefficients (psi-values) needed for the 
calculations. This will assign all the values provided by the user, to the corresponding connections in contact with 
the building body. As mentioned before the building body can be considered as a boundary layer separating the 
building with the ambient environment. Normally this would be sufficient for most building designs in IDA ICE.  
 
However, in this case where there is one additional outdoor zone around the core house, the building body must 
be transferred to this zone, (as this is the zone now in contact with the ambient). This will automatically unassign 
all thermal bridges from the core house and transfer these values to the shell of the semi-outdoor zone. 
 
 
Figure 37. Building body (top view) 
In order to re-assign heat losses from the thermal bridges on the core house, each zone must be configured 
individually in the “Zone level”, under the description “Loss factor for thermal bridges”.  
 
It is rather important to include this step and properly configure the thermal bridges to the core house in the case 
of 3rd room, in IDA ICE. The reason is that thermal bridges are important part of the heat losses of the core house. 
The higher the temperature difference between the inside of the house and outside air, the higher the heat losses 
from the building envelope, including the thermal bridges.  
 
At this point, a key limitation regarding thermal bridges in IDA ICE was established.  
 
In IDA ICE, heat losses from thermal bridges include direct heat transfer between room air and ambient air. That 
means that the heat losses through thermal bridges are not calculated depending on the temperature difference 
between the two sides of each connection. Instead they are directly linked to the ambient, disregarding any 
intermittent zones. Even though heat loses from thermal bridges are calculated, any temperature change in the 
outside zone will not further affect them.

On the contrary, the effect of heat losses from thermal bridges on the shell’s construction simulated as intended. 
Still, they have negligible impact on the overall performance of the 3rd room concept with usually less than 1% 
increase in the total space heating consumption of the core house.

incoming solar radiation affects room conditions in two ways:

radiation. In other words: Heat from window surfaces, including conducted heat, as well as absorbed and then

“First, it is calculated how much of the solar radiation that hits the different surfaces in the room, including the

backward ray tracing).”

3.6 
Impact of wind driven infiltration on the semi-outdoor space in IDA ICE 
Data in the infiltration form is used to specify unintentional air flows over the building envelope without having 
to visit each zone. The Leak area and Given additional in/exfiltration parameters of each zone are by default linked 
with the global Infiltration data. Two basic methods of infiltration modeling are supported by IDA ICE. Fixed and 
equal in/exfiltration flows, or wind pressure and thermal buoyancy effects. 
 
The wind driven flow (the latter) has been selected for use with the semi-outdoor space. Leaks are automatically 
introduced in each external wall of each zone (not in floors or roofs). The combined sizes of all these leaks is given 
here as a single flow/pressure point. This data is then distributed to each zone according the total wetted external 
area.  
 
Wind directions specified by the weather file must be configured here. These angles are relative to face azimuths, 
with zero for wind directed toward the face and raises clockwise for other wind directions. Pressure coefficients 
are also used here to calculate wind pressure on the different external surfaces of a building in relation to the 
speed of wind, at roof height. Pressure coefficients are dependent on the building’s shape as well as on 
surrounding aerodynamic conditions. For the simulations the whole construction was considered as “exposed” to 
the environment with no surrounding obstructions. 
 
A high infiltration rate at 1 L/s/m2 of floor area at a pressure differential of 50 Pa was simulated for the core 
house, according to BR18 section 263. One additional lower infiltration rate was used here for the investigation 
of the semi-outdoor space. Wind driven infiltration of 0.45 ACH at a pressure differential of 50 Pa on the shell 
surface of the semi-outdoor space [27].

As mentioned earlier IDA ICE has an option to set up fixed values for infiltration. However, setting up some fixed 
values for the infiltration is not an option here for two reasons.  
 
When fixed flows are specified in IDA ICE, that means constant envelope air flows to and from each zone are 
introduced. However, small envelope "natural ventilation" leaks are also introduced to absorb pressure 
differences because of for example imbalances in mechanical supply and return flows. A common problem when 
fixed flows are used in combination with unbalanced mechanical ventilation is that pressure difference over the 
envelope becomes unrealistically large. A warning for this is then issued during the simulation and the remedy is 
to specify larger leak areas in zones with unbalanced mechanical ventilation. Since the main AHU of the core house 
will be later designed to extract air from the semi-outdoor space, and exhaust it outdoors, it was decided that 
fixed infiltration was not an option. 
 
Moreover, if estimated properly, wind driven infiltration can have a considerable impact on the air exchange in 
the semi-outdoor space, so a separate way to simulate additional airflows without affecting the wind driven 
infiltrations was needed.  
 
To simulate additional airflows, controlled by the user, another custom AHU was designed for this purpose. This 
virtual AHU (Figure 48) is connected only to the semi outdoor zone. It has no fans or other mechanical parts and 
can be considered as a simple way to model additional airflows that are desired in a zone for any purpose. The 
absence of fans means there is no energy consumed for these air flows. The input of the desired flow rate can be 
found in the zone or building level as usual. A fixed (base) flow rate at 51 L/s was chosen in addition to the variable 
wind driven infiltration at 1L/s/m2, penetrating the shell (Figure 49). Several values were investigated, but 51 L/s 
was chosen to be displayed here as this is the same  value with the ventilation flow rate the core house, and It will 
be convenient for future comparisons and reference.

 Part IV - Implementation of the 3rd room and optimization strategies 
 
This chapter investigates the interactions between the ambient environment and the indoor climate conditions in 
the semi-outdoor space. The aim is to demonstrate how the 3rd room concept can affect the core building 
regarding its energy consumption for space heating and cooling. 
 
The energy performance of the core house, regarding space heating/cooling consumption is mainly affected by 
its heat transactions with the surrounding environment. There are six main areas where heat transfer takes place: 
 
a) Walls, roof, floor 
b) Windows and doors (with their frames) 
c) Solar heat gains 
d) Thermal bridges 
e) Infiltration/exfiltration 
f) Ventilation air flows 
 
The walls, roof, floor, doors and windows with their frames (a, b), characterize the building envelope of the core 
house. The heat transfer is direct and depended by the U-value of these constructions, (W/m2K). The glazings of 
the windows allow for additional heat transfer through solar radiation (c) in (W). The connections of these 
constructions are characterized by their thermal bridges, and air tightness (d, e). The thermal bridges directly 
affect the heat transfer between the two sides of the connection depended by its U-value in (W/mK). The 
infiltration and exfiltration are responsible for the transfer of air, in and out of the house, which indirectly affects 
the heat transfer in (W). For a building with balanced ventilation, the main driven force of infiltration/exfiltration, 
is the wind pressure around the house. The ventilation system is responsible to deliver fresh outdoor air in the 
house and remove the indoor polluted air outdoors. The air volume leaving the house contains some amount of 
heat and its being replaced by incoming air volume which temperature is lower or higher than the indoors (f). 
 
The 3rd room concept suggests that a shell, creating a semi-outdoor space that surrounds the core building, could 
have a positive impact on all these six areas of heat transfer, leading to lower space heating consumption. Indeed, 
the shell of the semi-outdoor space, affects the core building in several different ways: 
 
• 
It protects the core house from wind driven pressure around its construction, minimizing heat losses 
through infiltration and reducing the convection part of heat transfer from the building envelope.  
• 
Due to the expected greenhouse effect, temperature is increased in the semi-outdoor space, thus 
reducing the temperature difference (ΔΤ) between the semi-outdoor space and the indoor temperature. 
This directly affects the heat losses from walls, roof, doors, windows, frames, and their thermal bridges. 
The heat losses from the floor are not affected by the 3rd room concept.  
• 
The glazings of the shell will reduce part of the incoming solar heat gains. Additionally, the shell’s body 
structure as well as PV panels and solar collectors, create some partial shadows on the core house, also 
contributing to this effect. The decreased solar heat gains are expected to increase the total space heating 
consumption during heating season but also decrease the energy for cooling during summer.  
• 
The heat transfer trough the ventilation airflows could be affected by the 3rd room in the way that supply 
of air can be provided from the semi-outdoor space, instead of the outdoor environment.  
 
The following simulations include the main core house as described in chapter 1. The AHU monitors the return 
temperature and controls the heat exchanger with a linear segment as described in chapter 3.2.3. No ground loop 
is installed. The simulation period is for one year.

separate custom control for the opening of the window was critical for multiple reasons: 
 
•

is lower than indoors. Only then, the natural ventilation can provide cooling to the core house. 
 
• 
It is also critical to separate the cooling setpoints for natural ventilation and mechanical cooling. In IDA

the room cooling units (mechanical cooling). The opening of the windows should be the first priority starting 
earlier, to allow for natural ventilation to take place. Mechanical cooling should follow later, if the air temperature 
in the room continues to increase. In that case the natural ventilation is not enough to cool down the room. Either 
the outdoor temperature is higher than indoors or the air exchange from the opened windows is not enough to 
remove the excess heat from the room.

4.4 
Air supply to the AHU from the semi-outdoor space 
 
The main advantage of the 3rd room concept is the mild greenhouse effect that can be observed during sun hours. 
As a result of this effect, the air temperature increases inside the semi-outdoor space during these hours. The 3rd 
room can be considered as a thermal buffer zone. Utilizing this stored heat as much as possible, could increase 
the benefits and overall performance of the 3rd room concept. 
 
One of the simplest and cost-free ways, is to supply the AHU with air from inside the semi-outdoor space.  
Normally the AHU is supplied with air from the ambient environment. Since the air temperature in the semi-
outdoor space is generally higher than outdoors it could have a positive impact on the heat recovery of the 
ventilation system. It must be noted here that the indoor climate in the semi-outdoor space regarding moisture 
content, CO2 concentration, etc., could be different than the ambient conditions, which will lead to supply of air 
in the house, with somewhat different composition. 
 
To investigate the impact of suppling air to the core house from the semi-outdoor zone, the main AHU for the 
core house and the virtual AHU for the semi-outdoor zone, were modified to connect with each other (Figure 66).  
 
With the new custom design, the main AHU was supplied with air from the semi-outdoor space and then it 
exhausts the air coming from the house, to the ambient environment. The volume of air extracted from the semi-
outdoor space, is controlled by the total volume flow rate requested in the main AHU. Then, to maintain the mass 
balance inside the semi-outdoor space, the zone’s dedicated virtual AHU was designed to be supplied with the 
same volume of air from outdoors. In other words, the volume of air leaving the semi-outdoor space to enter the 
core house, is replaced with fresh air from outdoors, to maintain the volume of air in the zone, constant.

Conclusion  
The total space heating consumption of the core house was calculated at 1869 kWh/year. The 3rd room concept 
was estimated to decrease the consumption by 432 kwh/year to a total of 1437 kWh/year (about 28%). The 
implementation of a ground loop technology as described in this thesis, would further decrease the consumption 
by around 208 kWh/year (-14%) to a final 1264 kWh/year. Glazings with better performance on the shell could 
decrease it further by around 6%. 
 
The total space cooling consumption of the core house was calculated at 427 kWh/year. The 3rd room concept 
was estimated to increase the consumption by 562 kWh/year (57%) to a total of 989 kWh/year. The 
implementation of the ground loop technology would decrease it by 191 kWh/year (-19%) at a total of 796 
kWh/per year. Still the cooling consumption is higher than the standalone house. Facades that are completely 
open at all times are estimated to decrease the cooling consumption to the same levels as the standalone house.  
Glazings with better performance on the shell could decrease the space cooling consumption by up to 30%. 
Further research on shading strategies is essential to uncover cooling possibilities of 3rd room concept. 
 
The 3rd room concept have a positive impact on the space heating consumption during winter (up to 20-30%) and 
can potentially reduce the cooling consumption of a building during summer, if proper shading techniques are 
used together with open facades for natural ventilation. Increased temperatures can be observed mostly during 
sun hours and especially for low ambient temperatures with high direct solar radiation. Climates that fit to the 
above description are most likely to benefit from the 3rd room concept.

 Discussion and further research 
 
This section summarize some of the most important assumptions and limitations encountered during the present 
thesis.  
 
Floor plan: The original floor plan from the architectural team was slightly modified, in terms of geometry around 
corners, to improve the compatibly with the energy simulation software IDA ICE. This action also simplified the 
geometry of the semi-outdoor zone, when it was implemented later, avoiding simulation errors.  
 
Constructions: The target U-value for the core house constructions (wails, roof, floor) was calculated at 0.1156 
W/(m2K), for a design outdoor temperature at -12°C for Denmark. The same amount of insulation performing in 
Hungary’s climate would not be optimal as the energy demand for space heating is lower. During summer, cooling 
consumption in Hungary is higher further affected by the increased insulation thickness. When the semi-outdoor 
space is implemented, the space heating consumption is decreased. This should not be treated as opportunity to 
decrease the insulation thickness of the core house. The benefits are not steady during heating season for any 24-
hour period. Very low air temperatures during night in the semi-outdoor space are still a concern. 
 
Thermal bridges: The heat losses from thermal bridges used, are only estimations derived from standards and IDA 
ICE recommendations. More detailed calculations for the heat losses from the connections on the core house is 
needed as they seem to have considerable impact on the space heating consumption. On the other hand, heat 
transfer through the thermal bridges on the shell was relatively small compared with the heat loss from the shell’s 
glazings. They are the dominant source of heat loss from the semi-outdoor space occupying most of the external 
area in contact with the ambient environment. When the thermal bridges are configured in the general tab in IDA 
ICE, these values will apply to any construction connected with the ambient (Building Body boundary). This works 
as intended for normal buildings and all thermal bridges are assigned properly. However, in the case where the 
3rd room is implemented, the values of thermal bridges in the general tab, are not assigned to the core house 
anymore, but they are “transferred” to the shell, (where the Building Body boundary is transferred). As a result, 
the core house has then zero heat losses from the thermal bridges and they must be re assigned manually in all 
zones one by one. Implementing the semi-outdoor space around the core house, without the additional 
configuration of all thermal bridges in all zones one by one, will result to unreliable simulations. However, the 
heat losses from thermal bridges do not benefit further from the 3rd room concept in IDA ICE. When the semi-
outdoor space is implemented, heat losses from thermal bridges are miscalculated. The software links the thermal 
bridges with the ambient air temperature instead of the air temperature on the semi-outdoor zone. As a result, 
calculated heat losses are higher with no additional benefits from the 3rd room. An estimation of how much higher 
they are, could not be safely established at this point and future research should be conducted. 
 
Windows for the core house: The core must demonstrate very high performance in natural light for the SDE19. To 
earn full points, the  competition requires DF >4% in the living room, 2m from the windows. Large window areas 
and a skylight were initially used to achieve DF >4 in the middle of the room. However, the window areas were 
not optimized in terms of energy consumption. The optimization of the windows and glazed doors was conducted 
later in order to minimize the overall glazing area without compromising the daylight factor performance. Frame 
dimensions was assumed as 5% of the total window area with U-values for the frames at 2W/m2K. The frames 
could have a considerable impact on the space heating consumption of a building and proper values should be 
calculated in the future. Moreover, the impact of the semi-outdoor space on the heat transfer from frames was 
not examined in the thesis. Further investigation should be conducted regarding this topic. 
 
==PAGE:77==
76 | P a g e  
 
Occupants and schedules: A number of 4 adult occupants was chosen for the core house to evaluate the 
performance of the individual ventilation strategies. During the competition, up to 6 people can be present in the 
house. The capability of the strategies to maintain the CO2 concentrations below 750 ppm at all times was a 
challenge. The floor area of the house was relatively small compared to the number of occupants (about 16.9 
m2/person for floor area for 67.5 m2, compared to more typical values around 40 m2/ person for 160 m2 floor 
area). During the competition, CO2 measurement stop during public visits, Further investigations should include 
calculations on the decay of CO2 after public hours when the measurements start again. 
 
The plant: For this thesis the Plant was used with no changes in the configuration of the systems, schedules and 
controls. since the AHU and the ideal heaters/coolers were not connected with the Plant. Hydronic devices for 
space heating/cooling and distribution of domestic hot water, were not examined for this thesis. 
 
Thermal comfort: The “Energy model” was chosen for the simulations in this thesis since the “Climate model” is 
limited for zones with rectangular geometry. When the Climate zone model fidelity in IDA ICE has been selected, 
operative temperature will depend on the position of the occupant in the room. including vertical temperature 
gradient. Since the climate model has impact on the occupants, among others, the following parameters related 
to thermal comfort were not investigated: position of the occupants, directed operative temperatures, thermal 
discomfort due to radiant temperature asymmetry. Clothing, PMV and PPD parameters were also not examined. 
 
Fan power and efficiency: The specific fan power was chosen at 400 J/m³ per fan while the efficiency was at 60%. 
Both these fan parameters should be further investigated for the current ventilation system and optimized for 
decreased energy consumption. 
 
Heat exchanger and humidity control: Another heat recovery option, common in warmer climates with more 
demand in cooling energy, is an Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV) which is similar to a heat recovery ventilator 
but also allows for the recovery of moisture from the extracted air. This option could be more relevant for 
Hungary’s climate which is considered more cooling demanding, especially since the competition requirement for 
humidity are very strict (40-55% for full points).  
It is usually a common strategy, that when a house is considered unoccupied, the ventilation airflows decrease to 
save energy. The current ventilation configuration has no humidity control. A more advanced AHU would include 
also a humidity sensor in the exhaust stream from the house. A control system then, would check the outdoor 
and indoor absolute humidity deciding whether there is enough drying capacity and control the air accordingly 
 
Ground loop modeling: The energy savings from the ground loop proved quite steady under different conditions 
proving that it is a robust solution. Different efficiencies for the system were examined and the performance was 
promising even for lower values. Benefits include also frost protection for the heat exchanger and constantly high 
supply air temperatures during winter avoiding cool draught in the house. Opposed to heating coils the energy is 
free from the ground and the air temperature can never be higher than the indoor temperature, avoiding 
accidental oversupply of heat to rooms. On the contrary, the ground loop still requires pump power and it’s even 
possible to extract too much heat which would lower the ground temperature locally. All in all, the ground loop 
adds an additional investment cost for the owner, which in this case is the DTU Team. A detailed feasibility analysis 
should be conducted here.  
 
Daylight and solar heat gains: In IDA ICE, daylight measurements and solar heat gains from direct and diffuse 
radiation were found unrealistically high. The daylight measurements in the core house, found up to 30% higher 
than expected (when an open outdoor space [no windows] was implemented) when they should be the same. 
Moreover, in the case where the closed semi-outdoor space (with windows) was implemented, there was no 
decrease in the amount of daylight measured in the cores house; (it was expected lower due to the additional 
==PAGE:78==
77 | P a g e  
 
glazing on the shell). The heat from solar (direct and diffuse radiations) measurements, show the same behavior.  
However, the difference is up to 10% during summer and only marginal during heating season. For space heating 
energy calculations results can be considered as reliable while for cooling calculations the result should considered 
a bit higher (a percentage increase could not be estimated here with confidence) 
 
Shading techniques 
It is estimated that sliding windows on the shell, will reduce the heat gains when opened (due to double glazing 
effect) providing also natural ventilation. These two approaches combined, could increase the overall cooling 
performance during summer without compromising the solar heat gains and heating consumption during winter. 
This kind of investigation needs specialized software like DIVA and Rhino in order to support this argument. 
Optimizing the control of the openings in strategically placed spots around the shell, to follow the san path, could 
increase the performance of this strategy. 
 
Infiltration: Flow rates through the shell, due to infiltration, seem rather small compared to the total volume of 
the semi-outdoor space. Even for high infiltration rates, the actual flow rates are not enough to ensure a realistic 
air change with the environment. The moisture content was unrealistically steady with many hours of 
condensation. A minimum fixed flow rate must be set up by the user, to ensure adequate recirculation of air 
through the shell, for realistic moisture levels in the semi outdoor space. In general, the infiltration airflows on 
the shell seem unrealistically small, considered that the 3rd room concept is not intended as a completely air-tight 
construction. 
 
CFD simulations in IDA ICE 
The IDA ICE extension tool for CFD calculations (beta 11 & 13), was used to investigate various topics relevant for 
the thesis. Investigated topics were: natural ventilation between the semi-outdoor space and the core house; air 
flows between the kitchen and living room; airflows through closed and open (internal) doors; all shown 
reasonable results. Airflows around the core house in the semi-outdoor space were also examined with closed 
shell as well as  with open facades for natural ventilation. One of the most relevant results for the semi-outdoor 
space was that the vertical air temperature gradient showed a temperature difference of 2°C between ground 
level and roof (at 6.1m high). The results and conclusions were not presented in this these as the subject is rather 
complicated and needs specific skills and previous experience. Moreover, the CFD tool was in beta versions with 
considerable “runtime stop error” messages. 
 
Shape of the semi-outdoor space 
The shape and volume of the semi-outdoor space, as well as the shape and slope of the roof where not 
examined in this thesis, as these parameters can be found in the literature. [27] [28] [29] [30] 
Ventilation airflows and  
The sharing ventilation proportions among wet rooms, as proposed by DS/EN 15251 should be evaluated first  
according to the occupant behavior. The recemented values may need optimization to deal with moisture content 
extraction from the bathroom without compromising the CO2 performance. Acoustic performance and noise 
emissions from the house, as well as the sound levels from HVAC and active systems are also evaluated during the

Door leak area: Detailed parametric investigation on the leak area placed in all internal walls was not examined. 
However, both 0.01 and 0.02 m2 leak areas investigated showed similar air flows between rooms with negligible 
effect on CO2 concentrations. The difference was located in the outflow through the door from the bedrooms and 
it was 5% lower for the leak area of 0.01 m2 (about 1 L/s)

 Conclusions 
 
The aim of the present thesis was to investigate the impact of the 3rd room concept on the space heating and 
cooling consumption of the core building. The concept of the 3rd room, can be characterized by three distinctive 
areas: The core building, the semi-outdoor space surrounding the core building and the shell that constitutes the 
construction, separating the semi-outdoor space from the ambient environment. 
 
The focus of the thesis was the project LivingLab for the Solar Decathlon Europe competition (SDE19). It is a 
prototype house covered by a semi-outdoor space, also known as “3rd room”. The software used for energy 
analysis and the indoor climate of the LivingLab, was IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE 4.8). The first step 
was to accurately represent the 3rd room concept in IDA ICE. Designing the complicated geometry of the semi-
outdoor space on the software was very changeling, as the software is not actually designed for this purpose at 
this version. The most crucial part of the thesis was then to validate that the software was actually able to simulate 
important physical phenomena of the 3rd room concept, with reasonable accuracy. All relevant aspects analyzed 
separately and various methods for overcoming the problems or limitations in the program where developed. It 
was concluded at the end that the results from the simulations were not very accurate but reasonable enough, 
and the models were considered trustworthy for the purpose of the thesis. Then, the original floor plan was 
provided from the architectural team and the core house was designed according to the Danish building 
regulations. The final step was to analyze the interactions of the semi-outdoor space with the core house, and 
optimize the overall HVAC strategies, to utilize any benefits while trying to overcome the issues. 
 
The shell defines the semi-outdoor space that surrounds the core building with a positive overall impact on the 
heat losses, leading to lower space heating consumption. The shell of the semi-outdoor space affects the core 
building in three main ways.  
 
Infiltration/exfiltration (Medium to High contribution) 
Infiltration and exfiltration had a high contribution on the space heating consumption of the core house at 14%. 
The 3rd room concept provides protection from wind driven pressure differences around the core house 
minimizing heat losses through infiltration and exfiltration and possibly reducing the convection part of heat 
transfer on the building envelope 
 
Heat transfer through the building envelope (Low to Medium contribution) 
The temperature in the 3rd room is higher than the outside temperature only during the day where the solar heat 
gains increase the temperature of the air inside the 3rd room space (greenhouse effect). Due to the expected 
greenhouse effect, the temperature difference (ΔΤ) between the semi-outdoor space and the indoor air 
temperature becomes smaller. This directly affect the heat losses from walls, roof, doors and windows. As a result, 
from the reduced heat losses, the space heating consumption decreases. However, this should not be treated as 
opportunity to decrease the insulation thickness of the core house. Unfortunately, it seems that during sun hours 
with higher temperature in the 3rd room the heating room units, in the core house, are already OFF (not needed). 
Usually, no direct energy for heating is been saved. During nighttime, there is again a balance in the temperature 
between 3rd room and outdoors. Heat is lost through the shell construction, with no benefits during night, 
regarding reduced heat loss through the core house construction. The duration of the greenhouse effect is 
observed mostly during sun hours, and the benefits are not steady during a 24-hour period. Very low air 
temperatures in the semi-outdoor space during night are still a concern. The heat losses from thermal bridges on 
the core house have considerable impact on the space heating consumption. Normally the 3rd room concept have 
the same impact on them as with the other parts of the building envelope. Unfortunately, the heat loses from 
thermal bridges are not calculated properly in IDA ICE when the semi-outdoor space is implemented. This affects

mainly all energy results regarding space heating consumption, which are higher than they should be. An 
estimation of how much higher, could not safely established at this point and future research should be 
conducted. Finally, the solar heat gains that reach the core house (as calculated by IDA ICE) were a bit higher 
during summer. The results on cooling consumption can be considered as somewhat overestimated. 
 
Solar heat gains (Low to High contribution) 
The glazings on the shell of the semi-outdoor space, reduce part of the incoming solar heat gains. Additionally, 
the shell’s body structure as well as PV panels and solar collectors, create some partial shadows on the core house, 
also contributing to this effect. Reduced solar heat gains are expected to decrease the energy for cooling during 
summer but also increase the total space heating consumption during heating season. Indeed, the g-value of the 
glazings on the shell, seems to have a significant impact on the total space heating and cooling energy 
consumption of the core house, with higher relative impact on the space cooling consumption. The shell provides 
also some thermal insulation, although this is not its main purpose. The U-value of the glazing directly affects the 
heat losses from the semi-outdoor space to the ambient environment. Assuming the windows are closed, the 
semi-outdoor space acts as a thermal buffer zone which then will directly affect the thermal conditions in the core 
house as well as the occupant response and interaction with the space. During winter, the build-up of  heat in the 
zone during sun hours, is quickly lost after sunset through the shell’s construction. The aim here is to keep the 
heat that is stored during day, for as long as possible. This way during night the space heating can be reduced. 
Decreasing the U-value to minimize the heat losses at the beginning of the night hours is a solution. In theory, a 
shell equipped with glazings with very low U-value will have a positive impact on the space heating consumption. 
Indeed, simulations show a decrease of up to 33%. However, for an actual glazing solution, a decrease in u-value 
is accompanied by an increase in g-value which has counteractive effects. Different glazing solutions were 
examined showing that glazings with better performance can have a positive impact on both heating and cooling 
energy consumption. However, glazings with solar control coatings have so low g-value that surpasses the benefits 
from low U-values, leading to higher space heating consumption. The overall contribution of the glazings on the 
energy consumption is mostly depends on the expectations and the budget available. Again, a feasibility study will 
decide which is the optimized solution. A possible strategy to reduce the cooling consumption, without affecting 
the heating consumption would the use of double sliding windows for the openings in the shell. With this strategy 
one window slides behind another when opened, reducing the g-value, thus the heat gains (due to double glazing 
effect) while providing natural ventilation. These two approaches combined, could possibly increase the overall 
cooling performance during summer without compromising the solar heat gains and heating consumption during 
winter. 
Another consideration regarding the 3rd room concept is that suppling the AHU with air from inside the semi-
outdoor space, was proved to be a poor strategy, even though it’s a simple and cost-free solution. This strategy 
could possibly show benefits mostly during daytime when the air temperature in the semi-outdoor space is 
increased. However, it was proved that there is no need for space heating during these hours, with no impact on 
the space heating consumption. Moreover, the cooling consumption was negatively affected. An alternative 
proposal was to use closed-loop ground heat exchange method to pre-heat the air supplied to the house during 
winter and pre-cool it during summer. Additional benefits are frost protection of the heat exchanger and suppling 
the house with fresh outdoor air. The energy savings from the ground loop proved quite steady under various 
scenarios proving that it is a robust solution. 
 
The 3rd room concept have a positive impact on the space heating consumption during winter (up to 20-30%) and 
can potentially reduce the cooling consumption of a building during summer, if proper shading techniques are 
used together with open facades for natural ventilation. Increased temperatures can be observed mostly during 
sun hours and especially for low ambient temperatures with high direct solar radiation. Climates that fit to the 
above description are most likely to benefit from the 3rd room concept.
