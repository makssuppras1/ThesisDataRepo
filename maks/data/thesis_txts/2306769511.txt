Abstract
Technological advances and reduction of costs have made possible the use of multi-robots systems for
different purposes, mainly aiming to avoid dangerous situations. One of the applications is vehicle following,
useful for military and transportation missions in risky situations or automatic cruise control to increase
highway security.
Thus, the goal of this project is to implement a follow the leader system using SMR robots. Odometry and
laser scanner data are used to complete the task. The task is divided into three main steps: Identiﬁcation of
the leader vehicle, tracking of its position and following (motion control).
Even with some limitations, the ability of following the leader has been proved to work but there are room
for further improvements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Technological advances and reduction of costs have led to a progressive introduction of robots in many
different ﬁelds during the last years. While some decades ago only simple tasks were done with robots, the
development of computers and high accurate sensors make possible now to develop robotic platforms that
can achieve very complex works. The image we have now for robots is not only industrial robotic arms
developing the same movement for hours and hours but also mobile, ﬂying and humanoid robots capable
of doing almost everything, even playing football matches. Thus, nowadays it is taken for granted that the
robots are key to increase human security, provide assistance to people with special necessities and reduce
costs. In short, make life easier, cheaper and safer for humans.
In the same way as we, as humans, collaborate with others to simplify tasks that can be hard or even
impossible to achieve by ourselves, cooperation between robots have been recently widely studied. The use
of multiple robots to do a task allow the use of much simpler robots, reducing complexity and price. The
possibility of divide a big task into sub-tasks can also reduce completion time, which can be very helpful in
scenarios where time is a key factor such as rescue missions.
Multi-robots systems can be classiﬁed according to different parameters. According to Cao et al. (1997)
the main differentiation in a group architecture is if the system is centralized or not. A system is centralized
if there is only one control agent while a decentralized system have more than one control agent and it is also
known as self-organization, being much more robust. There are many examples of self-organization in nature
like molecular self-assembly in chemistry or birds ﬂocking in biology. A system can also be homogeneous or
heterogeneous if all units have the same capabilities or not. Finally, systems can be classiﬁed depending on
the type of interaction that exists between the units. This can be of three types: interaction via environment
(no communication, based on animal behaviour), via sensing (no explicit communication but one can sense
others and act consequently) or via explicit communication.
The potential uses of multi-robots systems are countless and in many different areas. For instance, in
rescue missions, a system of robots can cover an area much faster and in a more efﬁcient way. In (Burgard
et al. 2005), the authors studied how to make a coordinated exploration using several robots with potential
applications to planetary exploration, rescue missions or cleaning. Moving large or dangerous objects
are other applications where the use of robots is being studied. In (Rus et al. 1995), they focus on the
reorientation of the objects using different methods. Since the use of several robots leads to the necessity of

1.2
Problem formulation
Vehicle following or "robot follow the leader" can be considered as a special case of of vehicle formation.
Plenty of research have been carried out in this ﬁeld due to its huge potential uses. For instance, it can be
used for military and transportation uses. Thus, only one driver is needed while the other units would follow
it. This is particularly advantageous in difﬁcult environments where a large truck cannot be used so costs can
be reduced by only using one driver. It can be also helpful in dangerous environments where the less people
involved the better for security reasons. Other uses for vehicle following is the automatic cruise control
systems (ACC) (BMW-AG 2000) that aims to improve trafﬁc ﬂow rate and keep a safe distance between vehi-
cles, mainly in highways. Even the following task would normally be combined with other methods to avoid
external obstacles, trying to reduce the error between trajectories is desirable. Depending on the application,
keeping the distance between unit within a range might be needed. For example, in the automatic cruise
control a certain security distance is needed for security reasons in case of a sudden brake of the vehicle ahead.
Several studied have been done in this ﬁeld using both laser scanners, cameras or a combination of both
for the identiﬁcation of the leader unit. Even using cameras is cheaper and can be useful at times, there are
some limitations. For instance, normal cameras do not have a wide ﬁeld of view. In addition, some object
might not look the same over time or they can look similar to the background, leading to wrong detections.
Finally, to get the distance to obstacles can be difﬁcult and it may be not precise. On the other hand, laser
range scanners, even far more expensive, they have a wide full of vision (depending on the laser but often at
least 180º) and a high precession in measuring distances.
Even many works have been carried out using communication to send information from the leader to the
following units, these kind of systems have various disadvantages. One of them that is that communication
may not be available or data can be lost making the system unusable. In addition, more hardware is required
and this may increase cost and complexity.
1.3
Goals of the project
The goal of the project is to design a robot follow the leader systems using indoor mobile robots. To do
that, a laser scanner should be used along with odometry data to know the position of the follower unit. No
communication is used between units. The main objectives are:
• Study and apply methods to detect the leader robot using the laser scanner.
• Track the trajectory followed by the leader.
• Reduce as much as possible the error between the follower and the leader trajectories.
• Keep the distance between robots as constant as possible.

CHAPTER 2
Analysis
This chapter starts with a brief explanation about methods used in similar works and it continues with a
description of the robotic platform and the framework used. The limitations of the project are also explained
in this chapter.
2.1
Related work
The main steps to develop a robot follow the leader are three. Identiﬁcation of the leader, tracking of its
position and a motion control to move to the desired position and be able to keep estimating new positions.
2.1.1
Identiﬁcation
Identiﬁcation is the ﬁrst and probably the most important task. As it was mentioned in chapter 1, there
are two main alternatives. One of them is the use of cameras. Even they have several drawbacks as it
was previously explained, cameras are used sometimes for detection missions. For instance, in (D. 2004),
the author used a method to extract distinctive features matching individually the features detected with a
camera with a database of known objects. Combination of both camera and laser have also been studied.
However, due to the advantages explained in chapter 1, laser scanners are very popular sensors for detection
and identiﬁcation. Different methods are used to identify obstacles depending on the speciﬁc target. When
following humans, searching for the local minima is one of the methods often used (Wang et al. 2001).
On other hand, when the task consists on following a robot or another vehicle, line and shape ﬁtting are
commonly used. For instance, a Hough transform is used to identify the other robot in (Ng et al. 2007).
In addition, there are several works that compare different line extraction algorithms using laser scanner
focused on mobile robot applications. In, (Nguyen et al. 2007), some of the most popular line extraction
algorithms like Split-and-Merge, RANSAC or Hough Transform are evaluated. After ﬁtting the obstacle to a
line, the length is usually a good indicator to decide if it the obstacle detected is the target or not (Chen et al.
2004). Checking the number of points detected by the laser in the detected line is also a method commonly
used.
2.1.2
Tracking
Different algorithms are normally used for tracking the leader position. One of them, related to SLAM,
consists on checking periodically the map generated and look for differences in it. Thus, moving objects can
be detected and tracked. Other useful tracking methods are based on Kalman Filter and Particle Filter. The
Kalman Filter and its version for non linear systems (Extended Kalman Filter), are suitable for systems with

2.1.3
Motion Control
Most of the work related to motion control are based on systems where many robots are used so problems
may arise when manoeuvring. In these systems, a control scheme must be applied so robots do not crash
to each other. This is also known as formation control and different approaches are applied both with or
without communication. For example, in (Carpin and Parker 2002), a behaviour scheme is used using explicit
communication. Different rules or behaviours are set in two levels: team and individual robot. Another
common approach is to used a leader-follower. In these systems, each unit must follow its leader that is the
responsible for guiding the system.
2.2
Small Mobile Robot
The system is implemented using the Small Mobile Robot (SMR) platform. These are a group of mobile
vehicles developed by DTU since 1999 (Beck et al. 2010), mounting a Linux operating system. They are
powered by a 12V battery and are equipped with a Microsoft kinect camera,a Hokuyo URG-04LX laser
scanner, several IR sensors, 8 line sensors and wheel encoders among others.

MobotWare is the plug-in based framework used by the SMR. It has three basical modules:
The data from motors and sensors is handled by the Robot Hardware Daemon (RHD), a realtime hardware
abstraction layer.
Secondly, MRC (Mobile Robot Controller) is the controller responsible for robot motion. MRC interprets the
commands received by SMR-CL, a language developed at DTU (Andersen and Ravn 2004) for controlling
the robots.
Finally, Automation Robot Servers (AURS) are used for processing complex sensors like cameras or laser
scanners with a large amount of data.
2.2.1
Laser Scanner
As it was mentioned above, the SMR is equipped with a Hokuyo URG-04LX. This laser has a measuring
area of 240o with a step angle of 0.352o. Anyhow, in this project this will be limited to 180 o in order to avoid
that the laser reads some part of its own robot. The accuracy of the sensors depends on the measurement
distance. It goes from 10mm when the distance is between 60 to 1000mm to 1% of the measurement when
this is bigger than 1 meter and smaller than 4.095 meters.
Figure 2.2: Hokuyo URG-04LX Scanning Laser Rangeﬁnder
2.3
Limitations
The solution must be written entirely in C/C++ as a plug-in for the mobotware framework. As it was
mentioned before, only the laser scanner is used to detect the other robot so the plug-in is used under the
laser scanner server (ulmsserver). The motion control is implemented using the language SMR-CL and no
communication is implemented between units.
2.3.1
Physical limitation
As previously stated, it was decided not to make any change in the robots. However, the position of the
laser scanner will make the detection of the other robots more complicated.

3.6
Point-to-Point Matching
Once we have the data divided in different clusters, it is time to check if any of those clusters is the robot.
The ﬁrst thing that is done is a point-to-point matching between a template data set of the robot and the
cluster to be evaluated.
The robot template is a set of 60 points, being 60 the minimum number of points we need to consider the
obstacle as the robot no matter the distance and the orientation to the obstacle.
Point-to-point matching consists on a perfect matching points, calculating a cost function δ to each pair
of points. The ﬁnal cost is the sum of all the pair matching costs. Since the clusters will not have exactly
60 points in most of the cases, the cluster is divided into all the possible combinations of 60 consecutive
points and then match individually with the template. Before matching, all clusters are translated to zero so

4.1
Odometry
Even the odometry is already implemented in the SMR, a small explanation will be given here.
Odometry, can be deﬁned as the use of sensors to calculate displacement over time. If displacement can be
measured, the position can be easily estimated. Rotational displacement can be measured using different
sensors such as resolvers, inductive, capatitive or optical encoders, among others. In this case, the SMR are
equipped with wheel optical encoders, one in each of the two running wheels. Basically an optical encoder
beams a light to a photo detector that is interrupted by opaques slits of a rotating disc form by transparent
and opaques slits.
The encoders used have two channels, the second one displaced 90º from the ﬁrst one, so it can be determined
which one of the two channels is leading the other. Thus, the direction of the rotation can be known and the

CHAPTER 5
Motion Control
As it was explained in chapter 2, the MRC (Mobile Robot Controller) interprets the commands received
by the language SMR-CL. Thus, the motion control is implemented using functions available in the language
after receiving information from the laser server than handles the plug-in.
5.1
Speed Controller
The speed is calculated with a proportional controller according to the distance to the target point. The
controller has three levels (increase speed, normal calculation and stop). Thus, when the target robot is
further than a certain distance, the speed is set to a high value with no calculations trying to reduce the
distance as soon as possible. When the distance is within an acceptable range, the speed is proportionally
calculated. If the distance is too small, the speed is set to zero so the robot stops to avoid crashes.
However, as it was explained in chapter 3, in some cases, especially when turning, this point may not be
the rear part of the robot but a point in the middle of the robot body. Then, if using the distance to that point
to calculate the velocity some problems may arise. For instance, it may think that the robot is further than it
is in reality and increase the speed, which can led to a crash. To avoid that, it was decided not to use the
distance to the position calculated in chapter 3 but the closet point in the cluster that represents the robot. So,
the robot goes to the calculated point but the speed is calculated according to the distance to the closest point
no matter if it is the same or not.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A follow the leader system using SMR, laser scanner and odometry data have been developed. No
communication have been used between them and no change was made to the original design of the robots.
From the main goals of the project all were achieved in an acceptable way.
For the identiﬁcation, a several steps method was developed to discard as many other obstacles as possible.
However, due to the limitation of the laser scanner position, certain obstacles would give a false positive.
This is the case of objects with similar size to the robot and when faced with a certain orientation. Thus,
even if working in many cases, it is not completely robust.
The tracking of the leader trajectory have been achieved using a Kalman Filter. Since with the objective
of being able to identify the robot correctly, a number of thresholds were chosen during the identiﬁcation
process, it may happen that the follower loses the leader momentarily. In this scenarios the fact that the
Kalman ﬁlter keeps estimating the leader position have been proved useful. Setting a limit in the innovation
can also avoid noisy laser data.
Finally, the points are sent from the server to the MRC to control the motion of the robot. The robot
uses the "drive" command implemented in the SMR-CL, jumping to the next point when a certain time
has passed or a certain distance have been driven. This is done to compensate the difference between the
odometry coordinate of the leader and the one estimated by the follower. The problem arises mainly when
turning and it has been proved that this motion can reduce the trajectory error considerably. Additionally, a
speed controller have been set proportional to the closet distance to the leader achieving a fairly constant
inter distance between robots.

Even the solution have been proved to work, several improvements can be implemented.
The most important one should be focused on improving the identiﬁcation capabilities. In order to keep
the robots without any modiﬁcation, the use of computer vision along with the laser scanner could achieve
better results in identifying the robot. Moreover, if the pose of the leader can be estimated with accuracy, the
virtual link trailer presented in 5.3 could be implemented reducing the trajectory error.
Another possible improvement could be the reduction of odometry error. This could be done fusing the
odometry data with other sensors such as a gyroscope.
