Abstract
Offshore steel jacket structures are critical to marine operations, yet their welded joints are
particularly vulnerable to fatigue and crack propagation due to localized stress concentrations
and cyclic loading. This thesis introduces a framework for inter-joint correlation, aiming to
predict stress intensity factors (SIFs) across an entire structure using relationships between
initial, known, or pivotal parameters. Moving beyond traditional intra-joint analysis, this
research seeks to provide a more eﬀicient and generalized predictive approach for assessing
fatigue behavior.
To achieve this, two finite element models were developed: a full-fidelity model for detailed
structural analysis and a multi-fidelity model focusing on critical joints while simplifying the
rest of the structure. The multi-fidelity model demonstrated comparable precision to the full-
fidelity model while significantly reducing computational time, enabling extensive fracture
analyses. Crack propagation was analyzed using FRANC3D, resulting in a comprehensive
dataset of SIFs for key points of interest under tension. Correlation analysis investigated
the potential to predict these SIFs either from known values at specific welds or from static
analysis data.
The results revealed that inter-joint correlations are viable, with linear regression excelling
in single-weld predictions and polynomial regression performing effectively for full-structure
predictions.
Feature selection highlighted the importance of known SIF values for simple
relationships and stress data for more complex nonlinear predictions. With the optimized
correlation procedure, the predicted SIF values deviated by an average of only 2% from the
true values, demonstrating the effectiveness and accuracy of the approach.
This approach offers substantial practical benefits. It enables the prediction of SIF curves
across an entire structure with reduced computational effort, optimizing design and mainte-
nance strategies for offshore structures. In conclusion, this thesis provides a novel pathway for
predicting welded joint behavior, contributing to improved structural integrity in offshore ap-
plications. Future work should focus on refining regression models, exploring diverse loading
conditions, and applying the framework to a wider range of real-world offshore structures.

Introduction
Offshore steel jacket structures play a vital role in supporting a wide range of marine oper-
ations, from oil and gas exploration to renewable energy infrastructure. As these structures
endure extreme environmental conditions, including high winds, waves, and variable loading,
ensuring their structural integrity is critical to maintaining operational safety and reliability.
Welded joints, as one of the primary components of these structures, are especially susceptible
to fatigue and fracture due to stress concentrations and repeated cyclic loading. Consequently,
an in-depth understanding of the behavior of welded joints under such conditions is essential
for optimizing design and extending the service life of these structures.
Welded tubular joints form the backbone of offshore steel jacket structures, providing critical
connections between braces and chords. However, these joints are highly susceptible to lo-
calized stress concentrations, which often lead to the initiation and growth of fatigue cracks.
Over the years, various methods, including finite element analysis (FEA) and empirical para-
metric equations, have been employed to predict the behavior of these joints. While these
approaches have enhanced our ability to assess individual joints (intra-joint analysis), they
remain computationally expensive and fail to address a broader question: Can insights gained
from one joint be used to predict the behavior of others?
This thesis aims to address this gap by introducing an inter-joint correlation framework.
The central focus is to determine whether relationships between initial, known, or pivotal
parameters can be leveraged to predict stress intensity factors (SIF) at specific or arbitrary
points around the welds of the structure. This research seeks to move beyond traditional intra-
joint analyses and develop a more generalized and eﬀicient predictive approach for fatigue
assessment by exploring such correlations.
To achieve this, two finite element models will be developed as tools to generate a compre-
hensive dataset of SIFs. The first is a full-fidelity model that provides detailed insights into
the stress behavior of the entire structure. The second, a multi-fidelity model, focuses de-
tailed modeling efforts solely on the joints while simplifying the rest of the structure. The
use of the multi-fidelity model is particularly valuable, as it offers the potential to reduce
computational time significantly while maintaining suﬀicient accuracy for inter-joint corre-
lation analyses. This reduction in computational time is crucial for the subsequent fracture
analyses, where cracks are introduced into the model and tracked through multiple steps of
crack propagation.
A key advantage of the research objective lies in its potential to deliver substantial practical
benefits for professionals in the industry, particularly those involved in the design, mainte-
nance, and analysis of offshore structures. Traditional methods of evaluating SIFs in welded
joints often rely on detailed, computationally intensive models, which can require significant
time and effort. This approach, while accurate, may not always be feasible for time-sensitive
projects or when resources are limited. Additionally, the proposed framework for inter-joint
correlation could eliminate the need for extensive, localized SIF calculations across all joints
in a structure. Instead, insights gained from one or a few representative joints could be ex-
trapolated to predict the behavior of others. This not only reduces the computational burden
but also minimizes the need for extensive data collection and detailed modeling, which can
be particularly challenging in large-scale projects.

Literature review
2.1
Stress intensity factor prediction in tubular joints
Accurately predicting the SIF and estimating fatigue life is crucial for assessing the integrity
of welded tubular joints in offshore structures.
Several methods, including finite element
analysis, parametric equations, and failure assessment diagrams, have been developed to
address these challenges, particularly in the context of offshore jacket structures.
Efforts, like those by L.S. Etube et al. (2000) [6], introduced methods to predict Y correction
factors for SIFs, accounting for the crack aspect ratio evolution during crack propagation. This
method used a statistical model to improve the accuracy of existing semi-empirical solutions.
M.M.K. Lee and D. Bowness (2002) [11] proposed a methodology combining T-butt solutions
with stress concentration factors to estimate SIFs for welded joints.
This approach was
validated through comparisons with finite element analyses, providing a reliable means to
assess SIFs.
Building on these ideas, S.P. Chiew et al.
(2003) [4] developed parametric equations for
predicting SIFs in cracked T and Y joints by analyzing over 1000 joint models with various
geometries and crack profiles. These equations were validated against experimental results,
offering a reliable tool for SIF estimation.
2.2
Finite element modeling of tubular joints
Finite element analysis (FEA) plays a significant role in determining stress fields and SIFs in
tubular joints. D. Bowness and M.M.K. Lee (1995) [3] used FEA to study stress concentration
and distribution in uncracked tubular joints and cracks, validating their models through
experimental data. One of the innovative aspects of this research was the modeling of semi-
elliptical surface cracks using line-spring elements within the FEA framework. This approach
allowed the authors to simulate crack propagation more accurately than previous models,
which typically used simpler representations of cracks.
The role of stochastic finite element modeling in assessing hot-spot stresses was explored by
Larsen et al. (2021) [10]. Their study, incorporating 3D scanned weld surfaces, showed that
the FE models with the 3D scanned welds predict the strain ranges most accurately. The
stochastic results indicated that significant uncertainty exists in the hot-spot stresses, which
contribute to uncertainty in the fatigue life.
2.3
Failure assesment diagrams
Failure assessment diagrams are used to evaluate the integrity of cracked joints, incorporating
both elastic-plastic and linear-elastic energy release rates. Xudong Qian (2013) [18] intro-
duced failure assessment curves for surface cracks in circular hollow section X- and K-joints,
emphasizing the importance of geometric and material factors in failure risk evaluation.
In fatigue life estimation, S.T. Lie et al. (2012) [12] compared direct and indirect methods
for estimating SIFs in tubular K-joints. The direct method, which models the surface crack
explicitly, provided more accurate results than the conservative indirect method. The indi-
rect method where the SIFs were estimated by using T-butt solutions combined with stress
concentration factors and the degree of bending in the uncracked tubular joint, overestimated
SIF values significantly.

2.4
Review conclusion
The literature reviewed offers a comprehensive foundation for understanding SIF behavior,
joint-specific fatigue, and the computational tools available for these analyses. Existing meth-
ods for SIF estimation, including FEA and parametric equations, have advanced the field
considerably. However, these methods have primarily focused on predicting SIFs within in-
dividual joints or for configurations with majorly shared characteristics. This thesis seeks
to bridge this gap by proposing a framework for inter-joint correlation. The primary aim is
to investigate whether relationships derived from initial, known, or key parameters can be
utilized to estimate SIFs at specific or arbitrary locations around the welds of the structure.
By examining these potential correlations, the research aspires to move beyond conventional
intra-joint analysis and establish a broader, more eﬀicient methodology for predicting fatigue
behavior.

Theory
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation for the analyses and methods used through-
out this thesis. The primary theoretical background is rooted in fatigue and fracture me-
chanics, which is essential for understanding crack initiation and propagation in structures.
Furthermore, a brief introduction will be given to the finite element method and how it
is utilized in this thesis. Lastly, to develop the correlation procedure, basic knowledge of
correlation and regression theory is needed.
3.1
Fatigue
Fatigue is a failure mechanism that occurs in materials subjected to repeated or cyclic loading,
often at stress levels below their ultimate tensile strength. Over time, these cyclic loads can
cause the material to degrade, resulting in the initiation and growth of cracks that ultimately
lead to failure. This process is particularly insidious because it progresses slowly and is often
not detected until significant damage has occurred.
The fatigue process typically involves three stages.
First, micro-cracks form at locations
of stress concentrations. Stress concentrations are localized regions of elevated stress that
occur due to abrupt changes in a structure’s geometry, such as holes, notches, fillets, or
welds. These areas act as initiation sites for fatigue cracks, as the increased stress exceeds
the nominal stress applied to the material. The severity of stress concentrations is quantified
by the stress concentration factor, Kt, which is the ratio of the maximum local stress to
the nominal applied stress.
In welded structures, these stress concentrations will almost
always start to grow from welds. This is due to the welding process leaving small metallic
discontinuities from which cracks can grow and due to the sudden change in local geometry
around the weld toe and heel [16]. In the second stage, these cracks grow incrementally with
each loading cycle, driven by the repeated application of stress. Finally, the crack reaches a
critical size, where the remaining material can no longer support the applied load, leading to
sudden and catastrophic fracture.

3.1.1
S-N curves
S-N curves, also known as Wöhler curves, are a highly used tool in fatigue design, representing
the relationship between cyclic stress range, ∆σR, and the number of cycles to failure, N.
These curves form the foundation for assessing fatigue life in structural components in many
building codes around the world. In the Eurocode, S-N curves are grouped into categories to
try and account for variations in structural geometry and manufacturing details. S-N curves
are developed experimentally by subjecting specimens to cyclic constant amplitude loading
until failure occurs. The data is plotted on a logarithmic scale, with stress range on the
vertical axis and the number of cycles to failure on the horizontal axis.
Figure 2: Standardized S-N curve. Image from ESDEP lecture 12.8 [17]
The fatigue strength curve in the Eurocode is defined using the parameters σC, σD, and
σL, to characterize the fatigue performance.
The parameter σC represents the reference
fatigue strength at 2 million cycles. It is the stress range at which fatigue damage begins
to significantly accumulate for “good” structural details, such as those with minimal stress
concentrations or notch effects. Beyond this, the constant amplitude fatigue limit, σD, is
defined at 5 million cycles. This value serves as a compromise between details that stabilize
at 2 million cycles and those with severe notch effects that stabilize at 10 million cycles. For
stress ranges below σD, no fatigue damage is expected under constant amplitude loading.
To account for longer fatigue life, the curve introduces a cut-off limit at σL, the stress range at
10 million cycles. Beyond this, the curve adopts a steeper slope, transitioning from the initial
slope constant m = 3 to m = 5. This reflects the gradual reduction of the constant amplitude
fatigue limit caused by the cumulative effects of stress ranges above σD. By incorporating
this steeper slope, the model ensures that all stress ranges in a variable loading spectrum
eventually contribute to fatigue damage, even if they are initially below σD.
S-N curves are an excellent tool for preliminary fatigue design but have notable limitations
when it comes to understanding crack initiation and growth. They rely on total life estimation,
which encompasses both the crack initiation and propagation phases, making it challenging
to predict or monitor crack growth once it has begun. Additionally, S-N curves are highly
specific to material properties and joint geometries, requiring distinct curves for each detail

category configuration. Moreover, they offer no insight into crack stability or the conditions
under which a crack may propagate catastrophically.
3.2
Fracture mechanics
Fracture mechanics provides a detailed framework for analyzing the behavior of cracks in
materials under stress, making it a vital tool for understanding and predicting structural
failures. Unlike S-N curves, which focus on total life estimation, fracture mechanics models
crack growth and offer insights into crack propagation phases. This approach allows for the
assessment of structural integrity throughout a component’s life, particularly when cracks are
already present.
3.2.1
Energy release rate
The Griﬀith energy release rate is a key concept in fracture mechanics that describes the
energy balance during crack propagation.
Introduced by A. A. Griﬀith in 1921 [7], this
theory explained the failure of brittle materials, such as glass, by considering the interplay
between elastic energy release and surface energy consumption. Griﬀith proposed that crack
propagation is governed by the competition between two forms of energy:
• Elastic energy release: As a crack grows, stored elastic energy is released, lowering
the system’s total energy.
• Surface energy: The creation of new crack surfaces requires energy, which increases
the system’s total energy.
A crack will propagate when the energy released by the system is suﬀicient to overcome the
energy required to create new crack surfaces. The energy release rate, G, is the rate of change
of total potential energy per unit area of newly created crack surface:
G = −δU
δA
(1)
where
• U is the total potential energy of the system
• A is the crack surface area
Griﬀith’s criterion for crack propagation occurs when the energy release rate reaches a critical
value, denoted Gc, which is related to the material’s surface energy, γ:
Gc = 2γ
(2)
While Griﬀith’s theory effectively explains brittle fracture, it does not account for energy
dissipation in ductile materials.
In the 1950s, G. R. Irwin extended Griﬀith’s theory to
address its limitations, particularly for ductile materials where significant plastic deformation
occurs near the crack tip [14][8]. Irwin’s contributions include the incorporation of additional
energy dissipation and the introduction of the stress intensity factor as a key parameter.
6
==PAGE:13==
Irwin recognized that in ductile materials, energy is dissipated not only in creating new crack
surfaces but also through plastic deformation at the crack tip. He redefined the critical energy
release rate to include this plastic work:
Gc = 2γ + Gp
(3)
where Gp is the energy dissipated due to the plastic deformation occurring in the material,
particularly near the crack tip. For brittle materials, such as glass, the surface energy term
dominates, leading to the relationship G ≈2γ. In contrast, for ductile materials like steel,
the plastic dissipation term becomes the dominant factor, and the energy release rate is given
by G ≈Gp.
3.2.2
Stress intensity factors
The stress intensity factor (SIF), denoted K, is a fundamental parameter in fracture mechanics
that quantifies the intensity of a stress field near the tip of a crack. It characterizes the severity
of the stress concentration caused by the crack and depends on the applied load, geometry
of the structure, and size of the crack. The SIF provides a criterion for crack growth. The
material property fracture toughness describes the maximum SIF a material can sustain before
the crack propagation becomes unstable. The critical stress intensity factor, Kc, is used to
quantify fracture toughness.
The stress distribution around a crack is governed by three primary modes of loading:
Mode I - Opening mode: Crack faces are pulled apart by tensile stresses. This is the most
common and critical mode since it typically requires the least energy for a crack to propagate.
Mode II - Sliding mode: Crack faces slide relative to each other under in-plane shear
stresses.
Mode III - Tearing mode: Crack faces twist relative to each other due to out-of-plane
shear stresses.
Figure 3: The three fracture modes. Image from fracture mechanics Wikipedia article [20]
Every type of crack deformation can be viewed as a superposition of these three fundamental
modes. Real-world loading conditions often involve a combination of these modes, resulting
in mixed-mode fracture. A corresponding SIF and fracture toughness exists for each of the

3.3
Finite element method
This section will give a brief overview of the finite element method (FEM) or finite element
analysis (FEA), but by no means an in-depth dive into the mathematics and inner workings
of FEM.
FEM is a numerical approach used extensively in the field of civil engineering to solve complex
structural problems. FEM divides a complex physical system into smaller, simpler parts called
elements, connected at discrete points known as nodes. These nodes contain a set of degrees
of freedom (DOF), which can be contributed to the translation along or rotation about an
axis for that specific node. By solving equations for each element and then combining them,
FEM provides an approximate solution for the entire structure, capturing its behavior under
various loads and conditions.
Each element uses a set of shape functions to describe how the displacements vary within it.
The shape functions are often linear, quadratic, or even higher-order polynomials, depending
on the element type, and determine the interpolation of the nodal values across the element.
The use of these higher-order elements, like the quadratics, can increase the solution accuracy
by better conforming to the shape of the structure’s geometry and capturing the deformations
more precisely. To generate these smaller elements, meshing is used. Meshing is the process
of dividing the structure into elements, which can vary in shape depending on the geometry
of the given part of the structure. Selecting a suiting element type and size is crucial as
the accuracy of the FEM solution depends on a well-refined mesh, especially in regions with
high-stress concentrations or complex geometries.
Once the local equations for each element are established, they are combined, or assembled,
into a global system of equations that represents the entire structure. The assembly process
adds the contributions of each element’s stiffness, mass, and load vectors to form a com-
prehensive model of the entire structure.
This is also the step where load and boundary
conditions are applied, to conform to the global system of equations. Load and boundary
15
==PAGE:22==
conditions are applied by prescribing certain nodes or elements a set of DOFs that they have
to abide by. As an example, adding a completely fixed support would require that all the
DOFs of a node should be equal to zero.
With the global system of equations assembled various numerical approaches can be used to
solve for the unknown values, typically nodal displacements in structural engineering. From
these primary solved values, the secondary quantities can be calculated such as stresses and
strains which can be derived from the constitutive relationship of the material. Results can
then be displayed and visualized to identify areas where high stresses or large deformations
occur, to aid with assessing the structural integrity and performance of the structure.

3.4.1
Correlation
Correlation is a statistical measure used to describe the strength and direction of a relationship
between two or more variables. In data analysis, understanding correlation is fundamental
because it provides insights into how changes in one variable might be associated with changes
in another.
The degree of this relationship is quantified by the correlation coeﬀicient, a
value that ranges from -1 to +1.
A correlation coeﬀicient close to +1 indicates a strong
positive relationship, meaning that as one variable increases, the other tends to increase as
well. Conversely, a coeﬀicient close to -1 represents a strong negative relationship, where one
variable tends to decrease as the other increases. A coeﬀicient near 0 suggests that there is
little to no linear relationship between the variables.[2]
The most commonly used measure of correlation is Pearson’s correlation coeﬀicient, which
quantifies the linear relationship between two variables. It is calculated as the covariance of
the two variables divided by the product of their standard deviations:

relationship between the variables is linear.
When these assumptions are not met, other
correlation measures such as Spearman’s rank correlation or Kendall’s tau may be used,
which assess monotonic relationships, not necessarily linear.
When working with datasets containing multiple variables, a correlation matrix becomes
an invaluable tool. A correlation matrix is a table that shows the correlation coeﬀicients
between all pairs of variables.
It allows for a quick visual inspection of relationships in
large datasets and helps identify which variables are highly correlated. In addition to helping
understand relationships between variables, correlation is often a first step in feature selection
and dimensionality reduction. When building models, it is often desirable to select a subset
of features that are highly relevant and avoid including redundant features, simplifying the
model and potentially improving its performance.
3.4.2
Regression
Regression is a fundamental statistical technique used to analyze and predict the relationship
between dependent and independent variables. It enables us to model how changes in input
variables affect output variables, making it an essential tool in data analysis—particularly for
predicting numerical outcomes.
At its simplest, linear regression assumes a direct linear relationship between the input vari-
ables and the target variables. The goal is to find the best-fit line that minimizes the difference
between the predicted and actual values, commonly measured using the mean squared error
(MSE). This line is defined by a set of coeﬀicients that determine the weight or importance

4.2
Finite element models
The commercial finite element analysis software Abaqus was used to create two different model
variants of the structure described in section 4.1. The first was a full-fidelity model, where
the entire structure was modeled using solid elements. The second was a multi-fidelity model,
where only the joints were modeled with solid elements, while the rest of the structure was
simplified with beam elements. The goal was to determine whether a simplified model, which
focused detailed modeling on the most critical areas, could be used to save computational
power and significantly reduce analysis time while remaining accurate. The finite element
models were developed in tandem with each other but utilize different techniques and modeling
approaches, which will be described in the following sections.
4.2.1
Full fidelity model
The Full Fidelity Model (FFM) is designed to replicate the real structure as closely as possible.
Creating such a comprehensive and consistent model is a lengthy, iterative process. This often
involves trial and error, requiring the exploration of various approaches and revisiting earlier
steps when necessary.
Initially, the focus was on modeling the individual chords and braces with correct dimensions
and positions, based on the drawings. At this stage, interactions between the components
were not yet considered. Each part was created using a solid extrusion with hollow cross-
section dimensions as specified in Figure 13 and with the corresponding lengths. These parts
were then rotated and translated within the Abaqus assembly menu to assemble the structure.
By merging the parts, a single coherent instance was produced. This preliminary version of
the model is depicted in Figure 15.
Figure 15: Crude model with overlapping geometry
26
==PAGE:33==
While this initial model provides a basic representation, accurately depicting the joints in
greater detail is critical, as they are the key focal points of the structure.
All four chords were remodeled using a singular solid sweep method. This involved tracing
the center of the outer edge and applying the same hollow cross-section throughout. The
sweep approach inherently managed chord intersections, eliminating overlapping geometry
and creating closed shapes. The resulting sweep part is shown in Figure 16.
Figure 16: Chord sweep part
Next, the braces were created using the extrusion method and positioned at the chord centers
via rotation and translation. To remove excess material from the braces where they intersect
with the chords, a cutting operation was performed using a solid instance of the chord. This
ensured that no part of the braces extended into the chords, leaving their end faces flush with
the chord surfaces. A similar procedure was applied to where the braces intersect each other
to eliminate overlap, ensuring a realistic configuration. Although this method slightly altered
symmetry around the brace intersections, it did not affect the overall structural behavior. The
modified chords and braces were then merged into a single instance, resulting in a watertight
model.

as shown in Figure 17.
Figure 17: Weld round feature
Meshing is a critical step in finite element modeling, as it determines the accuracy and ef-
ficiency of the analysis. A coarser mesh offers faster computation but sacrifices accuracy,
whereas a finer mesh provides better results at the cost of increased processing time. For
a complex 3D model like the FFM, the meshing process is particularly intricate and time-
consuming.
Due to the model’s numerous small parts, unconventional shapes, and sharp corners, Abaqus
was unable to automatically generate the desired hex-dominated elements. Hexahedral ele-
ments were preferred for their superior accuracy but were not viable in all regions. In such
areas, tetrahedral elements were used instead.
To facilitate meshing, the model was partitioned into simpler geometries to maximize the
use of hex-dominated elements. This partitioning process involved extensive trial and error,
utilizing Abaqus’s datum axes, datum planes, and partition tools. For example, braces were
separated from the welds using the n-sided patch partition and further split lengthwise using
datum planes. Figure 18 illustrates an example of partitioning at a joint.

Fracture analysis model
FRANC3D, or Fracture Analysis Code 3D, is a specialized fracture mechanics software de-
signed to model and analyze crack growth in structural components. In this study, FRANC3D
was used to insert and propagate cracks within the steel jacket structure and to calculate SIFs
in critical areas. Integrating Abaqus finite element models with FRANC3D allows each soft-
ware’s strengths to be utilized for in-depth fracture analysis: Abaqus handles complex finite
element modeling and stress calculations, while FRANC3D specializes in accurately simulat-
ing crack behavior and growth. In this tandem workflow, Abaqus provides FRANC3D with
detailed stress, displacement, and material data from the model, which FRANC3D uses to set
up precise boundary conditions around the crack region. Abaqus then computes updated dis-
placement fields, which FRANC3D interprets to evaluate how cracks propagate and how SIFs
change with each propagation step. By exchanging results at each stage, the two programs
collaboratively refine the fracture analysis, with Abaqus modeling the structural behavior
and FRANC3D focusing on fracture mechanics. The workflow is presented in figure 44. This
section describes FRANC3D’s role in setting up fracture simulations and calculating SIFs.

5.3
Static analysis
Once the crack was inserted and the model was remeshed, the analysis process could begin.
The first analysis performed was a static analysis. This initial step did not propagate the crack
further but was used to run the first phase of the process. The remeshed model was integrated
back into the global Abaqus model by merging it with the retained boundary conditions from
the original mesh. Abaqus then recalculated the displacements for the updated model and
sent these results back to FRANC3D for the calculation of the SIFs.
The static analysis primarily served as a preliminary test to ensure the analysis could be
completed successfully and that no egregious error would occur, either from FRANC3D or
Abaqus. This was an effective way of telling in due time, if any changes had to be made to
the FEM model or crack definition before starting the full process of the crack propagation
analysis.
5.4
Crack growth analysis
A set of parameters was defined to control the modeling of the crack growth. Because this
analysis was by far the heaviest computational burden, it was necessary to be certain that the
input model and crack growth parameters were correct. No specific criteria or endpoint was
established for the analyses. Instead, it was allowed to continue running as long as possible,
typically reaching a crack growth of 2-5 millimeters before penetrating the full thickness. This
meant that a typical analysis runtime of a single crack would take around 40-60 minutes to
complete.
Firstly, the subcritical crack growth type was selected together with the kink angle model
prescribed to be 0, since the scope of this project was only on planar cracks. The crack growth
itself was modeled by Paris’ law, using the following input parameters to represent steel:

6.2
Correlation procedure
A Python script has been developed to manage the entire data handling and correlation
process. The script is designed to be highly parametric, allowing flexibility in how the data is
processed, which data is included in the model, and which regression model is applied, among
other options. The inner workings of the script will be explained in chronological order, and
the full script can be found in Appendix A.4.
Each crack step is loaded into a dictionary, which includes columns for the normalized distance
and the corresponding SIF value. These dictionaries are organized by point of interest, and
all such dictionaries are then consolidated into a single overarching dictionary.
The uncracked and cracked feature data are stored in Excel files and are loaded directly into
individual dataframes in Python.

6.2.1
Data normalization
To ensure consistency, the number of steps for each point of interest can be trimmed to a
desired range, ensuring that all points of interest contain the same amount of data. In Figure
62 below, a screenshot of the FRANC3D analysis status for some of the welds is shown.
Figure 62: Franc3D analysis status
From the figure, it can be observed that step 17 would be an appropriate common final step
to include in the correlation analysis. Only two fracture analyses end earlier than step 17 —
W4LL and W7LL — and these are therefore excluded from the analysis, bringing the total
number of points of interest to 33.
As concluded earlier, the SIFs on each side of the welds are mirrored. This is why fracture
analyses were conducted only on one side of each weld; the data can then be mirrored to
obtain the SIFs on the opposite side. In the Python script, the dictionaries for each point
of interest that needs to be mirrored are copied, and renamed, and their dataframes are
mirrored, resulting in a total of 57 points of interest, each with 18 steps.
The number of data points for each SIF curve also varies, necessitating normalization tech-
niques to standardize the SIF curves. One approach is to select a fixed number of points from
the total data. Alternatively, characteristic points such as the start, end, peaks, or maximum
SIF value could be chosen. Another option involves fitting a function to each curve and using
its coeﬀicients to represent the SIFs. When selecting a normalization technique, it is crucial
to consider potential information loss and its effect on the model. Reducing the number of
points limits the model’s ability to output more detailed data while fitting a function in-
troduces slight deviations from the original data, causing the model to learn based on these
approximations. The ability to select the representation method and the number of points is
one of the parametric features of the script, allowing for testing and comparison of different
methods.
62
==PAGE:69==
The most straightforward and commonly used method throughout the project is representing
the SIFs with a fixed number of points. Since most initial crack steps contain around 30
points, a fixed number at 20 points is chosen. This approach reduces the number of variables
the model must predict while minimizing information loss. The normalization is performed
by creating a list of 20 evenly spaced integers, ranging from zero to the total number of points
for that step, and selecting the corresponding points. An example of normalization by a fixed
number of points can be seen below in Figure 63.

curve shown in Figure 64.

This curve is a closer match. However, increasing the polynomial degree also increases the

of information with the risk of creating an overly complex model.

6.2.2
Features and targets
There are several methods for approaching the correlation problem, and each influences how
the data is divided into features and targets. The features represent the input to the model,
while the targets are the output. The prediction methods can be categorized into four ap-
proaches:
1. Predicting SIFs for all points of interest based solely on uncracked features.
2. Predicting SIFs for all points of interest based on uncracked features and a pivot weld.
3. Predicting SIFs for a target weld based solely on uncracked features.
4. Predicting SIFs for a target weld based on uncracked features and a pivot weld.
In method 1, the uncracked data from Table 4 serves as the feature data, and the SIF data from
Table 6 for all points of interest acts as the target data. The target SIF data is represented as
a dataframe with 18 steps x 57 points of interest = 1026 rows x 20 columns. The uncracked
dataframe, on the other hand, originally had 57 rows x 19 columns, as it contains only one
row per point of interest for a single static analysis. Although the number of columns in the
feature and target dataframes need not match, the number of rows must align.
One way to address this is by replicating each row in the uncracked dataframe to match the 18
steps of the SIF dataframe. This results in a feature dataframe with 1026 rows x 19 columns,
ensuring that the points of interest in both dataframes align and that the model can establish
a relationship between them. Additionally, it is beneficial to add an extra column containing
the step number to correlate with the SIF target data, making the feature dataframe 1026
rows x 20 columns. Once the model has been trained, the goal for this method is to perform
only one static analysis, obtain the uncracked feature data for the points of interest, and
input it into the model to predict the SIFs for all those points of interest.
Method 2 uses the same SIF target dataframe as in Method 1 but with additional feature
data. If fracture analyses have been conducted for points of interest along a weld (a so-called
”pivot weld”), the resulting SIF data can be used as an additional feature. Furthermore, since
the fracture analyses have been performed, cracked data (similar to the data in Table 5) can
be extracted from Abaqus and also added as feature data.
Practically, this involves removing rows from the target SIF dataframe, as the SIF data for
these points is already known and cannot be used as target data. Instead, this data is added
as new columns to the feature dataframe. For example, if the SIFs for all 8 points of interest
along weld number 5 are known, the target dataframe shrinks to 882 rows x 20 columns. To
ensure the number of rows in the feature and target dataframes matches, the corresponding
uncracked feature data for weld number 5 is removed from the feature dataframe. The new
SIF data for these points is then added to the feature dataframe, repeating the known SIF
values to match the positions in the target dataframe.
If both the known SIF data and the cracked data are incorporated into the feature dataframe,
it expands to 882 rows x 59 columns. While this approach increases the number of features to
help predict the SIFs, it also introduces a large number of variables relative to the available
data, highlighting the need for feature selection, which will be discussed later.
Method 3 is similar to Method 1, but it focuses on a single weld rather than all points of
interest. In this method, the model is trained to predict the SIF data for a specific weld using
65
==PAGE:72==
only the uncracked feature data for that weld. For example, if the goal is to predict the SIF
data for the eight points of interest along weld number 2, the target dataframe would consist
of 18 steps x 8 points of interest = 144 rows x 20 columns. By applying the same procedure
as in Method 1 to match the points of interest, the feature dataframe becomes 144 rows x 20
columns.
Since Method 3 focuses solely on one weld, it will not be able to establish a general relationship
between features and targets across multiple welds.
However, it may be more precise in
predicting the SIFs for the specific weld in question.
Method 4 shares similarities with Method 2, but it targets a specific weld using both uncracked
feature data and extra data from a pivot weld. For instance, if weld number 5 is chosen as the
pivot weld and weld number 2 is the target weld, the model would incorporate the known SIF
data and cracked data for weld 5 into the feature dataframe. The resulting feature dataframe
would have 144 rows x 59 columns, while the target dataframe remains 144 rows x 20 columns.
This approach aims to establish a relationship between the features from the pivot weld and
the target weld.
In some cases, relative features can be calculated to gain additional insight. These features
represent the differences between the uncracked features for the pivot weld and the target
weld. Although differences in uncracked features were initially tested, they did not improve
the model when used alongside the normal uncracked features, as they were dependent on
the original features and did not introduce significantly new information. As a result, the
relative features were not included in the final model. However, a different approach — such
as calculating the ratio between the features — could potentially provide new insights for
future models.

flexibility, as the total number of features can be adjusted depending on the model’s needs.

6.2.4
Model training
With the feature and target dataframes prepared, the next step is to train the model. The data
is first split into training and testing sets, with 80% allocated for training and 20% reserved
for testing. The training set is used to fit the model, while the testing set, which consists
of unseen data, is used to evaluate its performance and ensure that the model generalizes
well to new data. This split is performed randomly to avoid any unintended bias in the data
distribution.
Next, it is important to scale the data, as the features vary significantly in terms of scales
and units. Scaling ensures that all features contribute equally to the model, preventing those
with larger ranges from disproportionately influencing the training process. The features are
standardized by transforming them to have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. While
scaling may not always be necessary for every model, it is especially crucial for algorithms that
rely on distance metrics, such as Support Vector Machines and k-means clustering. Therefore,
scaling is always performed before training the model.
Feature selection is typically carried out after splitting and scaling the data to ensure that
the process is unbiased and replicable.
There is a wide variety of regression models available for training, and selecting the right one
can significantly impact the results. Fortunately, implementing and testing different models in
Python is straightforward. The script is designed to facilitate easy switching between models,
allowing for eﬀicient experimentation and comparison of their performance. Below are the
regression models used in this study along with their settings:
1. Dummy Regressor
2. Linear Regression
3. Lasso Regression (alpha = 0.1)
4. Polynomial Regression (degree = 3)
5. K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Regressor (n neighbors = 2, p = 2, weights = ”distance”)
6. Decision Tree Regressor (max depth = none, min samples leaf = 1, min samples split
= 2)
7. Random Forest Regressor (max depth = 20, max features = ”sqrt”, min samples leaf =
1, min samples split = 2, n estimators = 200)
8. Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) Regressor (hidden layer sizes = (100,50), activation =
”relu”, solver = ”adam”, alpha = 0.01, learning rate = ”constant”, max iter = 5000)
9. Gradient boosting regressor (n estimators = 200, learning rate = 0.2, max depth = 5,
subsample = 0.8, min samples leaf = 5, min samples split = 2)
10. XGBoost regressor (objective = ”reg:squarederror”, subsample = 0.8, n estimators =
200, min child weight = 1, max depth = 5, learning rate = 0.1, colsample bytree = 0.8)
While some models work effectively with default settings, others require fine-tuning of hy-
perparameters — settings that must be chosen before running the model, which influence
68
==PAGE:75==
its behavior and performance. For instance, in Random Forest, important hyperparameters
include the number of trees, the maximum depth of each tree, and the minimum number of
samples required to split an internal node.
Manually adjusting hyperparameters is one approach to finding the optimal configuration,
but a more eﬀicient method is to use hyperparameter optimization. This process automates
the search for the best hyperparameters. For this task, GridSearch was employed, which
systematically tests a predefined set of hyperparameter values and selects the combination
that yields the best model performance, as determined by cross-validation results. GridSearch
was applied to all models, using a grid of common hyperparameters to identify the optimal
values for each model. The final hyperparameters for each model are included in the list above.
The grid search was performed on method 2, and the hyperparameters were subsequently used
for the other models to ensure consistency. While these parameters are optimal for method
2 they might lower the performance of the other methods slightly.
The model training process utilized K-fold cross-validation, where the data is divided into K
subsets (or folds). The model is then trained K times, with each fold serving as the validation
set while the remaining folds are used for training. This technique provides a more reliable
estimate of model performance, as it ensures that every data point is used for both training
and validation, thus reducing the risk of overfitting.
6.2.5
Model evaluation
During each fold of cross-validation, the model is trained on a subset of the data, while
predictions are made on the unseen fold (the validation set).
These predictions are then
compared to the actual values, and the model’s performance is evaluated using various metrics.
Specifically, the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) is used to measure the average error across all
the SIF point predictions, while the Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) expresses this
error as a percentage of the true values. These performance metrics provide valuable insights
into the model’s generalizability, indicating how well it performs across different subsets of
the data.
Once the model has been trained and validated through cross-validation, it is fitted to the
entire training dataset. Following this, the model makes predictions on the test set, which has
not been used during training or cross-validation. The predicted values are then compared
to the actual test set values, and the test error is calculated using the same performance
metrics. The test error provides an estimate of how well the model will generalize to new,
unseen data.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the potential for inter-joint correlations between SIFs in offshore steel
jacket structures. By introducing a framework that leverages relationships between SIFs and
initial or pivotal parameters, this research aimed to develop a more eﬀicient and generalized
approach for predicting the fatigue behavior of welded joints across an entire structure.
A full-fidelity model and a multi-fidelity model were developed, with the latter focusing on
critical joints. The results demonstrated that the multi-fidelity model maintained precision
while significantly enhancing computational eﬀiciency, making it a valuable tool for fracture
analyses.
Fracture analyses were conducted for key points of interest under tension using Franc3D,
resulting in extensive SIF data. Initial analyses in Abaqus provided essential information
such as displacements, stresses, and other relevant parameters.
The correlation analysis
explored four distinct prediction methods: Predicting the SIFs of a specific weld using known
SIFs from another weld or relying solely on initial Abaqus data, and predicting all SIFs using
known SIFs from a pivot weld or utilizing just the initial Abaqus data.
Feature selection emphasized the importance of known SIF values for single-weld predictions,
indicating a straightforward linear relationship, while stresses were crucial for full-structure
predictions, revealing a more complex nonlinear relationship. Various regression models were
tested, with linear regression excelling in weld-to-weld predictions and polynomial regression
proving effective for predicting all welds. XGBoost showed promise but exhibited signs of
overfitting. With the optimized correlation procedure, the predicted SIF values deviated by
an average of only 2% from the true values, demonstrating the effectiveness and accuracy of
the approach.
The findings suggest that, despite the complexity of joint interactions, meaningful correlations
can be established, offering a promising pathway for optimizing design and maintenance
strategies. Predicting SIF curves for all welds across the structure using only a handful of
features — either known SIFs or static analysis data — represents a significant advancement.
In conclusion, this research presents a novel approach to predicting welded joint behavior,
contributing to offshore structural integrity. Future work should focus on refining regression
models, exploring diverse loading conditions, and applying the framework to a wider range
of real-world offshore structures.

Further work
This study has revealed a strong inter-joint correlation between the SIFs of different joints in
the analyzed structure. While these findings provide valuable insights into the relationship
between stresses in various parts of the structure, there is a concern that this correlation
might be overfitted to the specific geometry, loading conditions, and material properties
of the structure studied.
Overfitting in this context means that the results may fit the
characteristics of the analyzed structure too closely, potentially limiting their applicability to
other offshore steel structures. To address this, future work should focus on testing the inter-
joint correlation across a broader range of structural systems. This would involve analyzing
structures with different joint geometries, configurations, and loading scenarios to evaluate
whether the correlation holds under different conditions. If the relationship is found to persist
across diverse systems, it would confirm that the findings are not a result of overfitting but
rather represent a generalizable behavior.
One important aspect to consider in future studies is the inclusion of cyclic and dynamic load-
ing conditions. While this thesis primarily analyzed the structural response under static loads
and dynamic frequency analysis, real-world offshore structures are continuously subjected to
complex environmental loading. These include cyclic wave-induced forces, wind loads, and,
in some cases, seismic activity, which contribute to fatigue damage over time. Future work
could focus on applying more realistic loading scenarios that account for the stochastic nature
of environmental forces. Cyclic loading analysis could explore the progressive accumulation
of fatigue damage in the joints under operational conditions. This would include simulating a
full spectrum of cyclic amplitudes to better capture crack initiation and propagation behav-
ior. By combining these analyses with fracture mechanics tools, it is possible to determine
whether the observed inter-joint correlation holds under conditions that promote fatigue crack
growth, rather than just under static loading.
