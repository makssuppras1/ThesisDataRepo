Preface 
This Master's Thesis is a result of a study performed at the Department of Environmental 
and Resource Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. This project 
corresponds to 30 ECTS-points. The thesis was a result of the research carried out from 
January 2022 to June 2022. It was performed in cooperation with Rambøll A/S as a part of 
the research project "Improved methodology for CO2 based design of building ventilation”. 
The main objectives of the research were: to identify the ranges of indoor CO2 
concentration and factors that may have an impact on ventilation rate calculation based on 
metabolic CO2; to identify the impact of the identified factors on the ventilation rate 
calculation; to asses critically the applicability of the CO2-based demand-controlled 
ventilation method considering the accuracy of CO2 measurements; to propose an 
improved method for ventilation rate calculation based on metabolic CO2 and method for 
control of CO2 exposure of room occupants.

Abstract 
When calculating ventilation rate based on metabolic CO2, numerous factors may cause 
ventilation effectiveness of the room different than the values recommended by ventilation 
standards CEN CR 1752, EN 13779, and ASHRAE 62.1-2019. CO2 generation rate 
calculation in ventilation standards derived from the metabolic rate calculation model, 
which may differ from the actual CO2 generation rate. Outdoor CO2 concentrations vary 
in different locations at different times. The actual ventilation rate based on metabolic CO2 
may be different from the ventilation rate calculated by using guideline values in ventilation 
standards. The non-uniformity of indoor CO2 distribution causes different values of 
measured CO2 concentration when putting CO2 sensors at different locations. The reading 
of the measured CO2 concentration of the CO2 sensor may differ from the true CO2 
concentration at the measured point because of measurement error. The performance of 
CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation depends on the measured CO2. CO2 
concentration measured at different locations with different accuracy may result in different 
ventilation rates when implementing CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation.  
 
This study compared ventilation effectiveness based on measured CO2 concentrations, 
measured CO2 generation rates, and measured outdoor CO2 concentrations in the recent 
research to guideline values in ventilation standards. Ventilation rates calculated based on 
measured ventilation effectiveness, measured CO2 generation rates, and measured 
outdoor CO2 concentrations were separately compared to the ventilation rates calculated 
based on the guideline values in ventilation standards. An 8-occupant meeting room with 
the dimensions of 4,2 × 4,12 × 2,89 m located in the downtown of Copenhagen, Denmark 
was assumed to calculate required ventilation rates based on the results from recent 
research and ventilation standards. It was found that the required ventilation rates 
calculated based on the recent research were 83 % ~ 103 % of the ventilation rates 
calculated based on ventilation standards. 
 
The impact of position, accuracy of CO2 sensor, and outdoor CO2 concentration on the 
ventilation rate control when implementing CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation was 
investigated. The maximum required changes in ventilation rate to keep indoor measured 
CO2 concentration at 1000 ppm were calculated. The position of the CO2 sensor is the 
most influential factor that influences the ventilation rate control when implementing CO2-
based demand-controlled ventilation compared to the height, accuracy of the CO2 sensor, 
and outdoor CO2 concentration fluctuation. The measurement error of the CO2 sensor 
may result in at most 7.35 % overventilation and 8.33 % underventilation. Outdoor CO2 
concentrations fluctuating between 350 and 450 ppm may lead to required ventilation rate 
increases at most 3 % and decreases at most 13 %.

1. Introduction 
This chapter presents the background for understanding the importance of CO2 in 
ventilation design and control. Section 1.1 illustrates that ventilation is important to improve 
indoor air quality and CO2 can be used as an indicator to assess ventilation and indoor air 
quality. Section 1.2 introduces the method recommended and used at present to calculate 
ventilation rate based on metabolic CO2. Factors that may have an impact on the accuracy 
of the method are discussed. Section 1.3 introduces the method to control ventilation rate 
based on CO2 in order to keep acceptable indoor air quality while saving energy. Some 
factors that may influence the performance of this method are discussed. In Section 1.4, 
the keywords of reviewed literature were analyzed to identify the focus of the recent 
research and the associated relationship between each study.  
1.1 Indoor air quality (IAQ) 
Nowadays, people spend around 80 % ~ 90 % of their time in buildings [1]. The indoor 
environment (temperature, humidity, indoor air quality (IAQ), acoustics, and lighting) quality 
(IEQ) is of significance to occupants’ health, comfort, and performance. Among the 
environmental factors, indoor air quality highly affects the health and comfort of human 
beings [2]–[4]. 
 
Indoor air quality is influenced by both pollutions emitted indoors and outdoors. Indoor 
contaminants are generated by occupants (bio-effluents), occupants’ activities (cooking, 
smoking, etc.), building materials, furnishing, and equipment in buildings [5], [6]. Outdoor 
contaminants are emitted from vehicles, power plants, industrial boilers, and so on [6]. 
Outdoor pollutants can penetrate and influence indoor air quality.  
 
Indoor pollutants may result in allergies, respiratory symptoms, irritation of mucous 
membranes, sick building syndrome (SBS) symptoms, toxic reactions, infections, 
inflammation, endocrine and thyroid disruption, infertility, cancer, adverse effects on fetal 
and child development, and mutagenic effects [2], [4]. Moreover, they may affect the 
comfort level, mental and physical performance of the occupants [7], [8].  
 
To avoid the health hazards, and improve the comfort level and performance of occupants, 
it is of great importance to limit the sources of pollution and to use the ventilation system 
to remove or dilute the indoor pollutants. In the case that there is no ventilation, the 
concentration of indoor pollutants can be 2 ~ 100 times higher than outdoors [4]. Indoor air 
quality strongly depends on ventilation performance. 
1.1.1 Perceived air quality (PAQ) 
The perceived air quality (PAQ) refers to occupants’ environmental perceptions (dry air, 
==PAGE:19==
 
15 
 
stale air, stuffy air, odor) and the body perceptions (symptoms) caused by the indoor air. 
Occupants perceive the air by both the olfactory sense situated in the nasal cavity and the 
chemical sense situated all over the mucous membranes in the nose and eyes [5]. 
Perceived air quality is usually expressed as the percentage of dissatisfied people, i.e. the 
number of persons who are predicted to perceive the air as being unacceptable just after 
entering a space [5]. Perceived air quality is highly affected by the sensory pollution load 
in the air. Ventilation can be applied to remove or dilute indoor sensory pollution and thus 
it can improve perceived air quality. The percentage of dissatisfied people with PAQ as a 
function of the ventilation rate per standard person (average sedentary adult office worker 
feeling thermally neutral) is shown in Fig. 1.1 [5]:

1.1.2 CO2 as an indicator of IAQ and ventilation 
Carbon dioxide (CO2) produced by people is commonly found indoors. CO2 concentration 
increases along with the concentrations of other pollutants emitted indoors in occupied 
buildings and can be used as an indicator of the needed outdoor air ventilation rate per 
person [9]. For example, in buildings where pollution loads are high, high CO2 
concentrations can also be generally measured, and high CO2 concentration shows an 
inadequate ventilation rate per person. Monitoring CO2 concentration is one of the 
methods to implement demand-controlled ventilation (DCV). High measured indoor CO2 
concentration defines requirements of a higher outdoor air supply rate in order to remove 
or dilute other pollutants; acceptable measured indoor CO2 concentration means an 
==PAGE:20==
 
16 
 
adequate outdoor air supply rate to keep a healthy indoor environment; low measured 
indoor CO2 concentration defines requirements for lowering outdoor air supply rate to 
avoid overventilation of the ventilation system and to reduce the energy consumption.

1.2.1 Ventilation effectiveness 
The CO2 concentration is not the same throughout a ventilated space. The non-uniformity 
of CO2 concentration distribution is influenced by many factors. 
 
Occupants, as the main source of CO2 in the ventilated room, their arrangement, quantity, 
and occupancy time affect the distribution of CO2 concentration [12]–[15]. Occupants are 
also one of the heat sources in rooms. Other common heat sources include laptops, 
radiators, and solar radiation. The heat sources generate buoyancy flows that interact with 
the supplied ventilation flow. The distribution of these heat sources affects the interaction 
and thus impacts the distribution of CO2 concentration [16]–[18]. Occupants’ behavior, 
such as opening the window or door, also has an impact on the CO2 concentration 
distribution [14], [16]. 
 
Ventilation strategies, including determination of ventilation airflow rate, supply air 
temperature and velocity, ventilation running period, and selection between natural, 
mechanical ventilation, and hybrid ventilation, have big influences on CO2 concentration 
==PAGE:21==
 
17 
 
distribution [14], [19], [20].  
 
The layout of the ventilation air supply and exhaust diffusers in the room as well as their 
number affects the distribution and removal of CO2 [15], [19], [22], [22].  
 
Different air distribution methods (e.g., mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation) also 
result in different indoor CO2 concentration distribution [18], [23].  
 
In addition to the factors mentioned above, the room size, internal and external 
environmental conditions also affect indoor CO2 concentration distribution [14], [24].  
 
Table 1.1 lists the max. percent variance in CO2 concentration at one sensing position 
when changing influential factors as reported in reviewed papers. The values of percent 
variance in CO2 concentration at all measuring positions were calculated based on the 
data in the text, tables, and diagrams in the referred papers. Then the percent variances 
with maximum absolute value were collected. Table 1.1 shows the different influences of 
varied factors on CO2 concentration at one position. For example, in the research 
conducted by Snaselova et al. [18], the increase in ventilation rate from 57 l/s to 68 l/s led 
up to a 9.8 % decrease in CO2 concentration at one measuring position. The cancellation 
of solar gain while keeping the same indoor temperature led up to a 12.8 % increase in 
CO2 concentration at one measuring position. The review of the results in the references 
listed in Table 1.1 shows that factors mentioned in previous paragraphs in this section have 
an effect on the CO2 concentration at one and the same position in the room, and some 
effects can be significant. The non-uniformity and transient CO2 distribution when not 
considered may cause substantial uncertainty in the design and control of room ventilation. 
Furthermore, the variations of the CO2 concentration measured at different points are not 
the same as a result of changes of one and the same factor.

What matters for the occupants in a room is the CO2 concentration in the breathing zone.

An indoor CO2 concentration below 1000 ppm has long been considered an indicator of 
acceptable indoor air quality because of its link to the perception of human body odor by 
building occupants [9]. Only BR 18 limits 1000 ppm of indoor CO2 concentration from a 
health point of view. BR18 [45] stipulates that indoor CO2 concentration shall not exceed 
1000 ppm at design conditions in day-care centres and teaching rooms if ventilation 
systems with demand-controlled ventilation are used. In the calculation of the required 
volumes of outdoor air, outdoor CO2 concentration can usually be assumed at 400 ppm. If 
the outdoor air concentration is higher than 400 ppm, correspondingly higher indoor CO2 
concentration can be accepted. 
 
ASTM D6245-18 [34] refers that at least 80 % of unadapted persons (visitors) will find the 
level of body odor acceptable if the difference between the indoor and outdoor CO2 
concentration is less than about 650 ppm. The 650 ppm concentration difference, 
combined with an outdoor CO2 concentration of 350 ppm, is the basis of the commonly-
referenced guideline value for CO2 of 1000 ppm.  
 
CEN CR 1752 [5] classifies PAQ into three categories, in each category design value of 
indoor CO2 concentration above outdoors is recommended. In three categories, indoor 
CO2 concentration is recommended at 460 ppm, 660 ppm, and 1190 ppm above outdoors, 
and the corresponding predicted percentages of dissatisfied for PAQ are 15 %, 20 %, and 
30 %, respectively. Outdoor CO2 concentration is typically around 350 ppm. 
 
Compared with CEN CR 1752 [5], ISO TR 17772 [10] and EN 16798 [11] have one more 
category with 40 % predicted percentage of dissatisfied. In two standards, indoor CO2 
concentration is recommended the same in four categories, and at 550 ppm, 800 ppm, 
1350 ppm, and 1350 ppm above outdoors. The default outside average concentration is 
assumed 440 ppm (350 ppm to 500 ppm). 
 
EN 13779 [27] classifies indoor air quality into four categories: High, medium, moderate, 
and low indoor air quality. The default values of indoor CO2 concentration above outdoors 
in the four categories are 350 ppm, 500 ppm, 800 ppm, and 1200 ppm. 
 
The latest ASHRAE 62.1-2019 [26] does not provide a limit value for indoor CO2. Because 
CO2 was not considered a pollutant but a marker of body odor perception, humans emit 
both CO2 and bio-effluents at rates related to their metabolism [9].  
1.2.4 Outdoor CO2 concentration 
Outdoor CO2 concentration varied daily, seasonally, and annually [47]–[51]. Daily 
variations are generally small [52]. Seasonal variations are attributable to cycles of 
biomass and photosynthetic activity of plants, with CO2 being higher when plants are less 
active [53]. Up until the early twentieth century, global average CO2 concentrations were 
less than 300 ppm, first exceeding 300 ppm in 1912 [54]. For the next half-century, the 
average outdoor CO2 concentration grew slowly, reaching 317 ppm in 1960 as measured 
==PAGE:29==
 
25 
 
at the Mauna Loa observatory in Hawaii. Since that time, atmospheric CO2 concentrations 
have risen more rapidly, exceeding 400 ppm in 2013 and reaching 420 ppm in 2021 [9]. 
The annual growth rate has increased from less than 1 ppm per year in 1959 to roughly 
2.5 ppm per year [55]. According to the 2014 IPCC report, observed atmospheric CO2 
levels increased at a rate of 1.9 ~ 2.1 ppm per year [49]. More significant increase can be 
found in densely populated urban areas [50].  
 
Urban areas may experience much larger excursions of CO2 above the global average 
due to lack of vegetation and the effects of internal combustion engine vehicles, as well as 
large vertical variations [48], [56]. Generally, most residential areas in the city are situated 
close to heavy traffic roads, and therefore both outdoor and indoor air quality were largely 
affected by traffic emissions [48], [49], [57], [58]. Transient local concentrations may be 
hundreds of ppm above average in some locations, approaching or passing 600 ppm [59]. 
Local concentrations can also be below the average depending on the season, time of day

1.3 Demand-controlled ventilation based on CO2 
measurement 
CO2 measurement can be used for setpoint control, e.g. adjusting the ventilation rate to 
control the measured CO2 concentration at an acceptable level. CO2 measurement can 
also be used for occupancy level estimation. The occupancy level is widely used for 
regulating ventilation rate accordingly.  
1.3.1 CO2 concentration spatial distribution 
Indoor CO2 monitoring is one of the means of implementing demand-controlled ventilation 
(DCV). The current studies show that the spatial distribution of the CO2 concentration can 
vary substantially in a room at different locations as stated in Section 1.2.1. CO2 sensing 
at different positions may result in different ventilation rates when using CO2-based DCV.  
 
Mahyuddin et al. [14], [19] studied CO2 distribution in a complex indoor environment of a 
classroom. The studies showed that the horizontal and vertical differences in CO2 
concentration were high and having only one sensor to represent the CO2 concentration 
in a room could lead to inaccurate estimations of the average CO2 concentration. Mui et 
al. [61] measured the CO2 concentration at 12 different locations in an office room. The 
largest deviation from the average CO2 concentrations measured at the 12 locations 
during the sampling period was 24.8 %. Ruud et al. [62] conducted a test in the conference 
room. The results showed that the CO2 measured at the wall-mounted CO2 sensors was 
nearly identical to the CO2 concentrations measured at the exhaust. Fisk et al. [63] 
evaluated the spatial variability of CO2 concentrations within six meeting rooms in a 
commercial building. The results suggested that the measurement at return-air grilles may 
be preferred to the measurement at wall-mounted positions because the CO2 
concentrations at return grilles were more stable. A series of experiments in a climate 
chamber were performed by Snaselova et al. [18], [23], Melikov et al. [12], [28], and 
Bivolarova et al. [64]. Results showed that indoor CO2 concentration can vary substantially 
in a room with different air distributions and inhaled CO2 concentration was higher than 
that at exhaust except for the displacement ventilation. A test conducted by Pantelic et al. 
[65] showed that the CO2 concentration in the inhalation zone was around 200 ~ 500 ppm 
higher than the background CO2 concentration. Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) 
simulations were conducted by Kabrein et al. [66] and Pei et al. [67], [68] to study the 
spatial distribution of CO2 in the room. The research conducted by Kabrein demonstrated 
the maximum CO2 concentration at low level and around the human body. In Pei’s studies, 
the horizontal and vertical CO2 distributions were fairly uniform for mixed ventilation. While 
for the displacement ventilation, the CO2 stratification was obvious with the high 
occupancy scenario and the CO2 concentration increased with the increasing height. Wu 
et al. [24] studied CO2 concentration profiles in different mixing ventilation system 
==PAGE:32==
 
28 
 
strategies with a chilled ceiling cooling system. It was found that CO2 concentration varied 
more than one hundred ppm with the height under different ventilation and air conditioning 
strategies. Deng et al. [15] studied CO2 concentration spatial distribution in the breathing 
microenvironment when using displacement ventilation under stable and unstable 
conditions. Results revealed that the vertical height and the horizontal distance to the 
mouth of occupants of the CO2 sensor might lead to the difference in CO2 concentration. 
 
Table 1.6 lists the maximum difference in CO2 concentration between different measuring

diagrams in the referred papers. Then the maximum difference values were collected.

concentration measured in the exhaust air and at the walls can be substantially different

1.3.2 Accuracy of CO2 measurement 
CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation changes the supply airflow rate according to the 
CO2 concentration measured by CO2 sensor. The true CO2 concentration at the location 
of CO2 sensor may not be the same as the reading of CO2 concentration measured by 
CO2 sensor because of measurement error. E.g., if the accuracy of CO2 sensor was ± (30 
ppm + 2 % of reading). Then the measured CO2 concentration Cm may be (30 + 2 % Cm) 
above or below true CO2 concentration Ct. Ct is in the range of [Cm - (30 + 2 % Cm)] ~ [Cm 
+ (30 + 2 % Cm)] ppm, i.e. (98 % Cm - 30) ~ (102 % Cm + 30) ppm.

using both transient and steady-state CO2 mass balance equation to improve ventilation 
controls. It was found that the transient equation method was with approximately three-
minute time delay and more precise than the steady-state equation. Calì et al. [77] used 
similar transient CO2 mass balance equation to estimate occupancy in two office buildings 
and one residential building. The predicted occupant number was imprecise with an 
average accuracy of 69 % and the highest accuracy of 80.6 %. The study suggested that 
the estimation was highly influenced by airflow rate, infiltration, and outdoor CO2 
concentration.  
1.3.4.2 Occupancy level estimation based on data-driven approaches 
The data-driven approaches were widely studied to estimate room occupancy recently. 
Rahman et al. [78] explored a Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm to recognize 
the number and distribution of occupants in a multi-room office building based on indoor 
CO2 concentrations. It was concluded that the prior probabilities of input parameters such 
as the supply airflow rate, activity level of occupants, and outdoor CO2 concentration all 
had impacts on the estimation accuracy. Taking account of the significant uncertainties of 
some influential factors, Wolf et al. [79] proposed a stochastic differential method to 
estimate room occupancy. This method added a noise term to the mass balance equation 
to address the uncertainties of the infiltration airflow rate, inter-zonal ventilation airflow rate, 
and the sensor errors. Taheri et al. [80] estimated the occupancy number based on the 
predicted CO2 concentration by the Multilayer Perceptron algorithm. Arief-Ang et al. [81] 
proposed several decomposition-based algorithms with a zero pattern adjustment to 
estimate the occupancy number using a CO2 sensor. The testing results showed that the 
proposed method had a high average accuracy level of approximately 90 % for the low 
occupancy case and a modest average accuracy level of approximately 50 % for the high 
occupancy case. Alam et al. [82] constructed a dynamic neural network model for the 
occupancy estimation. The estimation accuracy was evaluated with the normalized root 
mean square error, and the variation was between 4 % and 23 %. Szczurek et al. [83] 
predicted the occupant count using a k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) algorithm from 60-
minute long segment datasets of CO2 concentration. The results showed that the occupant 
number could be successfully determined with an accuracy level of 98 % and a mean error 
of 0.5 persons. 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
34 
 
Other than the CO2 sensor, various sensors have been used to determine occupancy 
count. Direct-sensing methods rely on the use of passive infrared sensors [84], radio-
frequency identification tags [85], and video cameras [74], [86], [87]. Meyn et al. [74] 
developed novel sensor fusion algorithms to further improve the accuracy of occupancy 
estimation based on the data logged by the passive infrared (PIR) sensors and the video 
cameras, along with the CO2 sensors. The results showed that the average accuracy of 
occupant counting improved from 30 % to 79 % with this algorithm. KwangHoon et al. [88] 
also reported that the complementary use of motion detection and CO2 measurements 
with a smart-sensing network could significantly enhance the occupancy estimation 
accuracy from 53 % to 98 % via the new fusion approach.

2. Justification 
Ventilation effectiveness is important for ventilation airflow rate calculation based on the 
CO2 mass balance. However, the standard-recommended values only considered few 
influential factors, such as mixing and displacement air distribution methods, supply air 
temperature, supply air velocity, and the layout of the ventilation system[5], [26], [27]. Other 
influential factors, such as occupant-related factors (their behavior, CO2 generation rate, 
etc.), heat source distribution, ventilation airflow rate, and new air distribution methods, are 
not taken into account. This may result in a discrepancy between recommended values 
and practical values. The CO2 generation rate calculated by the empirical equation or 
measured value proposed in recent research differs from that of ventilation standards. 
Measured outdoor CO2 concentration differs in different locations at different time, and the 
average measured outdoor CO2 concentration is different from the recommended value in 
ventilation standards. Required ventilation rate calculated based on measured ventilation 
effectiveness, measured CO2 generation rate, and measured outdoor CO2 concentration 
may differ from that calculated based on the values in ventilation standards. The impact of 
ventilation effectiveness, CO2 generation rate, and outdoor CO2 concentration on practical 
ventilation rate requirements need to be studied. 
 
CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation controls supply airflow rate based on the 
measured CO2 concentration to keep measured CO2 concentration at an acceptable level. 
CO2 concentrations vary in different locations in a room. The position of CO2 senor 
influences the supply airflow rate. Moreover, the accuracy of CO2 measurement and 
outdoor CO2 concentration both have an impact on ventilation rate when implementing 
CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation. The impact of the position and accuracy of the 
CO2 sensor and outdoor CO2 concentration on CO2-based DCV needs to be investigated. 
 
CO2 is widely used as an indicator of PAQ and ventilation rate to dilute or remove pollution 
in rooms. However, for the virus in the air (which does not necessarily scale with CO2), 
respiratory activity, and type of pathogen, indoor CO2 concentrations cannot be used to 
assess the exposure to them [9]. Methods for control of indoor pollution exposure of room 
occupants are needed. As described in Section1.4, there are few related studies, and they 
most appeared in recent years. The method for controlling pollution exposure of occupants 
is worthy to be studied.

4.1.1.5 CO2 concentration measurement 
CO2 concentrations were measured by three sets of Multipoint Sampler and Doser 
INNOVA 1303 [96] and Photoacoustic Gas-Monitor INNOVA 1412i [97] and INNOVA 1312i 
[98]. Since the analyzing time for one sampling is longer than the breathing cycle, the tube

the manikin was led to the jar with a tube. A small fan inside the jar ensured a proper mixing 
inside. The sampling tube, used for measuring the CO2 concentration in the inhaled air, 
was also placed into this jar. Vaisala Transmitters [99] were used to measure CO2 
concentration on the walls. Measurement range of the Vaisala transmitter is 0…5000 ppm 
with the accuracy of ± (30 ppm + 2 % of reading).

The total uncertainty of CO2 measurements comprises random and systematic uncertainty 
of measurements. The random uncertainty is caused by random errors, such as statistical 
fluctuations in measured data. The random uncertainty can be reduced by repeated

corresponding to the range of 14.5 ppm to 186 ppm. Details about the total uncertainty of

6.3 Proposed methods for CO2 exposure control for 
room occupants 
CO2 was considered a marker of body odor perception since humans emit both CO2 and 
bio-effluents at rates related to their metabolism [9]. Acceptable indoor CO2 concentration 
can potentially correspond to acceptable perceived indoor air quality but not acceptable 
CO2 exposure. Ventilation standards CEN CR 1752 [5], ISO TR 1777-2 [10], and EN 16798 
[11] used mass balance equation (Eq.1) to control the pollution level in the breathing zone. 
Compared with workplace exposure standard EN 1540 [25], the breathing zone defined by 
ventilation standards ASHRAE 62.1-2019 [26] and EN 16798 [11] is wider, as mentioned 
in Section 1.2.1. For ventilation standards, defining a more concentrated breathing zone 
may help to control the pollution exposure for occupants. Because results show that 
ventilation effectiveness in the breathing zone defined by ventilation standards can vary 
significantly and what matters for occupants is the ventilation effectiveness in the inhaled 
air for evaluating pollution exposure. 
 
Figure 6.3 shows the ventilation effectiveness when mixing ventilation ceiling slots were 
used in different cases. The ventilation effectiveness in front of the occupants is lower than 
that in the area behind the occupants. It indicated that exhaled metabolic CO2 did not mix 
well with the room air in front of the occupants. The CO2 concentrations in front of the 
occupants were higher than the background CO2 concentration. Pantelic et al. [65] called 
this phenomenon the personal CO2 cloud and proposed that a small desktop fan could be 
used for dispersing the personal CO2 cloud, thus reducing the inhalation zone 
concentration to the background level. 
 
In the following, the referred cases are specified in Table 4.1. Differed from Case 14, Case 
15 changed the breathing mode from inhalation mouth / exhalation nose to inhalation nose 
/ exhalation mouth. The visualization of breathing mode of the breathing TM can be seen 
in Figure 6.2. The ventilation effectiveness in the center of the room decreased significantly 
at 1.2 m height (0.5 m above the table) but increased at 1.7 m height (1.0 m above the 
table). It might be caused by the reason that the jet of exhaled CO2 from mouth was

Displacement ventilation is an air distribution method that introduces cool air into a zone 
at low velocity, usually also at a low level. Buoyancy forces ensure that this supply air 
spreads near the floor level, allowing it to be carried up into the thermal plumes that are 
formed by heat sources, like occupants. Pollutants can be carried away from the mouth 
and nostrils by the rising fresh air. The performance of displacement ventilation depends 
on whether the free convective boundary layer is able to entrain the clean distributed over 
the floor air and thus to stratify the exhaled CO2 above the breathing zone. A suitable 
design could achieve high ventilation effectiveness in the inhaled air of the occupants. 
However, displacement ventilation cannot control CO2 concentration at a position far away 
from the heat sources. Figure 5.10 and 5.11 shows that when using displacement 
ventilation in CO2-based DCV, different CO2 sensor positions could result in a big 
discrepancy in ventilation supply airflow rates. 
 
When using a box diffuser, the ventilation effectiveness in the center of the room at 1.7 m 
height (0.5 m below the box diffuser) reached 1.85 and 1.76 in Cases 8 and 9, respectively. 
However, the ventilation effectiveness in the inhaled air of manikin was lower than 1 when 
using the box diffuser. In this research, the box diffuser did not show a significant 
improvement in CO2 exposure compared to mixing ventilation whose ideal highest 
ventilation effectiveness is 1. However, different types of box diffusers have shown helpful 
to improve the exposure to pollution exhaled by others in the room [102], [103]. 
 
The textile duct has several advantages compared to a galvanized steel air duct: low 
airspeed, lightweight, easy installation, and disassembly, corrosion resistance, easy 
transportation, reduced noise during operation, and the ability to wash and disinfect [104]. 
In this research, a significant improvement in CO2 exposure was not observed in the textile 
duct case with the inhaled ventilation effectiveness at 0.8.

6.4 Factors that influence the ventilation rate control 
When canceling chilled ceiling, increasing mixing ventilation supply airflow rate, and putting 
the CO2 sensor on the north wall at the height of 1.8 m in the simulated 8-occupant meeting 
room in the research of Snaselova [89] and Jensen [90], the ventilation rate will decrease 
by 47.65 % to keep the reading of measured CO2 concentration on the north wall at the 
height of 1.8 m at 1000 ppm. When using displacement ventilation, putting CO2 sensor on 
the north wall at the height of 1.8 m, the ventilation rate will increase by 54.22 % to lower 
the reading of measured CO2 concentration on the north wall at the height of 1.8 m to 1000 
ppm. Influenced by the position and accuracy of CO2 sensor, outdoor CO2 concentration, 
and the factors that have an impact on air distribution in the room, the designed base 
ventilation rate needs increase at most by 54.22 % or decrease at most by 47.65 % to keep 
the reading of measured CO2 concentration at 1000 ppm. 
 
Putting CO2 sensor at different heights could result in at most 17.74 % difference in 
ventilation rate when implementing CO2-based demand-controlled ventilation. 
 
With the accuracy of ± (20 ppm + 3 % of reading), the ventilation rate will increase at most 
by 8.59 % to lower the reading of measured CO2 concentration to 1000 ppm, even though 
the true CO2 concentration at the sensor position has already been kept at 1000 ppm. 
When the accuracy of CO2 sensor was ±50 ppm, the ventilation rate will decrease at most 
by 8.33 % to maintain the reading of measured CO2 concentration at 1000 ppm. Because 
of the measurement error of CO2 sensor, the designed base ventilation rate needs 
increase at most by 8.59 % or decrease at most by 8.33 % to keep the reading of measured 
CO2 concentration at 1000 ppm. 
 
With the outdoor CO2 concentration increasing from 350 to 450 ppm, the designed base 
ventilation rate needs increase by 9.0 % to lower indoor CO2 concentration from 1300 ppm 
to 1000 ppm, and the designed base ventilation rate needs decrease by 5.0 % to control 
indoor CO2 concentration from 800 ppm to 1000 ppm 
 
Table 5.1 shows the most of the highest CO2 concentrations were measured on the south 
wall, which was close to the exhaust. Most of the lowest CO2 concentrations were 
measured on the north wall, which was close to the supply. The highest measured CO2 
concentration resulted in the highest increase in ventilation rate, and the lowest measured 
CO2 concentration led to the highest decrease in ventilation rate. To avoid the 
overventilation and underventilation in the room, the CO2 sensor was recommended to put 
on the wall far away from the exhaust and supply or at the exhaust. Figure 5.16 shows the 
ventilation rate change based on the 1.4 m CO2 concentration measurement was between 
that based on the 1.1m and 1.8 m CO2 concentration measurement in the case when 
significant change in ventilation rate was needed, thus the height of 1.4 m was 
recommended to be the CO2 sensor height to avoid overventilation and underventilation.

6.5 An idea for ventilation effectiveness estimation 
In practice, ventilation effectiveness was measured in the room to calculate designed 
ventilation rate. However, ventilation effectiveness changes with the change of heat source 
distribution, ventilation rate, occupants’ number and behaviour (opening the window or 
changing the position), and it is not a constant value. A method of estimating ventilation 
effectiveness is proposed.

sensors [84], radio-frequency identification tags [85], and video cameras [74], [86], [87], 
high-precise occupant counting is available. The data obtained by these direct counting 
methods and the occupant estimation based on CO2 can be used to estimate the actual 
ventilation effectiveness of the room.

7. Conclusions 
Air distribution methods, supply air temperature, supply air velocity, number and location 
of the supply and exhaust, occupants’ behavior, CO2 generation rate, heat source 
distribution, and ventilation airflow rate influence CO2 concentration distribution in rooms. 
 
Several hundred ppm difference in CO2 concentration can be found between the different 
locations in the room. 
 
The air distribution method is the most influential factor that influences the indoor CO2 
concentration distribution. 
 
Heat loads distribution is the most influential factor on the indoor CO2 concentration 
distribution when using mixing ventilation. 
 
The measured ventilation effectiveness fluctuated around the minimum value suggested 
in ventilation standards in different cases. The lower limit values in the range of ventilation 
effectiveness in ventilation standards are recommended to design ventilation rates based 
on metabolic CO2 in most cases. 
 
The high outdoor CO2 concentration around auto roads may cause 33 % underestimation 
of the designed ventilation rate calculated based on outdoor CO2 concentration at 400 
ppm. It is recommended to use the measured outdoor CO2 concentration to design the 
ventilation rate when the building is close to the auto road.  
 
The CO2 generation rate in the recent research of Zhai et al. was at most 31 % lower than 
that in ventilation standard BR 18. The CO2 generation rate calculated based on ASTM 
D6245-18 was close to the measured CO2 generation rate in recent research.  
 
The ventilation rate calculated based on the measured ventilation effectiveness of the room, 
CO2 generation rate of occupants, and outdoor CO2 concentration was 77 % ~ 95 % of 
the ventilation rate calculated as recommended in the present ventilation standards. It is 
recommended to refer ASTM D6245 to calculate the CO2 generation rate and thus reduce 
the designed ventilation rate based on metabolic CO2. 
 
Mixing ventilation is normally used to dilute indoor pollution but may not be a good air 
distribution method for the control of CO2 exposure.  
 
The change in the number of mixing ventilation diffusers and the location of the exhaust 
cannot improve the performance of controlling CO2 exposure significantly.  
 
Efficient CO2 exposure control can be achieved by the suitable design of displacement 
ventilation. A suitable distance between table edge and occupants is needed to allow rising 
==PAGE:84==
 
80 
 
fresh air to carry away pollutants from the mouth and nostrils of occupants. 
 
In the simulated 8-occupant meeting room, the position and the accuracy of the CO2 
sensor, and fluctuated outdoor CO2 concentration resulted in at most 54.22 % increase or 
decrease 47.65 % decrease in the ventilation rate when implementing CO2-based 
demand-controlled ventilation. The CO2 sensor is recommended to be put on the wall far 
away from the exhaust and supply air diffusers at the height of 1.4 m or at the exhaust. 
 
The calibration of the CO2 sensor may result in at most 8.59 % overventilation and 8.33 % 
underventilation.
