Abstract
This thesis deals with self-supervised audio models for audio classification. We examine the application
and effectiveness of self-supervised learning (SSL) techniques using transformer-based audio models
for audio classification tasks. Specifically, this thesis focuses on the transformer-based architecture, the
Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram Transformer (SSAST), covering both the pretraining and fine-tuning
processes.
The study investigates how the performance of downstream tasks is influenced by data volume and
model complexity, It also examines how well these large models are calibrated when fine-tuned. These
experiments are carried out using available models pretrained on large, diverse datasets.
Our work finds that more data is beneficial for all models. Along with that, while larger models achieve
the best performance, it comes at the cost of being less well-calibrated. The positive effect of more
data was also observed in our work around the pretraining aspect of these types of models. As part
of this thesis, we also explore whether applying self-supervised learning techniques enhances model
performance, when trained on domain-specific data, albeit at a lower data volume, and examine to
what extent more general pretrained models adapt to these niche domains.
We find that any amount of SSL pretraining leads to better performance on the downstream task.
However, the specific pretraining implemented in the SSAST model was observed to be unstable for
very small datasets. Our work also finds that while domain-specific pretraining is beneficial, it does not
offer any advantage when compared to models pretrained on larger and more general datasets.
In conclusion, our thesis dives into the realm of SSL models for audio classification and establishes some
of the considerations connected with choosing an appropriate approach to using such models.
Keywords: Self-Supervised Learning, Transformer, Audio Classification, SSAST, Fine-Tuning, Pre-
training, Model Calibration, Downstream Tasks, Data Volume, Model Complexity, Representation Learn-
ing, Mean Average Precision (mAP), Maximum Calibration Error (MCE), Domain-Specific Pretraining,
Class-Level Calibration

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The ability to precisely classify audio is a task of great significance, and one which humans perform
constantly without any conscious effort.
Telling the difference between a telephone ringing and a
doorbell ringing, or recognizing a voice, are both tasks we don’t consider very diﬀicult. The challenge
does arise for us when the sound is weak, polluted by noise or very similar to another sound [Ger03].
The potential of having computers capable of classifying audio is tremendous, and the applications
manyfold.
Ranging from speech recognition systems being used for transcription or interpretation
of spoken language, to the analysis of environmental sounds, which could be everything from the
detection of wildlife to adaptive noise handling in the encountered environment. The utility of audio
classification also finds uses in sectors such as security and healthcare. All areas where the impact of
well performing audio classification systems can make a difference, not only by being always on but
perhaps also potentially beating the trained ear of a human expert [Wis+20; Lam+10].
One way to approach this challenge is using deep learning. The field of deep learning, has witnessed
remarkable advancements over the last decades, profoundly impacting various domains, including com-
puter vision, natural language processing, and, notably, audio and sound processing. The application
of machine learning techniques to the audio domain has opened new frontiers in understanding, gener-
ating, and manipulating sound in ways that were previously unimaginable. In this thesis, we focus on
the understanding aspect, by looking into the task of audio classification.
The challenges of audio classification stem from the inherently multifaceted nature of sound, which is
characterized by its temporal aspect and spectral complexity. This, along with the diverse range of
sources for sound and the environments in which it is produced and perceived, make the audio domain
a very diverse and tough domain. To tackle such a problem, multiple approaches exist and have been
explored over the years. Often the models either use the raw waveform or a type of spectrogram, which
is fed into a convolution neural network, auto-encoder or transformer. With some combining different
input domains for a multi-modal architecture [Liu+22].
One of the approaches that, in recent years has seen renewed interest, is to consider spectrograms, as
seen in Figure 1.1 and treat audio as images. Thus, the latest methods from image classification, building
on the transformer architecture which originally were seen in the field of Natural Language Processing
(NLP) have made their way to audio. One of the driving forces for these advancements has been self-
supervised pretraining, enabling models to learn from large-scale unlabeled datasets. The model can
then be fine-tuned towards the specific task at hand. The usage of transformers in combination with
self-supervised learning looks promising, and such an approach will be the focus of our thesis. We will
examine this field by utilizing the transformer-based architecture, Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram
Transformer (SSAST) as introduced by [Gon+21].

1.1
Literature Review
Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) has been a significant area of focus in recent years, also in the field of
audio and speech processing. SSL aims to discover general representations from large-scale data without
requiring annotations, and thus enables the use of vast amounts of data available online, but bypassing
the expensive and time-consuming task of labelling. The success of SSL in the fields of computer vision
and Natural Language Processing (NLP) has prompted its recent adoption into the field of audio and
speech tasks.[Liu+22]
The paper SSAST: Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram Transformer by Yuan Gong, et al. presents a
novel approach to audio classification and speech tasks. The authors propose to pretrain the Audio
Spectrogram Transformer (AST) model as presented in AST: Audio Spectrogram Transformer [GCG21]
with joint discriminative and generative masked spectrogram patch modeling (MSPM) using unlabeled
audio from AudioSet and LibriSpeech [Gem+17; Pan+15]. The AST model, is a part of the shift to
transformer-based models in the audio classification realm. AST is based purely on self-attention, has
been shown to outperform deep learning models constructed with convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
on various audio classification benchmarks. However, pure Transformer models tend to require more
training data compared to CNNs, and the success of the AST relies on supervised pretraining that
requires a large amount of labeled data and a complex training pipeline.
This increase in performance, compared to CNN-based models, is in part fueled by the introduction of
the Vision Transformer (ViT) in the paper An Image is Worth 16×16 Words: Transformers for Image
Recognition at Scale [Dos+20] in the image domain. This allowed for eﬀicient large-scale training of
transformers for image tasks with minimal modifications of the transformer architecture. They found
that the large-scale training the transformer allows for trumps the inductive bias an CNN has when
it comes to image tasks. This in turn made it relatively straightforward to use transformers for audio
tasks, as spectrograms are just that, images.
Prior to the introduction of the ViT, both the papers Mockingjay: Unsupervised Speech Representation
Learning with Deep Bidirectional Transformer Encoders [Liu+20] and Audio ALBERT: A Lite BERT for
Self-supervised Learning of Audio Representation [Chi+21] propose models which are building upon the
BERT architecture [Dev+19], where Audio ALBERT is a compressed version of Mockingjay utilizing
Weight sharing to reduce the model size, but attain similar performance.
Mockingjay was a novel
approach in the audio domain and proposed the Masked Acoustic Model as in the pretraining task,
heavily inspired by the success of BERTs masked language model (MLM) pretraining. In contrast to
preceding models that adopted a ”left to right” approach, wherein the task involved conditioning on
previous frames to predict information about subsequent frames. Dependence on the BERT architecture,
however, has its limitations, due to the tokenization process, which is integral and natural in written
language and NLP, conflicting with the continuous nature of audio. Moreover, both of these models are
predominantly designed for speech-oriented tasks, as opposed to a broader spectrum of audio-related
tasks such as classification.
In the paper Masked Autoencoders that Listen [Hua+23a], the authors present an extension of the
image-based Masked Autoencoder (MAE) to the audio domain. This is achieved by adapting the MAE
to learn from audio spectrograms. The proposed model, Audio-MAE, follows the encoder-decoder trans-
former structure employed in the MAE. As such, it firstly encodes the audio spectrogram patches with
a high masking rate, of which only the not masked tokens are fed through the encoder layers. The
decoder then reorder and decodes the fed through encoded patches along with the masked patches, to
reconstruct the input spectrogram. The authors found it beneficial to incorporate local window atten-
tion in the decoder, as audio spectrograms by nature are highly correlated in local time and frequency
bands. Lastly, the encoder is fine-tuned with a lower masking rate on the target datasets. Empirically,
the Audio-MAE presented at the time, achieved a new state-of-the-art performance on six different
audio and speech classification tasks, outperforming other recent models that used external supervised
pretraining.

In the paper BEATS: Audio pretraining with Acoustic Tokenizers [Che+22] the authors propose BEATs,
an iterative audio pretraining framework to learn Bidirectional Encoder representation from Audio
Transformers. At the core of the model ViT is used, but in this specific framework, an acoustic tokenizer
is proposed by them, for learning a discrete representation of the audio. This acoustic tokenizer and
an audio SSL model are optimized iteratively. In the initial iteration, the authors employed random
projection as the acoustic tokenizer to train an audio SSL model using both masking and label prediction.
Then, they train an acoustic tokenizer for the next iteration by using a student-teacher approach to
distil the semantic knowledge from the pretrained or fine-tuned audio SSL model. The iteration is
repeated with the hope of mutual promotion of the acoustic tokenizer and audio SSL model.
The experimental results demonstrate that their acoustic tokenizers can generate discrete labels with
rich audio semantics, and their audio SSL models achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) results across various
audio classification benchmarks. Specifically, they set a new state-of-the-art mAP the on AudioSet-2M
for audio-only models without using any external data, and SOTA accuracy on ESC-50, and still hold
it for non-multi modal models.
1.2
Problem Definition
As stated previously, this thesis concentrates on exploring Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) techniques
and the applications and adaptability of these models for audio scene classification. Among the different
approaches to this challenge, the usage of the transformer architecture in combination with SSL has
shown state-of-the-art performance [Hua+23b]. However, the properties and use cases of these models
have not been fully explored in the audio domain, hence the main goal of this thesis is to contribute to
an understanding of these models, more precisely, we pose the following questions,
I Investigate how transformer-based SSL models behave when fine-tuned for the specific task of audio
scene classification, considering factors such as data volume, model complexity, model calibration
and precision on downstream tasks?
II To what degree, does the application of SSL techniques, enhance the performance of models in
domain-specific down-stream tasks?
Towards the goal of answering the first question, we elaborate by asking, to what degree does the amount
of data used in the fine-tuning impact the performance? We’re also interested in the adaptability of the
models, and as such pose the questions In what way does the complexity of transformer-based SSL mod-
els affect their adaptability and performance in audio scene classification? Regarding the calibration,
we hope to answer, how does model calibration vary depending on model size and the complexity of the
down stream task?
Expanding on the second main question, we hope to answer by examining the performance of the SSL
models, asking how does the performance of SSL models vary with the amounts of labeled data used
for fine-tuning?, and to explore the robustness we pose the question how well do SSL models adapt to
handle out of distribution audio data during fine-tuning, and what measures can be taken to improve
their performance?
Thus, our primary investigations will revolve around the fine-tuning phase of SSL models, and specifi-
cally, we have chosen to work with the SSAST architecture proposed by [Gon+21].

CHAPTER 2
Background & Theory
The objective of this chapter is to offer an overview, introduction, and description of the ideas and
concepts underlying the Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram Transformer (SSAST). As the name implies,
this architecture utilizes a transformer-based approach, a paradigm shift that has significantly impacted
various machine learning domains. We will begin by outlining the general principles of self-supervised
learning in Section 2.1. In Section 2.2 and Section 2.3, the fundamental aspects of transformers will be
detailed, along with the adaptations made in order to apply transformers to images. We then touch
upon spectrograms in Section 2.4, paving the way for Section 2.5, in which we will introduce the AST
along with the adaptations necessary for utilizing transformers for this specific audio task, ultimately
leading to the development of the SSAST, which is detailed in Section 2.6.
2.1
Self-Supervised Learning
Self-Supervised Learning (SSL), inspired by human cognition, is a machine learning technique gaining
a lot of attention recently due to its success in Natural Language Processing (NLP). SSL addresses two
major challenges in deep learning: the need for task-specific architectures and labeled data. It allows
an upstream model to generate data representations usable for multiple downstream tasks, simplifying
model design and reducing training time for these.
Furthermore, SSL learns from unlabeled data,
mitigating the cost of data annotation and can therefore utilize the vast amount of data available e.g.,
online [JT19; Liu+22].
As the SSL concentrates on learning a proper representation with a suﬀicient level of generalization and
distinctiveness from the data itself, the main effort becomes designing pretraining tasks which facilitate
learning such a representation, and yet remains relevant for downstream tasks. These tasks are also
called pretext tasks.
2.1.1
Foundational Pretext Tasks
First, a brief presentation of different categories of strategies for the pretext task used in earlier works
will be presented. These are simpler strategies that have somewhat fallen out of fashion, as they no
longer achieve state-of-the-art results by themselves any longer, as more advanced methods have been
introduced. However, they are still of interest and important as they are the foundation of these more
modern methods, and the intuition behind is still applicable.
These more classical methods have been highlighted by [Bal+23] with the offset in image processing,
but as a many models for the audio domain utilizes spectrograms, which are visual representations of
images, the advancement in the image domain is therefore very much relevant for audio tasks.
2.1.1.1
Information Restoration
A widely used technique, implemented in various ways, focused on reconstructing corrupted or incom-
plete data, a principle readily applicable to audio. The straightforward approach is to mask a portion
of the audio and let the model regenerate the missing information in the mask. This approach can
be employed on both the raw waveform [Pas+19] or on spectrograms [Liu+20; Bai+21]. In the image
domain, reconstruction tasks such as color restoration, that is an image is made grayscale, and the

pretext task.
2.1.1.2
Learning Spatial and Temporal Context
Leveraging the inherent temporal structure of audio, early SSL explored the relationship between se-
quential audio frames, such as shuffling frames and making the model predict the correct order. For
images, this has also been seen implemented as making the model predict the direction of gravity in a
rotated image or the relative position of two or more patches.
2.1.1.3
Grouping Similar Images Together
Borrowing from classical machine learning, self-supervised models have shown that they can learn rich
features by grouping semantically similar images together, by using e.g., k-means as a pretext task with
neural networks. This can be done in an approach called Deep clustering in which we alternate between
assigning labels to images by performing k-means clustering in a feature space, and updating the model
to use these assigned class labels [Car+19].
2.1.1.4
Multi-view Invariance
The principle of learning invariant features from different views or augmentations of data is to achieve a
model which handles small transformations to the input in a robust manner. To this end, many models
now use contrastive learning to achieve this, the idea of which is to encourage a model to represent two
augmented versions of an input similarly. This can also be extended to incorporate negative samples.
Before contrastive learning was widely adopted, different approaches such as creating pseudolabels, and
using these for training, were proposed [Miy+18].
2.1.2
Modern approaches to Pretext Tasks
We are going to concentrate on a specific branch of the modern SSL techniques called Deep Metric
Learning, as this is what best describes the models that will be examined in this work.
For completeness, we are quickly going to touch upon the two other major branches of modern SSL
frameworks, known as Self-Distilation and Canonical-Correlation Analysis.
2.1.2.1
Self-distillation techniques
Self-distillation techniques in general utilize a strategy where they input two distinct views of a sample
into two encoders and use a predictor to map one of the views to the other. To mitigate the risk of the
encoders collapsing into a constant prediction for all inputs, several strategies can be utilized. With,
a prevalent method to prevent this collapse being, to keep one of the encoder’s weights updated with
a moving average of the other encoder’s weights. Examples of self-distillation techniques in the image
domain are BYOL [Gri+20] and SimSIAM[CH20].
2.1.2.2
The Canonical Correlation Analysis
The Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) family of models in Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) builds
upon the multivariate statistical method of CCA [Hot92], which is used to infer information from cross-
covariance matrices. The aim of the SSL framework is to infer the relationship between two variables
by analyzing their cross-covariance matrices after they have been transformed. An example of this
framework in the image domain is Barlow Twins [Zbo+21], where two identical networks are used. A
batch of samples, each with different views, is passed through each network. The goal is for the cross-
correlation matrix between the output embeddings to be as close to the identity matrix as possible.

The desired effect is for the embedding vectors of different views to be similar, while minimizing the
redundancy between the components of these vectors.
2.1.2.3
Deep Metric Learning
Deep Metric Learning (DML) is a family of methods that aim to learn meaningful representations of
data by encouraging similarity between transformed versions of the same input. Its origins stem from
the concept of contrastive models, DML trains a network to predict whether two inputs belong to the
same class by comparing their embeddings, and seeks to make their embedding close if they do belong
to the same class, and far away if not. As data is without labels, the variants of the inputs are called
positive pairs or examples; the samples we wish to make dissimilar, often other samples, are called
negatives.
Over the years, different versions of a contrastive model have been proposed, in the early versions, the
Contrastive loss made use of only one positive and negative sample in the comparison. Given a data
point x in a batch of samples X, the general idea of contrastive loss is two to learn an encoder that

From this foundational idea of contrastive loss, a plethora of variants of buildings on top have been
proposed, some being:
Triplet Loss, This method involves an anchor, a positive sample (similar to the anchor), and a negative
sample (dissimilar to the anchor), aiming to minimize the distance between the anchor and the positive

tinguishing between positive pairs and their K negative counterparts, thereby improving the estimation
of mutual information in self-supervised learning contexts. More details about the InfoNCE loss are
described in Section 2.6.1.1.
The evolution of these methodologies has significantly contributed to the domain of Self-Supervised
Learning (SSL), with notable implementations like SimCLR utilizing variations of InfoNCE loss to
achieve breakthroughs in image representation learning. This progression from contrastive loss through
(N + 1)-tuple and InfoNCE losses highlights the continuous refinement of techniques for learning data
representations in an unsupervised manner.

uses cosine similarity instead of dot product for the similarity measure.). As such, SimCLR learns

2.2
Transformers
In 2017, the transformer structure was introduced in the paper Attention is All You Need [Vas+17].
Initially, introduced as a new approach to solve Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) problems, such as
machine translation or text completion. It achieves this by combatting some limitations of the previously
dominating recurrence-based models within natural language processing (NLP).
The transformer model eschews the conventional recurrence mechanism used in previous models such
as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), in favor of an architecture entirely based on attention mecha-
nisms. This shift has addressed some inherent limitations of RNNs, such as diﬀiculty with long-range
dependencies and parallelization, leading to significantly improving the eﬀiciency and performance of
sequence modelling tasks.
The Transformer architecture is essentially composed of four main components: a tokenizer, an embed-
dings layer, Transformer layers, and an output layer. The tokenizer is responsible for converting input
text into discrete tokens, serving as the basic processing units. Following tokenization, the embeddings
layer maps these tokens to high-dimensional vector representations, incorporating positional encodings
to preserve sequence order. The core of the architecture, the Transformer layers, consist of a series of
self-attention mechanisms and position-wise feed-forward networks, with normalization applied to make
training more stable. These layers iteratively process and refine the input representations, extracting
task-relevant information. Finally, the classification head, typically a linear layer followed by a softmax
function, transforms the Transformer’s final representations into a probability distribution over the
output domain, such as the vocabulary in language modeling tasks, allowing for tailoring the output to
the specific task at hand.
In [Vas+17] the Transformer architecture is presented as an encoder-decoder model, where the encoder
maps an input sequence x = (x1, . . . , xn) to a sequence of representations z = (z1, . . . , zn). Given the
context z, the decoder then generates an output sequence (y1, . . . , ym) in an autoregressive manner,
where each element is conditioned on the preceding elements in the sequence. However, encoder and
decoder only models also exist, and hence it is not required to use both.
The choice of whether
to use, an encoder, decoder or encoder-decoder architecture depends on the task. For instance, the
encoder-decoder structure is applied to “sequence to sequence” tasks in [Vas+17]. Staying within NLP,
BERT [Dev+19], can be mentioned as an example of an encoder-only model, devised for comprehending
language to perform tasks such as sentiment analysis. Conversely, the GPT models [RN18; PH22] aim
at generating text and thusly employ a decoder-only model.

2.2.4
Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT)
The original Transformer model incorporates masking of future tokens within its decoder to maintain
the autoregressive nature of language generation tasks, such as machine translation.
This masking
ensures that the prediction of a specific token is only dependent upon the previously generated tokens.
However, this approach does inherently limit the model’s context to a unidirectional perspective, which
might not be appropriate for tasks such as sentiment analysis for which a comprehensive understanding
of the input is crucial.
In contrast, BERT [Dev+19] revolutionizes this concept by introducing a bidirectional context under-
standing through its Masked Language Model (MLM) training scheme. This technique has subsequently
inspired numerous later models across different domains. Unlike the masking in the original Transformer,
BERT masks a portion of the input tokens at random within its encoder, forcing the model to predict
==PAGE:19==
2.3 Vision Transformer (ViT)
12
these masked tokens based on the context of all remaining tokens in the sequence. This method en-
ables BERT to better understand the nuanced and context-dependent meanings of words. A simple
example could be that the word “broke” has different meanings in the phrases “John broke the glass”
and “John broke the news”. This context-dependent interpretation of words is a key aspect of language
understanding.
Another contributing factor to BERT’s performance is its use of self-supervised pre-training. In BERT’s
pre-training scheme, 15% of the input tokens are masked; however, ”masking” can lead to one of
three outcomes for a selected token. In 80% of instances, the token is replaced with a [MASK] token,
in 10% of cases it is substituted with an incorrect word, and in the remaining 10%, the token is
left unchanged. This architecture enables the pretraining of models on extensive, unlabeled datasets,
significantly enhancing their ability to represent and understand the complexities of language. This
bidirectional contextualization and training approach has allowed BERT to achieve state-of-the-art
results in various NLP tasks, and has in turn also inspired the adoption of purely attention-based
models in the domain of computer vision, due to their success in the field of NLP.
2.3
Vision Transformer (ViT)
In computer vision, convolutional architectures have long been the standard for tasks ranging from
image classification to object detection. Yet, the introduction of the transformer architecture led to
the development of the Vision Transformer (ViT), which can be seen illustrated Figure 2.1 [Dos+20].
Drawing inspiration from the transformer’s success in NLP, as exemplified by BERT, ViT adapts a pure
attention-based model for image processing with minimal alterations, to leverage the existing eﬀicient
transformer implementations. Inspired by BERT, the ViT employs an encoder only architecture and
successfully adapts the transformer to images. This means treating an image as you would treat a word
token. The naive approach of applying self-attention to images would be to treat each pixel in the same
way as a word token, and in turn, get the attention from all pixels, to all other pixels. This would,
however, rapidly become infeasible due to the quadratic scaling of the number of elements needed per
attention. Even for small images, this would lead to memory requirements in the hundreds of GB for
a single image.
The Vision Transformer addresses this challenge by segmenting the image into 16 × 16 patches, with
each patch subsequently treated as a token.
The tokenization process involves flattening each 2D
patch, passing them through a linear projection, and augmenting them by adding the 1D learnable
positional embeddings to provide important spatial context to the model. This sequence of vectors is
then processed by a standard transformer encoder.
The ViT has shown promising results in various tasks, outperforming traditional convolution-based
architectures in some cases. However, it’s worth noting that the performance of ViT is heavily dependent
on the size of the training dataset. Where larger datasets tend to result in better performance [Dos+20].

2.4
Spectrograms
Spectrograms are a way to visually represent audio, more formally, it is a representation of the spec-
trum of frequencies in a signal as they vary with time. They are both useful for generating a visual
representation of audio, but also widely used in the field of signal processing, where they are an impor-
tant part of analyzing the frequency content of signals, especially in the context of audio and speech
processing, music analysis, etc. In the domain of machine learning they have proven useful, as they
enable the usage of existing architectures developed for the image domain to easily be adapted to the
audio domain, and with great success.
In the general case, the generation of a spectrogram from a time-domain signal, i.e., in our case,
audio, involves the following steps; First; The signal is divided into short, overlapping segments or
frames.
Each segment is windowed, meaning a function is applied smoothly tapers the beginning
and end of each segment towards zero, reducing the abruptness of the start and end point. Second;
A Fourier Transformation is applied to each windowed segment.
This converts a segment into the
frequency domain, yielding the magnitude and phase of the frequencies present in that segment. A
spectrogram can now be constructed by plotting the magnitudes of the frequencies obtained from
the Fourier Transformation on a two-dimensional grid, where the x-axis represents time, the y-axis
represents frequency, and the intensity at each point on the grid represents the magnitude of the
frequency component at that particular time and frequency. Controlling the number of frequency bins
in the y-axis, determines the resolution of the frequency range. This then yields a basic spectrogram.
There are of several ways and design choices to be made for this transformation, to augment, enhance
or alter the features in the spectrogram.
A common variation is log-spectrograms, which apply a
logarithmic scale to the magnitudes from the Fourier transform, which can provide better perceptual
relevance for certain applications. To the end of enhancing perceptual relevance, the Mel scale has been
introduced as an alternative frequency scale to Hertz, which is commonly used in the audio domain.
The motivation for employing the Mel scale, as opposed to a linear Hertz scale, stems from the human’s

2.6
Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram Transformer (SSAST)
As previously discussed, Transformer architectures require substantial data volumes to outperform
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), primarily due to their lower image-specific inductive biases
[Dos+20]. To circumvent the need for the network to learn foundational image characteristics, the
Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST) model employs pretrained weights from a Vision Transformer
(ViT) trained on the ImageNet dataset as its initial state [GCG21]. But annotating data remains costly,
and labeled data scarce, this also holds true for the audio domain. However, an abundance of unlabeled
audio data is readily accessible, for example, via platforms like YouTube.
Self-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a method to leverage this unlabeled data, particularly
benefitting data-intensive models like transformers. SSL’s various methodologies are discussed in Sec-
tion 2.1. The Self-Supervised Audio Spectrogram Transformer (SSAST) [Gon+21] seeks to leverage
SSL techniques to enhance the AST model, enabling it to learn from unlabeled audio data and, ideally,
develop a robust representation of audio content, before fine-tuning to task specific labeled datasets.
The architecture of the SSAST model is based on that of the AST, with the raw audio input undergoing a
similar transformation to generate the input sequence. The pretraining consists of a joint discriminative
and generative masked spectrogram patch modeling approach, which is presented in detail below.
Although the SSAST closely mirrors the original AST, certain modifications have been implemented to
allow for the self-supervised pretraining. The AST, being solely for classification, incorporates a [CLS]
token at the start of the Transformer encoder’s input sequence, using this specific token’s output as a
representation of the audio clip for the linear classifier. In contrast, the SSAST denotes the output of
each patch by the Transformer encoders as O[i]. In order to get an audio clip level representation for
the downstream tasks, mean pooling is applied across the patch representations {O}. This setup allows
for both downstream classification tasks and the use of each patch’s representation, O[i], as input for
the classification and reconstruction heads during the self-supervised pretraining phase. Both of these
heads for the pretraining are two-layer Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), transforming the Transformer
encoder output dimensions to those of a single patch.

2.6.1
Pretraining Tasks in SSAST
For the pretraining tasks, the SSAST implementation takes advantage of how the underlying ViT
(Section 2.3) divides an image into patches and uses them as inputs to the transformer encoder. Each
spectrogram is split into 16 × 16 non-overlapping patches during pretraining. This is in contrast to the
fully supervised setup in the AST [GCG21] and the fine-tuning stage, where the patches are split with
an overlap. It should be noted that the SSAST framework supports patches of arbitrary size, but the
de facto standard of 16 × 16 was used.
In practice, for the pre-training performed in the paper, fixed-length audio clips of approximately 10
seconds duration are utilized. These are converted into 128 × 1024 log-Mel spectrograms. For 16 × 16
patches, this gives a total of 512 patches per spectrogram, 8 in the frequency dimension and 64 in the
time dimension.
The novel approach of SSAST lies in utilizing these patches for the masking of the spectrogram, and
they employ what they call Masked Patch Sampling.
This allows for not only employing frame or
frequency level patching, which simple masks all the data in some timeframe or frequency band. This
should, in theory, allow for learning both the temporal and frequency structure of the data in a more
fine-grained way.
To avoid only learning the representation of tiny patches, with the immediate context being available,
the authors employ a cluster factor C, which controls how the patches are clustered. For a cluster factor
of, for example, C = 4, when a patch is chosen at random to be masked, adjacent patches will also be
masked such that a group of 4 × 4 patches is masked, equaling a mask of size 64 × 64 pixels. During
masking, it is checked whether any patch in the cluster has already been masked, to avoid counting a
masked patch twice and guarantee the specified number of total masked patches is reached. During the
pre-training, the SSAST paper sampled the value of C from C ∽unif(3, 5) for every spectrogram.

2.6.1.1
Discriminative Pretext Task
The discriminative pretext task formulates a classification problem using the masked patches, where
the classification head aims to correctly associate pairs (xi, ci). Here, xi represents the input patch,
and ci denotes the corresponding output for patch i from the classification head, both with a dimension
of 256. The objective is for the model to identify the correct patch among all patches from the same
spectrogram.
The loss used for this discriminative task is the InfoNCE loss [OLV19], designed to
estimate and maximize the mutual information between two variables. In this case, the input patch
and the classification head’s output by contrasting positive pairs (the matching input and output for a
given patch) against negative pairs (the output paired with the input of other patches).
The InfoNCE loss builds upon the notion of that it might not be optimal to model the density p(x|c),
particularly if the original data x contains more information than the high-level latent variables, such
as the class label.

distributed vector representation, aiming to preserve mutual information [Sha48] to the greatest extent.

Calibration
One aspect of the SSAST architecture we aim to investigate is how well calibrated the final models
are. Calibration fundamentally refers to the alignment between predicted probabilities and empirical
outcomes. A well-calibrated model ensures that, for all instances predicted with a specific probability,
the frequency of occurrence of those instances in reality corresponds to the predicted probability.
For instance, in a binary classification scenario, if a model predicts an event with a probability of
0.7, we would expect that event to occur approximately 70% of the time across a large set of similar
predictions [DF83]. Various metrics exist to quantify a model’s calibration, with Expected Calibration

Audioset
AudioSet-2M is a large-scale dataset for audio and sound analysis that was created by Google’s research
team. Each audio clip in the dataset is 10 seconds long. These clips cover a wide range of audio content,

audio tagging, along with being used for pretraining. Furthermore, it is rather unbalanced, with some
labels seeing over a million samples and others as few as 127. As such, a balanced training set has been

the criteria of providing at least 59 examples per class with the fewest number of total segments.

complete dataset, as such the clips have to be scraped directly from YouTube. This poses the challenge,
that it is both time-consuming and at the risk of being throttled by YouTube, and that as time goes,
some clips get removed or made private on the platform, meaning they are no longer available, and this
is a well-known challenge [Gon+24; YF21]. As a result, roughly 16% of the total samples are missing
in our AudioSet-20K dataset.

5.2
Estimating Dataset Size Implications for Fine-tuning
Understanding the performance of a machine learning model in relation to its training set size is crucial
for evaluating its scalability and generalization. These experiments were designed to investigate the
relationship between the model’s main performance metrics and the varying sizes of the training dataset.
As such, we in this section present the results of a learning curve experiment conducted on various
SSAST models.
To understand the impact of training set size on model performance, we systematically vary the amount
of data used for training, while keeping the validation and test sets constant. The model was trained on
increasingly large subsets of the available data, starting from a small fraction and gradually increasing
to encompass the entire dataset. These subsets intersect, meaning that any smaller training set is a
subset of any larger training set, i.e., Tr5% ⊂Tr10% ⊂. . . ⊂Trorg. The data in the subsets were sampled
uniformly at random from the original dataset, without special considerations for class balance.
The results can be seen in Figure 5.2(a). The final performance of the base model across datasets and
dataset percentages can also be found in Table 5.2. Similar tables for the other model sizes can be seen
in Appendix B.1. All models were trained for 25 epochs, with the same hyperparameters as outlined in
Table 4.3.
For all datasets, we observe that the performance is positively correlated with the size of the allocated
training set. The increase is not linear, specifically, the base models show a plateau in performance when
approximately 80% of the data is utilized, a trend consistent across the datasets. Across all models and
datasets, the largest relative gain in performance is realized when increasing up to 50% of the training
data.

5.6
SSL Pretraining — Domain-Specific Models
In this section, we examine the impact of SSL pretraining, specifically focusing on domain-specific SSL
pretraining, rather than the broader pretraining datasets used in the models by [Gon+21].
A key
challenge in utilizing domain-specific pretraining lies in the significantly smaller volume of available
training data, which, in our case, is two orders of magnitude smaller than that of the AudioSet-2M plus
LibriSpeech dataset. The objective is to determine to what degree the domain-specific details of the
training data can outweigh a larger training volume.
5.6.1
Pretraining and Fine-tuning on FSD-50K: Necessary Number of Epochs
Our experiments now focus on the FSD-50K dataset. Although it shares similarities with the AudioSet
dataset in terms of class structure, the data from FSD-50K originates from a different source and was
not part of the pretraining data for the models trained in [Gon+21].
The configuration for SSL pretraining is as follows: The network is initialized with weights from a
model pretrained on ImageNet [Den+09], consistent with the approach of the original study.
The
pretext task is the joint task as outlined in Section 2.6.1, targeting a spectrogram length of 512 and
masking 200 patches (N = 200), with a batch size of 192. The model’s performance is evaluated every
500 steps. Pretraining utilizes the complete training split of FSD-50K, with the validation split used for
assessing loss and accuracy. The test split remains unused during pretraining. The training extends over
100 epochs, with model states saved every 10 epochs. These checkpoints are subsequently fine-tuned
according to the methodology described in Section 4.2. Only the base model size was used for these
experiments.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this chapter, we will further dissect the found results further by contextualizing and by discussing the
implications. This will be done in four sections, firstly we in Section 6.1, discus the findings surrounding
the model sizes, data volume and required training time, in Section 6.2 we go through the considerations
concerning the alignment of the learned representations. Section 6.3, contains a discussion of how well
the models are calibrated. The chapter ultimately finishes with Section 6.4, in which the examined
aspects of pretraining will be further addressed and discussed.
6.1
On the Topic of Model Sizes, Training Time and Datasets
In this section, we discuss the results found in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 regarding the amount of fine-tuning
and data needed to achieve acceptable performance. These sections also serve as a reproduction of the
results shown in [Gon+21], by which we demonstrate that we can obtain results comparable to those
in the paper. The authors achieved a mAP of 31.0 on AudioSet and an accuracy of 88.8 on ESC-50
for a base model. In contrast, we obtained a mAP of 23.9 and 88.3, respectively, showing that our
setup performs comparably. Some of this performance difference can be attributed to more careful
hyperparameter tuning and the usage of an ensemble of models on their end, which we have chosen not
to do due to hardware limitations and because it was not our main focus to achieve the best possible
performance. One of the main factors, as shown by the very comparable performance on ESC-50, might
also be that we chose to allocate 20% of the training data to a validation set, while [Gon+21] chose
not to use a validation set for fine-tuning. In conclusion, while we do not achieve completely identical
results for all tasks, we have shown that the model’s setup works as intended, and we can proceed with
our experiments.
For the fine-tuning of the pretrained SSAST models in the standard setting, provided by [Gon+21],
we observe that when using all data, training for 10 epochs is suﬀicient for the small and base models
to achieve top performance, with about 15 being suﬀicient for the tiny model. After this, only minor
changes in performance occur.
If this finding is compared with the epoch curve for the non-SSL pretrained models, as shown in Fig-
ure 5.4(a), we observe that the models without SSL pretraining do not plateau until just before 20
epochs. This gives a clear indication that SSL pretraining provides the models with a useful representa-
tion, allowing them to adapt to the dataset much faster. They also perform considerably better when
using the SSAST pretrained models, with an improvement of more than 50% when measuring the mAP
on the FSD-50K test set.
Not surprisingly, when it comes to dataset size, we found that utilizing more data results in better
performing models. This holds across model sizes, only with a minor tendency for the tiny models not
to improve in performance as fast as the small and base model does. The majority of the performance
gain happens when increasing from 10% to around 50-60% of the data, for all datasets. At this point,
the increase in performance as the amount of data increases is noticeably slower. Furthermore, it is
evident that the dataset for FSD-50K is larger than the two other datasets, as its plateau in performance
occurs earlier than for the two other datasets. The datasets and problems are obviously of different
sizes, but these findings suggest that using SSL pretrained models, it is possible to achieve good results
on more complex problems already from 10, 000 samples and simple problems like ESC-50 using just
1, 000 training samples.

6.2
On the Topic of Weight Freezing and Classifier Complexity
Continuing the discussion on the fine-tuning aspect, we will in this section discuss the impact of freezing
the transformer weights, i.e., not allowing weight updates except in the classification head, when fine-
tuning. These results are included in Sections 5.1 and 5.2, mainly in Figures 5.1(b) and 5.2(b). In
addition to this, we will also discuss the results concerning the complexity of the classification head.
These results can be found in Section 5.3.
In Table 5.1, we noticed how dramatically the performance is impacted by freezing the network. This
significant decrease was observed across models and datasets. This indicates that the representations
obtained by the pretraining are not well aligned with the requirements of the classification tasks, and
this is true across model sizes. However, the pretraining still leads to an increased performance when
training with the weights not frozen, compared with training from scratch. This aspect will be further
discussed in Section 6.4. The results, shown in Table 5.3, indicate that the performance decrease can
be mitigated to a certain degree by bridging the representation gap by using a larger and more complex
classification head.
However, an increase in classification complexity does not necessarily lead to better performance; it
should be proportionate to the demands of the specific classification task. This seems most relevant
for the simpler problem posed by ESC-50, where the performance increases when moving from the
linear classification head to the non-linear head, but then decreases when expanding the non-linear
classification head further, by adding additional layers. As an example, we can focus on the FSD-50K
base, where the frozen model with a non-linear classification head with two hidden layers can reach
around 50% of the performance found in the standard configuration. This is at the advantage of only
taking around a third of the computation time to train this model, due to the transformer weights not
being updated.
6.3
On the Topic of Calibration
We will now turn our attention to the calibration aspect of the investigated models and discuss the
implications of the results presented in Section 5.5. We will first touch on the calibration over all the
classes before narrowing in on a couple of specific classes. In the following section, we will mostly focus
on FSD-50K, but the same observations have been made for the models when working on AudioSet.
These results, along with additional plots, can be found in Appendix D.
6.3.1
Calibration Overall
Starting with the overall calibration errors presented in Table 5.5, we observe the general trend of very
low ECE values but relatively high MCE. A low ECE value is somewhat expected when working with
large multi-class datasets and classification, such as audio classification on AudioSet and FSD-50K,
which have 527 and 200 classes respectively, due to the many classes that are correctly classified with
a low prediction score. The ECE is on par with values found for the ViT on a subset of ImageNet
[Min+21], and as such, the observed values seem reasonable. In the MCE, we notice higher values,
and from Figure 5.6(a), it can be seen that it is the neighborhoods of the 0.2 and 0.8 mean predicted
probability that contribute to the calibration error. This could very well also be due to the nature of
the datasets, with many classes and a large difference in class samples.
Nevertheless, it may be that the most interesting observation from Table 5.5, and exemplified for FSD-
50K in Figure 5.6(a), is the connection between the model size and calibration error. We notice across the
two datasets that the larger the model, the higher the different error metrics get. Hence, leading to the
choice of choosing between a model that either is more accurate but worse calibrated, or another model
which is less accurate but better calibrated. We also see this trade-off happening during the training
of the individual models, as we notice from Figure 5.5(b) how as the models improve in performance,
they suffer in how well they are calibrated. These observations raise an important takeaway, namely

that in a practical setting, one must quantify the importance of these properties, which could depend
on the practical cost associated with ignoring the model prediction if confidence is low versus the cost
associated with a prediction error.
6.3.2
Class-wise Calibration
Diving deeper into the calibration of these models, we will now touch upon the results presented in
Section 5.5.2, focusing on the calibration of the individual classes. From these results, we notice some
patterns. Notably, classes (61, 121), which have the highest sample sizes among the examined corner
point classes, show both good calibration and precision. However, class 156, which has the third most
samples, ends up with a bit worse performance metrics, but is much worse calibrated. In the case of the
class Truck, 180, we notice that for all model sizes the AP stays low for all three model sizes, but the
calibration error increases with each increase in model size. From the calibration curves, we see that
this calibration error could be connected to the fact that the smaller models are more ’conservative’, by
nature, and place most predictions in the bins below 0.4. The individual classes seem quite differently
calibrated and with no clear and definite pattern. However, the found observations give rise to questions
and aspects to further explore to continue the discussion on the class-wise calibration.
6.4
On the topic of SSL pretraining
In Section 5.6, we aimed to examine and quantify the impact of SSL pretraining on smaller, more domain-
specific problems, and how a more domain-specific pretrained model fares against a more generally
trained model that has the advantage of more data.
Focusing on the findings for the FSD-50K dataset, it was found that using pretraining on only the FSD-
50K dataset was not suﬀicient compared to utilizing the pretrained SSAST models, as performance
was almost 20% lower even for the best models. As such, there is no performance gain for pretraining
models only on a smaller domain-specific dataset.
Setting aside the performance difference compared to the SSAST models, it is interesting to compare the
results of fine-tuning these domain-specific models to the results of the models where no SSL pretraining
was used, as shown in Section 5.4. As shown in Table 5.7, more epochs of SSL pretraining yield better
performance after fine-tuning. This, in turn, also means that when comparing with the model using
no SSL pretraining but is just initialized with ImageNet weights, we see an improvement in mAP from
0.347 to 0.408 when pretraining on FSD-50K for 100 epochs, and a mAP of 0.421 when training for a
full 24 hours, as per Table 5.8. This does indicate that for a given dataset size, using SSL pretraining
on the same data as one would use for supervised training, applying an SSL step to learn a general
representation can significantly improve performance, even for relatively small data sizes, if no more
general pretrained models are available or viable to use.
In an effort to quantify how pretrained models can handle very different datasets, and possibly the
best effort of finding out-of-distribution datasets, which proved diﬀicult as the AudioSet is very broad
both due to its size and origin, we introduced the Watkins Marine Mammal Sound Dataset, WMMSD.
This is not a widespread dataset, and only contains different whale sounds, which is very sparse in even
AudioSet-2M, and only results for Few-Shot Audio Classification are compared on leaderboards like
PapersWithCode.1.
To align with the rest of our test methodology, we aimed to perform classical classification on a test
split consisting of 20% of the data. While pretraining on the data was successful, and the downstream
model, only trained on WMMSD data, was able to very accuracy predict the test set, the domain-
specific model did not outperform the generally pretrained SSAST models, as shown from the final
results in Figure 5.16(d). In fact, as seen by the zoomed plot in Figure 5.16(b) the SSAST models were,
in general, slightly faster to attain a higher accuracy, than the domain-specific models.

This does give an indication that domain-specific pretraining is not necessarily a better approach, even
for more narrow use-cases and closer to out-of-distribution data, as WMMSD is assumed to be, as the
models can adapt quickly to this domain.
As alluded to earlier, the impressive performance of the models on the WMMSD dataset raises questions
about whether these results are representative. Many of the recordings for a given species are samples
from, for instance, a single day or two different days, and it is rare for multiple species to be recorded
on the same day. This might lead to some bias in the recording setup or background noise that the
models can exploit to classify the species correctly. Therefore, a more thoughtful split of training and
test data might be worth considering before definitively claiming this level of precision for the models.
To explore the minimal viable size for a pretraining dataset and its associated impacts, we conducted
experiments as detailed in Section 5.6.2. This investigation focused exclusively on the FSD-50K dataset,
where SSL pretraining was applied to various percentages of the original training set. For the pretraining
phase, all modelss were trained for the same duration. It was observed that the models trained with
the smallest dataset sizes showed clear signs of overfitting, and these signs occurred very suddenly.
This suggests that pretraining may lack robustness for very small datasets. Such instability might also
explain the observed fluctuations in the final performance of the fine-tuned models at lower dataset
percentages, as illustrated in Figure 5.14(a).
Hence, for very small datasets, domain-specific pretraining may be inadvisable due to potential instabil-
ity. As such, a more generalized pretraining approach, for example, using pretrained models provided
by others, might be more beneficial, as it typically leads to better performance.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, we explored the use and effectiveness of self-supervised audio models for audio clas-
sification tasks, focusing on a transformer-based architecture.
Our initial findings pertained to the
fine-tuning of publicly available models pretrained on large, diverse datasets. The aim was to investi-
gate how data volume and model complexity impact performance on downstream tasks and how well
these large models calibrate when fine-tuned for these specific tasks.
We found that more data is beneficial for all models, both in the pretraining stage and for fine-tuning.
Furthermore, it was shown that using self-supervised learning (SSL) pretraining of any kind, it was pos-
sible to achieve higher performance metrics across datasets. Notably, by utilizing generally pretrained
models such as those from SSAST, we were able to fine-tune models with only 1/6 of the data size
required to achieve comparable performance in a fully supervised setup.
We discovered that the representation learned in the self-supervised pretraining did not align perfectly
with the downstream tasks. This was found by examining the fine-tuning of models, in which only
the classifier is updated. If it was possible to only train the classifier and still maintain performance,
it would have led to a drastic reduction in the compute power needed to fine-tune the models for
different downstream tasks. By adding a more complex classifier, we were able to better use the learned
representation for classification tasks, and regain some performance.
However, overall, all of these
models were unable to perform on par with those trained in an end-to-end manner.
The examination of model calibration strongly indicates that smaller models are consistently better
calibrated, revealing a trade-off between mean Average Precision (mAP) and maximum calibration
error (MCE) during training. This observation suggests a balance between accuracy and calibration
error, aligning with findings reported in [Min+21] for image-related models. Additionally, the class-
level calibration analysis reveals significant variations in how well different classes are calibrated, with
smaller models tending to exhibit more uncertainty in their predictions for specific classes.
During our investigation, we also aimed to understand how applying self-supervised learning techniques
could enhance model performance when trained solely on domain-specific data and to what extent more
broadly pretrained models adapt to more niche domains.
It was found that any amount of pretraining using self-supervised learning leads to better performance
on the downstream tasks. As such, for a given dataset size, it is beneficial to add an SSL pretraining
step, as this will lead to improved performance on the downstream task. However, it was observed that
the SSL pretraining implemented in the SSAST model was not stable for very small datasets, so for
these cases, the pretraining step should be approached with caution.
While domain-specific pretraining proved beneficial, it did not offer advantages over using models pre-
trained on larger, more general datasets, like those provided by the authors of [Gon+21]. In all instances,
these broadly pretrained models either outperformed or matched the performance of domain-specific
models, illustrating that a broader understanding is more critical than the domain-specific knowledge
models acquire during pretraining.

7.1 Future work
48
7.1
Future work
Throughout this thesis, several topics and areas for further exploration have emerged. We would like
to conclude by highlighting potential directions for future research.
Further investigating the scalability of models could reveal whether the observed trends continue for
even larger models. The Vision Transformer (ViT), for instance, has two larger variants that could be
of interest to examine. Additionally, exploring the dependence on ImageNet in more detail could prove
insightful. Since self-supervised learning does not depend on labels, we could harness significantly more
data from sources like YouTube to augment the current AudioSet dataset.
Extending on this, larger models would also provide further evidence for the trend in the calibration
errors, where we saw the trade-off between performance and calibration. Continuing in the calibration
aspect, we found some interesting hints in the class-wise calibration. However, a bit too scattered to
gain any strong insight, here it could be of interest diving further into how the different models handle
the classes, and what aspects lead to a class being better or worse calibrated, e.g., the number of
samples.
Concerning the quality or rather the alignment of the representation, one obvious extension to this
experiment would be to examine a partial freezing of the transformer weights to further understand the
possibility of utilizing the given representation, without compromising overall performance. Particularly
in scenarios where computational resources are limited, or frequent retraining is needed.
While exploring the self-supervised pretraining, considerations were made on investigating the model’s
out-of-distribution behavior. Alternative methods, such as variations in perturbations and noise, could
be interesting to further this research. Additionally, the specific audio data used for pretraining is also a
subject worth investigating, to determine if it’s the general nature of audio spectrograms or the specific
content that is of greatest importance for the pretraining. This could be explored by creating equally
sized datasets from sources like FSD-50K and WMMSD, which could then serve as pretraining data.
