Abstract
Performance and stability of a mechanical system are greatly related to the unde-
sirable vibration caused by environmental and operational forces. Even the smallest
vibrations can be ampliﬁed in a system near resonance and may limit the accuracy of
sensitive instruments or contribute to system failure. The desire for vibration suppres-
sion is thus of signiﬁcance in many engineering applications - especially if damping
can be realized without usage of heavy dampers containing oil or electromagnets.
This thesis provides an overall guideline to the theoretical, numerical and ex-
perimental work concerning vibration suppression achieved by resonant piezoelectric
damping from passive resistive-inductive (RL) shunts. Two experimental setups - a
cantilever beam and a two-wing beam experiment - have been designed, manufactured,
installed and tested for validation of a single-mode RL shunt calibration procedure
for obtaining optimal damping performance of plate or plate-like structures.
The piezoelectric transducers are connected in either a parallel or a series bimorph
conﬁguration. It can be concluded that the series bimorph conﬁguration yields four
times as large eﬀective blocked capacitance as the parallel, which implies that the
optimally tuned inductance values are quadrupled. Although inductance values above
1 H are shown to be physically attainable, this causes the series bimorph conﬁguration
to become infeasible.
Techniques for estimating the natural frequencies and damping ratios are achieved
through Experimental Modal Analyses in LabVIEW with subsequent signal processing
in MATLAB. The experimental results are compared with theoretical predictions using
an existing ﬁnite element model for RL-shunt calibration including residual mode
correction. The experimental results showed no clear signs of optimal tuning, however,
indications exists that the optimal inductance value is close to the predicted.

Preface
This thesis is submitted as a partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for awarding the Master’s
degree in Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It is also the
tentative beginnings of experimentally validating a newly proposed calibration method to provide
optimal passive damping using piezoelectric transducers.
It is important to emphasize that great eﬀort has been put into the design of the experimental
setups. Beginning completely from scratch - without any design constraints - it has not been a
trivial task to decide on the experimental setups, specimen dimensions, data acquisition systems,
measurement equipment, and actuation techniques. Also the design and manufacture of piezoelec-
tric transducers and supplemental RL shunt have been quite time consuming. A lot of this work
lies implicit and can only be appreciated by taking a look at the Appendix in Chapter VI.
In addition to the experimental work, the thesis has required theoretical insight into both the
mechanical as well as the electrical constitutive equations. Acquaintance with numerical ﬁnite
element modeling in MATLAB, machine drawings in SolidWorks, and experimental modal analysis
in LabVIEW are some other outcomes of this thesis.

1
Introduction
Piezoelectric materials have the ability to convert electrical energy into mechanical energy
and vice versa due to the so-called electromechanical coupling, where straining generates a
potential diﬀerence between the electrodes of the piezoelectric material. This eﬀect can be
used to dissipate vibration energy if the piezoelectric transducer is attached to the structure
where the deformation amplitude is signiﬁcant. As the structure vibrates, the transducer
is deformed and an alternating current is dissipated passively by connecting the transducer
electrodes to an electrical circuit (a shunt) containing a resistance. Although not as eﬀective
as their active counterpart, the passive shunt has no requirement for feedback control and
costly sensing instrumentation, and can be manufactured directly into the structure as e.g.
a composite material.
This project concerns the calibration of so-called resonant shunt containing both a
resistance and an inductance. The purpose of the inductance is to align the resonance of
the electrical system with the resonance of the mechanical vibrations of a host structure,
in order to provide optimal energy dissipation in the shunt resistance. These RL shunts
are passive, cheap, and inherently stable, thus considered as the next generation of smart
devices to be installed in aeronautics, ﬂight and vehicle industries.
1.1
Motivation
As the performance of passive shunts rely on precise calibration of the shunt resonance
frequency, a numerical model is necessary. By using a ﬁnite element model that combines
Kirchhoﬀplate elements for the host structure and a plane stress assumption for the
bonded piezoceramic patches, the optimal shunt tuning can be evaluated and compared
with experimental results.
Two validation cases - a cantilever beam experiment and a
two-wing beam experiment - are designed, constructed and used for preliminary testing.
The cantilever beam is equipped with a pair of, respectively, driving and passively shunted
piezoceramic transducers. The two-wing beam is actuated by an electromagnetic shaker
at center clamping to reduce eﬀects of asymmetric beam ﬁxation, while a pair of shunted
piezoelectric transducers is bonded to each of the two beam wings. An experimental modal

analysis is performed prior to the tuning in order to describe the electromechanical systems
by their dynamic properties in terms of natural frequency, damping ratio, and mode shapes.
These properties depend on the composition of the shunt circuit and must be found for
both the open-circuit (OC) and short-circuit (SC) conﬁgurations. Upon determining the
optimal tuning parameters, the project includes selection, purchase and installation of the
piezoelectric resistors and inductors. The piezoceramic transducers are purchased from
Noliac and manually glued to the beams.
This project is furthermore the ﬁrst attempt to experimentally demonstrate the accu-
racy of a newly proposed calibration procedure that includes a residual mode correction
that take into account the ﬂexibility and inertia eﬀects from non-resonant modes. The
project serves as a preliminary study in a series of future projects that will consider theory
and experimental validation of optimal calibration techniques, and the results are to be
used in the research project “Resonant piezoelectric shunt damping of structures” supported
by the Danish Research Council.
1.2
Objectives
Following goals are listed as the thesis objectives:
• Understand the characteristics and working principles of piezoelectric transducers
and the supplemental electronics.
• Derive the governing dynamic equations for a 3-1 polarized piezoelectric material
under plane stress and determine the optimal calibration formulae for the shunt
components.
• Design, manufacture and construct experiment(s) for validating the damping perfor-
mance of shunted piezoelectric transducers.
• Select, purchase and install the piezoelectric transducers, resistors and inductors.
• Perform experimental modal analyses of beams with piezoelectric transducers with
short- and open-circuit electrode conﬁgurations.
• Conduct frequency response analysis of vibrating structure with optimally tuned
shunt circuits and several mistuned cases.
• Compare performance of experiment with theoretical prediction using an existing
ﬁnite element model for RL-shunt calibration including residual mode correction.
• Write a thesis with high level quality content.
1.3
Originality of Present Work
A vast majority of the originality lies in the design and setup of the experimental work.
No clear guidelines have been given, except that the specimen must have a plate or plate-

PIEZOELECTRICITY
An introduction will ﬁrst be given to the working principle of piezoelectric transducers.
This includes typical applications, the chemical structure and electromechanical properties.
Consequently, the constitutive equations for a general 3-1 polarized piezoelectric material
with longitudinal vibration modes will be considered. This type of piezoelectric laminae
will be used for passive resonant damping of a host structure in Chapter III and IV.
2
Piezoelectric Material
Piezoelectricity - derived from the Greek word piezein - meaning to press or squeeze, is the
coupling between electrical charge and mechanical stress, and can be divided into direct
and inverse piezoelectric eﬀect. Back in 1880 the French physicists Pierre and Jacques
Curie discovered the direct piezoelectric eﬀect, as certain crystalline materials develop
an electrical potential on their surface when exposed to a mechanical stress. One year
later Gabriel Lippmann proofed that the opposite phenomenon - a mechanical response to
electrical stimulation - should also exist. This is known as the inverse piezoelectric eﬀect
and was experimentally shown by the Curie brothers a couple of years later (APC, 2017).
2.1
Applications
Piezoelectric materials have been adapted to an impressive range of applications due to this
dual transformation ability. Although the generated voltages, displacements, and forces
may be insigniﬁcant and require signal ampliﬁcation, aspect ratios up to 12:1 can be read-
ily achieved by stacking multiple transducers together (Physik Instumente, 2017). Due
to the versatility of the transducer, applications range from everyday equipment such as
spark igniters, loudspeakers, hard-drives, and quartz watches, to high-precision industrial
applications within aerospace, automotive, ultrasonic and optical communication systems.
Often piezoelectric actuators are valuable in applications where fast transition and a high
degree of precision are required. Regarding vibration suppression of structures in bending,
various piezoelectric types can be used. However, most common are bimorph conﬁgura-
tions, where a pair of thin piezoelectric patches are symmetrically bonded to the structure.

Crystal vs Ceramic Piezoelectric Material
2.2
Crystal vs Ceramic Piezoelectric Material
According to PCB (2017) it is important to distinguish between naturally occurring piezo-
electric materials, piezocrystals, and artiﬁcially manufactured piezoelectric materials, so-
called piezoceramics.
Naturally occurring piezocrystals, such as quartz and tourmaline, usually exhibit weak
piezoelectric behaviour but high voltage sensitivity. They have good mechanical proper-
ties, excellent long term stability and are non-pyroelectric, which means their polarization
is unaﬀected by temperature changes. However, the crystals must be cut along certain
crystallographic directions in order not to lose polarization, which limits the possibilities
of geometric shaping.
Ceramic material, on the other hand, can be manufactured in various shapes, sizes, and
compositions to meet the requirements of a speciﬁc purpose. These materials generally have
good mechanical properties, are chemically inert, and have relatively low manufacturing
cost, but are inherently pyroelectric and prone to creep. The vast majority of piezoelectric
ceramics are made of lead zirconate titanate (PZT), barium titanate (BaTIO3), or lead
titinate (PbTIO3).
2.3
Hard vs Soft Piezoceramics
During the manufacturing process of piezoceramics, small amounts of dopant (∼1%) is
added to the ceramic formulation in order to change the piezoelectric properties. This
gives two general classes of ceramics: hard doped and soft doped materials (Noliac, 2017).
Hard materials can withstand high level of electrical excitation and mechanical stress
but are diﬃcult to polarize. They respond with low strain and have much lower dielectric
loss than soft material, thus are well suited for dynamic applications. Soft materials are
often used for static or semi static applications, where large deﬂections are required. Their
piezoelectric constants are large, but suﬀers from high dielectric losses and high dissipation
factors during long lasting dynamic operation.

2.5
Polarization of Piezoceramic
In order to provoke the piezoelectric behavior a poling process is used. Applying a suﬃ-
ciently high DC current (∼500 V), when the material is close to the Curie temperature,
will align the domains in the ﬁeld direction and result in a remanent polarization of the ma-
terial. However, polarization is not permanent as the polarized ceramic has higher energy
and lower entropy than prior to polarization (Piezo Technologies, 2017). Random varia-
tions caused by mechanical stress, opposite electrical ﬁeld, heat, or noise will eventually
result in overall depolarization and a decline in the electromechanical coupling. However,
it is always possible to reset the polarization to its preferred alignment by performing a
new poling process.
Due to the anisotropic material property, a piezoelectric laminae can be polarized
along three principal directions and vibrate in multiple modal conﬁgurations. Longitudi-
nal, thickness, shear, radial, and ﬂexural vibration modes are just a few response modes
that depend on the applied electrical ﬁeld direction. Figure 2.2 shows the longitudinal
vibrations of a typical 3-1 polarized piezoelectric plate with polarization between top and
bottom surface electrodes. If a negative voltage is applied in the poling direction (along
the 3-axis), the thickness will decrease and transverse expansion occur in the 1,2-directions
due to the Poisson’s eﬀect. In the notation i −j, i generally describes the direction of the
applied electrical ﬁeld or mechanical strain, whereas j indicates the direction of the reactive
quantity. Notice that deformation typically occurs equally in the two longitudinal direc-
tions, and that the poling direction is conventionally considered to be in the 3-direction
for piezoceramics, but along the 1-axis for piezocrystals (Piezo Technologies, 2017).

Summary
Section 2 has given a brief introduction to the world of piezoelectric materials consisting of
application examples, material types, chemical composition, and polarization principles.
The governing relations for a general 3-1 polarized piezoelectric laminae with longitu-
dinal vibration modes have been presented in Section 3. This set of equations describe the
electromechanical coupling between the host and piezo dynamics. Form conversion from
d- to e-conﬁguration was done to achieve a strain-dependent expression in compliance with
a displacement-based ﬁnite element model. The conversion equations are important, since
the constitutive relations are not always given in the desired format.
The constitutive equations are ﬁnally plane-stress reduced by assuming the piezoelectric
laminae to be thin and a pure transverse electrical ﬁeld to exist between the upper and
lower polarized surface electrodes. The reduced equation describing in-plane piezoelectric
motion is given in (3.17).

Chapter III
ELECTROMECHANICAL
PIEZO-PLATE THEORY
The equations of motion for an electromechanical piezo-plate structure will be derived from
the Principle of Virtual Work. Finite Element (FE) formulations will be obtained for plate
or plate-like objects that can be discretized by Kirchhoﬀplate elements. Attempting to use
traditional rectangular 3D elements for plate modeling would prove to underestimate the
true strain energy density because of shear-locking from the assumption of constant strain
variation (Cook et al., 2001). This could be circumvented by a mesh reﬁnement in the
thickness direction, but would increase the computational eﬀort greatly. A better solution
is to implement a higher-order element, such as the Kirchhoﬀplate element, to capture
the non-linear strain variation by modeling both displacement and rotation of each node.
A FE-approach is adapted as the resonant shunt calibration method rely on the pre-
cise estimation of the OC and SC natural frequencies obtained by solving the respective
electromechanical eigenvalue problems.

Piezoelectric Patch Dynamics
The dynamics of a 3-1 piezoceramic laminae (patch) is now considered. Linear piezoelec-
tric theory couples linear elasticity to the charge equation of electrostatics through the
piezoelectric constants. According to IEEE (1987) it is reasonable to disregard the full
electromagnetic equations governing the dynamics of a piezoelectric material. The mate-
rial properties can instead be adequately approximated as electrostatic because the phase
velocities of the acoustic waves are several orders of magnitude less that the velocities of
the propagating electromagnetic waves. Due to coupling with the dynamic mechanical
equations however, the electric variables are not completely static, but should rather be
considered as quasi-static.

Assumptions
Several assumptions have been stated in order to derive the modal EOM of the electrome-
chanical structure:
• Host structure and piezoelectric patches are restricted to plate and plate-like ge-
ometries with uniform thickness signiﬁcantly smaller than the leading dimensions.
Hereby the displacement can be adequately described by 2D rectangular Kirchhoﬀ
plate element.
• Normal and shear deformations are unconstrained in the 3-direction, which justiﬁes
the plane stress simpliﬁcation.
• Only 3-1 polarized piezoceramic laminaes are considered. This implies that the elec-
tric ﬁeld is applied purely in the 3-direction and the element serves through longitu-
dinal strains as response to transverse strains via Poisson’s ratio.
• Phase velocities of the structural waves are several orders of magnitude less than the
velocities of the propagating electromagnetic waves, why patch material properties
can be regarded as electrostatic.
• 2D boundary loads from patch vibrations can be approximated as concentrated loads
at the discrete coupling points.
• The ﬁnite element nodes used to discretize the patches are coinciding with the re-
spective nodes of the host structure.
• Continuous top and bottom patch surfaces are equipotential, meaning that all elec-
trodes possess same electrical potential.

8.3
Piezoelectric Transducer Design
Soft doped 3-1 polarized piezoceramic laminates of the material NCE51 from Noliac A/S
are used for both actuating and sensing purposes. The rectangular elements are selected as
thin as possible, see Figure 8.1a, to meet the requirements of a plane stress assumption. The
material is especially suitable for low-power non-piezoresonant (2 −4 MHz) applications
requiring high coupling factors and/or high charge sensitivity. It appears from the data
sheet in Appendix B.4 that the material coupling factor can be considered as temperature
independent (±1%) within −10◦C and 80◦C. No signiﬁcant temperature change has been
observed during the short-duration measurements.
The piezoelectric properties, as provided in the data sheet in Appendix B.4, have a
standard tolerance of ±5%. These can be converted between d- and e-form by use of the
transformation equations given in Section 3.3. The free capacitance is however dependent
of the patch geometry and thus measured in the thickness direction from dynamic tests.
According to Charles Mangeot, Senior R&D Engineer at Noliac (personal communication,
November 28, 2017), this is because the thickness mode is directly related to the thickness
frequency constant. Measurements of longitudinal modes are more diﬃcult as these are
not pure modes, since there typically will be inﬂuence of both radial and spurious modes
for a square plate. Conversion to longitudinal 3-1 response mode is thus not trivial and
properties will be diﬀerent once attached to the host structure. Although it would be
easier to convert using a disc with a radial mode contribution, it is recommended to instead
measure the eﬀective blocked capacitance once attached to the structure.

8.3.2
Parallel Bimorph
By conﬁguring the bimorph patches in a parallel setup as seen in Figure 8.1b, the main
challenge is that the bottom electrodes must be accessed. This can be done in numerous
ways, e.g. by using conductive adhesive or patches exceeding the host width. As previously
mentioned it can be troublesome to use a conductive adhesive due to risk of short circuiting.
Although wider patches are possible they will be more fragile and prone to fracture. Some
patches instead have a so-called wrap-around-electrode, where the bottom electrode extends
all the way to a small island on the top surface, meaning that both polarities can be accessed
from the same side. If these particular piezos are not available, a simpler alternative is to
insert a thin copper (or other conductive) foil between the patch and the host. Hereby, the
host can be used as an electrode, which means that the patches obtain common ground.
8.3.3
Series Bimorph
The series bimorph conﬁguration is more easily achieved by bonding the patch pair with
opposite poling as illustrated in Figure 8.1c.
There are however several complications
associated with this conﬁguration.
In case the patches are used for driving purposes, there will be some safety concerns
related to a non-zero voltage oﬀset. This can be circumvented by using a fully diﬀerential
driver (power ampliﬁer) with a ﬂoating ground. If the equipment does not possess this
feature, the host must be ﬂoating as the piezos form a capacitive voltage divider. It may
be an issue for some electrical measurement devices if both actuators and sensors are
used on the same host, since the average voltage level will always be at V/2. This can
be circumvented by only driving one side of the patch pair, but would naturally result in
half the control authority. Otherwise, the host can be discharged by grounding to e.g. the
anti-vibration steel table. However, it is not trivial to solder on aluminum and would thus
require mechanical fasteners such as lug and screw. In this case it would be preferable to
mount the transducers in a parallel conﬁguration.
Finally, there is a possibility of having two drivers (without ﬂoating ground); one of
them drives one side with voltage V and the other drives the opposite side with −V .
According to Charles Mangeot, the piezoceramic material is capable of tolerating a certain
limit of reverse voltage.
For a 0.5 mm thick NCE51 patch, it is recommended not to
exceed a reverse voltage of 250 V over a single plate, which is well above the driving limit
(personal communication, October 24, 2017). If the drivers only allow positive voltages,
the oscillations can be oﬀset by a constant voltage, implying that the piezos will be in
a state of constant compression. This is however not an issue but just a constant oﬀset
applied to the elements.
For the present experimental setups, the used single driver (B&K Power Ampliﬁer Type
2718) has no ﬂoating ground. The driver has a maximum power output capability of 75
VA (volt-ampere), which eﬀectively implies that no more than ∼25 V can be achieved
without signal distortion. This is in the lower end of the required control authority for
non-resonant vibrations, but in return poses no severe safety issue.

Although it may appear as a simple task to setup a resonant RL shunt circuit for passive
damping, several complications and limitations arise when attempting to do so. In the
present work only linear single-mode shunt networks consisting of a resistor and an induc-
tor are considered. These can be conﬁgured in parallel or series as seen in Figure 6.3 on
page 25. The shunt network can work in resonance with a single structural mode by tuning
the components depending on the inherent piezoelectric capacitance an electromechanical
coupling. By combining several piezoelectric patches and associated shunt circuits, single-
mode damping can be applied to reduce several structural modes. However, the optimal
tuning formulas are not completely linearly extendable since every network introduce ad-
ditional dynamics that must be accounted for.
8.4.1
Inductor Design
One of the main problems, when dealing with single-mode resonant RL-shunts, is the
requirement of large inductance values in the low frequency range. Typical optimally tuned
inductance values are in the order of 1-1000 H. However, conventional inductors with a
performance above 1 H are physically unattainable and introduce other problems such as
low-current, low-voltage, as well as signiﬁcant magnetic non-linearity (Fleming, 2004). The
inductance component thus poses a stringent design constraint on the allowable frequency
range of the targeted mode shape.
Several methods have been proposed to overcome this issue, such as adding an addi-
tional capacitance across the patch electrodes or by using a circuit of operational ampliﬁers,
a so-called synthetic inductor (Fleming, 2003). However, these methods are not ideal as
they introduce additional resistance or are impractical to implement in reality.
Loussarn et al. (2017) concluded that by wiring a coil of conductive material around a
magnetic core, inductances above 1 H can be obtained without requiring oversized compo-
nents. In their study they considered inductors consisting of closed magnetic cores made of
high permeability materials, such as nanocrystalline ferrite toroids and ferrite RM cores.
Several thousands of winding turns may still be required for nanocrystalline materials,
although the permeability is considerably larger than that of the ferrite material. The
number of coil winding turns nL is exponentially related to the inductance by:

Having setup the two experiments, it is now desired to obtain the actual SC and OC natural
frequencies and damping ratios for an associated target mode. This can be done through
experimental modal analysis (EMA) using diﬀerent modal testing procedures implemented.
The EMA applications have been implemented in LabVIEW whereas post-processing is per-

9.1
Modal Testing Procedures
Several types of modal testing procedures are applicable such as chirp signal, impact testing
and resonance decay response (Mains, 2016; Thomsen, 2003). Whereas the chirp excites a
broad-banded range of frequencies, the impact and resonance decay methods are targeted
at speciﬁc modes.
A chirp is a sine signal in which the frequency increases (or decreases) with time. This
implies that the sine is swept through a broad-banded range of frequencies that can be set
accordingly. This is advantageous for obtaining both crude and accurate estimations of
the natural frequencies.

9.2
LabVIEW Applications
When performing an impact test, also known as a bump or hammer test, the structure
is either perturbed by a sudden impulse or released from a non-zero initial condition. This
exploits the transient response as a free decay curve associated with the fundamental mode
shape of the structure.
The resonance decay method is a small variation to the impact test. The system is
here driven to resonance with a sinusoidal input and the free decay curve is measured by
shutting oﬀthe excitation signal when steady-state conditions have been reached. This
method can thus be used to exploit the dynamic properties of any other mode, but for
accurate results it requires that damping is small and proportional, and that the modes
are well separated.

Extracting Modal Properties
Although measurements will always be obtained in the time domain, signal post-processing
for extracting modal properties can be performed in either time domain or frequency
domain. The choice of domain must reﬂect the purpose of the parameters in need. It
should be obvious that the natural frequencies only can be obtained in the frequency
domain. However, the structural damping ratio can be estimated both by the transient
response in time domain as well as by the steady state response in the frequency domain.

Frequency Domain Method
Another way to extract the damping ratio is based on variation of the phase angle in
close vicinity of resonance excitation. Outside resonance, the response of lightly dampened
structures is either in or out of phase with the source of excitation. At resonance, the
response will be theoretically unbounded, but limited to some ﬁnite value due to structural
damping. The phase angle transition slope is related to the internal damping and can thus
be used for parameter estimation in the frequency domain.
Half-Power Bandwidth Method
The half-power bandwidth approach is a frequency domain method that assumes the multi
d.o.f. system can be represented by single d.o.f. system close to resonance. This is a
reasonable assumption if the modal frequencies are separated by a suﬃcient frequency
margin, which means that the spectral peaks are distinct.

11.2
Convergence Study
It is important to conduct a convergence study of solutions obtained by ﬁnite element
models. This is because the model will always underestimate the true compliance of the
structure, hereby making the structure stiﬀer than it is in reality (Sigmund, 2016). Con-
vergence and model accuracy are known to increase with the number of elements used, but
so does the computational eﬀort. The problem thus rest with ﬁnding the lowest number
of elements that provides a reliable estimate of the SC and OC frequencies.
Figure 11.3 shows the result of a convergence study performed for the 2W-222-25-P
model. The SC frequency is plotted in Figure 11.3a as a function of the total number
of elements along the x (width) and y (length) direction, respectively. Considering the x
discretization, an accurate solution can be obtained for even coarse grid resolution. This is
expected since the lateral displacements are known to be identical across the beam width
for the considered two ﬁrst bending mode shapes without torsion.
Large discrepancies were found for a coarse discretization of 40 elements in the y-
direction.
However, by redistributing the grid for a reﬁnement over the patch region
appears to increase the accuracy greatly without the cost of additional computational
eﬀort. A reliable solution is expected for 58 elements.

conﬁgurations. It was concluded that the parallel bimorph conﬁguration introduce less
complications related to driving applications, however require access to the bottom patch
electrodes. Several considerations regarding the design of the supplemental shunt were
discussed in Section 8.4. The main point being that the inductor selection should not be
based solely on maximizing the inductance factor (8.2), but also consider elements such as
manufacturing, maximum energy storage, nonlinearity and leakage. Inductance values of
2 H were found be physically achievable using a core of ferrite material with a inductance
factor of AL = 28µ H.
Experimental Modal Analysis has been performed by implementation of two modal
testing applications in LabVIEW. Section 9 presented a chirp test for the two-wing beam
experiment as well as an application for determining the resonant decay response of the
cantilever beam. Signal post-processing techniques for estimating the natural frequencies
and damping ratios have been provided from which Frequency Response Functions can
be obtained. As expected, the experimental results show a distinct separation of the OC
natural frequency from the lower SC frequency.
A newly proposed RL shunt tuning procedure for optimal passive damping of a single
resonant mode was summarized in Section 10.1. The calibration method is based on the
eﬀective EMCC and thus takes account for device imperfections. This may prove bene-
ﬁcial since rather large deﬁciencies can emerge between idealized and actual piezoelectric
properties.
The experimentally obtained results are now to be compared with tuning values found
using a numerical FE-model implemented by Toftekær et al. (2017). The method is based
on a balanced modal calibration procedure that takes both ﬂexibility and inertia eﬀects
of non-resonant modes into account. The two-wing beam is here modeled as a cantilever
beam by assuming symmetry of the beam ends. Model reliability has been veriﬁed by
convergence tests.

13
Conclusion
This thesis provides an overall introduction and guideline to the theoretical, numerical
and experimental work concerning optimal passive damping using resonant piezoelectric
transducers connected to a RL shunt circuit. It is intended for eﬀortless insight into the
world of piezoelectricity and electromechanical structure dynamics, and should be seen as
reference work for future students that may not be familiar with machine drawings, signal
processing methods or electrical circuit conﬁgurations.
The governing dynamic equations for a 3-1 polarized piezoelectric material under plane
stress was derived in Chapter II. The electromechanical equations of motion for a piezo-
plate structure were then thoroughly derived by a ﬁnite element approach in Chapter III.
Model reduction was achieved by considering parallel and bimorph patch conﬁguration.
It was concluded that the series bimorph conﬁguration yields four times as large eﬀective
blocked capacitance eCT
p as the parallel bimorph setup, but with only twice the eﬀective
plane-stress reduced 3-1 piezoelectric charge coeﬃcient ˜e31. This results in four times as
large inductance values for the series connection, making them impractical to implement.
In Chapter IV two experimental setups have been designed, manufactured and con-
structed for validating the damping performance of shunted piezoelectric transducers. The
experimental work includes selection, purchase and installation of the piezoelectric trans-
ducers, resistors and inductors. It can be concluded that inductance values above 1 H can
be physically obtained although this is not a common belief.
Techniques for estimating the natural frequencies and damping ratios are achieved
through Experimental Modal Analyses in LabVIEW with subsequent signal processing in
MATLAB. The experimental results are compared with theoretical predictions using an ex-
isting ﬁnite element model for RL-shunt calibration including residual mode correction
in Chapter V. Frequency Response Functions for an optimally tuned shunt circuit and
a mistuned case. No clear signs of optimal tuning were unfortunately found, however,
indications exists that the optimal inductance value is close to the predicted.
13.1
Future Work
The present work clears the way for more exhaustive experimental validation of passive
resonant damping. Following sections are suggestions to future work topics.
Model Inaccuracy
Some of the future work lies obviously with the misalignment of the numerical predictions
and experimental measurements of the SC and OC frequencies.
It is reasonable to believe that the poor electromechanical coupling is related to the
bonding quality. It is arduous work to manually apply a uniform epoxy layer, especially for
a square plate. To increase the EMCC, it is suggested to try patches manufactured with
a wrap-around-electrode, where the bottom electrode extends all the way to a small island
on the top surface, meaning that both polarities can be accessed from the same side.
For the current setups, the optimal inductance value could be alternatively obtained
using a synthetic or decade inductance. This could probably easy the somewhat tedious
coil winding procedure. Model inaccuracy due to ineﬀective clamping may be reduced by
performing an initial EMA of the pure host specimen to align the predicted and actual
target resonance frequencies. Also, the fact that the form conversion of the constitute
equations does not yield identical plane stress-reduced charge constant ¯e31 and blocked
capacitance CS
p should be investigated.

13.1
Future Work
The following experimental test procedure can be proposed for the experimental testing of
new specimen:
1. Decide on a target frequency range, which should reﬂect the performance of the mea-
surement equipment. It is recommended that frequencies above 250 Hz are avoided
for the current experimental setups.
2. Design the specimen by estimating the natural frequencies using algebraic formulas.
3. Conduct initial Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) of the specimen without piezo-
electric transducers.
4. Determine the eﬀective clamping by aligning the numerical and experimental target
frequencies of the host structure. Remember to check for model convergence.
5. Select, purchase and attach piezoceramic transducers to the host structure by fol-
lowing the guidelines given in Section 8.4.
6. Conduct EMA of SC and OC conﬁgurations. Calculate the eﬀective EMCC κe.
7. Calibrate the inductance by applying the formulas in Table 10.1. The modal mass can
be estimated from the numerical model or by curveﬁtting of the Frequency Response
Function. The 3-1 piezoelectric charge constant e31, found from data sheets, must
be corrected by comparing with measurements of the eﬀective blocked capacitance.
8. Manufacture the inductors. Contact Hans Christian Andersen from DTU Electronics
for usage of the coil winding machine.
9. Perform EMA of specimen connected to a pure L shunt. For optimal tuning, two res-
onance peaks (ω−, ω+) will appear symmetrically around the SC frequency. Calculate
the modal EMCC κr from (10.1).
10. Calibrate the resistance by applying the formulas in Table 10.1.
11. Perform EMA of specimen connected to a tuned RL shunt. If correctly calibrated,
the resonance peak will have turned into a ﬂat plateau.
EMA Modiﬁcations
The LabVIEW application for obtaining the free decay response using the resonance decay
method requires modiﬁcation. When shutting oﬀthe excitation, the electromagnetic shaker
rapidly returns to its base position. This appears as a sudden impact that impairs the
measurements.
Beam Model Implementation
Implementation of a numerical model consisting of (Mindelin) beam elements instead of
(Kirchhoﬀ) plate elements. This is because the plane stress assumption may not be justiﬁed
for the considered beam specimen.
A faster numerical model is also desirable, as the
implemented FE-model becomes computationally intractable for ﬁne discretizations of the
two-wing beam.
Electrical Conﬁguration
It could be interesting to investigate eﬀects due to asymmetry when using a unimorph patch
conﬁguration. Stacked transducers on larger/stiﬀer structures could also be considered, as
aspect ratios up to 12:1 are supposedly achievable (Piezo Technologies, 2017).
An alternative suggestion to the parallel and series bimorph conﬁgurations is the bi-
ased bipolar conﬁguration. This connection is compatible with parallel-poled, bimorph
and multimorph benders and appears to provide better voltage-to-deﬂection ratio than
conventional techniques (Rios and Fleming, 2015).

A.1
Variational Formulation of Host Structure Dynamics
Consider the 3-dimensional displacements ui and normal stress components Tij of an ar-
bitrary elastic host structure expanding the domain Ω. The body has prescribed displace-
ments ¯ui on part of the boundary Γu and prescribed surface traction Fj on the remaining
part of the boundary ΓF .
The dynamic equilibrium of any particle in the host body can be described by the
Virtual Work Principle (VWP). The VWP establishes the relation between variations
in internal and external work due to inﬁnitesimal displacement perturbations (Sigmund,
2016). The method dictates that the total work of the forces acting on a particle must be
zero for any given virtual displacement (Dasgupta, 2009). This is particularly useful for
ﬁnite element formulations of elastic bodies subject to known volume and surface loads
without requiring reference to reactive forces.
