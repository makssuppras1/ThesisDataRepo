Abstract
Phosphor is a vital element for life and has no substitute for its biological function. Phosphor in
its inorganic structure, phosphate, is mined from phosphate rock deposits and used as fertilizer in
the agriculture. This is necessary for sustaining the food supply for the global population. Studies
show that phosphorous is lost from erosion and runoﬀwhere it cannot be recovered. Meanwhile
the phosphate rock mines are being depleted, likely within the next century. Therefore a concerned
eﬀort must be placed on using the phosphate more eﬃciently and on recovering the lost phosphorous
from wastes.
Current technologies for recovering phosphate already exist but have faults in their mode of
operation.
This thesis investigates a biomimetic approach for recapturing phosphate by using a
synthesized hexa-peptide with sequence SGAGKT found in the literature. The peptide is originally
inspired by a protein sequence called the P-loop, that is responsible for binding phosphate groups
of organic molecules. It was shown that the hexa-peptide was able bind inorganic phosphate while
discriminating the similar sulphate which is attractive in order to avoid binding other anions. It is
believed that the biomimetic approach of using proteins or peptides, carries the potential to recover
lost phosphate in a reversible and energy eﬃcient manner. The hexa-peptide is considered as a
possible candidate for this.
This thesis therefore sets the objective to study the hexa-peptide interaction with phosphate
anions and its ability to discriminate similar anions, in order gain knowledge in the possibility for
using it in phosphate recovery applications. The study employs two approaches: Experimental and
computational.
The peptide is studied computationally by hybrid molecular dynamics and quantum mechanics
simulations, with the objective to gain an understanding of the peptide behaviour and also to ﬁnd
suitable methods of analysis of the peptide-phosphate interactions. The focus of investigation is put
on the HPO2−
4
anion found at neutral pH, while the H2PO−
4 , PO3−
4
and sulphate SO2−
4
anions are
used for comparison. The computational analysis is based three methods: i) cluster analysis that
ﬁnds static conﬁgurations, ii) principal component analysis (PCA) that ﬁnds the dynamical motions
of the simulation, and iii) binding free energy calculations using the MMGBSA approach. It is found
that the peptide-HPO2−
4
interaction composes a variety of conﬁgurations, some of them being a nest
structure as proposed in the literature. The motional change upon HPO2−
4
binding by PCA suggests
a more cooperative complex, where the binding mechanism is governed by desolvation eﬀects by a
favourable increase of entropy. Free energy calculations by the MMPBSA methods are incompatible
with the quantum mechanical treatment of the system and does not provide a reliable result, making
a direct comparison between the diﬀerent anions diﬃcult. However based on the PCA, the only anion
i
==PAGE:3==
that signiﬁcantly enhances the cooperative behaviour of the complex is HPO2−
4 , which could mean
that this anion binds the strongest.
Experimentally a competitive binding assay using radioactive phosphate is employed with the
objective to determine the aﬃnity for phosphate and selectivity to similar anions. However it is
found that the peptide has a too low aﬃnity for phosphate to determine the binding free energy.
Estimations clariﬁed that the aﬃnity should increase by several orders of magnitude in order to use
the experimental approach.
The combined results of the experimental and computational analysis shows that the peptide
does not bind strongly enough for use in any application. In order to meet the requirements of high
aﬃnity and selectivity two approaches are discussed. One way is to structurally enhance the peptide
to increase its binding aﬃnity for use in any applications. Another approach going forward is to ﬁnd
a more suitable protein from the literature, that also fulﬁls the aﬃnity and selectivity criteria. The
thesis gives a likely protein candidate for future studies of phosphate recovery.

Dansk Resume
Fosfor er et afgørende element for vores eksistens og der ﬁndes ingen erstatning for dens funktion i
biologien. Fosfor i dens uorganiske form, fosfat, udvindes fra fosfat miner og anvendes som gødning
i landbruget.
Dette er nødvendigt for at opretholde fødevareforsyningen til verdens befolkning.
Undersøgelser viser, at fosfor tabes p˚a grund af erosion og aﬂøb, hvor det ikke kan inddrives. Samtidig
udtømmes fosfatminerne hvilket sandsynligvis bliver inden for det næste ˚arhundrede. Derfor er der
brug for en koncentreret indsats for at gøre brugen af fosfat mere eﬀektiv, og p˚a at genvinde det
tabte fosfor fra aﬀald.
Der ﬁndes allerede eksisterende teknologier til genvinding af fosfat, men de har alle fejl i deres
virkem˚ade. Denne afhandling undersøger en biomimetisk tilgang for at genvinde fosfat ved hjælp
af et syntetiseret hexa-peptid med sekvensen SGAGKT som er fundet i litteraturen. Peptidet er
oprindeligt inspireret af en proteinsekvens kaldet P-loop, der er ansvarlig for binding af fosforgrupper
i organiske molekyler. Det blev vist, at hexa-peptidet var i stand til binde uorganisk fosfat mens det
kunne skelne det lignende sulfat. Dette er attraktivt for at undg˚a fange andre anioner end fosfor. Vi
mener, at den biomimetiske tilgang med at anvende proteiner eller peptider, bærer potentialet til at
genvinde noget af det tabte fosfat p˚a en reversibel og energi eﬀektiv m˚ade. Hexa-peptidet betragtes
som en mulig kandidat til dette.
M˚alsætningen for denne afhandling er at studere hexa-peptid interaktionen med fosfat og dens
evne til at skelne lignende anioner. Dette gøres for at f˚a viden om muligheden for at bruge det i
fosfat genvinding applikationer. Undersøgelsen anvender to metoder: En eksperimentel og comput-
erbaserede beregninger.
Peptidet studeres computationelt ved at anvende hybride molekylær dynamik og kvantemekanik
simuleringer, med det form˚al at f˚a en forst˚aelse af peptidets adfærd og for at ﬁnde egnede metoder
til analyse af peptid-fosfat interaktioner. Fokus for undersøgelsen er sat p˚a HPO2−

Introduction
1.1
Biomimetic Phosphate Recovery
Biomimetics is a broad ﬁeld in engineering, that ﬁnds inspiration from nature for the use in a
application. Biomimetics exploits nature, by using a biologically optimized feature, such as a protein,
that has been optimized for certain functions through evolution. The objective is to integrate this
feature into an application, where it can still perform its task, while being of beneﬁt to humans.
This thesis utilizes a biomimetic approach for phosphate recovery, by using a peptide inspired by a
protein sequence found nature [1].
1.1.1
The Phosphorous Crisis
The element phosphor, essential to all life, is naturally present in soil and biology. The current
food supply in the agriculture, relies on supplies of phosphorous rock which is mined only in a few
locations in the world. These mines are being depleted, predictably within the next century [2].
Without phosphorous to use for fertilizers, humanity faces a possible world spread famine, with
unimaginable consequences [3].
To understand the imperative need for phosphorous, one simply needs to consider its role in
biology. While phosphor (P) is not a constituent of proteins, it is vital in their synthesis. Phospho-
diester bonds are the linkage of mononucleotide units forming the long chains of DNA and RNA,
which contains the code for all genetic information, including the building of proteins. The energy
required to synthesize all of these complex molecules is released by the reversible P bond of adenosine
- diphosphate (ADP) and -triphosphate (ATP). ATP is thus fuel for the biological nano-machinery of
proteins that maintains the existence of life [4]. Phosphorous is a vital component of phospholipids
that creates the cell membrane shielding the inside of cells from the outside. In the meanwhile the
membrane is able to integrate transmembrane proteins that performs tasks necessary for the cell to
function.
Indeed phosphorous is a vitally necessary element for biology, however other elements are equally
important to life: Nitrogen, carbon, oxygen and hydrogen are the primarily building blocks of
biological matter. They diﬀerentiate themselves to P in their biospheric abundance compared to
P, making the loss of P potentially more devastating [4]. Plants require nutrients including P to
grow, and without suﬃcient growth of plants there will be a shortage of food, obviously vital to our

The issue is furthermore enhanced by the fact that phosphorous has no replacement [5]; i.e it
is unlike the sources of creating energy, that can be either nuclear, solar, wind or chemical (oil)
extracted energy sources.
1.1.2
Phosphor as Fertilizer and in the Ecosystem
Before the 20th century most crop production relied on the natural presence of P in soil while being
recycled by manure. In fact, manure has been used as a fertilizer in the agriculture for centuries. It
dates over 5000 years back, where the so-called ”night soil” (human excrete) was used as fertilizer
in China [5]. Although this was before the discovery of phosphor (P) as an element (17th century),
the recycling was still performed [5].
Urbanisation, increased population and the industrial revolution lead to a stop of returning the
”night soil” to the ﬁelds due to public health issues which was called the Sanity Revolution [5].
During the Green Revolution of the 19th century, the theoretical foundation of organic chemistry
boosted the understanding of the ecosystem circulation of organic elements, and the identiﬁcation of
phosphate rocks were shown to successfully enhance the growth of crops. The P-rocks thus needed
to be applied on the agricultural ﬁelds [5]. After the second world war there was an exponential
boost of the phosphate rock usage, see Figure 1.1, and today the world population is dependent on
the use of mined P for producing fertilizers.
Figure 1.1: Use of phosphorous fertilizers since the 1800. From [2]
The natural P in soil recirculates on short and long time as scales illustrated in Figure 1.2. The
short P-cycle is governed by living organisms and land- and water-based recycling. In this cycle, the
P in the soil is uptaken by plants and animals, and later returns to the soil as plant litter and dead
microorganisms. Living organisms decomposes the dead biomaterial and transforms the unavailable
P to being accessible again [4].
Mineralization, weathering, erosion and runoﬀtransfers soluble P into oceans where it eventually
sinks as a sediments, being beyond the reach of plants and humans for extraction. The time scale of
recycling these sediments by geological uplift is estimated to be 107 to 108 years [4, 6], an extremely
large time scale when compared to human history.

1.1.3
Unsustainable Situation
There is an increased loss of P due to human interferences: i) Larger erosion and runoﬀ, due to
removal of forests that is protective against soil loss [4].
ii) There is more untreated and less
recycled wastes [4, 8]. iii) There is a signiﬁcant increase of usage of the inorganic P through applying
phosphate rock in the agriculture [4]. The overﬂood of P coming from agriculture can reach rivers,
streams, lakes and oceans where it causes eutrophication. Eutrophication is the over-enrichment of
nutrients of ecological systems, and it causes severe algae growth and oxygen depletion which leads
to ﬁsh killing [9]. In nearshore oceans, eutrophication also has eﬀects on the marine life by causing
hypoxic and dead zones, and coral reef dead and shellﬁsh food poisoning of humans [9].
Phosphorous rock mines are found in only a few countries namely USA, China, Morocco, Jordan
and South Africa, and is being considered a globally scarce resource [5, 3, 8, 4]. Expectedly 85 %
of the remaining stock phosphate is located in Marocco [5]. Estimates say that the total global
stock remaining in the mines are expected to exhaust within 50-100 years [2], and the problem
will be critical much sooner than the full depletion [5].
Already now accompanying issues with
a phosphate scarcity includes economical, societal and political issues [5].
Developing countries,
especially undernourished African countries (below Sahara), cannot aﬀord P for fertilizers, and a
large part of the people undernourished are smallholder farmers, which places them in an unfortunate
situation. Politically there is a geological contradiction of the extraction and trading of phosphate

An increased global demand for P rock led to an unprecedented prize increase of 800 % in 2008,

There are two major approaches for meeting long-term goals of gaining sustainable phosphorous

1.1.4
Phosphorous Flows and Technologies
The most economically and technologically feasible phosphorous recovery technology lies in the phos-
phorous rich and low volume ﬂows of manure and wastewater [3]. Flows containing phosphorous are
mostly found in the form of organic materials from animal or plant tissue; nucleic acids, nucleotides
and phospholipids of organisms. To reuse the phosphorous of such ﬂows, this organic state must
be converted into an inorganic state, namely phosphate. In waste water treatment anaerobic mi-
crobiological systems can convert organic wastes, such as manure, into methane or hydrogen gasses
that can be used for generating electrical power.
The process releases P from the organic form
into an inorganic phosphate sludge from which it can be recovered. The process of transforming
organic phosphor from organic waste, into an inorganic form also comes with a gain of energy due
to the possibility of converting the produced gasses into electricity [3]. The entire process ﬂow of
this scheme is illustrated in Figure 1.3

How to extract the phosphate is a technological challenge. While there are developed technologies
that are commercially functioning, they all have faults either in eﬃciency or eﬀectiveness [3]. The
ideal demands of phosphate recovery includes the requirements of being a ”green” technology: i.e it
must not require a high-energy process, not consume other materials and not risking contamination
of the nature. A few current technologies are presented here based on [3].
Precipitation
Chemical precipitation, or also known as crystallization, is the most developed technique and involves
covalent binding of the phosphate to a metal cation for example Mg2+, Ca2+, Al3+ and Fe3+. Of these
examples, the iron complexes are bound too strongly and thus unavailable for plants, and aluminium
complexes are toxic for some plants and organisms. However, a commercially successful method is
the struvite precipitation where phosphate binds covalently to a Mg complex (MgNH4PO4 · 6H2O).
The process includes some complications. The reaction requires a suﬃcient amount of phosphate
and ammonium present while being most eﬀective at a high pH at nearly equimolar concentrations,
which requires bases. Furthermore the struvite can also be contaminated by heavy metals that exist
in the waste water treatment [10]. When producing the struvite, there is a risk of clogging the pipes
and systems, demaning a high maintenance [11]. As the last point, the technology also uses other
materials in a 1:1, such that Mg2+ also is consumed, which may eventually present new problems.
Adsorption
Adsorption technology is a transfer of liquid-phase solutes to solid-phase, where the phosphorous
is adsorbed by coagulation and subsequently removed through sedimentation and ﬁltration. It was
ﬁrst used in 1950, and is one of the most well-known and widely used methods for phosphorous
removal [3]. The coagulants utilized have low cost and are widely available. Drawbacks are the high
operational costs in matters such as energy consumption and maintenance.
Ion Exchange
Based on aﬃnity, phosphate ions can be exchanged with solid-phase ions by using electrodes. Unlike
the previous examples the method is reversible, however it is less well-known which is a major
drawback [3].
Biological Uptake
Biological uptake constitutes of a number of ways for utilizing biological organisms or proteins for
phosphorous capture. Enhanced Biological Phosphorous Recovery (EBPR) stores polymerized phos-
phate in a heterotrophic biomass, which produces organic phosphorous that requires additional treat-
ment before usage. A second method is to use phototrophic microorganisms that use carbondioxide
and sunlight to uptake phosphorous. A third approach is to ﬁnd inspiration from microorganisms
found in P-limited environments that are optimized to eﬃciently ﬁnd and select phosphorous or
phosphate from its solution. These organisms expresses high-selective phosphate-binding proteins,
that may be engineered for recovery. These processes are far from being application but are devel-
oping.
The latter approach of using biological uptake, is within the biomimetic ﬁeld, and although it is far
from ready for true recovery applications, it perhaps represents a potentially very powerful recovery
technique.
A biomimetic approach using proteins for phosphate recovery, can make a reversible

1.2
P-Loops
One sequence responsible for binding phosphorous to a protein is called a P-loop, or Walker A motif,
found by Walker in 1982 [12]. It is found in many diﬀerent proteins like kinases, guanine nucleotide-
binding proteins (including the G-proteins) [13] and it typically loops between the N-terminus of
an alpha helix to a beta sheet in the protein [1, 13, 14]. On of its functions is to bind the α or β
phosphates of ATP and GTP into place, allowing hydrolysis of the nucleoside and thus facilitating the
phosphorylation process of a protein [13, 15, 16]. This process is essential for intracellular signalling
and trans-membrane regulation [14].
A typical P-loop sequence is GXXXXGK-(S,T) with X being any amino acid [13]. Completely
distinct proteins may have the P-loop as the only similarity [13]. The lysine residue is conserved
in all P-loops and it is hypothesized that it is essential for the capturing of the phosphate, and the
conformation and stabilization of NTP (ATP, GTP etc.) [14, 17, 13]. The glycines of the P-loop
allows conformations not allowed by any other amino acid [14].
The P-loop folds up to make a concavity by its main NH groups called a nest conﬁguration
[1, 14, 18]. It can generally be deﬁned by the ﬁrst two dihedral angles (ﬁrst three main chain amino
acids), by a LR or RL formation, depending on the φ and ψ angles. Their deﬁnitions are [14, 18]

A nest is characterized by an alternating angle sequence of L and R. The smallest possible nest is a
LR or RL, where RL nests constitute 80 % of nests. Two overlapping nests will then form a LRL
or RLR nest, and three overlapping nests will form a LRLR or RLRL nest [1]. Three examples of
nests are shown in Figure 1.5.

The P-loop generally compose a LRLR nest forming a concavity of the center residue where all
the NH groups points inwards of the concavity, and can form hydrogen bonds with the anionic group
[14]. This way the nest eﬀectively sets a positively charged electrostatic ﬁeld surrounding the anion,

1.2.1
Bianchi et. al Hexa-Peptide
Recently, Bianchi et al., synthesized a hexa-peptide with the amino acid sequence Ser-Gly-Ala-Gly-
Lys-Thr (SGAGKT) [1], which resembles a P-loop of myosin that has the sequence of GESGAGKT
[13]. The sequence is chosen to enhance a nest conﬁguration, by having the two glycines in the
center of the chain. The Gly side-chain, a hydrogen atom, promote a low steric hindrance and thus
gives a larger degree of freedom to the neighbouring residues. The larger ﬂexibility allows them to
turn their side-chain or backbone N-H atoms towards a phosphate ion creating a nest conﬁguration.
Obviously, the peptide is not expected to form an α-helix, which was tested based on circular
dichroism by Bianchi et al.

hydrogen of the phosphate.

1.3
Peptide Application and Study Objectives
The goal of using the hexa-peptide in an application sets some demanding requirements. Selectivity
is important to avoid contamination by recovering toxic or otherwise unwanted species.
Such a
species could for instance be arsenate which is extremely similar to phosphate, by having the same
valence electrons and only diﬀers by a thermochemical radius of 4 % [20]. Arsenate is very toxic and
it is thus imperative to be able to recognize and discriminate arsenate from phosphate. Another ion
is the sulphate, which is found to not bind.
The other objective is to be able to bind the phosphate reversibly. The binding must be suﬃciently
strong, and be able keep the phosphate bound for a suﬃcient amount of time until its supposed to
be released. Since the phosphate has to be released again, the binding must not be too strong, which
would require enzymes or signiﬁcant perturbation to release the ion again. The goal is to be able
to reversibly bind and release phosphates to achieve a high ”throughput” of recovery to make an

The isolated peptide sequence SGAGKT, is too short to have a secondary structure, and may
thus be considered as a intrinsically disordered protein (IDP). IDPs are known to have selective

may be well matched by the use of this hexa-peptide, which is what this work aims to investigate
through experimental and computational studies.

Experimental Studies
The peptide application relies on its ability to bind phosphate and to discriminate similar ions such as
sulphate and arsenate. The experimental study makes a competitive binding assay for determination
of the ion selectivity in favouring phosphate over competing ions. The results of this study will give
a ﬁrst impression of how ready the peptide is for application testing.
Computational Studies
Simultaneously with the experimental binding assays, molecular dynamics simulations are employed.
The computational study has the objective to study the intrinsic peptide-phosphate interaction, in
order to gain an understanding of how to use the peptide in an application and how to improve it in
the future. The chaotic nature of such a biological process may not allow an easy and direct way of
obtaining meaningful results. Therefore it is of interest to investigate which methods for simulations
and analysis that can be successfully applied to such systems, and gain knowledge of how to apply
these in future studies of improved peptides or proteins.

2.1.2
Free Energy
The activity of a protein depends on its conformation and how it folds from a certain conformation
into another. This transition can for instance be that of an unfolded state to a folded state. The
diﬀerent conformational states have diﬀerent properties, for example it may be an closed or open
ion channel. The transition may be denoted by the following scheme
A →B,
(2.11)
and is best described by the Gibbs free energy, or free energy. The free energy of a chemical process
describes the gain or loss in energy in the transition of one chemical state to another. It accounts
for both the change in enthalpy (chemical binding energy), and the entropy (degrees of freedom).
Mathematically it can be described by the formula
∆G = ∆H −T∆S
(2.12)
The ∆accounts the change when going from state A to state B. The free energy holds for all
reactions. Often these reactions induce conformational changes in molecules/macro molecules. Such
conformational changes can be induced by the binding with a molecule or simply the spontaneous
transition from one particular conformation to another.
A chemical process is favourable if the free energy change is negative, meaning that the energy
of state B is lower than the energy of state A. An illustration of the energy landscape of a protein
can be seen on Figure 2.2. A molecule will populate the possible equilibrium states in accordance
with the Boltzmann distribution. The energy landscape of the molecule will not be smooth as drawn

in the ﬁgure, but rather rugged with many similar conformations, only separated by small energy
barriers. These small energy barriers are easily overcome, since they may correspond to orientations
of side chains or small changes in some ﬂexible backbone angles. The larger barriers correspond to
major changes in the backbone or deeply buried side chains, and thus corresponds to transitions
such as a protein folding.
Figure 2.2: Illustrative conformational energy landscape of a given molecule. The y-axis displays

may transform its conformation by going from state A to the global minimum of state B. However

may be changed by a enzyme or ligand, since they facilitate other chemical bindings. The actual

minima on its way.
The conformational change of a protein from the local minima A to the global minimum B, may
be reached through several pathways. The pathways are inﬂuenced by external factors of water,
binding of ligands and/or speciﬁc intra molecular changes in the molecule.
All of the diﬀerent
pathways will thus have diﬀerent energy barriers, but can all end up in the global minima (or folded
state).
A protein may have an extremely large barrier between the states A and B, such that state B,
only can be obtained by the binding with another molecule (ligand). This binding changes the energy
landscape by lowering the energy barrier, and makes the state of B accessible.
The Equation (2.12), holds for any conformational change and chemical binding. In the case of

that when calculating the free energy through Equation (2.13) the respective size of enthalpy and
entropy is unknown.

2.2.1
Experimental Approach
Here the design of the assays and experimental execution is explained.
The peptide-phosphate
binding will be measured by the use of radioactive isotopes, 33P.
The assay is created schematically as shown in Figure 2.5. The peptides are linked to biotin that
binds with streptavidin coated on µM sized sepharose beads. When biotin binds to streptavidin-
coated sepharose beads, the peptides are also indirectly bound to the beads. To ensure saturation of
the biotin-streptavidin bonds, the solution is incubated overnight. After the incubation the sample is
centrifuged, which causes the µM sized peptide-sepharose beads to sediment in a pellet. Unbounded
peptides will be in the supernatant and can be removed.
The ﬁnal assay is made by mixing the peptide-sepharose beads with radioactive (hot) and cold
(regular) phosphate. The assay is incubated a few hours and put on ice before ﬁltering to limit the
dissociation.
After incubation, a sample of the assay is ﬁltered through a glass microﬁber ﬁlter. The sepharose
beads will be retained in the microﬁlter, and the rest of the liquid solution will ﬂow through, including
unbound phosphates both hot and cold. To account for unspeciﬁc binding reference assays are made
with the same approach: one reference assay is with streptavidin and one is with biotin is bound
to the streptavidin, but without the peptides. Each assay will have a small volume extracted as a
control and data handling, and is henceforth referred to as the control.
The ﬁlters are put in a scintillation tube and 3 mL scintillation liquid is added. Radioactive
decays will cause the scintillation liquid to ﬂuorescence, and the photons will be counted.
The
eﬃciency of decay to ﬂuorescence is expected to be 70-80 %.
The assay is stabilized in TES and EDTA buﬀer at pH 8, the full protocol can be found in the
Appendix B.

into a large container, where all the ﬁltered waste ends up. The setup can be seen on Figure 2.6.
The container is subject to an under-pressure created by a pump not seen in the setup. The under-
pressure increases the ﬂow rate by dragging the liquid down.

Substrate: Hydrophobic micro glass substrate completely stopping any ﬂow, and thus had

Nothing can ﬂow when the substrate its
completely dewetted. A partly wetted substrate would still signiﬁcantly slow the ﬂow through the
ﬁlter. The problem was solved by wetting the substrate with ethanol, a solvent having less surface

Valve: Beneath the glass substrate there is a valve (a plastic stick with a small hole through).
Rotating this valve with the correct orientation would open the ﬂow coming from the ﬁlters on the
glass substrate above. A hydrophobic solution of the sample consisting primarily of buﬀer (water),
would not run through the small hole in the valve, or it would create a drop that would not fall into

valve, and inserting cotton buds instead allows the liquid to run through smoothly.
Filter: Some results showed count numbers of the ﬁltered samples having orders of magnitudes
lower count numbers compared to other measurements in the assay. Control samples would still have
similar counts, indicating problems with the ﬁltering.
The ﬁrst ﬁlters used were about a millimetre too large to precisely ﬁt in the substrate well.
Putting the ﬁlter on the substrate could result in folds of the ﬁlter, so it would not cover the
substrate entirely. During a potential ﬂooding of the ﬁlter, either during buﬀer ﬂush or sample
apply, the liquids could avoid the ﬁlter and ﬂow through the passage made by the fold of the ﬁlter.
This is assumed what happened for some samples.
Larger ﬁlters ﬁt better on the substrate, as they folded up along the edges. Flushing with buﬀer
and ﬂooding of the ﬁlter would not have any impact as all liquids would have to run through the
ﬁlter anyway.
Flush: Finally, having solved the clogging issue allows a large ﬂow rate and thus a signiﬁcantly
larger throughput, going from 20 min per sample to only 3-4 min. This made it possible to investigate
how much buﬀer that would be necessary properly ﬂush all unbound phosphate through the ﬁlter.
Such a test showed that it was suﬃcient to ﬂush with 2000 µL buﬀer.
Due to a high expected dissociation rate, ﬂushing with a large amount of buﬀer could drag the
phosphates away from the peptides. To minimize the dissociation the ﬁlters were ﬂushed with a phos-
phate rich buﬀer (50 mM) to sustain the equilibrium complexes, by keeping the bound phosphates
with the peptides.
Optimized Approach
Fixing the aforementioned problems by wetting the substrate with ethanol and inserting cotton
buds instead of the valve and using appropriate size of ﬁlters, almost eliminated the large errors.

Molecular Dynamics Theory
Computational studies of molecular interactions can be performed with a variety of simulation
techniques. Depending on the purpose of investigation the most suitable tool should be chosen.
Extremely precise calculations can be performed using quantum mechanics (QM), for example
Density Functional Theory as recently done by Thomas Østergaard [24]. These calculations use
the Schr¨odinger equation and calculates the electronic structure interaction. The electronic struc-
ture problem is extremely computational demanding[36], and are thus only able to calculate a few
molecules.
This approach is great for studying the most fundamental interactions and intrinsic
properties of molecules. However when studying biological systems where solvent interactions are
important, one must use other simulation approaches to understand their behaviour in the solvent
environment. There are two major simulation tools for studying a solute in solvent: Monte Carlo
and molecular dynamics.
Based on Boltzmann statistics and a ”kick” in a random direction, the Monte Carlo (MC) sim-
ulations explores the energy landscape around a current conﬁguration. The conﬁguration is allowed
to climb from a local minimum over an energy barrier in order to ﬁnd a new minima. The conﬁgura-
tion change of each step must be physically acceptable, and is achieved by having small step sizes of
change. The implication of this is that only the local energy landscape is explored. The MC method
is based on conﬁgurations, and thus does not include any temporal dependency including parti-
cle velocities and accelerations. The method is therefore not suitable for studying time-dependent
phenomena such as protein folding.
Molecular dynamics (MD) implements the time perspective of a simulation by applying Newton’s
second law of motion to a initial starting conﬁguration and velocity of a system. It hereby creates a
trajectory based on small ﬁnite time steps separating each frame of the trajectory. With typical time
steps of femtoseconds, it takes one million steps to reach 1 nanosecond of simulation trajectory. Since
protein time scales can be signiﬁcantly larger (> 100 ns), it takes signiﬁcant computational eﬀort
and time to obtain meaningful simulations. Solvent properties can be included explicitly, by having
water molecules in the simulation, or it can be included implicitly where the continuum properties
of water is represented by a dielectric constant. Furthermore MD can be combined with quantum
mechanical calculations, allowing handling of certain important residues to be treated with QM,
while other parts to be treated regular MD. Thus MD allows for precise calculations that are able
to include solvent eﬀects and the temporal evolution of the molecules. For these reasons the MD

3.1.3
Molecular Dynamics Implementations
In order to optimize the use of MD it is necessary to make some considerations of how to set up the
simulation to make it more eﬃcient (faster) and eﬀective (accurate). A few of such considerations
are discussed here based on the textbooks [38] and [43].
Minimization
The system must initially be minimized with respect to the energy in order to ensure the atoms
and bonds of the created structures are not overlapping. This procedure is done prior to the MD
simulation. The minimization ﬁnds a local minimum point on the energy hypersurface. In this project
there are used two diﬀerent algorithms for doing this: Steepest descent and Conjugate gradients
minimisation.
The steepest descent algorithm moves directly down the energy gradient of the 3N Cartesian
coordinate system. Each minimization step is obtained based on the previous conﬁguration. The
location of the minimum point can be found by a line search illustrated in Figure 3.2(a). The line
goes in the direction of the energy gradient that initially goes downwards, but may change direction
to upwards again. When it has found three points with the center point lower than the outer points,
the structure is minimized.
The method of conjugate gradients have orthogonal gradients at each point, see Figure 3.2(b). It
is conjugate due to its property of achieving the local minima in M steps when having M variables
(conjugate directions). It goes down the energy gradient to a point based on the previous direction
vector. At this point the new direction is orthogonal to the previous, but also going down the energy
gradient. At the last minimum point it is necessary to perform a line search to ﬁnd the minimum.
Within a certain amount of steps the minimization has to converge otherwise the system will fail.
Such a failure is likely a result of un-physical structures input to the system.
Vibrations
The simulation time step should be able to resolve the fastest atomic motions.
These are the
molecular rotations and vibrations with frequencies of 1011 −1014s−1 which requires a time step of
the order of femtoseconds (1015s) to catch the motions. Setting this demand requires 106 time steps
pr nanosecond which is a signiﬁcant computational time. One can half the amount of time steps in
a simulation by ﬁxing the fastest vibrational motion of a system. The fastest motion of atoms are
the vibrations of hydrogen, and since its degrees of freedom have little inﬂuence, it is beneﬁcial to
freeze the bond lengths to all hydrogens. Such a constraint is implemented in Amber14 by the name
SHAKE [44] and allows an increase of the time step from 0.1 fs to 0.2 fs.

Sampling Conditions
The MD simulations needs to be performed with certain thermodynamic ensembles where certain
variables are kept constant. The most common simulation ensembles are NVE, NVT and NPT.
Where N is the number of particles, V the volume of the simulation box, T the temperature and
P the pressure. The most common is the NVE where the temperature and pressure are allowed to
ﬂuctuate.
The conditions/ensembles inﬂuence the simulation, and thus needs to be considered. Algorithms
are implemented to keep the thermodynamic variables ﬁxed [46]. The thermostats and barostats are
algorithms applied to control the simulation temperature and pressure respectively. The thermostats
adds or removes energy, while the barostat is able to change the size of the simulation box to keep
the pressure constant.
During the heating phase of the simulation, an NVT ensemble (Canonical) is used to set the
constraints. The pressure is calculated inaccurately during the heating, due to instabilities from to
over corrections of the barostat. Thus a NVT ensemble is used with a Langevin thermostat. During
the production the NPT ensemble (isothermal-isobaric) is used. The constant pressure allows the
density of the system to equilibrate.
Water Modelling
Water molecules plays a large role in the interactions with the solute molecules even in a small MD
simulation there will be on the order of 1000 water molecules, which demands a large computational
cost. It is therefore necessary to ﬁnd a suitable treatment for the water molecules, that does not
require too much power, but also includes the essential behaviour. There has been developed simple
water models, where waters are modelled by a rigid geometry and three to ﬁve interaction sites.
The computational cost rises with the interaction sites. The TIP3P [47] used in this project, has
three electrostatic interaction sites. One for each of the partial positive charges of the hydrogen
atoms, balanced by a negative charge of the oxygen atom. Interactions between two water molecules
is treated by a Lennard-Jones potential with a single interaction point per molecule placed on the
oxygen atom. There are no van der Waals interactions involving the hydrogen atoms [43]. The
geometry of the waters is rigid, the OH bond length is 0.9572 ˚A and the HOH angle is 104.52°.
Implicit water models are also available. Here the explicitly modelled water molecules are sub-
stituted by a continuum model with a dielectric constant, this allows for much faster simulations.
Drawbacks is that the hydrogen bonds are lost, which makes the method less precise. The implicit
water simulations will be revisited in section 3.4.2.

Quantum Mechanics
Employing MD/QM hybrid simulations, enables a much more precise treatment of speciﬁc chosen
residues. The quantum mechanical properties, such as electronic orbitals and tunnelling phenomena
are included. For this project it is expected to give a much better description of the polarization
accommodated with the large negative charge of the diﬀerent phosphate ions, which is essential
for describing the weak hydrogen bonds interacting with the peptide. The hybrid method works

3.2.1
Semiempirical Methods
The quantum mechanical models used in this report are all based on semiempirical methods. The
issue with all quantum mechanical computations is the limitations in computational power to calcu-
late all the electron-electron interactions. Semiempirical methods are able to handle larger systems
than ab initio methods, that are purely theoretical build, however they lack the same kind of pre-
cision [48]. The semiemperical methods uses approximations and assumptions of the full quantum
mechanical problem, and they are based on experimental data to obtain and optimize parameters
for atoms and molecules used in the models. Many parameters are dependent on each other, making
a perfect experimental/calculation ﬁt impossible, since a change in one parameter will change the
next.
Errors in the semiemperical methods are due to three reasons [49]. i) Errors of obtained exper-
imental data will result in errors of the model parameters. ii) the model approximations can be
more or less inaccurate. iii) the optimization of all the parameters in the model cannot be fulﬁlled
as already mentioned.
Neglect of Diﬀerential Overlap Methods
Over the course of this and the previous project [23] two diﬀerent QM theories have been tried AM1
and PM6. They should be suitable for hydrogen bond calculations and furthermore there should
34

be a hydrogen-bond correction implementation available [48]. The semi empirical quantum models
utilized in Amber are based on the Neglect of Diatomic Diﬀerential Overlap (NDDO) approximation,
which as the name suggests, neglects the interaction overlap between the orbitals (basis functions) of
diﬀerent atoms [42, 43]. To compensate for the NDDO approximation, core-core interaction functions
are used for correction. Typically this core-core interaction term is made such that the valence and
core electrons are being treated separately. The valence electrons feels an eﬀective potential from
the nuclei and the inner-shell (core electrons). This way, only the valence electrons are treated in
the calculations.
An improvement of NDDO with optimized parameters for molecular properties is called Modiﬁed
Neglect of Diatomic Diﬀerential Overlap (MNDO)[50]. This model gives a much better description
of the molecular enthalpy of formation that agreed better with experimental results. In spite of this,
the models has several shortcomings, especially the inability to express hydrogen bonds [49].
AM1
The Austin Model 1 (AM1)[51] utilizes a core-core repulsion function described by Gaussian functions
to represent hydrogen bonds added to the MNDO model. The Gaussian functions are respectively
attractive and repulsive, and hereby aims more precisely in the short and long range interactions
between atoms. The most signiﬁcant attribute of this description is the ability to express hydrogen
bonds, due to the weaker short range repulsion.
Furthermore this approach does not add any
mentionable increase in the computational time compared to NDDO, and is more accurate in the
enthalpy of formation for molecules and in calculating the ionization energies [42].
PM6
Many optimizations in the three sources of error are corrected in the Parametric Method number
6 model (PM6) [49]. The main changes are: Parameter optimization process by including more
reference data points, the core-core approximation interaction is more realistic than the AM1 and
MNDO models, and thus limits the potential energy artifacts. Furthermore, low-energy interactions
such as hydrogen-bonding are described more precisely in their energies and geometries. PM6 is
the latest implemented semiemperical QM implemented in Amber which is suﬃciently tested and is
therefore the QM model choice in this project.

Molecular Dynamics Analysis
MD simulations results in large data sets and the analysis therefore become increasingly diﬃcult
to analyse and comprehend as the simulations grow larger. For this purpose many methods have
been developed to facilitate the analysis. This section presents the basic ideas behind some of those
methods which were used for data analysis in this work.
4.1
Clustering
Clustering is a technique used for statistical analysis of large amount of data in order to group
similar parts of the data into a cluster group. The technique can be used in diﬀerent ﬁelds to map
out comparable points of a data set. Our purpose is to ﬁnd structural conﬁgurations obtained during
a simulation, corresponding to possible local minima of the peptide-ion complex. The clustering can
be performed on diﬀerent parameters in a simulation, such as the Euclidean distance between atoms
given by the root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) and by RMSD of the dihedral angles.
Ideally, the goal is to ﬁnd a natural amount of clusters assumed in a simulation, while maintaining
eﬃcient processing of the datasets. The clustering should require minimum knowledge of the system
to make it less sensitive of user deﬁned parameters. For instance a single cluster should not be
divided into multiple clusters due to user deﬁned parameters. In Amber14 there are two available
algorithms for clustering: The hierarchical algorithm and a Density Based Spatial Clustering of
Applications with Noise (DBSCAN), both algorithm principles are described below.
4.1.1
Hierarchical Clustering
There are in principle two basic clustering algorithms: The partitioning algorithms and the hierar-
chical algorithms [58, 59]. The partitional algorithm randomly pre-deﬁnes a number of clusters, and
following places each object/data point in one of the nearest clusters. For each step a new centroid
for each cluster is deﬁned as well. This procedure is continued and repeated until the clustering has
converged. However this method is non-deterministic and the result depends on initial centroids and
the chosen amount of clusters [59].
The hierarchical algorithm creates clusters by hierarchical decomposition, which can be under-
stood visually by a dendrogram as shown in Figure 4.1. The dataset is iteratively split into subsets
(or branches). In this case each node in the dendrogram would represent a cluster. The hierarchical
clustering can be agglomerative - aggregating single elements into a cluster, or they can be divisive

- by dividing the dataset into clusters. These two methods correspond to moving downwards or
upwards in the dendrogram on Figure 4.1(b). The user deﬁnes a termination parameter that ends
the clustering when the minimal (or critical) distance between each cluster has been reached.

The main issue with the hierarchical clustering is to determine the critical parameter value, in
order to keep natural cluster intact in addition to separating multiple clusters.
4.1.2
Density Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise
Errors in the hierarchical approach is that for a given termination condition of a cluster there may
be variation in the density of data points within this cluster. For example a 3 ˚A of maximum RMSD
of structures for a cluster in a simulation, there may be a large density in the ﬁrst 1-2 ˚A, however in
the ﬁnal 3’rd ˚A, there could be a low density, and should thus not be included in the cluster. These
data points could perhaps either be considered as another cluster or even as noise. To account for
such issues, a density based algorithm called Density Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with
Noise (DBSCAN) has been developed [58].
The principle is to ensure that the density of points in a given area has to exceed a threshold
in order to deﬁne the cluster. A suﬃciently low density of points may then be recognised as noise
and not be added to a cluster. A vital element of the algorithm is also to recognise border elements,
where the density is lower due to missing neighbours, and keep them as a part of the cluster. By
taking the density based ”parameter” into account, the DBSCAN does not diﬀerentiate between the
ﬁrst point of a cluster and the last point of a cluster even if their RMSd values would be larger than
that allowed in the hierarchical method. That is, as long as the datapoint density is large enough the
addition to a cluster continues. When the density is diminished enough the algorithm will recognize
the data points belonging to a new cluster or even as noise.
Unlike the hierarchical clustering, the DBSCAN starts with a point and accumulates so-called
”density reachable” points. If no points are density reachable it will be deﬁned as noise, and if it is
a border point it will be revisited for a new point where it could be density reachable.
The user-deﬁned parameters of the method are the minimum number of points in a cluster and
the physical distance to the border of the cluster. The latter parameter can be determined with
help of a k-dist plot [58]. Plotting the distance between the points of the dataset, will show a kink

that separates the noise from the clusters. This value is deﬁned as the ”thinnest density” criteria
for a cluster and is the threshold between noise and clusters. The minimum number of points must
however be chosen with consideration of the given system.
Without any prior knowledge with this method, a speculated disadvantage is a suﬃciently dense
structure RMSD of a simulation will not create suﬃcient amount of clusters, because they are density
reachable. The impact of this would be extremely large RMSD values between the ends of the cluster
spectrum, that ideally should be divided into more clusters.
4.2
Principal Component Analysis
The cluster analysis only gives static structures obtained in a simulation, and does not give any
information about the motions of the complex. A method able to do this is the Principal Component
Analysis (PCA). Datasets containing many variables and thus being high dimensional, easily become
unmanageable to comprehend and treat because of their high dimensionality. The diﬀerent variables
may or may not be correlated, and furthermore the diﬀerent variables could be connected with
a quantity not directly observant from the given datasets. A great way to analyse such datasets
is to use PCA, also called quasi-harmonic analysis [61]. The basic idea of PCA is to represent a
possibly correlated dataset using an equal amount, or fewer, uncorrelated variables, called principal
components. Therefore the method is also not suitable for non-linear relationships.
The foundation of the PCA is built on the covariance matrix, that relates the correlation of two
variables by a scalar. The indices of the covariance matrix, Cij, is calculated by the covariance σij

4.3
Free Energy Calculations
An informative way to analyse receptor-ligand interaction and their competing ligands for reference,
is by estimating the binding free energy. Promising goals of such calculations are especially the
computer-aided drug design for medical science [70]. For our purpose, the goal is to estimate the
peptide aﬃnity for phosphate compared to other ions and gain insight of the intrinsic properties of
the peptide. In this project the MM-GB(PB)SA method is used. The method is implemented by a
python script in AMBERTOOLS as MMPBSA.py [71]. The theory behind the method is explained
below.
4.3.1
MM-GB(PB)SA Approach
In an explicit water MD simulation, the water molecules will represent most of the system energy.
Variations in the energy of the interesting molecules, such as the receptor protein and ligand inter-
action, will be drowned in the noise of thousands of water molecules. Additionally, calculations on
binding energies would take extremely long time to converge during an explicit water simulation [72].
Since water is a part of the binding energy it is however necessary to include this in the free energy
calculations somehow. The only way to do this is to approximate the solvation energy of a molecule
by using an implicit solvent model. This can be done using the MM-PBSA [73] or MM-GBSA [74].
MM-PBSA refers to Molecular Mechanics - Poisson-boltzmann Surface Area, and MM-GBSA to
Molecular Mechanics - Generalized-Born Surface Area. It is a postprocessing technique, that can be
applied on any MD or MC simulation and was implemented in Amber in 2005[75]. The two methods
follow the same approach, but using diﬀerent theories for calculation of the solvation energy.
Basically, the methods replaces the explicit water molecules and counterions with an eﬀective
dielectric constant or implicit solvent to calculate the solvation free energy of a molecule, ∆Gsolvation.
The full solvated energy of the molecule in the implicit medium is then governed by the equation
∆Gsolvated = Egas + ∆Gsolvation −T∆SMM,
(4.5)
where Egas is the molecular mechanical energy given by equation (3.9), and the ﬁnal term is the
solute entropy which can be approximated by normal mode analysis or quasi harmonic analysis that
will be discussed in Section 4.3.3.
The MM-PBSA method can be used for calculating the free energy change of a protein folding,
protein-ligand binding and protein-protein systems [73]. One of the ﬁrst applications have been to
study the DNA transition from state A to state B [76]. Later on systems such as proteins have
been studied with this technique such as binding free energies, protein-protein and protein-RNA
interactions among others [73, 77, 74].

The implicit models are used to calculate the solvation energy ∆Gsolvation term of a macromolecule
(protein, DNA, etc.). It is the energy diﬀerence between having the molecule in vacuum and in
an implicit solvent. Implicit water models replaces the explicit water with a continuum dielectric

There are two approaches for collecting the ensembles of the bound and unbound state of the
receptor-ligand complex. Ensembles of the complex, receptor and ligand can be extracted from a
single simulation called single trajectory protocol (STP). Alternatively a multiple trajectory protocol
(MTP) evaluates separate simulations of the combined receptor-ligand, isolated receptor and isolated
ligand. In the STP approach it is assumed that there will be no signiﬁcant conformational change
between the bound and unbound complex. The implication of this is that conformational/potential
energy terms in the molecular mechanics energy (EMM) will cancel out. This can lead to an over sta-
bilization of the binding, since the unbound states of the receptor and ligand may assume perturbed
conformations [71]. If there are signiﬁcant conformational changes between the bound and unbound
complex, MTP is preferred as it will take the free non-interacting states of the receptor and ligand
for reference in Equation (4.5). This approach is however more computationally demanding, since it
requires simulation ensembles of the three simulations.
4.3.3
Entropy in MM-PBSA
The entropy of the solute (receptor and ligand) is not treated in the implicit solvent methods [71].
Thus some of the energetic contributions to the systems is not included. This can be considered
assuming biological molecules obey a rigid rotor model, allowing usage of statistical physics to
estimate the translational and rotational entropy [70, 71].
Vibrational energy can be estimated
through two methods: Normal mode analysis (nmode) or quasi-harmonic approximation.
Normal Mode Analysis
To calculate the nmode entropy each structure of each frame is minimized to a local minimum
using the Amber module nab. The values are then inserted into a Hessian Matrix, consisting of n
double derivatives (H(f)ij(x) = DiDjf(x)), with i being the diﬀerentiation with respect to the i’th
arguments. The vibrational energies (eigenvalues) can be calculated by diagonalizing the matrix.
The nmode approach is computationally heavy due to the scale increase with system size (of (3N)3),
and it is also required to be solved for each frame. Unfortunately the nmode is not implemented for
MM-GBSA calculations with QM included terms, and will thus not be used in this project [71].
Quasi-Harmonic Approximation
The quasi-harmonic approximation (QHA) of the entropy [80] is less computationally demanding
than the nmode.
The chosen frames are aligned to an average structure and a mass weighted
covariance matrix of all atoms is calculated from these frames and then diagonalized to obtain pseudo-
vibrational frequencies. This approach is the same as the PCA, but diﬀers by being mass weighted
and by proceeding to ﬁnd the pseudo-vibrational frequencies by classical statistical mechanics [75, 81].
The QHA of entropy can however cause problems for an unconverged simulation [82], since the global

a large mixture of motional noise that is not describing the main motions of the system, and will
result in unreliable entropies.

Binding Energies
A directly quantitative analysis for comparison of the selectivity and aﬃnity of ions is to use the
MMPBSA.py script implemented in Amber14.
If such an analysis was proven reliable it would
simplify all further testing and screening of peptides and proteins in the search for the optimal
macromolecule for phosphate antions.

When calculating the binding free energy, the complex structure may enable the peptide to take
strained conformations that otherwise would be an unfavourable conﬁguration. The STP approach
would extract the strained conformation of the peptide and subtract its energy from the complex
structure, however it would have a much larger energy than the unstrained peptide, thus the binding
free energy would be underestimated. Such strained conﬁgurations have been seen previously in
Figure 5.15 of the zipper conformation that allows intra-molecular hydrogen bonds, by the ion
placing itself as an extra joint between the N terminus (Ser) and the Lys and/or Thr. This fact
argues in the favour of the MTP approach, where an independent simulation of the peptide (without
ion) is subtracted from the complex energy in terms of free energy.
To make a test of the actual diﬀerence between the two approaches the four cases are compared in
Table 5.2. There is a large diﬀerence in the enthalpy, ∆H between the STP and MTP. This means
that the peptide is strained with ≈11 kcal/mol when binding the ion compared to its unbound
relaxed state, suggesting that the MTP model is the correct to use. However the binding free energy,
∆G, also diﬀers signiﬁcantly with approximately +11 kcal/mol. This must mean that the receptor
strain is not compensated for in the binding of the ion.
Indeed, this is strange since the ion is
observed to bind in the simulations. The only explanation is that the QM interface is not correctly
calculated by the MM-GBSA model. As mentioned in section 3.4.2, the results from the previous
project of implicit models with the QM/MM hybrid method have not been successful [23]. This was
found to probably arise from the MM/QM interface when using implicit water models, and was also
likely related to the polarization of the phosphate ions. This can be identiﬁed by looking into the
MM-GBSA values for the H2PO−

Conclusions and Outlook
This chapter will summarize the results obtained in the experimental and computational studies in
section 6.1, and then use this for suggesting future approaches for enhancing the peptide or ﬁnding
new proteins, which is done in Section 6.2. A possible application design is discussed in Section 6.3.

6.1
Conclusion of the Study
The relevant considerations of a phosphate recovery application are the ”green conditions” that
include low energy consumption, low contamination and low material consumption in the capturing
process. These things are believed to be fulﬁlled by a reversible binding by proteins. Therefore
this project investigated the phosphate binding by a hexa-peptide with the sequence SGAGKT, by
experimental and computational methods.
Radioactive phosphate experiments were performed by using micro-ﬁlters to retain bound peptide-
phosphate complexes. The peptide aﬃnity for phosphates was found to be too low to be detected by
the method. The weak binding was conﬁrmed by calculating the expected complex concentration,
which was below the possible measuring limit. An estimation of the required aﬃnity was made, and
it was found that the dissociation constant should be on the order of around 10−8 M to use the
experimental technique. This furthermore led to the conclusion that an application for phosphate
recovery using peptides or proteins presumably requires a much larger aﬃnity than that of the hexa-
peptide. This is a discovery of extreme importance to keep progressing the search for an optimal
phosphate binding protein.
The peptide was studied by a molecular dynamics/quantum mechanical hybrid simulation to
evaluate the binding mechanism and methods that would be suitable for studying the phosphate
binding. Three methods were used for analysis to gain insight in the intrinsic behaviour and aﬃnity
for the phosphate and sulphate anions. These methods were clustering, principal component anal-
ysis (PCA) and free binding energy calculations by MMPBSA. The results of clustering and PCA
indicated binding aﬃnity for HPO2−
4
by cooperative binding while the other ions, H2PO−
4 , PO3−
4
and SO2−
4
seemed to have a lower binding aﬃnity based on their less cooperative behaviour. Bianchi
et al, found that only the HPO2−
4
bound at neutral pH, which could be what we are observing. They
also discovered that the binding energy of HPO2−
4
was favourable by the entropy, which related to

6.2
Future Peptides or Proteins
The combined experimental and computational results leads the torch forward for new project stud-
ies, with the objective to increase the binding energy of the peptide or ﬁnd another protein already
proven as a selective phosphate binder. These two approaches are both considered in the following.
6.2.1
Motivated Peptide Changes
Redesigning the peptide may be one way to increase the binding aﬃnity. The changes should however
be motivated and well considered. Increasing the sequence length to make a secondary structure
is believed to be able to increase binding energy of ligands [22]. The idea is to have a more rigid
structure and binding site speciﬁcally optimized for HPO2−
4
binding. This could be combined with
the insertion of hydrophobic groups, that would increase the hydrophobic surface area, which would
make it more favourable for the peptide to bind with the HPO2−
4
due to desolvation eﬀects. The
design of such a protein however is not straight forward. It requires that the hydrophobic group
is suﬃciently ”hidden” upon binding, while also being able to sustain the eﬀects of the phosphate
polarization. In a study of a globular protein the N terminus of Ala was exchanged with Phe, which
has a aromatic side group. This was proven to reduce Kd with a factor of 50.000 by hydrophobic
eﬀects [31]. This shows a large potential of improving the binding aﬃnity for ligands. However, the
peptide should still fulﬁl the demand of being selective to phosphate over sulphate and arsenate.
This is an incomprehensible and complex thought experiment to consider, and can only be studied
by MD/DFT or experiments.
The design and test of other peptide/protein structures is cumbersome to study with the cluster
and PCA as done in this project. Exactly for this reason it was hoped that the MM-PBSA method
would be reliable, since this method could have the potential for ”screening” diﬀerent peptide-
phosphate interactions to give a quick measure of the aﬃnity and selectivity. The simulations would
still need to run (for the 11-20 days or more depending on the size of the system). A suitable study
in the future could thus be to investigate other polarizable force ﬁelds that does not require QM
models. This would allow the MM-GB(PB)SA method to be applied, and make a more quantitative
measure of the binding aﬃnity.
6.2.2
Phosphate Binding Proteins in Bacteria and Archaea
This section presents a literature study of other high aﬃnity phosphate binding proteins.
One
suitable candidate has been found and will be described here. It origins from organisms, such as
archaea and bacteria, especially Escherichia coli (E-coli). These organisms are living in sulphate or
arsenate rich environments and must be able to maintain their metabolism by selectively uptaking
the phosphate and discriminate the competing ions.
In these organisms there are two types of
phosphate transport, phosphate inorganic transport (Pit) and phosphate speciﬁc transport (Pst)
[83]. Pit transports divalent metal cation complexed with a phosphate anion having a high phosphate
concentration [20]. It is a low speciﬁcity and low aﬃnity system. The other transport system is the
Pst, operating at low phosphate concentrations and high arsenate concentrations [20]. If the inorganic
74
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND OUTLOOK
P concentration is low, such that the Pit cannot supply suﬃcient amount of phosphate to the E-coli,
the Pst system is synthesized. Organic phosphates are transported from the outer membrane into
the periplasma of the cell, and then converted into inorganic phosphate by alkaline phosphatase
[83]. The Pst is responsible for transporting the inorganic phosphate into the cytoplasma of the
E-coli. It is an ATP-binding cassette transporter (ABC-transporter) that is composed of 5 proteins;
2 membrane permeases (PstA and PstC), 2 ATPases (PstB) and the high-aﬃnity phosphate binding
protein, PstS (or Phosphate-binding protein, PBP) [20, 83]. Here the PstS binds the phosphate in
the periplasma and transfers the phosphate to the transmembrane proteins PstA and PstC. The
transport through these is ATP-fuelled by the PstB. A schematic of this process is shown in Figure
6.1.
Figure 6.1: Phosphate speciﬁc transport (Pst) illustration. (a) the PBP has three conformations I:

it can translocate the phosphate to the PstA and PstC transmembrane proteins. (b) The PstA and

The PBP aﬃnity for phosphate and selectivity over arsenate and sulphate has been well-documented
as well as the governing mechanisms of the binding as explained in the following.
The PBP wild type of E-coli is a 321 residue protein, that consist of two globular domains hinged
together by short segments, forming a cleft in between [85, 84]. The crystal structure can be seen
on Figure 6.2. The cleft is known to have a large negative potential that falsely suggests repulsion
of anions [86]. As the ligand enters the cleft (binding site) it displaces the ordered water molecules
(that are hydrogen bonded to the polar residues of the binding site), giving rise to a favourable
entropy. The ligand preferentially binds to one domain of the cleft which, coupled with the complete
dehydration of water molecules, induces a bending motion of the hinge between the two domains.
This causes the other domain to participate in the phosphate binding and results in an entrapment
of the ligand, which can be deﬁned as a ligand-closed conformation [84]. Eﬀectively this creates two
possible conformations when the ligand is bound: Ligand-open and ligand-closed [85, 84]. In the
ligand-closed conformation, the PBP releases the phosphate upon interaction with the membrane
bound proteins (PstA and PstC) [84] as is also illustrated in Figure 6.1.

Due to their high aﬃnity and large selectivity for phosphate the PBPs have been recognized for use

from E-coli was labelled with two rhodamines.

the phosphate biosensor. As a part of the sensor development, they investigated the binding and
dissociation constants. The Kd,1 = 1/K1 = 2.2 µM of the ligand open conformation, when the PBP
closes the overall dissociation constant is 70 nM [90]. In the closed conformation the protein reﬂects
a 6.6 s−1 dissociation rate constant.

6.3
Application Device
A potential device design for phosphate recovery is presented here. The basic principle is to have
a ﬂow of sludge or waste water containing phosphate ﬂowing by suspended proteins, see Figure
6.5. The phosphate is then captured by the proteins or peptides (represented as pacmans). As the
proteins are saturated, a clean inlet ﬂows through and drags the dissociated phosphates away from
the proteins, and into the outlet where it can be collected.
Figure 6.5: Sketch showing the principle idea of a phosphate recovery device. Proteins (represented
by Pacmans) are ﬁxed in a matrix system that allows the ﬂow of a ﬂuid. First a sludge or waste
water inlet leads the phosphate rich solution into the system. The phosphates binds in the proteins
as it passes through them. The sludge is lead out of the outlet again. Once the proteins are saturated,
an inlet of clean water enters the system. The clean water drags the dissociated phosphates away as
it exits the clean water phosphate outlet.
In practice it is probably diﬃcult to extract the bound phosphates. If the proteins (such as the
PBP) have a very low dissociation constant, they are not easily dissociated. Recall the the PBPs
released the phosphate upon contact with the transmembrane proteins, and this system must prob-
ably also induce a perturbation in order to release them. Such a perturbation could be mechanical,
79
==PAGE:92==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND OUTLOOK
a temperature increase or a chemical change of the solution such as pH. The perturbation depends
on the system matrix, and should ideally fulﬁl the green conditions as well as possible. The matrix
could be fabricated of polymers able to incorporate the proteins, or it could consist of a hydrogel
that upon perturbation can change its shape. A mechanical perturbation that would ”force” the
PBPs to the ”ligand-open” conformation is imagined here. The energy required for such a pertur-
bation is however very diﬃcult is predict. A temperature increase is also known to cause complexes
to dissociate, however this approach is not environmentally considerate. A chemical release could
potentially be feasible, if a fertilizing compatible solution could drag and cause dissociation of the
phosphates this would be a suitable solution.
The actual implementation of such a device is of course also extremely complex. How can the
system avoid being clogged with ”dirt” of the sludge/waster water while maintaining a high-ﬂow
rate? What is the optimal cycle of saturating the proteins, removing all the dirty water and ﬁlling
in the clean water for phosphate removal? These are just a few glaring questions accommodated
with the system, and is probably also related with any other device design one could think of. While
these questions cannot be answered, they would need comprehensive studies on a long-term time
scale. The ﬁrst step is to test for the most suitable protein through experimental and computational
studies. This will be pointed out now.
6.4
Outlook, Perspective and Future Studies
In the course of this project some important question marks were answered, but a new set of questions
and challenges have also been revealed. These challenges are brieﬂy presented for the experimen-
tal and computational point of view, but are mutually cooperating for the progress of biomimetic
phosphate recovery.
Computational
Polarizable Force Field:
A polarizable force ﬁeld, without QM, could potentially provide suf-
ﬁcient accuracy for the receptor-phosphate interaction. Such a force ﬁeld could be tested on the
peptide-phosphate system for comparison with the current studies using hybrid MD/QM. This
method would speed up the simulations as the computationally demanding QM calculation could be
omitted. A few of these polarizable force ﬁelds are described in the Amber14 Reference Manual [46].
If the force ﬁeld seems to yield reliable results the MM-GB(PB)SA binding free energy method
could be used for evaluation of the binding energy. If reasonable results could be obtained, one could
take the step further and try to ﬁnd binding energies of protein candidates, for example the PBPs.
With this technique proteins can easily be ”screened” in the search for the most optimal protein.
Low Barrier Hydrogen Bonds:
In the master thesis of Thomas Østergaard, he discovered a
LBHB with the HPO2−
4
anion accepting an Ser hydrogen of the NH+
3 group [24]. While this has not
been considered in the report, it could be interesting to investigate the MD treatment of this. This
could also be extended even further to evaluate the PBPs selectivity for phosphate over arsenate,
that was believed to be governed by a LBHB where an Asp residue accepted the phosphate hydrogen.

E-coli Protein expression:

Appendix D
Implicit Models
D.1
Implicit Molecular Dynamics Simulations
Implicit water models can be used to avoid water molecules from simulations. This reduces the
number of atoms in the system, and therefore the total simulation time. Instead having the explicit
water molecules in the system, it is necessary to have an eﬀective dielectric constant, ϵ, replacing
the water molecules. To do this eﬀectively, one must ﬁnd correct energy functions to describe the
energy suitably.
D.1.1
Generalized Born Method
By replacing the water molecules with a dielectric solvent continuum electrostatics model, the sol-
vation energy now become a part of the total energy of the system. The total solvation free energy
of a molecule can be composed into two parts of the non-bonding term (fourth term) of Eq. (3.9):
∆Gsolv = ∆Gel + ∆Gnonel,
(D.1)
where the subscripts account for electrostatic and non-electrostatic (previously PB(GB) and SASA in
Section 4.3). The non-electrostatic term contains the van der Waals attraction between the molecule
and the solute, plus the hydrophobic entropic energy cost of rearanging water molecules. This energy
is approximated by being proportional with the accessible solute molecule surface area. Due to the
surface area proportionality, this term is fairly easy to calculate [48].
The electrostatic free energy contains Coulombic interactions and hydrogen bonding, and is
more diﬃcult to evaluate. Traditionally it has been calculated by the Poisson-Boltzman distribution
equation, and needs to be solved for every new conformation change of a molecule. To avoid this
re-calculation, Amber have implemented the generalized Born (GB) method that can estimate the
electrostatic free energy [46].
The GB model treats each atom as a hard sphere with radius Ri and charge qi at its center. The
surrounding material solvent has a high dielectric constant of ϵ = 80. The electrostatic free energy
is then approximated by
