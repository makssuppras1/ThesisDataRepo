Abstract 
Waste incineration has been one of the most commonly practiced technologies for waste 
management in industrialized countries (Christensen, 2011), due to its technological reliability and 
high energy recovery capability. The study evaluated and compared a promising WtE technology 
(gasification and melting technology) with a conventional mass-burning incineration with the Danish 
state-of-the-art technologies, on environmental perspective. Consequential LCA methodology was 
applied for their evaluation with use of EASETECH, which is a LCA program well-suited for 
evaluating waste management systems. As an example of gasification technology, Direct Melting 
System (DMS) that was developed in Japan was modeled for the study, since the system has been 
one of the commonly used WtE technologies and has the greatest number of references among 
gasification technologies in Japan. The technology has a capability to process various kinds of waste 
in addition to municipal solid waste (MSW), which is ensured by high-temperature gasification 
process with use of coal coke. Also high-quality slag has been reutilized as aggregates for 
construction even under Japanese strict regulation on bottom ashes/slag (JISC, 2005). In contrast, the 
use of coke in the technology is considered to have negative impacts on the environment.  
The objective of the study is to assess and compare the environmental performance of incineration 
and gasification technology by means of comparative LCA, and to evaluate quantitatively the 
trade-off relationship addressed in the above. In order to assess the flexibility of gasification 
technology in terms of waste types to be processed, the waste which are treated with gasification 
technology but may not be processed with incineration technology were addressed in addition to 
residual MSW. As examples of such waste, Automotive Shredder Residue (ASR) and residues from 
Construction and Demolition Waste (i.e. C&DW residue) were chosen. The environmental 
performance of the treatment of such waste in gasification technology was evaluated by comparing 
to the status-quo waste management (the combination of incineration of MSW and landfilling of 
other waste). Three different geographic regions were considered: Denmark, Italy, and Japan. These 
were selected because they represent different conditions with respect to typical energy recovery and 
to the surrounding energy system where the waste technology is placed. In addition to this, two time 
horizons were considered: a short- (2020-2030) and a long-term (2030-2050) to address the possible 
changes associated with the surrounding energy system. 
The LCIA results showed that the incineration scenarios have environmentally better performance 
within most of the selected impact categories than the gasification scenarios both in the short-term 
and long-term perspectives when processing only MSW. When considering the treatment/disposal of 
ASR and C&DW residue together with MSW, the gasification scenarios performed environmentally 
better than the status-quo management (i.e. the combination of incineration of MSW and landfilling 
of ASR/C&DW) in more number of impact categories compared to the scenarios where only MSW 
is treated. It was indicated that the gasification technology is more beneficial for the co-gasification 
==PAGE:4==
 
ii 
 
of some specific waste, such as ASR, C&DW residue, and maybe other hazardous waste. The 
scenarios where ASR/C&DW residue was co-combusted in incinerators showed that it may have 
smaller environmental impacts compared to both the status-quo waste management and 
co-gasification scenarios in most of the selected impact categories. The development of 
co-combustion technology and its relevant technologies (i.e. pretreatment) should be promoted in 
order to improve environmental performances of the waste management system studied. 
From sensitivity analysis, it was found that energy consumption/recovery processes are the most 
sensitive parameters for the both technologies. Other than those parameters, substitution ratio of iron 
scrap in the incineration scenario and consumption of limestone and coke in the gasification scenario 
were ones of the dominant parameters. According to a contribution analysis, emission of NOx was 
found to be the most influential parameter in most of the non-toxic impact categories, and 
significance of the impacts associated with NOx emission was relatively higher in the long-term 
scenarios. This means the future effort should be focused on reduction of NOx emission to improve 
environmental performances for the both WtE technologies (i.e. incineration and gasification). Also, 
releases of mercury and zinc to air were ones of the most dominant parameters in the toxic impact 
categories, although their magnitude of significance was small relatively to that in other impact 
categories. Wet Flue Gas Cleaning (FGC) system and activated carbon filter equipped in the 
incineration scenarios were considered to have large contribution to reduction of these emissions. In 
a threshold analysis, upgrading of syngas and energy recovery with combined cycle gas turbine 
(CCGT) technology could be a solution for the gasification scenario to achieve the same or higher 
level of environmental performance as the incineration. From a scenario analysis, it was confirmed 
that the transport of solid residues does not carry significant impacts compared to other processes.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Outline of Waste-to-Energy technologies 
With the increasing awareness for need of Circular Economy, European Commission has developed a 
concrete and ambitious program of action with measures covering the whole cycle of products, and 
higher target for recycling in Europe has been proposed (European Commission, 2016). However, it 
must be accepted that a unique management option cannot be suitable for all waste streams (Arena, 
et al., 2015). Although most fractions of solid waste can be reused, recycled or biologically treated, 
there are some waste fractions which cannot follow this pathway and should be treated to maximize 
the recovery of energy and resources (Arena, et al., 2015). Hence, the thermo-chemical treatments of 
solid waste such as incineration technology are considered to be still necessary even in the future 
waste management where the Circular Economy is well established. 
Incineration of waste has been practiced for more than a century in industrialized countries, and the 
incineration technology has been developed dramatically from basic mass and volume reduction and 
the solution for hygienic problems to modern Waste to Energy (WtE) plants with highly developed 
emission control systems (Christensen, 2011). Moving grate combustion process is today recognized 
as a sustainable waste management option that makes it possible to obtain a significant amount of 
energy and a considerable waste volume reduction (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). There were 455 WtE 
plants across European 18 countries in 2012 (CEWEP, 2012), and approximately 64.4 million tons of 
waste were processed in European 28 countries in 2014 (eurostat, 2014). The incineration has been 
the most dominant technology with a great number of references among WtE technologies in the 
world.  
The gasification and melting technology of waste is a promising technology, and one of the most 
commonly applied WtE technologies in Japan. When comparing to the conventional mass-burning 
waste incineration technology, the advantages of the gasification and melting technology are found in 
waste flexibility, high-quality recyclables and lower emissions of toxic substances in flue gas. The 
technology includes a trade-off associated with the use of coke. The high-temperature gasification 
with use of coal coke makes it possible to process various kinds of waste, such as Automotive 
Shredder Residue (ASR), Construction and Demolition Waste (C&DW) residues, asbestos, 
fluorocarbons, sewage sludge from wastewater treatment plants and clinical waste and so forth, 
together with Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) (co-gasification) (Tanigaki, et al., 2015). Also, the 
collected solid residues such as slag and metal are preferable to be reutilized due to their physical 
and chemical properties (Arena, et al., 2015). However, it has some drawbacks; the use of coal coke 
and lower net energy recovery efficiency may lead to high environmental impacts. 
In this study, the word “gasification” refers to the technologies of gasification and melting of waste, 
which is classified as an atmospheric moving gasifier, especially developed by Nippon Steel and

Sumikin Engineering Co., Ltd in Japan.  
1.2 
Relevant literatures (literature review) 
In this subsection the researches relevant to this study are presented, in order to clarify what has been 
known so far and what is not. Although the number of references that address the comparison 
between the two technologies is limited, several literatures are available to see a current development 
on this field. 
Arena and Gregorio (2013) investigated the element partitioning in combustion- and 
gasification-based WtE units (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). They collected and processed data from 
several moving grate conventional incinerators and high-temperature shaft gasifiers with direct 
melting under commercial operation worldwide, and a material and substance flow analysis was 
developed (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). It was shown that bottom ashes from combustion based WtE 
units (i.e. incineration) cannot be considered as immediately recyclable due to its heterogeneous 
composition and physical properties, while slag from gasification based WtE units could be assumed 
as immediately and completely recyclable because of their characteristic on element distribution; 
slag composition is dominated by Si, Ca, Mg and Al. The low-boiling-point heavy metals (e.g. Pb 
and Zn) are almost completely transferred to gas phase and then caught as fly ash, and the 
high-boiling-point metals (e.g. Cu and Fe) are mainly concentrated in metal stream and then 
completely reused (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). The study showed that the investigated 
gasification-based technology allows a reduction up to about 65% of the amount of waste landfilled 
compared with the conventional combustion-based WtE units (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). 
A previous study on the life cycle assessment comparing incineration and gasification technologies 
showed that, for the treatment of MSW, the environmental performances of moving grate 
incineration are better within most of the selected impact categories (e.g. global warming, 
non-renewable energy and etc.) (Arena, et al., 2015). The study was based on the LCA methodology 
of “Impact 2002+” and MSW alone was assumed to be treated in the both technologies. The study 
also revealed that, in the impact category of “non carcinogens”, the handling of bottom ashes from 
incinerators (i.e. 40% landfilled and 60% utilized) carries significant environmental impacts, whereas 
the handling of slag and metal has negative impact scores (i.e. savings) in that impact category. 
(Arena, et al., 2015) 
Osada (2011) studied on the comparison between the two technologies especially for the ASR 
mono-treatment and co-gasification of asbestos with ASR. In the scenarios of ASR mono-treatment, 
landfilling of ASR had better environmental performance compared to gasification technology on 
global warming, acidification, chronic toxicity and terrestrial ecotoxicity, while the landfilling 
scenario performed worse on carcinogens, water ecotoxicity and land occupation. Overall results 
showed that ASR mono-gasification had lower environmental impacts compared to landfilling or 
incineration, judging from weighted impact scores. Also, when treating asbestos with ASR in 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
 
gasification, the environmental impacts appeared to be lower than those of landfilling. However, the 
study did not include a whole life cycle of asbestos (e.g. production, use, demolition works, transport 
and end-of-life treatment/disposal), and therefore, further investigation should be necessary. (Osada, 
2011) 
In the study of Tanigaki et al. (2015), economic aspects of landfill minimization and material 
recovery via waste co-gasification were addressed (Tanigaki, et al., 2015). The study proposed the 
new waste management scheme for a region where bottom ash and incombustibles are landfilled or 
not allowed to be recycled doe to their toxic heavy metal contents. A case-study of the application of 
gasification technology, where waste is processed with incombustible residues and incineration 
bottom ash (IBA) discharged from existing incinerators, was conducted in that study. It was shown 
that such management could contribute to minimizing the final landfill amount and had great 
possibilities maximizing material and energy recovery from waste (Tanigaki, et al., 2015).  
However, none of them listed in the above evaluated the environmental performances of the two 
types of WtE technologies that manage both MSW and other hard-to-manage waste simultaneously. 
Therefore, it is considered to be necessary to evaluate and compare those technologies in terms of 
environmental performances, when treating such waste together with MSW. Application of LCA 
seems to make it possible to quantitatively evaluate the aforementioned trade-off that the gasification 
technology has.  
1.3 
Objectives 
The objective of the study is to assess and compare the environmental performance of incineration 
and gasification technology by means of comparative LCA, and to evaluate the trade-off relationship 
addressed in the above. In order to assess the flexibility of gasification technology in terms of waste 
types to be processed, the waste which can be treated with gasification technology but may not be 
processed with incineration technology are addressed, in addition to residual municipal solid waste 
(i.e. MSW after separation of recyclables). As examples of such waste in the study, ASR and residues 
from C&DW (i.e. C&DW residue) are chosen as waste, which have high and low heating value; ASR 
and C&DW residue have lower heating value of 13.4-25 MJ/kg, and approximately 5 MJ/kg, 
respectively (Viganò, et al., 2010; López & Lodo, 2014). The environmental performance of the 
treatment of such waste in gasification technology is evaluated by comparing to the status-quo waste 
management (the combination of incineration and landfilling).  
Three different geographic regions are considered: Denmark, Italy, and Japan. These are selected 
because they represent different conditions with respect to typical energy recovery (especially 
regarding heat recovery as some of them typically do not have infrastructure for district heating) and 
to the surrounding energy system where the waste technology is placed (i.e. energy sources used to 
generate electricity and heat). In addition to this, two time horizons are considered: a short- 
(2020-2030) and a long-term (2030-2050) to address the possible changes associated with the

surrounding energy system, i.e. type of energy sources (more or less fossil C-intensive) used to 
generate electricity and heat. This indeed, on the basis of the (extensive) existing literature on life

performance of WtE technologies.

2.2 
Automotive Shredder Residue (ASR) 
The amount of end-of-life vehicles (ELVs) worldwide reached over 40 million units per year in 2010 
(Sakai, et al., 2014). The management of ELVs is addressed using a series of different strategies in 
many countries (Cossu & Lai, 2015). In Europe, the Directive 2000/53/EC aims to minimize the 
environmental impact of ELVs by defining procedures on the design of vehicles, the requirements for 
collection and treatment facilities of ELVs, the targets for the reuse of some components, the 
recycling of secondary materials and energy recovery (Cossu, et al., 2014). The Directive raises the 
target of a total reuse and recovery rate of 95 wt% of an ELV from 2015 (Cossu, et al., 2014). It is 
considered that the management of ASR is a key issue in order to achieve the target. 
ASR is the remaining fraction after depollution, dismantling and shredding processes of ELVs 
(Cossu & Lai, 2015), and it accounts for about 20-22 wt% of an ELV, which is a heterogeneous 
material (Cossu, et al., 2014). As shown in Table 1, ASR has high contents of ash (30%) and high 
heating value (19.2 MJ/kg). It also contains high amounts of chlorine, heavy metals, fine particles 
and Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs), which puts technical difficulties to the application of 
conventional incineration technologies (Cossu, et al., 2014). A high content of fine particles together 
with high heating value leads to clogging at grates of incinerator. Also, high concentration of 
chlorine in ASR causes high concentration of acid gas in flue gas, which may damage the 
equipments of boiler and Flue Gas Cleaning (FGC) systems. High concentration of heavy metals in 
ASR is considered to be transferred to Incineration Bottom Ash (IBA).

3. Goal and Scope definition 
3.1 
Goal definition 
3.1.1 Intended application and target audience 
The objective of the study is to assess and compare the environmental performance of incineration 
and gasification technology by means of comparative LCA, and to evaluate quantitatively the 
trade-off relationship addressed in Section 1.1. In order to assess the flexibility of gasification 
technology in terms of waste types to be processed, the waste which are treated with gasification 
technology but may not be processed with incineration technology are addressed, in addition to 
residual MSW. As examples of such waste, ASR and residues from C&DW are chosen in the study, 
and the environmental performance of the treatment of such waste in gasification technology is 
evaluated by comparing to the status-quo waste management (the combination of incineration and 
landfilling). Also, the study aims to evaluate in which context one technology may be potentially 
favorable to the other. 
Three different geographic regions are considered: Denmark, Italy, and Japan. These were selected 
because they represent different conditions with respect to typical energy recovery (especially 
regarding heat recovery as some of them typically do not have infrastructure for district heating) and 
to the surrounding energy system where the waste technology is placed (i.e. energy sources used to 
generate electricity and heat). In addition to this, two time horizons were considered: a short- 
(2020-2030) and a long-term (2030-2050) to address the possible changes associated with the 
surrounding energy system, i.e. type of energy sources (more or less fossil C-intensive) used to 
generate electricity and heat. This indeed, on the basis of the (extensive) existing literature on life 
cycle assessment of WtE systems, is well-known to be a crucial factor in ultimately determining the 
performance of WtE technologies.  
The main target audience of the study is the Department of Environmental Engineering at the 
Technical University of Denmark. Also the stakeholders of WtE technologies are target audiences, 
since they may influence future decisions with regard to selection of waste management 
technologies. 
3.1.2 Method  
The LCA is conducted in agreement with the ISO standards (ISO 14040 and ISO 14044). The 
modeling follows a consequential approach. 
3.1.3 Decision-context situation 
The outcome of the LCA study might affect the selection of waste management technologies, 
although the study only covers some specific waste and locations. No structural/large scale 
consequences on the processes of background systems are expected, and therefore, the 
==PAGE:19==
 
10 
 
decision-context for the study is classified as Situation A (“Micro-level decision support”) in 
accordance with the ILCD handbook (European Commission, 2010).

3.2.2  Handling of multifunctional processes 
The main multifunctional processes are the electricity and heat recovered from the WtE processes, 
and they are credited by substituting “marginal” electricity and heat at the specific location and time 
horizon under study (see details in the following sections). Also, recovered solid resources, such as 
iron scrap, aluminum scrap, slag and metal, are credited by displacing corresponding production 
from virgin materials. The details are further explained in Section 3.4.4. 
3.2.3 Model descriptions ~EASETECH~ 
The model of each scenario in this study is built with EASETECH (Environmental Assessment 
System for Environmental TECHnologies). EASETECH is a well suited modeling program for LCA 
of waste management systems, which has been developed by the Technical University of Denmark 
since 2001. EASETECH models are set up in building blocks connected to each other. These blocks 
represent different unit processes or technologies. Material transfer and elementary exchanges are

4. Inventory analysis 
4.1 
Gasification technology 
4.1.1 General information on the gasification and melting furnace 
Gasification is the conversion of any carbonaceous fuel to a gaseous product with a usable heating 
value (Higman & Van der Burgt, 2003). Gasification is widely used for energy conversion from coal 
and biomass, and has been researched to apply MSW and biomass all over the world (Tanigaki, et al., 
2012). However the application of gasification technology for MSW is much less than conventional 
incineration technology in Europe (Arena & Gregorio, 2013). 
In Japan, there are many gasification plants for MSW under commercial operation, and it has been 
one of the most common WtE technologies. The Direct Melting System (DMS) is a shaft-furnace 
type gasification and melting process and is classified as an atmospheric moving bed gasifier 
(Tanigaki, et al., 2012), which has the largest number of references among different kinds of 
gasification processes in Japan (Ministry of the Environment, 2010). Therefore, DMS was chosen for 
the gasification technology to be evaluated in this study. Also it should be noted that the word 
“gasification” in the study refers to the combination of gasification and melting technologies.  
One of underlying factors for a popularity of the technology in Japan is social circumstance, such as 
strict regulations on handling of IBA and limited capacity of landfill. In Japan, IBA is not allowed to 
be utilized for construction by the regulation and generally dumped into landfill after some 
treatments (e.g. ash melting technologies). Also, reduction of final disposal amount has been a major 
concern and one of the main objectives of treating MSW (Tanigaki, et al., 2012). The lifetime of 
landfill sites for MSW was estimated to be only 19.7 years in 2012 (Ministry of the Environment 
government of Japan, 2012). Thus, the DMS, where only FA is transferred to landfill, has been 
chosen by many municipalities in Japan.  
Another factor for the spread of the DMS is its flexibility in terms of waste types to be processed. 
The high-temperature gasification technology ensured by the use of coke makes it possible to process 
various kinds of waste. In some plants, IBA from other incineration plants located nearby, shredded 
combustible and incombustible residues from a recycling facility are treated in the DMS in order to 
reduce the final disposal amount (Tanigaki, et al., 2012). Also the technology can process hazardous 
waste such as asbestos, clinical waste, ASR and fluorocarbons with MSW, and can recover both 
energy and material from waste in one process (Tanigaki, et al., 2015). Some municipalities which do 
not have enough capacity of landfill sites applies the technology to feed the waste dug up from their 
landfill, in order to make new leeway in their landfill. 
4.1.2 Process description (Tanigaki, et al., 2012)  
The processes of the DMS and the schematic view of a gasifier are described in Figure 9 and Figure 
==PAGE:35==
 
26 
 
10, respectively. The main components of the system are a MSW charging system, a gasifier, a 
combustion chamber, a boiler and a FGC system. One of the advantages of DMS is that no 
pretreatment of MSW, such as size reduction, is required, which differentiates from other gasification 
processes such as a fluidized bed gasification process. Thus, MSW is directly fed into a gasifier from 
the top together with coal coke and limestone. The coal coke is used as a reducing agent and works 
as a high-temperature grate at the bottom of the gasifier. The limestone is added to regulate a 
viscosity of the melt (i.e. liquid slag) so that slag can be smoothly discharged from the bottom of the 
gasifier without any clogging. The gasifier of DMS is categorized in an updraft gasifier, and 
oxygen-rich air (typically 36% of oxygen concentration) is blown from the tuyere equipped at the 
bottom of the gasifier. The MSW fed into the gasifier is firstly preheated and dried at an upper part of 
the gasifier. Then volatile solids (VS) of MSW are thermally decomposed gradually and syngas is 
discharged from the top of the gasifier, while MSW goes down in the gasifier. The syngas, which 
mainly consists of carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, methane, hydrogen, hydrocarbons and nitrogen, 
is transferred to the combustion chamber in the downstream of the gasifier, and it is completely 
combusted. The char generated from VS and inert fractions of MSW descend to the bottom part of 
the gasifier, and the former is partially oxidized and the latter is melted with the heat of coke burning. 
The gasification reactions such as solution-loss, water-gas-shift and water-gas reactions take place in 
this zone. The melts are intermittently discharged from a tap hole, which is equipped at the bottom of 
the gasifier, and subsequently quenched with water. They are magnetically separated into slag and 
metal. The pictures of the slag and the metal recovered in the DMS are shown in Figure 11.

4.1.3 The gasification processes modeled in the study 
The syngas generated from a gasifier is burned in a combustion chamber and the energy from waste 
is recovered within a plant in the conventional DMS in Japan. However, the syngas would be able to 
be upgraded and its containing energy could be recovered in a better efficiency in the future, and thus 
the gasification processes are modeled in two steps; “gasifier” and “combustion of syngas”. The 
FGC system is included in the modeled process of “combustion of syngas”. The dry FGC system 
was applied, where slaked lime (Ca(OH)2) is injected for desulfurization before a baghouse. 
Activated Carbon (AC) is also injected to remove low-boiling point metals and Persistent Organic 
Pollutants (POPs) from flue gas. For NOx reduction, a Selective Non Catalytic Reduction (SNCR) is 
considered to be used, where ammonia is directly injected into a combustion chamber. The FA 
recovered from a boiler and baghouse is considered to be transferred from the system without any 
post-treatment such as solidification with chelate agent.

 The use of combustion technologies (i.e. incineration) may be hindered by the fact that ASR has 
extremely high calorific value and frequently contains high amounts of fine particles, chlorine,

 High contents of fine particles and high heating value of ASR may lead to clogging at grates due

 High concentration of hydrochloric acid in flue gas caused by high chlorine content in ASR may

into FA.

5. Impact assessment results and interpretation 
5.1 
Normalized results 
In this section, normalized results are presented. This way of presenting results was chosen in order 
to compare results across all the impact categories so to evaluate the significance of environmental 
impacts relative to a reference situation. Characterized results are shown in Appendix A. 
5.1.1 Treatment of MSW 
Figure 18 shows the normalized impact scores for the scenario of MSW, “DK 2020-2030”. The letter 
“I” and “G” in the figures hereafter stand for incineration scenario and gasification scenario, 
respectively. The unit of normalized impact scores is presented in milli Person Equivalent (mPE), 
which means an annual contribution of an average person in the reference system to a given impact. 
The grouped contribution named “Energy production” consists of the WtE processes, and “Ash 
treatment” consists of the treatment/disposal of IBA, FA, slag and metal.  
When looking at the results for Denmark, the incineration scenario had lower environmental impacts 
compared to gasification in the impact categories of GW, TA, PM and HT(NC). The use of coal coke 
contributed a positive impact score (i.e. an environmental load) of gasification scenario in GW. 
Higher emissions to air in the gasification process made the impact scores of HT(NC) larger than that 
of the incineration process. This point will be further explained in Section 5.2 (Contribution analysis). 
In the impact category of ME, PO, HT(C), ET and RD, the environmental performances were almost 
the same between the two WtE processes.  
A big difference between the Danish and the Italian scenario was that CHP was assumed for the 
Danish scenario while power-only recovery was assumed for the Italian one. Also, in Italy, the 
marginal electricity was considered to be the power generated from natural gas with combined cycle 
process, whereas it was coal power in Denmark. Therefore, the credited impact scores from energy 
recovery (i.e. savings) were smaller in the Italian scenario than those of the Danish one. As a result, 
the net impact score of gasification on RD was higher than that of incineration. Also, the net impact 
of the gasification on GW became positive value, which means that the emissions of greenhouse 
gases from the processes outweigh the effect of substitution of marginal (fossil) energy.  
Although IBA generated from the incineration was disposed into mineral landfill while slag from the 
gasification was reutilized, the environmental impacts of the difference between the management of 
those solid residues were relatively small compared to other processes such as energy production and 
energy recovery. However, another LCA study on comparison of incineration and gasification shows 
that the difference of the management between IBA and slag leads to a large difference in the impact 
scores on HT(NC); a disposal of IBA has 20 mPE higher impact scores than slag reutilization. 
Therefore, it should be noted that the environmental impacts of IBA landfilling on toxic impact 
categories may be largely affected by the composition of waste, the modeling method, 
==PAGE:49==

characterization factors and normalization references used in the study. 
The results of the Japanese scenario were similar to those of the Italian one. The only difference 
between the two was the marginal electricity used in the models. Although they both used electricity 
from natural gas with combined cycle process, different inventories were used according to their 
location. As a result, the credits from the energy recovery process on HT(C) and ET for the both WtE 
technologies were larger than those in Italy. However, the rank between the two WtE technologies 
stayed the same.

5.1.2 Treatment/disposal of MSW+ASR 
Figure 20 shows the results for treatment/disposal of MSW+ASR. In this figure, black circles 
represent net impact scores for the system boundary defined in Section 3.5. Since the assumption that 
the incineration cannot process ASR may includes uncertainties, the impact scores in the case where 
ASR is co-combusted in an incinerator are shown with an error bar. The scenario of co-combustion is 
based on the assumption that there is no change in the emissions and the use of utilities from the 
treatment of only MSW. Therefore, it should be kept in mind that impacts may be underestimated or 
different.  
According to the result of the Danish scenario, incineration had less environmental impacts in two 
impact categories out of nine (GW and HT(NC)), and gasification had lower impact scores in four 
impact categories (PM, HT(C), ET and RD). Although the direct emission of fossil carbon from the 
gasification process made the impact on GW larger since all the carbon in ASR was from fossil 
origin, the energy recovery from ASR contributed to more credit. As a result, the difference in the 
environmental impact on GW between the two technologies became smaller compared to the 
scenarios of treating only MSW. In the impact category of ET, ASR landfilling had outstanding 
environmental impacts, which were mainly caused by the release of zinc and copper via the landfill 
leachate. When considering co-combustion (see the error bar in Figure 20), the impact of incineration 
on ET became environmentally better than the gasification scenario.  
For the Italian scenario, in the impact category of ME, the incineration performed environmentally 
better, in addition to GW and HT(NC). This is because the effect of energy substitution was 
relatively small in Italy, where combined-cycle natural gas fired power is used, and also because of 
higher NOx emission from co-gasification of ASR. The impacts on TA, PM, PO and HT(C) were 
almost the same between the two technologies. In this scenario, ASR landfilling had the largest

5.2 
Contribution analysis 
In this subsection, main contributing substances to each impact category were investigated for the 
two WtE technologies, as shown in Table 10. Although the main contributors were similar between 
the two in the non-toxic impact categories (GW, ME, TA, PM, PO and RD), they appeared to be 
different in the toxic impact categories (HT(C), HT(NC) and ET). In the impact category of ET, the 
copper emission to soil compartment was the third largest contributing substance in the incineration, 
which was released from IBA reutilization as construction material. When looking at HT(C), 
mercury was one of the main contributors in the gasification scenario while it is not in the 
incineration scenario. This was considered to be caused by the difference in the FGC system of the 
two technologies modeled in the study; the gasification applied dry FGC system and injection of 
activated carbon for removal of low-boiling point heavy metals, whereas wet FGC system and 
activated carbon filter was applied in the incineration. Also, in the impact category of HT(NC), the 
emission of zinc to air was one of the main contributors in the gasification, which was led by the 
same reason as the above. However, it should be noted that this difference in FGC system does not 
describe the inherent characteristics of the two technologies.

5.4 
Threshold analysis 
From the above sensitivity analysis, it appeared clear that energy recovery efficiency was the most 
important parameter discriminating the performance of the two technologies. In this subsection, a 
threshold analysis was conducted on the purpose of investigating which efficiency of electricity 
recovery the gasification processes should have, in order to achieve the same level of environmental 
performance of the incineration. The analysis was done for three impact categories (GW, ET and RD). 
The scenario of “IT 2020-2030” was chosen for this analysis, since the Italian scenarios apply the 
power-only recovery, thus simplifying the calculation and interpretation of the analysis. Figure 26 
and Figure 27 show the results of the analysis for the treatment of only MSW and MSW+C&D, 
respectively.

According to Figure 26, when the electricity recovery efficiency of the gasification rose up to 42%, 
32% and 31% in GW, ET and RD respectively, the environmental performances of the two WtE 
technologies would become the same. Similarly, from Figure 27, the efficiency became 46%, 15% 
and 28% in GW, ET and RD, respectively, the two technologies perform equally from an 
environmental perspective. The electricity recovery efficiency of more than 30% does not seem 
realistic with conventional steam-turbine system even in the long-term perspective (Danish Energy 
Agency (DEA), 2012). However, gasification technology has the potential where the generated 
syngas is not combusted but upgraded in order to make it possible to be processed in combined cycle 
gas turbine (CCGT), which has approximately 60% of the efficiency (NEDO, 2015).

5.6 
Discussion 
In this subsection, outlooks for the three different locations are explained first, and important 
technologies and system factors identified are addressed. Then, future perspectives are discussed in 
order to improve environmental performances of the WtE technologies selected in the study. Finally, 
limitations of the study and recommendations for a future study in this field are presented. 
5.6.1 Outlook for the Danish scenarios 
In the scenario for MSW only, the incineration in the short term performs environmentally better in 
four impact categories out of nine. The use of coal coke in the gasification puts environmentally 
negative effects on GW and RD. The NOx emission is found to be a key parameter across most of 
non-toxic impact categories when evaluating a WtE technology with LCIA (ME, TA, PM and PO). 
This indicates that the application of Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR), instead of SNCR modeled 
here, may improve environmental performance in those non-toxic impact categories. However, SCR 
usually requires reheating of flue gas, leading to lower energy recovery efficiency, which reduces 
environmental benefit mainly on GW and RD. The benefit from the application of SCR is considered 
to be more effective in the long-term period with renewable energy system, where benefits of energy 
substitution are very small. Also, in order to improve the SNCR process for the both WtE technology, 
the selective injection of the reducing reagent would be a possible solution. This technology consists 
of three or more sets of ammonia injection nozzles to ensure a continuous supply of ammonia 
regulated to the optimum temperature position (Hitz Hitachi Zosen Corporation, 2015). From the 
contribution analysis (see Section 5.2), wet FGC system and the activated carbon filter applied in the 
incineration scenario appears to be effective to lower the environmental impacts on the toxic impact 
categories (i.e. HT(C), HT(NC) and ET). However, this is not a technology that discriminates the 
incineration technology from the gasification. 
In the long-term perspective, the incineration is environmentally preferable to the gasification in six 
impact categories, but is worse than the gasification in two impact categories. Renewable energy is 
assumed for both electricity and heat substitution, and as a result, the processes other than energy 
recovery processes, especially energy production processes, are dominant processes across all the 
impact categories. This is significantly observed in some environmental impacts related to the 
inherent properties, such as the use of coke in GW and RD. Simultaneously, the component 
technologies such as NOx reduction and wet FGC systems are highlighted, which makes the 
incineration scenario further better. 
For the scenario of treating MSW+ASR, the gasification in the short term was environmentally 
preferable to the incineration in four impact categories out of nine, while the gasification has higher 
environmental impacts in two impact categories. This means that treatment of ASR in gasification 
gives more benefit compared to the status-quo management (ASR landfilling) in Denmark. On the 
other hand, when assuming co-combustion of ASR in the incineration likewise the gasification, the 
==PAGE:65==
 
56 
 
results show that it has potential to reduce the environmental impacts significantly on ET and RD. In 
the long-term scenario, where the environmental benefit of recovering the heat content of ASR in the 
gasification is limited, the incineration performs environmentally better in seven impact categories. 
However, the incineration scenario has significant impacts on ET as long as ASR will be still 
landfilled in 2030-2050.  
In the scenario for MSW+C&DW residue, the gasification has lower environmental impacts in three 
impact categories, while the incineration has lower impacts in two categories, in the short term. In 
the long term, the gasification is better in three categories and the incineration is better in five 
categories, in terms of environmental performance. Similarly to the scenarios of MSW+ASR, the 
number of impact categories where the incineration performs environmentally better increases when 
the marginal electricity and heat becomes to come from renewable sources. Industrialized countries 
aim to promote more and more sustainable energy in the future, and therefore, the gasification should 
introduce more efficient NOx reduction technology and FGC system for toxic substances such as 
activated carbon filter, in order to achieve the same level of environmental performance of the 
incineration modeled in the study. 
5.6.2 Outlook for the Italian scenarios 
For the scenario where only MSW is treated, the environmental performance of the incineration is 
better in six impact categories, and slightly worse in one category. Although IBA is assumed to be 
sent to landfill directly, the impacts from the landfilling of IBA is relatively small in the study. Thus, 
the net environmental impacts of the incineration on HT(NC) and ET are still lower than those of the 
gasification, where recovered slag with similar property as natural sand is used for construction 
material. However, the LCIA results in relation with IBA/slag handlings may be affected largely by 
waste composition and LCIA methodology, as aforementioned. Further investigation on this point is 
considered to be necessary. Also, it should be accepted that this study addresses only environmental 
aspects and the consideration of social aspects with regard to IBA/slag management (e.g. limited 
lifetime of remaining landfill site, difficulty of constructing new landfill and strategy of reducing 
landfilled waste in EU) are missing.  
In the MSW+ASR scenario, the incineration performs environmentally better in three impact 
categories, and the gasification performs better in two impact categories. Similarly to the Danish 
scenarios, it is found that co-combustion of ASR in incineration has large potential to improve the 
environmental performance. In the MSW+C&DW scenario, in four impact categories the 
incineration is environmentally better than the gasification, while it is worse in two categories.  
5.6.3 Outlook for the Japanese scenarios 
The outlooks of the Japanese scenarios of processing only MSW and MSW+C&DW residue are 
quite similar to the Italian one. In the MSW+ASR scenario, while the incineration performs 
environmentally better in one impact category, the gasification is better in two categories. By 
==PAGE:66==
 
57 
 
comparing with the MSW+ASR scenario, the number of the impact categories where the incineration 
has environmentally better performance is higher in the MSW+C&DW scenario (similarly to the 
scenarios for Italy). This indicates that gasification technology is more effective for 
high-energy-content waste such as ASR, because of higher benefit from substitution of such waste.  
5.6.4 Overall results 
It was found that the incineration performs environmentally better than the gasification in more 
number of impact categories when processing only MSW, and that when processing MSW+ASR the 
environmental performance of the gasification can be environmentally better than that of the 
status-quo management (i.e. incineration of MSW and landfilling of ASR) in more number of impact 
categories. Although the rank between the two technologies when treating MSW+C&D depends on 
location, the performance of the gasification relative to the incineration scenario is better than when 
processing only MSW. This indicates that the gasification technology is suitable for co-gasification 
of so called ‘hard-to-manage waste’ in terms of its environmental performance. This point is 
considered to be the potential benefit of gasification technology, especially in the short-term 
perspective. Similarly to waste selected in the study, the co-gasification of asbestos, fluorocarbons 
and combustible and incombustible residues from recycling facilities in gasification process is 
expected to have environmentally preferable effects compared to a current treatment/disposal. Also, 
co-combustion scenarios could be environmentally the best option of all the scenarios, including the 
status-quo management and co-gasification. 
5.6.5 Important technologies and system factors identified in the study 
First of all, co-combustion of ASR and C&DW residue is one of the important factors that 
significantly influence the environmental impacts. Although some advantages of the gasification 
scenarios are found when comparing with the status-quo management, the situation would alter if 
ASR and C&DW residue was co-combusted in incinerators. Similarly to the scenarios for only MSW, 
the incineration scenarios (co-combustion) perform environmentally better than the gasification 
scenarios in most of the selected impact categories. This indicates that promotion of further 
development in pretreatment of those types of waste such as screen classification and the application 
of co-combustion can improve environmental performances significantly from the status-quo 
treatment/disposal. If there still are difficulties hindering co-combustion, the application of 
gasification technology is the second best solution. 
From the sensitivity analysis (Section 5.3), it is found that energy consumption/recovery parameters 
are the most dominant processes, especially on GW and RD. This applies to the system where 
marginal electricity and heat are from non-renewable sources (compare Figure 18 and Figure 19). 
For most of the non-toxic impact categories (ME, TA and PO), control technology of NOx emission 
has the largest influence in terms of environmental performance. This point is further discussed later 
in Section 5.5.7. The difference in FGC systems between the two WtE technologies modeled in the 
==PAGE:67==
 
58 
 
study affects the environmental performances on the toxic impact categories (HT(C), HT(NC) and 
ET). The wet FGC system and the application of activated carbon filter modeled in the incineration 
scenario appear to be better than the dry FGC system and the injection of activated carbon in the 
gasification scenario, in terms of environmental performance on toxic impact categories. Also, the 
comparison between the status-quo management of ASR and C&DW residue (i.e. landfilling) and the 
co-combustion/gasification of the waste shows that the status-quo management includes significant 
environmental impacts. The latter management has much more environmental benefit than the 
former, especially on ET (zinc and copper) for ASR, and on ME (aluminum) and ET (zinc, arsenic 
and copper) for C&DW residue.  
5.6.6 Future perspectives 
The reduction in the amount of the additives such as limestone and coke used in gasification may 
give significant benefit in terms of environmental performance (see Section 5.3). In order to achieve 
this, the coke that is produced from biomass has been developed for the alternative to the 
conventionally used coal coke, and it was confirmed that the biomass coke can be effectively work in 
the gasification and melting process as well as coal coke (Kashiwabara, et al., 2012). Although the 
environmental impacts associated with its production must be taken into account on life-cycle 
perspective, impacts from the use of coke is considered to be improved significantly, especially on 
GW and RD. The evaluation based on biomass coke instead of coal coke might be interesting to 
deepen the understandings of the comparison between the two WtE technologies. Also, as an 
alternative for limestone, calcium carbonate originated from food waste (shell) can be used. This will 
also make the environmental performance of gasification technology better than the model in this 
study. 
From the results on the threshold analysis (Section 5.4), it is seen that gasification technology has the 
potential benefit to utilize syngas in more efficient way (e.g. the application of CCGT after syngas 
upgrading), so that the gasification may perform much better on the environmental point of view. 
Also, the upgraded gas could be storable and transportable. 
As shown in Section 5.5.5, the assumption in the management of ASR/C&DW residue in the 
incineration scenarios is an important factor, and co-combustion of such waste can immediately 
improve environmental performances of the studied system. Therefore, more efforts should be put on 
development of co-combustion technology in order to make the performances of incineration further 
better. 
5.6.7 Limitation of the study and recommendation for a future study 
In this subsection, recommendation for a future study related to the field of the study is presented 
according to the difficulty that the study found. The recommendation is made in order to ensure fair 
comparison of different WtE technologies. 
==PAGE:68==
 
59 
 
When comparing two or more WtE technologies, the inventories with regard to their emissions and 
the use of reagents should be based on the plants that follow the same level of regulations or 
standards. For example, the NOx emission is one of the main contributing factors in the study, but the 
amount of ammonia used to reduce the NOx emission is not sensitive parameter. This means that the 
WtE plant with lower NOx emission and higher ammonia injection would be environmentally better 
than the plant with higher emission and lower ammonia consumption, even if the FGC technology 
was fundamentally the same. In this study, the dataset of the incineration scenario is based on a 
Danish state-of-the-art incinerator following a strict regulation, while the gasification scenario uses a 
dataset with lower requirement in terms of emissions. This is because the dataset following the same 
level of regulation as the Danish one was not available in Japan (e.g. dry flue gas treatment is 
recommended by the Japanese government, due to its higher energy recovery (Ministry of the 
Environment, 2010).). Another example is that the process which is specific to gasification 
technology cannot be properly modeled, unless the inventory data of the both two technologies 
follows the same level of regulation/standard. For instance, limestone fed to adjust viscosity and 
fluidity of slag in gasification technology also has a role to neutralize acid gases generated by partial 
oxidation of waste (e.g. hydrogen chloride and sulfur dioxide), which results in lower concentration 
of acid gases in flue gas before reaching FGC system (Tanigaki, et al., 2013). However, without 
following the similar regulation/standard, the balance between acid gases emissions and the amount 
of reagent (e.g. slaked lime) cannot be correctly reflected to modeling. Therefore, a future study 
should focus more on collecting data for selected technologies that comply with similar level of 
regulation/standards. 
It should be kept in mind that the LCA-modeling of this study is not sufficient to support fair 
comparison between the two technologies as mentioned above. However, the study is considered to 
be meaningful in terms of that: 
 The study clarified in which context each of the two WtE technologies performs 
environmentally preferable. 
 The study identified the dominant parameters and important technologies used in the two WtE 
processes. 
 The study showed the potential benefits and drawbacks of the gasification technology in both 
short-term and long-term perspectives. 
 Limitations of the study and recommendations for a future study in the similar field were pointed 
out.

6. Conclusion 
The models for the incineration and the gasification were constructed for the three locations and two 
time horizons in EASETECH. The main results and important findings are concluded as follows: 
 The incineration scenarios have environmentally better performance within most of the selected 
impact categories than the gasification scenarios both in short-term and long-term perspectives 
when processing only MSW.  
 The parameters in relation with energy consumption/recovery are found to be the most dominant 
parameters in most of the selected impact categories. 
 Emission of NOx are found to be the most influential parameter in most of the non-toxic impact 
categories, and significance of the impacts associated with NOx emission is relatively higher in 
the long-term scenarios. This means that the future effort should be focused on reduction of NOx 
emission to reduce environmental impacts for the both WtE technologies. 
 Releases of mercury and zinc to air are ones of the most dominant parameters in the toxic impact 
categories, although their magnitude of significance is small relatively to those in other impact 
categories. Wet FGC system and activated carbon filter equipped in the incineration scenarios 
are considered to have large contribution to reduction of these emissions. 
 When considering the treatment/disposal of ASR and C&DW residue together with MSW, the 
gasification scenarios performs environmentally better than the status-quo management (i.e. the 
combination of incineration of MSW and landfilling of ASR/C&DW) in more number of impact 
categories compared to the scenarios where only MSW is treated. It is indicated that the 
gasification technology is more beneficial for the co-gasification of some specific waste, such as 
ASR, C&DW residue, and maybe asbestos and other hazardous waste (especially in the 
short-term perspective). 
 In the impact category of ET the application of the gasification is considered to have significant 
benefit in terms of environmental performance in comparison with the incineration scenarios 
when managing MSW+ASR. Also, co-combustion of ASR and C&DW residue, which has not 
been commonly practiced in Denmark so far, could have a significant potential to reduce 
environmental impacts, when comparing with the status-quo management (landfilling). 
Development in co-combustion technology for such waste is considered to result in further better 
environmental performances of incineration. 
 In order to improve the environmental performance of the gasification technology, upgrading of 
syngas and energy recovery with CCGT technology could be a solution. Also, the application of 
biomass coke alternative to coal coke is expected to reduce environmental impacts of the 
gasification, especially on GW and RD. 
 Overall, the application of the gasification is not a better choice on environmental point of view 
especially treating only MSW. However, the study shows the technology can be beneficial when 
considering a whole waste management system that addresses so called ‘hard-to-manage waste’. 
==PAGE:71==
 
62 
 
Also the technology may instead provide different services and products (e.g. syngas and its 
upgraded gases).
