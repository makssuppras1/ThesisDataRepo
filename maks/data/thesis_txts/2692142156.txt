Abstract
Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is a leading cause of cancer­associated deaths
and has the lowest survival rate of all cancers. Although the tumor is detected late in the
progression of the disease, PDAC initiation occurs years before the diagnosis. Thus, there
may be a large window of opportunity for early detection and treatment. However, experi­
mental models that recapitulate human PDAC development are lacking and the molecular
underpinnings of tumor initiation are largely unknown. Moreover, available models such
as cancer cell lines and tumor explants have a lot of accumulated mutations. Upon severe
tissue damage, acinar cells in the pancreas acquire properties of pancreas progenitors.
This mechanism alleviates tissue damage, induces acinar proliferation and is necessary
for regeneration. However, it makes the tissue susceptible to KRAS­mediated malignant
transformation. A major hurdle in PDAC research is the paucity of models and pathologi­
cal samples to study tumor initiation. To overcome this, we plan to established a model of
PDAC development in collaboration with Prof. Danwei Huangfu (Memorial Sloan Ketter­
ing, New Your, USA) using human embryonic stem cells (hESC) with ineducable KRAS
activating mutation (Hues8­KRAS). To do so, Hues8­KRAS cells (and the control Hues8­
GFP) were differentiated towards pancreatic progenitors in an attempt to recapitulate the
ADM cellular state. Differentiation was followed by morphology, gene expression (qPCR)
and flow cytometry. Doxycyline­inducible KRAS and GFP expression was assessed us­
ing flow cytometry and fluorescence microscopy alive and after fixation. To investigate
the effect of KRAS in orthotopic transplants of Hues8 cells at the pancreatic progenitors
stage, 12 mice injected. 6 of them were given doxycycline to induce KRAS expression.
Animal termination will occur shortly after the defence of this thesis.

Introduction
1.1
Initiation and progression of pancreatic ductal
adenocarcinoma
The pancreas is a glandular organ located in the abdomen. It generates pancreatic juices,
that contain enzymes that aid in digestion, and it produces several hormones, such as in­
sulin and glucagon. The pancreas is composed of endocrine and exocrine compartments.
The exocrine part, which makes up over 90% of the total pancreas volume, contains the
ductal ( 5%) and acinar ( 85%) epithelia. The most common type of pancreatic cancer,
pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), is thought to arise from the exocrine compart­
ment (Kleeff et al. 2016). It represents 90% of pancreatic cancer and is a leading cause
of cancer­associated deaths with the lowest survival rate of all cancers. With pancreatic
cancer deaths on the rise and the bad prognosis due to late detection, there is the need
to identify new biomarkers for the development of new treatments and early detection.
KRAS gene codes for the K­Ras protein, a GTPase involved in signaling the RAS/MAPK
pathway. It is mutation ally activated in 94% of PDAC and is considered as the initiating
genetic event for PDAC (Waters and Der 2018). Activating mutations of KRAS lead to
persistent GTP­bound and constitutively active kras independent of extracellular stimuli,
resulting in overstimulation of effector signaling pathways. The progression of normal
pancreatic tissue to PDAC involves a stepwise genetic transition projected to span 12
years (Iacobuzio­Donahue et al. 2012). Therefore there is a wide time­span that allows
for early detection. Additional loss­of­function mutations in tumor suppressors, such as
TP53, CDNK2A, DPC4/SMAD4, and BRCA2, lead to rapid PDAC progression.
As human pancreatic adenocarcinoma is often diagnosed at a stage of metastatic disease,
understanding the processes that lead to its initiation is important for the discovery of
markers for early detection, as well as options that enable an early intervention.
1.2
Acinar­to­ductal metaplasia resembles pancreatic
progenitors and is required for pancreas regeneration
In animal models, it has been seen that acinar cells in the adult pancreas show high
plasticity and can undergo transdifferentiation to a progenitor­like cell type with ductal
characteristics. This process, termed acinar­to­ductal metaplasia (ADM), is an important
feature facilitating pancreas regeneration. Upon severe tissue damage, acinar cells ac­
quire properties of pancreas progenitors (Krah et al. 2015, Cobo et al. 2018, Mallen­St
Clair et al. 2012). This mechanism alleviates tissue damage, induces acinar proliferation
and is necessary for regeneration. However, it makes the tissue susceptible to KRAS­
mediated malignant transformation (Ji et al. 2009, Guerra, Collado, et al. 2011, Kopp et
al. 2012). Data from animal models show that cells that undergo ADM are precursors for
pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia lesions (PanIN), which can further progress to PDAC.
Consistent with these observations in murine models, pathways of pancreas develop­
ment are aberrantly reactivated in cancer, and KRAS mutations are prevalent in virtually
all PDACs (reviewed in Storz 2017).
Furthermore, recent studies have shown that the molecular sub­types of PDAC (namely,
classical and quasimesenchymal) represent a continuum of cellular states in tumor evo­
lution, and classical tumors, which are associated with the reactivation of pancreas de­

Chan­Seng­Yue et al. 2020).
ADM is the process in which acinar cells differentiate towards a duct­like­state. It involves
the downregulation of acinar cell markers such as Ptf1a, amylase and Mist­1 and the
upregulation of ductal cell markers such as CK­19 and SOX9. ADM is also characterized
by the upregulation of the pancreatic progenitor marker PDX1.
When comparing gene expression of acinar cells, pancreatic progenitors and E14.5 and
pancreatic cancer in mice (Figure 1.1) it can be seen that although originated from acinar
cells, cancerous cells better correlates with pancreatic progenitors (data not published).
This suggests that pancreatic progenitors state could used to recapitulate initial lesions

1.3
Models of PDAC
Human cancer cell lines are inexpensive, easy to handle and allow for cytotoxic drug ef­
ficacy screening. But they tend to accumulate additional mutaitons over time, they lack
heterogeneity and poorly reflect early­stage cancer development (Mirabelli et al. 2019).
Patient­derived tumor xenografts (PDTXs), although they better repsresent tumor hetero­
geneity and presenve cell­cell interations and the tumor microenvironment, are expeen­
sive, time consuming and also harbour a lot of accumulated mutations. (Zhang et al.
2020) Animal models facilitat basic cancer research but they cannot completely recapitu­
late genetic alterations and heterogeneity in human cancers.

Current models, while they resemble the parent tumor cells (i.e. organoids, xenografts,

Recently, the induced

study cancer initiating events. To overcome this limitation, we propose the use of hESC
that together with genome engineering approaches, will provide a complementary plat­
form to model cancer progression.
1.4
Human embryonic stem cells and directed in vitro
differentiation
Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) originate from the inner cell mass (ICM) of the blastocyst,
a small hollow sphere that initiates embryonic development. As pluripotent stem cells
(PSCs), ESCs are characterized by the ability to proliferate indefinitely in vitro while main­
taining the capacity to differentiate into derivatives of all three germ layers (ectoderm,
mesoderm, and endoderm). Differentiation is a stepwise process that starts with specific
cell lineage determination and ends by irreversible terminal differentiation to a specific cell
type.
Differentiation protocols involve the use of specific growth factors or pharmacological
molecules that regulate specific signaling pathways. Protocols are developed by the re­
construction of crucial human developmental cues, to activate or inhibits the appropriate
transcription factors and alternative signaling pathways to mimic human development.
To perform the differentiation protocols it is important to accurately model the key stages of
pancreatic development. Due to the high similarity in mice and human pancreas ogano­
genesis and the limited access to relevant early and later gestational­age human pan­
creatic tissue, most differentiation protocols are based on the information from studies of
mouse development (Pan and Brissova 2014).
Embryonic development begins once oocytes are fertilized by sperm, and the formed zy­
gote forms a blastocyst. The blastocyst contains the Trophectoderm (TE) and the Inner
Cell Mass (ICM). TE cells contribute to the formation of the placenta (or extra­embryonic
tissues). The ICM segregates into a bilaminar embryonic disc which consists of two ep­
ithelial layers, each of a distinct lineage: the external (dorsal) epiblast and the internal
(ventral) hypoblast. The next development stage is gastrulation, the process that results
in the formation of three distinct germ layers in the early embryo. The three primary germ
layers in the very early embryo are ectoderm (outside layer), mesoderm (middle layer)
and ectoderm, the innermost layer. The three germ layers, often in combination, will con­
tribute to the formation of organs. Prior to outgrowth of the pancreatic buds, a ventral and
dorsal prepancreatic region is specified in the gut endoderm (Shih et al. 2013).
The ventral foregut region gives rise to both ventral pancreas and liver, which develop in
close proximity to each other (Shih et al. 2013).
Foregut endoderm has two regions, the ventral endoderm that gives origin to the thyroid

gland, lungs, liver, and the ventral part of the pancreas; and the dorsal endoderm, the
origin of intestine and the dorsal part of the pancreas. Thus, during embryogenesis, the
pancreas develops from distinct dorsal and ventral cell differentiation (Soria 2001).
Once the ventral and dorsal prepancreatic domains are specified, several transcription
factors become specifically expressed in the prospective pancreatic regions. Among the
earliest transcription factors that mark this region are Pdx1, Ptf1a, and Sox9. Gata4/6,
Foxa1/2, Tcf2, Onecut­1/2, Hes1, Prox1, and Mnx1 are also expressed in the prepancre­
atic domain, but their expression expands more broadly throughout the foregut endoderm
(Shih et al. 2013).
In addition to their role in pancreas specification, all early pancreatic transcription factors
are necessary for further development of pancreatic buds (primary transition, see Shih
et al. 2013). Nascent pancreatic buds are almost entirely composed of multi­potent pro­
genitor cells (MPSs). The transition of non committed MPCs into an organ with different
cell compartments is a multi­step process, of which the first step is the segregation of a
tip and trunk domain at the onset of the secondary transition.
At this point the new organ grows rapidly and its epithelium undergoes dynamic structural
changes that result in protrusions from the edges. In addition, formerly multipotent MPCs
have taken the first step toward lineage commitment. The trunk will predominantly gibe
rise to the endocrine and ductal cells, while the tips are restricted to acinar fate.
Due to the great interest in generating functional human β­cells in vitro for potential trans­
plantation or drug development in diabetes a lot of investment in the generation of differen­
tiation protocols towards β­cells. Multiple studies over the last decade have demonstrated
the generation of pancreatic cells from hPSCs. Stepwise protocols have been designed
to differentiate hPSCs into β cells by directing them along the stages of definitive endo­
derm, pancreatic foregut, pancreatic progenitors, and endocrine precursor cells that finally
mature into insulin­secreting cells (Memon et al. 2018).
The first step to differentiate hPSC towards definitive endoderm is the formation of the
primitive streak (PS), which is induced by Nodal/Activin A signaling (Abdelalim and Emara
2016) and the activation of WNT/β­Catenin pathway (Naujok et al. 2014). CHIR99021
is an aminopyrimidine derivative that is an extremely potent glycogen synthase kinase
(GSK) 3 inhibitor, inhibiting both GSK3β and GSK3α. GSK3 is a serine/threonine kinase
that is a key inhibitor of the WNT pathway; therefore CHIR99021 functions as a WNT
activator.
Sry­related high mobility box 17 expression (SOX17) is expressed in an early stage of
pancreatic development and its expression is crucial for endodermal development. In
humans its expression is detected before 4 weeks, continues for 1 week and subsequently
disappears (Al­Khawaga et al. 2018). Therefore, definitive endoderm differentiation in
hESC is usually measured by the by the co­expression of the DE specific markers, FOXA2
and SOX17 (Abdelalim and Emara 2016). Interestingly, Sox17 alone was found to be
enough to distinguish DE from the other germ layer (Abdelalim and Emara 2016).
Retinoic acid (RA) also participates in patterning the gut endoderm (Johannesson et al.
2009). In the paper published by Ameri et al. 2017 differentiation from the definitive en­
doderm to the posterior foregut stage is accoplished with RA treatment.
FOXA1 and FOXA2 are both expressed in the entire gut tube and are thus important for
the development of all gastrointestinal tract­derived organs(Ang et al. 1993). Therefore
FOXA2 is expected to be upregulated in the posterior foregut stage of the in vitro differ­

1.5
State of the art: modelling PDAC initiation using hESC
We hypothesize that KRAS­induced PDAC relies on developmental enhancers that be­
come activated during pancreas regeneration and function through targetable elncRNAs.
We then propose to develop a model to asses the function of developmental enhancers
in KRAS­induced PDAC.
We propose a model using directe diferentation of hESC towards pancreatic progenitors in
order to recreate the cellular state observed in ADM. To do so we rely on human embrynoic
stem cell with inducible KRAS activating mutation (Hues8­KRAS) and Hues8­GFP as a
control cell line. This innovative approach will allow us to investigate KRAS­dependencies
in early lesion of PDAC. A schematic representation of the proposed model is represented
in ??.

and are a valueble tool in combination with gene engineering technologies.

2.3
Cell culture and passaging
Cells were incubated at 37C, 5% and a humid atmosphere. Cells were cultlured in laminin­
coated plates or chambered coverslides with NutriStem hPSC XF Medium. Medium was
changed daily and cells were passaged every 3­4 days when reaching a confluence be­
tween 80 to 100%. Cells were washed with PBS­/­ and deatached with Accutase. Incuba­
tion was done at the incubator for 10 minutes. Then same amount of Accutase was added
of medium suplemented with Rock inhibitor. Cells were gently resuspended and trans­
ferred to a tube. Pellet was formed by centrifugation at 300g for 3 minutes, at 20C. Cells
were resuspended in medium supplemented with Rock inhibitor and counted using the
7
==PAGE:16==
Countess II FL from life Technologies. The required amount of cells was then seeded in
a laminin­coated plate with fresh medium. Maintenance as well as differentiation medium
was supplemented with Pen­Strep.
2.4
Defreezing and long term conservation
In order to start a new frozen vial (1­3M cells /vial) cells were quickly tawed at 37 C and
resuspended in 5 ml of Nustristem supplemented with Rock inhibitor and centrifuged at
300g for 3 minutes. After removing the medium, cells were resuspended in 2 ml of fresh
Nutristem with Rock inhibitor and the whole volume seeded in a laminin­coated well of a
6­well plate. After unfreezing cells need to be passaged between 2 and 3 times before
starting a differenitation or any other experiment.
2.5
Differentiation protocol
An adapted version of the protocol published by Ameri et al. 2017 was used. Differenti­
ation should be started when cells are confluent. Plate cells at 400­500k cells/well in a
6 well plate or 250­300k cells/well in a 12 well plate. Seeding should be low enough to
ensure a monolayer but high enugh to not have holes in the differentiating cultures. Cells
usually are ready to start differentiation after 4 days of growth.
Medium is changed every day during differentiation. Before each stage begin, wash the
cells with PBS +/+ at 37 C before adding the new medium.

Staining
Already fixed cells stored at 4C were spinned at 300G, 5 minutes at 4C. Supernatant was
removoved suing vacum and cells were resuspended in perm­blocking buffer for FACS
and incubated on ice for 30 minutes. Cells were then spinned at 700G, 5 minutes at
4C and resuspended in 200 ul of primary antibody soltuion. Primary antibody incubation
was done overnight at 4C. Cells were washed with 500 ul of IC buffer and spinned at
1500G for 3 minutes at 4C. Secondary antibody staining was performed using 300ul of
blocking solution 1:1000 at room temperature for 1 hour. When finished cells were washed
twiche with 500 ul of IC buffer. Before data aquisition cells were filtered using a 40nm
filter. Once stained samples were kept on ice until and during analysis. All incubaitons
were porformed protected from light and samples were manipulated avoiding direct light,
specially after the secondary antibody incubation. Supernatant was removed by gently
vacum at every step.
2.6.3
Flow cytometry gating and analysis
FCS files were analyzed using FlowJo. Median fluorescent intensity values and % of
positive cells were calculated from this data. Stain index was calculated using the Stain
Index plugin (Maecker and Trotter 2008). MFI is the median value of fluorescence for a
given fluorescence parameter. Because of the use of log scale and if working with non­
normal distributions arithmetic mean usually is not the best opption. Therefore, to account
for the log­normal behavior of flow data, usually geometric mean or median are used as
are less influenced by skew or outliers and shapes of the distribution.
For the GFP expression experiments the gating strategy was as represented in Figure

forward scatter. Doublets were excluded by representing the area versus the height of
the side scatter. Live cells were gated using a negative control not stained with DAPI (not
represented) when representing side scatter with DAPI intensity. Fianlly, gating for the
GFP positive cells was established using a population not treated with DOX. Manually
gated negative GFP populations was set in order to calculate de SI.

Validation of Hues8­KRAS embryonic stem cell state
I validated the embryonic state of Hues8­KRAS by morphology, embryonic marker expres­

In culture, morphology of hESC indicates the differentiated state of the cells and gives in­
formation about their general health and condition. Prominent/abundant nucleoli, a colony
with a tightly packed appearance and a well­defined edge indicate good quality cells and
colonies (Orozco­Fuentes et al. 2019).
Hues4 cells did show the morphological features of undifferentiated hESC with round cells
with high nuclear/cytoplasmic ratio, highly visible nucleoli (Orozco­Fuentes et al. 2019)
and well­defined colony edges.
On the other hand Hues8 cells had spindle­shaped and fibroblastic morphology and differ­
ent nucleus/cytoplasm ratio, with bigger cytoplasm indicating differentiation, as observed
in Figure 3.1.
After several passages colonies emerged with embryonic stem like morphology surrounded
by spindle shaped cells. Embryonic stem like colonies increased in number after passages

importance for experiments intended to reflect a snapshot of gene expression at the mo­

a shift towards shorter fragment sizes. As rRNA is the most prominent species of cellular

Integrity number (RIN) was developed by Agilen Technologies and is based in automated
microcapillary electrophoretic RNA separation (O. Mueller et al. 2004). RIN assigns an
electropherogram a value that range from 10 (intact) to 1 (totally degraded). (Schroeder
et al. 2006). Therefore, to validate the quality of the extraction protocol, RIN value was
determined for randomly picked RNA samples (Table 3.1).

3.3.2
Hues8­KRAS and Hues8­GFP show different GFP intensity
In flow cytometry, fluorescence intensity of a fluorophore can be evaluated using the mean
fluorescence intensity (MFI) and the stain index (SI). Because of comparing two cell lines
with respective negative controls it is valuable to look at the separation of positive and
negative populations rather than at absolute median fluorescence values. Overall bright­
ness of a fluorophore can be estimated by the relationship between the positive and the
negative signals or background. The stain index is the ratio of the separation between
the positive population and the negative population, divided by two times the standard
deviation of the negative population.
SI allows for the quantification of the effects of variations in instrument configuration upon
the separation of positive and negative populations. The purpose of SI measurements
is to see how instrument configurations affect the measurement capability with regard to
biological samples. Adjustments that yield high SI vales result in greater separation be­
tween positive and negative populations. If a fluorophore has a lower MFI for the positive
population but the same MFI of the background, the separation between the negative and

the pancreas of immunodepressed mice. 6 out of the 12 mice were given DOX at a
concentration of 2mg/ml in drinking water to induce KRAS expression. To mask the bitter

the other 6 mice were given regular autoclaved drinking water. Cells injected in mice
sumministred with DOX, were treated with DOX during 2 days previous to the injections.
For the transplants young mice between 8­12 weeks old were used. Mice were injected
in two different batches of 6 (3 with DOX and 3 without DOX). The first batch was injected

after 8 weeks of cell injections and monitored by palpation twice a week. If they don’t show
the formation of tumors, mice from the first batch are expected to be terminated on the
16th of July of 2021 and the second batch two weeks later.

Conclusions
Old batches of Hues8­KRAS cells were spindle­shaped, had low expression of pluripo­
tency markers are were unable to differentiate towards pancreatic progenitors. As vials
from other batches were ordered, Hues8­KRAS and Hues8­GFP embryonic stem cell
state was validated by morphology and its ability to differentiate towards definitive en­
doderm. Cells upregulated the definitive endoderm markers SOX17 and FOXA2 at the
expected time points, coupled with the downregulation of pluripotency markers OCT4 and
NANOG, as assessed by qPCR.
The shortening of the Activin A treatment allowed to successfully differentiate Hues8­
KRAS and Hues8­GFP into pancreatic progenitors. Validation was done based on the
characteristic round tube­like morphology at that stage, the downregulation of the embry­
onic marker SOX2 and the upregulation of PDX1.
Doxycycline treatment did not affect cell morphology neither induced aberrant expression
of selected markers.
Doxycycline­induced Hues8­GFP GFP expression was more intense than the one from
Hues8­KRAS. Difference in brightness is associated to the different processing of GFP
upon doxycyline treatment because of the co­expression of GFP and KRAS in the tet­
ON expression system of Hues8­KRAS. Further validation of mutant KRAS expressios is
needed.
Cell fixation with PFA did not quench GFP signal and maintained cell population distribu­
tion and profiles in both cell lines.
Nucleofection was the preferred DNA delivery method with efficiency around 20%.
From the 12 injected mice, 6 received cells that did not express PDX1 and therefore not
pancreatic progenitors, while the other 6 were injected with PDX1 positive cells. Termi­
nation and pancreas extraction and analysis will be done shortly after the defence of this
thesis.
Orthotopic transplant of Mel1­GFP hESC in immuodepressed mice lead to the formation
of teratomas. Cells differentiated to definitive endoderm also formed tumors but when
injecting cells at the posterior foregut stage no tumors were observed. Further analysis
of the observed lesions will give information about their origin. This results serve as a
proof­of­concept for the design of our proposed model of PDAC initiation.
Overall, the ability of Hues8 to differentiate to pancreatic progenitors, doxycycline­induced
mutant KRAS expression and nuclefection as a successful method for DNA delivery,
stands as promissing set up to model PDAC initiation that will allow us to investigate
KRAS­dependencies in early lesion of PDAC.

A.4
Cells injections in mice: Detailed protocol
Materials 
- 
Nude Mice : BomTacNMRI-Foxn1nu 
- 
HUES8 KRAS cells in the pancreatic progenitors 1 stage. 
- 
Isoflurane 
- 
Lidocaine hydrochloride 2% (1ml into 9ml PBS) 
- 
Hydrogel for eyes 
Antiseptic 
- 
Absorbable suture thread 5.0 
- 
Physiological serum 
- 
27G needle - insuline serynge 
 
Method 
CELL CULTURE 
Differentiate Hues8-KRAS cells until pancreatic progenitors 1 stage, with and without DOX. Harvest 2 million 
cells and resuspend in 50ul of cold PBS 1X and 50ul of matrigel. 
Keep on ice for going to the animal facility 
ANIMAL SURGERY 
• 
Animals under anaesthesia with 4% Isoflurane and 1.5ppm of Oxygen 
Reduce the flow to 2% if the animal shows difficulties for breathing 
• 
Trim the fur to the skin on the spleen side – it is possible to see the dark spleen through skin. 
 
• 
Desinfect the skin with Ethanol 70%. 
• 
Lidocaine hydrochloride (2%) diluted to 0.5%, do not exceed 7 mg/kg total dose – multiple (2-3) 
subcutaneous injection where the incision takes place. 
 
• 
Wait for 10 min  
• 
Cut the skin with scissors 1cm on the left side where the spleen is seen.

• 
Then cut muscle-tissue with scissors 1cm also 
• 
Pull out the spleen with smooth twizzle and lay the spleen-side down towards the skin : do not touch 
pancreas for avoiding any kind of inflammation 
 
• 
Hydrate with physiological serum 
• 
Collect 50ul of PBS in the serynge before the cells solution in PBS-matrigel 
it allows you to flush the whole solution into pancreas 
 
• 
The needle should penetrates the tissue but not before it 
go deep and come back on the top 
 
 
• 
A translucide bubble should appear showing that the injection succeed 
hold few second for the materiel to solidify before withdrawing the needle

• 
Hydrate with physiological serum 
• 
Catch the muscle borders of the wound in order to put the pancreas back without touching it 
 
• 
Suture the muscle with 2 stitches and suture the skin with 2-3 internal stitches for not letting the mice

•

• 
Leave the mouse to wake up in an isolated chamber with a warm red light before putting it back to

Provide the mice with wet food at the ground level the hours immediately after the surgery. 
Check the mice the next day and make sure the sutures are still in place. 
Note that half of the mice (the ones injected with Hues8-KRAS cells previously treated with doxycycline) 
should be given doxycycline in water at a concentration of 2 mg/ml. Change water every 3-5 days and keep
