Abstract
Previous numerical studies of Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) are based on
tanks without fish. This study focuses on the development of a Computational Fluid
Dynamics (CFD) model to accurately predict the effects of fish on the hydrodynamics
within a RAS system. The effect of fish is included by measuring turbulence production
terms for a single fish swimming and applying them to the RAS system by a turbulence
source term.
A generic fish model was used with a length of 0.7m representing an
Atlantic salmon. The numerical study of the swimming fish was conducted using the
k −ε Standard Low Re model, using a DFBI model to represent the fish. The motion of
the fish was based on the mathematical description of a Caranghiform swimmer found
by Videller and Hess [1] and was applied to the fish using mesh morphing. The fish
swimming was done for a Re ≈500000. Good agreement was found with previous
numerical studies and experimental data on fish.
Two methods were developed to include the turbulence production in the RAS system.
One method used the time-averaged volume integral of the turbulence production term.
The other method used the time-average but spatially resolved turbulence production
terms. Both methods were validated by trying to replicate the turbulence viscosity found
in the numerical study using these two methods. The time-averaged volume integral
method was not able to capture the turbulence created by the fish. The time-averaged
spatial resolved methods were able to capture the turbulence created by the fish with
fair accuracy.
None of the methods was able to include the effect of the fish in the RAS system.
Both methods reduced the turbulence viscosity in the tank and increased the velocity
which was contrary to experimental results found by Gorle et al. [2] and Plew et al. [3].
Including only the TKE production gave results that decreased the velocity and increased
the turbulence viscosity in the tank. The results looked similar to those obtained by
experiments but will need further investigation for validation of this method.
Keywords: RAS, Fish, CFD

1
Introduction
As the global population expands and world hunger continues to be a problem the demand
for improving how food is produced today is relevant. Meeting the UN’s sustainable
development goal (SDG) of zero world hunger requires innovation and change within the
food industry. Blue foods, which are food from the ocean, rivers lakes etc. are estimated
to play a vital role in meeting the future demand for food. UN anticipates that global
fish production will increase 32 % from 2020 levels until 2030 and double by 2050. Blue
foods will therefore be a key part of ensuring nutritious and healthy food for billions of
people in the future. It is therefore more important than ever to innovate and improve
aquaculture farming. [7]
Until now most fish farming has been done in the open ocean in large cages or nets, where
fish, shrimp and oysters are typically farmed. These farms have in past years been found
to ruin the marine ecosystems. The reason is that water can flow freely through carrying
things like pesticides and waste from the farm. The open-water cages also come with a
risk of spreading diseases from farmed fish to wild fish. Therefore closed land-based fish
farming known as Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) is gaining popularity. The
RAS systems benefit from being completely closed off from the environment and can
be installed in optimal production locations. Today it is mostly Atlantic salmon which
is farmed in RAS systems, but other fish species can also be farmed. Like open-water
farming, RAS systems have issues such as fish disease, mortality and poor water quality.
This is often caused by a poor choice of production parameters such as swimming speed,
oxygen levels etc.
When designing new RAS systems it is important to assess hydrodynamics before building
the tank to ensure the welfare and growth of the fish. The industry has historically based
its designs of the systems on empirical knowledge gained from experimental work. As the
RAS system becomes larger, these design rules do not apply well anymore. In recent years
the industry has sought help in numerical studies utilizing Computational fluid dynamics
(CFD). CFD has gained popularity with easier access to more computer power. As of
today, the numerical studies of the RAS systems are done without fish. The reason is
that it is difficult to include the fish due to a lack of computational power. Not having
fish in the numerical studies has been a concern for engineers since it is believed that
the fish have an impact of the tank hydrodynamics.
1.1
Project objectives
The goal of the project is to develop a CFD model capable of accurately predicting the
effects of fish on the performance of RAS systems under various operating conditions.
The model should enable an enhanced understanding of key parameters such as fish -size,
-velocity and tank densities that influence flow dynamics within RAS tanks. Designed
to focus on engineering principles, the model must prioritise considerations such as
computational efficiency, flexibility for adjustments, and accuracy.
The goal is that
future CFD simulations of RAS systems should be created including the effect of fish.
The project therefore emphasises including fish rather than investigating a specific RAS
1
==PAGE:13==
system using CFD. The method must therefore apply to all RAS systems. The goal of
the project is not necessarily to created an accurate model now, but to show a proof of
concept. The goal is therefore to show that the model has the potential to work, but
not necessarily a thoroughly tested model. The project will therefore not include any
experimental work, but exclusively a numerical study of a fish and RAS tank.
1.2
Method
The main idea is to do a numerical study of a fish swimming and measure the turbulent
kinetic energy and turbulent dissipation rate produced by the fish. To measure these
properties correctly the assumption is that the fish is swimming at the same velocity
as the water inside the tank meaning that momentum is 0 on the fish. This means
that from an outside observer, the fish is standing still. When momentum is 0 it means
that the fish will only contribute by adding turbulence to the tank. Therefore, it should
be enough only to add the turbulence properties of the numerical study of the fish to
the RAS system. Adding the turbulence should not require the simulation of several
thousand fish inside the tank. This will require a numerical investigation of a fish to
find the turbulence production values. It will also require a numerical investigation of
a RAS tank. The turbulence effects of the fish are included in the tank by adding a
turbulent source term in the turbulent transport equation to the tank model. In the
project, different methods to include and measure the turbulent source term will be
investigated.

2
Literature Review
In this section the relevant literature on the matter of fish swimming and fish effects on
the hydrodynamics of RAS systems is presented. The section will present the experi-
mental and numerical work done.
2.1
Experimental work
Some of the first work investigating how fish swim was performed by J. Gray in 1932 [8].
In his study, he classified different fish species types of swimming form, swimming speed,
swimming form etc. It was of interest to him how different fish used their body to propel
themselves forward. The study was done by taking a series of photos capturing the
motion of different fish species. The study enabled us to see that a fish motion can be
described as a backwards travelling wave. The work led J. Videler and F. Hess [1] to
create a mathematical model of the fish motion. The method was based on fitting a
Fourier series to the lateral displacement and body curvature of a fish. The motion was
based on a mackerel and saithe. The study found that small variations in kinematics
caused large variations in swimming speed. The mathematical model created was a
travelling wave (trigonometric function) multiplied with a second-order polynomial.
Two major experimental studies investigating the Atlantic salmon smolt effect on water
velocity have been performed. One was done by Phew et al 2015 [3] and one by Gorle
et al. 2018 [2]. Phew et al. investigated salmon’s effect on velocity and turbulence in
the tank. The study compared a tank with no fish to one with tank densities of 15.3 kg
m3,
36.6 kg
m3 and 79.4 kg
m3. Velocities were measured using Doppler velocimetry. The study
found a clear correlation between tank densities, velocity and turbulence. For the high
density, the velocity was found to be reduced by 57 % compared to the tank without
fish. The turbulence kinetic energy and turbulent dissipation rate were found to increase
with the presence of fish.
The study conducted by Gorle et al. 2018 [2] was performed on two large commercial
production tanks a circular and octagonal one. The objective of the study was to examine
the effect of biomass on the velocity in the tank. This was tested with a fish density of
35 and 48
kg
m3 for the circular tank and 54, 58, 64, 74
kg
m3 for the octagonal tank. The
introduction of fish was found to reduce the water velocity in the tank by 25 % compared
to no fish in the tank. The flow pattern in the tank was also found to be largely affected
by the presence of fish.
2.2
Numerical studies
Several numerical studies investigating fish swimming have been performed.
Recent
studies are M. Macias et al. [9] and A. Mertens [10]. A numerical study on the optimal
undulatory swimming patterns of danio-like fish was conducted by A. Mertens. The
study investigates swimming parameters and swimming forms, that minimize propulsive
power for a self-propelled fish at Re = 5000. Carangiforms are compared with optimized
swimming form and increased efficiency from 22 % to 34 % for a 3D fish. It also inves-
3
==PAGE:15==
tigates the effect of including recoil in the swimming form. They found that recoil has
a significant impact on efficiency.
Not many numerical studies have been performed investigating the effect of fish on RAS
systems. Two new numerical studies have been conducted by H. Liu et al. [11] and
Y. Zhou et al. [12]. H. Liu et al. investigate the effect of fish movement on the flow
field in RAS systems. The flow is investigated using CFD to capture the interaction
between fish and fluid. The RNG k-ϵ model is used to capture the flow characteristics.
The study researches a trevally fish and a circular tank. They modelled 4 conditions
with, no fish in the tank, 2 fish in the tank, 4 fish in the tank and 20 fish in the tank.
They introduced a uniformity parameter UC50 which measures the average 50 % lower
velocity volume divided by the average 50 % higher lower velocity volume. The study
found that the more fish introduced the velocity in the tank and the uniformity of the
flow field significantly decreased.
The study by Y. Zhou [12] also investigates the fish’s influence on RAS hydrodynamics.
The study was done by inserting 20 fish in the RAS tank with different tail beat fre-
quencies and comparing them with the RAS tank with no fish in a total of 14 cases. It
was concluded that the fish had a significant effect on both the average velocity and the
velocity uniformity, both being decreased by the presence of fish. These results seem to
agree well with the experimental results from Gorle et al. and Phew et al.
2.3
Turublence source term modelling
The method used to include the effect of fish in the RAS system relied on adding
turbulence source terms to the transport equations, hence other studies were found
where a similar approach was done. It was found that similar methods were applied
for canopy flows. Two canopy flow studies are used the study by F. Zeng et al. [13]
and Y. Zhao et al. [14]. The study by F. Zeng models trees’ effect on canopy flow
by adding momentum and turbulence source terms to the momentum and turbulence
transport equations. The source terms are found by applying a drag term to the Navier-
Stokes equation and derive them from that. The model was compared to a wind tunnel
experiment of a tree. The study found that the turbulence kinetic energy and turbulent
dissipation rate were sensitive to the model parameters.
The study by Y. Zhao [14] investigates how traffic affects canopy flow in a city. The
study has derived vehicle-induced turbulence (VIT) source terms and added them to a
realizable k −ε model. The source term was derived by adding the drag term from a car
to the momentum equation and from that the turbulence source term. For both studies,
the turbulence source term is dependent on the velocity of the flow, drag coefficients
and model coefficients determined from experiments.

3.1

CFD is based on solving the Navier-Stoke equations.
The equations are derived by
utilizing the conservation of mass, momentum and energy. The Navier-Stoke equation
can be used to describe the state of any fluid. The following section will explain the
main assumptions and models used in this project regarding the physics used.

3.2
Turbulence modelling
An important aspect of CFD is how to describe the turbulence in the flow. Turbulent
flow is a chaotic and highly disordered motion of fluid characterized by irregular fluctu-
ations in velocity, pressure, and other flow properties. Unlike laminar flow, where fluid
particles move in smooth, ordered paths, turbulent flow is characterized by the presence
of vortices, eddies, and swirls occurring at various scales within the flow field from large
structures to small scales. These turbulent structures constantly interact, leading to
mixing, stirring, and enhanced transport of momentum, energy, and mass within the
fluid. To model turbulence in CFD there are different approaches such as DNS (Direct
5
==PAGE:17==
numerical solve), RANS (Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes) and LES (Large eddy sim-
ulation). DNS models eddies within all the scales, therefore it can be very accurate.
DNS is the most computationally expensive model and is therefore rarely used for engi-
neering applications. LES solves for the largest scales eddies while modelling the smaller
scales, this makes it less computationally expensive than DNS, but still expensive. For
most engineering applications the RANS model is the best approach since it offers low
computational time and great accuracy.

3.3
Wall treatment
Wall functions are used to model the near-wall region turbulent flows without having to
resolve the extremely fine details of the boundary layer. This approach allows for a more
efficient simulation by avoiding a excessively fine mesh near the walls, which would be
more computationally expensive. The wall boundary consists of several different layers
as seen in figure 3.1. The viscous layer is dominated by viscous effects which makes it
mostly laminar. The log layer is equally dominated by the turbulence and viscous flow.
The buffer layer is the transitional region from the viscous layer to the log layer.
Figure 3.1: Starccm+ diagram showing the different layers in the boundary layer taken
from [4]
For RANS models the wall functions are used to determine the boundary conditions for
the flow at the walls. The wall function imposes the values at the centroid of the near-
wall cells. Wall functions are used for the main quantities such as velocity and turbulence.

3.4
Fish kinematics
Figure 3.2: The figure is taken from Fish Swimming by J. Videller [5]. A 2D represen-
tation of the wake generated behind the fish.
Fish create propulsion by undulating their body.
The undulation creates a pressure
difference on the fish that causes a forward momentum. It is primarily done by creating
a low pressure on one side of the tail and a high pressure on the other side.
The
undulation also creates vortices behind the fish as seen in figure 3.2, which is released
at the end of every tail stroke. The rotation of the vortices close to the tail is strong
and gradually decays as they move away from the fish. The vortices also grow in size as
they decay. The vortices generated by the fish form a reversed von Karman street. Von
Karman street is known as the flow past objects, where the flow is fluctuating behind the
object creating vortices. The reverse von Karman street means that the vortices rotate
in the opposite direction compared to a von Karman street. The reversed von Karman
street is characterized by fast-moving water just behind the fish and slow-moving water
further away perpendicular to the swimming direction.
The motion of a fish that utilizes body/caudal fin (BCF) varies depending on the species.
The different swimming forms in figure 3.3 were categorized by Lighthill [16] where the
locomotion of fish was split into anguilliform, subcarangiform, carangiform, thunniform
and ostraciform. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) locomotion is the carangiform motion.
For the carangiform swimming motion, most of the body movement is within the last

To capture the fish swimming motion in Starccm+’s a Dyniamic fluid body interaction

the movement of the fish. Only translation in the X and Y directions is enabled for the

circles due to the initial kick, hence this was disabled. The DFBI model uses the mass,
moment of Inertia and center of mass of the fish. The density of the fish is set equal

moment of Inertia and center of mass are seen in table 4.1. To avoid the fish swimming
outside the domain, a following motion is defined, following the position of the fish.
The following motion is attached to the background domain to ensure it translates at
the same speed as the fish, making the position of the fish relative to the boundaries
constant.
To move the fish in Starccm+ the morphing tool together with an overset mesh is used.
Morphing moves the vertices in the mesh in this case by a prescribed motion defined by
the user. One should therefore be careful if the mesh moves a lot. Morphing can cause
the mesh to be distorted causing the simulation to be wrongful. A distorted mesh will
cause bad interpolation within the cells. The overset mesh ensures that the mesh can
move freely in the background domain. The overset mesh boundary is therefore also
set to floating in Starcmm+, making the overset region boundary follow the prescribed
motion. As the fish would start to move forward it was found that the mesh would
become distorted caused by bad interpolation between the motion and the morpher. To
fix this issue the ”Linear Fitter” was enabled in Starccm+, which fits a linear model
to the movement of the mesh. The morpher method used in Starccm+ is the B-spline
method. This is used because it is found to be significantly faster than the RBF method.

The RAS system investigated is based on a setup at Billund Aquaculture, but is not a
replica. The system is first investigated without fish to capture the flow in the tank.
Secondly, the RAS system is investigated with the effect of fish. The effect of fish is
included by adding the measured values from section 4.6 using different methods.
5.1
Domain and geometry
The tank is circular with a diameter of 23m and a height of 5.8 m. The water comes
into the tanks through 10 holes in the inlet pipe. There are 4 inlet pipes placed 1 m from

tank and has a diameter of 3 m. The top of the tank is a free surface with water-air
interaction. The geometry is described in figure 5.1. The origin of the coordinate system
is located at the free-surface in the center of the inlet pipe.

Physics
The physics for the simulation of the tank are:
• Three-dimensional
• Constant density (Water)
• Steady
• Segregated flow
• Turbulent Reynolds Average Navier-Stokes
• All y+ wall treatment
• Standard k −ε Low Re
A three-dimensional study was selected since the tank could not be simplified into a 2D

also how it is simulated in the industry. The turbulence model chosen is the same as for
the fish simulation, therefore the Standard k−ε Low Re model is selected. The standard
k −ε Low Re model might not be ideal for strong rotating water, so the results should
be used with caution. The All y+ treatment is selected, where y+ < 1 or y+ > 30. The
segregated flow solver is chosen with a second-order solver using the SIMPLE scheme.

6
Including fish effect
In the following section, the method for including the effect of the fish in the RAS system
is explained. The method tries to replicate the effect of fish by including a turbulence
source term by using the turbulence production term from equation (4.2) and (4.3)
obtained by the numerical study of the fish. The method should also be able to replicate
the effect of a single fish swimming, hence the method is also compared to the study
of a single fish. Two different methods are developed. The first method will use the
time-averaged volume integral of the TKE and TDR production terms found in equation
(4.12) and (4.13). The second method will use the time average but resolve the spatial
part of the TKE and TDR production terms.
To compare the two methods two different setups are used one trying to replicate the
effect of one fish and one trying to impose the effect on the RAS system. The first setup
works by having a domain without a fish but with a turbulence source term including the
fish turbulence production. Suppose one could perfectly resolve the turbulence properties
in time and space. In that case, the numerical simulation of the fish and the one only
with the turbulence source terms should give the same result. The second study includes
the turbulent source terms based on the fish study imposed in the RAS tank system.
The hope is that it should thereby include the turbulence of the fish and results similar
to Gorle et al [2] and Plew et al [3] should be obtained.
The solutions from both numerical studies of the single fish and the fish with periodic
boundaries are used. Both studies are included using the same method.
6.1
Time-averaged volume integral method
To create the study replicating the turbulence of a single fish the same physics and
boundaries are applied as for the numerical study of the fish, but the fish boundary is
removed, so only the background domain is left. Because the mesh is not moving in the
test domain, the velocity inlet is equal to the fish’s swimming speed. The turbulence
production values found in the numerical study of the fish are included by adding a

in the tank. In the material received from Billund Aquaculture A/S a salmon with a
length of 0.7m weighs around 4.5kg see figure 3.5a. The density of fish in the tank for
a growout tank is 85 kg

tank grid is a cartesian grid with a spacing of 0.1m in each direction. In figure 6.3a a
graphic explanation of how the fish is inserted into the tank. The fish grid is rotated and
translated, so the fish are placed as they would be in the tank, this is done by having
1.05m between them in the angle direction, 0.2m in the radial direction and 0.32 in
the height direction. These lengths correspond to the unit fish volume. The fish points
(green points) are interpolated to the nearest points in the tank grid (blue points). Each
fish point will hold the scalar values of TKE and TDR production and the cell volume.
The turbulence values from the fish study are interpolated by adding to the turbulence
terms weighted by the volume of the cell from the fish points. To do all the operations
a Python script is created. The Python script can be seen in appendix C.

The two methods used to include the effect of fish in the RAS system are evaluated

2. The effect of multiple fish using the time-averaged volume integral method with

3. The effect of multiple fish using the time-averaged spatial resolved method with
turbulence production values obtained from the study of a fish swimming alone.
4. The effect of multiple fish using the time-averaged spatial resolved method with
turbulence production values obtained from the study of a fish swimming in a
school.
The methods are compared by looking at the turbulence viscosity ratio in the RAS system
and comparing it to the RAS system with no fish. The hypothesis is that the impact
of the fish induces turbulence in the tank leading to a higher turbulence viscosity as
observed by Plew et al. [3]. The turbulence viscosity in each solution is evaluated by
taking the volume average for the whole domain in the RAS tank. Furthermore, the
distribution of the turbulence viscosity ratio is observed to see if the turbulence viscosity
increases in the areas where the fish are located.
In figure 6.5 the turbulent viscosity ratio is shown for the different methods for including
the effect of fish. Figure 6.5a shows the turbulence viscosity ratio without any fish in
the tank. Examining figure 6.5 it is observed that none of the 4 cases produces more

solution with no fish. The time-averaged volume integral method produces no turbulence
in the fish volume. This is contrary to expected physical behaviour and suggests that this
method fails to capture the essential turbulence characteristics induced by the presence of
fish. While the time-averaged spatial resolved method does not produce more turbulence
in the tank compared to the solution with no fish, it still produces turbulence. Especially
the solution which uses the turbulence production values obtained by the fish study with
periodic boundaries. Here the turbulence viscosity ratio is around 10. This agrees well
with previous results since it was also the solution that produced the most turbulence
viscosity in the single fish study.
It is also likely the time-averaged spatial resolved
method would perform better if the turbulence values of the fish were interpolated into
a finer grid in the tank. The tank grid with dx = 0.1m is most likely coarse considering
the dimensions of the fish and the base size used in the fish simulation. Making the grid
finer is currently impossible due to the lack of computer power.

grid’s resolution is finer, the effect of one fish was inserted into the tank. The fish was
inserted 6m from the centre of the tank between the two periodic boundaries and 2.5m

6.4.2
Final considerations on the derived methods
The goal of this project is to develop a method that can include the effect of fish, which
is useful for engineering purposes.
Unfortunately, the time-averaged volume integral
method is not able to produce any useful results. The time-averaged spatial resolved
model on the other hand has the potential to be able to capture the fish effect but
is flawed when it comes to being applied for engineering purposes. The time-averaged
spatial method is heavily dependent on the resolution of 3 things the fish grid, the tank
grid, and the tank mesh. The problem for this method is that the fish mesh needs to be
fine to capture the turbulence created, this means that the tank grid needs to be fine
to resolve the inserted production terms with a fair accuracy. Since the fish turbulence
values need to be interpolated onto the tank grid and mesh they have to be at least the
same resolution as the fish grid, to capture the turbulence production. To set things in
perspective there are ≈107 cells in the fish mesh within 0.05m3. The volume of the tank
44
==PAGE:56==
is ≈1500m3. It would therefore require a very fine tank grid and mesh, to be able to
resolve the turbulence production values accurately. As the RAS systems are predicted
to be bigger in the future the time-averaged spatial resolved method does not seem to
be a great solution for engineering purposes. For this to work it would have to be shown
that the fish effect could be captured for low resolutions with a great accuracy as well.
6.4.3
Suggestions for future work
As it was shown that by only including the Pk term in the turbulence source term, the
desired effect was obtained, this should be investigated further in the future. There could
be a probability that the time-averaged volume integral when only including the Pk term
would work. It would be desirable to capture the fish effect with the time-averaged
method since this does not require any changes to the existing numerical study of the
RAS system.
One could also try to build similar turbulence production models as done for the canopy
flows by Zeng et al [13] and Zhao et al [14]. This would require obtaining experimentally
found values for the turbulence production terms and then fitting them into the RAS
system. This method has shown good results in the field of canopy flows and could
possibly also work for including the fish effect in RAS systems.

7
Conclusion
The primary objective of this study was to develop and compare methods for incorpo-
rating the effect of fish within CFD modelling of a Recirculating Aquaculture System
(RAS). The challenge lies in replicating the fish-induced turbulence through source terms
in the turbulence model equations. The goal was to capture the turbulence production
from a CFD study of a fish and include them in the RAS system.
A preliminary numerical study of a single fish swimming was conducted to obtain val-
ues for the turbulence production terms. The study used the k −ε Standard Low Re
model, as this was found to be the best way to measure the turbulence production
terms. Convergence studies of both mesh and time showed that the velocity of the fish
and the turbulence production terms could be estimated with reasonable accuracy. The
results indicated that the fish reached a terminal velocity of approximately 0.88BL
s , which
closely matched Videler’s [5] observations for similar swimming styles. The velocity and
pressure fields around the fish, as well as the vortex patterns, were consistent with theo-
retical predictions and previous studies, validating the model’s accuracy. The turbulence
production terms were obtained for two scenarios, a fish swimming alone and a fish
swimming in a school. The turbulence production was evaluated by a time-averaged
volume integral. For the fish swimming alone, they were found to be Pk = 0.02W and
Pε = 1.66W
s . For the fish swimming in a school, the values were found to be Pk = 0.09W
and Pε = 4.95W
s . In both scenarios, the fish was found to produce turbulence by looking
at the turbulence viscosity added to the simulation. The fish with other fish swimming
nearby was found to be producing the most turbulence.
A numerical study of the RAS system without fish was conducted to begin with to ensure
it was ready to include fish. The study used a quarter of the tank and a steady time
solver to reduce the computation time. The study also used the k −ε Standard Low Re
model as this was needed to include the found turbulence values from the fish. A time
and mesh convergence study was also performed to ensure convergence of the velocity
in the tank. The tank without fish was found to have velocities around 0.5m
s in the fish
region, which was in agreement with the fish swimming speeds. The turbulence model
was found to introduce large amounts of turbulence viscosity in the centre of the tank.
To include the effect of fish in the RAS system two methods were proposed. The first
method would use the time-averaged volume integral of the turbulence production terms
and the other would use the time-averaged but spatial resolved turbulence production
terms. To try and validate the methods a study trying to replicate the turbulence effect
of the single fish swimming was created. This was done by inserting the turbulence
production terms into an empty domain in the fish region using the two methods. The
methods were judged by seeing how well they replicated the turbulence viscosity created
by the study of the single fish. From this validation experiment, it was found that the
time-averaged volume integral method was not able to capture the turbulence created
by the fish. The method resulted in no turbulence created in the region near the fish.
The time-averaged spatial resolved method was able to capture the turbulence produced
by the fish. The method produced similar amounts of turbulence as for the numerical
46
==PAGE:58==
study of the single fish.
None of the two methods were able to produce enough turbulence when inserted into the
RAS tank. The time-averaged volume integral method gave rise to a lower turbulence
viscosity ratio in the fish volume. Furthermore, this increased the velocity in the Tank,
which was contrary to the experimental studies by Gorle et al and Plew et al. The time-
averaged spatial resolved method produced a bit of turbulence viscosity in the location
where it was applied, but the turbulence viscosity was higher in the same areas for the
tank study without the fish. The reason could be that the spatial resolution of both the
tank and the fish turbulence values was not high enough. This was tested by inserting
the effect of fish into the tank with a very high resolution. The result was that the
effect of the single fish still reduced the turbulence viscosity. It therefore seems that
both methods proposed failed to include the effect of fish in the RAS system.
Because none of the methods worked it was tried only including the TKE production
term Pk in the turbulence source term. This was done using the time-averaged spatial
resolved method.
It resulted in an increase in turbulent viscosity and a decrease in
velocity in the tank. This agrees with the experimental results but will need further
investigation to be validated.
