Abstract
In the current trend of ever-growing rotors for wind turbines rotors, load mitigation has
become a crucial and often limiting factor for the development of wind energy. More and
more sophisticated control systems are integrated into the turbine, including complex
active pitching systems. One simpler passive control method that has been the subject of
much research in the past decades is Bend-Twist Coupling (BTC). The aim of this thesis
is to provide with an overview of the diﬀerent methods used to introduce BTC into a
blade design, and their eﬀects on the stability of the turbine. The methods investigated
are material coupling through either spar cap or skin ﬁber rotation, blade sweep and
spar cap oﬀsetting. A wide-range parametric study is conducted with those design
variables, using a multi-disciplinary aeroelastic design tool. The steady-states results are
computed and compared across BTC range, validating the introduction of coupling and
investigating the impact on power and aerodynamic loads. Some other parameters, such
as blade stiﬀness properties, are also analyzed across the diﬀerent designs. A stability
analysis is then conducted over the dozens of produced aeroelastic turbine models. The
eﬀects of BTC on the frequencies and damping ratios of the diﬀerent aeroelastic modes
are investigated. Additional investigations are conducted to understand the causes for the
observed changes across BTC types and range, tying the results to the theory developed
in previous studies. To conclude and verify the obtained results, a time-domain load
analysis is conducted on a selection of BTC-to-feather models. The ability of the created
designs to provide signiﬁcant load mitigation is demonstrated, and the advantage gained
through aeroelastic coupling is compared to the loss of energy production. The study
concludes on the good potential of two of the designs (spar cap ﬁber angle and spar cap
oﬀset) for load mitigation in a large-scale wind turbine, at a reasonable cost for stability.
Skin ﬁber rotation does not procure signiﬁcant amount of coupling, nor load reduction.
Backward sweeping of the blade is on par with the ﬁrst two designs for BTC induction,
but presents poor performance in fatigue load alleviation that can be explained by a
degradation of turbine stability.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The wind energy sector has seen signiﬁcant changes over the past decades. From 24 GW
in 2001, the global wind power capacity has increased to 651 GW in 2019 [18]. This
trend of exponential growth is forecast to continue in the foreseeable future. In the
European Union alone, the EU Strategy on Oﬀshore Renewable Energy plans for 300
GW in oﬀshore wind capacity by 2050 (from 12 GW in 2020) [7]. In order to match
that growth, wind turbine manufacturers have been introducing larger Wind Turbine
Generators (WTG) over time. Today’s commercial models are reaching the 10 GW
mark (MHI Vestas V164-9.5 MW [51]), with the average rating being only of 1 MW in
2000 [26]. Major Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) have already announced 14
GW models, that will be introduced in 2024 [14].
The size of the rotor has been increasing with turbine ratings. General Electric biggest
model, the GE Haliade-X, is designed with 107-meter-long blades [11], as long as a
football ﬁeld. Following the square-cube law, the swept area of the rotor (hence the
energy passing through it) goes up with the square of the blade length, while its mass
scales with the cube of the radius. As a result, scaling up older designs to create bigger
WTGs will cause an unsustainable increase in loads. Therefore, new models are made
with evermore slender blades, using new lighter materials with high stiﬀness that can
meet the design structural requirements.
Modern wind turbines also make use of advanced control methods to mitigate the
loads. Active methods such as blade pitching using an actuator, are now the main way to
control the turbine behaviour. It has gradually replaced stall-control, a passive method
that has been mostly seen in smaller WTG and tends to lead to unstable vibrations for
upscaled models [20].
Another passive control method, based on smart aeroelastic tailoring of the blade
is Bend-Twist Coupling (BTC). Initially investigated in the 1980s, Karaolis et al. [28],
Kooijman [30] and Lobitz & Veers [33] pioneered the use of BTC in Horizontal Axis
Wind Turbines (HAWTs) for either load mitigation or energy capture enhancement.
Over time, studies of the impact of BTC on blade and/or turbine performances (loads,
stability, Annual Energy Production [AEP], etc.) have become prominent. A number
of blades designed for BTC response to aerodynamic loading have also been proposed,
with their dynamic characteristics compared to non-BTC reference models. Whilst these
studies have contributed to the ﬁeld, they have tended to be either theoretical (BTC is
implemented through mathematical tools and studied in a general sense) or very narrow
in scope (presenting only one or a few BTC designs to be compared).

“Bend-Twist Coupling can be implemented into realistic blade designs and provide signiﬁ-

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter provides the necessary background to understand the principles of Bend-
Twist Coupling as applied on a HAWT, a summary of past and current research on the
subject, as well as a description of the potential and shortcomings of BTC with regards
to commercial application. First the terminology related to wind turbine dynamics is
introduced, as well as a presentation of the constitutive elements of a WTB (Section 2.1).
Next the principle of BTC is explained, with an introduction to the diﬀerent kinds of
BTC (BTC-to-stall, BTC-to-feather) and their speciﬁcities (Section 2.2). The state
of the art for BTC is then summarized (Section 2.3). The history of BTC research is
covered, from the ﬁrst implementation of the method on HAWTs to the most recent
studies of BTC impact on turbine stability and loads. Finally, the combination of factors
required to enable BTC implementation into commercial WTG designs is discussed, with
a mention of how each of them is considered in this study (Section 2.4).
2.1
Wind Turbine Terminology
In order to understand the results in this study, especially the ones linked to the stability
analysis, some common terms related to wind turbine modal dynamics must be deﬁned.
The terminology is explained in this section, applied to the case of the three-blade HAWT.
A description of the blade cross-section and its diﬀerent constitutive elements is also
provided.
In most modern WTG designs, a three-blade rotor connects through the hub to the
rotating shaft. The nacelle, which holds the shaft, is connected to the tower, which itself
is anchored to the ground. The yaw bearing that connects tower and nacelle allows for
the alignment of the rotor with the wind by rotating it around the tower main axis.
Other possible degrees of freedom (DOFs) for the WTG (tilt, roll, etc.) are presented
in Figure 2.1. The blades, which are a central focus of this study, are attached at the
root, reducing their DOFs to 6. The six DOFs are: rotation around the blade main axis,
called torsion or twist; displacements along both axes orthogonal to the blade main axis,
edgewise for in-rotor plane deﬂection and ﬂapwise for deﬂection perpendicular to the
rotor plane; as well as extension along the blade main axis and two shear deformation
DOFs. The ﬁrst 3 of these DOFs, illustrated in Figure 2.1, are used in this study to
describe the blade motion. The bearing joint linking each blade to the hub also allows
for controlled individual pitching of the blades.
==PAGE:26==
6
2 Background
Figure 2.1: Terminology for wind turbine degrees of freedom [20]
When looking at the blade structure itself, several elements can be identiﬁed, each
with their own role and properties. These elements are presented in Figure 2.2. The
spar is a beam (often box- or I-shaped) that runs through all or part of the blade span
to ensure its structural stiﬀness. It is composed of a top and bottom cap (which can
be incorporated into the airfoil shape of the blade) and of two shear webs linking these
caps. The spar caps are usually mainly composed of a thick uniaxial layer of Glass
Fiber Reinforced Plastic (GFRP) or Carbon Fiber Reinforced Plastic (CFRP) to ensure
ﬂapwise stiﬀness. The shear webs can be made of a sandwich structure where the core
material (often foam or balsa wood) is reinforced by bi or triaxial layers of composite
(GFRP, CFRP). They provide the structure with resistance to shear deformation, as
well as additional bending strength. Finally, the skin gives the blade its aerodynamic
properties due to its airfoil shape. It is made of a suction and pressure side, that are
usually built as two separate pieces before being assembled through two epoxy joints,
respectively at the leading and trailing edges along the chord line. The material stacking
in the skin also has a sandwich structure, usually with triaxial ﬁber layers surrounding
the core material. The leading and trailing edge are usually reinforced, for example
by adding uniaxial ﬁber layers, as they concentrate higher amounts of loads due to
aerodynamic and gravitational forces.
2.2
Bend-Twist Coupling
Bend-Twist Coupling is a passive control method that has been investigated for load
reduction and/or power production enhancement on WTGs. It consists of having a design
where the blade torsional DOF is coupled with one of its bending DOFs. Coupling of

2.2 Bend-Twist Coupling
7
Figure 2.2: Wind turbine blade cross-section elements [41]
the torsion to the ﬂapwise bending motion is referred to as ﬂapwise BTC while coupling
to the edgewise bending motion is called edgewise BTC. Due to the blade structural
and aerodynamic properties, ﬂapwise BTC is more favorable, and is therefore the only
kind of BTC covered in this thesis. Under aerodynamic loading, the lift force acting on a
blade section induces a ﬂapwise bending moment that will grow with the wind speed.
That ﬂapwise deﬂection will be larger than the edgewise deﬂection caused by the drag
force as a result of blade properties. The lift force itself depends on the blade angle of
attack (AoA). Therefore, by coupling the blade torsion to its ﬂapwise bending motion, a
control loop is introduced on the blade aerodynamic loading. The linear lift force L is
given in N/m by the following equation [1]:
L = 1
2ρW 2cCl(α)
(2.1)
where c is the chord length, ρ the air density, W the wind relative velocity calculated
in the blade frame, and Cl the lift coeﬃcient that is a function of the AoA α. The
relation between the lift coeﬃcient and the AoA is given by the airfoil characteristics
and is therefore speciﬁc for each blade section. However, the coeﬃcient will usually
grow approximately linearly with the AoA in the airfoil normal operating region, before
peaking as it reaches the optimal AoA value, and decreasing thereafter for higher angles
in what is called the stall region.
Using that characteristic, two kinds of BTC methods can be developed. First, BTC-to-
feather twists the blade towards a lower AoA when the aerodynamic loading increases.
The goal for BTC-to-feather is to reduce the loads on the turbine while keeping the
AoA within the operating region. The method by itself cannot fully regulate the turbine.
However, it can be coupled with an active control method, such as blade pitching, in
order to achieve extreme and fatigue load reduction. This will increase the WTG life
and potentially allow for the design of larger turbines, capable of capturing more energy
from the wind while keeping a load envelope similar to non-BTC models.
BTC-to-stall, on the other hand, pushes the AoA value up when the forces on the
blade get higher (see Figure 2.3). It aims at pushing the AoA into the stall region,

where the ﬂow separates from the blade surface. This leads to a quasi-linear drop in lift
coeﬃcient and, therefore, to a reduction in the aerodynamic loading of the rotor. Stall
control has long been used as a control method for WTGs. BTC-to-stall can be used
to provide higher power output in the operating region by increasing Cl, while ensuring
the control of the turbine at higher wind speeds. Although BTC-to-stall seemed more
promising in the early days of BTC study, the advent of pitch-controlled large WTG
models has recently put more focus on the BTC-to-feather method. Both methods are
investigated in the study, as it aims at covering the full range of possibilities for BTC
design.

(left) and BTC-to-stall (right) [15]
Introducing BTC into a blade design can be done in several ways. The diﬀerent
design methods are separated into two main groups: geometrical coupling, which achieves
BTC through tailoring of the blade geometry, and material coupling, which utilizes the
anisotropic properties of the composite materials that make up the blade. More details
on the diﬀerent designs studied in the thesis are given in Section 4.2. The general case of
material coupling is detailed here, based on the research from Ong and Tsai [38].
In their study, Ong and Tsai investigated the potential for inducing BTC through
material coupling, by variating the composite ply orientation in a D-shaped thin-walled
spar. Using 3D numerical modelling and experimental results, the research ﬁnds a
practical limit to the amount of BTC achievable with GFRP and CFRP. The maximum
BTC coeﬃcient (see Section 2.3) attainable for GFRP is γ = 0.5 while CFRP reaches
an higher value of γ = 0.8. The highest values of γ are obtained in the 15-30◦ply angle
range (see Figure 2.4). The main factors for material coupling are found to be material,
ply orientation and proportional volume of anisotropic layers in the laminate. In another
study, Fedorov and Berggreen [16] conduct experiments on small WTB sections with
uniaxial GFRP spar caps. They conclude that rotating the spar cap ﬁber at a 20-25◦
results in 30-35% loss in bending stiﬀness. The balance between BTC and bending
stiﬀness is a central problematic for material coupling BTC designs, due to constraints
like tower clearance.

2 Background
The early research on BTC was mostly focused on BTC-to-stall designs. These tended to
be based on constant-speed and stall-controlled rotors. Kooijman [30] studied the poten-
tial of BTC for power generation enhancement below rated speed for a constant-speed
pitch-controlled rotor. BTC-to-feather is applied to the inner section of the blade (up to
40% span) to counterbalance the BTC-to-stall applied to the outer section. The study
found a potential for increasing the AEP by a few percentage points, at the cost of a
10% increment in starting torque. Lobitz et al. [31] observed a decrease in loads at rated
speed for a constant-speed stall-controlled turbine with a BTC-to-stall blade design. A
model with larger rotor diameter was obtained while keeping the same maximum power,
allowing for an increase in AEP. However, a later study [32] showed substantial increase
in fatigue damage for BTC-to-stall blades, while the turbine was also more prone to stall
ﬂutter. These ﬂaws, and the overall shift of the market towards large, pitch-controlled
WTG models, reduced the interest for BTC-to-stall designs. Instead, BTC-to-feather
became more attractive to researchers, for its potential in reducing fatigue and extreme
loads, thus allowing for slender blade designs.
Lobitz et al. [35], and later Lobitz and Veers [34], both investigated the impact of
BTC-to-feather on fatigue damage for HAWTs, using blades with a coupling coeﬃcient
of γ = 0.6 and a pretwisting method to preserve power output around rated speed.
Turbulent wind time simulations were ran based on the IEC Category I Class A standard
(at 100% and 50% of the standard intensity). Both studies showed a signiﬁcant reduction
in fatigue and maximum loads for a number of blade materials (welded steel, aluminium
and composite). A similar pretwisting method for BTC-to-feather blades was introduced
by Stäblein et al. [45].
General studies of the impact of BTC on stability have also been conducted. Lob-
itz and Veers [33] analyzed the impact of BTC (both towards stall and towards feather)
on the aeroelastic stability of the Combined Experiment Blade. A mapping for the diver-
gence and ﬂutter limits is provided for γ values ranging from −0.8 to +0.8. The results
highlight that BTC-to-feather reduces the ﬂutter limit, while BTC-to-stall increases it.
However, BTC-to-stall models present an issue with divergence instabilities that is far
more limiting than the ﬂutter limit for the stable rotor-speed/BTC-coeﬃcient range.
Stäblein et al. [44, 46] studied the aeroelastic modal properties of both a blade section
and a full blade model with BTC. The studies conﬁrmed Lobitz and Veers’ ﬁndings on
BTC eﬀects on the ﬂutter speed limit. Overall the ﬂutter limit for BTC models is well
below the rated rotor speed and its reduction is not considered a risk to the wind turbine
stability. Stäblein also showed that BTC-to-feather tends to increase frequencies and
reduce damping ratios for the ﬂapwise modes, and vice-versa for BTC-to-stall.
Finally, a number of studies looking at the impact of speciﬁc BTC-to-feather designs
have been conducted, involving both material and geometric coupling. Bottasso et al. [6]
investigated the potential of material coupling for load alleviation and Actuator Duty
Cycle (ADC) reduction, using an optimization-based multilevel method. Several designs

blade are produced. The “best compromise” solutions are selected using a cost function

the aeroelastic properties of the NREL 5-MW reference turbine. Backward sweep is
shown to introduce a twist towards feather. The frequency response of the ﬂapwise blade
root bending moment from wind excitation decreases. It falls under the ﬁrst ﬂapwise
frequency that itself is increased by the eﬀects of BTC-to-feather. This results in lower
ﬂapwise loads on the backward-swept blade models. The classical ﬂutter limit is found
to be reduced compared to the reference turbine, but still well above the operational
rotor speed range of the turbine. Swept blades are also studied by Ding and Zhang [10],
who used an non-dominated sorting generic algorithm to ﬁnd the optimal sweep with
respect to AEP and loads. The study concludes that a backward-swept design can be
obtained that increases AEP by more than 1% while reducing extreme and fatigue loads
signiﬁcantly. Finally, a thorough research covering blade sweep, structural angle, skin
and spar cap ﬁber angle designs is conducted by Bortolotti et al. [5]. The study is based
on the work from Bottasso [6] and uses the same multidisciplinary optimization tool. The
study concludes that swept blades and ﬁber angle designs are suitable passive control
options for load mitigation, while the structural angle designs shows less potential when
applied to the studied turbine. Optimal designs are found with the help of a Levelized
Cost of Electricity (LCoE) maximizing function, and are using larger rotor diameters to
increase the turbine AEP.
2.4
The BTC Trifecta
Whilst being discussed and studied for decades now, BTC has not yet been broadly
adopted by WTG manufacturers. Indeed, in an industry where the LCoE is the bench-
mark factor, OEMs have preferred to keep with more traditional blade designs despite the
==PAGE:32==
12
2 Background
fast increase in turbine size. That is because, to be commercially viable, a BTC design
needs to show three main characteristics vis-à-vis its non-BTC counterparts: provide
signiﬁcant load mitigation; have no or a contained impact on the turbine stability; and
possess a similar price-point to a non-BTC model, i.e. any increase in manufacturing
cost must be balanced by beneﬁces from the design (such as longer lifespan, increased
AEP, reduced maintenance, etc.). This BTC “trifecta” has to be considered in order for
a study to provide valuable results to the wind energy research ﬁeld.
In this thesis, these three factors are considered when investigating a large set of BTC
designs. As the main focus of the study, stability is thoroughly examined. Parametric
studies are conducted to provide a guidebook of the impact of each variant of a BTC
design (to-feather or to-stall) on turbine stability. Fatigue and extreme loads are in-
vestigated on a set of the most promising designs for load mitigation (BTC-to-feather,
studying the case of a modern large-scale WTG). The load values are compared to the
ones from the initial design. Finally, the cost parameter is one that is diﬃcult to quantify
due to manufacturers keeping their actual cost and processes a secret. Therefore, it is
replaced by a manufacturability constraint on the designs. The assumption is made
that, by keeping the BTC designs close to the standard design, big changes in the
manufacturing process can be avoided, ensuring that the increase in manufacturing cost
will stay moderate.

CHAPTER 3
Methodology & Theory
In this chapter, the diﬀerent methods used in this Thesis are presented, alongside some
important parts of the theory on which those methods are based. This consists mainly
in presenting the diﬀerent software tools that have been used to conduct the study. Most
of these tools are created and maintained by the DTU Department of Wind Energy. In
addition, resources provided by DTU Wind Energy, such as the Jess and Sophia HPC
clusters, have also been used to run heavy computations.
The Thesis is split into three main phases: BTC design creation, stability analysis
and load analysis. HAWTOpt2 (Section 3.3) is used to conduct the design phase. The
stability analysis is run on HAWCStab2 (Section 3.1), while HAWC2 is used for the load
analysis (Section 3.2). Due to the fact that HAWTOpt2 has built in interfaces with the
other two software, that are used in parts of HAWTOpt2 workﬂow, the HAWCStab2/HAWC2
sections are presented ﬁrst, despite being mainly used later on in the study.
3.1
HAWCStab2
HAWCStab2 is a software for wind turbine modelling and aero-servo-elastic stability anal-
ysis [13]. It was developed by the DTU Department of Wind Energy. It is suitable for
steady-states computations and can perform stability analysis on either full WTG or
isolated blade models. Controller tuning and optimal operational data calculations can
also be performed. The model is based on an analytical linearization of a geometrically
non-linear Finite Element Method (FEM) model in a nonlinear co-rotational kinematic
formulation [21]. The FEM model uses two-noded prismatic Timoshenko beam elements,
that accounts for shear deformations and rotational inertia of the beam cross-sections [22].
A newest version of the Timoshenko beam formulation, implemented by Kim et al. [29],
also accounts for fully coupled cross-section and, therefore, a correct modeling of ma-
terial coupling blades. Each nodes has 6 degrees of freedom (DOFs), including three
displacements and three rotations.
The structural model is coupled with an unsteady aerodynamic model, based on the
Blade Element Momentum theory. Each beam element contains two aerodynamic cal-
culation points, placed at the second node and at midpoint between the nodes. Each
of these calculation points includes two states approximating the frequency response of
the Theodorsen theory, and two states describing trailing edge separation and dynamic
stall [21]. Although frozen wake is assumed in this study, HAWCStab2 also incorporates a
model for dynamic inﬂow [47]. Analytical linearization is performed around steady-state
conditions, that are obtained by reaching an equilibrium between elastic, aerodynamic,
==PAGE:34==
14
3 Methodology & Theory
and centrifugal forces. The co-rotational formulation used in the model allows for the
capture of nonlinear eﬀects due to large deﬂections and rotations. The model does not,
however, account for gravity or other anisotropic eﬀects. A more in-depth description of
the aeroelastic model is provided by Hansen, applied to an isolated blade [21].
Finally, it is to be highlighted that HAWCStab2 result accuracy has been veriﬁed by
several studies. Among them, the 2016 paper by Verelst et al. [50] compared the steady-
states results from HAWCStab2 with the results from time-domain simulations conducted
on HAWC2. Stäblein’s 2017 study [46] was speciﬁcally comparing frequency results for the
case of BTC blades. Both studies concluded a consistently good agreement between the
results from the two software.

3.2
HAWC2
HAWC2 is a nonlinear aeroelastic code, used to calculate the wind turbine response in
time-domain. It is part of the suite of commercially available software from DTU Wind
Energy. The tool is based on an aeroelastic model largely similar to the one used in
HAWCStab2 (see Section 3.1), with a structural formulation based on multi-body dynamics
with a FEM Timoshenko beam model with a ﬂoating frame of reference formulation [42],
while the aerodynamic model is based on the unsteady BEM theory. Unlike HAWCStab2,
the aeroelastic formulation in HAWC2 accounts for nonlinear eﬀects from large deﬂections
and rotations, and the aerodynamic model is extended to handle dynamic and skew
inﬂow, dynamic stall (based on a Beddoes-Leishman model) and shear eﬀects on the
induction [23, 37].
Another major diﬀerence with HAWCStab2 is the implementation of a controller sys-
tem through a Dynamic Link Library (DLL). Combined with the diﬀerent modules that
can be added to model external forces such as wind or hydrodynamic loads, the controller
system allows for the simulation of various load cases. Notably, this enables certiﬁcation
of WTG models – onshore and oﬀshore – to be performed using HAWC2, by running the
diﬀerent Design Load Cases (DLCs) deﬁned in the IEC 61400-3 standard [25]. One such
a DLC is DLC1.2, which simulates normal operation on a turbine under turbulent wind.
It is used in this study to determine fatigue loads on several of the BTC models. A more
detailed description of the DLC, and its run within the HAWC2 framework, is given in the
following section.
3.2.1
DLC 1.2
DLC 1.2 is meant for assessing the turbine fatigue loads while producing power in normal
turbulence conditions. The IEC 61400-3 standard recommends running 600-second
simulations for wind speeds between 4 and 26 m/s with a 2 m/s step. The IEC Normal
Turbulence Model is used, and a minimum of 6 seeds is recommended per wind speed. In
this study, a total of 30 seeds per inﬂow case (22 inﬂow cases, one for each wind speed)

done in this study in order to save time on the computations.

3.3
HAWTOpt2
HAWTOpt2 [52] is a tool developed by the DTU Department of Wind Energy for aero-servo-
elastic optimization of wind turbines. Its workﬂow combines aeroelastic and structural
solvers, allowing for simultaneous fully coupled optimization of the blade outer geometry
and internal structure. Multi-objective multi-constrained optimization is enabled, using
a cost function that can be based on an exhaustive set of parameters such as AEP maxi-
mization, geometry constraints, fatigue load constraints or natural frequency placement.
In the context of wind turbine design, where a lot of often conﬂicting objectives are to
be achieved with deeply interconnected parameters, HAWTOpt2 can be used to produce
aeroelastically tailored blades, as was done by Zahle et al. [53]. Using the DTU 10MW
RWT as a starting point, the study shows a 8.7% increase in AEP with overall similar
extreme and fatigue loads, demonstrating the interest of an integrated multidisciplinary
design approach.

BECAS
The BEam Cross-section Analysis Software (BECAS) is a ﬁnite element based tool
suitable for the analysis of laminate beam composite cross-sections of complex geometries.
It was developed by José Blasques [2] and is based on the theory introduced by Giavotto
==PAGE:41==
3.3 HAWTOpt2
21
et al. [17]. The tool is written in MATLAB and returns the 6 × 6 stiﬀness matrices
for a set of chosen cross-section along the blade span. This allows for the use of a
Finite Element beam model for the turbine rotor, where the element stiﬀness properties
are based on BECAS output. BECAS can also be used as a post-processor to conduct
stress and fracture analysis based on the loads obtained through aeroelastic simulation.
However, the tool does not provide for this middle step, which has to be carried by
another software, as shown in Figure 3.3 (HAWTOpt2 uses HAWCStab2 and HAWC2 to run
the aeroelastic simulations).
BECAS takes as input a 2D mesh of the cross-section, as well as its element anisotropic
and material properties. Equilibrium equations are set up for each element of the 2D
discretized cross-section geometry, using the principle of virtual work. These equilibrium
equations result in a set of 2nd order diﬀerential equations that, once solved, lead to both
a central and an extreme solution. From these solutions, the 6 × 6 stiﬀness matrix of the
cross-section can be derived. More details on the theory behind BECAS can be found in
the work from Giavotto et al. [17] and José Blasques [2].

results [40]. In addition, BECAS also models the eﬀects of warping and shear deformation,
which have been found to be both signiﬁcant for BTC blades, according to Stäblein’s
research [43].

4.1
The IEA 10-MW Reference Wind Turbine
In the wind energy research ﬁeld, studies often focus on narrow part of the turbine
design, sometimes a single component, on which innovative approaches and/or improved
methods are implemented. As discussed previously, wind turbine design is an highly multi-
disciplinary process, where complex interactions have to be accounted for. Therefore, the
use of Reference Wind Turbines has been widely adopted by the scientiﬁc community
to benchmark study ﬁndings against a well-known and well-described model. The IEA
10-MW RWT is a class IA oﬀshore turbine model that was developed by the National
Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), as part of the IEA Wind TCP Task 37 [4]. It is
based on the DTU 10-MW RWT, with which it shares numerous characteristics such
as airfoil data, tower dimensions and material properties. A direct-drive generator is
featured to better reﬂect the current trend in oﬀshore WTG models, while a new design
developed by the Norwegian University of Science and Technology is implemented for
the hub, shaft and nacelle.
The rotor aerostructural design is optimized using an integrated design tool, producing
blades tailored for AEP maximisation while staying within the load envelope of the DTU
10-MW RWT. The optimization process results in an 11% increase in blade length, a
slender blade proﬁle due to reduced thicknesses along the span, and an increase in blade
prebend (from 3.2 m to 6.2 m). The later, combined with the higher rotor cone and shaft
tilt angles, allows for a signiﬁcant increase in static tip clearance. Rated speed is also
raised to 11 m/s as part of the optimization process. The rotor structural layout includes

4.2
BTC Designs
Bend-Twist Coupling is a term descriptive of the blade behaviour. As such, it is not
an initial parameter but rather a resultant characteristic of the blade overall design.
There are, indeed, many ways to induce BTC through the change of some blade design
parameter. If they appear to be similar in the amount of coupling they produce, two
BTC designs can have drastically diﬀerent characteristics when it comes to, for instance,
the structural stiﬀness of the blade or its stability. It has been stated that the goal of
this thesis is to study the physical eﬀects of BTC by implementing it into realistic blade
designs, and not just through theoretical means (e.g. by inserting coupling terms in the
blade stiﬀness matrix). Therefore, it is important to consider the work done here as a
study of BTC-inducing designs rather than a study of BTC.
The list of designs considered here is as exhaustive as a thesis framework allows for. It
encompasses both material and geometric coupling and is overall representative of the
methods used in the diﬀerent scientiﬁc publications about BTC (see Section 2.3). In
the following sections, each of the four designs of the study are described and linked
to similar models found in the literature. For the sake of coherence in the study, all
BTC-to-feather models have a positive variable value and all BTC-to-stall models a
negative one. This sometimes implies a deﬁnition of the variable opposite to the one
used by the design software.
4.2.1
Spar Cap Fiber Angle
As mentioned in Section 2.1, the spar cap elements provide essential structural stiﬀness
to the blade. For the IEA 10-MW turbine, they are mostly made of a thick uniaxial
layer of GFRP. That layer is rotated symmetrically with respect to the chord line for the
top and bottom cap, inducing ﬂapwise BTC. The values of spar cap ﬁber angle used in
the parametric study vary from the 0 degree of the original blade to +8 and −8 degrees
(respectively BTC-to-feather and BTC-to-stall).
The choice of +8 degrees for the upper limit is in accordance with the work from
Bottasso et al. [6], that studies the impact of spar cap and skin ﬁber angle induced BTC
on turbine loads and AEP. The paper highlights a 7% increase in blade mass for the +10
deg spar cap ﬁber angle design, in order to comply with structural constraints such as
maximum tip deﬂection. In the designs considered here, no mass is added to balance the
loss in structural stiﬀness. Therefore, the absolute angle rotation is limited to 8 degrees
to ensure a realistic blade model, with an overall stable behavior.
Using HAWTOpt2, 9 runs are made producing 9 diﬀerent models, that diﬀer only by
their spar cap ﬁber angle. As mentioned, the range goes from +8 to −8 deg, which
means a 2-deg interval between each model. The blade planform geometry is not changed
in material coupling designs (as can be seen in Appendix A). HAWTOpt2 calculates the
adjusted optimal pitch curve for each model.

4.2.2
Skin Fiber Angle
Based on the same principle as Spar Cap Fiber Angle BTC, the Skin Fiber Angle models
are obtained by rotating the angle of the triaxial composite ﬁber layers in the skin element
of the blade (i.e. the entire envelope minus the spar caps). These triaxial layers represent
a much smaller proportion of the leading and trailing panels, which are mostly composed
of balsa wood. This, and the more complex geometry of the skin section, means that
higher angles will be needed in order to achieve meaningful BTC. This more complex
geometry also means a more diﬃcult manufacturing process than for the Spar Cap Fiber
Angle solution.
Bottasso et al. [6] test in their paper BTC-to-feather with skin ﬁber angles going
up to +30 degrees. As for the spar cap, this angle is found to increase signiﬁcantly the
mass of the blade, when satisfying the structural constraints. Therefore, a range of +20
to −20 degrees is decided for the Skin Fiber Angle model of this study.
11 runs are made with HAWTOpt2, resulting in models with 4 degrees of interval. Optimal
pitch is calculated as part of the computations and, once again, no change is made to
the blade basic geometry.
4.2.3
Structural Angle
As the ﬁrst example of geometric coupling, the Structural Angle designs present an
horizontal oﬀset between the top and bottom spar caps. This is done by rotating the
vertical axis of the shear webs with respect to the rotor plane. The angle of that ro-
tation is called the structural angle. An illustration of the parameter is presented in
Figure 4.4. That oﬀsetting of the caps as been shown [53] to induce torsional coupling
by moving the shear center forward to the leading edge (inducing BTC-to-feather) or
backward to the trailing edge (BTC-to-stall). This change in the blade geometric prop-
erties can be observed in Appendix A, and is commonly referred to as shear-twist coupling.
According to Zahle’s paper [53], the design does not lead to a loss of ﬂapwise stiﬀ-
ness as seen in material coupling solutions. Ease of manufacturing is also considered an
advantage compared to other geometric coupling designs such as Blade Sweep, as the
design stays closer to a standard blade (no change of mold). It is also to be noted that
the original IEA 10-MW turbine already has a structural angle value of 6.61◦.
Starting from that value, the Structural Angle is changed 2 degrees at a time to produce
7 diﬀerent models using HAWTOpt2 . The range covered is from −6 to +6 degrees from
the reference design (and will be referred to forward by their diﬀerence to it). Optimal
pitch values are calculated, as per the other designs.

4.2.4
Blade Sweep
Another way to achieve BTC through geometric coupling is by sweeping the blade either
backward (BTC-to-feather) or forward (BTC-to-stall). Sweeping eﬀectively introduces a
chordwise geometric oﬀset in the blade, giving a non-null lever arm to the aerodynamic
forces which are now generating a torsional moment on the blade.
This concept has been explored by Hansen [21], whose study highlighted a decrease in
ﬂapwise Root Bending Moment (RBM) for blades with a backward sweep of 2 and 4
meters respectively (using an arc design slightly moving forward in the inner section of the
blade before bending backward in the outer section - see Figure 4.5). Hansen’s reference
blade comes from the NREL 5-MW RWT and is 61.5m long. When compared to the IEA
10-MW RWT, which has is 96.5-meter-long blades, an equivalent blade length/sweep
ratio leads to a maximum sweep of about 6 meters.
Building on the results from Hansen’s study [21], the Blade Sweep range is chosen
to be +6 m to −6 m. The sweep parameter is deﬁned by moving the blade tip in the
planform x-direction, while keeping its z-value identical. This is done for the sake of
simplicity considering HAWTOpt2 ways of deﬁning the blade geometry. Therefore, the
blade length diﬀers slightly for the diﬀerent models. The spar cap position along the
blade is also redeﬁned to be adaptable to sweep. The blade “neutral” design of 0 m
sweep will therefore slightly diﬀer from the original IEA 10-MW RWT model. This is
necessary to not mix the impact of BTC with other factors in the analysis. Once again, 7
models are made, with a 2-meter interval, each with a re-calculated optimal pitch curve.

Steady-States
After the design creation phase, steady-states computations are run for every BTC model,
using HAWCStab2. Some of the results from these computations are discussed in the
following sections. First, the operational data is presented (Section 4.3.1) so that the
eﬀects of BTC on the turbine normal operation can be discussed. Next, the steady-states
tip displacements and torsion are studied (Section 4.3.2). A qualitative analysis is given
on the potential for BTC induction of the diﬀerent designs, as well as the possible impact
on tower clearance. Finally, a few selected moments are investigated (Section 4.3.3) as a
preliminary study of the impact of the BTC designs on the loads (a more in-depth load
analysis is later conducted on a selection of BTC-to-feather designs – see Chapter 6).

4.3.2
Tip Displacements and Torsion
In order to verify the introduction of Bend-Twist Coupling in the blade behaviour, the
tip motion is investigated, looking at the torsion as well as the ﬂapwise and edgewise
displacements. The results analyzed here are the steady-state values that are returned
by HAWCStab2 after the tip motion convergence has been established the model. Whilst
not accounting for the transient phase of the blade response, these values do provide
some valuable information on constraints like tower clearance. Figures 4.14 to 4.17 show
the tip displacements and torsion for the four BTC design cases.
The Spar Cap Fiber Angle case shows good torsion on both BTC-to-feather (up to
−6.3 deg) and BTC-to-stall models (up to +6.8 deg). The spread is proportional to the
amount of ﬁber rotation introduced and is at its highest around rated speed. It is to
be noted that the reference turbine shows a slight tip torsion towards feather (up to
−1.7 deg at rated speed), induced by the low amount of structural angle in its design.
The tip ﬂapwise displacement curves change accordingly to the level of aerodynamic
thrust applied on the rotor, peaking at rated speed before falling down in Region 3. The
spread between the curves is also proportional to the aerodynamic loading, and conﬁrms
the presence of BTC, with higher values for BTC-to-stall and lower for BTC-to-feather
models. Tower clearance becomes an issue for the BTC-to-stall designs, with up to +9.2
m tip ﬂapwise displacement for the −8 deg model compared to the +3.1 m maximum for
the reference turbine (where positive displacement goes in the direction of the tower).
This is due to the loss of ﬂapwise bending stiﬀness characteristic of material BTC designs.
The BTC-to-feather models, that are suﬀering equal bending stiﬀness losses than their
BTC-to-stall counterparts, show smaller tip ﬂapwise deﬂections than the non-BTC model.
If tower clearance issues cannot be dismissed, due to the limitations of the steady-state
analysis, these results do highlight that the reduction in aerodynamic loading caused by
material BTC-to-feather overcomes the losses in ﬂapwise bending stiﬀness at equilibrium.
The impact of BTC on the aerodynamic loading of the blades shows in the asymmetric
spread of the ﬂapwise deﬂection curves. Edgewise deﬂections show little impact from
the introduction of BTC for this case, with only a slight increase in the pitch-control
region for BTC-to-stall models. However, while the values for the three DOFs converge
towards the reference at low wind speeds, high wind speeds show an unexpected shift in
behaviour, starting after 21 m/s. The tip torsion becomes negative for stall models and
positive for the feather ones, with the bending DOFs following a similar trend.
The results for the Skin Fiber Angle case conﬁrm the hypothesis from the operational
data analysis that this design does not produce signiﬁcant BTC. Even at ±20 deg ﬁber
angle, the model does not depart more than 0.25 deg from the reference turbine, which
is insigniﬁcant. Moreover, the change in tip torsion appears to diminish as the angle
value gets higher, which nullify the interest in higher angle models. Accordingly, no
discernible change in ﬂap or edgewise deﬂection is observable. The lack of eﬀectiveness
of the Skin Fiber Angle design in inducing signiﬁcant BTC is conﬁrmed. It can be

4.4
Blade Structural Stiﬀness
Each blade element cross-section has its structural properties described by a 6 × 6 fully
populated stiﬀness matrix. This matrix is calculated by BECAS as part of the HAWTOpt2
workﬂow (see Section 3.3), and describes the structural stiﬀness of all 6 of the node
DOFs, as well as their interactions. An analysis of the matrix elements (i.e. the stiﬀness
coeﬃcients) along the blade span allows for the comparison of the diﬀerent couplings that
are introduced in each designs, as well as the changes in stiﬀness in the diﬀerent direc-
tions. A complete description of the 6 × 6 stiﬀness matrix KS is provided in Appendix C,
alongside the full set of stiﬀness coeﬃcient, compared across the range of models for
each designs. These are either given in their original unit and as variation percentages
relative to the reference model, in the cases where the coeﬃcient is not initially null. A
selection of the most interesting stiﬀness coeﬃcients for each BTC type is presented in
this section.
Starting with the Spar Cap Fiber Angle case, the apparition of a non-null ﬂapwise BTC
coeﬃcient can be observed (Figure C.5c). The values are anti-symmetrical, as expected,
between the BTC-to-feather and BTC-to-stall models. In the HAWTOpt2 design process,
the uniaxial ﬁber is only rotated from 19-meter span onward. This is a design choice,
that is made considering the mostly cylindrical geometry of the blade in its root section.
The coupling coeﬃcient starts there at its highest value before decreasing progressively
along the span. This is due to the spar caps being wider in the inner part of the blade
span, with a thicker uniaxial layer (see Figures 4.1 and 4.3). That section of the blade
carries most of the structural load. Therefore, the coeﬃcients will have higher absolute
values than in the blade outer region. It can also be noticed that some degree of edgewise
BTC has been introduced into the models (C.5e). The coeﬃcient value stays an order of
magnitude below the ﬂapwise BTC, but this shows that introducing exclusively BTC
in one direction is almost impossible when working on complex geometries like a WTB.

Material coupling also has an impact on the ﬂapwise (C.5a), edgewise (C.5d) and

more so at the blade tip). This transfer of structural stiﬀness is due to the fact that the
uniaxial ply keeps the same material properties for all models, which means that rotating
the ply will change the direction of maximum strength for the blade section. Overall the
rate of torsional stiﬀness increase is not as high as the rate of ﬂapwise bending stiﬀness loss.

stiﬀness, are varying. As mentioned in Section 4.2.3, the introduction of structural angle
into the blade shifts its shear center, which leads to shear-twist coupling. Both coeﬃcients
evolve asymmetrically with structural angle. The ﬂapwise shear-twist coupling coeﬃcient
is higher for BTC-to-feather models, with changes mostly located in the outer part of
the blade. Inversely, BTC-to-stall models are seeing increased values for the edgewise
shear-twist coupling coeﬃcient, with the bulk of the change occurring in the middle
section of the blade.

understand the value disparities between the stall and feather models. Firstly, the refer-
ence model already has a 6.61 deg structural angle in its design. Secondly, the oﬀsetting
of the spar caps is done on the complex geometry of the blade envelope, which means
that the evolution of the structural properties will not be exactly linear or symmetric
with respect to the structural angle variable. However, the general impact is that higher
absolute structural angle value will decrease the ﬂapwise bending stiﬀness and increase
the edgewise bending stiﬀness. This is easily explicable by considering the structural

4.5
Angle of Attack Investigation
One of the unexpected results from the steady-states calculations is the shift in behaviour
occurring for many of the parameters (tip position, aerodynamic loads, etc.) at high
wind speeds. BTC-to-feather and BTC-to-stall will present in that range characteristics
opposite to what is expected and what is found overall in the rest of the turbine operating
wind speed range. Furthermore, the magnitude of the shift, its onset (starting wind
speed), and the parameters on which it is visible variate depending on the BTC design.
Generally, the amount of BTC present in the design – measured using the steady-state tip
torsion and displacements – seems to be proportionally correlated to the shift intensity:
starting at a lower wind speed, with a wider spread in model values, and impacting more
parameters.
An analysis is conducted on the induction parameters to try to understand the ori-
gin of that shift. An explanation is found by looking at the AoA for the diﬀerent models
at low, rated and high wind speeds (respectively 5 m/s, 11 m/s and 25 m/s). Figures 4.24
to 4.26 show the AoA along the blade span for each of these wind speeds for the Blade
Sweep case, where the shift is the most visible. At low wind speeds, the spread in AoA
values is as expected: BTC-to-stall increases it and BTC-to-feather decreases it. It
is mostly located on the outer region of the blade, where the aerodynamic forces are
maximum. As the wind speed goes up to rated speed, the aerodynamic forces follow suit
and the variation in AoA induced by BTC spreads to a larger section of the blade span
(although still more prominent at the tip). However, at this range of wind speeds, the
blade starts pitching following the optimal curve calculated for each model. This means
that the AoA is reduced identically across the blade span. This reduction will be more
pronounced for the BTC-to-stall models, as the pitch in this region is optimized for stable
output at the rated 10 MW value and, therefore, tries to compensate for the higher lift
coeﬃcient that these models produce. This can be seen on the plot with BTC-to-feather
models showing higher AoA values than the reference blade on the inner section of the
blade (where the BTC eﬀect is minimal), while BTC-to-stall has higher-than-reference
AoA on the outer section.

CHAPTER 5
Stability Analysis
This chapter discusses the results from the stability analysis. Using HAWCStab2, an
aeroelastic stability analysis is run on both the isolated blade and the full turbine, for
each BTC case, using a selection of models covering the entire BTC-to-feather/BTC-to-
stall range. The blade modes with frequencies below 10 Hz are identiﬁed, as well as the
turbine modes until frequencies of about 2 Hz. Mode identiﬁcation is achieved through
analysis of the mode shapes, where the highest amplitude component is used to name the
mode. The full slate of results, including Campbell’s diagrams and plots of the damping
ratios against wind speed, is presented in Appendix D. First, the general ﬁndings from
these results are brieﬂy covered. A case speciﬁc analysis is then given, focusing on a
selection of aeroelastic turbine modes that are the most impacting turbine stability.
The impact of all three BTC designs on the damped frequencies of the 1st Flapwise BW,
symmetric and FW modes is investigated. Their placement with respect to the 3P and
6P frequencies is discussed, as well as the potential impact on stability. A similar analysis
is conducted on the 1st Edgewise BW and FW modes, with regard to the 6P frequency.
Then, the damping ratios for these two modes, as well as the 1st Torsion symmetric mode,
are compared across models for the full operational wind speed range. These modes
are singled out as they are the lowest damped modes for the turbine (excluding tower
modes which are mostly unaﬀected by BTC). Finally, the causes for the diﬀerent changes
induced by BTC on these modes are investigated, by looking into the mode shapes as
well as the blade aerodynamic characteristics.
5.1
Overview
The diﬀerent types of BTC have a similar overall impact on the mode frequencies (see
Appendix D). For both the blade and turbine ﬂapwise modes, damped frequencies are
increased for BTC-to-feather and reduced by BTC-to-stall. This is in agreement with the
ﬁndings from Stäblein et al. [46], which also ﬁnds the edgewise aeroelastic frequencies to
be unaﬀected by ﬂapwise BTC. This is seconded here by the results for the Spar Cap
Fiber Angle and Blade Sweep cases, where the edgewise frequencies are almost identical
across the BTC range. However, the Structural Angle design (which uses shear-twist
coupling to produce BTC, unlike Stäblein’s model) does impact the edgewise frequencies,
once again increasing them for feather coupling and decreasing for stall coupling.
The blade 1st Torsion mode shows an increase in frequency going from BTC-to-feather
to BTC-to-stall. This increase is clearer and spreads on a wider wind speed range (up
to 21 m/s) for the Structural Angle case, while the other two BTC designs only show a
==PAGE:74==
54
5 Stability Analysis
bump in frequency around rated speed. On the other hand, the turbine torsion modes
show an opposite behaviour with higher frequencies for the BTC-to-feather models. The
diﬀerences across BTC types are similar to the blade modes. It is important to remember
that both blade and turbine torsion modes have relatively high damped frequencies,
which means that they are more coupled modes. The edgewise and ﬂapwise components,
for instance, can have a prominent impact on the mode behaviour. Finally, “non-blade”
turbine modes such as the tower and pitch actuator modes are left largely unaﬀected by
the introduction of BTC.
In term of damping, the study ﬁnds that BTC-to-feather sensibly decreases the damping
ratio of the blade 1st Flapwise mode (as well as the associated turbine modes), which
was also highlighted by Stäblein et al. However, an opposite impact is seen on the higher
ﬂapwise blade modes, where BTC-to-feather shows higher damping then BTC-to-stall.
Similar behaviour is observed on the turbine modes. Again for both blade and turbine
modes, the damping ratios of the edgewise modes are negatively impacted by BTC-to-
feather and exhibit signiﬁcant bump around rated speed for the BTC-to-stall models.
However, the presence of said bump is not constant across all edgewise modes and seem to
depend on the BTC case. Lastly, the blade 1st Torsion mode shows decrease in damping
for BTC-to-feather and increase for BTC-to-stall, with a rather small spread except for
the Structural Angle case where the variation is signiﬁcant.
5.2
Spar Cap Fiber Angle
In the Spar Cap Fiber Angle case, a clear spread is visible in Region 2 for the 1st Flapwise
BW, symmetric and FW turbine modes (see Figure 5.1). The maximum variation is
reached around rated speed, with a similar increase on the three modes for the BTC-
to-feather models. The BTC-to-stall models show lower frequencies, with a noticeable
dive at rated speed for the −8 deg model. The BW frequency even drops to zero for
this model, which is characteristic of a critically-damped mode where the damping ratio
reaches 100%. The damping ratio plot for the case (see Figure D.17 in Appendix D)
shows that this increase in damping is preceded by an important dive, which puts the
overall stability of the model into question. At low and above-rated wind speeds, all
frequencies converge towards the reference value, with the exception of some overshoot
for the BTC-to-stall models in Region 3.
BTC-to-feather brings the BW mode closer to the 3P frequency and pulls the symmetric
and FW modes away from it, while keeping a healthy distance to the 6P frequency.
Therefore, BTC-to-feather will reduce the loading on the turbine due to an overall lesser
excitation of the 1st Flapwise modes by the harmonics of the rotor rotating motion (as
the mode frequencies are on average further away from 3P). Inversely, BTC-to-stall
increases the loading, as the mode frequencies get closer to 3P.

5.5
Further Investigations
In order to understand the changes observed in the modal frequencies and damping ratios,
further investigations into the model response and induction characteristics are conducted.
First, the linear mass is plotted for all BTC cases (see Figures A.6 to A.9 in Appendix A).
No signiﬁcant mass change is observed, except for the Structural Angle design who is
seeing small variations on the blade outboard region, due to the rotation of the spar caps.
Structural modal analysis is run for the isolated blade and full turbine model. The results
show no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the structural frequencies and damping ratios, although
the few variations are coherent with the change in blade stiﬀness induced by the BTC
types. For example, an identical decrease in ﬂapwise mode frequencies is seen on both
BTC-to-stall and BTC-to-feather models for the Spar Cap Fiber Angle design. This is
caused by the loss of structural ﬂapwise bending stiﬀness induced by ﬁber rotation (see
Section 4.4).
Since no important variation in mass and stiﬀness have been found across BTC range
and types, the diﬀerences in the stability results have to be driven by the changes in
aerodynamic stiﬀness and damping induced by BTC. This can be seen in the system
steady-state equilibrium equations used by HAWCStab2 – see Equation (3.2) in Section 3.1.

To understand the changes in aerodynamic stiﬀness, the lift and drag coeﬃcients and
their gradients are compared at low, rated and high wind speeds (5, 11 and 25 m/s
respectively). The spanwise variations across BTC range are plotted in Figures 5.15
to 5.18 for the Spar Cap Fiber Angle case (similar to the other cases – see Appendix E
for the full slate of results). They show an increase in absolute lift coeﬃcient values for
BTC-to-stall models and a decrease for BTC-to-feather. This is expected from the AoA
variations observed in Section 4.5. The trend across wind speeds is similar to the one of
the AoA, with a spread mainly on the blade outboard region (that concentrates most of
the aerodynamic loading), lower Cl values for BTC-to-stall in the blade inner section at
rated speed due to pitching, and negative values from around 75 m span at high wind
speed.
On the contrary, the lift coeﬃcient gradient decreases for BTC-to-stall and inversely for
BTC-to-feather. This is due to the AoA moving closer to the peak of the Cl vs AoA
curve (a.k.a. stall) when the blade twists towards stall, where the curve slope diminishes
progressively to zero. Here again, the variations follow the changes in AoA across wind
speeds, with almost no spread between the curves at high wind speeds. Now looking
at the drag coeﬃcient and its gradient, the variation across BTC range is found to be
insigniﬁcant compared to the variation of the lift parameters. However, the changes are
consistent with the impact of BTC on the AoA (higher AoA leads to higher drag).
A correlation of all these variations with the change in damped frequencies, leads
to the conclusion that a higher lift force leads to a decrease in ﬂapwise aerodynamic
stiﬀness for the BTC-to-stall models. On the contrary, BTC-to-feather induce lower
ﬂapwise aerodynamic stiﬀness. As higher stiﬀness increases the modal frequency, the
changes in ﬂapwise damped frequencies of the diﬀerent BTC models are explained. A
similar conclusion was reached by Stäblein et al. when studying the impact of BTC on a
blade section [44]. The diﬀerences in spread witnessed across wind speeds and BTC types
are also explained by the variations in AoA. Finally, the shift in behaviour noticed post
rated speed on the 1st Flapwise modes for the Structural Angle case (but only on a few
stall models for the other designs) appears to be caused by an increase in the torsional
component magnitude for said modes (see Figures 5.19 and 5.20).

CHAPTER 6
Load Analysis
For the case of modern large-scale WTGs, BTC is mainly applied towards feather and
used for load alleviation. In this chapter, the loads of a selection of BTC-to-feather
models are compared. Since no signs of critical instability or other major ﬂaws has been
found in the previous investigations, the models selected as are the most BTC-to-feather
from the previously investigated range (i.e. +8 deg Spar Cap Fiber Angle, +6 deg
Structural Angle and +6 m Blade Sweep). Time-domain simulations are conducted using
HAWC2 (see Section 3.2 for the methodology). From the returned results, mean loads
and power output are ﬁrst compared (Section 6.1), covering a selection of relevant load
channels. The DELs are then computed and compared on the same channel selection
(Section 6.2). Finally, the lifetime fatigue loads are investigated, as well as the AEP,
giving us an overview of the long-term impact of BTC on the turbine characteristics
(Section 6.3).
6.1
Mean Loads & Power Output
One of the trade-oﬀs inherently associated with BTC-to-feather is the loss of power
production. This is easily understandable as the reduction in AoA – which triggers the
wanted aerodynamic load alleviation – causes a reduction of the lift force that drives the
rotor rotation. The results from the DLC 1.2 simulations exhibit that exact pattern, with
a reduction of electrical power output on the three BTC models compared to the IEA
10-MW RWT. The relative diﬀerence to the Baseline is plotted against wind speed in
Figure 6.1. The bulk of the reduction is concentrated below rated speed, in Region 1 and
2, with a peak at 8-9 m/s for all three models. This is expected as this region sees high
aerodynamic loading (and therefore high impact of BTC), with no compensation from
active blade pitching. Spar Cap Fiber Angle presents the most power reduction (up to
8%), followed by Blade Sweep and Structural Angle (7% and 5% respectively). Structural
Angle power output is across the board the closest to Baseline. These results, however,
are to be put into perspective with the diﬀerent amount of BTC introduced in the
models. The general impact of the losses will be investigated through AEP comparison.
However, it is to be noticed that most losses happen in the region right under rated
speed, where an major part of power production usually occurs. In a bid to limit the
losses in this critical wind speed range, pretwisting methods have been introduced in
several studies [34, 45]. The potential for such a method can be seen in the trend in
Region 2 – where the blades start pitching – with the power losses decreasing rapidly
across all designs, before converging with the Baseline in Region 3.

this change is diﬃcult, however, because of the diﬀerent pitch values used for the diﬀerent
models in this region. Diﬀerent pitch results in diﬀerent frame axis orientation, which
could be the cause of the observed load increase (the convergence/shift at high wind
speed actually supports this theory). As for the Blade Root Torsion channel (Figure 6.8),
the Structural Angle model leads to more mean load reduction than the Spar Cap Fiber
Angle model. The peak decrease is obtained around 10 m/s wind speed, where the mean
load value is the highest. The Blade Sweep design exhibits a very singular behaviour
compared to the three other models: the mean load is of opposite sign and is much
higher in absolute value than the Baseline. This is because sweeping the blade introduces
a lever arm for the lift force, that is no longer applied close to the pitch axis axis in
the outboard region of the blade. This very signiﬁcant results could be discarding the
studied Blade Sweep design, as the higher mean load could cause material failure (more
investigations are necessary to see if that is the case). However, blade sweep could also
be used to bring the Blade Root Torsion mean load to zero, as the lift-induced torque is
opposite to the one seen on the standard design.

6 Load Analysis
6.2
Damage Equivalent Loads
In order to determine the eﬀects of BTC on fatigue damage, the DELs are computed for
each load channel. DELs account for both the number of cycles in the load signal and
their amplitudes. Lobitz and Veers [34] have shown that BTC-to-feather signiﬁcantly
reduced the peaks of the blade ﬂapwise bending moment, without obvious changes to
the number of cycles. This would translate into lower DELs, hence lower fatigue damage
on the turbine. Overall, the results obtained in this study are in agreement with these
ﬁndings. However, the impact of BTC is diﬀerent across load channels. The three BTC
designs also eﬀect the DELs in diﬀerent ways, especially the Blade Sweep design that
shows consistently higher DELs than the other BTC types and, sometimes, than the
Baseline model. It is important to note, however, that DELs are heavily inﬂuenced by
the controller parameters. Although some limited controller tuning as been conducted on
the diﬀerent models, it cannot be excluded that a more in-depth controller optimization
(which would have been out of the scope of this thesis) would have yield signiﬁcant
changes to the results presented here. For the sake of simplicity, no major diﬀerence in
controller tuning eﬀects across models is assumed here. The DELs are plotted here as
the relative diﬀerences compared to Baseline. The load relative diﬀerences are plotted in
Appendix F. On most channels, the DEL values are increasing with turbulence standard
deviation (i.e. with wind speed). However, some like the Shaft Torsion or the Blade
Root Torsion are seeing a (local or global) peak around rated speed, where the mean
aerodynamic loading is maximum.
The impact of BTC-to-feather on the Tower Bottom Fore-Aft and Side-Side DELs
(Figures 6.9 and 6.10) is not as clear as for other load channels. The values oscillate
around the Baseline, with a peak above it for all BTC types (albeit in the 5-10 m/s range
where the loads are the lowest). In the case of the Fore-Aft DEL though, a moderate load
reduction is observed in Region 3 for the Structural and Spar Cap Fiber Angle models.
Blade Sweep, on the other hand, presents slightly higher-than-baseline values in this
region. A clearer positive impact can be seen for the Tower Yaw Bearing Torsion DEL
(Figure 6.11), with a load reduction peaking around 15% at at 7-9 m/s (albeit with lower
absolute values) and averaging 7% and 5% respectively for the Spar Cap Fiber Angle
and the Structural Angle models in Region 3, where the DEL values are the highest.
Once again, the Blade Sweep DEL is higher than the other BTC models at higher wind
speeds, matching the Baseline.
Looking at the shaft-related channels, it can be seen that both the Mx and My have high
DEL values, which was not foreseen by looking at the mean loads. This indicates either
a high number of cycles in the load signal or a wide amplitude for those cycles. Looking
at the standard deviation for these load channels (see Appendix F), both of them present
high values, which would indicate cycles of large magnitude centered around a near-zero
mean moment. On the contrary, the Torsion DEL is rather low, with the standard
deviation for the channel pointing towards narrow cycle amplitude. Both bending loads
are showing a similar trend, with a decrease in DEL above rated wind speed for the

BTC models (Figures 6.12 and 6.13). In this cases, the Structural Angle design shows
the most load reduction (up to 6%) versus the Blade Sweep and Spar Cap Fiber Angle
models (up to 3%). The divergence from Baseline grows progressively with wind speed
in Region 3. For the Torsion DEL (Figure 6.14), all designs result in a similar decrease
in Region 2, where the load values peak. Moderate increases are seen for all models in
Region 1 and 3, with slightly less impact at high wind speeds for the Structural Angle

Concluding with the blade root channels, the Flapwise DEL is signiﬁcantly lowered

Angle design fares the best with only 1.7% AEP decrease. However, this is to be put
into perspective with the lower amount of BTC observed in this model compared to the

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A wide-range parametric study has been conducted, using single variables covering
a complete range of ﬂapwise BTC. A total of 34 diﬀerent WTG aeroelastic models
have been produced based on single parameter variation from the IEA 10-MW RWT
design, and using state-of-the-art aeroelastic design (HAWTOpt2) and simulation tools
(HAWC2/HAWCStab2) provided by DTU Wind Energy. The models are split into four
diﬀerent BTC types (Spar Cap Fiber Angle,Skin Fiber Angle, Structural Angle and
Blade Sweep) that are representative of the main BTC design strategies used in the wind
energy research ﬁeld (including both material and geometric coupling). The introduction
of coupled twisting towards stall and towards feather in the models has been conﬁrmed
by comparison of the steady-state tip torsion and displacements. The impact on the
steady-state aerodynamic loads has been investigated, showing an overall increase in
loads for BTC-to-stall and a decrease for BTC-to-feather (especially around rated speed).
This ﬁnding is in agreement with the state-of-the-art and with the functioning of BTC, as
understood in this study. Results are qualitatively similar across the BTC types, although
the Skin Fiber Angle design does not prove to be eﬀective in inducing signiﬁcant BTC
for the chosen RWT model, due to insuﬃcient thickness and anisotropy of the triaxial
ﬁber layer in the skin, and was therefore not considered for the rest of the study. A
comparison of the diﬀerent coeﬃcients of the blade element stiﬀness matrices is conducted,
highlighting, among others, a loss in ﬂapwise stiﬀness in the Spar Cap Fiber Angle design.
The Structural Angle design presents a transfer of bending stiﬀness from the ﬂapwise
to the edgewise direction. The two designs have been shown to diﬀerently aﬀect the
stiﬀness coupling coeﬃcients (bend-twist versus shear-twist structural coupling), although
ending up with a similar adaptive blade behaviour. Lastly, the AoA variation across wind
speeds and BTC have been investigated, allowing for an explanation to be found for the
unexpected high-wind-speed shift observed on several steady-state parameters (loads,
pitch angle, etc.).
Stability analysis have been conducted on all BTC models, using the aeroelastic software
HAWCStab2. The general inﬂuence of each BTC design on the blade and turbine modes
(aeroelastic frequencies and damping) have been detailed, with results similar to the ones
found in other studies. BTC-to-feather has been shown to increase the frequencies of the
1st Flapwise modes, improving turbine stability, while it results in lower damping ratios
for the lowest damped modes (including the 1st Edgewise whirling modes), which has
a negative impact on stability. BTC-to-stall was found to produce the opposite eﬀects.
The spread across the models was generally found to be proportional to the amount
of BTC introduced by the design, although some design speciﬁc variations have been
highlighted (e.g. impact of Structural Angle on the 1st Edgewise whirling frequencies).
==PAGE:110==
90
7 Conclusion
Only one model (Blade Sweep −6 m) was found to have a negatively damped mode in
the turbine operational range, which would lead to inherent instability in the turbine.
The various observations from the analysis have been tied to the theory and at least
qualitatively explained through a series of investigations (covering, among others, the
aerodynamic coeﬃcients and their gradients).
To conclude on the interest of the designs, a load analysis was conducted on the most
BTC-to-feather model produced for each BTC type (as modern BTC studies focus on
the use of BTC-to-feather for load mitigation on large-scale WTGs). 2640 simulations
have been run (4 models, with 22 inﬂow cases and 30 seeds per case) following the IEC
61400-3 standard DLC 1.2 recommendations. The results from these simulations have
been post-processed to allow for comparison of the mean loads, the DELs and the lifetime
fatigue loads. The potential for load mitigation was compared to the decrease in power
production, with the computation of the AEP for each model. All BTC designs were
found to decrease the lifetime fatigue loads as well as the AEP compared to the Baseline
model. The study concludes that signiﬁcant load reductions are achieved by the Spar
Cap Fiber Angle model, at a non-negligible but acceptable cost for AEP. The Structural
Angle design was found to be a good compromise between these two parameters, with
potent load alleviation for a sensibly lesser decrease in AEP than the other BTC types.
Finally, the eﬀectiveness of the Blade Sweep design was disappointing, with much lower
lifetime fatigue load reduction than the other kinds of BTC, and as much an impact
on the AEP as the Spar Cap Fiber Angle model. This could be explained by the lower
stability found for this model during the previous analysis, where backward sweeping
was found to severely decrease the damping margin on the lowest-damped turbine modes.
For the other two designs, a conﬁrmation have been brought to the initial hypothesis
that BTC could be introduced into a modern WTG design to reduce the (fatigue) loads
while preserving the overall stability of the turbine.
7.1
Limits of the Thesis and Future Work
The present study has produced a great number of results. It constitutes a solid basis
for understanding how diﬀerent BTC types aﬀect the steady-states, stability, loads, etc.
However, although numerous analysis have been conducted and the initial aim of the
thesis has been fulﬁlled, this study also generated a great amount of uninvestigated
data and questions (more than could be covered within a thesis time frame). Several
additional ﬁndings could be extracted from the computed results and explained. For
instance, the investigation of higher turbine and blade modes, through the analysis of
the mode shapes, could show how the impact of BTC on the lower frequency modes
translates for more complex mode shapes. A proper study of the phases of said mode
shape components would also allow for the calculation of the damping coeﬃcient, as
deﬁned by Hansen [20]. The investigation of the Frequency Response Functions for the
diﬀerent models would also provide an interesting overview of how the changes observed
during the Stability Analysis aﬀect the turbine loads.
==PAGE:111==
7.1 Limits of the Thesis and Future Work
91
Other examples, stemming from the Load Analysis, would be the study of the ex-
treme loads for the BTC-to-feather models or the investigation of the eﬀects of controller
tuning on the DELs. Another possibility considered to expand on the present study
would be to design an optimized BTC turbine, taking into account the diﬀerent ﬁndings
highlighted here to produce a tailored blade/turbine model that would constitute a
signiﬁcant improvement over the original IEA 10-MW RWT (lower loads, higher AEP,
etc.).
Although this study aimed to cover a wide range and be inclusive of the diﬀerent
types of BTC found in the literature, it is to be noted that the a complete overview
of the eﬀects of BTC is impossible to achieve, and that all results are subjective to a
number of study parameters such as turbine model choice (see AoA results) or design
decision (a Skin Fiber Angle design may have been successful if another material stacking
was introduced instead of the original one). The complexity of the interactions between
the design parameters in WTG studies was one of the main lessons learned during this
thesis, as it introduces a non-negligible amount of uncertainty as per the reproducibility
of the results in a diﬀerent environment. This has to be kept in mind when applying the
ﬁndings from this study to new WTG and blade models. However, one of the successes
of the thesis has been to overall good level of agreement found between the “physical”
results from the created BTC designs and the ones found in other studies, including
those working with “theoretical” BTC. This conﬁrms some general truths of BTC and
its eﬀect on turbine behaviour.
