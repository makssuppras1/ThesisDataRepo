Abstract
English version:
The composition of green coffee beans—including free amino acids (FAAs), volatile
organic compounds (VOCs), sugars, organic acids, etc.—plays a key role in determining
the final quality of coffee. This thesis investigates whether microbial starter culture
inoculation (with Pichia kluyveri (PK-1) and/or Lactobacillus plantarum (LB-1)) can
modulate the FAA and VOC profiles of green beans, thereby influencing precursors of
flavor and aroma.
FAAs were analyzed using HPLC following the development and optimization of a
sample preparation method. VOC data were obtained from a previous master’s thesis
conducted at the University of Copenhagen (KU) by Garcia and Sørensen June 2025,
using GC-MS on the same set of coffee samples.
By combining these datasets and
applying multivariate statistical analysis, the study aimed to explore both the individual
and interactive effects of starter cultures on green bean chemistry.
Variety and processing method were found to be the most influential factors struc-
turing the biochemical composition. Nonetheless, microbial inoculation—particularly
with PK-1—produced measurable changes in both FAA and VOC profiles under specific
conditions. The strongest effects were observed in Kenyan SL28/34 naturals after 24
hours of fermentation. In contrast, LB-1 showed weaker and more inconsistent effects,
likely due to lower colonization. Co-inoculation did not consistently enhance results.
While starter cultures were not primary drivers of green bean composition in this
study, they demonstrated potential for fine-tuning biochemical profiles when used strate-
gically. These findings contribute to Novonesis’ aim of integrating starter cultures in
the specialty coffee market, as well as contributing to sustainable food systems through
biosolutions.

CHAPTER1
Introduction and
objectives
Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages globally (Samoggia and Riedel
2018) and ranks among the most traded agricultural commodities (International Trade
Centre 2021). Moreover, the coffee industry provides employment for approximately
125 million people worldwide (Voora et al. 2019). While commodity coffee continues
to grow and dominate global trade—driven especially by rising demand in producing
and emerging economies (Voora et al. 2019)—the specialty coffee sector is growing in
parallel. This growth has brought a stronger consumer focus on flavor, quality, and the
whole value chain—attributes that are highly valued in specialty markets but often less
emphasized in commodity-grade coffee (International Trade Centre 2021, Voora et al.
2019).
One of the key post-harvest steps that contributes to flavor development in coffee is
fermentation. During this stage, microbial activity modifies the chemical composition of
the coffee beans, influencing both the quality and character of the final cup (Poisson et al.
2017). In specialty coffee production, where sensory attributes are critically evaluated,
the outcome of fermentation can significantly impact the value of a batch. However,
because fermentation is a biologically complex and sensitive process, it is also a major
source of inconsistency. Controlling and standardizing this step is therefore essential
for specialty producers aiming to achieve consistent, high-quality products.
To understand how fermentation impacts cup quality, it is essential to examine the
composition of green coffee beans, which serve as the foundation for flavor development
during roasting. For example, free amino acids (FAA), though making up less than 1%
of green coffee, are key precursors in the roasting reactions that generate flavor-active
molecules (Poisson et al. 2017). Similarly, volatile organic compounds (VOC) present
in green beans can contribute directly to aroma, particularly those that survive the
roasting process (Gonzalez-Rios et al. 2007). Both FAA and VOC profiles are shaped
in part by microbial metabolism during fermentation.
==PAGE:12==
1 Introduction and objectives
2
In light of the role microbial fermentation plays in shaping green bean chemistry,
this project was developed as a collaboration between DTU and Novonesis, a Danish
biosolutions company with both scientific and commercial interests in coffee fermenta-
tion, as the company is beginning to market its microbial starter cultures—LB-1 (lactic
acid bacteria, LAB) and PK-1 (yeast)—to coffee farmers. These farmers often aim to
sell specialty-grade green beans to exporters and roasters at premium prices but may
struggle due to inconsistent quality and high batch-to-batch variability. By using de-
fined microbial cultures, they stand to benefit from greater control over fermentation,
leading to more consistent flavor profiles and a stronger position in the specialty market
(Novonesis A/S 2025).
This thesis studies the effect of specific starter cultures in coffee with an ultimate
commercial goal.
A central challenge in marketing starter cultures to farmers—who rarely experi-
ence the roasted product—is effectively communicating how microbial fermentation im-
pacts bean composition and ultimately flavor. Objective, quantifiable data on chemical
changes in green beans would provide valuable feedback to producers and help close the
information gap. Furthermore, enabling more predictable fermentation outcomes aligns
with Novonesis’ broader goal of supporting a more sustainable and fair food system
through biosolutions.
Therefore, this thesis investigates how different coffee fermentation approaches—
spontaneous, mono-inoculated, and co-inoculated—using specific microbial strains from
Novonesis (LB-1 and PK-1) affect the biochemical composition of green coffee beans.
The main research question of this thesis, as well as the subsequent hypotheses are
described below:
Research question: How do microbial starter cultures influence the biochemical
composition of green coffee beans?
Hypotheses:
• We expect that inoculated green coffee beans will show distinct profiles of FAAs
and VOCs compared to spontaneously fermented beans, with the former expected
to exhibit a more desirable chemical composition.
• We expect that green beans inoculated with yeast (PK-1) will differ in FAA and
VOC profiles from those inoculated with bacteria (LB-1).
• We expect that co-inoculated samples will have a more desirable biochemical pro-
file (in terms of FAA and VOC) than mono-inoculated samples.
To address the research question, the following objectives were defined. While some
directly target the core research question, others are peripheral but essential to enable
its resolution:

• Develop and optimize an High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)
method for analyzing FAA in green coffee beans within the Novonesis laboratory

• Assess the impact of each starter culture on the FAA profile of green beans to

• Assess the impact of each starter culture on the VOC profile of green beans to

CHAPTER2
Background
This section introduces the role of starter cultures in specialty coffee by first providing
context on coffee consumption, bean composition, varieties, and processing methods.
It also outlines the dynamics of FAAs and VOCs during fermentation and roasting, to
highlight how microbial activity can influence flavor development.
2.1
Introduction to Coffee
2.1.1
The rise of specialty coffee
The coffee industry has evolved through four distinct waves, that have influenced not
only consumption patterns but also production practices, including the relevance of
microbial inoculation during coffee processing—a topic particularly emphasized in the
third and fourth waves (International Trade Centre 2021).
The first and second waves were defined by mass consumption of coffee, convert-
ing coffee into a commodity, and progressively focusing more on quality and turning
consumption into a social and experiential act. The emergence of the term “specialty
coffee” happened at the end of the second wave (International Trade Centre 2021).
This term refers to a more exclusive cup that has a higher quality than commodity
coffee, and unique flavour and characteristics (Janne Carvalho Ferreira et al. 2023). To
assess whether a coffee is considered within the specialty coffee category, the beverage
needs to achieve at least 80 points in the sensory analysis released by certificated Q-
graders. This score is currently the main form of evaluation in the global coffee market
and it can provide farmers with significantly greater prices for their coffee (about 8-
fold prices for specialty coffee compared to commodity) (Janne Carvalho Ferreira et al.
2023).
The third wave, beginning in the early 2000s, further elevated specialty coffee by
framing it as a craft product. Consumers sought transparency in sourcing, and produc-
ers responded with experimental processing methods and innovations at the farm level.
==PAGE:15==
2.1 Introduction to Coffee
5
Greater emphasis was placed on flavour, quality, and the entire value chain (Interna-
tional Trade Centre 2021).
The fourth wave, currently unfolding, aims to scale up the specialty coffee model.
It seeks to combine the commercial scalability of the second wave with the quality,
sustainability, and transparency principles of the third. A key objective is to provide
producers with opportunities to achieve fairer prices through increased volumes rather
than relying solely on niche markets (International Trade Centre 2021).
Achieving this scalability requires consistency in quality, which in turn depends on
controlling critical variables during production. One of these variables is the microbial
population involved in fermentation.
Managing microbial communities—through in-
oculation or controlled fermentation—can enhance the reproducibility of coffee quality,
thereby empowering producers to standardize outputs and better meet market demands.
2.1.2
The coffee cherry
The coffee fruit, commonly referred to as the coffee cherry, has a distinct multilayered
structure (Figure 2.1). The outer skin—green when unripe and red when ripe—encloses
the pulp (mesocarp), a moist, carbohydrate-rich tissue. Beneath this lies the mucilage,
a highly viscous, hydrated layer primarily composed of pectin. This is followed by the
parchment (endocarp), which encases the silverskin, a thin protective layer surrounding
the coffee bean (A. Iriondo-DeHond, M. Iriondo-DeHond, and Castillo 2020).
Fermentation processes occur mainly in the outer skin and pulp (in natural pro-
cessing) or in the mucilage layer (in washed processing), where pectin is the dominant
fermentable carbohydrate. Notably, the concentration of soluble and fermentable sug-
ars in the outer skin and pulp on a dry matter basis is highly dependent on the degree
of ripeness (Baptistella et al. 2024).
The carbohydrates present in the green beans are mainly sucrose, some polysac-
charides (heteropolymers from galactose, mannose, arabinose and glucose), and a few
monosaccharides, like fructose, glucose or galactose. Green beans also contain a consid-
erable amount of insoluble carbohydrates, mainly cellulose and hemicellulose.
Coffee oil is the major lipid in green coffee beans, where linoleic acid and palmitic
acid are the main fatty acids. Moreover, green coffee beans contain relatively high levels
of protein, from which only 2-6% of it are free amino acids (Poisson et al. 2017).
2.1.3
Coffee varieties
The two primary cultivated coffee species are Coffea arabica (Arabica) and Coffea
canephora (Robusta).
These species differ significantly at the genetic and composi-
tional levels, as well as in their preferred growing conditions—Arabica thrives at higher
altitudes, while Robusta is typically cultivated at lower elevations (International Trade

Centre 2021).
Arabica is generally considered to produce higher-quality coffee than
Robusta, largely due to its more desirable flavor attributes. Thus, its dominance in the
specialty coffee sector.
The relative abundance of individual compounds varies notably between the two
species. Robusta beans are typically richer in proteins, arabinogalactans, certain acids
and phenolic compounds such as chlorogenic acids (CGA) and caffeine. In contrast,
Arabica beans contain higher levels of carbohydrates (including sucrose, oligosaccha-
rides, and mannans), lipids, trigonelline, and organic acids such as malic, citric, and
quinic acids. These compositional differences play a crucial role in shaping the sensory
properties and overall quality of the roasted coffee (Poisson et al. 2017).
Interestingly, the presence of volatile organic compounds in green coffee beans is very
minimal (Poisson et al. 2017).
Most of the coffee varieties analyzed in this study belong to the Coffea arabica
species, with some others being a hybrid between Arabica and Robusta. Below is a
short description of each variety analyzed in the present work:
• SL28 and SL34 are among the most renowned Arabica cultivars in Africa (World

Mancuso, et al. 2025) and are well adapted to medium and high altitudes, where
they are capable of producing exceptional cup quality (World Coffee Research

• Ruiru 11 is a Kenyan hybrid variety known for its robustness and high productiv-

Bourbon. Ruiru 11 grows across a wide range of altitudes and yields coffee of

• Caturra is a natural mutation of the Bourbon variety, hence it is relatively close

2.2
Coffee processing styles
Just after harvesting but before roasting, the ripe coffee cherries need to be processed
in order to become green coffee beans (Janne Carvalho Ferreira et al. 2023).
Green beans intended for specialty coffee, as defined by the Specialty Coffee Asso-
ciation of America (SCAA), must be free from major defects — such as stones, sticks,
black or sour beans—and exhibit uniformity in size, color, and drying. They should also
possess distinct and desirable sensory attributes. Achieving this level of quality places
coffee producers in a key position, as they play a crucial role in shaping the final prod-
uct. To meet the high standards expected by buyers and consumers, producers must
implement meticulous and reproducible post-harvest processing practices that preserve
and enhance the quality of the coffee cherries (Magalhães Júnior et al. 2021).

As discussed in subsection 2.1.1, the third and fourth waves of coffee place a strong
emphasis on specialty coffee. In this context, the story behind the cup, the craftsman-
ship involved, and the exploration of innovative post-harvest processes have become key
elements valued by consumers. This shift in perspective is precisely why it makes more
sense to speak of processing styles rather than merely processing methods.
This section outlines the main existing processing styles, with a particular focus on
the methods applied to the samples used in this project.
The dry, honey, and wet
processing methods all begin with the same crucial step: sorting the ripe cherries. This
initial selection is essential for ensuring the production of high-quality green beans (L.
Pereira 2020). Despite their differences in intermediate steps, these processing styles
also share a common set of final operations, namely: hulling, sorting, and packaging.
Hulling involves removing the remaining outer layers from the dried coffee beans—
typically the parchment (in washed and honey processes) or the dried husk (in natural
processes)—depending on the method used. Following this, sorting is carried out to
separate defective or non-compliant beans from those that meet quality standards. Only
the compliant beans are then packaged for commercialization.
2.2.1
Dry processing
The dry processing method, shown by the brown arrow in Figure 2.3, produces what
is commonly known as natural coffee. In this approach, ripe, whole cherries are dried
intact—typically on raised beds or patios—until they reach a target moisture content of
11–12%, without any prior removal of fruit layers (L. Pereira 2020, International Trade
Centre 2021).
A dedicated fermentation step may optionally be included before drying. This step
varies widely in form—spontaneous or inoculated, wet or dry, aerobic or anaerobic.
==PAGE:19==

Figure 2.3: Overview of the main post-harvest coffee processing styles. The methods applied to the samples
used in this project are highlighted in brown, green and purple. Adapted from Covoya Coffee Europe 2025.
During fermentation, microorganisms colonize the cherry surface, accessing limited fer-
mentable substrates such as mucilage, which they break down using endogenous pecti-
nolytic and cellulolytic enzymes (Janne Carvalho Ferreira et al. 2023).
The brown arrows in Figure 2.3 represent the “Whole Cherry Fermented Natural”
samples used in this study. In these cases, whole cherries underwent spontaneous or
inoculated fermentation in closed tanks for a variable duration, followed by drying.
After drying, hulling removed the outer layers, including the dried pulp, or cascara.
2.2.2
Honey processing
Honey processing does not involve actual honey. The name refers to the sticky, honey-
like appearance of the beans after drying (L. Pereira 2020). Also known as semi-dry
processing, the honey process produces pulped natural coffee and blends elements of
both wet and dry methods. Cherries are first mechanically pulped, as in the washed
process, but instead of washing off the mucilage, the beans—still coated with mucilage—
are dried directly (International Trade Centre 2021).
==PAGE:20==
2.3 Coffee fermentation
10
As in dry processing, an optional fermentation step may be added, during which
mucilage is partially degraded by microbial activity and enzymatic action.
The “Whole Cherry Fermented Pulped Natural” samples in this study are repre-
sented by the green arrows in Figure 2.3. While not strictly a honey process—since
fermentation in closed tanks occurs before pulping—it is still considered semi-dry, as
the mucilage remains through drying and is not removed by washing.
2.2.3
Wet processing
Wet processing, also known as the washed method, is commonly used in specialty coffee
production for its potential to yield high-quality beans.
However, it is one of the
most resource-intensive methods, requiring technical expertise and substantial water
use, which contributes to its high production cost (International Trade Centre 2021).
In this method, cherries are mechanically pulped, and the mucilage is removed
through de-mucilation—either mechanically or via microbial fermentation. Once the
mucilage is suﬀiciently degraded, the beans are thoroughly washed and dried to the
target moisture content (International Trade Centre 2021).
The “Washed (Submerged Fermentation)” and “Washed (Dry Fermentation)” sam-
ples in this study followed this method (see purple arrows in Figure 2.3). The key step
was microbial de-mucilation, carried out in either an anaerobic submerged environment
in water tanks (submerged fermentation) or under direct anaerobic conditions in closed
tanks without water (dry fermentation).
2.3
Coffee fermentation
This section will dive deeper into the fermentation unit operation of the post-harvest
processing of coffee.
2.3.1
The importance of coffee fermentation
Unlike many fermented foods and beverages, coffee was not a historically purposely fer-
mented seed. Rather, any microbial activity during coffee processing has been largely
a consequence of environmental conditions and processing practices, rather than a de-
liberately controlled fermentation step (Clarke 1985). As such, limited attention was
historically given to this unit operation until more recent developments in specialty
coffee production (International Trade Centre 2021). In fact, in large-scale commodity
coffee production, mechanical mucilage removal has become a widespread alternative,
significantly reducing processing time and costs (Clarke 1985).

However, with a growing emphasis on flavour and differentiation, the fermentation
step has gained increasing attention among producers, roasters, and consumers. The
complex microbial dynamics that occur during this stage are now recognized as crucial
contributors to the development of diverse and distinctive sensory profiles in coffee
(Janne Carvalho Ferreira et al. 2023).
From a chemical perspective, the composition of the green coffee bean is key to
determine the aroma and flavour profile developed during roasting. Since fermentation
significantly influences the chemical composition of the green coffee—i.e. the formation
of flavour precursors —it is a critical step in shaping the sensory qualities of the final
cup (Poisson et al. 2017).

2.3.2
FAA kinetics during fermentation
This section aims to explain the kinetics of free amino acids in coffee during post-
harvest processing, focusing on the fermentation step. Composition changes in green
beans during fermentation are due both to coffee bean metabolism (i.e. germination)
and microbial action (Waters, Arendt, and Moroni 2017), which happen parallelly (see
Figure 2.4).
2.3.2.1
The role of germination
Germination-related reactions start with post-harvesting events, and are highly depen-
dant on the processing methods employed. Different bean metabolism patterns have
been observed in natural and washed processes (Waters, Arendt, and Moroni 2017).
In washed coffees, the removal of the fruit mucilage early in processing leads to the
deactivation of germination-suppressing factors, prompting earlier onset of germination
compared to naturally processed coffees (G. Bytof et al. 2007).
Proteases active during germination hydrolyse storage proteins, releasing FAA, which
are then utilized by the developing embryo. Although limited information is available
on coffee-specific proteases and peptidases, several endopeptidases have been isolated
(Ludwig et al. 2000). These enzymes play a crucial role in protein degradation during
germination.
However, the released FAA are not accumulated in the bean; instead,
they serve as precursors for new biosynthetic pathways in the embryo (Waters, Arendt,
and Moroni 2017). In support of this, Shimizu and Mazzafera 2000 reported an overall
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Coffee fermentation
13
reduction in total FAA content by more than 50% during germination, with tyrosine
being the only amino acids (AAs) that increased in concentration.
In summary, germination plays a complex but critical role in shaping the FAA com-
position of green coffee beans.
Its impact is closely linked to the chosen processing
method and must be considered alongside microbial activity during fermentation when
evaluating post-harvest biochemical changes.
2.3.2.2
The role of microbial activity
Nitrogen availability for microbes during fermentation can have an effect on both fermen-
tation performance and the flavour precursors generated during that process (Miguel
et al. 2024). AAs are one of the major sources of assimilable nitrogen in coffee (Miguel
et al. 2024, Waters, Arendt, and Moroni 2017), so the way they are absorbed and me-
tabolized by microbes highly depends on the starter culture as well as the fermentation
conditions.
During microbial metabolism, the overall FAA composition in coffee beans is shaped
by both de novo synthesis of FAA and their catabolism (L. L. Pereira et al. 2021). The
extent to which FAA are produced or consumed depends not only on the microbial
species involved but also on the specific fermentation conditions. Figure 2.5 shows an
schematic example of how yeasts and LAB catabolize AA, among other molecules, to
produce volatiles during coffee mucilage fermentation.
Moreover, the metabolism of yeasts and bacteria is closely linked to the demands
of cell growth and division. As microorganisms progress through different phases of
the cell cycle, specific metabolic pathways are activated to support biosynthesis. This
means that the production and consumption of FAA—and other metabolites—can also
vary depending on the microbial growth stage.
Altogether, this makes it diﬀicult to predict the final profile of FAA and other com-
pounds produced during fermentation, as it results from the interaction of many dy-
namic and interdependent factors.
2.3.3
VOC kinetics during fermentation
Although the quality of green coffee beans is primarily determined by their non-volatile
constituents—such as carbohydrates, FAA, lipids, trigonelline, and phenolics (L. L.
Pereira et al. 2021)—volatile compounds also contribute to the odor and flavor of the
final roasted coffee. In this context, Hadj Salem et al. 2020 demonstrated that during
submerged fermentation, a mass transfer of VOCs occurs from the liquid phase to the
solid coffee beans. This suggests that fermentation can influence the final coffee aroma
by depositing metabolites onto the beans. Consequently, drier fermentation conditions

Moreover, it is estimated that approximately 200 of the 300 identified VOCs in

formed and their relevance to coffee quality.
It is known that two types of VOCs are formed during post-harvest processing:
thermal-derived compounds (during drying) and microbial-derived compounds (during
fermentation, see Figure 2.5). The drying process triggers the Maillard reaction, which
is known to form some of the aldehydes present in the green beans. On the other hand,
the fermentation process is known to produce VOCs like alcohols and esters. Acids and
aldehydes can be produced by both ways. Table B.1 in Appendix B lists the flavor-
active volatiles that have been considered as quality indicators in green coffee, as well
as an explanation of their origin and whether they are desirable or not.
section 2.5 goes deeper into the metabolism of the inoculated yeast and bacteria
species, to understand how the VOCs are produced by these specific microorganisms
during fermentation.
2.4
Major chemical reactions upon roasting
Roasting transforms flavour precursors in green coffee beans into aroma and flavour
compounds through intense heat (Figure 2.4).
Understanding these transformations
==PAGE:25==
2.4 Major chemical reactions upon roasting
15
can help predict the final coffee flavour based on green bean composition. The main
flavour precursors in green coffee include sugars, proteins, FAAs, trigonelline, and CGAs.
Given the FAA analyses conducted in this study, particular attention will be placed on
this group of compounds (Poisson et al. 2017).
Only trace amounts of VOCs are typically present in green beans prior to roasting
(Poisson et al. 2017). These VOCs are thought to have minimal impact during roasting,
as most are volatilized and lost in the process. Nevertheless, their presence may offer
insights into microbial activity and the metabolic pathways active during fermentation.
In contrast, certain volatile acids—such as formic and acetic acid—can persist through
the roasting process. These compounds, which originate from fermentation and can
be quantified by GC-MS, strongly contribute to the total sensory perceived acidity
of roasted coffee. However, their presence highly depends on the roasting level (light
roasted beans are more acidic than dark roasted coffee), as they are also formed during
roasting (Poisson et al. 2017).
Although FAA represent a very small fraction of the green beans, they are known to
play a key role in the Maillard reaction and Strecker degradation, yielding important
aromatic compounds.
In fact, the distribution of the FAA present in the bean can
determine the aromatic profile developed during these reactions (Wong, Abdul Aziz, and
Mohamed May 2008). Trigonelline, another nitrogenous compound, is partly degraded
during roasting and converted into VOCs like pyradines and pyrroles, as wellas nicotinic
acid (Poisson et al. 2017).
Lipids are also key flavour precursors as they thermally degrade and form aldehydes,
which can further react with other coffee constitutents (Poisson et al. 2017).
2.4.1
The Maillard reaction as the main transformation of FAA during
roasting
FAA play a crucial role in the flavour formation process as the Maillard reaction and
Strecker degradation are the main way of coffee aroma formation, as they are responsi-
ble for the generation of compounds like pyrazines, pyrroles, thiols, furans, furanones,
pyridines and thiophenes (L. L. Pereira et al. 2021).
The Maillard reaction or nonenzymatic browning is a complex cascade of reactions
initiated with an amino-carbonyl coupling of AAs (or peptides/proteins) and reducing
sugars (Figure 2.6). These reactions lead to aroma, taste, and colour (melanoidins).
Sucrose, being the most abundant carbohydrate in green beans (more abundant in
Arabica than Robusta varieties) is a major contributor to the Maillard reaction, after
it is decomposed into glucose and fructose by heat treatment. The aldosamine (glucose
and AA complex) and ketosamine (fructose and AA complex) compounds can cyclize or
react with other compounds (and then cyclize). When the temperature reaches 160ºC

Most FAA play a role in aroma formation through the Maillard reaction and Strecker

2.5
Starter cultures
2.5.1
The importance of starter cultures
Specialty coffee sets high standards for producers in terms of quality and, more impor-
tantly, reproducibility. However, green coffee production is complex and influenced by
many variables that are diﬀicult to control. Climate change adds further unpredictabil-
ity, particularly affecting fermentation through shifts in temperature and humidity,
which directly impact the coffee microbiota (Pham et al. 2019). Elevated temperatures
may weaken endogenous microbial cultures and increase pest and spoilage pressure, pro-
moting aflatoxin production. This threatens global coffee yields, harms millions of small
producers, and contributes to food insecurity (Dos Santos et al. 2021).
The use of previously selected strains (i.e. starter cultures) is known to yield a greater
microbiological uniformity, and consequently, the uniformity of metabolites (Janne Car-
valho Ferreira et al. 2023). On this matter, studies have shown that same variety coffee
processed under the same conditions can be differentiated by selected starter culture.
This type of research proves that the use of selected starter cultures on coffee can
modulate the fermentation outcomes and consequently make a difference in the final
attributes of the cup and sensorial properties of roasted coffee (Waters, Arendt, and
Moroni 2017; S. Dias, A. Bressani, Batista, et al. 2025; Shimizu and Mazzafera 2000.
There are several reasons why Pichia kluyveri and Lactobacillus plantarum were cho-
sen as starter cultures for coffee fermentation. First, both microorganisms have been
previously identified as part of the natural coffee microbiota associated with differ-
ent processing methods (Waters, Arendt, and Moroni 2017; Gilberto Vinícius de Melo
Pereira et al. 2014). Additionally, they have shown bioprotective properties (Waters,
Arendt, and Moroni 2017), which are highly valuable during coffee processing. Finally,
the choice of these species is also influenced by commercial interests from Novonesis.
2.5.2
Pichia kluyveri
Pichia kluyveri is one of the most abundant coffee-associated yeast strain, as it is capa-
ble of promoting pectin breakdown (Gilberto V. de Melo Pereira et al. 2019; Gilberto
Vinícius de Melo Pereira et al. 2014). It is also a well-known yeast in the wine industry,
and the most commercially available in the yeast market, as it can improve the compo-
sition of wine with aromatic compounds, like thiols, terpens, and fruity esters (Vicente
et al. 2021).
SmartBev™Boost PK-1 (Novonesis A/S, Hørsholm, Denmark) is the specific P.kluyveri
strain used to inoculate some of the green coffee samples analyzed in the present study.
This strain has specifically been designed for green coffee inoculation to improve its pro-
file by boosting fruity flavors in the green beans, which translate into pleasant aromas in
==PAGE:28==

the final cup. It also has bioprotective properties. This strains comes in a freeze-dried
powder and it is recommended to be applied in 6-72h fermentations.
This species is known to produce desirable volatiles like isoamyl acetate, ethyl acetate,
or isoamyl alcohol (Gilberto Vinícius de Melo Pereira et al. 2014).
2.5.3
Lactobacillus plantarum
Lactobacillus plantarum is a heterofermentative, facultative anaerobe LAB. This species
has been isolated from different food environments including meat, fish, fruits, vegeta-
bles, milk, and cereal products (Todorov and Franco 2010). It has also been found as
the main LAB species in coffee (Melo Pereira et al. 2020; Vanos and Bindschedler 1985).
L.plantarum can be used as a starter culture, as it has the GRAS status (Generally
Recognized as Safe), and is known to bring desirable organoleptic properties, flavor and
texture, as well as antimicrobial compounds to the final food product (Todorov and
Franco 2010).
SmartBev™Harvest LB-1 (Novonesis A/S, Hørsholm, Denmark) is the specific
Lb.plantarum strain used to inoculate some of the green coffee samples analyzed in
the present study. This strain has not specifically been designed for coffee processing,
but rather for kettle-soured beer production. It has been used in this industry to obtain
consistent, flavorful beers with a short fermentation time (Novonesis A/S 2025).
As most LAB, the fermentation of carbohydrates by Lb.plantarum, which generally
takes place through the Embden Meyerhof Parnas (EMP) pathway (Todorov and Franco
2010), yields lactic acid as the single metabolic product (homofermentative pathway of
the LAB cell in Figure 2.5).
Lb.plantarum can also produce a range of volatiles from AA catabolism, such as 2-
methyl-butanol, from Ile; 2,3-butanediol and diacetyl, from Asp; or phenylacetaldehyde
and 2-phenylethyl acetate from Phe. Moreover, Lb.plantarum can also participate in
the esterification reaction between alcohols and free fatty acids, thereby producing other
aromatic VOCs in coffee (Melo Pereira et al. 2020).

3.4.2.2
Derivatization
The purpose of the derivatization reaction is to improve the detection limits, the chro-
matographic behavior of the analytes, or both. This step is particularly important when
certain compounds cannot be eﬀiciently detected by the HPLC detectors employed —–
such as UV or fluorescence detectors.
By derivatizing the analytes, new derivatives
are formed that exhibit stronger or more specific responses to the detectors, thereby
enabling accurate quantification (Snyder, Kirkland, and Dolan 2010).
The pre-column derivatization step for FAA analysis was fully automated within the
HPLC system. Each sample was paired with a dedicated reaction vial (300µL – 32
X 11.6 mm, Chromacol, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) in which the
derivatization occurred prior to injection. As a result, this step is not part of the sample
preparation protocol (see Figure 3.3).
According to Novonesis’ SOP for FAA analysis, the derivatization reagents used were
o-Phthaldialdehyde (OPA) and 9-Fluorenylmethyl Chloroformate (FMOC). OPA reacts
with primary AAs (excluding cysteine and cystine) in the presence of 2-mercaptoethanol
or 3-mercaptopropionic acid to form highly fluorescent adducts. Due to its structure as a
secondary amine, proline does not react with OPA either (Dai et al. 2014). The inclusion
of FMOC enables the detection of secondary AAs, such as proline, by facilitating a
separate derivatization pathway.
A borate buffer was used prior to derivatization to adjust the sample to an alkaline
pH (9–10), optimizing conditions for both OPA and FMOC reactions.
All the chemicals used in this step were purchased from Agilent Technologies, Santa
Clara, CA, USA.
3.4.2.3

To ensure accurate analyte quantification, the HPLC system was calibrated using
matrix-based standards across a wide concentration range.
Peak areas were plotted
against known concentrations to generate calibration curves, and sample concentrations
were confirmed to fall within this range.
The external standard solution for FAA analysis in green coffee beans included the
AA Standard Solution AA-S-18, asparagine, and tryptophan.

peaks were too small or outside the calibration range, so the dilution factor was halved

Calibration curves were constructed using serial dilutions of the standard solution
at factors of 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, and 256.

3.4.5.2
HPLC outlier detection
As shown in Figure 3.4 and Figure 3.5, quality control runs varied across sequences, likely
due to variability introduced during HPLC operation. To account for this, statistical
methods were applied to screen for outliers. These were not detected on a run basis,
but rather on individual peaks.
Since the FAA dataset could not be assumed to follow a normal distribution (due
to high biological variability, noise, and potential technical errors), and only triplicates
were available, Modified Z-scores were used. This method is well-suited for small, non-
normally distributed datasets (Iglewicz and Hoaglin 1993).
==PAGE:42==
3.5 VOC Analysis
32
The Modified Z-score quantifies the deviation of each value from the median, scaled
by the median absolute deviation (MAD)—a robust alternative to the standard devia-
tion in the presence of outliers (Leys et al. 2013).
Data points with |Modified Z-score| > 3.5 were flagged as outliers and excluded from
further analysis (Iglewicz and Hoaglin 1993, Leys et al. 2013).

3.5
VOC Analysis
VOC analysis was carried out by Garcia and Sørensen June 2025 in the KU laboratories
with untargeted GC-MS. The employed method is summarized in this section.
Dynamic headspace sampling was employed for VOC quantification. Ground green
coffee beans mixed with water were equilibrated at 37°C and purged with nitrogen
to trap VOCs on Tenax-TA adsorbent tubes. Thermal desorption released the VOCs
for GC-MS analysis, which used a ZB-Wax column and a mass selective detector. A
total of 38 samples were analyzed in duplicate.
While triplicates would have been
preferable, time and material constraints limited replicates. Processing was conducted
over two days by different lab workers without randomization, possibly introducing
systematic variation. Lack of internal standards also limited correction for instrumental
drift. Despite these limitations, the method provided detailed and reproducible aromatic
profiles useful for distinguishing coffee types.
Chromatographic GC–MS data were processed using PARADISe software, which
applies the PARAFAC2 model for untargeted deconvolution. The model explicitly ac-
counts for baseline drift, noise, and overlapping peaks, improving peak detection and
integration accuracy. Peak areas and spectra were extracted and identified by matching
with the NIST Mass Spectral Library.

3.7.1
Comparative analyses
This section covers the comparative analyses done on the raw data to determine whether
two groups were significantly different. Different tests were carried out depending on
the characteristics of the analyzed groups:
The t-test or Student’s t-test was used to determine statistically significant difference
between the means of two independent and normally distributed groups (assumed when
comparing more than 30 samples per group) (Thakkar 2025). If the data could not be
considered to have a normal distribution, the Mann–Whitney U test, also known as
the Wilcoxon rank-sum test was used. This is a nonparametric statistical test used
to compare continuous variables among two independent groups when the data is not
normally distributed (Thakkar 2025).
Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was employed when three or more groups were com-
pared. Tukey’s honestly significant difference (Tukey’s HSD) test was carried out as a
post hoc analysis to the ANOVA in order to analyze the pattern of difference between
means by making specific comparisons (pairwise comparisons) (Abdi, Williams, et al.
2010).
3.7.2
Principal Component Analaysis
Principal component analysis (PCA) was used as an unsupervised exploratory multivari-
ate analysis to reduce data dimensionality and generate graphs based on two principal
components. Two plots were obtained for each PCA: the scores plot, which shows how
samples relate to each other along principal components, and where each point repre-
sents a sample; and the loadings plot, which shows how variables contribute to each
principal component, and where each point represents a variable.
PCAs were carried out on the averaged data (after removing outliers), and they were
used to analyze FAA and VOC data separately, and FAA and VOC data together.

It is likely that LC-MS provides more accurate quantification for the following rea-

• LC-MS runs were performed by a Senior Laboratory Technician, reducing the
likelihood of technical error.
• MS generally has lower limits of detection and quantitation for most AAs com-
pared to fluorescence detection, making it more sensitive for certain applications

• MS can assign peaks more accurately, especially when fluorescence detection suf-

• In aqueous solutions, fluorescence can be reduced by quenching effects, where
water molecules or other substances absorb or interfere with the fluorescent light
emission, further lowering the detected signal. In contrast, MS detects ions based
on their mass-to-charge ratio and is less affected by pH or quenching so its signal
depends mainly on ionization eﬀiciency and instrument settings (Naffa et al. 2020).
• Preparative and chromatographic conditions in HPLC may not effectively remove
certain negatively charged compounds like chloride and phosphate ions from the
sample or mobile phase. These residual ions can accumulate in the system and
interfere with the detection process, reducing the detector response (Le et al.
2014).
In conclusion, LC-MS is better suited for applications requiring high sensitivity and
specificity, whereas fluorescence detection remains a robust and widely used method for
routine analysis. Although HPLC may underestimate certain AAs, it offers suﬀicient
reproducibility for the purposes of this study, where consistency across measurements
is prioritized over sensitivity.

4.2.2
General FAA exploration with PCA
The PCA of FAAs across the averaged dataset shows that the first two principal com-
ponents (PC1 and PC2) explain 76% of the total variance (Figure 4.2), with PC1
accounting for 56.5% and PC2 for 19.5%.
According to the loadings plot (Figure 4.3), the AAs contributing most strongly to
PC1 include Tyr, Ile, Arg, His, Phe, Lys, Val, Leu, Met, Gly, Pro, and Ser, indicating
they co-vary positively along this component. In contrast, Glu, Asp, Trp, and Ser are
the main positive contributors to PC2, while Thr and Met contribute negatively.
The loadings plot reveals which variables drive variation across the dataset. Vari-
ables that cluster together (e.g., certain FAAs) are likely to co-vary and may be bio-
logically or functionally related. In this case, Glu and Asp are positioned orthogonally
to most other FAAs, suggesting they vary independently—potentially due to their dis-
tinct metabolic role. As preferred nitrogen sources for many microbes, they support
microbial growth and protein synthesis by releasing high amounts of ammonia during
deamination (Thorne 1949). This may explain why they don’t co-vary with other FAAs.
These metabolic differences imply that the loadings plot may reflect microbial ac-
tivity, distinguishing FAAs used for biomass production (e.g., Glu, Asp) from those
involved in secondary metabolite synthesis. Although sample separation by inoculation
is not evident in the PCA, underlying metabolic patterns linked to both starter cul-
tures and endogenous microbiota may still be captured. This is further discussed in
section 4.4, where FAA levels are correlated with volatile compounds.
FAAs can also be classified based on their desirability, as defined in subsection 2.4.1.
This classification is based on their potential as flavour precursors in the green beans,
which affects the final coffee flavour through Maillard reactions and Strecker degrada-
tion during roasting. Some FAAs contribute to desirable aroma compounds, others to

grow. On the other hand, the yeasts (S.cerevisiae or T.delbrueckii) were able to secrete
polysaccharides, AAs, and vitamins, which support the growth of LAB.
These synergetic dynamics could explain the trend of the co-inoculated samples in

due to the growth of yeasts, and the subsequent decrease of total FAA after 72 hours
could be caused by the catabolization of FAA by the LAB.

4.3
Analysis of VOC results
This section builds upon the work of Garcia and Sørensen June 2025, which presents a
comprehensive statistical analysis of VOCs in green coffee beans. Their study applied
several multivariate analysis techniques — including PCA, ANOVA–simultaneous com-
ponent analysis (ASCA), and partial least squares-discriminant analysis (PLS-DA) —
primarily focusing on the SL28/34 and Catimor varieties. While their research yielded
numerous insights, the main takeaways are summarized below:
• PCA indicated that coffee variety is the primary factor influencing VOC compo-
sition in green coffee beans. In comparison, the effect of starter culture appeared
minimal, especially when contrasted with post-harvest variables such as processing
style and fermentation time.
==PAGE:58==
4.3 Analysis of VOC results
48
• ASCA was employed to assess the role of inoculation in SL28/34 and Catimor
samples separately, as these groups had the most consistent experimental design.
The analysis revealed statistically significant interactions between starter culture
and fermentation time in SL28/34, as well as near-significant effects related to
processing style in Catimor. Additionally, ASCA showed that LB-1 samples were
characterized by elevated levels of organic acids compared to other inoculations.
In contrast, PK-1 samples were strongly associated with increased concentrations
of acetate esters, higher alcohols, fusel acids and fusel aldehydes.
• PLS-DA supported the findings from PCA and ASCA, concluding that the im-
pact of inoculation is highly context-dependent — strongly influenced by variety
and bean origin. This analysis also reinforced the notion that the control group
should not be treated as a general baseline across all samples but rather as a
reference specific to each variety.
The aim of this section is to confirm, contrast, or expand upon the findings of Garcia
and Sørensen June 2025, using similar statistical approaches to those applied to the FAA
data. However, since the general PCA-based exploration has already been conducted by
Garcia and Sørensen June 2025, this analysis focuses exclusively on the effect of starter
culture using PCA.
As discussed in previous sections, the role of VOCs in green coffee beans is nuanced
and remains partially understood. It is still unclear which VOCs survive the roasting
process, and among those that do, which ones meaningfully impact the final cup quality.
Therefore, the classification of VOCs as ”desirable” or ”undesirable” in the PCA plots
should be seen as indicative only, based on molecules highlighted in previous studies
(Bertrand et al. 2012; Gonzalez-Rios et al. 2007; A. P. P. Bressani et al. 2018). Desir-
ability ratios and percentages have not been used in this analysis, as they may imply a
greater degree of certainty or impact than the current evidence supports.

Total VOC
VOCs were quantified based on peak area normalized with DM rather than absolute
concentration, preventing direct comparison with literature values.
The untargeted
GC-MS analysis identified 91 compounds: esters (22), aldehydes (19), alcohols (14),
hydrocarbons (13), ketones (7), carboxylic acids (6), furans (5), ethers (2), pyrazines
(2), sulfur compounds (1), and terpenes (1).
The relative abundance of compound
classes aligns with previous reports, where alcohols, aldehydes, and acids are typically
dominant (S. Dias, A. Bressani, Batista, et al. 2025, Silva Vale et al. 2019). However,
the higher number of esters detected here may reflect the use of starter cultures and
the wide diversity of coffee origins and cultivars analyzed.

Inoculation generally altered the VOC composition of green coffee beans, support-

with PK-1 alone. This suggests that co-inoculation may not guarantee additive benefits
and could result in microbial competition or inhibition under certain conditions.

suggests that the presence of BCAAs and Met contributes to higher levels of esters and
certain alcohols, hinting at microbial activity influencing VOC formation.
At the same time, the fact that Glu and Asp are negatively correlated with VOC
formation—especially aldehydes—may reflect their role as preferred nitrogen sources

toward biomass production rather than secondary metabolite synthesis (Thorne 1949).
This could explain the reduced formation of VOCs, which typically occurs during the
stationary phase, when AA catabolism becomes more active.

ing this phase are less well studied. However, Ser has been observed to be degraded
by Lactiplantibacillus plantarum during both the exponential and stationary phases
(Fernández and Zúñiga 2006).

quality, suggesting that green bean VOCs may survive roasting to a greater extent
than previously believed, and their contribution to aroma may be more significant than
expected.

beans lead to amplified effects during roasting, due to their role as precursors in Maillard
and Strecker reactions. Finally, the impact on cup quality may also stem from other
unmeasured non-volatile precursors—such as carbohydrates, lipids, free fatty acids, or
proteins—which could play an even more dominant role in shaping the sensory profile
of the final brew.

5.1
Conclusions
This thesis explored the impact of microbial starter culture inoculation on the biochem-
ical composition of green coffee beans, with a focus on FAAs and VOCs. The work
aimed to test three key hypotheses:
1. That inoculated green beans would show more desirable FAA and VOC profiles
than spontaneously fermented ones;
2. That yeast (PK-1) and bacterial (LB-1) inoculations would produce distinct bio-
chemical outcomes; and
3. That co-inoculation would result in a more favorable profile than mono-inoculation.
To test these hypotheses, a combination of HPLC, GC-MS, and multivariate statisti-
cal analyses was employed across a diverse sample set encompassing multiple cultivars
and processing styles. To support these hypotheses, FAA and VOC data were generated
via HPLC and GC-MS, respectively, and analyzed using multivariate statistics across
several coffee varieties and processing methods. The first objective was to develop and
optimize an HPLC method for FAA analysis within the Novonesis laboratory context.
While the analytical method was already established, sample preparation had to be
optimized. The method yielded relatively high variability, but further reproduicibility
assessment should be done when the technical variability decreases after further train-
ing.
Comparative analysis with LC-MS showed that HPLC tends to underestimate
FAA concentrations but remains suitable for the intended application, prioritizing re-
producibility over sensitivity.
The analysis confirmed that coffee variety and processing method were the most
influential factors determining FAA and VOC profiles, as seen in the PCAs and in
alignment with the findings of KU’s thesis Garcia and Sørensen June 2025. However,
==PAGE:75==
5.1 Conclusions
65
when starter culture was isolated as the only independent variable, biochemical trends
emerged. A focused subset of samples enabled this more targeted investigation, allowing
direct comparison of inoculated and control fermentations.
In terms of FAAs, starter cultures produced positive shifts in several cases, particu-
larly for SL28/34 naturals and washed/pulped Catimor coffees, both of which showed
improved desirability ratios and positive desirability percentages. These results support
the first hypothesis and demonstrate that microbial inoculation can, under the right con-
ditions, modulate FAA composition toward more desirable profiles. PK-1, in particular,
showed the strongest effect—especially in short (24 h) fermentations—delivering the
highest DR values observed across all experiments.
Its effectiveness is likely linked
to its ability to colonize the coffee cherry surface more successfully, as it is already a
dominant species in East African coffees.
This ecological compatibility enhances its
metabolic influence during fermentation. LB-1 also showed some beneficial effects but
to a lesser degree, likely due to lower colonization eﬀiciency in the SL28/34 natural
matrix.
VOC profiles, measured via GC-MS, were more variable but generally more respon-
sive to microbial inoculation than FAAs. PK-1 again stood out, especially in SL28/34
samples, where fermentation led to substantial increases in esters, ketones, and alde-
hydes linked to fruity and floral aroma notes. Simultaneously, undesirable sulfur com-
pounds and bitter aldehydes decreased over time, with the clearest improvements ob-
served at 24 hours.
In contrast, LB-1 inoculated samples showed minimal VOC en-
hancement and in some cases, a net decline in volatiles—possibly due to ineffective
colonization or microbial competition.
Interestingly, co-inoculation with PK-1 and LB-1 did not lead to additive or syner-
gistic effects either in the FAA nor in the VOC profiles, in alignment with the findings
from the KU thesis. In SL28/34 samples, co-inoculated fermentations showed moderate
increases in some alcohols (e.g., isobutanol), but desirable esters such as ethyl acetate
decreased, resulting in a less favorable VOC profile compared to PK-1 alone. This out-
come contradicts the third hypothesis and suggests that microbial interactions during
co-fermentation may be competitive or even inhibitory under certain conditions.
A final layer of analysis explored the relationship between FAAs and VOCs across
samples. Joint PCA and hierarchical clustering revealed that FAAs and VOCs varied
largely independently, contributing to orthogonal principal components. VOCs were
more tightly clustered, suggesting higher internal correlation—likely driven by micro-
bial metabolism—while FAAs showed greater dispersion across samples.
Still, some
metabolic links could be inferred: branched-chain amino acids (Leu, Ile, Val) and Met
were positively associated with ester and alcohol production, supporting their role as
aroma precursors. In contrast, Glu and Asp were negatively correlated with VOC for-
mation, possibly due to their early consumption as preferred nitrogen sources during
microbial growth. These findings offer biochemical insight that supports hypothesis 2,
==PAGE:76==
5.2 Future directions
66
indicating that yeast and bacterial fermentations leave distinct signatures on FAA–VOC
interactions.
In summary, the findings support hypothesis 1 and partly confirm hypothesis 2, while
contradicting hypothesis 3.
Starter cultures can indeed modulate green coffee bean
chemistry, but their effectiveness depends on multiple interrelated factors: microbial
strain, fermentation time, processing style, and cultivar. PK-1 emerged as the most
promising strain under the conditions tested, while LB-1’s effects were more limited.
Co-inoculation, while conceptually appealing, did not enhance results in this study and
may require further optimization.
Beyond advancing academic understanding, the findings of this thesis have practical
relevance for Novonesis and its commercial objectives. The demonstrated ability of de-
fined microbial cultures—particularly PK-1—to reproducibly modulate the biochemical
composition of green beans provides a scientific foundation for communicating their
value to coffee producers. While farmers often lack access to sensory or roasting data,
this study highlights how fermentation-driven shifts in FAA and VOC profiles can be
objectively measured at the green bean stage, offering a tangible quality indicator. Such
data can support more consistent fermentation practices and help build trust in starter
culture solutions.
5.2
Future directions
While this study offers valuable insights into the effect of microbial starter cultures on
green coffee chemistry, it is important to acknowledge several limitations. The samples
analyzed span four countries of origin, seven distinct varieties, and four processing
styles. This diversity, while representative of real-world conditions, resulted in minimal
alignment in experimental design. Consequently, analyses were restricted to smaller,
more comparable subsets, reducing the statistical power and limiting the generalizability
of conclusions. This challenge was further compounded by limited technical experience
with the analytical techniques, which introduced additional variability during sample
preparation and data generation.
Another key limitation was the lack of detailed metadata regarding processing param-
eters. The absence of quantitative information on microbial loads (e.g., CFU counts),
fermentation pH, temperature, humidity, carbohydrate availability, oxygen levels, and
drying conditions necessitated a black-box approach. Interpretations often relied on hy-
pothesized mechanisms rather than confirmed process variables. Similarly, no baseline
samples from the start of fermentation (0 h) were available, complicating the ability
to track temporal changes accurately. While these data gaps limit precision, they also
reflect broader structural issues in the coffee industry, where traceability and process
control remain underdeveloped.
==PAGE:77==
5.2 Future directions
67
Looking forward, future research should aim to fill these knowledge gaps through
a more controlled experimental design.
Quantifying additional compounds—such as
carbohydrates, organic acids, chlorogenic acids, and lipids—would provide a more com-
plete picture of how microbial inoculation influences the formation of flavor precursors.
Evaluating the final cup quality alongside biochemical data would also allow for a more
comprehensive assessment of the sensory relevance of FAA and VOC shifts. Moreover,
determining the actual contribution of green bean VOCs to the roasted coffee aroma
would help clarify their commercial importance. Tailored inoculation strategies adapted
to specific varieties and processing methods—such as increasing LB-1 concentrations in
East African matrices—could also enhance effectiveness.
Together, these future directions would not only strengthen the scientific foundation
for microbial starter culture use but also empower producers with practical strategies to
improve consistency, quality, and market access in a growing specialty coffee industry.

C.2
Grinding Optimization Experiments
Two different grinding times (1 min and 3 min) were tested and analyzed for FAA
using the conventional coffee grinder.
While 3 minutes produced a finer and more
homogeneous grind, it caused the grinder to overheat. One minute produced slightly
coarser particles, but the equipment remained cool.
Both methods were tested for
FAA analysis, with reproducibility (measured as relative standard deviation, RSD) used
to evaluate performance.
The goal was not maximum accuracy but a reliable and
repeatable method for future green coffee bean samples. These tests were conducted on
the BR.
Figure C.2:
Experimental design to test several grinding times.
It follows a 3×3×2 layout (grinding ×
extraction × analysis) for each grinding time, resulting in 36 samples: samples 1–18 were ground for 1 minute,
and 19–36 for 3 minutes. Only duplicate determinations were performed for the analytical method, as it is
known to yield low RSD.
This design allowed to assess the grinding method while also estimating the variability
from the extraction and analytical steps. A null hypothesis was defined in advance to
support decision-making regarding the grinding conditions:
• H0,P E: ”There is no statistically significant difference in the variance of the FAA
analysis results between grinding GCB for 1 minute and grinding them for 3
minutes.
In other words, RSD1 min ≈RSD3 min, meaning that both grinding
durations yield comparable reproducibility of the method, as reflected by similar
RSD in the results.”
These are the resulting average RSD obtained:
• Analytical method: consistently low RSD across all amino acids (5.43% ± 0.97),
except for Pro (19.7% ± 16.19), likely due to its low detectability.
• Extraction method: Low average RSD (below 10% (8.69% ± 0.74))
• Grinding method: The two grinding durations yielded comparable variability:
10.42% ± 2.92 for 1-minute grinding (samples 1–18) and 11.43% ± 4.06 for 3-
minute grinding (samples 19–36). No significant difference in RSD was observed
between the two grinding times (p = 0.71 > 0.05).

– Total FAA detected was consistently higher in the 3-minute samples — by
an average of 17.46% ± 9.28 — compared to the 1-minute samples.
Based on these results, the following conclusions were drawn:
A. The analytical method did not require replicates due to its low variability; however,
reducing sample dilution was recommended to improve proline detection.
B. Although the extraction method yielded a low RSD, triplicates were performed,
as it had not previously been tested for GCB in the Novonesis laboratory.
C. One-minute grinding was selected due to its higher feasibility and no significant
difference in reproducibility compared to the 3-minute method, as the null hy-
