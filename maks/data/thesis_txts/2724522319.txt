Abstract
Terahertz (THz) 2-Dimensional (2D) spectroscopy is a relatively new field in
THz science.
Other optical 2D techniques have been used for decades to
investigate the vibrational modes, their couplings, and anharmonicities of
materials. This is done by the use of multiple ultra-fast optical pulses creating
frequency correlation spectra. The techniques for performing the same analysis
for THz have emerged more recently allowing the observation of the lowest
vibrational modes. The lowest modes, so-called soft-modes, are connected to
phase transitions in some classes of materials.
This makes THz 2D
spectroscopy a powerful tool in the exploration of phase transitions and energy
dissipation for e.g. photovoltaics, ferroelectric materials, and phonon-mediated
super-conductors.
This thesis works with various versions of 2D THz spectroscopy to assess the
capabilities of the new THz 2D spectroscopy setup at Stanford Linear
Accelerator Center (SLAC) National Laboratory.
This is done through the
analysis of the scintillator material Bismuth Germanate (BGO) and the
perovskite Strontium Titanate (STO). The system features both DSTMS and
OH1 as generation crystals reaching hundreds of kV/cm in peak field strength.
Two choppers are used to control the combination of pulses allowing for
nonlinear THz spectroscopy.
BGO has the same point group as detector and generation materials such as
Zinc Telluride (ZnTe) and Gallium Phosphide (GaP), making it an interesting
material for THz science. Reflection measurements of BGO showed multiple
nonlinear features which were discarded later as artefacts of the generation or
detection schemes. Later room temperature measurements in both polarimetry
and transmission geometries showed no nonlinear signals, suggesting the
behaviour of BGO is very linear. The room temperature measurements were
recreated by simulating the equations of motion of the lowest-order phonons.
Cryogenic measurements of BGO showed a nonlinear signal, which was not
explained by theory or simulation.
STO has a centrosymmetric structure which breaks below 110 K. This allowed
for different modes and couplings.
STO has a temperature-dependent
soft-mode which behaves as if there is a ferroelectric transition at low
temperatures. The 2D THz system was used to probe the soft-mode of STO in
polarimetry and transmission, for a sample of 250 µm thickness and a thin-film
of 100 nm thickness. STO thin-films behave differently from bulk samples due
to strain from the substrate.
Polarimetry measurements had low Signal to
Noise Ratio (SNR) and were generated by the Kerr effect. The spectra were
not directly related to the material response but to the convolution with the
square of the pump signal.
This observation was confirmed using the
simulation of different soft-mode frequencies.
Transmission spectra showed
==PAGE:3==
ii
third-order nonlinear features confirming the existence and frequencies of the
processes predicted by literature.
It was shown that the frequencies of the
third-order processes were not dependent on the soft-mode frequency, like in
the literature, but on the peak pump signal frequency.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Terahertz (THz) is a loosely defined frequency range usually referring to the
frequencies between 100 GHz and 10 THz, sometimes extending up to 30 THz.
The high frequencies of the Terahertz Radiation (TR) limit the use of traditional
electronics for generation and detection. At the same time, the energy of TR
is low compared to other optical frequencies limiting the use of photodiodes.
These limitations make THz experimental setups complicated and reliant on
nonlinear interactions for both generation and detection.
TR frequencies match the frequencies of the phonons of many materials,
allowing probing and manipulation of crystals, proteins, drugs etc. [1]–[5] An
important technique is THz Time Domain Spectroscopy (TDS), which
measures the change in frequency and phase content of the TR as it passes
through a medium.
Depending on the compound in question, TDS allows
characterisation of the properties such as purity, and dielectric value. [6]
The crystal structure and composition of many modern materials are complex
allowing for the existence of many phonon modes of different properties. The
properties of the phonon modes are important to the material properties,
which
can
change
drastically
for
e.g.
ferroelectric
materials
and
phonon-mediated superconductors. Common for these materials is that they
have a transition temperature.
Approaching a transition temperature might
cause values to diverge such as the relative dielectric constant, or cause values
to go to zero, such as the frequency of the soft mode phonon. [7], [8] TDS can
be used to probe the materials close to the transition temperature allowing for
characterisation and understanding of the transitions and how the exotic
properties of the materials come to be. [9], [10]
The idea of multidimensional spectroscopy is well established in optics, with
most techniques using wavelengths between Infrared Radiation (IR) and
ultraviolet.
[11] It can be used to probe the modes and their nonlinear
couplings for a higher understanding of the internal workings of the molecule,
crystal,
etc.
Multidimensional spectroscopy techniques using THz have
emerged more recently, with two-dimensional THz spectroscopy of organic
substances appearing in the last decade.
[12] The setups for performing
multidimensional
THz
spectroscopy
have
quite
complicated
geometries,
however, suggestions have been made on how to make simple extensions to
traditional spectroscopy methods.
[13] Traditional THz spectroscopy uses a
single THz pulse and a higher frequency ultra-short probe pulse to measure
snapshots of the THz field.
In two-dimensional spectroscopy, an additional
THz pulse is required.
The first dimension is the same which is used in

Knowing the energy pathways and dissipation is important for new materials

Project Description and Goals
The scope of this project is to use two-dimensional THz spectroscopy to
explore the properties and nonlinearities of Bismuth Germanate (BGO) and
Strontium Titanate (STO). Up to the start of the project and during the first
period of the project a two-dimensional THz spectroscopy setup was built at
Stanford Linear Accelerator Center (SLAC) by PhD student Martin Cross in
the laboratory of Matthias Hoffmann, featuring ultra-fast high THz field
strengths, in a temperature controlled vacuum environment.
Through the
analysis of BGO and STO, this project aims to explore, characterise, and
understand the capabilities of multidimensional THz spectroscopy.
The analysis will feature reflection spectroscopy, transmission spectroscopy,
and polarimetry measuring the birefringence of the sample.
Each method
interacts with either infrared active or Raman active phonons, allowing for a
nuanced analysis of the phonon modes and their nonlinearities. Additionally,
STO undergoes a phase change with temperature making it possible to
showcase the capabilities of multidimensional THz spectroscopy in the analysis
of phonon properties above and below a phase transition.

• Explore the capabilities, limitations, and pitfalls of 2D THz spectroscopy
• Produce and analyse 2D spectra and nonlinear spectra of BGO and STO
• Use simulations to support and gain further insight into 2D THz spectra

Chapter 3
Terahertz Spectroscopy
THz radiation is a region of the electromagnetic spectrum with energy higher
than microwaves but lower than IR often referred to as the THz Gap. THz
refers to the frequencies between 100 GHz and 10 THz, sometimes up to
30 THz i.e. energies between 4 µeV and 120 meV. The relatively low energy
and high frequency mean that common methods of detection cannot be
applied. The photoelectric effect requires too much energy and electronics such
as oscilloscopes are limited to frequencies below 100 GHz. [18]
To overcome the difficulties presented a number of other methods have been
devised.
These are split into two groups:
Focal plane arrays (FPA), and
coherent detection schemes. The aim of this chapter is to give an overview of
the detection methods used,
namely the coherent detection scheme of
time-domain spectroscopy.
3.1
Time Domain Spectroscopy
TDS uses an ultra-short probe pulse with a variable delay to probe the quasi-
static intensity of the THz pulse at different times. The THz generation process
often includes pumping with an ultra-short visible light pulse, e.g. an 800 nm
generated by a Titanium Sapphire Laser (Ti-Sapphire), which can also be used
as the probe. TDS requires a detector which is sensitive to the electric field
of the THz pulse. One method is Electro-Optic (EO) sampling. EO-sampling
uses an EO-crystal to apply an intensity-dependent polarization to the probe
pulse. This is achieved using the linear EO effect, or the Pockels effect, which
changes the birefringence of the EO-crystal and thereby the polarization of the
pulse. To measure the polarization, a Quarter Wave Plate (QWP), a Wollaston
prism, and two balanced photodiodes are used. An illustration of this basic
EO-sampling setup can be found in fig. 3.1. By putting a delay stage in the
beam path of either of the pulses, the probe can be temporally swept across
the THz transients, providing a time domain measurement. The time domain
measurement can then be Fourier-transformed yielding the frequency and phase
content of the pulse.

4.1
Theory
This section is based on the discussion of K. Reimann et al. [1]
Multi-dimensional spectroscopy uses the interaction of a sequence of pulses with
the sample to probe its higher-order properties. Each pulse introduces a degree
of freedom with delay to the other pulses. It is the convention to fix the timing
of a single pulse and to define other delays based on the timing of the fixed
pulse. The theory can be expanded to N pulses, however, to keep the maths
simple while still showing the generality of the physics, the equations for two
and three pulses will be presented.
For a perfectly linear medium, the timing of the pulses is arbitrary, and the
resulting field will be the superposition of the original fields.
Elin
ABC = EA + EB + EC

EA, EB, and EC being the fields of the individual pulses, and EABC being
the measured field. This result changes when there are nonlinear interactions.
Starting with only two pulses, fixing the timing of pulse B, and introducing the
relative timing between pulses A and B called τ, the equation for the nonlinear
signal becomes as follows.
ENL(τ, t) = EAB(τ, t) −Elin

subtracted.

wait time T, which defines the third time parameter needed to separate the
three pulses in real time. Like in the case of eq. (4.2), the idea is to remove the
linear signal while conserving the nonlinear signal. In this case, however, the
second-order nonlinear signal is also removed. This is expressed as follows.

nonlinearities can be measured with only two pulses. [1]
It is clear from the logic above, that multiple pulses must be present to produce
the nonlinear signal of the order desired. Therefore, it is possible to separate
a 2D/3D measurement into areas of different content. For the 2D plot, there
are three areas.
The area where no pulse has arrived should be completely
empty in both the linear and nonlinear plots. The area where only one pulse
has arrived should be empty in the nonlinear plot, but not in the linear plot.
Lastly, the area where both pulses have arrived and where a signal is allowed in
the nonlinear and linear plots. Any deviation from the definition of each area
must be considered nonphysical or an artefact. Cases, where this is not true,
exist e.g. long duration ordering in the material carrying over between shots.

4.2
Setup
Performing 2D IR spectroscopy is well known, however, only recently have
simpler THz spectroscopy setups become available. Possibilities for expanding
single beam setups to 2D setups with few extra steps were suggested by
Knighton et al. [13] The simplest suggestion is to use a beam splitter before a
translational stage resulting in the creation of two pulses with a variable delay
and a relative intensity factor of two.
This setup would also create several
smaller pulses with equal temporal spacing.
The simple setups suggested in
Knighton et al., do not allow for multiple simultaneous pulses, as only one
THz generating medium is in use. [13] This means the instantaneous signal
and signals close to the overlap cannot be measured. Other setups exist, where
the THz generating pulse is split and guided to separate generation media
allowing for any desired pulse timing.
[1] Several problems exist with using
multiple different THz generation media.
These differ from the large use of
laboratory space and expensive equipment to practical problems with aligning
multiple beam paths and focusing them onto the same spot.
The detection scheme used for 2D spectroscopy is equivalent to the standard
TDS detection scheme using a detection crystal, a half waveplate, a Wollaston
prism and two diodes. A detection crystal can be placed after the sample to
perform transmission spectroscopy, however, the sample itself can also be used
as the detection crystal. Using the sample as the detection crystal will for the
remainder of this thesis be denoted "polarimetry", as it probes the polarization
rotation caused by the presence of the pump.

Fourier transform. [21]

signal. [2] Solving the equations of motion for each type of interaction resulted
in distinct features allowing the primary interaction to be identified.

let the axis of rotation be the z-axis and go directly through an atom. A 2-fold

contributing −1 to the sum, while z remains unchanged producing 1. The sum
of the operation C2 is thereby −1 per atom on the axis of rotation.
Similar arguments to the above can be made for each individual operation. A
rotation C3 will always produce 0, while S4 or as previously referred to ¯4, will
result in −1 per atom. The mirror operation σd is an inversion around an axis
and is equivalent to a C2 operation resulting in −1.

6.3
Parameter Optimization
After having analyzed which phonons are present in the THz excitation region,
determining how they each will interact with the electric field, and coupled to
each other, several unknown parameters appear. The parameters which provide
the best fit to the data without introducing new features are desired. A 1D
signal has two spaces in which it can be analyzed: The time and the frequency
space. By numerically solving the equations of motion for the phonon system
after introducing a time-dependent electric field some response will appear. The
response can then be compared to the measured signal. This section will discuss
ways of doing parameter optimization to acquire approximate values for each of
the couplings, dampenings, interaction strengths, and frequencies.
Optimization in the current case, is finding the minimum of an error function
defined on the signals. The function is minimized using a differential evolution
solver from scipy.optimize in Python.
The solver allows for a costume error
function and bounds to limit the area of the search for each parameter. As seen
in the PESs above, a lot of parameters are necessary for even simple systems.
The parameters can be seen as spanning a space of n dimensions which for each
additional parameter increases the complexity and computation time greatly.
When possible it is therefore beneficial to reduce the number of parameters or
use approximate values if they are easy to determine.
The error function requires a solution to the equations of motion as input, as
well as a way to compare the measured and simulated signals. There are several
ways to select the error function measure. The first choice is the space to work
in. Depending on the complexity of the system and the SNR it can be beneficial
to optimise on one of the two spaces or some weighted average of the two.
High noise will be more clear in the real-time signal making real-time optimized
signals also fit noise and artefacts. The frequency space optimisation can create
real space signals which are not realistic, but sometimes be necessary to include
the smaller features or peaks which are associated with certain couplings. In
short real-time optimisation is good for simple systems with low noise, while
frequency space is good for more complex systems. A combination of the two is
almost desired unless the system is very simple or very noisy. The selection is
therefore a case-by-case decision.
The error function itself can be designed for individual systems.
There are
several simple error metrics which emphasize different parts of the system. For
this thesis, only power difference Σ|x −xs|d, is used.
Setting d to 2 the
optimization is a nonlinear least squares fitting function.
Some features of
d = 2 include the absolute value of the difference at each point avoiding
auto-balancing effects, features having large weights in the frequency space,
and noise having large weights in real space. In frequency space, the features
tend to be much larger than the noise due to them being peaked. The squared
error, therefore, puts more weight on features in the frequency space.
The

7.5 Systematic Error Sources

7.5
Systematic Error Sources
A number of sources causing drifts or less-than-ideal conditions for the processes
described in this chapter were present in the lab. The two most important,
which are directly visible in the data or when comparing data are temperature
fluctuations and laser power drift.
Temperature fluctuations in the lab caused pathlengths and power to
fluctuate at regular intervals of about 15 min to 20 min.
The drift in path
length was large enough to be clearly visible in all 2D scans. Because the drift
was sufficiently slow, horizontally neighbouring points could be assumed to be
measured at approximately the same path length and laser power.
This is
important because the sequence of pulses at each points A, B, and AB should
all be done under the same conditions to be used to calculate the nonlinear
signal.
Laser power drift was most often caused by the crystal in the Micra Oscillator
getting dirty over time reducing the throughput. The power fell in the order
of 0.5 mJ after the OPA per week. The very long scans took around 14 hours
meaning the power drop across a 2D scan was in the order of a few percent.
Other errors occurred such as the phase of the choppers being off or the
alignment quality going down suddenly or over time. In each case, the error
could be corrected when redoing a measurement though the quality might not
be constant over the course of months of measuring.
The sample temperature which is reported for cryogenic measurements is based
on a temperature sensor placed in the upper part of the sample holder. This
is indicated in fig. 7.6 at "temperature measurement position 2". Position 1 is
located further up along the cooler. The agreement between the temperature
measurement and the temperature of the samples were dependent on the thermal
contact between the cold finger mount, the sample holders, and the samples.
For the STO samples silver paint was used, which should have a decent thermal
conductivity, while for BGO nail polish was used. The fragile nature of the
samples meant it was not possible to assure good direct contact between the
samples and their holders, but require glue to do most of the heat transport.
Temperature values are therefore a lower boundary for the temperature of the
samples.

STO: Polarimetry and Transmission
The previous section on the results of BGO presented polarimetry and
transmission data with almost no nonlinear signals. The lack of nonlinearities
suggests that the system does not create measurement artefacts, which could
impact the STO measurements. Having verified the methods of polarimetry,
transmission, and simulation for a linear material, the methods will be
extended to nonlinear materials. This section will apply the methods to STO.
In section 5.2 it was discussed that bulk STO has a temperature-induced phase
transition at 110 K. A similar transition can be induced in STO thin-films using
strain caused by the substrate.
Two samples were available, a 100 nm thick

of the two samples and transmission measurements of the thin-film sample.

Discussion
In chapter 8, measurements of reflection, transmission, and polarimetry for three

some of the points of consideration made on the results, how they might be right,
how they might be used, and how they might be wrong.
9.1
BGO
9.1.1
Reflection
2D reflection scans of BGO were presented along with a reference measurement
where an Au mirror was put in place of the sample. The BGO scans showed two
nonlinear features of which one was present in the reference scan. The signal
was vertical, only appearing after the arrival of pulse B and growing with the
separation of the pulses. The origin of the vertical signal could be an excited
Si beam splitter which was excited by the remaining 1.3 µm light from pulse A
before pulse B was transmitted through the beam splitter. The lifetime of the
excitation could be explored by doing a much longer scan focused around the
arrival of the B pulse. This could provide support for the beam splitter being
the cause if it showed excited carrier lifetimes in Si. Note that the nonlinear
signal only appears for pulse sequence AB and not BA, which suggests that it
is caused by asymmetry. The asymmetry could be pulse A being reflected off of
the beam splitter, while pulse B was transmitted.

motion, by moving each line by the value of the sinewave at the given index.

Conclusion
Through this thesis, the capabilities of the 2D spectroscopy setup at SLAC has
been tested in three geometries for three samples.
BGO was measured in the reflection geometry revealing two strong nonlinear
signals, none of which originated from the BGO. The first signal was most likely
the excitation of an optical component, growing with larger pump-delays. This
signal was eliminated by blocking the 1.3 µm excitation beam right after the
THz generation. The second signal was a checkerboard pattern located at the
overlap of the pulses. The pattern was recreated in great detail using only a
sinusoidal dependence on the electric field and a reference of the pulses used in
the experiment. This suggests that the second signal was caused by breaking
the small field assumption of the probe polarization, resulting in over-rotation
during detection.
The polarimetry measurements of BGO revealed a low-dampened harmonic
oscillation pattern with a frequency of 2 THz and a more highly dampened
harmonic oscillation at 3 THz. The transmission measurement showed a strong
absorption at the frequency of the 2 THz phonon.
The high-temperature
nonlinear signal of both polarimetry and transmission contained only noise
suggesting that BGO is a highly linear material.
The low-temperature
measurements contained a weak nonlinear signal in zones of the map forbidden
from containing nonlinear signals.
These were analysed using two different
types of noise, but the spectrum of the nonlinear signal could not be
replicated.
Simulations of single and coupled phonons were performed recreating the
measured signal of BGO. 2D maps were created with identical features to the
undistorted
measurements.
The
nonlinear
signal
of
low-temperature
polarimetry BGO measurement was not recreated using phonon couplings but
did show the sum and difference frequencies of the two present phonons.
In conclusion, 2D measurements of reflection, transmission, and polarimetry
were performed, producing 2D spectra and 2D nonlinear spectra of BGO. The
highly linear nature of BGO, made it possible to identify sources of error in
the system and eliminate them. This included nonlinear signals from optical
components, over-rotation, and temperature fluctuations causing misalignment,
which was possible to correct. Using simulations of the equations of motion for
the two IR-active phonons, it was possible to recreate the linear polarimetry
maps of BGO. A nonlinear signal was present in the low-temperature BGO
measurements, which behaved like an artefact, though the origin of the signal
was not found. By coupling the phonons it was possible to simulate a nonlinear
==PAGE:112==

response pattern having frequencies at the peaks of the measured nonlinear
signal. However, the simulated signal did not match the measured one.
Polarimetry measurements of thin-film and bulk STO were limited by the SNR
requiring the integration over a large number of pulses. The nonlinear signal
of the 2D STO polarimetry measurements showed different features at different
temperatures. In the vector-based interpretation of the nonlinear maps DFG
and SFG was important, with only DFG being present at high temperature. Due
to inversion symmetry, no second-order processes were allowed in phase I STO
suggesting that the origin of the nonlinear features was not DFG. The thin-film
STO showed nonlinear features at room temperature different from the ones in
bulk STO, suggesting that strain from the substrate has shifted the soft-mode
phonon frequency or caused the transition to phase II. Through simulation of
the soft-mode phonon, it was possible to recreate the general structure of the
nonlinear polarimetry signals. This was done by convolution of the response of a
linear system containing a single dampened harmonic oscillator and the square
of the pump signal. This means 2D nonlinear THz spectroscopy can exhibit
nonlinear features even though the material behaves completely linearly.
Transmission measurements of thin-film STO showed nonlinear features of the
third order for both room-temperature and low-temperature measurements.
The features were associated with the pump-probe signal, the AAB∗, and the

resulted in nonlinear spectra related to the shape of the pump signal and
spectrum. The high noise made it impossible to separate the pump and the
material response.
The shapes of the nonlinear polarimetry spectra were
supported by simulations using different soft-mode frequencies. Transmission
measurements showed only third-order features correlated to the spectrum of
the
pump
signal,
corroborating
the
polarimetry
conclusion
that
the
interactions in STO are dominated by third-order interactions.
The 2D THz system was tested in various geometries and showed after
adjustments, no artefacts in the nonlinear spectra caused by the system.
High-resolution scans were performed in reflection geometry, transmission
geometry, and for the Pockels effect dominated BGO in polarimetry geometry.
The Kerr effect produced much less signal and required a deconvolution
making the technique difficult on the current system. Using simulations of the

equations of motion,
it was possible to recreate the behaviour of the
polarimetry scans for both the Pockels effect and the Kerr effect.
