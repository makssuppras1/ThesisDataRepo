Abstract
Traditional cement plants account for atleast 8% of global CO2 emissions in the atmo-
sphere. In order to curb this increase, the use of calcined clay in cement manufacturing process
has been proposed since it can partially replace clinker and reduce carbon emissions from the
calcination process. Since it requires lower temperature in the pyro-processing stage com-
pared to a traditional clinker, the traditional combustion process using coal can be replaced
with means of electriﬁcation using a hot gas generator (HGG). In this thesis, It is deemed nec-
essary to understand the basics of the calcination by electriﬁcation process and emulate the
same in DigSilent PowerfactoryTM. With the introduction of the electriﬁcation, it is possible
to integrate renewables with the plant which would help in offsetting CO2 emissions. The heat
generated by the HGG is typically around 1000◦C and the temperature to calcine clay is around
750◦to 850◦C.
In this project, A pilot cement plant with the electriﬁcation process is designed and devel-
oped in DigSilent PowerfactoryTM. Since it is a pilot plant, the power consumption of the load
is minimal compared to that of a traditional sized plant. The plant is planned to be built in the
north of Denmark so the corresponding renewable energy sources such as Wind and Solar are
considered for this plant. Furthermore, a BESS is also implemented to store surplus power and
help in allowing the plant to be capable of operation for a short time if and when the grid is
ofﬂine and simultaneously enhance power ﬂexibility in the plant.

Introduction
1.1
Background
With rapid urbanisation and the development of infrastructure to support it, the demand for
cement has not reduced. The cement industry has continuously put pressure on the environ-
ment and contributed signiﬁcantly towards climate change. It is one of the leading contributors
to CO2 emissions, owing to 8% of the total CO2 emissions globally [1]. To meet carbon net
zero goals set by the United Nations, which states that CO2 emissions globally should be re-
duced by 45% by 2030[2], various steps to reduce emissions are currently being researched.
Recently, the use of supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs) in composite cement has
been proposed. Considering multiple factors, calcined clay is reviewed as a reliable SCM and
is being researched by various institutions as it can effectively replace the high composition of
clinker content in traditional cement [1]. This would reduce the emissions from the calcina-
tion process signiﬁcantly. This could be improved further by replacing conventional modes of
calcining, which is usually carried out by fossil fuel combustion, such as coal, with electricity.
Electriﬁcation of the calcination process would be the next step in moving towards carbon net
zero.
Figure 1: Proposed model of cement plant
This project aims to analyse and report on the performance of the electriﬁed clay calcina-
tion process plant powered by renewable energy sources. This is performed by modelling a
pilot plant comprising the electriﬁcation module in DigSilent PowerfactoryTM as depicted in
Figure 1.
This project is in collaboration with FLSmidth and part of their ECoClayTM whose ob-
jective is to reduce CO2 emissions from the cement industry by 50%. The preliminary stage
of the partnership is to design and build a pilot plant at a R&D center in Denmark and to ex-
hibit the plant’s performance in terms of efﬁciency, reduction in capital and lower emissions
[3].FLSmidth has made its data and expertise available for this project. The initial layout is

1.2
Traditional Cement Plant with layout
Currently, the most common cement used globally is ordinary Portland cement (OPC),
which is widely used in concrete production for general construction work [4]. OPC consists
of several materials, of which 95wt% is collectively termed "clinker", and the remaining 5wt%
is gypsum. Gypsum doesn’t contribute to CO2 emissions. Cement production occurs through
3 stages: raw material preparation, clinker production and cement grinding. The majority
of energy consumed and CO2 emissions from the plant is from limestone calcination, which
occurs at 900◦C, accounting for 60% of the total plant’s emissions [4]. From here, it enters the
rotary kiln, where the cement clinker is produced at around 1500◦C.

Calcined Clay and Electriﬁcation of Pilot plant
To curb CO2 emissions from traditional cement types, the industry has been researching the
use of Supplementary Cementitious Materials (SCM) in composite cement, which can replace
a part of the clinker used in cement. From the various SCMs available, calcined clay, a type of
clay ﬁred at very high temperatures, ﬁts the prerequisites of being a reliable SCM owing to its
availability, low cost and pozzolanic properties [5], [6].
The cement obtained from blending limestone and calcined clay is termed Limestone cal-
cined clay cement (LC3). The composition of LC3-50 is 50% clinker, 30% calcined clay, 15%
limestone and 5% gypsum. It is considered by the cement industry as an alternative to tradi-
tional cement owing to the reduction in CO2 emissions. LC3 can signiﬁcantly reduce the level
of clinker content in the cement, with some cement compositions consisting of just 40% clinker
[5].
Figure 2: Schematic diagram of Electriﬁed Calcination process[6]
Regarding LC3 production, the calcination of clay used in the cement occurs in tempera-
tures 750◦- 850◦. Then, the calcined clay and limestone are ground or mixed to form a blend.

3.1
Integration of renewables
Being energy-intensive and conventionally dependent on fossil fuels, the cement industry
has been under pressure to limit its carbon emissions. The urgent need for sustainable ad-
vancement has propelled the integration of renewable energy sources in manufacturing plants
to offset the carbon footprint further.
The pilot cement plant is planned to be erected by FLSmidth at one of their R&D centres in
Assens, Northern Denmark. Denmark is at the forefront when it comes to utilising renewable
energy sources. In 2023, Renewable energy such as wind and solar was able to provide 63%
of the total electricity supply to the country. This translated into renewable energy covering
almost 44% of total energy consumption [7]. Based on the data, the chosen renewable energy
sources for this project are wind and solar photovoltaic. Further, a battery storage is added to
provide power ﬂexibility to the plant. Integrating renewables into the grid brings uncertainty
regarding continuous power ﬂow and frequency deviations.
3.1.1
Wind Energy
The geographical location makes Denmark rich in wind resources owing to being sur-
rounded by the sea [8]. It has been the most abundantly available renewable energy source
followed closely by solar. The incentives and schemes provided by the Danish government to
the locals around wind farms and to the players in the wind industry in recent years have helped
develop several wind farms across the country and made them a reliable energy source [8]. In
2023, wind power accounted for almost 80% of total renewable energy generation and 53% of
the total electricity consumption in Denmark [9].
Since the total load of the pilot plant is only 327.9 kW, Enercon-48 has been chosen as the

3.1.3
Battery Storage
Introducing a battery storage helps mitigate electricity costs while simultaneously help in
reducing CO2 emissions. It is mostly utilised to provide energy arbitrage which is storing
power during off-peak hours or when prices are low and using it during peak hours of demand
in order to save costs. They can also be used to stabilise the grid at times of frequency or
voltage deviation by either delivering or storing power to satisfy energy equilibrium [11].

The battery integrated in this project is of type power minimisation which is a quasi-
dyanmic simulation model (QDSL) that is designed to regulate and optimise power ﬂows in
an electrical grid [12]. This battery type is predominantly used to minimise the power ﬂow
through a speciﬁc transmission line. In this project, the battery is used to minimise the power
ﬂowing through the transmission line connected to the grid Line_ext. This would limit the
power transfer between the grid and the plant and utilise the battery to store and discharge
when necessary [13].
The behavior of the battery is modelled by a QDSL script and it follows the DigSILENT

Results and future work
The aim of this thesis was to analyse the power ﬂexibility in a renewable-powered cement
plant which incorporates electriﬁed clay calcination process. The model provided by FLSmidth
was developed in PowerfactoryTM. Renewables were integrated with the electriﬁcation module
in order for the plant to be self sufﬁcient and also help reduce the plant’s carbon footprint.
Storage plays a vital role in power balance and also helps in mitigating production cost by
storing power when prices are low and utilising it when prices are high or when the demand is
high.
Further, this project can be researched further by integrating the electriﬁcation module to a
cement place essentially replacing the traditional method of calcination, and analyse the power
ﬂuctuations as the module needs to be scaled up along with the renewables and storage. This
could pose problems in capital investment cost when it comes to renewables.

Appendix: Load Flow Equation code QDSL model
double Plimit;
if ({mode = 1}) {
! Unlimited power can be used to minimise power flow
SetEquation(0, Pline - 0);
} else if ({mode = 2}) {
! Battery is storing with maximum power
SetEquation(0, Pset - Pfeed);
} else if ({mode = 3}) {
! Battery is feeding with maximum power

} else if ({mode = 4}) {
! Battery is limited by SOCmax in next time step while storing
Plimit = ((SOC-SOCmax)*Eini*3600)/(timeDif*100);
SetEquation(0, Pset - Plimit);
} else if ({mode = 5}) {
! Battery is limited by SOCmin in next time step while feeding
Plimit = -((SOCmin-SOC)*Eini*3600)/(timeDif*100);
SetEquation(0, Pset - Plimit);
}else {
! Battery is at SOCmin or SOCmax and has an output of zero
SetEquation(0, Pset - 0);
}
