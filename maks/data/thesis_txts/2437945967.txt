Abstract 
 
Hydroeconomic modelling and analysis provides the water managers with a tool to support and guide water resources 
management and is particularly useful in area subject to water curtailment, as it delivers insight on optimal water allocation 
strategies. This thesis aims at developing a hydroeconomic model representing the water resources system of Northern 
Laos. 
The study area is represented as a node network that incorporates all the major hydrologic and engineering features of the 
system, on which the different users are connected. The requirements for the different water uses, including hydropower 
water demand, agriculture and domestic demand are defined, along with the economic value of the water for each user, 
which establish the willingness to pay for water of the users. The water availability is evaluated using a rainfall-runoff model. 
The model describes the constraints of the system, such as the water mass balance or the capacity of the infrastructure, 
using mathematical formulation and defines the optimal water allocation decision that maximize the economic return of 
water allocation. 
In addition, a tool to support investment planning decision is implemented. It optimizes the choice and the timing of possible 
investment projects in order to maximize the resulting profits. The choice of hydropower and irrigation expansion 
investment projects are optimized under different budget constraints. It is shown that in Lao PDR, hydropower investments 
are favored to the detriment of irrigation expansion projects, but also that hydropower projects can shadow otherwise 
profitable irrigation expansion investment when coupled together. This emphasizes the need of a global overview when 
developing water resources systems, which can be provided by hydroeconomic models.

1. Introduction and Objectives 
 
Water is not only a vital resource for humans but is also essential for the development of most sectors of the society. In the 
last century, water use has increased at a rate of more than twice the rate of population growth, which is highly increasing 
the pressure put on the water resources. Water scarcity is becoming one of the biggest challenges of this century, as it is 
estimated that two-thirds of the global population will be under water stress by 2025 (UN-Water, 2018). In the context of 
water scarcity, water allocation strategies need to be reconsidered to maximize the economic and social returns of water 
allocation (UN-Water, 2006). The fourth principle of The Dublin Statement on Water and Sustainable Development states 
that water has an economic value and should be managed as an economic good to achieve efficient and equitable use and 
to encourage the protection of water resources (United Nations, 1992). 
Hydroeconomic modeling and analysis integrates this principle by considering and including the economic aspect of water 
use in the water management problem. In a hydroeconomic model, water allocation decisions are driven by the economic 
value of water for the different uses and services that it provides. It thus deliver insight on the optimal management 
strategies, but can also outline wasteful water management decisions (Harou et al., 2009).  
Water serve several purposes, both to the ecosystems and for human consumption, thus water resources management 
needs to consider multiple objectives that are often contradictory. By considering the economic value of water for its 
different use, hydroeconomic modelling transforms a multi-objective management problem into a single objective problem, 
which allows for comparison of the water uses and thus reveals the interactions and compromises between the different 
water users (Harou et al., 2009). 
The integration of economic principle in water engineering and water management is not a new concept, and the first 
hydroeconomic models (HEM) date back to the 1960s in arid regions of the globe. HEM have since been applied to various 
water resources problems, involving both quantity and quality of the water resources. It includes domains such as cross-
sectoral water allocation and use, groundwater and surface water joint use or land use management but it is also used to 
evaluate the impact of climate change, policy changes and infrastructure expansion for instance (Harou et al., 2009). 
Evaluating the impacts of a planned change is one of the strength of hydroeconomic modelling. By comparing with and 
without scenarios, the changes can be evaluated on a global scale, for all the actors of the water system, and can therefore 
assess the benefits or costs amongst the different sectors, for a single or a combination of changes (Bauer-Gottwein et al., 
2017). 
 
With a context of strong economic development and rapid hydropower expansion, Lao PDR is a perfect example of the 
importance of managing the water resources efficiently to support the further development of the country. Hydropower 
and agriculture development are the main development sectors in Lao PDR. Hydropower development is driven by its high 
profitability and is seen as an efficient means to reduce the poverty and enhance the economic growth of the country 
(Ministry of Energy and Mines, 2014a). However, the consistency of hydropower development with other socio-economic 
and environmental development objectives, such as irrigation expansion, have not been abundantly investigated (Bartlett 
et al., 2012). Hydroeconomic modelling and analysis can provide important insight on the trade-offs between competing 
water uses and on the ideal water management approaches, as well as evaluating the repercussions of the different 
development projects that could be realized in the future.

2. Study Area 
This study focuses on the northern part of Lao PDR, more precisely on the 12 following provinces: Bokeo, Houaphan, Louang 
Namtha, Luangphrabang, Oudomaxai, Phongsali, Vientiane and its prefecture, Xaignabouri, Xayasomboun, Xiangkhouang 
as well as the most northern part of Bolikhamxai. The study area is shown in Figure 1 as the blue hatched area. 
 
Figure 1 - Project area and corresponding provinces 
Lao PDR is mainly a mountainous country with most of the land covered by forests. Cultivated lands represent only 6% of 
the total country area and are mainly situated in the plain of the Mekong river. The climate is ruled by the southwest 
monsoon, with a rainy season that extends from mid-April to mid-October. About 75% of the precipitation occurs during 
this season. Lao PDR is rich in water resources and the country contributes 35 % of the total flow of the Mekong (FAO, 2012b, 
p.297–310). During the wet season, the left bank tributaries from Lao PDR are by far the greatest contribution to the 
mainstream flow of the Mekong (Mekong River Commission, 2010).  
Over the last two decades, Lao PDR has been undergoing rapid economic development and is one of the fastest growing 
economies in East Asia (World Bank, 2018). The GDP increased by about 7.8% per year during the years 2000 to 2009. The 
country has abundant natural resources, mainly water, minerals and forests and their use contribute to around one third of 
the economy.  
==PAGE:14==
 
 
Master thesis | 4 
 
 
 
 
Lao PDR has a very big hydropower potential and the country is one of the richest countries in south-east Asia with respect 
to hydropower resources (International Hydropower Association, 2016). Since 1993 and the opening of the power sector to 
foreign investors by the Laotian government, the installed hydropower capacity increased from 206 MW to over 4000 MW 
in 2015. Two-thirds of the hydropower production is exported to its neighboring countries, mainly to Thailand and Vietnam 
(International Hydropower Association, 2016). 
Agriculture accounts for 35 % of the GDP and 75 % of the economically active population is engaged in the agricultural sector 
(FAO, 2012b). Rice is the dominant crop and is mainly cultivated to ensure self-subsistence. The cultivation of cash crops, 
produced for export, such as sugar cane or maize, is becoming more important (Thongmanivong and Fujita, 2006). 
Cultivated land area is limited due to the topography of the country; in the northern region, the slopes are below 20% in only 
6% of the of the land area (Asian Development Bank, 2005). Area equipped for irrigation represent 27% of the total cultivated 
area, with rice being the main irrigated crop, followed by vegetables, cotton, citrus and sugar cane (FAO, 2012b). 
Having a high development potential, hydropower is seen by the Laos government as a cost-effective energy source. The 
current government is promoting the development of hydropower projects and is aiming at reducing poverty using the 
profits from hydropower sales (Ministry of Energy and Mines, 2014b). In northern Laos, at least 30 hydropower projects are 
under consideration for future development.

3. Data and Methods 
3.1. System representation 
The study area is conceptualized and represented as an association of catchments, to which users, reservoirs and transfer 
links are connected. For each catchment, the catchment situated downstream is defined and is used to represent the river 
network in the model. 
Several user types are defined, being domestic, industrial, agricultural and hydropower users. Domestic, industrial and 
agricultural users are each associated to their corresponding catchment and are defined by their water demand and 
willingness to pay for water. Reservoirs are situated at the outlet of the catchments and are characterized by their storage 
volume, while transfer links are defined by an inlet and an outlet catchment and characterized by their transfer capacity. 
Hydropower plants are either linked to a reservoir or, for run-of-river, to a catchment. Their power production depends on 
the installed capacity and the water-energy equivalent (i.e. the amount of energy produced per volume of water discharged 
through the turbines) and they are also defined by a willingness to pay for water. An illustration of the system representation, 
with 4 catchments, 3 users and 2 reservoirs, is shown in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2 – Example of the system representation, for four catchments, including 2 reservoirs and 3 users of two different types (orange and 
green). 
 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
Master thesis | 6 
 
 
 
 
The catchments and river arcs are defined automatically using a digital elevation model (DEM) and a hydro-processing 
routine. This is done using the QSWAT plugin in QGIS 2.18.15, which uses the TauDem watershed delineation feature. The 
delineation is executed in three processing steps: First, the stream network is delineated by specifying a delineation 
threshold which defines the area that must drain into a given pixel to consider it as part of the river network. A threshold of 
6000 pixels which corresponds to 375 km2 (with a DEM resolution of 250 m) is chosen, to ensure that important outlet points, 
like reservoirs, are part of the stream network. The second step consists of defining the inlets and outlets in the basin. At the 
confluence of streams, outlets are automatically placed, but some are manually added at points of specific interest: at 
reservoirs, both constructed and planned, at the country boundaries and at the main outlet of the study area. Finally, the 
watershed is delineated and divided into catchments and the nodes are placed at the outlets of each catchment. 
In the study area, a total of 316 catchments are defined, in which 33 reservoirs, 32 hydropower plants and one transfer link 
have been identified. In each catchment, 6 types of users are distinguished (one domestic/industrial and five agricultural 
users, see 3.4), for a total of 1896 users.

3.2. Hydroeconomic optimization model 
Hydroeconomic models (HEM) can take multiple forms, with different goals associated to them. Simulation models answer 
the question of ‘what if’ and are suitable to evaluate the effects of management policies or infrastructure development for 
example. Simulation models can incorporate the water management strategies to reproduce actual water allocation 
decisions. Optimization models, on the other hand, answer the question of ‘what is best’ and tend to optimize water 
allocation by either maximizing the net economic value of water allocation or minimizing the costs of water allocation and 
water-supply curtailment (Bauer-Gottwein et al., 2017; Harou et al., 2009). Optimization models necessitate the evaluation 
of the economic value of water, for all water uses. Simulation and optimization models can be used independently or 
together. 
In this study, a holistic optimization model with deterministic hydrologic forcing (perfect foresight) is used. A holistic model 
includes the economic model in the water management model. All inputs and outputs are calculated in a single model, with 
the exception of the rainfall-runoff process, which is modelled separately (see section 3.3.1). When time is represented with 
deterministic time series, inputs and decision variables are time series.  
 
3.2.1. Optimization model 
As suggested in Harou et al. (2009), linear programming is often used to solve hydroeconomic optimization models. The 
optimization problem is described as a linear problem. In a linear problem (LP), the objective function and the constraints 
are expressed as linear functions. The objective function is minimized or maximized, subject to a number of inequality or 
equality constraints. The standard form of a linear problem, in the matrix form, is defined as follow:

3.3.1. Rainfall-Runoff model: Input data and Model representation 
The rainfall-runoff model described above requires two types of input data: Climate forcing data and parametrization of the 
river basin, which includes information on subbasin latitude and area. The datasets used in this study are described below. 
Digital Elevation Model (DEM): To delineate the watershed and determine the rivers courses, the DEM (version 4.1) from 
the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) is used. The DEM has a resolution of 3 arc second, being about 90 m (Jarvis 
et al., 2008). Four 5 degrees tiles corresponding to the study area are downloaded, merged and cropped to the area of 
interest. The DEM is resampled to a lower resolution of 250m to comply with the computational limits of the delineation 
process. 
Temperature: Daily minimum and maximum temperature are needed to estimate potential evapotranspiration. The 
temperature data used in this study are obtained from the ERA-Interim product of the European Centre for Medium-Range 
Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). The dataset provides four daily analysis of 2-meter temperature, with a spatial resolution of 
79 km, since 1979 (Dee et al., 2011). 
Precipitation: At basin-scale, precipitation is the most important input in the water balance (Guo and Liu, 2016). Therefore, 
accurate measurements are necessary as the quality of the precipitation data has a strong impact on the performance of 
hydrological model. In the absence of rain gauge stations, the precipitations data are obtained using Satellite-based Rainfall 
Estimates (SRFE). The data used in this study is from the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission (TRMM), which provides near-
real-time precipitation estimates. The dataset consists of the daily accumulated rainfall from January 1998 to June 2017, 
with a spatial resolution of 0.25°. 
 
The following forcing input and parametrization are required for the Budyko model: 
− 
Daily minimum and maximum temperature time series 
− 
Daily precipitation time series  
− 
Subbasin area and latitude (at the centroids of each subbasin) 
− 
River network drainage pattern 
− 
Calibration parameters (α1, α2, Smax, d, n and K) 
The subbasins and river network are defined in the system representation (See section 3.1).

3.3.2. Calibration 
To optimize the model performance, the parameters of the rainfall-runoff model are adjusted with automatic calibration by 
comparing the simulated with the observed variables. The calibration is performed using the automatic calibration tool 
SPOTPY in Python, developed by Houska et al. The plugin includes the evolutionary global search algorithm, the Shuffled 
Complex Evolution algorithm – University of Arizona (SCE-UA) which is suited to calibrate models with high parameter space 
(Houska et al., 2015). The algorithm samples multiple parameter sets within the parameter boundaries defined by the user. 
The model performance is evaluated for each parameter sets, which are then ranked by performance and divided in multiple 
complexes. Each complex is evolved individually before being shuffled to ensure that the information about the parameter 
space is shared between the complexes. For the calibration of the hydrological model, 8 complexes are evolved and 
convergence is defined as less than 0.5 % change in the objective function and parameter value change over 100 model runs, 
with a maximum of 50’000 model runs allowed (Kittel, 2016). 
The calibration is made using a multi-objective approach, to incorporate the different type of available observations. Two 
objective functions were used in this study, the flow-duration curve and the climatology benchmark. Both of them, as well 
as the global objective function are described below. 
 
Flow Duration Curves: Flow duration curves (FDC) are cumulative frequency curves that show the percentage of time during 
which specific discharges are equaled or exceeded (Searey, 1959). They allow the calibration of models for which the time 
period of the observation data and the model input do not overlap, if sufficiently long historical data are available 
(Westerberg et al., 2011). To determine the FDC, the N discharge data are ranked from highest to lowest and a rank M is 
given to every discharge value. The probability of exceedance is calculated as

3.4. Water use model 
The water use model quantifies the water demand for the different water users. Multiple categories of water demand are 
distinguished: agricultural, domestic, industrial, hydropower and environmental water demands. Agriculture is by far the 
biggest consumer of water and represents 70% of the world total water withdrawal. In Lao PDR, 91% of the water withdrawal 
is intended for agriculture (FAO, 2016). The calculation of the different water demand is detailed below. 
3.4.1. Domestic and industrial water use 
The domestic and industrial water demand is derived for every catchment using the national statistics on water use from the 
AQUASTAT database of FAO and the LandScan 2016 Global Population Database (Bright et al., 2017). The LandScan 2016 
provides global population distribution at a resolution of approximately 1 km and is used to compute the population in each 
catchment.

Additionally to the water demand, the return and loss rate of water allocation is defined. The Water Supply and Sanitation

same time step, the return rate is set to zero. 
 
3.4.2. Irrigation water use 
To ensure their optimal growth, the crops need to receive sufficient water to fulfill their water needs. Water can be brought 
naturally through rainfall, provided by irrigation or a combination of the two. The irrigation water demand (IWD) is defined 
as the additional water that needs to be supplied through irrigation to fulfill the crop water need. If the rainfall is sufficient, 
irrigation is not necessary and the IWD is zero. On the contrary, if there is no rainfall during the growing season, irrigation 
must fulfill the entire crop water need. In the most cases, rainfall provides part of the water needed and the rest is supplied 
by irrigation. In this case the IWD is the difference between the crop water need and the effective rainfall, rainfall being 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
Master thesis | 23 
 
 
 
 
effectively used by the plants. The irrigation water demand of a catchment is the sum of the IWD of every crop cultivated in 
the catchment. 
To estimate the irrigation water demand for each catchment, the type of crop cultivated as well as the area of land that is 
irrigated for every crop type need to be determined. For this, the MIRCA 2000 dataset is used, which consists of monthly 
irrigated and rainfed crop areas. The dataset has a spatial resolution of 5 arc-minute and refers to the years 1998 to 2002 
(Portmann et al., 2008). The data provides information for 26 crop types and was shown to be consistent with the water use 
and irrigated area statistics of the AQUASTAT database of the FAO. 
Only the major crops are used in the estimation of the irrigation water demand and are determined using the MIRCA 2000 
dataset and the FAO irrigated crop calendar (FAO, 2012a). They include rice, maize, sugar cane, citrus and cotton. The 
irrigated area of each crop type is determined by using the monthly map showing the highest crop area.

The evaluation of the irrigation water demand is made in three steps. First, the crop evapotranspiration is evaluated, using 
the FAO 56 guidelines for crop evapotranspiration. Then, the crop water demand is calculated, based on the crop 
evapotranspiration and the crop area and eventually the irrigation water demand is defined, from the crop water demand 
and precipitation. The crop evapotranspiration is evaluated following the procedure shown in Figure 8 and described below.

Reference evapotranspiration E0: The reference evapotranspiration corresponds to the evapotranspiration of a reference 
crop, a hypothetical grass with fixed parameters like crop height, surface resistance and albedo. The FAO recommends the 
FAO Penman-Monteith method as the standard method to compute E0 (Allen et al., 1998). However, due to missing 
meteorological data (air humidity and wind speed), the Hargreaves equation is used to calculate E0 (equation (3.32)). 
Crop coefficient Kc: The crop coefficient Kc is defined as the ratio between the crop evapotranspiration and the reference 
evapotranspiration. It differentiates a specific crop from reference grass by integrating the effect of four characteristics that 
distinguish them: Crop height, albedo, canopy resistance to vapor transfer and evaporation from the soil. As the crop 
develops, those characteristics will change, therefore the crop coefficient will vary over the growing period. Four different 
growth stages are defined: the initial, crop development, mid-season and late season stage. The different growth stages are 
shown in Figure 9. 
The initial phase goes from the sowing date to approximately 10% ground cover. Soil evaporation is predominant during this 
stage, therefore Kc ini is highly influenced by the wetness of the soil. Kc ini is determined by the time interval between wetting 
event, the evaporation power of the atmosphere (E0) and the importance of the wetting events. 
The crop development phase is from 10% ground cover to effective full cover. During this stage, Kc corresponds to the extent 
of the ground cover and the plant development. 
The mid-season stage runs from effective full cover to the start of maturity. The crop coefficient is at its maximum during 
this stage and is rather constant for most growing and culture conditions. 
The late season stage goes the start of maturity to harvest. The Kc end value reflects the irrigation practice. It will be high if 
the crop is frequently irrigated until harvested fresh but will be low if the crop is drying out in the field before the harvest.

3.4.4. Willingness to pay (WTP) of users 
To evaluate the willingness to pay of users, two types of water users must be distinguished: producers, who use water to 
produce a product, with the goal of maximizing their profit with the sale of the product and consumers, for whom water is a 
final good, who aim to maximize the utility of water and other goods under a budget constraint (Bauer-Gottwein et al., 2017). 
Producers include agricultural, hydropower and industrial users while consumers include domestic users or recreational 
water uses. For producers, the WTP will depend on the value of the final product and on the production processes. For 
consumers, the WTP is defined by the utility of water and is influenced by the preferences and budget constraints of the 
user. The methods used to define the WTP of each user type is explained below. 
Agricultural users: For the agricultural users, water is a necessary input to the production process. As an intermediate good, 
its value depends on the final output. The WTP is derived using residual imputation (based on Young, 2005), by subtracting 
all non-water input costs to the gross benefit, the residual value being the one of water.

4. Investment planning 
Hydroeconomic modeling and analysis can be used in many applications, particularly in planning problems. The economic 
effects of changes, as institutional changes or hydraulic infrastructure development, often need to be evaluated beforehand. 
To assess whether a project is economically acceptable, hydroeconomic modeling can be used to evaluate the variation in 
costs and benefits created by the implementation of the project (Bauer-Gottwein et al., 2017). The advantage of 
hydroeconomic analysis is the possibility to evaluate the economic impacts of a change through the different sectors of the 
entire system, but also to evaluate the impacts of a combination of changes.  
Development of new infrastructures being always subject to limited budgets, hydroeconomic modeling is a useful tool to 
evaluate which project or combination of projects will be favorable to maximize the economic impact of infrastructure 
development under a given budget.  
The implementation of investment planning in the existing optimization model is described below. 
4.1. Implementation of investment planning 
The goal of implementing investments planning in the HEM is to create a tool that optimizes the choice and the timing of 
possible investment projects to maximize the resulting profits. The investment projects can be the construction of new 
infrastructures, the expansion of existing infrastructures, changes in water demand or even the implementation of new 
water, power or food related policies.  
Each investment project is defined by its type (reservoir, hydropower plant, transfer link, user and more), additional capacity, 
cost, construction time and whether it is dependent on other projects. Multiple investment phases can be defined over the 
time span of the model, each of them characterized by a maximum investment budget and a duration.

4.2.3. Cost-Benefit Analysis 
For each investment project, a cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is performed, to assess its economic feasibility. In a CBA, the 
profitability of a project is evaluated by comparing the additional benefits, resulting from the implementation of the project, 
to its costs. The net benefit of a project is defined as the difference between the additional benefit and cost. As money is 
seen to be more valuable now than in the future, the notion of discount rate is introduced, to incorporate the time value of 
money. The net present value is used as the indicator of the profitability of a project and is defined as the sum of the net 
benefits over the chosen planning horizon, discounted to the current value (Griffin, 2006).

5. Results and Discussion 
The main results obtained with the HEM are the economic benefits resulting from water allocation and power production, 
the water distribution between the users, as well as the shadow prices of the different constraints of the system. Shadow 
prices are giving important insight on the limits imposed by the constraints on the optimal decision. The shadow price 
represents the additional benefit resulting from relaxing, by one unit, one of the constraints of the system. If a constraint is 
not binding, the shadow price will be zero. The shadow price of the water, given by the water balance constraint (equation 
(3.3)), gives the value of adding one unit of flow in any catchment. The shadow price of the infrastructure capacity constraints 
gives the value of expanding infrastructures, thus giving information on which would benefit an expansion. The 
environmental flow shadow price reveals the costs of maintaining the environmental flow requirement. It can be interpreted 
as the additional benefits that could be made if the environmental flow requirements was decreased by one unit (Harou et 
al., 2009).

scenario are used as a base to later compare the effects of the different investment scenarios.

5.2.2. Investment decision for multiple budget constraints 
For each of the budget constraint, the model defines the optimal combination of projects in order to maximize the economic 
benefits. The decision variable defining the investment choice for the different budgets is presented in Appendix 6. 
Figure 19 shows the additional installed capacity and irrigated area for the various budget constraints. It can be noticed that 
hydropower capacity increases continuously as the investment budget increases. On the other hand, no pattern is visible for 
the additional irrigated area. When observing the repartition of the budget between the two sectors, it is clear that 
hydropower investments are prioritized over irrigation expansion projects (Figure 20). Irrigation projects having low costs, 
they are implemented after hydropower projects using the remaining budget.

For a budget of 50 million $, only irrigation expansion projects are implemented, as the budget is smaller than the least 
expensive hydropower project. A budget of 100 million $ would be sufficient to implement a hydropower plant, as three 
projects have cost below 100 million $. However, even though irrigation projects have a lower NPV than hydropower, the 
IRR of most irrigation projects is higher than the IRR of these three project, therefore implementing multiple irrigation 
expansion projects is more profitable. 
For budgets between 200 and 1600 million $, the budgets only allow for hydropower projects with smaller capacities, and 
the hydropower investments are chosen based on their NPV and IRR. The projects with the highest NPV and IRR will always 
be implemented first, followed by the project with highest NPV and IRR that complies with the left-over budget, until it is 
not possible to invest in hydropower anymore. 
With budget higher than 1600 million $, the large hydropower projects are implemented, in an order that respects their 
relative profitability, namely from highest to lowest NPV. With a budget of 3200 million $, only one of those larger projects 
can be implemented and the project who has the highest NPV, Santhong-Pakchom, is chosen. For the next two budgets 
tested, 4800 and 6400 million $, Luang Prabang, even though it has the highest NPV, is not implemented, as instead two or 
three large projects can be constructed, leading to a higher total capacity and generating more benefits than a single project. 
When multiple projects with similar costs are possible, the ones with the lowest NPV will always be dismissed. For example, 
with a budget of 8000 million $, Luang Prabang is implemented, leaving funds for two additional large projects, which are 
Santhong-Pakchom and Pak Lay, as they have the largest NPV of the four possible projects.  
Irrigation expansion projects are not influencing the choices of hydropower projects and irrigation expansion 
implementation depends mainly on the remaining budget. However, hydropower projects do influence the choice of 
irrigation expansion projects. As agriculture has a consumptive use of water, allocation to agricultural users can reduce the 
water allocation to hydropower plants situated downstream of the users, which would decrease the profit generated by the 
hydropower plant. As hydropower generates the large majority of the profits (96% for the baseline scenario), it is prioritized 
over irrigation, and thus some irrigation expansion projects that are profitable without hydropower become 
disadvantageous with hydropower. This mainly applies to rice, as it has the largest water demand of all crops. As an example, 
in the Nam Suang watershed, rice irrigation expansion is planned for budgets of 100 and 200 million $ but is never invested 
in again once either the Nam Suang 1 or Nam Suang 2 hydropower projects, situated in the same watershed, are 
implemented. 
For hydropower projects, it can also be noticed that the Nam Ngao hydropower plant is never selected for investment, since 
it has the lowest NPV and IRR of all projects, but also has the highest cost per installed capacity of the smaller projects.

5.3. Improvement of the model and further work 
The quality of the data used in the model having a large impact on the overall results, it is important to recognize which data 
could be improved. Some of the area in which the data could be improved are: 
• 
Calibration of the rainfall-runoff model 
• 
Definition of the irrigation water demand 
• 
Willingness to pay of hydropower user 
• 
Willingness to pay of agricultural users 
 
Calibration of the rainfall-runoff model: As shown in section 5.1.1, the inflow values have a large impact on the results of 
the HEM, which stresses the importance of calibrating the rainfall-runoff to represent the reality as closely as possible. As 
mentioned in section 3.3.2, considering additional in-situ discharge stations, equitably distributed on the study area is a first 
step to improve the quality of the calibration. Some of the discharge stations should also be used as validation station only 
to validate the model. Additionally, including remote sensing observations in addition to in-situ discharge observations 
could improve the quality of the rainfall-runoff model calibration. Observation data such as the GRACE total water storage 
change could be used to calibrate the catchment total water storage (Kittel et al., 2018). 
Definition of the irrigation water demand: As irrigated agriculture is by far the biggest water consumer, it is important to 
use an accurate estimation of the water demand. Multiple improvements could be made when estimating the irrigation 
water demand. First, the MIRCA 2000 dataset, which gives the irrigated and rainfed area for different crop types, date back 
to the years 2000. In the last 20 years, Lao PDR has been developing considerably and this dataset might be outdated. 
Secondly, rice culture was estimated to be only paddy rice, even though in the mountainous regions, upland rice is mostly 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
Master thesis | 58 
 
 
 
 
cultivated, which requires less water, since the fields are not flooded. With a lower water demand, the optimal solution could 
be affected, especially in the catchments with large upland rice cultivation. 
WTP of hydropower user: About two-thirds of the hydropower produced in Lao is exported to its neighboring countries, 
mainly Thailand, Vietnam and Cambodia (International Hydropower Association, 2016, p.75). For most of the hydropower 
plants, memorandum of understanding (MOU) are signed between the Lao government and the receiving country, which 
defines a supply commitment and selling prices (Ministry of Energy and Mines, 2014a). As hydropower export is an important 
source of income for Lao, information on selling prices for each hydropower project would improve the accuracy of the 
hydro-economic model. 
WTP of agriculture user: The WTP for water is deduced based on the residual imputation method, which is very sensitive to 
the assumptions made on non-water inputs. If an input costs that is relevant in the production is omitted, it will lead to an 
overestimation of the WTP (Young, 2005). Having to get around the lack of available data on non-water inputs, the resulting 
WTP is most probably overestimated. Additional information on non-water inputs quantities and prices would increase the 
precision of the WTP estimation. Cash crops such as sugar cane and maize being mostly produced for export 
(Thongmanivong and Fujita, 2006), the export prices of those crops would be a better estimation of the product price (𝑝𝑦). 
 
As presented above, some input data can be improved to increase the accuracy of the model and should be done as a first 
step. Secondly, some addition can be done to the existing model, to better represent the system and some of its limitations. 
As hydropower generates the majority of the benefits, the limitations applied to this sector have a high impact on the model 
results. Therefore, the power sector could be modelled in greater details, to better represent the limitation that applies to 
it. Currently, only the hydropower plants are represented, and they are not linked to power network nor constrained by 
power demand. Transmission lines, that represent the power network and link the power production infrastructures to the 
power market, can represent an important limitation to the power production if the transmission capacity is not large 
enough. In addition, the power demand of the country and for export could be included as a constraint to the production, as 
if the demand is lower than the current modelled production, water allocation could change to favor agricultural users. 
The current system being divided in over 300 catchments, with large fluctuation in size between them, the interpretation of 
the processes occurring in the system is made more difficult. To solve that, catchments could be merged together in larger 
area, that respect the flow network and keep the reservoirs as outlets of catchments, which would considerably decrease 
the number of catchments and possibly better underline the interactions between the reservoirs, hydropower plants and 
the water allocations to consumptive users.

6. Conclusion 
This study aimed at representing the water resources management problem of Northern Lao with a hydroeconomic model 
and defining the optimal decisions in term of water allocation through the different water use sectors. Additionally, the goal 
was to evaluate the impact of infrastructure development for multiple scenarios, representing various investment budgets. 
The study area was represented by 316 catchments in which 33 hydropower plants were identified. The domestic and 
agricultural water demand was defined in every of the catchments and the water value was evaluated for every user. As few 
measurements are available in the area, the water availability was estimated using a rainfall-runoff model. Based on these 
information, the optimal decisions for water allocation were obtained.  
The model showed the importance of hydropower production in the optimal decision, as it generates 96% of the total 
benefits. In irrigated agriculture, rice is the predominant crop and receive 96% of the water allocation. Even though water is 
abundant during the wet season, irrigated agriculture mainly takes place during the dry season, which leads to water 
curtailment for the agricultural users of almost one fifth of their demand, mainly impacting rice cultivation. The water 
shadow price is high in the catchments situated upstream of hydropower plants. During the dry season, its value also reflects 
the curtailment of agricultural users. The environmental flow shadow prices are representing the curtailment of users during 
the dry season. It is zero along the main Mekong and in most of the catchments during the wet season. Hydropower 
production reaches its maximum at the end of the wet season, when inflows are high and the reservoirs are at their fullest. 
By implementing an investment planning tool, the model defines the most profitable combination projects amongst a 
collection of projects. 10 watersheds were selected for irrigation expansion projects as well as 10 reservoirs-linked 
hydropower and two run-of-river hydropower projects were proposed. Different investment scenarios were defined for 
different maximum investment budgets. A cost-benefit analysis was performed for all projects and revealed that all projects 
were profitable, with some hydropower projects having very high internal rate of return. Generally, hydropower projects 
have much higher net present value and higher internal rate of return than irrigation expansion projects. Thus, hydropower 
projects largely overtake the irrigation expansion projects and generate 90% of the total additional profit. Irrigation 
expansion implementation is dependent of the budget remaining after the choice of hydropower projects is made. The 
development of irrigation for cash crops, such as citrus, cotton and sugar cane, was shown to be greatly profitable. 
It was also defined that, in some case, hydropower development could shadow otherwise profitable irrigation expansion 
projects, as they can compete for the same resource. It demonstrates the usefulness of developing a HEM when planning 
multiple development projects, as it offers a global overview of the system and can reveal rivalry between different sectors 
that would not have been visible otherwise.
