Abstract
Left ventricular hemodynamics has attracted much attention of researches,
due to its key role in the functionality of the heart. The use of compu-
tational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) permits a detailed prediction of the intra-
ventricular ﬂuid ﬂow, which is valuable in the clinic for diagnosis and
treatment of heart diseases. The choice of boundary conditions is crucial
in any CFD simulation since they are essential determinants for the ﬂuid
ﬂow. In this thesis, a CFD study of the blood ﬂow inside a phantom left
ventricle (LV) was carried out. The ﬁnite element method was used to
numerically solve the incompressible Navier-Stokes equation for the ﬂuid
domain. It was investigated how the blood ﬂow was aﬀected by its inlet
boundary condition and its inlet geometry. Four CFD models deviating
by their inlet geometries (main and extended geometry) and their spatial
inlet proﬁles (plug and parabolic proﬁle) has been constructed and solved.
The main geometry represents the in vivo anatomical LV, whereas the ex-
tended geometry reﬂects the phantom LV. It was found, by qualitative
and quantitative analysis, that the eﬀect of the spatial inlet proﬁles on
the prediction of the blood ﬂow pattern was highly depended on the ge-
ometry. The position of the vortex ring, which arises in the LV cavity
during diastole, has been quantiﬁed by the parameter vortex depth (VD).
VD represents the vertical position of the center of the vortex ring rel-
ative to the LV long axis. For the main geometry, VD at end-diastole
was 0.23 for a plug proﬁle and 0.43 for a parabolic proﬁle.
Contrary,
for the extended geometry only a minor diﬀerence was found, where the
VD at end-diastole was 0.52 and 0.53 for plug and parabolic inlet pro-
ﬁle respectively. The temporal velocity proﬁle was prescribed from the
LV movement, which permitted a realistic condition where the blood ﬂow
through the aortic oriﬁce was insigniﬁcant during diastole. The blood ﬂow
patterns were visualized using the λ2-method, which was implemented in
COMSOL Multiphysics. A script-based modeling method in COMSOL
Multiphysics with a LiveLink for MATLAB has been developed for the
CFD model construction and simulation. The method was advantageous
to handle multiple models due to its automation and ﬂexibility.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) is, mainly in the developed countries, a common cause of
death [9]. Pathology in the left ventricle (LV) is especially crucial since the LV is the
terminal chamber before the blood enters the rest of the body (apart from the lungs).
Computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) has been widely used in the evaluation of cardiac
functionality, where hemodynamic ﬂow patterns and parameters have been predicted and
analyzed [9]. This opens up new opportunities regarding early diagnosis, surgical decisions,
follow-up treatments, the design of heart protheses, and general clinical examinations of
properties within the heart [12]. This can increase survival rates and enhance the quality
of life for patients.
1.1
Motivation
Many studies concerning numerical simulation of the intra-ventricular blood ﬂow have been
published during the last couples of decades. Despite this, there is still no clinical, patient-
speciﬁc simulation tool available in the clinic. This is caused by the very complex nature
of the heart, which includes anatomy, electro-dynamics, metabolism, and ﬂow, which is
challenging to capture in a holistic model.
Common to all CFD simulations is that researchers must make certain assumptions
concerning boundary conditions. It is essential that the choice of boundary conditions
do not make the results to be untruthful. The motivation for doing this study is that
previous literature concludes that the boundary conditions have a high impact on the
intra-ventricular ﬂow [9]. The issue is not fully examined; hence this study will contribute
with knowledge about how diﬀerent types of boundary conditions will aﬀect the CFD
simulation.
This study is an input to an ongoing project in the research group at the Center for
Fast Ultrasound, where a patient-speciﬁc geometry prescribed simulation model is being
developed. Previous work in the group has focused on using the COMSOL Multiphysics
graphical user interface (GUI) and its built-in functions for visualization.
When hav-
ing multiple simulations, a more automated workﬂow for generating, solving, and post-
processing is needed.
The possibilities for obtaining this is investigated in this thesis.
To visualize, interpret, and compare the vortex formation from the ﬂuid velocity ﬁeld of
diﬀerent simulations, a robust vortex detection algorithm is needed.

1.2. PURPOSE
1.2
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the impact of diﬀerent inlet boundary conditions
when applied to a ﬁnite element (FE) based CFD model of the human LV. The following
steps are taken to achieve the aim of the thesis:
1) A CFD model is constructed in COMSOL Multiphysics with inspiration from the
theses of R. Hvid [11] and M. Lopez [19]. The geometry originates from a dynamic
heart phantom DHP-01 provided by SHELLEY Medical Imaging Technologies.
2) Four simulations, which diﬀer by their inlet geometry and their spatial inlet boundary
condition, are constructed. Two geometries are simulated, each with a ﬂat and a
parabolic spatial inlet velocity proﬁle.
3) A method for volume-prescribed determination of the temporal inlet velocity is de-
veloped and applied as the inlet boundary condition for the simulations.
4) It is assessed if the impact of the boundary condition on the ﬂow pattern depends
on the LV geometry.
5) A script-based modeling method is developed. The strengths and weaknesses of this
method are presented and discussed.
6) A commonly used algorithm for vortex identiﬁcation; the λ2-method, presented in
[13], is in this thesis implemented in COMSOL Multiphysics with a LiveLink for
MATLAB. It is hypothesized that the λ2-method is more robust to vortex detection
than using pressure isosurfaces.
7) It is investigated how the simulated blood ﬂow inside the ventricle is aﬀected by its
inlet boundary condition. This is done by qualitatively and quantitatively comparing
simulations which diﬀer only by their boundary conditions and geometry. The results
are also compared to similar published studies.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter contains the theory behind the project and is dived into three independent
sections:
1) Clinical Background
2) Fluid Dynamics
3) Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)
The anatomical and physiological background concerning the heart is presented in the ﬁrst
section. Some basic ﬂuid dynamics properties are presented in the second section together
with a superﬁcial description of vorticity and vortex dynamics, which is necessary to un-
derstand the visualization of the ﬂuid motion, at the end of this thesis. The third section

2.1
Clinical Background
Making and assessing decisions about assumptions and simpliﬁcations demands on knowl-
edge about the modeled system.
To understand a realistic model of the left ventricle
knowledge of the true human heart and its blood ﬂow is needed.
2.1.1
Heart Anatomy
The human heart is a hollow muscular organ with four chambers; left ventricle (LV), left
atrium (LA), right ventricle (RV), and right atrium (RA). On both sides, the atrium is
placed superior to the ventricle, and a valve connects the two. The LA has four relatively
uniform openings that receive blood from the lungs through pulmonary veins. The mitral
valve is placed between the LA and the LV and it is the inlet to the LV. Blood is leaving
the LV through the aortic valve (the outlet of LV), into the aorta [30]. The anatomy of
the heart with its diﬀerent chambers and valves is seen in Figure 2.1a.
2.1.2
Heart Physiology
The heart is vital; it circulates the blood, ensures one-way blood ﬂow, regulate the blood
supply, and generates the blood pressure in the human body. The heart acts like a pump
divided into two. The right side which receives the deoxygenated blood and pumps it into
the pulmonary circulation, where the blood is entering the lungs. Here it is oxygenated and

The Cardiac Cycle
The blood ﬂows from regions with higher pressure to regions with lower pressure. The heart
generates these pressure diﬀerences which makes the blood ﬂow through the circulation.
The cardiac cycle is divided into two phases based on the conditions in the ventricle:
• Diastole: The ventricles are relaxed and are being ﬁlled with blood (ﬁlling phase).
• Systole: The ventricles contracts and pumps blood out to the body.
The atria contracts at the end of the diastole and are relaxed under the systole. In the early
part of diastole, the pressure inside the LV becomes smaller than the pressure in the LA,
and the mitral valve opens. This results in a passive ventricular ﬁlling, which gives rise to
the so-called E-wave (early rapid ﬁlling). The transmitral velocity at this time is called the
E-ﬁlling velocity and accounts for approximately 80% of the total ventricular ﬁlling. Some
blood will remain in the LA and is afterward pumped into the LV by an active contraction
of the LA called active ventricular ﬁlling. This gives rise to the so-called A-wave. The
transmitral velocity of blood entering the LV at this time is called the A-ﬁlling velocity.
The same mechanism happens on the right side of the heart. The shape of the spatial
transmitral velocity proﬁle (EA-wave), for a healthy person, is seen in Figure 2.2, where
the cardiac pressures and the LV volume are seen as well.

2.2.3
Vorticity and Vortex Ring Formation
Vorticity is a measurement of the local tendency for a ﬂuid’s particles to spin and it is
expressed by
ω = ∇× u,
(2.8)
[15]. The spatial distribution of vorticity leads to diﬀerent ﬂow structures. An example is
a vortex, which is an accumulation of vorticity in a compact region [15]. Another example
is a shear layer: an elongated distribution of vorticity. The boundary layer is a shear layer
which adjoins the boundary.
A three-dimensional vortex ring is a stable, axis symmetrical, torus-shaped vortex
structure. A ﬂow from a circular oriﬁce can form this ring, when a shear layer originating
from the oriﬁce is rolling up into a ring around the inﬂow jet. This ring has a self-induced
velocity away from the oriﬁce [15]. The phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 2.3b and is
seen in the healthy LV, when the ﬁlling jet passes the mitral valve.
The quantitative parameter vortex depth (VD) is deﬁned as the vertical position of the
vortex center (orange line in Figure 2.3c) divided by the LV length (red line in Figure 2.3c),
and is used for quantitative comparison in Chapter 5.

2.3
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)
Fluid ﬂow can be predicted based on equations obtained from the conservation laws for
mass, momentum, and energy, which leads to the Navier-Stokes and the Euler equations.
The equations are very involved, and in many cases, an analytical solution is not obtainable.
CFD is the exercise of numerically solving the Navier-Stokes or Euler equations in 2D or
3D to achieve quantitative, physical insight into the ﬂuid dynamics [29].
A physical ﬂow ﬁeld is continuous, and so are the partial diﬀerential equations de-
scribing it. Computers work with discrete values, and the equations must, therefore, be
approximated by discretization. Many methods exist for doing this, such as ﬁnite diﬀer-
ences, ﬁnite volumes, and ﬁnite elements. This project will use ﬁnite elements.
2.3.1
The Finite Element Method
The ﬁnite element method (FEM) dominates computations of stresses and strains in solid
mechanics. The FEM has also been successfully used to solve problems in ﬂuid mechan-

analytically. A major beneﬁt of FEM is that irregular geometries can be accommodated.
This feature is useful for bioengineering, in which structures of the body typically present
complicated geometries. The domain is divided into smaller, simpler parts to solve the
problem. The elements are called ﬁnite elements and are connected using so-called nodes.

2.3.2
The ALE Formulation
A deformed mesh is required, when simulating moving boundaries, which is the case for the
pumping LV. The arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) formulation is the method used in
this thesis and is described in the following inspired by [7] and [8].
Two traditional methodologies exist to describe material kinematics; the Lagrangian
and the Eulerian, which mainly diﬀers by the position of the observer.
In the Lagrangian approach individual particles of the material are observed as they
move through space and time. Material coordinates X are used to deﬁne the material
domain RX. This method is convenient for numerical simulation of solid materials where
deformation may occur.
In the Eulerian approach the material is observed from a ﬁxed point in space, where it
is described as function of time regardless of the speciﬁc particles of the material. Spatial
coordinates x is used to deﬁne the spatial conﬁguration Rx. This method is mostly used
to simulate ﬂuids [9].
However, the Eulerian approach has a drawback; it cannot handle moving domain
boundaries, which is the case in this thesis. Therefore the ALE formulation is used to
solve the PDE’s in COMSOL Multiphysics. The main idea in the ALE formulation is to
combine the best aspects of each methodology. The ALE formulation succeeds with this,
by introducing a computational mesh Rχ which can move with a velocity independent of
the velocity of the material particles. The Lagrangian method is used at the boundary, and
the Eulerian method is used in the ﬂuid domain. This demands a new reference coordinate
system: the mesh coordinate system. Mesh coordinates χ is used to deﬁne the reference
conﬁguration Rχ. A one-to-one mapping to the material and spatial coordinates exist,
illustrated in Figure 2.5. The ALE version of the governing equations binds the velocity
of the ﬂow u to the velocity of the deforming mesh umesh. The ﬂuid mesh can now move
independently of the ﬂuid particles. The Navier-Stokes equation (2.4) in its ALE-form
is obtained by subtracting the mesh velocity in the convective term of the Navier-Stokes

2.4. STATE OF THE ART
2.4
State of the Art
CFD has been widely used in the evaluation of cardiac functionality, where reliable ﬂow
dynamics has been demonstrated for many years. Regardless of this, a clinical patient-
speciﬁc simulation tool is still not present. All of the presented models have signiﬁcant
limitations and complications due to the modeling of a very complex system - the human
heart.
The following three topics will be discussed below:
1) Main approach to the applied CFD technique inspired from [9]
2) Boundary conditions
3) Atrial ﬂow patterns.
The studies within the ﬁeld can be divided into two main groups based on their approach
to the numerical simulation by the applied CFD techniques.
The ﬁrst group uses the
geometry-prescribed method that applies the movement of the endocardium as the bound-
ary condition for the ﬂuid domain. Only the governing equation for the liquid domain is
solved when using this method. This is the most popular approach due to its convenience
and its relatively low demand for computing resources. It is also the method applied in this
project. The prescribed wall movement can be deﬁned directly by extracting data from
medical imaging as T. Schenkel et al. do in [25] or indirectly by formulating mathematical
equations as M. Nakamura do in [21]. The second group uses the ﬂuid-structure-interaction
(FSI) technique, which numerically solves the governing equations for both ﬂuid and struc-
ture domain. The publications by Y. Cheng et al. [4] and M. Areﬁn et al. [1] fall within
this second group. The feedback from the structure on the ﬂuid is considered when using
this method.
Common to all CFD simulations is that boundary conditions (BC) must be set for the
wall, the inlet, and the outlet. Q. Long et al. and B. T. Chan et al. concludes in [18] and
[3] respectively that the inlet velocity proﬁle has a high impact on the ﬂow patterns within
the ventricle. The inlet BC can be expressed by either pressure, velocity, or ﬂux/mass
ﬂow. Q. Long et al. presented in [18] a new hybrid method. It splits up the inlet area
into two regions; One major velocity boundary region and one smaller pressure boundary
region (patch). This is done to achieve the goal of global mass conservation. The idea is
that the velocity is not prescribed in the pressure region; hence, it can act as a safety valve
for obtaining global mass conservation. It turns out that the CFD simulation is highly
sensitive to the inlet BC and a more realistic inﬂow proﬁle is seen when using the hybrid
BC compared to the commonly used pure pressure BC. The eﬀect of diﬀerent transmitral
velocity proﬁles is investigated by B. T. Chan et al. in [3], where CFD simulations with
parabolic and ﬂat proﬁle are compared. It is shown that only the ﬂat proﬁle gives rise to a
ﬂow pattern which is consistent with that reported in the literature. This agrees with the
short mitral valve length that disables the ﬂow to be fully developed since the ﬂuid enter
the LV from a reservoir more than from a tube-like artery. It should be mentioned that
the study uses a very simple, axisymmetrical geometry, which can aﬀect the reliability of
the results.

ure 2.6.

is discussed in the literature whether this aﬀects the ventricular ﬂow too. One study [20]

Modeling
A physiological model will always be a simpliﬁcation of the true system. It is essential to be
well-considered when choices about assumptions and implementation are taken. Every time
a component is added to the model, complexity will be added as well. Consequently, only
the necessary components should exist in the model to make it realistic and certain. In this
study, the real system is the human heart, which is very complicated. Among other things,
anatomical, physiological, and electronic perspectives exist in the heart. Furthermore is
the heart located inside an even more complex system, namely the human body.
A two-ventricle heart phantom can model the true physiological heart. Such a phantom
can produce a simple movement and a ﬂuid ﬂow which is more available and reproducible
than the conditions in the human heart. Simple non-invasive validation experiments can
be performed on the phantom, which is valuable in the research phase. Modeling of the
phantom heart is an intermediate step toward achieving a patient-speciﬁc simulation of
the human heart.
The CFD LV simulation can be a model of the heart phantom, which is illustrated
by the orange path in Figure 3.1.
Every time modeling decisions are made may it be
considered how it actually is in the phantom, and how this is modeled beneﬁcially. On the
other hand, the true heart can directly be modeled by a CFD simulation, and decisions
must, therefore, depend on how the conditions actually are in the real heart. This way
is illustrated in Figure 3.1 by green. Both approaches are applied in this thesis and CFD
modeling of either the true heart or the heart phantom is a recurring issue through this
report.
This chapter contains a description of the LV geometry, its prescribed movement, and
the ﬂow properties, which together comprise the model. An argumentation of choosing a
volume-prescribed inlet velocity is given as well, where the actual temporal velocity proﬁle
is compared with an analytic proﬁle.
Through the chapter, the reasoning behind the

the results. This leads to a list of modeling simpliﬁcations and lastly a motivation for
building up the model as described in the chapter.

Simpliﬁcations
Some of the simpliﬁcations when going from the true physiological heart to the CFD model
are listed in the following:
• The twisting behavior of the cardiac contraction, described in Section 2.1.2 is ex-
cluded from the model.
• Only the E-wave of the cardiac cycle is simulated when the temporal inlet velocity is
prescribed. The physiological contraction, described in Section 2.1.2, exists of both
an E- and an A-wave. This complicated behavior has been simpliﬁed to a one-wave
inﬂow velocity as seen in Figure 3.7a. It is assumed that all of the blood in the LA
is ejected during this one wave.
• A physiological cardiac cycle consists of diastole, which accounts for 2/3 of the full
period. In the CFD simulations the cardiac cycle is divided equally between the
diastole and the systole.
• The stroke volume (SV) of the physiological heart is around 70 ml leading to a cardiac
output (CO) of 5.25 l/min. The SV and CO of the simulation are only 11.5 ml and
0.9 l/min, respectively.
These values are out of the possible physiological range.
The deviation originates from the very simpliﬁed prescribed movement in the CFD
simulation.
• No valves or papillary muscles are included in this model.

3.5. MOTIVATION
When going from the phantom to the CFD model the following simpliﬁcation is performed
• The prescribed movement described in Section 3.2 assumes a linear displacement
along the arc length of the LV. The phantom is made of an elastic material, and
the true function describing the displacement is therefore not linear. It is known
that the base has zero displacement and the apex is displaced 10 mm.
A linear
function is chosen because it is simple to implement, and the chosen function must
be diﬀerentiable.
• The inﬂow BC is constructed to simulate the conditions in the anatomical heart and
does not match the present inﬂow in the phantom. It is supposed that these can be
adjusted to correspond the BC used in this thesis, by programming the inﬂow.
3.5
Motivation
The following modeling motivation is presented to underline the reason of simulating the
ﬂow in two diﬀerent geometries.
• The extended geometry is used for better validation options. However, the validation
against in vitro phantom experiments is beyond the scope of this thesis.
• The BC’s are constructed to reﬂect the scenario in the real heart.
Therefore, a
validation against in vitro measurements, in future work, requires the creation of an
in- and outﬂow in the dynamic heart phantom, which matches the simulation.
• It is assumed that the main geometry better reﬂects the real heart geometry and is
therefore interesting when comparing with literature.
• As an input to the ongoing project in the research group, the extended geometry is of
interest since it represents the ﬂuid ﬂow in the dynamic heart phantom. It would be
preferred to extract the geometry directly from a CT-scan of the phantom, instead
of the .stl ﬁle provided by SHELLEY Medical Imaging Technologies, to secure the
phantom and the simulation is in agreement.
• It can be assessed whether the geometry aﬀects the impact of the boundary conditions
on the prediction of blood ﬂow patterns when having two geometries.

4.1.3
Geometry Shape Order
It is imperative to be aware of the geometry shape order of the component when performing
any simulation in COMSOL Multiphysics.
The geometry shape order is the order of
the curved mesh elements that determine the geometry shape [5]. The default option in
COMSOL MultiPhysics is Automatic and can, therefore, change according to, e.g., the
physics in the model. The domain volume is aﬀected by the geometry shape order since
a higher order come closer to the true boundary. Since the volume is the link between
the movement simulation and the CFD simulation, it is essential that the geometry shape
order is the same in the two simulations. The geometry shape order is set to Quadratic
in the simulations to obtain a precise volume. This choice is quantitatively veriﬁed in
Section 4.7.2.
4.2

COMSOL MultiPhysics has two interfaces for implementing deformed meshes namely De-
formed Geometry and Moving Mesh. When applying Deformed Geometry the deformation
of the geometry boundaries corresponds to addition or removal of material. Moving Mesh is
recommended when it is studied how a solid object deforms as the results of physical load,
and how ﬂuids in adjacent domains react to the displacement of the domain boundaries
[5]. The interface Moving Mesh is therefore used in this thesis.
The movement function zDisp(t, z), deﬁned in Equation 3.1, is added as an Analytic
Function in COMSOL MultiPhysics. The movement simulation is run with only one cycle,
whereas the CFD study is solved for four cycles; this choice is elaborated in Section 4.7.4.
A Free Deformation node is applied to the entire domain with no initial displacement.

element size. This implies:
• a ﬁne double boundary layer at the wall
• a corner reﬁnement with decreased element size at sharp edges
• an unstructured tetrahedral mesh calibrated for ﬂuid dynamics for the remaining

4.5.1
Solver
Two types of solvers exist in COMSOL Multiphysics; a fully coupled and a segregated. The
fully coupled solver forms one large system of equations, which is solved for all unknowns
across multiphysics at one single iteration. Contrary, the segregated solver subdivides the
involved physics into segregated steps. These are solved sequentially within one iteration,
which requires less memory than the fully coupled approach [5]. The applied solvers are
concisely described in the following for reproducibility.
Movement Simulation
A fully coupled solver is used to solve the movement simulation-study. Since there is only
one physics in this simulation, it does not make sense to split it up into segregated steps.
A direct linear MUMPS solver is called when solving the movement simulation.
The solver is time dependent and solves from t = 0 s to t = T, i.e. one cycle. A strict,
ﬁne time-stepping with ∆t = 0.001 s is chosen, to get a accurate measurement of dV/dt
as deﬁned in Eq. (3.3). The time stepping is based on a backwards diﬀerentiation formula
(BDF) method with 2 as maximum order and 1 as minimum order.
CFD Simulation
A segregated solver is used for the CFD studies 1a to 2b. The solution is split into two
steps; one for calculating the spatial mesh displacement originating from the Moving Mesh
physics, and one for calculating the velocity and pressure originating from the Laminar
Flow physics. A direct MUMPS solver is used for the Moving Mesh, whereas the Laminar
Flow is solved with a PARDISO solver.
The time stepping is free based on the same BDF method as in the movement simu-
lation. The study is likewise transient and solves from t = 0 s to t = T · ncycles, where
ncycles = 4 based on the veriﬁcation in Section 4.7.4.

4.6
LiveLink for MATLAB
Livelink for MATLAB can be used to extend the COMSOL modeling environment. The
COMSOL graphical user interface (GUI) facilitate the build up of a model in a very
intuitive and user-friendly way. The functionality parametric sweep makes it possible to
solve a sequence of models where a single parameter changes value within a speciﬁed
range, without having to change the parameter and solve the study manually. Some types
of variations across models are not incorporated in the GUI, such as changing the boundary
condition type. This can be done when handling the model with LiveLink for MATLAB,
where the ﬂexibility increases, and a host of opportunities for model building, solving, and
post-processing arises. In this thesis, LiveLink for MATLAB is used as described in the
following.
4.6.1
Creating Multiple Models
A template movement model is build up in the COMSOL GUI and is saved as an .mph
ﬁle.
This ﬁle is loaded into MATLAB and is extended with ﬂow physics, solver and
mesh by scripting. Four models are generated with diﬀerent geometry and inlet boundary
conditions. These models are saved as .mph ﬁles through the LiveLink. One script creates
all four ﬁles within a loop, which are saved and afterwards send to a cluster for solving.
See Appendix A for the MATLAB code.
The advantage of this approach is that you easily and quickly can change parameters
as mesh size, solver type, etc. for all simulations without opening them and manually
changing it in the GUI. Another strength of the scripting approach is that there is no
doubt about what has been changed. Mistakes will more frequently occur when manually
adjusting the parameters in the GUI, where clicking around can introduce errors.
The scripting way of building up the model is especially smart when you are comfortable
with your model and exactly know how it should be. The procedure is not recommended
when you are in the early and exploring phase, where the GUI helps you keep a better
overview.
4.6.2
Plotting across Simulations
The solved .mph ﬁles can be post-processed in either the GUI or by the LiveLink for
MATLAB. Again, the GUI is useful to investigate the solution and to customize a plot
group with the desired plotting types, view, color range, etc. The code, representing exactly
this plot group is then exported, and LiveLink is used to make the same plot group across
simulations. The plotting code is saved as a function, and a script runs a loop over the
four simulations and calls the plot function which generates the ﬁgures. This is a very
eﬃcient way of plotting when having multiple ﬁles. The .mph ﬁles containing the plots do
not even have to be saved, hence only the solved .mph’s, and the plotting script is needed
for generating and exporting ﬁgures.
4.6.3
External MATLAB Function (The λ2-method)
The LiveLink can also be used to call an external MATLAB function from within the model,
for instances when working in the GUI. This allows you to implement functions which are
not supported in the COMSOL alone. An external MATLAB function is developed in this
thesis for implementing the λ2-method presented in Section 2.2.4. The external MATLAB
function is used in the post-processing step, for visualization of vortex formation.

The λ2-method depends on the possibility to calculate eigenvalues of the tensor S2+Ω2,

4.7.2
Geometry Shape Order
The volume of the geometry is a decisive factor in obtaining a correct temporal inlet
velocity. The geometry shape order has a relatively large impact on the domain volume.
The start volume when using a quadratic geometry shape order is 102.4 ml whereas a linear
geometry shape order gives rise to a start volume, which is 1.3% smaller (101.1 ml). Since
the true volume of the phantom is unknown, a cylinder with a known volume, is used to
decide the shape order. It turns out that a linear geometry shape order gives a volume
which is 2% smaller than the ground truth. A quadratic shape order gives rise to a volume
which is less than 0.1% from the ground truth, so there is no need to use an even higher
shape order. Based on this investigation, a quadratic geometry shape order is used for all
the simulations.

high, will be found in the following mesh convergence study.

In simulation 1b, a full ring is seen at t = 0.2 s, which propagates down through cavity,
and begins to break down in start systole (t = 0.5 s).
A full formed ring is not seen
in simulation 1a; hence this simulation deviates from the ﬂow patterns seen in literature
[25]. The ring formation in simulation 2a seems to stick to the vortex structure inside the
extending tube for t = 0.2 s and t = 0.3 s. Later on, is the ring detached and has an
irregular shape.
5.4.1
The λ2-method
As mentioned in Section 2.2.4 the λ2-method search for a local pressure minimum. The
local pressure distributions for the three simulations at four times are plotted for the mid-
plane surface in Figure 5.10 together with the contour of the λ2-detected vortex formation.
A local pressure minimum exists in the center of the vortex ring. This veriﬁes the imple-
mentation of the method. Pressure isosurfaces can detect the vortex ring as in [11], but
the value has to be adjusted for each simulation and each time. This is seen in Figure 5.10
where the color inside the black contour diﬀers across both time and simulations. The
λ2-method is more robust since the same parameter can be used to visualize the vortex
formation across simulations and time, even in systole where a signiﬁcant pressure increase
is seen, the λ2-method maintains the ability to detect the vortex. This indicates that the
hypothesis presented in Section 1.2 is correct; the λ2-method is more robust to vortex
detection than using pressure isosurfaces.

5.5
Validation - Comparison with Literature
The overall ﬂow structure is in the following compared with the results obtained by Schenkel
et al. in [25], which are in reasonable agreement with in vivo measurements. In [25] a 3D
patient-speciﬁc pumping ventricle was simulated based on the geometry-prescribed method.
The geometry was reconstructed from magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) technology. A
pressure boundary condition was used for the inlet where a 2D planar model represented
the mitral valve.
Quantiﬁcation of the vortex formation in [25] has been performed for easier comparison
with the results in this thesis. The metric used for comparison is the vortex depth (VD),
which represent the vertical position of the vortex center relative to the LV long axis,
see Figure 2.3c. The VD is manually estimated by measuring the lengths in the presented
ﬁgures at three diﬀerent times during diastole. In general, it is seen from Table 5.3 that the
value of VD is notably lower in the simulations made in this thesis, compared to [25]. The
VD estimated from the simulation 2b is closest to values in [25], whereas the simulation
1a is furthest. As mentioned in Section 3.5 the geometry in the simulations 1a and 1b is
though more suitable for comparing with literature, whereas the geometry in simulation 2a
and 2b reﬂects the phantom. Therefore simulation 1b has a geometry which, to a certain
extent reﬂects the true heart and a vortex depth, which is closer to [25] than 1a.
A major reason for the deviation between this study and [25] is found when looking at
the metric ESV/EDV, which quantify the maximal volume change. A high value means
a small volume change, and vice versa a lower value represents a bigger volume change.
Due to the simpliﬁed movement in the simulations 1a to 2b (described in Section 4.2) is
ESV/EDV very high (0.9) compared to [25] (0.6). The simulated ﬂow in this thesis has a
lower inlet velocity, a less penetrating inﬂow jet, and a shallow vortex ring. When the inlet
area decreases as in simulation 2a and 2b, the inlet velocity will increase, and the vortex
ring will appear deeper in the LV cavity.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The ﬁrst part of this chapter provides a discussion of the results presented in the previous
chapter. Subsequently, the limitations of the model are presented which lead to suggestions
for future work.
6.1
Results Interpretation
In general, very complex ﬂow patterns arise when simulating a pulsating ﬂow into a com-
plicated and moving geometry. Their origin and eﬀects on the ﬂuid ﬂow are, therefore,
the foundation of discussion. The focus will be on the eﬀects of the inlet BC’s, but other
relevant topics are also considered.
6.1.1
Spatial Velocity Proﬁles
Sharp local variations in the spatial velocity proﬁles are seen for all the simulations. Two
vortex structures in Figure 5.3e give rise to a reversed ﬂow direction on each side of the jet.
Most likely, this happens because of the vortex ring formation, where the peripheral layers
of the inﬂow jet spin away and generate the vortex structure, as described in Section 2.2.3.
The velocity proﬁle is similar to the one, which arises at boundary layer separation, where
the ﬂow reverses due to an adverse pressure gradient (see Figure 2.3a). It is known from
theory, that this can happen when the geometry expands as in a diﬀuser, which also is
the case here.
It is therefore hard to distinguish these two phenomena and they will
undoubtedly interact leading to the proﬁles seen in Figure 5.3.
However, the reversed ﬂow direction is seen in all four simulations but happens at
diﬀerent times and positions due to the diﬀerent jet velocities and jet widths. It is clear
that the velocity proﬁle at the cut line is highly aﬀected by the inlet boundary condition.
6.1.2
Velocity Magnitude
The velocity ﬂow patterns at the mid-plane in Figure 5.4 are highly aﬀected by the inlet
BC for the main geometry. The inﬂow jet in simulation 1a does not penetrate far through
the cavity; hence the velocities are almost zero in the apex during the whole diastole. For
the simulation 1b, the jet penetrates further, leading to a swirling ﬂow behavior in the
apex at late diastole. The overall ﬂow patterns of simulation 2a and 2b are not aﬀected
by the inlet BC which is seen in Figure 5.5. An apical washout, as in the physiological
heart, is not seen in any of the simulations. This phenomenon should therefore not be
expected when later measuring on the phantom, under the assumption of implementation
of the BC’s applied in the simulations.

6.2. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
When handling inlet BC’s it is important to take into account what is happening later
in the spatial domain. As shown in Figure 5.6 where simulation 2a and 2b are compared
with tube-simulations, the eﬀect of a parabolic inﬂow BC almost vanishes through the
tube. Contrary to the main geometry, the overall velocity ﬂow patterns in the LV cavity
of the extended domain are therefore only to a lesser extent aﬀected by the inlet BC.
This underlines the complexity to determine appropriate BC’s since it highly depends on
simulated geometry.
6.1.3
Pressure, Vortex Formation, and LV Geometry
The pressure distributions at the mid-surface deviate between simulation 1a and 1b, seen in
Figure 5.7. This is due to the vortex formation, which dominates the local pressure ﬁeld.
Since the vortex ring is formed at diﬀerent depths in the two simulations, the pressure
distributions are also deviating from each other.
From the isosurface of λ2 in Figure 5.9, it is clear that the vortex depth and shape also
depend on the inlet BC. Since the inﬂows diﬀer, the vortex formation occurs at diﬀerent
places in the geometry. It is not possible to distinguish whether the vortex shape depends
on the jet proﬁle or the diﬀerent geometry surrounding it. Figure 5.11 indicates that the
narrow domain precludes the formation of a full vortex ring; this is though an eﬀect of the
spatial inlet proﬁle.
The missing similarity between the phantom geometry and reconstructed physiological
geometries seen in literature, together with the very simpliﬁed movement, complicates
comparison with published studies.
6.2
Limitations and Future Work
When compared to the phantom, the main limitations of the CFD simulation is
• The suspense about the geometry, which is extracted from the .stl ﬁle provided by
SHELLEY Medical Imaging Technologies. It is therefore recommended to extract the
LV geometry directly from a CT-scan of the phantom. With this, the uncertainty
about dimension and shape could be omitted and the exact geometry of the tubes
could be extracted as well.
• The solutions’ dependency on the mesh size. It was found in the mesh convergence
study that the used solution with a normal mesh size had a relatively high deviation
from the solution with a ﬁner mesh. This should be evaluated against the intended
use when this is established.
• The assumption; that the viscosity of blood can be used when simulating the blood
mimicking ﬂuid without having a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the solution. This statement
is not veriﬁed.
• The omitted investigation of the eﬀect of geometry shape order in Laminar Flow
physics; As mentioned in Section 4.7.2 it is unknown how the geometry shape order
exactly aﬀects the simulated ﬂow. An investigation of a linear geometry shape order
instead of a quadratic shape order is advised.
The straightforward next step is to validate the simulation against in vitro phantom-
experiments, which demands a repair of the phantom as well as an implementation of the
simulation BC’s on the phantom setup. It turns out that the inlet BC do not aﬀect the
overall ﬂow patterns in the extended geometry; which indicates that these BC’s are less
critical when simulating the phantom.

When starting up patient-speciﬁc simulations, the inlet BC’s are very important and
should be considered in relation to actual conditions in the heart. An investigation of the
sensitivity and robustness of the inlet boundary condition would be valuable. Additionally,
all the simpliﬁcations listed in Section 3.4 should be considered.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
A 3D CFD model of the phantom LV has been constructed and solved in COMSOL Mul-
tiphysics with diﬀerent inlet geometries and inlet boundary conditions. The geometry has
been extended to replicate the actual setup in the LV phantom. It turns out that the
geometry has a major eﬀect on the impact of the boundary conditions on the prediction
of ﬂow patterns within the LV cavity.
The ﬂow patterns in the main geometry (1a and 1b) are very aﬀected of the inlet
boundary condition; A notable longer penetration of the inﬂow jet is seen for a parabolic
inﬂow BC compared to a plug ﬂow. This aﬀects the vortex formation and with this the
overall ﬂow patterns.
The vortex depth at end diastole is 0.23 and 0.43 for plug and
parabolic inﬂow, respectively.
Contrary, the ﬂow patterns in the extended geometry (2a and 2b) are similar in spite
of their diﬀerent inﬂow conditions. The vortex depth at end diastole is 0.52 and 0.53 for
plug and parabolic inﬂow, respectively.
It can, therefore, be concluded that the eﬀect of the spatial inlet BC’s on the ﬂow
pattern depends on a very complex interaction between BC’s, geometry, movement, and
temporal velocity proﬁle.
The temporal change in the domain volume has successfully been coupled to the temporal
inlet velocity, which hereby is volume prescribed.
This method enables the ﬂuid ﬂow
through the aortic oriﬁce to be negligible during diastole. This is valuable for future work
in the research group.
A LiveLink for MATLAB has been used to construct and post-process multiple models by
a script-based modeling method. The LiveLink is convenient to change parameters and
compare results across simulations automatically. It is recommended to use the COMSOL
GUI in the startup phase, where the graphical interface helps to overview the model.
The λ2-method for vortex detection has successfully been developed and implemented
in COMSOL Multiphysics which improves the visualization of the results. It has been
conﬁrmed that the λ2-method is more robust for vortex detection than using pressure
isosurfaces.

Original Project Plan
This plan describes the objective and justiﬁcation of my Master’s thesis, which is written
at DTU, department of Health Technology, in the spring semester of 2019 and accounts
for 32.5 ECTS points. The overall tasks for completing the thesis are described. In
addition, it is explained how the overarching learning objectives are met. These are
formulated for a graduate of the MSc Eng programme at DTU. Finally, a time plan for
the project is presented.
Objective
This project will contain an investigation of the impact of diﬀerent boundary conditions
when applied a ﬁnite element (FE) computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) model of the
human left ventricle (LV). Especially the inlet through the mitral annulus will be inves-
tigated. It will be analyzed how the CFD simulated blood ﬂow is inﬂuenced by this. The
robustness of the diﬀerent boundary condition methods will be evaluated. The outlet
boundary condition through the aortic annulus will be examined too, if there is time.
The correctness of the solution will be veriﬁed by a mesh convergence study. If there
is time, experimental work will be carried out to validate the results which will also be
compared to in-vivo data from relevant literature.
The motivation for doing this study is that previous literature concludes that the
boundary conditions have a high impact on the intra-ventricular ﬂow [9]. The issue is not
fully examined hence this study is relevant to obtain knowledge about the robustness as
well as pros and cons for diﬀerent types of boundary conditions. This study is needed as
an input to the ongoing research project in the group, where a patient-speciﬁc geometry
prescribed simulation model is developed.
Data
This project is based on a LV-phantom (Shelley Heart Phantom DHP-1) which exist
in the group. The geometry is already constructed, and this will be the basis for my
simulation which will be made in COMSOL Multiphysics Version 5.3a.
Work tasks
This section presents the main work tasks, which should be performed during the project.
A motivation and a description of how to do every task are written.
COMSOL start-up
To learn the program COMSOL some basic online tutorials will be followed. For further
training a simple simulation of a 3D tube will be made. Moving geometry and diﬀerent
inlet types will be applied to the tube. A practical investigation of the 3D LV phantom
model is performed to learn the actual procedures in COMSOL. Concretely, this means
working with a moving geometry and diﬀerent inlet types.
Simple changes in inlet and outlet
To test the model and to brieﬂy see how the boundary conditions impact the blood ﬂow
some simple changes are applied to the 3D heart simulation. A simple surface will be
used to reduce the number of parameters and computation time.
