Abstract
Street sweeping practices around the world are very diﬀerent and are very location spe-
ciﬁc. They are aﬀected by the characteristics of the location, be it urban or rural, the
political and social systems, the available resources, and the work rules in the country.
The street sweeping practices in the municipality of Copenhagen are consistent with the
general practices of an urban street sweeping system, but are characterized by a speciﬁc
union enforced practice of requiring the driver of the vehicle to be back to the main
depot to take a set of breaks during the day at speciﬁc times, other than the require-
ment to be back to the depot to replenish the water tank of the vehicle when needed.
Based on these speciﬁc practices, the resulting representative model was taken to be
the Capacitated Arc Routing Problem with Intermediate Facilities and Multiple Length
Restrictions (CMARPIF), which is an extension of the Capacitated Arc Routing Prob-
lem with Intermediate Facilities and Length Restriction (CLARPIF), that considers one
single total length restriction. A heuristic approach was designed to solve the resulting
model, and can be summed up by two main phases: a hybrid GRASP phase, which
tries to ﬁnd a set of good feasible initial solutions using the Path Scanning algorithm
procedure adapted from the CARP to the CMARPIF, followed by a post-optimization
improvement phase that tries to improve the best solutions obtained in the GRASP
phase using a route-ﬁrst-cluster-second approach. The algorithm was run on a set of
three instances representing a real-life street network from the city of Copenhagen, tak-
ing into consideration the current workday schedule. The obtained results showed that
the program is robust, consistent and performs well, giving good solutions that are at-
tractive in terms of the planning context of the problem, especially that the program
was able to service the given network in the course of one workday as opposed to the
current weekly schedule adopted by the municipality.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In most cities around the world, local authorities such as municipalities have been re-
sponsible throughout history for the provision of amenity, public health, public safety,
and infrastructure maintenance as part of their duties towards the city and its citizens.
The level at which the local authorities are able to eﬃciently provide these services, and
thus increase public welfare, plays a signiﬁcant role in the global image of the city, and
its authorities, towards its citizens, and towards the rest of the world. The nature of
these activities and their importance vary from a location to another based on many
factors such as climatic conditions, the population density, the level of urbanization,
the size and variety of the road network, the political and economic systems, as well
as common social behaviors. These activities include (but are not restricted to) the
collection and delivery of goods, solid waste collection, snow plowing, winter gritting,
mail distribution, electric network maintenance, and street sweeping.
Street sweeping is a central municipal practice that is constantly required and is
often included as part of the daily municipal operations. Clean streets do not only reﬂect
well on the image of the city and its authorities, but are also vital for the reduction of the
risk on public health associated with street pollution. For example, street sweeping is
considered an eﬃcient mitigation strategy that helps reduce the amount of PM10 concen-
trations accumulated on the streets, which are particulate matter that contribute to air
pollution and create serious health and environmental problems (Blazquez et al., 2012).
Moreover, street cleanliness, environmental care, and infrastructure maintenance are es-
sential aspects for any city to be able to maintain and increase its power of attracting
touristic and economic activities, but are hampered by the positive consequences these

activities have on the per capita income and the urban population density, rendering
the street cleaning services less eﬀective as these increase (Benito-L´opez et al., 2011).
Since the above-mentioned aspects vary based on the characteristics of the city
along with its political and social systems, municipal sweeping practices are very diﬀerent
throughout the world and are very location speciﬁc. For example, sweeping in an urban
setting requires a quasi-daily sweeping of the streets and a careful consideration of
traﬃc regulations and restrictions, as well as parking regulations, while sweeping in a
rural setting requires a less frequent sweeping schedule and may allow the disregard
of traﬃc regulations. Moreover, sweeping operations are governed by social work rules
dictated by the government, syndicates, or unions, where the workday can be limited
to just a few hours during the day and needs to adhere to these timings, as opposed
to allowing the workday to extend into overtime until the whole region is swept. Also,
due to restricted budgets, available resources may be limited and unable to cater for the
totality of the network, hence why some municipalities may opt to tender the sweeping
to external contractors, instead of owning some or the totality of their vehicles, and
hiring the personnel to operate them (Eglese and Murdock, 1991). Yet, regardless of the
local sweeping practices, all sweeping operations share the same objective: to minimize
the total sweeping time and distance traveled, or alternatively, to minimize the total
makespan, that is to make sure that all vehicles have a balanced sweeping time and
distance traveled close to that of other vehicles.
1.1
Street Sweeping in the City of Copenhagen
According to the municipality of Copenhagen, the city owns nearly 45 street sweeping
vehicles to clean approximately 1014 kilometers of public and private roadways, bicycle
lanes, and sidewalks it has to sweep. Since the city has a high rate of bicycles per capita
and acclaims it as the most environmentally friendly mode of transport, a particular
focus is given to providing clean bicycle lanes at all times for its citizens. The city is
divided into three municipal work areas: Copenhagen North, Copenhagen Central, and
Copenhagen South (see Figure 1.1). Each area is mainly serviced by a central depot
where the ﬂeet of sweeping vehicles is located (but can include some smaller depots
servicing a subarea of it). Each area is divided into smaller subareas that are serviced
by diﬀerent teams operating a limited number of sweepers to clean their subarea. The

subarea is then further partitioned by the team into districts for each day of the working
week, where all streets in that district are expected to be swept during their assigned
workday, and drivers are given the freedom to devise their own routes based on best
known practices and alter their routes based on live traﬃc conditions. The schedule of a
typical workday is the product of agreements between the municipality and the workers’
unions, and is required to include a ﬁxed coﬀee break and a ﬁxed lunch break during
the day where the personnel return to the main depot to take them, as per union rules.

A sweeping vehicle (or sweeper) is mainly deﬁned by two cleaning functions: a
sweeping function that consists of collecting the dirt, debris, and waste from the street
into a collection tank of a given capacity, and a watering function that consists of
spraying water on the street from a water tank of a given capacity to increase the

eﬃciency of the collection function, and thus, the cleaning. The ﬂeet of vehicles at each
depot is heterogeneous, that is there exists diﬀerent models of vehicles with diﬀerent

The sweeping operation during a day goes as follows. All vehicles ﬁll their water
tank in the morning and leave the central depot at the same time at 7:00 with an
empty collection tank and a full water tank. The sweeper travels to its daily subarea
to commence its sweeping, following traﬃc rules. The sweeper services the streets in
the subarea until either the water tank is empty, or the collection tank is full, or it is
time for one of the two breaks. The sweeper returns to the central depot if any of those
cases occur. It is common practice that the sweeper empties its collection tank or reﬁlls
with water often when it goes back to the depot during the day, even if the tanks have
not reached their full/empty capacities. The sweeper stays in operation until 14:00 on
Monday and Tuesday, and until 13:00 from Wednesday to Friday, which corresponds
to the time it returns to the depot, empties its collection tank, and cleans the vehicle.
The speed of the sweeper when servicing a street segment is much less than the speed
of traversal and is on average around 5 km/hour, while the speed of the sweeper when
deadheading a street segment (i.e. traversing it without servicing it) is equivalent to the
free-ﬂow driving speed on the street segment.

1.2
Thesis Motivation
Like any other public entity, and due to restrictive public budgets, the aim of the munic-
ipality of Copenhagen is to reduce the total cost of its sweeping operations by running a
very eﬀective and eﬃcient service using the limited resources it owns, all while maintain-
ing a certain level of service that keeps citizens satisﬁed and workers content with the
amount and pressure of the work they have to do within a workday. Yet, since the di-
vision of the three areas into subareas by the management is done based on best known
practices as well as the districting of the subareas by the teams, and the daily route
planning is done manually by the drivers and varies from day to day based on diﬀer-
ent factors such as current traﬃc conditions, the subsequent routing schedules are most
probably not optimal and therefore the sweeping operation is far from being eﬃcient
and cost reductive. Therefore, the motivation behind this thesis is:
”How can the speciﬁc Street Sweeping Problem (SSP) for the city of Copen-
hagen be generalized and modeled using mathematical integer programming,
and solved using heuristic approaches, in the aim of producing improved solu-
tions and subsequent routing schedules that minimize the total routing cost?”
Since the ﬂeet of sweeping vehicles is a capacitated ﬂeet that collects dirt (which
can be perceived as a demand along the street segments) and sprays water (which can be
perceived as a supply or delivery made along the street segments), the SSP falls under
the umbrella of Capacitated Arc Routing Problems (CARP), yet the general case of the
CARP cannot be applied in this case due to the fact that we have extra side constraints
on the problem such as a heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet that is double capacitated, and
there exists a restriction on the time available daily for sweeping.
The requirement
that drivers return to the central depot for their two breaks makes it that the sweepers
spend a signiﬁcant amount of their limited operational time deadheading from their
daily district to the depot and back (doing the two-way journey at least three times
per day), a time that could be put into use for sweeping. Therefore, the management
is considering the possibility that the coﬀee and lunch breaks as well as the water tank
reﬁll be done at intermediate facilities within the bigger area, as a mitigation strategy
to try and reduce the total time spent on traveling from and to the depot.
5
==PAGE:20==

Accordingly, the SSP in Copenhagen can be perceived as being a variation of the
Capacitated Arc Routing Problem with Intermediate Facilities and Length Restriction
(CLARPIF). Given a connected and directed graph and a set of required edges that
need to be swept, associated with a traversal cost and demand, a ﬂeet of capacitated
vehicles stationed at the central depot, the vehicles start at the central depot and traverse
the edges on the graph, collecting demands on the required edges, and visit Intermediate
Facilities (IF) to unload. The total demand accumulated between the depot and the ﬁrst
IF, two consecutive IFs, and the last IF and the depot should not exceed the capacity of
the vehicles, and that the path between the ﬁnal IF and the central depot does not collect
any demands and is unconstrained. Moreover, the total length of any route should not
exceed a certain length restriction. The objective of the CLARPIF is to design a least
cost set of vehicle routes that satisﬁes the constraints of the problem.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter presents a literary review of studies related to the topic of this thesis.
Since previous applied academic works tackling the Street Sweeping Problem have been
insightful yet scarce, the chapter will present an academic overview of the Capacitated
Arc Routing Problem and some of its relevant variants in section 2.1 while section 2.2
will present a review of the works on the SSP.
2.1
The Capacitated Arc Routing Problem
The Capacitated Arc Routing Problem (CARP) is the problem of servicing a set of
required edges on an undirected graph using a ﬂeet of vehicles that are constrained by a
certain capacity and are initially located at a central depot. The objective of the CARP
is to minimize the total routing cost, which corresponds to minimizing the total deadhead
cost, that is the cost of all streets traversed without being serviced. The problem was
ﬁrst introduced by Golden and Wong (1981) and has since been extensively studied.
The CARP is an NP-hard problem, as it has been proven by (Golden and Wong, 1981).
Attempts at solving it to optimality have only been successful at solving relatively small
instances and are highly prohibitive in terms of computational time for larger instances.
The main exact methods for the CARP include the branch-and-cut approach (Belenguer
and Benavent, 1994), the cutting plane approach (Belenguer and Benavent, 2003), and
approaches based on the concept of transforming the CARP into an equivalent Node
Routing Problem such as the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP), which is

then solved by state-of-the-art algorithms (Baldacci and Maniezzo, 2006; Longo et al.,
2006).
Therefore, heuristic and metaheuristic approaches have been implemented to at-
tempt to solve the CARP within a reasonable computational time. Since the CARP
consists of two interdependent sub-problems – the assignment problem where required
arcs are divided into subsets and clustered together to be serviced by the same vehi-
cle, and the routing problem to determine the route each vehicle will use to service the
arcs in its cluster (Amberg et al., 2000) – two types of construction heuristics have been
suggested in the literature: parallel simple heuristics that consider both sub-problems si-
multaneously see, e.g., the augment-merge (Golden and Wong, 1981), the path-scanning
(Golden et al., 1983; Pearn, 1989), the construct-strike (Christoﬁdes, 1973; Pearn, 1989),
the parallel-insert (Chapleau, 1984), and the augment-insert heuristics (Pearn, 1991);
and sequential or two-phase construction heuristics, see, e.g., the cluster-ﬁrst route-
second (CFRS) (Benavent et al., 1990) consisting of solving the assignment problem
ﬁrst followed by the routing problem, and the route-ﬁrst cluster-second (RFCS) consist-
ing of creating one giant Rural Postman Problem (RPP) tour and partitioning it into
smaller routes (Ulusoy, 1985).
More recently, metaheuristics have proven to be successful methods to solve the
CARP. The CARPET Tabu Search of Hertz et al. (2000) allows but penalizes solutions
violating vehicle capacity and applies seven improvement procedures – Shorten, Drop,
Add, Paste, Cut, Switch and Postopt (which will be presented in detail in section 2.1.4).
The CARPET found the known-optimal solution for 20 out of the 23 benchmark in-
stances of DeArmon (1981), where the size of these instances varies between 7 and 27
nodes, and 11 to 55 edges, all of which are required. The algorithm also found 17 best
known solutions out of the 34 benchmark instances of Benavent et al. (1992), with an
average deviation from the best solution between 1.13% to 8.10%. The size of the (Be-
navent et al., 1992) instances varies between 4 to 50 nodes and from 34 to 97 edges, all of
which are required. Finally, the CARPET was tested on newly developed 270 instances
that were larger than the previous benchmark instances, varying between 20, 40 and 60
nodes, and 3 to 1,562 required edges, ﬁnding 158 optimal solutions.
Hertz and Mittaz (2001) suggested a Variable Neighborhood Descent (VND) con-
sidering as many neighborhoods to be explored as the minimum number of vehicles

Benavent instances.
Some of the most recent works on the CARP include the Tabu Search of Brand˜ao
and Eglese (2008) which, like the CARPET, allows capacity violating routes, but is
simpler, fully deterministic and twice as fast, and uses relocation moves similar to (La-

to 4 DeArmon instances with average deviations between 3.53% and 7.27%, and 13 for
the Benavent instances with average deviations between 3.28% and 9.27%.

Procedure (GRASP) algorithm with evolutionary path-relinking for the CARP. The con-
struction phases use the PSEU heuristic of (Santos et al., 2009) adapted to include a
restricted candidate list, followed by a local search that allows high quality infeasible so-
lutions and includes moves such as the insertion and reversal of a single or double edges,
the swap and reversal of an edge, and the insertion of a block of edges. The GRASP also
includes a reactive parameter tuning based on the quality of previously obtained solu-
tions, a statistical ﬁlter to discard initial solutions unlikely to improve the best solution,
and an evolutionary path-relinking procedure that acts as an intensiﬁcation strategy and
consisting of continuously relinking (or connecting) a pair of elite solutions previously
produced by the GRASP while improvements are observed on the elite solutions set.
The algorithm found all best-known and optimal solutions of the DeArmon instances
and those of 30 out of 34 Benavent instances, with an average deviation of 0.33%.
For a more detailed overview of the CARP and its variants in terms of exact meth-
ods, lower bounds and heuristics, refer to (Corber´an and Prins, 2010; Dror, 2000; Wøhlk,
2008).

problems that often involve some additional side constraints such as one-way streets,
a heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet, service time restrictions, multiple or mobile depots, or
prohibited U-turns. The following section will look at the CARP applied on Directed
(DCARP) and Mixed (MCARP) graphs, the CARP with Multiple Depots (MD-CARP),

2.1.1
The Directed CARP and the Mixed CARP
When the CARP is applied on an undirected graph, it is referred to as UCARP, yet
real life applications cannot be represented as such, especially in the presence of one-way
streets, U-turn restrictions, and streets whose sides need to be cleaned in parallel. Hence,
most real life applications require the deﬁnition of the CARP on a directed graph (where
each street is represented as one or two arcs in the direction the street will be serviced
in) or a mixed graph (where some streets are represented as directed while others are
undirected).
The DCARP has been mainly studied in the context of municipal refuse and waste
collection.
Mour˜ao and Almeida (2000) applied the DCARP to refuse collection in
Lisbon where bilateral collection is not allowed. They deﬁne a lower bounding procedure
based on the transportation problem and a RFCS heuristic. Maniezzo and Roﬃlli (2008)
presented a transformation of the DCARP into an Asymmetric CVRP in the context
of solid waste collection, and applied three variants of the Variable Neighborhood Search
(VNS) on the resulting Asymmetric CVRP: a multistart local search algorithm, a genetic
algorithm and a data perturbation procedure. Note that since the DCARP was applied
to garbage collection, both papers included extra side constraints non-existent in the
original DCARP where garbage vehicles have to empty their load in one recycle facility
or landﬁll diﬀerent from the main depot.
Belenguer et al. (2006) adapted and applied three UCARP construction heuris-
tics to the MCARP: the augment-merge heuristic (Golden and Wong, 1981), the path-
scanning heuristic (Golden et al., 1983; Pearn, 1989) and the RFCS heuristic (Ulusoy,
1985). The memetic algorithm of (Lacomme et al., 2004) was also adapted to mixed
graphs. The MCARP was also studied by Mour˜ao and Amado (2005), which can be
perceived as a generalization of the DCARP of (Mour˜ao and Almeida, 2000), where the
ﬁrst step consists of transforming the mixed graph into a directed one by choosing a
direction of service for each edge in a way that maintains the balance of in and out
edges on each node. The second step consists of a RFCS heuristic procedure where the

ble DCARP tour) by solving a transportation problem on the unbalanced nodes, which
is then subsequently partitioned into feasible DCARP tours.
2.1.2
The Multi-Depot CARP
The Multi-Depot CARP (MD-CARP) is an extension of the generalized CARP problem
where more than one depot exist in the network. Diﬀerent variations of the MD-CARP
exist in terms of constraints on the initial and ﬁnal locations of the vehicle ﬂeet. The
most common variation is when vehicles are distributed on the diﬀerent depots and
must start and end at their respective assigned depot, but other variations dictate that
vehicles can start and end at any depot, or that they all need to start and end at the same
main depot, but can empty or recharge their capacities at any of a set of intermediate
depots (which will be discussed in sections 2.1.3 and 2.1.4). The vehicle ﬂeet is mostly
assumed to be heterogeneous. The following is an overview of literature works on the
most common variation of the MD-CARP.
Amberg et al. (2000) studied the MD-CARP on an undirected graph with a hetero-
geneous ﬂeet of vehicles distributed at the diﬀerent depots. The heuristic used follows a
RFCS procedure, with the ﬁrst routing phase consisting of computing the giant tour as
a minimal Eulerian graph through a modiﬁed version of the Chinese Postman Problem
matching algorithm (Edmonds and Johnson, 1973) that considers non-required edges.
The Eulerian graph is then transformed into a multiple center arc-constrained Capaci-
tated Minimum Spanning Tree Problem (CMST) with as many root nodes as the number
of depots, each with as many subtrees as the number of vehicles in the depot, and with
added capacity constraints on the sum of node weights (limited by the vehicle capacity)
and arc weights (limited by the maximal allowed total length of a route) of the subtrees.
Since the CMST is NP-hard, the problem is solved using a two-stage heuristic procedure,
starting by computing an initial feasible CMST solution and trying to improve it by a
node exchange improvement based on a local search guided by a metastrategy (simu-
lated annealing or tabu search). The best resulting CMST solution is transposed into
a respective CARP solution and a marginal route improvement procedure is applied to
reduce redundant non-required edges added by the CMST solution and replacing them
by shortest paths between the required edges. The computational results yielded on

time to run.
2.1.3
The CARP with Intermediate Facilities
The CARP with Intermediate Facilities (CARPIF) is a variant of the CARP which
includes a set of intermediate facilities (IF) (possibly including the main depot) where
the vehicles can unload or recharge their capacity along the route. The vehicles hence
start at the main depot, service a set of required edges whose cumulative demand does
not exceed the vehicle capacity, and then empty their load at any IF as many times as
required along the route, before returning back to the main depot at the end of the route.
If the ﬁnal IF in the route is not the main depot, the vehicle takes an unconstrained
shortest path (without demand collection) between the IF and the main depot. The
CARPIF was ﬁrst introduced by Ghiani et al. (2001). Just as the generalized CARP,
the CARPIF is NP-hard and can be perceived as being a speciﬁc case of the CARP,
where a one depot CARPIF can be generalized to the CARP, yet the CARP solution
cannot be considered as a lower bound for the CARPIF.
Ghiani et al. (2001) proposed two lower bounds for the CARPIF: an RPP relaxation
and an integer linear formulation with deadheading variables. Note that the CARP does
not provide a valid lower bound for the CARPIF, while the RPP provides a natural
lower bound for it, but since the RPP in itself is also NP-hard and ignores capacity
constraints and IF locations, the need for a stronger lower bound is necessary. Yet,
since any integer linear formulation of the CARPIF requires an exponential number
of variables and constraints (similarly to the the CARP), a good relaxation based on
(Belenguer and Benavent, 2003) can be formulated in terms of deadheading variables,
deﬁned as the number of times an edge has been traversed minus one. The formulation
includes connectivity constraints, capacity constraints, the standard R-odd inequalities
used in the RPP (stating that if a set of nodes has an odd degree, at least one deadheaded
edge with the remaining set of nodes will be in the CARPIF solution), and backtree and
twin backtree inequalities.
Two construction heuristic procedures were suggested in (Ghiani et al., 2001): an
RPP-based heuristic and a CARP-based heuristic. The RPP-based heuristic is an RFCS
procedure where a giant RPP tour is ﬁrst constructed by computing a shortest tree

to the subset of IFs selected by the CARP algorithm, and the set of edges containing
one required edge for each IF-to-IF chain in the previous CARP solution, as well as
one non-required edge for each shortest path on the CARPIF graph between two nodes

the optimal matching of odd-degree nodes algorithm (Frederickson, 1979), and then a
SHORTEN procedure similar to the one used in the CARPET heuristic. Once a feasible
CARPIF solution is obtained, for each chain of nodes between two IFs, the heuristic
ﬁnds all maximal chains including the IFs chain and made up of exclusively deadheaded
edges, and tries to ﬁnd if any savings would occur by crossing the ﬁrst node in the chain
with the second IF and the last node in the chain with the ﬁrst IF using a shortest path
algorithm. The savings that still yield a feasible CARPIF solution are then applied one
by one from the highest saving to the lowest. The procedure was tested on three bench-
mark instances adapted from the CARP benchmark instances. The results obtained
showed a favoring of the RPP-based heuristic over the CARP-based heuristic, but at
the expense of a signiﬁcant increase in computation times. Moreover, the deviation of
the best solution to the best lower bound ranged between 1.25 to 2.78 on average.

2.1.4
The CARP with Intermediate Facilities and Length Restriction
Ghiani et al. (2004) presented a variation of the CARPIF by introducing a constraint on
the total length restriction on each route, where the length of any route may not exceed
a certain preset bound. Unlike the CARP and the CARPIF, the CARPIF with length
restrictions (CLARPIF) can be infeasible if the total length of a route servicing a single
edge and going from the main depot to any of its nodes, traversing it, going to an IF to
empty the capacity, and returning to the main depot exceeds the length restriction. The
same RPP-based construction heuristic presented in (Ghiani et al., 2001) was adapted
to the CLARPIF by determining all least cost routes in the auxiliary graph that have
a total length less or equal to the length restriction, and then ﬁnding a least cost set of
feasible routes covering all required edges by creating a second auxiliary directed graph
following the same principle described above. The initial solution obtained from the
construction heuristic is further improved using two Tabu Search procedures TS1 and
TS2.
The main procedure in the TS1 algorithm is called SEARCH, where the neighboring
solutions are obtained using diﬀerent move operators. The ﬁrst type of moves are Edge
Transfer moves, consisting of the transfer of a serviced arc from a route segment into
another route segment.
Diﬀerent speeding up strategies are employed in the choice
of the candidate edge: selecting an edge from a route already violating the distance
restriction and whose removal from it would bring the route length closest to the length
restriction, or selecting an edge from a route already violating the capacity restriction
and whose removal would bring the route length closest to the capacity, or selecting
the edge whose removal will decrease the segment length the most. The second type
of moves are IF Insertion moves, where an IF is inserted into a route segment whose
total demand exceeds the capacity, while the third type of moves are IF Ejection moves,
where an IF is removed from a route segment whose demand is less than or equal to
the capacity. The ﬁnal two types of moves are Route Split moves, where a route is split
in two only after the vehicle is back to an IF to empty, and Route Merge moves, which
consists of the merger of two routes. The SEARCH procedure is used on the initial
solution obtained from the construction heuristic as a diversiﬁcation phase, and used a
second time on the best obtained solution in the intensiﬁcation phase by increasing the
number of iterations performed and the number of moves evaluated.

The TS2 procedure is similar to the CARPET algorithm of (Hertz et al., 2000)
and applies the seven moves of CARPET to the CLARPIF. A neighborhood is deﬁned
by considering a serviced edge in a route in the current solution and a second route
with a serviced edge ”close” to the ﬁrst edge, or an empty route. The procedure applies
DROP (removing the edge from the route) and ADD (adding the edge to the second
route) and accepts edges that reduce the solution cost. TS2 also calls the POSTOPT
procedure every certain number of iterations, consisting of PASTE (creating a single
RPP route containing all serviced edges), SWITCH (modifying the order of service of
edges in the tour), CUT (partitioning an RPP solution into feasible CLARPIF routes)
and SHORTEN (replacing deadheaded paths in the solution by shortest paths linking
the end points). The CARPIF benchmark instances were adapted to the CLARPIF
by generating a maximum route length in a way that the number of routes required
is uniformly distributed between 5 and 20.
Results showed that the TS2 procedure
performs better than the TS1 procedure, but at the expense of a much larger running
time in the order of a few hundred of seconds, which is still a reasonable running time.
Polacek et al. (2008) applied a Variable Neighborhood Search (VNS) to the CLARPIF.
They used two construction heuristics to obtain an initial solution: a totally random
route creation algorithm where edges are added until capacity is reached or the length
restriction is reached and a new route is added, and the RFCS construction heuristic
of (Ulusoy, 1985). The next step of the VNS is to generate neighborhoods by shaking
the initial solution using a CROSS-exchange operator, which consists of exchanging two
serviced edge sequences in diﬀerent routes. The two routes are chosen at random and
can include a new route, and the number of serviced edges in the chosen sequence is
uniformly distributed between a minimum and a maximum value.
The ﬁnal step of
the VNS is applying a local search on the neighborhoods obtained from the shaking
phase, by trying to invert the direction of serviced edges and linking it to the route
with a shortest path and accepting cost improving inversions. The resulting solution is
accepted and replaces its incumbent solution if it is better than its incumbent, or if it
is non-improving within a certain threshold deﬁned by a deviation percentage from the
best found solution value. The results showed that the VNS performance was robust
regardless of the quality of the initial solution, and hence the use of the random con-
struction heuristic is justiﬁed for a faster construction phase. Applied to the CARPIF
and CLARPIF instances, the VNS outperformed the results obtained by (Ghiani et al.,

Finally, Ghiani et al. (2010) proposed an ant colony algorithm to solve the CLARPIF.
The algorithm uses an auxiliary multigraph where two anthills are located at each ver-

that can be serviced from the depot and back to the depot without exceeding the total

movement of the ants is guided by the pheromone trails and is done in a probabilistic
way. The results obtained show that the ant colony algorithm performs better than the

2.2
The Street Sweeping Problem
The SSP, while being one of the most fundamental road maintenance problems in the
public sector, has been scarcely studied with signiﬁcant gaps between the works that
directly tackled it, even though it was one of the earliest Arc Routing Problems to be
studied (Wøhlk, 2008).
To the extent of the author’s knowledge, the earliest academic work that studied
the SPP was by Bodin and Kursh (1978, 1979), where they devised a computer-assisted
system to route and schedule sweepers in New York City and Washington DC, by testing
diﬀerent sweeper strategies and studying their eﬀect on the system’s total cost and
eﬀectiveness. The resulting model can be perceived as a mixed graph k-vehicles RPP.
Their approach to the problem coupled the theoretical aspects of the RPP with real-
life practices by including in the model time windows side constraints relating to car
parking regulations and restrictions on each street (ranging from no parking at all times,
to partial parking in oﬀ-peak traﬃc hours, to alternate side of the street parking, or a
combination of these). Two heuristic strategies were used: a CFRS approach where arcs
are divided into k-sub-clusters and then CPP subtours are created from these clusters,
19

and a RFCS approach where a “giant CPP tour” is ﬁrst created and then decomposed
into k-subtours. The CPP in both approaches tries to minimize the total deadheading
time in the tour. The RFCS proved to be the better strategy in terms of deadheading
minimization (from 65 miles to 38 miles when applied on a real-life network in District 33
of New York City) and highly eﬀective in the reduction of the number of vehicles needed,
but, unlike the CFRS, it presented an abundance of overlaps and intersections between
its routes, where commonly accepted municipal practices and requirements dictate that
sweeping routes should be contiguous and non-overlapping.
The second eﬀort at addressing the SSP was by Eglese and Murdock (1991) where
they applied the SSP to the rural area of Lancashire County in north-west England. The
county is divided into districts serviced by a single capacitated sweeper based at a local
depot, and the streets are divided into four types based on the frequency of sweeping they
require annually. The objective of the model is to minimize the deadheading distance
travelled while servicing the streets requiring sweeping and producing sweeping routes
for diﬀerent days of the year in a way that the road sweeping frequencies are respected
over the course of the year. The problem is constrained by the capacity of the sweeper,
where on average the sweeper needs to empty its content twice during its operations
time, and once at the end of its day of operations before returning to the depot, at
one of many tipping sites. Moreover, the Lancashire County Council required that both
sides of the same road be swept in the same day. The fact that the study is conducted in
a rural setting - as opposed to the urban setting in (Bodin and Kursh, 1978, 1979) - does
not impose any parking restrictions and allows U-turns without penalizing them. The
network of arcs is taken to be directed, where one way streets are represented as two-
ways, assuming that the sweeper can go against traﬃc, and dual carriageways requiring
the sweeping of both the nearside and oﬀside curbs are represented as two independent
arcs between the same two nodes. The problem was thus modeled as a Multi-Period
Directed CPP and included all the above constraints. The problem was then solved
using a heuristic approach. The authors deﬁned four subsets of road types (based on
frequency) that were assigned to diﬀerent days of the year, and determined the closest
tipping site to each road, ensuring that the sweeper empties at the nearest one. At
each junction, the algorithm chooses the next arc based on a priority order, starting
with dead-end roads, lowest frequency routes, any route that had its other side already
swept, and lastly the next road on the left. The problem was tested on an instance

being “feasible and eﬃcient”, requiring minimal manual intervention and presented a

Blazquez et al. (2012) studied the street sweeping problem from the perspective of
it being an eﬃcient mitigation strategy to reduce the amount of PM10 concentrations
cumulated on paved streets in the city of Santiago, Chile. The objective of the problem
is to minimize the total time of street sweeping, which includes a mixture of the traveling
time on the length of the street and the service time based on the quantity of PM10
swept from that street. The vehicles are taken to be typical sweeping vehicles equipped
with water tanks and sprayers, and able to clean one side of the street while passing
through it (where a street without a median needs to be swept twice and one with a
median needs to be swept four times). The authors also assume that the daily working
period is suﬃcient to cover all the streets in the study zone, that no parking regulations
or time window constraints exist, and that the problem is a single vehicle uncapacitated
Rural Postman Problem (RPP), where the vehicle does not need to empty its tank or
reﬁll its water tank during the day. The ﬁrst step in the methodology of their algorithm
is to determine the set of critical or high priority PM10 streets in the network based on
an emission factor per street. The second step is to transform the Arc Routing Problem
with the set of critical streets into a Node Routing Problem (NRP) by turning street
segments into nodes and building a directed graph where every street has a corresponding
number of nodes in the graph as much as the number of times it needs to be swept. The
arcs between the nodes in the NRP represent the shortest paths between two streets
in the original network (obtained using Dijkstra’s algorithm in ArcGIS), taking into
consideration traﬃc ﬂow and turn restrictions, and dividing “shortest path” arcs that
include critical streets in the NRP into segments linked to the “critical street” nodes it
passes through, as to minimize the total deadheading on critical streets. The ﬁnal step
of the algorithm is to solve the resulting directed graph as an Asymmetric Travelling
Salesman Problem (ATSP) (since the shortest paths between two streets can be diﬀerent
in both directions based on traﬃc and turn rules constraints). The method employed
in the paper uses a nearest neighbor heuristic as a ﬁrst step, and an insertion heuristic
by evaluation the cost of every insertion for nodes unvisited using the nearest neighbor
heuristic (since there is no guarantee that the ARP-to-NRP transformation yields a
fully connected graph). The algorithm was applied for the northeast area of the city

of Santiago, constituted by 9 one-way streets and 2 two-ways streets, and compared to
TSP results obtained from the ArcGIS TSP solver and the Google TSP solver. The
algorithm yielded a solution that is better by 14% to 37% than the other solvers, but
the fact that the algorithm was no tested on a larger network or on more instances is not
a strong indication that the algorithm suggested fairs better than the other methods.
The latest publication on the SSP were by Cerrone et al. (2014), where they applied
a genetic algorithm heuristic procedure to solve a multi-period scheduling and sweeping
problem for a single sweeper that incorporates scheduling of parking constraints in an
urban setting similarly to (Bodin and Kursh, 1978, 1979). The problem was divided into
two sub-problem: a scheduling problem that decides what street sides are to be swept
on each of two periods corresponding to an even and odd day, and a Directed Rural
Postman Problem (DRPP) to route the sweeper for each of the resulting schedule of the
two days. The objective of the problem is to ﬁnd an optimal two-days schedule given
the parking constraints on each street such that the total deadhead distance travelled by
the sweeper on both days is minimized. A variant of the model also studies the eﬀect of
changing some of the parking restrictions on the objective function. The authors applied
a genetic algorithm heuristic on a population of feasible schedules and use a modiﬁed
greedy version of the heuristic suggested by Christoﬁdes et al. (1986) for the DRPP to
return a tour whose length corresponds to the ﬁtness function. A set of initialization
procedures was implemented to reduce the network of non-required edges to a subset
of those edges included in the shortest path between all nodes connected to the set
of required edges. A further reduction in the state space was conducted by collapsing
every shortest path that include strictly non-required edges into one representative edge
of length corresponding to the total length of the shortest path. The breeding process
consists of ﬁnding good sub-cycles on a random day of the ﬁrst schedule and adjusting
the second schedule to allow for the same sub-cycle on the same day. Three mutation
operators were applied with diﬀerent probabilities: the Stable Vertex Mutation that
balances the ﬂow of edges on an unbalanced random node linked to a required edge as
to reduce the need for deadheading to occur; the Random Path Mutation that ﬁnds a
random path of a random length on a random day and adjusts the schedule as to all
the same path on the alternate day; and ﬁnally the Random Change Mutation that
changes the edge-day for one-way or two-way streets - with diﬀerent probabilities - in
the schedule. The algorithm was tested on randomly generated instances as well as the

3.1
Assumptions
This section will present the assumptions taken in order to simplify the real-life applied
practices that the municipality employs when conducting the street sweeping into notions

3.1.1
Street Network
Since the SSP in Copenhagen is in an urban setting, and especially on constantly busy
bike lanes and streets during rush hours, it is very important that sweeping vehicles
follow the direction of traﬃc as not to disturb the traﬃc ﬂow and create delays for
motorists and bikers. Moreover, U-turns are considered common practices by sweepers
in Copenhagen, where the sweeper transfers to the other side of the street when the
traﬃc lights on the intersection allows for a safe maneuver. Therefore, the resulting
street network assumptions can be summed up as:
• The resulting network forms a directed graph G = (V, A) where V is the set of
nodes in the network and A is the set of arcs in the network.
• It is assumed that when a street requires sweeping, both sides are swept.
• A one-way or a two-way road with bike lanes and/or sidewalk curbs on both sides
is represented as two required arcs of the same length having opposite directions
and starting and ending at the same set of nodes.
• Since U-turns are common practices, when at an intersection, the vehicle is allowed
to maneuver directly to any street from that intersection, including returning on
the street the vehicle just came from. That is, when the vehicle is at a node i ∈V ,
the vehicle can go to any arc (i, j) ∈A that starts at node i.
• The waiting time at traﬃc lights and intersections is not taken into account and
is assumed to be zero.
3.1.2

Some assumptions were taken in relation to the speed of the vehicle, and the costs of
deadheading and servicing streets as follows:

3.1.3
Intermediate Facilities
As it has been mentioned in the introduction, the current situation requires that the
sweepers return to the main depot every time it needs to either replenish the water tank
or take the coﬀee and lunch breaks. This means that if we assume that no water tank
replenishing is required during the day and the vehicle only needs to replenish when it
is back to the main depot at the end of the route, the sweeper is still required to leave
and come back to the depot at least three times per day. If the vehicle is servicing a set
of arcs within a signiﬁcantly far distance from the main depot in the zone of service, the
vehicle will have to use a big part of the total time in operation deadheading from and
to the depot, forming up to 20% of the total daily operation time. Therefore,
• In order to reduce the total deadhead time to and from the main depot, it was
agreed with the management of the municipality of Copenhagen that it could be
possible to identify a set of municipal buildings and facilities that can be used as
intermediate facilities where the sweepers can replenish with water and/or take
their coﬀee and lunch breaks.
• Two types of IFs were deﬁned: the set of water IFs (Iw) where a water tap exists
for water replenishing, and the set of break IFs (Id) where there exists a space for
coﬀee and food consuming purposes.
• If an IF can be used for both purposes, it is included in both sets of IFs.
• The main depot is included in both sets of IFs since it is currently used for both
purposes.
3.1.4
Breaks and Time Restrictions
Moreover, the CLARPIF with a single total length restriction is not suitable to model
the SSP in Copenhagen due to the requirement that sweepers be back at speciﬁc times
to a depot in order to take the coﬀee and lunch breaks. Therefore, some assumptions
were taken in order to adapt the CLARPIF to the SSP in Copenhagen.
• The concept of multiple length restrictions was introduced to the CLARPIF in
order to be able to model the SSP in Copenhagen. Since the objective function

aims at minimizing the total deadhead time, the length restriction can be perceived
as being a time restriction based on the fact that the time of servicing an arc (twij)
and that of deadheading it (tdij) are derived from the length of the arc, and thus
the term time restriction will be used in the rest of the thesis.
• The total time restriction, corresponding to the total time available purely for
sweeping within a workday, is divided into smaller time restrictions each corre-
sponding to a certain break time.
• Given that the breaks are deﬁned as the set b ∈B and there exists |B| breaks that
need to be taken, the number of smaller time restrictions is equal to |B|, and each
time restriction is characterized by an end time eb, and a start time corresponding
to the end time of eb−1 (since the model will not consider the duration of the break
into account and subtract the duration of the breaks from the total workday time
to obtain the total time restriction).
• The end time eb of the ﬁnal break |B| corresponds to the total time restriction.
• For a solution to be feasible, the model requires that all vehicles be back to a break
IF before the end time eb of the next break arrive, and leave the depot no sooner
than that end time. Additionally, all vehicles must be back to the main depot
before the end time of the workday.
• The total workday for a member of a sweeping team is constituted mainly by
the sweeping operation, but also includes other tasks that are essential for an
eﬀective smooth running of operations. The morning tasks could include a team
meeting, the repletion of the water tank (since doing so the night before under
the cold winter weather would result in the water freezing inside the tank), and
some minor vehicular maintenance and repairs. The afternoon tasks include the
washing of the vehicle to clean it, team meetings, and minor or major maintenance
and repairs. These tasks usually take between 30 min to an hour in the morning
and around an hour in the afternoon. Hence, the total time available for sweeping
operation is reduced by an hour and a half to two hours everyday.

3.1.5
Vehicle Fleet and Capacity
In order for the sweeping to be eﬃcient, the vehicle sprays water on the street as it
moves along its length to congeal small dirt particulates into a paste that can be easily
picked up by the sweeper. In that sense, a large quantity of water is required throughout
the day to perform an eﬃcient dirt pick up, especially on hot days where the amount
of water needed per street is higher than in cold days. It has been observed that the
sweepers rarely go back to the main depot to empty the dirt tank during the day, where
the dirt tank rarely ﬁlls up to full capacity before the end of the day. However, the
water tank depletes at least once during the day (depending on the weather and the
amount of dirt present on that day), and the sweeper is required to go back to the
depot to replenish with water at least once per day. Based on these observations, it has
been assumed that the vehicle’s sweeping capacity is only constrained by the water tank
capacity, and that the emptying of the dirt tank is not considered as an active depot
operation during the total sweeping operation time, but rather a task that is performed
before cleaning the vehicle when the vehicle is back to the main depot.
Moreover, it is a current common practice that whenever the sweeper is back to
the main depot, the water tank is replenished before heading out again. Hence, it was
considered that whenever a vehicle arrives to a node that is a water IF, the vehicle stops
at the IF to replenish with water and leave again, especially that the model does not take
into consideration the water replenishment service time at IFs. Lastly, the municipality
owns a number of diﬀerent sweeping vehicles that have speciﬁc characteristics based on
the vehicle brand, its main usage (for motorized, bicycle or all purpose lanes usage),
and its model year.
These characteristics include variant water and dirt tanks size,
where some vehicles can hold more water than others. Based on that fact, the model
will assume a heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet with diﬀerent water tank capacities, and each
route will have a ﬁxed speciﬁc vehicle assigned to it from the start.
With the water tank being the only capacity constraining the problem, the demand
(or rather the water supply requirement) of each street is calculated proportionally to
the length of the corresponding arc segments and is multiplied by a water ﬂow constant
f (deﬁned in Liters of water required per sec of sweeping) as shown in equation (3.3).

on the vehicle until the end of the day without switching with another crew member
during the day. Hence, it can be assumed that the vehicle is not constrained by any
crew swapping operations occurring at any point in the workday, and in consequence,
a vehicle, and its driver, are both available to be used over the whole timespan of the
workday and the sweeping operation. Furthermore, due to team meetings that usually
take place in the morning and at the end of the operations, all vehicles are required to
start their sweeping route from the main depot and end it by return to the main depot
at the end of the day.

Problem Properties
The following section discusses some of the properties speciﬁc to the CMARPIF, pre-
senting two infeasibility checks for CMARPIF instances and discussing lower bounds for
the problem. Since the CMARPIF is an extension of the CLARPIF, the CMARPIF is
also NP-hard (Ghiani et al., 2004).

As mentioned in (Ghiani et al., 2004) and (Ghiani et al., 2010), no strong lower bounding
algorithms and procedures were developed until this day for the general case of the
CLARPIF, and the only lower bounding procedure available is based on an integer
linear formulation of the problem with a single intermediate facility. Therefore, no lower

Chapter 4
Solution Approach
This chapter presents the solution approach suggested to solve the CMARPIF, describing
in detail the structure of the algorithm and the diﬀerent components of the methodology.
4.1
Algorithm Structure
The algorithm developed to solve instances of the CMARPIF is constituted of three
distinct sub-algorithms: the Floyd-Warshall (FW) algorithm, the GRASP (GRASP)
phase, and ﬁnally the Post-Opt Improvement (POI) phase, as can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1.
The Floyd-Warshall algorithm is run on the directed graph G = (V, A) in the
instance in order to compute and store the shortest paths in terms of deadheaded time
between all nodes of the graph. The instance and Floyd-Warshall matrices are then
given to the GRASP that runs a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure. The
GRASP tries to ﬁnd feasible solutions to the problem, and returns a set of best solutions
found. If no feasible solutions are found, the GRASP is run a second time, switching the
order of vehicles considered. If after this second run no feasible solutions are found, the
program returns that no feasible solution was found for the given CMARPIF instance.
A post-opt improvement heuristic algorithm (POI) is then applied on the set of best
feasible solutions from the GRASP, and the best solution found after the POI phase is
returned as the ﬁnal solution. A detailed description of each of the three phases is given
in the following sections.

Since the CMARPIF includes some extra sets (such as the heterogenous ﬂeet of
vehicle, the intermediate facilities, and the breaks) and extra constraints associated with
these sets that are not present in the CARP, the solution space for the CMARPIF is
more constrained. For example, while in the CARP no consideration should be given to
which vehicle is used as they are all homogeneous, choosing a vehicle instead of another
in the CMARPIF has an impact on the number of times the vehicle has to go back to
an intermediate facility to replenish with water. Moreover, a route that may be feasible
in terms of capacity might not be feasible in terms of break constraints, and making
it feasible in order to satisfy the break constraints might make it infeasible in terms of
the total time restriction constraints, and vice verca. Based on these two observations
about the CMARPIF, the algorithm was developed in the aim of trying to explore as
large a part of the solution space as possible with a relatively fast construction heuristic
that is adapted to cater speciﬁcally for the capacity and break constraints since they
are the most constraining elements of the problem. In that spirit, the initial idea was to
apply a GRASP with a local search (LS), but as will be discussed in section 4.3.6, the LS
techniques did not perform well given the structure and the constrained solution space of
the CMARPIF, and hence did not bring any signiﬁcant improvements (if any) as opposed

4.2
Floyd-Warshall Algorithm
The ﬁrst step of the algorithm is to compute and store the shortest paths in terms of
deadhead time between all nodes in the directed graph of the instance. The term STi→j
will be used in the remainder of this thesis to refer to the shortest deadhead path from
node i ∈V to node j ∈V . The purpose of this step is to be able to use information
about the shortest deadheading path between all nodes in the graph at later stages of the
algorithm without having to compute them every time. As will be seen in sections 4.3
and 4.4, information about the shortest serviced path between two nodes, or about the
shortest path formed by a mixture of deadheaded and serviced arcs, are not required, and
hence do not need to be taken into consideration when running the algorithm. For the
purpose of this phase, the Floyd-Warshall algorithm (Floyd, 1962) was used to compute
and obtain the shortest paths.
The CMARPIF instance (mainly the set of Nodes and Arcs) is given as input to
the FW algorithm and the output consists of two matrices:
• The Time Matrix, of size corresponding to |V | × |V |, where timeij corresponds to
the value of the shortest deadhead time from node i to node j
• The Next Matrix, of size corresponding to |V | × |V |, where nextij corresponds to
the next node to visit to reach node j from node i

4.3
GRASP Construction Heuristic
This section presents the GRASP metaheuristic implemented in the construction phase
to obtain CMARPIF solutions, starting by presenting the Path Scanning Algorithm
adopted as the construction method, followed by the GRASP framework.
4.3.1
Path Scanning Construction Heuristic
As discussed in Chapter 2, the CARP can be decomposed into two interdependent
sub-problems: the routing problem and the assignment problem, and most construc-
tion heuristics developed for it fall under two categories: those that consider both
sub-problems simultaneously, and those that consider them sequentially. Similarly, the
CMARPIF can be decomposed into similar sub-problems, where the assignment prob-
lem consists of assigning a set of required arcs to be serviced by each route, and the
routing problem consists of determining the routing of the arcs in each route while re-
specting the CMARPIF constraints. Moreover, the literature on the CARP seems to
suggest that simultaneous construction heuristics are simpler and faster than sequential
ones, especially than those that cluster-ﬁrst-route-second, which are better adapted to
node routing problems than arc routing problems (Amberg et al., 2000). Therefore, in
the spirit of the Path-Scanning Algorithm (PSA) of Golden et al. (1983) for the CARP,
which was also used by Santos et al. (2010), a similar strategy has been developed and
adapted to the CMARPIF.
The PSA for the UCARP consists of constructing feasible CMARPIF routes one at
a time by adding required edges to it one at a time using a certain strategy. The route
starts from the depot and keeps on adding to the route the most attractive unserviced
required edge based on a myopic optimization criterion, linking edges with shortest
paths between them, until the vehicle’s capacity is exhausted or all edges were serviced
(Golden et al., 1983). Five diﬀerent criteria are usually considered:
47
==PAGE:62==

1. Minimum Service Cost/Demand: choose the edge from the current node with
the minimum cost per unit of demand ratio. This strategy maximizes the payoﬀ
by servicing the arc with the most demand at the lowest cost ﬁrst.
2. Maximum Service Cost/Demand: choose the edge from the current node with
the maximum cost per unit of demand ratio. This strategy ensures that expensive
edges are not left towards the end of the algorithm, allowing more ﬂexibility of the
algorithm when the routes are reaching capacity.
3. Minimum Deadhead Distance to Depot: choose the edge with the minimum
distance to the depot. This strategy minimizes the deadhead cost by ensuring
that the vehicle is always the nearest to the depot as possible at all steps of the
algorithm.
4. Maximum Deadhead Distance to Depot: choose the edge with the maximum
distance to the depot. This strategy ensures that edges that are too far from the
depot are serviced early in the solution, allowing more ﬂexibility for the algorithm
when the routes are reaching capacity.
5. Hybrid Criterion: apply strategy (2) when the vehicle occupancy is below half
of its capacity, and strategy (1) when the vehicle occupancy is above half of its
capacity. This strategy allows the service of expensive arcs when the vehicle is
empty, but goes back to maximizing payoﬀwhen the vehicle nears capacity.

4.3.6
Local Search
The second phase of a typical GRASP metaheuristic consists of applying a local search
procedure on the the obtained construction solutions from the ﬁrst phase. Yet, as will
be discussed in this section, the suggested LS did not prove to be a good improvement
strategy and an alternative improvement strategy (the POI phase) was developed in-
stead. The idea behind the suggested LS was to remove a serviced arc (i, j) from a given
route and add it in a position at another route where the cumulative demand between
the two Iw in that segment of the route would still be under or equal to the vehicle
capacity. Algorithm 7 presents the full LS procedure. The LS evaluates the deadhead
time gain/loss by computing two terms in the original route: the gain from the removal
of the arc from its original route by computing the deadhead time in the route between
i and the ﬁrst serviced arc before it (or the MD) and the deadheading time between
j and the ﬁrst serviced arc after it (or the MD) (referred to as CToriginal), as well as
the cost of linking the two serviced arcs/MD before and after it with a shortest path
between them (referred to as SToriginal). In terms of its insertion in the other route, two
elements need to be considered. The ﬁrst element is the gain obtained by the removal
of the current deadhead path between the ﬁrst serviced arc/MD before the position
of insertion p and p, plus the removal of the current deadhead path between the ﬁrst
serviced arc/MD after p and p (referred to as CTnew). The ﬁnal and second element
to be considered in the evaluation is the cost of adding the arc at p, which consists of
the shortest path from the end node of the ﬁrst serviced arc before p and i, as well as
the shortest path between j to the start node of the ﬁrst serviced arc after p. The sum
of these two terms will be referred to as STnew. The evaluation can therefore be done
without moving any arcs by simply computing the best value for all routes, all positions
and all arcs, and choosing the best improving arc. However, the removal and addition

Post-Opt Improvement Heuristic
Since the LS suggested did not prove to be a useful technique in ﬁnding better solutions
of the CMARPIF, a diﬀerent post-optimization improvement strategy was developed
taking into consideration a minimization based on a similar deadhead time evaluation
than that of the LS, but on a more global scale. The Post-Opt Improvement phase (POI)
consists mainly of taking a set of existing routes in the solution, completely destroying
those routes, and rebuilding them using a Route-First-Cluster-Second (RFCS) strategy.
The global algorithm for the POI phase is presented in algorithm 8. The POI takes the

and the most attractive RFCS routes. Note that the consideration of all permutations

be discussed in chapter 5, or else only a subset of those needs to be considered following
a certain adaptive strategy or at random.

istics of a Node and includes also a list of all Edges linked to the Node, which will
be used in order to determine which Nodes have only two Edges linked to them,
and hence can be joined into one representative Edge of a length equal to the sum
of their lengths, in order to avoid the situation where the algorithm considers these
Nodes as intersections while they aren’t.
7. Remove all Nodes that consequently have no edges linked to them anymore and
therefore can be deleted from the network.
8. Once the resulting undirected graph G = (V, E) is corrected for all the previous
inconsistencies, two Arcs are created from each Map Edge, with the same char-
acteristics except in the start and end Nodes, which are opposite in both Arcs,
resulting in the ﬁnal directed graph G = (V, A) that will be used in all three
instances of the problem.
Note that the size of the ﬁnal network is |V | = 1579, |A| = 4606, and |R| = 1750.

Since the GRASP takes around one hour of the total time horizon for the algorithm,
seven hours remain for the POI phase. The run time of the POI phase is highly dependent
on the number of vehicles available in the instance. For example, with the six vehicles
available now, the total number of permutations of locked and non-locked routes possible
is 63, which corresponds to 63 RFCS algorithm runs, and takes around 45 min on average
to run. This allows the program to check 9 diﬀerent best GRASP solutions. However,

the check of 4 GRASP solutions only. Hence, a trade-oﬀexists between the number
of permutations that should be used and the number of GRASP solutions that can be
checked. It has been assumed that the program will favor checking the entirety of the
neighborhood of all permutations over taking more GRASP solutions.

Copenhagen Network Context Analysis
The fact that the algorithm was able to ﬁnd feasible solutions for all three instances
means that for the given area of the city of Copenhagen studied in this thesis, the algo-
rithm was able to ﬁnd a solution that services all required streets in the network within
one workday. This is a major improvement when compared to the current sweeping
schedule where each of the three sub-areas (Tivoli, Christianshavn, and Vestebro) are
serviced independently over the course of the week. Moreover, the introduction of the
intermediate facilities seems to have helped a lot in reducing the deadhead total time,
since if one is to consider the case of the weekly schedule with one main depot and the
two breaks that needs to be taken, it means that each vehicle does a trip to or from the
main depot to its respective sub-area at least six times a day, be it 30 times a week. This
is a very high number of trips from and to the depot per vehicle corresponding to a lot
of deadhead time lost on a weekly basis, which in the case of the CMARPIF is reduced

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis aimed at optimizing the street sweeping practices in the city of Copenhagen
by combining these practices and consolidating them into a representative mathemati-
cal model, which was then solved using a heuristic approach to produce good feasible
solutions to real-life instances from the city of Copenhagen.
The street sweeping problem in Copenhagen is governed by a lot of constraints,
such as a heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet, double capacitated vehicles, and a strict work day
schedule that includes ﬁxed breaks, among others. Some assumptions were taken in
order to include these practices into a representative mathematical model, the Capaci-
tated Arc Routing Problem with Intermediate Facilities and Multiple Length Restrictions
(CMARPIF). The aim of the model is to minimize the total deadhead in all routes, while
respecting a set of constraints, of which are the capacity and break constraints.
Since the CMARPIF is NP-hard like most of its arc routing problems counterparts,
a heuristic approach was developed to solve it. The approach mainly consisted of two
phases: the hybrid GRASP phase that tries to ﬁnd good feasible initial solutions using
the Path Scanning construction heuristic algorithm, while the second phase, the POI,
consists of an improvement heuristic that tries to further improve the best obtained
solutions in the GRASP phase, using a route-ﬁrst-cluster-second strategy.
The model was applied on a real-life street network from the central area of Copen-
hagen between Tivoli, Vesterbro and Christianshavn, and the resulting directed graph
included 4606 arcs of which 1750 are required, and 1579 nodes, including a set of three
purely water intermediate facilities, two both water and break intermediate facilities, and
89
==PAGE:104==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
the main depot. The vehicle ﬂeet included six vehicles of four diﬀerent types varying in
water tank capacity stationed at the main depot, and a seven hour work schedule includ-
ing two breaks. Three instances were generated using the same network and workday,
with a varying water demand per arc.
The algorithm was able to ﬁnd good feasible solutions for all three instances, even
though it did not perform as well on the third instance, which is the most constrained one.
The algorithm proved to be consistent, robust and well performing, and the obtained
results seem to be eﬀective in terms of the planning context of the problem, mainly in
the utilization of deadhead time available. This has proven that considering the given
area of Copenhagen as one section and servicing it by all vehicles available during one
workday is feasible and can be done, as opposed to the current division of the area into
three smaller areas, serviced over a week by manual districting.
8.1
Future Work
8.1.1
Mathematical Model
In light that the CMARPIF was developed by making some assumptions on the real-life
sweeping practices in Copenhagen, these assumptions might not be totally representative
of reality. For example, the speed is considered to be constant and uncongested on arcs
regardless of the time of day. It would be interesting to see what the eﬀect of speed does
on the solution by introducing diﬀerent speeds at diﬀerent time windows corresponding
to the time of day (peak and non-peak hours for example), and changing the time costs of
each arc accordingly. This however brings an extra layer of diﬃculty to the problem, and
the CMARPIF needs to be adjusted to include some kind of time window consideration.
Moreover, the consideration of the free ﬂow speed is not as accurate but necessary in
the absence of real-life GPS speed data on sweeping vehicles. An improvement in the
set of data provided by recording current sweeping speeds on route segments can lead
to determining average speeds that are more realistic, and even divide the day into time
windows as mentioned before based on the clustering of diﬀerent speeds at diﬀerent time
of day into average speeds.
90
==PAGE:105==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
Additionally, the model does not consider waiting time at intersections, which is
a hard component to include in a model, since the model would need to include traﬃc
light schedules at intersections for each direction (i.e. arc that the vehicle can go to
next), and check these schedules versus the current time. This added layer of reality
would be complicated to model.
Finally, since no lower bounds have been discovered for the CMARPIF or the
CLARPIF, a very interesting ﬁeld of study would be to try and ﬁnd lower bounds for
the problem to be able to compare it to the obtained solutions.
8.1.2
Solution Approach Improvements
While the solution approach found good solutions for all three instances, further im-
provements can be introduced in order to try and obtain even better solutions.
The PSA algorithm builds routes one by one, leaving the least/most critical arcs
in terms of the path scanning strategy to the later routes. It would be interesting to
see what eﬀect would building all routes in parallel have on the ﬁnal solution, where the
path scanning strategy shifts from being a global strategy to a route optimizing strategy.
Moreover, further strategies can be developed and considered, such as considering the
demand only or the cost only with rules that ensure the vehicle is near an intermediate
facility when it nears the capacity and/or nears a break time. The GRASP algorithm
can be tested therefore for these diﬀerent combinations, and the best set of strategies is
adopted. In fact, it could be beneﬁcial to trade-oﬀincluding the ”minimum distance”
strategies, since they performed worse than the other strategies, for more GRASP it-
erations for the remaining four strategies. Also, dynamically keeping track of the total
deadhead time cost of a solution whenever an arc or path is added in the PSA proce-
dure, and comparing it to the global best solution could improve the running time of
the algorithm by stopping the algorithm from building whole solutions that are known
to be worse than the best. Alternatively, other proven CARP construction heuristics
could be tested and adapated to the CMARPIF.
As for the local search component of the GRASP, and since the developed move
of an arc from one route to the other failed to improve solutions, diﬀerent local search
techniques could be studied, such as exchanging two required arcs or a set of arcs in
91
==PAGE:106==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
two routes, or destroying a number of sections of routes between two water or break
intermediate facilities that have high deadhead time, and rebuilding these sections from
the extracted required arcs.
Finally, in terms of the Post-Opt Improvement phase, as it has been mentioned,
considering all permutations of locked and non-locked routes might be too exhaustive in
terms of running time, and therefore an adaptive strategy could be developed to consider
a subset of these permutations within the time limit for that phase. Such strategies
could be moving the focus to a more vehicle-based approach, where the best routes for
each vehicle are saved from both the GRASP phase and previous POI iterations, and
combined together whenever possible.

A.1
Instance Structure
An Arc is deﬁned by the following components: a unique id, a start Node, an end Node
and a boolean Type that is equal to true if it is required for sweeping, false otherwise. It
also has the following constants associated with it: the water ﬂow constant (in L/s), a
length (in m), a water demand (in L), the sweeping speed constant (in km/h), a free-ﬂow
sweeping speed (in km/h), a sweeping service time (in s) and a deadhead traversal time
(in s). Note that the demand, the service times, and the deadhead times are calculated
as shown in chapter 3. Finally, the arc has two variables: a boolean ”Serviced” that is
true if the arc is required and has already been serviced in the solution, false otherwise,
and an integer variable ”Deadheaded” that corresponds to the number of times the arc
was deadheaded in the solution.
A Node is deﬁned by the following components: a unique id, an x coordinate, a y
coordinate, and a boolean indicating whether the node is a depot or not. Additionally,
each Node includes two lists of arcs: the ﬁrst list, InArcs, includes all arcs that end in
the Node, and the second list, OutArcs, includes all Arcs that start at the Node. Finally,
each of these two lists has a variable associated with them that counts how many of the
Arcs in that list are required Arcs and have not been serviced yet, based on the value
of the ”Serviced” variable in each Arc. These lists and their variables are at the core of

the route building process in the GRASP phase and are created for that purpose, and
to speed up the process.
A Depot is a sub-class of a Node, where each Depot is an existing Node in the
Nodes list, but has in addition to the components of the Node the following compo-
nents: a depot name and a depot type, which can only only be one of three types
{Water, General, Main}. Water indicates that the Depot can be used as a water IF,
General indicates that the Depot can be used both as a water and break IF, and Main
indicates that the Depot is the main depot. Note that MD will be used in the remainder
of the chapter to refer to the main depot.
A Vehicle is deﬁned by a unique id, a vehicle type and a vehicle water tank capacity
(in L) based on its type.
Finally, A Break is deﬁned by a unique id, a start time (in s from time 0), an end
time (in s from time 0), and a duration (in s).
A.2
Solution Structure
A Route is deﬁned by the following components:
• The Assigned Vehicle from the CMARPIF Instance.
• A list of consecutive Arcs as serviced by the Vehicle.
• A list of boolean Required (of the same size as the list of Arcs) indicating whether
the Arc in the list of Arcs in the same position is serviced (true) or deadheaded
(false).
• A list of cumulative capacity (of the same size as the list of Arcs), increased by
the demand of the Arc when Required is true, and set back to 0 when the next
Arc added in the route has a Depot as an end Node.
• A list of consecutive Nodes visited by the Vehicle (where the ﬁrst Node is preset
to the Main Depot and the remaining Nodes correspond to the end Nodes of the
Arcs in the Arcs list).

• A list of cumulative time (of the same size as the list of Nodes with the time of
the ﬁrst Node preset to 0), incremented by the deadhead time of the added Arc if
Required is false, and with the service time of the added Arc if Required is true.
• A list of integer Break IF Positions that indicates where in the list of Nodes a Break
IF was visited (used by the GRASP and POI phases to reduce the computational
time of the algorithms).
• A list of integer Water IF Positions that indicates where in the list of Nodes a Water
IF was visited (used by the GRASP and POI phases to reduce the computational
time of the algorithms).
Every time an Arc is added into the route, all the lists are updated accordingly.
Finally, Since Arcs and Nodes include a lot of components and are cyclically inter-
related (the start and end Nodes in the Arc are Nodes, and the InArcs and OutArcs
lists are made up of Arcs), it becomes expensive in terms of computational time to make
a deep copy of a Route, and hence of a Solution. Moreover, since the Arcs and Nodes
(which are part of the Instance) include variables used by the solving algorithms, mak-
ing a shallow copy of the Routes and Solution will not save the current state of these
variables and any change to the value of these variables would alter the copied solution.
Therefore, A Route Copy class was deﬁned in order to be able to extract all the informa-
tion required to make a static copy of a Route within a relatively small computational
time, which can be used later to rebuild the Route if needed. The Route Copy consists
of making an exact copy of the Capacity, Required, Time, Break IF Positions, and Time
IF Positions lists, and Assigned Vehicle. As for the Nodes and Arcs, two lists are created
in the Route Copy with the unique ids of the Nodes and the Arcs as they appeared in
the Route. If a Solution is to be rebuilt from the Route Copies, the Instance is reset
(all variables discussed earlier are set to their original value), and the Nodes and Arcs
lists are recreated for each Route by adding the Arc/Node corresponding to its id in the
Route and updating its variables accordingly.
