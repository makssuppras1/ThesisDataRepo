Abstract
The cost of the electrical infrastructure of an oﬀshore wind farm is signiﬁcant, of up to
10% of the overall infrastructure costs. In this master’s thesis a method is proposed to
optimize the electrical collection system with the objective of reducing the levelized cost
of energy (LCOE) of oﬀshore wind farms.
After reviewing current literature a metaheuristic method based on genetic algorithms is
chosen for dealing with the problem. The optimization process is divided in three tools.
First, the wind farm layout generator in which the disposition of the wind turbines, oﬀ-
shore substation and all the possible connections are calculated. Second, an optimization
algorithm which uses genetic algorithms to optimize the collection system costs by taking
into consideration the capacity of the cables and not allowing cable crossings. Finally, a
power ﬂow simulation is performed to calculate the losses and LCOE of the given solution.
The method is benchmarked against other heuristic methods and the results show that
the method proposed is an eﬀective tool at ﬁnding feasible solutions in reasonable time
and thus, reducing the LCOE of oﬀshore wind farms.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Structure of thesis
The structure of the master’s thesis is divided in four chapters and it is based on the
IMRAD Format (Introduction, Methods, Research and Discussion). The chapters are the
following: Introduction, Methods, Case Studies and Conclusion.
The thesis starts with an introduction chapter in which the reasoning for solving the prob-
lem, as well as its extent, are described. The chapter starts with a section dealing with the
nature of the problem. Next an overview of the current literature related to the topic is
presented. This is followed by an introduction to the approach used to solve the problem
and the reasoning behind this selection. The introduction section also contains a brief
explanation of the objective of the problem as well as the assumptions and limitations of
the model.
The second part of the thesis deals with an explanation and thorough description of the
methods used for solving the problem. This includes descriptions and ﬂowcharts of the
three main sub-tools of the optimization tool: the layout generator, the optimization solver
and the power ﬂow analysis.
Thirdly, a selection of case studies is showcased. Each case study represents a comparison
of solutions between the metaheuristic method proposed in this work and a selection of
heuristic methods. A collection of ﬁgures and tables compares each of the results and is
followed by a thorough analysis.
To wrap up, a conclusion summary and future work section are presented in the conclusion
section.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.2
Nature of the problem
Since the ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm was commissioned in 1991 in Denmark, development has
grown quickly. From 2007 to 2017 the global installed capacity has increased by a factor
of nineteen, adding up for a total of 19 GW in 2017 [1], a trend which can be partially
seen in Figure 1.1. One of the main sources for this growth has been a signiﬁcant decrease
on the cost of energy [2].
Figure 1.1: Global annual installed capacity and operating capacity of oﬀshore wind,
2001-2015 [2]
Even though the decrease on the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) has been signiﬁcant, the
technology is still far from other competitors such as solar PV or onshore wind. According
to the EIA, oﬀshore wind has an LCOE of 138 $/MWh while solar PV and onshore wind
have costs of 63.2 $/MWh and 59.1 $/MWh respectively. [3]
The breakdown of the LCOE for a typical oﬀshore wind farm has evolved in such a way
that the CAPEX has taken a bigger importance. The electrical infrastructure accounts
for 8.6% of the CAPEX of the typical oﬀshore wind farm project, as seen in Figure 1.2
[4]. The electrical connection components in 2015 were at around 5% of the LCOE while
in 2001 they were 3%. The increase is partially due to the reduction of the overall LCOE
but there is a slight increase due to several reasons. These include development of oﬀshore
wind farms in deeper waters, higher commodity prices, reduction of supply chain compe-
tition and an overall increase of demand for common supply. [5]

Diﬀerent types of cables are available in the market, each with diﬀerent mechanical and
electrical properties as well as costs. Relevant properties include the capacity and the
electrical resistance of each cable. Thus, it is important to optimize the cable sizing in
terms of both capital costs as well as the losses incurred by the electrical characteristics.
Early oﬀshore wind farms presented grid-like patterns for the distribution of wind turbines
to harness as much wind energy as possible, which meant that ﬁnding a feasible inter-array
cable layout was not very complicated. In recent years, thanks to the advancement of op-
timization techniques that consider other factors in the wind resource assessment phase,
e.g. wake losses, there is a trend to create more irregular distributions of wind turbines.
This is exempliﬁed in the Horns Rev Field, shown in Figure 1.3. In this Figure three
diﬀerent wind farm layouts can be observed, each wind farm was developed in diﬀerent
years; Horns Rev 1 in 2002, Horns Rev 2 in 2008, and Horns Rev 3 is currently under
development. We can see that as time has passed the distribution of the wind turbines
has become more dispersed and irregular.

Based on the previous details it could be deduced that there is a margin for reducing the
costs of the electrical collection system of oﬀshore wind. Due to the increased complexity
of the disposition of the wind turbines, optimizing the inter-array cables of the oﬀshore
wind farm shows potential for helping lowering the capital expenditure of the project.
Moreover, targeting the losses in the optimization problem could potentially ﬁnd an opti-
mum point between minimizing losses and capital expenses. To achieve this, the problem
has to optimize the physical topology of the grid as well as the sizing of the cables. If
done so correctly the LCOE of the project should be improved.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.3
Scope
The scope of the thesis is to explore and develop an optimization method that reduces the
part of the LCOE related to the internal array of cables of an oﬀshore wind farm. This
optimization method must serve as an eﬀective tool, be ﬂexible and easy to implement.
Due to the nature of the present work, a master’s thesis, a factor that must be taken
into consideration is the time required to undertake the approach. Implementing diﬀerent
methods is time consuming and as such, only one optimization method is to be developed
and implemented.
1.4
Literature review
Literature dealing with grid optimization on oﬀshore wind farms is currently very active.
The number of publications has risen in the past few years, in correlation to the commer-
cial expansion of oﬀshore wind energy. Numerous techniques have been proposed to deal
with the optimization problem. Each author chooses a way to formulate the problem and
then proposes a method or a combination of methods to solve it.
The optimization of the collection system problem, or more speciﬁcally the inter-array
cable routing optimization, can be formulated in a variety of ways depending on the scope
of the work. The most basic way to do this is to connect the wind turbines to a single sub-
station, for this purpose one of the most explored problem formulations is the minimum
spanning tree (MST). As the minimum spanning tree only warrants connectivity between
all the elements in the wind farm and does not considers the capacity of the of the cables
or other constraints, modiﬁcations of this approach are needed.
A common variant based on this problem is ﬁnding Minimum Steiner Tree Problem, which
adds new nodes along the segments to ﬁnd a shorter path. This is used by [6]. In con-
junction with other methods, this formulation can be used to avoid restricted areas inside
the wind farm, as exempliﬁed in the works of Fischetti [7].
The capacitated minimum spanning tree (CMST) takes into account the capacity of the
cables but this problem is NP-hard and ﬁnding an optimal solution is complicated using
classical methods. The complexity of the problem is analyzed by [8].
Another variant, which has not been used to solve the inter-array cable routing problem
speciﬁcally, but that is applicable, is the hop-constrained minimum spanning tree. [9]
shows that this type of problem is also an NP-hard problem.
Besides the minimum spanning tree, the travelling salesman problem (TSM) can also be
used. This type of formulation is especially useful for looped grid topologies and in the
case of dealing with radial grids a variation of this formulation must be used, the open
travelling salesman problem (OTSM), which is explored in [10].
Regarding the methods for solving the optimization problem, a wide selection is present
in literature, using both using classical and non-classical mathematical methods. In the

area of classical mathematical methods, works can be found dealing with mixed integer
linear programming such as [7] or mixed integer quadratic programming such as [11]. The
selection of non-classical methods is more extensive and include both heuristic and meta-
heuristic methods. Heuristic methods focus mostly on solving the MST problem, i.e. using
the Prim-Dijkstra algorithm. The selection of metaheuristic methods is extensive, includ-
ing genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, swarm intelligence, k-means or ant colony
based. [6] uses k-means for radial clustering on the feeders and then the Dijkstra Algo-
rithm to solve the MST of each cluster.
Due to the complexity of the problem most of the approaches belong to the non-classic
methods as they provide more ﬂexibility for ﬁnding a solution.
Usually, non-classical
methods do not guarantee ﬁnding the global solution but can ﬁnd optimal solutions which
is preferred over an expert-based solution. Among the meta-heuristic methods, genetic
algorithms show potential thanks to their ﬂexibility as shown by [12], [10] and [13].
[13] considers the optimization of the initial investment in the internal array cabling con-
sidering the power ﬂow, multiple substations, diﬀerent types of topologies such as radial
and meshed, and not allowing cable crossings. The method proposed avoids cable cross-
ings by limiting the possible connections between nodes to its neighbors in the adjacency
matrix. This limits the number of variables, greatly reducing the complexity of the prob-
lem, which has the drawback of limiting the search space of the optimization problem.
[10] adds to the optimization the sizing of the cables. The method proposed in this work
takes a very pragmatic approach, based on the open multiple travelling salesman problem
(omTSP): it assigns each wind turbine to one feeder and tries diﬀerent conﬁgurations.
Other relevant literature relates to the cost of diﬀerent components in oﬀshore wind farms,
[5] summarizes the data available in current bibliography showing the importance of the
costs on inter-array cables.
1.5
Objective
From the literature review in Section 1.4 it could be concluded that there is not a meta-
heuristic method that would consider the capacity of the cables and avoid cable crossings
all while contemplating all possible connections between wind turbines. There is also clear
lack of literature on optimizing the LCOE through the losses of the cable system.
The objective of this work is to develop an implementation of a method to optimize the
part of the LCOE that corresponds to the inter-array cables by considering the routing
and the sizing of the cables. This must be done while avoiding cable crossings and in
conjunction with an analysis of the losses caused by the cables. The objective function of
the optimization problem must consider the trenching length of the cables on the seabed
as well the cost of the cables. The objective can be summarized in the following points:
• To implement an appropriate way to formulate the optimization problem.
• To implement an appropriate optimization method according to the scope of the
problem.

• To implement a procedure for analyzing the cable losses.
• To compare the solutions of this new optimization method with others.
1.6
Assumptions
Based on the scope presented and the literature review presented above, several assump-
tions have been made to deal with the optimization problem.
• The wind farm is composed of one oﬀshore substation and a group of wind turbines.
Also, the location of these elements is known.
• The target size of the wind farm is between ﬁfty and sixty wind turbines.
• The wind turbines produce the same active power at each given time.
• The length of the cables is the horizontal trenching length.
• The topology of the grid is radial or star based.
• Each cable has to be sized subject to a given catalogue.
• Cables crossings are not allowed, except for at the endpoints of each segment, that
is where the oﬀshore substation and the wind turbines are located.
1.7
Limitations
The main limitation of the project is the duration of the thesis. Due to time constraints,
it is not possible to scale up the problem for wind farms larger than sixty wind turbines,
which is a problem in terms of computational time in regular computers.

Chapter 2
Methodology
2.1
Method implementation
The optimization tool here presented is divided in further sub-tools, these have been cre-
ated for convenience and modularity of the optimization tool. The three sub-tools of the
developed methodology are: the wind farm layout generator, the optimization algorithm
and the power ﬂow analysis. Each one focuses on one task and communicates to the others
through the import and export of data. The general work ﬂow is depicted in Figure 2.1.
Inside the red dashed line is represented the metaheuristic workﬂow, while the blue dashed
line represents the heuristic workﬂow.
The metaheuristic workﬂow consists on several runs as metaheuristics do not guarantee
the global solution of the problem and are is not deterministic. As such running the meta-
heurisitc method several times could give diﬀerent solutions each time.
The heuristic workﬂow uses the same sub-tools as the metaheuristic workﬂow except for
the optimization algorithm. The heuristic workﬂow is used to benchmark the results given
by the metaheuristic workﬂow. The heuristic algorithm sub-tool can run implementations
of either the Kruskal Algorithm, the Esau-Williams Algorithm or the Vogel’s Approxima-
tion Method and outputs a cable layout solution. The reasoning behind choosing these
methods is explained in Chapter 2.6.
The layout generator serves two purposes. First and foremost, as a tool to create realistic
as well as complex oﬀshore wind farm layouts, the tool places the wind turbines and oﬀ-
shore substation in a coordinate map. Later the tool also calculates the complete graph
between elements using graph theory. The edges from this complete graph will serve as
the variables for the optimization algorithm.
The optimization algorithm imports the complete graph and the coordinate data of the
wind farm and through an iterative process, using genetic algorithms, optimizes the the
cable layout of the wind farm. The output is the solution with the ﬁnal routing and the
types of cables selected.
The power ﬂow analysis imports the solution given by the optimization algorithm or the
heuristic algorithm and runs a series of power ﬂow calculations, measuring the losses of
each of the cables. Using this data it then calculates the economical aspects relevant for
comparison.

Mathematical formulation
Since the scope of the thesis only covers the optimization of a wind farm assigned to a
single oﬀshore substation with a radial or star topology, based on the literature review,
the problem can be formulated as a minimum spanning tree or a open travelling salesman
problem. The classical travelling salesman formulation cannot be considered as it would
create a looped grid using one feeder cable.
The minimum spanning tree is the preferred formulation to tackle this problem due to the
abundance of literature on this formulation. Note that the the open multiple travelling
salesman problem could be explored but literature dealing with the capacity of each seg-
ment is scarce.
To deal with the sizing of the cables a variant of the minimum spanning tree is proposed.
The capacitated minimum spanning tree, previously covered in literature, is subject to an
additional constraint and limits the ﬂow of energy in each branch.
Fischetti [7] proposed a mixed integer linear programming formulation that includes a
constraint for the non-crossing of the cables. The formulation is described in the following
manner:

2.3
Wind farm layout generator
2.3.1
Purpose
The wind farm layout generator is a tool which purpose is twofold. First, to automatize
the process of generating wind farm layouts and secondly, to convert this coordinate-based
data into graph theory which the optimization algorithm accepts. The tool also provides
relevant plots that help visualize the layout as well as the the complete graph using graph
theory.
The wind farm layout consists on the locations on the Cartesian axis based UTM projec-
tion, of both the wind turbines and oﬀshore substation. The tool is prepared to generate
three types of layouts; two semi-automatic, grid based and randomized layouts and one
manual layouts. Grid based layouts represent classical disposition of wind turbines on oﬀ-
shore wind farms, in these wind farms the wind turbines are arranged in rows and columns.
Randomized layouts represent complex distributions of wind turbines, these serve as a test
for the optimization tool considering that future wind farms will move in this direction.
There is also the possibility of adding the coordinates of the OSS and the wind turbines
manually.
The conversion from an UTM based coordinate system to graph theory formulation allows
for the creation of the variables that the optimization algorithm works on. First the wind
turbines and oﬀshore substation are converted into vertices, then all possible connections
between vertices are computed as segments. This represents the complete graph, the seg-
ment string coded into a binary form are the variables for the solver algorithm.

2.4
Optimization algorithm
2.4.1
Purpose
The optimization algorithm is the tool in which the inter-array cable topology is opti-
mized for the wind farm layout previously generated. The optimization method chosen is
an implementation of genetic algorithms which falls into the category of metaheuristics.
Before describing the process it is necessary to explain why this optimization method was
chosen.
Optimization method selection
This problem is NP-hard and can not be solved in polynomial time using classical methods.
As such only heuristic and metaheuristic approaches are considered. Several implemen-
tations of heuristic methods have been explored already for solving the CMST problem,
these include the Kruskal Algorithm, Esau-Williams Algorithm and the Vogels Approx-
imation Method. Adding new constraints to these methods is complicated due to the
nature of heuristics and the optimal addition of new constraints needs to be done from
conception of the algorithm.
Metaheuristics on the other hand, oﬀer more ﬂexibility and in particular, thanks to the
available literature, genetic algorithms show great potential with successful implementa-
tions thanks to its ability to easily add extra constraints.
Genetic algorithms are computerized search and optimization algorithms based on the
mechanics of natural genetics and natural selection. Professor John Holland envisaged
the concept of these algorithms in the mid-sixties. The working principles of genetic al-
gorithms are based on iterating through ﬁtness assessment, selection and breeding, and
population reassembly. The biggest diﬀerence between genetic algorithms and traditional
methods is that genetic algorithms work with a population of solutions instead of just one,
increasing the chances of ﬁnding the global solution. Another big diﬀerence is the use of
probabilities in the operators, meaning that none of them works deterministically forcing
the algorithm to explore more feasible solutions.
The general workﬂow of a genetic algorithm is as follows. First a random initial population
of variables is created. The second step involves the sequencing of the new populations.
This step is composed of the following sub-steps: First the raw ﬁtness scores are obtained
from the current population. Then the raw ﬁtness scores are scaled into a usable range
of values, these are the expectation values. Based on these expectation values the parents
are selected. The individuals with the best ﬁtness values are chosen and passed to the
next generation and new individuals are created via mutation or crossover, these are the
children of the current generation. Finally, the current population is replaced with the
children to form the next generation. The algorithm stops when the termination criteria
is met.
Genetic algorithms have the particular advantage for graph theory-based formulations of
working with a coded string of variables. Meaning that it is relatively fast and simple to
implement the variables which in graph theory can be either the edges or the vertices. In
the case of this problem the coding of the genetic algorithm is binary and the variables
17

are the edges of a complete graph.
The variables chosen will evaluated through the ﬁtness function. Within the ﬁtness func-
tion it is easy to add information about the variables such as length of the edges or the
cost of the cable. By adding penalizations to the ﬁtness function it easy to constrain the
problem for unfeasible solutions.
Overview of the optimization algorithm
The optimization algorithm is the key element of the the proposed optimization method.
I consists on the implementation of a genetic algorithm solve the problem. The algorithm
uses the layout data of the wind farm provided by the wind farm layout generator and a
list of cable data to generate a solution for the problem.
The genetic algorithm uses the complete graph of the wind farm layout as boolean vari-
ables to determine which edges will be active for the ﬁnal solution. The objective function
is assesed via the ﬁtness function of the genetic algorithm, which considers the trenching
length associated to each edges and the type of cable selected for that edge. The cable
selection process is done separately if the candidate solution is a tree.
The constraints of the problem are implemented through the addition of a penalization to
the ﬁtness function, this promotes candidate solutions that violate the least constraints
possible. The constraints the problem is subject to are the following: Total connectivity
of nodes, tree graph, cable capacity and non-crossing cables.
• Total connectivity of nodes: This constraint determines if all nodes are connected
in the graph. Having non connected nodes would imply disconnected elements such
as wind turbines or the oﬀshore substation.
• Tree graph: A tree is deﬁned by having one less active edge than the sum of all
vertices. This constraint forces the solution to have a radial or star topology, as if
the number of edges is equal to the number of vertices the topology would be looped
of meshed.
• Cable capacity: The energy ﬂow limits of the cables can be modeled considering the
maximum capacity of the cables in terms of how many wind turbines are connected
upstream of a sub-tree. The number of wind turbines that can be handled per cable
is calculated beforehand using the maximum allowed current speciﬁed for the cable,
the rated power of the wind turbines and the voltage of the inter-array cable system.
This constraint enforces than no edge has more wind turbines connected upstream
in its sub-tree than the highest capacity cable from the list. The penalization is
proportional to the number of sub-trees that violate this constraint.
• Non-crossing cables: If two or more edges cross each other the constraint penalizes
the ﬁtness function. The penalization is proportional to the number of cable crossings
detected. Cables are allowed to run parallel and to cross at the endpoints of the edges,
where the elements of the wind farm are located. This is particularly important for
grid based wind farm layouts, where some of the edges are colinear and the elements
are aligned.

The constraints are nested, establishing a hierarchy. For calculating the cable capacity
constraint, the graph needs to be a tree, this is only the case if all nodes are connected and
the number of edges is inferior to the number of nodes. Calculating the cable crossings
only makes sense if the solution meets the cable capacity constraint.
The whole algorithm has been made as parametric as possible, this is to allow the algo-
rithm to scale to diﬀerent sizes of wind. For example, the cost of the penalizations depened
on the number of edges of the complete graph given by the wind farm layout generator.
Once the termination criteria of the genetic algorithm is satisﬁed the optimization algo-
rithm outputs a solution accompanied by relevant plots and the data structures necessary
for the power ﬂow analysis tool.

The crossover operator exchanges variables between two parent strings to create a new

was the uniform crossover as it performed better for this type of problem. For each chil-
dren two parents are selected using the roulette wheel selection operator, a random binary

The last used genetic operator is mutation, this operator helps the genetic algorithm avoid
local solutions in hopes of ﬁnding a the global solution. The mutation process consists
on creating new children by assigning a random probability for a set of random variables
to change their value. A predeﬁned number of new individuals according to the mutation
percentage pm will be created using the following method. A random string from the

Modiﬁed Bentley-Ottmann
A variation of the Bentley-Ottmann algorithm is used to ﬁnd possible intersections between
cables. The algorithm is convenient as it helps reducing the time complexity of the prob-
lem compared to analyzing all possible intersections between segments. A modiﬁcation of
the algorithm is implemented as the original is limited to detect certain types of intersec-
tions. The original Bentley-Ottmann algorithm has a time complexity of O((n + k)logn),
whilst the brute force method is O(n2).
The Bentley-Ottmann algorithm is a line sweep technique for ﬁnding intersection among
a set of line segments developed by Jon Bentley and Thomas Ottmann in the late seven-
ties. [14]. The method is implemented in hopes of reducing the time complexity of the
overall algorithm, a traditional brute force search has a time complexity of O(n2) while
the Bentley-Ottmann algorithm has a time complexity of O((n + k)log(n)), where n are
the number of line segments and k crossings. The algorithm has some shortcomings such
as its inability to detect multiple intersections in the same point, co-linear intersections
and intersections with lines that are parallel to the y axis. A variation of the algorithm
is proposed here so that it can operate under the ﬁrst two conditions, as these are quite
common on grid based wind farm layouts.
The workﬂow of the algorithm is shown in Figure 2.12 and can be described as follows.
Let S be a set of line segments for which it is needed whether there are any intersection
between them. A sweep line SL is created parallel to the y axis and moved along the x
axis. During the sweep information about the intersections between S and SL are stored
and removed dynamically in various data structures. Three data structures store all the
relevant information, these are the event queue EQ, the sweep line SL and the intersection
list I. The EQ one stores all reported events, these are, left endpoints, right endpoints
and intersections. The SL stores all active segments that are being seen by the sweep line.
The intersection list I stores all reported intersections.
The algorithm can be set to not report co-linear intersections if parallel or overlapping
cables are to be allowed on the optimization problem.

Cable selection
The cable selection algorithm is a tool that assigns a cable type to each edge of the graph
inside of the ﬁtness assessment of the genetic algorithm. By not treating each type of cable
as variable in the objective function the performance is not aﬀected as much as it would
have been otherwise. The algorithm can only run when all the sub-trees in the graph have
less nodes than the cable with the highest capacity. This value is given as a input to the
genetic algorithm and it is based on the rated power of the wind turbines, the voltage of
the inter-array grid and the maximum current capacity of the cables. Thus, the algorithm
runs only when the cable capacity constraint has been met.
The work ﬂow of the algorithm is depicted in Figure 2.13. The inputs are a list of cable
prices, a list of the capacity in terms of nodes, also wind turbines, and a list of the number
of nodes that can be seen upstream at each node. The ﬁrst step is to check which cables
from the list have a higher capacity Cmax than the capacity of each branch Cbranch. The
prices of the available cables are retrieved from the list and then both the cable and price
with the lowest available capacity are selected, Cselected and Pselected, thus minimizing the
cost of the cable while respecting the capacity of the branch. In order for the ﬁtness as-
sessment to be able to compute this information a matrix containing the complete graph
is needed. This graph lists all possible edges between all nodes. Added to this list is the
information about the cable type in the form of the capacity selected Cselected and the
price selected Pselected.

Purpose
The solution given by the solver only shows the capital expenditure of the cable but it
does not take into account the eﬃciency of the inter-array cable conﬁguration. The Power
Flow analysis uses the solution given by the solver to determine the cable losses of the
given layout using a single time series of power production of a typical wind turbine. It
is then possible to determine the lost revenue due to cable losses and the loss in annual
energy production (AEP), allowing for a more thorough comparison.
The scope of this analysis focuses on the main source of losses, which are the active power
losses incurred by the cables. Other sources of losses such as eﬃciency of equipment and
switch gear are not considered.
Another assumption taken is that the production of the wind turbines is assumed to be the
same at a given time for simplicity. It is also assumed that the power production probabil-
ity distribution for a year is that similar to the probability distribution of the time series.
In this way a large time series can be discretized in terms of power production probability
and that probability distribution can be used to calculate the energy production of the
wind farm for an entire year. By doing so the number of power ﬂow calculations performed
is reduced signiﬁcantly.

2.6
Heuristic methods
The problem of designing minimum-cost collection systems for oﬀshore wind farms, which
connect turbines to a substation accounting with cables ﬁnite capacities is, as mentioned
before, NP-hard. All the existent methodologies for tackling this problem can be grouped
as follows: exact solutions, branch exchange solutions, CMST heuristic solutions and
metaheuristic solutions. Also new methodologies combining two or more of these can be
considered to create hybrids.
Exact solutions includes linear integer programming and branch-bound methods; due to
the lack of ﬂexibility and the exponential growth rate of the computer running time with
respect to the number of turbines, the exact solution type approaches have only limited
practical usefulness. Branch exchange solutions contains heuristics algorithms, which im-
prove the network cost by exchanging lines from diﬀerent subtrees. Heuristics overcome
some of the objections mentioned above and allow obtaining better solutions than exact
methods in a comparable amount of time; the basic concept underlying most of these algo-
rithms is the initialization of turbines in separate components and pairs of components are
then joined by the shortest or the least-cost segment separating them. It has been proved
that algorithms of this form will always generate tree-structured networks of minimum
total length, if they are not restricted in their choice of cuts. In designing oﬀshore wind
farms collection systems, each of these algorithms must consider the constraints during the
course of execution and therefore their choice of cuts is restricted (capacities of cables and
non-crossing constraints). Thus, they do not in general generate the minimal-cost tree
satisfying the constraints. Experience with these algorithms has shown, however, that
they do generate good solutions, within a few percent of the optimum in most cases.
In this work three modiﬁed-heuristic methods have been implemented: Kruskal, Esau-
Williams and Vogel’s Approximation Method.
Initially proposed in [15], Juan-Andr´es
P´erez-R´ua implements in a uniﬁed code these heuristics. They are presented with a time
complexity of N2logN accounting for cables capacities and non-crossing cables constraints;
the basic diﬀerence among those methods is the assignation of weights for all turbines and
thus, the order of selection of segments when forming the tree. The main disadvantage of
these methods are that the objective function selection is limited to the installation costs
of cables and their length instead of sizing them dynamically and the impossibility to
consider power losses as well; additionally the steps are not reversible, so for non-crossing
constraints ﬁnal solutions can be unfeasible. This is the main reason to consider methau-
ristics as they make use of randomness to jump all over the search space avoiding the
saturation into non-feasible solutions in reasonable time.

3.2.2
Random based layout
The second case allows all the heuristic methods to come up with a feasible solution as it
is easier to avoid cable crossings. All the heuristic methods reach the same solution and
the the meta-heuristic methods reach another, being the latter one the best.
In Table 3.7 the results are shown, the total cost diﬀerence between the metaheuristic a
the heuristic solutions is small, less than 1%. The LCOE is practically the same in both
solutions as the lost revenue to losses is slightly lower in the heuristic solution and thus
compensating for the higher CAPEX. The losses are smaller in the heuristic solution due
to the usage of bigger cables. It is seen that the losses represent a small part of the AEP
of the wind farm.
In terms of the layout, the two diﬀerent solutions diﬀer in the northeastern group of wind
turbines. The metaheuristic solution clusters the wind turbines in two sub-trees in groups
of four and ﬁve wind turbines respectively. The heuristic solution bundles the nine wind
turbines in a single sub-tree, requiring a higher capacity cable coming from the OSS. The
other sub-trees starting from the OSS are the same.
Because of the metaheuristic always converges in the same solution, it could indicate that
the metaheuristic solution is indeed the global solution for the problem.

3.3
Large wind farm
This case deals with a proposed layout for the Ronne Bank South oﬀshore wind farm,
from the Baltic InteGrid project and it presents similar results to the ﬁrst case. Both the
Kruskal algorithm and the Vogel’s Approximation Method are unable to provide a feasible
result and in fact, the two provide the same solution. The Esau-Williams algorithm in the
other hand provides a feasible solution, as well as the metaheuristic method.
The metaheuristic solutions obtain the best overall costs, with the solution number three
outperforming the rest, as seen on Table 3.8. Solution number 1 and number 2 fare worse
with diﬀerences in terms of LCOE of 0.3% and 1.71% compared to the best solution.
The Esau-Williams solution is 4.1% higher than the best. At the same time there is a
correlation between how expensive the solution is and the losses associated to it, the most
expensive layouts have higher losses. This is easily explained by the fact that the most
optimized the solution it is, the smaller cables it uses, hence increasing the active power
losses. In terms of LCOE the third solution is best as it can be seen that the losses do
not impact signiﬁcantly the AEP, thus making the CAPEX the main contributing factor.
From the graphs in Figure 3.8 it can be seen that again, the metaheuristic method cluster-
izes in a more eﬃcient manner the wind turbines. The Esau-Williams Algorithm solution
contains up to eleven wind turbines in its sub-tress, which is the highest capacity from the
cable list. While on the metaheuristic solutions it is ten. Additionally, the metaheuristic
solutions do not surpass a node depth of six, while the Esau-Williams Algorithm reaches
seven. Because of this, the heuristic uses a broader variety of bigger and more expensive
cables, as seen on Figure 3.7.

Chapter 3. Case studies
3.4
Discussion on the case studies
When comparing the solutions between the diﬀerent case studies a few things become
apparent. These relate to the methods used for solving the problem, the solution routing
layouts and also to the optimization problem itself.
Regarding the performance of the algorithms in terms of the layout, it is worth to note the
following. The ﬁrst case exempliﬁes a small sized, grid based oﬀshore wind farm. The OSS
is slightly oﬀ-centered to the east, making ﬁnding the global solution not so evident. All
the solutions tend to maximize the number of cables parallel to the axis of the disposition
of the wind turbines, as those are the shortest distances between elements. Especially
the north-south direction in detriment of the west-east component. This is because the
north-south separation between wind turbines is smaller than the east-west separation.
The second case shows a random distribution of wind turbines. As the wind turbines are
no longer aligned, it is easier to ﬁnd a solution without cables crossing.
The last case is based on a proposal for an oﬀshore wind farm part of the project Baltic
Intergrid. This wind farm includes a large number of wind turbines with a grid disposition.
Again all methods favor connections in the north-south axis as they are the shortest. This
case represents the biggest challenge of all. Eﬃcient clustering the wind turbines to mini-
mize the number of branches from the OSS, while avoiding the cable crossings, is essential.
In terms of the results, it is clear that in all of the cases the losses are negligible. The
ratio between AEP and active energy losses in the cables are in the range of 0.0013% and
0.0033%. Even considering the lost revenue due to these losses they represent a minuscule
part of the sum of the annualized CAPEX and OPEX, at between 0.17% and 0.20%.
In comparison with the heuristic methods, the metaheuristic method always improves the
solution given by the heuristic methods. While it is true that they have diﬀerent objective
functions, the meta-heuristic method results in being a superior tool in terms of optimizing
the cost of the infrastructure. The heuristic methods fail in some occasions to provide a
feasible solution, as they are not able to cope with the no crossings allowed constraint.
The metaheuristic method allows for these implementations in a more simple manner, and
although it hinders its performance, solutions were provided in all cases. As such, new
constraints and changes in the objective function are relatively easy to implement making
the method the most ﬂexible option.
In terms of computational time, the heuristic methods outperform the meta-heuristic.
The heuristic methods have very short computational times for ﬁnding the solution, in
the range of a few seconds, while the metaheuristic method takes hours. Even so more,
when scaling up the problem the the computational time in metaheuristic method in-
creases notably, this is not a problem in the heuristics where the increase is imperceptible.

3.5
Discussion on the metaheuristic method
The metaheuristic method is an iterative process and as such it is worth to analyze its
performance when converging into a solution. The converging times and iterations for
ﬁnding a solution or meeting the corresponding constraints can be found in Tables 3.6,
3.7 and 3.8. In all metaheuristic solutions the iterative process of the genetic algorithm is
terminated due to stall of the ﬁtness values and not by reaching a maximum number of
iterations.
The total time for obtaining a solution does not seem to be too dependent on the dis-
position of the wind turbines. In the small wind farm case two diﬀerent dispositions are
analyzed with the same number of wind turbines. For the grid based disposition the total
times for the three solutions are 1:35, 1:38 and 2:01 hours and minutes respectively. On the
random based disposition the total times are 2:17, 2:07 and 1:46 hours and minutes respec-
tively. As it can be seen the grid disposition obtains a solution slightly faster. This is due
to the time required to ﬁnd a solution without any crossings, which in the grid based is be-
tween two and nine minutes and in the random based is between nine and sixteen minutes.
The big diﬀerence in the total time comes with the increase the number of wind turbines.
In the large wind farm, the times for each of the three solutions are 20, 11 and 16 hours
respectively, representing a big step up from the previous cases. This shows that increase
in computational time by adding more wind turbines is very steep.
In terms of converging for each of the constraints the solutions tend to ﬁt a pattern. The
ﬁrst constraint is always met in the ﬁrst iteration, as there are always a candidate solution
connecting all wind turbines, then genetic algorithm starts removing connections as they
give an improved ﬁtness value until a tree solution is found. The following constraints are
more time consuming. This is due to the complexity of the algorithms used for searching
the trees and checking for cable crossings.
As shown in Figures 3.3, 3.6 and 3.9, when comparing the performance with the iterations
and the time elapsed, it is clear that resolving the constraints, albeit taking a signiﬁcant
amount of iterations, is relatively fast, but improving the solution to levels below those of
the heuristic methods takes most of the time, this is due to two reasons. The ﬁrst being
that checking for cable crossings and for the capacity of the cables are demanding tasks.
The second is that ﬁnding new feasible solutions becomes a complex task as the layout is
mostly deﬁned, and very few combinations of the variables result in better solutions. This
process is largely achieved through mutations in the genetic algorithm, requiring large
pools of mutated individuals.
With regard to the number of iterations and the performance of the ﬁtness value, it was
noticeable that the larger wind farm tended to stall for more iterations. This is because
the more complex the problem is the hardest is to ﬁnd improved solutions. This has im-
plications in the terminations parameters of the genetic algorithm, as they would have to
be scaled according to the size of the wind farm.

Chapter 4
Conclusions
In this work the objective described in Chapter 1.5 has been addressed. An optimization
method has been implemented that reduces the LCOE corresponding to the inter-array
cables that are part of the electrical collection system of oﬀshore wind farms.
After reviewing current literature the appropriate mathematical formulation has been
selected, this is a modiﬁed version of the CMST problem. For solving this problem a
metaheuristic method based on genetic algorithms has been chosen. This method is cho-
sen because of the ﬂexibility it provides and its eﬃciency in dealing with this class of
problems.
The metaheuristic method has been implemented in the optimization algorithm accom-
panied of two other tools that allow the optimization diﬀerent wind farm layouts and the
analysis of the losses in the inter-array cable system.
The method has been implemented and benchmarked in a series of case studies to other
heuristic methods. The case studies demonstrate that the metaheuristic method is an
eﬃcient tool for optimizing the cable layout within reasonable time. It is also proven that
outperforms the heuristic methods that is compared to.
4.1
Future work
The proposed method can be expanded beyond the initial assumptions considered in Chap-
ter 1.6. Some areas of future work are described below:
• Optimization in the genetic algorithm: The GA parameters can be further tweaked
for performance improvements, this includes both the termination and GA operator
parameters.
• Intermediate vertices: Adding intermediate vertices, or nodes that can be used but
do not need to be connected, to the initial graph would ﬂexibility to the optimization
method and it would enable the addition of forbidden zones inside the wind farm
boundaries.
• Include seabed depth information: The trenching length of the cables can be pro-
jected on the seabed to obtain a more realistic distance of cable needed. The vertical
