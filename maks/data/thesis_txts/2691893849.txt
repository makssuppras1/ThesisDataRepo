Abstract
Renewable energy sources installed capacity raises in order to reduce carbon dioxide emis-
sions during the electricity production stage.
Oﬀshore wind farms are one of the most
dominant and mature technologies which enhance this aim. The motivation of the current
thesis project is to provide the ﬁrst step of validation of the DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPow-
erLab laboratory in order to conduct further experiments in the oﬀshore wind farms research
area. Four converters are installed and can be utilized in order to simulate the oﬀshore wind
farms conﬁguration under HVDC connection to the shore. The obtained results show that
the converters can be used for that purpose and thus it enables to simulate the so called
Point-to-Point system interconnection. Finally, the results validate the converters expected
operation and consequently additional scenarios can be conducted, such as droop frequency
control, fault ride through etc, in order to meet the strict grid code requirements imposed
by the TSOs.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Nowadays, the penetration of renewable energy sources increases, in order to meet the goal of
zero carbon dioxide emissions. Oﬀshore wind farms seem to be one of the leading technologies
together with photovoltaics.
Especially, in European countries the installed capacity of
renewables has been signiﬁcantly risen. For instance, in Denmark, the total installed capacity
of renewables has gone up from 4916 MW to 9012 MW during the last decade [1]. Oﬀshore
wind farms tends to produce signiﬁcant amounts of active power due to the presence of
high wind speeds.
Traditionally, they were connected to the grid through High Voltage
Alternate Current (HVAC), but the use of High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) system
becomes more and more eligible. The reason for the transition from HVAC to HVDC is
that HVAC imposes some drawbacks, such as harmonics due to low resonance frequencies
of transformers and submarine cables. The harmonics are increased even more due to the
operation of non-linear devices such as converters, making the HVAC topology diﬃcult to
meet the strict grid codes that transmission system operators (TSOs) require. Additionally,
due to the capacitive behavior of cables, as the distance from shore goes up, it is necessary to
compensate the generated reactive power. Therefore, for large distances, the use of HVAC
is limited. In order to overcome, the aforementioned issues, it is possible to connect the
oﬀshore wind farms (WFs) through HVDC networks. The main advantage is that it can be
used for large distance (50 km or even more), due to the inherent absence of reactive power
in DC systems. Additionally, oﬀshore wind farms have less impact to residential areas in
terms of noise, visual pollution and ﬂickering [2]. The main topology of HVDC connected
wind farm is depicted in Figure 1 together with a typical Single Line Diagram (SLD) in
Figure 2.
Figure 1: Oﬀshore Wind Farm Connected Through HVDC Network [3].

The topology is consisted of the wind turbines (WTs), which are connected to the oﬀ-
shore substation by means of submarine cables. Each WT has its own step-up transformer,
meaning that the voltage increases from 690V to 33kV or 66kV. Next, at the oﬀshore AC
substation the High Voltage (HV) transformer raises the voltage even more (usually 150kV)
and is driven to the oﬀshore HVDC converter station, where the voltage is converted from
AC to DC. After that, the power is delivered to the shore through HVDC submarine cables
±320kV and ﬁnally reaches the shore. At shore, the voltage is converted back to HVAC in
order for the system to be connected to the main grid.
Figure 2: Typical SLD of Oﬀshore Wind Farm Connected Through HVDC Network [4].
1.2
Thesis Objectives
The scope of this thesis is the development of average VSCs models and their control sys-
tems in Simulink© software. In order to validate them, real-time simulation scenarios are
conducted using the DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab laboratory facilities. The com-
missioning of the laboratory converters (two-level and Modular Multilevel (MMC)) and
the control systems design have been performed by Tecnologies de Control de l’Electricitat
i Automatització (teknoCEA©) using PLECS© software. For each converter type, three
operation control systems are given, namely P/Q (grid following active-reactive power con-
trol), VDC/Q (grid following DC voltage-reactive power control) and G/F (grid forming
AC voltage/frequency control). Figure 3 shows the overall procedure that has been followed
through out the thesis. Starting from the top, teknoCEA© has provide the detailed models
of two-level and MMC VSCs together with their corresponding control systems in PLECS©
software. The term "detailed" refers to the utilization of switching components (diodes,
IGBTs etc.) in order to represent converter operation. In order to speed-up the simulation
process average models are developed in PLECS©. Next, identical control systems are repli-
cated from PLECS© into Simulink© for both types of converters. As it is discussed in the
next chapters, only the two level VSC is developed in Simulink© and not the MMC. It is
also illustrated that, in each step, validation tests are carried out between Simulink© and
PLECS© signals. Finally, having the Simulink© models developed, they are uploaded to
the Speedgoat© target machine in order to perform real time simulations.

1.3
Thesis Structure
The thesis is structured as follows: in Chapter 2 the developed average models of two-level
and MMC VSCs are analyzed, while in Chapter 3 the principle operation of the main control
system components is given together with the description of the overall control system.
In the next two chapters the results from Simulink© and real-time simulations are given.
Furthermore, Chapter 6 presents ideas and incentives for further work, based on the results
of the current thesis project. Next, in Chapter 7 the conclusions that are extracted from
the thesis results are discussed, including the main experiences when dealing with real-time
simulations.

3.2
PI and PR controllers
PI controllers are the dominant type of controllers, especially in industrial applications.
Diﬀerent alternatives of PI controllers exist, such as the proportional (P) controller and
Proportional-Integral-Diﬀerential controller. P controller is also one of the most common
choices and they are used in the control system of essential power systems components such
as the droop controller of conventional generators. In general, P, PI and PID controllers are a
mature technology since they have been used extensively over the last decades. PI controllers
receive as input the error signal which is obtain by subtracting the real measurement from
a reference value. Next, as the name indicates, the P term is proportional to the error,
meaning that if there is a large positive error, the control output will by proportionally large
and positive. One the other hand the I term, accounts and integrates for the past values of
the received error signal. In principle, the transfer function of a PI controller is given by the
following equation:
GPI(s) = Kp + Ki

(13)

to extract the proper controller operation, the two gains need to be tuned. In practise, the
tuning of a PI controller is an art and as such, diﬀerent tuning methods have been developed
over the years such as Ziegler–Nichols, Cohen–Coon and trial and error [21].
Although
their well-known technology and good performance, PI controllers come with two major
drawbacks: it is impossible to track sinusoidal references without a steady state error (Figure

Future Work
In this chapter proposals for further work are given, in order to take advantage of the results
of the current thesis project. Speciﬁcally, the following points are mentioned:
■Use of Simulink© blocks instead of C-function block: in PLECS© software, some
of the developed components, such as notch ﬁlters, PI controllers etc.
have been
implemented in C programming language. This is due to the limited discrete blocks
in PLECS© software and therefore an alternative is to use C language in order to
implement the mathematical models in discrete domain. In order to replicate this,
the C-function block has been utilized in Simulink©. There are two main reasons
for using Simulink© blocks: avoiding the C language complexity (hundreds of coding
lines) and also the accuracy of the control system. Speciﬁcally, when comparing the
output signals of PLECS© blocks (developed in C language) to the corresponding
Simulink© C function blocks, negligible diﬀerences occur, but they may aﬀect system
performance.
■Performance improvement of two-level VSC in VDC/Q mode B and of MMC as it is
addressed in sub-chapters 5.2.5 and 5.2.6.
■Examination of fault-ride through and low voltage ride-through capabilities of the
converters, in order to meet grid operators requirements.
■Investigation of the performance of frequency, DC voltage and AC grid voltage droop
capability and execution of scenarios having converters in mode C.
■Improvement of VDC/Q Simulink© model: as addressed in sub-chapter 4.1.2 there
are diﬀerences in the active power of the detailed and average models as well as minor
diﬀerences in the chopper operation.
■Development of average and detailed MMC models in Simulink©.
■Comparison of real time results and oﬄine simulation results.
■Increase Speedgoat© capabilities, meaning further improvement of the models for real-
time applications: In order to successfully compile the developed Simulink© models
for real time application, it is necessary to add the so called rate transition Simulink©
block. This block shall be used whenever there are two connected components (blocks)
having diﬀerent sampling rates. This maybe investigated even more, meaning that it
should be assessed if the transition blocks are optimally used, since it is possible to
limit the sampling rate capabilities of the Speedgoat© real time computer machine.
■Execution of real-time simulations, based on the results of sub-chapter 4.2.4: since one
of the main objectives of the laboratory facilities, is to examine the operation of the
VSCs in a P2P network, the models and the results from sub-chapter 4.2.4, can be
used as the ﬁrst step.

Conclusions
In this thesis project average VSC models have been developed in order to speed up sim-
ulation process. Moreover, a replicated version of the control systems from PLECS© into
Simulink© software has been implemented. Next, the developed models from Simulink© are
compared and validated with the corresponding PLECS© models. Additionally, real time
simulations are conducted using DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab laboratory facilities.
It has been concluded that, in most of cases, the developed models perform properly and
can be used as the initial step in order to carry out diﬀerent simulations as it is described
in Chapter 6, with most important the P2P system.
Real-time applications are one of the most state-of-the-art procedures, in order to validate
oﬄine models, such as systems developed in Simulink© or PLECS© software. During the
execution of scenarios in the software domain, it has been tried to understand the theory
behind and next when implementing the model in the real-time computer, the real-time
world reveals the consequences of the non-ideal features that exist in nature. For example,
in the thesis project the transformer saturation algorithm is operated as expected, but the
active and reactive powers of the MMC VSC are not, due to the signiﬁcant oscillations.
Apart from the results, the overall procedure for preparing and building models for real-time
application is not so straightforward. For example, at the very begging of the simulation
models, the C caller block has been used in order to replicate the C functions that are used
in PLECS© software. During the building of the model for real-time, it has been assessed
that the C caller is not compatible for such applications and so the C-function block has
been used. Although it sounds relatively simple to use C-function instead of C-caller, in
reality it is not, since it is necessary to spend time and to realize the diﬀerences between the
two Simulink© blocks. Overall, the experiences from the conducted real-time applications
of the current thesis project, can be used for research activities as it has been discussed in
the previous chapter.
