Abstract 
This thesis presents a comprehensive study aimed at characterizing the effects of terrestrial 
ecotoxicity effects in the context of life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) and exploring the potential 
for extrapolation using freshwater aquatic ecotoxicity effect data. Despite the critical role of soil 
ecotoxicity in evaluating environmental impacts of chemicals in LCIA, especially those resulting from 
anthropogenic activities, a significant gap exists in the ecotoxicological effect data for terrestrial 
environments. This research addresses this gap by developing extrapolation factors for converting 
various acute and chronic ecotoxicity effect endpoints to chronic 10% response effect 
concentrations (EC10) for terrestrial species in line with current global recommendations for LCIA 
ecotoxicity characterization, marking a novel contribution to the field, and subsequently deriving 
LCIA ecotoxicity effect factors for different chemicals in terrestrial environments. 
The proposed approach starts from a detailed curation and harmonization process of existing 
terrestrial ecotoxicity data, resulting in a refined dataset that facilitated the development of 
chemical-specific Species Sensitivity Distributions (SSDs) and deriving related Hazard 
Concentrations (HC20) across species-specific chronic EC10s for 553 distinct chemicals. 
Furthermore, we employed a unit conversion across terrestrial and freshwater effects, enabling a 
comparative analysis that, however, revealed poor correlation, suggesting significant differences in 
chemical effect potency across these environments. 
The thesis underscores the urgent need for more comprehensive terrestrial toxicity data and 
standardized testing methodologies to improve ecotoxicity assessments in LCIA. Furthermore, it 
proposes future research directions, including refining currently proposed extrapolation equation, 
incorporating chemical mode of action in ecotoxicity analyses, and exploring multilinear regression 
analysis for better correlating terrestrial and freshwater effect data. 
This research lays the groundwork for enhancing our understanding of chemical impacts on 
terrestrial ecosystems in the context of LCIA and advances the field of ecotoxicology by proposing 
methodologies that could lead to more effective environmental protection and sustainable 
chemicals management strategies.

1. Introduction 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) serves as a comprehensive method for evaluating the environmental 
impacts associated with all the stages of a product's life from cradle to grave ("ISO 14040:2008 - 
Environmental management - life cycle assessment - principles and framework,"). However, one 
critical area is often overlooked in LCA is the ecotoxicity of chemical emissions in terrestrial 
ecosystems. The characterization of terrestrial ecotoxicity is vital for a holistic understanding of a 
product or technology's environmental footprint. This oversight in current LCA methodologies can 
lead to biased decision-making processes, favoring products or technologies that, while appearing 
less harmful in other aspects, might have significant adverse effects on terrestrial ecosystems. 
(Kirchhübel & Fantke, 2019).  
Soil ecotoxicity, particularly within terrestrial ecosystems, is of very importance regarding product 
system influence by anthropogenic activities. These activities include application of pesticides, 
fertilizers, irrigation with treated wastewater, accidental oil spills or leaching from landfills, all of 
which can significantly affect soil health and, consequently ecosystem functionality (Vareda et al., 
2019). A chemical can impact the life of the organism living in or on the soil, as well as the quality of 
the soil itself, which is concerning given the soil’s role in important ecological functions like filtration 
of water and nutrients, biomass production, and carbon storage. These impacts of a chemical 
possess a threat not only to biodiversity but also jeopardize the soil’s ability to support critical 
functions (Bamgbose & Anderson, 2020; Wong et al., 2018). 
The standard approach for assessing the ecotoxicity of chemicals on ecosystems involves measuring 
the concentration of the chemical and observing the response of a species to exposure. This method 
enables the quantification of terrestrial ecotoxicity by establishing a correlation between chemical 
concentration levels and the observed biological response in exposed organisms (Fantke et al., 
2018). This approach is employed in Life Cycle Impact Assessments (LCIA) to quantify the impacts 
of chemicals and pollutants emitted during a product’s lifecycle. The quantified ecotoxicity is then 
converted into effect factors and then to characterization factors by linking the effect factors with 
fate and exposure factors, facilitating a detailed analysis of the environmental consequences (Hou 
et al., 2020). USEtox, a scientific consensus model applies the LCIA methodology to assess the 
chemical ecotoxicity (Rosenbaum et al., 2008).  
However, USEtox is limited to ecotoxicity impacts of aquatic ecosystems and does not have impacts 
for the terrestrial soil ecosystems. This is due to scarcity of effect data on terrestrial species (Fantke 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
et al., 2018). This shortage is largely due to the complexities involved in conducting toxicity tests on 
terrestrial soil organisms as compared to their freshwater counterparts. The variability of soil 
parameters, such as texture, organic matter content, and moisture levels, can significantly affect 
chemical toxicity, making standardized testing more difficult (Owsianiak et al., 2013; Rosenbaum, 
2015). There have been some developments in methods to characterize the effects of terrestrial 
ecotoxicity, but no single approach has gained universal acceptance (Fantke et al., 2018). The 
complexity arises because the existing framework relies on using data from freshwater effect tests 
as a proxy to indicate the potential toxic effects on terrestrial soil organisms (Magid et al., 2020). This 
approach involves correlating the bioavailable fraction of chemical in soil pore water with its 
potential to induce toxic effects in freshwater organisms (Golsteijn et al., 2013; van Beelen et al., 
2003). This is challenging as ecotoxicological effects observed in freshwater species may not 
accurately reflect those in terrestrial soil environments and it is because chemicals are not confined 
solely to soil pore water but are also present in the soil matrix and pore air, leading to behavior that 
can markedly differ from their presence in freshwater environments(Schafer et al., 2012). 
Several physical and chemical parameters determine a chemical’s bioavailability in soil, directly 
influencing its potential impact on organisms through physicochemical accessibility. For instance, 
the partitioning between phases—a process that varies significantly among different chemicals and 
soil types—affects a chemical's mobility within the soil. Moreover, soil pH and adsorption to organic 
carbon are critical factors that influence a chemical's bioavailability in the soil, thereby affecting its 
ecotoxicological potential (Michael Hooper, 2009). Therefore, the intricate interplay of soil 
characteristics and chemical behavior calls for a nuanced approach for effect factors, specifically 
tailored to understand the impacts on soil-dwelling organisms. 
The past half-century has seen significant advancements in the availability and development of soil 
test data and our understanding of soil ecotoxicity, marking progress in bridging this knowledge gap 
(van Gestel, 2012). Some examples are (Kumar et al., 2008) that tested the effect of various 
pesticides on different soil terrestrial organisms and even (Baylay et al., 2012) that researched in the 
ecotoxicological effects of multiple toxicants on soil terrestrial organisms. However, we still lack the 
toxicity data on terrestrial soil organisms for many chemicals. One rationale to improve the chemical 
coverage for the soil data is to rely on freshwater effect data to estimate ecotoxicity in terrestrial soil 
organisms as we have abundant data on chemicals for freshwater species. The increasing availability 
of soil effect data, coupled with the lack of a unified method to characterize soil ecotoxicity, 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
underscores the need for a comprehensive framework. Such a framework should integrate both soil 
and freshwater test data to establish a consistent set of factors for quantifying soil ecotoxicity, 
considering the varying levels of data availability. This would enhance the application of these 
insights within LCIA methodologies. 
To bridge this research gap, we propose a framework designed to quantify the effects of chemical 
emissions on soil organisms for application in LCIA. To accomplish this goal, we break down the work 
into the following objectives: 
(a) Conducting a comprehensive search of terrestrial ecotoxicity databases and harmonizing 
and curating the gathered test data. 
(b) Converting all standard data points to a single endpoint, EC10 chronic, by developing 
regression analyses and deriving extrapolation factors for various endpoints and exposure 
durations (i.e. acute, chronic). 
(c) Deriving Species Sensitivity Distributions (SSDs) and associated multi-species HC20 values 
and effect factors from a curated set of experimental effect test data for soil organisms. 
(d) Exploring the feasibility of extrapolating soil effects from freshwater species test data for 
chemicals with limited or absent soil test data. 
2. Methods 
2.1. Data collection, curation, and harmonization 
2.1.1. Data collection 
For our research on terrestrial ecotoxicity, we required specific data on soil organisms. Our data 
needs included the chemical name, species, species group, toxicity value, units, endpoints, study 
duration, media, exposure route, and source information. An internet search for terrestrial 
ecotoxicity data yielded several databases, including ECOTOX (US Environment Protection Agency), 
ETOX (German Environment Agency), and ToxValDB (United States Environment Protection Agency), 
among others. ToxValDB (Toxicity Value Database) version 9.2 (Judson, 2019) was selected for its 
comprehensive compilation of data and coverage of all the required parameters. While originating 
from ECOTOX, ToxValDB aggregates and refines data from a variety of sources, including PA, DOE, 
DOD, CDC/ATSDR, FDA, ECHA, EFSA, Health Canada, and the California EPA, ensuring a cleaned 
and filtered dataset for analysis. A total of 364,016 data points, pertaining to 7,398 unique chemicals, 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
were identified. However, a significant portion of these data points were later excluded from the 
current study. The reasons for exclusion included the incompatibility of units, which were not readily 
interpretable, the relevance of endpoints, or the fact that the species involved do not inhabit 
completely in terrestrial environments. Section 2.1.2 provides a detailed discussion on the methods 
employed for the harmonization and curation of this data, elucidating the criteria and processes used 
to refine the dataset for analysis. Appendix 1 contains the comprehensive set of raw data obtained 
from ToxValDB. 
2.1.2. Data Curation and Harmonization 
The collected set of reported raw effect test data required meticulous curation to ensure that each 
data point, representing a chemical tested for soil terrestrial ecotoxicity, was comprehensive and 
included all necessary parameters for analysis (Aurisano et al., 2019). This included chemical name, 
studied duration class (acute or chronic), endpoints (e.g., EC10), units, species name, species 
groups, media (soil) and exposure route. Data points lacking these necessary parameters or those 
that could not be interpreted were excluded from the analysis. Furthermore, data points related to 
species not exclusively occurring in terrestrial environments or not being significantly exposed via 
soil (e.g., bees, birds, rabbit, etc.) were also excluded to maintain focus on soil exposure-related 
terrestrial organisms. The curated data points were then harmonized for effect type, units and 
accordingly their ecotoxicity values. Figure 1 Workflow diagram applied for curation and 
harmonization of Terrestrial toxicity data coming from ToxValDB. Appendix 2 includes an Excel sheet 
detailing the curation and harmonization processes applied to the dataset.

The endpoints were further harmonized. All harmonized endpoints (NOEC chronic, NOEC acute, 
EC10 acute, EC50 chronic and EC50 acute) were systematically converted to EC10 chronic as 
recommended in (Owsianiak et al., 2023) for building SSDs and subsequently calculating effect 
factors. However, the extrapolation factors proposed in (Aurisano et al., 2019) were for the freshwater 
species and so new extrapolation factors for terrestrial species needed to be developed. To 
determine the factors for extrapolation, endpoints were compared pairwise (e.g., NOEC vs EC10) 
along with test duration categories (acute vs chronic). A regression analysis was conducted to 
examine the correlation between these pairs across the chemicals, considering each defined 
species. Another regression was conducted across the chemicals considering species group instead 
of species to increase the number of paired data points. Appendix 3 contains an Excel sheet that 
outlines the procedures undertaken during the regression analysis. 
Equations derived from regression analyses were utilized to extrapolate different endpoints to EC10 
chronic, chronic data points were prioritized over acute ones for the same chemical-species 
combinations. This approach acknowledges that extrapolations from acute tests introduce greater 
uncertainty than those from chronic tests. The underlying reason is that effect levels observed within 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
the same test tend to show stronger correlations than those reported from different tests. After 
converting all data points to the EC10 chronic endpoint, we averaged the values for identical 
chemical-species combinations, ensuring uniformity in endpoints across the dataset. This process 
resulted in a refined dataset comprising 1,830 data points, spanning 566 unique chemicals. 
The averaged EC10 chronic data points were subsequently augmented with taxonomy information 
for each species, encompassing Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, and Genus and data points 
for which taxonomy information couldn’t be produced were excluded from the study. This left us with 
1,785 data points for 553 distinct chemicals. 
2.2. Species Sensitivity Distributions, HC20 and Effect Factors 
After curating, harmonizing, and adding taxonomy information to the data points, the subsequent 
step involved generating Species Sensitivity Distributions (SSDs) for the chemicals. The SSD is a 
statistical method that is used to estimate either the concentration of a chemical that is hazardous 
to no more than x% of all species (denoted as HCx) or to determine the species potentially affected 
by a specific concentration of the chemical (Fox et al., 2021).  
The chemicals were first categorized into data-rich and data-poor groups to make SSDs. Data-rich 
chemicals can generally be defined as those with at least three data points from distinct species 
groups (e.g., chemical x having at least one data points from each plant, vertebrates, mammals) to 
cover the maximum species toxicity variability towards a chemical, in line with earlier studies 
(Aurisano et al., 2019). This approach is justified by the fact that ecosystems predominantly 
comprise three trophic levels: primary producers, primary consumers, and secondary consumers. 
These levels are also considered in other assessment frameworks, such as risk assessment, to 
ensure a comprehensive representation of species within an ecosystem (Oginah et al., 2023). While 
(Oginah et al., 2023) utilized three distinct species groups to define data-rich SSDs, the current study 
also considers the similar approach. However, due to scarcity of terrestrial toxicity data the study 
considers three different species genera coverage thus, not necessarily covering three different 
trophic levels but as broad as possible coverage of species per chemical. 
The data-rich chemicals were defined as those with at least three data points from distinct genera, 
and data-poor chemicals as those with fewer than three data points from different genera. This 
classification was substantiated by plotting the genus and the average toxicity values for each 
chemical, as depicted in Figure 2 illustrates the average toxicity levels of a chemicals across species,

Once the chemicals were classified the SSDs for data rich chemicals were derived based on the log-
normal distribution of species sensitivity data, specifically chronic EC10 equivalents. A log-normal 
distribution is defined by its mean and standard deviation, which serve as the moments of the 
lognormal SSDs, represented by μ (mean) and σ (standard deviation), respectively. For data-poor and 
data-rich chemicals, SSD generation involved calculating μ in the same manner for both i.e., average 
of all the toxicity values for different species for a particular chemical, while σ was assigned as the 
average standard deviation observed across data-rich chemicals, applied uniformly to all data-poor 
chemicals. 
Following the generation of SSDs, the next step was to calculate the hazardous concentration for 
each chemical The ideal approach would involve determining HC10 based on the 10th percentile 
effect concentrations (EC10s), mirroring practices in human toxicity assessments as highlighted by 
(Fantke et al., 2021). The preference for EC10s over the median effect concentrations (EC50s) stems 
from their closer approximation to environmentally realistic concentrations and their alignment with 
No Observed Effect Concentrations (NOECs), which are predominantly chronic. This alignment 
facilitates a more straightforward extrapolation to EC10s, thereby focusing on the long-term 
ecological effects crucial in LCA (Fantke et al., 2018; Sala et al., 2022). 
Nevertheless, the practical application favors the use of HC20 over HC10 due to data availability 
constraints. For most chemicals, the available data points are fewer than six, a limitation that 
extends to even for aquatic species which has many more data points than terrestrial species. To 
robustly estimate HC10 within an SSD, at least ten EC10s are required. This requirement poses a 
significant challenge, prompting the adoption of HC20 as a more feasible alternative, necessitating 
only five different EC10s for calculation. This methodological adjustment ensures that the approach 
remains conservative while accommodating the often-limited data sets available, particularly in the 
context of soil ecotoxicology. The decision to align with the HC20 metric across both aquatic and 
terrestrial ecotoxicity assessments underscores a commitment to consistency, despite the distinct 
challenges posed by data scarcity in soil ecotoxicology. 
Following the determination of HC20 values, these were utilized to calculate the effect factor for 
each chemical. An effect factor is a quantitative measure that translates the environmental 
concentration of a chemical into potential impact on ecosystems, specifically reflecting the extent 
of harm per unit of chemical released. This metric is instrumental in integrating ecotoxicological data 
into LCIA frameworks, facilitating the comparison of the relative ecotoxicity of different chemicals 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
(Gade et al., 2021). By employing the HC20 values, which emphasize the sensitivity of the most 
vulnerable species, the calculated effect factors provide a nuanced insight into the potential 
ecological impacts. This allows for a more informed assessment of the environmental burdens 
associated with chemical pollutants. Appendix 4 provides detail of the calculations for µ, σ across 
the chemicals and subsequently calculating HC20s and effect factors. 
2.3. Freshwater organisms effect test data 
A significant volume of effect data on freshwater organisms is available for a broad array of 
chemicals. As highlighted in Section 1, this opens up the potential to extrapolate terrestrial species 
effect data by establishing a correlation between freshwater and terrestrial ecotoxicity data (Fantke 
et al., 2018). This approach suggests a strategic pathway to leverage existing aquatic toxicity data to 
predict impacts on terrestrial ecosystems, enhancing our understanding of chemical effects across 
different environmental contexts. The freshwater ecotoxicity data, including HC20, HC50, and HC5 
values, were sourced from the (Oginah et al., 2023; Posthuma et al., 2019). For the 553 chemicals 
analyzed in our terrestrial ecotoxicity study, freshwater data were available for all but 79 chemicals. 
Consequently, in our efforts to correlate terrestrial with aquatic data, these 79 chemicals, many of 
which were metals, were omitted from the analysis. This exclusion was necessary to ensure the 
integrity and applicability of the correlation efforts between aquatic and terrestrial ecotoxicity data. 
The detailed data is provided in the Appendix 5 for freshwater species. 
2.4. Harmonization of units across effect test data 
For comparing HC20 values between terrestrial and freshwater species, it was crucial to standardize 
the units of measurement. The HC20 for terrestrial organisms was initially expressed in mg/kgdw, 
while for freshwater organisms were in µg/L, which were converted to mg/L (subtracting the log HC20 
by 3). To enable a direct comparison, the terrestrial HC20 values were converted to mg/L considering 
all parts of soil (air, water, and soil particles) or aquatic HC20 converted to terrestrial units i.e., 
mg/kgdw. This conversion utilized a factor based on the air-water partition coefficient (Kaw, Lwater/Lair) 
and the solids-water partition coefficient (Kp, L/kg), ensuring that the HC20 values for both terrestrial 
and freshwater organisms could be compared on the same scale. The complete derivation for the 
conversion process is detailed in Equation 1and Equation 2. Appendix 6 provides detailed 
information on the harmonization of units for aquatic data and the subsequent comparison between 
aquatic and terrestrial ecotoxicity data.

The analysis of regression plots for both species level and group level regression revealed several key 
observations regarding the extrapolation factors for terrestrial ecotoxicity. Chronic to chronic 
regressions generally demonstrated the strongest performance, with R² values exceeding 0.5, even 
at the group level, attributed to the comparisons being made between similar test types. Conversely, 
acute to chronic regressions exhibited lower performance, which can be attributed to the 
comparison of different effects and test types, with some regressions yielding R² values below 0.5. 
It was noted that extrapolations from EC50 to EC10 were the weakest among the endpoint 
conversions. The position of EC50 on the Species Sensitivity Distribution (SSD) curve, being further 
from EC10 compared to NOEC, likely contributes to the weaker correlations observed. 
Despite species level regression have only around 25% of the data points compared to group level 
regression, it still outperformed the latter. This suggests that including a broader array of species 
introduces more variability, or 'noise,' into the data, overshadowing any potential benefits from 
increased data points. This finding underscores the importance of precise species matching in 
regression analysis to minimize noise and enhance the robustness of extrapolation factors.

plotted against each other under two conditions: directly compared without unit conversion, and

little improvement even after unit conversion.

trend might be attributed to the distinct modes of exposure and uptake of chemicals in aquatic 
systems, as well as the differences in organism physiology and bioavailability of chemicals in water 
versus soil matrices.

4. Discussion 
4.1. Applicability and limitations of the presented approach 
The development of extrapolation factors to convert different endpoints (NOEC chronic, NOEC 
acute, EC10 acute, EC50 acute, and EC50 chronic) to EC10 chronic for terrestrial species represents 
a novel contribution to ecological science, offering a valuable tool for future terrestrial toxicity 
assessments. Additionally, creating effect factors for a significant number of chemicals introduces 
a new dimension to our understanding of their impacts on terrestrial ecosystems.  
A notable limitation of this study is the insufficient availability of terrestrial ecotoxicity data, which is 
also unevenly distributed across trophic levels and species groups. For example, while data on 
worms are abundant, information on mammals is comparatively scarce. Additionally, the lack of 
standardization within the available data—manifesting in inconsistent endpoints and units—

Another constraint is the difficulty in finding meaningful correlations between terrestrial and 
freshwater effect factors, a complex issue that limits the extrapolation of freshwater toxicity data to 
terrestrial environments and underscores the need for further investigation in this domain. This 
finding highlights a critical gap in our current understanding and methodology, underscoring the 
urgent need for targeted research efforts in this area. Enhanced investigation into the distinct 
mechanisms of chemical behaviour and species response across different environments is essential 
to develop more robust models for ecological risk assessment. Ultimately, advancing our knowledge 
in this domain will facilitate more effective environmental protection strategies and contribute to the 
sustainable management of chemical pollutants. 
4.2. Recommendations and future research needs 
To enhance our understanding of terrestrial ecotoxicity, it is imperative to expand the availability of 
terrestrial toxicity data. Currently, much of this data is focused on a narrow range of species group, 
primarily worms and plants. Broadening the scope to include a more diverse array of terrestrial 
organisms would provide a more comprehensive view of ecological impacts. There is a pressing need 
for data that spans various trophic levels and extends beyond a handful of species groups. 
Additionally, standardization in toxicity data reporting is necessary, including the adoption of uniform 
units, media, and endpoints for a more coherent and interpretable dataset. 
In terms of correlating terrestrial and freshwater ecotoxicity data, our initial approach of linear 
regression may have been too simplistic, given the complexity of factors influencing the conversion 
between terrestrial and aquatic data. A more nuanced approach, such as multilinear regression 
analysis, could offer deeper insights. Additionally, rather than solely comparing effect factors, 
examining ecotoxicity at the species group level could yield more precise correlations. For instance, 
comparing the ecotoxicity data of mammals in freshwater environments with those in terrestrial 
settings might provide valuable information. Lastly, further research into the behaviour of chemicals 
in soil and their effects on resident species is essential for a thorough understanding of soil 
ecosystem dynamics. Below are some of the proposed things for future research aimed at 
elucidating the relationship between terrestrial and freshwater ecotoxicity data – 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
1. Converting effect factors into characterization factors for a comprehensive environmental 
impact assessment. 
2. Comparing these newly developed effect factors against existing terrestrial ecotoxicity 
factors derived through different methodologies. 
3. Enhancing correlation analysis with aquatic data by categorizing chemicals based on their 
behaviour, such as pesticides or hydrophilicity, to facilitate more nuanced comparisons and 
many other ways. 
4. Refining the current extrapolation equations to account for variability in soil composition and 
properties, acknowledging that homogeneity in soil may not always be present. 
5. Incorporating the mode of action of chemicals into the toxicity data analysis to better 
understand and predict their ecological impacts. 
5. Conclusions 
The development of extrapolation factors for converting various endpoints to EC10 chronic for 
terrestrial species introduces a novel approach that could significantly enhance research in 
terrestrial ecotoxicity in LCA and other comparative assessment frameworks. This study highlights 
the superiority of species-level regression over group-level regression for extrapolation, suggesting 
that individual species within a group may respond differently to chemical exposure. 
The derivation of effect factors for chemicals based on HC20 represents a novel contribution, as it 
marks the first-time effect factors have been derived for such a broad array of chemicals within the 
terrestrial environment. These newly calculated effect factors have the potential to be integrated with 
fate factors and exposure factors, leading to the development of characterization factors. Such 
factors could serve as new indicators within Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) methodologies, 
specifically for assessing terrestrial ecotoxicity. This advancement opens the door to more nuanced 
and comprehensive evaluations of chemical impacts on terrestrial ecosystems, enhancing the 
environmental relevance of LCIA. 
The inability to find a correlation between freshwater and terrestrial ecotoxicity data raises critical 
questions about the validity of previous extrapolations and underscores the need for more rigorous 
scientific investigation into the behaviour of chemicals in terrestrial environments. It also opens up 
the possibility that a direct correlation between terrestrial and freshwater ecotoxicity may not exist, 
challenging assumptions in current ecological risk assessment practices. 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
This research underscores the importance of accumulating more comprehensive terrestrial toxicity 
data. Despite the limited abundance of data available, inconsistencies in units, endpoints, and 
media limit their utility for cross-ecosystem comparison. Therefore, there is a pressing need for 
enhanced data collection efforts in terrestrial ecotoxicology, coupled with the standardization of 
testing protocols and reporting formats, guided by clear regulations and guidelines. 
In conclusion, this work paves the way for future studies in terrestrial ecotoxicology, calling for 
improved methodologies in data collection and analysis. By addressing these challenges, the 
scientific community can achieve a better understanding of chemical impacts on terrestrial 
ecosystems, facilitating more effective environmental protection and sustainable chemical 
management strategies.
