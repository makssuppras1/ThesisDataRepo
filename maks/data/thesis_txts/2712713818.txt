Abstract
Atrial fibrillation is the most diffused arrhythmia that affects the atria, which acts by incre-
menting and modifying the heart rate. Recent research works show that the atrial activation,
significantly related with atrial fibrillation, is affected by the orientation of the fibers that con-
stitute the atrial structure. However, due to the complexity of the atrial chamber, the relation
between the atrial structure and its hemodynamics and mechanics has been poorly investi-
gated. This thesis presents the implementation of the fibers distribution in the atrial wall of
a 3D model derived from a previous research. The implementation includes the determina-
tion of the strain energy function to define the introduction of the fibers, the calculation of
the fibers parameters to insert in the function and ultimately the definition of a proper coor-
dinate system for the fibers. Furthermore, a fluid structure interaction study is performed
to determine the effects of the fibers implementation. The results obtained confirm that the
atrial walls composition, and in particular the atrial fibers, have relevant implications on the
hemodynamics and mechanics of the left atrium. The results also identified the fibers stiffness
as the fibers parameter with the most relevant implications. The outcomes of the research are
confirmed by clinical studies, which reported an association between the alteration of the left
atrial structure, mostly connected with atrial fibrillation, and hemodynamic parameters.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Atrial fibrillation (AF) represents the atrial arrhythmia with the highest diffusion, influencing
around 1% of the population wold wide [1]. One of the risk factors of AF is advancing age since
the probability of developing atrial fibrillation is higher for the elderly. For instance, the oc-
currence rate of AF for people over 80 years is about 5%. Other risk factors are represented
by hypertension, diabetes, and obesity. The role of the heart is to deliver blood in different
compartments of the body and for this to happen properly, the atria and ventricles need to be
efficiently regulated from an electrical point of view [1].
1.1
Motivation
It has recently been discovered that the electrical activity that spreads across the atria is highly
influenced by the orientation of the fibers of the atria itself. In particular, Kharche et al [1]
performed models with different ranges of diffusion anisotropy to assess the function of the
latter in the process of initiation of AF. It was found that high fiber orientation anisotropy
(FOA) affects the activation of the atria, which also stimulates atrial fibrillation [1].
It is important to consider that the denomination ‘fiber’ is used to indicate myofiber bundles,
myocytes with an analogous orientation which follow the fiber’s course [2]. The direction of
the fibers is significant to study the electrical performance of the atria under a physiological
perspective, since the speed of the electrical wave is higher if considered along the direction
of the fibers. Furthermore, obtaining a good model of the fibers architecture is relevant for
the cardiac muscle mechanics as well. It has in fact been reported, from experiments done
on the left ventricle, that dissimilar architectures present dissimilar outcomes in terms of the
mechanics of contraction [2].
Even if the atrium has been generally represented and modeled as a simplified version of the
ventricles, a further understanding of its structure is fundamental to improve the practices
used to treat atrial fibrillation, such as catheter ablation and defibrillation. The present study
aims to research and implement the fiber distribution in the atrial wall and study its effect.
In particular, the hypothesis of the study is that the atrial walls architecture, and in particular
the fibers complex, have a significant impact on the hemodynamics and mechanics of the left
atrium.

2.1
Anatomy of the heart
The human heart acts as a muscle pump and it is characterized by the shape of a cone and the
dimensions of a fist. It is located in the center of the thorax and it can be subdivided into the left
and right side and further on into four chambers, two atria and two ventricles, as presented
in figure 2.1. Both the right and the left atrium, just mentioned, are connected at the sides
with one of the lungs and a phrenic nerve. The heart’s anterior portion is located at the back
of the sternum. The components of the anterior and inferior portions of the heart strongly
depend on the inter-ventricular septum’s placement, which divides the heart in the way that
a third of its body is located on the right of the septum and the remaining part on the left [3].
Furthermore, the right side of the heart delivers blood coming from the venae cavae to the
pulmonary circulation, while the left side pumps the blood from the veins of the lungs to the
systemic circulation.

2.2
Histology of the Heart
The heart’s wall is subdivided into three main layers: epicardium, myocardium and endo-
cardium, as evidenced in figure 2.2. The myocardium is composed by fibroblasts and cardiac
myocytes, alongside with the extracellular matrix (ECM). The cardiac myocytes cannot divide
but they are able to modify their dimensions and structure through the production of new
proteins, for example due to environmental alterations [5].
Figure 2.2: Representation of the layers that compose the heart’s wall, adapted from [5]
The myocytes present a diameter that stands between 10 and 20 microns and their length is
between 80 and 125 microns. The cytoplasm of the myocytes is mostly composed of myofibrils
(diameter of 1-2 microns). Every myofibril is composed of a filament of small contractile com-
ponents (length of 2.2 microns) defined as sarcomeres, which arrangement defines the typical
striated configuration. Every sarcomere is constituted by overlapping filaments of actin and
myosin (respective diameters of 5 and 10 nm). The transmission of electric waves is enabled
by the presence of gap junctions among the myocytes. The ECM of the cardiac muscle is com-
posed mainly of collagen type 1 (75-80%) and type 3 (10-20%), both produced by fibroblasts.
Furthermore, collagen types 4 and 5 form the myocytes’ basement membrane. In terms of the
myocardial dry weight, the myocytes constitute 90% while the collagen is only responsible for
2-5% of it [5].
2.2.1
Cardiac cycle
The cardiac cycle defines what happens, under a mechanical and electrical perspective, in the
cardiac muscle during the systole and diastole, which respectively represent the phase of con-
traction and relaxation of the heart. During the cycle, the relaxation and contraction of the
atria and ventricles, in turns, aims to push the blood from the atria to the ventricles and fur-
ther on to the pulmonary and systemic circulation. The cardiac cycle can be divided into four
moments, characterizing every chamber of the cardiac pump: filling, isovolumetric contrac-
tion, ejection and isovolumetric relaxation [6]. If the phases of systole and diastole are further
examined, the cardiac cycle can be split in seven different sections, as presented in figure 2.3.
The first has an onset alongside the P wave (in the electrocardiogram) and it constitutes the
==PAGE:23==

depolarization of the atria, and it represents the last part of the diastole. The sections from the
second to the fourth constitute the systole and finally, sections from the fifth to the seventh
stand for early and mid-diastole. The seven sections of the cycles are hereby further analyzed
[7]:
• Atrial contraction The contraction of the atria occurs, alongside with an increment
in the pressure of the atrial chamber. This causes an increase in the blood flow through
the atrioventricular (AV) valves, which at this stage are open, as well as an increment
of blood volume inside the ventricles. The contraction of the atria causes a rise in the
venous pressure, identified as the ”a-wave” of the pressure of the left atrium (LAP), which
is indicated as the first peak in the bottom pressure curve of figure 2.3.
• Isovolumetric contraction When the pressure inside the ventricles is greater than
the pressure of the atria, the AV valves close. The phase between this (AV valves closed)
and the moment the aortic and pulmonary valves open is characterized by an increase
in pressure, which occurs without modifications in terms of blood volume, since all the
valves are closed. The shape of the ventricles undergoes a relevant modification, and the
cardiac muscle assumes the form of a sphere. The ”c-wave” (second peak of the bottom
pressure curve in figure 2.3) the LAP could be caused by the protrusion towards the left
atrium of the leaflets of the mitral valve.
• Rapid ejection The blood flows into the aorta from the left ventricle and into the pul-
monary arteries from the right ventricle. This phase starts with the pressure inside the
ventricles that is higher that the pressure in the aorta and pulmonary arteries, which
makes the respective valves to open. The pressure in the left atrium decrements because
the chamber increases in dimensions. Blood is directed from the veins into the atria and
therefore the pressure inside the atria increases, this occurs until the opening of the AV
valves.
• Reduced ejection The ventricles re-polarize and this takes the active contraction to
drop. As consequence, the pressure inside the ventricles and the velocity of the outflow
decreases. The latter does not stop because of the kinetic energy of the blood. The pres-
sure inside the left atrium increases because of the venous return from the lungs while
the pressure inside the right atrium increments due to venous return from the systemic
circulation.
• Isovolumetric relaxation The pressure inside the ventricles drops and when this
drop is consistent enough, the pulmonary and aortic valves close. Because of all the
valves being closed, the relaxation of the ventricles happens at a constant volume. The
LAP keeps increasing, due to the venous return coming from the lungs, up to its peak,
termed ”v-wave”, represented by the third peak of the bottom pressure curve in figure
2.3.
• Rapid filling The ventricles relaxation proceeds and when the pressure inside the ven-
tricles is inferior to the one in the atria, the AV valves open, initiating the passive filling
of the ventricles. The LAP rises up to a peak in the ”v-wave”, followed by the mitral valve
opening and a consequent drop in LAP, presented by the y-descent.

The architecture of the atrial walls influences considerably the electric activity of the atrium

coordinate system was identified for what regards the tracts of the myocardium present on the
atrial roof. This system, presented in figure 2.7, uses the horizontal line that connects the mid-

2.4.1
Finite Element Method (FEM)
The finite element method is a mathematical method employed to estimate the solution of
problems that present a high level of complexity and therefore would be otherwise difficult to
approach. The method is based on the subdivision of the base structure in smaller elements,
which are expected to act as continuous parts, termed finite elements (FE) [13]. The elements
are projected to connect at the nodes, specific points in the structure which establish both the
geometry of the system and its degrees of freedom. Possible 3D element geometries are pre-
sented in figure 2.10. Furthermore, the nodes are generally located at the edges of the elements
and they represent the position in the FE in which the field variable is determined explicitly
[14]. For instance, if the solution of each component is estimated correctly, they should overall
converge to the precise solution of the whole structure as the reduction of the FEs’ dimensions
occur. The ultimate solution is achieved through the fulfillment of the equations of equilib-
rium at the nodes. In particular, the Comsol Multiphysics software will be adopted to execute
the numerical analysis in this project [13].
Figure 2.10: Possible element geometries: tetrahedrals, prisms and hexahedrals, adapted
from [14]
2.4.2
Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian Method
The Eulerian method, usually utilized for fluid physics, defines the dynamics of a complex by
evaluating the transformation of the complex respect a fixed scheme of coordinates, defined
spatial frame in Comsol. In contrast, the Lagrangian method is mainly applied to mechanics
related aspects and it defines the equations with respect to singular elements of the material.
Each of these elements changes location within the material as the latter moves or distorts
its shape. The defined scheme of coordinates is the material frame in Comsol [15]. Fluid-
structure interaction (FSI) studies, as the one that will be further presented in this work, re-
quire a combination of both the Eulerian and the Lagrangian method, termed the arbitrary
Lagrangian-Eulerian Method (ALE). The ALE method utilizes an ulterior coordinate system,
termed mesh frame in Comsol. Respect to the Lagrangian method, the ALE method introduces
further equations to describe the motion in the spacial frame of the elements in the mesh. The
latter occurs to define the deformation in the mechanics that potentially results in an alteration
in the boundaries’ shapes of the domains defined by the Eulerian method. The supplementary
equations that have just been described are defined as Moving Mesh in Comsol [15].

2.4.3
Mesh
Applying a mesh to a geometry is a fundamental aspect of the modeling procedure in since it
can be determinant for the accuracy of the results. In this scenario, both the geometry and
the size of the mesh elements are relevant features to consider in the process. In particular,
in Comsol Multyphysics it is possible to select from four distinct element classes: tetrahedra,
hexahedra, pyramids and triangular prisms. For what concerns the elements’ size, there are
nine different possibilities, which span from extremely fine, to indicate an extremely small
mesh size, to extremely coarse, corresponding to large dimensions of the mesh elements [16].
Ultimately, the Comsol interface Mesh statistics allows to evaluate the quality of the mesh
selected for the model; the quality values range from 0, characterizing a poor element choice,
and 1, which represents an ideal element choice [17].
2.4.4
Solver
In Comsol Multiphysics, there are two possible paths to be selected in order to solve the equa-
tions’ complex defining the solution. The first is the fully coupled approach, which constitutes
one complex of equations which finds the solution to the problems all at once, in one itera-
tion. The second is the segregated approach, which breaks down the problem into multiples
segregated steps, and their solution is found in sequence within one iteration [18]. The solver
setting has determinant consequences on the model simulations, since every step, whether it
is time-dependent or stationary, has to be accurately set in order to allow the simulation to
terminate and return the results.

Blood
Blood is constituted by different groups of cells: red and white blood cells and platelets that

electrolytes, organic molecules and proteins. Because of its composition, plasma represents
a Newtonian fluid. For instance, a fluid is considered to be Newtonian if it complies with
Newton’s law of viscosity, which states that viscosity is the constant that represents the ratio
between the shear stress and the shear strain when a fluid undergoes mechanical stress. This
approximation, to consider blood as a Newtonian fluid, can be made in situations that do not
involve small arterioles and capillaries, because for the latter the relation between the particles
and the lumen dimensions becomes more significant and requires further studies [25].

Kinetic Energy
The cardiac muscle has to provide the systemic and pulmonary circulation with a certain vol-
ume of pressurized blood. Furthermore, stroke work and kinetic energy (KE) constitute the
cardiac external work. The first accounts for most of both left ventricular and right ventricular
external work while the second represents the load of work required to transport blood, caused
by its inertia. The KE of the atrium contributes to the left ventricular (LV) external work since
the contraction of the ventricle leads to the flow of blood into the atria [27]. In particular, the
KE of the left atrium derives from equation 2.11.

2.6
State of the art
The organization of the fibers in the atria and ventricles are significantly different. This vari-
ance explains the lack of models to represent the structure of the fibers in the atria. In fact, the
fibers in the ventricles present a neat structure, with respect to the ones in the atria that are
grouped in singular bundles which do not follow the same directions across the atria. It has
also to be considered that even high levels imaging techniques are not applicable to the walls
of the atria because of their thinness[2] [29]. In fact, it has been reported that the average
thickness for the atrial wall is 1-4 mm while for the ventricular walls it reports values com-
prised in a range between 4.5-8.3 mm [30] [31]. The starting point for these models is usually
represented by images obtained with techniques such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
and computer tomography (CT). Sachse et al [32] used this approach to define how myocar-
dial fibers are oriented both in the atria and ventricles. Their work focus was to add details,
obtained from studies on the morphology of the heart, to the anatomical models coming from
the imaging techniques described above. The orientation of the fibers was assigned through
the information previously acquired from anatomical researches on the left atrium [32].
A relevant work that incorporates the architecture of the atria into its anatomical model was
done by Flores et al [33]. The semi-automatic approach involves base models employed to
obtain a new one, adapted for the patient. The description of the base models’ creation was
considered particularly significant. In particular, the process included 5 steps, from the user
subdivision of the initial mesh into various orders of muscle bundles to the definition of the
fibers’ orientation. The semi-automatic models presented different flaws, mostly related to the
base models resolutions and the algorithm used but, because of the idea to utilize one main
atrial model and adapt it according to the patients, it is still considered a relevant starting point
to produce realistic atrial models [33].
A more accurate model focused on the atria was implemented by Krueger et al [29]. Images
obtained through the voxel method were employed and, like Flores research work, the seed
points were labeled by the user to distinguish the fiber bundles. For instance, the method
used 22 seed points since it involved both the atria. A more accurate explanation of the seed
points location is present in the work of Wachter et al [34]. The model developed by Krueger
showed relevant results in terms of accuracy to represent the atria, even if it excessively relies
on the accuracy of the seed points [29].
Hoermann et al [2] developed a procedure that identifies the architecture of the fibers lo-
cated in atria with a random structure by employing imaging techniques and models of re-
orientation. In particular, the latter considers atria with known fibers orientation (atlas atria)
and uses them to define the architecture of the fibers in the atria that have to be yet defined.
The process included the definition of the atlas atria’ architecture, through imaging techniques,
and realization of the patients’ fibers with the altered atlas, after the fibers reorganization has
occurred. The last step consisted in the realignment of the fibers. The results proved good
similarities between the new realized atria and the real one. Some limitations include the fact
that more detailed models of the walls of the atria could be realized with better imaging tech-
niques [2].
Finally, a relevant work on fibers implementation was done by Mao et al [35]. The subject of
the work was the aortic and mitral valves. The study proved that, with the implementation
of the proper strain energy function and an accurate definition of material parameters, it is
possible to obtain fluid-structure interaction results that resemble with a good approximation
what happens during the cardiac cycle. The strain energy function presented in the model is

the modified anisotropic hyperelastic Holzapfel-Gasser-Ogden material (MHGO) model [35].
The research works previously presented exacerbate the necessity to improve and increment
the investigation on the atrial structure. In fact, the atrium is an increased subject in electro-

of the atrium.

kinetic energy is evaluated; for the mechanics, the stress and stretch of the left atrial chamber
are investigated. The results are obtained by running the 3D model for three cardiac cycles;
the first cycle is disregarded since its purpose is to stabilize the model. The accuracy of the
results increases with the number of cycles run in the simulation and, therefore, the final nu-
merical values showed in the present chapter refer to the third cardiac cycle. The first part of
the chapter is dedicated to the analysis of the base model used for the FSI study, characterized
by the fibers parameters presented in table 5.2, and its comparison with reported physiologi-
cal values. The second part of the chapter, instead, aims to study how the profiles obtained in
the first part vary if the fibers stiffness or the fibers dispersion values are modified.

6.2
Investigation of Fiber dispersion
In this section, the influence of the fibers dispersion on the left atrial hemodynamics and me-
chanics is investigated. It has been reported that in healthy hearts the fibers dispersion is
minimal, since the latter present marginal angular deviation (around 12-15°) from the char-
acteristic helical arrangement of the myocardial fibers. On the contrary, the fibers denote an
angular deviation of around 20-25° when the heart is affected by hypertrophic cardiomyopa-
thy (HCM) or infarction of the myocardium [44]. A previous study done on the myocardium of
the left ventricle demonstrated that, even if scarcely found in the literature, the fibers disper-
sion is a significant parameter as it effects the mechanical activity of the myocardium. Indeed,
the increase in dispersion acts by decrementing the stress response and altering the way the
myocardium deforms [44]. The most relevant impact of the dispersion is reported on the pres-
sure, volume, stress and stretch pattern. Therefore, the present section focuses on the compar-
ison of different values of the fibers dispersion parameter on the four aspects just mentioned.
As described in section 5.1.2, the fibers dispersion values evaluated correspond to k3=0, 0.16
and 0.3. The fibers stiffness used throughout the section is the same as the one reported for
the base model. In the appendix (section A.4) the effects of the fibers dispersion on the left
ventricular stiffness, which present similar coutcomes, are also reported.

7.1
Base Model Results Discussion
The intent of the FSI simulation was to define the outcomes of the fibers implementations
on a previously defined 3D model, alongside with the determination of which fibers parame-
ter has a more relevant impact on the hemodynamics and mechanics of the left atrium. All
simulations were ran for three cardiac cycles, in which every cycle has the function to stabi-
lize the results. In contrast to previous studies, the second and third cycle, as presented in
the appendix, still show a certain degree of discrepancy in terms of the waves denoted in the
hemodynamic curves. The latter would suggest the necessity of further cycles to be added in
the simulation to obtain more precise outcomes. Since adding more cycles implies an expo-
nential increase in the computational time and the introduction of a further adjustment of the
solver setting, three cycles was established to be the best trade off between time and accuracy.
Coming to the results evaluation, the base model reported interesting outcomes. Firstly, the
pressure curve (figure 6.2) shows that the strain energy function used to define the hyperelastic
behaviour of the atrial walls with fibers (equation (2.4)) allows to obtain a left atrial pressure
profile that contains the main traits of the physiological curve. In fact, the v-peak, a-peak and
y-descent are reported in the obtained profile, and their respective values are within the phys-
iological range defined in table 6.1. The main discrepancy is found with the y-descent, since at
the opening of the mitral valve the atrial conduit phase begins and the blood should flow into
left ventricle in a continuous and not abrupt way, as it is instead depicted in the calculated
wave pattern. A possible explanation of this abrupt pressure drop is related to the fact that
the mitral valve is not present in the geometry of the 3D model of the current study. The lack
of the valve also results in the absence of the c-wave, as portrayed in the physiological curve
(figure 6.1). Therefore, when the internal wall is removed, due to the difference between the
atrial and ventricular pressure, the blood flows instantaneously inside the left ventricle, caus-
ing the pressure in the left atrium to reach its lowest point. The missing mitral valve could
also explain the two peaks found during the ED phase; since there is no valve preventing the
blood flow to return to the atrium after entering the left ventricle, there is a continuous flow of
blood between the atrium and the ventricle, which is depicted with the altering pressure curve
during the ED phase.
Passing to the volume results, the profile obtained presents a considerable similarity with the
pressure profile, which could have been anticipated since the left atrial volume highly depends
on the pressure generated within the atrial chamber. The minimum and maximum volume
values derived from the FSI study are within the reported ranges but the minimum volume
occurs in a different instance compared to the physiological profile. In fact, the minimum vol-
ume should be reached at the end of the atrial contraction because in this instance the left
atrium actively pumps all the blood remaining in its chamber to the left ventricle. Instead, in
the FSI results there is, just as reported for the pressure, an abrupt decrement in the atrial

volume within the instants that follow the opening of the mitral valve. The explanation fol-
lows what previously stated for the pressure: the mitral valve and the left ventricular chamber
are not present in the considered model and therefore they can not prevent the pressure drop
at the opening of the valve and the consequent movement of the blood pool out of the atrial
chamber. Furthermore, a relevant disparity with the physiological curve is represented by the
volume pattern during the ventricular systolic phase. Indeed, the atrium doubles in volume
from the moment the blood starts flowing in from the pulmonary veins to the end of the reser-
voir phase (point A in figure 6.3), passing from 50 to 100 ml, while it only increments by a fifth
of its value in the calculated FSI results, going from 55 to 67 ml (table 6.2). This could occur
because of the fibers parameters definition, which influences the way the hyperelastic atrial
chamber behaves, and therefore determines the way it expands.
The transmitral velocity profile derived also portrays the consequences of the missing mitral
valve in the geometry of the model. Indeed, in the results, the E peak is present and within
the physiological range (0.5 m/s) but the two further waves that appear during the ED phase
and reach negative values suggest the retrograde flow of the blood inside the left atrium, which
could be due to both the absence of the mitral valve and the strain energy function defined to
characterize the solid structure of the atrium. Furthermore, the lack of the A peak during the
LD phase (figure 6.6) and the negative values presented by the velocity profile indicate that
also during the atrial contraction there is a backwards movement of the blood pool inside the
atrium. If the kinetic energy profile is considered, it can be noted that it presents the main
peaks of the physiological curve but with considerable differences in terms of the reported val-
ues, as depicted in table 6.4. These discrepancies are mainly related to the lack of the mitral
valve, which explains the different movements of the blood pool during the ED phase and the
considerably greater ED peak in the FSI profile, which reaches a value of 13.6 mJ opposed to
the physiological value of 3.5 mJ. Different movements of blood between the atrium and the
simplified ventricular structure during the ED phase, with no physiological correspondence,
are also confirmed by the transmitral velocity curve in figure 6.6. Ultimately, the stress and
stretch profiles have been derived to delineate the mechanics of the left atrium during the car-
diac cycle. Interestingly, both profiles present a very high similarity with the pressure curve
acquired, suggesting that the hemodynamics and the mechanics of the atrial structure consid-
ered are substantially related. Indeed, the stress reaches a maximum of 73 kPa at the end of
the reservoir phase, indicating that the when the pressure inside the atrial chamber is maxi-
mum, also the resulting stress acting of the structure reaches its peak. The same behavior is
encountered in the stretch profile (figure 6.10), with the highest stretch, of 1.8, appearing in
the moment of maximum expansion of the atrial chamber, as also confirmed by the volume
profile in figure 6.4.
7.2
Fibers Dispersion Results Discussion
The fiber dispersion parameter has been investigated because it has relevant implications un-
der a pathophysiological point of view, but mostly for what concerns the atrium this parame-
ter has been scarcely studied. Using as a starting point a study conducted on the left ventricle
[44], the aim was to understand whether the 3D model presented allowed to visualize signifi-
cant discrepancies between the hemodynamic and mechanics profiles obtained from different
dispersion values, similarly as presented in the literature. Overall, the findings allow to draw
similar conclusions as previous studies. At first, the pressure profiles were determined. The

curves, presented in figure 6.11 show that the architecture of the atrial walls has a major im-
pact on its hemodynamics. In fact, even if the profiles of the curves are the same, the values
of the v-peak and the a-peak are considerably different. Indeed, there is a relative difference
of 15.5% between the v-peak value of minimum (k3=0) and average dispersion (k3=0.16) and
a 23% difference between the minimum and maximum (k3=0.33) dispersion value. The max-
imum fibers dispersion describes a situation characterized by fibers that diverge of at least 15
degrees from the defined orientation, which is represented by 45 degrees in the epicardium
and -45 degrees in the endocardium for instance. Therefore, under these circumstances, the
fibers of all the three layers presented in section 5.1.1 are not parallel to each other anymore
but they overlap, influencing the pressure inside the left atrium, which presents a considerably
lower v-peak at the end of the reservoir phase. The same behaviour has been confirmed by the
study of Eriksson et al [44]. If the volume profiles are evaluated, a considerable alteration in
the minimum volume can be noted. Indeed, when the fibers denote no dispersion the mini-
mum volume reaches a value of 44 ml, in contrast with 47 and 49 ml for average and maximum
dispersion values, respectively. These findings indicate that the dispersion has an effect on the
ability of the atrium mostly to deflate when the blood flows out of the atrial chamber. The lat-
ter confirms previous studies, that indicated a structural change leading to an increase in the
minimum volume reached by the atrium [44]. The maximum stress acting on the atrial walls
evidenced also what previously stated for the pressure. Since the stress on the walls strongly
depends on the pressure measured inside the atrial chamber, the alteration of the fibers ori-
entation affects the stress in the same way it affects the pressure. The dispersion effects are
relevant at the end of the reservoir phase, with a clear decrease in the stress on the walls re-
spect to the state of no dispersion. In fact, a value of 73 kPa has been reported for a state of
no dispersion, against 64 and 55 kPa for average and maximum dispersion, respectively. The
outcomes just presented are confirmed by previous works, which reported that the increase
in the fibers dispersion in the myocardium of the left ventricle induced an increment in the
stress evaluated in the same volume [44]. Ultimately, the stretch profiles derived from the
FSI model delineated an important outcome of the fibers dispersion on the topology of the
atrium. Indeed, if the fibers are considered in the state of maximum dispersion, the same are
overlapping and as a result the atrial walls expand with more difficulty, reaching a stretch of
1.7 as opposed to 1.8 in the non dispersion scenario. The same behavior has been reported for
the left ventricle, defining a situation in which the maximum fibers dispersion would lead to a
minimum stretch of the ventricular section.
7.3
Fibers Stiffness Results Discussion
The fibers stiffness influences in a determinant way the hemodynamics and mechanics of the
left atrial chamber. This statement has both been confirmed by studies on left atria affected
by atrial fibrillation and from the FSI model developed throughout the present research [47].
Patients with AF present a characteristic enlargement of the LA antero-posterior diameter and
an increment in the maximal atrial volume [46]. The latter has been evaluated in the current
study by comparing the effect of the atrial fibers stiffness obtained in the study and a fiber stiff-
ness six times smaller, corresponding to the fibers stiffness of the left ventricle, as reported by
[22]. The magnitude of difference between the two parameters allowed to visualize relevant
outcomes. In fact, by increasing six times the fibers stiffness, there was a resulting increment
of 28% in the v-peak and 26% in the a-peak, indicating once again an existing correlation be-

tween the topology of the atrium and its hemodynamics. The mentioned findings also suggest
that the fibers stiffness has a greater impact on the hemodynamics compared to the fibers dis-
persion since, between the maximum and minimum value of fibers dispersion model, there is
a relative difference of 23 % in the v-peak and 18% in the a-peak, as opposed to the values of
28 and 26 just mentioned. Furthermore, the volume results (figure 6.16) reported a relative
enlargement of the atrial chamber characterized by greater stiffness at the end of the reservoir
phase, with a reached value of 67 ml as opposed to 66 ml for lower stiffness, which could sug-
gest that higher stiffness is also related to possible modifications of the atrial structure. The
correspondence between higher stiffness and volume increase has also been described by the
study of Rahman et al [46]. Ultimately, the maximum stress and stretch analysis (figures 6.17
and 6.18) was determinant to define the connection between the hemodynamics and mechan-
ics of the atrium, which resulted to be highly related. In fact, the stress pattern resembles the
pressure profile, which could have been anticipated since a higher pressure inside the atrial
chamber determines a higher stresses acting on the walls of the structure. Furthermore, the
stretch curve evidences a less significant impact, respect to the stress curve, of the stiffness
increase in the atrial mechanics during the reservoir phase. In fact, if the end of the reser-
voir phase is considered, there is a relative difference of 28% between the lower stiffness and
higher stiffness case for the maximum stress, while only a 6% difference was reported for the
stretch profile. The latter can be re-conduced to the modest increment in the volume during
the same phase, which leads to a moderate expansion of the atrial chamber. Ultimately, it
can be noted throughout all the profiles presented that the curve corresponding to k1=10230
Pa and k1=1685 Pa are not synchronized, mostly after the opening of the mitral valve, imply-
ing that the characteristics of the fibers are of vital importance to determine the onset of the
hemodynamic waves.

7.4
Model limitations
Various limitations have to be considered for the FSI study presented in the current paper. At
first the strain energy function used for the curve fit, in order to obtain the fibers parameters,
does not take into account the matrix defined by the Di Martino model but it considers the
atrial walls composed exclusively by the fibers complex. This has an effect on the accuracy
of the fibers parameters which resulted from the curve fit. Furthermore, the 3D model taken
into account is a simplified version of the left atrium since the structure is composed of basic
geometries, such as the sphere and the cylinders that define the pulmonary veins and the left
ventricle. This simplification is determinant for what regards the hemodynamics and the me-
chanics of the study since the profiles obtained lack of accuracy. Indeed, utilizing a realistic
geometry would instead allow to reach curve profiles closer to the physiological ones. Fur-
thermore, the fibers distribution, in terms of number of layers and fibers inclination, has been
considered homogeneous throughout the whole atrial structure. Instead, differentiating the
various areas according to the fibers distribution would ideally lead to increment the precision
of the results obtained.
7.5
Future work
Future works could include improve the study done in the present research by implementing
the fibers on a realistic left atrial geometry, instead of utilizing basic geometries. The men-
tioned improvement would exponentially increase the computational time required by the
simulation but at the same time it would also probably allow to obtain values closer to the re-
ported physiological calculations. Furthermore, the realistic geometry would make it possible
to further develop the characterization of the atrial fibers. Since in the present study only one
area of the left atrium has been considered, for what regards the number of layers and fibers
inclination in the model’s geometry, future researches could implement the fibers in different
ways depending on the area of the atrium considered. This would increase the probabilities to
obtain a more accurate definition, also visually, of the atrial walls.

CHAPTER8
Conclusion
The present study investigated the influence of the fibers implementation on a simplified 3D
geometry of the left atrium. The implementation allowed to obtain a simplified visual repre-
sentation of the fibers’ orientation and subdivision in layers through the atrial wall, with the
configuration reported from anatomical studies. The results obtained support the hypothesis
elaborated at the beginning of the study, which postulated that the atrial walls composition,
and in particular the atrial fibers, have relevant implications on the hemodynamics and me-
chanics of the left atrium. Indeed, the variation of two fibers parameters, defined as fibers
stiffness and dispersion, resulted in altered left atrial pressure, volume, stress and stretch pro-
files during the cardiac cycles portrayed. The outcomes of the research are also confirmed by
clinical studies that investigated pathologies affecting the left atrium. The relevance of the re-
sults obtained sets this research as a starting point for further investigations in the field, still
tendentially unexplored, of the left atrial structure.

1
Introduction
An overview of the motivation and learning objectives of the thesis will be presented
in this project plan. The master thesis project will take part at DTU in the department of
Health Technology during the spring semester of 2022. For instance, the start date for the
thesis is January 17 and the end date June 17 and it will account for 30 ECTS points. The
supervisor of the MSc.project is Marie Sand Traberg.
1.1
Hypothesis
The project aim is to further develop a simplified simulation model of the human left
atrium with the implementation of fiber distribution in the atrial wall. Since the imple-
mentation will occur on the simplified model, characterized by four inlets and one outlet
arranged onto a sphere, the fibers implementation will also present a simplified architec-
ture. The latter will be obtained through the subdivision of the 3D geometry and the use
of mathematical models to insert the fibers architecture. Obtaining a good model of the
fibers architecture is relevant for the cardiac muscle mechanics. It has in fact been reported,
from experiments done on the left ventricle, that dissimilar architectures present dissimilar
outcomes in terms of the mechanics of contraction. Based on what has just been presented,
the hypothesis for this project is that the fibers architecture of the left atrium has an effect
on the flow and mechanical response of the wall.
2
Work task
The work task presents the activities that will take part during the course of the project.
These are represented by:
2.1
Anatomical and histological studies
The central focus of the project is represented by the left atrium and the fibers that
constitute its anatomical structure. For this reason a thorough literature research on the
anatomy and histology of the left atrium will be performed.
2.2
Methods investigation
Very few models are currently present to describe the fibers orientation and distribution
in the left atrium, therefore these will be thoroughly investigated and relevant considerations
will be used as a reference point to build the final model with Comsol.
2.3
Model development
Comsol Multiphysics version 5.6 will be used at first to reproduce the simple model
(starting from a 2D geometry) that has currently been developed to represent the left human
Page 1 of 4
==PAGE:96==
atrium. The current model is represented by a spherical model with a surrounding wall of
2 mm thickness and it has four inlets and one outlet. This model just described will be
implemented with a simplified version of the fibers architecture. Finally, simulations will be
run to compare fiber re-inforced wall material and isotropic wall material models.
2.4
Results Analysis
The results will be analyzed and compared with the literature.
2.5
Report writing
Two reports will be written, one in the middle of the semester and the final one for the
hand in date on June 17.

on AF and, therefore, further advances on the fibers orientation will have a significant
impact on the knowledge related to the disease. Previous methodologies and theories
will constitute a relevant part of the research process, as well as Softwares that will be
used to formulate the final model.
