Abstract
This master project is made in collaboration with the world record holding team in fuel
eciency DTU Roadrunners. The project is under supervision of Jesper Schramm and
has the purpose to design, construct and test a small high eciency IC engine that can
run on both ethanol and gasoline. Currently the team use a 49cc Yamaha NEOS T4
4-stroke engine from 2009, but this engine has been modied over 7 years and reached
its optimisation potential. Therefore this project was started to build a new platform
for pushing the eciency limit further. In a preliminary study it was concluded it was
possible to downsize the engine.
This was further investigated by using a topology
map of the track to simulate optimisation parameters.
This showed that the engine
had to be optimised to produce a high power and eciency rather than downsizing the
engine. This lead to a study of how the engine should be dimensioned. Here the stroke-
bore relationship and the size of the engine was investigated in a 2D thermodynamical
simulation.
In conclusion it was decided to reuse the NEOS T4 crankshaft to save
manufacturing time. Further simulations showed that dierent size engines using the
crankshaft had almost identical indicated specic fuel consumptions. It was concluded
the ideal engine size was between 37 and 49cc. A 35 mm piston was chosen to make a
42cc engine. The piston was taken from the Honda GX25 mini 4-stroke engine and has
a optimised piston ring design to reduce friction. To account for the power loss of the
smaller engine size it was planned to optimize the eciency by using the Miller cycle and
make it possible to go to a high compression ratio. Further the cylinder was simulated
with a thermodynamical heat transfer model under race conditions along with a stress
analysis. Here it was concluded that there had to be as much water as possible to stabilise
the cylinder temperature. At the same time it was benecial to use a liner as it reduce
thermal stresses. The cylinder head was designed to have two spark plugs and valves.
Two spark plugs was used to reduce the ame propagation time and thereby reducing
pumping loss. A special piston top, that made it possible to reach a compression ratio
of 21 and at the same time reduce the risk of knocking, was designed. Unfortunately
manufacture of the engine was delayed leaving no time to test the engine. While waiting
for the engine an improved test stand was designed to be able to test the new engine
and ease the alignment. At last there was made a new engine control system which was
tested at the Shell Ecomarathon in 2016 on the old engine.

1
Introduction
This master project has the purpose to design, construct and test a small high eciency
IC engine that is optimised to run on both 2nd generation bio ethanol and 95E lead free
gasoline. The project is supervised by professor Jesper Schramm and made in collabora-
tion with the DTU Roadrunners. The DTU Roadrunners is a team of students designing,
dimensioning and manufacturing fuel ecient cars. As oil is running out alternative fuels
has to be used in the near future. Therefore the team uses 2nd generation bio ethanol as
fuel for their world record holding car Dynamo. This is produced by the waste products
in food production and can therefore be made CO2 neutral. Since 2009 Dynamo has
used a Yamaha NEOS T4 4-stroke combustion engine modied to run on ethanol.
Over the last 7 years the engine has been modied and has reached geometrical limita-
tions without using the full potential of ethanol as a fuel. Compared to gasoline there
are some dierences in the chemical probabilities, which can be accounted for in the
engine design. These dierences was clear as the DTU Roadrunners want to use the
engine running on gasoline in a newly developed car. By using gasoline in the NEOS
T4 engine it was seen that here the limitations for optimisation was the fuel. Gasoline
started to knock (self ignite) before the engine geometry became a problem. This means
that there has to be done something new to improve the eciency for both using ethanol
and gasoline.
Therefore it was decided to design a new engine that could utilize ethanol as a fuel and
improve the eciency of running on gasoline. This means that the new engine design has
to have more parameters to optimise. These have to be based on the problems encoun-
tered by the limitations of the NEOS T4 engine for each fuel along with new ideas. But
to fully understand why the new engine is important it has to be known that the DTU
Roadrunners has a story of pushing the limit of fuel eciency. Therefore it is important
to get a new platform that can provide the possibility of doing so. The results achieved
by pushing the limit is made at the competition Shell Ecomarathon Europe which will
be described further.

Shell Ecomarathon
The competition Shell Ecomarathon is a worldwide competition for universities and ed-
ucational institutions, planned by Shell Global and is held in North America, Asia and
Europe at current time. Shell arrange these events as they want to learn more about
the future fuels for transportation. Within Shell Ecomarathon there are two categories
to compete in: Urban concept, where the car has to simulate an urban driving pattern
and comply with strict rules. Then there is the Prototype class, here the car looks like
a soapbox car with three or four wheels and the goal is to get as great fuel eciency as
possible. Within each of the two car categories, there are currently 6 subcategories of fuel
types: Gasoline, Alternative fuels, Diesel, CNG, Battery-electric and Hydrogen
fuel cell. The current car is competing in Alternative fuels as this category contain
ethanol. Each car has to complete a certain number of laps within a dened time to even
out the competition. Therefore the driving method coast and burn is used by most
teams. Here the car burns till it reach a velocity determined by the team and then coasts
(rolls) to keep a certain mean speed.
In the European competition there is a large number of competitors and the competition
is tough. This means that each team has to innovate and take their car to the limit. This
is no dierence for DTU Roadrunners and the last two years there have been focus on
engine eciency. The modied Yamaha 49cc NEOS T4 4-stroke petrol engine has been
improved so the brake eciency is increased by roughly 9-12% so it ranges between 34%
and 37% [1] [2]. This has been done by increasing the compression ratio and tweaking
the valve timing to t the driving pattern. In addition there is a conclusion from the
report of R. Frausing and O. Olsen [1] which state that a downsizing of the engine could
give higher eciency by increasing the compression ratio at the same time and keeping
the same brake power produced.
Furthermore it has been decided to built a new Prototype class car. To this there was
made a preliminary parametric study on the fuel savings of weight reduction, eciency
and brake power. This showed that the weight has most impact on the fuel usage, as the
rolling resistance is higher than the air resistance, when travelling with a velocity under
35 km/h [3]. With this study and the conclusion from R. Frausing and O. Olsen [1] it
seems that it is reasonable to look into decreasing the size of the engine.

from this consumption.

Here is where it gets tricky. It is preferred to get a slightly higher SB relationship, but at
the same time the engine may not lose too much power output. Furthermore the engine
must be able to use both gasoline and ethanol, by still remaining within the strict design
frame. This lead to deciding on a piston with 35 mm bore. This give an engine size of
42cc and increase the SB ratio to 1.24 which will not change on the power output too
much, but reduce the absolute fuel consumption and keep a good ISFC.
For using the engine with ethanol the power output reduction may not be a huge disad-
vantage as ethanol has a high knocking resistance. The current engine has reached its
maximum possible compression ratio at 14.5 without reaching the fuels knocking limit.
This means that with a new engine design it is possible to increase the geometrical limit
for the compression ratio. By doing so the power output from the engine will increase
and thereby eliminate the problem.
By using gasoline the problem with a lower power output may be more profound. It
is not possible to increase the compression ratio more than to 14, as gasoline start to
knock at a ratio of 14.5 (experimentally found fo the NEOS T4 engine). Therefore a
small power lose will be seen for this setup. Instead the eciency may be increased by
utilizing the compression ratio in other ways. Here the Miller cycle could be used as it
change the thermodynamic process by basically reducing the compression work and heat
loss. A downside to this is the decrease of the peak pressure which reduce the power
output of the engine further. To work against this power loss a higher expansion ratio
can be used to keep the peak pressure stable. This would increase the eciency and
keep the power output without producing knocking. (The expansion ratio is used for
describing the Miller cycle along with the compression ratio. Increasing the compression
ratio increase the expansion ratio. See section 5 for a full description and analysis of the

After the study in section 4.3 it has been found that the engine should have a bore of 35
mm and a stroke of 43.5 mm making it a 42cc engine. Therefore the next step is chosen
to be designing the cylinder as the top face has to be used in the cylinder head design.
In the design of the cylinder three main aspects are necessary to take into account without
considering the internal cylinder dimensions which were found in section 4.3. The rst
consideration in the cylinder design is in the heat loss. When the engine is running it
should not get hot which will wear the cylinder and possibly ruin the engine. On the
other hand the engine should be able to keep a fairly constant temperature as a too cold
engine will not run optimally. To summarize it is necessary to design the cylinder after
the running conditions such that it wont get too warm or too cold during use.
At last an important consideration is that the cylinder should to be able to withstand
the pressure and temperature inside without breaking. The engine should be able to
withstand the forces within it to be able to function.

7.4
Stress considerations
Beside controlling the temperature in the engine it was also necessary to make sure that
the walls can handle the stresses they will be subjected to when the engine is running. To
nd the stresses in the walls, the solid stress modelling capabilities of Star CCM+ were
utilized. It was here assumed that the walls would behave as linear isotropic materials.
For getting a measure of the total stresses in the walls the Von Mises stress was calculated
and the point where the walls have the highest stress was used to represent the stress
in the wall.
The stresses were then taking the temperature change of the walls into
consideration. To account for the pressure in the engine the maximum pressure in the
engine cycle was used for a worst case scenario. Further it was decided to use the highest
stress in the walls during one lap as the stresses would change with the temperature.
This highest stress was found around the time when the engine was the warmest. Finally
it was assumed that the walls were stress free when the race started to get the best
representation of the eects of the engine running.

The cylinder head
The design process of the cylinder head dier from the other parts. It was not possible
to nd any theory on the relation between engine size and valve port areas. Therefore
it was decided to investigate other engines, especially the Honda GX25 where the piston
is taken from, as there is the same amount of space to use. The GX25 use at valves,
which mean they are parallel with the piston top. At the same time it is chosen to have
a slightly larger intake area than exhaust. This is present in all engines investigated and
may be due to the scavenging process. Here there is a pressure in the cylinder when
exhaust gas is ushed, but the fresh air has to be drawn in and there need to be a low
pressure loss. This is reduced by the larger area. At the same time it was noticed that
all engines smaller than 49cc have a 2-valve setup.
Further the old NEOS T4 engine was looked into.
Here there are used two smaller
intake valves to increase the ow at lling, which makes it a 3-valve engine. To gain
enough space to do so they have angled the two intake valves and the exhaust valve
approximately 23 degrees.
This is smart as it may increase turbulence, as the air is
bounced on the walls right after entering the cylinder. A downside by the angled valves
is that it create a large dead space that can not be reached by the piston. This is also the
geometrical limitation that makes it impossible to increase the compression ratio further
on the NEOS T4 engine. As it is wanted to design an engine with the possibility of high
compressibility the at valve design used in many engines is highly desirable, but it is
not possible to use it in this case. To keep the production cost and time to a minimum,
it was decided to reuse valves, spring assembly and the cam system with re-designed
rocker arms. It is chosen to do so as the exhaust valve from the NEOS T4 engine is
16.5 mm in diameter, while the intake valve is 15.5 mm. So by using the exhaust valve
as intake valve and vica versa, the tendency with a slightly larger intake valve seen in
other engines are kept. The problem lies in that there is only 35 mm in bore which is not
enough space to place the two valves at, reusing the cam and at the same time full the
desire of two spark plugs without angling everything. Therefore other plans was made
to ensure the high compressibility, which involves a custom made changeable piston top.
This can further be seen and read about in section 10.4.

ensure that these parts can be assembled. This component is the top gasket which ensure
that the hot gases from the combustion as well as oil and coolant will not leak.
In designing the top gasket it is necessary that it conforms to the shape of the cylinder as
well as the shape of the cylinder head. As the engine is water cooled around the cylinder
and inside the cylinder head, the top gasket should allow water to ow between these
parts while preventing the water from leaking into the combustion chamber.

Finally it have high pressure and temperature resistances making it suitable for use. The
major drawback of this material is that it is based on asbestos which will make it a haz-

Redesign of piston assembly
After nishing the cylinder, liner, cylinder head and dening the combustion chamber,
the piston is the next step in the process. At rst it was discussed what was important
of the piston. It is clear that the piston rings play a huge role on friction and sealing.
The rings use a spring eect to seal against the walls, as a cylinder always will have an
ovality. Further can the material of the piston rings be chosen so they provide better
lubrication to reduce friction or a material that decrease wear. The piston itself has to
have an ovality as a result of thermal expansion. The ovality are already a part of the
Honda GX25 piston but at rst there were no information about the piston rings. If these
are not designed properly it will increase friction which is highly unacceptable. Therefore
it was chosen to investigate the NEOS T4 piston rings and compare their design with a
handbook about piston rings. The handbook is made by one of the biggest producers of
piston rings in the world to guide engineers. [24] This was done to examine what have
been done to reduce friction in the NEOS T4 engine and transfer it to the 35 mm GX25
piston if the piston rings did not have a proper design.
10.1
Yamaha NEOS T4 piston rings
The top ring has a combination of two methods. It has a tribology optimised contact face
which reduce the contact surface and thereby the friction (barrel face). [24] Furthermore
it has an internally bevelled top face. This provides an edge relief as it produce a twist
eect so only the lower part of the ring is in contact with the cylinder wall. This should
improve oil consumption. [24] By combining these two the ring can easily glide on the
wall without producing a large amount of friction.
For the second groove there is used a taper faced ring which is mainly used as it improves
oil scraping of the wall and shortens the running-in. At the same time it introduce a
kind of pressure relief with a small contact surface. [24]

10.6
Wear and friction
As the piston rings are in contact with the cylinder wall at all times they wear the
wall and produce friction. As described earlier in the section the current piston rings
are design optimised to decrease friction and thereby wear. The only thing that is not
discussed, which has a high impact on friction and wear are the coating of the piston
rings. Most common coating in the industries is Physical Vapour Deposition (PVD). The
reason for this is that a PVD coating reduce friction and wear after running-in while other
previous types keep the same level of friction or increase the wear. [30] It is therefore
highly wanted to have PVD coated piston rings, but there is no information about this
anywhere. As PVD is the most common in industry it is assumed that the Honda GX25
piston rings have this coating. This has to be looked into to ensure the lowest friction
and wear in the engine.

Maximum possible compression
Now that both the combustion chamber and piston top are designed, it is possible to

some safety distances have been taking into consideration, as the distance between the
piston top and combustion chamber. These have been set to be more than 0.1 mm to be a

tools on the cylinder head and piston top in the CAD program.
These volumes are
used along with simple volume calculation of a cylinder to calculate the compression
ratio. From these calculations the corresponding compression regulator thickness and
safety distances can be found. A small script where it is possible to get the compression
ratio from typing in the size of compression regulator thickness or vice versa has been
developed. By changing the thickness of this compression regulator it was possible to
achieve a compression ratio of more than 21 with proper safety distances. All calculations
can be seen in the main appendix D.
10.8
Balancing crankshaft and piston
In an combustion engine it is important to minimize vibration during running conditions.
The piston movement creates a system in unbalance if nothing is done to prevent it.
Therefore a counter weight is built in on the crankshaft in an attempt to cancel out
the outwards force from the piston. If there is a change in the piston mass the weight
blocks of the crankshaft has to be a changed. This means that to keep the balance in the
engine the new piston has to weigh nearly the same as the original piston else the whole
system has to be rebalanced. As the new piston is smaller than the original and has to
be modied, it is fair to assume that it will be a little lighter. The unknown factors are
the bolts, extra bushing and new top mount.
In the start of the project everything was weighed to estimate the total weight of the
engine. Here the original piston with rings was found to weigh 47 grams. By estimation
from the CAD models and material properties the new piston with everything assembled
weigh about 40.7 grams. This may be to the low side, but it is possible to attach some
weights in the piston if this turns out to be a problem. Therefore it may not be needed
to rebalance the crankshaft but the new piston assembly has to be weighed before a nal
conclusion can be made.

13.1.2
Encoder input module
Another aspect of the engine control is the timing of the injection and ignition. To not
make the timing dependent on the engine revolutions it is normally given in CAD. It is
therefore necessary to have a system that can measure the position of the piston. It was
chosen to do this by using an encoder on the cam. The encoder works by generating
a digital signal every time it has done one revolution which is known as the Z-pulse.
Furthermore two more digital signals are generated 720 times evenly spaced on each
revolution making it possible to determine where in the revolution the encoder is. These
evenly spaced signals are called A- and B-pulses and they are shifted a half pulse width
making it possible to determine the direction in which the encoder is spinning. It was
chosen to place the encoder on cam and not the crankshaft as the cam only does one
revolution every time the crankshaft does two. This way it is possible to determine the
position of the piston in the full four stroke engine cycle. To acquire these encoder signals
another digital input module is needed for the compact RIO. The digital I/O module
used for the control of the ignition and injection is unfortunately not fast enough to
sample the encoder so a high speed digital input module was chosen. The exact module
chosen was the NI9411 which is described in more detail in section A of the Legacy ECU
appendix report.

13.4.4
Closed loop control
Figure 13.9: Illustration of the Closed Loop Control VI. The VI is based on a stacked

To help achieve the greatest possible engine eciency it has been tried to implement
a closed loop control system in the engine control. The idea of the closed loop control
system is to have the engine adjust to varying conditions by itself and thereby run with
the best eciency without the need for an intervention by an operator. In gure 13.9
an illustration of such a system can be seen. It was chosen to implement the closed loop
control system such that it is based on the injection as this is a parameter that is highly
dependent on the environment and engine temperature.
This parameter is therefore
the control parameter and to get a feedback on this the excess air ratio was chosen.
The excess air ratio is therefore the feedback parameter. By measuring this it makes it
possible to estimate how much the injection length has to be changed. In the current
implementation of the closed loop control a special closed loop map has been created
which the closed loop control adjust based on the excess air ratio.
Just as for the ignition control and injection control the closed loop control is based on
a sequence of processes. The rst of these processes is to determine if the closed loop

control sequence should be run. For the sequence to be run rst it should be enabled
together with the engine running at sucient engine revolutions.
If these conditions
are satised the closed loop control sequence can be initialized. The initial step in the
control sequence is to sample the current engine revolution as well as the current injection
length. After the data has been sampled the next step in the control sequence is to wait
for the excess air ratio delay. The reason for this is that there is a large delay before the
changes are seen on the excess air ratio. To correctly correlate the engine revolutions
and injection length to the excess air ratio it is necessary to wait for this delay. After
the delay the actual closed loop control is performed. Here multiple processes happen
at once but the most critical process is the PID controller which calculates the actual
adjustment. The PID controller is a built in function in LabVIEW which supplied with
some control parameters like the gains, a target and a feedback value will do the actual
control. In our case an excess air ratio map is dened with the desired excess air ratios
in relation to the engine revolutions. The values in this map are then used as the target
which the PID controller tries to match the excess air ratio with. The sampled engine
revolution is then used to look up in the map such that the correct excess air ratio is
used as target. The sampled injection length is then used to calculate the amount of
air in the cylinder. In these calculations the subVI for calculating the fuel mass, dened
in section 13.4.3, is used to change the injection length to the injected fuel mass. This
fuel mass together with the excess air ratio and the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio can then
be used to calculate the mass of air trapped in the cylinder. The trapped air mass and
the output from the PID controller as well as the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio are then
used to calculate the needed adjustment of fuel. This fuel mass is then changed to an
injection length through a subVI doing the inverse fuel mass calculation. The injection
length found is then added to the closed loop map and thereby the injection length for
the given engine revolution is adjusted. This change on the injection length in the closed
loop map of course then have an eect on the excess air ratio making it possible to control
this parameter.

During the report there have been made many studies in the attempt of making a opti-
mised engine design that can help the DTU Roadrunners push the limit of fuel eciency
even further. To do so the conclusion of a preliminary study was investigated and proved
to be correct within its assumptions. It stated that a downsizing and weight optimisation
was the best way of increasing the fuel eciency. At the point of this study the track was
at with less turns than the new track in 2016. Here there was introduced a large hill that
had to be climbed. By using a topology map of the new track and the simulation program
DTUdrive, it was proven that the optimisation parameters had changed. As the cars had
to climb the large hill it was better to have more power to accelerate and a high eciency.
At the same time it was shown that based on the driving patterns alone a weight opti-
misation would give 55% less saved fuel on the new track compared to the one from 2015.
These conclusions changed how the project was addressed. Instead of thinking mainly on
downsizing there had to be though about how to keep the power output and increasing
the eciency. This lead to a study of how geometrical engine parameters aect the e-

possible to understand how the stroke-bore relationship (SB), displacement volume (Vd)
and a combination of these two aect the eciency. The last study was made as there
was a lack of time in the project and therefore it was decided to reuse the crankshaft in
the 49cc Yamaha NEOS T4 4-stroke engine that is used currently.
In the study of the SB ratio it was shown that when friction is not considered a high
SB ratio increases the turbulence under combustion and produce more power with the
same fuel amount. This thereby increase the eciency which is desirable. A downside
to increasing the SB ratio is that it produce a higher mean piston speed which should
increase the friction based on some empirical correlations. This means that the SB ratio
may not be increase too much as it also introduce a problem in smaller engines of tting
valves inside the combustion chamber.
Further the study of Vd showed that it is desirable to have a large engine as the heat
loss will be smaller. This should be held against that it would be benecial to use a
small engine if the power output is small. This is due to a smaller engine consume less
fuel than a larger engine and therefore the engine size depend on needs. This left the
last study to clarify if there could be anything to gain from decreasing the piston bore,
thereby decrease Vd and increase the SB ratio. Here both ethanol and gasoline showed
the same tendencies. It is feasible to decrease Vd and at the same time increase the SB
ratio. As the SB ratio is increased the benets from this increase counter work the losses
