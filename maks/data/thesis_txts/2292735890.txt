Abstract
Xylose, followed by glucose, is the main monosaccharide that can be found in
the liquid streams coming from pretreatment of biomass. In order to use xylose
for subsequent processing or reaction e.g. for production of xylitol or xylonic acid,
glucose needs to be removed from such stream, so a pure stream of xylose can be
obtained. One of the most promising alternatives to perform such separation is to
convert glucose into gluconic acid, and then use nanoﬁltration to separate xylose
from the gluconic acid coming from the reaction.
This master thesis investigated the eﬀect of selected operational conditions on the
separation of xylose and from gluconic acid using nanoﬁltration. The separation
of these monomers can improve the economical feasibility of bioreﬁnery by in-
creasing the output value.
Two potential membrane candidates were examined
on a 0.15M molar 9:1 xylose:gluconic acid mixture: NF270, a polyamide membrane
with a MWCO of 200-400 Da, and NTR7450, a polyethersulfone membrane with
a MWCO of 600-800 Da. The NF270 membrane was tested at pH 5.4 and 9.5 at
4 bars and pH 5.4 at 10 and 15 bar. The NTR7450 was tested at pH 5.4, 6.6, 8.1
and 9.5 at 4, 10 and 15 bar, in order to evaluate what are the optimal conditions to
attain right retention of gluconic acid and low retention of xylose (that will be then
recovered in the permeate). The xylose to gluconic acid ratio was 9:1, temperature
was 25◦C and the total concentration was 0.15M. The NF270 was also tested with
a high concentration of 0.85M.
A diaﬁltration model was derived and a diaﬁltration experimenteds was conducted
using the NF270 membrane. The fouling mechanisms were investigated using Her-
mia’s Model.
The NTR7450 oﬀered a low xylose retention 0.08, but also a low gluconic acid re-
tention of 0.68 at pH 9.5 af 15 bar.The NTR7450 showed an increase in gluconic
acid retention with increasing pH at 10 and 15 bar. The NF270 showed a high
xylose retention 0.38 and a high gluconic acid retention of 0.98 at pH 9.5 and 4 bar.
The diaﬁltration experimentsed showed that xylose and gluconic acid could be sep-
arated and thereby obtaining high xylose recovery and purity. The Hermia’s model
indicated that the most predominant fouling mechanism was complete blocking for
the low concentration experiments and cake ﬁltration in the high concentration
experiments, using NF270 at the ideal optimized conditions.

1
Introduction
1.1
Project Scope
The aim of this project is to ﬁnd the optimal working parameters for the separation
of gluconic acid from xylose by nanoﬁltration after the enzymatic convertion of glucose
to gluconic acid, see ﬁgure 1. Furthermore, studies on the fouling mechanisms and the
corresponding ﬂux reduction will be examined to suggest stable operation condition for
separation process. This separation can have a positive impact in order to optimize the
economical feasibility of second generation bioethanol production based on wheat straw.
The ﬁnal output from a complete lignocellulosic biomass treatment contains multiple
species. This project will only be focusing on the binary mixture of xylose and gluconic
acid. Xylose is an important biochemical monosaccharide occurring from degradation
hemicellulose. Xylose has many production opportunities, such as the conversion to xyl-
itol or xylonic acid.
This work includes investigation of the properties of membrane
materials and the inﬂuence of pressure, pH-value, feed concentration and operation mode
on the separation of xylose and gluconic acid. The ideal condition would be a cheap
membrane, which has a high gluconic acid rejection and at the same time a high xylose
permeation, at mild pH and low pressure and high permeate ﬂux.
Gluconic acid is normally not present in the treated biomass, but can be converted from
glucose using the enzymatic reaction in ﬁgure 1, which follows the reactions listed in equa-
tion 1. The conversion of glucose is performed by the glucose oxidase (GOD) enzyme that
transforms glucose and oxygen into the intermediate product gluconolacetone and further
to gluconic acid [50]. Simultaneously hydrogen peroxide is produced, this by-product is
inactivating the GOD enzyme [18]. To reduce the levels of hydrogen peroxide a second
enzyme is added, catalase, that converts it into water and oxygen. The decomposition of
hydrogen peroxide by catalase is faster than the production by glucose oxidase [49]. The
optimal condition for the conversion of glucose to gluconic acid is at pH 5.4 and 35◦C.

1.2
Nanoﬁltration
Nanoﬁltration, NF, is a pressure-driven ﬁltration process that separates particles in the
nano scale region using a membrane. The particles are ranging from 200-1000 Da (g/mol)
and the pore size is ranging from 1-7 nm, where 1 nm corresponds to a molecule with
a molecular weight of 300 Da [1].
NF is considered to be in between ultraﬁltration
and reversed osmosis, and it combines both sieving (UF) and solution-diﬀusion (RO)
mechanisms. In the beginning it was named after the already existing membrane types
UF and RO: Tight UF, loose RO or low-pressure RO. Later on came the idea of naming
it nanoﬁltration based on the pore-size, which for some of the membrane is close to one
nanometer in diameter [21]. A general overview of the pore diameter is given in ﬁgure 3

1.2.1
Operational Modes
The two most common operational modes in membrane technology are dead-end and
cross ﬂow ﬁltration. The cross ﬂow ﬁltration membrane is typically used in industries
where large throughput is desired e.g. desalination plants [20]. The dead-end ﬁltration is
typically used in industries where the output product has a very high price, e.g. pharma-
ceutical manufacturing. The cross-ﬂow membrane can be conﬁgured as a spiral wound,
where membrane sheets are pleated in a speciﬁc way to allow permeate and retentate to
pass through without being in contact after the separation. The purpose of the spiral
wound is to give a large surface area in a compact space that leads to high ﬂow. [21].
The spiral wound are typically set up in parallel to obtain a very high throughput. The
dead-end membrane conﬁguration is simple and is set-up by single sheet of membrane
and the ﬂow is perpendicular to the surface of the membrane. The molecules retained by
the membrane will accumulate in the feed solution resulting in either cake-layer formation
or/and concentration polarisation [20]. A schematic illustration of both modes is shown
6
==PAGE:16==
in ﬁgure 6. The cross-ﬂow ﬁltration is also subject to fouling. To reduce the ﬂux re-
duction both systems must undergo regular cleaning. Additionally the membrane system
can have added features to prevent fouling from occurring, examples of such add-on fea-
tures are Vibratory Shear Enhanced Processing, VSEP system or back-shocking [53]. The
VSEP system vibrates the membrane which causes the fouling layer consisting of solids
and foulants to be lifted oﬀthe surface and remixed with the feed solution [52]. The
back-shocking technique applies a high pressure from the permeate side to blow out the
foulants that are stuck inside and on the surface of the membrane. Fouling mechanisms

1.3
Transport Models
The transport model is a tool which can be used to estimate the ﬂow of a solution
(solvent and solutes) through a membrane.
There are three main types of transport
models connected to membrane technology: Phenomenological Transport Models, Non-
porous Transport Models and Porous Transport Models. These models do not apply for
every membrane situation, the model must to chosen to ﬁt the given membrane types.
For example the friction model would be better in describing the permeate ﬂux of gasses
through a porous membrane [57] than the solution diﬀusion model, which is better at
explaining the permeation of liquid through a dense membrane [11].

1.3.1
The Solution-Diﬀusion Model
The solution-diﬀusion describes the transport through dense membranes. The model was
ﬁrst proposed by Thomas Graham, but did not gain much attention until the nineteen-
forties [21]. The solution-diﬀusion model suggests that a solution or compound is initially
dissolved in the membrane, dictated by its solubility of the given component and then
it diﬀuses through the membrane [11]. The diﬀusivity is a combined term including size
and shape of the molecule and its functional groups.
For a polymer the diﬀusion is
governed by the polymeric structure and the chemical behaviour of the repeating unit.
Diﬀusivity is inversely proportional to the molecular size, the large molecule will have a
long residence time. Furthermore, the diﬀusivity depends on the concentration of the feed
solution and it is proportional to the concentration gradient [12]. A simpliﬁed expression
of the solution-diﬀusion model can be expressed as in equation 2
Permeability = Solubility × Diffusivity

1.5
Charge Eﬀects
The ability to apply charge to the membrane is one of the main advantages of membrane
where it becomes superior to ﬁltration processes based on sieving as separation mecha-
nism. The skin layer contains functional groups which can lose or gain electrons inﬂuenced
by acidic or alkaline conditions.
1.5.1
Ions
The active layer of the membrane consists of functional groups, which can become charged.
The surface charge can be changed by adjusting the pH. The NTR7450 membrane is a
polyethersulphone and is regarded a rather hydrophobic membrane [4], but the active

1.7.1
Traditional Methods of Separation
The traditional way of separating monosaccharides is typically done by chromatographic
method. The column material and its chemical properties is of highest importance for the
separation. The solvent with dissolved or dispersed solutes has to pass through a complex
structure. The column material is carefully picked on the basis of the species’ chemical
and physical properties. The column of chromatography is a long thin tube. There are
many diﬀerent types of operating mode in chromatography, including: Size Exclusion,
Ligand Conversion, Partition and ion-Exchange [60]. The size exclusion is very similar to
membrane ﬁltration. Often the packing material is a hydrophilic polymer and the solvent
is usually water with some simple additives. In order for size exclusion chromatography
to be eﬀective a high diﬀerence in molecular size of the solutes is required. The column
26
==PAGE:36==
should have a high tortuosity, to retain the larger particles [60]. The Ligand Conversion
uses a counter-ion in a sulfonated polystyrene gel to interact with the hydroxyl groups
of the sugars to form complexes, which changes the retention time [60]. Therefore the
molecular mass is less relevant compared to number of hydroxyl groups. This technique
is used to separate both mono- and disaccharides.

2.5
Water Permeability
The water permeability is an essential step in the analysis of the membrane performance.
A water permeability test is performed before and after every experiment. The initial
test indicates if the membrane is similar to previously tested membrane. If the ﬂux is not
31
==PAGE:41==
the same, with a variance of maximum ±10%, it can not be characterised as behaving
normally and must therefore be discarded. After an experiment is conducted a second
water permeability test is performed. It reveals if the membrane is fouled or is subject
to pore swelling.
If the membrane is fouled it can generally be detected from visual
inspection, but the test will show a decreased water permeability and it gives a measure
of the severity of fouling. To test the water permeability of a membrane an ultra ﬁltration
cell, Millipore Amicon Stirred Cell Model 8050, with an eﬀective membrane area of 13.4
cm2 is used. The membrane is cut to ﬁt the cell by hand and then put in a 50 vol% ethanol
solution. This step is done to remove the protective layer on the membrane surface, which
is applied by the manufacturer to keep the membrane wet. If this was not applied the
membrane could dry out and the performance of the membrane would be drastically
reduced. Secondly, the membrane is pressurized for 20 minutes at the highest pressure (4
bar), with deionized water passing through the membrane. This is done in order for the
membrane to adjust to the operational environment. The test of water permeability is
then carried out by recording the amount of permeated water over a period of 5 minutes
with measurements taken every minute and an average ﬂow is calculated as average over
the 5 minute period. This is done for varying pressure from 4 to 1 bar.
2.6
Mapping the Work Condition for Gluconic Acid Retention
Experiments to investigate the inﬂuence of pH and pressure on the retention of gluconic
acid was done to ﬁnd the optimal working condition for the separation of xylose from
gluconic acid. Four diﬀerent pH-values were chosen: 5.4, 6.6, 8.1 and 9.5. At 5.4 the
enzymatic conversion of glucose to gluconic acid has its pH optimum, 6.6 is the pH values
when equilibrium has occurred in the gluconic acid and xylose system, 8.1 and 9.5 was
chosen in order to investigate the eﬀect of increasing the pH, and thereby lowering the zeta
potential. The feed solution was prepared as a 20 mL solution for the low pressure and 50
mL for the high pressure experiments with a concentration of 0.15M by using D-Gluconic
acid sodium salt(G9005) ≥99% from Sigma-Aldrich and D(+)-Xylose (1.08692.1000) from
Merck. The pH was adjusted by a solution of vinegar and acetic acid buﬀer for the pH
5.4 solution and by 0.4M NaOH for solution with pH 8.1 and 9.5.

a MWCO of 600-800. The ﬂow at 4 bar is respectively 1.6±10% mL/min and 0.9±10%
mL/min, equivalent to a variation of 44%. The NF270 has a contact angle of 28.8◦and
NTR7450 has a contact angle of 72◦, which means that the NF270 is more hydrophilic
and should theoretically allow more water to pass through. A good agreement between
theory and experimental data is observed for the water permeability.
The membrane material proved to have a major inﬂuence on the water permeability. This
is in good accordance with the information on the contact angle with water; 28.8◦for the
NF270 and 72◦. The higher the contact angle is, the more hydrophobic the membrane
material is. This is clearly the case when comparing the WP of NF270 to the WP of
NTR7450. At low pressure the water permeability of NF270 was 1.6 mL/min and for
NTR7450 is was 0.9 mL/min. V. P. Khare et al. found that polyamide with cross-linked
network structure revealed a higher water ﬂux as well as salt rejection compared to the
linear polyamide [38]. Furthermore they discovered that the hydrophilicity was increased
from hydrolysis of unreacted carboxylic acid groups in the polyamide structure. [38].
This is in good agreement with the ﬁnding by Y. Jin et al who discovered that the
carboxyl groups are critical to the performance of the polyamide membrane (NF270)
(hydrophilicity) [39]. Tremblay, AY et al. described that polysulfone has a hydrophobic
nature and it can be a limitation separating low molecular weight solutes in aqueous
systems [40]. Li, Shui et al reported that polysulfone blended with Cellulose nanoﬁbers
could improve hydrophilic property of polysulfone membrane [41].

could be increased from 0.116 at pH 4 to 0.73 at pH 8 both at 20 bar pressure. Luo et
al. [7] reported an increase in membrane permeability for the NF270 by increasing pH
from 6.84 to 9.56 in the treatment of diary waste water. Additionally, they found that
a swelling eﬀect, caused by the alkaline condition, made the neutral organic solutes pass
through the membrane more easily. Both results are in good cohesion with the results in
this thesis. This is backed up by the study of Braghetta et al. that found a relation of a
more expanded membrane matrix due to greater intramembrane electrostatic repulsion at
high pH combined causing the membrane pore size to be slightly larger [8]. Furthermore,
M. Mänttäri et al. found that the retention of neutral glucose was lower at high pH [4].
The NTR7450 has low rejection of xylose, but the low rejection of gluconic acid will result
in diﬃculties for obtaining high purity and recovery of both xylose and gluconic acid, this
will be further investigated in section 3.9.
The pH was shown to have an increasing eﬀect on the retention of gluconic acid, this is
in good agreement with the theory of surface repulsion (zeta potential) and pH. Boussu,
K. et al. [14] reported that the zeta potential is inversely proportional to the pH for both
hydrophobic and hydrophilic membranes including NTR7450. Diop, S. N. et al. found
that ζ-potential measurements of the NF270 membrane showed that it was negatively
charged in pH range of 4-12, with is minimum at pH 9.5 [42]. The experiment with pH
5.4 did not show any changes in water permeability and the mild conditions are not strong
50
==PAGE:60==
enough to change the membrane structure. When the pH is changed to 9.5 an increase in
water permeability is observed after end experiment. This is strengthening the hypothesis
that a pH can cause a temporary change in the active layer, pore swelling, temporary in
the sense that the swelling can diminish if the operational conditions are changed back
to neutral pH. The pore swelling causes a higher permeation ﬂow of solvent and a lower
retention of xylose, which was demonstrated for both NF270 at 4 bar and for NTR7450 at
10 and 15 bar. The hypothesis of pore swelling would be that high pH (>9.5) will lower
the xylose retention, but keep the gluconic acid high. The hypothesis is backed up by
the high concentration experiment, see section 3.7, with the NF270, where the membrane
was exposed to high pH for a long time. The results revealed both an increased water
permeability (18%) and a remarkable lowered xylose retention (50% decrease) compared
to the low concentration at same conditions. An interesting test to perform to clarify the
relation of pore swelling and retention of xylose would be to pre-expose the membrane
to high pH for a longer time, e.g. half an hour and then perform a separation for the
low concentration (0.15M). To support the suggested hypothesis the results would have
to show a low xylose retention and at the same time a high gluconic acid retention. If the
hypothesis is correct, the NF270 would be a even better candidate for the separation of
xylose and gluconic, which was demonstrated by the diaﬁltration modelling.
KH Mah et al. [17] reports a of a separation factor between glucose and xylose to be 2
using the NF270 membrane, where the inﬂuence of glucose to xylose had a huge inﬂuence
on the separation factor. The highest separation factor was achieved using a ratio of
1:9, a total sugar concentration of 2% and 2 bar pressure.
Sjoman, Elina et al. [16]
found that the separation between xylose and glucose depended strongly on the permeate
ﬂux. For the NF270 membrane highest separation factor of 2.4 was achieved with a high
permeate ﬂux 60
kg
m2 h and a 9:1 ratio between xylose and glucose. Soﬁe T. et al. [18]
achieved a separation factor of 34 between xylose and gluconic acid, by introduction a
new procedure using the NF270 membrane. The conditions were a 9:1 feed molar ratio
of xylose to gluconic acid, 0.15M total feed concentration, pH 9.5, 25◦C and 4 bar. The
ﬁndings of this project are in accordance to the results of Soﬁe T. et al. with a separation
factor of 32, achieved at the same condition.

Conclusion
The water permeability test shows a small increase in ﬂux after separation experiments
with high pH at low pressure. The increase was not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the initial
ﬂux when applying the standard deviation. The diﬀerence in water permeability before
and after an experiment with high pH could be explained by pore swelling, but alternative
analytical methods, like SEM, must be applied to conﬁrm this theory.
The pure water permeability for the NF270 membrane was found to be 18.6±0.6
L
hourm2bar.
The pure water permeability for the NTR7450 was 9.3±0.2
L
hour m2 bar. The conclusion
from the water permeability test is that the membrane material has a higher inﬂuence in
the water permeability than the pore size when comparing the NF270 membrane to the
NTR7450.
The ﬂow reduction was increasing with an increase in pH for the NTR7450 at both 10
and 15 bar pressure. The ﬂow reduction increased from 7% to 11% by changing pH from
5.4 to 9.5 at 15 bar. The ﬂow reduction at 10 bar varied 6% to 13% by changing pH from
5.4 to 9.5. The NF270 was only tested at one pH value (5.4) and the ﬂow reduction was
25 % at 10 bar and 21 % at 15 bar. The ﬂow reduction at low pressure for the NTR7450
was generally at a comparable level as for the high pressure with values varying from 7 %
to 15 %, but no clear tendency on the inﬂuence of pH could be concluded at low pressure.
The ﬂow reduction for the NF270 at low pressure was slightly higher at values of 27%
and 32%.
pH had an increasing eﬀect on the retention of gluconic acid for the high pressure experi-
ments with the NTR7450 at 10 and 15 bar. The retention of gluconic acid for the 15 bar
experiments was 38% at pH 5.4 and 63% at pH 9.5. For 10 bar the retention was 44% at
pH 5.4 and 73% at pH 9.5. The eﬀect of pH was clear and it almost doubled the retention
of gluconic acid. Even though the retention of gluconic acid was improved, the membrane
is not favourable compared to the NF270 which had a retention of 81% for both 10 and
15 at pH 5.4. At low pressure the retention of gluconic acid was increased from 91% to
98% by changing the pH fra 5.4 to 9.5 for the NF270 membrane.
A high concentration (0.85M) experiment was performed using the NF270 membrane at
4 bar, pH 9.5 and keeping the xylose to gluconic acid ration of 9:1. The water permeabil-
ity tests showed an increase in permeability after the experiment, this was explained by
the membrane being exposed to high pH for a longer period for the high concentration
experiment. The hypothesis of alkaline condition causing the pores to swell are backed

The Hermia model was used to investigate the fouling mechanisms leading to a reduction
of the initial ﬂow. The model was applied for both low and high concentration at the ideal
conditions using the NF270 membrane. For the low concentration experiment, the best
correlation between the model and experimental data was found be the complete blocking
mechanism, suggesting that the size of xylose is close to the size of the NF270’s pores. At
high concentration the Hermia model indicated that cake ﬁltration was the predominant
fouling mechanism resulting in the signiﬁcantly reduced ﬂow.
The diatiltration model showed good relation between predicted recovery and purity to
the experimental data for xylose and gluconic acid during 10 rounds of diaﬁltration ex-
periment. The diaﬁltration experiment revealed a diﬀerence in purity and recovery of
both xylose and glucose when changing the permeation volume from 8 mL to 10 mL. A
90% recovery of xylose was achieved after 6 rounds for the 10 mL permeation volume,
whereas for the 8 mL permeation volume, it took 7 rounds to achieve 90% xylose recovery.
The purity of xylose was slightly higher for the 8 mL compared to the 10 mL permeation
volume, but at the same time the recovery was correspondingly lower for the 8 mL per-
meation volume. The model predicts for the purity of xylose and the recovery of gluconic
acid were very accurate. The model predict of xylose recovery and gluconic acid purity
was a little less accurate. The set point of xylose retention had a huge inﬂuence on the
xylose recovery and gluconic acid purity. Five values of the xylose retention were tested
for the xylose recovery and gluconic acid purity. Overall the 38% xylose retention, which
was the measured retention for the system, was the best value for describing the changes
throughout the diatilfration experiment. Another conclusion that can be made from the
model is that a low retention of xylose gives a faster recovery of xylose and a higher purity
of gluconic acid. Lastly, the diaﬁltration model has challenges since the operational con-
dition and the feed solution composition will change during the diaﬁltration experiment.
As more rounds are performed, the feed solution becomes more diluted, this can change
the retention of xylose and the initial value of 38% might not be valid for each cycle. To
correct this a more dynamic model must be derived where the retention of xylose and
gluconic acid are variables instead of constant based on initial conditions.
