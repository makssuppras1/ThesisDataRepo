Abstract
There is a renewed interest for separations based on cyclically operated trays in column
separation processes. Old and recent studies suggest a signiﬁcant increase in tray and
column efﬁciencies with cyclic distillation and stripping. This project presents an inves-
tigation into the possible advantages of cyclic operation mode of a maritime ﬂue gas
desulphurization scrubber. It also deals with commissioning of a pilot-scale NH3 stripper
column located at DTU.
A model was setup describing a cyclic scrubber column. Using realistic exhaust gas
amounts and compositions, the model was solved numerically. In order to do a compar-
ison, a scrubber operated conventionally was also designed using the classic McCabe-
Thiele method. The simulations of the cyclic scrubber showed tray efﬁciencies of up to
1.86. Due to the high tray efﬁciency, a cyclic scrubber with 4 trays was found to obtain
the same degree of separation as a conventional scrubber with 7 trays. Alternatively,
the cyclic scrubber could reduce the required liquid absorbent (seawater) amount with
33.53% and 25.07% on 4 and 7 trays, respectively.
The second part of the project deals with commissioning of a three-staged pilot-scale NH3
stripper column with sequentially operated COPS trays. This part gives a more practical
insight into the possibilities and limitations of cyclically operated separation process.
First, investigations were made regarding the cyclic operating sequence. It was found to
be faulty and was ﬁxed to operate properly. Control of the vapor ﬂow and the supply
of liquid hold ups were investigated. The vapor ﬂow control was found satisfactory,
while the supply of liquid hold ups using a pressure drop method was found to give
relatively consistent hold ups. The weeping limits of all three trays were determined at
various vapor ﬂows, and found to exceed the normal operating hold ups. The reliability
of the pH measurements on the trays were tested and found unsatisfactory to determine
continuous NH3 concentration on the trays. The column temperature regulation system
was thoroughly tested, and the optimal temperature control procedure was found. Point
efﬁciencies were determined by single-tray experiments at a range of M/G ratios. These
were found to range between 0.7 and 0.99, while tray efﬁciency of the bottom tray was
found to range between 1.24 and 1.88. During commissioning, safety features to avoid
ammonia gas leaks were implemented, and an overall safety assessment was carried out.
Based on the experiences gained during the commissioning process, an operating manual
for future use was developed.

1
Introduction
Distillation is a separation technique widely used within the chemical industry. The ﬁrst
use of distillation is believed to have been in China around 800 BC for production of
alcoholic beverages and concentration of essential oils from natural products[1]. In the
past century, as the chemical and petroleum industry expanded rapidly, distillation has
become the dominant separation process with more than 40,000 industrial distillation
columns operating in worldwide[1, 2]. Presently, one of the best known ﬁelds of use
is the reﬁning process of crude oil into naphha, kerosine and gas oil[3]. It is also used
for puriﬁcation of nitrogen, oxygen and rare gases, and for separation of alcohols from
aqueous solutions in the bioindustry[3]. Actually, most products on the market contain
chemicals separated using distillation[1]. However, distillation does require a signiﬁcant
amount of energy. In fact, 40% of the total energy used[2] and 70% of the plant cost[4] in
the chemical industry is attributed distillation, and as the chemical industry in total uses
a signiﬁcant amount of energy, chemical separations account for a staggering 10-15% of
US energy consumption.
Due to the large amount of energy consumed by distillation and the focus on sustainability
in modern day society, optimization or process intensiﬁcation of distillation, and separa-
tion processes in general, is of importance. Within the past decade, several techniques
have been studied including Heat-Integrated Distillation Column (HIDC), membrane
distillation, HiGee distillation, thermally coupled distillation systems, dividing-wall
column and reactive distillation[1, 5]. All of the above operate using the conventional
continuous counter-current contact between vapor and liquid with simultaneous ﬂow
through the column. However, there is also a renewed interest for separations based
on periodically, or cyclically, operated trays. Recent studies show that cyclic distillation
can provide increased column throughput, lower energy usage and better separation
performance compared to conventional distillation[5]. Due to promising studies within
cyclic distillation, expanding the concept of the cyclic operation mode to adjacent types
of column separation, e.g. solvent swap, stripping and scrubbing, is of interest.

2
Background
In this section, the concept of cyclic distillation is brieﬂy explained and the current state
of cyclic distillation development is examined. Cyclic operation mode is not necessarily
limited to classic distillation, and could in principle be implemented into any type of
separation involving a column. Thus, possible applications of the cyclic operation mode
for use in solvent swap, absorption and stripping will be discussed. Additionally, as
this project includes commissioning of a NH3-stripping column, relevant procedures of
commissioning of chemical and process plants will be discussed.
2.1
Cyclic distillation
2.1.1
Concept
Cyclic distillation differs signiﬁcantly from conventional distillation on one fundamental
aspect, namely the operation mode. Conventional distillation runs continuously, i.e.
liquid ﬂows down the column and vapor moves up through the column simultaneously
and uninterrupted. In cyclic distillation, however, the vapor and liquid ﬂows are run
periodically. During the vapor ﬂow period (VFP), the vapor ﬂows up through the column,
while the liquid is held on each tray. The VFP ends and the liquid ﬂow period (LFP)
commences after a prespeciﬁed period of time. During the LFP, the liquid on each tray is
dumped to the tray below without mixing, the reﬂux is added to the top tray and the
feed enters the column. Also the distillate and bottoms product are collected. Once this
procedure ﬁnishes, one cycle is complete and the next cycle begins with the VFP. Figure
1 shows one cycle (VFP left and LFP center), and the VFP of the subsequent cycle (right):
Figure 1: Illustration of the working principle of cyclic distillation. Vapor period (left),
liquid period (center) and vapor period of the subsequent cycle (right)[1].
4
==PAGE:11==
The description above is the very basic idea of cyclic distillation. However, there are
especially two practical difﬁculties to overcome with this procedure; liquid mixing and
interrupted vapor ﬂow. Liquid mixing is the mixing of liquids from different trays
with different compositions during the LFP. The consequence being a reduction of the
efﬁciency increase of cyclic distillation compared to conventional distillation[5, 6, 7].
Meanwhile, interruption of the vapor ﬂow during the LFP could cause pressure varia-
tions throughout the distillation column[6].
In the next section, the original idea of cyclic separation and the current technology of
cyclic distillation are presented, including different methods to overcome the practical
issues stated above.
2.1.2
Original idea and current state of cyclic distillation technology
The concept of cyclic distillation, initially called Controlled Cycling, was ﬁrst mentioned
by Cannon[8] in 1961. The idea was to use sieve or screen plate trays, where the thrust of
the rising vapor would prevent the liquid ﬂowing down during the VFP. I.e. the vapor
velocity would have to exceed the weeping limit. During the LFP, the vapor ﬂow was
stopped, allowing down-ﬂow of the liquid through the holes in the trays eliminating the
need of downcomers on each tray. Cannon argued that this type of cyclic distillation
would increase the capacity of the distillation column compared to a conventional distil-
lation column, make the plate design simpler and cheaper and an increased ﬂexibility as
the operating conditions could be changed easily[8].
The same year, Gaska and Cannon[9] conducted the ﬁrst experiments using packed
trays. A visual representation of the setup can be seen in ﬁgure 2:
Figure 2: Experimental setup used by Gaska et al. using a 6-inch diameter column with
7 plates containing 2 inches of packing showing the VFP (left) and LFP (right)[10].
5
==PAGE:12==
Due to leakage in the setup, extensive data was not obtained. Whether liquid mixing
occurred or not is not stated, but judging from the appearance of the LFP in ﬁgure 2
there must have been some liquid mixing. Despite this, plate efﬁciencies of 110 % were
found[10]. Also in 1961, McWhirter and Cannon[10] conducted further experiments with
a mixture of methylcyclohexane and heptane on a column with 8 packed plates. They
stressed the advantage of cyclic distillation ﬂexibility as experiments showed a clear
link between plate efﬁciencies and overall capacity, and the relative lengths of the VFP
and the LFP. A high average plate efﬁciency of 130% was obtained when the VFP was
active 75% of each cycle, while it dropped to 60% when the VFP was decreased to 43.4%
of each cycle. On the other hand, shorter relative VFP did increase the capacity of the
column. Both advantages are of interest in industry. An increase in capacity would allow
larger feed ﬂows to be processed, and by increasing tray efﬁciency, one could obtain the
same separation with fewer trays[11]. Alternatively, separations could also be achieved
using less energy with the same number of trays by lowering the reﬂux ratio for cyclic
operation[12]. However, this is provided that the conventionally operated column ratio
of reﬂux ratio to minimum reﬂux ratio (R/Rmin) is not too close to unity, as the minimum
reﬂux ratio is the same for both conventional and cyclic distillation. Su et al.[12] showed
that the higher the reﬂux ratio is, the larger energy savings can be obtained by reducing
the reﬂux ratio using cyclic operation mode.
The ﬁrst mathematical model for cyclic distillation was derived by McWhirter et al.[13]
in 1963. The model showed that the overall column stage efﬁciency was more than 200%
using cyclic operation mode assuming 100% point efﬁciency. The theoretical calculations
were accompanied by experiments on a packed-plate column operating cyclically and
experiments run on conventional columns with downcomers. Running the experiments
with and without cycling on the same column is not representative as conventional
distillation shows higher efﬁciency with downcomers. Results showed an overall plate
efﬁciency of approx. 210% and 80% using cyclic and conventional operation mode
respectively.
During the following decades, some advances were made to improve liquid ﬂow control.
E.g. by Szonyi et al.[14] in 1985. They introduced a new sieve tray design illustrated
in ﬁgure 3. During the LFP, liquid on each tray would drop onto an inclined surface
under the tray leading the liquid to a vessel on the column wall. Once the tray below is
emptied, the liquid from the vessel above would empty. Theoretically, this would avoid
liquid mixing. However, experiments showed unexpectedly low efﬁciencies due to liquid
dropping more than one tray down.
6
==PAGE:13==
Figure 3: Sieve trays with inclined surfaces and vessels on the column wall introduced
by Szonyi et al.[14].
Despite the promising results in the early stages of this technology and improvements in
avoiding liquid mixing, basically no new developments were made during the 90s and 00s.
This was largely due to the inability to completely avoid liquid mixing during the LFP[15]
which hampers the efﬁciency of the cyclic operation mode[5, 6, 7, 15]. In addition, limited
computing power available at the time also prevented accurate simulations[5, 11, 16].
In 2011, however, Maleta et al.[17] presented a new tray design, that would ensure
two important aspects of cyclic distillation: lack of liquid overﬂow during VFP and lack
of liquid mixing during the LFP. This was to be ensured by perforated trays with sluice
chambers shown in ﬁgure 4.
Figure 4: Perforated trays with sluice chambers developed by Maleta[17].
7
==PAGE:14==
During the VFP, the perforated trays inhibit liquid overﬂow by the vapor thrust. When
the vapor ﬂow stops at the end of the VFP, the liquid overﬂows from each tray to the
sluice chambers ﬁxed below. The time this takes constitutes the LFP, after which the next
VFP begins. Here the thrust from the vapor ﬂow opens the bottom of the sluice chambers
allowing the liquid to be dumped onto the tray below. This procedure effectively elim-
inated the problem of liquid mixing of liquids with different compositions. Currently,
this type of trays are used in the food industry at the Lipnitsky Alcohol Plant in Ukraine.
Here a 20,000 L/day cyclic distillation column increases the alcohol concentration and
another column concentrates impurities[16, 17]. In fact, reports have shown that the
cyclic distillation column with 15 trays increased the separation efﬁciency 200-300% as
compared to a conventional bubble cap column with 56 trays[17].
In 2014, Patrut et al.[16, 18] presented a new rigorous model to be used to design
cyclic distillation columns. It was made to be able to handle ideal and non-ideal binary
and multicomponent mixtures. It was tested on a number of case studies from easy
separations such as benzene/toluene to a ternary mixtures of benzene/toluene/o-xylene.
In order to compare conventional and cyclic operation, conventional distillation columns
were also designed using classical methods for each mixture. Results showed that cyclic
distillation required almost half the number of tray to obtain the same purity with the
same vapor ﬂow rate as compared to conventional distillation.
In a paper from 2015, Maleta et al.[11] presented the "perfect displacement model",
which, based on the new sluice chamber tray design, could assume perfect displacement
of ﬂows. In continuation of this model, they also introduced operating lines of cyclic
distillation allowing the ability to determine the number of theoretical stages in a cyclic
distillation column analogous to the operating lines in a classical McCabe-Thiele diagram.
Simulations of distillation of 9% vol ethanol in water on a pilot-scale distillation column
were conducted. Results showed an average Murphree efﬁciency of 100% for conven-
tional distillation on 16 trays, 285% and 145% using cyclic operation mode on 6 trays
and 10, respectively. Through further simulations, they found that steam usage, which
accounts for most of the energy consumption in distillation, was only 1.2 kg/L for cyclic
operation mode as compared to 1.7 kg/L for conventional distillation. The R/Rmin-ratio
is not stated, but as discussed previously, the R/Rmin-ratio must have been large enough
in order for the reﬂux ratio to be decreased when operating cyclically as compared to
conventionally.
As described above, cyclic distillation has usually been operated with two distinct
periods, namely the LFP and VFP. However, in 2016 Toftegård et al.[6] proposed a new
tray design called Cyclically Operated Perforated Sheet (COPS). This design allows an
8
==PAGE:15==
operation mode, where the liquid from each tray is drained sequentially rather than
simultaneously allowing the vapor to ﬂow continuously. The principle is illustrated in
ﬁgure 5:
Figure 5: Illustration of sequential tray drainage operation with 3 trays[6].
In the proposed design, the trays are perforated sheet type trays where the vapor
continuously ﬂows through the perforations, while drainage is controlled mechanically
by a pneumatic cylinder mounted inside the column. The liquid drainage time (LDT) of
only a few seconds. During operation, the bottom tray is opened and drained ﬁrst. When
the bottom tray is emptied, it is closed and the tray above is opened and drained. This
procedure continues until the liquid on all trays are drained to the tray below. Structured
packings are also placed on the trays in order to avoid dumping due to oscillation. In
addition, this also increases the mass-transfer area enhancing the tray efﬁciency. As with
the Maleta tray design, this design and operation mode eliminates the issue of liquid
mixing during the LFP, and as the vapor is not interrupted, pressure variations in the
column are avoided. Experiments on stripping ammonia from water were conducted
with this operation mode and showed column efﬁciency a factor of 2 above that obtained
with conventional sieve trays.
2.2
Other possible applications
Recently, a considerable amount of attention has been paid to cyclic distillation. However,
other applications of the cyclic operation mode in columns are also of interest. These
include solvent swapping, absorption and stripping, where the gains of cyclic operation
mode might also be present. In the following, these unit operations are brieﬂy described,
and the possibility of cyclic operation mode discussed.
9
==PAGE:16==
2.2.1
Solvent swap
Solvent exchange, or solvent swap, is a common unit operation used extensively in
active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) manufacturing processes in the pharmaceutical
industry[15, 19, 20]. Solvent swapping is often applied as different solvents are needed
for different processing steps during production[20]. Solvent swap involves replacing the
API containing solvent (feed solvent) with another solvent (replacement solvent), while
the solute remains solubilized. In industry, constant volume diaﬁltration is often used
when the replacement solvent is more volatile than the feed solvent[15, 21]. However, in
the opposite case where the feed solvent is the more volatile component, batch distillation
is usually employed[15, 21].
Traditionally, the pharmaceutical industry has mostly used batch distillation, but cur-
rently there is a push towards the use of continuous distillation[15]. Therefore, solvent
swap could be an application suited for cyclic distillation. In fact, Wankat[15] developed a
simple model able to be solved using a spreadsheet, describing binary solvent swapping
of a nonvolatile solute while using sequential drainage of the trays. He argues that each
tray in the column acts as a constant volume batch distillation, and therefore naming
the setup the batch stack. A binary system of methanol (feed solvent) and water (replace-
ment solvent) in a three-stage cyclic distillation column was analyzed showing a 25.4%
reduction of solvent amount compared to batch distillation, while maintaining a high
distillate and bottoms concentrations of 99.3% methanol and 90% water, respectively. It is
also stated that this separation would require a ﬁve-stage conventional distillation column.
Methanol is, together with ethanol, iso-propanol, toluene, and methylene chloride,
common solvents used in the pharmaceutical industry[22]. However, distillation of a
binary mixture of methanol and water is rather easy due to a quite high relative volatility.
It could be of interest to develop a more rigorous model to be used on case studies of
more complicated separations used in solvent swapping.
2.2.2
Stripping
In a stripping column, or desorbtion column, a liquid mixture and a gas are contacted in
order to remove certain compounds, often contaminants or pollutants, from the liquid
into the gas[23]. Stripping is used widely in wastewater treatment to remove Volatile
Organic Compounds (VOC) or other pollutants, e.g. ammonia, from wastewater. As early
as 1970, Gerster et al.[7] calculated and conducted experiments for ammonia stripping
from water by air using cyclic operation mode. Good agreement between experiments
and calculations was found with "considerable efﬁciency improvement" for the cyclic op-
eration mode over conventional operation mode. Also, as stated in section 2.1, Toftegård
et al.[6] ran ammonia stripping experiments showing column efﬁciencies increased by a
10
==PAGE:17==
factor of 2.
Thus, promising results have already been found for ammonia stripping using cyclic
operation mode. Another application for cyclic operation mode in a stripping column
could be removal VOCs from wastewater. Common VOCs in wastewater treatment plants
include, but not limited to, toluene, benzene, xylene and chloroform[24].
2.2.3
Scrubber
In an absorption column, the opposite happens where gas mixtures are separated by
contact with a liquid. Preferably, only certain species, often pollutants, are transferred to
the liquid as solutes[23]. This technology is extensively used for cleaning ﬂue gases in
industry, and in the maritime sector, Exhaust Gas Cleaning Systems (EGCS) are employed
to clean exhaust gases in different conﬁgurations. Between 2007-2011, 80% of the fuel
used in commercial shipping was low-grade fuel containing high amounts of sulphur
emitting large amounts of SO2, NOx, CO2, CO, aerosols with particle matter (PM) during
combustion[25, 26]. However, in recent years, efforts have been made to reduce the
environmental impact of shipping as especially SO2 and NOx pose a signiﬁcant threat
to the environment and to human health[27]. Solutions for combined SO2 and NOx
emission reduction is rather complex and costly, and most processes are still in the
development stage[28]. Therefore, separate systems are more likely to achieve emission
reductions.
Reduction of NOx emissions can be achieved by use of natural gas as fuel, or by Exhaust
Gas Recirculation (EGR) where the exhaust gas is recirculated back into the air intake.
This lowers the oxygen intake and increases CO2, slowing the combustion and reduces
the temperature, thus lowering NOx emissions[29]. However, removing NOx from the
exhaust gas is usually still necessary and a common technique is Selective Catalytic
Reduction (SCR)[26, 29].
As for SO2, EU regulations dictate that as of 2015, ships must use fuels with a sul-
phur content of no more than 0.1% in certain areas and as of 2020 fuels with sulphur
content of no more than 0.5% are allowed in European waters. However, fuels with higher
sulphur contents are still allowed if proper exhaust cleaning systems are installed[30].
One way to comply with the regulations is to use low-sulphur fuel containing less than
0.1% sulphur. However, this type of fuel is rather expensive[28], and supply is also an
issue as the reﬁning capacity of low-sulphur fuel is currently insufﬁcient[26, 29]. An
alternative is Flue Gas Desulphurization (FGD). One technique used in the maritime
sector is wet open loop scrubbing, where seawater is pumped up and contacted with the
exhaust gas in a scrubber column. In the scrubber, the SO2 is absorbed as sulfate in the
11
==PAGE:18==
seawater which is discharged back into the sea. High sulphur containing fuels usually
contain around 3.5% sulphur[26, 28] as this is the current worldwide sulphur limit[25].
Current scrubbers are capable of reducing sulphur content to below that of untreated
exhaust gas from fuel containing the regulatory limit of <0.1% with SO2 reductions of
95-99%[26, 28, 29]. In addition, an economic study shows that the use of high sulphur
fuel with a scrubber to clean the exhaust gas has a cost advantage over use of low sulphur
fuel without a scrubber[26].
Plate and packed columns mounted with a liquid distributor ensuring uniform dis-
tribution of liquid are usually used during absorption applications like scrubbing[23].
Much like in distillation and stripping, absorption, or scrubbing, could be operated
cyclically and potentially with the same advantages. In regards to scrubbers on ships,
the advantage of fewer trays and a smaller column might be even more signiﬁcant due to
their weight of up to 55 tonnes[29]. Despite the possible advantages, no investigations
into the possibility of cyclic operation mode in absorption applications have yet been
done.

Commissioning
A three-stage stripper column capable of being operated cyclically with sequential
drainage is located at the pilot plant in building 228 at DTU. Recently, the Labview
systems engineering software has been installed to ease operation of the column. As the
software controlling the system is new and several parts of the setup are rather new, a
thorough examination of the setup should be done.
Such an examination, or commissioning, should be done prior to operating the setup for
scientiﬁc purposes to ensure that the setup runs properly. Commissioning can be deﬁned
as an activity involving testing, calibration and validation of all systems, software and
networks[31]. Key points include ancillary equipment assessment and development of
Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs), including start-up and shut-down protocols[32].
In literature, there are different commissioning strategies. These are ad hoc, where prob-
lems are solved as they appear, template, where checklists developed based on previous
commissioning experiences. However, these strategies only work under the assumption
that commissioning is a simple routine set of tasks, or similar commissionings have been
done. The third strategy, the methodological, on the other hand, uses a more systematic
approach. Here the situational needs are analyzed and the most relevant commissioning
methods are chosen[31].

Problem statement
As described in section 2, there is a renewed interest in cyclic distillation. A range of
experiments and simulations show great increases in efﬁciencies, and as discussed, this
could also be the case in other applications, such as solvent swapping, stripping and
scrubbing. Scrubbing being the only application of these where no investigations have
been made. Therefore, the ﬁrst part of this project involves modelling and simulating a

4.3
Cyclic design
In the following, a cyclically operated scrubber is designed for the same separation.
However, this procedure is more complicated, and cannot be made graphically by a
McCabe-Thiele diagram as was the case for the conventional scrubber. In the following
section, a model describing the system is set up, followed by a description of the design
method, and ﬁnally, simulations are carried out in MATLAB R2018a. The model and the
design method is analogous to the design method for distillation columns developed by
Patrut et al.[18].
4.3.1
Assumptions
The model and simulations are made under the following assumptions:
• Perfect mixing on each tray
• No liquid mixing between trays during LFP
• Constant temperature
• Constant pressure
• Only SO2 is transferred among the phases
• Constant molar hold up and vapor ﬂow rate
The assumption of perfect mixing on each tray is a rather normal, though important,
assumption as inadequate mixing could impact the efﬁciency on the tray. As discussed
in section 2.1, liquid mixing between the trays during the LFP is highly undesirable. In
practice, this is avoided by Maleta-type trays with sluice chambers. Temperature and
pressure is assumed constant throughout the column and time. Variations would imply
changes in the solubility of the SO2. The assumption that the temperature is constant
is fair, but a pressure drop would realistically be observed through the column. Due
to the high solubility of SO2 compared to the other components in the exhaust gas, it
is assumed that only SO2 is absorbed in the water. Some other components would be
absorbed, but not to an extent having an impact on the SO2 absorption. Consistent with
this, the molar hold up and vapor ﬂow rate are considered constant. I.e. the total hold up
on each tray and the total vapor ﬂow rate do not change over time. This is not entirely
true, as SO2 is absorbed by the liquid over the VFP, increasing the total molar hold up
and decreasing the total ﬂow of gas. However, the amount of SO2 in the gas and liquid
phases is very small, and the absorption of SO2 would therefore have a negligible impact
on the total hold up and total vapor ﬂow.

and III derivations made by Lewis[39] in 1936. In conventional column separations,
Lewis assumed incomplete liquid mixing across the trays. Lewis Case I assumes that
the inlet vapor is completely mixed horizontally across the tray. Lewis Case II assumes
that the vapor rises vertically from tray to tray without mixing horizontally, while the
liquid ﬂows cocurrently on all trays. In reality, both cases are highly unlikely to occur.

bottom tray resembles a Lewis Case I tray as the inlet vapor composition is constant
over time, analogous to conventional operation with Lewis Case I where the inlet vapor
composition is uniform horizontally. All other trays above, however, act as Lewis Case II
trays as the entering vapor composition changes over time, analogous to conventional

4.6
Discussion
The higher tray efﬁciencies calculated above explain the reduction of the needed seawater
for the absorption. As shown in table 2, the seawater savings operating cyclically on four
trays is 33.53% and 25.07% on seven trays compared to conventional operation. There are
two opposing aspects to take into account when designing a suitable scrubber, namely
size and liquid consumption. Scrubbers generally weigh many tonnes and are usually
located at the top of vessels potentially making them top-heavy[29]. In addition, the space
required for scrubbers is an issue. It is therefore desirable to minimize the size of the
scrubber. On the other hand, reducing the liquid consumption is also an aspect of interest.
Up to 2% of the power generated from diesel engines on vessels are consumed by the
connected scrubbers where most of the power consumption is associated with seawater
pumping[29]. Thus, there is a trade-off between power consumption and size/weight
considerations. Regardless, the simulations and calculations made here show that cyclic
separation with simultaneous drainage use signiﬁcantly less seawater regardless of the
number of trays compared to conventional operation, and therefore is a more efﬁcient
method for scrubbing exhaust gas.
An issue for scrubbers in general, conventional and cyclic, is variations in exhaust
gas ﬂow. The amount of exhaust gas varies over time as vessel engines do not run at full
speed all the time. Therefore, the seawater pumping to the scrubbers must be regulated
to ensure adequate seawater feed. However, a unique problem with cyclic operation
arises during the LFP. Here the exhaust gas ﬂow to the scrubber must be momentarily
shut off when using simultaneous drainage with Maleta-type trays. This is, of course, not
possible to do during every LFP. Shutting off the exhaust gas ﬂow to the scrubber would
cause a problematic pressure build-up. In order to avoid this issue, a scrubber could
operate with sequential drainage with COPS trays, as illustrated in ﬁgure 5, instead of
simultaneous drainage. During sequential drainage, the trays are drained one at the time
starting with the bottom tray. The advantage being that the vapor ﬂows continuously
and uninterrupted throughout every cycle, even when draining during the LFPs.
37
==PAGE:44==
Currently, despite the obvious advantages of cyclic operation, only two industry-scale
cyclically operated columns are in operation worldwide. These are two cyclic distillation
columns operating at an alcohol plant in Ukraine. The very small number of implemented
systems might be due to the fact that cyclically operated columns are more complex than
conventionally operated columns in terms of moving parts and controllability, and simply
because conventional columns are known to work. Analogous to this issue, in terms of
conventional tray design Sommerfeld[40] stated in 1966: "Lewis Case III is probably the
most common because of designers’ understandable reluctance to install downcomers
in the conﬁguration of Lewis Case II". Despite Lewis Case II tray conﬁguration was
known to be more efﬁcient, the relative complexity of the design compared to Lewis
Case III tray conﬁguration, made Lewis Case III tray conﬁguration the norm. The same
reluctance might apply here. In the next chapter, commissioning of a pilot-scale NH3
stripper column deals with practical issues, controlling and possibilities of a cyclically
operated column.

5.2
Cyclic sequence
During cyclic operation, the vapor ﬂows continuously through both the VFP and the
LFP. It is such with sequential drainage as opposed to than simultaneous drainage. I.e.
the bottom tray, tray 3, is opened and the product drained after which the tray is closed.
Next, tray 2 is opened, the hold up drained onto tray 3 and closed again. Finally, tray 1
opens, drains onto tray 2 and closed, after which the feed pump is activated and the feed
enters tray 1. This is shown in ﬁgure 5.
At the beginning of the project, the operating drainage sequence and ﬁlling sequence
were setup in the wrong order. During operation, tray 1 was drained ﬁrst, followed by
tray 2 and ﬁnally tray 3. The sequence was changed in Eurotherm to the procedure
described above. Also, there was no delay between the drainage sequences. Once one
sequence was ﬁnished, another started. Therefore, a deﬁnable time delay, representing
the VFP, was inserted into the Labview interface. Thus, it was made possible to deﬁne
the length of the VFP simply by inserting the desired number of seconds. Finally, the
start-up ﬁlling sequence was altered. During start-up, the column is, of course, empty.
The sequence for ﬁlling was ﬁxed so that the pump ﬁrst activates and ﬁlls tray 1. Tray 1
is then drained to tray 3 by opening tray 1 and tray 2. Next, the pump activates again
and ﬁlls tray 1, which is drained to tray 2, and ﬁnally, tray 1 is closed again and the feed
pumped onto it. After the ﬁlling sequence, the operating sequence described above takes
over.
In the Labview interface shown in ﬁgure 22, the following parameters in terms of
the sequence are speciﬁed:
• Seconds open [s]
• Seconds between [s]
• Vapor ﬂow period [s]
The Seconds open is the number of seconds each tray is open when draining. It is of
interest to keep the drainage time as short as possible, as one would like the LFP to be
as short as possible. This is due to the fact that during the LFP’s drainage sequence,

entire hold up must be drained from the trays. Therefore, there is a minimum drainage
time. This time length was found by visually inspecting the drainage through the small
windows below each tray during operation. It is found that approx. 7 seconds are needed
for the entire hold up to be drained when operating with large hold ups. The speciﬁable

closes and the feed is added. This delay may be set as low as 1 second, as this shortens
the LFP and does not affect drainage in terms of liquid mixing between the trays. Finally,
the length of the VFP may be deﬁned. This is technically programmed as a time delay
between the sequences, but effectively represents the VFP. Choosing the length of the
VFP is an important parameter and should be deﬁned taking the length of the LFP, vapor
ﬂow, equilibrium data and the hold ups into account.

5.4
Liquid ﬂow
The liquid hold up on the trays is an important parameter during operation. During
experiments, one would want to run experiments over several cycles. Therefore, it is of
utmost importance that the liquid hold up can be deﬁned precisely and be constant from
cycle to cycle. The liquid ﬂow to the stripper column is provided by pump, P2, located
below the feed tank, T2. The pump runs at a constant speed, so a reduction valve, V17,

manually be set between 0 and 100, 0 being completely closed and 100 completely open.
As the stripper column is able to operate conventionally with sieve trays and cycli-

• Batch L [l]
• Batch dP [bar]
• Flow [l/min]

5.5
Weeping limits
During stripping, or other column unit operations like distillation or absorption, using
sieve trays or the cyclic trays, it is the thrust of the vapor ﬂow that keeps the hold up on
each tray. However, if the liquid hold up is too large compared to the vapor ﬂow, the
liquid drops through the perforations in the tray to the tray below. This phenomenon is
called weeping. It is highly undesirable as it results in liquid mixing between the trays
which, as discussed, lowers the efﬁciency.
Experiments were made to determine the weeping limits for all trays at various va-
por ﬂows. This was done on tray 1 ﬁrst by keeping valve V04 between the bottom of
the column and the product reservoir, T3, closed and valve V05 opened. A bucket of
known mass was placed under valve V05. Once vapor ﬂow steady state was achieved,
a large amount of water was supplied to a closed tray 1, while tray 2 and tray 3 were
kept open. The exact amount unknown but large enough for weeping to occur. This was
possible to determine visually below tray 1, and by noticing a steady stream of water
dumping into the bucket. The pressure drop over the tray was continuously followed.

also visually conﬁrmed as the water dumping into the bucket had stopped and weeping
under the tray had stopped. Next, the bucket was emptied and the empty bucket was
again put under V05. The top tray was then opened and the hold up dumped into the
empty bucket. The bucket was weighed, and the total mass noted. This procedure was
repeated on tray 1 after the trays had been taken out and put in again for maintenance

5.8
Point/tray efﬁciencies
One way to describe the performance of the sequentially operated NH3 stripper column is
to determine the point efﬁciency. This gives an indication of the proximity to equilibrium
reached on a tray. Single-tray experiments were set up to determine these at various
vapor ﬂows and liquid hold ups. In addition to point efﬁciency determination, these
experiments also investigate pH-electrodes ability to determine concentrations. Successful
point efﬁciency determination from pH measurements would render time-consuming
titration-based concentration determination obsolete.

5.8.1
Experimental method
Single-tray experiments analogous to those conducted by Gerster et al.[7], were made
where tray 2 was closed and tray 1 and 3 open. Any tray could be used, but tray 2 was
chosen as the valve (V15) was the most accessible for sampling. The vapor ﬂow was set
to 5 mol/s and a suitable hold up of an approx. 0.1M NH3 solution was supplied to tray
2 and held there for 180 seconds. 15 mL samples were taken out at the start and every 30
seconds yielding 7 samples. After 180 s, tray 2 was opened and the liquid collected in a
bucket. The bucket was then weighed to determine the liquid hold up. The feed was
25°C and throughout the experiments the temperature in the column was held constant
at approx. 25°C (± 1°C) by manually controlling V18. pH, temperature and vapor
ﬂow were continuously logged, and the average vapor ﬂow and average temperature
were calculated and used for further calculations. The concentration of each sample
was determined by 0.1M HCl titration using methyl red indicator. The experiment was
repeated with varying vapor ﬂows and liquid hold ups to investigate the effect of the
M/G relationship on the point efﬁciency.

5.9
Safety
The NH3 stripper column operates with rather low NH3 concentrations below 0.1M.
Regardless, liquid spills and gas leaks are undesirable. Therefore, some safety measures
must be taken. To avoid potential gas leaks due to ventilation issues, a pressure switch is
located that the top of the column. In the unlikely case of ventilation failure, the pressure
switch registers this and sends a signal to the computer. Changes in the controlling
software, Eurotherm, have been made to use this signal to automatically shut off the
vapor ﬂow and the feed pump. Spillage and gas leaks could potentially also occur from
other places, e.g. through bad ﬁttings. A NH3 gas detector has therefore been acquired
and is located next to the column. An Abort button is present in the Labview interface to
quickly empty the column to avoid further NH3 gas leaks.
In the Lift setup section of the Labview interface, the column can be lifted and opened
at different sections for inspections and grants the ability to exchange tray types. The
lifting is pneumatically driven. When a section of the column has been lifted to the top,
clamps are activates to ensure the column section stays in place in case of air pressure
failure. In the Labview interface, the activation of the clamps can be seen in Clamps not
active section.
As with all experimental equipment in the pilot plant, a safety assessment of the NH3
stripper column must be made to ensure all relevant safety aspects are evaluated. A
safety assessment was carried out and approved by persons connected to the project and
the pilot plant. The safety assessment is found in appendix D.

5.10.1
Feed tank stirrer
A stirrer is mounted to the feed tank to ensure proper mixing. At the beginning of this
project, a very small stirrer was mounted. Due to the small size, it was only able to reach
the liquid if the feed tank contained more than 300l. Even with this large amount of
feed, the blades were too small to properly mix the feed solution. Therefore, the feed
tank stirrer needed to be replaced. A longer stirrer with larger blades was available and
mounted to the feed tank. In addition to the new stirrer being able to properly mix
the feed, the hole through the top of the feed tank through which the stirrer goes, was

5.10.2
Tray 1 closing failure
The sequentially operated trays are normally open, and close pneumatically by pressur-
ized air. During initial liquid ﬂow experiments described in section 5.4, it was noticed
that the top tray, tray 1, from time to time did not close as expected. The reason behind
this was investigated. The end of the hose leading away from the cylinder below the tray
was monitored, while the tray was opened and closed several times. Every time the tray
closed properly, pressurized air would come out of the hose. However, every time the
tray failed to close, air did not blow out equally hard, meaning that leakage occurred.
The most likely place would be in the cylinder itself as the hose was seen to be intact.
Consequently, the cylinders on all three trays were replaced. Since the replacement of
these cylinders, all the trays have opened and closed properly and never failed.

6
Conclusion
The scope of this project has been to investigate possible advantages of cyclically operated
column separation processes. The project has been divided into two different, yet con-
nected parts: modelling and simulation of a cyclic scrubber column and commissioning
of a pilot-scale NH3 stripper column.
New regulations regarding maritime SO2 emissions have recently been imposed. Instal-
lation of scrubbers on vessels is one way to comply to the regulations. To investigate
the possible advantages of a cyclic scrubber column, a case study of a typical maritime
two-stroke diesel engine was conducted. Realistic exhaust gas amounts and compositions
were used. A model was setup describing a cyclic scrubber column, and the model was
solved numerically using MATLAB R2018a software. In order to explore the advantage
of a cyclically operated scrubber, a conventional scrubber column was designed using
the McCabe-Thiele method. Both scrubbers were designed with 4 and 7 trays. It was
found that a cyclically operated scrubber would save of 33.53% and 25.07% seawater on 4
and 7 trays, respectively. As discussed, the reasoning behind this is the increase in tray
efﬁciency in cyclic operation. Tray efﬁciencies were found to be between 1.47 and 1.86.
Alternatively, it was found that the same degree of separation could be achieved with 4
trays cyclically as compared to 7 trays conventionally. This would reduce the space and
weight of the scrubber column on the vessel, which as discussed, is a desirable trait.
The second part of the project involved commissioning of a pilot-scale NH3 stripper
column located at DTU. First, the automatic cyclic sequence of the column was ﬁxed.
Experiments were made to describe the vapor and liquid ﬂows during sequential op-
eration. The vapor ﬂows were found to be very reliable, as the average vapor ﬂow
over several cycles matched the set point with insigniﬁcant deviation. The supply of
liquid hold up using the pressure drop over the tray was found to a reliable method.
However, variations of up to 2l were observed, and it is therefore recommended to
take out samples to determine the exact liquid hold ups during experiments. Weeping
limits of all trays were found for varying vapor ﬂows. These were found to greatly
exceed the weeping limits found on the same trays in the same column in 1993, and
the weeping limit of tray 1 was found to increase after being reinserted. Therefore, it
is suggested that the weeping limits are tested every time the trays are taken out and
reinserted into the column. pH electrodes were calibrated and found to measure the
pH with good precision. However, the pH measurements were found to be inadequate
in determining NH3 concentrations within the column. The temperature control of the
system was thoroughly tested. The optimal operating procedure regarding temperature
control was found, and the limitations of the temperature regulation mapped. Based
75
==PAGE:82==
on the experiences gained during the commissioning process, a safety assessment was
made, and an operation manual including start-up procedures, operating procedures,
shut-down procedures, safety precautions and pH calibration procedure, was developed.
Finally, single-tray point efﬁciency experiments were conducted. The point efﬁciency
was found to range between 0.7 and 0.99, and a slight trend was observed as the point
efﬁciency increased with the hold up to vapor ﬂow ratio. The tray efﬁciency of tray 3
was found to range between 1.24 and 1.88.
6.1
Future work
6.1.1
Scrubber
As discussed in section 4.6, it is unlikely that simultaneous drainage with Maleta-type
trays can be used in scrubbers on maritime vessels due to interruption of exhaust gas
during the LFP. The sequential drainage method used in the NH3 stripper column could
be applied instead. Modelling and simulations of a scrubber column with sequential
drainage should be made in the future. In addition, further calculations should be done
to determine the actual dimensions and of a cyclically operated scrubber compared to a
conventional scrubber, and ﬁnally, an economic evaluation should be made to investigate
the economics of cyclic separations.
6.1.2
Stripper
During commissioning of the NH3 stripper column, the state of the column and current
optimal operating procedure have been mapped, and several mechanical and operational
features have been improved. However, in order for the column to operate even better,
further work must be done.
• As discussed in section 5.4.1, the reduction valve V17 needs replacement to facilitate
conventional sieve tray operation in the column. A new reduction valve of correct
dimensions should be installed.
• The positioning of the pH electrodes on the trays could be optimized. During
commissioning, it was deemed that the vertical positions are acceptable. How-
ever, moving the pH electrodes further into the column might improve the pH
measurements within the column.
• Several PI and PID loops are present in the system to control and regulate various
aspects of operation. During commissioning, PI and PID parameters have not been
optimized. The control of the vapor ﬂow and feed tank temperature were found to
work successfully. However, the temperature regulation within the column could
use tuning. Also, after a new reduction valve V17 is installed and tested, FIC1
might need tuning.

E
Operating manual
Open Labview on the computer next to the column and turn on main switch on the
switchboard. In Labview, open Data ﬁle, Sripper column and Graph. Figure 66 shows the
left hand side of Labview interface window from which operation is controlled. The
right hand side of this window shows the system in a PI diagram type manner with
most continuous measurements. Figure 67 shows a PI diagram of the column including
enumeration of the equipment. The procedures below refer to these ﬁgures.
E.1
Start-up procedure
First, the feed of desired NH3 concentration should be supplied to the the feed tank, T2.
Make sure valve V11 below the feed tank is closed, V10 open, and valve V13 between the
feed tank and product tank is open. Add water by opening valve V02. The feed tank can
hold 400l. On the side of the tank, a level indicator is written with a marker showing the
liquid volume in the tank. Note, the heater in the feed tank is rather small and heats the
liquid slowly. Therefore, do not ﬁll 400l in the tank. 200l is enough for many cycles. After
water ﬁlling, close V13. Next, 13.3M NH3 is weighed in a ﬂask under in fume cupboard.
Protective glasses, lab coat, gloves and breathing equipment must be worn when handling
the concentrated ammonia. After weighing, the ﬂask is sealed and carefully carried to the
feed tank and poured into the feed tank. Remember to close the feed tank lid. Turn on the
feed tank stirrer on the switchboard to ensure uniform concentration of the feed. Note,
the stirrer can only run for a couple of minutes at a time without overheating. Therefore,
turn it off for a short period of time every couple of minutes. The amount of 13.3M NH3
should be calculated to yield the desired concentration in the feed tank (maximum 0.1 M).
The feed tank heater is turned on on the switchboard. The feed temperature set point
is speciﬁed in the Labview interface under Setpoint HE2. Note, the feed temperature
should be higher than the actual desired operating column temperature. For short VFPs
(<50s) and a desired column temperature of 25°C, set the feed temperature to 26°C and
for a column temperature 30°C set the feed temperature to 32°C. Slightly increase the
temperature of the feed when operating with longer VFPs. The feed tank temperature
may be followed on the computer next to the Labview interface and in the graph window
once this is set up.
While waiting for the feed tank temperature to reach the set point, setup the real-time
graph and data logging. The Labview graph window is shown in ﬁgure 64.
A31
==PAGE:119==
Figure 64: LabView graph window.
On the right hand side of the Labview graph window, one can choose relevant measure-
ments to be plotted. These include the vapor ﬂow, G, temperature in the feed tank and on
each tray, and pH and pressure on each tray. Two y-axis scales are available. By pressing
on the colored graph next to the chosen measurements, the y-axis scale can be chosen.
To log data, open the Data ﬁle window (ﬁgure 65). Note, Labview will not automatically
choose the same data chosen for the graph.
Figure 65: LabView Data ﬁle window.

==PAGE:120==
Under Select data lines for ﬁle logging, choose the relevant measurements, or simply select
all by pressing Select all. The ﬁle header is written in the ﬁeld on the right hand side of
the window. Press the Start and choose the location where the Excel-ﬁle is to be saved.
Data logging will now begin. During the experiments and while data is logged, short
comments can be written into the spreadsheet which will be displayed at the time they
are added. The Excel-ﬁle is visible in the folder during data logging, but do not open it
until data logging has stopped as this potentially could mess up the data logging. To
stop data logging, simply press the Stop button.
Turn on the ventilation, and turn on the compressor, P4, on the switchboard, and
specify the vapor ﬂow under Setpoint G in Labview. The possible operational vapor
ﬂow is between 4.2-8,5 mol/s (4.2-13.3 mol/s if the ventilation safety system is turned off).
Take out the pH electrodes from the 3M KCl storage unit placed on the table and
rinse them with deionized water. The numbers written on the side of the electrodes
correspond to the trays in which they are to be used. Put the electrodes into the corre-
sponding sockets and insert them into the column.
In the Labview interface, specify the VFP and the LFP. The length of the VFP is written
in the Vapor ﬂow period box. Seconds open determines the time at which each tray is open
during drainage. This should be set to 7s to ensure drainage of the entire hold up on
each tray. Seconds between is the time between drainage of each tray and the time between
drainage of the top tray and activation of the feed pump, P2. Set this to 1s. Select Batch
dP in the Filling type dropdown box. The value here determines the approximate amount
of liquid (hold up) added at each cycle. The input is in mbar. When liquid is supplied at
the end of each LFP, the feed pump activates until the speciﬁed pressure drop over the
top tray is reached. During commissioning of the column, it was found that the dP input
should be between 3 and 10 mbar to ensure liquid hold ups between approx. 3.5l and
11l. Larger pressure drop inputs yields larger hold ups. However, the correlation is not
perfectly linear and the hold ups vary with the vapor ﬂow.
To ensure uniform temperature throughout the column, the temperature of the va-
por entering the column must be heated. This is done in the humidiﬁer, T4. A level
indicator is located on the side of the humidiﬁer. Fill the humidiﬁer with water to approx.
85% by opening valve V03. Make sure valve V08 is closed and valve V09 is open in
order for the water to recirculate from the humidiﬁer through the heat exchanger HE1.
Fully open valve V23, and open valve V19, V20 and V22 to such a degree that PI7 shows
a pressure of approx. 3 bar. In the Labview interface, set the PID TI06 manual/auto to
manual to manually specify the degree at which valve V18 opens. When operating at
A33
==PAGE:121==
approx. 25°C in the column, set it to 85%. When operating at approx. 30°C, set it to
95%. At higher/lower temperatures, specify either slightly higher or lower percentages.
Special case: At vapor ﬂows exceeding 6 mol/s, water from the humidiﬁer will blow into
the column. During operation, open valve V03 when the water level in the humidiﬁer
drops. Ensure that the humidiﬁer does not overﬁll or empty completely. As cold tap
water is continuously added, the temperature in the humidiﬁer will remain quite low,
and automatic temperature regulation is not desirable. Manually set and keep PID TI06
at 100% to maintain column temperature at the found maximum of approx. 22°C.
Once the temperature in the feed tank has reached the set point, experiments can
begin. In order to collect the product, close valve V05 and V06, open valve V04 and turn
on the product pump, P1, on the switchboard. This pump pumps the liquid product
from the product reservoir to the product tank. Turn on the recirculation pump for the
humidiﬁer, P3. This is done in the Labview interface. Graphically follow the temperature
increase in the column (TI06, TI07 and TI08). Once the average temperature in the column
reaches the desired operating temperature, switch PID TI06 manual/auto from manual to
automatic (Not at vapor ﬂows > 6 mol/s). Start the operating sequence by pressing Start
in the Labview interface. If unforeseen problems arise (e.g. gas leaks, liquid leaks etc.),
press the Abort button in the Labview interface. This will immediately stop the sequence,
open all trays and stop the feed pump.
E.2
Operating procedure
The ﬁlling sequence will begin where the pump activates and ﬁlls tray 1. Tray 1 is then
drained to tray 3 by opening tray 1 and tray 2. Next, the pump activates again and ﬁlls
tray 1, which is drained to tray 2, and ﬁnally, tray 1 is closed again and the feed pumped
onto it. After the ﬁlling sequence, the operating sequence automatically begins with a
VFP. Note, the vapor ﬂow decreases quite signiﬁcantly during the ﬁlling sequence, but
pseudo steady state is found rather quickly once the operating sequence begins.
During operation, the hold ups should be determined. This is done when the bot-
tom tray drains by closing valve V04 and opening valve V05 under which a bucket of
known mass is placed. It has been found that the hold ups vary slightly, so do this for
a couple of hold ups. Weigh the liquid-ﬁlled buckets to determine the liquid hold ups.
During the experiment it is also possible to take out sample from each tray using valves
V14, V15 and V16.

==PAGE:122==
E.3
Shut-down procedure
When the experiment is ﬁnished, stop the sequence by pressing Stop in the Labview
interface. Stop data logging by pressing Stop in the Data ﬁle window. Turn off the air
compressor, feed tank stirrer and the product pump on the switchboard, and turn off the
recirculation pump, P3, in the Labview interface. Close the steam supply by closing valve
V19, and empty the water from the humidiﬁer. Empty the product tank through the feed
tank by opening valve V13 and V11. Rinse the column by opening valve V01, and rinse
the feed tank with the hose located next the column. Empty the product reservoir to the
drain through valve V06. Take out and rinse the pH electrodes, and put them back into
the 3M KCl storage unit on the table. Finally, turn off the main switch on the switchboard.
In terms of data handling, the temperatures in the column ﬂuctuate slightly throughout
every cycle. However, pseudo steady state is found within only a couple of cycles. The
vapor ﬂow also ﬂuctuates. Use the data logged in the Excel-ﬁle to determine the actual
average temperatures and vapor ﬂows during steady state. This is rather easy as data is
logged every second.
E.4
Safety
Due to health risks associated with ammonia, some safety precautions have to be made.
When handling the 13.3M NH3, protective glasses, lab coat, gloves and breathing equip-
ment must be worn.
However, once the ammonia is diluted in the feed tank and
throughout operation, only lab coat and protective glasses are required. A pressure
switch is located at the top of the column, which will turn off the vapor ﬂow in case of
ventilation failure. However, gas could potentially leak from the column despite proper
ventilation. An ammonia gas detector is therefore located next to the column. In case the
alarm sounds, press Abort to empty the column. Alternatively, turn off the main switch
on the switchboard.
E.5
pH calibration procedure
For pH calibration use pH 4 and pH 7 buffer solutions in glass or plastic beakers. Put
magnets in both beakers. First, put the pH 4 buffer solution on a magnetic stirrer and
dip the pH electrode into the solution. On the corresponding display on the switchboard,
press Menu −→Calibrate −→Sensor 1 −→pH −→Buffer Cal −→Auto −→Start Auto Cal −→
Enter. Once calibration is ﬁnished, choose the displayed pH closest to 4. Take out the
electrode, rinse it with water and dip it into the pH 7 buffer solution on the magnetic
stirrer. Press Enter on the display. Wait for calibration to ﬁnish and choose the displayed
pH closest to 7. Calibration is ﬁnished, press Menu to return to the main menu.
