Abstract 
The serotonin 2A receptor (5-HT2A) is a subtype of the serotonin receptor subfamily that belongs to the 
G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) family. Furthermore, it is also the most abundant excitatory 5-
HT receptor in the human brain. 5-HT2A has been widely used as a target to treat various brain disorders 
such as schizophrenia, depression, and Alzheimer's disease. In this project, a quinoxaline-fused tropane 
was chosen as the primary scaffold to build a collection of second-generation modulators of 5-HT2A. 
The quinoxaline-fused tropane was synthesized in three steps and the synthesis of analogues was 
performed directly on the primary scaffold. Three methylene carbon linkers and three ethylene carbon 
linkers were synthesized and fully characterized. In summation, the project amounted to the synthesis 
and optimization of an alkaloid-inspired compound library to identify GPCR modulators in the central 
nervous system (CNS), with special focus on optimizing the selectivity and increasing the bioactivity 
of 5-HT2A agonists.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Alkaloid 
1.1.1 General 
Alkaloids are a class of basic natural organic compounds containing at least one nitrogen atom. They 
are an important resource which are found throughout the natural environment and are often isolated 
from plants. For example, opium alkaloids were found in the opium poppy (Papaver somniferous) [1]. 
Furthermore, they have been used in a wide range of pharmaceutical applications, such as in antimalarial 
(e.g., quinine) and psychotropic (e.g., psilocin) drugs. Regrettably, alkaloids have a history of being 
difficult to isolate from natural sources making them prime targets for synthetic chemists. However, the 
structure of natural alkaloids is often complicated with high stereo diversity which can be challenging 
to broach with traditional synthetic methods. 
The nitrogen atoms contained in alkaloids are frequently found in ring (cyclic) systems such as indole 
alkaloids, which contain nitrogen in an aromatic ring system. Other well-known alkaloid structures are 
quinolines, pyrrolidines, pyridines, pyrrolizidines, and tropanes. Today, organic chemists, biologists, 
and pharmacists still have great interest in alkaloids. The most commonly known alkaloids include 
morphine, atropine, quinine, caffeine, ephedrine, and nicotine. 
1.1.2 Medicinal Application of Alkaloids 
Morphine is one of the most widely known alkaloids, which was first isolated from opium poppy around 
1803 by Friedrich Sertürner. However, it was not until 30 years later (1833) that chemists in Edinburgh 
were able to isolate and purify it on a commercial scale. From the structure of morphine (Figure 1), the 
secondary alcohol and the phenol moieties are important binding groups, though they make morphine 
poorly absorbed orally. Morphine was gradually used in medicine until the hypodermic syringe was 
invented in 1853. Until now, morphine is still one of the most effective painkillers. It acts primarily on 
the central nervous system (CNS) and appears to work by raising the pain threshold, thereby reducing 
the brain’s awareness of pain. Unfortunately, it has many side effects, which include addiction, 
tolerance, and respiratory dependence. For this reason, the medical use of morphine is also limited to a 
certain extent [1].

Codeine (Figure 2) is another alkaloid, which is isolated from opium poppy. It has the same structure 
as morphine, but the phenol group is masked as methyl ether. While codeine can be directly extracted 
from opium, the most efficient way to obtain codeine is demethylation from morphine. This process 
was first completed by Jillian Hagel, a postdoctoral scientist in Facchini's lab, who identified codeine 
O-deaminase (CODM) gene that produces the plant enzyme which converts codeine into morphine 1. 
Codeine has excellent analgesic activity and is mainly used to treat pain, coughing, and diarrhea. 
 
Figure 2: Structure of codeine. 
Quinine (Figure 3) was first isolated from the bark of the Cinchona tree native to Peru in 1820, hence 
it was also called cinchona alkaloid. It is primarily known for its powerful antimalarial effect, but 
unfortunately it also has many side effects, such as headaches, tinnitus, vision disturbances and 
sweating2. More severe side effects include low platelets, deafness, and irregular heartbeats. Nowadays, 
quinine is not so well used, as synthetic drugs are more potent and less toxic2.  
 
Figure 3: The structure of quinine comes from the cinchona species2. 
Nicotine (Figure 4), obtained from the tobacco plant (Nicotiana tabacum), is a potent stimulant and the 
main constituent of tobacco smoked in pipes, cigars, and cigarettes. This alkaloid is highly addictive [1].

Cocaine (Figure 5) is a CNS stimulant and is an active principle of coca bush. Cocaine has local 
anesthetic properties, and its simplification stimulates the development of local anesthetics. 
Simplification is a method which is easier, quicker, and cheaper to synthesize in the laboratory. For 
example, procaine was discovered in 1909, which was obtained by simplifying the structure of cocaine. 
The process involved keeping only the pharmacophore (benzene ring, ester group and nitrogen atom) 
and removing unnecessary functional group, which also removes side effects. However, 
oversimplification may also result in reduced activity, selectivity, and increased side effects. 
 
Figure 5: The simplification of cocaine. 
Natural products have been a major source of new drugs for CNS diseases since the discovery of 
morphine 200 years ago3. Due to the close relationship between natural products and the human brain, 
modern medicine has relied on natural product-based therapies for CNS disorders. A recent report 
estimated that approximately 84% of all drugs approved to treat CNS disorders are natural products or 
natural product-inspired, and 400 clinically approved CNS drugs can be traced to 20 natural product 
scaffolds3. Alkaloids stand out on this list; notable examples of clinical use include morphine, atropine, 
physostigmine, papaverine, and galantamine. 
As seen through the various applications of alkaloids in medicinal chemistry there is a large therapeutic 
relevance for future development. Particularly, in fields such as signal transduction in humans where 
natural ligands often contain a basic moiety, for example GPCR receptors. 
1.2 G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) 
1.2.1 General 
GPCRs are proteins located on the cell surface. By activating guanine nucleotide-binding proteins (G-
protein) that are responsible for signal transduction inside the cell, GPCRs can recognize extracellular 
substances and transmit signals across the cell membrane. The signal delivery is crucial for various 
cellular responses including cell growth, gene transcription, post-translational changes, and 
communication with other cells. These signals can bring on adjustments in the human body to adapt to 
the environmental changes, such as, increased heart rate when feeling threatened or a change in vision 
in response to dim light. 
A GPCR consists of three regions. The extracellular part detects and binds the ligand. Then, the seven-
transmembrane region undergoes a conformational change. Finally, this change activates the 
intracellular C-terminus that triggers the corresponding G-protein. The mechanism of signal

that is detected by the extracellular domain of the GPCR. This binding activates the GPCR by inducing 
a conformational change to the receptor. Further effect depends on the type of G-protein. G-proteins 
are subsequently inactivated by GTPase4.

1.2.2 Trends in GPCR drug discovery 
GPCRs have long received attention as drug targets as they regulate many different physiological 
processes and possess drug action sites on the cell surface. GPCRs are quite important to drug discovery, 
with approximately 34% of all FDA-approved drugs targeting 108 well-defined GPCRs 5. 
CNS diseases still account for a high proportion of GPCR-targeted drugs. Grouping the indications of 
approved GPCR-targeted drugs into higher-level disease revealed that CNS diseases are the most 
abundant, accounting for 124 (26%) of all approved GPCR-targeted drugs. Furthermore, at least 79 
GPCR-targeted drugs are currently in clinical trials for CNS treatments. Serotonin (5-HT) receptor 
modulators belongs to GPCR are of particular interest 5.

1.2.3 5-HT2A 
5-hydroxy-tryptamine (5-HT) or serotonin is a neurotransmitter and its receptors are found in the CNS. 
It is one of the primary targets for antidepressants, in fact around 80% essentially target 5-HT signaling 
6. Besides being implicated in depression, 5-HT has also been found associated with obsessive-
compulsive diseases, social phobia and anxiety. 
There are multiple different 5-HT receptors. With the exception of 5-HT3, which is a ligand-gated ion 
channel, all other 5-HT receptors belong to the GPCR family 7. One example is the 5-HT2 receptor 
family, which consist of 5-HT2A, 5-HT2B and 5-HT2C. The 5-HT2A receptor is a cell surface receptor and 
is mainly coupled to the G q/11 signal transduction protein7, shown in Figure 6. Its activation causes 
excitation through stimulation of inositol phosphatase and increased cytosolic intracellular [Ca2+] 
levels8. Therefore 5-HT2A is important for medicinal development, and it has attracted the attention of 
many researchers as potential therapeutic targets for neurological diseases 9.

1.3 Library Synthesis Strategies 
Biologically active molecules can be identified by high throughput screening (HTS) of small-molecule 
libraries. Typically, the compounds of these libraries often share similar structure. However, when more 
compounds were screened, the number of hits did not increase with the amount screened11. Therefore, 
the diversity of the library in terms of molecular structure and function is more important than the size 
of the library. 
Natural product (NP)-derived libraries, diversity-oriented synthesis (DOS), biology-oriented synthesis 
(BIOS) and Pseudo-Natural Products (PNPs) are four important strategies to meet the demand for 
extended libraries. “Privileged” scaffolds or structures is an important concept in DOS, BIOS and PNPs. 
When the decorative scaffold is modified with suitable and different substituents, it will lead to the 
product binding to different target proteins, thereby improving selectivity 11. 
==PAGE:16==

1.3.1 Natural Product-Derived Libraries (NP) 
As previously mentioned drug design and development has taken great inspiration from NPs. Several 
studies in the early 2000s showed that NPs have more SP3 hybridized bridgehead atoms and chiral 
centers than synthetic small molecules12. Furthermore, due to their increased complex three-
dimensional character, which also correlates with higher success rates in drug discovery projects13, so 
NPs clearly have advantages over synthetic small molecules in the development of drug candidates. 
However, NPs also have some disadvantages. For example, the molecular structure is very complex, 
the extraction takes a lot of time, and its toxicity often limits its clinical potential, so structural 
modification is particularly important14. For this reason, the focus of many researchers is not solely on 
the biological activity of the NP, but they also pay great attention to structurally related compound 
libraries for biological evaluation. Therefore, NP's core scaffold is called "privileged", which refer to 
multiple compounds that share the same core scaffold and are biologically active15. The diverse 
substitution patterns of scaffolds in NPs result in different bioactivity, whilst improving selectivity by 
different functional group. NP molecular scaffolds can be applied to the synthesis of NP-inspired 
compound libraries with simplified, more synthetically drug-like structures. 
1.3.2 Diversity-Oriented Synthesis (DOS) 
DOS aims to generate structural diversity in an efficient method, through the simultaneous synthesis of 
multiple target compounds with a high degree of structural diversity 16. The idea is that the high degree 
of structural diversity result in DOS generating a small-molecule collection with a high functional 
diversity, which enable the coverage of large areas of the chemical space. Importantly, this means that 
not only is the known bioactive chemical space explored, but also unknown regions, that have not been 
tapped into before, will also be explored in an efficient manner. The hope is that these unexplored 
molecules might contain potentially interesting and useful biological properties, which could be further 
investigated for the purposes of developing new drugs for clinical purposes 17. There are two main 
approaches, which DOS employs to achieve scaffold diversity, the reagent-based approach and the 
substrate-based approach 16 (Figure 8).

There is another strategy called the build-couple-pair strategy based on common strategic features

the next phases. In the couple phase the fragments are combined into one molecule. In the pair phase 
the functional groups are coupled in various ways. The result is a set of skeletons with diverse shapes

1.3.3 Biology-Oriented Synthesis (BIOS) 
One major problem with exploring the vast chemical space for novel molecules with medically and 
chemically interesting properties, is that it is too big for an exhaustive search by synthetic chemistry. 
There are simply too many possibilities, making it unfeasible to fully explore it all. Therefore, methods 
that can narrow down the areas with biologically relevant functions are of high value. BIOS is one such 
method, which is based on the basic principles of structural conversation in evolution. Proteins and 
natural products have evolved over time, but important primal bioactivities and corresponding structure 
underlying those functions are often conserved and these can be used to guide the synthesis of BIOS 
libraries. These libraries, based on biologically relevant scaffolds, have compounds collections with 
enriched biological relevance and the hope is that they can be used for discovery of useful bioactive 
small molecules. Overall, BIOS can also be seen as a hypothesis-generating tool, which can pinpoint 
interesting areas of the chemical space for potential novel bioactive molecular scaffolds 18. 
1.3.4 Pseudo-Natural Products (PNPs) 
PNPs are compounds which have similar characteristics to NPs, but they are not found naturally 11. PNP 
libraries are designed through the combination of NP libraries with DOS or BIOS to create new 
scaffolds. This means PNP libraries also have some of the characteristics of the other methods, e.g., a 
PNP library might have specific biological activity, based on the NP-inspired library, while also have 
very complex structures, reflecting the DOS libraries it was based upon. The usefulness of PNP libraries 
lies on the opportunities that arise from combining NP-fragments together to achieve new types of 
privileged scaffolds not seen in nature 19. Depending on the specific NP-fragments combined, different 
chemical and biological functions can be targeted for specific needs, and it is thought that combination 
of NP-fragments can cover wider areas of the chemical space than what is being explored through 
guiding NPs 11. 
The Laraia Lab utilizes this method to synthesize an alkaloid-inspired library. In this library, the group 
has already found several active scaffolds which have agonist activity for both 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C. The 
focus of this project was on improving the biological activity and selectivity of a quinoxaline-fused 
tropane scaffold for 5-HT2R.

3. Aims and objectives 
The objective of the project is to synthesize a second generation of alkaloid-inspired compounds to 
further optimize both the potency and selectivity for 5-HT2A agonism. The lead compounds 6b and 6c 
will be utilized for further investigation of biological activity. To achieve this, variations in N-alkylation 
of the tropane moiety will explore a previously neglected chemical space. 
This idea was inspired by Emil Marcher-Rørsted, who showed that variations of the length of the side 
chains could improve selectivity for the 5-HT2 receptor or a combination of 5-HT2 and D2 receptors22. 
The synthetic plan is depicted in Figure 11 and has been designed to afford extensive investigations 
into the N-alkylated chemical space. The planned synthetic approach for the methylene linked 
analogues, 4, incorporates a single reductive amination of benzylic aldehydes on the lead compound 3. 
However, for the generation of the 2-carbon linker, amide coupling of a carboxylic acid and a 
subsequent amide reduction will be carried out to yield variations of analogue 5.  For the generation of 
the 4-carbon linker derivatives, a series of nucleophilic substitutions on the respective butyl halides will 
be carried out. All the analogues will then be screened for biological activity against various GPCR 
proteins, particularly, the 5-HT2 receptor subfamily.

5. Conclusions and future work 
In this project alkaloid-inspired dichloro-substituted quinoxaline-fused tropanes were synthesized 
successfully in three steps. The synthesis of the alkaloid-like compound was carried out by first 
preparation of α-bromo ketone to afford 1 (61%) followed by a quinoxaline synthesis to afford 2 (42%) 
and lastly, the Boc deprotection to obtain scaffold 3 (93%). The overall yield being 24 %. This scaffold 
served as a starting material for the synthesis of six analogues (4a-4c, 5a-5c, Figure 20) as a 2nd 
generation pseudo-natural product-inspired compound library. Specifically, three methylene linkers and 
three ethylene linkers. Unfortunately, due to time constraints, four carbon linkers were not made. All 
six analogues were fully characterized and submitted for biological screening. 
==PAGE:34==
24 
 
Multiple attempts were made to determine an optimal method for synthesizing ethylene linkers from 
the amine coupling with a carboxylic acid. In the first method compound 5a was easy to synthesize, but 
the reduction step was difficult. Thus, three different reducing agents were tested, however, it was not 
possible to obtain the pure products. Therefore, a second, less convenient, three step approach was 
attempted. Using this method, two further analogues, 5b and 5c, were obtained with moderate yields. 
Unfortunately, three of the synthesized aldehydes were unstable, thus, the reactions either failed or the 
product was lost during purification as possible aldol condensation may have occurred. Therefore, the 
future work could include a look into neutral alumina purification instead of silica gel column 
chromatography. Furthermore, a method for the four carbon linkers should be addressed.

All reactions were run under a nitrogen atmosphere (except for special circumstances) and were

Guduran Si 60 (40-63 mm) silica gel with pressure applied from a handpump.
