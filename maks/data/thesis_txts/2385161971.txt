Abstract 
 
Nowadays, the need of advanced technologies and innovative approaches to better understand and 
monitor vegetation biophysical properties are commonplace. Through multispectral images, 
properties such as chlorophyll and LAI can be highly assessed. Although light differently scatters in 
some directions, traditional methods often consider Lambertian surface, carrying some errors and 
uncertainties. Bidirectional reflectance analysis can provide further knowledge about vegetation 
condition and overcome those errors. 
Vegetation behavior and anisotropy effects were investigated in the Willow-Field located in Risø 
(Denmark) by two approaches. The first was the radiative transfer model PROSAIL, which combines 
PROSPECT leaf radiative transfer and SAIL canopy bidirectional reflectance models. The second was 
based on three flight campaigns conducted with an Unmanned Aerial System (UAS) during the summer 
2017. The UAS used was a hexacopter S900 equipped with an RGB camera, a six band multispectral 
camera (MCA) ranging between the visible and near-infrared wavelengths, an RTK-GPS coordinates 
system and an IMU system, which provided accurate GPS coordinates and navigation angles. In order 
to better understand bidirectional reflectance effects, different sun-target-sensor geometries were 
considered based on the field of view of our MCA and perpendicular flight patterns.  
Overall, similar response was observed from the model simulations and the sensor reflectance, 
showing higher anisotropy effects from backward scattering at larger viewing zenith angles (30⁰) and 
minimum from forward scattering. In the perpendicular line to the incident light, reduced changes due 
to different VZA and VAA were found. Thus, perpendiculars flights to the sun are encouraged. The most 
sensitive wavelengths to anisotropy were the green band 530nm and the red band 670nm with a 
variability of around 20% and 40%, respectively. 
We studied four vegetation indices (NDVI, PRI, PSNDc and OSAVI), from the MCA as well as from the 
model. Anisotropy effects of VI were found insignificant showing a variation of under 4.5% CV. The 
anisotropy indices ANIX and ANIF were investigated. ANIX provided an interesting correlation with the 
nadir reflectance, slightly better in the model than the MCA. Due to the dispersion of the MCA data, 
ANIF was only evaluated for PROSAIL, showing a good correlation. However, it was not validated by 
field data.

Chapter 1 
1. Introduction 
The lack of knowledge is one of the worst enemies to environmental conservation of our planet. Plants, as 
primary producers of biomass are highly beneficial, providing oxygen and ecosystem services that help to 
keep the balance in nature, absorbing CO2 emissions or maintaining soil fertility. Better understanding and 
monitoring of vegetation biophysical properties can help to address and solve local and global environmental 
problems. Another important matter related to vegetation monitoring involves agricultural production and 
research. With a growing population and consumption there is an urgent need of advanced and innovative 
technologies (Burkart et al., 2015). Thus, new approaches to characterize vegetation properties such as Leaf 
Area Index (LAI) and chlorophyll are demanded.  
LAI, defined as the area of a leaf side related to ground area (Duan et al., 2014), is an essential index to 
describe biosphere processes and a key property to model evapotranspiration or gross primary production 
(Meroni, Colombo and Panigada, 2004). Chlorophyll is a pigment that absorbs visible light to perform 
photosynthetic processes (Sims and Gamon, 2002). This green pigment provides the color of the plants and 
controls vegetation dynamics and health (Sims and Gamon, 2002; Süb et al., 2015). 
Vegetation properties are not easy to acquire using field sampling, since they are spatially and temporally 
dependent (Meroni, Colombo and Panigada, 2004). In this sense, remote sensing techniques offer a non-
destructive approach to measure and extract vegetation information. According to Campbell and Wynne 
(2011), remote sensing systems are used to acquire images of the Earth´s surface from distance, using sensors 
that measure reflected or emitted electromagnetic radiation of the surface of interest.  
In this study, remote sensing data were acquired from an Unmanned Aerial System (UAS). UAS provide better 
spatial and spectral resolution, they are more economic and easy to handle, providing data in shorter periods 
of time than other remote sensing acquisition systems (satellites, aircrafts, goniometers and based towers) 
(Berni et al., 2009; Matese et al., 2015; Uysal, Toprak and Polat, 2015). Furthermore, UAS can be equipped 
with different types of payloads carrying various sensors such as thermal and multispectral cameras (Berni 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
2 
 
et al., 2009), hyper spectral cameras (Burkart et al., 2015), photometric elevation mapping sensor (Shahbazi 
et al., 2015) and Light detection and ranging sensors (LiDAR). Due to the large range of possible payload 
combinations, UAS can provide a great variety of applications in agriculture, forestry and environment (Nex 
and Remondino, 2014), turning UAS highly promising.  
In order to explore vegetation dynamics, multispectral and hyperspectral imaging in the optical or solar range 
(400 - 2500nm) have been proved to be very relevant (Cunliffe, Brazier and Anderson, 2016). A unique 
spectral reflectance signature can be observed when plant pigments absorb light in the visible spectrum (400-
700 nm) (Ustin et al., 2009). For instance, since chlorophyll molecules use blue and red light for 
photosynthesis, sunlight is not absorbed equally and a major percentage of green light is reflected, showing 
the green predominant color that we appreciate in vegetation (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). In the near 
infrared spectrum, a significant amount of energy is reflected due to the internal structure of the leaf. 
Changes of spectral features in the visible and infrared ranges might be caused by disease, insect infestation 
or moisture shortage (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). Therefore, by looking at the visible and near-infrared 
spectrum of the plants it is possible to understand photosynthetic processes and detect and monitor foliar 
condition (Ustin et al., 2009).  
Canopies are composed by many leaves with varied dimensions, shapes and orientations. Thus, canopy 
spectral behavior can be determined through the reflectance of leaves within a plant and the canopy 
structure (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). Traditional methods often assume Lambertian surfaces, where the 
light is reflected equally in all the directions (Mobley, 2014). However, the light scattering from canopies is 
preferentially scattered in some directions depending on the vegetation type, the canopy structure and the 
sun position with respect to a target (Govaerts et al., 1996). Therefore, for the same level of pigment contents 
or LAI, the spectral signature of a plant can change depending on the time of the day or the year. The signal 
recorded by a remote sensor will also change depending on the location in regard to the sun and the target 
(surface of the canopy). For instance, larger spectral responses are usually observed in the backward 
scattering (opposite direction as incident light) than in the forward scattering (solar direction) (Kimes, 1983), 
leading to different anisotropic behaviors. Another clear effect is called hotspot, which represents a sharp

Explicit consideration and correction of bidirectional reflectance (BR) or anisotropy effects have shown 
improvements when assessing ecosystem properties such as leaf area index (LAI), Leaf Angular Distribution 
(LAD), vegetation water content, nitrogen, chlorophyll content (Tagesson et al., 2015) and canopy roughness 
(de Moura et al., 2015). On the other hand, the lack of information about illumination and viewing conditions 
or sun-surface-sensor geometry can lead to misunderstandings in the interpretation of spectral signatures. 
An example was found in the Amazon forests, where the consideration of BR effects corrected an apparent 
‘green up’ appearance observed during the dry season that was in fact an artifact related to changes in BRDF 
with the seasons (Morton et al., 2014). 
In practice, measuring BRDF is difficult as infinite solid angles are involved  (Coca, Gilabert and J.Meliá, 2001). 
However, anisotropic reflectance effects can be sampled in space and time based on sun-surface-sensor 
geometric variations (Masek et al., 2014), identified by sun and sensor zenith and azimuth angles as displayed 
in Figure 1. UAS can collect multi-angular remote sensing data in an efficient and sophisticated manner by 
changing flight patterns (Roosjen et al., 2017). In addition, based on the geometric center of an image (nadir 
position) captured by cameras and depending on the field of view (FOV) of the sensor, different off-nadir 
views can be observed at the edges (Burkart et al., 2015). Thus, different sensor zenith and azimuth angles 
respect to a target can be obtained from different UAS positions. 
Another tool to understand BR effects on plant canopies is by using model simulations. Radiative transfer 
models (RTM) simulate how light interacts with leaves and canopies depending on the amount of light

absorbed and transmitted by different biophysical constituents such as pigments or dry matter, canopy 
structure parameters, solar position and the type of light (diffuse or direct). For instance, the models can be 
used in forward mode to understand canopy light interception and how reflectance of vegetation responds 
to changes on sun-sensor viewing geometry (Jacquemoud et al., 2009). PROSAIL is a widely used RTM in 
agriculture applications. PROSAIL is a combination of PROSPECT leaf radiative transfer model and SAIL canopy 
bidirectional reflectance model (Verhoef, 1984). This blending couples spectral and directional variation of 
canopy reflectance to leaf biochemical constituents and canopy architecture, respectively (Jacquemoud et 
al., 2009). RTM can be helpful to perform sensitivity analysis and to retrieve vegetation properties from 
remote sensing data by model inversion (Jacquemoud et al., 2009).  
In order to monitor vegetation biophysical properties, some studies have shown that some vegetation indices 
can estimate properties such as chlorophyll content (Martinez and Ramos, 2015) and carotenoids content, 
which add precious data regarding physiological status of plants (Hernández-Clemente, Navarro-Cerrillo and 
Zarco-Tejada, 2012). Moreover, Vegetation Indices (VI) are a useful tool to assess vegetation properties while 
minimizing anisotropy impacts, caused by sun-viewing angles (Verrelst et al., 2008). 
The objectives of this project were: 1) Assess and characterize reflectance effects obtained from UAS optical 
cameras due to changes in sensor - sun azimuth and zenith angles. 2) Extract vegetation properties from 
vegetation indices acquired from UAS sensors and compare to field estimates. 3) Explore spectral indices 
related to changes in BRDF that could be potentially used to correct anisotropy effects.

 Radiative Transfer Modeling 
In this study we used the Radiative Transfer Model (RTM) PROSAIL to assess reflectance changes at canopy 
level. PROSAIL combines the leaf radiative transfer model PROSPECT with the canopy bidirectional 
reflectance model SAIL to connect the leaf biochemical constituents to the canopy architecture. The 
objectives were 1) better understanding of reflectance responses due to angular viewing variations 
(anisotropy effects) by simulating a range of potential UAS and sun angles that could happen in our study site 
and flight campaigns and 2) helping to identify changes in the MCA reflectance bands corresponding to 
specific site conditions.  
Leaf reflectance was modeled with PROSPECT4, which estimates leaf reflectance, absorption and 
transmittance, based on the quantity of different leaf optical constituents (parameters) such as chlorophyll, 
leaf structure, water thickness or dry matter of leaves, using specific light absorption coefficients for each 
constituent and parameter. PROSPECT4 is based on a previous version of PROSPECT, improving its spectral 
resolution (Verrelst and Rivera, 2016) and widely used in several forest and agricultural applications (Meroni, 
Colombo and Panigada, 2004; Duan et al., 2014). Regarding RTM in the canopy, we used the SAIL model, 
where the parameters that determine bidirectional canopy reflectance include leaf area index (LAI), leaf 
angle distribution (LAD), soil reflectance coefficient together with the relative amount of diffuse and direct 
radiation, hot spot effect, sensor and solar zenith angles and relative azimuth angle. In this project, we 
selected the SAIL model because it considers bidirectional reflectance effects while taking into account a 
limited number of variables and providing a good approximation to reality (Meroni, Colombo and Panigada, 
2004). Furthermore, this model has been applied and validated in several studies (Duan et al., 2014). Figure 
10 represents the PROSAIL RTM, the parameters used and the possible outputs.

while the sun is located straight behind the sensor (Gatebe and King, 2016; Singh et al., 2016). Therefore, the 
hotspot effect is expected to be maximum when the relative azimuth angle is close to zero and minimum

Soil coefficient is associated to soil spectra reflectance which is dependent on soil moisture, surface 
conditions organic matter, particle size distribution and iron content (Sinha, 1986). Since soil reflectance is 
related to water absorption, changes in soil reflection are highly notable in the near infrared and shortwave 
infrared bands (Philpot, 2010), meaning that variations of the soil parameter were expected to be minor in 
the MCA wavelengths, and, therefore, a literature value was taken.  
The solar zenith angle (SZA) is the angle between the vertical line perpendicular to the horizon (zenith) and 
the sun. Likewise, the observer (or sensor) zenith angle (OZA or VZA) is the viewing angle from the UAS 
camera to the zenith (see Figure 12).

The solar zenith angle at a specific time was determined from NOAA calculator (explained in the section Flight 
Campaigns). Moreover, based on the MCA FOV (a maximum opening angle of about 20⁰), observer zenith 
angles were considered between -30⁰ and 30⁰, to also take into account roll and pitch movements of the 
UAS. The Relative Azimuth Angle (RAA) is the angle between sun and sensor in the horizontal plane as shown 
in Figure 12. To evaluate anisotropy effects RAA was ranged from 0⁰ to 180⁰. 
Direct radiation is the shortwave radiation coming straight from the sun to the earth surface. On the other 
hand, diffuse radiation involves scattered light, caused by particles in the atmosphere such as gas molecules, 
water vapor, aerosols and clouds (Spitters, Toussaint and Goudriaan, 1986). The SAIL model presents a 
diffuse/direct radiation parameter based on direct and diffuse light as presented by Francois et al., (2002), 
where a null value of the diffuse/direct coefficient represents 100% direct radiation.

In this section, we assessed which spectral bands show higher reflectance variability and which spectral 
indices are more affected due to changes in viewing geometry, under cloudy and sunny conditions and 
depending on the time of the day based on the sun elevation.  
In order to investigate changes on reflectance and evaluate all the data obtained, statistical approaches such 
as mean, standard deviation and coefficient of variation were used. Since a huge amount of data was 
collected, the mean provided information about the center tendency focusing on angular changes. Standard 
deviation measures the dispersion of the data set, which is considered a good indicator to measure the 
variability found between values (Lane et al., 2009). The coefficient of variation is also a good metric to 
compare the data variation as it normalizes the standard deviation divided by the mean value and can be 
expressed in percentages (Estepa-castro and Ortega-moya, 2006). Therefore, we assumed that the standard 
deviation and the coefficient of variation (CV) were good criteria to measure anisotropy effects.

3.2.1. General Flight Pattern Design 
In order to assess and characterize reflectance effects obtained from MCA due to changes in sensor and sun 
azimuth and zenith angles, we first predefined flights features and trajectories. Since it was important to 
reach high resolution capturing the largest area possible, 90 meters height was selected as the optimum

 Photogrammetry Techniques  
The production of an orthoimage or orthophoto, which is a specific map projection of geometrically corrected 
aerial images, requires detailed geographic information about the positioning of each image (Bakker et al., 
2001). RTK-GPS and IMU sensors are intended to provide accurate GPS coordinates and navigation 
orientation of the UAS improving image georeferencing, defined as the process to assign geographic 
coordinates to each pixel of an image (Bakker et al., 2001). Therefore, an increment of GPS accuracy and 
knowledge of navigation angles leads to a better image correction (orthorectification) and stronger 
relationship between images (mosaicking). 
In this project, two photogrammetry techniques were conducted. The first approach was using the 
commercial software Agisoft PhotoScan and the second method was mainly to investigate a new alternative 
to georeference and orthorectify the MCA images based on RTK-GPS and IMU data. In both photogrammetry 
approaches, RTK-GPS and IMU information were used for one of the flight campaigns (22.05.17). Thus, a 
comparison for these flight campaigns between both techniques was made. In the last two flight campaign 
(26.05.2017 and 18.06.2017), single receiver solution to obtain GPS coordinates was used and an 
interpolation with the MCA images was made. Note that since the navigation payload was in phase of 
development, numerous steps were followed in the most precise manner to transfer the raw data generated 
by the sensors to a feasible format and suitable coordinate system (ETRS89 / UTM zone 33N) and to later 
synchronize with the gathered MCA images. These steps can be found in the appendix.  
Before orthorectification and mosaicking, some preprocessing was conducted. Raw data obtained from the 
RGB camera and the MCA were converted to a feasible format (i.e. tiff). RGB images were transferred with 
ImageDataConvert software and MCA images with Tetra PixelWrench2 software, where we also unified and 
aligned the six bands of our MCA. Vignetting is an effect that darkens the corners of the images, therefore, 
vignetting correction was applied to remove this defect and introduce better implementation. In this process 
the exposure time of each band was considered and predetermined as shown in Table 7.

3.4.1. Orthorectification and Mosaicking with Traditional Stereoscopy 
(Agisoft PhotoScan) 
Agisoft PhotoScan is a commercial software used to perform photogrammetric processing that produces 3D 
model reconstruction based on the overlaying of an arbitrary number of images. Agisoft includes four main 
steps: camera alignment, dense point cloud building, DEM generation and orthophoto generation (Agisoft, 
2014).  
Once the image format was suitable and RTK GPS coordinates were matched with their respective image, the 
cameras were aligned. Agisoft aligns photos by searching common features on the images to match them. At 
the same time, it finds camera positioning and refines camera calibration parameters (Agisoft, 2014).  In 
order to have a better alignment we set the highest accuracy, which increases the performance of the 
alignment producing more precise estimation of the camera position and reduces the error reconstruction 
(Agisoft, 2014; Wulfsohn and Zamora, 2016). To increment the georeferencing quality and to minimize 
uncertainty, ground control point (GCP) coordinates were imported to Agisoft and manually set and 
optimized to correspond to their specific location in each image. The amount of GCP used in each flight 
campaign are shown in Table 8. 
Afterwards, a dense point cloud was built based on the images and their estimated locations. The dense point 
cloud is a step needed in further processes that allows to modify and reduce points in the 3D model (Agisoft, 
2014). The Digital elevation model (DEM), which provides surface elevation of the mapped area, was built 
based on the dense point cloud. Finally, a geometrically corrected total image (orthophoto) of the interest 
area was generated.

the incoming solar irradiance (𝐸 {𝑊𝑚 −2 𝑠𝑟−1}) (ASD Inc, 2002).

Sensors measure short-wave radiation flux in raw digital numbers (DN). Thus, in order to obtain radiance 
reflected by the surface, we collected DN data from the orthorectified MCA images. Then, for each MCA band 
and based on their exposure time shown in Table 7, we applied specific calibration factors derived from an 
integrating sphere procedure (Wang et al., 2017, unpublished). Once radiance reflected from the surface and 
incoming solar irradiance were estimated, we transferred the data to 10 bits, which is the radiometric 
resolution of the MCA to, finally, obtain the reflectance of vegetation surface.  
By looking at the detected reflectance of a specific pixel in the different orthorectified images from different 
locations (varying VZA and VAA), anisotropy effects on reflectance were found. Thus several points in each 
flight campaign were taken. Just as RTM, statistical analysis to evaluate changes on reflectance was 
performed, where the mean, the standard deviation and the coefficient of variation of the data set for each 
flight campaign were calculated. In order to perceive more UAS angular locations, pixels were selected to 
correspond the top of the canopy in the image and where there were high amount of observations. Since our 
focus was to evaluate vegetation behavior and not soil, a restriction was made to evaluate values larger than

 Anisotropy Effects on Vegetation Indices from RTM and MCA 
Vegetation indices (VI) are “based on digital brightness values that attempt to measure biomass or vegetative 
vigor” (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). VI are mainly values obtained from comparison between bands in order 
to correct or potentiate specific characteristics of vegetation. Therefore, VI can add extra information, 
minimizing or exaggerating impacts obtained in determined bands (Verrelst et al., 2008). Although VI provide 
quantitative uses, they are not as effective as LAI for every plant in any situation due to atmospheric 
conditions, type of crop and agricultural practices. Nevertheless, VI are useful tool, for instance, to 
differentiate between vegetative and non-vegetative areas, classify vegetation types and densities and 
determine seasonal variations. (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). 
From VI, leaf biochemical constituents, such as chlorophyll and carotenoids, can be estimated adding 
information about plant condition (Hernández-Clemente, Navarro-Cerrillo and Zarco-Tejada, 2012). Based on 
canopy architecture, VI can be affected by external factors such as viewing angle, soil background, 
atmospheric effects and leaf orientation (Campbell and Wynne, 2011). Thus, VI are key factors to assess 
multi-angular effects caused by sun-surface-sensor geometries (Verrelst et al., 2008).

Based on navigation angles and GPS coordinates, the orthorectified output was not as expected. We found

4.2.1. Comparison between MCA and RTM 
From the reflectance average derived from PROSAIL and from the MCA for the two flight campaigns (see 
Figure 25, in Figure 35 and Figure 37), higher reflectance values are appreciated at 800nm from the MCA 
(about 0.5) than from RTM (slightly over 0.4). However, green bands reflect more from PROSAIL output 
(around 0.1) than from our sensor (over 0.05).  
From the MCA, the highest dispersion was found in the near-infrared wavelength with a maximum value of 
0.08 for the 22.05.17 and over 0.09 for the 18.06.17. However, in the RTM output, standard deviation was 
approximately half of those values. On the contrary, at 710 nm, a peak of around 0.06 standard deviation 
was shown in the model, doubling the values obtained from the camera. Although, at 470 nm similar standard 
deviation of about 0.01 was appreciated in both approaches, at 670 nm the PROSAIL dispersion doubled the 
MCA dispersion, and similar effects occurred in the green bands. More dispersion on the model can be 
connected to the fact that more simulations were made. However, only 9 pixels were selected in the flight 
campaigns. 
Overall, different standard deviation response is observed between the model and the sensor 
measurements. Nevertheless, the coefficient of variation shows slightly similar patterns between data from 
the 22.05.17 and the model, with peaks in 530 nm and 670 nm. The 18.06.17 shows totally different variability 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
56 
 
than the RTM, being steadier and showing less CV in the visual range as compared with the data from the 
first flight campaign. 
Another important contrast between methods is the ranges involved. While in RTM, CV varies from 5 to 50%, 
in MCA fluctuates from 10 to 30% in the 22.05.17 and from 15% to 25% in the 18.05.17, showing less slope 
between bands. This phenomena can be due to the target selection. To sum up, if we consider the agreement 
between data of the first flight with the outcome from the model, the more affected bands due to changes 
on VZA and RAA are the green (530 nm) and the red band (670 nm), providing a variation of around 20% and 
40% in MCA and 30% and 50% in RTM, respectively. 
In order to better evaluate and compare changes due to VZA and VAA, Figure 38 and Figure 39 show the 
reflectance in 6 polar plots for the different MCA wavelengths of the flight campaign of the 22.05.17 and 
compared with the reflectance obtained from PROSAIL. The plots are divided by VZA rings and VAA slices. 
Inside the areas, the reflectance average between observations from the nine selected pixels provide the 
output. In the color scale, blue represents low reflectance, green middle reflectance and red high reflectance. 
Since the flight campaign was performed under sunny conditions around midday, the first scenario with low 
diffuse radiation (10⁰) and high sun elevation (SZA = 35⁰) was used to compare with the MCA outcome. 
Furthermore, the sun azimuth location was set at approximately 142⁰. Note that there is an empty piece at 
30⁰ VZA and between 180 and 210⁰ VAA due to the lack of information. 
Overall, in both methods, large spectral response is shown from backward scattering (approximately 
between 60⁰ and 240⁰) and minimum reflectance is observed from forward scattering (view in the same 
direction as incident light), which correlates well with literature review (Kimes, 1983; Sandmeier et al., 1998; 
Burkart et al., 2015a). In all the visual wavelengths, the spectral response captured by the sensor is lower 
than the obtained from the model. However, in near infrared band the opposite effect is appreciated. 
In RTM, reflectance clearly increases with a rise of VZA in backward scattering in all the bands, showing a 
hotspot at 30⁰ VZA and between 120 and 150⁰VAA. In addition, every wavelength shows a medium value of 
reflectance at nadir position. Low variation is noticed in the perpendicular line to the incident light, which 
induces to consider this flying direction for further flight campaign in order to avoid anisotropy effects. 
However, around 20% variation is perceived in all the bands from the MCA in this line, except for the 530nm 
band. In MCA, more dispersed data is observed. Thus, the hotspot is not as clear as in the model. However, 
the most sensitive bands (530nm and 670nm) show an obvious hotspot between 90 and 120⁰, which is 
relative shifted and relatively lower compared with RTM. Low reflectance is seen at nadir in the green and 
red bands and medium reflectance at 470, 710 and 800nm compared with the other viewing positions.

 Further Discussion 
The aim of the radiative transfer model PROSAIL was to introduce BRDF to get an overview of how reflectance 
was changed due to off-nadir observations for our study site with different cloudiness effects and time 
conditions. In general, models pursue a reality approximation, intended to find specific behavior within a 
significant amount of variables. However, models do not consider numerous external factors occurring in 
reality that might add changes or useful information. Therefore, although models provide a good insight to 
real behavior, they are not 100% reliable and they can overcome extra uncertainties. For instance, PROSAIL 
could have not captured well the willow vegetation, providing different output. In addition, although we 
were very careful in the parameter selection, the model was not validated. Thus, in future work, field data of 
reflectance could be used to validate the model output.  
On the other hand, in this study a UAS carrying a multispectral camera was used. This system provides real 
knowledge about vegetation condition and anisotropy effects. However, more external factors no considered 
in the models (e.g. wind effects, vegetation variability, UAS movement) are introduced. Thus, more 
uncertainties and error are involved. For instance, when evaluating ANIF more noisy data was obtained from 
==PAGE:87==

the MCA than the RTM. Another example can be linked to overlapping errors due to sensor noise, vibration 
and location and correction process of reflectance.  
Although both methods present drawbacks, both can provide precious data to analyze and better understand 
anisotropy effects.

5. Conclusion 
In order to investigate bidirectional reflectance in the Willow-Field during the summer 2017, three flight 
campaigns with a UAS carrying a 6 band multispectral camera were conducted. The spectral response of each 
wavelength was compared with the radiative transfer model PROSAIL, where fours scenarios were explored 
varying diffuse/direct radiation and solar zenith angle. 
From PROSAIL, varying VZA, scenario 1 (sunny 11:30 am) showed changes of less than 5% reflectance. 
However, the other three scenarios varied over 20% reflectance. Although the total mean was more 
significant in the first scenario, the second scenario (sunny 19:30 pm) revealed the highest spectral response 
at 30⁰ VZA, which agrees with the fact that a reflectance increase is connected to a VZA rise. Between 
overcast scenarios (scenarios 3 y 4) similar response was obtained with neglected differences. Thus, changes 
on solar zenith angles practically do not affect bidirectional reflectance obtained from measurements with 
large diffuse radiation. Regarding RAA, higher reflectance was appreciated from backward (0⁰) than from 
forward scattering, more affected in scenarios 1 and 2. Thus, whereas overcast scenarios seem to be more 
sensitive to changes on VZA, in sunny scenarios more changes are perceived due to different RAA. 
In the MCA range, higher dispersion and variation were found in scenario 2, which indicates higher anisotropy 
effects. The bands more affected were the green bands with around 60%CV and the red band with 65%CV. 
In scenario 1, variations were between 45%CV and about 55%CV, respectively. Similar CV patterns obtained 
from data from the MCA during the flight campaign of the 22.05.17 and the model were observed. The MCA 
showed 20% CV at 530nm and 40% at 670nm, which were 10% lower than PROSAIL CV in scenario 1. Lower 
coefficient of variation in the MCA data than the model can be due to lower amount of data selection. The 
18.05.17 flight campaign presented a suspicious steady CV between 15% and 25%. This effect could have 
occurred due to a reduced number of gathered observations or an increased error on orthorectification 
process, for instance. Thus, comparing the first flight campaign with the model, we found higher spectral 
response in the near infrared wavelength captured by the MCA than the simulated by the model. However, 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
74 
 
opposite behavior was presented in the visible bands. Regardless of these differences, the greatest spectral 
response was appreciated from backward scattering (between approximately 60⁰ and 240⁰ VAA, since the 
sun was at around 142⁰) for 30⁰ VZA. From forward scattering, minimum reflectance was observed, 
supported along the study and by other authors. In RTM polar plots, the hotspot was clearly between 120 
and 150⁰ VAA. Since the MCA data was more disperse, the hotspot was only identified in the most sensitive 
bands and a bit sifted compared with the model. In addition, the perpendicular line to the incident light 
showed low variability along different VZA, practically neglected by the model and about 20% variation based 
on the MCA data. Therefore, in order to reduce anisotropy effect, flights directions perpendicular to the sun 
are recommended in future flight campaigns. 
Comparing the first scenario and data from the 22.05.17, VI showed similar behavior with higher variability 
in OSAVI than NDVI, and with insignificant change in PSNDc. Note that PRI was not evaluated due to MCA 
data incoherence, likely caused by significant high differences between the green bands. From model 
simulations, we observed that vegetation indices are affected by anisotropy effects varying considerably from 
different VZA and regarding sun position. However, the variability found was considerably low from the 
model and field data (under 4.5% CV). Thus, anisotropy effects on vegetation indices for our study conditions 
can be neglected, always knowing that there is a maximum variability of about 4% for the selected indices. 
Since we obtained good correlations (R-square between 0.88 to slightly over 1) between the UAS sensor data 
and ground field data, the MCA validation performed with the ASD was revealed successful. On the other 
hand, we found poor correlations between vegetation indices and field ground measurements such as 
Chlorophyll and LAI performed during the flight campaigns of the 22nd of May and the 18th of June, 2017. 
Some explanations to the low dependence might be due to the homogeneity observed on the Willow-Field, 
scarce amount of field measurements gathered and short ranges of dispersion between measurements and 
vegetation indices. Note that vegetation indices showed a quite homogenous pattern more appreciated 
during the flight campaign of the 18.05.2017. 
Two anisotropy indices were investigated, ANIX which provides an overview of the amplitude of anisotropy 
effects and ANIF which normalizes bidirectional reflectance considering the reflectance at nadir position. 
From ANIX versus nadir reflectance, we found excellent correlation from PROSAIL (R2 = 0.93) and slightly 
worse approximation from MCA reflectance, but adequate (R2 = 0.76). Therefore, knowing the FOV of the 
sensor, the hotspot and the minimum value occurring in the same viewing angle, corrections could be made 
or errors could be estimated. However, further analysis on BR effects is needed to validate this approach. In 
order to obtain ANIF with data gathered with our sensor, a lot of complications were found. Thus, this index 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
75 
 
was only assessed based on RTM. We found similar behavior between visible bands with an increase of ANIF 
for higher VZA (maximum at -30⁰, backward scattering). Overall, maximum ANIF were found for the blue and 
red band due to changes of VZA (ANIF = 1.2). ANIF differences among VZA on other bands were practically 
minimum. Regarding RAA, an apparent level off was shown in all the bands. Thus, according to the model, 
we could conclude that ANIF would be need to be applied at 30⁰ VZA for more sensitive bands. However, this 
result cannot be conclusive as further analysis and the use of other field techniques are recommended.  Note 
that due to the dispersion of the data obtained in the Willow-Field, ANIF could not be appropriated to correct 
anisotropy effects from the procedure followed. 
Two different photogrammetry techniques were tested along this project. The first method was relying on 
Agisoft, a commercial software meant to orthorectified images and mosaic maps. Since Agisoft takes long 
periods to process and orthorectified images carrying a possible preventable error, the second approach was 
performed to investigate new alternatives. Thus, we used navigation angles and RTK-GPS coordinates to 
orthorectify and mosaic the area of interest. However, this second method ended to be slower than Agisoft 
with a significant error, showing differences with Agisoft of between 2.4 and 5.3 meters and up to 101⁰. Thus, 
data from Agisoft was applied along the reflectance analysis. After Agisoft processing, the second flight 
campaign in RGB orthophoto showed twice the error of the pixel and 3 to 6 times the error in meters found 
in the third and the first flight campaigns, respectively. These errors likely produced the failed DEM result. In 
addition, around 4 times the distance error in MCA orthophoto was found. Thus, data from this day was not 
used.  
To sum up, we found that anisotropy effects are highly marked from backward scattering at large VZA and 
minimum from forwards scattering. According to the model, perpendicular flying directions to the incident 
light and up to around 20⁰ VZA can avoid anisotropy effects in the MCA bands. However, from MCA field data 
this effect was not as visible as in PROSAIL. Overall, the green band 530nm and the red band 670nm were 
more disturbed than other bands. On the other side, the near infrared wavelength showed the less impacts, 
which can also explain the low VI variability (NDVI, OSAVI and PSNDc) due to changes on viewing angles. From 
different scenarios, we revealed that at sunny conditions, bidirectional reflectance change more with VZA 
while at overcast conditions, it changes more with RAA. Thus, based on the model simulations, since the MCA 
shows a short FOV, future flight campaigns should be conducted under sunny conditions when the sun 
elevation is the highest. From this project, BR corrections were not achieved, the data was highly noise and 
notably uncertainty was supported by numerous approximations. Therefore, ANIF was not very reliable 
approach to correct anisotropy effects.  
==PAGE:91==
 
 
76 
 
As we were expected, more optimistic results were found by model simulations than data gathered with our 
MCA. However, we found a connection, which encourage us to continue this path. With this project we 
increased our knowledge about anisotropy effects and how to overcome some new challenging in future 
flight campaigns. However, further analysis is highly recommended to validate the models, improve the 
procedure and find BR corrections. 
Whereas bidirectional reflectance analysis can provide insights to avoid errors and overcome uncertainties, 
they can only be used for a particular surface and specific period of time due to biophysical and structural 
differences between species and vegetation fast growth along the year. Thus, analysis carried in this study 
can only be used for the Willow-Field in Risø (Denmark) and during summer time.

Steps to convert raw RTK-GPS and IMU data to feasible format and synchronize with the image 
coordinates. 
 
Convert raw observations from the navigation payload sensor and the base station to Rinex format 
with NovAtel Convert software.  
 
With teqc software from the command window, add the kinematic field to the data. 
 
Insert raw modified data and files of the broadcast obits from NASA website (using Filezilla software) 
in LGO software to produce a post-processed kinematic (PPK) solution, which considers the 
movement of the UAS and the day of the year. 
 
Apply a novel tool box designed by D.H. Olesen (DTU-space), where IMU data, GNSS code-based 
position and velocity and PPK position are extracted.  
 
The initial heading of the UAS, the distance between the antenna and the IMU sensor, the start time 
and the end time of the flight need to be set. Therefore, measure the initial heading by positioning 
the UAS with the face to north and based on the origin of the IMU. The distance between the antenna 
and the IMU were measured with a rule in x, y and z axis (see Figure 57). 
 
Synchronize the RTK GPS coordinates with the coordinates triggered by the BBB of the MCA payload. 
 
Convert coordinates to ETRS89 / UTM zone 33N, using KMSTRANS2 software.

Orthorectification method based on RTK-GPS coordinates and 
navigate data 
% -------------------------- IMPORT INITIAL DATA -------------------------- 
% Read images 
folder = 'D:\Veronica\22.05.2017\'; 
filename = struct2cell(dir(strcat(folder,'*.tif'))); 
[n,m] = size(filename); 
  
% Open csv file with coordinates. (Before import DEM) 
filename_coord = 'D:\Veronica\22.05.2017\position2.csv'; 
data_coord = readtable(filename_coord); 
data_coord = table2cell(data_coord); 
[v,w] = size(data_coord); 
  
for ii=1:m 
    % Read bands data creating an array. 
    clear output results data; 
    data = uint16(loadtiff(strcat(folder,filename{1,ii}))); 
    data_coord_n = cell2mat(data_coord(:,2:8)); 
  
    % Coordinates information for specific image 
    E = data_coord_n(ii,1);         % Easting (m): x coordinate of sensor 
    N = data_coord_n(ii,2);         % Northing (m): y coordinate of sensor 
    Al = data_coord_n(ii,3);        % Altitude (m) 
  
    % Navigation data for specific image 
    P = data_coord_n(ii,4)*pi/180;  % Pitch (rad) 
    R = data_coord_n(ii,5)*pi/180;  % Roll (rad) 
    Y = data_coord_n(ii,6)*pi/180;  % Yaw (rad) 
  
    DEM = data_coord_n(ii,7); 
    h = Al - DEM;                   % Height: distance to the surface (m) 
  
    % Rotate image 
    angle = (Y*180/pi);             % Yaw (degrees) 
    data_n = imrotate(data,angle); 
    figure(ii),imagesc(data_n(:,:,1)) 
     
    [px, py, pz] = size(data_n); 
%     px = 1024; 
%     py = 1280; 
  
    % --------------------------- MCA SPECIFICATIONS -------------------------- 
    % These values should not change unless another camera is used. 
    % FOV MCA. 
    alpha = 38.26;                    % opening horizontal angle (degrees) 
    resolution_horizontal = 1280;     % pixels 
  
    betta = 30.97;                    % opening vertical angle (degrees) 
    resolution_vertical = 1024;       % pixels 
     
    % ------------------------------ CALCULATIONS ----------------------------- 
    % Field of view in each sensor position. 
    x = h*2*tan((alpha/2)*(pi/180));  % Picture width on ground (m) 
    y = h*2*tan((betta/2)*(pi/180));  % Picture height on ground (m) 
  
    % The dimensions of the resulting pictures will change:   
    % According to ROLL ANGLE: 
    dR = h/(cos((R+alpha/2)*pi/180));  % right diagonal line (m) 
    hR = h/(cos((R-alpha/2)*pi/180));  % left diagonal line (m) 
    zR = h/(cos(R*pi/180));            % transformed h (m) middle line 
    xR = E + sqrt(hR^2 - h^2);         % x-projection of roll (m) 
  
    % According to PITCH ANGLE: 
    dP = h/(cos((P+betta/2)*pi/180));  % front diagonal line (m) 
    hP = h/(cos((P-betta/2)*pi/180));  % back diagonal line (m) 
    zP = h/(cos(P*pi/180));            % transformed h (m) middle line 
    yP = N + sqrt(hP^2 - h^2);         % y-projection of pitch (m) 
  
==PAGE:110==
 
 
95 
 
    % New projection of sensor coordinates depending on navigation angles 
    center_E = E + sqrt(zR^2 - h^2);        % affected by roll 
    center_N = N + sqrt(zP^2 - h^2);        % affected by pitch 
    center = [center_E, center_N]; 
  
    % projected distances of FOV (m) 
    x_R1 = abs(center_E - xR);                   % Left side 
    x_R2 = abs(E + sqrt(dR^2 - h^2) - center_E); % Right side 
  
    y_P3 = abs(center_N - yP);                   % Back side 
    y_P4 = abs(N + sqrt(dP^2 - h^2) - center_N); % Front side 
  
    % Corners 
    pa = [(center_E + x_R2),(center_N - y_P4)]; 
    pb = [(center_E + x_R2),(center_N + y_P3)]; 
    pc = [(center_E - x_R1),(center_N + y_P3)]; 
    pd = [(center_E - x_R1),(center_N - y_P4)]; 
  
    % Angles from sensor projection to the corner (degrees) 
    theta_a = (atan(y_P4/x_R2))*180/pi; 
    theta_b = (atan(x_R2/y_P3))*180/pi; 
    theta_c = (atan(y_P3/x_R1))*180/pi;  
    theta_d = (atan(x_R1/y_P4))*180/pi;    
  
    % Diagonals from the sensor to each corner (m)     
    da = sqrt(x_R2^2 + y_P4^2); 
    db = sqrt(x_R2^2 + y_P3^2); 
    dc = sqrt(x_R1^2 + y_P3^2); 
    dd = sqrt(x_R1^2 + y_P4^2); 
  
    % Projected corner coordinates of the image rectangle considering yaw 
    A = [(center_E + da*cos((90 - theta_a - Y)*pi/180)),(center_N - da*sin((90 - theta_a - Y)*pi/180))]; 
    B = [(center_E + db*sin((90 - theta_b - Y)*pi/180)),(center_N + db*cos((90 - theta_b - Y)*pi/180))]; 
    C = [(center_E - dc*cos((90 - theta_c - Y)*pi/180)),(center_N + dc*sin((90 - theta_c - Y)*pi/180))]; 
    D = [(center_E - dd*sin((90 - theta_d - Y)*pi/180)),(center_N - dd*cos((90 - theta_d - Y)*pi/180))]; 
     
    % Associate center to image coordinates. Corrected by pitch, roll 
    % and yaw 
    coord_E = zeros(px,py); 
    coord_N = zeros(px,py); 
     
    % New easting and northing arrays.   
    Evector = [A(1),B(1),C(1),D(1)]; 
    Nvector = [A(2),B(2),C(2),D(2)]; 
    minE = min(Evector); 
    minN = min(Nvector); 
    maxE = max(Evector); 
    maxN = max(Nvector); 
  
    cellSizeE = abs((maxE - minE)/py); 
    cellSizeN = abs((maxN - minN)/px); 
    coord_E(:,1) = minE; 
    coord_E(:,py) = maxE; 
    coord_N(1,:) = maxN; 
    coord_N(px,:) = minN; 
     
    % Complete matrix  
    for kk = 1:1:py-1 
        coord_E(:,kk+1) = coord_E(:,kk)+ cellSizeE;  
    end 
    for ww = 1:1:px-1 
        coord_N(ww+1,:) = coord_N(ww,:) - cellSizeN;    
    end           
     
    nameAsc = strcat(strrep(strtok(filename{1,ii}),'.tif',''),'.asc'); 
     
    % Export data to ASC format 
    fid = fopen(nameAsc,'wt');     % name picture 
    fprintf(fid,sprintf('%s','ncols         ',num2str(py),'\n')); 
    fprintf(fid,sprintf('%s','nrows         ',num2str(px),'\n')); 
    fprintf(fid,sprintf('%s','xllcorner     ',num2str(coord_E(px,1)),'\n')); 
    fprintf(fid,sprintf('%s','yllcorner     ',num2str(coord_N(px,1)),'\n')); 
    fprintf(fid,sprintf('%s','cellsize      ',num2str(cellSizeE),'\n')); 
    fprintf(fid,'%s\n','NODATA_value  -9999'); 
    for j=1:px 
        for k=1:py 
               fprintf(fid,' %10.2f ',data_n(j,k,1)); 
        end 
        fprintf(fid,'\n'); 
    end 
    fclose(fid); 
end
