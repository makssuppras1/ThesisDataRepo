Abstract
The rapid development of modern technology is in constant demand of new potential materials. The most
reliable way to test a material is through experiments but this is limited by how fast physical experiments
can be done. Therefore a screening method should be made that makes sure that only the best potential
materials make it to the experimental phase. Numerical simulations are often used for such screenings by
employing computational modeling methods such as density functional theory (DFT). An alternative and
potentially faster choice for such screenings is to employ cluster expansions which is often used for binary
nanoalloy systems.
Cluster expansions are based on discrete variables describing an atomic system’s property, e.g. the energy.
Optimization methods such as simulated annealing are most commonly used for minimization of such
cluster expansions. This project proposes an alternative optimization method for a given cluster expansion
based on relaxing the discrete variables and then using continuous optimization methods.
It was found that a continuous method inspired by topological optimization, molecular dynamics with a
thermostat was comparable to a zero temperature Metropolis method at finding the groundstate energy
configuration. The continuous method was outperformed by simulated annealing but was successful at
consistently finding low-energy configurations of a cluster expansion.

Introduction
Technology is continually evolving at a rapid rate and new ideas are always being tested. Processes are
constantly being improved and optimized and this is partly due to the discovery of new materials. New
materials can have improved properties that are useful for some specific processes and an example is the
search for new nanoparticles that can be used in heterogeneous catalysis. Heterogeneous catalysis is the
process used for ammonia synthesis which consumes around 1.8% of the yearly global energy output as of
2020 [1].
The search for new and useful materials is usually done either experimentally or numerically. Experimen-
tal production of a material allows for qualitative studies that are reliable while numerical production of
materials allows for quantitative studies that exceed at finding candidates for experimental production.
Quantum systems are governed by the Schr¨odinger equation which describes all the relevant physics but
solving the Schr¨odinger equation numerically for multiple particles takes a long time. Density functional
theory is a commonly used approximation of the Schr¨odinger equation which allows calculations of the
system to be made faster. But density functional theory also struggles when the number of particles be-
comes even larger and we are interested in multiple configurations of the same system. Then cluster expan-
sions are functions fitted to some density functional theory results which allows for further approximations
of the system and quicker calculations of possible configurations.
Cluster expansions are numerically made approximations that can be used to study new potential nanopar-
ticles by expressing some property (e.g. the energy) of the atomic system with discrete site variables. The
discrete variables can be used to define what type of atom is located at a specific site in a predetermined
lattice. The energy of an atomic system can be seen as a sum of single site energies, pair interaction ener-
gies, triplet interactions energies and so on. By defining which atoms are situated at each site you get a
value for the energy due to all of these interactions and then the cluster expansion approximation is the
”cut-off” for the number of relevant interactions.
When a cluster expansion has been constructed it is interesting to find the lowest energy configurations.
This can be done by checking the value of each possible configuration of the discrete variables but this
takes a lot of time and therefore numerical optimization methods like simulated annealing are used in-
stead. In this project we propose a relaxation of the discrete site variables and allowing the variables to
change continuously. Then continuous optimization methods employing gradients can be used to find the
groundstate of the cluster expansion.
The continuous variable methods will be introduced, improved and investigated in this project. Then a
conclusion will be made on whether such a method can be used to improve the minimization of cluster
expansions, and then indirectly improving the understanding of new materials.

Theory
This section goes through the atomic system and cluster expansions for the energy of this system. Then it
introduces optimization methods and continues onto the most common optimization method, Metropolis
algorithm and simulated annealing. Then we mention the theory necessary for understanding our contin-
uous optimization methods of cluster expansions.
2.1
Atomic system
The atomic system focused on in this project is a binary nanoalloy consisting of 55 atoms gathered into a
sphere-shaped polyhedron, specifically a cuboctahedron, see figure 1. The atoms are either palladium (Pd)
or platinum (Pt) and the choice of how many of each atom affects the groundstate. The atomic system can
be divided into layers. The first ”layer” is the center atom. The second layer contains 12 atoms and are the
nearest neighbors of the center atom. The third layer contains 6 atoms, the fourth layer 24 atoms and the
fifth layer has 12 atoms.
Figure 1: The atomic system consisting of 55 atoms arranged in a cuboctahedron shown in 3D (left) [2].
Both figures show the system in the groundstate of 38 palladium atoms (blue) and 17 platinum atoms
(grey), i.e. the groundstate for npt = 17. The atomic sites are numbered starting at 0 in the center and
increasing outwards from the center (right).
This atomic system/particle is used for heterogeneous catalysis which can be used in many different areas.
The general idea is to let a gas consisting of some molecules interact with the catalytic particles to split it
up and create new molecules. The energy needed for the reaction between the gas molecules is lowered
by interacting with the catalytic particles. Adjusting how reactive these particles are also adjusts the speed
of a reaction. The atoms in the particle that participate in the reaction are normally the atoms located at
the vertices on the polyhedron due to a lower amount of neighbors. Since the groundstate depends on the
number of Pt and Pd present in the system then the atom placed on a vertex also depends on the number of

2.2
Cluster expansions
A cluster expansion is a quick way to approximate the potential energy of an atomic system making it only
depend on the configuration of atoms in the lattice. It is a sum over products of lattice site variables, si,
which specify the atom on each lattice site, seen numbered in figure 1. The terms in the sum are represent-
ing clusters which are groups of 1 site, 2 sites, 3 sites or more, see (d) in figure 2. Each term is multiplied
with a constant known as the effective cluster interaction (ECI), ci. The ECIs specify how significantly a site
or site interactions impact the potential energy and finding the best ECIs for a system can be quite tricky.
It is usually done using density functional theory (DFT) results for data points and then fitting a cluster
expansions ECIs to the data points [3]. If there is only 1 site in a cluster then the associated ECI only defines
the energy cost of having a specific atom at that site. If there are 2 sites in a cluster then the ECI defines the
interaction between the 2 atomic sites. If there are 3 sites in a cluster then the ECI defines the interaction
between the 3 atomic sites and so on.
An example of a cluster expansion and its clusters can be seen in figure 2. Including all possible combina-
tions of clusters from a system in a cluster expansion gives the correct value for the energy of the system
(if fitted correctly) since all possible interactions are then accounted for. Thereby, a cluster expansion is
similar to a Taylor expansion except with a limited number of contributing terms.
The cluster expansion used in this project is a scalar function used to represent the energy of the atomic
system. For our purposes we choose to focus on a binary nanoalloy system consisting of a set amount of
Pd and Pt always adding up to 55 atoms, as described earlier. Thus, we have 55 atomic sites and the energy
only depends on whether a site ”i” is Pd or Pt which we choose to represent with the discrete site variable,
si as si = 0 (Pd) or si = 1 (Pt). The choice could have been si = 1 and si = −1 instead but this would only
result in different ECIs. Either way, this results in a function of the form

2.3
Finding groundstates of cluster expansions
The groundstate of a system is defined as the lowest energy configuration and for cluster expansions there
are multiple methods for finding groundstates. Since we are finding groundstates we would like to min-
imize the energy of the system. A simple but crude method would be the brute force method of checking
the energy of all configurations and then selecting the lowest energy found as the groundstate. If we have
Pd55 then there is only 1 configuration, Nc = 1 because the palladium atoms are indistinguishable. If we
have Pd54Pt then there are 55 different configurations, Nc = 55 because we can just move the 1 platinum
atom to all 55 atomic sites. If we have Pd53Pt2 then there are Nc = 55(55 −1)/2 = 1485 configurations
and in general the number of configurations is given by the binomial coefficient:

Here we see that the number of configurations becomes very high when the number of platinum atoms is
between npt ≈8 and npt ≈47. With Pd28Pt27/Pd27Pt28 we have the maximum number of configurations,
Nc = 3.82 · 1015 and so we can not simply check the energies of all the possible configurations within a
realistic time frame (and especially if we try systems with more than 55 atoms).
Another method is to start the system in a random configuration and then allowing the atoms in the sys-
tem to physically move around according to physical forces exerted by atoms on each other. This allows a
physical system to relax itself from higher energy states to the groundstate. The problem with this is that
the system might need to overcome physical energy barriers to get into other lower energy configurations.
This can be seen with an example of an atom that is located at the center but has the lowest energy at the
edge. The atom in the center has to pass by multiple atoms on its way to edge which means overcoming
potential energy barriers and moving the system around. Therefore, it would be good to allow for unphys-
ical changes to optimize the system for example by simply swapping atoms around in lattice sites and then
checking the energy of the new configuration without worrying about how the atoms would physically
swap.
Cluster expansions are often optimized by simulated annealing which we will compare our proposed
methods to. The actual groundstates of our cluster expansion were found using the Branch and bound
method and plenty more methods exist which can optimize discrete systems.

2.3.1
Metropolis method
The Metropolis method is based upon the Monte Carlo method that uses randomness and importance
sampling for optimization and can be used both in continuous and discrete systems. In general a random
change is done to a systems variables and the change is accepted or denied depending on a probability
distribution [7].
The decision to accept or deny depends on what sort of sampling we want. For the Metropolis method
we want to visit a position s according to a Boltzmann distribution, exp(−E(s)/T) and we always accept
a new position, s′ when the energy becomes lower in the new position. We also accept positions, s′ with
energies, E(s′) that increase the energy relative to the previous energy E(s) according to the probability
exp(−[E(s′) −E(s)]/T). This is based on statistical physics where the probability of having a specific
energy depends on the energy and how many configurations give this energy.
The Metropolis method routine can be more briefly be summarized as:
1. Set a temperature, T and start at random point, sn.
2. Suggest a random change from a uniform distribution to sn defined as strial.

This can be improved by doing a simulated annealing which has a changing temperature, Tn during the
Metropolis method. Starting at high temperatures allows the system to overcome most potential energy
barriers. Then by slowly lowering the temperature while running the Metropolis method the system can
usually ends up in the optimal configuration with a significant probability but since there are usually
many possible non-optimal configurations then it is possible that it will not reach it. One of the issues with
simulated annealing is that it can struggle with 2 configurations that are separated by a potential barrier
which is significantly higher than the difference in energy between the 2 configurations.
2.3.2
Gradient descent
A gradient descent method works on continuous functions whose gradient can be calculated. The principle
is that the gradient, ∇f of a function, f shows which direction the function increases the most. By going the
opposite direction then we move the in direction where the function decreases/descends the most. Thus,
by following −∇f for a sufficient amount of steps then we either end up at an extrema where ∇f = 0 or
the function diverges, f →−∞.
The interesting situations for us are finite points where the gradient becomes 0. This occurs at maximums,
minimums and saddle points. Using a simple gradient descent it is only possible to get to a maximum if
the starting point is the maximum. Otherwise the function will decrease towards a minimum or a saddle
point. If the function ends up at a saddle point then a small perturbation can help decrease the function
further towards an actual minimum and at these points the gradient descent method is converged.
This method is a good illustrative method of minimization but is not very good in general. There are
methods that include the idea of gradient descent and then build further onto it by introducing velocities,
Hessian matrices and more.
The gradient descent method without perturbations can be summarized as:
1. Set a step-length, h and start at random point, sn.
2. Calculate gradient, ∇E(s)|s=sn
3. Set sn+1 = sn −h∇E(s)|s=sn. Go back to step 2 now replacing sn with sn+1.

2.3.4
Constrained optimization
Optimizing the atomic system using the Metropolis method with constraints is not very tough. A trial
move for the Metropolis method could be to change s1 = 0 into s1 = 1 but this would break the sum
constraint. Therefore, we should suggest swapping sites instead and since 0’s are indistinguishable and
1’s are indistinguishable then only a swap between 0’s and 1’s would change the system. Therefore trial
moves for the Metropolis method would be to swap 0’s and 1’s, e.g. a trial move could be s1 = 0 and s2 = 1
becomes s1 = 1 and s2 = 0.
Optimizing a continuous system under constraints can be tougher. Using Lagrangian multipliers can help
to keep a equality constraint, e.g. the sum constraint in our problem. For inequality constraints we have
to choose a more general method called Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions instead. KKT conditions
employ Lagrangian multipliers for inequalities that are only active when the inequality is broken and oth-
erwise they are 0. Unfortunately, it was difficult to implement in a way that did not affect other properties

3.1
Average energy of system at room temperature
The average energy of the system at room temperature gives a rough idea of how important the groundstate

depends both on the energy where the probability is given by the Boltzmann distribution and it depends
on how many configurations give a certain energy. For example, we could be at a temperature where the
groundstate for the system is less likely overall due to the multiple configurations possible for degenerate
excited energies.

groundstate but manages to consistently find low energy configurations.

3.2.1
Rounding
Allowing a discrete system to minimize continuously might seem strange because it is expected that most
values would take on values that are not 0 or 1. But it was observed that most of the variables take on
value of either 0 or 1. This is possibly due to the variables only having nearest neighbor interactions
and the cluster expansion being multilinear. But a method of forcing all fractions to integer values is still
needed.
If a value is between 0 and 1 when the minimization is finished then it has to be forced to an integer to be
a valid suggestion for the groundstate of a configuration. The more values that need to be forced at the
end, the important the choice of forcing method but only a small fraction of variables take on non-integer
values, see figure 4. Therefore, we choose a rounding method such that the values closest to 1 are rounded
to 1 and the values closest to 0 are rounded to 0. The rounding is done such that the sum of the rounded
variables before and after rounding is conserved for the equality constraint. If there are multiple si with
the same value then the lowest i corresponding to a more central i is rounded first.

or si = 1.

accepted or not but since we choose zero temperature this means we only accept trial moves that result in
a lower energy. Cluster expansions are commonly minimized using simulated annealing but we choose
a supposedly worse method as a baseline by only allowing zero temperature and not a decrease towards

3.3.1
Method
We start the method by choosing a random start configuration of site variables, si which only have values
0 or 1 and add up to npt. Then trial moves of swapping random site variables, si are attempted and only
accepted if the trial move results in a lower energy. We choose to swap si to make sure that ∑i si = npt.
First it was thought that 55 · 20 = 1100 trial moves or 20 sweeps (55 variables multiplied by 20) were enough
to make the system converge. This was chosen since the SLSqP method took roughly 10-20 steps until
convergence and in each step it evaluates the gradient which contains 55 elements. But after a convergence
check (see appendix) it was seen that we atleast needed 100 sweeps (5500 trial moves). The summary of
the zero temperature Metropolis method is:
1. Set a convergence criterion (in our case it is 100 sweeps)
2. Choose random si values that fulfill constraints
3. Perform Metropolis method on E(s) with T = 0.
4. Save final configuration and energy and repeat from step 2 for a set number of runs

3.4
Potential energy barriers
It was noticed that the zero temperature Monte Carlo is better than the stochastic SLSqP method at finding
the groundstate. It was thought that this could be partly due to a potential energy barrier located between
si = 0 and si = 1 that was blocking the SLSqP method. Thus the SLSqP method could be improved if the
potential energy barrier is removed.
Due to the conservation of the sum of site variables then a change of 1 site must mean the change of atleast
one other site. Assuming we only have a 2 sites that can vary then we can define si = x and sj = 1 −x
and only vary x to conserve the sum. When si = 0 then sj = 1 and vice versa. This could give a potential
energy barrier due to the product of these 2 sites ci,jsisj = ci,jx(1 −x) in the cluster expansion which would
result in a quadratic function of x. A counter to this would be to add a quadratic reward/cost term to the
energy function which leads us to the continuation method.
3.5

This method builds further onto the SLSqP method by adding a reward/cost term to the energy and is
inspired by [6]. We define a new energy defined as the ”G-energy”:

We will now optimize the G-energy instead of the normal energy. This can remove the quadratic part in

the reward/cost term it is possible to change the shape of the energy landscape to try and improve a SLSqP
method. When L < 0 then the extra term is a reward term and when L > 0 then it is a cost term. So when
L > 0 we can use the cost term as a method of rounding by making integer values preferred.

coupling a heatbath to the energy such that the minimizer can move across potential energy barriers due
to a temperature set by the heatbath. Comparing the two it can be said that the SLSqP method can be seen
as a ball that rolls downwards and stops rolling due to friction. Our new method will not stop moving and

Compared to the normal continuation model there are more parameter choices to make for the thermal

expressions and kB = 1 such that the temperature is given as meV and is therefore of the same order of
magnitude as the potential energy barriers. The mass, m could be changed to optimize the method.

n = 3000 then the simulations do not take too long either. This could be optimized further with a possibly
lower value for τT or n but we chose to stay on the safe side of slow convergence towards temperature and

Conclusion
In this project we have introduced the atomic system of interest and the theory behind cluster expansions,
the discrete system and its relevance for binary nanoalloys. We mentioned the theory of optimization by
looking at a steepest descent method and then introduced the Metropolis algorithm/simulated annealing
which is one of the most commonly used methods for optimizing cluster expansions. We calculate the
average energy of the atomic system at room temperature using the Metropolis algorithm. Our approach
to the optimization is based on relaxing the discrete system variables allowing for the use of continuous
optimization methods involving gradients. We showed results for these continuous methods, found the is-
sues and improved the methods accordingly. Then we found that the thermal continuation method (TCM)
was the best continuous method which is inspired by topological optimization methods. The TCM could
find the groundstates at least 1 in 100 runs and had a low average deviation from the actual groundstate.
This is important for room temperature simulations since low-energy configurations are relevant for these
simulations. The simulated annealing found the groundstates at least 1 in 5 runs which is significantly
higher but the average deviation from the actual groundstate was 10-100 times higher than the TCM.
Outlook
If we are interested in looking at low energy configurations the stochastic SLSqP might be good enough and
might be faster at finding these configurations. If that is the case then we can use the method to simplify
the system and possibly use either brute force to correctly round all remaining integer values or potentially
implementing another method, e.g. Metropolis algorithm for this purpose.
With these finished optimization methods we should test the methods against bigger systems or periodic
systems to see whether it can outperform the zero temperature Metropolis method, simulated annealing
and potentially other optimization methods.
The variables of the TCM were not fully investigated so we could potentially improve the TCM a bit by
varying τ. It could also be interesting to see whether an implementation of simulated annealing into the
TCM could help by varying T0 during a run.

Issues with use of KKT conditions
Before using a coordinate change for the inequality constraints we chose to use the KKT conditions for the
constraints. The implementation was done in 2 ways and both implementations used rescaling of the si to
fulfill the constraints.
The first implementation said:
1. If ∑i si ̸= npt then change all si such that it is fulfilled.
2. If si < 0 then set si = 0
3. If 1 < si then set si = 1
4. Go back to step 1 and repeat for m times.
Which is a quick way to solve the problem but assumes that the constraints will converge to a valid solution.
The updates made by one step might affect whether another constraint is fulfilled, e.g. step 1 might move
an si such that 1 < si or vice versa. The ”change” in step 1 was chosen as a translation of all si by a constant
but it could potentially have been chosen as a rescaling of all si by a constant.
