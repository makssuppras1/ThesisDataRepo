Abstract
This thesis investigates which actions that needs to be taken by the consultants and
contractors in the Norwegian construction industry to successfully implement drawingless
model-based construction into infrastructure projects. Case studies provided by Multi-
consult AS has been investigated and how they have utilized the beneﬁts and tackled the
challenges by implementing model-based construction. The ﬁndings show that the four
most inﬂuencing factors to succeed with the implementation of model-based construction
are; Competence, Process, Technology and Contracts.
Seven semi-structured interviews along with other research methods ﬁnds that the
competence and willingness to change within the project organization needs to be present.
It needs to be competence within all three other factors. Competence in process and project
phases, competence within technology to tackle technical challenges, and it needs to be
competence within contractual relations.
The communication between the consultants and contractors is the key factor within
the process, leading to many of the beneﬁts regarding model-based construction. A "play-
ing at the same team" mind-set is necessary, and will facilitate the needed technological
innovation. There are several technological challenges, but ﬁndings state the three most
important actions and minimal requirements to accomplish a further efﬁcient model-based
operation, which are "statussetting" (Level of Development), "quality assurance of param-
eters in models" and "the ability of thinking and modelling sequences in design phase".
These three technological factors create a foundation for further technical development
and success.
It is proven that a successful implementation is not fully dependent on a multi-party
contract, but is a very powerful tool which facilitates the other three crucial factors needed
to successfully implement the MBC approach.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introductory
A project can be understood as a temporary production system. A ’Lean project’ is
by deﬁnition these systems when they are structured to deliver a product which maximize
the value and minimize the waste. Consequently, Lean Construction aims to eliminate the
waste of activities and create value to the client. In 1992, Lean philosophy was presented
to the construction industry by Lauri Koskela, and is continuously developing (Koskela,
1992).
The ability to control and inﬂuencing the total project cost is greatest in the design
phase, as the cost of the design changes increases with time and the longer you get into the
project (Forbes and Ahmed, 2011). This recognition has resulted in an increased focus on
the design process. As a result, different integrated methodologies and technologies have
been developed.
The use of Building Information Modelling (BIM) is being developed in the construc-
tion industry. BIM can be used as communication and project platform, as well as a
technical tool. In the wake of the development drawingless BIM projects have started
to make progress in the industry, and in this thesis called Model-based Construction
(MBC) projects. MBC projects are projects focusing heavily on the implementation of new
technology aiming for a completely drawingless environment. Traditionally, designers
produced drawings which represented the design basis, and later 3D- models where created
for visualization. In a MBC project, the models represent the design basis and prevails
over drawings. In MBC projects, the information is only attached to the geometry, and
the client is typically only receiving the model. When MBC is fully utilized it ensures
an integration of all parts improving the quality, collaboration and communication of
the process. The improvements may give an increased understanding, simpliﬁes work
and leads to fewer errors and deviations. In addition, the model could provide increased

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
reliability and better decisions, which could provide a better project results. A conversion
to new working methods is demanding and can therefore cause some challenges for the
various players. The new processes, on the other hand, require changes in organizations,
with redeﬁned regulation and allocation of responsibilities. The boundaries between the
players are obscured and require an exceptionally good interaction. Communication is the
link between human beings used for creating understanding, learning, and improvements.
In order to achieve time and cost-effective design, Virtual Design and Construction
(VDC) has been developed by the Center of Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) at
Stanford University. VDC are both the implementation of technical tools like BIM and a
methodology. VDC is based on the lean philosophy, focusing on how to increase value
in the project and streamline it when focusing on eliminating waste. As part of VDC,
jointly conducted Integrated Concurrent Engineering (ICE) meetings are conducted. In the
ICE meetings, all the relevant actors participate and collaborate in an iRoom designing
(Rekola et al., 2010). This form of meeting involves the whole design team contributing to

Projects are increasing in complexity and the demand for new approaches for planning

3.2.1
Traditionally Project Planning and Management
A project is both unique, complex and to some extend uncertain. Planning and
scheduling is a standard requirement for construction projects and a formal process that
identiﬁes what needs to be done, what resources are needed, and when they are required
(Forbes and Ahmed, 2011). It is the project managers’ role to navigate within complexity
and uniqueness and dealing with changes and challenges. A project plan can be graphical
represented by a schedule; typical a bar chart or critical path method (CPM) chart, which
documents every activity in a horizontal time scale. The planning process is often structured
by a principle of decomposition of all the activities (Meland, 2000). The scale of the CPM
depends on the size and complexity of the project. Procedurally, a master schedule may
involve following:
1. The project is subdivided into major work activities.
2. The work force requirements and duration are determined for each separate activity.
3. Placing the activities in a logical sequence to form the master schedule.
4. Project duration is determined from overall sequence of activities and their respective
durations.
5. Evaluate the overall duration with the contractual requirements of each project.
6. Further develop the master schedule by identify critical path and identify the "ﬂoat"
or "slack time".
The duration of activities is determined according to the resources available (Forbes
and Ahmed, 2011). The master schedule is often constructed early in the tendering process
by using number and experiences from former successful projects. It is high degree of
uncertainty connected to the schedule in the early phase (Meland, 2000) and 4D planning
is increasingly used for optimizing the schedule.
The engineering process illustrates the ﬁnal product in a project, and the project
management involves to lead, facilitate, and coordinate work in the engineering team to
execute the product. According to Meland (2000) project management is management of
the process of launching conceptual ideas and processing the chosen idea to a complete
immaterially product.
The contractors have the responsibility of establishing the management system covering
economy, quality, and time scheduling (Meland, 2000), where the management system

3.2.2
Integrated Engineering
Integrated engineering is an early involvement of all relevant players (Westergaard,
2010). By involving all the players at an early stage of the project an increased interdisciplinary-
and interface control will occur when those who are performing, building and using the
project, are participating in the early phase alongside with the designers.
By a traditionally designing process, as the design progresses, the team and effort
increase and the maximum effort is put during construction documentation phase. The
integrated design phase leads to decision making with essential importance of the life-
cycle costs and usability in an early phase of the project when the ability to control
the cost are the highest and the cost of design changes are lowest. As more of the
project are documented changes becomes more difﬁcult and the cost of design increases.
However, by shifting effort forward in time it is more effort to develop and task design
alternatives, and less effort needed for documentation. Managing this the results will be
better resolved and the coordination will be more precise and free of errors leading to
a smooth construction process. Figure 3.3 is known as the MacLeamy curve and often
referred to when considering BIM and Integrated Project delivery (IPD). It contains four
curves and illustrates how the involvement of all players at early phase can impact the
project cost and effort. The MacLeamy curve clearly shows the beneﬁcial changes by
the implementation of integrated work and moving the traditional effort forward in time
reducing the cost of design changes while increasing the ability to control the cost. By
including the contractor in an early phase more expertise and competence are brought
earlier to the designing table, and they are able to make inﬂuences on budget, design
solutions in model and scheduling to name a few. Currently, the implementation of
this methodology is varying in the Norwegian Construction Industry, but the tendencies
indicates that the design process is moving towards an integrated methodology.

Introduction to BIM
BIM is the information required to design, construct and maintain a project (Statsbygg,

this being the geometric model (Sampaio, 2012). Eastman et al. (2008) deﬁnes the BIM

BIM facilitates a more integrated design and construction process that results in better
quality buildings at lower cost and reduced project duration. The model improves both
analysis and control of the process by supporting the design throughout all phases with
containing precise geometry and data needed to support the construction, fabrication and
procurement activities.
According to Knotten and Svalestuen (2014) BIM is a 3D model that enables the use of
Quantity Take Off (QTO), 4D-simulations, clash detection and the opportunity to optimize
the design. Their research experience expresses that the key issue to achieving a good BIM

the actual physical assembly of the project. A good structure helps contractors, designers
and project owners managing complex projects containing large amount of information
(Krygiel et al., 2008). This is done by linking the information to the respective objects in
a model, and evaluate which information are relevant to whom and does the information
beneﬁt the project as a whole?
A prerequisite to an efﬁcient project is a good communication between the stakeholders.
BuildingSMART presents the openBIM model. An openBIM model is a universal approach

by one open format and a standardized terminology called data dictionary. The openBIM
model and practice used in vertical construction projects contains three main elements
needed for an optimal utilization of BIM. These elements are a Data Model (Industry
Foundation Classes- IFC), a library (International Framework for Data Dictionary- IFD)
and a process (Information Delivery Manual- IDM). The triangular relationship between

Data Model enables, along with buildingSMART Data Dictionary and buildingSMART
Process use of openBIM and gives the players the opportunity to use and share 3D models
with essential information, unambiguous descriptions of architectural object and support
processes that assure the quality of projects.

5.1
What is Model-based Construction?
Model-based Construction (MBC) is like a BIM model a common 3D model and
the source of design, construction, project process and support like a BIM model. What
that separates a model-based construction approach from a BIM approach is the way of
using the information. In a traditional BIM approach, the information are further used,
to for example create drawings. In a MBC approach, the information are stored in the
geometry. Geometry is information, and not used to further develop drawings. The models
prevail and symbolizes the deliverance with geometry and parameters. In model-based
construction projects there are not produced drawings, and therefore called drawingless
projects.
The quantity of information in infrastructure projects are greater than ever before, and
the data management can be improved, in order to provide better client value and more cost-
efﬁcient production (Laitinen, 1999). In order to do so, there are need for re-engineering
the information management process. Traditionally, the design process is divided into
the different phases; starting with brieﬁng and ending with the production of working
drawings. In integrated model-based design, there is no such clear distinction between the
phases, instead, the phases of the design process constantly interact and complement each
other. All new data are produced once and used as an input data for the next phase. The

Other than the listed positive relations MBC can increase information inventory if

5.2.2
Affecting the Construction Process
The functionalities previous described do also have a major impact on the construction
phase of projects. The sophisticated civil design for MBC allows users at the construction
site to more accurately capture the accurately existing conditions modelled in the design
and greater latitude in exploring alternate design solutions if changes needed. It also
enables robust analysis project performance. Conﬂicts at the construction site is one
of the top current challenges. Data presented by McGraw-Hill (2012) states that 69%
of construction sites in infrastructure projects using BIM reduced conﬂicts and changes
during construction. Reducing conﬂicts and changes during construction can have the
grates impact on improving project schedule and productivity and reducing the risk of cost
and schedule overruns. The lower risk and better predictability of outcomes are positive
impacts on all players. It also delivers more accurate dimensions, including in elevations,
and generates the preconditions for improved design of intersections, connections and
other details (COBIM, 2012b).
The accuracy of the models is essential for the production; the designs are to be
made technically correctly and checked by the designer, as well as coordinated with the
other disciplines. A simple technical common core is that the dimensions, position, and
identiﬁers of modelled structures are correct (COBIM, 2012b). For example, the concrete
elements with a speciﬁc ID must be correct and modelled with the right tools, and the
model should not show any alternative solutions after the ﬁnal solution is approved.

5.2.3
Re-Engineering Change Management Process
A model-based construction environment will change the way product deﬁnition-
traditionally is carried out, controlled, maintained by engineering drawings, distributed
annotated, validated, approved, released and stored (Quintana et al., 2011). The term
product deﬁnition is deﬁned here as a set of product attributes, features or characteris-
tics that coexists in a speciﬁc state of balance in order to meet physical and functional
requirements, as well as multidisciplinary constraints. Within the engineering change
management process context, drawings are traditionally considered as the ofﬁcial and
legal documents that carry the history of a product’s evolution through time, supported
by revisions, providing the part’s revision, a description or identiﬁcation of the change
authorization document, the date and approver’s name. Every time a revision is made to a
drawing, the change information recorded. The difference in a MBC approach is that now
the changes are applied to the models and parameters. The information is developed and
updated with time.
Findings in the aerospace industry by Angers (2002) states that a successful imple-
mentation of an engineering change management process, in a drawingless environment,
would save over 35% of the manufacturing resources devoted just to managing changes
to engineering drawings, manufacturing plans and scheduling requirements. To avoid
manually converting the model to its lightweight representation format each time the model
is updated, a trigger can be set within the system to initiate automatic creation of the 3D
PDF ﬁle. It is assumed that equivalent savings applies for the infrastructure projects.
To that end, companies needs to actively control, distribute and maintain the product
deﬁnition throughout the product life-cycle in absence of engineering drawings. It is
necessary to provide standard rules for the display, orientation, and query of annotations
when they are stored in a 3D-model instead of being part of a drawing (Quintana et al.,
2011).

5.2.4
BIM Collaboration Format in a Server Based Workﬂow
BIM Collaborated Format (BCF) is an open standard which is made to communicate
issues in a BIM model during its life cycle (Berlo and Krijnen, 2014). BCF ﬁles contains
information of issues, including a description, a status, a link to the objects, a picture of
the issue and camera orientation. The BCF standard is based on ﬁle exchange. A BCF can
either be packed into a ZIP ﬁle or be exported as a XML based schema before sent to the
project partners. The research conducted by Berlo and Krijnen (2014) states that "project
partners have been able to create issues, manage them online and evaluate them in context
of the actual BIM model". The research questions investigated within this project included
whether the BCF format can describe the status of building objects in addition to merely
describing their issues and what extent the XML based BCF schema proved to be suitable
for a more centralized online storage paradigm. A MBC could be a model as a collection of
aspects models or as being fully integrated and centralized. Experts feel an increasing need
to exchange additional information about the objects in a BIM, and the ability to exchange
information on the status on the individual objects or parts of the model. Furthermore, due
to the complexity of the projects, the project client and the project manager may beneﬁt
from the ability to obtain a more detailed, more accurate and real-time overview of the
status of the overall project. By using a BCF the manager and the client, in addition to
the status of individual objects, may obtain an aggregated overview of how many objects
are considered deﬁnite, how many are still to be approved. They get an overview of the
priority of outstanding issues and which project partners are responsible for many of the
unresolved issues (Berlo and Krijnen, 2014).
Beneﬁts From Using BCF Format
There are quite a few beneﬁts when using BCF. The most effective part of BCF is that the
issues found for instance through collision control in Solibri Model Checker, can be saved
in a BCF ﬁle which can then be sent out to the responsible parties in the project. This is an
effective way of communicating issues that need to be resolved. The BCF format makes it
possible to for the project parties to access the issues through different software tools. The
XML format can also be used to export the issues, if there are project parties who do not
have access to BCF compatible software. The process of creating the issues is typically
done once a week by a delegation from the project team. During the session, the issues
are discussed and the issues are sent out to the relevant parties. Another beneﬁt of using
BCF, is that it is possible to store very precise information about and issue in the BCF ﬁle,
which makes it easier for the person who are to solve it (Lehne and Kristensen, 2016).

Challenges With BCF Format
There are many challenges concerning the BCF format and there are certainly areas that
could be improved. One of the challenges with the BCF format, according to Treldal et al.
(2016a) is exporting the results from the BCF compatible tools to XML format. The output
ﬁles can be very different depending on the tool used and there are often information
missing. Often there are issues related the output in topic and comment. In the worst case
this could mean that information gets lost. This is especially a problem if there are project
partners that do not have access to BCF tools. There is also room for improvement when it
comes to communication of BCF issues. One of the limitations of BCF is that the BCF
ﬁle works mostly as a to-do list, where all the discussion of the issues was held on the
weekly meeting according to Berlo and Krijnen (2014). It is not possible to see directly
if the issues were solved. This can only be done by running a new collision control and
making sure that the issue is no longer there. Because of this, it is also possible that some
issues might not get solved. So one of the things that could be very useful is to have a
central BCF server, as suggested by Berlo and Krijnen (2014). This can help creating
a better up to date overview of the issues. It would also make it possible to change the
status of the issues as they get resolved. Another limitation to BCF is that it only relates
to BIM tasks such as collision control. There are also many other activities that could
be beneﬁcial to include, such as user requirements, risk management etc. (Treldal et al.,
2016a), subsection from (Lehne and Kristensen, 2016).

abolish this bottleneck there is a need of a higher degree of openness of IFC like in the
vertical construction projects, to get closer to a solution. The widely used software for
Norwegian infrastructure projects, Novapoint, do not support add-ins enabling BCF ﬁles.
To approach a solution the project owner has to make some demands to force progress and
a development. The potential of BCF is large in the infrastructure projects, and the project
participants’ needs to agree that it is a tool gaining the design phase, and not some extra
work the BIM-coordinator is forcing through, but for BCF to make any sense standards for
objects used in infrastructures needs to be developed.

5.3
Reasonable Use of Model-based Construction in Terms
of Level of Development
To succeed with the implementation of the MBC approach consensus and agreements
have to be made in order to ﬁnd a reasonable use of the model, with a rational Level of
Development (LOD) throughout all discipline models. Speciﬁcations related to descrip-
tions of a model content for the various versions of BIM models, at different stages of the
construction process, can create confusion and problems between parties. Without any
clear deﬁnition of the speciﬁcations like LOD, standards are difﬁcult to implement to a
project. As the number of LOD concepts arises the need of a clear deﬁnition escalates.
Advantages, challenges and possibilities are discussed related to working with different
LOD concept through a process ﬂow.
According to The US chapter of buildingSMART International, “The Level of Devel-
opment (LOD) Speciﬁcation” is a reference that enables practitioners in the AEC industry
to specify and articulate with a high level of clarity in the content and reliability of the
BIM at various stages in the design and construction process (BIMForum, 2016).
The common understanding of LOD is that it represents the combination of require-
ments, for the concretization of both graphical and non-graphical information during the
project. It is a measure of conﬁdence and reliability of information at various time and
stages of the project, where it enables project teams to particularly understand the content
and reliability of the data a model may receive. The model elements in different building
systems at different LOD are deﬁned and illustrated, and this allows the model authors to
deﬁne what their model could be relied on for, and helps other parties or users to clearly
understand the usability and the limitations of the models they are receiving.
The term Model Element is deﬁned by American Institute of Architects as "a portion
of the model representing a component, system or assembly within a building or building
site" (AIA, 2013), and the LOD concept deﬁne their requirements based on type-speciﬁc
model elements deﬁnitions. Different design disciplines, project execution models and
project organizations require different information to be available at project milestones.
In order to address the solution of the different LOD concepts developed, a LOD table
is needed to deﬁne the requirements for the element types at the project milestones. The
complexity of such LOD Table quickly increases to such an extent that ordinary users lose
all track of the relationship between desirable use cases and requirements (Treldal et al.,
2016b), (Lehne and Kristensen, 2016).

5.3.1
Detailing and Concretization
The detailing of the model describes how the model elements are presented visually
and a commercial program illustrates the elements. In a commercial program, there is easy
to change the detailing of an object from coarse to medium or ﬁne, but the detailing does
not state the objects content (Treldal et al., 2016b). Therefore, increasing the detailing of
an object in a model does not imply that the LOD level has increased.
The concretization describes the completeness of the model element and the scope of
the included components. The different levels could illustrate the increase of concretization
in a project. A model is added more information in the form of more component types and
properties. Concretization can add sub-elements to an assembly, and adds property types to
the elements and more precise property values. An increase in concretization will therefore
represent an increase in LOD level compared to the level of detail. Treldal et al. (2016b)
also introduces two new concepts; Level of Completeness (LOC) and Level of Information
(LOI) concluded that LOD deﬁnitions describe completeness and not detailing, and LOI
to deﬁne the requirements for non-graphical information accurately. The LOC is deﬁned
based on concretization of the model element and the scope of included components and
the combination of description and illustration deﬁnes each LOC, as the LOI requirements
are deﬁned explicitly adding a more modular approach to the LOD concept (Lehne and
Kristensen, 2016).
5.3.2
Data Reliability
It is crucial that a model contains reliable data. A model with unreliable data is a model
without any great value to the project, and someone would say that the model is no greater
than the data. According to the DiKon (2015) workshops reliability is a useful factor to
include, in order to the concept can be used as part of a contractual agreement, and similar
conclusions is reached by the BIMForum (Treldal et al., 2016b). A project could have
inﬁnite access of information, but if not the information is trustworthy the information
does not contain any value to the project.(Lehne and Kristensen, 2016).

Introduction to Virtual Design and Construction
Virtual Design and Construction is a framework and a performance model developed
by Center of Integrated Facility Engineering (CIFE) at Stanford University, USA in 2001
and is deﬁned by Kunz and Fischer (2012) as:
"The use of integrated multi-disciplinary performance models of design-construction

VDC brings multiple stakeholders together and different stakeholders all have speciﬁc
business objectives, including standards of their ﬁelds such as architecture, engineering,
construction or ﬁnance which provides complementary perspectives to a project (Kunz
and Fischer, 2012). The VDC models are referred to as virtual because they show the
computer-based description of the projects, and the model emphasizes those aspects of the
project that can be designed and managed. This is the product, the organization, that will
deﬁne, design, construct and operate it, and the process that the organization teams will
follow. The models are performance models in the sense that they predict some aspects
of the project performance and integrated in the sense that all players access shared data
and highlight or change the aspects of related models. The reason the models are multi-
disciplinary is that they represent Architect, Engineering, Contractor and Project Owner of
the project including relevant sub-disciplines (Kunz and Fischer, 2012). VDC is based on
the Lean-philosophy aiming to increase the project value simultaneously eliminating waste
(Khanzode et al., 2006). VDC is both implementation of new technology and methodology,

6.5
Integrated Concurrent Engineering (ICE)
Integrated Concurrent Engineering (ICE) uses: a singularity rapid combination of
expert designers; advanced modelling, visualization and analysis tools; social processes
and a specialized design facility; to create preliminary designs for complex systems
(Chachere et al., 2004). In ICE meetings, all the relevant players are located together to
engineers regularly over a limited period of time (Chachere et al., 2004). ICE is based on
the Lean Construction philosophy and aims to maximize the value and minimize the waste
by integrated engineering. ICE was originally developed by NASA for the time pressure
engineering that demanded the highest quality and achievement. Later ICE has been further
developed for the construction industry by Center of Integrated Facility Engineering.
ICE achieves extraordinarily rapid design with a quality similar to or surpassing
traditional methods and lower costs (Chachere et al., 2009). Chachere et al. (2009) states
that when comparing a traditional parallel engineering method with successful ICE users,
the latter reduces project schedule by several orders of magnitude by; singularly rapid
combination of expert designers; advanced modelling, visualization and analysis tools; a
set of consistent social processes, and a specialized design facility to create preliminary
designs for complex systems. ICE enables a multi-disciplinary collaboration where the
players make fast decisions together. By gathering all relevant expertise and decision-
making the interface between project participants is more manageable.
The better information ﬂow and the reduced response time in the planning process
enables exchange of experience and knowledge between the disciplines. An overarching
agenda is attached to every meeting and engineering activity. In a typical ICE meeting, the
design team plan and clariﬁes design until next meeting and interdisciplinary controls and
challenges are discussed.

Culture- Willingness to Change
To describe the full range of organizational impacts of technology that must be managed
previous research has typically used the term "human and organizational issues" or more
simply "organizational issues" (Doherty and King, 2003). The project organization consist
of the multidisciplinary personnel working with multiple tools. According to (Linderoth,
2010) project workers are part of a social network, and due to disruptive nature of building
and construction projects, the challenges are to maintain and re-establish the network in
which BIM is used in consecutive projects. When the project organization changes from
one project to another, mode of operation changes requiring people to adapt and learn new
practices. This lead inevitably challenges in technology implementations (Tulenheimo,
2015). High level management as well as project managers should be enlightened and
interested of potential new business products realized by new technologies. Without this
vision and the executive leadership behind it, the effort to adopt new business practices
will struggle and waste decided resources (Autodesk, 2012). The return of investment
has to be taken into consideration and realize the implementation in such a way that it
does not weaken productivity, meaning that some risk have to be tolerated to achieve
tools that have potential to overcome other competitors (Tulenheimo, 2015). Conservative
people may be more easily persuaded to work differently and according to Dulamini

In order to get owner’s project organizations in the direction of a more collaborative

Change is most often motivated by dissatisfaction. One of the largest changes in this

8.1
Improving the Process and Collaboration with Lean
BIM and Lean Construction affect the current construction industry (Sacks et al.,
2010). According to Sacks et al. (2010), there are expectations that BIM (i.e. technology)
and Lean will make the construction industry process more efﬁcient. MBC (i.e. a BIM
approach with a different way of handling the information) and Lean are two separate
initiatives and are not dependent on each other, but to optimize the construction projects
it is necessary to implement them both and simultaneously, due to their many common
elements and synergies. MBC and Lean share many of the same values and both aim to
reduce waste and improve transparency. Implementation of MBC makes it possible to
transform the traditional design process into a Lean process and provides the basis for
some of the results as expected Lean Construction delivers (Sacks et al., 2010).
MBC is a productive tool for implementing Lean Design (Forbes and Ahmed, 2011).
MBC can be used to structure and visualize construction design, while Lean is a method
of coordinating and developing the design in practice (Tjell, 2010). By using MBC engi-
neering consultants, contractors and architects can collaborate on the design to achieve
maximum constructability, lower project cost and an offensive construction plan. The team
can collaborate with a virtual model of the project where the design is not only focused
at the product, but also on the plans and processes. Lean and MBC do also focus on
information management, where Lean creates a transparent structure for information and
simpliﬁes model-related documentation of information (Tjell, 2010). Both concepts focus
and facilitate visualization of the construction process, relationship and collaboration in
the project team and learning and improvement.
Visualization of the Construction Process
The production control, performed with the Last Planner System (LPS) in Lean is supported
by the technology in many ways, especially through the visualization of the construction
process (Eastman et al., 2008). The idea behind MBC and Lean is to visualize the product
so that problems are identiﬁed and solved before the design option is continued to the pro-
duction process (Tjell, 2010). This principle is mentioned in Lean as a "sea of inventory"
illustrated in Figure 8.2. The principle focuses on lowering "water levels" in order to be
able to see the stones, to have the least possible inventory to detect the problems. An ocean
of inventory describes that there are a large amount of ongoing work and inventory in the

A principle of Lean Construction is to create a good network between the parties and Lean

One of the contributions to the construction industry’s improvement process is the develop-

Early involvement of all the actors like contractor and sub-contractors is also an

should adapt to the construction site and challenges that meet you in the terrain. A
Norwegian construction site is unique in world scale, not only in the topography, but since
all the equipment is fully computer-controlled and digitized. All equipment, whether it is
a drilling rig, tunnel boring machines, excavators, are all rigged and capable of receiving
data. It will be an inﬁnite discussion regarding best formats, but the state of the art is
working, considering the data and information ﬂow between what is designed and the
output.
The discipline models are mainly used at the construction site, and downloaded from
the cloud solution. To access the updated information on site some of the contractors
have started to use different solutions. These solutions includes BIM-kiosks, tablets
and smartphones. The BIM-kiosk is a station where the model is "brought out on the
construction site" by placing a stationary computer, centrally located, able to run all the
design software. This is typically used as a meeting point at the construction site and used
in discussions between workers. The tablets are a more mobile solution, where the models
are downloaded from a cloud or server, enabling the workers by any doubt to quickly
review what they are building in 3D. The smartphones are used more for the reporting and
photographing of deviations. Then the models are used to re-design solutions if challenges
occur during construction.
Another way of using the models in an early phase is to use it for modelling the
construction site. It can be very useful for the contractor to have a good area plan of the
construction site. This can be used to optimize the placement of machines, for example
the placement of cranes. It can also be used to plan when and where the materials should
be delivered to site. Some contractors have developed their own software measuring the
mass transfer and mass balance, with machine capacity, route selection for machines, need
for temporary roads, and existing trafﬁc ﬂow as parameters (4D analysis).
Safety is an important aspect of a construction site and many have their own health,
environmental and safety practises (HMS), where BIM is a good tool and precautions
like safety railings can be incorporated into the 3D model. This makes it clear from the
beginning which safety measures should be taken, and incorporating them into the model
could make it more likely to be executed.

9.2.3
How does the people and their culture affect the model-based
construction?
When working with internal and external customers on a project, it is essential to pay
close attention to relationships, context, history, and the corporate culture. Behaviour
impacts every aspect of project delivery. Successful projects in a behaviour context goes
beyond delivering the output to time, cost and quality. It is possible to deliver a project to
time, cost and quality using undesired and unpleasant behaviours. However, very few of
those people involved would consider this to have been a successful delivery. Success is
achieved when desired behaviour is consistently and regularly exhibited, and undesired
behaviours are mitigated and eliminated. The corporate culture refers to the beliefs, at-
titudes, and values that the organization’s members share and the behaviours consistent
with them. The corporate culture sets one organization apart from another, and dictates
how members of the organization will see you, interact with you, and judge you. Some
aspects of corporate culture are easy to observe others are more difﬁcult to discern. It
is easy to see that not all the projects to the respondents are drawingless, but they all
acknowledge the importance of the development and innovation in the industry. The
organizations all have the willingness to change their methods and tools to keep up with
the competition, but some struggle internally. The one thing all the respondents highlight
is that the commitment to the MBC should come from above in the organization in order
to the change reluctant people to understand the importance. One respondent stated that
the most experienced and competent people in the industry are often the older generations,
who are used to work with drawings. One of the reason to the reluctance is the fear of
more work. These ﬁndings shows that the reluctance probably occurs due to lack of
competence. The fear of doing more work exists because some do not have sufﬁcient
competence for the methods and tools in the organization. The willingness to change
internally in an organization can be individual and varies from ﬁrm to ﬁrms.

9.3.1
What are the beneﬁts by using model-based construction?
Some of the beneﬁts and positive inﬂuences with MBC projects is already mentioned
where the model increases the visualization and transparency of the projects. It provides
improved communication line between the players, greater openness, collaboration and
information ﬂow, it can provide a better interdisciplinary understanding by better coordina-
tion and clash detection. It is emphasized that projects can anticipate problems earlier in
the process and faster changes are made and better decisions.
The owners of the projects investigated have required MBC in the contract with clear
intention of saving money. They believe the direct consequence of the mentioned beneﬁts
between the contractor and consultants leads to a more efﬁcient project and that they
will get a better product. The product will be "right at the ﬁrst time", meaning that
there will be less errors, mistakes, and re-design. Shorter communication lines between
contractor and consultant allows the project owner to avoid sitting as an intermediary
with all communication. The model also gives the opportunity for a more efﬁcient and
reasonable maintenance of the product.
In addition, with MBC it can be seen how changes affects other parts of the project,
thus eliminating the domino effect that may occur with little thoughtful changes. This
efﬁciency is pointed out especially with regards to updates in the workﬂow, whether it
concerns change orders or technical clariﬁcations. As opposed to sending out revised
drawings and making sure that the latest editions are used at all times, changes in models
are now made in on discipline model and automatically updated in other coordination
models - which is beneﬁcial both for schedule and budget. It is pointed out that surplus
companies can produce higher quality, but the tender competition determines who gets
the jobs, which makes it uncertain how the development beneﬁts the various players. It is
nevertheless unanimous that all players have something to gain from a successful use of
MBC, as all the respondents insures that this is the future.
The planning and scheduling, solutions and pricing becomes easier for the contractors
and easy to record planned and executed work. MBC provides better basis for calculations
and by making calculations in the model material waste and unnecessary mass transfer is
reduced. The information is collected and gathered at one place making it easier to extract.

The investigations have not found any negative consequences with the model-based

The transition to new knowledge, a new working methodology and new technolo-

project participants have to be willing to learn and gather information differently. The
use of the model is much based on what kind of information one feeds the model with
and how to extract the needed information, which can be a challenge for new users. It is
a lack of trust in the model. This creates conﬂicts, not only between the drawings and
the description as earlier, but now also between the models against the two mentioned,
although the contract states that the models prevails. By producing both models and
drawings, duplication is done. Most of the respondents had a shared understanding of the
existence of a transition phase from traditionally project towards drawingless model-based
construction projects, but for some it has been longer and more painful than expected. One
of the respondents representing one of the road projects commented that it is a lack of trust
in the model and back-up, in the form of drawings, are made in case of a lawsuits.
Several Norwegian infrastructure projects are built using models today as the machines
gets the land survey data directly from the model, but consultants also admit that there are
still details built from drawings.
"Most of our infrastructural projects today are model-based projects. We have the tech-
nology to model everything, but we are only performing at a level equivalent to modelling
95%, and the last 5% is represented by drawings. This due to the poor efﬁciency regarding
the time consumed modelling the details versus standard drawings with low production
cost." -Engineering manager

(2012). The importance of the project execution model (i.e. connection between project
execution, organization and process) may now become even more important as the interac-
tions within the project organization increases, and the sub-processes provided by Meland
(2000) becomes more overlapping.

contracts are doing so. The contracts are not the deciding factor if a project succeed
with a MBC approach or not, but contributes by helping the parties in the right direction.
The large governmental organizations providing new infrastructural contracts are trying
to create a motivating operation environment with more collaborative contracts, like the
NASFA et al. (2010) recommends, by pointing their design team in the direction they
want them to go. The case studies which refers to the infrastructure projects are utilizing
Design-Build contracts, and it is believed that these contracts could be further optimized.
One of the case studies did have an IPD contract, presented in Figure 7.3. IPD is by the
author’s knowledge not been tested at Norwegian infrastructure projects, and is believed to
further optimize the beneﬁts with the MBC approach.
COBIM (2012b) divides project management of BIM based projects into three pro-
cesses; design, execution and supervision. One of the things that could complicate the
project management is increased risk, if the parties involved does not have the previous ex-
perience and competence with MBC. Mentioned by the respondents, this is something the
contractors are starting to look into by hiring the competence of a typical BIM-coordinator.
Also by handpicking the PO to ensure the right competence. The parties will then most
likely agree on the project BIM objectives and the confusion of roles and responsibilities
is likely to be minimized. Also, both schedule risk and cost risk are minimized to the
minimum through better risk management.
As stated in Table 9.3 fully focus on implementing MBC from early phase will mini-
mize the implementation challenges. With a design team thinking and modelling building
sequences the whole process would be optimized, but is not implemented due to technical
challenges. The designers have not succeeded with modelling the building sequences
with the right time aspect. According to Mejlaender-Larsen (2015) the three interrelated
dimensions; process, people and technology are the key to improve the transition from
engineering to construction by using BIM given an EPC contract. According to the
respondents technology is where the focus should be.

This chapter presents the conclusion on the topic question and the research questions.
This study focuses on the possibilities of optimizing the building process, by implementa-
tion of tools and methods in the design- and construction phase. The following sub-sections
refer to the research questions based on the empirical data and discussion chapters. There

into infrastructure projects?"

What is the status of the Norwegian infrastructure
projects on model-based construction?
With reference to the literature study and the case studies it can be concluded that the
Norwegian infrastructure industry is at the beginning of a paradigm shift. The infrastructur
industry has not yet deﬁned how the information is going to be handled in models within
the new paradigm. Nye Veier has deﬁned their road-map for implementing MBC in
their infrastructure projects. They have developed their execution model called "Best
Value Procurement", by restructuring contract forms and setting new requirements and
demands for the project organizations. Consultant engineering companies and Design-
build contractors are now in an experimental phase where they try to develop and optimize
their execution model speciﬁcally against the new contract standards from Nye Veier.
Digital deliverance are not digital drawings, but now fully constructable models with the

11.2
How does the model-based construction process af-
fect the building process?
The model-based construction process affects both the design- and construction phase
according to the respondents. The ﬁndings state that the model-based construction pro-
cess has a positive inﬂuence on the overall collaboration and communication in project.
The communication has a common reference point with better dialogue and the project
participants have a better understanding of design- and construction conﬂicts due to visual-
ization and integration. The communication is also improved due to the co-location and
ICE meetings in the design phase. The collaboration model is used as a communication-
and information platform. This platform provides a much better understanding of the
construction scope.
The building process is moving towards a more integrated process. This includes all
the relevant actors at an earlier stage, and the integrated decisions are taken earlier. In
the integrated process, the ability to control cost is higher and design changes are less
expensive compared to the traditional process. Lean Construction and VDC facilitates in-
tegrated design together with the collaborative contract. An integrated project organization
facilitates good information ﬂow and knowledge sharing across disciplines, and improves
the interdisciplinary coordination and the overall engagement to the participants. The front-
loaded design phase reduces the amount of confusion and re-work during the design- and
construction phase. The MBC approach along with ICE meetings improves the interaction
and collaboration in projects between contractors and consultant engineers. Tools like
iRooms increases the efﬁciency of these meetings and ICE achieves extraordinary rapid
design with a quality like or surpassing traditional methods with lower costs. Integration
can be provided by different mechanisms like contractual, organizational or technological.

The integrated design facilitates the implementation of MBC and is a success factor of

New technologies within the MBC approach challenges the implementation. The MBC

schedule. In the multi-party contracts, there are a need of trust, transparency in every
process, shared risk, and the stakeholders’ success is tied to project success. It is a
necessary outer motivation to succeed with the innovation.

The MBC approach provides several beneﬁts, and addresses the main elements of Lean
and VDC. It focuses on early involvement of the actors and co-located design leading
to better virtual collaboration, good communication between participants, and efﬁcient
project execution, "Right at the ﬁrst time", with high quality. Lean and VDC facilitates
optimal utilization of MBC’s potential. In the ICE meetings conducted in VDC, the design
team can prepare the collaboration model, thus developing a better reliable model with
optimal design solutions. The case studies show that the participants are positive to sit
together and the overall perception is that the design- and construction process can be
more effective if they work together to develop the models in project meetings through a
MBC approach. The construction phase will be more efﬁcient by improved scheduling
and planning and the solutions developed are expected to be more constructable.
Early involvement of all actors and co-location increases the possibility of solving the
technical challenges of implementing 4D, 5D, and 6D analysis improving the constructabil-
ity and maintenance. Co-location makes information more accessible and improves the
information ﬂow. The integrated working method provided by a multi-party contract
