Abstract
The balanced-armature receiver (BA-receiver) is by design a nonlinear device, and
therefore it produces unwanted distortion products under certain operating condi-
tions. If the behaviour of receiver can be accurately modelled, the added distortion
can be compensated for by means of control theory. Although, unlike the moving-
coil transducer, the working principles of the BA-receiver has not been extensively
described in literature.
In this thesis the BA-receiver working principles are investigated through thorough
analysis of the magnetic circuit. In particular two model proposal will be presented,
a model proposed by Joe Jensen et al. and an extended model proposed by Wolfgang
Klippel et al. The extended model is argued to give a more accurate representation
of the receiver, as the effects of magnetic saturation of the armature into account.
Based on the analysis, the nonlinear parameters of the receiver will be derived for
both models to better understand the inherent nonlinearities of the receiver. In order
to derive the nonlinear parameters of the extended receiver model, it was necessary
to model the magnetic flux in the armature. The armature flux was estimated based
on blocked impedance measurements conducted on a scale model of the BA-receiver.
Both models have been simulated in MATLAB and the performance of them have
been evaluated based on vibration displacement measurements conducted the scale
model.
In the end of the project period it was realised that the extended BA-receiver
model could not be fully evaluated for large signals, as the blocked impedance mea-
surements should have been conducted for larger driving voltages in order to fully
saturate the armature.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The technology behind the balanced-armature receiver (BA-receiver) has been around
since the 1920s, where the receiver was primarily used in telephone systems [Hun54].
Today the BA-receiver is fundamentally different from its early predecessor and much
much smaller. Due to their small form factor BA-receivers are widely used in audio
applications such as in-ear monitors and hearing aids. In hearing aids, sound pres-
sure is captured by a microphone and converted into an electrical signal, which is
processed by an integrated DSP-unit. The electrical signal is processed, amplified
and sent to the balanced-armature receiver, which converts the signal into a me-
chanical movement of the armature. Figure 1.1 shows a sectional view of a typical
hearing aid receiver, which from the outside consists of a metal case. Inside the case
is the balanced-armature receiver/transducer itself, which consists of an armature
surrounded by a coil and two equally strong permanent magnets. When no current
is flowing in the coil, the armature will stay balanced between the magnets, as both
magnets attract the armature with an equal but opposite force. When the coil is
excited with an AC current a time-varying magnetic field is created around the coil
which in turn magnetises the armature. Depending on the polarity of the current the
armature will be attracted by one magnet and repelled by the other. The loudspeaker
diaphragm is connected to the armature via. a drive rod, hence the diaphragm moves
simultaneously with the armature. As the diaphragm vibrates the pressure in the
front volume is compressed and decompressed, consequently sound is produced in the
sound outlet. Voila!

The BA-receiver is unfortunately a nonlinear device by design, as any displacement
of the armature will consequently modify the electrical and magnetic characteristics
of the system. Furthermore, magnetic saturation of the armature plays an important
role in the nonlinearity of the device. When the armature becomes saturated any
increase in the excitation level does not produce further increase in the displacement of
the armature. In hearing aids this means that no more output is generated regardless
of an increase in the input level, which results in peak-clipping of the output signal.
As a result of the inherent nonlinearities in the receiver, harmonic distortion products
are generated under certain operating conditions. In hearing aids the added nonlinear
distortion reduces the signal-to-noise ratio, which can negatively influence measures
like speech intelligibility of the hearing impaired person, particularly at high sound
pressure levels. However, it is possible to reduce the nonlinear distortion generated
in the BA-receiver by means of control theory. With a control system the unwanted
nonlinearities can be compensated for by modifying the input signal to include the
inverse of the distorted output signal. This requires a good understanding of the
working principles of the BA-receiver, as well as an accurate model describing the
behaviour of the receiver.
1.1
Thesis Objective
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate and present the balanced armature re-
ceiverвҖҷs working principles through magnetic circuit analysis. Two BA-receiver model
proposal will be investigated, namely the model developed by Joe Jensen et al. (2014)
and the model developed by Wolfgang Klippel et al. (2016). These models will be
evaluated and compared based on measurements conducted on a scale model of the
balanced-armature receiver.
The aim is to determine a model that describes the
behaviour of the receiver, and hopefully in the long run it will be possible to imple-

CHAPTER 3
Nonlinearities in the
Balanced-Armature
Receiver
As already touched upon the BA-receiver has two main nonlinearities, namely the
expansive nonlinearity through variation of the air gap length, hence the reluctance,
and the compressive nonlinearity through magnetic saturation of the armature. In
this chapter the underlying reason for the saturation of the armature will be explained.
Lastly the measure of linearity used in this thesis will be presented.
3.1
Magnetic Hysteresis
Magnetic materials are materials that exhibit magnetic polarisation under the pres-
ence of an external magnetic field. In the BA-receiver the armature is typically made
of a magnetic material such as steel, and when the coil surrounding it is excited with
a current the armature is magnetised. The receiver also contains two permanent mag-
nets, that are able to produce magnetic fields on their own. In order to understand
the effects of magnetic saturation it is necessary to explain magnetic hysteresis.
Before magnetisation of a magnetic material, its molecular magnetic dipoles are
by nature oriented randomly, resulting in no net magnetisation (see Figure 3.1, left).
When an external H-field is applied to the material, the dipoles are oriented in the
direction of the applied field, thus increasing the magnetic flux density (see Figure
3.1, right). This effect is equivalent to that of a positive magnetisation.
When a H-field is applied to a ferromagnetic material, the dipoles align more
and more with the applied field, i.e. the more the aligned domains, the stronger the
magnetic field. At some point, theoretically, the dipoles will be aligned completely
with the magnetic field, and any further increase in the external field will not result in
an increased magnetisation of the material. Thus the material is said to be magneti-
cally saturated. When the applied field is removed after saturation has been reached,
some of the domains will go back to their preferred orientation, while some will keep
their changed orientation. Hence, the magnetisation of the material will not drop to

In the BA-receiver magnetic saturation of the armature is one of the inherent
nonlinearities. When the armature becomes saturated any increase in the excitation
level, hence the applied magnetic field, does not displace the armature further. Thus
saturation will limit the maximum output of the BA-receiver and the saturation point
of the armature will affect the overall performance of the BA-receiver. As a result
the generated armature displacement will be distorted. Therefore, it is perceptive to
chose a magnetic material with a high saturation point for the armature, in order to
preserve a linear relationship between the applied and induces field at high excitation
levels.

Throughout this thesis a couple of assumptions have been made about the BA-
receiver. First of all it has been assumed that the resting position of the armature is
always at рқ‘Ҙ= 0, however this might not be the case. Although the armature might
seem balanced between the permanent magnets when no current is flowing in the coil,
the equilibrium position might be a bit offset during excitation. As mentioned by
==PAGE:53==
6.2 Klippel model
39
Klippel et al. the suggested BA-receiver models neglect any offset of the equilibrium
position, which can cause an asymmetry in the nonlinear parameter characteristics.
Hence, the measured blocked impedance as well as the inductance might have a
maximum peak a bit offset from the centre of the combined air gaps. This would in
turn mean that the nonlinear flux function would likewise have a maximum peak a bit
offset from the centre. This offset was observed for some displacement measurements.
However the offset was usually very small, and most likely due to the permanent
magnets not having an equally strong mmf.
Second of all it was assumed that the material damping of the armature was neg-
ligibly small. Although this is a valid assumption to make, it should be investigated
how the actual damping factor can be determined. For the simulations the damping
factor was arbitrarily chosen to be 0.146 N s mвҲ’1. The simulated armature displace-
ment in Figure 6.5b could suggest that arbitrarily chosen damping factor was too low,
since the simulation exaggerates the displacement.
Lastly, the models used for the simulations do not take into account the effects
of eddy currents in the armature. Since eddy currents generate an magnetic field
that opposes the magnetic field generated by the coil, the resulting inductance will
be prone to losses.
The overall point is that there has been made some assumptions, that are going
to affect the simulation results. Hence, it can not be expected that the simulation
results match the measurements results to a tee. Nevertheless, there is still a lot of
work to be done in the investigation of BA-receivers.

Future Work
7.1
Blocked impedance
As mentioned the blocked impedance measurements were only conducted for 500 mV,
which showed to be too weak a driving voltage for the armature to become saturated.
Hence, the measurements should be conducted for higher driving voltages up to 5 V,
where the measured vibration displacement THD was observed to be dominant.
7.2
Finite-element model
During this thesis period a finite-element analysis of the BA-receiver has been con-
sidered, and a CAD-drawing of the scale model was made for the intended purpose.
However, due to lacking knowledge about material properties of the scale model, it
was decided to scrub the analysis. If more knowledge about the materials can be
acquired, it is possible to model the magnetic saturation of the armature directly in
Comsol Multiphysics (a multiphysics simulation software). However, this would also
require some knowledge about the actual nonlinear magnetic stiffness compensation
and the nonlinear transduction force of the scale model.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis BA-receiver models proposed by Jensen et al. and Klippel et al., re-
spectively, have been analysed and simulated, and the differences between the models
have been discussed. Through a comparison between measurements conducted on a
scale model of a BA-receiver it was demonstrated that the first model did not accu-
rately model the receiver. It was argued that this was mainly due to the fact that
the model did not take into account the effect of magnetic saturation.
The second model did however take into account saturation of the armature, but
due to an underestimation of the driving voltage needed to reach saturation, the per-
formance of the model could not be fully evaluated. In the model it was not possible
to accurately fit the approximated flux function to the measured flux function. This
could be due to the fact that the blocked impedance measurements, from which the
nonlinear flux was determined, was not conducted at a large enough driving volt-
age. However, the BA-receiver model proposed by Klippel et al. suggested that the
nonlinear flux function is linearly proportional to the coil inductance, but the approx-
imated series expansion was found to always peak at рқ‘“рқҗҝ(рқ‘Ҙ, рқ‘–) = 1 whenever magnetic
saturation of the armature is present. If the measurements had been conducted for
larger voltages where magnetic saturation is dominant, the inductance would have
peaked at a higher level and the flux function would peak accordingly at a higher
level based on Equation 2.37. This suggests that the series expansion approximation
of the nonlinear flux function does not approximate the function accurately as a peak
at рқ‘“рқҗҝ(рқ‘Ҙ, рқ‘–) = 1 is not always going to be the case.
The final conclusion is that additional measurements should be conducted at larger
driving voltages, in order to fully evaluate the performance of the model proposed by
Klippel et al.
