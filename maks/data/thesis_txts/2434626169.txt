Abstract 
The immune system is known to protect us from bacterial and viral infection. It is now established that the 
immune system is also a key player mediating protection against cancer development and progression. 
Utilization of the immune system for combatting cancer has gained lot attention over the past decade.  
Adoptive cell transfer (ACT) is one approach used in cancer immunotherapy; infusion tumour infiltrating 
lymphocytes (TILs) generated from excited tumour material, posing one approach. The production of 
genetically modified chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells for infusion posing another approach to ACT. 
For therapies with either TILs or CAR-T cells, tumour regression has been seen. Despite these impressive 
results not all patients benefit from this type of therapy and tumour recurrence has been observed. Recent 
studies have shown that generation of young phenotypic CD8+ T cells for both CART cells and TIL has better 
tumour killing potential; consequently resulting in increased overall survival. Moreover, generation of TILs 
often result in expansion of virus-specific and regulatory T cell responses alongside the desired tumour 
specific T cells. This unspecific production of TILs as well as exhausted phenotypic characteristics and lack of 
potential killing might be overcome by the stimulation of TILs and CAR T cells with an artificial antigen 
presenting cells, namely antigen-presenting scaffold (scaffold) stimulation. Scaffolds consist of; a dextran 
backbone with peptide major histocompatibility complex directs the specificity of the scaffold, co-
stimulatory molecule, and cytokines provides survival signalling and proliferation of the CD8 specific T cells. 
In this thesis, generation of CMV-specific CD8+ T cells was conducted by scaffold stimulation. Expansion of 
CMV-specific T cells from donor material showed an increase of antigen-specific CD8+ T cells. Expansion of 
CMV-specific T cells from naïve donor PBMC’s and OVA-specific CD8+ T cells from murine splenocytes 
showed no expansion of CD8+ T cells. Consequently, expansion of naïve T cells proved difficult, and 
strategies for optimization of this approach are needed.  
Scaffold stimulation of CAR-T cells were attempted but did not show to be successful. Instead, functional 
evaluation of CD3/CD28 bead expanded CAR-T cell cultures was performed. Intracellular staining and 
cytotoxicity studies were applied to test for production of effector molecule and specific killing potential. 
Here, CAR-T cells showed specific killing of CD19+ target cells, with the ability to produce effector cytokines 
including TNF-α, IFN-γ, and CD107a. For future work, functionality of scaffold-expanded CAR-T would be of 
high interest.

Resumé 
Immunsystemet er kendt for at beskytte os mod bakteriel og viral infektion.  Det er nu veletableret at 
immunsystemet spiller en vigtig rolle i beskyttelsen mod udvikling og progression af cancer. Anvendelsen af 
immunsystemet i kampen mod cancer har fået stor opmærksomhed i løbet af det sidste årti. Adaptiv celle 
transfer (ACT) er en metode, der anvendes i cancerimmunterapi. Produktionen af tumorinfiltrerende 
lymfocytter (TIL'er) fra tumormateriale kan dyrkes under foretrukne betingelser, eller produktion af 
genetisk modificerede kimære antigenreceptor (CAR) T-celler til infusion er to separate grene af ACT. 
Tumorregression er set i TIL eller CAR-T celle terapi. På trods af disse imponerende resultater har alle 
patienter ikke nytte af denne slags terapi og der er blevet set tilbagevendende tumor i flere tilfælde. Nye 
studier har vist at produktionen af CD8+ T celler med en ung fænotype for både CAR og TIL’er har en bedre 
evne til at dræbe tumor; hvilket resulterer i en øget samlet overlevelse. Genereringen af TIL’er resulterer 
ofte i en ekspansion af virusspecifikke- og regulatoriske T-celler ud over de ønskede tumorspecifikke T-
celler. Produktionen af disse specifikke TIL’er, udmattede fænotypiske egenskaber og manglende 
drabspotentiale kan overvindes ved at stimulere TIL’er og CAR-T celler med et kunstigt 
antigenpræsenterende scaffold. Scaffold består af; dextran som danner fundamentet med bunden peptid 
major histocompatibility complex som dirigerer specificiteten af scaffold’et, dertil costimulatorisk molekyle 
og cytokiner som signalerer for overlevelse og proliferering af de CD8+ specifikke T celler. 
I denne afhandling blev genereringen af CMV specifikke T celler udført ved stimulering af scaffold. 
Ekspansionen af CMV-specifikke T celler fra aktiverede donor materiale viste en forøgelse af antigen-
specifikke CD8+ T celler. Ekspansionen af humane CMV-specifikke T celler fra naive PBMC’er og murine 
OVA-specifikke CD8+ T splenocytter viste ingen specifikke CD8+ T celler. Af denne grund har ekspansionen 
af naive T celler vist sig at være vanskelig, der er brug for strategier til at optimere denne tilgang.  
Det var forsøgt at stimulere CAR-T celler med scaffold, dette viste dog ikke at være vellykket. I stedet blev 
funktionaliteten af de CD3/CD28 bead ekspanderede CAR-T celler undersøgt.  Intracellulære farvnings- og 
cytotoksisk forsøg blev udført for at se produktion af effektor cytokiner og det specifikke drabspotentiale. 
Her viste CAR-T cellerne specifik drab af CD19+ celler samt evnen til at producere effektor cytokiner 
herunder TNF-α, IFN-γ, and CD107a. Til følgende studier ville funktionaliteten af scaffold ekspandere CAR-T 
celle være af stor interesse.

1 Introduction 
1.1 The immune system 
The body is protected from various infectious agents, such as bacteria, viruses, and toxins which encompass 
a threat to the host. This protection is provided by the immune system and consists of several types of 
cells. Overall, the immune system can be divided into two groups, namely the adaptive and the innate 
immune system.  
The innate immune system is the first line of defense. When a pathogen successfully breaches a host 
immune barrier, the immune system starts to respond. Soluble molecules such as antimicrobial peptides 
and the complement system target the infectious agent for phagocytosis by macrophages or cells lysis. 
Secondly, the cells of the innate immune system can target foreign pathogens via pattern-associated 
molecular patterns (PAMP’s) and eliminate them via different effector mechanisms, such as secretion of 
pro-inflammatory cytokines [1].  
If a pathogen overcomes the first line of defense, the adaptive immune system engages. The adaptive 
system can be further divided into two types; cell-mediated immunity and the humoral immune response. 
B cells are key players of the humoral immune response, where fully differentiated B cells, known as 
plasma cells, can secrete antibodies targeting the pathogen. In details, the extracellular pathogens can be 
cleared by pathogens by neutralization, opsonisation, or activation of the complement system [2]. Prior to 
becoming a mature antibody-secreting plasma cell, the naïve B cell needs to be activated. During this 
process, the naïve B cell is dependent on signals provided by T helper cells; thereby, linking the humoral 
and the cell-mediated immune response [3]. While B cells are crucial mediators of the humoral immune 
response, T cells are crucial for the cell-mediated arm of the adaptive immune system, where they can 
eliminate both intracellular and extracellular pathogens. T cells can be divided into two major subsets 
based on their expression of the two co-receptors CD4 and CD8. CD4+ T cells are activated via engagement 
with peptides presented by the major histocompatibility complex II (MHC-II). Peptides presented on MHC-II 
are derived from extracellular origin. Following activation, CD4+ T cells can secrete cytokines, which recruit 
other cells to site of infection which kill extracellular pathogens. In contrast to the CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells 
recognize peptides presented in the context of MHC class I and secrete cytotoxic effector molecules, 
therefore referred to as cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTL). Since MHC class I present intracellularly-derived 
peptides, CD8+ T cells recognize and kill intracellular pathogens that have compromised a host cell. 
Importantly, and due to the process known as cross-presentation, certain APCs can present peptides of 
extracellular origin in the context of MHC class I; thereby allowing activation of CD8+ T cells towards for 
instance tumour cells.

1.2 Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) 
The Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) is a protein, which function is to load a peptide fragment and 
display this peptide-MHC complex (pMHC) for T-cell recognition on the cell surface. There a two distinct 
types of MHC molecules, namely class I and class II. MHC class I molecules are present on all nucleated cells 
and present endogenous proteins to CD8 T lymphocytes. In contrast, MHC class II molecules are present 
only on specialised antigen presenting cells (APCs), where they present exogenous proteins to CD4+ T 
lymphocytes. Since the focus of this thesis is on CD8+ T cells, MHC class II will not be discussed in details 
here. Cross presentation can occur, where extracellular material are engulfed and are presented on MHC-I, 
as for tumour derived proteins. 
The MHC class I complex consists of three α-subunits namely α1, α2, and α3. The α3 subunit is non-
covalently bound to a single β-microglobulin chain; thereby forming a four subunit structure. The subunits 
α1 and α2 together form the peptide-binding cleft (Figure 4). 
In total, humans have three MHC class I α-chain genes, Human leukocyte antigen-A (HLA-A), HLA-B and 
HLA-C. Together with this polygenecity, HLAs are also highly polymorphic. In fact, the MHC class I molecule 
is the most polymorphic molecule in the human body [4]; with more than 500 genes encoded in the HLA-C 
α-chain alone. Each MHC complex binds to a large variety of peptides, but these binding peptides for a 
given MHC molecule all share the position of certain amino acid residues and structural similarities. Due to 
its more closed conformation when compared to MHC class II, MHC class I molecules bury their peptide 
within the peptide-binding cleft and therefore have a preference for shorter peptides with a length of 8-10 
aa.

complex interacting with corresponding TCR and its costimulatory CD8 molecule. Modified (left) from Janeways Immunobiology 
8th edition and (right) [5]. 
1.2.1 Processing and antigen presentation 
Upon viral infection, the virus replicates their own viral genome within the host cell in the cytosol. Proteins 
in the cytosol are continually replaced with new proteins. Some of these proteins generated in the cytosol 
can be targeted by an ubiquitin ligase, which attaches ubiquitin to the protein. Ubiquitinated protein are 
recognised by a cylindrical protein called the proteasome, which first unfolds the protein, introduces it into 
the proteasome, which then breaks down the protein into short peptide fragments. These peptide

fragments generated can be further processed by aminopeptidases which cut the peptide into a preferable 
length. 
Newly synthesised MHC class I α-chain assembles in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). Peptide fragments 
generated from the proteasome are transported into the ER via the transporters associated with antigen 
processing (TAP) transporter. Here, a peptide with high affinity is loaded onto a given MHC class I molecule. 
Upon binding of the peptide into the binding cleft, the pMHC complex induces its final folding; resulting in 
the MHC complex being transported to the cell surface via the Golgi apparatus. Antigen processing and 
presentation pathway is illustrated in figure 2.

1.3 T-cell development  
T cells are derived from multipotent hematopoietic stem cells generated in the bone marrow. These 
progenitor cells leave the bone marrow, enter the blood stream and migrate to the thymus, where they 
start to develop into mature T cells. Initially, the T-cell precursors, known as thymocytes, start as being 
double negative (CD8-CD4-), and the thymocyte undergoes gene rearrangement of its T cell receptor (TCR) 
during this process. When a functional TCR is produced, the thymocyte is now double positive (CD8+CD4+)  
and ready to undergo positive and negative selection [7]. Firstly, the thymocytes undergo positive 
selection, where the thymocytes are tested for recognition of self-MHC affinity by interaction with cortical 
epithelial cells expressing both MHC class I and class II. Interaction with MHC class I or class II leads to 
commitment to the CD8+ or the CD4+ lineage respectively. Next, the thymocytes surviving the positive 
selection then undergo negative selection. Here, they are tested for strong recognition of self-peptides, and 
thymocytes with a TCR strongly reacting to self-peptides are deleted to avoid autoimmunity. At the end of 
selection, a T cell becomes a single positive, completes their maturation and migrates to the peripheral 
lymphoid tissues as a mature naïve T cell. 
Because of the strict selection both during the positive and the negative selection, less than 5% of the 
thymocytes survive and become mature CD4+ or CD8+ T lymphocytes

1.3.1 T cell receptor 
The TCR is responsible for the recognition of peptides presented by the MHC molecule, which in turn leads 
to the activation of the T cell. The TCR is composed of a two separate transmembrane glycoproteins, the α 
and the β chain. Each chain is composed of two domains namely the variable region (V) and the constant 
region. Importantly, the variable region is the portion of the TCR which interact with the peptide-MHC 
(pMHC) complex.  
The TCR is a multi-subunit complex with the CD3 molecule playing an important role. Since binding of the 
TCR to the pMHC complex is not enough to induce a response, intracellular signalling events are mediated 
through the CD3ζ chain and other CD3 co-receptor subunits (comprised of one CD3γ chain, one CD3δ chain 
and two CD3ε chains). Specifically, all of these CD3 chains have the so-called immunoreceptor tyrosine-
based activation motifs (ITAM) located on them. When the TCR binds to its respective pMHC complex, 
phosphorylation of the ITAMs is initiated, subsequently resulting in activation of a cascade of downstream 
intracellular signalling events, which in turn leads to T-cell activation.

1.3.2 Priming of naïve CD8+ T cells 
Naïve T cells circulate in the periphery, where they search for their cognate antigen presented by APCs. In 
the lymph node, the naïve T cell may encounter dendritic cells and momentarily bind to them via cell 
adhesion molecules. In this way, a naïve T cell can scan through large number pMHC complexes on a 
dendritic cell. Upon specific antigen recognition (cognate antigen), conformational changes occur in the co-
adhesion molecule, which prolongs the cell-to-cell interaction.  
Specific antigen recognition of the pMHC complex with the TCR together with its CD8 co-receptor, known 
as signal 1, is solely not enough for the T cell to become fully activated. Two other signals are needed for 
the full activation of the T cell. The second signal is provided by a co-stimulatory molecule, which promotes 
expansion and survival of the cell. B7 is a co-stimulatory molecule presented on the APC, and interacts with 
the CD28 molecule on the T cell. The third signal needed for the T cell to differentiate into diverse subsets is 
primarily driven by, but not restricted to, cytokines. Specifically, dendritic cells secrete cytokines like IL-12, 
IL-7, and IL-15, which then directs the T cell to differentiate into individual T-cell subsets; described in 
details in section 1.4 Cytokine stimulation.

1.3.4 CD8 T cell effector function  
When an activated effector CTL encounters its target, the CTL induces programmed cell death by apoptosis, 
thus resulting in target-cell killing. The mechanism by which an activated CTL induces apoptosis is via the 
release of specialised cytotoxic granules; a specialised type of vesicles that carry distinct cytotoxic 
molecules. Upon target recognition, the CTL becomes polarised, which rearranges the cytotoxic granules 
towards the immunological synapse (the interface between the CTL and the target cell). Here, the vesicles 
fuse with the cell membrane, resulting in release of the vesicle content to the target cell. Two major 
cytotoxic molecules are found in these vesicles, namely perforin and granzymes. Firstly, perforin, which is a 
calcium dependant pore forming protein, forms holes in the cell membrane and thereby  allows for the 
delivery of granzymes to the target cell. Although the detailed mechanism underlying this currently remains 
to be fully understood, it is believed that perforin places itself in the cell membrane forming a pore in which 
granzymes can pass. Both granzymes and perforin are needed in the killing of target cell. When delivered 
into the cytosol, granzymes target BID and caspase-3. BID is cleaved into truncated BID (tBID) which acts on 
the mitochondria to release cytochrome c into the cytosol, while activated caspase-3 activates another 
molecule CAD which is a DNase. Cytochrome c, promotes the apoptosis while CAD fragments DNA, thereby 
resulting in target cell killing. 
In addition to the abovementioned cytotoxic effector molecules, CD8 T cells also produce a variety of 
effector cytokines, in particular IL-2, Interferon γ (IFN-γ), tumour necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) and CD107a 
which all contribute to the immune defense. IL-2 is defined as an early cytokine and is produced shortly 
after a naïve T cell has been activated. IL-2 is essential for the proliferation and survival of activated T cells. 
TNF-α can bind to TNFR-I/II, which has an intracellular death domain, that induces apoptosis via activation 
of caspase-8. Besides inducing apoptosis, TNF-α also plays a role as an inflammatory cytokine by  
recruitment of other immune cells to site of infection [8]. INF-γ has a wide range of functions, amongst 
others activation and recruitment of macrophages to the site of infection. Moreover, IFN-γ also increases 
the expression of MHC class I and II [9] molecules on the surface of T cells and is involved in inhibiting of 
viral replication. The lysosomal-associated membrane protein-1 is commonly referred to as CD107a, which 
is a component of the lysosomes membrane [10]. When degranulation of a cytotoxic cell occurs, lysosomes 
containing CD107a in its membrane are transported to the cell surface where they then become accessible 
[11]. 
1.3.5 Subsets and effector functions 
As previously mentioned, T cells can be divided into distinct subset of effector cells based on their 
phenotype. As T cells differentiate, they lose or gain expression of distinct surface molecules, which are 
used to characterize individual subsets. It was first suggested in the 1980’s that naïve and memory T cells 
could be separated on the basis of expression of surface molecules including the two isoforms of the CD45 
molecule (CD45RA and CD45RO), the lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1  (LFA)-1, and LFA-3 [12].  
More recent studies have identified additional surface molecules that are now used for the more complex 
identification of CD8+ T-cell subsets as illustrated in figure 4.  
Naïve T cells (TN) are characterized as CD45RA+, CCR7+, CD28+, CD57- cells. Upon antigen recognition splicing 
of the CD45 gene is triggered and CD45R0 is expressed on the cell surface instead. CD45RO is associated 
with the TCR, which in turn makes the cell more sensitive for antigen recognition. LFA-1 and LFA-3 are 
adhesion molecules which play a role in lymphocyte homing, cell-cell interactions and migration. As TN 
becomes activated, it firstly differentiates into an effector cell and potentially subsequently differentiating 
into a memory T cell. Memory T cells have been further divided into central memory T cells (TCM), effector 
memory T cells (TEM), and stem memory T cells (TSCM). These TSCM  cells resemble a naïve T cell on several 
==PAGE:16==
   
 
 
15 
 
markers, it still express the CD45RA surface marker, but in contrast to the naïve T cell, the memory stem T 
cell express CD95 also known as FAS; a common death receptor expressed on CD8+ T cells. Overall, FAS-
mediated apoptosis works to induce homeostasis of peripheral tolerance [13]. Upon binding to its ligand 
(FASL/CD95L), the ligation of FAS induces apoptosis in the target cell expressing the ligand. 
Central memory T cells are characterised as CD45RA-, CCR7+, CD28+, CD57+ cells.  As their name indicates, 
central memory cells have the potential of homing to secondary lymphoid tissues, thereby resembling TN 
and TSCM but without the CD45RA expression. C-C chemokine receptor type 7 (CCR7), a peripheral tissue 
homing receptor, directs the migration of T cells into the lymph node via the high endovascular venules 
(HEVs) by the binding of CCL21. The effector memory cells (CD45Ra-, CCR7-, CD28-, CD57+) lack the 
peripheral tissue homing receptors CCR7 and CD62L in contrast to the less differentiated subsets of T cells. 
Instead, these cells display characteristic adhesion and chemokine receptors required for homing to 
inflamed tissues (Phenotypic TCM, TEM) such as CCR3 and CCR5 
Terminally differentiated T cells or Effector T cells (TEFF) express high levels of cytotoxic effector molecules 
in comparison to memory T cells. These TEFF cells have lost the expression of CCR7, CD28, and CD27, while 
they re-express CD45RA. While having a high potency to kill by their high expression of cytotoxic effector 
molecules, TEFF lack the potential for self-renewal and proliferation. They also express high levels of CD57, 
which is used to define exhausted and senescent T cells [14]. 
A linear model has been suggested for the differentiation of T cells based of their phenotype by up or 
downregulation of certain markers [15]. The less differentiated T cells have more proliferative power and 
self-renewal potency; whereas the more differentiated T-cell subsets display more potent effector 
functions (Figure 4).

1.4 Cytokine stimulation 
Cytokines is a broad term classifying a pool of effector molecules produced by a given cell used to affect 
another cell. Cytokines can be produced by various types of immune cells such as DCs, T cells, 
macrophages, and B cells amongst others. The cytokine repertoire involves chemokines, interleukins (IL), 
interferons (INF) and tumour necrosis factors (TNF). Several cytokines have been identified for the 
activation of naïve CTLs, mostly recognized are IL-12 and IL-18 produced by DCs, which induce the 
production of IFN-y by the CTLs [16] [17]. 
1.4.1 Gamma chain receptor and their cytokines 
IL-2 is a cytokine which induces proliferation and differentiation of activated T cells by binding to the IL-2 
receptor (IL-2R) on the surface of the T cells. A naïve T cell only expresses the γ and β-chain of the IL-2R, 
which has a moderate affinity for IL-2. When a naïve T cell is activated in the presence of co-stimulatory 
CD28, it induces the T cell to co-express the α-chain which then forms the high affinity IL-2R. The T cell also 
produces and secretes IL-2; thereby creating an autocrine loop.  
==PAGE:18==
   
 
 
17 
 
It has been identified that binding of IL-2 to its receptor does not itself induce signal transduction in the 
cell. Once in complex with the γc-receptor, it mediates signal transduction for proliferation and survival 
[18]. The common γc-chain receptor family involves IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21 [9]. Their internal 
signalling utilizes the JAK-STAT signalling pathway. Specifically, IL-2, IL-7, and IL-15  activate STAT5, whereas 
IL-21 activates STAT3; thus resulting in different downstream signalling and cytokine secretion [19].

1.4.2 Inhibition of T cells 
Besides expressing co-stimulatory receptors, T cells also express co-inhibitory receptors on their surface. 
The inhibitory receptors are important in maintaining homeostasis and play a crucial role in controlling 
auto-reactivity [21,22]. Several inhibitory receptors exist on the surface of T cells such as the cytotoxic T-
lymphocyte–associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4), which binds the same co-stimulatory molecule as CD28, namely 
B7-1/B7-2. Since CTLA-4 has a higher affinity for B7, the receptor acts as competition to CD28. Other 
receptors such as the programmed death-1 (PD-1), lymphocyte-activation gene 3 (LAG-3), T-cell 
immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3 (Tim-3), and B- and T-lymphocyte attenuator (BTLA) also 
induce an inhibitory signal pathway; thus playing a role in the control of T cells. Inhibitory receptors are 
transiently expressed after T-cell activation, and it has been shown that these receptors can be upregulated 
on the surface of exhausted T cells [21]. 
1.5 Cancer immunotherapy 
Cancer cells are abnormal cells which have lost the response to control cellular growth and death. An 
intrinsic balance of genes that controls growth and those that supress are present in a normal cell. A 
normal cell undergo over 20.000 Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) damaging event per day, which is either 
repaired by different pathways or killed by tumour immunesurveillance system [23]. As tumour cells have 
multiple genetic alterations some genetic alterations are expressed as tumour associated antigens (TAA). 
Some normal cells become tumours as they evade immune surveillance, cells expressing stress signals as 
well as TAA are recognised and killed. Some cancerous cells evade elimination and enters a state of 
equilibrium by expressing fewer antigens on their surface as well as loss of their MHC class I expression 
[24]. They may also express immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1, TGF-β and soluble Fas ligand [25]. 
The tumour not only evade elimination by downregulating or upregulating certain molecules but also by 
creating a tumour microenvironment that supports tumour proliferation. The tumour microenvironment 
shields itself from the immune system by recruiting cells like MDSC and regulatory T cells that supress TIL’s, 
==PAGE:19==
   
 
 
18 
 
natural killer (NK) cells and macrophages. To combat tumours, several types of cancer therapies have 
emerged as chemotherapy, surgery and more recently immunotherapy.  
1.5.1 Immunotherapy  
For a long time, it has been known that the immune system play a key role in the development and control 
of cancerous cells. Therefore there has been an interest in harnessing and or using the immune system in 
combatting tumours cells. ACT is a type of immunotherapy in which cells is isolated from a patient tumour 
and expanded in-vitro of tumour specific CD8 T cells which are the infused back into the patient. There are 
underlying two types of ACT; generation of tumour infiltrating lymphocytes TILs and genetically engineered 
T cells.   
One arm of cancer immunotherapy is ACT. Here, the patient’s own T cells are used for autologous transfer. 
In details, the TILs are excised from the tumour and cultured in the presence of high doses of IL-2, OKT3 
(anti-CD3) and irradiated feeder cells. Overall, ACT with expanded TILs have shown impressive clinical 
effects especially in patients with metastatic melanoma [26]. The very first time such TILs were used in ACT 
therapy was for treatment of a cohort of twenty melanoma patients. Here, the objective response (OR) rate 
reached 55% [27]; a very impressive clinical outcome. Despite this, the average OR rate in the majority of 
similar trials has remained way below 50% [27].  
In addition to this, the duration of response following treatment with ACT has often been fairly short, and 
the re-infused TILs are rarely found in circulating even a few days post administration [27]. Since the 
presence of these re-infused TILs is important for response to therapy, approaches such as lymphoid 
depletion by chemotherapy prior to ACT has been attempted. In a study cohort comprising 93 melanoma 
patients receiving lymphoid depletion prior to ACT, only 22% of the patients showed complete tumour 
regression, while 20% showed ongoing complete regression [28]. Although lymphoid depletion increases 
the amount of circulating TILs following re-infusion, the OR rate remains approximately the same as 
without lymphoid depletion; thus underlining the need for further improvement of ACT strategies.  
In line with this, another limitation associated with conventional ACT therapy includes the remerging of 
regulatory FoxP3+ T cells, which have been shown to negatively correlate with the clinical response to ACT 
[29]. Moreover, another challenge lies within the repertoire of T-cell specificities present in the expanded 
TIL cultures prior to ACT. Rather than being encompassed solely tumour-specific T cells, the expanded TIL 
product often contains a broad variety of T-cell specificities. This repertoire may for instance include virus-
specific T cells, which do not provide any beneficial effects when it comes to generating an anti-cancer 
immune response in the patients following ACT. In fact, rapidly expanded cultures has shown to favour 
virus specific TILs in contrast to cancer specific TILs [30]. Taken together, the current approach for 
generating TIL products for ACT therapy could greatly benefit from strategies specifically providing a 
targeted expansion protocol.   
In addition to the conventional ACT therapy, infusion with genetically engineered T cells has become a 
desirable approach due to the possibility of providing the T cells with a new and improved T cell receptor 
(TCR) prior to infusion. In particular, chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cells have in recent years 
revolutionized the field of cancer immunotherapy of B cell lymphoma. These modified CAR-T cells express 
an artificial antigen receptor towards a given specificity. The first generation of CAR-T cells were derived 
from three distinct parts. Firstly, an extracellular antigen recognition domain composed of a single-chain 
variable fragment from the heavy and light chain of a B-cell receptor separated by a linker. Secondly, a 
hinge and transmembrane domain, and lastly an intracellular domain composed of composed of a TCR-
derived CD3ζ-domian [31,32]. Recently, more potent CAR-T cells have been developed encompassing 
==PAGE:20==
   
 
 
19 
 
different intracellular domains such as the co-stimulatory CD28 or 4-1BB molecules; referred to as second 
generation CAR-T cells. Third and fourth generation of CAR-T cells have also been developed, where the 
CAR-T cells encompass three or four intracellular domains [31].  
For all CAR-T cell generations, the single chain variable fragment of the antibody drives the specificity 
against the target, for instance tumour antigens. Once the CAR-T cell recognized and binds its target, the 
cell becomes activated, starts to proliferate and expand, and mediate cytotoxic effector T-cell functions 
leading to tumour cell killing [33]. In 2017, the anti-CD19 CAR-T-cell therapy Tisagenlecleucel [34] (Novartis) 
received FDA approval for the treatment of children and young adult patients with relapsed and/or 
refractory lymphoblastic leukemia [35]. Such approval was based on an impressive overall remission rate of 
more than 80% alongside a 1-year survival of 80% [36]. Shortly following this approval, a second CAR-T-cell 
therapy also targeting the CD19 molecule received FDA approval for the treatment of adult patients with B-
cell refractory large B-Cell Lymphoma patients, namely Axicabtagene Ciloleucel (Kite Pharma) [26]. 
Moreover, these agents showed great promise in patients with B-cell lymphomas, and studies are on its 
way in several solid cancer types including lung, prostate, pancreas, breast, and colon cancer [35].  
Despite these impressive results in the clinic, there is still room for improvement when using CAR-T cells for 
cancer immunotherapeutic purposes. Overall for ACT, strategies allowing differentiation of less exhausted 
CD8+ T cells have great potential. Notably, the therapeutic efficacy has recently been shown to be highly 
influenced by the CAR-T-cell phenotype used for infusion [33]. In details, CAR-T cells displaying a T memory 
stem cell TSCM phenotype were reported to be more persistent  and effective at mediating anti-tumor 
immunity when compared to T central memory cells TCM [37]. Thus, the efficacy of CAR-T cell-based therapy 
can be improved by infusing cells with a more optimal (less differentiated and less exhausted) phenotype.  
When expanding the infusion product prior to ACT, cytokines are added to the cultures. Conventionally, the 
use of IL-2 has been the golden standard for in vitro T-cell expansion, as this cytokine has shown to be 
crucial for both the proliferation and subsequent differentiation of T cells. However, such approach with T-
cell expansion in the presence of high dose IL-2 is not optimal as it primarily drives the differentiation of 
effector T cells rather than formation of T-cell memory [38]. Several studies point towards a T-cell 
expansion protocol with low doses of IL-2 favouring the generation of early memory T cells [39,40]; a 
phenotype beneficial for therapeutic purposes. Moreover, and even though IL-2 induced the greatest 
overall expansion, addition of IL-21 to an anti-CD3-based stimulation protocol was shown to be superior in 
generating functional cytotoxic memory CD8+ T cells [41]. Notably, this approach also avoided the 
concurrent generation of regulatory T cells.  
In general, combining various cytokines has shown great promise for expansion of T cells. For instance, 
combining IL-15 with IL-21 was found to boost the generation of IFN-y-producing cytotoxic T cells [42,43]. 
Specifically relating to the expansion of CAR-T cells, it has become evident that CD3/CD28 stimulation in 
combination with IL-7 and IL-15 generates CAR-T cells with potent effector functions while still retaining the 
desirable TSCM phenotype [44]. In addition, expansion protocols for ex vivo stimulation of CAR-T cells with 
IL-7 and IL-15 showed increased frequency of CD8+ TSCM cells, which exhibited greater anti-tumour activity 
[45]. In another study, CAR-T cells were once again stimulated with IL-7 and IL-21, which confirmed the 
generation of TSCM cells able to mediate a long-lasting anti-tumour immune response [46]. Taken together, 
a great potential for improving the persistence and efficacy of CAR-T-cell therapy lies within the expansion 
protocol used for expansion prior to administration of the cells.

1.6 Scaffold  
Although ACT for generating TIL’s provides a way to expand tumour reactive T cells, this way of generating 
T cells still lack the ability to expand only tumour reactive T cells, the ability to generate T cells with a 
preferably young phenotype with the ability for self-renewal properties and a non-exhausted phenotype. 
These expanded T cells also lack the potential to generate high expression of tumour killing cytokines as 
INF-γ, TNF-α. For the generation of CAR-T cells the ability of generating a young and non-exhausted 
phenotype with current technologies has shown problematic. In this study we try to overcome the many 
obstacles seen with ACT by stimulating T cells with an artificial APC, namely scaffold stimulation. 
A scaffold consists of a polysaccharide dextran backbone. To this backbone multiple streptavidin conjugates 
are coupled. To each streptavidin conjugate, four separate molecules can be coupled. Biotinylated 
molecules can be coupled. The interaction between biotin and streptavidin is one the strongest non-
covalent bindings there is, this ensures that molecules bound to the streptavidin dextramers stay bound. 
Molecules as pMHC are bound to the dextran backbone. Generally the interaction between one pMHC and 
its receptor is weak, but with more pMHC molecules bound to the dextran backbone increase the avidity of 
the interaction of the scaffold with a T cell. Co-stimulatory molecule such as CD86 and cytokines can also be 
coupled to the streptavidin dextran backbone. By coupling all molecules, pMHC, co-stimulation and 
cytokines the scaffold mimic that of a dendritic cell which has the ability to stimulate naïve T cells to 
become primed and set in motion the proliferation and differentiation of the T cell.  
By coupling pMHC, co-stimuli and a preferable cytokine profile, proliferation of T cells with a given 
specificity will be expanded and not those with a different TCR. The cytokine profile can vary from one to 
several cytokines. This gives the opportunity to generate a set of cytokines that stimulates a proliferating T 
cell to become a T cell of young phenotype.

2 Aim 
The aim of this study was to generate phenotypically relevant and antigen-specific CD8+ T cells from a naïve 
repertoire by antigen-directed scaffold stimulation, co-attached with co-stimulatory CD86 and various 
cytokine profiles.  
Throughout this study, AviTag cytokines were tested for their proliferative power of CD8+ T cells. These 
cytokines were then attached to the artificial antigen-presenting scaffold in combination with chemically in-
house cytokines, with pMHC being the driving force to expand CMV-specific CD8+ T cells from PBMC 
material from healthy donors. Moreover, these scaffolds were used to expand CAR-T cells, with the CD19 
molecule replacing the pMHC as the driving force for specific expansion. Scaffold stimulation of CAR-T was 
expanded with cytokines directing them towards a young phenotype while retaining their cytotoxic effect by 
the secretion of effector cytokines.  
The specific aims investigated in this thesis were as listed below:  
 Investigating the potential for using quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR) for 
identification of CMV positive and negative PBMC donors. 
 Comparison of AviTag cytokines with in-house chemically biotinylated cytokines for their ability

 Exploring the ability of Antigen-Scaffold to induce proliferation and differentiation of naïve CMV-

 Evaluating CD19-specific scaffold stimulation for proliferation and differentiation of CAR-T cells.

3.1 Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction 
Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a method used to amplify very little amounts of DNA or 
RNA from various types of samples to a very large amount. The qPCR method is based on a normal 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR), where an additional primer (probe) or DNA binding dye is added as a 
fluorescent. The DNA binding dye such as SYBR® green binds double stranded DNA (dsDNA), while probes 
are specific to a certain DNA fragment. The probes illuminate fluorescence when the DNA polymerase 
degrades the probe and releases a fluorophore from its quencher. Fluorescence is continuously monitored 
during PCR amplification.  
The process of PCR includes is denaturation of DNA, annealing of primers, and lastly elongation. In a sample 
tube, DNA is heated to approximately 95°C where the dsDNA is separated. The temperature is then 
lowered to around 60°C; thus allowing a set of primers to bind to each of the single stranded DNA 
sequences. The annealing temperature varies with the experiment due to the melting temperature (Tm) of 
the primers used. When primers are bound to their complementary DNA sequence, the temperature is 
raised to an optimal temperature for the DNA polymerase. The DNA polymerase synthesises a new dsDNA 
strand by adding free deoxynucleoside triphosphate.  
3.1.1 DNA isolation 
Isolation of DNA is the first step towards an analytic purpose such as the qPCR. The DNA extraction kit used 
here, PathogenFree DNA Isolation Kit (GeneProof, Brno, Czech Republic), uses the Solid-phase Nucleic acid 
extraction method, which allows for a quick and effective purification. In practice, there four steps are 
involved in the extraction: lysis of sample material, adsorption of nucleic acids to a membrane, washing to 
clear unwanted material, and elution of nucleic acids [47]. 
DNA extraction is executed as described in the “body fluid protocol section” in the PathogenFree DNA 
Isolation Kit. In brief, PBMC were diluted 1:10 in sterile PBS, and 200 µl of the diluted sample were 
transferred to a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. Serum samples remained undiluted. The samples were then lysed 
and purified using microcentrifuge according to manufacturer’s protocol. The eluted DNA obtained were 
either used directly for qPCR quantification afterwards or stored at -20°C.

3.2 Magnetic Associated Cell Sorting (MACS) 
3.2.1 Naive CD8+ T Cell Isolation 
MACS is an effective way to separate cells depending on their surface molecule expression. In this thesis, 
the Naïve CD8+ T Cell Isolation Kit human (Miltenyi Biotec, Bergish Gldabach, Germany) was used, which is 
developed to isolate naïve CD8+ T cells from PBMCs. Isolation is based on two separate steps; first the 
sample is depleted of non-naïve CD8+ T cells with a mix of biotinylated monoclonal antibodies against 
surface molecules CD45RO, CD56, CD57, and CD244. These molecules have been described to be present 
on effector and memory T cells as well on NK cells [48]. The monoclonal antibody mix is then mixed with 
microbeads conjugated to anti-biotin monoclonal antibodies. These beads will bind the biotinylated 
antibody, which is already bound to effector/memory T cells as well as NK cells. By adding the mixture to a 
MACS column placed in a magnetic field, the naïve CD8+ T-cell population and other determinants will pass 
through the column while effector/memory T cells and NK cells remain bound to the column.  
Second, the naïve CD8+ T-cell population is subjected to positive selection. The mixture of CD8+ T cells are 
mixed with microbeads conjugated to anti-CD8 antibodies. The mixture is then added to a MACS separation 
column, where naïve CD8+ T cells will stay bound while other determinants will pass through. Lastly the 
pure naïve CD8+ T-cell population is eluted from the column and collected. An illustration of the process is 
shown in figure 7.   
The specific procedure of isolating naïve CD8+ T cells was performed according to the Protocol for Naïve 
CD8+ T Cells Isolation Kit human. Depletion of non-naïve CD8+ T cells was done through a MACS LS column 
(Miltenyi Biotec, Bergish Gldabach, Germany). Positive selection of CD8+ T cells was done with a MACS MS 
column (Miltenyi Biotec, Bergish Gldabach, Germany).

3.3 Ultraviolet (UV) exchange  
UV-mediated exchange is a key aspect of generating several pMHC complexes for staining of antigen-
specific T cells in short amount of time.  UV-mediated exchange is dependent on a so-called conditional 
ligand, which is a peptide that can be triggered to break to pieces upon exposure to UV light.  As depicted in 
Figure 8, the UV-sensitive conditional ligands are cleaved, and the conditional ligand loses its ability to bind 
to the MHC monomer; consequently the peptide dissociates from the MHC complex. In the absence of the 
conditional ligand, the complex disintegrates. However, in the presence of another ligand, such peptide can 
bind to the MHC complex and thereby rescue the complex [49].  
3.3.1 UV exchange protocol 
HLA monomer and peptide were diluted to a concentration of 200 µg/mL and 400 µM, respectively. The 
diluted HLA monomer and peptide were mixed in equivalent ratios (1:1) in either a 96-well or 384-well 
plate dependent on the volume of mixed HLA and peptide. The final concentration of monomer was 2 µM 
and 200 µM for the peptide. Importantly, the peptide was added in excess to ensure that a peptide was 
present in abundance at all time to minimize potential disintegration of MHC molecules. Monomers and 
peptide were UV-exchanged in a CAMAG UV Cabinet 4 (CAMAG, Muttenz, Switzerland) at 366nm for one 
hour at room temperature. The plate was then centrifuged at 3,300 rpm for 5 min followed by transfer of 
the supernatant to a new 96-well or 384-well plate.

3.5 Scaffold filtration 
For the majority of assays, a scaffold filtration step was used prior to stimulation in order to remove 
unbound components. To a “Pall Nanosep® centrifugal device with Omega membrane 300 kDa” (Pall, Port 
Washington, United States of America), 500 µl of sterile filtered 10% bovine serum albumin (BSA) solution 
was added in order to saturate the filter in the column. The spin column was centrifuged at 10,000 g for 5 
min so approximately 100 µl was left in the column. The spin column was then washed twice in 500 µl PBS, 
centrifuged at 10,000 g for 5 min until 100 µl was left in the column. At this point, the filter in the column 
has been saturated. 
The plate containing the assembled scaffold was centrifuged at 3,300 rounds per minute (rpm) for 5 min to 
settle any aggregates that might have formed. The assembled scaffold was then transferred to the 
prepared spin column and put on ice. Next, 500 µl of PBS was added to the spin column, centrifuged at 
7,000 g at 4 °C until approximately 100 µl was left in the spin column. This step was repeated and the final 
100 µl of filtrated scaffold is ready for use.   
3.6 Cell culturing 
3.6.1 Thawing 
Cells were thawed in a 15 ml falcon tube containing preheated (37 °C) 10 mL RPMI1640/10% human serum 
(HS); referred to R10 media from now on. The cells were centrifuged at 1,500 rpm for 5 min and the 
supernatant was discarded. The cell pellet was disrupted in residual volume and re-suspended in 10 mL R10 
media. From this, 10 µl was taken for cell count according to the description in 3.6.3 Cell count. The 
counted cells were centrifuged at 1,500 rpm for 5 min and re-suspended to an appropriate concentration in 
R10 media.  
3.6.2 Freezing 
Cells for freezing were placed in a 15 mL falcon tube containing 10 mL R10 media. The cells are centrifuged 
at 1,500 rpm for 5 min and the supernatant was discarded. Then, 1 mL freezing media, consisting of 10% 
DMSO and 90% FBS, was added to 15 ml tube containing the cells, and the mixture was transferred to a 1.5 
mL cryotube. These cryotubes were placed in a cryobox and quickly moved to a -80 °C freezer. For long 
term storage, the cryotubes were moved to liquid nitrogen.   
3.6.3 Cell count 
In order to count the cells, the cell suspension was thoroughly mixed with an equivalent amount of trypan 
blue (DETAILS). From this mixture, 10 µl were carefully pipetted into a hemacytometer glass grid and 
placed under the microscope for counting. Dead cells were stained by tryptophan blue and therefore 
distinguished from viable cells.  
3.6.4 Cell culturing 
Cells were thawed as according to 3.6.1 Thawing. The cells were cultured in X-vivo 15 media containing 5% 
heat inactivated HS and scaffolds containing pMHC, co-stimulatory molecule B7.2 (CD86), and two sets of 
different cytokines. Cells were co-cultured with 20 U/mL IL-2 if the scaffold added to the culture itself does 
not already contain IL-2. Cells were grown for a total of either two or four weeks at 37 °C with 5% CO2 in a 
round-bottom tissue culture treated 96-well plate. In order to increase the likelihood of the scaffolds and 
the cells to interact, the cultures were often kept for 1 hour in a 96-well plate prior to transfer to a larger 
plate. PBMC cultures were initiated with a concentration of 2-5∙106 cells per well depending on the 
experiment. For naïve expansion, all material was used rather than a specific cell number. Based on cell

density, the cultures were either kept in the 96-well plate for the entire four weeks or transferred to a 
larger plate (48-well, 24-well, or 6-well).  
Cells were stimulated twice a week with new filtered scaffold and fresh media. During the new stimulation, 
half the media was removed, scaffold was added, and the cell cultures were re-suspended to increase

3.8 Flow Cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a powerful tool for analysing multiple characteristics/parameters of a single cell within a 
heterogeneous sample/population. Flow cytometry can be used for several types of analysis such as 
immune phenotyping and cell quantification. Overall, the flow cytometer is composed of three different 
systems namely the fluidics, optics, and electronics[52]. 
The fluidics system transports particles/cells to a laser beam for interrogation. A sample in injected into a 
stream of sheath fluid, which flows at a lower rate than the sample solution. The difference in flow rates 
==PAGE:31==
   
 
 
30 
 
restricts the sample to the core referred to as hydrodynamic focusing []. This centres the cells in the middle 
when they pass through the laser, one cell at a time (single cell level). 
The optic systems consist of different lasers and optical filters, which directs the light scattered from a 
passing cell into the correct detectors. When a cell passes the laser, light will be scattered/reflected in all 
angles; the extent of the light scattered depends on the physical properties of a cell. Such scattered light is 
measured by two detectors: Forward scatter (FSC) is positioned ahead of the laser whereas side scatter 
(SSC) is placed ninety degrees relative to the laser. FSC measures the size of the cell and the forward 
scattered light is proportional to the size of the cell. SSC is proportional to the internal complexity or 
granulation of a cell. Correlation between FSC and SSC allows for differentiation of different cell types, 
typically viewed in a 2D dot plot.    
Another way, by which cells can be analysed, is by fluorescent-coupled antibodies or tetramers, which is 
used in this thesis. Fluorescent antibodies, (antibodies coupled to fluorochromes) are used for determining 
the expression of surface molecules or cell function in the case of intracellular staining. The fluorescent 
compound absorbs light at one wavelength (excitation) and emits light at another wavelength. The 
transition of energy is known as fluorescence, and this fluorescent signal is collected in the same path as 
the SSC. The light passes through series of different filters and mirrors that direct the light to its correct 
detector. To ensure that the light emitted from a fluorochrome reaches the right detector, filters of various 
sort redirects or passes the light. A narrow range filter, for instance a 530/30 bandpass (BP) filter, allows 
light at a length of 530 nm ± 15 nm (515 to 545 nm) to pass (Figure 10). Other filters are also located in the 
optic system namely longpass (LP) and shortpass (SP) filters. E.g. a 670 LP filter allows light of 670nm and 
higher wavelength to pass, while for instance a 620 SP filter only allows light at 620 nm and below to pass 
(Figure 10).

3.8.1 Compensation 
When working with multiple fluorochromes, spectral overlap can occur. Spectral overlap is when two or 
more fluorochromes emit light, which overlap in the light spectrum. As seen Figure 11, Fluorescein 
isothiocyanate (FITC) and Phycoerythrin (PE), two different fluorochromes, are often excited by the same 
laser (seen by the dotted line) and have an emission maximum at 520nm and 575nm, respectively. Despite 
being detected by two different BP filters, FITC still emits light which overlaps into the PE detector, referred 
to as spill over (Figure 11). For this reason, compensation is needed. Compensation calculates how much 
interference a given fluorochrome has to an undesignated channel (in this case the PE channel), and the 
emitted light is therefore subtracted to correctly identify the truly PE-stained cells.  
 
Figure 11: Spill over by different fluorochromes. The fluorochromes FITC and PE are both excited by the 488 argon laser and emit 
light at different wavelengths. FITC is registered by a detector with a BP filter of 530 ±15 nm, while PE is detected at 575 ±15 nm. 
Some fluorescence from FITC is detected by the PE detector, referred to as spill over. By compensation, the FITC signal detected 
in PE BP filter is taken into account and subtracted by a compensation matrix. 
In order to calculate the compensation matrix allowing for the subtraction of spill over, compensation 
tubes were made.

3.8.2 Data analysis  
CD8+ lymphocytes and HLA-specific CD8+ T cells were gated in the following manner on the BD LSRFortessa 
and in FlowJo. First, the lymphocytes were identified by the FSC and SSC scatter plots. From the isolated 
lymphocytes, single cells were gated by the FSC-A and FSC-H scatter to exclude any doublets that may 
otherwise interfere with further analysis. From the singlet population, live cells were gated on the basis of 
Near-IR dead cell marker. This compound binds to free amines on dead cells; thus leaving viable cells 
unstained in the histogram for this marker. From here, CD3/CD8 double-positive cells were identified by 
two separate fluorochromes: BV-480 and FITC (Appendix 10.C). Lastly, HLA-specific CD8+ T cells were 
identified based on their tetramer-specific stains. Only double-positive tetramer-stained cells were 
regarded as HLA-specific T cells.

4.4 MACS separation 
In order to obtain a pure suspension of a given cell type, Magnetic Associated Cell Separation (MACS) is 
often used. Here, MACS was conducted before cell culturing for a single experiment to separate naïve CD8+ 
human T cells from a PBMC samples. The cells were stained with surface markers targeting CD45RA, CCR7, 
CD28, and CD57 to identify and differentiate between different CD8+ T-cell subsets after separation. 
For analysis, lymphocytes were gated based on FSC-A and SSC-A and singlets on FSC-H versus FSC-A. Live 
cells were then gated on NIR negative cells followed by selection of CD8+ cells by gating of CD8-BV480

representative example in Appendix 10.B. The CD8+ subset was separated using CD45RA-PE-Cy7 and CCR7-
APC, which can distinguish the subsets of TEFF, TN, TEM, and TCM by CD45RA+CCR7-, CD45RA+CCR7+, CD45RA-
CCR7-, and CD45RA-CCR7+ respectively. Here, the TSCM subset is not distinguished from TN due to the lack of 
antibodies allowing for the separation of these populations.

4.6 Murine scaffold expansion 
The murine scaffold stimulation experiment was conducted using splenocytes from mice. Specifically, 
C57BL/6 female mice (wild type strain) carrying the H-2b haplotype were either injected with PBS (control 
mice) or primed with the full OVA protein together with poly I:C as an adjuvant. These mice were then 
boosted after 21 days with PBS alone (control mice) or OVA protein with no adjuvant. The mice were 
sacrificed one week after boosting, where spleens were harvested for analysis. 
SIINFEKL is an H-2b-restricted OVA-derived peptide. This immunodominant peptide is an octamer at 
position at 257-264 in the OVA protein [52],which has a high affinity towards the H-2Kb MHC class I 
molecule, also underlined by in silico prediction of SIINFEKL as a strong binder towards H2-Kb (%rank score 
= 0.0095) [53]. 
Splenocytes were cultured in RPMI media containing 10% fetal calf serum, 10 ng/mL recombinant murine 
IL-12, 1M HEPES, 10,000 U/mL Penicillin-Streptomycin, 200mM L-Glutamine, and 50mM 2-
Mercaptoethanol . Scaffold cultures were stimulated for the first two weeks with a scaffold containing 
human IL-2, human IL-7, and human B7.2 co-stimulatory molecules twice a week. For the remaining two 
weeks, the cytokines on the scaffold were replaced with human IL-15 and human IL-21; once again the 
scaffold was added to the cultures twice a week. Stimulation profiles (OVA, free molecules (referred tp as 
FREE), and SIINFEKL) were done in duplicates.

4.6.3 Cytotoxicity assay 
For each of the ten culturing conditions (listed in Table 7), a cytotoxicity assay was performed. Here, three 
different plates were made; one for each time point. The time points were at zero hours (0h) working as a 
baseline, 24 hours (24h), and 48 hours (48h). For each of the culturing conditions and for each time point, 
two wells were made. One was made as a background control with no effector cells (CAR-T cells) added 
(reffered to as No Effector in Figure 24) and one for evaluation of potential CAR-T-cell mediated killing 
(referred to as Effector in Figure 24). In both No Effector and Effector wells, CD19+ cells stained with

5 Discussion 
Patients suffering from either melanoma or B-cell malignancies have shown to have improved clinical 
outcome following treatment with ACT rather than conventional therapies. Melanoma patients receiving 
TIL therapy have shown objective clinical response of 50% [55]. For patients suffering from B-cell 
malignancies, CAR-T cell therapies have shown great promise with a clinical response of 90% [56].  
In many cancer types, patients with high numbers of TILs within their tumours have overall better survival 
and response to immunotherapy when compared to patients without [57–59]. Based on this, human 
tumors are classified as hot (many TILs) and cold (few TILs) based on the degree of immune cell infiltrates 
[60]. Another aspect of immunotherapy is that patients with a higher mutational load have overall better 
clinical outcome [61]. Cold tumors also harbor more regulatory immune cells, making it more difficult for 
the immune system to detect. From this, it is important to generate a high number of specific T cells 
towards cold as well as hot tumors. Importantly, if too few TIL’s are present within the tumor, the idea of 
expanding a naïve repertoire of T cells from blood or tumor represents a promising way to circumvent the 
unspecific expansion and focus the attention toward T cells with reactivity towards tumor. 
5.1 Cytokine proliferation 
Cytokines constitute an important element of the scaffold, directing the differentiation of CD8+ T cells. N-
terminally biotinylated cytokines (AviTag) IL-2 and IL-15 were tested for their bioactivity in comparison to 
naturally occurring IL-2 and IL-15. AviTag cytokines were also tested on scaffold in comparison to chemically 
biotinylated cytokines. The new AviTag cytokines performed almost identical to the recombinant cytokine 
IL-15, but difference in the proliferation profile was seen for IL-2. As with any biological agent, 
modifications such as biotinylation can affect the molecule. For instance, alterations of the structure as well 
as the bioactivity of the molecule might become less active following biotinylation. For the scaffold 
expansion, it was seen that the fold expansion was less than that of the chemically biotinylated cytokines. It 
currently remains unknown why Chem is better than AviTag, but alteration prior to folding of the protein 
might have an impact. This experiment was done once, with single cultures. To have a better interpretation 
of which approach is superior, multiple experiments need to be performed.   
5.2 qPCR 
The relevance of identifying a donor which has a response or no response towards CMV in this case is of 
great significance, a false negative donor sample can tamper with the results. The qPCR method used here 
showed no positive results for confirmed positive CMV donors. From each donor, 10 mL serum was 
collected. From here only 200 µl of serum was taken for DNA isolation as indicated from the DNA extraction 
kit. From PBMC’s a single vial was thawed and DNA isolation was performed. Consequently, the chance of 
obtaining complete CMV-derived DNA is very low. 
CMV is found throughout the body but lies dormant in white blood cells preferable in the salivary glands 
[62]. Therefore, most of the CMV DNA is found in the leukocytes in the salivary glands and less in PBMC-
derived leukocytes. It is not known if CMV is present in the plasma as circulating virus or as free DNA; and 
importantly, serum contains a lot DNase’s which may cut the CMV DNA into fragmented pieces [63]. This 
could explain why the qPCR method does not give any response as CMV DNA could be fragmented and the 
primers therefore do not recognize the fragmented DNA. Moreover, the DNA was obtained from PBMCs, 
where the chance of finding CMV-derived DNA is reduced as mentioned above. A serology CMV kit could 
serve as a replacement to qPCR detecting circulating antibodies 
==PAGE:57==

 in serum. Nevertheless, qPCR stands as a powerful tool as it can detect very low amounts of DNA, as seen 
in the experiment DNA with 101 copy number (Appendix 10.D) was tested as positive.  
5.3 MACS 
Separation of cells into a naïve and a primed T-cell compartment helps strengthen the confidence of the 
naïve scaffold stimulation assay. By isolating the naïve compartment reduces the risk of already primed T 
cells being the source of the expanding T cells. From MACS separation, it was seen that the purity of the 
naïve compartment constituted 74.3 - 90.2% naïve cells. Miltenyi Biotec claims that the separation kit has 
up to 97% purity, but since no lower limit is defined, it is difficult to estimate how to optimise the 
separation. A thought could be that not enough anti-CD45RO, CD56, and CD57 antibody were present, so 
that some of the primed cells would escape the column. Another scenario is that not enough microbeads 
conjugated to anti-biotin are present. Another experiment with excess anti-CD45RO, CD56, CD57, anti-
biotin Ab, and beads could have been performed to see whether the performance of such experiment 
compares with the one presented in the thesis.  
On the other hand, the primed selection showed less than 1% being naïve T cells. This shows that only a 
very little amount of the naïve T cells are lost during this step. When searching for one naïve T cell with a 
given specificity, it is important that such a small fraction of naïve T cells remains in the primed section.  
5.4 Scaffold expansion 
Scaffold expansion of naïve CD8+ T cells did not show any expanded CMV-specific CTLs. On the other hand, 
expansion of PBMC comprising primed cells from CMV-positive donors showed an expansion of CMV-
specific CD8+ T cells. Based on this, it is clear that the scaffold expansion itself is functional; consequently, 
this indicates that other steps in the process of expanding naïve T cells need to be reconsidered. From 500 
mL of blood sample taken from a given donor, PBMCs are collected, which is a representative sample of the 
T-cell compartment within a single donor. Here only a small amount of the 500 mL is used to collect PBMCs. 
However, more than 1011 circulating naïve T cells exist within the body [64], where the majority is T cells 
with different unique TCR’s. The V(D)J gene rearrangement gives the ability to generate a large number of 
different TCRs (1015 possible aa sequence of TCR [65]), and the  sheer number of different T cell specificities 
are vast. For this reason, the chance of a donor which has not encountered CMV and has one naïve T cell 
towards CMV in millions of cells and that the particular T cell was in the blood sample taken from a patient 
is exceptionally small. The possibility of expanding a naïve repertoire in theory sounds reasonable but in 
practice has proven really difficult.  
Another aspect is the inexperience of T-cell expansion. As seen by the work in this thesis, all experiments 
are challenged by the low cell viability following four weeks of culturing. Cells growing in culture can die of 
various reasons, contamination being the most fatal. However, no contamination was observed at any 
point. Moreover, it is important to be aware of the cell culture density so that the T cells do not die of 
starvation. On the other hand, cultures with too much space might die as well, as they die if there are no 
nearby cells. As this can be very different from culture to culture, it can be difficult to deciding e.g. when at 
culture is too dense.  
Scaffold expansion of murine splenocytes was also performed. In general, the foreign antigen OVA, with the 
SIINFEKL being the immunodominant peptide, have been used widely and is a well characterised model 
system in mice. From the murine study, no SIINFEKL-specific T cells were seen in any of the samples. As 
seen from previous scaffold experiments, no response was seen when attempting to expand naïve CD8+ T 
cells. Even when attempting to expand OVA-positive mouse splenocytes (obtained from OVA-immunized 
mice) by stimulation with SIINFEKL scaffold, no SIINFEKL-specific CD8+ T cells were observed. To rule out 
==PAGE:58==
   
 
 
57 
 
that the murine H-2Kb/SIINFEKL tetramers did not work, OT-1 cells were spiked into a random splenocyte 
sample. This OT-1 spiked sample showed a nice separation of SIINFEKL-positive T cells. This confirms that 
the PE/ APC tetramers are functional and therefore the lack of SIINFEKL-specific CD8+ T cells must be due to 
other reasons. To why no SIINFEKL-specific CD8+ T cells are shown could be due to different reasons. 
Murine cells are often reported to be difficult to culture compared to human T cells. In addition, murine 
cells appear more prone to death by freezing or thawing. Another important aspect is that activated T cells 
has different phenotypic characteristic. If all SIINFEKL-specific CD8+ T cells were comprised of TEM and TEFF, 
which theoretically could be the case for the primed splenocytes due to immunization, they would not 
express the lymph node homing receptor CCR7 and therefore not be found in the spleen but in blood. 
Consequently, when using these splenocytes for the scaffold expansion experiment, the T cells of interest 
might not even be present in the splenocyte sample.  
5.5 CAR-T cells 
Scaffold expansion of CAR-T cells was also performed, since it has been shown that CD8+ CAR-T cells can be 
expanded into a preferable phenotype by certain IL profiles, which then generate CAR.T cells capable of 
producing high amounts of effector molecules. Prior to stimulation, CAR-T cells were phenotypically 
characterised, which revealed that the majority of the cells was TEFF. As the majority of the CAR-T cells 
displayed a TEFF phenotype prior to expansion, the CAR-T cells might have been exhausted already. From 
this, the scaffold stimuli may not have been sufficient for the CAR-T cells to expand or to direct the 
phenotypic profile of the cells.  
Stimulation with anti-CD3/CD28 beads is a very potent activator, and efficiently induced CAR-T cell 
expansion. From the expanded cultures, a three-fold higher number of cells were present in bead expanded 
cultures compared to control and scaffold expanded cultures. This indicates that only the beads were able 
to expand the CAR-T cells.  Several reasons might explain why the scaffolds did not induce CAR-T cell 
expansion. In theory, stimulation through the anti-CD19 receptor should activate its internal CD28, CD3, 
and 4-1BB compartments and thereby stimulating proliferation. Importantly however, the functionality of 
the biotinylated CD19 has not been tested. One thought is that biotinylation of CD19 may not have occur at 
all. This would explain why no scaffold stimulation of CAR-T cells was seen, as CD19 was not bound to the 
backbone and therefore could not active the intracellular compartment of the CAR-T cell receptor. Another 
possibility is that biotinylation of CD19 has occurred at the target site or close to the target site of the anti-
CD19 antibody binding site. Both scenarios can explain the lack of scaffold stimulation and the failed 
attempt to generate a CD19 tetramer.   
From the cytotoxicity study, it was seen that none of the scaffold expanded or control expanded CAR-T cells 
had any killing effect. Importantly and due to the lack of expansion, it is difficult to conclude whether the 
scaffold expanded cultures would comprise killing potential since too few cells were present. Only bead 
expanded cultures showed cytotoxicity towards CD19+ target cells. From the bead expanded cultures, the 
decline in target cells and stationary amount of control cells shows that specific killing has occurred. For all 
scaffold cultures, no specific killing was seen (data not shown)  
Other studies have evaluated CAR-T cell cytotoxicity. In one study, a 4 hour cytotoxicity assay with anti-
CD19 CAR-T cells with 1:1, 1:3 and 1:10 effector to target ratios was performed. This study showed 
approximately 40%, 60%, and 70% specific killing [66]. Based on this, another E:T ratio could have proven 
more efficient as well as selection of additional time points. This would however have required more CAR-T 
cells to be used in the assay.

In addition, ICS was performed on bead expanded CAR-T cells. For each CAR-T cell stimulation condition is 
the assay; for different wells were used: a negative (Neg) well containing no CAR-T cells working as a 
negative control, a LAC stimulated well with CAR-T serving as positive control, CD19+ cells mixed with CAR-T 
cells serving as the target well, and a well containing CD19- cells with CAR-T cells serving as a control to the 
CD19+ well.  For the LAC stimulated well containing only CAR-T cells, it was seen that all the CAR-T cells had 
a cytokine secreting profile. As for the negative control well, all the cells produced very little or no effector 
molecules. For CD19+ wells, cytokine production was seen in over half of the CD8+ T cell compartment and 
for the CD19- just under half of the T cells produced cytotoxic effector molecules. Since the CD19 
separation kit only separates on CD19, the CD19- fraction contains T cells from the separated PBMC’s. Since 
the CAR-T cells cannot be separately gated out from this other T cells, the cytokine-producing CD8+ T cells 
in this sample might be a mix of CAR-T cells and PBMC-derived T cells. The high amount cytokines produced 
in the CD19- negative sample indicates that an alloreaction has occurred. Since the MHC profile of the CAR-
T cells remains unknown, there is a high risk that the PBMC-donor comprised a different set of MHC 
molecules. An alloreaction happens when there is recognition of foreign MHC molecules. Consequently, the 
suspected difference in MHC profiles would induce both the CAR-T cells and the PBMC-derived T cells to 
produce large amounts of cytotoxic effector molecules. When looking at the CD19+ well, the cytokine 
profile is higher than that of the CD19-, but due to the suspected alloreactivity, identification of what the 
CAR-T cells produce towards the CD19- fraction cannot be determined. This problem could have been 
overcome if the CD19 tetramer was working, thus allowing for specific gating of the cytokine producing 
CAR-T cells. However, the specific killing of the CD19+ cells in the cytotoxicity assay clearly support a specific 
recognition of the target cell population.

6 Perspectives 
ACT provides a platform for cancer immunotherapy showing impressive OR of up to 56% [28]. Not all 
patients respond equally to ACT and some does not benefit from treatment. For ACT with TILs, expansion of 
the excised tumour material poses complications. The expansion of T cells is not only restricted to TILs but 
also T cells of other specificities, such as regulatory T cells and virus-specific T cells. Other than the 
expansion of antigen specific T cells, it has been established that CTL expanded T cells with a younger 
phenotype has been correlated with a better killing potential for TIL and CAR-T cell expansion [41,42,43]. 
Moreover, it has been shown that stimulation with various cytokines in combination or alone drives the 
differentiation of young phenotypic characteristics. By expanding T cells with an artificial antigen-specific 
derived scaffold, it seems possible to direct the expansion of the tumour-specific T cells and circumvent 
expansion of other irrelevant T-cell origins such as virus-specific T cells. By the addition of cytokines to the 
scaffold, expansion of phenotypical preferable CD8+ T cells can be expanded. 
Culturing of naïve cancer specific T cells from patients with no TILs has proven to be possible. A study 
expanding neoantigen specific naïve T cells has been performed [67]. To this, another group has reported 
the use of expanding specific T cells by an artificial APC, a nanoparticle with anti-CD28, pMHC and adsorbed 
IL-2 [68]. Together, these data indicate a great potential for scaffold expansion of naïve TILs, from either 
blood or tumour. As seen, scaffold expansion of an existing T-cell response is possible. This application can 
then be used in cancer immunotherapies such as treatment of melanoma patients to generate TILs in 
combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors for better clinical outcome. If a solution to the scaffold 
protocol for expansion of naïve cancer-specific T cells is provided, using this approach for treatment of 
patients with no TILs or few, as seen in cold tumours for instance, could prove beneficial.   
In addition, scaffold-expansion of CAR-T cells also has promise. These genetically modified T cells provide a 
great potential for cancer immunotherapy. Although durable remissions have been observed, the clinical 
benefit of CAR-T cell therapy is limited due to severe toxicities either by cytokine storms or cross-reaction 
with self-proteins [69]. Moreover, CAR-T cell therapies are currently limited to B-cell malignancies and still 
have a long way to go in terms of use for solid tumors cases as there is a lack of unique tumor antigens. 
Scaffold-expansion is not a solution to the lack of defined antigenic targets or toxicities. However, a 
combination of protocols allowing for instance the incorporation of suicide genes into CAR-T cells with a 
scaffold-based expansion protocol may provide a strategy for efficient and persistent in vivo functionality of 
CAR-T cells, which can be switched off when needed.

7 Conclusion 
Scaffold expansion of naïve CD8+ T cells obtained from peripheral blood provides a great alternative to 
current therapies applied for ACT. Specifically, such scaffold expansion from a naïve T-cell repertoire could 
allow efficient ACT therapy towards cold tumours.  
Firstly, AvitTag cytokines tested in this thesis showed to have a proliferative power of CD8+ T cells in 
comparison to recombinant cytokines. When used on the scaffold, these Avitag cytokines showed to have a 
slightly lower fold expansion of the HBV specific T cells in comparison to the in-house chemically 
biotinylated cytokines. Scaffold expansion of CD8+ T cells towards a specific CMV peptide was only possible, 
when expanding from a primed selection of PBMCs, but not from naïve T cells. Expansion of naïve murine T 
cells also proved to be difficult. Next, expansion of CAR-T cells was attempted both with beads and scaffold; 
however, only the beads were able to induce proliferation. Evaluation of CAR-T cell cytotoxicity showed 
that bead expanded cultures had the ability to kill specifically CD19+ cells; thereby showing the potency of 
these genetically modified cells. Moreover, ICS proved that CAR-T cells can produce large amount of 
effector molecules including CD107a, IFN-γ, TNF-α and IL-2, but to what degree this was caused by 
alloreactivity needs to be further examined. Also, the mechanism behind the specific killing observed in the 
cytotoxicity assay should be evaluated in more details.   
Together, the work presented in this thesis shows that scaffold expansion of CD8 T cells has great promise 
but needs optimization to functionally work in a naïve setting. The established and functional ICS and 
cytotoxicity assays will be of high importance when evaluating future optimization approaches in the 
expansion of CAR-T cells. In conclusion, the scaffold expansion of PBMC’s attempted here as a proof of 
concept did not show expansion of any naïve CD8+ T cells. This shows that scaffold expansion of specific 
CD8 T cells proves to be difficult and need further work in order to be successful.

8 Future Prospects 
Overall, changes in the approach towards the naïve scaffold stimulation protocol in order to get an 
expansion of CMV specific CD8 T cells are needed. If an approach with successful expansion can be 
established, further experimentation with expansion of cancer-specific CD8+ T cells, for example melanoma, 
would provide the next step towards scaffold stimulation becoming a part of therapeutic cancer 
immunotherapy. Down the line, scaffold expansion would to be tested on tumour materials and in vivo for 
treatment of tumour-bearing mouse models as a last step. 
In this thesis, functional cytotoxicity- and ICS assays were established. These assays should be applied to 
the expansion of naïve CD8+ T cells, when a functional expansion protocol has been made, to determine the 
secretion of effector molecules produced.  
Another aspect, which might benefit from optimization, is the cytokine profile applied to the scaffold. 
Different sets of cytokine have shown the ability to generate different subsets of the CD8+ T-cell 
compartment. These cytokine profiles, as well as others could be tested to exploit the best possible 
combination on scaffold for proliferation, differentiation, and production of effector molecules in the CD8+ 
T-cell compartment.
