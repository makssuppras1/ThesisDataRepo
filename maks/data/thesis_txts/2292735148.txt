Abstract
 
Abstract 
The use of steel sheet piling has become popular among different types of structures, due to 
their availability and efficiency from structural point of view. The subject of the present thesis 
emerged from the interest in employing the sheet piles as bridge abutments. This situation is 
particularly met in the railway project “The new line Copenhagen – Ringsted” where a series 
of bridges are needed to be considered. The objective of the thesis is to identify if there is any 
significant load spread at the lower part of the sheet piles under vertical load actions that is 
able to influence the present design approaches towards potentially more economical designs.  
In order to accomplish this objective, a scaled model is aimed to be used in performing a 1-g 
experiment and numerical model with various scenarios will be defined. The load spread will 
be analysed by investigating the distributed reaction force at the lower part of the wall. 
The materials in the two models will be defined as having linear elastic behaviour for both the 
soil and the structure. The aim of the experiment is to confirm the viability of the numerical 
model, thus the later can be used in further investigations concerning the problem. 
Appropriate geometry setup and scaling laws for the 1-g test will be adopted. Three test 
phases will be defined performing under appropriate instrumentation. There will be used 
strain gauges for identifying the horizontal strains on the wall. The data recorded will have a 
statistical assessment in taking mean value from the right samples. 
Appropriate boundary conditions following the experiment will be defined for the numerical 
model. Considering the linear elastic assumption, Mohr-Coulomb model will be used for the 
soil formulation. Stresses, strains and displacements will be investigated during this method. 
Comparison in terms of strains will be performed between the experiment and numerical 
model. It will be investigated a vertical and a horizontal section through the wall. 
In the end, a small parametric study is expected to be performed, investigating the total 
reaction force under the sheet pile wall in order to investigate the load spread.

Chapter 1.Introduction
 
Chapter 1. 
Introduction 
Due to the relatively reduced weight, the use of steel sheet piling has become popular among 
different types of works and structures. Produced in a wide range of sizes and widths in Z and 
U sections, steel sheet piling provides the maximum strength and durability at the lowest 
possible weight consistent with good driving qualities. The availability of the sections in 
different grades of steel makes their use a very good choice from economical point of view, 
covering the requirements of any given environment conditions. The continuous shape is 
assured with the interlocking system which is available also for corner and junction piles to 
cover all the requirements. 
According to Piling Handbook (Piling Handbook 8th edition, 2008) sheet piles are typically 
used for: 
• River control structure and flood defence 
• Ports and harbours 
• Pumping stations 
• Bridge abutments 
• Road widening retaining walls 
• Basements 
• Underground car parks 
• Containment barriers  
• Load bearing foundations 
• Temporary works 
The subject of the present thesis emerged from the interest in employing the sheet piles as 
bridge abutments. This situation is particularly met in the railway project “The new line 
Copenhagen – Ringsted” where a series of bridges are needed to be considered.  The bridge 
abutments have to carry vertical loads in addition to the horizontal loads from the retained 
soil. The vertical load spread through the sheet pile walls is not well defined in the literature. 
Therefore investigating the behaviour of this type of structures under in plane loading is the 
main focus of the present study. 
1.1 Background 
“The new line Copenhagen – Ringsted” is an on-going project of Banedanmark consisting of 
a new dual track, electrified railway running from Copenhagen to Ringsted via Køge. The 
budget is DKK 10.4 billion and the line is due to open in 2018. The line is designed to handle 
speeds of up to 250 km/h for passenger trains and is Denmark's first railway for high speed 
trains. (BaneDanmark).  
This project implies the construction of numerous road and rail bridges where sheet piling is 
used for bridge abutments. There are two ways that this can be done (Piling Handbook 8th 
edition, 2008):

There are several situations when in plane loading can occur in a bridge abutment. One case is 
when the use of anchors is not perpendicular to the wall, thus inducing an in plane component 
of the force from the anchor. This can be the situation when anchors in the embankment side 
of the wall are needed and the layer of soil is not big enough to overtake the load, hence the 
anchor can be rotated to a better position either horizontal or vertical.   
Another case is the axial loading of the sheet piles from the upper structure of the bridge. This 
includes the loads from the deck and from the trains or vehicles depending on the purpose of 
the bridge. These two cases are illustrated in the Figure 1.2 showing the load component

1.2 Problem statement 
In case a sheet pile is used as bridge abutment, the axial loading from the upper structure of 
the bridge will induce in plane loading in the sheet pile. The important aspect is whether the 
load spreading is critical or not, which can lead to a more economical design. This implies the 
use of shorter sheet piles as the structure needs to carry less vertical load. The spreading of the 
load leads to the occurrence of the compression forces at the upper part of the elements and 
tension forces at the bottom part. Hence it is important to check if the sheet piles can resist to 
these horizontal forces. They are depicted in Figure 1.3, where the additional load from train 
combined with the reaction from soil leads to the occurrence of horizontal forces. 
For the problem introduced above only one track is considered and isolated. Therefore, for 
simplicity the problem will focus on a reduced area of the sheet pile wall. In addition to that, 
the load considered is the load coming from train.  
The objective of the thesis is to identify if there is any significant load spread at the lower 
part of the sheet piles under vertical load actions that is able to influence the present design 
approaches towards potentially more economical designs. In order to accomplish this 
objective, a scaled model is used in performing a 1-g experiment and numerical model with 
various scenarios is considered. The load spread is analysed by investigating the distributed 
reaction force at the lower part of the wall.

2.2 Vertical load capacity 
Axial loads acting on the top of the steel abutment are loads directly from the bridge deck 
superstructure. The vertical loads from the bridge deck superstructure comprise dead loads, 
traffic loads, and environmental loads, and are transferred from the bridge deck to the 
abutment via bridge bearings. These bridge bearings are commonly seated on a reinforced 
concrete pile capping beam (which forms the top of the sheet pile abutment) and are 
positioned on or as near as possible to the vertical axis of the steel sheet pile. This 
arrangement at the top of the sheet pile therefore enables an effective direct transfer of load to 
take place from the bearing via the capping beam and thereafter axially through the pile. 
Finally the axial load in the pile is transferred to and resisted by the surrounding soil.

3.2 1g-experiment 
3.2.1 Introduction 
Currently, there are commonly two ways of approaching engineering problems depending on 
their complexity. If there is a simple problem, an engineer may use empirical or semi-
empirical methods or closed form solutions. On the other hand, if there is a complex problem, 
finite element analysis or testing of physical models at small scale are preferred.  
A structural model is “any structural element or assembly of structural elements built at 
reduced scale (in comparison with full size structures) which is to be tested, and for which 
laws of similitude must be employed to interpret test results” (Harris & Sabnis, 1999). 
Depending on the material properties of the real scale prototypes, structural models at reduced 
scale can be part of 1g experiments or n-g experiments. Typically, models including materials 
with linear elastic behaviour are part of 1g experiments, whereas models consisting of non-
linear plastic materials are part of n-g experiments.  
 N-g experiments require an increase in the acceleration value and are usually conducted in 
centrifuge laboratories. Using this method, the conditions in the real scale prototype can be 
obtained in the small scale model. 
Geotechnical problems involve knowing the constitutive behaviour of soil i.e. stress-strain 
behaviour. It is known that the soil is highly non-linear and plastic as shown in Figure 3.1. 
Therefore it is very difficult for the physical model to contain prototype stresses and strains. 
This cannot be done without increasing the gravitational acceleration and the easiest way to 
do it is by spinning the soil sample in a centrifuge.

Sometimes it is not easy to identify the parameters dominating the problem, therefore some

Some examples such as the analysis of slip surfaces, the passive earth pressure problem, piles

loading care carried out with success using 1-g tests (Hettler, 2010). 
As mentioned before, the objective of the current study involves analysis of the axial load 
spreading in the steel sheet pile walls. As steel is an elastic material the problem can be 
treated using 1g experimental method.  
3.2.2 Scaling relationships 
Experiments at less than full-size may generate incorrect responses if the materials 
mechanical parameters are not considered accordingly. Therefore, at 1-g engineering 
properties of scaled-building components must be modified to compensate for the lower 
allowable soil stresses to generate realistic building responses to soil displacements. Reactions 
at scaled-loads must be akin to reactions of the prototype behaving under full loading. 
By considering the length in any direction scaled 1/N, the scale for the other parameters can 
be computed. 
There are some important aspects to be considered for 1g experimental work: 
• The length in any direction is scaled with n factor, L*=1/N 
• Since it is 1g test, the acceleration remains the same, g*=1 
• The material has the same density, ρ*=1 
Having the basic parameters, the other mechanical properties of the soil and retaining 
structure can be determined: 
• Volume, V=L3 => V*=1/N3 
• Area, A=L2 => A*=1/N2

3.2.3 Geometry setup 
The experiment elaborated for the present study was carried out in a 0.50 m wide, 0.71 m long

continuous trapezoidal profile. The general dimensions of the wall are chosen such as they are 
close to the spreading area assumption in the railway bridge abutment mentioned earlier. The 
thickness and the profile section are characteristic to Areco TP 20/35, found on the market.

3.2.7 Known limitations and problems 
The main problems and limitations observed during the 1-g experiment carried out are the 
following: 
• The overall geometry of the wall might not be accurate, since the cutting process of 
the trapezoidal plates is not accurate. This means that the wall might not be in contact 
to the top plate or to the bottom of the container on the whole length, providing 
inaccurate results; 
• The base support might induce small bending moments at the lower part of the wall if 
the model tends to tilt when the load is applied. 
• The paint and coating on the profile was not removed completely only on the areas 
where the gauges were applied.  
• Imperfections in the trapezoidal profile have been observed. Not all the flanges have 
the same width which is 35 mm and the height is not constant everywhere.  
• The strain gauges might not be positioned in the middle of the flanges therefore 
signals might not come from the expected locations. 
• The sand density cannot be increased too much due to the drop height limitation. 
(Leth, 2011); 
• During all the experiments, big fluctuations of the signals are observed. It was noticed 
that even if small movements or noises are taking place next to the container without 
being in contact with it, small signal fluctuations occur; 
• The angle of friction and void ratio are not the actual parameters of the sand. They are 
assumed based on the volume and density of the first 200 mm layer of sand. 
• Same parameters for sand are assumed for the remaining soil added in phase 3 
considering the same conditions for pouring the sand. 
• During the phases 2 and 3 the vertical load capacity of the wall was not computed due 
to approximation of the sand sample. Therefore the maximum load is not known and 
some slips can occur as the load increases.  
• The wall has been pushed in the sand manually, therefore it might not be perfectly 
vertical

3.3.3 Conclusions 
Considering the limitations and problems for carrying out the experiment as described earlier 
in this chapter and the plots for strains illustrated above, it is most likely that some errors in 
measurements that cannot be asses, have occurred. There are gauges that are consistent and 
follow relatively linear behaviour when the load is increased. They are mostly the gauges 
investigated on the vertical section of the wall. However the horizontal section indicates that 
gauges behave differently under different boundary conditions whether is soil or steel base 
support. In this area, as the load increases there are recorded values changing from 
compression to tension. This is an indication that the gauges are very sensitive when the 
material they are applied to is in contact with elements with larger stiffness, as is the case of 
the base support.  
Given that the increase in load has not linear elastic increase in strains, it can be concluded 
that some errors in the measurements are produced and were not assessed. It is most likely 
that the errors are linked with the observations described in section 3.2.7.Known limitations 
and problems.

4.2 Modelling aspects 
4.2.1 Element type 
In this section the elements used throughout the study will be introduced. Since the steel 
elements have the thickness much smaller than the other dimensions, shell elements have 
been used. Therefore all the steel parts are modelled as shell elements.  
Conventional shell elements use this condition to discretize a body by defining the geometry 
at a reference surface. In this case the thickness is defined through the section property 
definition. Conventional shell elements have displacement and rotational degrees of freedom. 
In contrast, continuum shell elements discretize an entire three-dimensional body. The 
thickness is determined from the element nodal geometry. Continuum shell elements have 
only displacement degrees of freedom. From a modelling point of view continuum shell 
elements look like three-dimensional continuum solids, but their kinematic and constitutive 
behaviour is similar to conventional shell elements. (Abaqus Analysis User's Guide). The 
differences between the two types of shell elements are illustrated in Figure 4.1. 
 
Figure 4.1 – Differences between conventional and continuum elements (Abaqus Analysis 
User's Guide) 
 
==PAGE:50==
Chapter 4. Numerical model
42
 
In order to reduce the computation time, it was decided that the model uses in this case 
conventional shell elements. All the steel parts modelled throughout the present study use 
conventional stress/displacement shell elements with 4 nodes (S4).

4.6.4 Conclusions 
Considering the assumptions made in this chapter, the models investigated are consistent. The 
first phase is strongly connected to the second phase by producing similar behaviour on the 
retaining wall. The sand around the wall acts like a support for the wall and generates same 
stress and strain distributions above its level. Furthermore, the embedded area in Phase 2 has 
similar tendency like the embedded area of Phase 3. It was expected that the results are not 
much different between the phases, since the soil has not reached its full vertical stress. This 
leads to the conclusion that the friction applied in the wall should be very small. 
The parameters of the wall material are influenced by the way the vertical load is transmitted 
to the wall. Acting as point loads in the middle of the covered flanges and given their uneven 
number, the vertical load is transmitted eccentrically to the retaining wall. This induces 
bending moment in the retaining wall which influences the overall response.

Chapter 6. 
Conclusions 
The use of steel sheet piling has become popular among different types of structures, due to 
their availability and efficiency from structural point of view. The subject of the present thesis 
has been developed from the interest in employing the sheet piles as bridge abutments. This 
case leads to the occurrence of in plane loading from the upper structure which is the case of 
numerous rail bridges from the “New line Copenhagen-Ringsted”. The objective of the thesis 
was to identify if there were any significant load spread at the lower part of the sheet piles due 
to vertical in plane loading. This has been investigated by conducting a 1-g experiment and a 
numerical model developed in Abaqus/CAE. 
The experiment had the role of approving the numerical model for a deeper investigation in 
this sense. It was assumed that the materials constituting the experiment behave linear-elastic 
and that the model is in relation with a real scale prototype in some regards. A simple steel 
model has been defined and Fontainbleau Sand has been used. Seven tests conducted in three 
phases have been carried out. The focus was to investigate gradually the behaviour of the 
wall, from the case without soil (Phase 1) to a case similar to prototype case (Phase 3). The 
Tests have been performed based on the limitations and observation described in the 
corresponding chapter. Statistical interpretation of the data obtained in order to pick the right 
value for every load has been assessed. By analysing the results it has been concluded that 
some errors in measurements have occurred for some gauges in some tests, and that parts of 
the results can be reliable in other tests.  
The numerical model has been created based on the assumptions made in the experiment, 
following the same density and angle of friction. The sand has been defined using Mohr 
Coulomb model assuming linear elastic behaviour. It has been concluded that the results are 
not much different between the phases, since the soil has not reached its full vertical stress 
and that the parameters of the wall material are influenced by the way the vertical load is 
transmitted through the flanges. Acting as point loads in the middle of the covered flanges and 
given their uneven number, the vertical load is transmitted eccentrically to the retaining wall 
inducing bending moment in the retaining wall which influences the overall response. 
Given that the Phase 3 is the model following a prototype, comparisons in terms of strains has 
been conducted between the two methods. It has been concluded that the second set of 
experimental data gives responses closer to the numerical model than first set and gauges with 
eligible response have been identified.  
Based on the assumptions made throughout the study and on the comparison between the 
models, given that the Phase 3 has some parts confirmed by the experiment, a small 
parametric study has been conducted. It has been deducted that given the conditions in the 
numerical model a small value of the vertical load is spread throughout the wall. This is due 
to the reduction in the Young’s Modulus or due to the increasing in angle of friction with a 
more influence from the stiffness parameter.

7.5 Appendix E – Abaqus preliminary model 
 
In order to choose the optimal general shape of the wall, two Abaqus analyses were 
performed. One considers a rectangular general shape, while the other a trapezoidal shape.  
The contour plot of the stress distribution across both models reveals important information 
regarding the plate to be used. Figure 7.9 and Figure 7.10 illustrate the stresses in x direction 
for both plates. Since only the tensile stresses are on interest, the plots have been normalized 
to 0 for the lower limit, such that the distribution is better seen. As can be noticed, the values 
are pretty much close to each other, which lead to conclusion that using the rectangular plate 
it’s the better choice thus keeping the experiment simple.
