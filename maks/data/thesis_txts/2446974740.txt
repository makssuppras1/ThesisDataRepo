Abstract 
Sustainable corporate transition seems to be of particular difficulty in the ICT sector. Through a 
comprehensive literature study, it was determined that the underlying reason and a solution to the problem 
were to be found through analysis of linkage between three different areas: 1) A general struggle in all sectors 
of implementing sustainability measures (the SDGs) into corporate practice. 2) ICT sector issues and 3) 
Corporate sustainability reporting. The reason why companies of the ICT sector are struggling with 
implementing the SDGs (implicitly sustainable business conduct) was found to be rooted in inadequate 
linkage between these three areas.  
It was found that focus of the ICT sector is on being the enabler of the SDGs for businesses of other sectors, 
neglecting own sector issues. This, combined with insufficient legal requirements to disclose on impact 
hotspots and sector issues as well as a general tendency to conduct “SDG washing” in corporate sustainability 
reporting, was found to be the main reasons for the lack of convincing sustainable corporate performance.   
An SDG sector roadmap was found to be a sound solution for alleviating corporate sustainable transition in 
the ICT sector. Such roadmap was found to i) help unify sector goals, targets and action plans, ii) generate 
cohesive attention on supply chain management and iii) generate foundation for transparent and 
comparable sustainability reporting.

This master thesis project has been conducted at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) at the 
Department of Management, Sustainability Division, Qualitative Sustainability Assessment (QSA) group. The

recommendations for future SDG implementation in companies.  
The present study, the third in line “The Next Step Towards Sustainable Business – An UN SDGs and ICT sector 
case study”, narrows down the subject to one sector, the ICT sector, and aims at shedding light on what it 
takes for this sector to get the next one step in the right direction for achieving the Global set 2030 Agenda.

1 Introduction 
Electronic products and services bring extensive benefits to societies on a global scale, and rapid innovation 
and lowering costs have dramatically increased the access to the different application areas, and as a result, 
the use of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) products and services (TechTarget, 2007). In the 
ICT sector one major focus is on how the ICT solutions can facilitate and even fast-forward the progress of 
sustainable transition and development for any of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) within any 
sector, represented by the #ICT4SDG project and tools launched by International Telecommunication Union 
(ITU). (ITU, 2019)  
Although ICT offer new opportunities for development and for problem solving, it is widely recognised that 
in the wake of this development follows a wide range of negative and very serious impacts on our global 
society and environment and new reports puts emphasis on how the current system of the ICT sector itself 
needs to reboot. (ITU et. al., 2019)  
The Reporting Matters initiative has through its research, since the launch of the Sustainable Development 
Goals (SDGs) in 2015, proved that corporate Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG) reporting, also 
called sustainability reporting, does matter for successful integration of sustainable practices into corporate 
conduct. (WBCSD and Radley Yeldar, 2018) SDGs are relevant for corporate reporting. They are intended to 
constitute a shared set of priorities among businesses, to enable companies to report on sustainable 
development and performance using a common set of indicators. (GRI et. Al., 2016) However, the complex 
nature of the SDGs and the immense amount of unsorted and uncategorised material on implementation of 
the SDGs makes it overwhelming and confusing for companies to find a successful approach towards the 
integration of the goals into their business conduct. (Piche, 2018) Many states that the SDGs in general have 
been obliged to by the private sector, here amongst the consultancy PwC in their comprehensive research 
“SDG Reporting Challenge 2018”.  However, the same research also shows that though 72 % mention the 
SDGs in their corporate reporting, only 50 % chose priority SDGs, 27 % embedded SDGs into business strategy, 
and only 23 % manage to disclose on meaningful targets and Key Performance Indicators (KPI) related to the 
SDGs. (PwC, 2019 (a)) 
Although the issues of negative impacts created by the ICT sector are recognised by global leading 
organisations (ITU et. al., 2019) and although sustainability reporting is widely applied in various sectors, 
compliance with the concept of sustainable corporate transition seems to be of slow and difficult character 
in the ICT sector. These observations raise the question of why sustainable transition seems to be of difficulty 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
in this specific sector? The problem of incorporating responsible and sustainable business conduct (SBC) into 
the ICT sector seems to consider three aspects:  
1. The incorporation and implementation of the SDGs into business, as these are considered the 
contemporary level for sustainable business conduct  
2. The nature of the negative impacts of the ICT sector  
3. Corporate sustainability reporting, as this is known to have an effect on successful transition towards 
SBC 
1.1 Research hypothesis 
The hypothesis underlying this study: 
The difficulties of corporate sustainable transition in the ICT sector are rooted in 
inadequate linkage between sector issues, relevant SDG material and reporting 
frameworks, but, the SDGs have the potential to alleviate this transition. 
To investigate this hypothesis following research questions were set up:  
1) How does ICT relate to the SDGs? 
2) What is the status within the ICT sector in terms of communicating on sustainability, the SDGs and 
responsible business conduct, respectively? 
3) What are the key issues within the ICT sector related to the SDGs and to communication on 
sustainability? 
4) What can be done to alleviate the issue of transition to sustainable business conduct within the ICT 
sector? 
5) How can ICT companies use the SDGs to transform their business conduct into sustainable business 
conduct? 
1.2 Aim of study 
This study aims at providing businesses within the ICT sector information about that next step necessary to 
take to reach sustainable business conduct.

1.3 Scope of study 
This section lists the boundaries and scope defined for the study and its extensive subject.  
The study takes on a corporate perspective on matters, as the research intends to provide solutions for 
businesses.  
The standard of sustainability considered in this study were based on the Sustainable Development Goals as 
these are considered the contemporary level for sustainable business conduct 
The ICT sector was defined to cover any private producer or provider of software solutions and/or services, 
and electronic hardware equipment as well as the components supporting these. This covers any type of 
computer hardware, e.g. laptops and phones but also the intelligent electronic equipment of machineries in 
e.g. vehicles, production units, touch-screen controlled coffee machines etc. E-commerce is not included in 
the scope. 
The boundaries for sustainability reporting frameworks were not based on geographical regulations. 
Geographical limitations were sought to be avoided as the ICT sector is a global sector with multinational 
enterprises interacting across borders. To be considered a framework had to be of relevance to the ICT sector 
independent of location, it had to provide guidance on incorporating of- and reporting on sustainable 
business conduct and it had to be a holistic framework considering all aspects of sustainability, as it must 
represent all the SDGs.

About the literature 
The amount of literature available on this subject is very vast and most of the time available for this study 
has been used for going through this, to limit the material of which to go into depth with, to be the most 
updated, and good and sound references.  An approach to narrow down the assessed material to be the most

systematic as possible.  
The following criteria made up the overall frame for the literature study.  
o This study takes on the approach to view the issues from a company perspective 
o This study builds on previous studies which have researched both relevant scientific material and 
grey (non-scientific) literature on SDG implementation into companies from 2015 – 2017:  
Lock-Lorenzen, K (2017): Assessment of the SDG Compass and Guide in relation to the Danish 
follow-up Plan and the use of the compass by a Danish multinational enterprise by same Author as 
this study (maiden name; Katrine Lock-Lorenzen), 2017, and  
Piche, A. (2018): Implementation of the UN Sustainable Development Goals in companies by Aline 
Piche, 2018 
o This study focuses on the ICT sector 
o Though the SDGs set the contemporary standard for sustainability, sustainability is not defined by 
the SDGs. Therefore, other frameworks can represent sustainable conduct.   
 
As established in Piche (2018), the pool of grey literature available online focusing on SDG implementation 
into business is huge and very unmanageable and it has been expanding exponentially with time since 2015. 
Furthermore, the study of Aline Piche only addressed the material of the World Business Council for 
Sustainable Development (WBCSD) SDG Hub and relevant material might lie beyond this. For this reason, a 
comprehensive literature search for company orientated and publicly available online material, which lay

beyond previous studies was conducted. As this study researches a specific sector (ICT sector), material on 
sustainability, not necessarily including the SDGs, was also included in the review.

transition in business as general and the ICT sector specifically. Due to limited time and the extensiveness of 
the subject, the study was forced to single out the most important, updated and relevant material to make

2.1 Sustainable Development Goals and Business 
The SDGs was launched by the United Nations (UN) in 2015 and are comprised of 17 global goals, under 
which lies 169 targets, that by 2030 should all be met on a global scale. This global recognised political 
agenda, that shall transform our world, known as 2030 Agenda, aims at eradicating poverty and create a life 
of dignity and opportunity for all by redirecting investment flows toward the challenges the goals represent. 
(UN (a), 2015; GRI et. Al., 2016) The 2030 Agenda represent a framework for everyone (governments, 
individuals and companies) to act on, however, the SDGs especially call on all business to take actions on 
solving the challenges of sustainable development as the success of the UN 2030 Agenda relies heavily on 
action and collaboration among all actors (GRI et. Al., 2016; UN (b), 2015).  
Concrete policies and action plans alongside with global partnerships on reaching the SDGs and targets are 
essential, however, the full implementation of the Addis Ababa Action Agenda (A.A.A.A.) is critical for 
reaching the 2030 Agenda. (UN (a), 2015) The A.A.A.A. encourages sustainable corporate practice, including 
ESG reporting, and impact investing; combining a return of investments with non-financial impacts. (UN (b), 
2015) This means that companies must do an effort to close the gap between standards of sustainable 
business performance and reporting prior to the SDGs and the new expectations which come with the 2030 
Agenda – an ambitious and aggressive universal agenda to transform our world.  
Sustainable development in business is only achievable through integration and acknowledgment of 
economic, social and environmental concerns throughout corporate decision-making processes. Weak 
sustainable development is defined by appointing more value to manufactured capital than value of social 
or natural origin. Strong sustainability recognises the unique features of capital derived from all thee. (Emas, 
2015) The expectations for industries to strive for strong sustainability is reflected in the 2030 agenda. They 
are to equally consider people, planet and prosperity in all their endowers to drive sustainable transition in 
the industry. (UN, 2015 (a)) The interaction between the three elements: people, planet and prosperity 
represents the social, environmental and economic aspects of sustainability and is commonly displayed as in 
Figure 1. The core principle of this model is that sustainability only can be obtained through a balanced model,

achieved from addressing all three aspects of sustainability in business conduct assessments and decision-
making.

2.1.1 Incorporation of SDGs into Business in general 
To call upon industry sectors, to play a major role in promoting greater efficiencies in their life cycles and to 
develop sustainable consumption and production patterns, is not a thing emerging with the SDGs. It is an 
ideal formed at the Earth Summit in Rio in 1992 and the move towards a sustainable life here on earth has 
been ongoing in ever more ambitious steps ever since, latest as the UN Millennium Development Goals (2000 
- 2015) and now the Global Goals (2015 - 2030). (UN (c), 1992) The SDGs are, thus, just a new and higher 
standard for what it means to conduct responsible and sustainable business and as subjects to the UN 
businesses carry a responsibility to strive for this transition and reach of the 2030 Agenda.  
Corporate and organizational success requires stable economies and healthy, skilled and educated workers, 
among other factors and high-quality information on business performance is one of the major drivers for 
business having success with sustainable transition. Research show proof companies taking interest in Social 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) and sustainability experience increased performance, competitiveness 
and investor support. (UNGC (a), 2019; Bernal-Conesa J. A., 2017) By integrating sustainability considerations 
across the value chain, companies can protect and create value for themselves by increasing sales (ex. due 
to increased brand trust), developing new market segments, strengthening the brand (brand trust, investor 
support), improving operational efficiency, stimulating product innovation and reduce employee turnover. 
Benefits that many leading companies have experienced by successfully integrating sustainability principles 
into corporate strategy and practice. (GRI et. al., 2016; WBCSD, 2019 (a))  
The idea behind applying the SDGs directly onto business, which is encouraged but a voluntary initiative, is 
to enable companies to report on sustainable development and performance using a common set of 
indicators related to the goals and targets and thereby constituting a shared set of priorities among 
companies. (GRI et. Al., 2016) Companies are encouraged to build the SDGs into their communication and 
sustainability reporting and to advance sustainable development through the investments they make, the 
solutions they develop and their business practice, the latter by reducing their negative impacts while 
enhancing the positive contribution to the sustainable development agenda (UN (a), 2015). Thus, in 
summary, the SDGs are intended to provide companies with a solid framework for assessing the sustainability 
of business practice and to help them communicate more transparently on how they address their 
responsibility and how they act on this by conducting their business. However, though a vast amount of 
material on SDGs for business purposes exist, however, previous studies shows that approximately 75% of 
these are on the topics of: (a) describing the current situation of SDG application, (b) reasons for companies 
to get involved with the SDGs and (c) financially opportunities which the SDGs can deliver to companies, none 
of which describes how companies goes about implementing the 2030 agenda into their strategies and 
transforming their business to be SDG orientated. (Piche, 2018) As recognised by many companies, 
organisations and researchers there exist a gap between the political set 2030 Agenda and the translation 
and incorporation techniques into business goals and targets, resulting in companies struggling with the 
implementation of the SDGs into business strategy and conduct.  (Lock-Lorenzen, 2017; Piche, 2018)  
Regarding the translation of the SDGs into business opportunities and providing inspiration on application 
ways into business strategy, Piche (2018) found this area to be covered with several tools. Especially Blueprint 
for Business Leadership on SDGs, 2017 was deemed helpful by Piche (2018) for this purpose and for this 
reason this study focuses not on the translation and understanding of the SDGs, but on the actions on 
implementation into corporate practices.  
As a general tool for implementing the SDGs into corporate strategy and practice three guides, directly 
emanated from the SDG compass constituting a tripodal SDG-tool: Business Reporting on SDGs, is deemed 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
the best single standing tool for companies on integrating the SDGs into corporate strategy and business 
conduct. This valuation is based on previous studies on the subject by Katrine Lock-Lorenzen and Aline Piche. 
(Lock-Lorenzen, 2017; Piche, 2018) The Guides are: the SDG Compass Guide (GRI et. al., 2016), Integrating 
the SDGs into Corporate Reporting: A practical Guide (GRI and UNGC, 2018 (a)) and An Analysis of the Goals 
and Targets (GRI and UNGC, 2018 (b)). The interlinkage between the three and how the second and third 
report supports the SDG Compass Guide is illustrated in Appendix I. The SDG Compass and Guide where in a 
previous study deemed insufficient in terms of letting companies to understand how to use the SDG 
Compass, how to implement the political set goals into business strategy as well as how to properly 
communicate on the usage and conformity of the SDGs. (Lock-Lorenzen, 2017) Though this was considered 
true in 2017, the new additions to the Guide, improve radically on the weaknesses of original material and is 
the only material found to address both the subject of the positive impact business can have on SDG but also 
the positive impact SDG can have on business, if implemented both effectively and efficiently. The material 
will be addressed further in the chapter Reporting on the SDGs. 
Reflections on effectiveness and efficiency and the information provided in the SDG compass Guide reveals, 
that a company being effective in generating its SDG strategy can fail in achieving the benefits from pursuing 
the SDGs if not being efficient in doing so (implementing it). However, the opposite is true as well. A company 
being ineffective (not complying properly to the SDGs) can still gain market benefits from being efficient at it 
from doing nice sustainability reports (thus, giving the impression of complying with the SDGs in the way 
intended by the 2030 Agenda, without doing so, and thereby creating a “image” at a lower cost). This paradox 
result in conflict of interest, since the latter approach requires much less resources. This leads to a necessity 
to look further into the principles, requirements and guidelines on corporate sustainability reporting, to get 
an understanding of how reporting affects the transition of companies and sectors towards a sustainable 
practice.  
In terms of investigating the complications of incorporating sustainability practices into businesses the 
WBCSD advise to focus implementation of the SDGs into sectors rather than companies, as it is the entire 
sector which must change and that lone standing companies cannot drive this change (WBCSD, 2018). Based 
on this argument this study analyses the challenges of sustainable transition within a sector but aims at 
keeping in mind the company perspective towards solution generation.  
Apparent from the literature study on the SDGs and its interlinkage with business, corporate reporting plays 
a major role in successful sustainable transition, implementation of the SDGs, and sustainable business 
conduct and growth. For this reason, the nature of corporate reporting and its linkage to the SDGs was further 
investigated and is elaborated on the subsequent sections of the chapter Business and Reporting.

2.1.2 Sub summary 
 
 
 
 
The SDGs, 17 goals and their 169 targets, were launched by the UN in 2015 and constitute the 2030 
Agenda. This global recognised political agenda is set to transform our world. It aims at eradicating 
poverty and create a life of dignity and opportunity for all by redirecting investment flows toward the 
challenges the goals represent. 
The SDGs are binding goals for the World’s governments but are also intended to provide companies with 
a solid framework for assessing sustainability of business practice and helping them communicate more 
transparently on how they address responsibility and how they act on this by conducting their business. 
The SDGs are intended to constitute a shared set of priorities among businesses to enable companies to 
report on sustainable development and performance using a common set of indicators. 
The 2030 Agenda and the Addis Ababa Action Agenda clearly states that sustainable corporate practice, 
including ESG reporting, and impact investing; combining a return of investments with non-financial 
impacts, are crucial to achieve the global political agenda set out for 2030. The number of frameworks, 
material and standards about SDGs and business is exponentially increasing. However, most of the 
material is not directly on how to incorporate the SDGs into business, let alone reporting on the practice. 
Thus, there is a gap between the politically set 2030 Agenda and the translation and incorporation 
techniques into business goals and targets. Therefore, companies struggle with the implementation of 
the SDGs into business strategy and conduct.

2.2 ICT Sector 
ICT represents all devices, networking components, applications and systems which combined allow people 
and organisation to interact in the digital world. It encompasses both wireless networks and the internet-
based sphere. It includes antiquated technologies (landlines telephones, radio and television broadcast) 
alongside cutting edge technologies like artificial intelligence and robotics. Figure 2 illustrates the 7 overall 
categories of the ICT sector. Sometimes ICT and IT (Information Technologies) are used synonymously, but 
ICT represent a broader more comprehensive list of all components related to computer and digital 
technology. (TechTarget, 2007)

2.2.1 Sector trends 
The ICT sector is one of the fastest growing sectors in world trade and accounts for approximately 10 per 
cent of global merchandise exports. (WTO, 2019 (a)) It is ground for profound concern regarding 
management and disposal of related products (E-waste). Current regulations for the sector are recognised to 
be insufficient for ensuring security in this area. Especially the possible negative impacts occurring from great 
national variations in ICT product regulations and standards are of great concern. However, the challenge 
which yet has to be resolved lies in fashioning a set of global regulation to ensure consumer safety, health 
and environment without discriminating against any members of the WTO. (WTO, 2019 (b)) 
The number of ICT products are projected to reach up to 50 billion by 2020 and as a result of rapid growth in 
the amount of ICT hardware, e-waste is the fastest growing waste stream in the world and reached 48.5 
million tonnes in 2018. If future scenario is business as usual, this amount is estimated to reach 120 million 
tonnes by 2050. Today only 20% of the e-waste is dealt with appropriately. The majority of the remaining 
80% ends up at landfills or are disposed of by informal workers, often children, in primitive conditions of high 
health risk. (ITU et. al., 2019; WHO, 2019 (a)) This disposal method is the main way of retrieving valuable 
metals from electronics and for the dominating part constitutes the recycling model of the industry. 
However, e-waste represents mayor financial opportunities for business and the ICT sector, but it requires 
improvement of resources management. In terms of precious metals alone the material value of annual 
produced e-waste is worth € 55 billion, equal to three times the annual output of the worlds silver mines and 
in one tonne of e-waste there is 100 times more gold than in one tonne of gold ore. Harvesting the resources 
from disposed electronics significantly reduces the CO2-emissions compared to mining for these metals. (ITU 
et. al., 2019)

impact of the use stage of any ICT product or service is not considered significant compared to the above

2.3 Business and Reporting 
In general, corporate reporting can be divided into two categories:  
1. Annual reporting, which provides publicly available information on business conduct and 
performance for various stakeholders. 
2. Legislative reporting, which inform authorities about measures taken on various subjects required to 
report on. For this study focus will be on the annual reporting.  
Annual reporting can in general as well be divided into two categories: financial reporting and non-financial 
reporting, here referred to as ESG reporting (Environmental, Social and governmental). The financial 
reporting, the Annual Corporate Report, covers financial activities: assets, revenues and sales, and disclose 
on the economic state of the business. It is comprehensive, publicly available and intended to provide 
information for shareholders and other bodies taking an interest in a company’s financial performance 
throughout the preceding year. (Governance Professional of Canada, 2016) ESG reporting conducts the same 
purpose, however, as implied, it does not include financial performance, rather it discloses on the two other 
aspects of sustainability, thus, provides information on how a company operate and manage environmental 
and social challenges related to its business. The enticement for ESG reporting is to help shareholders and 
investors, consumers, policy makers and other stakeholder to evaluate the performance and value creation 
of non-financial factors of a business. Further, it serves as encouragement for companies to develop a 
responsible approach to business. (European Commission, 2019 (a)) Both financial and ESG reporting serve 
as a picture of the efficiency and effectiveness of a company’s strategy and operations and both are in most 
cases nationally or regionally regulated to a minor or greater extend.  
Financial information is what drives the global capital market, but a trend of investing in businesses and 
projects based on sustainable performance has immerged. Investors see a strong link between corporate 
sustainability and financial performance. The Sustainability Stock Exchange Initiative argue that stock 
exchange can grow green finance and therefore data on sustainability are increasingly used as rationale for 
investment decisions. The financing world is increasingly on the lookout for ways that allows both private 
and corporate investors to easily comprehend and compare corporate performance, impacts and outcomes 
of businesses within sectors. (IFRS, 2017; Unruth et- al., 2016; Sustainable Stock Exchange Initiative, 2017; 
Trolle, 2019) The CFO of Unilever and Trade and Development Finance Division Vice-Chair puts it this way :“It 
is just a matter of time before most investors will actively ask companies for information on forward looking 
disclosures about potential future financial impacts of climate change - In many cases, this is already 
happening” (WBCSD and Radly Yeldar, 2017)

2.3.1 Annual corporate ESG reporting 
As this study only deals with “annual ESG reporting” this will here forth just be referred to as “ESG reporting”. 
This study distinguishes between ESG reporting and sustainability reporting. Sustainability reporting is equal 
to ESG reporting in the case when all aspects hereof (Environmental, Social and Governance) are equally and 
holistically considered, based on the standard set by the SDGs, as these are deemed the contemporary level 
of sustainability.  
ESG reporting has first and foremost been viewed as a communication tool. A way for companies to elaborate 
on the information they desire to communicate to external stakeholders and not a tool for evaluating the 
actual sustainability of business performance. A serious communication gap between companies and 
investors exist, due to format and inconsistency of information companies put forth in their reporting. It is 
not because companies do not take ESG reporting seriously or lack of commitment to produce good and 
sound reports, rather it is a result from missing ability to produce standardised, rigorous data to support 
investment decisions on long-term value creation strategies for growth opportunities and the related risks. 
(PwC, (2019) (b)) 
The past 25 years have brought more than a ten-fold increase in corporate ESG reporting requirements. 
(WBCSD, 2019 (b)) The Reporting Matters initiative stated in their annual report from 2017, that it is observed 
that a continues growing numbers of investors and stakeholders are nudging companies to report on 
sustainability issues and demand greater accountability and transparency from corporations. NGOs, 
stockbrokers and others look to companies for transparency on their sustainability performance and as a 
result the resent years show an increasing robustness in ESG reporting, that offers the structure through 
which companies can continue to ensure long term sustainability. (WBCSD and Radley Yeldar, 2017) 
However, though the trend shows more robust corporate sustainability reporting, a lack of coordination of 
frameworks still makes corporate reporting not only fragmented, but overwhelming and too time consuming 
for companies to keep up with all the requirements. As the complexity of reporting continues to grow, it 
increasingly makes it harder for companies to make sense of and keep up with the latest trends and good 
practice. It results in variable quality and relevance of disclosures, which in turn, also prevents investors and 
stakeholders from getting the information needed. (WBCSD, 2019 (b)) 
Sustainability reporting is here to stay, and disclosure requirements are expected to increase to cover new 
subjects within ESG reporting. The one sustainability report framework that captures everything desired by

2.3.3.1 
Integrated reporting – the <IR> Framework 
IR requires and creates integrated thinking within an organisation and aims at improving information quality 
available to providers of financial capital. An integrated report is a concise communication of how an 
organization’s strategy, governance, performance, and prospects, in the context of its external environment, 
lead to the creation of value in the short, medium and long term. It considers all aspects of ESG reporting 
and, thus, the framework enables a business to bring these mentioned elements together through the 
concept of 'connectivity of information', to best communicate this value creation. Usage of IR can lead to 
behavioural changes and improvement in performance throughout an organisation. Integrated reporting is 
thought to be an evolution of corporate reporting, with a focus on conciseness, strategic relevance and future 
orientation. (WBCSD, 2019 (b); International Federation of Accountants, 2019)) 
In IR value creation refers to instances when the overall stock of capital is unchanged, increased or decrease. 
Capital stock is defined as all the shares that a company has and is officially allowed to sell, or, the total value 
of the assets which are used to produce goods and services, within a particular economy, or owned by a 
particular company. All organisations depend on various forms of capital for success. Capital in IR covers six 
categories: financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social and relationship, and natural, however, 
organisations using IR are not required to adopt this categorisation into their reporting. Thus, to elaborate, 
==PAGE:29==

the capitals (the six categories) are stocks of value which are increased, decreased or transformed through 
the activities and outputs of the organisation. For further elaboration on the six capitals see Appendix VII. 
(IIRC, 2013; Cambridge Dictionary, 2019)

2.3.4 Reporting on the SDGs 
The SDGs are meant to enable companies to report information on sustainable development performance 
using common indicators and a shared set of priorities. Companies are encouraged to build the SDGs into 
their communication and reporting and to advance sustainable development through the investments they 
make, the solutions they develop and the business practice, by reducing their negative impacts while 
enhancing the positive contribution to the 2030 Agenda. By doing so the SDGs are expected to allow leading 
companies to demonstrate how their business helps to minimising negative impacts and maximising positive 
impacts. (GRI et. al., 2016)  
Reporting Exchange was not used for discovering frameworks or standards, addressing the issues of reporting 
on the SDGs. Rather, the tripodal framework Business Reporting on SDGs (BR-SDG), mentioned in Section II: 
Incorporation of SDGs into Business in general, was assessed as this combined framework sets the standard 
deemed the most contemporary for the purpose.

on following five aspect: Input, activity, output, outcome and impact. Finally, it presents the 4C’s of effective 
reporting (Concise, Consistent, Current and Comparable) and a checklist for what to disclose on in corporate 
sustainability reporting, see Figure 8. (GRI and UNGC, 2018)

2.3.5 Sub Summary 
 
 
ESG reporting is primarily a means for communicating on the information desired by shareholders and 
stakeholders and most commonly it is not used as a mean for continuous iterative business performance 
assessment. Increasing desire to invest in sustainable business results in a demand for more standardised 
data and a format making the specific and desired information easily accessible. The vast amount of 
available ESG reporting requirements, frameworks, standards and initiatives and the lack of coordination 
makes the reporting quality fragmented and the task overwhelming for companies. However, companies 
are in many cases required to conduct annual ESG reports, however the requirements of disclosure vary 
greatly. 
In EU are companies with more than 500 employees required to conduct an annual report in which they 
disclose on the way they operate and manage environmental protection, CSR, respect for human rights, 
anti-corruption and bribery, and diversity on company boards. There is no set standard for the form and 
way of reporting on this. Further, companies which apply activities listed in Appendix V must report on 
their inventory of GHG emissions, cutting down GHG emissions, climate change adaption, low-carbon 
strategy, commitments under the 2009 Copenhagen Accord and 2010 Cancún Agreement, and national 
governments’ use of revenues from the auctioning allowances in the EU emission trading system. 
The ESG reporting frameworks provided by Reporting Exchange and relating to the ICT sector was 
researched and as a result, it was found that four standards were deemed within the scope of this study: 
IR, GRI, ,UNGC and GMNE. These four standards could be grouped into two categories; the ones who 
relate sustainability reporting to corporate financials commonly known as integrated reporting, 
containing only the IR framework, and those addressing ESG reporting separate from corporate 
financials; GMNE, UNGC, and GRI.  
The tripodal framework Reporting on the SDGs Framework consisting of SDG Compass Guide, Integrating 
the SDGs into Corporate Reporting: A practical Guide, and An Analysis of the Goals and Targets comprise 
the most comprehensive reporting framework designed specifically for corporate reporting on the SDG. 
It is designed to avoid “Cherry picking” and “SDG Washing”. It is not a new framework but is based on 
the standards and principles of GRI and UNGC.

3.2 Key findings from the literature study 
This section presents the key findings of the literature study, especially relevant for the analysis of the bridges 
between the areas of the ICT sustainability model (Figure 9). As this section refers to information provided in 
section II, only additional information is provided with references.  
3.2.1 Issues regarding the SDGs 
Incorporating sustainability practices into business means to take responsibility for the products and services 
produced and delivered, not just by considering own activities but also those of the supply chain. However, 
a sector roadmap for the ICT sector does not exist.  
The 2030 Agenda and Addis Ababa Action Agenda clearly states that impact investing and sustainable 
corporate practice (implementation of the SDGs into business), including ESG reporting, is crucial to achieve 
the global political agenda set out for 2030. High-quality information on business performance is key to a 
successful sustainable transition, but the majority of this material is not directly on the subject of how to 
incorporate the SDGs into business, let alone reporting on the practice. This results in a gap between the 
political set 2030 Agenda and the translation and incorporation techniques into business goals and targets. 
This gap is deemed the main reason why companies struggle with implementing the SDGs into business 
strategy and conduct. Closing this gap is key for corporate survival in the world of the new global agenda. 
3.2.2 Issues regarding the ICT sector 
The ICT sector is the foundation on which we design and build our global society. (Smart Society Academy, 
2019; Cities Innovation Center, 2019) Low products and service cost enables fast and extensive application 
of the technology and ICT is universal recognised to be the enabling factor for achieving the 2030 Agenda. 
Simple economic and value chain logic lets us understand that low product cost only can be achieved through 
low manufacturing cost which can be achieved through highly effective and low energy consuming 
manufacturing and/or through low manufacturing expenses (cost of labor, energy, material and 
transportation). This model puts pressure on the manufactures of computers and consumer electronics, 
which in continuation hereof puts pressure on the upper supply chain. This being one of the reasons for the 
call for a new circular model for electronic products. (ITU et. al., 2019) 
At the same time, the extensive negative impacts following in the wake of the production of products are 
found to make up the hot-spots of negative impacts created by the industry. These hot-spots are found in 
the areas of mining, manufacturing, transportation, retail, resources consumption and end-of-life treatment. 
There was found no further elaboration of the nature of the impacts, but from general knowledge on the 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
subject and research has led to the understanding that, the nature of the hot-spots is of environmental and 
social character. 
Special emphasis is put on the production and management of E-waste in general, not just the ones covered 
by the scope of this study, however, especially E-waste relating to this scope contains the majority of rare 
metals and scarce resources, see Appendix IX. It is not apparent if this is due to the fact, that E-waste is the 
absolute hotspot of the sector, exceeding the issues of negative impacts resulting from mining and extraction, 
or if it is due to a future scarcity scenario and immense potential of generating financial capital from the 
poorly managed resource, see Appendix IX. As it is clear, that better management and utilisation of E-waste 
will enable cheaper and more profitable ways of extracting precious metals than conventional mining, E-
waste is viewed as the area from which to drive sustainable transition in the industry. Even so, the other hot-
spots are significant as well, and attention must also be directed to these as necessary areas to seriously 
address and transform by the industry to achieve sustainable business conduct, hence Figure 1 presented in 
Section II on page 14.  
Current regulations for the sector are recognised to be insufficient for ensuring security regarding  E-waste. 
Especially the possible negative impacts occurring from great national variations in ICT product regulations 
and standards are of great concern. To address regulations not related to reporting requirements is outside 
the scope of this study, however, the above stated is likely to be the factor resulting in the lack of global or 
regional disclosure requirements for the ICT sector, which in turn is likely to be part of the sector hotspot, 
particularly illegal deportation of E-waste.  
One framework is provided by ITU for ICT companies to address the issues related to business conduct. 
However, this only covers the environmental perspective and a holistic tool for overall sustainability 
assessment and guidance for the sector does not exist. To generate SBC, this single standing environmental 
guide must be supported by universal guiding principles on human rights, labour standards etc. (excluded 
from this study, reference to Section II: voluntary disclosures and standards of sustainability reporting for the 
ICT sector). 
Based on the negative impacts on the global environment and society, world leading organisations, including 
ITU (representative for the ICT sector), call for a sector reboot and a new circular model for electronic 
products. It is therefore concluded, that the key issues of the sector result from the production and end-of-
life treatment of electronics hardware and that this is likely to be a result of great national variations in ICT 
product regulations and standards. The latter can in extension be interpreted as lack of global regulations 
regarding multinational ICT enterprises. 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
The main issues of the sector are found to be related to electronic hardware. For this reason, the study here 
forth focuses on issues related to manufacturing of computers and consumer electronics. 
3.2.3 Issues regarding reporting 
Reviewing reporting requirements unveils that companies to a greater extent are obliged to disclose on 
financial aspects of business than social and environmental. This, in itself, is not necessarily a problem, unless 
linked to the definition of weak sustainability. At the same time, not addressing the economical aspect of 
environmental and social impacts, as is the case of most sustainability reporting frameworks separating 
business finance from business impact assessment, does not support the concept of strong sustainability, see 
Section II, Sustainable Development Goals and Business, p.10. 
IR is the framework which encompasses value creation from all capitals, see Appendix VII, however the 
defining boundary for assessing and including value (both positive and negative) is defined by what has a 
significant effect on the financial value creating to the company. This boundary to the approach might very 
well leave out significant outcomes and impacts of business.  
The format of sustainability reporting is to address the non-financial outcomes and impacts of business 
focusing on input, output and impact, but not on the outcome of impact, e.g. linking environmental impacts 
upstream in the supply chain to societal outcomes or lowering product manufacturing costs (GRI, 2019 (b)); 
UNGC, 2019 (b); OECD; 2018 (b)) 
To sum up the focus areas of the reporting frameworks addressed in Section II (not including BR-SDG):  
• 
IR focus on outcome of business from a perspective of financial value creation.  
• 
GRI focus on comprehensive and detailed communication of impact created by business.  
• 
UNGC focus on addressing what is the minimum requirement in terms of SBC.  
• 
GMNE focus on addressing and avoiding negative impacts throughout the supply chain.  
Together these principles embrace the concept of strong sustainability, but separately they do not.   
Two issues were found regarding the BR-SDG. From a company perspective is it a competing approach to the 
existing well-established frameworks. Though it makes clear it is not a new framework, it still is a new 
approach to present and communicate business conducts with associated targets, which are not developed 
to match business requirements. (UN, 2015) Secondly, this result in “SDG-washing” or “cherry picking”, thus 
undermining the entire purpose of the SDGs; to constitute a common framework for companies, in a 
comparable and transparent manner, to report on measures taken to reduce negative impacts while 
enhancing the positive ones. (IISD, 2018)

3.3 Correlation analysis between ICT, SDGs and Reporting  
This section cross-match the information presented in key findings from the literature study to understand 
the level of establishment and function of the bridges presented by the ICT sector sustainability model. This 
is done by addressing adjacent areas in the ICT Sustainability model with the linking factors (Figure 9).  
3.3.1 B1) SDGs-ICT 
B1 represents the link between the ICT sector and the SDGs. There is no sector framework linking business 
activities and corporate practice specifically to the ICT sector. This is deemed to make it increasingly more 
complex for manufacturing companies of computers and consumer electronics to address supply chain 
impacts and incorporate these considerations into business.  
As the SDGs are voluntary for private corporate practice there are yet no requirements on using the SDGs in 
business or corporate ESG reporting.  
In terms of achieving the SDGs ICT is globally recognised as the enabler to do so, represented by the ITU 
initiative #ICT4SDG. Stating that ICT can elevate performance on any SDG within any sector and that ICT can 
fast forward the progress of implementing and adapting to the SDGs, being the factor making the 2030 
Agenda possible. (ITU, 2018) However, in terms of addressing the issues of its own sector only one aspect is 
mentioned by this initiative; E-waste. It is mentioned in relation to SDG 12, but no targets are mentioned, or 
any other correlation made. Reference by ITU is given to The Global E-waste Monitor 2017. (ITU, 2019) 
There is no specific recommendation other than the general presented by the BR-SDG and UN. The only 
guiding material related to SBC provided specific for the ICT sector is the Toolkit on environmental 
sustainability for the ICT sector. (ITU, 2012) From this, the guiding material on Sustainable product, End-of-
life treatment, General specifications and key performance indicators, and Assessment framework for 
environmental impacts are found to be of relevance and helpful in terms of addressing environmental 
precautions and impacts in the sector, see Appendix IIX.  
The gap between necessary means to achieve the 2030 Agenda and actual available tools for translating and 
incorporating the SDGs and targets into corporate practice is just as much an issue for the ICT sector as in 
general terms.  
This bridge is deemed weak based on the facts that: 
• 
a holistic framework on SBC directed specifically for the ICT sector (a sector framework) is missing. 
• 
The sector focus on the SDG is screwed as it concentrates on what ICT can do for the SDGs 
(#ICT4SDG), undermining what the SDGs can do for the ICT sector. 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
3.3.2 B2) Reporting-SDG  
There is no existing single standing framework for reporting on the SDGs. Most of the material on SDGs for 
business is not directly on the subject of how to incorporate the SDGs into business, let alone reporting on 
the practice. To solve this issue, together GRI and UNGC have generated guidelines on how to integrate the 
SDGs into reporting (BR-SDG), see Appendix II. This guide is however in itself a comprehensive tool for 
companies to familiarise itself with on top of the exciting frameworks (GRI being very comprehensive, UNGC 
less so). It does provide the company with good understanding of the SDGs and the consequential nature of 
the goals and implementation into corporate practice. 
GRI, UNGC and IR relates their framework to the SDGs, however, companies cannot truly be said to be 
reporting on the SDGs if they do not add and incorporate the related targets themselves. Otherwise, they 
just address the same areas of the SDGs, but not the specific hotspots represented within the SDGs and the 
political 2030 Agenda. Applying the SDGs to reporting frameworks of GRI, UNGC or IR are thus thought likely 
to results in “cherry picking” or “SDG-washing”. 
GMNE does not relate its framework to the SDGs, however it is the only recognised framework specifically 
addressed to multinational enterprises applying a consequential life cycle management (LCM) approach to 
enterprise supply chain assessment and impacts addressing the challenges of having facilities undertaking 
different tasks of business, with different impacts in different regions having different requirements to 
emissions, facilities, environmental protection, employment and salary etc. It applies an ethical aspect to SBC 
requirements which is found of relevance to the business and its impact assessment and reporting.  
BR-SDG was the only “framework” found to be directly on the subject of reporting on the SDGs. However, it 
was also found to support the paradox of effective and efficient reporting and the relating issue of “cherry 
picking” and “SDG-washing”.  
This bridge is deemed to be of relative strength based on the facts that: 
• 
Comprehensive guiding material exist on incorporating the SDGs into well-known reporting 
frameworks. 
• 
Though the above is true, it is deemed a weakness to the bridge, that no single standing framework 
on SDG reporting exist as this, based on the analysis, seems to be what is lacking in terms of avoiding 
“SDG-washing”. 
==PAGE:45==

3.3.3 B3) ICT-Reporting 
This bridge is represented by two aspects; mandatory requirements and voluntary requirements. Besides 
annual reporting on financial aspects, ICT companies are required to disclose the Non-financial Reporting 
Directive 2014/95/EU and Commission Regulation No 601/2012. These only apply to companies located or 
with activities within EU countries.  
All the world’s top manufactures of computers and consumer electronics, listed in Table 1, have offices in 
EU. It is therefore not evident if reporting is a mandatory choice or based on legal requirements. Since 
research show that many leading companies have recognised the benefits of communication on CSR and 
sustainability it is deemed fair to assume, that their reporting is not only due to the requirements of EU. 
(UNGC (a), 2019; Bernal-Conesa J. A., 2017) This assumption has further been validated by the review of 
these reports which show that several of them are of a quality deemed more likely for impressing 
shareholders and other stakeholders, than actually providing true and transparent information of SBC 
(reference to the missing Key Performance Indicators (KPI) in many of the reports).  
Based on the reviewed reports, there seems to be a trend in the ICT sector of focusing communication on 
positive impacts of products and services and retain from communicating on the negative impacts and 
outcomes of its businesses. Only very few are found to report on the defined sector hot-spots.  
This bridge is deemed to be weak based on the facts that: 
• 
The current reporting requirements issued by EU are deemed insufficient in terms of getting ICT 
corporations to adapt to SBC.  
• 
Though it is known that good and sound reporting matters in terms of driving sustainable transition, 
communicating transparently with shareholders and other stakeholder, no requirements are set on 
reporting specifically on the targets relating to the hot-spot of the sector.

3.4 Sub conclusion 
  
This section presents the key issues derived from analysing the bridges of the Sustainability model 
developed in this study for the ICT sector and the review of resent ESG reports from the top manufacturing 
companies of computers and consumer electronics.  
Reviewing the analysis of the bridges it can be concluded that the bridge between SDG and Reporting 
(B2), represented by the recommendations on corporate sustainability reporting relevant for the ICT 
sector, (represented by reporting on the SDGs), is the most established bridge of the Sustainability model 
for the ICT sector (Figure 9). However, it is not deemed strong, as the frameworks available to business 
to support this bridge are found not to be sufficient in terms of avoiding “SDG washing”. 
B1, represented by sector initiatives to incorporate sustainability practices (the SDG) into business culture, 
do not have an established framework linking the SDGs to business activities and corporate practice 
specifically to the ICT sector. This results in a general focus of the sector on how ICT can enable the SDGs 
(#ICT4SDG), undermining how the SDGs can elevate sustainable transition in the ICT sector. The latter will 
here forth be referred to as “SDG-4-ICT”.  
B3, represented by reporting requirements covering the ICT sector, is not sufficiently regulated to enforce 
transition towards SBC. Though voluntarily following disclosure standards these do not ensure 
transparent and comparable data, demanded by stakeholders.  
The results from reviewing the corporate reports clearly states awareness of the SDGs but imply lack of 
comprehension of how to apply them and draw benefit from them, other than symbolic display, and that 
there is an inclination towards SDG-washing in the ICT sector. The investigation also shows awareness of 
the importance of KPIs for generating high quality information of business performance and looking 
beyond own practice and address the issues of the supply chain. 
In addressing the problems of sustainable transition of the ICT sector it will be important to recognise the 
fact that companies do not desire yet another new framework to work and that they are likely to find the 
SDG targets insufficient or out of match in terms of the actual activities of the companies or available data 
on the supply chain.

These conclusions reveal that half of the hypothesis of this study is true. That the difficulties of corporate 
sustainable transition in the ICT sector is rooted in inadequate linkage between the issues of the sector, 
the SDGs and related material, and applied reporting frameworks.  
To design a solution that can help elevate corporate sustainable transition in the ICT sector all issues 
unveiled in this study must be considered simultaneously. To determine the potential of SDGs to alleviate 
sustainable transition within the ICT sector, a solution to the key issues brought to light will be sought 
through the SDGs and related frameworks.

4.2 SDG Sector Roadmap 
The WBCSD has developed the only existing guidelines on how to design and generate an SDG sector 
roadmap. These guidelines were released April 2018. The intention is to create detailed “roadmaps” through 
pioneering collaboration of sector peers, coming together to plot a new course focused on a common SDG 
vision and guide their industry’s shift to sustainable development in line with the SDGs. (WBCSD, 2018 (a)) 
A sector roadmap is a strategic plan that translate a vision into actionable goals and activities. It defines a 
common approach on how to maximize the potential of the industry to contribute to achieving the 2030 
Agenda through business solutions. (WBCSD, 2018 (a))  
The purpose of the roadmap is to provide practical guidance and inspiration to the sector itself. It also 
composes a useful reference point for politicians, investors and civil society as it provides easily accessible 
and detailed information about how companies of a specific sector should contribute to SBC and the maturity 
of its efforts. The Key components and enablers of an SDG sector roadmap are presented in Figure 11.  
 
Figure 11:  
Key components and enablers of an SDG sector roadmap (WBCSD, 2018 (a)) 
4.2.1 Information contained in SDG sector roadmap 
The SDG sector roadmap should in a transparent and easily understandable manner communicate on the 
opportunities and challenges of the sector. It should state facts about the industry and its interactions in 
relation to all the SDGs. It should contain illustration of the current position of the sector by outlining the 
value chain in a model, see Figure 12 and Figure 13, It should map the level of impacts using a sectoral SDG 
materiality matrix. This makes it also increasingly easier to compare goals of different sectors, see Figure 14 
==PAGE:55==

and Figure 15. It should state the key goals for the sector and common sector targets, impact pathways and 
opportunities, and outline possible trade-offs between actions towards the goals and targets. (WBCSD, 2018 
(a); WBCSD, 2018 (b)) 
As the purpose of the roadmap is to provide practical guidance and inspiration to the sector itself, it is not 
intended to act as communication platform for stakeholders of sector companies. The result which comes 
from the creation of an SDG sector roadmap and the following individual transformation in business conduct 
should be communicated through corporate reporting.

4.2.2 Steps of creating an SDG sector roadmap  
Developing an SDG sector roadmap is a three-step process, see Figure 16.   
First, the current level of interaction with the SDGs throughout the value chain must be established. The 
sector must map out the SDG impacts (positive and negative) on the value chain and state prioritized SDGs 
for the sector. A suggested method for mapping out the SDGs and targets are provided in the template shown 
in Figure 17. Second, the most significant opportunities for impact on the SDGs (positive and negative) needs 
to be identified and the allocation of these throughout the sector assessed. Third, identify barriers, potential 
solutions, impact accelerators and key actions on short-, medium- and long-term goals and opportunities, 
and set up monitoring-, data aggregation and reporting systems to track and communicate on progress.

4.2.4 The SDG Sector Roadmap as enabler for the ICT sector 
The issues unveiled in this study was hold against the SDG sector roadmap model and the expected benefits 
from drawing up an SDG sector roadmap. This was done to validate the SDG sector roadmap as solution to 
the issues of the ICT sector and to determine the potential of the SDGs to alleviate the sustainable transition 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
within the sector. Only expected benefits can be taken in to consideration, as the only sector roadmap 
existing has not existed long enough to make a show for how it improves corporate performance after the 
launch of the roadmap compared to prior to its existence.  
Issues potentially solved by the SDG sector roadmap: 
 
• 
Unified sector goals, targets and action plans 
 
Creating a unified sector vision, accompanied by specific goals and targets, helps companies with the 
first and most important actions towards implementation of the SDGs into business (Understanding 
the SDGs, defining priorities and setting goals). It creates a sector forum for addressing the SDGs 
from a business perspective and creates the foundation for companies to efficiently communicate 
on business performance towards responsible and sustainable practice.  
Mapping the SDGs through supply- and value chain considerations for a sector rather than a single 
company, will boost sector cohesiveness and competition on SBC. This will in turn enhance focus on 
sustainability performance and lead focus of investors and politicians to corporate actions towards; 
innovative solutions, reaching for opportunities and addressing the negative impacts of business. 
This is further expected to put pressure on further regulations towards eliminating illegal trade and 
treatment of E-waste and help drive a change in the economy of ICT products. If sustainable solutions 
and business models become the focus of investments, it might have the opportunity to compete 
with the current focus on low product price. This would help drive the change towards a circular 
economy for electronics.  
 
• 
Cohesive attention on supply chain management 
 
Underlining priority SDGs through SDG mapping against the value chain will stress the importance of 
effective and efficient supply chain management and its relation to corporate collaborations 
throughout the value chain. Detailed and transparent disclosure on sector hotspots and 
opportunities will motivate corporate impact investments and create common ground for 
establishing competitor- and supply chain collaborations to avoid negative impacts. This will level out 
the screwed focus of the ICT sector, currently being on #ICT4SDG, to also include SDG-4-ICT. It will 
result in innovative business models and solutions, as well as creating foundation for, in unity, to 
address negative impacts of business activities. 
  
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
• 
Transparent and comparable sustainability reporting  
 
Creating a sector specific list of SDG related targets helps companies to identify key impact areas of 
business and sound and effective KPIs for monitoring and reporting on performance while easing 
information sharing throughout the supply chain. This is expected lighten the burden of corporate 
reporting and improve transparency of corporate performance toward SBC and comparability of 
corporate reporting, and help reducing “SDG-washing”.  
 
Issues expected not to be solved by the SDG sector roadmap: 
 
• 
True interlinked capital value creation 
 
Though an SDG roadmap increases the probability of corporate Impact investing, it does not 
necessarily link capital value creation (positive and negative) of natural or social nature to financial 
value creation (bottom line figures) of a company. If ESG accounting and reporting is separate from 
financial accounting and reporting the links between the economical aspect and the environmental 
and societal aspects of the sustainability model (Figure 1) are not truly established and the strive for 
sustainability cannot be said to be established on the foundation of a strong sustainability 
perspective.   
 
• 
Specified guiding material on addressing negative hot-spots 
 
An SDG sector roadmap will underline the hot-spots and the opportunities specific for ICT sector, 
however, it does not go into depth with how to address each specific area of need. For the ICT sector 
the Toolkit on environmental sustainability for the ICT sector from 2012 would still be the most 
contemporary framework for the purpose. It is only deemed valid for addressing environmental 
aspects and even so, it is not updated to match the desire for a new circular vision for the sector, 
which makes it outdated. An SDG sector roadmap would thus, make up the foundation for the 
transition, but the sector lack of guiding material and frameworks to help them address the vision 
and goals put forth by such roadmap.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
• 
Easy comparison of sustainability performance of multiple companies 
 
Though multiples sector defined targets would streamline reporting format, comparing corporate 
sustainability performance would still require reading through every report, locating targets, 
matching up performances etc. for each individual firm of possible interest. This because, companies 
are free to choose format of reporting, as it should be. However, demand from investors on easily 
accessible and highly transportable and comparable data is not in line with the current reporting 
format. This could possibly be solved by adding a comparison framework, a “sustainability 
performance evaluation tool (SPET)”, to the SDG roadmap. Scoring corporate performance and 
development against the sector specified targets and KPIs would allow investors to easily compare 
multiples corporations in an efficient manner. Careful considerations towards such SPET would be 
necessary, to avoid both “cherry picking” and “SDG-washing”.

5 Conclusion 
Through the five research questions (RQ) set up for this study, the hypothesis is, based on the results, deemed 
to be true. The difficulties of corporate sustainable transition in the ICT sector are rooted in inadequate 
linkage between sector issues, relevant SDG material and reporting frameworks, but, the SDGs have the 
potential to alleviate this transition. 
Below is given an account of the RQs and the related conclusions: 
RQ1) 
How does ICT relate to the SDGs? 
RQ2) 
What is the status within the ICT sector in terms of communicating on sustainability, the SDGs and 
responsible business conduct, respectively? 
RQ3) 
What are the key issues within the ICT sector related to the SDGs and to communication on 
sustainability? 
RQ4) 
What can be done to alleviate the issue of transition to sustainable business conduct within the ICT 
sector? 
RQ5) 
How can ICT companies use the SDGs to transform their business conduct into sustainable business 
conduct? 
RQ1, RQ2 and RQ3 were first addressed through the ICT sector sustainability model designed for that sole 
purpose. It was found, that, the difficulties of sustainable transition in the ICT sector are related to three 
general aspects:  
1. A general struggle in all sectors of implementing sustainability measures (the SDGs) into corporate 
practice.  
2. ICT sector issues 
3. Corporate sustainability reporting made up the foundation for the ICT sector sustainability model.  
Analysing the strengths of the correlations (bridges) between these three aspects respectively (1 and 2, 2 
and 3, 3 and 4), reviled that none of them can be deemed strong, in the sense that, none of them fulfil 
corporate needs in a way that enable conditions for achieving sustainable corporate practice in the sector. 
Moreover, RQ1, RQ2 and RQ3 were addressed through analysis of the 16 most recently released ESG-
/sustainability reporting of manufactures of computers and consumer electronics. These specific companies 
were sampled because the impact hotspots of the sector were closely correlated with electronic hardware, 
thus manufactures of these and their supply chain.  
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
The analysis reviled following:  
• 
That companies have many strong and well-developed frameworks available to follow on corporate 
sustainability reporting, however, these frameworks have not yet integrated the SDGs in a manner 
that eliminates “cherry-picking” or “SDG-washing”.  
• 
That there are no sector initiatives or specified framework to incorporate sustainability practices (the 
SDGs) into business culture and corporate practices of the ICT sector. Instead, it is found that there 
is a general focus of the sector on how ICT can enable the SDGs (#ICT4SDG), undermining how the 
SDGs can elevate sustainable transition in the ICT sector (SDG-4-ICT). 
• 
That requirements for corporate ESG-reporting are not regulated in a matter that ensures the level 
of transparent and comparable data demanded by stakeholders or to enforce transition towards SBC 
in the ICT sector and 
• 
Awareness of the SDGs among companies, but, implies lack of comprehension of how to apply them 
and draw benefit from them, other than symbolic display.  
• 
Indications that companies are aware of the importance of KPIs for generating high quality 
information about business performance 
• 
Indications that companies are aware of the importance of looking beyond own practice and address 
the issues of the supply chain. 
• 
That there is an inclination towards SDG-washing in the ICT sector.  
RQ4 and RQ5 were addressed through reflections on the results of RQ1, RQ2 and RQ3. This was carried out 
through the flipped ICT sector sustainability model designed solely for this purpose. It gave clear indication 
that the SDGs have potential to alleviate sustainable transition within the ICT sector through an SDG sector 
roadmap. It is anticipated that such roadmap can provide ICT companies with a unified action agenda 
outlining a set of common hot-spots, targets, monitoring systems and a common language to communicate 
on advances towards both positive and negative impacts. This is thought to ease data generation, sharing 
and documentation throughout the supply chain and laying down the foundation for unified action towards 
a sustainable sector. An SDG sector roadmap is, thus, thought to help close the gap between the political 
2030 Agenda and the current level of SBC within the ICT sector. 
The results also show that, on an individual level the SDGs can only be used to transform business into 
sustainable conduct if companies are being both efficient and effective in addressing and implementing them 
into corporate strategy, practice and reporting.

The evaluation of the SDG sector roadmap as solution concludes that such roadmap will have the potential 
to:  
✓ Help ICT companies to implement the SDGs into business. 
✓ Boost sector cohesiveness and competition on SBC. 
✓ Enhance focus on sustainability performance and lead the focus of investors and politicians to

✓ Put pressure on further regulations towards eliminating illegal trade and treatment of E-waste and

✓ Motivate corporate impact investments and create common ground for establishing competitor- and

✓ Level out the screwed focus of the ICT sector, from #ICT4SDG, to also include SDG-4-ICT.

6 Reflections and Perspectivation 
This section provides reflections on the most significant results and conclusions. Especially, thoughts are 
given on the matter of the three aspects not expected to be alleviated by an SDG sector roadmap and if SBC 
in the ICT sector can be achieved without conducting this roadmap. 
6.1 Wy an ICT sector SDG roadmap do not exist already 
With all the expected benefits for business to draw from an SDG sector roadmap, one could wonder why a 
roadmap for the ICT sector do not already exists. It is easy to just draw a connection between this and the 
issue of #SDG4ICT - a predominantly outward focus on how the sector can be the enabler for achieving SBC 
in other sectors and neglecting own sector issues. However, this question is not just relevant for the ICT 
sector, as only the SDG chemical sector roadmap has been conducted since the release of the guide in April 
2018. Moreover, this roadmap was conducted as case sample for the roadmap-guide, thus, no additional 
sector has yet undertaken the task of developing a roadmap. It could be that awareness of the roadmap has 
yet to be established, that sector roadmaps are under development or that studies like this, establishing how 
sectors can benefit from such roadmap, are still not conducted.  
6.2 Holistic assessment of capital value creation 
The review of different reporting frameworks provided understanding of the different approaches companies 
usually takes on assessing business performance. The foundation of the IR approach is considered to best 
match the holistic principle of sustainability, addressing all capitals (financial, environmental and societal) in 
coherence with each other. However, it was found that the principle of the framework for setting the 
boundaries for what aspects of business to asses do not match the principles of strong sustainability and the 
fundamentals of the 2030 Agenda; taking responsibility for people, planet and prosperity, not letting 
corporate prosperity being the overshadowing factor.  
This leaves companies with two options, if choosing to follow global recognised reporting frameworks. 
Separate financial aspects of business from environmental and social (non-integrated reporting frameworks), 
or an approach which fundamentally builds on true principles of sustainability, but in reality, through its 
scoping principles, base its impact inclusion and assessment on corporate financial value creation (IR 
framework). This enhances the risk of leaving out impact areas from business activities important to 
environment and society, thus, for investors, politicians and other stakeholders. Ultimately the latter is also 
critical for the companies, as the 2030 agenda is considered to set the contemporary and future level of SBC 
and that nations have committed to regulate business according to the SDG. This means that companies need 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
to be aware of all aspect and impacts of business activities and practice, so that it can uphold effective 
decision making and efficient business conduct to obtain and uphold competitive advantage among 
competitors.  
This leads to reflections on whether or not existing reporting frameworks are sufficient in terms of guiding 
companies to reach for the 2030 Agenda, or if a new comprehensive and holistic framework.  
6.3 ICT Sector specific assessment frameworks 
An SDG sector roadmap provides goals, visions and overall guidance on the desired approach of ICT 
companies towards the SDGs, however it does not go into depth with assessment methods directly on the 
subject on the prioritised SDGs. The ICT sector has already acknowledged the need for sector specific 
assessment tools represented by the Toolkit on environmental sustainability for the ICT sector from 2012. 
This framework is though considered to be outdated and insufficient in terms of reflecting the contemporary 
and future level of sustainability as it includes environmental considerations and do not take the global 
agenda and its targets into consideration.  
This gives room for reflections on whether or not an ICT sector SDG roadmap alone can alleviate the 
sustainable transition of the sector, or if additional assessment tools should be developed to support 
companies in their effort to asses performance and address impacts related to the prioritised Subjects and 
SDGs (just like the Toolkit on environmental sustainability for the ICT sector). 
6.4 Sustainability Performance Evaluation Tool 
It is clear that the SDG sector roadmap provides shareholders and stakeholders with sound and good 
information about the sector, issues and opportunities and a bar against which they can evaluate business 
performance of companies of interest. However, it does not provide what investors really desire, an easy and 
quick way of compare corporate sustainability performance for the purpose of guiding them on in which 
companies to invest. The issues of “cherry-picking” and “SDG washing” is particularly relevant to this as well.  
Though multiples sector defined targets would streamline reporting format, comparing corporate 
sustainability performance would still require reading through every report, locating targets, matching up 
performances etc. for each individual firm of possible interest. This because, companies are free to choose 
format of reporting, as it should be. However, demand from investors on easily accessible and highly 
transportable and comparable data is not in line with the current reporting format. This could possibly be 
solved by adding a comparison framework, a “sustainability performance evaluation tool (SPET)”, to the SDG 
roadmap.  
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
Such tool could perhaps be designed from the output of roadmap, focusing on the priority SDG and 
associated targets stated in the roadmap. Scoring corporate performance and development against the 
sector specified targets and KPIs would allow investors to easily compare multiples corporations in an 
efficient manner if converted into a graph from which companies could easily be compared. If incorporated 
into the ICT sector SDG roadmap as expected for companies to disclose on, it might have the potential to 
ensure a high data quality, heighten reporting transparency, eliminating “cherry-picking” and “SDG washing”, 
while alleviating decision-making amongst investors.

Appendix I  
 
Short description of various topics 
The Addis Ababa Action Agenda 
The Addis Ababa Action Agenda is the outcome document of the third International Conference on Financing 
for Development and is an integral part of the 2030 Agenda of sustainable development. It states that Global 
Partnerships are key to realise sustainable development as the success of the UN 2030 agenda relies heavily 
on action and collaboration among governments, civil society and business. 
It urges companies to generate a core business model that takes account of their environmental, social and 
governmental impacts of their activities. The Addis Ababa Action Agenda further encourage impact investing, 
combining a return of investments with non-financial impacts, and sustainable corporate practice, including 
integration of environmental, social and governance factors into company reporting as each country decide 
on the balance between voluntary and mandatory rules. (§ 37) 
(UN, 2015)  
Dow Jones Sustainability Index 
The DJSI Is indices specifically for use by the asset management industry and more broadly in driving the ESG 
movement worldwide and is thus not a framework intended for corporate ESG reporting. 
(Robecosam, 2019) 
“Cherry picking” and “SDG-washing” 
Cherry-picking’ refers to the selection of goals and targets based on what is easiest for companies rather than 
what accounts for the highest priorities.  
SDG-washing is the act of purposely reporting mainly on positive contributions to the Global Goals and 
ignoring important negative impacts.  
(GRI and UNGC, 2018)

Appendix VI 
 
EU Directives on ESG reporting 
Following is a short description of the legislative reporting requirements for companies operating within EU. 
These are not requirements which companies only has to report on when relevant as are thus not addressed 
in this study alongside the annual ESG requirements.  
 
Directive 2012/18/EU require information on the control of major-accident hazards involving dangerous 
substances, is also referred to as Seveso III. It aims at controlling major chemical accident hazards and 
requires operators of establishments present with significant quantities of dangerous substances to conduct 
a safety report for the competent authorities. The safety report must disclose on details of the 
establishment, the dangerous substances present, the installation or storage facilities, possible major-
accident scenarios and risk analysis, prevention and intervention measures and the management 
systems available. A list of these dangerous substances can be found in Appendix I of Directive 
96/82/EC, thus, is not provided in this report. 
Directive 2010/75/EU on industrial emissions (IED) is put forth to reduce harmful industrial emissions across 
the EU. It requires a company using any stationary technical installation, and other directly associated 
activities hereof, listed in Appendix I or in part VII of the directive to report on the installations before a 
permit is updated.  
Directive 2011/92/EU on the assessment of the effects of certain public and private projects on the 
environment, has been amended by directive 2014/52/EU. It sets a minimum requirement to; (a) type of 
projects subject to assessment, (b) main obligations of developers, (c) content of assessment and (d) 
participation of competent authorities. The Strategic Environmental Assesment (SEA) requires, for any 
public plans or project, an environmental report in which the likely significant effects on the environment

Appendix VII 
 
<IR> Framework 
This appendix gives an account of the six capitals of IR, the Guiding Principles and the Eight Content Elements 
of an integrated report.  
The six capitals of IR: 
Financial capital 
 
 
 
The pool of funds that: 1) available to an organisation for use in the production of goods and 
services 
 
2) Obtained through financing, such as debt, equity or grants, or generated 
through operations or investments 
Manufactures capital 
Manufactures physical objects (as distinct from natural physical object) that are available to an 
organisation for use in the production of goods or the provision of services, including: 
 
 
 
 
1) Buildings 
 
 
 
 
2) Equipment 
3) Infrastructure (such as roads, ports, bridges, and waste and water 
treatments plants) 
Manufactured capital is often created by other organizations, but includes assets manufactured by 
the reporting organization for sale or when they are retained for its own use. 
Intellectual capital 
Organisational, knowledge-based intangibles, including:  
1) Intellectual property, such as patents, copyrights, software, rights and 
 
licences  
2) “organizational capital” such as tacit knowledge, systems, procedures and 
protocols 
==PAGE:87==
86 
 
Human capital 
People’s competencies, capabilities and experience, and their motivations to innovate, including their: 
1) Alignment with and support for an organization’s governance framework, 
risk management approach, and ethical values  
2) Ability to understand, develop and implement an organization’s strategy 
3) Loyalties and motivations for improving processes, goods and services, 
including their ability to lead, manage and collaborate 
Social and relationship capital 
The institutions and the relationships within and between communities, groups of stakeholders and other 
networks, and the ability to share information to enhance individual and collective well-being. Social and 
relationship capital includes:  
1) Shared norms, and common values and behaviours  
2) Key stakeholder relationships, and the trust and willingness to engage that 
an organization has developed and strives to build and protect with external 
stakeholders  
3) Intangibles associated with the brand and reputation that an organization 
has developed 
 
 
 
 
4) An organisations social licence to operate  
Natural capital 
All renewable and non-renewable environmental resources and processes that provide goods or services that 
support the past, current or future prosperity of an organization. It includes:  
1) Air, water, land, minerals and forests  
2) Biodiversity and eco-system health.
