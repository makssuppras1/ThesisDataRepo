ABSTRACT
Topology optimization is a method for generating structures tailored to speciﬁc load cases. This
work studies the application of topology optimization on shell structures. Two shell element for-
mulations are used; a degenerated quadrilateral element, and a hexahedral element. Two material
models are applied; the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP), and orthogonal rank-2
laminates. By including passive material layers in the shell formulation, it was possible to cast the
topology optimization problem as one of placing material reinforcement. It is found that both im-
plemented shell elements resulted in acceptable, although not ﬂawless, accuracy. When comparing
the resulting structures of the two element formulations, it is found that the hexahedral element
does not result in structures with zero material supports, which can occur when using the quadrilat-
eral formulation due to zero bending moment patches. Using the hexahedral shell formulation and
a multigrid preconditioner, a high-resolution topology optimization problem, placing the interior
reinforcement of an aircraft fuselage, was solved with 4.3 million shell elements.

1. INTRODUCTION
A shell is deﬁned as a structural element with a small thickness in one direction compared to the two
other so-called planar dimensions, where the thickness direction might change over space. Shells
have been called the prima donna of mechanical structures [1]. This nickname stems from shell
structures being very stiff in comparison to their weight, whilst being very sensitive to parameter
changes. Shells are already widely used in a wide range of high-performance mechanical systems
such as the outer skin of aircraft and rockets, ship hulls, and bicycle frames.
When modelling shells, various approaches exist. The continuum mechanics of shells are a gener-
alization of plate theory, and is usually based on either Kirchhoff-Love or Mindlin-Reissner theory.
Kirchhoff-Love theory assumes thin shells, while Mindlin-Reissner theory can be referred to as
the theory of thick plates. Both theories assume that the normal strain in the out-of-plane direc-
tion is negligible. Finite element formulations for shells are also varied and there exists no gold
standard element formulation. Very generally, some shell formulations are based on an application
of specialized shell continuum mechanics, while other formulations are based on regular three
dimensional continuum mechanics which have been specialized to reduce the modelling errors
associated with the thin domain.
A major challenge when modelling shell elements is the ill-conditioning of the resulting stiff-
ness matrix [2]. The ill-conditioning arises from the small thickness of the shell in one direction,
which leads to large numerical difference between out-of-plane bending modes and high energy
in-plane deformation modes. This problem worsens as the shell becomes thinner, since the dif-
ference between deformation modes increases. When solving the global stiffness matrix, the
ill-conditioning reduces both the numerical accuracy and increases the required iterations for con-
vergence in iterative solvers. The reduced numerical accuracy can be treated as negligible when
compared to the analysis errors from the ﬁnite element approximation, and if absolutely necessary
higher accuracy ﬂoating point numbers can be used. The increased solving time for iterative solv-
ers generally forces the application of direct factorization to solve the resulting linear system of
equations. This severely limits the size of the system which can be solved, as factorization routines
scale neither in time nor memory.
Structural optimization is the combination of numerical structural mechanics and mathematical
programming. These two ﬁelds are combined by deﬁning a mechanical structure through a set
of parameters which are used as design variables in constrained programming. The resulting
structures are said to be optimized with respect to the given boundary conditions and loads of
the mechanical system. Structural optimization is usually divided into size, shape, and topology
optimization. In size optimization, a ﬁxed set of system parameters, e.g. size and position of a
hole, are used as the design variables of the mathematical programming. In shape optimization,
the design variables are some general parametrization of the outer structural surface, e.g. splines.
While shape optimization has complete control of the outer surface it is limited to the originally
deﬁned topology of the structure, i.e. the optimizer is unable to create holes in the solid domain. In
topology optimization, the entire volume is discretized into voxels, where each voxel is considered
a design variable, thus allowing the optimization algorithm to freely change any part of the structure.
Usually, the used voxels for topology optimization are the elements of the ﬁnite element mesh.
Some similarities are found between optimized and shell structures, as both have a high sensitivity
to changes in system parameters such as local thickness and loading. This sensitivity is not desired
as variations of system parameters, such as thickness, can occur during manufacturing or operation.
Combining shells and topology optimization potentially carries a risk of over-specializing the mech-
anical system, resulting in yet higher sensitivities to parameter changes. However, the combination
of shells and optimization also makes it possible to obtain very stiff low-weight structures.

==PAGE:9==
When combining structural optimization and shells, several approaches are viable. For purely solid
mechanical systems an obvious approach is to apply shape optimization, as the shape of the shell
has a large impact on mechanical performance. An alternative approach could be to generate shell
structures directly by topology optimization as presented by Clausen et al. [3]. In multi-physics
applications the shape of the shell can be determined by some other factors, e.g. the desired lift
of a wing. In this case, topology optimization can be applied to generate the layout of stiffening
supports or ﬁber orientations for anisotropic materials.
Applying topology optimization to shell structures has long been an active research ﬁeld. Examples
include Ringertz [4] who studied the optimization of global shell thicknesses for geometrically
non-linear responses. Yang et al. [5] and Luo and Gea [6] optimized the orientation of stiffening
beads of shells but not their placement on the shell surfaces. Moita et al. [7] studied methods
for ﬁnding the optimal ﬁber orientation and relative thicknesses for shells consisting of multiple
laminate layers. Ansola et al. [8] presented a combined shape and topology optimization approach
for shells, which results in interesting structures where in-plane effects of shells are primarily used
to carry the loads. Stegmann and Lund [9] presented a gradient based optimization method for
discrete material choice, which was used in shells to choose from a predetermined set of laminates.
The purpose of this work was to study the application of high resolution topology optimization on
shell structures. In order to do so, a parallel framework for topology optimization on unstructured
meshes was developed. The project title refers to multi-scale optimization, which is one approach
to generate high resolution designs as presented by e.g. Groen [10]. During the project the develop-
ment of efﬁcient solvers was prioritized, in order to achieve high resolution designs by performing
topology optimization directly on a ﬁnely resolved mesh. Within the developed framework two
linear shell elements were implemented; a degenerated element and a continuum shell element. A
Helmholtz type PDE ﬁlter [11] and a geometric multigrid preconditioner were applied to both shell
elements in order to allow the solution of large systems.
2. PROJECT SUMMARY
This section serves as an overview of the project itself. In order to give an overview of the
contributions provided during the project, the conducted work is summarized in near chronological
order. During this project, the author
• designed and implemented a preprocessing routine for reading an Exodus II [12] ﬁle, parti-
tioning the mesh using METIS [13], and writing an Exodus II ﬁle for each parallel process.
This was necessary due to the lack of existing tools able to perform this partitioning and
include ghost layers, which are required for performing ﬁltering, into the partitioned ﬁles.
• designed and implemented a PETSc [14, 15, 16] based parallel ﬁnite element framework for
unstructured grids from scratch in C++. The framework was written in a general structure,
such that it uses a single element type with multiple design variable ﬁelds. Some features of
the framework are:
– A compact mesh data structure for an arbitrary element type, which stores both local
and ghost-layer information sequentially in memory. This includes a routine for reading
the Exodus II ﬁle produced by the preprocessing routine.
– A non compact assembly class which assembles all the required element information
in a cache friendly manner during a setup phase, in order to reduce the assembly time
for repeated assemblies. The assembly class also includes PETSc application orderings
in order to map the “natural” numbering to a PETSc numbering, which ensures that

the corresponding PETSc matrix and vector entries are created on the same process.
Finally, the assembler computes the exact sparsity pattern of the operator matrices from
the mesh information, which greatly increases the performance of both assembly and

– An output writer class where each process writes its own elements to an individual ﬁle.

• implemented a linear degenerated shell element with Mixed Interpolation of Tensorial Com-
poents (MITC) and Enhanced Assumed Strains (EAS) in the parallel framework.
• tested the accuracy of the implemented shell element. Three tests were performed, including
thin and doubly curved shells.
• implemented ﬁnite difference approximations to gradients to ensure correctness of sensitivit-
ies and ﬁlter corrections of sensitivities in later steps.
• applied the Helmholtz type PDE ﬁltering to shell elements. A three dimensional ﬁnite
volume implementation of the ﬁlter from Dilgen and Dilgen [19] was used as a basis for the
shell implementation, which works by extruding the ﬂat shells to a three dimensional mesh
which is used to solve the three dimensional volume ﬁlter equations.
• performed some simple topology optimization cases in order to verify that all implementa-
tions were correct.
• recovered the constitutive law for orthogonal rank-2 materials in shells, from corresponding
plate material models, and implemented this constitutive law as a material model of three
design variables. The rank-2 material was implemented as the ﬁrst step towards a multiscale
topology optimization approach, where the optimized rank-2 ﬁelds are mapped to a single
length scale mesh. This approach was eventually abandoned due to time constraints.
• applied a preexisting multigrid preconditioner for unstructured meshes to the degenerated
shell element. This required implementing a series of shell speciﬁc methods, e.g. determin-
ing if a point in three dimensional space is within a quadrilateral shell element. The observed
performance of the multigrid preconditioner with the degenerated shell formulation was not
sufﬁcient to solve large meshes.
• implemented a linear continuum shell element with MITC, EAS, Assumed Natural Strains
(ANS), and Scaled Thickness Conditioning (STC). The continuum implementation was
made in a degenerated shell element data structure by reusing the extrusion concept, de-
veloped when implementing the PDE ﬁlter. The additional shell element was implemented
in hope that it would result in lower condition numbers for the stiffness matrix, which would
enable the multigrid preconditioner to work better. Lower condition numbers were not
achieved, but the multigrid preconditioner was found to work satisfactorily with this shell
formulation.
• prepared a ﬁnite element shell analysis of an aircraft fuselage from the NASA Common
Research Model [20], and successfully performed topology optimization on a mesh of 4.3
million shell elements.

3. SHELL ELEMENT FORMULATION
Two linear shell element formulations were implemented during the project. The ﬁrst formulation
was a degenerated shell element presented by Trabal and Pujol [21]. Degeneration is the process of
collapsing the shell continuum to a quadrilateral element deﬁned in 3D space, sometimes called a
2.5D model. The used degeneration is a so-called ﬁve parameter formulation, illustrated in ﬁg. 1a,
with ﬁve nodal degrees of freedom - three displacements with respect to a global reference and two
rotations with respect to a local reference. The degenerated element requires vectors normal to the
shell surface deﬁned at each node, so-called node directors, in order to properly model the shell
curvature. Two enhancements, MITC and EAS, are applied in the formulation in order to increase
the accuracy, as the original element is prone to shear locking resulting in a spuriously stiffened
element. This element formulation is developed with the assumption that the normal strain in the
direction normal to the shell surface is negligible.
The second implemented element was a continuum shell element presented by Klinkel et al. [22].
A continuum shell element is a fully three dimensional hexahedral element as illustrated in ﬁg. 1b.
This continuum element is implemented as a degenerated element with 6 nodal degrees of freedom
in order to use meshing software for degenerated elements. The details of this implementation are
described in section 5.1. The continuum element contains three modiﬁcations in order to avoid
locking and to improve the element accuracy; MITC, EAS, and ANS. Furthermore, the STC for
continuum shell elements [23] was implemented in order to reduce the condition number of the
global stiffness matrix. The continuum element formulation makes no assumptions of zero normal
strain in the shell normal direction, making the formulation useful for thick shells.

The continuum element formulation was introduced as an alternative to the degenerated element
for multiple reasons. It was speculated that the condition number resulting from applying STC
to continuum elements would be lower than the condition number of degenerated elements, thus
improving the performance of iterative solving strategies. Also, it was observed that imposing
symmetry boundary conditions on the degenerated shell element would require adding a sixth nodal
degree of freedom through penalization. This addition would both increase the ill-conditioning
further as a consequence of the penalization and increase the size of the system matrix.

It was found that the continuum formulation with STC works well with the multigrid preconditioner,
and it was therefore used for the high resolution topology optimization. The used continuum ﬁnite
element formulation is primarily presented in this work. The degenerated formulation is very close
to the continuum formulation, as the main differences are how the displacements and strains are
interpolated. Therefore, the degenerated element formulation is presented alongside the continuum
formulation, using the subscript deg to denote operators of the degenerated formulation when a
distinction is needed.

3.2. COORDINATE SYSTEMS
In order to correctly model the displacement and strain ﬁelds within the shell elements several
coordinate systems are used. All coordinate systems are deﬁned by three basis vectors indexed by
i = 1,2,3. A brief overview of the coordinate systems is provided for reference:
Reference coordinate system {ei} - The usual orthonormal basis for a three dimensional space,
applied for deﬁning the mesh and deformations. This is the only globally deﬁned coordinate
system, i.e. the same basis vectors are used for all elements.

{e2} = {0 1 0}⊤,

Node director basis {Vi} - an orthonormal basis deﬁned anywhere inside a shell element. This
basis serves to deﬁne material properties at any point in the element. Note that {V3} is

Covariant basis {Gi} - A non-orthonormal basis deﬁned by derivatives of spatial coordinates

The nodal director {V3}I is the unit vector normal to the shell surface at node I. The nodal
directors can be approximated by summing all of the normal vectors of the connected elements
and normalizing the resulting vector, as no geometric representation of the domain is available.

the equatorial nodal directors of a hemisphere will never be exactly on the plane of the equator.
It was found that a substantial error was introduced by the misalignment of the node directors.
Therefore, an additional heuristic method was written in order to manually correct a subset of the
nodal directors by projecting the vectors onto a plane.

4. OPTIMIZATION FORMULATION
Topology optimization is performed in order to minimize the so-called compliance. Two different
material parametrizations are used; the well known Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization
(SIMP) and the orthogonal rank-2 parametrization. The Helmholtz type PDE based density ﬁlter
[11] is implemented in order to ensure a minimum length scale. The resulting optimization problem
is solved using the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) [29].

5. IMPLEMENTATION
The element and optimization formulations were implemented in a purpose built framework for
performing topology optimization on unstructured meshes. Currently, the two presented shells and
a bi-linear hexahedral element are available in the application, with the possibility to implement
more elements as needed. The application is written for MPI based distributed memory parallelism,
with the exception of a preprocessing application which reads a single Exodus II mesh-ﬁle and
partitions it into individual Exodus II mesh-ﬁles for each process.
The application is built on top of PETSc [14, 15, 16], which implements distributed parallel sparse
linear algebra such that the parallelism is abstracted away from most of the application. The other
direct external dependencies of the application are:
Exodus II The Exodus II ﬁle format and API are used for both input and output of the application
[12]. Exodus II is a portable binary ﬁle format which is supported by multiple meshing tools.
Since the ﬁle format is binary the ﬁles have reduced size and faster IO operations. Exodus II
is installed through PETSc.
Eigen Eigen is used to perform all dense linear algebra in the element formulations [35]. Eigen
has a very high-level syntax which eases implementing and debugging features, at some per-
formance cost for the abstraction. While Eigen is not the best choice in terms of performance,
it can be set to use BLAS and LAPACK as backends for the dense linear algebra through C
preprocessor macros. Eigen is installed through PETSc.
BLAS/LAPACK BLAS and LAPACK implement high performance dense local linear algebra.
BLAS and LAPACK can be installed through PETSc for debugging purposes, but when
performance is required a version which has been optimized for the used architecture is
needed. Optimized BLAS and LAPACK are available on most HPC clusters.
MUMPS The MUMPS package is used to perform the LU factorization of the resulting global
stiffness matrix. The direct factorization is used in the code, mostly to provide good solutions
for the smaller ﬁnite element systems for debugging purposes. MUMPS is installed through
PETSc.
METIS The METIS library [13] is a library with graph partitioning routines. METIS is used to
partition the mesh in the sequential preprocessing application. METIS is installed through
PETSc.
ANN The Approximate Nearest Neighbors (ANN) library is used in the application [36]. ANN
implements efﬁcient KD-trees, which are needed when constructing the multigrid prolonga-
tions. ANN is the only dependency which cannot be installed through PETSc.
24
==PAGE:29==
In addition to the external libraries, some parts of the application have been ported from other
projects. The PETSc implementation of MMA and the optimization module which initializes
design vectors originate from Aage and Lazarov [18]. The three dimensional ﬁnite volume solver
for the Helmholtz type PDE ﬁlter stems from Dilgen and Dilgen [19], where it is originally written
for hexahedral and tetrahedral meshes. The multigrid preconditioner which is included in the code
originates from Träff [37], which in turn originates from Alexandersen et al. [38], where it was
originally written for hexahedral, tetrahedral, and quadrilateral elements.
The application is made using templated classes in C++, which can be considered as a type of
static polymorphism. Most ﬁnite element “bookkeeping” is done within abstract classes that are
generated by class templates depending on the chosen element and material model. Hence, all
local system sizes, such as the number of nodes pr. element, are ﬁxed at compile-time, allowing
the C++ compiler to optimize the assembly. This allows the ﬂexibility to easily swap elements and
material models in the application, while maintaining the performance gained by having compile-
time information about local system sizes. Currently, the application is built for applying only one
simultaneous element and material combination for all elements, although extending the code to
multiple element and material types is possible. If a varying number of nodal degrees of freedom
is desired, an alternative numbering scheme for the degrees of freedom is necessary, as the current
scheme assumes a constant number of nodal degrees of freedom for all nodes.
5.1. MESH DATA STRUCTURE
The ﬁrst implemented shell element was the degenerated formulation, where the shell elements
are deﬁned as quadrilaterals in 3D space. This format is also the most commonly supported shell
format in meshing applications. It is the only supported shell element in Trelis/Cubit [39, 40],
which is the used meshing tool in this work. It was therefore chosen to implement the hexahedral
element as a quadrilateral, which is able to compute the hexahedral node coordinates when they
are needed.
The quadrilateral surface mesh is interpreted as a hexahedral mesh with one element in the shell
normal direction during analysis. When element integrations are performed the quadrilateral
elements are “regenerated” into hexahedral elements locally by extruding the surface nodes in the
shell normal direction, as illustrated in ﬁg. 7. Thus, the nodally deﬁned directors for the material
reference coordinate systems, are also used to deﬁne the node coordinates in the hexahedral mesh.
This regeneration strategy was originally developed to generate a hexahedral mesh for solving the
ﬁltering equations using a three dimensional ﬁnite volume approach.

5.2. MULTIGRID PRECONDITIONER
A geometric multigrid preconditioner is included in the implementation. The implementation uses
the PETSc multigrid preconditioner implementation PCMG, but builds its own custom prolongation
matrix based on a set of unstructured meshes with varying reﬁnement. This preconditioner has been
tested with both the hexahedral and the two shell elements. For hexahedral elements acceptable
performance and good strong scaling has been observed using the framework for approximately 1.3
million elements, as shown in ﬁg. 8. The benchmark was performed on the DTU Jess cluster using
two Intel Xeon E5-2680v2 ten-core CPUs for each compute node. From this small benchmark
it should be clear that the implementation of the multigrid approach scales well. This does not,
however, guarantee good performance, as the iterative solver has to converge in relatively few
iterations for the solver to be feasible.
The multigrid preconditioner was expanded to work on shell elements. The unstructured multigrid
preconditioner is largely element type agnostic. Applying the preconditioner to a new element type
requires implementing three element speciﬁc methods;
• a method to determine whether a given point is inside or outside the element.
• a method which computes the isoparametric coordinates from a point inside the element.
• a method which computes the shape functions at an isoparametric point.
For both shell elements, the resulting multigrid preconditioning strategy strongly resembles the
method presented by Fish et al. [42], although the article does not discuss continuum shells. When
generating the prolongation, the 2D quadrilateral shape functions are used. The shape functions
are computed using the two in-plane isoparametric coordinates ξ and η, while the out-of-plane
coordinate ζ is discarded. The resulting interpolation is applied for all six degrees of freedom at

solver was able to solve the systems within acceptable time limits, allowing topology optimization
on large meshes as shown in section 7.3. The full solving strategy closely resembles that of the
TopWing project [18]. In the solving strategy, the ﬂexible generalized minimal residual solver is
used both for the ﬁne grid and coarse grid solvers, Chebyshev solvers are used as smoothers for
each geometric multigrid level, and an algebraic multigrid preconditioner is used to precondition
the coarse grid solver.

the eigenvalues, and thus increase the condition number.
6.5. SUMMARY
When testing the elements, some unexpected results were found. The implementation of the
degenerated element performed well in two of the three test cases, while the third case had a
substantial error of approximately 12% in the displacement. For the continuum element formulation
it was consistently found that the version without EAS provides better accuracy than the one with
EAS. Both element implementations are on this basis accepted for use in topology optimization.
The continuum element is used without the EAS correction for optimization, due to both the
accuracy results presented and improved performance with the multigrid preconditioner.

8. DISCUSSION
During the element tests, the degenerated element performed well in most cases, but converged
to solution with 12% error for the hemisphere test case. This is suspected to be due to a mistake
in implementation as Trabal and Pujol [21] did not observe this error for the same element and
test case. The continuum element with EAS converges to solutions with errors of around 10%
in two of the test cases. The continuum shell element without EAS performs very well in all of
the presented test cases for element accuracy. The degenerated element implementation uses the
same implementation of EAS as the continuum element, but obtains the correct solution in some
of the test cases. Thus it is possible that the EAS is implemented correctly, but does not have the
desired effect on the displacement ﬁelds. When computing the displacement of the plate example
using a very ﬁnely resolved hexahedral mesh an acceptable agreement with the Kirchhoff solution
is found. This implies that either the EAS correction introduces an error to the continuum shell
element formulation in the linear case, or that there is an error in the implementation of the EAS
correction.
The various presented material models allow the consideration of a broad spectrum of design
problems. Using SIMP alone on a shell is equivalent to studying which parts of an existing shell
should be removed in order to reduce the weight to a desired level. Using the rank-2 material
model on a shell is difﬁcult to interpret directly, but if the rank-2 material is used in combination
with a passive material layer, it could be considered placing thin stiffeners along the two material
directions. Unfortunately, the integration of multiple materials through the thickness was not
implemented for the degenerated element in this project due to time constraints, and thus the
rank-2 material could not be used with passive material layers to solve a reinforcement problem.
Combining SIMP with a passive material layer also represents a reinforcement design problem as
used in the fuselage example, albeit only with an isotropic reinforcement.
A notable difference in the used shell formulations was found during the optimization test case
with a ﬂat square plate, where the resulting structure varied signiﬁcantly. The continuum shell
element did not result in near zero thickness structural members near the supports, as observed
in the degenerated element and other works, due to the bending moment being represented by
rotations which can go to zero [46]. This is possibly due to the fact that the degenerated formulation
represents the out-of-plane shear strains solely through the rotational degrees of freedom. As these
regions do not cary load the optimization algorithm removes material from them, resulting in the
observed zero thickness structures. The continuum element does not exhibit this behavior, making
it worthwhile to consider for topology optimization of plates.
The presented setup with a multigrid preconditioner was used to solve a high resolution aircraft
fuselage with body and pressure loads. The resulting fuselage structures have many intermediate
densities. This is due to the distributed loads on the fuselage, which incentivizes the optimization
algorithm to spread the available material. By employing the robust formulation [47], the structure
could be forced to a 0-1 design while maintaining control of the minimum length scale.
The load cases applied to the aircraft fuselage are very simple and do not correctly represent the
loads which a fuselage is subjected to in reality. This is reﬂected in the resulting designs, as they
are a direct result of the choice of load cases. Furthermore, an aircraft fuselage is usually stiffened
by some internal structure, which is not present in the presented model. This is very apparent as
most of the material is used to support large bending moments, which should be carried by the
internal structure. Instead, supporting the outer shell across the entire aircraft becomes a secondary
objective, and a very thin outer shell is needed to force the algorithm to place material across the
entire fuselage.
38
==PAGE:43==
Some problems were observed if the element side lengths were smaller than approximately 2 times
the shell thickness for the continuum elements on the fuselage. Using the scaled Jacobian mesh
quality metric it became clear that this was due to the extrusion approach, which generated meshes
with negative scaled Jacobian values. Thus, as the mesh was reﬁned, the shell thickness also had
to be decreased to some extent. It is speculated that this is due to a high local curvature in parts
of the fuselage which would result in severely distorted elements as element node directors vary
greatly. If the multigrid approach could be applied to the degenerated approach, or if continuum
shell meshes which are generated for the purpose were used, this problem would be avoided.
When resolving the fuselage in high resolution with continuum elements, another unexpected
phenomenon occurs due to the high resolution of the mesh. As the mesh is reﬁned, the average
element side length decreases, while the shell thickness remains constant. The resulting mesh
has an average element side length of 1 cm with thickness 2.54 cm. In this case, resolving the
shell by using multiple hexahedral elements through the shell thickness suddenly becomes a viable
alternative, which is not considered in this work. Also, it is worth noting that the base deﬁnition
of a shell having low thickness in one direction is no longer satisﬁed on element level. When
solving the fuselage the presented STC was applied to the shell elements. From the presented study
of condition numbers, the STC might actually increase the condition number for shell elements
which are thicker than they are wide. In future work it could be tested whether removing the STC
from the element formulation increases the solving time for the fuselage example. It could also be
tested whether the shell formulation remains accurate in these extreme conditions, or if the solution
diverges at some point of reﬁnement.
The performance of the multigrid solver is also very sensitive to the solver parameters. Speciﬁcally,
the performance is sensitive to the restart number for the ﬁne and coarse grid solvers, the number
of geometric multigrid levels, and the number of iterations spent in the coarse grid solver. The
correct setting of these parameters makes the difference between acceptable solving times, and not
being able to solve the problem at all. For instance, the scaling results presented in section 5.2
for hexahedral elements were obtained using suboptimal solver parameters, and the performance
could be improved by using better parameters. The best choice of solver parameters depends on
many factors including mesh geometry, the considered load case, and the distribution of material
parameters. This is for instance seen in section 7.3, where the required wall-clock time to solve 200
iterations of optimization varied from 13 to 28 hours with identical solver parameters, mesh, and
load cases. This variation was solely due to some of the resulting density distributions being better
suited for the set solver parameters. For this reason, the ﬁrst step of performing any high resolution
topology optimization with a multigrid preconditioner should be to tune the solver parameters.
9. CONCLUSION
The two presented shell element formulations were implemented and tested against some standard
test cases for shells. It was found that the continuum element without the enhanced assumed strains
was the most accurate of the considered formulations and was therefore subsequently used for
performing topology optimization. Another reason to opt for the continuum element without the
EAS formulation is that the discontinuous strain ﬁelds from the EAS were found to work poorly in
combination with the multigrid preconditioner.
Both the degenerated and continuum formulation with EAS had notable errors of up to 12% in
at least one of the three considered load cases. These elements can still be used in topology
optimization, but it should be noted that the resulting structures might take advantage of some
element defects.

in order to show that the resulting structures are in accordance with the mechanical principles of the
presented systems. The resulting structures when optimizing a ﬂat square plate with central loading
were found to drastically vary between the two considered formulations. Using the degenerated
formulation resulted in load carrying regions with near zero material, due to the modelling of out-of-
plane shear strains with rotations. This phenomenon was not observed when using the continuum
formulation, where the out-of-plane shear strains are modelled by differences in displacement.
Having validated the implemented procedures, topology optimization was performed on a high-
resolution mesh of an aircraft fuselage using 4.3 million elements. This number of elements is
rather unimpressive when compared to the number of elements in high-resolution three dimensional
meshes, which range from 100M to 1.1 billion [48]. This discrepancy is due to two factors. Firstly,
the ill-conditioning of the resulting shell ﬁnite element system matrix greatly increases the difﬁculty
of solving the linear systems of equations. Generally, it was observed that the shell model required
near 100 iterations to converge, while less than 20 iterations would do for a hexahedral mesh. The
second reason for the discrepancy in required elements is the two dimensionality of shell elements.
As a shell is a quadrilateral, far fewer elements are required to obtain a small average element
side length. A clear example of this is the presented aircraft fuselage, which with its 4.3 million
elements obtains a maximum element side length of 1.7 cm, compared to the maximum element
side length of 0.8 cm obtained by 1.1B hexahedral elements by Aage et al. [48] in a structure of
similar size.
