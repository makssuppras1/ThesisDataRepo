Abstract
The purpose of this project has been to design a test rig for a turbo expander
supported by active magnetic bearings for an ongoing PhD project. The test
rig is based on a general concept from Siemens of a turbo expander supported
by active magnetic bearings. Through a design phase this concept has been
modiﬁed to be able to create the test conditions needed. The design phase
has regarded speciﬁcation of which sensors to use, how to create for test forces
needed, tolerance chain analyses and general machine element design.
The ﬁnal design is speciﬁed with a full bill of materials and machine drawings
ready to be produced.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The goal of the project is to design a test rig for a turboexpander supported by
active magnetic bearings. The test rig will be used in an ongoing PhD proejct
in order to validate computer models of the system and to test the eﬀects of
diﬀerent sealing designs. The test rig will be based on a 700 kW turboexpander
concept by Siemens Turbomachinery. This is designed for nitrogen as the pro-
cess gas, running at temperatures down to −150 ◦C.
Turboexpanders consist of an central axle suspended in bearings with a ex-
pander on one side and a compressor on the other. Process air is run through
the expander which then drives the compressor. The most common turboex-
pander is a turbocharger for a car. Here the expander turbine wheel is driven
from the exhaust gas, while driving the compressor to create a higher pressure
for the engine intake. In the industry turboexpanders are used for many pur-
poses, eg. separation of natural gas liquid from natural gas, where it is part
of a larger chain of processes.
Turboexpanders operate at very high angular velocities. Using ball bearings
to support the axle is uncommon because of the high wear on ball bearings
at high speeds. Fluid oil bearings have typically been used for this purpose.
The advantage of using active magnetic bearings is that there is no wear on
the bearings themselves prolonging need for maintenance. Furthermore vibra-
tions can be minimized by actively controlling the axle and by making the
axle spin around its inertia axis. The use of active magnetic bearings for turbo
expanders is not a new invention. Several companies produce and sell whole
turbo expander solutions with active magnetive bearings in the 3kW to 14MW
power range. The results from this project and attached phd is to give Siemens
Turbomachinery insight into active magnetic bearings to be able to produce
their own turbo expanders running on active magnetic bearings.

Chapter 2
General speciﬁcation
The general speciﬁcations of the test rig are based of the speciﬁcations for
the 700 kW turbo expander from Siemens. This is in terms of radial and axial
forces the magnetic bearing should be designed for. The test rig will not mimic
the extreme demands for low temperatures and will only be running at ambient
temperatures.
2.1
Radial magnetic bearings
The radial bearings must be able to create a force of Fr = 1200 N.
2.2
Backup bearings
The axle should be constrained by back up bearings to prevent the axle from
coming in contact with the magnetic bearings or other surfaces which could
damage the axle. The axle should be constrained in both the radial and axial
direction.
2.3
Axial magnetic bearings
The axial magnetic bearings should be able to control the axial placement of
the axle, and deliver a force up to Fa = 10500 N.
2.4
Housing
The housing needs to be pressure-tight up to pmax = 10 bar.
2.5
Sealing
There must be a central sealing around the largest diameter of the axle which
can divide the housing into two pressurized zones. This pressure diﬀerence
must be able to generate an axial force of 10500 N on the axle. This sealing
must be removable.
The test rig must contain process seals that mimic the seals of the real turbo
expander separating the expander and compressor from the central housing.
These must be removable.
2.6
Axle
The axle must contain a dummy disc in replacement of the compressor. A
pressure diﬀerence under 10 bar must be able to generate an axial force of
10500 N. The axle assembly must be able to be balanced outside of the test

The test rig must contain displacement sensors close to each magnetic bearing

Design

can be slid around the axle from each side. These cassette assemblies contain

will be the naming convention for this report. The test rig is designed with a
turbine wheel on one side and a dummy disc on the other, see ﬁgure 3.1. The
side of the thrust disc where the turbine is mounted will be referred to as side

The production method for the plates is very important because the radial
gap s0 between the rotor and stator has to be very precise. The best method
for producing identical sheets is with stamping. For prototype quantities this
is however not an option because it requires making a stamping tool which
would be too expensive. Laser cutting the plates has been the best possible
solution. A company has been found that can laser cut the plates, stack them
and bound them together to one pack. The bounding is done with a backlack
coating, which enables the plates to bound together when heated to a certain
temperature. The plates are stacked in a custom built jig, to ensure the correct
alignment before the plates are heat treated.
The laser cutter used has general tolerance of ±0.1 mm and a repeatability of
±0.03 mm. This means that all the plates will be within a the same dimen-
sions by 0.03 mm but it could vary ±0.1 mm from the nominal values. This
could result in the air gap s0 varying with 50%. To correct this the plates
will be made to be over dimensioned and afterwards processed to the correct
dimension. Experiences from another magnetic bearing phd project has shown
that is it possible to grind the rotor down in dimension. It is unclear if honing
is possible on the stator, but if it is not, the rotor can be grinded down until
the air gap is the correct value.

Rotor
The rotor is made from the same material as the stator and will also be fabri-
cated into a packed stack. This has been chosen to ease the assembly with the
axle. Instead of ﬁtting 112 sheets of thin metal sheets to the axle, only one
solid cylinder has to be assembled to the axle.
Axle assembly
The rotor pack will be sandwiched between two package rings as seen on ﬁgure
3.6. The inner ring is used as a spacer and ensures the correct position of the
rotor pack. The outer ring is made with a tighter ﬁt, than the rotor pack to
ensure the rotor pack stays in position if it should come loose during operation.

a mass unbalance and the rotor running the rotational speed corresponding
to a natural frequency. The natural frequencies are not ﬁxed, as they change
with rotational speed. Therefore a thorough analysis of this must be done.

6.3
Stability
In order to analyse the stability of the system the whole system needs to be
included, which entails the magnetic bearings. The magnetic bearing can only
pull on the axle, and this pulling force increases as the distance gets smaller.
This means that the magnetic bearings will introduce negative stiﬀness to the
system, making the system unstable. Therefore a controller is needed to dy-
namically control the force of these bearings.
The design of the controller is beyond the scope of this project, but is being
developed by PhD student Jonas Lauridsen. The current design of his con-
troller has been used for the following analysis. The controller is based on a
closed loop system roughly sketched in ﬁgure 6.5.

Chapter 7
Discussion
There are some areas of the design which should be discussed further. The
magnetic saturation limit of the thrust bearing could be a issue. Given that
the saturation limit of the axle material is unknown the maximum axial force
may not be able to be produced. The only way to lower the magnetic sat-
uration needed for the same force, is to make the pole area larger which is
not possible. The test-rig is however designed to be able to test if a pressure
diﬀerent over the thrust disc could relieve the axial bearing. Given that the
axial force will be applied by making a pressure diﬀerence over the dummy
disc the axial force can be controlled. If it turns out that the thrust bearing
will not be able to produce the needed force, a smaller force could be applied
instead of the maximum force. To ﬁnd the saturation limit the B/H curve
should be investigated. DTU Electro has as machine to ﬁnd B/H curves for
diﬀerent materials. It is recommended that this is used for the axle material
to ﬁnd the saturation limit.
The sealing eﬀect of the plane seals has not been investigated further. The
radial gap speciﬁed is relatively large for gas sealings which could mean that
they are not able to provide the needed sealing eﬀect. A labyrinth sealings
needs to be designed to the given requirements, or they can be replaced with
a carbon ﬂoating ring seals which are known to provide a good sealing eﬀect
for gases. The sealings however also serve the purpose of introducing damping
eﬀects to the axle, which have to be included in the model for the controller.
This is still possible for the plane seal.
The encoder system speciﬁed for the test rig could be a problem, because it
is not rated up to the operational speeds needed. This has to be investigated
when the test rig is assembled and running. The high operational speeds will
ﬁrst be applied when the test rig is fully operational and the controller has
been tuned to prevent a critical failure. For the tuning process and measure-
ment of the axle behaviour the high resolution of the disc is preferred. The
high resolution could help determine if the axle has become unbalanced after
being mounted in the housing.
The ability to run at the high operational speeds should also be considered.
The turbine driving the axle is very small compared to the expander wheel on
the real system. The test rig will however not have the same angular moment
forces acting on the system, because there is no compressor wheel. The only
force acting against rotation is the air resistance around the axle. The max-
imum pressure possible at DTU is 80 bar, which should be enough to obtain
the high operation speeds.

Discussion
The eddy current sensors used for the project may be over dimensioned, and
the high precision of them may not be needed. The ﬁrst objective of the test
rig is to get a functional controller for the magnetic bearings. The controller
is based on knowing the correct position of the axle, so to minimize the source
of a measuring error the high precision sensors are used at ﬁrst. A later study
can be made to investigate if smaller, less expensive positioning sensors can be
used.
The assembly process for the rotor packs has proven to be complicated, and
entitles that the rotor must be able to be grinded on the inside surface. If this
is not possible the axle must be ﬁtted to the rotor pack dimensions. Because of
time constraints the axle and rotor pack will be ordered at the same time from
two diﬀerent suppliers. This means that the axle must be processed two times
and new package rings are needed. The extra process time could be expensive
so this is not desired.
The heat generated from the copper wires has been evaluated from the as-
sumption of one solid coil. The wire coil will contain several individual wires,
so there is the possibility that the inside wires will have a higher temperature
than what the analysis showed. The wire stacking has been calculated from a
conservative ﬁlling factor. It could be possible that the wiring could be done
with an AWG size lower than the chosen, which would decrease the power
dissipation in the wires. This must be evaluated when the coils are fabricated.
The heating from eddy current losses has not been considered in the heat gen-
eration, but this could also contribute to an extra heating of the system.
The concentricity of all the part is a concern. The fabrication process has
to be very precise in order for the machine to ﬁt together. There are nine
near contact surfaces along the rotating axle which all must be aligned cor-
rectly. The machine drawings have been speciﬁed with concentric tolerances
and these must be upheld in order for the machine to be able to run without
interference.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The test rig has been design with a central tube housing.
To ensure the
housing can be pressurized all connecting surfaces to housing are sealed with
an o-ring. To create the axial forces the test rig has been divided into four
pressure zones all separated with plane seals. To imitate the properties of the
compressor wheel, a dummy disc with the same axial moment of inertia has
been designed. The pressure diﬀerence over the disc needed for the maximum
axial force is p = 7.5 bar. The pressure diﬀerence needed over the thrust disc
for the same axial force is p = 3.9 bar. To be able to test diﬀerent designs of
sealings these have been design to be changeable.
Five high precision eddy current sensors that can withstand pressures up to
100 bar are implemented to measure the position of the axle. Two sensors are
located close to each magnetic bearing, measuring the x and y position of the
axle and one is placed at the end of the axle, measuring the axial position.
The axle is powered by a turbine from an old truck turbocharger. The turbine
wheel is mounted on the axle by a conical connection and a KMFE lock nut.
The same connection type is used for the dummy disc.
The radial bearings are designed to have a ﬂoating stator during the assembly
process. A jig will be used to center the inner diameter of the stator correctly
in regards to the rest of the system.
The stator will consist of 112 plates
manufactured into one pack using backlack technology. The same process is
used for the rotor, which will be shrink ﬁtted to the axle. The radial bearings
will have N = 140 windings per pole pair and be able to deliver the maximum
force at a current of Imax = 5.9 A with an air gap of s0 = 0.4 mm. The axial
magnetic bearing will have N = 144 windings and deliver the maximum force
at a current of Imax = 7.1 A with an air gap of s0 = 0.55 mm.
To measure the angular position and velocity an Avago encoder solution is used.
The sensor output will be feed through the housing with a Lemo connector.
Lemo connectors are also used to feed through power cables to the magnetic
bearings.
Two sets of backup bearings will constrain the axle from damaging component
in the machine. The radial clearance between the axle and backup bearings
is sbbr = 0.150 mm and the axial clearance is sbba = 0.250 mm. The design
has incorporated shims throughout the test rig, to be able to calibrate the
placements of diﬀerent component so the needed clearances can be made.
The rotor dynamic analysis has shown that the ﬁrst ﬂexible mode shape will be
higher than the operating speed. Furthermore is has been shown the nodes for
the ﬁrst mode shape lays between the position sensor and magnetic bearing on
