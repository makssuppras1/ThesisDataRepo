Abstract
This project aims to investigate the potential of unused roof attics and the sustainable renovation
potential by conducting a Life Cycle Assessment of three renovation cases found in the industry.
Further, methods for assessing the sustainability of the outcome are examined, and the eﬃciency of
these was found by comparison with the results from the cases.
The ﬁndings of this project showed that even though the diﬀerence is not signiﬁcant, the renovation
cases have shown to be a more sustainable way of ﬁnding new housing square meters than building
new. The cases in the thesis have shown to be either better or comparable to other roof renovations
or new build cases found in literature. Further, even though the operational impacts do not get as
low as for new build the improved operational impact from the cases have shown to pay back the
embodied impacts. Thus, it is proﬁtable to renovate the roof when looking holistic at the complete
building. Lastly, there have been found alternative materials that improve the embodied impacts
of the cases for several of the examined materials. Thus, materials should be chosen with care as it
have been shown to have a signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁnal result.
Further, it has been concluded that there is no uniform way to assess the sustainability of the
outcome. The threshold limit values have shown to be usable to produce results for evaluating
respectively whether the renovations are sustainable as the results gained by the threshold value
can be compared to other renovations and new build, or the climatic payback time of the individual
renovation can be assessed.

1 | Introduction

1.1
Motivation
The motivation for this project has been to focus on a theme that needs more focus. We face an
urgent climate crisis, where the construction sector plays a signiﬁcant role in both the challenge and
the solution. Worldwide, buildings are responsible for 39% of global energy-related carbon emis-
sions, from which 28% is from operational energy consumption by heating, cooling, and powering
them and 11% is from the embodied energy by the production of building materials used for new
construction, maintenance, and renovation [Victoria Kate Burrows & Matthew Adams, 2019]. "In
Denmark, where there has been focus on reducing operational energy consumption for many years,
greenhouse gas emissions from construction are estimated to account for 30% of total emissions, split
between 20% from operational energy and 10% from materials" [Klimapartnerskabet for bygge-og
anlaegssektoren, 2020]. However, one should remember that new agreements have been made to
expand the energy system with a more signiﬁcant share of renewable energy sources, meaning the
above percentage can be seen as a bit conservative [Leonora Charlotte Malabi Eberhardt et al.,
2022]. Denmark has tried to solve this with the Climate Act [Klima, 2021]. With this act, Denmark
has committed to reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 70% in 2030 compared to 1990 and being
climate neutral by 2050.
The European Commission has launched the initiative A Renovation Wave for Europe -greening our
buildings, creating jobs, improving lives [European commission, 2020] as the potential of renovating
the existing building stock and thus reducing operational energy consumption, rather than demol-
ishing buildings and building new ones, is pointed out as an essential part of the climate agenda.
Denmark has tried to follow this by the National strategi for baeredygtigt byggeri [Indenrigs- og
Boligministeriet, 2021] where initiatives are taken towards a more sustainable construction sector,
which is a signiﬁcant renovation eﬀort in the social housing sector. The eﬀort will bring the social
housing stock up to standard in terms of maintenance requirements, and there will also be a focus
on energy eﬃciency.
Climate impacts from new construction and the potential for possible climate reductions are rela-
1
==PAGE:12==
tively well documented in Denmark, while the climate potential of renovation compared to demo-
lition and new construction is currently less documented. Moreover, a comprehensive overview of
the scale and type of renovations and demolitions are lacking. For new buildings, the limit values
have already been determined in the Building Regulations 2023 [“Bygningsreglementet 2023”, 2023],
but determining limit values is more complex for renovations. One possibility for getting closer to
determining limit values for renovations is to look at the individual renovation types.
This task aims to deﬁne the climate impacts of roof renovation of older brick buildings often seen in
larger cities. In the report Boligmarkedsanalyse for hovedstaden - Boligbehov, udviklingspotentialer
og-strategier [HelgeSigurdNæss-Schmidt et al., 2018], it is described that there is a shortage of
73,000 homes in Copenhagen. Hence, it will be interesting to investigate whether it is advantageous
to incorporate unused drying lofts when renovating roofs rather than building new homes. The
critical question is how to ensure the renovations are sustainable so it makes sense to renovate. In
late 2022, a report, [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022], was published and it included suggestions on
how threshold limit values for renovations were given. These limit values will be evaluated against
the results from this thesis, as it is interesting to look into whether the conclusions from this report
can be used in other cases.
A renovation can result in CO2 emissions greater than any CO2 reductions from subsequent opera-
tional energy consumption. Hence attention must be paid to climate considerations when choosing
solutions and materials. Indenrigs- og Boligministeriet, 2021 Therefore, the CO2 emissions from
renovation versus the CO2 reductions from subsequent operational energy consumption will be ex-
amined in this thesis.
The above mentioned issues are an inspiration to carry out research in order to answer the following
questions:
What is the potential of renovating existing available attics rather than constructing new buildings,
how can the impacts of embodied impacts be reduced and what is the most eﬀective method for
assessing the sustainability of the outcome?
1.2
Objectives
This project aims to investigate the potentials of unused roof attics and the sustainable renovation
potentials in these. The investigation includes:
• Establishing an understanding of the background of renovation LCAs and the existing knowl-
edge of sustainability within renovations, change of use renovations and the possible threshold
limit values for these.
• Collecting renovation cases where unused drying lofts have been converted into new liveable
square metres by establishing peaked ceilings. These cases should include the documents that
2
==PAGE:13==
describe the case including drawings of the case, a bill of materials and the energy usage before
renovating.
• Form an understanding of the building, the renovation, the building components, and thus
the amount of materials used.
• Conduct a life cycle assessment to assess the embodied impacts and calculate the energy
requirements in order to assess the operational impacts of each of the three cases.
• Evaluate the cases by matching them up against one another, matching them with results from
literature and comparing them with known values for life cycle assessment and the threshold
limit value for energy for new build cases. Thus the results will be compared with threshold
limit values from the literature.

2 | Background
This chapter presents the background theory behind the thesis.
There is currently a strong focus on the societal potential of promoting sustainability in environmen-
tal, economic and social aspects. We are facing a pressing climate agenda where construction plays
a signiﬁcant role in both the challenge and the solution. 39% of global greenhouse gas emissions can
be attributed to the total energy consumption and 11% from the production of building materials
used in new construction, maintenance, and renovation [World Green Building Council, 2020].
Sustainability in renovation has only recently come onto the agenda. Therefore, the literature on
the topic is minimal yet relatively new and up to date. To eﬀectuate the contribution of the existing
building stock to CO2 savings, the European Commission has launched the strategy A Renovation
Wave for Europe – Greening our buildings, creating jobs, improving lives to boost renovation in the
EU in 2020. The strategy was to promote more profound energy renovations, using the life-cycle
approach and conversion to renewable energies.[European commission, 2020] If energy eﬃciency
leads to real CO2 reductions, renovations must be considered from a life-cycle perspective, including
the impacts of the materials used to generate the energy savings. [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022]
The Renovation Wave [European commission, 2020] aims to double annual energy renovation rates
over the next ten years. It aims to unlock the carbon-saving potential of the existing building stock,
an initiative that will promote more and larger-scale energy renovations. These renovations will
enhance people’s quality of life and reduce emissions living in and using the buildings and should
create several additional green jobs in the construction sector.
Denmark has also published a national strategy for a more sustainable construction sector called
National strategi for baeredygtigt byggeri [Indenrigs- og Boligministeriet, 2021] in 2021. Here ﬁve
initiatives towards a more sustainable construction sector are set.
These include more climate-
friendly buildings and construction, high-quality, sustainable buildings, resource-eﬃcient construc-
tion, energy-eﬃcient and healthy buildings, and digitally supported construction. In order to comply
with these initiatives, activities such as "The voluntary sustainability class", phase-in of threshold
limit value for the climate impact of buildings, promoting climate-friendly building materials, over-
all assessments for renovation and many more have been launched. The activities led to reports
published at the end of 2022 by BUILD, the department of the built environment, on the overall
4
==PAGE:15==
assessments for renovation and how they could be ensured to be more climate-friendly. By this, the
beginning of a more sustainable renovation of the existing building mass is set.
2.1
Renovation versus new build
Among several reports, including a report from Rambøll called Analyse af CO2-udledning og to-
taløkonomi i renovering og nybyg [Lise Hvid Horup Sørensen & Mathilde Mattson, 2020] and a report
from BUILD Helhedsvurdering ved renovering [Leonora Charlotte et al., 2022], it is documented that
both from a climate perspective and a total economic perspective, it is more advantageous to ren-
ovate than to demolish and build new. Across single-family houses, terraced houses, multi-storey
buildings, commercial buildings and public buildings, Rambøll’s report shows that, compared to
new construction, renovation oﬀers the most signiﬁcant climate beneﬁt for the money. Also, re-
sults from the studied cases in the report from BUILD of the multi-family building type show that
renovation continues to be better in terms of climate than demolition/new construction.
The report Helhedsvurdering ved renovering [Leonora Charlotte et al., 2022] shows that operational
energy contributes signiﬁcantly to the climate for the renovation of the example buildings impact
(30-66%). At the same time, the climate impact on demolition and construction of newbuild is
mainly related to materials, and operational energy represents a minor part of the climate balance
(19-24%). Thus it is energy renovation that should be the focus here and now.
The climate impact of demolition is currently overlooked, as the demolished building is typically
not included in the climate footprint of the new building. However, demolition accounts for 6-20%
of the total climate impact when the existing buildings are demolished before constructing a new
building rather than being renovated. This determines whether renovation appears better or worse
than demolition/new construction. [Leonora Charlotte et al., 2022]
2.2
Renovation potential
As most of the danish building mass is already built, renovations are a big part of the danish
building industry. The report BUILD Rapport 2021:08 Varmebesparelse i eksisterende bygninger
[Søren Aggerholm & Jesper Kragh, n.d.] states that energy saving renovations have the largest
potential to reduce buildings’ climate impact. Thus a signiﬁcant climate impact reduction from
the renovation can be achieved when the focus is on measures that reduce the operational energy
consumption’s climate impact.
As seen in Figure 2.1, the main renovations are windows, with the majority replacing the entire
window, then roofs, with the majority replacing the roof covering but retaining the load-bearing
structure. Finally comes external walls, with most activity replacing the entire facade. This is one
of the reasons for the choice taken to investigate roof renovations and the possibility of making
them more sustainable and extending the renovation to not just including a roof renovation but
5
==PAGE:16==
using unused drying attics. These types of renovations are categorised in “Bygningsreglementet”
[“Bygningsreglementet”, 2018] as change of use, and these potentials will be investigated in the
section below.
Figure 2.1: Most frequent renovations - Number of renovations of residential buildings by type of
renovation based on [Viegand Maagøe A/S & Wilke A/S, 2021] and scaled up to national level using
The Building and Housing Register (BBR) [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022]
As seen in Figure 2.1, roofs are, according to property managers, the number two most renovated
building component. Furthermore, as demonstrated in the report Analyse af CO2-udledning og
totaløkonomi i renovering og nybyg [Lise Hvid Horup Sørensen & Mathilde Mattson, 2020] and
seen in Figure 2.2, renovations of roofs have a higher overall embodied and operational impact as
the operational impacts are not lowered. The analysis has been conducted from three renovation
scenarios.
Respectively a roof renovation, a renovation of both the roof and building envelope
and a renovation of the roof, building envelope and installations. The results are based on the
embodied impacts from the materials used in the renovation and the operational impacts. The
more comprehensive renovation scenario where the roof and walls are re-insulated and windows
are upgraded increases the insulation performance so much that the operational energy is reduced
signiﬁcantly. The reduction is so signiﬁcant that the overall impacts over the 50 year consideration

2.2.1
Renovations with change of use
As described in the introduction, there was a shortage of 73,000 homes in Copenhagen, and this
number has possibly only grown [HelgeSigurdNæss-Schmidt et al., 2018]. As roof renovations have
a high embodied impact compared to operational impact, renovating unused attics into apartments
should be considered in the same process as it would make it possible to distribute the embodied
impact over more square metres. Further, it should also be considered if the building envelope can
be renovated simultaneously; however, due to aesthetic and conservation value, this can be diﬃcult
in the older, which is looked at in this thesis.
A 2012 master’s thesis from DTU [Daniel Clausen & Nicklas Engdam, 2012] showed that housing
for 22,000 residents could be developed in Copenhagen alone if the full potential of drying attics
in older Copenhagen buildings were exploited. The study investigated single-storey buildings in
Copenhagen during 1850-1950, as a lot was built during this period, and the majority was built
according to the same building principle. The study identiﬁed more than 300,000 unused square
metres of Copenhagen lofts that could be developed into housing for approximately 22,000 people.
According to the same report, these attic apartments could accommodate one in four newcomers
to Copenhagen. Copenhagen needs cheaper square meters, so converting old drying lofts into new
apartments could be a possible partial solution to this challenge. As seen in Figure 2.3, it was most
common to build with pitched roofs before 1930, and therefore it is probably in these buildings that
most of the potential can be found.

2.3
Measuring climate impacts of change of use renovations
As limit values for the CO2 emissions from newbuild have been studied a lot, it is time to set
threshold limit values for renovations. This topic has only been studied a little, and the danish
published Klimapåvirkning fra renovering - Muligheder for udformning af graensevaerdier til LCA
for renovering [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022] suggests setting up six types of threshold limit
values. Threshold limit values can not be deﬁned in the same way by renovations as with newbuild
as renovation of buildings can look completely diﬀerent. Hence, it is interesting to identify the
literature that has already been written on this subject. As one of the objectives of this thesis is
to investigate the most eﬀective method for assessing the sustainability of the analysed renovation
cases, methods are looked into to quantify this. Therefore, the conclusions on relevant threshold
limit values for change of use renovation will be compared with the results in this thesis in Section
6.2. The comparison can give an insight into the advantages/disadvantages and whether they are
realistic.
Only two values will be examined as they are relevant to the investigation in this thesis.
The
investigated cases are all in the change of use category as the area in the old drying attic is changed
into liveable rooftop apartments. The deﬁnition of change of use renovations is found in Section 4.
For renovations in the change of use category, the limit values speciﬁed as 1 and 3 in the report
[Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022] and presented in Section 2.3.1 and 2.3.2 are relevant. It is due to
the limit values’ ability to view the whole building from their perspective.
8
==PAGE:19==
The change of use category is found to cover cases which can have a speciﬁcally high variety in the
climate impacts from the materials as seen in the report [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022]. However,
all the cases have an emission below the median from a newbuild.
2.3.1
Deﬁnition of Limit Value 1
In the report, [Alberte Mai Lund et al., 2022], it is found that there is a possibility to use the
threshold limit value called Limit Value 1 in combination with limit values for building elements,
in line with energy requirements.
Limit Value 1 is a benchmark for the complete building. This limit value sees the building as a
complete unit in line with the demand for new buildings, based on a limit for the total climate impact
of the renovation, which includes the embodied and operational impacts. The limit value can be
linked to the renovation classes, where the developer can see the renovation more holistic, where the
requirement is raised from the building component level to the whole building level by performing
an energy framework. For this limit value, it is necessary to perform a complete LCA of the whole
renovation, where all materials must be mapped. The material mapping must include all activities
within a 50 years consideration period. It includes the existing materials that are demolished either
during the renovation or after 50 years and the added materials. It is also essential to perform an
energy frame calculation of the building to include the heat and electricity needed.
This limit value allows compliance with either individual component requirements or a component
requirements framework. A whole-building limit value allows for greater ﬂexibility in the combina-
tion of options, while the requirements for building elements will require less work to implement.
Meeting this limit value may require a material mapping from all renovation interventions in the
building. In addition, an energy performance calculation is required, which is currently optional
to be carried out for renovations, so it may not automatically be included in all projects. It could
be helpful to consider whether legal requirements should be introduced, such as energy perfor-
mance calculation of a renovation project, which could address the lack of data and standardise the
methodology for designing renovations.

2.3.2
Deﬁnition of Limit Value 3
The report also found that there is a possibility to use the threshold limit value called Limit Value 3.
Limit value 3 assesses the climatic payback time of an energy renovation and is seen as a motivator
for considering energy-saving measures to reduce the overall energy consumption of a building when
renovating. This way, a CO2 saving in the operational phase can be achieved to oﬀset the climate
impact of the materials added in the renovation.
For this limit value, it is necessary to perform a complete LCA of the complete renovation, where
all materials must be mapped.
For this type of requirement value, where the existing building
is compared to a renovated scenario, it can be argued that demolition of all existing materials
will occur in both scenarios and can therefore be omitted from the calculation. The mapping will
only concern the new materials added during the renovation. In addition, when documenting the
potential energy savings of the energy renovation, two energy framework calculations are required,
one for the existing building before renovation and one for the renovated scenario. It can therefore
lead to a signiﬁcant workload in meeting this threshold.
Looking at the complete renovation holistically, with both the before and after cases, can be a way
to ensure reasonable renovation solutions that will not increase the climate impact of the building.
However, this limit value is bound to renovations that lead to energy savings. When the energy
saving of a renovation is a determining factor in a threshold, it can be challenging to compare
buildings with diﬀerent energy starting points (energy consumption before renovation). A building
with poorly insulated elements and high energy consumption will achieve a higher energy saving
when the energy performance standard is met than buildings with a lower energy consumption
before renovation. Thus, the existing conditions in the building signiﬁcantly impact the energy
savings that can be achieved; the more insulation there is before the renovation, the less eﬀect
additional insulation has. Since change of use, by deﬁnition, had a lower energy demand before the
renovation, it can be assumed that, in most cases, there will be no energy saving for this renovation
category, and a calculation of the climate payback time cannot be performed.

6 | Discussion
In this Section, interpretations of the results from the LCA will be addressed, topics such as material
choices and change of use renovations will be discussed, and the results will be compared to other
literature ﬁndings.
6.1
Interpretation of results
It is essential to evaluate the LCA results before making conclusions. The output of the complete life
cycle assessment has overall shown that all three cases are more or less comparable with newbuild
through comparison with the Danish reference building in Section 5.3.1. The cases are comparable
as the total impact from the operational impacts of the attic and embodied impacts/m2 of attic/year
are close to the result from the Danish reference building. However, the result is not as favourable as
expected, as it was anticipated that the roof renovations would have a considerably lower embodied
impact than the Danish reference building. It was expected as the material usage, and thus the
embodied impacts of the three roof renovation cases, are much smaller than for a completely new
building. This is due to several reasons, one being the way the results are studied, which opens up
for interpretation and will, as such, be discussed further in this section, where the results will also
be compared to results from the literature.
Since it is not known how high a percentage of the energy supply will be CO2 neutral in the future,
it can be diﬃcult to compare cases against the background of the operational impacts. As seen
in Figure 3.5, the CO2 emissions have been projected until 2040, and hereafter the emissions from
energy are set to 0.06804 kg CO2-eq./kWh/year. As more knowledge is gained over time, it can
cause the projection of CO2 emissions to look diﬀerent in the future than what is predicted now.
Focusing on the embodied impacts ﬁrst and foremost and then the operational impacts is a point
to discuss.
Both the original and alternative cases of Case 2 and Case 3 have been proven to be better than the
Danish reference building regarding the initial embodied impacts. Thus, Case 2 and 3 are better
than the Danish reference building. Moreover, alternative cases for Case 2 and 3 are better than the
Danish reference building if the embodied and operational impacts account equally throughout the
entire consideration period. The best alternative in Case 1 has also been shown to have an embodied
37
==PAGE:48==
impact of 6.9 kg CO2-eq. / m2 / year versus the Danish reference buildings 7.1 kg CO2-eq. / m2 /
year. Hence Case 1 is also better than the Danish reference building if only embodied impacts are
considered. All three cases have therefore shown to have the possibility of being better than the
Danish reference building if the choice of material is considered.
One of the reasons that Case 1 has so high embodied emissions per square metre is the low amount of
new square metres compared to the amount of square metre roof that is renovated. If the embodied
emissions were divided by the square metre of both ﬂoors in the attic, the result would have been
diﬀerent. The total area of both ﬂoors would end up with 270 + 80 = 350m2. This would have
resulted in an embodied impact of 2.1 kg CO2 eq./m2/year instead of the result used through the
thesis of 9.4 kg CO2 eq./m2/year. Case 1 would, in this case end up as the case with the lowest
embodied impacts per square metre. It is thus very important to be careful when choosing the
square metre for comparison.
6.1.1
Reﬂections on change of use renovations
Renovations categorised as change of use always add an amount of new liveable square metres, as
already existing unheated square metres are changed into liveable heated square metres. The side
eﬀect of the converted room is higher energy consumption. Whether the area of the attic or the
whole building is used to calculate the operational and embodied impacts inﬂuences the result and
whether the renovation can be determined to be proﬁtable. This issue can, in particular, be seen in
the results in Section 5.2 and 5.1.
In Section 5.2, a higher energy requirement is seen when the energy frame calculation is made on
the complete building instead of the new attic apartments. As it is only the attic that is new square
metres, it is more comparable to consider just the energy requirement from the new attic if it is to be
compared with newbuild through the danish reference building. Therefore, this energy requirement
has been used in the ﬁnal comparison in Figure 5.15.
A discussion point in performing LCA’s for change of use renovation scenarios is where the limit is
reached concerning the materials included in the LCA. For instance, it could be questioned whether
gutters, drainpipes, ventilation hoods etc., should be included in the LCA which the area of the
new attic spaces is accountable for. In this LCA they were a part of the roof renovation inventory
list for Case 1. So it could be questioned, if only materials directly concerning the attic area should
be included in the resulting GWP.
When looking into the results from the LCA’s of the cases, it is clear that Case 1 has higher
embodied impacts than Case 2 and 3 even though the renovated area is transformed into smaller
extra rooms for the apartment below and not complete apartments with doors, radiators etc. like
Case 2 and 3.
When comparing Case 1 to the Danish reference building, they have almost similar embodied
impacts. As the Danish reference building is newbuild, many of the same materials as in Case 1
38
==PAGE:49==
are needed. Here Case 2 and 3 have an advantage as the impacts from the roof cladding, rafters,
gutters, drainpipes, ventilation hoods, etc., are already there and are not changed in this renovation,
so they are not considered in the comparison.
It could therefore be discussed whether it is fair that the embodied impacts from Case 2 and 3 are
compared to newbuild as very few materials are used compared to what is needed to build new.
6.1.2
Material improvements in the renovations
As shown in Section 5.1.1, considerable improvements to the embodied impacts were seen by using
alternatives to the most emitting materials used in the renovations. It has been chosen to investigate
the consequences of changing three of the most emitting materials as they have almost directly
exchangeable alternatives. In Figure 5.15, it can be seen that if the best alternative materials were
chosen particularly for Case 1, a considerable improvement can be seen. The alternative Case 1 is
25.7% better than the original case. Also, for Case 2 and 3, considerable improvements were seen
by just changing the type of insulation.
As skylights were the most emitting material/component in both Case 2 and 3, the necessity of
the amount of skylights used in the projects could be considered. There are requirements for the
amount of daylight in the apartment. However, approximately 11.5 m2 of skylights for a 60 m2
apartment could be reduced. In the Danish building code §379-381, it is said that the sum of the
glazed area should be at least 10% of the total ﬂoor area[“Bygningsreglementet”, 2018]. If the glazed
area were reduced to 10% of the total ﬂoor area, it could have reduced the glazed area by 50%.
Thus the CO2 impacts by more than 90%. Furthermore, the energy requirement would have been
lowered.
The emissions from diﬀerent types of insulation have been investigated. However, insulation with
better properties could also have been explored, where less insulation is needed and which conse-
quences it would have had to both the embodied and operational impacts. Furthermore, it could
also have been explored how to improve the amount of wood needed for formwork by improving
how the new roof is built on the old rafters.

6.2
Eﬃciency and accessibility of threshold limit values
As described in Section 2.3, two threshold limit values have been outlined in the report Klimapåvirkn-
ing fra renovering - Muligheder for udformning af graensevaerdier til LCA for renovering [Alberte
Mai Lund et al., 2022] to be able to be used with change of use renovation scenarios. The potentials
seen in this thesis by conducting the material needed for the two limit values can be seen in the
sections below.
6.2.1
Threshold limit value 1
As described in Section 2.3.1, Limit Value 1 is a benchmark for the complete building. For this
limit value, it is necessary to perform a complete LCA of the whole renovation, where all materials
must be mapped. The material mapping must include all activities within a 50 years consideration
period. It includes the existing materials, which are demolished either during the renovation or after
50 years, and the added materials. At the same time, it is essential to perform an energy frame
calculation of the building to include the heat and electricity needed in the calculation.
It is done for the three cases in Section 5.1 and Section 5.2. The found beneﬁts and issues with this
Limit Value are the following. Estimating how many years it will take before it is proﬁtable can be
diﬃcult. It may be easier to compare several diﬀerent units of the same case. It is easier to split
embodied and operational impacts and detect where the impacts lie.
6.2.2
Threshold limit value 3
As described in Section 2.3.2, Limit Value 3 is seen as a motivator for considering energy-saving
measures to reduce the overall energy consumption of a building when renovating. For this limit
value, it is necessary to perform a complete LCA of the complete renovation, where all materials
must be mapped.
For this type of requirement value, where the existing building is compared
to a renovated scenario, it can be argued that demolition of all existing materials will occur in
both scenarios and can therefore be omitted from the calculation. The mapping will, therefore,
only concern the new materials added during the renovation. In addition, when documenting the
potential energy savings of the energy renovation, two energy framework calculations are required,
one for the existing building before renovation and one for the renovated scenario. It can therefore
lead to a signiﬁcant workload in meeting this threshold.
It is done for the three cases in Section 5.3.2. The found beneﬁts and issues with this Limit Value
are the following. It can be misleading when looking at a longer period, and CO2 is only projected
until 2040, making the value static afterwards. It may be harder to estimate as it compares before
and after and is not so much a comparison of embodied and operational impacts. It is easy to divide
the emissions in year 0 from the emissions in year 50 and thus make a choice about proﬁtability.

7 | Conclusion
The outset of this thesis was to answer the following questions:
What is the potential of renovating existing available attics rather than constructing new buildings,
how can the eﬀects of embodied impacts be reduced and what is the most eﬀective method for
assessing the sustainability of the outcome?
Even though the ﬁndings were not as promising as hoped for compared to new build, they have
shown that the potential of renovating available attics is signiﬁcant and research in this thesis has
led to the following conclusions:
1. The potential of renovating existing available attics compared to building new square metre is
present but not signiﬁcant. This conclusion is based on the research carried out – speciﬁcally
it can be pointed out that;
– The research has shown in Figure 5.15 that compared to the Danish reference building,
Case 2 and 3 are compatible. Case 1 is less compatible with the Danish reference building;
however compatible if there is looked at the complete attic area and not only the new
area as discussed in Section 6.1.
– Additionally, the cases have shown to be either better or comparable to other roof reno-
vations or new build cases if they were compared to the literature ﬁndings.
– As stated in Section 5.3.2 it can be concluded that the improved operational impact pays
back the embodied impacts of all three cases. Thus, it is proﬁtable to renovate the roof
when looking holistic at the complete building.
– Lastly, alternative materials that improve the embodied impacts of the cases existed for
all the examined materials. Thus, materials should be chosen with care as it have been
shown to have a signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁnal result.
2. There is no single best method for assessing the sustainability of the outcome, rather the best
method is dependent on the desired outcome according to analysis in this thesis
This conclusion is based on the following;
– Threshold limit value 1 and 3 have shown to take up almost the same amount of work to

the threshold limit value 3. Threshold limit value 1 has shown to be easier to compare

Given the ﬁrst conclusion, it is not only commercially beneﬁcial but also desirable that developers
