Abstract
Protein production is essential nowadays in the biopharmaceutical and food industries.
There is a bottleneck in downstream processing for protein production. Chromatography
currently is the main DSP step for protein purification. To address the issues and limita­
tions of chromatography such as the high cost of resins, scale­up issues and capacity;
There is an ongoing research interest in researching alternative downstream operations.
The use of PAT in combination with hybrid models can allow for the successful operation of
these processes and their establishment as part of a QbD required in the FDA­regulated
biopharmaceutical and food industry. Therefore it is important to explore the design space
of these processes to allow better process control and the possibility of developing a digital
twin.
The goal of this research was to build a hybrid modeling framework to correlate the pro­
cess conditions to the particle length distributions or other outputs of the system.
Population balance Models were used and agglomeration and breakage were assumed
to be the main phenomena. The model was fitted for every experiment and the model
parameters were fitted using a global optimization and the objective function was the KL
divergence of the measured distribution with this simulated distribution from the population
balance model. A Feed­forward artificial neural network was trained on the optimized
parameters. The hybrid model is the combination of the population balance model and
the feed­forward artificial neural network.
A separate support vector machine classification was trained on the turbidity and the pro­
cess conditions the turbidity being the dependent variable and the process conditions
being the independent variables.
The classification model gives insight to the relation between the process conditions and
the maximum turbidity at the end of the experiment which can be an indicator of achieving
precipitation in those conditions.
By using the hybrid model and the classification model, one can implement better process
monitoring, design, and control in combination with quality­by­design methods. This can
help in designing better and more optimal process conditions and enable the implemen­
tation of precipitation as an alternative to chromatography, much faster.

Introduction
 Protein production is crucial in several industries, where proteins are used to enhance
the nutritional value, texture, and flavor of products.  For example, S. cerevisiae enzymes
are widely used in the food, beverage, and bioethanol industries.  In biopharmaceuticals,
diabetes treatment is based on the production of insulin from a reliable source such as
recombinant DNA technology which produces therapeutic proteins such as insulin and
monoclonal antibodies ([1]).
The increasing demand for pharmaceuticals and product titer will cause bottlenecks in the
downstream processing (DSP), especially steps such as purification([2], [3]). Downstream
processing is crucial for achieving regulatory compliance, meeting quality standards, and
ensuring the desired efficacy of biopharmaceuticals or any other protein products([4]).
DSP involves the steps following the cell culture or microbial fermentation, where har­
vested cells or media are processed to extract and purify the desired product. This stage
includes purification steps, such as chromatography, filtration, and viral clearance, to iso­
late and refine the product to meet quality standards for clinical or commercial use ([5]).
Currently, chromatography is the most widely used unit operation for purification of bio­
pharmaceuticals. In industrial processes, traditional chromatography methods such as
affinity chromatography (AC), ion exchange chromatography (IEX), and hydrophobic in­
teraction chromatography (HIC) are widely utilized. However, traditional chromatogra­
phy techniques are not without limitations, as they often suffer from poor mass transfer
and high back pressure[3] also Conventional chromatography methods are often time­
consuming, costly, and have limitations in scalability ([4]).
The high costs associated with chromatographic adsorbents, water consumption, and en­
vironmental impact are concerns that need to be addressed. Additionally, the complexities
arise from the diverse nature of biological products, the need for higher productivity and
titer from upstream processing, and the regulatory requirements for product safety and
quality ([4]).
An alternative to chromatography can be precipitation. The method works by altering
solvent conditions to exploit changes in the solubility of the target protein relative to other
proteins and macromolecules present in a cell extract. This change in solubility leads
to the formation of protein aggregates, which can then be separated from the mixture
through centrifugation or filtration processes ([6]).
The process is flexible in a way to either precipitate impurities while keeping the target
protein in solution or aggregate the target protein itself, depending on the specific needs
of the purification process. This flexibility, combined with the ability to use scalable equip­
ment like continuous stirred tank reactors (CSTR) and filter­based tubular reactors, makes
protein precipitation a potentially viable alternative to chromatography ([7]–[9]).
Protein precipitation is a promising alternative to chromatography but faces challenges in
selectivity, scalability, and regulatory acceptance. Ongoing research aims to overcome
these challenges through the development of selective precipitation agents and improved
process understanding using DoE.

Literature review
2.1
Overview of bio­pharmaceutical production processes:
The biopharmaceutical production process is typically divided into one upstream and three
downstream processing stages ([5].)
In the upstream processing stage, the focus is on the initial steps of production, which
involve cell culture or microbial fermentation to grow the cells or microorganisms that will
express the desired product, such as proteins or antibodies. This stage includes activities
like seeding cells, cell growth in bioreactors, and expansion of cell numbers to produce
the required amount of product ([5]).
On the other hand, downstream processing involves the steps following the cell culture or
microbial fermentation, where the harvested cells or media are processed to extract and
purify the desired product. This stage includes purification steps, such as chromatogra­
phy, filtration, and viral clearance, to isolate and refine the product to meet quality stan­
dards for clinical or commercial use ([5]).
Downstream processing is crucial for efficiently recovering and purifying biopharmaceu­
tical products, which ultimately ensures the consistency, purity, and safety of the final
product. This stage is crucial for achieving regulatory compliance, meeting quality stan­
dards, and ensuring the desired efficacy of the biopharmaceuticals ([4]). Downstream
processing is especially significant as manufacturing costs typically account for about
20% of total product sales, with downstream processing alone representing about 75% of
total manufacturing costs ([10]).
Downstream processing poses several challenges and complexities. Conventional chro­
matography methods are often time­consuming, costly, and have limitations in scalability.
The high costs associated with chromatographic adsorbents, water consumption, and en­
vironmental impact are concerns that need to be addressed. Additionally, the complexi­
ties arise from the diverse nature of biological products, the need for higher productivity,
and the regulatory requirements for patient safety and product quality ([4]). Decker and
colleagues highlight that conventional packed­bed chromatography is often considered a
bottleneck for cost and scalability in protein purification processes. Despite its versatility
and predictability, chromatography can be costly and limit productivity, making it a chal­
lenge for cost­effective and flexible manufacturing processes ([10]). These costs can be
considerable, with resin prices ranging from 5,000 to 14,000 € per liter and high buffer
consumption per batch ([11]).
The shift towards processing higher titers and volumes of biopharmaceuticals has ne­
cessitated the exploration of new technologies for process intensification. To address
the limitations of traditional chromatographic processes like protein A chromatography,
new approaches called ”Anything but Chromatography (ABC)” methodologies are gain­
ing interest ([4]). These alternative methods aim to reduce or replace chromatographic
operations in the purification pipeline. Innovative techniques such as membrane pro­
cesses, liquid­liquid phase extraction, magnetic separation, precipitation, and crystalliza­
tion are being explored as promising alternatives to traditional chromatography methods
([11], [12]).

2.2
Alternative purification unit operations:
Protein precipitation: According to Burgess, Protein precipitation is the most often used
second step in protein purification processes after cell lysis. It involves altering solvent
conditions to take advantage of the changes in solubility of the target protein relative to
other proteins and macromolecules present in a cell extract ([6]).
After cell disruption, there will be a lot of biological material such as DNA or fractures of
the host cell. There are multiple strategies to purify the product. In one scenario the target
protein is kept in the solution and the impurities are precipitated and in the other one, only
the target protein is separated by aggregating ([6]). Since there are a lot of impurities, the
second strategy is favored because the process can be tailored to a specific compound.
In both strategies, the change in solubility leads to aggregate formation which then can
be separated by centrifugation or filtration.
Removing proteases early in the protein purification process is vital for maintaining the
integrity, stability, and purity of the target protein, as well as ensuring the success of
downstream applications. Proteases can rapidly degrade proteins, affecting both yield
and quality, while also compromising stability during storage or experimental procedures.
By eliminating proteases at an early stage, the risk of degradation is minimized, enabling
the preservation of the target protein’s integrity and stability. Moreover, the removal of pro­
teases enhances the purity of the protein sample, eliminating unwanted enzymatic activi­
ties that could interfere with downstream experiments. This proactive approach facilitates
more efficient downstream processing and yields reliable results in various applications
such as enzymatic assays and structural studies. Therefore, early protease removal is
essential for optimizing protein purification workflows and maximizing the utility of purified
proteins in diverse research endeavors ([6]).
Equipment commonly used in protein precipitation includes continuous stirred tank reac­
tors (CSTR) or filter­based tubular reactors. Precipitation equipment can handle higher
throughput can chromatography columns. Volume of CSTR reactors are easily saleable
and continuous process can be done to increase the throughput([7]–[9]). There has been
an increase in popularity of precipitation as more cost­effective method alternative to chro­
matography [11], [13]. While it offers potential cost advantages, there are several chal­
lenges associated with protein precipitation that have stopped its widespread adoption:
• Selectivity and Purity: Precipitation methods often face difficulties in achieving
the high levels of selectivity and purity required for pharmaceutical products ([10],
[14]). Chromatography, although more expensive, excels in separating closely re­
lated proteins and effectively removing impurities to meet Strict regulations for phar­
maceuticals .
• Scalability and Reproducibility: Scaling up precipitation processes while main­
taining consistent results can be quite challenging ([14]). In contrast, chromatog­
raphy is a well­established technique that generally provides more predictable and
reproducible outcomes across different scales.
• Protein Stability and Activity: Proteins might denature in precipitation process
specially when using different salts for salting out methods, there might also be
concerns in the subsequent resolubilization steps ([14]). However, chromatography
typically maintains protein integrity better.
• Limited Applicability: Precipitation methods may not be applicable to all types of
proteins or impurities. On the other hand, chromatography offers more versatility
and can be adapted to a wider range of substances ([10]).

ParticleTrack (Mettler­Toledo, USA) is an instrument that employs a probe­based tech­
nique using focused beam reflectance measurements (FBRM) to measure particle distri­
bution. A laser beam is emitted through the probe tube and focused into a concentrated
spot of light at the sapphire window. The instrument’s optics rotate at a constant speed
of 2 m/s, causing the focused beam spot to rapidly scan across particles as they pass by
the window. When the concentrated beam interacts with the particle system, individual
particles or particle structures reflect the laser light back to the detector. These distinct
pulses of backscattered light are detected and counted, and the duration of each pulse is
multiplied by the scan speed to determine the chord length.
It is important to note that the instrument does not measure the particle diameter or length
directly; instead, it captures any chord length, resulting in a broader measured distribution
with fewer distinct peaks. The data from the instrument is then exported to iC FBRM
software to be used to calculate statistical information such as mean, sq wt mean and
median or other statistical information. The range that the particle tracker can be used is
from 1 µm to 1000 µm.

acterized by the random and chaotic movement of particles or molecules, which is
highly influenced by the system’s temperature. It becomes notably slower for parti­

of the bottom of the reactor. The settling velocities of particles would be different
based on the difference in their size and densities.

within the system, typically generated by impellers inside the reactor. It finds exten­
sive applications across various industrial processes. The shear and mixing created
by these velocity gradients lead to a higher frequency of collisions between parti­
cles. Additionally, it is important to note that increased shear can also enhance the

Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) consist of input layers, hidden layers, and output layers.

5.2
First principles modeling
In this study, agglomeration and breakage are considered primary phenomena governing
the process. A Population Balance Model (PBM) was developed based on these phe­
nomena to predict the system’s dynamics over time. The PBM predicts particle length
distributions for each experiment, with model parameters influencing the kinetics of pre­
cipitation. To enhance the accuracy of the model, a global optimization problem was
defined to fine­tune the variables, ensuring that the predicted particle distributions closely
align with those measured by the ParticleTrack G400.
The experimental conditions, results, and optimized model parameters are summarized in
Table 5.3. To ensure fair comparison only the experiments in which precipitation happened
in the first 24 hours were chosen.
The Differential Evolution method from the Scipy.optimize Python library was selected
for optimization. While other methods were tested, local optimization algorithms often
became trapped in local minima, failing to adequately explore the search space. The
Differential Evolution method, although computationally intensive, running for nearly 7
hours, proved more effective.
Interestingly, the optimization returned similar model parameters for most experiments.
The optimization parameters were selected to balance computational efficiency with the
need for a thorough search, employing a high population size (popsize) and a relatively
low maximum number of iterations.

Discussion
It is assumed that protein concentration, salt concentration, temperature and rotation
speed have an effect on the kinetics of the system. This can be seen by the change
in the measured Paricle size distributions on changing these conditions.
The experiment data from Victoria Saad, although very rich in detail was very diverse
and there were a lot of the design space unexplored, several of her experiments were
repetitions and in some, she changed the process conditions in the experiments. To allow
for a PBM to be fitted to the data, some conditions need to be kept constant such as the
process inputs which are protein concentration, salt concentration, rotation speed, and
temperature.
To explore the design space with a more organized method and also keep the experiments
that satisfy the criteria. Only 6 experiments from 20230328 to 20230608 were used in this
study.
This meant that new experiments had to be performed by conducting a main effect DoE
with variables of temperature, protein concentration, salt concentration, and rotation speed.
These new experiments were carried out by Zeina Aref Abdul Hamid during her research
stay at BTEC. Only the experiments under 24 hours from Victoria were chosen to keep
in harmony with the new experiments as well since the classification model’s output is
based on the turbidity at the end of the experiment and It is impossible to know what will
happen after 24 hours with the new experiments. Only the experiments’ data under 24
hours were used from Victoria’s experiments data.

Hybrid­Modelling discussion
The experiments that lead to precipitation were used to fit a PBM and tune the PBM
parameters based on the objective function being Kl divergence between predicted and
measured particle distribution as the objective function over time.
It was noted that the ParticleTrack although a very powerful tool that can give measure­
ments for the particle counts over time, often has a lot of error in its accuracy at the be­
ginning of the experiment where there can be impurities or even bubbles that can cause
it to give incorrect measurements.
This can be seen by looking at all the plots where the measured mean chord length starts
from very high numbers, meaning that the ParticleTrack probe is sensitive in the beginning
of the experiment.
Victoria has performed some experiments having a solution of predetermined particles
with a specific size and then measuring the particles with ParticleTrack probe, the mean
chord length for particles of size 40nm was measured 11.59µm, for 4.6µm was measured
21.6µm, for 21µm was measured 85.56µm and for 99.1µm was measured 73.28µm ([39]).
This further approves that the ParticleTrack has a very inaccurate measurement in the
beginning of the experiment where there are no particles to be measured or they are in
nanoscale.
It is also important that the ParticleTrack probe can only see particles between 1 mi­
cron to 1000 microns meaning that particles smaller than 1 micron are in the reactor but
the ParticleTrack cannot measure the nanoscale particles. However, PBM starts from 2
nanometers particles all the way to 1 centimeter particles.
Therefore, It is only fair and logical to fit the model where we have measurements. In most
of the experiments, there will be particles detected after 2 hours of starting the experiment.

Therefore the objective function is calculated for the measurements after the 2 hours to
limit the errors caused by bubbles and high sensitivity.
The results of the PBM optimization seem to indicate that the optimization algorithm is
struggling to converge to a unique solution or close to a unique solution in the design
space for every experiment. Further improvements can be made by implementing the
ode system in other Python libraries to improve the speed of the optimization algorithm.
There can be many reasons, a more complex model can have better predictions there­
fore a lower convergence time and as well lower objective function. The current objective
function which is Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence, has the benefit of calculating the sim­
ilarity of two distributions and the closer it is to zero the more similar. The benefit of an
Objective function like KL divergence over calculating mean chord length over time from
the measurements and the simulation is that comparing the distribution directly is much
more accurate than calculating secondary statistical information because two different
distributions can have the same mean chord length. Further improvements can be made
by implementing the ode system in other Python libraries to improve the speed of the
optimization algorithm.
Another reason can be that Since the ParticleTrack probe has limitation to accurately
predict particle counts for every particle size and it might be better to fit the model in a
range of particle sizes where the measurements are most accurate. This is reinforced
by the initial experiments by Victoria where she studies the limitation of the ParticleTrack
probe and the fact that ParticleTrack broadens the distributions by measuring the chord
lengths rather than the particle diameter.

Conclusion
In this study it was assumed that the precipitation process is governed by two main phe­
nomena, agglomeration and breakage. This assumption can help to model the process
using the relevant birth and death terms for PBM that can describe the system. The PBMs
were fitted to the selected experiments with an optimization algorithm. The optimization
inputs were the models parameters and the objective function was the KL divergence be­
tween the measured particle distribution over time and the simulated particle distribution
over time. Optimizing the model parameters takes a long time (8 hours) and even the
proposed estimation for the model parameters are simplifications.
One of the goals of the thesis was to develop a hybrid­Model framework to combine the
strengths of both first­principle models and data­driven models. It is assumed that the
process conditions such as protein concentration, salt concentration, rotation speed and
temperature have an effect on the kinetics of the system. A FFANN was trained on the op­
timized model parameters, and was able to predict all of them with a reasonable accuracy
while exhibiting a bit of overfitting. It can be seen from the results of the optimizations and
the predictions of the data­driven model that the kinetics are correlated with the process
conditions.
The ParticleTrack probe although very versatile, has different accuracy for different parti­
cle lengths as can be seen with prior work done by Victoria Saad. A future outlook might
be determining the best and most accurate range of particle lengths to improve the con­
vergence of the optimization algorithm.
A hybrid model framework between the population balance model and FFANN that pre­
dicts the model parameters from process conditions have been made. The framework
can be expanded with new experiments until where it can be effectively used to predict
the output of the experiment just by the process conditions with a good accuracy and
regularization.
