Abstract
Concrete connections in structures have long been subject to extended testing, as the
load bearing capacity can be tricky to estimate. In this thesis, the reinforcement design
of a beam-column connection is tested through rigid plastic modeling and compared to
experiments carried out for similar beam-column connections.The reinforcement designs
tested include a L-bar layout and a U-bar layout.
The rigid plastic modeling is a numerical limit state analysis which can be used to ﬁnd
the ultimate load bearing capacity of a given structure and thereby the stress distribution
at the point of collapse. An interior point optimizer is provided by Mosek to solve a
second-order cone programming problem. In order to carry out the optimizations, a 2D
disk element and beam element is developed based on elements proposed by Poulsen
and Damkilde in 2000. Stress distributions are analyzed through plotting of principal
stresses, and dual solution variables are used to estimate a collapse mechanism.
In addition to the comparison with experimental results, the numerical limit state anal-
ysis presented in this thesis is further validated by hand calculations including a lower
bound solution assuming homogeneous stress ﬁelds and an upper bound solution utiliz-
ing yield line theory.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this report beam-column connections exposed to a closing moment are investigated
by means of rigid plastic modeling. The beam-column connections, or frame corners,
are made of concrete and steel reinforcement. In order to investigate the frame corners
thoroughly a 2D Finite Element (FEM) tool is developed to evaluate the feasibility of
various reinforcement layouts. Since the calculations are all rigid plastic optimizations,
the results will only contain the failure mode of the frame corner. The frame corner load
cases investigated in the report can be seen in Figure 1.1. The focus is on a simple load
case of constant moment and the more complex load case of normal force, shear and a
varying bending moment.

in order to verify the validity of the FEM tool. Furthermore, results are compared to
experiments carried out by BSc students working on similar projects.

1.1
Relevance
Connections are always of high importance in reinforced concrete structures. The con-
nections are often critical for the load bearing capacity and as such require a lot of
detailing and veriﬁcation.
Beam-column connections in concrete have been tested a lot for corners aﬀected by open-
ing moments, which is seen as the most critical load case (Campana et al. 2013)[1]. The
closing corner, or a beam-column connection aﬀected by a closing moment, is the scope
of this report inspired by the impending high cost, international scale project Fehmarn
Belt Fixed Link. The immersed tunnel will be 18,2km long, and the pre-production
process has been covered extensively by the national media [2]. A tunnel is a great
example of a frame corner exposed to a closing moment, where a lot of reinforcement is
needed. If a tunnel spanning several kilometers is to be build, a lot of time and money
can be saved if an eﬀective reinforcement design solution is chosen. The aim is to cre-
ate a ﬁnite element model able to test diﬀerent reinforcement designs by assessing the
ultimate capacity of the frame corner.
Closing corners have been investigated for various designs by Johansson (2001)[3]. A
reinforcement design of U-bars in column and beam showed promising results, and this
design is looked further into in this thesis.
The corner angle between column and beam has been investigated thoroughly by nu-
merous authors, see (Abdul-Wahab and Salman, 1999)[4]. The focus in this project is
on the reinforcement design in a 90 degree corner.
1.2
Developing A FEM-Tool
Instead of using commercially available software, the development of a Finite Element
tool serves a number of purposes. Firstly the disk elements used are very speciﬁc and it
is important to know that the elements have the unique properties that we need. This
includes embedded reinforcement in order to give the elements extra tensile strength and
a yield surface composed of two opposite rotated cones that deﬁnes the shear strength of
the disk elements as well as the compressive- and tensile strengths. Developing the ele-
ments for concrete (disks) and concentrated reinforcement (beams) gives the advantage
of controlling how they interact.

1.2.1
Concrete Plasticity
Plastic behavior of reinforced concrete has been used by engineers for decades. A large
contributor is Danish Ingerslev who developed the yield line theory for reinforced con-
crete plates in the 1920s.
The upper bound method has always served as practical
manual approach, but the solution is an upper bound solution, making the load unsafe.
The research of rigid plastic disks did not get as much attention as slabs early on. In
1984 M.P. Nielsen presents yield conditions and analytical solutions for reinforced con-
crete disks (M.P. Nielsen, 1984)[5]. These yield conditions are used for the disk elements
in this thesis.
1.2.2
Linear and Conic Programming
The disk and beam elements used in the thesis are based on elements presented in an
article by Poulsen and Damkilde (2000)[6]. The article presents a linear programming
problem, with a linearisation of the conic yield surface for the reinforced concrete disk
elements. In this thesis second order cone programming (SOCP) with an interior point
optimizer provided by Mosek [7] is used to reduce calculation time and to utilize the
yield surface (Herfelt, 2013)[8].
1.2.3
Assumptions
A series of assumptions have been made in order to simplify the ﬁnite element model.
First of all a 3D problem is modeled in 2D. This brings up some complications that one
must be aware of when setting up the model as some cases in 3D might not translate
well into 2D. The behavior of the materials have been simpliﬁed in order for it to be
incorporated into the model. This includes the assumptions of rigid plasticity as well
as simpliﬁed yield surfaces for the given materials. The rigid plastic elements limit the
analysis to show the results at the ultimate capacity of the model. There is no results of
how the model is acting in the elastic zone, and the ductility of the specimen can not be
determined. The serviceability limit state is therefore not considered, only the ultimate
load bearing capacity.

One must also be aware, that by using the ﬁnite element method some error is to be
expected. This is however not a problem as long as the error does not exceed a tolerance
set by the user.

the load should converge towards an accurate load bearing capacity. The time it takes
to carry out a calculation increases with the number of elements in the model, so it is
of interest to ﬁnd an element size that provides results within an acceptable tolerance

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this chapter the results obtained and the work carried out so far will be discussed.
Further development of the FEM-tool is also proposed.
7.1
FEM-tool vs. Experiments and Hand Calculations
In Figure 5.2 and 5.3 and Table 5.1, the results from the numerical analysis can be seen
in addition to the experimental results. In the load case with pure bending moment, the
results of the numerical model line up almost perfectly with the experimental results for
all reinforcement layouts except for the Ø20 L-bar reinforcement where the numerical
model over estimates the load bearing capacity.
The results from the numerical model of the MVN-setup deviates more from the exper-
imental results. The numerical and experimental results align neatly for the Ø12 L-bar
and Ø12 U-bar reinforcement as well as the Ø16 U-bar reinforcement. This is however
not the case for the rest of the results. The numerical results for the Ø20 U-bar rein-
forcement deviate ∼25% from the experimental results. For the Ø16 L-bar and the Ø20
U-bar, however, the numerical results are ∼100% larger than the experimental results.
At ﬁrst sight the diﬀerence looks like a factor 2 error (See Table 5.1 for exact values.)
but looking at the development in strength in relation to the reinforcement diameter
suggests that the experimental results may be ﬂawed in one way or another, rendering
the comparison between these numerical and experimental results invalid.
83
==PAGE:98==
Chapter 7 - Discussion
84
Both lower bound and upper bound hand calculations have also been carried to evaluate
the results of the L-bar reinforcement for both the M- and the MVN load cases. For the
M load case, the lower- and upper bound solutions are both a little above the numerical
result. This deviation is probably caused by the reﬁnement of the mesh in the numerical
model. For the MVN load case, the numerical result lies in between the lower bound
solution and the upper bound solution, which is expected. A more exact solution is
deﬁnitely achievable if more parameters are varied.
7.2
Reinforcement Layouts
For the Ø12 reinforcement bars and the Ø16 reinforcement bars, the results for the U-
bar layout is slightly higher than the L-bar layout results in both the M and MVN load
cases. for the Ø20 reinforcement bars, however, the L-bar layout is much stronger than
the U-bar layout. This decreasing slope for the U-bar layout is expected to be due to
the increased bend radius at the corners and the bond strength between the concrete
and steel limiting the amount of force that can be transferred between the to U-bars.
The FEM-tool can not predict the deﬂection or ductility of the frame corners prior to
collapse, so nothing can be said of the serviceability of the frame corners.
The L-bar reinforcement layout is the most viable for bars with increasing diameters
making it the best reinforcement layout overall.
The U-bar reinforcement layout is,
however, a bit stronger than the L-bar at low reinforcement diameters.
7.3
Sources of Error
Whilst numerical modeling can save a lot of time and money, there are many parameters
which must be tweaked in order to obtain reliable results, all of which may be possible
sources of error. In addition to all the parameters, a series of assumptions are made,
that must be taken into account when evaluating the results. Some of the main sources
of error are listed below.
==PAGE:99==
Chapter 7 - Discussion
85
Material Properties
If the material properties used in the numerical model does not match the properties of
the actual component that is being simulated, the results obtained will, of course, be oﬀ.
One must therefore be absolutely certain that the properties speciﬁed in the model is
either correct or at least conservative. In the numerical analysis no eﬀectiveness factor is
used when handling the compressive strength of the concrete. This conservative safety
factor considers cracking of the concrete reducing the actual strength, which is besides
the point of rigid plastic modeling.
Properties such as the yield strength, fy, of the reinforcement steel and the compressive
strength of the concrete, fc, has been determined through cylinder tests for the concrete
and tensile strength tests for rods of reinforcement steel. The tensile strength of the
concrete is set to zero. This is done to simplify the model whilst still being a conservative
assumption.
Whether the correct bond strength between the concrete and the reinforcement steel has
been used in the model, is questionable at best, since no experiment has been carried
out to determine the bond strength. According to the national annex (DS/EN 1992-1-1)
[16] the bond strength can be set to fbd = 2.25 · fctd assuming ’good conditions’.
Since the tensile strength of the concrete in the model is set to 0 MPa, the tensile
strength used to ﬁnd the bond strength is based on a qualiﬁed guess. 4 MPa is used for
fctd, which is well below 10% of the compressive strength.
Modeling in 2D

aware of. First of all, only stresses in the plane is considered.
Furthermore, the reinforcement bars in the model does not interact with the concrete
as actual steel rods would when cast into concrete. The diﬀerence between the model
and real reinforcement can be seen in Figure 7.1

In the numerical model, the beam-elements interact with the concrete as if it was smeared

7.4
Versatility of the FEM-tool
In its current state, the FEM-tool is fully capable of simulating the rigid plastic behavior
of concrete disks of any shape, size and boundary conditions. The option to add grid
reinforcement as part of the material properties further increases the possibilities. Due
to the speciﬁed yield surface of the disk elements, the tool is in its current state, only
able to model concrete disks.
7.5
Future Work
Despite having established a functional model, many steps can be taken in order to
improve on the numerical analysis and investigate other aspects of the beam-column
connection. A few of the possible options for further work may include expanding the
FEM-tool to support 3D-modeling, looking at diﬀerent reinforcement layouts as well as
new load cases and varying geometry of the beam-column connection.
Modeling in 3D
In order to get a better idea of the stress distribution within the frame corner the
model could be expanded to three dimensions. This would render a lot of assumptions
regarding the reinforcement layout obsolete. First and foremost, the reinforcement could
be modeled as bars rather than a ’band’ passing through the model. additionally, the
U-bars could be modeled next to each other instead of having to pass through each
other.
Expanding the FEM-tool into 3D would require a lot of work, as new 3-dimensional
elements are required along with new yield criteria taking into account the new possible
stress states.
Reinforcement layout
In addition to expanding the model into three dimensions it would also be interesting
to look at other potential layouts for the reinforcement and other load cases such as the
ones proposed by Campana et al. (2013) [1], where the frame corner is subjected to an
==PAGE:102==
Chapter 7 - Discussion
88
opening bending moment as opposed to a closing bending moment. Bend radius and
bar diameter are other parameters worth researching further.
7.6
Cost/Time Evaluation
When deciding on what reinforcement layout to use, the load bearing capacity is not
the only concern. The manufacturing cost and the time it takes to produce is of high
importance as well as the safety of the personnel during construction. The following
pros and cons apply to the two layouts.
L-bar
• Less steel is used, reducing the material cost.
• The reinforcement bars must ﬁrst be cast into the column, then bent as desired
where-after the rest of the connection is cast. This takes time during construction
and the pointy end of the reinforcement bar poses a potential security hazard.
U-bar
• More steel is used increasing the material cost.
• The reinforcement bars can be bend as desired prior to the casting of the concrete.
which potentially reduces manufacturing cost and time.
The security problem
mentioned above is also averted.
• The amount of force that can be transfered from U/bar to U-bar is limited by the
concrete between the bars.
Choosing the best solution is not always simple and depends heavily on the speciﬁc
project at hand.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
During this project a FEM-tool has been developed in order to perform rigid plastic
modeling on reinforced concrete beam-column connections. The ﬁnite element tool can
reproduce stress states and collapse mechanisms of real test specimens by numerical
analysis and thereby assess the viability of diﬀerent reinforcement layouts. It is possi-
ble to develop the ﬁnite element program further, incorporating other load cases and
reinforcement designs and moving towards 3D modeling.
In order to develop the FEM-tool a disk element and a bar/beam element proposed by
Poulsen and Damkilde (2000) were modiﬁed to suit the requirements of the problem
at hand. The developed elements were tested thoroughly by comparing test models to
known solutions and thereby ensuring that they work as intended. The optimization
problems have been solved using Mosek’s interior point optimizer for second-order cone
programming. The optimization is a lower bound solution that relies on iterations to
arrive at an optimal stress distribution. The dual solution to the primary conic program-
ming problem has been used as an upper bound solution, and by transformation the dual
variables have been interpreted as deformations showing the collapse mechanism.
Two reinforcement layouts were tested under both M and MVN load cases at varying
reinforcement diameters: L-bar layout and U-bar layout with Ø12, Ø16 and Ø20 rein-
forcement bars. It was found that the U-bar layout performed slightly better at both
Ø12 and Ø16 in both M and MVN, while the L-bar layout performed vastly better at
Ø20.
These results were also compared to experiments carried out on similar beam-column
89
==PAGE:104==
Chapter 8 - Conclusion
90
connections where a clear correlation between the numerical model and the actual ex-
periments was evident for most load cases. Lower and upper bound method hand cal-
culations supported the numerical model as well.
The two investigated reinforcement designs both have pros and cons worth consider-
ing, and the choice of design is heavily dependant on the project at hand. The L-bar
design seems to utilize the steel better, especially for increasing bar diameters. The
U-bar design could have some advantages during construction, in regards to safety and
construction time.
