Abstract
Inconsistencies in mitochondrial genomes involving different rotations and orientations make
these circular DNA sequences unsuitable for sequence alignment. MARS, the current state-of-
the-art method for improving multiple circular sequence alignment, cannot handle reverse
complemented sequences and has long runtimes.
A new method using an entirely different approach is designed and implemented in this study.
The algorithm constructs a genome variation graph from the circular mitochondrial DNA se-
quences that are to be aligned, finds an optimal cutting point, and performs rotation and orien-
tation of the genomes accordingly. Whether the subsequent alignments are improved or not is
evaluated by comparing global alignment scores.
On datasets containing taxonomically similar mitochondrial sequences, this new variation graph
approach was found to perform just as well as MARS, additionally being able to handle reverse
sequenceorientations. Improvingthealignmentofsequencescontainingreversecomplemented
regions was not successful. On very divergent datasets, the sequence-to-graph alignment fails
during the graph construction because of issues with the vg toolkit. Runtime-wise, the new
approach is faster on small datasets, but slower when larger input files are used. Even though
the algorithm is not optimal, this variation graph approach has a lot of potential, being able to
handle both cyclic shifts and different sequence orientations.

1
Introduction
1.1
Project Motivation
Analysing multiple sequences simultaneously is a fundamental task in biological research. Align-
ments are used to infer homology and study the evolutionary relationships between the se-
quences at either protein or DNA level.
Circular DNA molecules are present, in abundance, in all domains of life. They include mitochon-
drial genomes in eukaryotes, chloroplast chromosomes, bacterial plasmids, extrachromosomal
circular DNA (eccDNA), and also some viral genomes. Multiple sequence alignment of these
circular molecular structures has proven to be a challenge when comparing genomes across
different taxonomies.
Most multiple sequence alignment tools are not aware of the input sequences’ directionality.
They also consider DNA sequences to be contiguous and therefore take into account both ends.
In circular DNA, different standards of linearisation make these end-positions inconsistent. Fur-
thermore, in some cases of genome assembly, the orientation (i.e. the DNA strand) of assembled
DNA sequences is unknown. Sequences with different orientations and rotations (cyclic shifts)
are not suitable for multiple sequence alignment.
This project is restricted to animal mitochondrial DNA. Differences between mitochondrial
DNA molecules of different populations of the same species, as well as mitochondrial DNA of
different species of the same genus, are being extensively studied to gain information regarding
evolutionary histories of a wide variety of organisms and also population structure and genomics.
Moreover, in the field of ancient DNA, mitochondrial DNA is widely used because of its haploid
structure and relative abundance in ancient and degraded samples in comparison with nuclear
DNA. Furthermore, mutations occur at a much higher rate in mitochondrial DNA than in nuclear
DNA due to the lack of repair mechanisms.
The idea for this project originated from Gabriel Renaud and Nicola Vogel from the Modern
and Ancient Genomes group in the Section for Bioinformatics, who are both supervisors of this
thesis. During their research, they discovered that mitochondrial sequences across different
taxonomies do not necessarily start in the same genetic region, making the task of multiple
sequence alignment challenging. The state of the art program, which improves multiple circular
sequence alignments by shifting the sequences, left much to be desired.
The goal of this project is to address the issue of multiple sequence alignment of mitochondrial
DNA and implement a method that will use reverse complementation and cyclic shifts to obtain
refined circular sequences, which will improve the subsequent alignment. The strategy for
rotating and orienting mitochondrial genomes is to take advantage of the growing usage of
genome variation graphs and specifically the sequence-to-graph alignment methods.
1.2
Mitochondria
Mitochondria are membrane-enclosed organelles of eukaryotic cells, whose main function is to
produce energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP). Figure 1.1 illustrates the structure
of a mitochondrion. It has two membranes; an outer membrane that is freely permeable to
ions and small molecules, and an inner membrane, which is selectively permeable. The inner
membrane has convolutions called cristae, which increase the surface area. The intermembrane

space (IMS) is an aqueous sub-compartment containing numerous nuclear-encoded proteins
imported from the cytosol. Because of the free diffusion through porins in the outer membrane,
the IMS is chemically equivalent to the cytosol.
Enclosed by the inner membrane is the mitochondrial matrix; a viscous sub-compartment,
which contains the enzymes responsible for fuel-oxidation pathways such as the citric acid
cycle, the fatty acid β-oxidation, and the amino acid oxidation [1]. Mitochondria have their own
genome, the mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), which is also located in the mitochondrial matrix
together with mitochondrial ribosomes (mitoribosomes) and RNA granules. Both mitoribosomes
and RNA granules associate with the inner mitochondrial membrane. The mitochondrial RNA
granules are fluid-compartments comprised of newly synthesised RNA, RNA-binding proteins
and mitoribosome assembly factors. They are able to rapidly exchange content and undergo
fusion, and via membrane association they regulate positioning of these components along
the mitochondrial network [2]. It has also been suggested that RNA granules play a role in
post-transcriptional and epigenetic gene expression [3].
Mitoribosomes synthesise proteins encoded in the mtDNA. Just like bacterial and eukaryotic
cytosolic ribosomes, mitoribosomes are composed of two subunits, however, they differ in
structure and composition [4]. The sedimentation value of the mammalian mitoribosome is 55S
(28S small and 39S large subunits).
Figure 1.1: Anatomy of a mitochondrion. Figure adapted from https://commons.wikime-
dia.org/wiki/File:Animal_mitochondrion_diagram_en.svg.
There exists a great variation in mitochondrial size, shape, and degree of inner membrane convo-
lution across different taxonomies. Moreover, tissues with a high demand for aerobic respiration
such as brain tissue and skeletal and heart muscle contain many hundreds to thousands of
mitochondria per cell, each with more cristae. [1]. Tissues with less metabolic activity, on the
other hand, have fewer mitochondria per cell with less convolutions of the inner membrane.
1.2.1
Oxidative phosphorylation
The metabolic pathway, in which the energy of nutrient oxidation drives the synthesis of ATP,
is called oxidative phosphorylation. Electrons flowing through a chain of membrane-bound
carriers result in the pumping of protons across the inner membrane, making the mitochon-
drial matrix alkaline relative to the intermembrane space [1]. The consequent proton gradient

provides energy for the synthesis of ATP from ADP and Pi by the enzyme ATP synthase.
All components of the electron transport chain are embedded in the mitochondrial inner mem-
brane. There are four unique electron carriers, organised into a membrane-embedded complex.
Figure 1.2 shows the flow of electrons and protons through the four complexes as well as the
final ATP synthesis. Complexes I through IV catalyse electron transfers between the donors
NADH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide hydrogen) and succinate, the intermediate carriers
ubiquinone (Q) and cytochrome c, and O2. Complex I, also called NADH:ubiquinone oxidore-
ductase, is composed of 43 polypeptide chains and catalyses the electron flow from NADH to Q.
It is also a proton pump, since protons are transferred from the matrix to the intermembrane
space. Complex II is called succinate dehydrogenase and catalyses electron transfers from succi-
nate to Q. It is the smallest complex of the oxidative phosphorylation system, containing only
four structural subunits [5], where two of them extend into the mitochondrial matrix and the
rest are membrane-embedded. Q is reduced to QH2 (ubiquinol) and complex III couples the
electron transfer from QH2 to cytochrome c, simultaneously transporting protons across the
inner mitochondrial membrane. Protons are released when QH2 is oxidised to Q, and QH2 do-
nates electrons to cytochrome c and b. Cytochrome c is a soluble protein of the intermembrane
space [1], whereas cytochrome b is part of enzyme complex III called ubiquinone:cytochrome
c oxidoreductase. The last complex, cytochrome oxidase, carries electrons from cytochrome
c to O2, reducing it to H2O. The energy of this redox reaction is used to pump protons into the
intermembrane space.
Figure1.2: Oxidativephosphorylationinsideamitochondria. Electrons(bluearrows)andprotons
(pink arrows) flow through the four enzyme complexes and ATP is produced in the final step.
Figure from [1].
The energy of the described electron transfer is conserved in a proton gradient across the inner
mitochondrial membrane (see Figure 1.2). This chemical and electrical gradient is transformed
into ATP by a rotational catalysis performed by ATP synthase (complex V). The mitochondrial
ATP synthase complex is an assembly of 29 proteins [6]. Two of them, F0 and F1, are functional
domains. The flow of protons back into the matrix through F0 causes the three active sites of F1
take turns catalysing the ATP synthesis from ADP and Pi [1]. The newly synthesised ATP is then
released from the enzyme surface.

1.2.2
The mitochondrial genome
In a typical mammalian cell, each mitochondrion contains around five identical copies of the
mitogenome [1]. The human mtDNA is a double-stranded, circular molecule of 16,569 base pairs
(bp) and contains 37 genes coding for two rRNAs, 22 transfer RNAs (tRNAs) and 13 subunits of
enzyme complexes of the respiratory chain [7]. The two DNA strands are distinguished as heavy
(H) and light (L) based on their G+T base composition. The H strand contains two rRNAs, 14
tRNAs, and 12 polypeptides, whereas the L strand codes for eight tRNAs and a single polypeptide
[7]. Table 1.1 shows an overview of the respiratory proteins (complex I - V), the total number of
subunits, and how many of them are mtDNA-encoded.

Human mitochondria contain around 1,500 proteins [1]. Most of them are encoded by nuclear
genes and translated in the cytoplasm. They contain targeting signals that direct them to
mitochondrial surface receptors and further into the proper mitochondrial sub-compartment.
The 13 proteins of the respiratory chain enzyme complexes encoded in the mtDNA are mostly
hydrophobic and are co-translationally inserted into the inner mitochondrial membrane [8]. It
has been proposed that the extreme hydrophobicity of some of these mtDNA-encoded proteins
can explain the presence of DNA in mitochondria; their import from the cytosol to the inner
mitochondrial membrane would be very inefficient [8].
Figure 1.3 illustrates a map of the human mtDNA. The genes are closely packed and lack introns,
and the intergenetic regions are either absent or very short [7]. The seven subunits of complex I
are marked in pink and are known as ND1, ND2, ND3, ND4, ND4L, ND5, and ND6. Cytochrome b,
which is coloured in blue, is the only subunit of complex III encoded in the mtDNA. The three
subunits of cytochrome oxidase (complex IV) contained in the mitogenome are named COI-III
and are marked in green. Finally, subunits 6 and 8 of the ATPase synthase complex (ATPase6
and ATPase8) are shown in purple. These two subunits are both part of the F0 transmembrane
proton pore.
Within the mitogenome, there are a few gene overlaps. Some overlaps are between the 3’ ends
of two genes encoded in the H and L strands, respectively. Two overlaps; however, occur on the
same strand (H). There is a 46 bp gene overlap between ATPase6 and ATPase8, and the ND4L
gene overlaps the ND4 gene by 7 bp [9].

The mtDNA contains a large non-coding region (NCR), marked in gray in Figure 1.3. It is 1,124 bp
in size (positions 16024-576) [10] and is designated the control region, because it contains the
replication origin of the H-strand and also essential sequences for the initiation of transcription
[9]. The replication origin of the L-strand is located in a non-coding sequence between tRNA
genes C and N (see Figure 1.3). Transcription is initiated from the H-strand promoter (HSP) and
L-strand promoter (LSP), respectively [11], which are situated within 150 bp of one another [7].
Several metazoan species contain directly repeated sequences in the mtDNA control regions.
The NCR is the most variable in mtDNA, especially at the three hypervariable regions; HVS1 at
positions 16024–16383, HVS2 at positions 57–372, and HVS3 at positions 438–574 [11]. HVS1
and HVS2 overlap the displacement loop (D-loop), which is a stable, triple-stranded region of
approximately 650 bp. The D-loop is formed by incorporation of a third, short DNA strand known
as 7S DNA, because of its sedimentation value. Figure 1.4 shows the structure of the human
mitochondrial NCR and D-loop.
In animal species, where D-loops have been detected, not all mtDNAs maintain one. [11]. To
this day, the reason why mitochondria synthesise, maintain, and turn over this structure at a
considerable energetic expense, is largely unknown.
The mitogenome in Figure 1.3 also contains rRNA and tRNA, which are unusually small in
mitochondria [9]. The 16s and 23s rRNA encode components that are located in the large and
small mitoribosome subunit, respectively. Metazoan mtDNA uses an altered genetic code, which
makes the 22 tRNAs encoded in the mitogenome sufficient to translate all 13 mitochondrial
protein genes [7]. The mitochondrial tRNA specificity is indicated by their corresponding one-
letter amino acid code.

In most eukaryotes, paternal mtDNA is eliminated by diverse mechanisms, and the mitogenome
is transmitted exclusively through the female germ line [7]. Over time, the maternally inher-
ited mtDNA molecules accumulate substitutions, small deletions/insertions, and sequence
rearrangements. Mutations in mitochondrial genes can cause a variety of diseases that mostly
affect muscle, heart and the brain [1]. Examples of such diseases are Leber’s hereditary optic
neuropathy (LHON) and myoclonic epilepsy and ragged-red fiber disease (MERRF). Arrows in
Figure 1.3 show the positions of mutations causing LHON and MERRF.
1.2.3
Comparative mitogenome analyses
Analysing and comparing complete mitogenomes of different species can provide insights about
phylogenetic relationships as well as mitogenome features and differentiation. The absence
of recombination and the high mutation rate in mtDNA enable a direct inference of the phy-
logenetic evolution with a high discriminative power. A search in the NCBI (National Center
for Biotechnology Information) nucleotide database reveals 111,077 complete mitochondrial
sequences from various taxonomies.
MitochondrialDNAdiffersdrasticallywhencomparinganimals, fungi, protistsandplants; itmight
be even more variable than nuclear genomes [12]. Among bilateral metazoans (multicellular
animals), however, the structure and gene organisation of mtDNA is highly conserved [9]. In
general, the animal mitochondrial genome is approximately 16.6 kb (kilobases), but can range
in size from 14.5 to 19.5 kb. Both gene content and, to some extent, gene order are conserved
[13]. The greatest size variations among metazoan mtDNAs result from differences in the length
of the control region; however, gene lengths do also vary [9].
Mitochondrial sequencing of more species has later revealed even more variation in size, shape,
gene content and genetic code of mtDNA. These noticeable exceptions to the description above
were, among others, unusual mtDNA organisations, changes in gene content, and transient linear
mitogenomes. It has been shown that mitogenomes range from just over 11 kb in chaetognaths
(marine worms) to over 50 kb in the ark shell Scapharca broughtonii [13]. All these significant
exceptions exist in non-bilaterian animals, which comprise organisms in the phyla Porifera,
Cnidaria, Ctenophora and Placozoa.
1.2.4
Mitochondrial DNA sequencing, assembly and annotation
MtDNA is abundant, relatively small, and easy to isolate [7], and has therefore been sequenced
since the beginning of the DNA sequencing era. Moreover, its haploid and monoclonal nature
simplifies the interpretation of the sequencing results.

The first DNA sequencing technology, Sanger sequencing, was presented in 1977 and is now
known as first generation sequencing. This method can produce long reads (up to 1200 nu-
cleotides) [14] and consists of 1) amplification by PCR (polymerase chain reaction) with fluo-
rescent chain-terminating modified dideoxyribonucleotides, 2) size separation by capillary gel
electrophoresis, and finally 3) gel analysis by laser excitation and detection, which outputs a
chromatogram.
EventhoughSangersequencingisstillwidelyusedformitogenomesequencing, high-throughput
next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies have revolutionised mitogenomics. They were
introduced in the past decade and allow for massively parallel sequencing reactions. The four
main DNA sequencing methods used by the different NGS systems are pyrosequencing, sequenc-
ing by synthesis, sequencing by ligation, and ion semiconductor sequencing. The shift from
Sanger sequenced long reads to these short read NGS technologies is now trending back towards
developing methods that can generate longer read lengths, while maintaining the massively
parallel nature [15].
Nowadays, the most popular technologies for high-throughput sequencing of complete mito-
chondrial genomes are the Illumina MiSeq platform, the PacBio Sequel System, and the Oxford
Nanopore technology. The Illumina MiSeq system utilises the sequencing by synthesis method-
ology and produces short reads (maximum 300 bp). The Single Molecule Real Time (SMRT)
sequencing technology developed by Pacific Biosciences (PacBio) and the Oxford Nanopore
method are both long-read sequencing (LRS) technologies. The development of these methods
has revolutionised small genome sequencing, because they are able to capture the majority or
even the whole mtDNA sequence in a single read [16]. PacBio SMRT sequencing can produce
fragments up to 30–50 kb, and the Oxford Nanopore can theoretically detect hundreds of kb of
DNA passing through [15].
When the DNA sequencing is completed, there are two methods for NGS data assembly; refer-
ence mapping and de novo assembly. Reference mapping is a comparative approach in which
reads are aligned to a reference genome from the same or a closely related organism using a
mapping algorithm. De novo assembly requires no reference and is used when the genome to be
reconstructed is not similar to previously sequenced genomes [17]. Reads are examined against
each other and checked for overlaps. With the new LRS technologies, de novo assembly of small
genomes becomes a much simpler task.
Assembled mitogenomes need to be annotated before being uploaded to a public database.
However, as it turns out, different sequencing, assembly and annotation methods produce
different mitogenome sequences [18]. This sensitive dependence on the sequencing and assem-
bly/annotation methods affects the mitogenome completeness and sequence accuracy, and no
guidelines have yet been established for verification and validation of mitogenome sequences.
Further to the above, when it comes to numbering the nucleotide positions in mitogenomes,
there is no official protocol. In the human mtDNA, nucleotide positions are numbered according
to the revised Cambridge Reference Sequence (rCRS, NC001807) [14]. The numerical designation
of each base pair is initiated at an arbitrary position within the control region on the H strand and
continues around the molecule (see Figure 1.3). Assembled human mitochondrial sequences
are aligned and reported relative to rCRS, however, for most other species, no such guidelines
exist, and not all species even have a reference sequence.

Multiple Sequence Alignment
Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is the task of locating equivalent regions of three or more
DNA or protein sequences to maximise their similarity. When comparing only two sequences,

it is called pairwise sequence alignment. Sequences can be aligned to detect homology or to
compare novel sequences with those already existing in databases [19]. There are two types
of alignment; global and local. In a global alignment, entire sequences are lined up against
each other and their alignment covers the full range of each sequence. Gaps are inserted to
make the sequences the same length. The global alignment is especially useful when comparing
closely related homologous sequences, because they will, in most cases, be of approximately
the same length [19]. When only parts of the sequences are related, the local alignment is
most appropriate. In the local alignment only a substring of the query sequence is aligned
to substring(s) of the target sequence(s); an example is similar domains in different proteins.
Protein sequences are easier to align than DNA sequences. The difference in the number of
letters impacts the probability of matches occurring by chance, and protein sequences change
less over time than DNA sequences. In this project, however, only global alignments of mtDNA
sequences are considered. Figure 1.5 illustrates an MSA of eight mitogenomes from pigs.

MSA is far from straightforward. During evolution, insertions/deletions (indels), duplications,

expensive, complex, and time-consuming optimisation problem. Dynamic programming al-
gorithms can find a best scoring alignment of k sequences with mean length n in the running
time O(nk) [20]. Most MSA programs, therefore, use heuristic approaches rather than global
optimisation to find the best sequence alignment.
1.3.1
Scoring alignments
Sequences can be aligned in a variety of different ways, and heuristic methods only produce
near-optimal solutions. To determine the best possible alignment, quantitative scores that
measure the alignment quality and accuracy are needed. Scoring schemes must reflect the
degree of similarity and assess the validity of inserted gaps [19].
The simplest way of quantifying sequence similarity is percent identity (number of identical
matches divided by the total length of the aligned region) [19]. The identity score, however, does
not take gaps into account and is generally too simple. Gaps inserted in a sequence to maximise
similarity and achieve correct matches require a penalty that will affect the overall alignment
score [19]. Gap penalties and gap extension penalties can usually be varied in alignment pro-
grams, and it is also worth noting that indels leading to gaps occur more often in non-coding
and intergene DNA regions.
AnotherpopularoverallmeasureforscoringMSAsisthesumofpairs(SP)score, whichisobtained
by considering all pairwise alignments in the MSA. Both substitutions, matches, and induced
gap intervals contribute to this score [21]; however, it requires a reference alignment of the same
sequences. Alignment scores can also be related to sequence difference, known as distance, in
which case closely related sequences result in a low score [19].

1.3.2
MAFFT
Currently, many different MSA programs with various algorithmic approaches are in use. One of
them is MAFFT (Multiple Alignment using Fast Fourier Transform), which works for both amino
acid and nucleotide sequences. It was published in 2002 [24], and is known for its fast and
reliable alignments [25]. MAFFT offers various alignment strategies, which are classified into
progressive methods, iterative refinement methods, and consistency-based iterative refinement
methods.

==PAGE:20==
The progressive heuristic is the most widely used MSA algorithm [26] and includes the meth-
ods FFT-NS-1 (one-cycle progressive method), FFT-NS-2 (two-cycle progressive method), and
PartTree, which is more scalable and applicable to ∼50 000 sequences [26]. The alignments are
based on fast Fourier transform (FFT), which allows rapid detection of homologous segments
[24]. In the FFT-NS-1 strategy, a guide tree is created from the rough distance matrix that is
based on all-to-all pairwise comparisons. An MSA is then constructed using a group-to-group
alignment by FFT at each node of the guide tree so that the sequences are aligned according to
the branching order [26]. In FFT-NS-2, the guide tree is re-computed from the FFT-NS-1 alignment
and the second progressive alignment is performed. Figure A.1 in Appendix A illustrates the
calculation procedure of the FFT-NS-1 and FFT-NS-2 method.
In progressive alignment methods, incorrectly introduced gaps and other alignment errors are
not corrected in later steps. The iterative refinement and consistency-based iterative refinement
method can overcome this drawback. The iterative refinement method, FFT-NS-i, tries to correct
mistakes in the initial alignment by using a weighted sum-of-pairs (WSP) score. The MSA is parti-
tioned into two groups, which are then re-aligned using a group-to-group alignment algorithm.
The iterative refinement is repeated until no further improvements in the WSP score are made
[26]. Figure A.2 in Appendix A illustrates the calculation procedure of the FFT-NS-i method. In
the consistency-based iterative refinement method, the accuracy of an MSA is assessed by its
consistency with pairwise alignments, and the following three algorithms are implemented
for pairwise alignment; i) global alignment (Needleman-Wunsch), ii) local alignment (Smith-
Waterman) with affine gap costs (Gotoh), and iii) local alignment with generalised affine gap
costs (Altschul). These three objective functions are G-INS-i, L-INS-i, and E-INS-i, respectively,
and they are all combinedmin the WSP score.
The three described alignment strategies, the progressive, iterative refinement, and consistency-
based iterative refinement method have been described in their order of speed. Their order of
accuracy is reverse.
Aside from the different alignment methods, MAFFT has several other options and advanceed
settings. One of them is the automated option that selects an appropriate strategy from FFT-NS-1,
FFT-NS-2, FFT-NS-i, and L-INS-i, depending on the size of the data. The program also makes
it possible to create MSAs that are aware of forward and reverse complement directions [27].
The two options, one is fast and the other one is much slower but more accurate, generate
reverse complement sequences, when necessary, and align them together with the remaining
sequences.
It should be noted that MAFFT assumes a conserved order of aligned sites and blocks among all of
the sequences in the MSA. This assumption can be violated by genome inversions, translocations,
or duplications, which is why MAFFT is not suitable for whole genome alignment [26]. However,
as it was described in Section 1.2.3, mitogenomes are relatively small and highly conserved,
making it possible to adopt MAFFT in this project.
1.4
Multiple Circular Sequence Alignment
MSA programmemes take into consideration the left-and right-most positions of the sequences.
In mitochondrial DNA however, the start and end sequence positions are arbitrary and the
numbering does not always follow the same protocol, as it was described in Section 1.2.4. The
arbitrary definition of the first position can result in a false high genetic distance in an MSA. This
issue applies to other circular molecular structures as well, making multiple circular sequence
alignment (MCSA) methods and specialised tools desirable. MCSA is a generalisation of MSA, but
is not a straightforward task. When aligning circular DNA sequences, they must first be rotated

so that they all begin at the same position, before they can be aligned by a standard MSA tool. To
this day, only a few programmes exist, whose goal is to improve the MCSA of the input sequences.
In the computer science research field, several algorithms for the alignment of two cyclic se-
quences have been proposed. The cyclic alignment problem is to find the optimal alignment
of two strings, a = a1a2...am of length m and b = b1b2...bn of length n, for m, n ≥2, where a
is the prototype string, and b is a test string that is allowed to be cyclically shifted k symbols
0 ≤k ≤n −1 [28]. The cyclic shift operator σ rotates a string by one position and is defined
by σ(a1a2...am) = a2a3...ama1. In the cyclic alignment, the function d assigns non-negative,
real-valued costs to all match, substitute, insert, and delete operations. Dcyc(a, b), the optimal
cost of the cyclic alignment of a and b, is then defined as:
Dcyc(a, b) = min(d0(a, b), d1(a, b), ..., dn−1(a, b))
Thebrute-forceapproachtofindDcyc(a, b), rotatingbonek atatimeandrealigningeachrotated
string with a, has time complexity O(mn2). In 1990, [29] presented an algorithm with time
complexity O(nm log n) and consuming O(nm) space to solve the string-to-string correction
problem for cyclic strings. In 1993, [28] presented a dynamic programmeming solution with time
and space complexity O(mn). The method is based on computing bounds on costs of aligning
substrings of a and b using d values from an (m + 1) x (n + 1) initial matrix M. A guided search
algorithm uses these bounds on actual alignment costs to find all optimal cyclic shifts.
When the pairwise cyclic alignment algorithms described above are generalised to multiple
sequences, they require cubic or quadratic time computation complexity. In 2006, two pro-
grammes that dealt with the alignment of circular biological sequences were published. Cyclope
addresses short circular sequences, especially circular RNA [30]. The programme computes the
optimal linear alignment of two strings a and b in O(nm max(n + m)) time and O(nm) space.
The other programme, Circal, aligns mitochondrial gene arrangements by a progressive align-
ment approach [31]. The mitogenomes are regarded as cyclically ordered lists of the 37 genes.
By use of the cyclic alignment procedure, all pairwise distances are computed and combined
into a multiple alignment of gene orders. Both of these programmes can only deal with short
sequences because of their time complexity.
TheCSA(CyclicDNASequenceAligner)toolwaspublishedin2009andusesacompletelydifferent
algorithmic approach than the previous tools. It was specifically tested on mtDNA and it was
shown that a pre-processing step in the form of rotation of circular DNA sequences significantly
improves the efficiency of public available MSA algorithms [32]. A generalised cyclic suffix tree
is constructed for the set of mitochondrial DNA sequences in linear time. This suffix tree data
structure represents every rotation of every sequence. The algorithm identifies the largest chain
of longest common subsequences that belongs to all the sequences, and makes the sequences
linear by cutting them at the starting position of the first block from that common chain [32]. The
CSA tool used to be available through a web interface, but is no longer maintained. Furthermore,
the input dataset could only contain up to 32 sequences, and a correct rotation required the
existence of a block that occurred in every sequence only once [33].
BEAR (BEst-Aligned Rotations) is another programme for improving MCSA, published in 2015 [34].
Twomethodsareimplemented; oneforsimilarsequences,andoneformoredivergentsequences.
Both methods use agglomerative hierarchical clustering to find the best rotation for each input
sequence. The first method is based on the approximate circular dictionary matching algorithm,
whereas the second method uses fixed-length approximate string matching. In the first method,
for a set of d circular input sequences (s1, s2, ...sd) the algorithm constructs a matrix M of size d
x d. M holds pairs of e and r values. M[i, j].e contains the edit distances between sequences si
and sj, and M[i, j].r holds their best rotations. Agglomerative hierarchical clustering is then

Graph-based structures are extensively used in the field of bioinformatics, especially in biological

the following, the graph types used in the genome perspective will be introduced.
A genome graph is used to represent a pangenome (a collection of genomic sequences that are
analysed together or used as a reference) [36]. Since highly polymorphic regions of a genome are
poorly represented by a single reference sequence, a richer reference structure can be beneficial.
Genome graphs can compactly represent a cohort of genomes, simultaneously reducing the
reference bias. Figure 1.6 illustrates how a set of reference genomes can be integrated into a

Sequence-to-graph alignment
A graph-based MSA structure requires the possibility of aligning sequences to the graph. In 2002,
it was shown that approximate string matching on graphs can be solved in time O(M(N + E)),
where N and E are the number of nodes and edges, respectively, and M is the length of the
query sequence [38]. In 2002, a new algorithm, partial order alignment (POA), was introduced
[39]. POA is an alternative alignment strategy to the progressive MSA methods that were de-
scribed specifically for MAFFT in Section 1.3.2. A series of pairwise alignments and the following
evolutionary tree do not guarantee optimal MSAs. Throughout the years, many problems with
progressive alignment have been pointed out. First of all, MSAs depend on local minima and
the choice of alignment parameters. Moreover, the MSA to a profile mapping results in a loss of
information, because profiles do not contain all the necessary information to reconstruct the

XG and GCSA2 indexes through an alignment process based on maximal exact match (MEM)
sequences between the query sequence and the reference graph [37]. The Generalized Com-
pressed Suffix Array (GCSA) is a path index based on Burrows-Wheeler Transform (BWT). GCSA2 is
a new version of the original GCSA and is constructed by using a de Bruijn graph as a k-mer index
of the variation graph, pruning (compressing) its complex regions, and encoding the pruned
graph by BWT and suffix tree operations [40]. For additional functionality, GCSA2 uses the longest
common prefix (LCP) array. The GCSA2 index stores the reference graph in a succinct self-indexed
data structure optimised for substring search.
XG is a low-memory representation of the graph it indexes. It contains the nodes, edges, and
paths of the graph, but not their sequences, i.e. it saves only the structure of the graph. Instead
of using the raw graph, all queries can be performed on this index to maximise the speed.
The vg map workflow requires a graph stored as an XG index and a GCSA index with the LCP
array. It then aligns the read to the indexed version of the graph. The extensions based on suffix
trees in the GCSA2 index are used for finding super-maximal exact matches (SMEMs) of the read.
SMEMs are exact matches between the query substring and a reference substring that cannot
be extended in any direction, and for which there is no extension of the query substring that
matches elsewhere in the graph [37]. All SMEMs are then clustered and chained within each
cluster by selecting the maximum likelihood path through a Markov model that rewards long
SMEMs and short colinear gaps between SMEMs. For each consistent sequence of SMEMs, a
subgraph containing the cluster is obtained. The query string is aligned to the variation graph
using POA; however, POA is defined only for acyclic graphs. Since variation graphs may have
cycles and inversions, the cyclic bidirected graphs are unrolled and unfolded to make DAGs that
are amenable to POA. Figure 1.10 illustrates the unfolding process of a bidirected sequence
graph. The alignment is then transformed back into the original graph’s coordinates.
Figure 1.10: Bidirected sequence graph (A) being unfolded into a DAG (B), in preparation for POA.
Nodes 6, 7, and 8 are reversed views of nodes 1, 2, and 5, respectively. Figure from [36].
When mapping very long reads, they are split into overlapping chunks (default size 256 bp with
a 32 bp overlap) [37]. These chunks are mapped independently and then chained together
by the same Markov model. Although vg is capable of mapping long reads to graphs, it is

especially targeted for aligning short reads, leading to slow runtimes for long read alignment
[41]. The GCSA2 index size grows combinatorially with local graph complexity and is therefore
very challenging for large graphs. vg solves this problem by indexing a pruned graph and thereby
only some of the variation.
1.5.3
File formats
vg uses a variety of file formats besides the XG and GCSA2 indexes that are described in the
previous section. The other file types used in this project are explained below. The VG and GAM
files are both Protocol buffers (Protobuf) which are Google’s free, open-source, language-neutral,
and platform-neutral data interchange format used for serialising structured data and supported
by many programming languages.
VG
TheVGisaProtobufgraphformatandstorestheactualgraphobjectinafilewiththe.vgextension.
The data is compressed and binary; however, for the sake of efficiency, vg often operates on the
graph indexes instead of the graph itself.
GAM
The Graph Alignment/Map Protobuf format has the .gam file extension and is vg’s main format
for aligned reads. It is composed of one record per read that describes the alignment of that
read to a sequence graph. The data is binary and compressed.
GFA
Graphical Fragment/Assembly (GFA) format is a text-based format for storing graphs and their
embedded paths. It has the .gfa file extension and is specifically designed to allow data inter-
change between programs operating on sequence graphs, making workflows trouble-free and
straightforward. The GFA format is described in detail in Appendix F.
ODGI
The Optimized Dynamic Graph Interface format has the the .odgi file extension. It links a dynamic
in-memoryvariationgraphdatamodeltoasetofalgorithmsdesignedfor, amongothers, scalable
sorting, pruning, transformation, and visualisation of large genome graphs. ODGI includes
Python bindings that can be used to directly interface with its data model.

Tools
This section describes the tools and software that have been used in the project.
2.2.1
MSA
MAFFT was the chosen MSA tool because of its accuracy and speed. The bioinformatics cluster
hascurrentlyMAFFTversion7.487[27]installed, whichincludestheoptionforparallelprocessing,
reducing the CPU time even more. The different algorithms are described in Section 1.3.2. Due
to the many and long mitogenome sequences (> ∼10,000 bp), the default fast progressive
algorithm FFT-NS-2 was used. When the length of the alignment exceeds a threshold, FFT-NS-2
automatically switches the dynamic programming algorithm to a memory saving one that only
requires O(L) RAM space, L being the sequence length. For highly conserved sequences, FFT-
NS-2 also reduces the calculation time. Figure A.1 in Appendix A illustrates the procedure of
this method.
MAFFT accepts a set of unaligned sequences in FASTA format and returns an alignment of the
sequences also in FASTA format. The following command was used for running MAFFT in FFT-
NS-2 mode:
mafft --retree 2 --maxiterate 0 input [> output]
The --retree option is the number of times the guide tree is built in the progressive stage, and
the number of cycles of iterative refinement is set with the --maxiterate option. For all other
alignment parameters, such as gap opening and extension penalties and offsets, the default
values were used.
The online version 7 of MAFFT [42] 2 was used in the benchmarking part of the project, because
it outputs plots in which the top sequence is plotted against the others. These plots can visualise
reverse-complemented parts of the mitogenomes. MAFFT online was run with all the default
parameters, including the auto-strategy.
2.2.2
MSA comparison
Since MAFFT does not provide a quantitative score of its alignments, the wentropy score, which
was explained in Section 1.3.1, is used for MSA comparison in this project. The wentropy score
has been implemented by Guillaume Collet (2012) in the Multiple alignment analyser MstatX 3
that is freely available on GitHub and installed on the bioinformatics cluster.
mstatx takes an MSA in FASTA format as input and outputs the wentropy score in a .txt file. The
following command was run to compute the static:
mstatx -g -i input -o output
The -g flag indicates a global score of the MSA, which is the mean of all the column scores.

Benchmarking
The goal of the benchmarking part of the thesis is to identify the current issues with MCSA.
Inconsistencies in the NCBI nucleotide database are identified using MAFFT and MITOS. The
performance of the state-of-the-art programme, MARS, is also assessed. MARS is freely available

quantified by wentropy scores; the alignments on the refined sequences by MARS are compared
to the original ones. The performance of Methods 0 and 1 is compared by means of wentropy
scores as well as the runtime. The runtime of MARS is an important aspect, and it is evaluated

input file. Finally, an attempt is made to “break” the programme by running it on more and more
taxonomically divergent datasets.

mars -a DNA -i input -o output -m 0 -T 10
-a indicates the nucleotide alphabet, the input and output files are both in FASTA format, -m is

2.4
Implementation
Rotating and orienting mitochondrial genomes for MCSA with the variation graph approach
consists of three parts:
• Constructing the variation graph using the vg toolkit;
• Finding a cutoff node; and
• Sequence rotation and orientation.
Separate scripts have been developed for each of the tasks and will be described in detail in the
following. The scripts are implemented in Bash and Python.
2.4.1
Graph construction
The Bash script for constructing a variation graph takes a multiFASTA file as input, iteratively
builds a variation graph from the sequences contained in the file, and saves the final variation
graph in three different formats. Figure 2.1 illustrates the pipeline for constructing a variation
graph from all mitogenomes in the input file. The figure shows all vg commands and file formats
used in the workflow.

The multiFASTA file is split into individual files containing one mitogenome each. A VG graph

2.4.2
Finding a cutoff node
When all mitogenomes are aligned, the variation graph is ideally circular and should be cut at
the most conserved node. The longest node traversed by all paths is chosen as the cutting point.
The script for finding the cutoff node uses the Library for BiDirected Sequence Graphs (bdsg)
version 0.3 [45]. The library is written in C++, but here, the Python binding was used. The script
takes as input the variation graph in ODGI format and outputs the longest node(s) that all paths
go through only once. If no such node is found, the mitogenomes cannot be correctly rotated;
however, the second best node can be used.
In this project, the goal is to cut all paths in the variation graph at the same position in the
mitogenome. The genomic position of the cutoff point is not important, i.e. the sequences could
potentially be cut in the middle of a particular gene.
2.4.3
Sequence rotation and orientation
The mitogenomes are rotated and oriented by a file parsing script written in Python. It takes
as input the graph in GFA format (see Appendix F) and the ID of the cutoff node, and outputs
a multiFASTA file with rotated and oriented mitogenomes. For each path, the ordered list of
oriented nodes is shifted to start at the given cutoff node. If the orientation is “+”, the sequence is
already in the correct orientation, and in case of “-”, the node sequence is reverse complemented.
2.4.4
Implementation challenges
Many issues and challenges were encountered during the development process, especially with
the vg toolkit. Given that the objective was to align sequences to the whole variation graph, vg
align and vg map were the only command options. vg align aligns a sequence to the whole
graph using full-length POA and would be the ideal method; however, it only works on small
graphs. In each iteration, the graph grows in size, eventually causing vg to crash, because it
attempted to allocate more RAM than was available on the node. vg map works on large graphs,
but requires indexing and pruning, which results in a lot of information being lost. The effects
will be evident in the results for some of the large divergent datasets in Section 4.2.3.
Problems of insufficient RAM also appeared in the graph circularising attempts. A variation graph
of circular sequences should be circular as well, however, after augmenting the graph with a
mapped sequence, the new path could not be circularised using vg circularize. Only the first
sequence, from which the initial graph was constructed, could be circularised. This bug resulted
in only some of the paths being circular in the final graph, meaning that several edges between
the first and last node of mitogenomes were missing. The reason for vg crashing when using the
vg circularize command remains unknown, pointing towards a bug in the vg programme.
Testing it would be too time consuming and out of the scope for this project.
Overcoming these issues and challenges resulted in the final workflow illustrated in Figure 2.1.
It is not the most optimal solution for variation graph construction; however, it is suitable for the
purpose of this study.
Another challenge encountered was extracting the rotated and oriented mitogenomes from the
variation graph. It turned out that vg has no option for this task, therefore, other tools had to be
used.

3.2.5
Benchmarking conclusions
This chapter attempted to illustrate the inconsistencies in published mitochondrial genomes
and assess the performance of the state-of-the-art programme for rotating circular sequences,
MARS.
Mitogenomes in the NCBI nucleotide database begin at arbitrary positions and they can have
different orientations. Reverse complemented parts are also quite common. All these discrepan-
cies make mitochondrial genomes not suitable for MSA without an initial refinement. Especially
reverse complemented sequences can considerably affect the overall alignment score.
MARS works very well on input files containing similar sequences, but does not improve the
alignment of very divergent mitogenomes. Given that MARS cannot handle different sequence
orientations in an input file, a prior mitochondrial genome annotation is required if the orienta-
tions are unknown. The runtimes of Methods 0 and 1 differ drastically, but none of them can be
considered fast. Based on the tests performed, Method 1 is slightly more accurate; however, the
lower wentropy scores do not make up for the long execution times.

Graph Pruning
Section 4.2 revealed that sequence rotation and orientation for the 10animals, Haplorrhini, and
Eutheria datasets was not possible. The variation graphs for these three datasets all contained
big nodes with the maximum length of 1024 bp and many dead ends. These poor results can
be explained by the graph pruning for GCSA2 indexing. Pruning the variation graphs for GCSA2
indexing reduces the number of nodes and edges. For divergent sequences, removing complex
regions of the graph clearly has a very negative effect, making the alignment much more difficult.
During graph augmentation, nodes are added, which might contain sequences not yet well
aligned, andthesubsequentpruningcombinesthesemultiplenodesintoonelargenode, making
it impossible for an alignment to occur later in the process.

5
Discussion
Animalmitochondrialgenomesarehighlyconservedregardingtheirstructure, geneorganisation,
and genome length, making them perfect candidates for whole genome MCSA. Visualisations of
correctly aligned mitogenomes reveal a characteristic region of about 1000 bp containing many
gaps and mismatches belonging to the control region. These misalignments are caused by the
fact that the D-loop, directly repeated nucleotide sequences, and hypervariable regions are all
located in this large non-coding part of the mitogenome. Therefore, initiating the numerical
designation of each nucleotide at an arbitrary position within the control region, as it is the
case for the human reference mtDNA, can seem somewhat unreasonable. Instead, starting
the numbering of nucleotide positions at the 5’ end of a particular mitochondrial gene, could
potentially make the task of MCSA much less troublesome.
The inconsistencies in sequence databases pointed out in Section 3.1 could, to some degree,
be avoided if specific guidelines and protocols for annotation and numbering existed for mi-
togenomes of all animal species. Especially reference sequences are important to address, since
these are used in the reference mapping method of NGS data assembly. Mitogenome annotation
is a relatively straightforward task and could be a compulsory step before uploading a newly
assembled mitogenome to any public sequence database. The MITOS tool used in this project
was able to identify and designate the start and end positions of all protein-coding, rRNA, and
tRNA genes in the mitogenomes.
Wentropy scores proved to be a quick and reliable way of determining the alignment qualities.
In each shown alignment in Chapter 3 and Chapter 4, the alignment visualisations corresponded
well with the quantitative score obtained. Therefore, incorporating the wentropy score in the
rotation and orientation process could be considered. Changes in wentropy could be applied
to indicate whether the refinement is correct or not. Differently oriented mitogenomes in the
10animals, Eutheria, and Mammalia datasets considerably increased their overall wentropy
scores.
Using the MAFFT progressive method FFT-NS-2 for MSA worked well on mitogenomes. The
alignment visualisations clearly showed the cyclic shifts, whereas the reverse complemented
sequences could be recognised by the many mismatches throughout the whole genome. Rotat-
ing sequences of the same orientation can be done manually based on the alignment; however,
it is far more time consuming. Furthermore, the ability to simultaneously handle different ori-
entations in the datasets is crucial, as it was shown is Section 3.1. MARS, the state-of-the-art in
MCSA, works very well on input files containing similar sequences, but it does not use reverse
complementation to orient the mitogenomes.
This project addresses a real bioinformatics challenge and implements a method using an
approach that has, as far as it is known, never been tried before. The usage of genome variation
graphs in biological sequence analysis is growing, so it is a natural step to make use of these
powerful tools to address the new task at hand, namely rotating and orienting mitochondrial
genomes for MCSA improvement. The algorithmic design of dividing the task into three parts
was necessary in the development process. The variation graph construction turned out to be
a challenging ‘trial and error’ undertaking because of several encountered issues with the vg
toolkit. The failed attempt to use vg align necessitated the use of vg map for the sequence-
to-graph alignments. Working on the graph indexes instead of the graph itself will always have
drawbacks, although for datasets containing similar sequences, pruning did not seem to affect
the proper alignment of mitogenomes. For divergent sequences however, pruning the variation
Algorithms to Find Appropriate Rotations and Orientations for Circular Sequences
51
==PAGE:62==
graph for each GCSA2 proved to have a significant impact on the final graph, in several cases
making it unfit to use for finding a cutting point. The pruning parameters of vg prune could not
be tuned to allow less pruning and thus more variation.
Another vg issue was circularising each path to reflect the true nature of mitogenomes. Only after
the completed graph construction, it was possible to circularise all paths; however, the intention
was to circularise each new path after the graph augmentation step. Paths not being cyclic
meant that the first and last node of some mitogenomes were not connected. The long nodes,
high number of dead ends, and final graphs looking like the ones in Figure 4.8, Figure 4.11 and
Figure 4.12, are likely to also be caused by the lack of circularisation during the variation graph
construction.
vg map uses POA, which is sensitive to the sequence alignment order. To address this depen-
dence, a progressive alignment algorithm similar to the one used by MAFFT could be applied.
Using a guide tree to establish the best order for aligning the mtDNA sequences could be worth
a try on divergent datasets. This would imply implementing separate methods for less and more
divergent datasets. It should also be noted that vg uses heuristics in place of a true probability
model for its mapped reads.
Quite late in the project period it was discovered that the vg toolkit used was not of the latest
version. A total of five updates had been released since version 1.37.0, the current one being
1.42.0. It remains unknown whether any differences in these versions would affect the results.
The next step in the algorithm was to choose an appropriate cutting point for the graph, and the
longest node traversed by all paths was chosen. All paths going through that node only once, in
any orientation, ensures that all mitogenomes have the same cyclic shift. For the most divergent
datasets no such node existed; however, this is most likely caused by the entire sequence-to-
graph alignment failing in these cases. Theoretically, it should always be possible to have a
common node for all paths, because nodes can be of just 1 bp length. Additionally, since the
genomic position of the cutoff point is not taken into account, the sequences could potentially
be cut in the middle of a particular gene. The variation graphs for the similar datasets contained
several potential cutoff nodes, so finding the optimal node out of these could be a way to avoid
cutting mid-gene.
Overall, the results of the variation graph approach to rotate and orient mitogenomes for MCSA
are promising. For the datasets containing similar sequences (Suina and Hominoidea), very good
results were obtained, and even the reverse complemented mitogenomes were perfectly aligned
to the graph and assigned the proper orientation. Aligning divergent sequences will always be
more difficult, and the method for handling these particular datasets needs to be revised. The
algorithm could not handle only parts of the sequences being reverse complemented either;
vg map did not recognise these sequences. Luckily, these types of inconsistencies are not as
common in sequence databases and have, so far, only been observed in Arthropods. Runtime-
wise, this new approach leaves much to be desired. For the Suina dataset the runtime was the
same as - even slightly better - than MARS, but the alignment of 18 Hominoidea sequences to the
growing variation graph proved to be very time consuming. All in all, even though the variation
graph approach is far from optimal, it does have a lot of potential.
5.1
Future Work
This project focuses on animal mitochondrial DNA, but the methods could be extended to all
types of circular DNA sequences as well as naturally-occuring circular proteins. It is worth
mentioning that the vg toolkit is not the only graph aligner out there. vg is not made for aligning
long reads, resulting in long runtimes for this particular task. Better results might be obtained

with another graph tool, such as GraphAligner [41], which is made specifically for aligning long
reads to genome graphs. However, GraphAligner does not have as big a community of users,
and contributors actively solving any occurring issues and adding new features.
At the moment, the variation graph method for rotating and orienting mtDNA consists of separate
scripts for each of the three tasks and is a mix of Python and Bash. The goal would be to combine
them into one programme and publish it as, for instance, a command-line tool or Biopython
module. However, the issues of graph pruning and path circularisation need to be resolved first.
Presuming that the vg toolkit is still used for variation graph construction, it seems like more
computational power is needed to resolve the current issues and not run out of temporary disk
space when working on large, divergent datasets.

6
Conclusion
This project addressed the issue of multiple sequence alignment of animal mitochondrial DNA.
Mitogenomes in the NCBI nucleotide database have different cyclic shifts and orientations, and
there are also examples of only parts of the mitogenomes being reverse complemented. These
inconsistencies make them unsuitable for MSA without a prior refinement.
MARS, the current state-of-the-art in MCSA, works very well on input files containing similar
sequences, but cannot handle datasets containing different orientations and also has long
runtimes. Method1wouldalmostneverbepreferredoverMethod0, becausethelongerruntimes
would rarely make up for the slightly lower wentropy scores achieved using this method in some
cases.
A new method for rotating and orienting mitogenomes for MCSA improvement using variation
graphs has been designed and implemented. The algorithm constructs a genome variation
graph from the mtDNA sequences that are to be aligned, finds an optimal cutting point, and
performs rotation and orientation of the mitogenomes accordingly.
The new approach obtained good results for taxonomically similar datasets, producing more or
less the same global alignment scores as MARS. The variation graph method can also correctly
orient the input sequences, provided the entire genomes are reverse complemented. In the latter
case, the sequence-to-graph alignment did not recognise the reverse complemented nucleotide
sequences, and the final variation graph proved to be unfit to use for accurate rotation and
orientation.
Finding a node in the graph, through which all paths go, is a crucial step for correct sequence
orientation. This however, provedtoberatherdifficult when dealingwith verydivergentdatasets.
Their final graphs did not map the sequences accurately enough due to issues with pruning and
circularisation in the vg toolkit. For divergent datasets neither MARS nor the new variation graph
approach performed well. It was also demonstrated that the order, in which mitogenomes in
divergent input files are aligned to the graph, has an impact on the final graph and should be
taken into consideration.
As to runtime, the new method left much to be desired. For small datasets, the runtime was
the same as - even slightly better - than MARS, but the alignment of more sequences to the
growing variation graph proved to be very time consuming. With the current vg workflow for
graph construction, it will be difficult to decrease the runtime significantly, because the duration
of mitogenome string mapping increases as the graph grows in size.

Appendix
Example of profile alignment in MARS stage 3
Consider the following five input sequences:
s0: TAGTAGCT
s1: AAGTAAGCTA
s2: AAGCCTTTAGT
s3: AAGTAAGCT
s4: TTAATATAGCC
Three profiles, A, B, and C are defined.
Profile A:
s0: A-G-C–TTA-GT
s1: AAG-C–TAAAGT
s2: AAGCC-TTTA-GT
Profile B:
s3: A—AGTAAG-C–T
s4: A-ATA-TA-GCC-TT
Profile C:
s0: A-G-C–TT-A–GT
s1: AAG-C–TA-A-AGT
s2: AAGCC-TTT-A–GT
s3: AAG-C–TA—AGT
s4: A-GCC-TTA-ATA-T
By looking at the original set of sequences, it is clear that s2 in profile A and s3 in profile B have a
rotation of 0. All other sequences have been rotated and aligned with the remaining sequences
in their profile. From the original sequences it can also be observed that M[3, 2].r = 4, since s3
shifted 4 positions to the left aligns with the first 4 letters of s2. However, when aligning profile B
with profile A, s3’s rotation r = 4 is no longer valid due to the inserted gaps. As g = 3 gaps have
been inserted between positions 0 and r of sequence s3, the final accurate rotation for profile B
is M[3, 2].r + g = 4 + 3 = 7. Profile C is the final alignment [33].

Appendix
The GFA Format Specification
The GFA format is a tab-delimited text format for describing sequence graphs and is specified in
detail in 1. The GFA files from vg contain four types of lines; H (Header), S (Segment; continuous
sequence or subsequence), L (Link; overlap between two segments), and P (Path; ordered list of
oriented segments, where each consecutive pair of oriented segments is supported by a link).
Each line in GFA has tab-delimited fields and the first field defines the type of line. Below is an
example of each line type.
1
H VN:Z:1.0
2
S 67
GAGTTGGTAAATCTCGTGCCAGCCACCGCGGT
3
P AP003428.1
540+,4521+,409+,1724+,1725+,4584+,4585+, ... ,5192+,5193+ *
4
L 2989
+ 5505
+ *
The Header line contains a version number. The Segment line contains the name (node ID) and
the sequence. The Path line contains the path name and a comma-separated list of segment
names and their orientations. ”+” indicates that the segment sequence should be used as-is,
whereas ”-” means that the sequence should be replaced with its reverse complement. The Link
line consists of the name of the from segment (node ID), orientation of the from segment, name
of the to segment, and the orientation of the to segment.
