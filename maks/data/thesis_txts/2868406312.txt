Master’s Thesis
Abstract
Urban noise pollution is a pressing issue in densely populated European cities, affecting
public health and quality of life. This thesis examines the link between urban morphol-
ogy and traffic noise levels, focusing on Copenhagen and Paris as case studies. Combining
insights from literature and from data analysis, the study explores how urban parameters
such as built density, road traffic configuration, and green spaces influence day-evening-night
noise levels.
A machine-learning-based clustering method is used to group districts based on their
urban characteristics. Key parameters are evaluated for their role in noise variation. The
study identifies clear differences in noise distribution patterns between the two cities. In
Copenhagen, noise levels are mainly influenced by factors like Annual Average Daily Traffic,
Floor Space Index, Compactness, Ground Space Index, and Sky View Factor. Meanwhile,
Paris shows greater sensitivity to parameters such as Water Surface Ratio, Public Use Ratio,
Green Space Ratio, Commercial Use Ratio, and population density. The robustness of clus-
tering methods is also explored, with double-level hierarchical clustering proving effective
for Copenhagen, while best parameter-based clustering worked well for Paris.
This research offers a replicable framework for understanding noise propagation in dif-
ferent urban environments. Regression models created for Copenhagen achieved an error
rate of just 4% when applied to Paris, showing that predictive models can work across cities,
but with some limitations. The findings highlight the need for city-specific calibrations, as
local noise context plays a major role in shaping noise patterns.
By integrating urban morphology into noise reduction strategies, this study provides
valuable guidance for urban planners looking to create quieter, healthier cities.

Introduction
1.1
Context and questions
Urban noise pollution is a growing concern, particularly in densely populated European
cities, where traffic constitutes one of the most significant contributors. As discussed in
1.2 Current State of Research, noise from air, rail, and road traffic impacts urban residents’
health and well-being, with road traffic posing the most uncontrolled and pervasive challenge.
Unlike air and rail traffic, road noise is ubiquitous, penetrating city centers and affecting di-
verse urban settings. While certain urban fabric characteristics - such as building placement,
density, and materials - can attenuate noise, the extent of their effectiveness remains poorly
understood. This raises a fundamental question: To what extent can the urban fabric be
relied upon as a passive noise mitigation tool, and how can its effectiveness be optimized?
Furthermore, European cities like Paris and Copenhagen showcase highly varied ur-
ban morphologies shaped by historical, political, and socio-economic forces. For instance,
Parisian neighborhoods reveal traces of Haussmannian urban reforms, while Copenhagen’s
districts reflect commercial expansions over centuries. These distinct urban patterns influ-
ence how traffic noise propagates and is perceived. Yet, the relationship between specific
urban parameters and noise levels remains underexplored. Do certain urban morphologies
inherently perform better at mitigating traffic noise? This gap in understanding necessitates
a closer examination of the correlations between traffic noise levels and urban patterns.
Lastly, while studies have linked urban patterns to noise levels, many fail to identify the
specific parameters driving these correlations. Urban landscapes are multifaceted systems,
with factors like traffic flow, street geometry, and land use interplaying to shape noise out-
comes. The lack of clarity around the relative importance of these factors poses a critical
question: What are the key parameters that determine the correlation between urban pat-
terns and traffic noise levels? Addressing this requires a structured framework to quantify
the influence of these parameters, offering insights that can guide urban design and policy.
In response to these challenges, this thesis will explore the following research question:
What urban parameters are substantial in the correlation between ur-
ban pattern and noise levels in the districts of Copenhagen and Paris?
For the sake of clarity, this problem can be broken down into four sub-questions:
1. What is the most effective method for clustering districts based on their urban metrics?
2. Can a predictive model for noise levels be developed using urban parameters?
3. Which urban parameters have the greatest influence on noise levels?
4. How do different urban types respond to these parameters?

INTRODUCTION
1.2
Current State of Research
Environmental noise is a growing concern in cities, affecting both the quality of life and
health of urban residents. This section reviews the current research on this topic, covering
key aspects such as the health impacts of noise, the influence of road and rail traffic, and
the role of urban design. It also explores strategies for reducing noise, the effect of greenery
on noise perception, and the regulations that address environmental noise.
1.2.1
Health Implications of Environmental Noise for Urban Residents
According to Vos and Beek (2019), concerns about environmental noise began in the
early 20th century in Western Europe. Several factors contributed to this growing aware-
ness: urbanization, defined as the increase in population density within confined spaces, led
to higher built density in cities; mechanization and industrialization intensified occupational
noise in residential areas; the widespread use of private and public transport significantly
increased road and rail traffic, now recognized as the primary source of environmental noise.
Additionally, globalization has driven substantial growth in freight transport and global
trade.
Today, these concerns continue to escalate, with an estimated 2.5 billion people
worldwide, including 150 million in Europe, exposed to harmful levels of road and rail traffic
noise (Vos and Beek, 2019). This makes noise pollution the second most harmful environ-
mental factor after air pollution (Fang et al., 2020). Furthermore, according to the World
Health Organization (WHO, 2011), traffic-related noise in Western Europe leads to the loss
of at least 1 million healthy life years annually, with 903,000 due to sleep disturbance and
587,000 due to annoyance. It’s important to note that noise annoyance is a subjective re-
sponse to environmental noise. As noted by Klaeboe (2019), studies indicate that about
20 to 30% of the urban population reports being highly annoyed by environmental noise,
particularly from traffic, industry, and neighbors.
Certain groups, such as children, the
elderly, and individuals with pre-existing health conditions, are more susceptible to noise
annoyance and interference. A study by Gomez-Jacinto and Moral-Toranzo (1999) surveyed
42 residents about their perception of noise, its impact on daily life, and the frequency of
minor health issues, such as headaches and insomnia. The same survey was conducted after
the implementation of insulation that reduced noise levels by 12dB(A), although levels re-
mained above the city’s recommended threshold of 45dB(A). The study found a significant
correlation between perceived noise and self-reported health, but despite the decrease in
noise levels, improvements in residents’ health were minimal, indicating the persistence of
noise as a stressor in daily life. The paper concludes that psychological factors mediate the
relationship between noise exposure and health.
Noise during sleep can cause immediate disturbances, such as reduced sleep duration, al-
tered sleep structure, and autonomic nervous system responses. According to Muzet (2019),
studies have shown that noise levels as low as 35dB(A) can cause brief awakenings, even if
the person does not fully wake up. This is particularly concerning in urban environments
where background noise often exceeds this level. In fact, noise levels above 45dB(A) are
commonly associated with significant sleep disturbances (Muzet, 2019). Furthermore, re-

Beyond physical health impacts, Stansfeld and Clark (2019) stated that exposure to
high levels of noise can also contribute to mental health symptoms such as anxiety and
increased use of sedative medications. Some studies even suggest that noise exposure may
impair children’s psychological well-being, particularly among those exposed to high levels of
aircraft noise (Stansfeld and Clark, 2019). However, associations with severe psychological
disorders are less clear, and socioeconomic factors often complicate these findings.
1.2.2
Influence of Road and Rail Traffic
Traffic noise is the primary cause of noise pollution in central urban areas. According to
Shi (2010), road vehicles are the major contributors to this noise, including sources such as
engine noise, transmission gear noise, tire noise caused by air pumping effects, aerodynamic
noise from high-speed vehicles, and brake squeal. Rail vehicles are the second largest con-
tributors, primarily due to wheel-rail noise from rail surface irregularities, dynamic system
noise from motors and compressors, and aerodynamic noise in tunnels and elevated struc-
tures. These sources create a noise range between 60 and 80dB near urban roads (Fang
et al., 2020). Additionally, the study by Bakowski et al. (2019) emphasized the importance
of frequency-specific analysis in understanding urban noise. The paper concluded that light
motor vehicles are the dominant source of urban traffic noise, with the highest noise levels
observed in the 1000Hz frequency band. This finding highlights the need for effective noise
mitigation strategies, such as adjusting traffic flow and implementing noise barriers that are
tuned to critical frequencies.
Moreover, Lercher (2019) explains that combined noise exposure can lead to different
perceptual effects compared to isolated noise sources, including auditory masking and syn-
ergism. The study also highlights the "combined noise paradox", where the total annoyance
from multiple noise sources is rated lower than the sum of individual annoyances. It adds
that noise exposure is often accompanied by other sensory inputs, such as vibrations and air
pollution, which should be considered when conducting surveys on noise impacts in house-
holds.

Regarding road layout, Ariza-Villaverde et al. (2014) found a significant positive correla-
tion between total noise pollution (measured as Lden) and the street width-to-building height
ratio. This correlation is stronger in areas with regular urban morphology. On a macro scale,
Margaritis and Kang (2016) observed that an increase in the Local Roads Length ratio was
positively correlated with higher traffic noise levels, suggesting that more road connections
can lead to increased noise levels. In the city of Shenzhen, Han et al. (2018) found that
traffic noise was strongly related to vehicle flow but also increased with road density, with
roads covering more of the landscape being associated with greater noise levels. Moreover,
Tang and Wang (2007) highlighted that urban forms with narrower roads experience lower
overall noise levels. Their study on the Macao Peninsula showed that larger roads exceeded
the national noise level of 70dB(A). Finally, Han et al. (2018) underscored that irregular
building shapes along roads can help reduce traffic noise.
1.2.3
Influence of Urban Morphology
Urban morphology has a significant influence on noise mitigation, particularly from traf-
fic. To begin with, noise perception varies greatly depending on the urban morphology of a
region. As stated in the article by Chen et al. (2024), the high-density building morphology
of Manhattan is linked to improved acoustic environments due to better noise absorption in
street canyons. Conversely, regions like Staten Island, characterized by low building height,
open blocks, and high vegetation, exhibit the lowest noise complaint rates, indicating the
effectiveness of less dense urban forms in noise reduction. These complaints are influenced by
various urban morphology factors. Residential noise complaints are most strongly correlated
with building height, while transportation noise complaints are most related to road density
(Chen et al., 2024). Finally, the study finds that three-dimensional urban morphology in-
dices, such as tree shape and Google View Images building index, offer a better explanation
of noise perception than two-dimensional indices like building height and street width (Chen
et al., 2024).
Different urban morphology indicators can help explain noise levels in urban areas. Den-
sity and, to some extent, compactness play a notable role but seem to have different effects
in scientific studies. According to Silva et al. (2014), compactness correlates positively with
noise levels. More compact forms result in higher noise levels, with a maximum Leq. This
is consistent with Weber et al. (2014), who stated that areas with high building and edge
densities, such as multi-story tenement blocks, experienced higher noise levels. In the city of
Shenzhen, Han et al. (2018) observed that higher building density tends to increase regional
noise (e.g., from industry, construction sites, traffic, and social activities) due to intensified
human activities and higher urban heat. Additionally, Margaritis and Kang (2016) found
that the number of buildings was the only variable significantly correlated with noise lev-
els, with more buildings generally leading to higher noise. However, this correlation varied
depending on the architectural style and historical context of the city center. Interestingly,
this tendency for higher noise levels in denser areas seems to reverse when the density be-

In non-built areas, implementing green spaces is recommended. The opportunities dif-
fer depending on the city’s pattern, whether radial or linear. According to Margaritis and
Kang (2016), cities with a dispersed green space pattern experience lower traffic noise levels
compared to those with clustered patterns. Radial cities tend to have more dispersed green
spaces and are generally quieter than linear cities under similar traffic conditions. Further-
more, radial cities were found to have a significantly higher Natural Urban Green Ratio
compared to linear cities, which correlates with lower noise levels (Margaritis and Kang,
2016). Additionally, Han et al. (2018) observed in Shenzhen that a higher percentage of
vegetation, particularly when aggregated and less fragmented, contributes to a quieter envi-
ronment. Moreover, vegetation along or near roads helps to alleviate traffic noise, with more
continuous and aggregated vegetation patterns being more effective.
Within the same city, different patterns can be developed, and even slight changes in
the pattern can impact noise propagation. Salomons and Pont (2012) found that the shape
and layout of urban blocks significantly affect noise levels. Closed building blocks create
quieter facades compared to open blocks. On a larger scale, Silva et al. (2014) suggested
that urban forms with higher complexity in their perimeter tend to have lower average noise
levels, suggesting that more irregular boundaries can create shadow zones that reduce noise
exposure. Han et al. (2018) agreed, noting that scattered building distributions are more
effective in reducing regional noise compared to concentrated layouts. However, a macro-
scale analysis by Ariza-Villaverde et al. (2014) concluded that irregular urban morphology
leads to more complex noise patterns, and regular urban patterns might help achieve more
predictable and manageable noise levels. Additionally, Margaritis and Kang (2016) observed
that, in British towns, higher traffic noise was associated with larger building perimeters and
more road intersections.
In conclusion, neither density nor urban pattern alone provides a clear understanding
of noise mitigation in a city. However, some recommendations can be drawn from various
studies. All the articles cited in this section emphasize the importance of considering urban

and reduced air-pumping effects; third, lower stiffness, as seen in pavements incorporating

such as Open-Graded Friction Course (OGFC), can reduce traffic noise by approximately 3
to 6dB compared to standard asphalt pavements. Moreover, when used as an overlay for

noise by approximately 4dB - outperforming the traditional SMA 8 mixture by 0.8 to 1dB.

As annoyance arises from the perception of noise, it can vary significantly from one
person to another or across different contexts. A study by Li et al. (2010) conducted in
residential estates in Hong Kong, situated near parks and grassy hills with varying degrees

portant role in moderating the noise annoyance experienced by individuals in their homes.

health, and individual noise sensitivity. Additionally, the amount of visible greenery influ-
ences this perception. Specifically, for individuals with a view of garden parks, the greater
the view, the less annoyed they felt. Conversely, for those with a view of grassy hills, an-
noyance levels remained similar regardless of the extent of the view. It is important to note
that this study focused on individuals from a relatively low-income and less-educated social

1.2.5
Regulations regarding Environmental Noise
Luz (2019) discussed various scales of international regulations on noise pollution, fol-
lowing the transmission chain from the emitter to the receiver. These regulations often focus
on aircraft noise, which remains one of the most significant sources of noise pollution. The
only truly international noise regulation related to noise emission is found in Annex 16 of
the Convention on International Civil Aviation, managed by the International Civil Aviation
Organization (ICAO). This regulation has significantly reduced aircraft noise levels since its
inception in 1973. Additionally, the European Union has taken the lead in regulating out-
door equipment noise through the EU Outdoor Equipment Directive (2000/14/EC), which
impacts over 50 types of equipment. U.S. manufacturers must comply with these standards
to remain competitive in global markets. Noise contours are commonly used for airport
noise management in many countries to map areas of noise exposure. Residential areas are
often governed by urban planning rules based on these contour maps, with restrictions on
construction or specific architectural standards required in high-noise zones. Furthermore,
the European Environmental Noise Directive (END) mandates strategic noise mapping every

The urban form gives insights about the robustness and viability of a clustering, looking

2.3.2
Street-based Local Areas
A key component of the methodology of Wu et al. (2024) is the use of Street-based Local
Areas (SLAs) as adaptive units of analysis. Unlike traditional units based on administrative
boundaries or uniform grids, SLAs are derived directly from the street network’s topological
and configurational properties. This allows for a more accurate and flexible representation
of the urban fabric, capturing the unique characteristics of street patterns in different cities.
The study utilizes a community detection technique to partition the street network into SLAs.
For both cities, it has been chosen to study an area, centered on the historical city
center. For Copenhagen, the area is a square of 10km. As Paris has a subsequently bigger
city center, the area is a square of 12km. Accordingly to Wu et al. (2024), the function ex-
plained in Github uses the OSMNX package (Boeing, 2024) to add communities to a graph
of streets. Communities are found using Clauset-Newman-Moore greedy modularity max-
imization. Greedy modularity maximization begins with each node in its own community
and repeatedly joins the pair of communities that lead to the largest modularity until no
further increase in modularity is possible (Python, 2024). The graph is then exported as a
shapefile to be treated on QGIS. The edges are converted to summits and then to Voronoi
polygons. The merged polygons, according to their GMC attribute, are the districts that
are analysed in the following parts.

2.3.3
Acoustics
Following the European Directive 2002/49/EC, countries have developed their compu-
tational models to provide comprehensive, up-to-date exposure maps in major cities. On
the Nordic Council of Ministers initiative in 1996, Nordic2000 (Delta, 2002) was created to
replace empirical methods with theoretically base noise predictions. In France, the fourth
iteration of mapping is currently active, with CNOSSOS-EU (BruitParif, 2022).
Nord2000 cover one-third octave bands from 25Hz to 10kHz, using an atmospheric sound
propagation model influenced by weather, terrain and barriers. The model applies ray acous-
tics, which optimizes the balance between computational speed and accuracy. An hybrid
approach is employed, using modified calculations for regions with uneven ground or dense
urban areas. The simulation also accounts for phenomena such as scattering zones in forests
and urban settings, adding a statistical scattering model for areas where deterministic mod-
eling is impractical. Similarly CNOSSOS-EU considers topographical data including terrain
relief, buildings and noise barriers.
The methodology relies on advanced modeling tech-
niques, where propagation and reflection laws are applied.
In the Nordics, the data come from environmental and transportation agencies, categorizing
vehicle types, track types and road surfaces. BruitParif (2022) takes their information from
public organizations such as Regional Directorates or transportation agencies. Additionally
partners provide essential data on topography and buildings.
Regarding Nordic2000, noise predictions at greater distances have not been widely vali-
dated due to practical and budgetary limitations, leaving some assumptions about long-range
propagation unverified. This remains the main limitation of the prediction model. As for
CNOSSOS-EU generalized atmospheric conditions and uniform source modeling potentially
obscure local variations. On traffic flows, assumptions are made on roads with fewer than 3
million vehicles annually, where standard value replace data when unavailable. The leading
limitation is the logistical feasibility of frequent updates, that cannot reflect the rapid trends
of noise sources in urban areas.
Once the noise map has been exported to QGIS as polygons with Lden and Lnight at-
tributes, their are cut depending on which SLA they intersects. The mean value is calculated
weighing on the area of noise zones. As the precision is of 5dB, the calculation are made on
the middle value of the range. For DEN values, they are 45-50-55-60-65-70-75 dB and for
night values they are 43-48-53-58-63-68-73.
Although night levels are shortly presented in the report, only day-evening-night noise levels
are analyzed, as noise source, i.e. traffic, doesn’t have data for night time.

2.3.6
Limitations
The analysis of urban metrics using OpenStreetMap (OSM) and other related datasets
has inherent limitations that affect the accuracy and interpretability of results. Firstly, the
street width (SW) and street width to height ratio (SWH) are derived from spatial ap-
proximations, which may not capture real-world variations due to the lack of detailed road
coverage data. Indeed, a default road width of 10m has been applied on roads that are not
documented, which is the majority of them.
Secondly, building attributes, such as height and the number of floors, are inconsistently
reported in OSM. This can result in inaccuracies in metrics like the Floor Space Index (FSI),
especially since not all floors of a building necessarily have the same floor area. Furthermore,
the calculated ratios for commercial, office, and public building functions may be mislead-
ing, as these attributes are often assigned to the whole building in the dataset, ignoring that
usually upper-floor uses are for residential purposes.

2.4.1
Preliminary analysis
First step was to identify the variables that shouldn’t be included in the analysis. It
means that, among the 17 variables, some were strongly correlated. The correlation ma-
trix, of symmetrical form, is calculated and variables corresponding to Pearson’s coefficient
r above 0.9 are eliminated of the study. Indeed, r2 > 0.81 indicates an extremely strong
linear relationship (positive or negative) between variables, suggesting they provide nearly
identical information. Including both in an analysis can lead to redundancy and potential
multicollinearity issues. Thus, one variable is typically omitted to pursue the analysis while
preserving its robustness. This preliminary analysis is conducted independently for every
city, resulting in possibly different sets of parameters.
In order to understand the impact of clustering, one first round of correlation analy-
sis is applied to "raw" data. It gives the opportunity to reveal potential trends between
urban metrics, traffic flows and noise levels. Moreover, it gives a new perspective for the
interpretation of the coefficients on clustered data.
2.4.2
Wu et al. (2024) inspiration
The article from Wu et al. (2024) adopts hierarchical clustering as the core machine
learning technique to classify street patterns. This unsupervised learning method groups
SLAs based on their calculated network metrics, allowing for the identification of patterns
without predefined labels. Hierarchical clustering is particularly suited for this study due to
its flexibility in handling varying granularities of data and its ability to reveal the underlying
structure of street networks. By adjusting the number of clusters, the method can highlight
different levels of detail, from broad urban distinctions to subtle local variations.
Although this project takes great inspiration from Wu et al. (2024) paper, I’ve taken the
liberty to lead different scale of clustering. Indeed, the hierarchical type has been kept,
but in single and double level versions. Additionnally, another clustering, with only keeping
data from the ten most correlated variables has been tested. These methods are assessed
and compared, accorded to their robustness with predicting noise levels in districts in cities.
2.4.3
Clustering scripts
For every clustering type, the same process is applied. First the number of clusters to
extract are determined with two methods of optimization: Summed Square Errors (SSE) and
Silhouette Score (see script). The aim is to find the compromise between maximizing the
silhouette score and reaching the plateau of low SSE. The hierarchical clustering script can
be found in Github. From that algorithm a dendogram and the list of clusters are issued.
To have centroids data with similar structure than districts’, a function calculates their
statistics. In order to have the best correlated variables, another function goes through the
coefficients (see script). All of that is saved in an Excel sheet as exposed in Github.

2.5.2
Generalized Linear Model
Initially, my approach focused on simple linear regressions, analyzing the relationship
between one single parameter and Lden at a time. While this provided insights into individual
contributions, it overlooked the interactions and combined effects of multiple parameters.
Moving to a Generalized Linear Model (GLM) allows for a more advanced and comprehen-
sive analysis. The matrix-based modeling of GLM enables the simultaneous consideration
of all parameters, capturing their combined influence on Lden. Moreover, GLM incorporates
targeted uncertainty quantification for each parameter, offering a clearer understanding of
their respective roles and predictive strengths.
GLM extends the classical linear regression approach by modeling the relationship be-
tween the response variable and multiple predictors in a matrix form. Mathematically, the
GLM can be expressed as:
Y = X · β + ϵ
(5)
where:
• Y is the vector of observed response values in the districts (e.g., Lden in this study).
It has as many rows as they are observation points, i.e. 58 or 61 districts for Paris and
Copenhagen SLAs.

• X is the design matrix, where each row corresponds to a district and each column repre-
sents an urban parameter. There are as many columns as there are urban parameters,
i.e. 14.
• β is the vector of regression coefficients, each representing the contribution of a corre-
sponding parameter to the noise level. There are 14 rows.
• ϵ is the vector of residuals, capturing the deviations between observed and predicted
values. There are specifically claculated to their observation point.
Python script performs a predictive analysis using a GLM with a Gaussian distribution.
It first imports data from an Excel file, imputes missing values using the mean strategy, and
splits the dataset into training and testing sets. After fitting the model, it evaluates the
residuals (differences between observed and predicted values) and visualizes them to assess
the model’s fit. Additionally, it generates a summary of the model’s coefficients, their stan-
dard errors, and statistical significance.
I drew inspiration for my methodology from the study by Mann and Singh (2024), which
demonstrated the importance of addressing spatial and temporal variability in traffic noise
prediction. Their use of a random effect generalized linear model (REGLM) to account for
unobserved heterogeneity in urban environments seemed close to my approach. Similarly to
their work in Delhi, I focused on developing a generalized linear model for one city, intending
to test its predictability in another. This cross-city validation aims to assess the robustness
of the model under varying urban conditions.
However, I did not include random effects in my model, even though GLMM (Generalized
Linear Mixed Model) has a big advantage over GLM. For example, GLMMs account for
random effects like grouping within zones or time periods, which helps capture variations
that a GLM might miss. In this study, the sample size wasn’t large enough to reliably es-
timate the additional complexity of random effects. Additionally, time constraints limited
my ability to run, validate, and interpret a GLMM effectively. For these reasons, I opted to
use a GLM to keep the approach straightforward and manageable while still addressing the
interrelationships of urban parameters.
2.5.3
Assessment of clustering
With combined linear modelizations, criteria such as R2, slope A, intercept B, or AIC,
BIC and Pseudo-R2 were used to evaluate the precision of models.
Regarding linear regression, the determination coefficient R2 gives the level of confidence
we can attribute the noise level variation to the corresponding variable. Especially, it was
used to rank the parameters by importance in the correlation with Lden.
The slope A is issued from the linear regression equation y = A · x + B. It enables a pre-
diction of noise levels (y), starting from variables values (x). When assessing the robustness
of clustering methods, we ensured that methods weren’t contradictory in the sign or value
DUBUISSON G.
41
==PAGE:43==
Master’s Thesis
METHODOLOGY
of this coefficient. Indeed, a positive or negative slope gives opposite information, moreover
when the parameter has a high correlation and so is very influential.
Intercept B was not used when assessing the robustness of clustering. However it’s been
useful when evaluating if Copenhagen’s equations were able to predict Paris’ noise levels.
As for GLM, Python function provided the following criteria to assess the quality of
the model. The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion
(BIC) are metrics used to evaluate the quality of a GLM while balancing goodness of fit and
model complexity. Both penalize the addition of predictors to avoid overfitting, but the BIC
applies a stricter penalty, especially when the sample size is small. Lower AIC or BIC values
indicate a better model.
The Pseudo-R2, on the other hand, measures the proportion of variance in Lden explained
by the model. While it doesn’t hold the same interpretation as R2 in linear regression, it
provides a useful measure of overall model fit. Higher Pseudo-R2 values suggest a better
model.
In evaluating a GLM, AIC and BIC are ideal for comparing multiple models, while pseudo-
R2 offers insight into the explanatory power of the chosen model.
2.5.4
Limitations
There are a few limitations to consider in this assessment process. First, there’s some
subjectivity in how the methods and results are interpreted, especially when weighing the
importance of the different statistical coefficients. The relatively low R2 values in some cases
suggest that the model doesn’t fully explain the variation in noise levels, which limits how
much confidence we can place in the results.
Another challenge was balancing the use of R2 and the slope A. While R2 helps rank the
importance of different factors, the slope A is crucial for making predictions. Keeping these
two in balance, especially when some factors had strong correlations, wasn’t always straight-
forward, especially when the slope varied a lot across clustering methods. This balancing
may have affected the choice of the best clusters and thus the rest of the report.
Regarding the use of Generalized Linear Model, it is to be noted that application to a
limited sample size is unrelevant. The three-criteria evaluation was conducted to be able to
comfort the choice from linear regression.
For the same sizing reason, Generalized Linear Mixed Model was not applied, as the random
effects couldn’t be modeled with so few observation points. The study could have included
smaller SLA to be able to model random effects in the nosie distribution.
Finally, although GLM gives a more detailed model of noise distribution according to urban
parameters, it requires to have access to the whole set of metrics to be able to predict one
district noise level. One-dimension linear regression, despite its relative predictability, allows
to apply the prediction according to a selection of parameters we have access to.

4.1
Which of the clustering methods is the most robust?
Comparison of correlation coefficients.
For both Copenhagen and Paris, the best clustering was when considering only the 10
most correlated variables. Indeed, it was the highest r2. As a reminder, here are the variables
omitted when applying the corresponding method. For Copenhagen, it was Road Length,
Commercial Use ratio, Green Space ratio and Public Use ratio. For Paris, it was Public Use
ratio, Sky View Factor, Road Length and Number of Intersections.
Since intercepts showed a low SD in Table 12, the robustness of the method only relies
on the variation of the slope. Some variables could suffer from misinterpretations if choos-
ing the method "Top10". In Copenhagen for instance, Water Surface, Public Use and Office
Use Ratios would be negatively correlated to Lden although they’re supposed to be positively
correlated with any of the 4 other methods. On the contrary, Number of Buildings or Road
Length are positively correlated with "Top10", in contradiction with other clusterings.
Hence, it seems that "Top10" is not a good idea, when we look further than only r2. However
we can explain why this happens. Indeed, contradicting variables are, for most of them, one
of the variables that were not considered when defining the clusters.
The choice of method for Paris also lies on the minimization of variation for slope A (see
Table 14). Contradicting variables are here: GSI, FSI, Green Space Ratio, Compactness,
Road Length and AADT. They are in a higher number than for Copenhagen because the
standard deviations were significantly higher. Not all misinterpretations can be explained
because of the omitted variables. We should now aim to minimize the variations on variables
that matter in the correlation analysis.
For Paris, the five dominant variables, according to "Top10" r2 are not in contradic-
tion with any other method considering the slope. It should be reasonable to keep "Top10"
method as clustering.
In Copenhagen, Water Surface Ratio and Road Length are respectively 1st and 2nd in r2
ranking but present contradictory slope trend in Table 12. Reasonably, we should consider
the second best method, according to r2, thus the main clusters from the double level hi-
erarchical clustering. But, this would imply to have different clusters elaboration from one
method to the other.

4.2.3.2
GLM equation
As another form of linear model was obtained from Copenhagen data, I tried applying
it on Paris urban observations. On Figure 50 represents the results from the prediction. The
predicted noise level seem to be overall overstated (see Figure 50a). Moreover, the residuals
on Figure 50b are higher and higher depending on the predicted Lden. The blue lines repre-
sent the average Relative Error of 18.59%. For noise level around 75dB, the error can be up
to 20dB above the observed value.
However, the residuals seem to follow a decreasing slope, in regards with predicted value.
The slope is of -0.80, with a determination coefficient of 0.85.
Correction by the λ coefficient is useful yet not sufficient. It would lower the predicted noise
level by 4.6dB, bringing the Relative Error to 12.57%.
On the other way, I tried applying Paris GLM on Copenhagen urban observations. The
results are gathered on Figure 51. Here, the prediction is still above the actual noise level,
with a relative error of 18.36%, very similar to the previous prediction.
Here, the correlation to the slope is almost perfect, with a determination coefficient of 0.98,
and a slope of -1.03.
Here, λ correction would increase the relative error, as it would add 4.6dB to the predicted
noise values, bringing the RE to 32.40%. Thus, it seems that the best strategy would be,
not to apply the λ correction, but to subtract a number to the predicted Lden, accordingly to
the regression equation. The higher the predicted value is, the higher the subtraction should
be. Overall, the mean relative error would be the same than with λ but the districts would
have a correction adapted to their residuals, lowering the individual error rates.
Prediction with Generalized Linear Model is not satisfactory. It could be because of
the over-representation from some district types, as the data are not clustered. Yet, we
concluded that GLM on clusters was not relevant, due to the limited sample size.
However, we came to a different correction strategy from one-dimension regression equa-
tion. It would need to have some noise levels in a minimum of 10 districts in the application
district. Then, we draw the regression line between residuals and predicted value. When
applying this equation on the predicted noise level on all the districts, it would reduce the
relative error, accordingly with the district level.

4.6
Strengths, limitations and implications of this study
1. What is the most effective method for clustering districts based on their urban metrics?
2. Can a predictive model for noise levels be developed using urban parameters?
3. Which urban parameters have the greatest influence on noise levels?
4. How do different urban types respond to these parameters?
These were the sub questions I tried to address while writing my thesis.
The first one was the hardest to answer, as assessment methods were mainly subjective. How-
ever, this report exposed a method, based on theoretical framework and statistical methods,
that showed the assets and weaknesses of every clustering methods I tested. Nevertheless, it
has been shown that building a linear model based on clusters is challenging since they are
too few to present a satisfying determination criterion.
The second question also showed challenges when applying Copenhagen regression equa-
tions to Paris. Once again, I proposed a method to be able to adapt one city’s correlation
model to another one, using a corrective coefficient so the predicted Lden is as close to the
simulated one.
However, this has not been confronted to onsite measured levels in this
project. Moreover it seemed that generalized linear model still presented residuals, despite
correction coefficient.
The most correlated variables with noise levels have been found comparing r2 from
linear regression only. Although, the determination coefficient was not high enough to the
correlation to be satisfying. Some hypothesis issued from the literature review have been
validated or rejected, thanks to the found coefficients.
The sensitivity analysis of parameters on clusters gave some indications on how one pat-
tern reacts to the variation of urban metrics. However clusters showed that they were not
pattern exclusive, as the robustness analysis found. So we couldn’t answer the last question,
as urban types, according to theoretical foundation about urban form, were not gathered
under one cluster. Nonetheless it’s been shown that Paris’ clusters had different influential
parameters.
This study presented several methodological limitations that must be acknowledged.
Firstly, the theoretical framework regarding urban form relies heavily on older references,
specifically Marshall (2005) and Lynch (1981), which may not fully capture contemporary
urban dynamics. The relevance of these works is limited by their age, although the studied
cities predominantly exhibit older urban patterns, somewhat mitigating this issue. Addition-
ally, the application of typologies designed for large urban areas to smaller regions introduces

interpretative challenges despite the scale adaptability of street configurations.
Secondly, about urban pattern, the assessment of the clustering robustness is hindered
by an insufficient number of clusters and inadequate sample sizes. The random sampling
of districts does not adequately represent entire clusters, largely due to time constraints
that limited the expansion of sample sizes. Furthermore, the methodology heavily relies
on subjective pattern recognition, which introduces potential biases, particularly when dis-
tinguishing subtle differences between patterns. Finally, the time didn’t allow me to apply
the whole described process, when it comes to street classification and configuration (see
Figure 10).
Thirdly, regarding data analysis, reliance on OpenStreetMap (OSM) and related datasets
introduces inaccuracies. Sky View Factor estimations are based on spatial approximations
(on street width), often applying a default value due to incomplete road coverage data.
Building attributes, such as height and the number of floors, are inconsistently reported,
affecting the accuracy of metrics like the Floor Space Index (FSI). This inconsistency is
compounded by the generalization of building functions, which can misrepresent mixed-use
buildings.
Population density data also exhibit anomalies, particularly in districts like Osterbro, ne-
cessitating the use of mean values to mitigate extreme outliers. Traffic flow data, such as
Annual Average Daily Traffic (AADT), is unevenly distributed across street segments, lead-
ing to assumptions that may oversimplify the actual traffic distribution.
Urban data that were expressed as ratios, such as Green Space or Water Surface, were some-
times shown as above 1. This value is physically impossible. This can be likely explained
because of the OSMnx features extraction, that considered every features that intercepts the
studied district, even though it’s not fully included in the area.
Finally, statistical analysis faced challenges, particularly with the subjectivity in inter-
preting methods and results. The relatively low r2 values indicated that the models do not
fully capture the variation in noise levels. Balancing r2 and the slope (A) for ranking and
prediction purposes was complex, especially given the variability in slopes across clustering
methods, which may have influenced the cluster selection and overall findings discussed later
on.
Generalized Linear Model was used as a backing to one-dimension linear regression. Al-
though, this method couldn’t be applied on clusters, as the sample size was too small. A
deeper understanding of the model and the inclusion of random effect (GLMM) could have
reinforced the prediction of noise levels.
The study also faced limitations in comparing different cities due to the use of distinct pa-
rameter sets, a consequence of preliminary correlation analyses. This inconsistency prevents
a comprehensive comparison and obscures the influence hierarchy of the parameters, neces-
sitating careful consideration when interpreting dominant parameters.

DISCUSSION
In conclusion, this research project could be significantly enhanced by implementing the
following improvements.
Firstly, expanding the methodological process to include a larger sample of cities would pro-
vide deeper insights into the robustness and relevance of the clustering approach and the
correlation of parameters with Lden.
Secondly, the assessment of clustering robustness based on urban patterns was not con-
ducted as thoroughly as desired. With additional time, the report could have offered greater
clarity on the effectiveness of the method, particularly if the sample size of cities were in-
creased beyond the current two.
Thirdly, while an explanation has been provided for the urban ratios exceeding 1 in some
districts, the project would greatly benefit from refining these variables and the associated
Python scripts. Addressing these issues would enhance the overall accuracy and performance
of the analysis.
Finally, the project lacks insights into noise exposure and noise perception. In fact, per-
ception results from an individual’s experience of noise levels within a specific environment,
and it is ultimately what matters when assessing the effects on human health. Without go-
ing that far, the impact of noise on the urban population could have been analyzed through
noise exposure, converting noise levels into DALYs to evaluate their effects on city residents.
Indeed, urban policies focus on confining the impact of noise on population and do not deal
with urban noise that does not disturb urban residents.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
The project highlighted four takeaways. The study reveals significant differences in how
urban morphology impacts noise levels in Copenhagen and Paris, with each city displaying
distinct noise distribution patterns. In Copenhagen, the most influential parameters affect-
ing noise levels are Annual Average Daily Traffic, Floor Space Index, Compactness, Ground
Space Index and Sky View Factor. Conversely, in Paris, the dominant factors include Water
Surface, Public Use, Green Space, Commercial Use Ratios and Population Density.
Clustering analysis also exhibited varying effectiveness between the two cities. In Paris,
the clusters based on the most correlated parameters provided more consistent results, cap-
turing nuanced variations in noise patterns. Meanwhile, in Copenhagen, the main clusters
issued from double-level hierarchical clustering proved more robust, effectively grouping ur-
ban areas based on noise influence.
Noise prediction models developed using regression equations from Copenhagen demon-
strated a satisfying 4% error rate when applied to Paris. This highlights the importance of
tailoring models to specific city average noise context to enhance predictive accuracy. The
findings emphasize that while models can be transferable, calibration to local urban parame-
ters is crucial. On the other side, generalized linear model didn’t perform in noise prediction,
with an error rate of 16%.
Additionally, Paris’ urban pattern types showed a more significant influence on which
parameters affect noise levels. This suggests that the interaction between urban morphology
and noise is more complex in Paris, leading to variability in parameter significance depending
on what cluster is considered. In contrast, Copenhagen’s clusters remained stable, indicating
a more uniform relationship between urban form and noise levels.
Implications regarding urban planning should thus be different for Copenhagen and
Paris. Of course, diminishing urban road traffic is, by far, the best solution, to combine
with the development of active mobility and common transportation. Road density should
remain the same but attributed to other way of transportation.
While wide streets in Copenhagen could help mitigate traffic noise, Paris should have nar-
rower streets.
In Copenhagen, wider streets should go with lower buildings, as building
height is positively correlated with noise levels. Furthermore, going towards compact build-
ing blocks is the solution for Paris but not for Copenhagen.
As Paris is considered as a high-density city, increasing its density would be a good idea.
However, extreme ground floor index is often seen in historical city center and is not com-
patible with present-day urban policies regarding public services. In Copenhagen, as the city
is not highly densely populated, the policy should aim to lower this density or to go over
the threshold of population. Although, this needs to be discussed in regards with overall life
quality.
