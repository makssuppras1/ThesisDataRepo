Abstract 
Increased greenhouse gas emissions are expected in the future, as fossil fuels are the backbone of 
energy generation for several countries worldwide. Carbon capture and storage (CCS) is a technology, 
which can mitigate the greenhouse gas emissions from fossil fuel fired power. The purpose of CCS 
is to selectively capture CO2 from flue gases.   
In this work, the absorption of CO2 into aqueous liquid solvents was measured in order to determine 
the reaction kinetics between CO2 and these solvents. The four liquid solvents utilized in the 
experiments are 30 wt% MEA, 25 wt% KOH, 25 wt% KOH with 4 wt% PZ, and 35 wt% sodium 
glycinate (NaGly). Experiments were performed using a wetted wall column (WWC) with operating 
temperatures at 20 в„ғ, 40 в„ғ, and 60 в„ғ. The most effective second order rate constant was measured 
for unloaded MEA at 60в„ғ where k2 = 13.94 m3/mol/s. The second order rate constant was determined 
for the other liquid solvents to compare with MEA. It can be concluded that KOH with PZ deviated 
the least from the results of MEA. The liquid solvent NaGly deviated the most. The second order rate 
constant for KOH+PZ at 60 в„ғ was determined to k2= 8.66 m3/mol/s while for NaGly it was k2 = 2.02 
m3/mol/s. The second order rate constant for KOH was determined to 5.96 m3/mol/s. If the 
temperature is higher than 20 в„ғ  then the use of the mentioned liquid solvents will favor the CO2 
kinetics. The liquid solvents were loaded and performed in the same experimental setup in WWC. 
However, NaGly was not performed with a loading. The reason for this was due to complicated issues 
in the laboratory and due to its low second order rate constant obtained for the unloaded NaGly. When 
loading the other liquid solvents, it was with a loading amount of 0.5

Problem Statement 
The project aims to determine the kinetics of novels solvents involving KOH, PZ, and Glycine. These 
solvents are of increasing interest in applications involving solvent-based carbon capture for post 
combustion carbon capture, biogas upgrading, capture from cement plants, direct air capture, etc. Use 
of KOH as a solvent is of interest in Ph-Swing Carbon Capture, which is expected to be highly energy 
efficient in comparison to the conventional temperature-swing capture.  
The task will be to determine the reaction kinetics in amine-H2O-CO2, KOH-H2O-CO2 and sodium 
glycinate-H2O-CO2 systems at varying conditions (initial loading, temperature, and concentration of 
solvents). Modelling and correlations are utilized to support the experimental findings. A discussion 
and comparison of the kinetics of the different solvents will be demonstrated. Additionally, the 
different solvents will be compared with the state of the art 30 wt% MEA. 
 
Learning objectives: 
1. Collect and overview literature, which discuss CO2 capture in relation to different sectors 
2. Review literature concerning reaction kinetics for CO2 in MEA (and the other solvents) 
3. Plan experimental work for known MEA system 
4. Validate reaction kinetics of amine solutions experimentally 
5. Extend experimental campaign on reaction kinetics to the other solvent systems 
6. Correlate properties of amine solutions with KOH, PZ, NaGly 
7. Compare measurement with data from literature 
8. Discuss possible process implications on the use of the novel solvents 
9. Summarize and discuss the obtained results  
10. Disseminate the results in a report and presentation

1. Background and Introduction 
This project focuses on CO2 capture using amine scrubbing. This section will review what CO2 is, 
which plants CO2 can be captured from, and an overview of the different processes used for CO2 
capturing. In addition, an explanation of why the mentioned solvents is used is presented. Finally, the 
number of CO2 capturing plants worldwide, specifically in Europe, will be reviewed. 
 
1.1 What is carbon dioxide? 
The emission of greenhouse gases increases in the atmosphere due to the growing level of 
industrialization and urbanization in several countries worldwide. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is 
volumetrically the most emitted greenhouse gas and is the primary cause of global warming and hence 
climate change [1]. To avoid catastrophic climate change and an increasement in global temperature, 
it is necessary to reduce the emission of greenhouse gases and the level of CO2 [1]. 
To reduce the emission of CO2, it is essential to know where the gas derives from: 
вҖў Which sectors contribute the most?  
вҖў How can we utilize this information to develop effective solutions? 
 
Figure 1 shows greenhouse gas emissions worldwide, indicating that 77% of the greenhouse gas 
emission is CO2, 14% is methane, 8% are nitrous oxide, and the remaining 1% is HFCs, PFCs, and 
SF6 [2]. It is seen that the emission of CO2 mainly derives from the energy sector as electricity and 
heat are generated by burning fossil fuels. The energy is primarily utilized in residential buildings, 
commercial buildings, oil/gas extraction, refining, and processing.  In addition, CO2 derives from 
transportation, other fuel combustion, industry, and fugitive emissions [2]. This includes emissions 
from burning petrol and diesel from road vehicles for cars, motorcycles, etc. It also includes emissions 
from burning other fuels to generate energy, such as biomass and the production of chemicals [2]. 
Furthermore, land-use change contributes to the emission of CO2 by a positive emission of 
deforestation while there is a negative emission of afforestation and reforestation. The other 
greenhouse gases, methane, nitrous oxide, and the remaining gases derive from agriculture, waste, 
and the energy sector [2].

1.2 Industry  
It does not go unnoticed that CCS has the potential to make a significant contribution to achieving 
the goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions. It is essential to achieve a negative CO2 emission to

Currently there are 160 commercial CCS facilities in the project pipeline globally where 29 are fully

1.3 Carbon capture 
One of the essential tools to prevent the climate crisis and achieve the goal of stabilizing the 
greenhouse gas concentration in the atmosphere is to capture CO2 from process gases. Carbon Capture 
and Storage (CCS) works by capturing CO2 and depositing it underground. Following the climate 
agreement for energy and industry of 20 June 2020, Denmark has decided that the capture and storage 
of CO2 must be an essential part of Denmark's climate policy goals [10].  
CO2 from different industries, e.g., biomass combustion, can be captured by various chemical unit 
operations methods. The chemical unit operations are designed from either the cleanliness of the 
desired product or the chemical property of the needed product. Since CO2 can occur in different 
scales, concentrations, pressure, etc., various capturing methods can be used. Several technical 
solutions are available and in use for CO2 capture. It is an industry that is constantly evolving to 
reduce the cost of more innovative solutions [14]. 
The most common CO2 capturing methods are absorption, adsorption, and membrane separation, as 
seen in Figure 4. Absorption is a unit operation where the desired gas is separated from a gas mixture. 
Molecules will cross the surface and move into the solvent after adding a solvent, separating them 
from the gas mixture. [15] Adsorption is a method in which atoms, ions, or molecules settle on the 
surface of a solid, called an adsorbent. In contrast to absorption, a solvent occupies atoms or 
molecules, as illustrated in Figure 4. Using membrane separation to separate gas, one component of 
a gas stream will pass through faster than the other gas. The gas separation depends on differences in 
solubility and diffusion of gases in polymers or different types of membrane materials. Therefore, the 
membrane material must be carefully selected for the process, which depends on separating the gases 
[15]. 
==PAGE:17==
Page 7 of 143 
 
 
Figure 4. Illustration of absorption, adsorption, and membrane separation [17] 
Economically, membrane gas separation is a good alternative, but the separation does not have high 
efficiency and results in low product purity [15]. This means that more membrane separations must 
be used to achieve high product purity, and then a higher energy consumption and generally higher 
capital costs follow. Therefore, more preparation is needed to use the membrane separation on a larger 
scale for CO2 capture. Consequently, it is essential to design the separation based on the desired 
product cleanliness and cost [15] [16]. Absorption and adsorption make up a large part of the CO2 
capture industrially and are advantageous due to their high purity. However, both methods can still 
be economically inefficient. Absorption can also be challenging due to the precision in chemical 
reactions and phase transformations. However, chemical absorption achieves the highest purity of 
99% [18] . 
 
1.3.1. Absorption 
As previously mentioned, absorption is a unit operation where the desired gas is separated from a gas 
mixture. Atoms or molecules will cross the liquid surface and move into the solvent, separating them 
from the gas mixture, as seen in Figure 4. Some of the best-known examples are removing acid gas 
using an alkaline aqueous solution. By absorption, it is possible to regenerate the solvent using 
desorption, where the collected gas is released from the solvent by adding energy or pressure. There 
are two types of absorption methods, chemical absorption, and physical absorption, and in this 
project, the focus will be on chemical absorption [14]. 
 
==PAGE:18==
Page 8 of 143 
 
Chemical Absorption 
A chemical reaction occurs between the gas and the solvent in the absorber. The absorption takes 
place at low temperature, as the solubility of gas is highest at low temperature [14]. On the other 
hand, a change in temperature or pressure must occur in the desorption since the solubility decreases 
when the temperature rises for the gas. Although this method requires a large amount of energy, it 
achieves a high CO2 purity [14]. 
 
 
 
Figure 5. Chemical absorption according to partial pressure and solubility [14]. 
Figure 5 illustrates a gas-liquid equilibrium diagram by chemical absorption. The chart indicates that 
solvents absorb the gas at low partial pressure and low temperature, at approximately 40В°C, while 
desorption happens at a higher temperature, at approximately 120В°C. The graph shows that the gas-
liquid equilibrium exhibits a high absorption even under low gas partial pressure at low temperatures. 
Shifting a reaction equilibrium by heating operation will the chemical solvent containing a solute gas, 
recover the solute gas, and regenerate the solvent. In the case of chemical absorption, a large amount 
of thermal energy must be used to separate the gas from the solvent [14]. 
The most used chemicals for CO2 capture are amines due to the reversible reactions between amine 
and CO2, which enables the CO2 to be captured and released afterwards. Chemical absorption using 
amines is called amine scrubbing. This method has often been used in industry since 1930 [14].

Amine scrubbing  
In amine scrubbing, the CO2 molecules from the process gas bind to the amine by an exothermic 
reaction.  A gas stream meets a liquid stream, which is the amine solvent, leading to the dissolution 
of one or more components in the liquid. Amine scrubbing uses reactions of the amine solvent with 
dissolved CO2 to promote the mass transfer from the gas stream to the fluid [18] 
 
The three primary unit operations in amine scrubbing are absorber, desorber, reboiler, and heat 
exchanger, as illustrated in Figure 6 [19]. 
 
Absorber  
The raw gas added to the system contains CO2 and several impurities. Therefore, it is called mixed 
gas in Figure 6. The mixed gas can be based from gas combustion, biomass combustion, biogas, etc. 
The gas will be added to the absorption column, marked with A in Figure 6, at the bottom at 1-2 bar. 
This gas encounters an unloaded amine solvent counter-current called lean solvent SL [20]. The 
temperature of the unloaded amine will be around 35-50В°C to lower the vapor pressure of the liquid 
stream. In the amine solution, CO2, will be absorbed by the solvent, which causes the amine solution 
to be rich in CO2 at the bottom, SR. The treated gas will leave the absorption column from the top. 
The absorption is exothermic, which results in the rich solvent stream from the bottom having an 
increased temperature [20].  
 
Desorber and reboiler 
The rich amine solution, SR, is passed on to the top of the desorber, marked as D, for regeneration. In 
the desorption column, D, there is a pressure of 1.5-3 bar, and a reboiler, R, at the bottom heats the 
amine solution to a temperature of 120-160В°C. The added energy causes the amine molecule and CO2

2. Theory 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the reaction kinetics and physical properties of CO2 
capture solvents. The studied solvents are MEA, KOH, KOH with PZ and NaGly. The aim is to 
determine the second order reaction rate constant, k2. There are several considerations within this 
determination. The considerations are the two-film theory, material balance, overall, gas- and liquid- 
side mass transfer. 
The setup used to determine the kinetics of CO2 uptake in a solvent solution is the wetted wall column 
(WWC). Previous students have also used the same setup to determine the CO2 capture kinetics of 
amine solvents [37] [38] [39]. This means that the work performed in this project is a continuation of 
their work. In this study the obtained results will be compared to data based on former theses. 
The absorption occurs when CO2 molecules have diffused through an interface from gas to the liquid 
phase. This section describes the different reaction mechanisms between CO2 and the four different 
solvents. The CO2 capture takes place by physically transferring CO2 from the gas mixture to the 
liquid solvent. This is called mass transfer. The mass transfer takes place through thin gas films and 
liquid films that have different resistances. The CO2 from the gas mixture must overcome the different 
resistances so that the mass transfer can take place. The solvents used will react with CO2 that has 
been transferred to the liquid phase. This means that there will be a chemical reaction in the liquid 
phase, which increases the uptake rate in the liquid phase. 
A general description of the WWC utilized for carbon capture is followed by the theory behind mass 
transfer and the two-film theory. In addition, this section describes how the reaction rate constant is 
expressed and how the mass transfer of CO2 through the interface can be affected by the reaction 
kinetics.

2.4 Mass Transfer 
The transfer of a component from one phase to another is called mass transfer [46].  Figure 9 shows 
the type of absorber used in the chemical scrubbing, a wetted wall column.  
In Figure 9, the focus is only on the absorption, and therefore, there is a zoomed-out drawing of the 
absorber. The gas stream that enters the absorption column is a mixed gas of CO2 and impurities. 
When the gas is treated and CO2 is absorbed in the solvent, the treated gas will leave the column from 
the top, as shown in Figure 9. The solvent will exit the absorber as a rich solvent, as it has absorbed 
the CO2. The faded yellow colour, in Figure 9, focuses on the performance inside the absorber. The 
blue arrows illustrate the liquid solvent flowing down the WWC. The orange arrows indicate the gas 
inlet, where the gas is flowing up. The gas mixture is CO2 and N2, as shown in Figure 9. The liquid

==PAGE:33==

Figure 9. Illustration of the absorption in the wetted wall column. 
In the two-phase system, the mass transfer from one phase to another is caused by diffusion. Diffusion 
is a process resulting in the random movement of particles moving from an area of high concentration 
to an area of low concentration [47]. The solvent used in the system does not have a high 
concentration of CO2. Therefore, the concentration of CO2 in the gas phase will be much higher than 
the concentration of CO2 in the liquid phase. This high concentration of CO2 in the gas phase makes 
the transfer of CO2 from the gas phase to the liquid phase easier. This transportation is called mass 
transfer. Diffusion will continue until equilibrium is achieved throughout the system, and a steady 
state will be reached [65].  
The mass transfer concept is the aspect of mass transferred in a certain distance through a medium in 
order to achieve a specific purpose. This happens when the forces applied to the mass are greater than 
the resistance in the medium. The forces can be described by the driving forces consisting of physical 
properties between mass and medium. The physical driving force properties include gradients of 
temperature, concentration, or pressure while the resistance in the medium is caused by density, 
viscosity, or thickness of transfer layer [65].

The absorption of CO2 in the chemical scrubbing is divided into two steps, physical absorption, and

bulk gas phase to the interface, followed by the transport of CO2 from the interface to the bulk of the 
solvent [41].

2.6 Sensitivity analysis 
The sensitivity analysis defines how the changes in input variables (measured values used in 
equations) influence the output variables (target variables). The physical or chemical property is 
calculated by an equation consisting of multiple measured variables as stated in the previous section. 
It is essential to consider the importance of how sensitive the calculated property (target variable) is 
to changes [39].  
Sensitivity is defined as the percentage of change in the output variable compared to the specified 
change in the input variable. There are two general methods to perform the sensitivity analysis; 
simulation method and differential method [39].  
The simulation method consists of simulating a change by changing a small percentage of an input 
variable and determining the percentage change in the output variable [39].  
The sensitivity analysis will be performed on diffusion, k2, and the enhancement factor variable [39].

3. Experimental setup and work 
The purpose of the experiments was to investigate the CO2 capture kinetics. In this chapter, the 
materials used for the experiments, the experimental setup of the WWC, a procedure and an overview 
of the experiments are presented. A total of 20 experiments were performed and a description of each 
experiment will be provided.   
 
3.1 Materials 
The chemicals used for the experiments are monoethanolamine (MEA, >99%), piperazine (PZ, 99%), 
sodium hydroxide (NaOH, 98-100%), glycine (Gly, >99%), potassium bicarbonate (KHCO3, >99.5), 
potassium carbonate (K2CO3, >99%) and potassium hydroxide (KOH, >85%). All the chemicals were 
obtained from Sigma Aldrich and were used without further purification. 
The gases CO2 and N2 were supplied from Air Liquid and were both with purities of 99.99%. 
 
3.2 Experimental setup 
3.2.1. Apparatus 
The absorption flux in the gas-liquid system is measured with the WWC. A technical scheme of the 
WWC used in the experiments is illustrated in Figure 17 while on Figure 18 the experimental setup 
in the laboratory is shown. 
On Figure 17 it is seen that the setup consists of a liquid system (blue) and a gas system (red). The 
two phases are in contact with each other inside the reaction chamber. Firstly, the liquid is stored in 
a liquid reservoir and pumped through a cycle. The liquid passes a flowmeter, and then it flows into 
a water bath with an integrated heat exchanger, then it enters the reaction chamber. Here it flows 
down the outside of the stainless-steel pipe, creating a thin liquid film. The liquid then exits at the 
bottom of the reaction chamber, being pumped into the liquid reservoir again. This way, the liquid 
system is closed and in recirculation. The two gases, CO2 and N2, come from two separate gas bottles 
and are mixed using two mass flow controllers. The mixed gas passes through a pre-saturator at 
ambient temperature and then another saturator inside the water bath, which is set to the desired 
temperature placed in the water bath. This will ensure complete saturation of the gas mixture. 
==PAGE:60==
Page 50 of 143 
 
After the last saturator, the gas can either enter bypass or enter the reaction chamber. When the gas 
enters the reaction chamber, it will get in contact with the liquid flowing down the stainless pipe. 
After this, the gas will exit the reaction chamber with a changed composition depending on the 
absorbed CO2. When the gas leaves the reaction chamber, the pressure is measured with an 
overpressure gauge (can measure in a range of 0 вҖ“ 10 barg) and then the gas enters a condenser where 
the water vapor of the gas is condensed. After the condenser, the gas meets the CO2 probe. However, 
if the gas enters bypass, the gas will not enter the reaction chamber but instead it will go directly to 
the condenser and then the CO2 probe. This means it is possible to measure the CO2 content before 
and after the WWC while the parameters (CO2 content, pressure, flowmeter and the temperature of 
gas and liquid) are measured in both cases.

3.2.2. Procedure 
The solvents were each mixed with demineralized water.  
The experimental procedure is as follows: 
a. The water bath was set to the desired temperature while starting the condenser.  
b. The whole apparatus was flushed with pure N2 for approx. 20 minutes and then the gas stream 
shifted to bypass mode. 
c. The solvent liquid was pumped into the liquid reservoir until it was filled up. 
d. Solvent liquid enters the system while ensuring that the stream is stable and air bubble free. 
e. The desired values of CO2 and N2 were set, still on bypass mode.  
f. When the CO2 concentration, pressure, and the temperature of gas and liquid were stable in 
10 minutes, the gas was ready to enter WWC, and bypass mode was off.

3.4.1. Preparation of the solvents and the system 
The first experiment was performed with MEA. 5 kg of solvent mixture with 30 wt% MEA was made. 
However, when performing the experiments with other liquid solvents, the amount was reduced to 3 
kg as this was sufficient. The preparation mixture of each liquid solvent can be seen in Appendix 2.  
Before each experiment was performed, the system was entirely emptied by blowing pressurized air 
through an opened pipe. Afterwards, the system was purified with demineralized water, and nitrogen 
was run throughout the system to remove the remaining CO2. Then nitrogen bottles are opened, and 
nitrogen is sent into the system. The condenser is switched on to ensure condensation of liquid 
impurities. The solvent was then injected into the liquid pump, and the pump was started. The 
pumping of solvent from the container continues until solvent comes out of the container valve. Then 
close the container valve to have a closed system. Then it is observed whether the flow meter shows 
a stable flow, and if there are bubbles in the system, then a valve is opened to release air from the 
system. The solvent is then sent up to the column and is observed to ensure a laminar flow on the 
outer tube and a sufficiently small liquid layer so that no solvent enters the gas stream. A saturator 
heats the solvent to the needed temperature, and when the solution has reached the required 
temperature, CO2 can be sent into the system. 
 
3.4.2. Operation of the experiment and measurements 
When a stable thin layer of liquid solvent is flowing down the stainless-steel pipe of the WWC, CO2 
is sent into the system. Before this can be done, the gas flow must be controlled through a computer 
program called FlowView, where the N2 and the CO2 fractions are controlled from. FlowView allows 
the user to set any desired volume flow for N2 and choose a ratio of CO2 corresponding to the volume 
flow of N2. The communication is set up with a master slave system, where the N2 mass flow 
controller is the master, the CO2 mass flow controller is the slave, and the pressure controller is the 
local host. It is therefore, impossible to adjust the CO2 flux independently from the nitrogen flux. 
Channel 1 in FlowView shows the whole mixture, while Channel 2 shows the nitrogen, and Channel 
3 is the CO2 channel, as seen in Figure 19. The gas stream is set in normal litre per min in the downer 
field. The factors that are set in for the CO2 correspond to the setpoint for the nitrogen stream. For 
example, if you are running an experiment with a factor of 150% for CO2 and a volume stream of 
nitrogen of 2 l/min (which equals a 10% setpoint), the setpoint on the CO2 controller will be set to 
15% ((1.5 вҲҷ0.1) вҲҷ100% = 15%). It will result in a volume flow of 0.3 nl/min of CO2. The reason 
this setpoint results in a volume stream of 0.3 nl/min and not in 3 nl/min, which would be expected 
==PAGE:65==

of the nitrogen is 20 nl/min and the mass flow controller corresponds with the setpoint to the 
maximum possible volume flow. This means that the gas flow of CO2 during operation is regulated 
using the program FlowView, which can be seen in Figure 19.

4.6 Sensitivity analysis 
In the various calculations, it has been assumed that the density, viscosity, Henrys constant and the 
diffusion coefficient for the liquid solvents are the same as for water. These assumptions can influence 
and change the value of the second order rate constant. This must now be analyzed, by doing a 
sensitivity analysis. Through a sensitivity test of the second order reaction rate constant as a function 
of viscosity, density, diffusion coefficient and henrys constant, this section will examine the accuracy 
of these assumptions. Viscosity, density, Henrys constant and diffusion coefficient are input 
parameters, as they have direct and/or indirect influence on the experimental results and the calculated 
values. 
A dynamic simulation method has been performed, where all calculated values will change when one 
of the parameters are changed. This means that if density changes by a certain percentage, then some 
values will change which leads to k2 also changing by a certain percentage. 
The density and viscosity of the liquid solvent is a variable in different equations, like the liquid flow, 
film thickness and the concentration of solvent. An Excel sheet was made to investigate the changes 
in the second order rate constant where the density was changed Вұ40% deviation and the viscosity, 
Henrys constant and the Diffusion coefficient was changed  Вұ60% deviation. The sensitivity analysis 
is made for the unloaded mixture of 25 wt% KOH + 4 wt% PZ at 40 В°C to get an insight into what 
these variables can change at k2.  
Figure 48 shows the sensitivity analysis of the viscosity and density where the viscosity changes 
Вұ60% and the density changes Вұ40% deviation. The following figure shows the analysis that presents 
the effect of the density and viscosity on the k2 value at 40 В°C for KOH+PZ. 
==PAGE:118==
Page 108 of 143 
 
 
Figure 48. Sensitivity analysis of k2 by changing the viscosity and density. 
Figure 48 shows that an increase in viscosity will decrease the k2-value and the opposite tendency 
occurs for the density, where an increase in the density will increase the k2-value. An increase in 20% 
density will increase the k2-value with 16% while a decrease in -20% density will decrease the k2-
value with 25%. An increase with 20% viscosity will decrease the k2-value with -10% while a 
decrease with -20% will increase the k2 value with 12%. This means that the viscosity and density of 
the solvent have a major influence on the second order rate constant. Therefore, it will be necessary 
for future studies and projects to measure the right density and viscosity, to achieve more accurate 
results.  
The content and temperature of the liquid solvent have effect on how the density varies and commonly 
the density only varies by Вұ10% deviation. It can be seen on sensitivity analysis that density has the 
greatest impact on the k2 value, as already at -40% change in density, k2 will decrease by -67% while 
a -60% change in viscosity, k2 will decrease by -53%. The temperature has a significant impact on 
the viscosity, which varies greatly amongst mixes. The main reason for the viscosity's impact on k2 
is that the diffusion coefficient depends on it. An increase in viscosity causes the diffusion coefficients 
to decrease, which raises the k2.

Future work 
In this section, suggestions for future work will be presented, as during the experiments several factors 
were considered that could be changed or improved. In addition, a suggestion to achieve more 
effective reaction kinetics is provided by performing the experimental work with a different liquid 
solvent.  
Future work for the experimental setup: 
o The experimental setup could be changed so that greater liquid flow is possible to run inside 
the column. This must be considered to avoid air bubbles. 
An example of a change could be that the gas pipes where the gas enters the column could be 
longer so that more liquid could be in the column without the problem occurring. 
o It is recommended to run with stable liquid flow in all experiments, as this will make the 
measurements easier to work with. This was not possible in this experiment as air bubbles 
appeared in the system and therefore the liquid flow was adjusted frequently. 
o A CO2 fraction greater than 20% could not be measured by the experimental equipment. To 
examine how the various solvents performed when using larger CO2 percentages, future 
studies may involve switching out the CO2 probe which can measure a higher threshold. 
o It is suggested to use another flowmeter for the experiment, as it is not measuring accurately. 
A flowmeter that is perhaps more readable, like an electric flowmeter, could be a suggestion. 
o In addition, it has been suggested to use a better isolation of the tubes as it could lead to more 
accurate results. It was observed during the experiments that a large amount of heat was lost 
when the liquid flowed from the heat exchanger in the main saturator into the column.  
o In addition, it is recommended to replace the pressure gauge with a pressure gauge that 
measures more accurately.  
 
Future work for the liquid solvents: 
o It is recommended to test further on the KOH and KOH+PZ solutions, as it showed effective 
results. It could perhaps be investigated with a higher weight percentage, where i.e. KOH is 
examined. 
o The same can be investigated for NaGly. Although NaGly achieved the least effective results, 
using 35 wt% NaGly, it may be possible to investigate using a higher weight percent NaGly, 
==PAGE:123==
Page 113 of 143 
 
which might make the kinetics faster. It is also recommended to test it in a mixture with 
another solvent, which might speed up the reaction. 
o It is recommended to study the reaction kinetics using a WWC with a mixture of deep eutectic 
solvents as Choline Chloride and urea. Deep eutectic solvents are liquids prepared by 
combining two or three inexpensive and safe chemicals. The choline chloride-based deep 
eutectic solvents have the potential to be used as liquid absorbents for CO2 separation/capture.

SOP for wetted wall column 
General Information 
The wetted wall column is a gas/liquid contactor. It consists of a gas system, a liquid system and a 
reactor chamber in which both phases get in contact with each other. The liquid enters the chamber 
by rising inside the metal pipe until the opens and the flowing down the outside of the pipe creating 
a thin liquid layer all around. The gas is injected at the bottom with 3 gas-inlets and flowing up 
countercurrently to the gas outlet on top. The liquid film flowing down and the gas stream flowing 
up are in contact and depending on the thermodynamics some components going from one phase to 
the other phase. By measuring the concentration before and after the wetted wall column and by 
knowing the total gas stream it is possible to calculate the mass transfer of a component. With the 
liquid flow and dimensions of the reaction chamber it is possible to calculate the mass transfer per 
area and the overall mass transfer resistance. With correlations for the gas side mass transfer 
resistance it is possible to determine kinetics of reactions in the liquid phase.

2. Liquid system 
The liquid system (blue part of  
 consists of a liquid reservoir, where the liquid is stored, a sho rate flowmeter, a heat exchanger, the 
reaction chamber and a pump. The liquid is pumped from the wwc into the liquid reservoir. When 
the liquid reservoir is closed  the liquid is further pumped through a sho rate flowmeter, the height 
of the ball gives information of the liquid flow. After that the liquid enters the heat exchanger that is 
built into the lid of water bath. Then the liquid enters the wetted wall column. The standing liquid at 
the bottom of the wetted wall column is then pumped into the liquid reservoir to close the loop. The 
temperature of the liquid is measured before (Sensor T1) the wetted wall column and can be read 
out on the computer with the Datalogger software. 
2.1. 
Filling Liquid into the Reservoir 
Before filling liquid into the system and circulation the liquid you should have a small gas stream 
running inside the apparatus to prevent the liquid entering the gas system. Set therefore the N2 
stream on 0,1 L/min, read more about how to set the gas stream in the next section 2. Operate the 
gas stream. 
Before pumping in the liquid check the following: 
Гј Check that the liquid tank is empty 
Гј Valve at bottom of liquid is closed 
Гј Valve at the top of liquid tank is open to ensure that the liquid tank is first filled before the 
liquid enters the flowing system and the wetted wall column. Attach a rubber pipe at the top 
opening and place the open end into the sink on the side of the reactor.  
Гј Place the dead end red pipes into the liquid container, set three way valve pointing away from 
the liquid container and open the closed valve on the other red pipe.  
Гј Fill a liquid syringe and pump some liquid into the blue pipe right before the pump, until the 
liquid is flowing back into the liquid container. 
Now the pumping can start, the total volume of the tank is about 2.2 L or 0.7L, depending with tank 
is used. In the beginning the pumping speed can be kept high, but after about 2 l or half a liter the 
speed should be reduced. Continue pumping until the liquid exits at the top of the tank and rises in 
==PAGE:146==
Page 136 of 143 
 
the rubber pipe, a slow speed will ensure that there is enough time to act until the solvent runs into 
the sink. The close the top of the liquid reservoir and continue pumping into the column. One the 
liquid flow is flowing constantly out of the wetted wall column back into the liquid container and 
the flows are steady, the position of the three way valve on one red pipe is changed into the opposite 
direction and the valve on the other red pipe is closed. 
2.2. 
Experimental run 
Since the flowmeter is dependent on the temperature and placed before the heat exchanger, make 
sure that all liquid has the same temperature; this can be done by pumping around the liquid with 
working water bath for about 45 to 60 min. You should be able to feel that the temperature of the 
liquid reservoir is about the temperature of the absorption. The volume flow of the liquid is set by 
the pump speed and can be regulated with the controller on the flowmeter. The level of the liquid in 
the wwc is dependent on the pressure in the system. In order to lower the liquid level in the wwc 
open the valve on top of the liquid reservoir for a very short time, this will interrupt the flux 
entering the wwc. For a stable liquid flow it is better to not fully open the valve, otherwise it could 
be that the liquid flow interrupts and the pump is not able any more to provide stable film.  
Make sure, that the gas outlet is not connected to the CO2 probe and put it on a dry paper towel, to 
make liquid transportation in the gas system visible. For a normal start up close the valve with 
gasinlet to the wwc and start running the pump at higher speed until you can see a stable ripple free 
film on the wwc: Now you can reduce the pumping speed, this will lead to a rise in the liquid level 
of the wwc chamber, open the valve on top of the liquid reservoir slighty until you see that the level 
decreases. Repeat this step until you are in the desired range of your experiments, i.e. you can 
recognize the movement of the ball in the flowmeter. Small adjustments can be done with the 
regulator on the flowmeter. Be aware, that the switch of the gas stream changes from calibration 
loop to wwc as well as the change of the gas mixture through the MFC (mass flow controller) 
changes the pressure of the system, therefore it is recommended to release some pressure from the 
system before switching valves by opening the top valve on the liquid reservoir. You will also most 
probably experience that the height of the ball in the flowmeter changes when you switch from 
calibration loop to wwc. The gas composition or the total flow should never be changed, when the 
gas is running through the wwc, this can lead to flooding of the column and solvent entering the gas 
system and contacting the CO2 probe, switch to the calibration loop before changing the gas stream 
composition or volume stream. 
2.3. 
Calibration of the flowmeter 
The height of the liquid ball in the liquid flow is dependent on the flowrate as well as the viscosity 
and density and is therefore solvent and temperature dependent. The flowmeter should be calibrated 
when a new solvent will be used or a new temperature will be investigated. To do that, take a 2 l 
conic beeker add about 1 to 1.5 liter of solvent to it and place it in the waterbath, use the lid opening 
to stabilize the beeker position. Turn on the waterbath to the desired temperature and start filling the 
liquid reservoir with the solvent (as described in the the chapter handling the liquid), stop when the 
liquid comes out of the pipe on top of the reservoir. Detach the pipe, going from the flowmeter to 
the heat exchanger and attach an open pipe at this position, place the open end into the 2 l conic 
beeker and place the suction pipe as well there. Now you can start pumping the liquid around. The 
liquid in the liquid reservoir must have the same temperature as the water bath, because this will be 
the temperature for which the flowmeter will be calibrated. You can fasten this process by warming 
up the liquid before pumping in to the liquid reservoir, but make sure this will not change the 
composition or loading of the solvent. To make sure, that the liquid in the waterbath is at the desired 
temperature you can detach the temperature sensor T1 from the wwc and put it into the beeker and 
==PAGE:147==
Page 137 of 143 
 
follow the temperature with the Datalogger program. If the experiments should be continued after 
the calibration using the identical solvent, check with the BaCl2 method whether the CO2 loading 
has changed or not. For stops the time and pump some of the liquid into another beeker; weigh the 
amount stop the time, repeat it several times at different heights of the ball. Notate the mass of the 
liquid, the time and the height of the ball in the flowmeter. Check during the run that the height of 
the ball does not change, for this purpose it is also very important that during the pumping the open 
end pipe is in a stable position, because the height of the open end pipe has direct influence on the 
volume flow, you will observe a movement of the ball if you move the open end of the pipe up and

3. Gas system 
The gas system (red part in Fejl! Henvisningskilde ikke fundet.) consists of 2 big gas bottle with 
purest (4.5-5.0) nitrogen and carbon dioxide, a Bronkhorst mass flow control (MFC) device, that 
makes it possible to mix the two gases with a desired ratio using the PC software, a presaturator, 
humidifying the gas stream at room temperature and a saturator, saturating the gas stream at the 
absorption temperature; with 2 valves the gas stream can be directed to the calibration loop or the 
wwc. After the wwc or the calibration loop, the gas temperature, gas pressure will be measured and 
after a pressure gauge, where the gas pressure is set to ambient pressure the CO2 concentration is 
measured with an NDIR CO2 probe. The data of the gas temperature and pressure as well as the 
CO2 probe can be read out at the computer (software: Datalogger).

connected to the gas inlet and the lower one to the gas outlet. Disconnect the gas purge from the 
CO2 probe to avoid contact of liquid with the CO2 probe in the start up phase, make sure the last

Flowview: This program allows us to set any desired volumeflow for N2 and choose a ratio of CO2 
corresponding to the volumeflow of N2. The communication is set up with a master slave system, 
where the N2 MFC is the master the CO2 MFC is the slave and the pressure controller is the local

number into the white field above вҖңAdvancedвҖқ or by entering a вҖңSetpointвҖқ. In the downer field you 
set the gas stream in norm liter per min ( which is reference to a gas stream with a temperature of 0 
ВәC and a pressure of 1013 mbar), whereas in the setpoint field you enter a value in percentage 
corresponding to the total capacity, which is 20 nl/min. So entering 10.0 in the downer field is equal 
to entering 50% in the setpoint field. The Controller accepts both values and regulates to the last 
entered value.

The factor you are setting in for the carbon dioxide corresponds to the setpoint for the N2 stream, 
for example if you are running an experiment with a factor 150 % for CO2 and a volume stream of 
N2 of 2 l/min (which equals a 10 % setpoint), the setpoint on the CO2 controller will be set to 15 % 
(1.5*0.1=0.15=15%) and will result in a volume flow of 0.3 nl/min. The reason this setpoint results 
in a volume stream of 0.3 nl/min and not in 3n l/min which would be expected with a factor of 
150% is that the maximum capacity of the CO2 MFC is 2nl/min and of the N2 is 20 nl/min and the 
MFC corresponds with the setpoint to the maximum possible volume flow.

Datalogger: This program reads out all the electrodes for this setup and plots them over time. It is 
also possible to implement correlations in order to get direct values instead the sensor-signal in mA. 
In this setup, the temperature of the liquid entering the wwc (sensor T1), the gas temperature after 
the wwc (sensor T2), the gas temperature at the CO2 probe (sensor T3),  the pressure of the system 
close to the wwc as well as the concentration of the CO2 in the exhaust gas will be measured and 
recorded by this program. To get started contact the technican or person in charge of this apparatus 
to help you set up a configuration file for this program, you can also just try another config file from 
the saved data that should generally work if no changes have been done on this Datacollector. 
Before starting the program make sure, that all temperature probes, pressure probe and CO2 probe 
are connected and the Agilant Data Acquisition box is turned on. Now click on top on the Scan and

the gas concentration reached a value of 20 %. These electrodes can just be used inside the 
published range of the producer.

It is possible to calibrate the electrodes and implement this into the Datalogger program. To do so 
you need to have a reference standard for your probe, e.g. a pressure cell that can accurately 
measure pressure. You need to connect this reference cell to your pressure probe and note the actual 
pressure and the response of the sensor given in mA by the Datalogger program. If you have several 
pressure points and the corresponding sensor response you should plot the sensor response in mA 
(x-axis) over the actual pressure in bar, the linear correlation can be implemented when no 
experiments are running by choosing вҖңConfigure ChannelsвҖқ on the top and set a вҖңgain (M)вҖқ which 
is the slope and a вҖңOffset (B)вҖқ which corresponds to the y-axis intersection. In the field вҖңLabelвҖқ you 
can choose the value, e.g. bar or Celsius. After all sensors are calibrated and the channels are 
configured, make sure to check the probes again and see if the values given by the Datalogger are in 
agreement with some reference probes. 
It is mainly not necessary to calibrate the CO2 probe and implement this into Datalogger, since this 
will allow you to switch probes with a different spectrum during the experiment. The calibration 
can be done during the experiment when the stream is bypassed. Notate the set CO2 concentration 
and the associated response of the CO2 probe in mA and make sure that You account for the water 
vapor, that alters the real CO2 concentration of the gas stream.

3.2. 
Experimental run 
The gas system needs the longest time to start up in the system because it is very temperature 
sensitive. Make sure the pre-saturator and saturator are filled with approx. 500-600 g of DI water 
and connected properly and set the wanted temperature in the water bath. Close the fume hood and 
let the gas system warm up, if the system warms up to slow use a convection fan to warm up the 
surrounding air, but keep the fume hood as closed as possilble. When the whole system is heated up 
and the liquid is circulated around, you can set a CO2 concentration while being on the bypass. 
Monitor on Datalogger program, that the pressure, CO2 concentration and temperature of the gas 
system is stable(notate, CO2 signal and pressure in excel file), before switching the valves to go into 
the WWC, this can take between 10 and 15 min. Await until the gas system stabilizes again and 
notate temperature (Celsius), CO2 signal (mA), height of flowmeter (mm), pressure (mbar); Do not 
change the flowrate or composition, when the gas stream runs through the wwc, this can lead to 
flooding of the column and then liquid enters the gas system and the CO2 sensor gets in contact 
with the solvent. After running this test with different gas composition take out the liquid and wash 
the wetted wall column with DI water for several minute, make sure that both the wwc column as 
well as the reservoir are free of any chemical solvent. An example for
