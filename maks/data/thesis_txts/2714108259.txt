Summary
PuriﬁAudio is specialized in high-end transducers and audio ampliﬁers for audio
enthusiasts and to be included in commercial products. Their ampliﬁer module
1ET400A needs to be driven by a power supply, which they need to get from
third parties. They are therefore interested in making their own power supply.
The goal of the project is to design, build and test an AC-DC converter to be
used for the 1ET400A ampliﬁer module. This requires a bipolar output which
can handle bus pumping. Furthermore, it must include Power Factor Correction
(PFC) and an EMI ﬁlter in order to satisfy regulations for commercial products.
The main focus of this project is the DC-DC converter, which is based on the
topology in [1] with the diodes replaced by MOSFETs.
This topology have
bidirectional current capabilities in order to handle the bus pumping, which
can greatly reduce the size of the output capacitors. In order to reduce the
complexity of the circuit, the primary side switches are driven by a constant
PWM and the secondary side switches are driven directly from the transformer.
Audio is often listened at lower power levels at home, and it is therefore desired
to increase the eﬃciency in that range. The components are therefore chosen
and dimensioned to reduce the power losses at lower power levels.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Project Background
PuriﬁAudio is a company specialized in making world class commercial audio
products. Their transducers and ampliﬁers are made for DIY audio enthusiasts
and to be integrated into third party audio systems.
They have developed transducers with ultra low distortion and the 1ET400A
class-D ampliﬁer module to match their performance. The ampliﬁer module
needs to be driven by a third party power supply, and the company is therefore
interested in developing their own power supply.
The power supply needs to provide a bipolar output and be able to handle
bus pumping. Since the power supply is connected to the mains network, it
must be designed to satisfy current regulations on EMI and power factor. Since
commercial ampliﬁers are often set at moderate to low volume at home, it is
desired to focus on increasing the eﬃciency at low power levels.
==PAGE:26==
2
Introduction
1.2
Prior Art
The Hypex SMPS400A [2] power supply is often used for the ampliﬁer mod-
ule. This provides the main bipolar output rails and various auxiliary outputs
required by the ampliﬁer module.
It handles bus pumping by having a bi-
directional DC-DC converter, as indicated by the two quadrant operation.
The output voltage is proportional with the input AC voltage, and is therefore
unregulated. This is acceptable for the typical 230 V, but the ampliﬁer will not
be able to deliver the full rated power at lower AC voltage.
Looking at the eﬃciency for the full power range, it is seen that it will have to
deliver more than 40 W to reach 80 % and more than 150 W to reach 90 %. The
power supply is therefore not very eﬃciency at lower power levels.
Consider also adding papers for prior art
1.3
Problem Description
This project aims to design and build an AC-DC converter which is able to
drive the 1ET400A ampliﬁer module. Only the main bipolar output rails are
considered to limit the scope of the project.
The main focus is on the DC-DC converter, where it is desired to ﬁnd a topology
with bi-directional current ﬂow to eliminate the need for large output capacitors
to control bus pumping. Care will be taken to increase the eﬃciency at lower
power levels to better match the use case for commercial ampliﬁers.
Since the aim of the project is a commercial product, it must satisfy the current
regulations on Power Factor and EMI. This required the use of a PFC controller
to shape the input current and an EMI ﬁlter to mitigate the conducted noise
going into the mains network.

Operating Principle
The AC-DC converter is responsible for converting the mains AC voltage to a
DC voltage, which the following DC-DC converter can utilize to provide the
outputs for the ampliﬁer. This can be made using a full bridge diode rectiﬁer,
which is simple and cheap to implement. However, it is necessary to add Power
Factor Correction (PFC) to comply with regulations on power factor and current
harmonics [3], [4].
The PFC can be implemented with a boost converter topology as seen in ﬁg. 2.1.
The full bridge rectiﬁer and input capacitor Cin rectify and smooth the mains
voltage.
A PFC controller switches the MOSFET Q in order to shape the
current waveform iac to be proportional to the input voltage Vac. Furthermore,
it is used to control the output voltage Vo to the DC-DC converter. This also
makes it possible to work at a wide range of AC voltages, since the topology
can boost the input voltage to a desired DC output.

to the simplicity and build-in start-up circuit [5].

A requirement of the DC-DC converter is to handle the power supply pumping
from the ampliﬁer. A simple solution is to add large output capacitors to limit
the voltage ripple to a level where it won’t have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the audio
distortion or result in an over-voltage condition. This will however reduce the
power density of the converter since the large capacitor size required will take
up a lot of space. An example of this is seen in [1] where output capacitors in
the hundreds of µF is needed to limit the output voltage ripple to a few volt.
A solution to this problem is designing the converter to be bidirectional, so it
can consume the current fed back from the ampliﬁer. A converter design has
been proposed in [1] which consist of a half-bridge inverter on the primary side
and a current bidirectional rectiﬁer on the secondary side made of two diodes

3.3.2
Driving the Switches
Some circuitry is required to control the switches in the circuit. The switches
should be driven at a constant 50 % duty cycle, and a small dead time is re-
quired to avoid shoot-through between the supply rails both on the primary-
and secondary side.
Primary-side Switches
The primary-side switches are driven by a high- and low-side gate drive IC to
enable fast turn-on and turn-oﬀ. The Fairchild FAN7392 have been chosen since
it can operate up to 600 V, source and sink up to 3 A and have separate inputs
for the high-side and low-side switch signals, which makes it possible to drive it
from an external function generator with adjustable dead time [14].

Power Losses and Component Choice
The power losses consist of the total switching losses and the conduction losses.
These are calculated for a selection of MOSFETs and evaluated in order to
choose suitable components for the converter.
The primary side switches are controlled by a gate driver with a constant dead
time. This means that an increasing current through the primary side winding
results in the switches experiencing hard switching, Reduced Voltage Switching
(RVS), Zero Voltage Switching ZVS, and body diode conduction. The dead time
will therefore be chosen to achieve ZVS for a desired signal amplitude, A = Azvs.
This is chosen to minimize losses at lower power levels without causing too much
of an increase in switching losses at higher power levels.
The dead time is calculated based on the current through the primary winding,
Ip, at the time both switches are in the oﬀ-state, which will be assumed constant
for simplicity. This current will charge the output capacitance Coss of one switch

Hardware Design and Assembly
3.5.1
PCB Design
The PCB design was made in KiCAD using the component symbols and foot-
prints available in the included component library where possible. If none were
available, they were made based on the component datasheet and manual mea-
surements. The ﬁnal circuit schematic and layout can be seen in appendix D.
The clearance between conductors are deﬁned by IPC-2221B [35, Table 6-1]
for category B4, external conductors with permanent polymer coating and the
design constraints are set by the manufacturers manufacturing capabilities. The
primary side and secondary side are separated by a 5 mm barrier to ensure proper
isolation.
It is important to avoid coupling between the power traces and the signal traces
to ensure the gate signals are not disturbed by electrical noise, this is especially
important for the switch-nodes which carry large currents and switch at high
voltage and high frequency. This can be seen on the primary side, where the
traces Vin, SN, MP and GND are implemented as power planes stacked on all
layers. This provides a low resistance current path and avoid excessive capacitive
coupling with signal layers.
The primary side ground plane is also split to
provide a separate current path for the power- and signal electronics.
The
switch nodes on the secondary side, SNA and SNB, are placed above each other
with no other traces between them.
The primary side gate driver needs to switch Q1 and Q2 fast. This requires
good decoupling and grounding to ensure the gate driver can source and sink
current from the gate properly. This is done by placing the decoupling- and
bootstrap capacitors close to the gate driver pins. The gate driver is placed close
to the switches to ensure a short current path. The decoupling capacitors are
connected to VDD and GND using vias opposing the gate driver, as suggested
in [36], which ensure the current path goes directly from the gate driver to
the capacitors instead of the power planes. Extra pinholes were added to enable
adding an external network for turn-on and -oﬀto use instead of the gate resistor.
The secondary side switches are driven directly from auxiliary windings on the
transformer via a turn-on and -oﬀnetwork. These networks are placed on the
opposing side of the MOSFETs to save space and enable a short current path.
Several test points are added to enable easy troubleshooting and measurements
of the gate signals and switch-nodes. The signal traces were mostly placed on

4.1
Regulations for Conducted Emission
The switching operation of the converter results in undesired noise being fed
from the converter back into the mains network. This especially occur at the
switching frequency and its harmonics.
Ringing can occur due to resonance
between parasitic components, which also generates noise.
If the noise is not controlled, it can aﬀect nearby electronics connected to the

mum noise an electronic device are allowed to generate in a speciﬁed frequency

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This project aimed to design, build, and test an AC-DC converter for the Puriﬁ
1ET400A ampliﬁer module. Due to time constraints, only the DC-DC converter
was implemented in hardware. This could not operate at the full input voltage,
and it was therefore not possible to compare it with prior art.
The AC-DC converter was designed around the STM L6563H PFC controller.
This can be designed to work with a wide range of AC input voltages and provide
a regulated DC voltage for the DC-DC converter.
The DC-DC converter was based on the topology in [1], but with the diodes
replaced by MOSFETs to increase eﬃciency.
The MOSFETs enable a bi-
directional current ﬂow, which greatly minimizes the size of the output ca-
pacitors. The output voltage is directly proportional with the input voltage,
and regulation is therefore not necessary since the AC-DC converter provide a
regulated DC voltage.
The secondary side MOSFETs were driven from auxiliary windings on the trans-
former via a gate charge- and discharge circuit. A Python script was made to
choose a few suitable cores from a large selection in order to ﬁnd the best trans-
former in terms of size and eﬃciency. The transformer was wound in hand, and
it was shown from the hardware test that it works satisfactory and was able
to provide stable gate signals for the secondary side MOSFETs and the correct
==PAGE:88==
64
Conclusion
output voltage.
The conducted noise was measured in simulation and a simple EMI ﬁlter was
implemented to attenuate the noise below the limit of the regulations. This was
only tested in simulation, and it can therefore not be concluded that the ﬁlter
works as well in hardware, where the parasitic eﬀects of the ﬁlter components
can aﬀect the performance signiﬁcantly at higher frequencies.
The converter design could not be evaluated fully, but there are indications
that the chosen topology could be useful for this application. The bi-directional
capabilities and the simplicity of the gate driver circuits can result in a more
compact and cheap commercial product. However, more work is required before
a working prototype can be build and evaluated.
==PAGE:89==
Chapter 6
Future Work
The DC-DC converter did operate fully, and it was therefore not possible to test
the design and to compare the converter with prior art. It is therefore desired
to build a working prototype in order to evaluate the chosen converter topology
and design process.
In order to achieve the goal of building a converter which can drive the ampliﬁer
module, the AC-DC converter, PFC and EMI ﬁlter will also need to be imple-
mented in hardware. The various auxiliary supplies for the ampliﬁer will also
need to be designed and implemented in order to operate the ampliﬁer according
to its speciﬁcations, along with various protection circuits.
The PFC converter was based on a transition mode topology, which might cause
EMI problems at higher power levels. It would therefore be beneﬁcial to use a
ﬁxed-oﬀ-time regulation instead, which can be made with the same regulator
IC as seen in [40]. It might also be possible to reduce the component count by
implementing PFC regulation in the half-bridge power stage.
The primary side gate driver signals were provided by an external function
generator. This is useful for prototyping purposes, but a PWM controller should
be designed and implemented to make the converter run oﬀthe mains network
alone. This would also require a low voltage supply on the primary side, which
could be provided from an auxiliary winding winding on the transformer and a
==PAGE:90==
66
Future Work
start-up circuit.
An adaptive dead time in the PWM controller could be used to ensure ZVS at
all power levels and increase eﬃciency, especially at lower power levels. Burst
mode control could be added so the converter only switches when the power is
needed, which could reduce the power losses signiﬁcantly at lower power levels.
The simulation model showed various problems which should be resolved. This
includes ﬁnding good component models and investigating the large voltage-
and current spikes during the dead time. Replacing the current sources with
purely resistive loads could result in better simulation results, and would still
be useful for evaluating the converter operation.

1
Introduction
The 1ET400A single-channel ampliﬁer module from PuriﬁAudio is a premium
class-D ampliﬁer with negligible low THD and IMD, a low output noise of
11.5 µV, an eﬃciency of > 94 % and a 131 dB dynamic range. To achieve these
speciﬁcations, it is necessary to drive it from a stable power supply.
2
Problem Statement
The goal of this project is to design, build and test an AC/DC converter which
is able to drive the ampliﬁer module from PuriﬁAudio. This requires stable,
properly balanced outputs with low voltage ripple, which can drive the ampliﬁer
up to 425 W with the dynamic characteristics of audio. Audio is often listened
at relatively low volumes at home, so it is also desired to focus on achieving a
high eﬃciency at lower power levels. Since the power supply is to be connected
to mains power, it must satisfy current EU regulations with regards to power
factor and the EMI standards for consumer power electronics.
3
Limitations
To ﬁt the allotted timeframe of a M.sc. project, the main focus is limited to
the most important parts of the converter, including the main output rails and
PFC. It is time consuming to design and test diﬀerent solutions, so the project
will therefore focus on a single design.
4
Target Group
This project is made in collaboration with PuriﬁAudio, and the power supply
is therefore targeted to be used for their ampliﬁer module. Although, it can
be expected that the power supply will be able to drive similar ampliﬁers from
other brands too.
If this was to be sold as a product, the target group would most likely be
audio brands looking for ready-made modules to include in their products or
for hobbyists and audio professionals to include in their audio setups.
1
==PAGE:105==
Project Plan
April 20, 2022
5
Solution Method
The solution method is split into diﬀerent stages, which also correspond to the
ones used in the project plan.
Theory & Design
The theory phase is the initial research and calculations which will lay the
necessary groundwork for the design.
This phase requires some initial research into diﬀerent topologies and choos-
ing one which is suitable for the application.
The chosen topology will be
analyzed, and the design will be based upon the results from this analysis to
satisfy the power requirements of the converter. The components will be chosen
based on the theoretical results, and simulation will be used for veriﬁcation.
PCB Design
The aim of the PCB Design phase is to make a circuit schematic and layout based
on the paper design and simulations. This will include choosing components
based on the required speciﬁcations, tolerances, form factor, etc.
The price and lead time of a PCB can be quite high, and it is therefore
unlikely to revise the PCB design later on. The PCB design should therefore
be ﬁnished early in the project phase and inspected closely to avoid errors. In
order to avoid failure, the design should focus on ease of debugging issues that
may arise. This includes adding test points for electrical inspection, components
that are quick change or remove, i.e. not too small, extra spaces for components
that may be used, and if possible, a 2-layer design to avoid hidden traces that
are hard or impossible to ﬁx.
Assembly
The assembly is the practical work of soldering the components onto the PCB,
winding inductors, etc. This requires precision work and can be time consuming.
Test
The circuit will have to be tested for errors before it can be evaluated, this
includes testing connections, components, sub-circuits and ﬁnally the circuit as
a whole to ensure proper operation. The converter testing phase will consist of
making the test setup, conﬁguring and automating certain test sequences and
the actual testing. The EMC Lab will also be used to verify if the power supply
satisfy the EMI standards.
Paperwork
The paperwork will be written throughout the project. This includes the project
plan, project log and ﬁnal thesis. The thesis will therefore document the work
throughout the project, and less work will be required in the end to correct and
ﬁnalize it.
