Abstract
EnvironmentalôÙdataôÙcontrolled automated windows show significant opportunities as a
lightôÙrefurbishment strategy to enhance the thermal performance and indoor air quality
while limiting energy use of heating units in existing dwellings. The work is conducted
within RePUP (RealôÙtime Performance Upgrade of Buildings) and uses a calibrated buildôÙ
ing performance simulation model to test a commercial ruleôÙbased control (VELUX AcôÙ
tive). Calibration against measured indoor data from the case dwelling employed the
Coefficient of Variation of the Root Mean Square Error, CV(RMSE), following ASHRAE
Guideline 14, achieving acceptable agreement between measured and simulated data for
operative temperature.
The control logic was implemented in IDA ICE using thermostats as the core decision
elements, combining temperature, CO2, humidity, and weather watchdogs. Thermostat
setpoints and deadbands were tuned to examine sensitivity and tradeôÙoffs among thermal
environment, indoor air quality, and energy. Weather inputs were based on a customôÙ
generated file for 2024 using measured data, and a projected 2050 dataset to test robustôÙ
ness under a future climate.
Across configurations, Velux Active window and solar shading control increased the therôÙ
mal performance and indoor air quality remained predominantly within Categories I and II
compared to measured indoor conditions in the case dwelling. Thermostat tuning was inôÙ
fluential: in the primary zone, moving from a wide to a tighter deadband reduced heating
energy use by about 11% while preserving thermal performance and indoor air quality.
These results indicate that automated windows driven by actual weather and indoor meaôÙ
surements and tuned to local conditions, can provide meaningful thermal performance
and indoor air quality gains.
Keywords: Building performance, Smart Buildings, Resilience, VELUX Active, autoôÙ
mated windows, natural ventilation, EN 16798ôÙ1, indoor air quality, CV(RMSE), simulation
model calibration, thermostat control, solar shading, custom weather data, retrofit

1
Introduction
In the face of growing energy demands, aging building stock, and the urgent need for
climate resilience, improving the performance of existing buildings by retrofitting shows
potential results and offers significant opportunities for reducing global energy consumpôÙ
tion. This is being considered as one of main approaches to achieving sustainability in
the built environment at relatively low cost and high upgrade rates[1][2].
This thesis investigates how automated window control can improve indoor air quality
and thermal environment in existing residential buildings. At the core of the project is
the development of a customized control strategy that responds to indoor and outdoor
environmental conditions, including temperature, CO2 levels, solar radiation, wind, and
precipitation. By adjusting window operation in response to these variables, such systems
can provide natural ventilation when appropriate and limit unwanted heat loss or gain.
This responsiveness may reduce the need for mechanical systems to maintain thermal
comfort. SimulationôÙbased studies have shown that the introduction of window automation
ôÙ even incrementally ôÙ can reduce the consumption of HVAC electricity by 3% to 15%,
depending on the way the strategy is implemented [3].
Automated window control has been shown in another study to have substantial potenôÙ
tial to improve indoor environmental quality while reducing energy demand in buildings.
Nateghi and Kaczmarczyk [4] used a multiôÙobjective optimization approach to refine winôÙ
dow control parameters and thermostat setpoints, demonstrating that combining environôÙ
mental quality targets (CO2 and PPD) with energy demand constraints leads to improved
performance across both temperate and extreme climates. They found that temperatureôÙ
based window control improved indoor air quality significantly but increased energy use
unless optimized in tandem with thermostat settings. Their results underscore the imporôÙ
tance of locationôÙspecific calibration of control parameters to account for local climate and
building characteristics.
In addition to control logic, the physical characteristics of openings also influence system
effectiveness. The study by [5] highlights that the window opening area directly affects
thermal response times and ventilation rates. Large openings allow faster air exchange
but can cause abrupt indoor temperature fluctuations, while smaller openings provide
more controlled changes. In this thesis, the use of roof windows, typically smaller than
faûÏade windows, offers an inherent moderation in thermal impact, helping to maintain
comfort during the colder months without excessive cooling.
Accurate performance evaluation of environmental data driven automated systems also
depends on realistic environmental inputs. Weather files play a critical role in building perôÙ
formance simulation, but standard EnergyPlus Weather (EPW) datasets are often based
on outdated averages over broad regions. These may not capture urban microclimates,
local weather variability, or recent climate change effects. A study on climate data in
Sweden revealed that outdated weather files can overestimate heating demand by 12ã
19% when both climate change and urban heat island effects are considered [6]. In this
thesis, a custom weather file will be generated from recent data in 2024 from DTU SoôÙ
lar Station [7] to better reflect the actual environmental conditions at the case building,
improving the accuracy and relevance of simulation results.
Model calibration is another essential factor for credible simulations. Calibration aligns
model outputs with measured data, improving accuracy and predictive reliability [8]. WithôÙ

system dynamics or occupant effects. Standard metrics such as Mean Bias Error (MBE)
and Coefficient of Variation of Root Mean Square Error (CVRMSE) are widely used to
quantify calibration quality [9]. ASHRAE Guideline 14 [10] provides thresholds for acceptôÙ

While calibration aligns the modelãs thermal and environmental outputs with measured
conditions, its accuracy also depends on realistic representation of how the building is
used. Occupant presence and behavior directly influence ventilation, internal gains, and
the performance of control systems. Without accounting for these dynamics, even a wellôÙ
calibrated model can misrepresent indoor comfort and energy use.
To address this, the literature emphasizes dataôÙdriven occupancy modelingãoften deôÙ
rived from environmental and motion sensors, as a critical input for building performance
simulations. Environmental sensors that measure CO2, temperature, humidity, and light
are common tools for indirect occupancy detection. A study by [11] showed that CO2ôÙ

proves with sensor fusion.
Motion detection technologies, such as Passive Infrared (PIR) sensors and lowôÙresolution
thermal imaging, provide privacyôÙpreserving realôÙtime occupancy estimates. When comôÙ
bined with machine learning, PIR arrays have achieved high accuracy in occupancy deôÙ
tection. For example, a 15ôÙnode PIR sensor array integrated with Support Vector Machine
(SVM) models achieved 90.9% test accuracy in identifying both occupancy location and
activity intensity in a residential case [12]. This demonstrates the potential of integrating
environmental and motion sensor data to produce accurate occupancy profiles, which are
crucial for evaluating intelligent control algorithm designed by VELUX.
These findings directly support the relevance of this thesis, which evaluates the perforôÙ
mance of the VELUX Active [13] system, a commercially available adaptive window conôÙ
trol, in a residential case of dwelling in Denmark. This work tests a real commercial system
using default parameters to assess how effectively it adapts to seasonal and occupancy
variations. The study also considers the implications of refining system setpoints to imôÙ
prove performance under local conditions.
The project is carried out within the framework of the RePUP initiative. The RePUP (RealôÙ
time Performance Upgrade of Buildings) project operates within this framework, aiming
to accelerate energy renovations in Denmark by demonstrating how realôÙtime data, smart
control systems, and light refurbishments can work together. RePUP envisions a future
where buildings are not static entities, but intelligent systems responsive to both occuôÙ
pants and environmental conditions. Retrofitting automated window systems represents
one such opportunityãoffering a scalable way to improve indoor climate through natural
ventilation and adaptive control [14]. A singleôÙfamily house participating in the project was
selected as the case study. The building is equipped with a network of environmental senôÙ
sors connected to the Climify platform [15], providing longôÙterm measurements of indoor
temperature, CO2 concentration, relative humidity, and presence.
The aim is to contribute to a better understanding of how adaptive window control guided
by actual environmental data can improve the thermal environment, indoor air quality
and energyôÙsensitive indoor environments. In buildings where conditions vary daily and
occupant behavior is not always predictable, automation offers a way to bridge the gap

2.4
HVAC systems and configurations
The case house is modeled with an ambient airôÙtoôÙwater heat pump as its primary heating
source. The heat pump is configured in the simulation with a nominal capacity of 9 kW and
a coefficient of performance (COP) of 4.55, reflecting typical performance for residentialôÙ
scale systems of this type. Space heating is delivered through waterôÙbased radiators
distributed across the buildingãs occupied zones.
There is no mechanical ventilation system installed in the house. As such, the ventilation
strategy in the simulation is based entirely on infiltration and window operation. While the
kitchen is equipped with a local exhaust hood, it functions only as a localized extraction
system over the stove and is not connected to a balanced mechanical ventilation system.
Due to this localized function and the limited scope of air exchange it provides, the kitchen
hood has not been explicitly modeled in IDA ICE.
The same principle applies to the bathrooms, where localized exhaust ventilation may
exist in practice but is not integrated into the simulation model. Moisture generation asôÙ
sociated with activities in the kitchen and bathrooms has also been excluded, as these
local sources are assumed to have minimal impact on the broader indoor environment.

The refinement process was based on the analysis of CO2 concentration trends and presôÙ

The graphs reveal that there is no direct oneôÙtoôÙone relationship between presence readôÙ
ings and CO2 levels. In principle, when presence is detected in a room, an increase in CO2
concentration is expected due to the air exhaled by occupants. However, the observed
data show that this trend does not always occur. In some cases, CO2 levels decrease
even though presence sensor values indicate that someone is in the room. This can likely
be explained by window openings or partially open doors, which allow fresh air to enter
and dilute indoor CO2 levels, even while the room remains occupied. In addition, presôÙ

person might briefly enter a room and then leave, yet the sensor could still register presôÙ
ence for a period. Therefore, while presence signals can help to estimate usage patterns,
they do not provide a fully accurate record of occupancy.

3.4.1
Simplified window operation control macro
Modeling window opening behavior is a crucial part of building performance simulations,
as window operation directly influences indoor temperature, air quality, and heating energy
use. This effect becomes particularly significant during the heating season, where the
timing and duration of window openings can substantially impact both comfort and energy
performance.
To reflect userôÙdriven behavior and set a baseline for evaluating automated window conôÙ
trol, a simplified window operation model was developed in IDA ICE. This model was
intended to represent typical occupant behavior under varying environmental conditions
17
==PAGE:24==
SimulationôÙBased Evaluation of Automated Window Control for Indoor Climate and Energy Performance in
a SensorôÙCalibrated Residential Case Dwelling
and to serve as a reference scenario against which automated control logic could later be
compared.
The modeling approach was guided by findings in literature, which show that the probôÙ
ability of window opening increases with indoor air temperature during the cooling and
transition seasons. In contrast, during the heating season, the relationship tends to be reôÙ
versed. In one study, 71% of observations during the heating season showed a negative
correlation between indoor temperature and window operation, indicating that occupants
were less likely to open windows as temperature increased. The study also highlighted
specific comfort thresholds: window operation peaked when temperatures exceeded apôÙ
proximately 23.2,ô¯C in the transition season and 26.3,ô¯C in the heating season, suggestôÙ
ing that occupant comfort is a key trigger for manual ventilation.[19] Additional research
confirms that indoor temperature is among the strongest predictors of window operation
behavior. In one study, indoor temperature was found to be a statistically significant influôÙ
encing factor in approximately % 90 of both window opening and window closing models.
[20]
The same study also examined the role of indoor CO2 concentration. When CO2 levels
were below 1500 ppm, a negative correlation was observed, meaning that lower rates
of window opening were associated with higher CO2 concentrations, likely as a result of
insufficient ventilation. However, when CO2 levels exceeded 1500 ppm, the relationship
became positive, indicating that elevated CO2 levels may begin to act as a direct cause
of window opening, possibly due to discomfort related to stuffy air or odor.[19]
Based on these findings, a ruleôÙbased macro was created in IDA ICE to approximate winôÙ
dow operation driven by occupant behavior. The logic used indoor air temperature and
CO2 thresholds to trigger window opening events. This behaviorôÙbased macro, shown

Discussion
Model Fidelity and Calibration Accuracy
The calibrated model demonstrates strong alignment with measured operative temperaôÙ
ture, with CV(RMSE) values for all monitored zones falling below the ASHRAE Guideline
14 threshold of 30% for hourly data.
However, when the same model calibration approach is applied to CO2 concentration,
the results fall outside the ASHRAE acceptance threshold. While the general seasonal
profile of CO2 trends aligns with the measured data, notable discrepancies occur at
shorter time scales. Several factors likely contribute to these deviations:
ãÂ Zone door operation: In the simulation, door opening and closing is controlled
by a fixed daily schedule in IDA ICE. The doors open and close at identical times
each day, which does not reflect the variability of actual occupant behavior. Door
position can directly influence CO2 concentrations within a room regardless of
window operation, creating a limitation in accurately representing the dynamic air
exchange between spaces.
ãÂ Physical property assumptions: The precision of the building envelope paramôÙ
eters is inherently constrained by the limited documentation available for the case
house, which was built in 2000s. While wall and roof construction were known
from sectional drawings, properties of envelope elements was estimated based on
typical construction practices of the period. These assumptions introduce some
uncertainty into the airtightness and ventilation modeling, both of which can affect
CO2 behavior.
These deviations provide important context for interpreting subsequent results and unôÙ
derscore the need to consider model limitations when assessing automated control perôÙ
formance.
Limitations of Sensor Data
The availability of measured sensor data from the Climify platform provided a robust
foundation for model validation. However, the dataset presents several seasonal limiôÙ
tations that constrain the comprehensiveness of the calibration and subsequent perforôÙ
mance evaluation.
The measured data covers the period from April to November, representing primarily the
shoulder seasons and summer months. Only the month of October offers observations
from the heating season. As a result, while the model is wellôÙaligned with conditions durôÙ
ing warmer months, its ability to reflect the thermal environment in terms of calibration,
and control performance during the full winter period remains limited.
This restriction should be considered when interpreting the results, particularly for conôÙ
clusions related to heating energy demand and indoor climate behavior under extended

Relative humidity: limitations and implications
One of the analysed zones is a bathroom, yet detailed moisture generation and exôÙ
traction were excluded: shower events, door opening patterns, and local exhaust flows
(on/off rates, runôÙon times) were simplified or omitted. As a result, the model likely unôÙ
derestimates short, highôÙRH spikes after use and may misplace the timing of recovery
to baseline levels. Similar simplifications apply to other rooms (e.g., kitchen), where
latent loads from cooking were not explicitly scheduled. Future work should introduce
realistic moisture schedules and event profiles (frequency, duration, flow rates), couple
them to door/window states and infiltration, and include moisture buffering of interior
surfaces. This would allow a more reliable assessment of RH exceedances and any
risk of condensation.
Recommendations for Further Investigation
The results from this study demonstrate that the VELUX Active control algorithm, when
implemented in simulation, effectively modulates window operation in response to inôÙ
door and outdoor environmental parameters. However, one critical limitation is excludôÙ
ing the time delay automation into the control macro due to complexity.
As described in Table 3.5, the time delay conditions ensure that:
ãÂ Windows remain open for a fixed period (15 or 30 minutes) depending on outdoor
and indoor temperature thresholds.
ãÂ A pause period of at least 2 hours occurs before another opening cycle begins.
This mechanism is essential to reduce frequent cycling but also critical to maintaining
thermal comfort and minimizing unnecessary heat loss, especially during the heating
and shoulder seasons.
For further investigation, it is strongly recommended that time delay automation is incorôÙ
porated into control macro in IDA ICE as the optimal delay and opening durations may
have direct impact on operation time and frequency.
UserôÙadjustable default settings in VELUX Active provide flexibility to adapt control strateôÙ
gies to diverse occupancy patterns and seasonal demands. The twoôÙhour closed interôÙ
val is a default threshold intended to balance energy conservation and thermal comfort
under typical residential conditions.
This adaptability is valuable in principle. For instance:
ãÂ In scenarios with higher or more dynamic occupancy (e.g., hosting guests or inôÙ
creased activity in the room), a shorter interval ensures more frequent ventilation
and prevents excessive CO2 accumulation.
ãÂ In lowôÙoccupancy conditions during colder weather, extending the interval may
minimize heat loss while maintaining indoor air quality.

While such flexibility allows tailoring the control system to specific building use patterns,
it also introduces a practical challenge: expecting the average user to actively manage
such nuanced adjustments may be unrealistic.
It is also important to note that the discussions in this section are based on simulation
outcomes. As no operational data from the installed VELUX Active system in the case
house was not investigated at the time of this study, the performance evaluation reflects
modeled behavior rather than measured realôÙworld performance in terms of Velux Active
operation.
This underscores the importance of designing control systems that are adaptive by deôÙ
fault, automatically adjusting delays based on occupancy detection, seasonal condiôÙ
tions, and indoor climate trends, while still allowing advanced users to override settings
when desired.
Energy analysis: scope and limitations
Measured energy data from the case dwelling were not availableëƒ consequently, the
energy assessment compares simulated configurations only (baseline vs. automated
control variants). The model was calibrated against indoor climate (operative temperôÙ
ature, CO2) but not explicitly tuned to match metered energy use. Key energy drivers
retain uncertainty. The resulting energy findings should be interpreted as relative differôÙ
ences between configurations rather than absolute predictions for the dwelling. While
the sensorôÙbased calibration delivers indoorôÙenvironment results, the absence of onôÙsite
energy benchmarking limits the accuracy of the energy comparison. Future work should
include targeted energy calibration against measured consumption to validate absolute
levels and reduce parameter uncertainty.

Conclusion
This thesis evaluated whether automated windows driven by actual weather data and
indoor environment data can improve the thermal environment category distribution deôÙ
fined by EN 16798ã1 in an existing dwelling, and how sensitive thermal environment,
indoor air quality, and heating energy use are to thermostat settings within that conôÙ
trol. Across the tested configurations, VELUX Active window control combined with
solar shading increased the share of occupied hours in Category I and reduced time in
overheating bands relative to the measured sensor data in the case dwelling. These
findings support the feasibility of environmental dataãdriven automated windows as a
light refurbishment to enhance thermal environment and indoor air quality.
Thermostat settings had a clear impact on performance. Setpoint levels and deadband
width determined when the temperature control allowed or blocked airing, which in turn
affected CO2 levels and heating demand. Tighter deadbands produced more reactive
temperature control but at times suppressed ventilation in cool shoulder periods, alôÙ
lowing CO2 concentrations to rise. Moderately tightened deadbands, paired with solar
shading, provided the best overall tradeôÙoff: reduced warmôÙseason peaks, high shares
in Category I, and only a minor reduction in indoor air quality which largely remained
within Categories IãII. Tuning the thermostat from a wide to a tighter deadband reduced
heating energy use by approximately 11% while maintaining comparable thermal perôÙ
formance and indoor air quality.
In direct response to the research questions: first, automated windows controlled by acôÙ
tual weather data and indoor environment sensors, improved the thermal performance
compared to the measured sensor data in the case dwelling, especially when comôÙ
bined with solar shading, thereby supporting their use as a retrofitting solution. Second,
outcomes are sensitive to thermostat settings. The most balanced performance was
achieved with tuned setpoints and moderately tight deadbands that avoid temperature
drops particularly in shoulder season, preserve ventilation opportunities, and limit enôÙ
ergy use of heating units.
Overall, the results demonstrate that VELUX Active window control strategy, tuned to
local climate and indoor targets, can meaningfully improve thermal environment and
indoor air quality in existing homes with minimal intervention.
