Abstract 
 
Considering that complications arising from borehole instability result in major expenses 
incurred on the drilling industry, predictive methods such as extensive analysis and 
determination of the most suitable drilling mud window remain one of the important aspect 
of research to mitigate such obstacles. The focus of this thesis is primarily directed at means 
of predicting the drilling mud window for a geothermal well located in northern Jylland. 
This is accomplished by interpreting and using the available log data from the deviated 
sections of the well as well as the vertical sections of an offset well to develop a 
geomechanical model that analyses the borehole stresses. The model is based on a number 
of cases, including different horizontal stress ranges, which were developed in order to 
navigate through the complexity of the process due to the unavailability of field tests and 
sufficiently accurate log data. The suitable mud window is proposed and further 
recommendations for the use of field tests, such as the leak off tests, as well as a different 
failure criterion than the one used is suggested.

1. Introduction 
The stability of a wellbore is considered as one of the most critical aspects in a drilling 
project and it remains as one of the main concerns that engineers have during the 
operations. It is often the case that unpredicted rock behavior results in multiple drilling 
problems, of which the stuck drill-pipe is the most famous, resulting in loss of time, money 
and often the need to abandon whole or part of the borehole.  
Generally, underground formations experience far field principal stresses such as the 
overburden and the two horizontals as well as stresses caused by other phenomena like 
tectonic and geological factors. All these different types of stresses can create an extremely 
complicated stress regime that is both difficult to be predicted and risky to be simplified. As 
a result, when drilling formations in a complicated stress environment, instability 
phenomena may occur that might cause expensive loss of time, equipment and increase of 
risk. To ensure wellbore stability, there are several requirements that have to be met that 
are often related to the type of the drilling fluids and the equipment used. One of the most 
significant parameters is the correct prediction and utilization of the required drilling mud 
weight that will result in a fitting pressure against the wellbore wall. This will keep the 
wellbore wall from collapsing by counter-acting the pressure effect caused by the local 
stresses that have been redistributed around the open hole in the post drilling phase and 
which are actually the cause of the instability.

2. Causes of Wellbore Instability 
The causes of wellbore instability can be either natural which are uncontrollable or induced 
which are controllable [1]. Natural causes are mostly related to:  
a) Tectonic or geological phenomena 
b) Low consolidation levels of formations  
c) Plastic behavior of formations in high stresses  
d) Abnormal formation fluid pressures or overpressured formations.  
The first cause includes faults, fractures or geological formations such as salt domes that 
create an extremely anisotropic stress environment. Even though this state is balanced prior 
to drilling, any weaknesses that might exist can be triggered during the drilling phase, for 
example, by a vibration caused from the drill bit or from the opening of the hole.  
 
Figure 2-1: Fractures near the wellbore causing circulation loss leading to instability [2] 
The second sub-category is related to the level of consolidation in an underground 
formation, usually shallow [1]. When the formation is unconsolidated, the bonding 
between the grains is not sufficient enough to hold the walls after the removal of the 
supporting rock, thus, a part of the rock formation will collapse in the well in a gradual or 
sudden situation [1]. 
 
Figure 2-2: Loose rock around the well will collapse within the well if the mud weight is not adequate [1] 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
Another factor that can lead to conditions of instability is the behavior of a formation when 
subjected to high stresses. Some rock types, such as salt, experience a plastic deformation 
when exposed to high levels of stress. This creep behavior might result the rock to move 
gradually within the openings creating tight spots [3].  
 
Figure 2-3: Plastic behavior of a rock will form tight spots and might cause the drill bit to get stuck [1] 
Another cause of wellbore instability is overpressure conditions either in rocks with low 
permeability such as shale or salt formations, near faults, or close to geological formations 
such as anticlines or salt domes [1]. The depth or the magnitude of the overpressure if not 
predicted properly, might cause either lost circulation or kicks [1]. 
 
Figure 2-4: Drilling through overpressured formations [1] 
The second main category involves causes that result in instability within the wellbore that 
has been induced, as such, they are connected to the human factor. These causes can be 
either:  
 
Physical-chemical or  
 
Mechanical.

Physical – chemical origins are related to the characteristics, type and properties of the 
drilling fluid. Regarding the drilling mud weight, when it is lower than the pore pressure a 
kick may occur whereas if it is significantly higher a lost circulation could take place, which 
if not controlled may cause an important decrease in the well´s pressure. Consequently, the 
drilling mud’s ability to support the wall and to keep the formation fluids from flowing in 
the well can be both jeopardized if the correct mud range is not used. Furthermore, if the 
mud weight is relatively higher than the maximum allowable pressure, a potential 
fracturing can take place which if not managed in the proper time it could result in tensile 
failure of the formation surrounding the well. In addition, increased drilling fluid 
temperatures might give rise to significant thermal stresses which might be destructive 
towards the wellbore stability. Another cause related to the drilling fluid is its ability to 
react with the formation fluids. One example is the difference in salinity between the 
drilling mud and the formation fluids which is capable of creating osmotic pressure that 
could destabilize the wellbore. Also when shale formations are drilled with water based 
mud, the adsorption of water by the clay minerals often results in a swelling phenomenon 
occurring in the shale formation. Swelling of clays is one of the major issues and concerns 
when drilling shale formations due to its ability to create tight spots in the drilled well, 
where the latter problem often can causes the drill bit to get stuck. 
 
Mechanical causes of instability are related to the drilling equipment such as the drill bit or 
casing. For the drilling bit, strong vibrations that result from the cutting while in operation 
often breaks the borehole wall and causes formation rock to collapse within the well. 
Similarly, based on [4] the swab and surge pressures are essential for the drilling engineer 
to keep in mind. Swab pressure is when the drill bit is pulled out too fast resulting in a 
significant decrease in the bottom hole pressure and increasing the potential of the 
formation fluids to flow in the well. On the other hand, swabbing is when the drill bit is run 
down to the hole too fast, and as a result of this, possibly, the bottom hole pressure might 
increase significantly and damage the formation or cause lost circulation (Figure 2-5) [4]. 
Furthermore, in the case of a deviated well, the inclination is one more essential factor that 
if not planned properly, could lead to potential drilling problems such as wash outs.

3. Geo-Mechanical Model Parameters 
In the previous chapter, the causes of borehole instability were briefly discussed, while in 
this chapter the main aim is to discuss in detail the different parameters required for 
building the geo-mechanical model that will aid in preventing borehole instability. The 
geomechanical model relies on three important parameters: overburden stresses, horizontal 
stresses and pore pressure. The model then utilizes these parameters, together with rock 
properties which will also be calculated, to analyze the bore wall stresses both in the vertical 
and deviated well sections. Finally, a failure criterion will be discussed, which the model will 
use in order to obtain a suitable mud window. 
3.1. Overburden Stress, Sv 
The overburden stress, denoted by Sv, describes the vertical stress or pressure at a certain 
depth that results from the weight of the overlying rock material and the fluids contained 
within the rock material pores. Since the overburden pressure compacts the formation 
vertically, it tends to push the formation material horizontally in the lateral direction (a 
behavior described by Poisson’s ratio) [5].  However, because the rock is often confined 
from all direction by surrounding formation materials, the vertical squeezing and the lateral 
pushing results in the creation of the lateral maximum horizontal stresses, denoted by 
Shmax, and minimum horizontal stresses, denoted by Shmin, [5]. As such, the overburden 
pressure or stress is computed using the following formula:

3.2. Minimum Horizontal Stress, Shmin 
The Shmin is one of the three principal stresses which need to be determined in order to 
create the wellbore stability geomechanical model used for predicting wellbore collapse and 
for planning borehole stability [8]. The importance of this parameter relies on the fact that 
the mud weight needs to be maintained lower than the Shmin in order to prevent formation 
fracture to avoid lost circulation of the mud. The methods used for obtaining a quantitative 
knowledge of the Shmin include the direct and indirect methods as described below.  
3.2.1. Direct Method: Leak-off and Extended Leak-off Test (LOT, XLOT, ELOT) 
The theory behind using the tests discussed below for determining the Shmin, is due to the 
fact that Hubbert and Willis have shown that the work required to create a fracture is 
proportionally related to the stress perpendicular to the fracture plane indicating that 
‘fractures tend to propagate perpendicular to the orientation of the least principal stress’ 
[9]. As such, the pressure required to cause stable fracture propagation needs to be equal to 
or higher than Shmin. Otherwise when this pressure becomes lower than that stress acting 
perpendicular to the fracture plane, the fracture will close.  
The most commonly used direct method for determining the value of the minimum 
horizontal stress is by performing the Leak-off tests (LOT). The LOT is conducted a few 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
meters below the cashing shoe, which is placed into the new formation that will be 
penetrated, in order to determine the maximum pressure of the wellbore before fracture 
initiation starts [10]. 
In order to perform the LOT test, the well must be shut in and pressurized by pumping the 
drilling fluid into the borehole at a constant rate to steadily increase the pressure on the 
formation alongside the well walls until the fluid starts penetrating or leaking off into the 
rock formation [3]. The LOT results indicate the maximum drilling fluid density or well 
pressure that the well can withstand without leading to formation fracture or loss of 
circulation. As such, this test does not aim at estimating the Shmin, but rather to determine 
the proper mud density for the next borehole section [10].  
 
Figure 3.2.1-1: Schematic illustration of the LOT [10] 
In reference to Figure 3.2.1-1, the first section of the curve shows the linear relationship 
between the pressure applied and the volume increase, indicating the linear compressibility 
of the drilling fluid until the point where fracture initiation starts, indicated by the leak-off 
point (LOP), after which the curve deviates from the linear behavior [10]. Usually the LOT 
is stopped before the formation-breakdown pressure (FBP) is reached in order to prevent 
inducing large fractures, to save time and money and avoid problems that might appear 
while drilling the rest of the well [11]. 
However, in order to reliably determine the value of the Shmin, the so-called extended leak-
off test (XLOT) needs to be used instead, whereby the well pressure is increased beyond the 
LOP as well as the breakdown pressure in order to create a fracture within the formation 
such that the pressure needed to close the fracture can be recorded [12].  
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
As such, for the XLOT, ensuring a stable fracture growth, such that fracture penetrates 
approximately 2–3 wellbore diameters (for vertical wells) away into the formation, is 
essential to guarantee that only the minimum horizontal stresses play a role in the fracture 
closure  and to remove influences or effects caused by the near wellbore stresses [10, 11]. 
This is because when the LOT test is stopped after the LOP, the fracture created is unstable 
and very short, often resulting in major overestimations of  the smallest horizontal stress 
and can result in major wellbore stability calculation errors as explained by Raaen and 
Brudy (2001) [13]. Thus, when the fracture propagation pressure (FPP) is reached, which 
is the pressure responsible for stable fracture propagation within the formation, the decline 
shown in Figure 3.2.1-1 following the instantaneous shut-in pressure (ISIP) shows the 
fracture closure pressure [12]. As such, if the XLOT is performed, the fracture closure 
pressure (FCP) is the most applicable approximation of the minimum horizontal stress, 
because it will dictate at which pressure the fractures closes, such that if the well pressure is 
higher than the FCP the fractures will open and if the well pressure is less than or equal to 
FCP the fractures will close [10, 12]. As a result, the XLOT’s FCP should be used in the 
determination of the maximum static mud weight throughout the wellbore section studied 
instead of the LOT’s LOP which only indicates  the fracture initiation point.

yielded on the constant 0.3. When the equation was tested for reliability on further obtained 
data from offshore Gulf of Mexico, the equation predicted lower values of the minimum 
principal stress than what was the case, so the constant was then changed to 0.5 to fit the 
new data results.

3.5. Pore Pressure 
In any rock mechanics study, pore pressure is considered as one of the most fundamental 
parameters due to its critical influence in the behavior of the porous rock systems. The 
maintenance of the control of a wellbore throughout its whole lifespan is highly dependable 
on the knowledge of this behavior, thus making it very crucial for the success of any drilling 
project. Misinterpretations of the magnitude of the pore pressure and unexpected 
deviations, can lead to wellbore instabilities and complications, which may i) expose the 
drilling crew as well as the project itself to high risks and ii) increase the drilling operation 
cost [30]. Before analyzing the term “pore pressure” the phenomenon of compaction will be 
introduced.  
If we consider a rock sample, compaction is the decrease of the volume of this sample caused 
by compressive stresses (stresses acting inwards and perpendicular to its surfaces). This 
phenomenon in underground rock mechanics is caused primarily by the overburden stress 
applied from the deposition of new sedimentary layers. This process can take up to millions 
of years during which, a rock formation is being exposed to compressive stresses more and 
more as the burial depth increases. This causes the incompressible fluids to escape via flow. 
If the rate of the compaction is equal to the rate of the fluid outflow, the pore pressure is 
equivalent to the hydrostatic pressure. If we plot pressure versus depth, the values of the 
pore pressure will progressively increase proportionally with depth, following a normal 
trend with a constant slope (see Figure 3.5-1) [3]. The equation that gives the formation 
pressure is:

3.5.1. Overpressure 
As it was pre-mentioned, when there is a state of equilibrium between the rates of 
compaction and formation fluid flow, the pore pressure profile is identical to the hydrostatic 
one. However, in most sedimentary rock formations in great depths the pore pressure is 
higher than the hydrostatic one, a condition which is known as overpressure (see Figure 
3.5.1-1). Based on [37], the equation that relates overpressure with pore pressure and 
hydrostatic pressure is: 
P୭୴= P୮−P୦ୱ  
 (Eq. 24) 
Overpressure is an important parameter in drilling and production regarding safety, 
composition and density of drilling mud, as well as casing design.  It can occur due to 
different reasons as it depends on the geological structure at the area of interest, the 
depositional environment as well as the depositional rates and processes [32]. These 
reasons can be divided into two main categories which point out the timing when the 
overpressure was initiated in relation to the sedimentation process [33]: 
1. Building of overpressure during sedimentation process.  
2. Building of overpressure during post-sedimentation processes. 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
The overpressure in the first category can occur when the compaction rate is higher than 
the rate of the pore fluid escape (a process known as under-compaction). This excessive 
amount of fluid within the pore will cause overpressure [33].  
In the second category, the overpressure can be generated by several factors: 
A. Fluid expansion, which can be caused from: 
 Generation of vapors/gases due to increasing temperature of the rock formation along 
with increasing burial depth.  
 Creation of hydrocarbons during maturation of source rocks.  
 Mineral diagenesis, such as the transformation of clay mineral smectite to the more 
stable illite which releases intermolecular water within the pores of the rock [33]. 
B. Fluid expulsion via cementation/precipitation processes. Cementation is a chemical 
reaction, which produces precipitated minerals within the pores forcing the pore fluids 
to a smaller space, thus, increasing their pressure [34]. 
C. Tectonic phenomena, such as fault displacements or diapirism. For example, in 
tectonically active zones close to faults, high pressurized zones can be displaced along 
the fault line through elevation while maintaining their pressure [32].

3.5.3. Prediction of Pore Pressure via Well Logs 
There are numerous methods and formulas used for the direct and indirect calculation of the 
pore pressure, some of them will be discussed in this part. One of the first methods was the 
Normal Compaction Trend method or NCT, originally presented by Hottman and Johnson 
(1965) which is based on the interpretation of data from either resistivity, sonic or 
conductivity logs. According to this method, the given values of a measured parameter for 
example in the case of sonic log, the time that the seismic waves need in order to travel 
through the rock is plotted versus depth. The profile shows a normal trend line, with 
decreasing travel time values as the depth increases which indicates the continuous decrease 
of porosity due to compaction. This is because the seismic wave´s travel time through the 
rock and its porosity are parameters proportional to each other. The higher the porosity 
within a rock the more time the seismic wave will need to travel through it. However, there 
are points that deviate from the trend line by showing higher values than the normal ones. 
This phenomenon is considered as an indicator of abnormally high porosity values which 
are likely related to abnormally high pore pressure, higher than the normal hydrostatic 
pressure (see Figure 3.5.3-1).

3.5.4. Methods for measuring the pore pressure at the site 
It is very crucial when estimating the pore pressure with the use of empirical equations as it 
was showed above, to have the ability to compare, the predicted pressures with the actual 
(measured) ones in order to evaluate the accuracy of the chosen prediction method. There 
are different ways to measure in situ the formation pore pressure. Some of them such as the 
Repeat Formation Test or the Drill-stem Test are being described briefly in this chapter.  
Drill-stem Test 
The Drill-stem Test is a very common as well as reliable method to measure the formation 
pressure and is being conducted during the drilling process. Based on [51], usually there 
are one or two packers that they are installed in the well in order to seal and isolate the 
depth interval of interest.  In any of these intervals besides the bottom hole, two packers are 
used, one at the bottom of the interval and one at the top. If the section of the well is its 
bottom, one packer is used. When the isolation and sealing of the zone has been succeeded, a 
valve is opened and the pressure inside the interval drops to the surface pressure. The 
pressure difference between the isolated part of the well and the formation pressure drives 
the formation fluids within the well and the drill pipe, which is perforated at that part. 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
Then, the fluids travel through the drill pipe up to the surface where they are sampled and 
analyzed. 
 
Figure 3.5.4-1:  Typical DST tool showing sealed interval of interest with 2 packers at top and bottom [51] 
Repeat Formation Test 
Based on [50], the Repeat Formation Tester (RFT) measures the formation pore pressure 
quickly and with a good accuracy during wireline logging procedures. The measurement 
takes place at specific depths where the wireline tool is lowered down the open hole before 
casing is run down to it although RFT measurements can be done also after the casing has 
been installed in the well. As soon as the RFT tool reaches the depth of interest, it is jacked 
against the borehole wall from where it collects fluid samples and measures the pore 
pressure.

4.2. Borehole Collapse and Fracturing 
There are two main types of drilling-induced failures in a wellbore which this thesis is 
mostly focused on. The tensile and shear failure of rocks which occur when the rock is 
subjected to a stress field where it’s tensile or shear strength are exceeded respectively. 
These two types of failure are shown in the following figure: 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
 
Figure 4.2-1: On the left, tensile failure occurs when the rock sample is subjected in tension and on the right, 
shear failure occurs under compression [3] 
Similarly, in a wellbore these two types of failure occur dependable on the pressure exerted 
by the drilling mud. These failures are known in drilling as: 
a) Tensile or hydraulic fracture either horizontal or vertical 
b) Shear fracture or borehole collapse 
A tensile fracture is a fracture caused by the mud pressure, and it is initiated when the 
drilling mud pressure exceeds the minimum horizontal stress of the formation at the 
wellbore. The way that a tensile fracture propagates, depends on the local stress regime. If 
the highest of the three principal stresses is the overburden, the fractures will be vertical 
and will propagate parallel to the maximum horizontal stress [41]. Likewise, if the 
maximum horizontal stress is the highest of the three principal stresses and the well is 
horizontal, the fracture will propagate vertical. 
A shear failure in a borehole occurs if the hoop stress exceeds the compressional strength of 
the formation at that point. In a vertical borehole within an anisotropic horizontal stress 
condition, the shear failure will appear parallel to the direction of the minimum horizontal 
stress as it is illustrated below [3]:

5.2.1. General lithology with respect to Caliper and Gamma Ray log 
The first task performed in the log interpretation was to analyze the borehole geometry. 
This was done through the caliper log which is used to identify borehole geometry and 
indicate permeable and impermeable layers. In addition, the gamma ray log is used to 
identify bed boundary and lithology as well as to quantify shale volume. As a rule of thumb, 
often high values of the gamma radiation indicate shale lithology whereas low values 
characterize predominantly sedimentary rocks such as limestone and sandstone and 
dolomite [45, 46]. Shale has the most gamma activity due to its high clay content, while the 
gamma activity of sandstones and carbonates rather similar depending on how much clay 
traces they contain [46]. 
The 4 arm caliper log and gamma ray log of Well-1 has been plotted for all datasets and is 
presented in Figure 5.2.1-1. It was stated in the well drilling report that the well was drilled 
with 12.25 inches bit size from 490m-1054m and from then on the well was drilled with 8.5 
inches bit size from 1054m-2640m. The bit sizes are indicted in the caliper log by the 
vertical blue lines in Figure 5.2.1-1.

As a rule of thumb: 
• 
Intervals where hole is in gauge with bit size are the intervals where bit size line and 
caliper are aligned [46]. 
• 
Intervals where hole is not in gauge with bit size are the intervals that show 
deviations between the caliper readings and the bit size line [46] 
In general, the hole is not always in gauge for several reasons. If the hole diameter is larger 
than the bit size, this could indicate that the formation might be soluble in the drilling fluid 
used, formations become soluble if the drilled fluid used is fresh water [45, 46]. Hole 
diameters larger than the bit size could also indicate that the formation is weak and as a 
result caves in as in the case of unconsolidated sands. In addition caving occurrence is 
typical for shaly formations, usually young and unconsolidated [45,46].

If the diameter is smaller than the bit size, then it may mean that parts of the formations 
may contain shale that have swelled and flowed into the borehole or that a mud-cake has 
developed, which is the case in porous, permeable sedimentary rocks [45,46]. The 
disturbances from the straight line in Figure 5.1-1 and Figure 5.1-2 indicate both small and 
large caved holes and tight spots as shown, and the possible reasons for this have already 
been discussed. 
Since the caliper log indicates numerous caving, these caving and washout indicate missing 
formation material, which in turn mean that other log data quality could be poor. This 
missing information means that the logs experience the following in-discrepancies [45,46]:  
 
Gamma ray readings will be lower than they should be (more missing material thus less 
radioactive emitting material)  
 
Density log readings are lower than they should be (more missing material thus less 
density)  
 
Neutron log readings are higher than they should be (more missing material thus more 
porosity)  
 
Sonic log readings are higher than they should be (more missing material, slower the sound 
waves thus the higher the Δt)  
 
Resistivity log readings are lower than they should be (more missing material thus less 
fluid)

Shale  
Generally, neutron log shows high values of porosity in shale or clay intervals because of 
the high hydrogen index due to the presence of both free and bound water [45,46]. Typical 
value for this log can be 40-50% for shale and clay intervals. This confirms the fact that the

the latter due to their lower density compared to shale [45,46]. 
Sandy Shale 
Sandy shale intervals have a large gamma log readings, the much higher neutron porosity 
readings is thus due to large clay content in the interval studied or presence of water (or 
also presence of water in sandy shale pores). This is because of the high hydrogen index 
which is usually due to free and bound water [45,46].

5.4.2.5. Far Field Principal Stresses 
Far Field stresses include the overburden and the minimum and maximum horizontal stresses.  
The data provided by the client contained no LOT data, as these tis test was not conducted on 
site, and only on FIT point was provided which is insufficient to give any calibration for the 
minimum horizontal stress. Since the faulting regime in the area where Well-1 and Well-2A 
are located is not known, the stress polygon method as well as some of the other often used and 
suggested empirical equations cannot be used.  
The approach method for estimating horizontal stresses will be as follows: 
The minimum and maximum horizontal stresses were assumed to be equal to each other, i.e. 
isotropic case. 
Case 1: assume an undrained situation where the fluid pressure in the formation is different than 
the fluid pressure in the well as there is no communication or flow between the two fluids. 
Case 2: assume a drained situation where the fluid pressure in the formation is in equilibrium 
with the fluid pressure in the well. 
Even though the Eaton method, also known as bilateral constraint and case zero, is accurate 
only if LOT data is available to perform calibration of to the empirically generated minimum 
horizontal stress log to the actual field stress to account for the tectonic stress, it will 
nonetheless be used.  
Case 3: assume horizontal stresses equal to the overburden.

6. Conclusion and Recommendations 
The geomechanical model created relied on several cases taken for the horizontal stress 
estimation. Accordingly, the results from the mud weight analysis verify the problem of the 
stuck pipe faced in Fjerritslev. The two most critical cases which were identified are those of 
the undrained case (Case 1) and the drained case (Case 2d) where maximum Biot coefficient and 
mud pressure were used for estimating the horizontal stress. 
The cases result in a geomechanical model that provides a narrow mud weight window but 
with sufficiently safe outcome.  
In order to recommend a single mud weight value irrelevant of the case situation would be 
difficult as it would require us to identify what is the closest situation in the field: that of Case 1 
or that of Case 2d, which is impossible at this stage. 
 
There are several factors which will contribute to a more accurate result to the mud window 
analysis. The first is to obtain field test results such as the LOT. This is particularly essential 
for obtaining an estimate for the Shmin and allowing the calibration of the Eaton equation for 
developing the Shmin log. Due to the lack of this test the horizontal stress calculation are highly 
inaccurate. For the pore pressure the FRT and the DST are very important to be able to 
provide an accurate input of this parameter into the geomechanical model. The availability of 
imaging logs to provide a picture of the wellbore wall would have helped evaluate the 
orientation and breakout width of the Shmax so as to estimate the horizontal stress magnitude 
and provide more accurate results while calculating the stresses around the wellbore. In the 
case that it was possible to at least obtain an estimation of the Shmin and then by use of empirical 
equations to calculate the Shmax, it would have then been possible to assume an anisotropic 
horizontal stress environment, which is more probable in reality. 
Future analysis could utilize different failure criteria such as the Mogi-Coulomb instead of the 
simplified Mohr- Coulomb. However, to do so would require more complicated calculations, 
and as the cases to be investigated increases this becomes a very tedious task. 
The availability of high quality logs cannot be stressed enough. The sonic log values worked 
with in this thesis were of extremely low quality requiring several quality checks resulting in 
our need to disqualify values and to rely on empirical equations and formed trend lines. The 
result of such low quality data is that it adds to the inaccuracy of the estimations made for the 
sonic porosity (where neutron porosity and density porosity logs were not provided), density 
==PAGE:113==
112 
 
(where density log was not provided) and elastic properties governing the Biot coefficient 
calculations, which in turn has a sensitive effect on the horizontal stress cases. As such, it is 
essential that the neutron porosity and density porosity logs are provided together with a 
qualitative sonic log. 
 
The ability to perform laboratory tests to verify the UCS, tensile strength and angle of friction 
are of utmost importance as it is nearly impossible to obtain a clear understanding of the mud 
window without obtaining actual test values and not only those calculated empirically. The 
main challenge was that in order to calculate those rock properties many of the empirical 
equations available, and which were actually used in this geomechanical model, relied on the 
values of the sonic log velocities. As such, since the sonic velocities provided had high 
inaccuracy, this will have a ripple effect on the values estimated empirically for the rock 
strength. 
 
Finally, since the scope of this thesis relies mainly on developing an excel based geomechanical 
model, it was not possible to analyze or incorporate the chemical effect of salinity on the 
Fjerritslev Clay minerals. A possible addition to the geomechanical model would be performing 
separate chemical analysis of shale samples from the formations in order to experiment with the 
interaction between the mud fluid and the shale minerals
