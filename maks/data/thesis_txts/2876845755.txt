Abstract
This thesis investigates the performance and stability of Pt/C thin-film mirror coatings designed
for the NewAthena X-Ray telescope. Certain multilayer samples coated on SPO plates have
shown severe instability with catastrophic reactions to wet chemistry resulting in complete de-
lamination, serving as the core motivation of this study. Single, bi-, tri-, and multilayer coated
witness samples, along with select multilayer SPO samples, have been examined and char-
acterized using X-Ray reflectrometry, scanning transmission electron microscopy, and stylus
profilometry. Measurements are used in combination with modeling techniques to study and
estimate the coating structure and residual stress. Results are used to examine the impact
of evolution and wet chemistry on multilayer coatings. A comparative evaluation of findings is
used to determine whether the observed instability is an isolated issue or whether instability
can be detected in additional coating samples indicating an inherent flaw of the coating process
or design.

1
Introduction
NewAthena, or the New Advanced Telescope for High ENergy Astrophysics, is the reenvisioned
Athena X-Ray mission, planned by the European Space Agency, slated for launch at the end
of the next decade[1]. NewAthena is the successor of current X-Ray missions like Chandra[2]
and XMM-Newton [3], both launched at the turn of the millennium, and will leverage new tech-
nologies, such as Silicon Pore Optics (SPO) developed since the launch of these missions[4].
The current mirror design utilizes silicon mirrors with a thin-film platinum and carbon multilayer
coating, replacing the previous design based on iridium and carbon[5]. Ensuring that the per-
formance and stability of the mirrors can satisfy the strict requirements inherent to spaceborne
observatories is essential, even more so with the approaching NewAthena mission adoption
review scheduled for 2027.
1.1
Project Purpose & Motivation
Early studies of Pt/C thin-film coated prototype mirrors have shown promising results, with a
few exceptions. Some of the SPO samples have shown critical stability issues and exposure
to wet chemistry as part of the process of lift-off and cleaning were seen to exacerbate the
issues[5]. The problematic SPO plates were all based on the same Pt/C multilayer design for
the 13th row of the mirror optics.
The investigation of these mirror samples and the desire to determine the cause and extent of
the instability affecting them lie at the heart of the motivation behind this project. This project
hopes to qualify the Pt/C designs as a viable and feasible option for the NewAthena mirror
optics despite the observed stability issues. Demonstrating the performance and stability of a
series of samples aims to show that the observed issues are not an inherent consequence of
the design and that the cause must lie elsewhere. The goal of this project has been formalized
as:
The project will aim to qualify and confirm the performance of the Pt/C multilayer mirrors for the
NewAthena mission. To that end, the project shall focus on modeling the mirror performance
based on specifications and testing the performance of the mirrors through various tests. The
primary test will be mirror X-Ray Reflectivity (XRR), which, combined with models, will allow
for comparing and confirming the performance of the mirrors. Secondary testing will combine
various experimental techniques to investigate and address aspects of the thin film structure
and stability. To conclude the performance and quality of the mirrors, the project will compare
expected results from models with experimental results.
1.2
Scope & Expectations
The work carried out in the effort to achieve these goals will be described throughout this the-
sis, including the experiments performed, modeling, and analyzes carried out over the past
five months. This thesis will strive to provide and explain the theoretical basis behind the ex-
perimental and analytical work described in the following chapters. Furthermore, this thesis
will detail the experimental and analytical methods employed to justify the validity of subse-
quent results. This thesis will examine, analyze, and discuss the results and findings of each
method of characterization employed separately. This will focus on the individual findings and

conclusions of each method while still allowing relative comparisons of the results and per-
spectives between them to be made. The last method of characterization that will be examined
will draw upon the findings of all preceding methods and will serve the secondary function of

five months. It will discuss what can and cannot be concluded based on the available evidence,

This project examines the available samples using XRR measurements across a range of an-
gles and energy obtained from the 8.048 keV FCXR setup at DTU Space and in collaboration
with the PTB laboratory at the BESSY II synchrotron facility in Germany, both of which are
further described in Section 3.2.1. Measurements would have been made using the 1.487 keV
LEXR setup also at DTU Space. Unfortunately, the LEXR was out of order during the time frame
when measurements could have been obtained. This thesis will only be working with specular
XRR measurements. Non-specular XRR measurements are deemed to be outside the scope
of this project. This project will examine images of samples obtained using electron microscopy
thanks to a collaboration with DTU Nanolab. These images and XRR measurements will be
used to estimate a model of the coating thicknesses. Measurements of all witness samples
will be used in conjunction with these thickness estimates to determine the residual stress of
the coatings. This thesis will examine and compare the design with models estimated based
on measurements for the purpose of determining the overall quality of the coatings. The sig-
nificance and implications of any findings regarding the reflectivity of the coatings will primarily
be focused on aspects relating to the performance and stability of the samples. The aim is to
determine viability primarily by investigating whether the witness samples show any signs of
instability similar to the row 13 SPO plates. This project will not focus on all aspects and implica-
tions relating to the reflectivity and its measurements, such as non-specular reflection, off-axis
light or effective area of the optics. This thesis will primarily use measurements of SPO plates
for comparative purposes to determine the similarity and deviation between witness samples
and SPO plates. The project will not be examining any SPO stacks. This project will not be
able to conclusively determine the cause of instability in the row 13 SPO plates. The intention
is to show that the instability is not inherent to the general design or materials and that no other
samples show signs of instability in general or due to wet chemistry.
This project expects to find no significant signs of instability of the measured samples outside
the row 13 SPO plates. This thesis expects to find that samples exposed to wet chemistry will
show no significant change in reflectivity. Likewise, this thesis expects to find no significant
difference between measurements of witness samples and corresponding SPO plates, with
the exception of row 13 samples. This thesis does not expect to find any excessive amounts
of stress in the witness samples and that similar samples will show similar levels of stress.
Furthermore, it is expected that the trilayer samples will be less stressed than the single layer
and bilayer samples.

The X-Ray optics of NewAthena will consist of approximately 92,000 thin-film coated mirrors

shipped off to two subcontractors where the wafer is diced and a photoresist pattern is applied.
The plates are brought back to cosine, where they are coated and stacked into the SPO stacks.
1.3. NEWATHENA
3
==PAGE:19==
The SPO stacks are sent off to either BESSY II in Germany or ALBA in Spain where they are
characterized and assembled as aligned mirror modules. These mirror modules constitute the
building blocks of the NewAthena X-Ray optics. This process is illustrated in Figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: From silicon wafers to a functional NewAthena mirror. Source: Collon et al.[4]
The NewAthena mirrors are designed to maximize the reflectance around 6.8 keV in accor-
dance with the NewAthena science case, while ensuring the reflectance at 1 keV and below
remains performant.[5]. The goal is to obtain a set of mirrors with the highest reflectivity of
X-Rays of certain energies at a certain angle of incidence determined by the radial position of
the mirror. The incidence angle of mirror modules in the five innermost rows, is low enough
that a Cr/Pt/C trilayer thin-film coating provides sufficient reflectivity. Mirrors from the sixth row
and out will be based on a specific multilayer recipe as defined in Table 2.1. The mirrors are
evaluated based on reflectivity between ∼0.1-10 keV. This project focuses primarily on reflec-
tivity between 3.4-10 keV. The mirror optics will have a diameter of ∼2-2.5 m (13/15 rows of
mirror modules) and a focal length of 12 m[4][5].
Significant issues with instability have been observed in row 13 SPO samples. Severe dete-
rioration of the coating is not isolated to the exposure to wet chemistry, it has also been seen
to a lesser degree in untreated samples. The deterioration, taking form as delamination of the
thin-film deposition from the silicon substrate, can be seen in Figure 1.4. The figure presents
two samples based on the row 13 design, of which one has undergone wet chemistry. The
pictures on the left show the passive evolution of the coatings and the pictures on the right
show the catastrophic results of a tape test. In both cases, the tape test can be seen to peel
the entire coating clean off. Preliminary examination of the coating logs have shown that some
errors occurred during coating of the row 13 samples. It appears that given the position of the
SPO plates in the chamber and the rotational velocities required to achieve the specific layers
meant that the chamber in some cases began de-/accelerating before the layer had been fully
deposited. One theory is, that this velocity differential led to non-uniformity in the thickness of
some layers and significant deviation from the design specifications. Which, in turn, resulted
in an excessive amount of residual stress of the coating causing the observed instability.

2.1.1
Electromagnetic Radiation & X-Rays
Electromagnetic radiation, commonly referred to as light, is often measured in either its wave-
length, λ, or frequency, f. The wavelength or frequency of light varies greatly across the
electromagnetic spectrum. The electromagnetic spectrum consists of seven regions: Radio,
microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-Ray and gamma ray. From radio waves measur-
ing in kilometers to gamma rays smaller than the width of an atom. In some cases, X-Rays
and gamma rays are described not by wavelength or frequency but by energy, E, typically
measured in eV. The relation between these three units is defined as

EM radiation offers a vast range of applications and has become a foundational pillar of our

Or rather, it was the only tool for millennia up until very recently when we measured the gravity

enabling us to peer progressively deeper into the universe. There are two ways to accomplish
this. Lenses and mirrors. Refraction and reflection.

section will focus on double reflection designs, specifically the Wolter geometry. Alternatives

use of two mirrors means that the effective reflection is the product of the reflectance of each

lower grazing incidence angles, focus and handling off-axis light. Wolter geometry offers sev-

Thin-Film Coatings
With the basics of X-Rays and optics discussed in Section 2.1 this section aims to examine the
key concepts in the design and production of thin-film coatings as they relate to their use as X-
Ray mirrors. The ultimate goal is to gain a theoretical understanding of the how’s and why’s of
the issues currently affecting the row 13 SPO prototype, aiding in determining their root cause.
These concepts could further support the development of solutions to these issues, such that
the Pt/C multilayer design might be finally qualified for the NewAthena mission ahead of it’s
mission adoption in 2027.
2.2.1
Coating Design
Optimizing a design for a specific set of performance metrics is no straightforward task. There
are a large number of parameters that may interact to affect performance in ways that might be
difficult to predict. It is not just a process of choosing one layer for one specific energy level and
another for a second energy level. Tweaking one parameter may affect performance across
both the incidence angle and energy spectrum. However, it is not entirely a guessing game.
Through concepts outlined in Section 2.1 and previous experimental work in the field of thin-film
coatings, the effect on performance can be approximated[15]. The use of modeling tools such
as IMD can further qualify theoretical designs. The use of IMD has been further described in
Section 3.1. Furthermore, complexity grows exponentially with the introduction of additional
design parameters. Coating designs can be grouped into three general categories: Single, bi-
and multilayers. Each of these is further described in Section 2.2.2, 2.2.3, and 2.2.4. The use
of IMD modeling is explored in Chapter 4, in which Figure 4.7 offers a modeled comparison of
single, bi- and multilayers.
Another important consideration when designing a coating is the feasibility of production. This
encourages a certain level of knowledge and experience regarding the coating process when it
comes to designing a realistically producible mirror. An example is the importance of material
choice. Not all materials can be deposited with the same smoothness. The atomic structure

of a layer, such as crystallinity and homogeneity, may also affect the reflectance. Material
and other production parameters may also introduce an internal residual stress to a coating
that might prove unsustainable considering potential requirements such as long term stability.
Residual stress of thin-film coatings is of specific interest, as it presents a likely explanation to
the issues plaguing the row 13 SPO prototype[5]. The importance of residual stress including
how it might be determined is examined further in Section 2.2.7.
The primary parameters of interest when describing the structure of thin-film coatings are as

• σ: Roughness/diffuseness

2.2.2
Single Layer Coatings
The simplest coatings are single layer coatings, consisting of a substrate and a single layer
deposited on top. This type is fairly limited in regards to number of parameters. The primary
ones being material, layer thickness z and roughness/diffuseness σ. For this type there are two
σ’s to consider, the surface of the coating, and the interface between the coating and the sub-
strate. Dense materials are often used for single layer reflectors including Au, Pt, Ir and W as
explained by Equation 2.5 and 2.6[11]. As seen in Section 4.1 the improvement of reflectance
caused by an increase in z quickly falls off. Instead, the thickness is seen to heavily influence
the reflectance at specific angles based on perceived oscillations in constructive and destruc-
tive interference. Meaning, that a single layer can be optimized for a specific angle based on z.
The biggest impact to reflectance is the choice of material, though realistically there is a limited
pool of materials to choose from, and certain materials might prove too expensive as was the
case for Ir in earlier NewAthena design iterations. σ generally causes a damping effect on the
reflectance. Rougher interfaces result in poorer reflectance
2.2.3
Bilayer Coatings
Bilayers represent a step up in complexity over single layer coatings. Comprised of a light
material (low-Z) coated on top of a dense material (high-Z), in the case of NewAthena. Bilayers
offer some improvement over single layers. One of the biggest advantages over single layer
coatings, is the increased possibility of optimization with the addition of a second thickness
parameter. The addition of a second material can help combat the impact of absorption edges,
by choosing complementary materials. Diffuseness becomes more important compared to
single layer coatings. As seen in Section 4.2 the reflectance is quite similar to that of a single
layer. Bilayers introduce the Γ parameter, usually given by the fraction between the thickness
of the heaviest layer z and the bilayer thickness d

Depending on the material(s) and the circumstances under which it was deposited, a thin-film
coating will contain a certain level of residual stress. In some coatings, this stress is negligible.
In some cases, residual stress can severely affect the stability, viability, and performance of a
coating. As mentioned in Section 2.2.6, there are certain factors in the coating process that
can reduce the development of residual stress in a coating. Some materials are more prone
to stress than others. The stress of a thin-film is classified as either tensile or compressive.
The impact of both becomes apparent when looking at Figure 2.7. Too much tensile stress can
lead to cracks in the film and compressive stress can lead to the film delaminating from the
substrate[24].

Methods of Characterization
This chapter will introduce the methods of characterization that have been employed in the ex-
amination of the NewAthena thin-film samples. Measuring the X-Ray reflectance of a coating
does not reveal anything about the coating and it’s structure. Except, of course, it’s ability to

can be extracted from experimental data by combining modeling tools with measurement. This

limits. Lastly, this chapter will present the insight necessary to verify that the combined applied
methods of characterization provide an adequate basis of evidence to reach a well founded

3.1

Through the implementation of thin-film reflectance modeling, a best fit structure can be ap-
proximated by comparing and evaluating the likeness of measured and modeled reflectance.

reproduce the reflective features of a coating. Experimental data of a material’s X-Ray prop-
erties can be combined with the theoretical basis to ensure that the performance of a thin-film
design can be estimated to an acceptable degree.

3.2
Experimental Characterization
Thin-film coatings can be characterized in a variety of ways. Thorough examination typically
warrants the use of multiple types of tests used in tandem. There is no one experiment that will
reveal everything about a coating, and examining the same parameters through several exper-
imental methods is a good way to confirm the validity of the experimental findings. The type(s)
of experiments best suited for testing is ultimately dependent on the goal. What parts of the
coating is actually of interest. Is it the layer structure, the surface, the chemical composition,
or some other parameter. These questions should be among the first to be asked in order to
determine which tests are relevant. Other important factors is whether a destructive method is
acceptable, if not then tests must be limited to the non-destructive options.
Among the characterization methods not described in the following subsection, Atomic Force
Microscopy (AFM) and X-Ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) are among the most com-
monly used[11]. AFM allows for highly precise examination of the surface of a coating and can
be either destructive or non-destructive. XPS is a destructive option for determining the exact
chemical composition of a coating and even the specific layers. Optical microscopy can be
used to examine large scale defects but offers limited usefulness beyond that.
3.2.1
X-Ray Reflectometry
X-Ray Reflectometry (XRR) presents the most common method of testing the reflectance of
X-Ray mirror thin-film coating, in a non-destructive way no less. Reflectance is the main pur-
pose of the coatings after all. However, as detailed in Section 3.1 the reflectance can be used
to investigate many aspects of a coating, including estimating the structure of the coated lay-
ers. Specular reflectometry measurements are used to evaluate the effective reflectance of a
coating, whereas non-specular reflectometry offers insight into the extent of scattering. Non-
specular XRR can be performed by illuminating the sample at a set θi and measuring the X-Ray
intensity at varying detector angles. Unless the sample is a very poor reflector, the measured
data should show a definite peak at the detector angle equivalent to θi where it will measure
the reflected X-Rays and not just the scattered. Non-specular XRR has not been performed
as part of this project; it was considered very early on as a nice-to-try experiment to perform
if all other experiments had been completed. Specular XRR, however, constitutes the back-
bone of this project. Following these facts, this thesis generally refers to specular XRR as XRR.
XRR is typically measured as a function of either θi or E. These are typically referred to as
angle and energy scans. An angle scan is done using a set energy level with measurements
captured at varying θi and reversely so for energy scans. If it was even remotely feasible, mea-
suring the reflectance across the 2 dimensional parameter space presented by θi or E would
obviously be preferred. However, the astronomical amount of data measurements and, by ex-
tension, time required to do so completely negates any benefits of that. As such, measuring
the reflectance as an angle scan at one or a few energy levels and vise versa for energy scans
is the best alternative. Reflectance at any other permutation of θi and E must be found by ex-
trapolating from measurements. One way of doing so would be through modeling as described
in Section 3.1.
Angle scans are by far the ”simplest” to perform as an experimentally setup only requires pro-
duction of X-Rays at a single energy level. Energy scans, on the other hand, effectively require
a synchrotron to generate the necessary number of energy levels to perform a proper energy
scan. Energy scans can be a great way to measure the impact of absorption edges of the

LEXR

3.2.1.3
BESSY II
The PTB laboratory at BESSY II offers a much more impressive test setup than what is available
through FCXR and LEXR at DTU Space. The measurements of NewAthena samples taken at
BESSY II were measured in an energy range of 3.4 keV to 10 keV at a spectral resolution of 1
eV. The measurements were taken at θi = [0.7◦, 1.0◦, 1.3◦]. The X-Ray illumination is provided

sample is controlled by a monochromator crystal. BESSY II measurements offer a great in-
sight to the working performance of the mirrors on top of data foundation for characterization of
the coating. Considering that the coatings for each row will be angle at one specific angle, it is
easy to test the real performance through an energy scan at the given angle of the specific row.

Electron Microscopy
Electron microscopy offers an impressive view of the nanoscale structure of a thin-film coating.
There are several types of electron microscope with varying levels of resolution. A witness
sample from row 6 and 13 have been imaged using a Scanning Transmission Electron Micro-
scope (STEM). The best image captured as a pixel size of 19.5 pm. STEM as opposed to a
Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) measures the transmission of electron through a sample.

To accomplish this, the sample must be thinner than 100 nm. It takes a complicated multi-step
process to obtain such a thin sample.
Sample preparation is done using Focused Ion Beam (FIB) in combination with a SEM. The first
step is to identify a region on the surface of the sample that might yield an acceptable sample.
This means finding a part of the surface with little or no surface imperfections. To protect the
coating being unnecessarily damaged a thin layer of platinum is applied on the surface. Any
damage done will be done to this protective layer. An area in the shape of a horseshoe around
the sample spot is ”carved” out by the FIB. The FIB does this by bombarding the surface with
ions chipping away small parts of the coating with each impact. Once the the area around the
sample has been carved out, the FIB is used to separate the upper part of the sample for the
underlying substrate by aiming the FIB at a converging angle from both sides. This leaves the
sample hanging only attached at one end. The sample is then ”welded” to a tungsten needle by
applying more platinum. Once attached, the last part connecting the sample to the rest of the
coating is carved away and the sample is lifted out. The sample is then attached to a sample
”grid” or sample holder similarly by applying platinum and the needle is detached using the FIB.
The final step is using the FIB to carve away material until a sliver is left. The STEM will be
seeing through the side of this sliver of the sample, which is why it must be so thin. The thinner
the sample can be cut, the better the STEM image will generally be.
Once all samples have been prepared, they are introduced to the STEM. The STEM can mea-
sure using several different detectors: High-Angle Annular Dark Field (HAADF), Bright Field
(BF-S), Dark Field Sum (DF-S), Dark Field-Inner (DF-I), Dark Field-Outer (DF-O). Each can
be used to generate a image. Different detectors detect materials differently. Dense elements
like Pt will appear bright in HAADF while lighter elements like C will appear dark. The oppo-
site is the case of the remaining detectors. STEM can also be used in combination with an
Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDX) detector to analyze the composition of the im-
aged coating. This compositional data will not be as accurate as what could be analyzed using
XPS.
The structure of the STEM images can be analyzed using rudementary image analysis tools.
Converting the pixel y-axis (corresponding to the depth of the coating) to a 1 dimensional vector
(by averaging the value of each row, taking a single column or a mix) which can be treated as a
signal of the depth profile. This signal profile can be analyzed to identify the peaks and valleys,
equivalent to the center position of each layer. The distance between each peak (or layer) can
be found through the Full Width Half Maximum (FWHM) of the signal around a peak. Slight
variations in depth across the length of the TEM image means averaging each row of the im-
age into one image average (aka the whole image mean) might result in a damping or blurring
effect on the signal. Instead by splitting the image into several column strips, the image can
be analyzed in sections generating a large set of thickness values. The distribution of these
thicknesses can be analyzed and through statistics, can be analyzed to examine the standard
deviation of each layer thickness across the image.

Modeling Multilayer Coatings
Stacking multiple bilayers constitutes a multilayer. The multilayer modeled for this section is a
periodic multilayer consisting of 10 Pt/C bilayers. The design can be seen in Table 4.3. With

Relevant parameters

By averaging or ”collapsing” the horizontal rows of pixels turns the 2D image matrix into a ”sig-

as briefly discussed in Section 3.2.2, to identify the positions of the peaks and valleys of the
signal, which can be used to determine the thickness. The thickness of a layer is determined
by the pixel width between the interfaces on either side of a identified peak. The interfaces are
determined by the FWHM of the signal for each peak and valley. This means that the position
of the two interfaces of a peak is determined by the position where the signal intensity between

peaks identified by the peak finding algorithm.
Figure 7.3 also shows the position of the signal peaks. These peaks are identified autonomously
using a simple peak finding algorithm from the SciPy Signal Python library[37]. However, as
the figure clearly shows in the cs0337 signal, the signal contains multiple peaks to each actual
layer which are not marked. That is because several steps have been taken to ensure that only
one peak is identified per layer. Figure 7.4 shows what would happen if no steps were taken to
remove invalid peaks and how the whole image signal would look without improvements. The
peaks are split into two categories, top peaks and bottom peaks (also known as valleys). The
top peaks describe the position of the platinum layers, whereas the bottom peaks describe the
position of the carbon layers. At least this is the case for the HAADF images, where high inten-
sity (lighter) equals higher density (platinum) and lower intensity (darker) equals lower density
(carbon or silicon).
The first step in filtering the peaks is smoothing the signal by applying a 1D uniform filter of size
K to calculate the K-mean of the signal at each point or pixel. The effect of this step can be
seen by comparing the signal of cs0337 seen in Figure 7.3 and 7.4. This smoothing dampens
the effect of any larger outliers while keeping the shape of the signal. The next step is to tune
how the peaks are found by determining how small an intensity ∆should constitute a peak.
After this step, peaks are discarded by some logic statements based on the relative intensity ∆
between a peak and it’s neighbors, if the ∆is too large on a relative scale the peak is removed.
The last step in the filtering process is to check the order of the peaks. If the list of peaks
contains two or more top peaks or bottom peaks in a row, then only the first is kept while the
rest are discarded. Because the layers are ordered as alternating platinum and carbon, there
cannot be two layers of the same type in a row. The process of identifying and filtering is based
on Python script supplied by Diego Parades Sanz, a PhD student at DTU Space. The script
has been heavily customized to suit the needs of this project.

Calculating the signal by averaging all 4096 pixels in each row causes the features of the sig-
nal to become very damped. Because of blanketing and other similar effects, averaging a row
means that all the deviations compound to decrease the contrast. This leads to the idea of
splitting the image up in to several vertical pieces or strips that can each be turned into a signal
and used to determine layer thicknesses. The image was split into 1024 strips each with a width
of 4 pixels, leading to 1024 signals, and tens of thousands of peak positions and thicknesses.
Using this method the distribution of identified peak positions and thicknesses of each layers
can be plotted and examined. By examining the distribution of thicknesses of a layer might be
used to (partly) ignore the influence of blanketing. Assuming that the effects somewhat follow a
gaussian distribution, then it might be possible to determine a mean µz and standard deviation
σz of each layer thickness z. One important aspect of the pixel width of each strip is balancing
getting a high enough number of signals with an acceptable quality. A wider strip means more
pixel to average leading to outlier pixels having a smaller impact. A higher quality signal means
peak identification will be more successful. Not all signals will be able to identify every layer,
so higher quality signal means more peaks detected. But wider strips also mean fewer signals,
which means a smaller number of thicknesses to examine the distribution of. While doubling
the width to 8 pixel might increase the number of peaks detected by 10-15% (estimated purely
on experience), it will also halve the maximum number of peaks that can be found. Where
1024 strips (4 pixel wide) might roughly find 8-900 peaks per layers, the number would fall to
450-500 peaks per layers with 512 strips (8 pixel wide). These numbers are purely estimated
based on experience serving only to illustrate the importance of balancing and should not be
considered as factual.
Experience has also shown that the positional accuracy of the identified peaks does not sig-
nificantly improve with increasing pixel width of a strip beyond 3-4 pixels. 1 or 2 pixel wide
strips were found to give signals of much lower quality and were much more vulnerable to the
effects of outlier data. Although it was never fully examined, it is assumed to be connected
to the application of the 1D uniform filter. With a strip width of only 1 or 2 pixels, outliers will
have a much stronger influence. As Figure 7.3 shows, the image of cs0337 gives a much more
complex signal compared to that of cs0340. Much of the work fine-tuning the filtering was fo-
cused on the cs0337 signal. Once the filtering algorithm gave acceptable results it was shown
to work flawlessly with the cs0340 signal requiring no alteration to generate acceptable results.
Given enough time, the filtering could surely have been further improved to correctly identify
and filter false peaks at an even better rate. Unfortunately, time is the enemy of perfection.
The shape of the cs0337 signal at lower layers (higher depth based on Figure 7.3) means that
the FWHM method of determining the positions of boundaries and thereby the thicknesses is
insufficient. The problem lies with the ”double peak” shape of the signal. At greater depths,

7.6
Conclusion
This chapter has found a new source of information that sheds further light on the coating struc-
ture of the row 6 and 13 witness samples. As the analyzes have shown, there are a number of
things that these measurements cannot say for sure. In part, due to limitations of the analysis
tools, the algorithms struggle to correctly identify the peaks and estimate the thicknesses. The
examination of the cs0340 image has shown that the pixel size proved to be a limitation in es-
timating the peak positions and layer thicknesses. Analysis of both samples has revealed the
inadequacy of the layer interface identification method that leads to the thickness estimates. It
would seem that the FWHM is not nearly enough to properly estimate the borders. The best ex-
planation for this is to consider the contrast of the image and the layers. Based on this relative
measure of intensity, it is difficult to determine exactly which intensity level best explains the
position of the interface. Tweaking the contrast, brightness, Gamma (not related to the coating
parameter Γ), and saturation of the images could all have an effect on the relative intensity of
the layers. The intensity of the images is, after all, only a relative measure of density. The al-
gorithm might be more successful in properly identifying layers and interface if used on images
zoomed in on the bottom layers. However, these images will not show the entire coating, and it

==PAGE:91==
might be difficult to properly compare estimates of the zoomed image to estimates from images
of the full coating.
However, this has not just been an exercise in futility. The analyzes have yielded concrete
and realistic estimates to the coating thicknesses. Although not every layer thickness that has
been found is equally realistic, it has still yielded very valuable insight into the general coating
structure. The d-spacings of the bilayers have been much simpler to estimate and seem accu-
rate in most cases. The total thickness of each coating will be able to verify the models found
in Chapter 5 and 6. So too will the d-spacings and partially the layer thicknesses (given their
uncertainty). Further work might also yield results regarding the roughness and diffuseness
of the interfaces of the layers. The code used for this chapter does yield roughness estima-
tions of the layers. The issue is that the values varied a lot and were, in some cases, in the
order of several nanometers, which is not realistic. Due to this and time constraints, the use
of these values was abandoned. Further work might also improve the filtering of the signal
peaks. Some of the STEM images are stitched together of up to a hundred images. These
would be useful but due to the speed the images were taken, significant charge was built up
causing the sample to drift slightly causing the images to smear. Feature tracking should be
able to account for the drift and lead to clearer images. Due to time constraints, this has not
been implemented. The thickness of cs0337 was determined to be ∼164 nm and the thickness
of cs0340 was determined to be ∼103 nm.

8.6
Conclusion
Examination of the stylus profiler measurements have shown varying levels of stress in the sin-
gle, bi-, and trilayers, while indicating that the application of a chrome underlayer decreases the
compressive stress of Pt/C coatings. All measured thin-film coatings have been found to con-
tain compressive residual stress. The chapter has found that the multilayer coatings present
a far more consistent level of stress with the stress also being much more uniform across the
sample. Examinations of all coatings have shown that while the choice of thickness model and
νs has an impact on stress estimation, none have led to contradictory results that would call
into question the general validity of the thickness models or the stress results.
Although the stress of the row 13 coatings have been found to be less consistent than the row
6 coatings, they have been found to consistently be and significantly less stressed than the row
6 coatings. With the row 13 coatings having an average compressive stress of ∼420 MPa, less
than half that of the row 6 coatings. These results, along with everything seen in the previous

Conclusion
Everything in the universe has a beginning and an end; so too must this thesis and the as-
sociated project. The only goal of this chapter is to summarize the key concepts and findings
identified in the preceding chapters along with re-evaluating any conclusions made along the
way under a more holistic framework. Chapter 2, 3, and 4 sought to introduce, establish, and
explain a framework of the theoretical and experimental methodologies applied in the project
with the dual purpose of ensuring that someone not intimately familiar with NewAthena and
thin-film coatings for X-Ray mirrors would be well suited to follow the work laid out in this thesis
and to substantiate the validity of the analytical results and findings. Chapter 5 and 6 presented
the experimental results gathered using XRR and the core of the consequent analytical work
and findings. Chapter 7 presented the image analysis of the STEM images and the findings
providing a secondary distinct source of characterization compared to XRR measurements.
Chapter 8 presented a critical evaluation of the characterizations presented in previous chap-
ters and their subsequent analytical use in combination with profilometry measurements and
the findings regarding the residual stress of the thin-film coatings.
9.1
X-Ray Reflectometry
Analysis of FCXR measurements found a set of fit models that provided a decent and accept-
able approximation of the data with overall realistic model parameters. The bottom carbon
thickness of the row 6 best fit model was found to be potentially problematic. However, it might
be a reflection of the reality seen in the STEM images where the bottom carbon layers were
almost indistinguishable. The total thickness of the best fit models were found to be +4.5%
(cs0336) and +2.8% (cs0339) of the design. The cs0336 best fit found +9.8% average Γ from
design, ranging from Γ = 0.68 −0.78 while the cs0339 best fit found +12.8% average from de-
sign with Γ = 0.53 −0.55. Comparison of samples before and after exposure to wet chemistry
showed no measurable change in reflectivity of row 6 and row 13 witness samples. Compar-
ison of measured reflectivity of witness samples and corresponding SPO plates showed only
minor and insignificant deviation between row 6 cs0336 and SPO 2443-08, suggesting a high
degree of similarity between them. The reflectance of cs0339 and SPO 2445-06 was seen to
be similar at most angles with three exceptions where the reflectivity varied significantly. Anal-
ysis showed that majority of the deviation could be explained by the bottom layer thickness
parameters. The preliminary SPO 2445-06 fit model, which could be much improved with fur-
ther work, showed a significant difference from the cs0339 best fit model. The thickness of the
five uppermost layers in the SPO 2445-06 best fit model were on average -4.6% of the cs0339
fit model, for the five bottommost layers this average was +11.5% for carbon and +39.5% for
platinum. Given the quality of the SPO 2445-06 best fit, these results should be seen as pre-
liminary rough estimates.
Likewise, analysis of BESSY II measurements found a set of fit models providing decent and
acceptable approximations of data also with realistic parameters. The BESSY II measurements
of cs0336 were able to decently constrain the thickness of the carbon cap layer unlike the FCXR
data. Unfortunately, the same was not possible for BESSY II measurements of cs0339. The
measurements struggled to properly constrain the bottom thicknesses, as such the best fit mod-
els based the value of these bottom layers on the FCXR best fit models. The total thickness
of the fit models differs from the designs by +5.5% (cs0336) and +2.5%. The Γ of the cs0336

9.2
Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy
The method, with which the thicknesses were determined based on the STEM images, was
seen to be incapable of properly determining the position of the interface and thereby the thick-
ness at thinner layers. It was mostly seen to be an issue for the cs0337 image due to the
irregular shape of the image signal. However, the estimated d-spacings were seen to be much
more in agreement with the XRR best fit models. The thickness of the edge layers were found
to be completely unrealistic due to several reason, including the relative contrast and the al-
gorithm not being able to estimate the position of the interface between the platinum and the
substrate. The pixel size was seen to be a limitation in the result of the cs0340 image analysis.
Using the FWHM, to determine the interface, proved problematic for the cs0337 image due to
the shape of the signal and the ”double peaks” of the lower platinum layers losing contrast. For
these reasons, the values of the platinum cap layer thickness of cs0340 along with the carbon
cap layer and bottom platinum layer thicknesses were based on the BESSY II and FCXR best
fit models. The total thickness estimates differed by +14.2% (cs0337) and +12.8% (cs0340)
compared to the design model. The accuracy of these estimates are somewhat questionable
based on the findings of Chapter 7. The estimated d-spacings are in almost every case found to
be slightly thicker than those of the XRR best fit models. Although the estimates are not 100%
accurate, they do provide further indication to the samples being coated thicker than designed.
It was not possible to determine whether the barely distinguishable carbon layers at the bottom
of the coatings were only due to their thinness or whether the FIB process might have smeared
platinum over the carbon, clouding the image intensity.
9.3
Residual Stress
The samples were all found to contain compressive residual stress. The residual stress did not
differ significantly between the single layers and bilayers, with the exception of cs0328 which
also had a significantly more non-uniform stress. The trilayers were seen to be significantly
less stressed with the exception of cs0333 with a stress an order of magnitude larger than the
other two trilayers. The deviation of the stress of this sample was also seen to be significantly

contain less than half the amount of stress that was found for row 6.
This thesis has found that both row 6 and row 13 witness samples can survive exposure to
wet chemistry with no significant impact to reflectance. The row 6 SPO sample is also seen to
survive the process without issues. The XRR measurements of cs0335, cs0336, and cs0337
show no deviation in reflectance, indicating that the witness sample coatings are functionally
identical. The same is seen in XRR measurements of cs0338, cs0339, and cs0340. The coat-
ings of the row 6 witness sample and SPO sample are seen to be very similar based on XRR
measurements. None of the witness samples are seen to be excessively stressed, with row 13
containing half the stress of row 6. The row 13 witness and SPO samples are seen to deviate
significantly based on XRR measurements. This means that the coating of the unstable row 13
SPO plate is not equivalent to the coating applied to the witness samples. All of this indicates
that the row 13 sample should show no stability issues or problems with wet chemistry if prop-
erly applied to an SPO plate. As such, this thesis finds no reason why the row 13 design could
not be viable for the NewAthena mission. However, further studies are needed to conclusively
determine why the row 13 SPO plates became unstable and to examine how to prevent future
row 13 coatings from experiencing similar production problems.
9.4
Future Work
Due to time constraints and other various reasons, there were a few things that could not be
accomplished during this project. One of the bigger parts is the analysis of the STEM images.
With more time, the method could likely be further refined to produce better results, including
better estimation of the thicknesses. Future work might also improve the quality of the image
signal (primarily of cs0337) by adjust image parameters, such as brightness, gamma and con-
trast. The images can also be used to estimate the roughness of the layers. The code for this
estimation did not produce anything remotely realistic, with one layer having a roughness of
several nm and the next layer a roughness of 0.1 nm. Due to limited time, attempts to make
this method work properly were abandoned in favor of improving the thickness estimation. The
filtering method could also be improved. Further work could also be used to implement drift
correction of the stitched images, potentially leading to clearer images.
As previously stated, the LEXR setup at DTU was unfortunately out of order at the time when
measurements could have been obtained. This means that the estimation of the carbon cap
layer is limited to the BESSY II measurements of cs0336. This value has been used as the
best estimate in other models but further measurements focused at the upper layer would be
an improvement in this regard. Once again due to lack of time, the SPO fit models are not
as refined as the other XRR fit models. They could likely be improved given more attention.
The FCXR and BESSY II measurements found some fairly differing results of roughness which
could stand to be improved. Modeling the BESSY II best fit models with a slight shift in angle
could potential explain the small discrepancy in BESSY II measurements of witness samples
taken five months apart.
