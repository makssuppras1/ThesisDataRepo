Abstract
Globally, the demand for cooling is on the rise as global warming rises global temperatures
and developing countries comfort standards get higher. Since cooling demand is closely
related to solar radiation, the use of solar energy for cooling becomes an interesting idea.
This project aims to evaluate the performance of two solar­driven cooling technologies.
The widely used vapour compression air conditioner powered by solar panels is compared
to the solar thermal­driven ejector cooling. The performance of these technologies is
evaluated from an energy as well as economic point of view in three different locations
(Copenhagen, Barcelona and Delhi) and three different sizes (5 kW, 20 kW and 300 kW).
To do so, component­based simulation models of the ejector and the vapour compression
systems are implemented in EES (Engineering Equation Solver).
The results show that the vapour compression cycle is superior to the ejector in every
aspect of the comparison. The area of solar collectors needed for the ejector cycle in
order to have similar energy consumption to the conventional vapour compression air
conditioner is excessive. The effect of different assumptions made on the design process
of the system is also evaluated and the findings show the importance of a correct design
of the ejector in order to optimize its performance.
The higher energy consumption and cost of the ejector system make the conventional
vapour compression units a far better investment. The addition of solar panels to these
conventional vapour compression air conditioner units is found to be very beneficial. The
synergy between the cooling demand and solar generation is specially beneficial in loca­
tions were the electricity price is high. Facilitating the implementation of PV panels and
vapour compression air conditioning units that use natural, low­Global Warming Potential
(GWP) refrigerants is therefore crucial to face the growing demand of cooling.
Keywords: Cooling, air conditioning, solar energy , energy efficiency, sustainability, pho­
tovoltaics, solar collector, flat plate collector, evacuated tube collector, heat storage, con­
denser, evaporator, ejector refrigeration, vapour compression cycle, natural refrigerant

Introduction
1.1
Background
The International Energy Agency (IEA) identified the growing demand for cooling as one
of the three major areas of energy use that fail to attract the level of attention from policy
makers that they deserve. Using air conditioners and electric fans accounts for nearly
20% of the total electricity used in buildings around the world today [1] and there are
various reasons to expect an increase in cooling demand all over the world.
The Paris Agreement’s long­term temperature goal is to keep the increase in global aver­
age temperature to well below 2 °C above pre­industrial levels [2]. Global warming will,
even if the goal is reached, substantially increase the cooling demand in many parts of the
world. As the planet gets warmer, the market for air conditioning expands. Between 1990
and 2016, sales of air conditioning (AC) units have quadrupled from 33 million to 130 mil­
lion per year. There are now more than 1.6 billion air conditioners in use around the world
and more than half of them are localized in China and the United States. As incomes
rise and populations grow in developing countries, higher comfort standards increase the
demand of space cooling. Most of these developing countries are located in the world’s
warmest regions, and in countries like India and Indonesia less than 8% of households
had an AC in 2018. For this reason, the IEA predicts that the number of AC units will
increase to 5.6 billion by 2050 which will therefore increase the energy consumption for
cooling from 850 GW in 2016 to 3,350 GW [1]. In addition to the massive use of fossil
fuel­powered air conditioners, the environmental impact of AC units is further worsened
due to the considerable adoption of high­Global Warming Potential (GWP) refrigerants,
i.e. hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). This way, as the climate warms, the growing demand for
cooling is creating more warming in a destructive feedback loop [3].
Increasing access to efficient cooling with low­Global Warming Potential (GWP) refriger­
ants is crucial for the development of a sustainable future. It will, in fact, contribute to
most of the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). For example, sustainable cold
chains not only increase incomes for farmers and fishers but also help reducing hunger
and malnutrition and increase access to vaccines and medicines.
Since cooling demand is closely related to solar radiation, collecting solar energy to power
AC units is an interesting concept. There is a wide variety of pathways to produce cooling
effect from solar radiation. The first includes collecting solar radiation using photovoltaic
panels to produce electricity and use this electricity in a conventional vapour compression
(VC) cycle. The other pathway would be using a thermal collector to produce heat and
use this heat in a thermally­activated chiller to produce the cooling effect.
Solar thermal driven equipment offers again a wide variety of options. Heat can be used
to power direct cycle machines such as Stirling or Rankine engines to drive a vapour
compression cycle but the efficiency and temperature requirements of such cycles make
them unsuitable for domestic heating [4]. Most of the studies or experiments are instead
focusing on sorption (absorption and adsorption) machines, which use physical or chemi­
cal attraction between different substances to produce a cooling effect. Lastly, the ejector
system refrigeration which has been in use for the last 100 years uses a high pressure
motive flow that is accelerated in the ejector to rise the pressure of a secondary flow and
produce the cooling effect.

==PAGE:16==
The impressive cost reduction and efficiency improvement of photovoltaic cells now seem
to favour electrically driven cooling equipment [4]. The comparison, however, may depend
on the size of the system and the climatic conditions; as, for example, efficiency of pho­
tovoltaic cells drop with higher temperatures while thermal solar collectors improve their
performance. In addition, solar thermal technologies have other advantages over pho­
tovoltaic (PV)­driven cooling such as enabling energy storage in thermal storage tanks
or having the possibility of being retrofitted in already existing water­heating installations.
Therefore, the evaluation on which technology is better suited to use solar energy for
refrigeration may not be trivial.
1.2
Literature Review
This section presents previous studies on solar cooling systems to provide context on the
current state of the investigated topic. There are various studies involving the energy and
economic comparison of different solar cooling technologies involving PV and thermal­
driven air conditioning.
The study by Fong et al. conducted in 2009 [5] compared different solar cooling tech­
nologies using component­based simulation models. The study compared the PV­driven
refrigeration using vapour compression cycles with some solar thermal technologies in­
cluding solar absorption refrigeration, solar adsorption refrigeration and solar solid desic­
cant cooing. From all these, the study found that PV­driven vapour compression refrig­
eration and solar absorption refrigeration had the highest energy saving potential in the
subtropical Hong Kong.
A similar study conducted in 2010 by Hartmann et al.[6] used simulation methods to com­
pare the performance of a solar electric system to a solar absorption system in two differ­
ent climates for a typical small office building exposed to two different European climates
(Freiburg, Germany and Madrid, Spain). The study also concluded that the thermal­driven
cooling was not competitive to the solar electric system mainly due to the higher cost of
the absorption chiller.
Despite being a lesser used technology, the solar ejector cooling cycle (SECC) has also
some potential thanks to its simplicity, low cost and reliability; as stated in investigations
such as the paper from Chesi et al. [7]. This paper studied the system in which the solar
powered ejector can be used to reduce the energy consumption of a vapour compression
air conditioner. The paper compares three different configurations: a conventional vapour
compression system operating alone, an ejection refrigerating system which delivers cool­
ing directly to the load and a cascade system combining both ejector and vapour com­
pression cycles. It concluded that the cascade system can save a considerable amount of
energy (up to 50% in mild climates such as Pantelleria, Italy) with respect to the reference
vapour compression air conditioner unit; specially in warmer, sunnier locations.
Other available reports about the current state of the ejector refrigeration technology are
the year­round simulation performed by Pridasawas et al. [8] and the simulation model of
the ejector presented by Yan et al. [9].
The article from Pridasawas et al. studied the performance of the solar­driven ejector
refrigeration system using iso­butane (R600a). It describes the effects of different oper­
ating conditions in year­round simulations using TRNSYS and EES simulation tools. The
study concluded that an efficient ejector system can only work in locations with high solar
radiation and where low condenser temperatures can be kept.
The article by Yan et al. describes the equations that model the operation of the ejector

cycle and compares the performance of different low­GWP refrigerants including HCFO­
1233zd(E), HFO­ 1336mzz(Z), HFO­1234ze(Z), HCFO­1224yd(Z) and HC­600. The study
concluded that HFO­1234ze(Z) had the highest COP of these alternative refrigerants. The
predicted performance of the ejector model is compared in this article with the experimen­
tal investigation carried out by Śmierciew et al.[10].
The simplicity of the ejector makes it attractive for retrofitting the system into other existing
thermal processes such as trigeneration, which uses waste heat from power production to
produce heating and cooling. The paper by Ahmadzadeh et al. [11] studies an interesting
alternative for the usage of the ejector by a solar driven combined power and ejector
refrigeration system (CPER) which uses evacuated tubes solar collectors to power an
organic Rankine cycle to produce power as well as the ejector for refrigeration. The study
includes a detailed economic evaluation of the system as well as a parametric study and
the results show how the performance closely dependant on the evaporator and generator
temperatures.
The literature for PV­powered solar cooling systems is more extensive than the ejector
cooling as this is a more mature technology. The studies from Li et al. [12] and Salilih
et al. [13] present interesting strategies to model a PV system coupled with a vapour
compression cycle for cooling. While the first provides a wider view of the state of the
technology, evaluating different configurations and strategies to combine both PV and
vapour compression refrigeration, the latter focuses on the direct coupling of the PV panel
to a direct­current compressor. Both papers show the incredible results of the synergy
between these two technologies.
1.3
Aim of project
The literature in solar cooling is extensive, but further investigation on a straight up com­
parison on the performance of the ejector cooling system and the solar electric cooling
system under the same conditions may be useful to have a better understanding on the
advantages and disadvantages of the solar ejector system with respect to the conven­
tional vapour compression air conditioner. This project uses the existing knowledge on
both technologies to compare the performance of the systems from a technical and eco­
nomic point of view.
Similarly to the studies from Fong et al. and Hartmann et al., the goal of the project is
to compare different solar cooling technologies in terms of efficiency and economy for
different cooling demands and locations. This study, however, will focus on PV­driven
vapour compression cycles and the solar ejector cooling cycle, which is not included in
any of those. The systems will be compared using three different size ranges; small­
size (5 kW), medium­size (20 kW) and large­scale (300 kW); at three different locations;
Copenhagen (Denmark), Barcelona (Spain) and Delhi (India).
It is hypothesized that under some circumstances, heat­driven solar cooling using ejectors
outperform photovoltaic­driven conventional vapour compression air conditioning units.
In order to study the performance of these systems under different circumstances, a model
(with a manageable number of inputs and outputs) is made for each component of the
system in EES. The goal of the models is to calculate the key parameters to compare the
performance of the different systems. The main indicators that will be used to compare the
overall performance of the system is the annual energy consumption and the equivalent
uniform annual cost (EUAC). The model requires not only parameters for the performance
of the components but also the addition of economic­related parameters to each of the
models that are based on real commercially available components whenever it is possible.

2.1.1

chosen base temperature is assumed to be 22 ºC [15].

PV­driven air conditioner
This system uses the energy absorbed by a photovoltaic panel to run a mechanical com­
pression cycle. The vapour compression cycle is widely used, and the photovoltaic solar
panels have greatly improved their availability during the last 10 years.
There are different configurations for PV­driven air conditioner. The usage of direct­current
(DC) compressors and battery system may allow the system more independence from
the grid as well as avoiding the use of an inverter, which function is to transform the DC­
generated output from the PV to useful alternating current. However, the modelled system
in this study consists on a PV system and inverter connected to the grid. This configuration
is more commonly used due to the excessive price of a battery storage system [16].

spring and autumn or in the evening if the temperatures are above the base temperature.

does not match the PV­generated electricity. Therefore, system is not using any storage
or backup generator.

In order to maintain an acceptable efficiency in the ejector , the generator temperature,

as inputs. This process results in an hour by hour evaluation of the energy consumption
from the auxiliary heater and the pump and the supplied cooling capacity of the system.

3.4
System Comparison
This section the advantages and disadvantages of the systems regarding the energy and
the economic performance are compared.
3.4.1
Energy performance
Both the COP of the vapour compression air conditioner and the ejector system decrease
as the average ambient temperature increases.
While the COP in the vapour compression cycle is between 2.5 and 5.5 (see Figure 3.3)
and accounts for the energy delivered to the compressor, the COP of the ejector is never
higher than 0.32 (see Figure 3.8), but includes the heat delivered to the generator as well
as the energy consumption of the feed pump. The systems must therefore be compared
in therms of the annual energy consumption. The heat demand on the generator implies
a massive energy consumption of the ejector air conditioner compared to the vapour com­
pression. However, in the SECC, the heat delivered to the generator is provided partly
by solar collectors. The results show that the area of solar collectors needed to have a
similar consumption to the vapour compression air conditioner is enormous, as the solar
34
==PAGE:49==
fraction has to be at least 95% in every case. By using the more efficient ET collectors,
the area of the solar field can be reduced, but it can still be excessive (see Figure 3.17).
Figure 3.17: Annual energy consumption of the 20 kW system in Barcelona using FP and
ET collectors compared to the VC electricity consumption
With a sufficient number of solar collectors, the energy consumption of the system is
comparable and even smaller than the vapour compression air conditioner. In locations
with less cooling demand, the necessary area is reduced. In Copenhagen, the necessary
area of FP solar collectors in a 20 kW system is less than 120 m2, while in Delhi is more
than 800 m2.
If the VC system is combined with PV panels, the energy consumption is greatly reduced.
If the same area used to power the ejector system effectively is used instead by PV panels
to power the compressor, the electricity consumption of the system is reduced by more
then 95% in Copenhagen and Barcelona, In this cases, the cooling demand wouldn’t just
be practically met just by the solar panels, but the majority of the electricity generated by
the PV could be sold to the grid.
However, due to the lack of energy storage, the standalone PV system would never be
able to fully meet the energy demand. In Copenhagen and Barcelona the energy demand
matches the solar radiation up to 95% of the time; so with a sufficient number of PV
panels, the demand energy imported from the grid is insignificant. In the case of Delhi,
on the other hand, the higher temperatures set up cooling demand even during times
with no solar radiation. In this case, the PV system can only provide at most 75% of
the annual electricity demand of the compressor, independently of the number of solar
panels used. With the ejector system, the tank provides more flexibility to better use
the solar energy for the system. Therefore, with sufficient solar collectors, the SECC
can overperform the PV systems in situations where the cooling demand does not match
the solar radiation (for example Delhi). This, however, would imply an extremely large
number of solar collectors. A better solution to a mismatch between the demand and the
PV generation may be complementing the PV system with a battery storage.

Economic results
As shown, the energy consumption of the SECC with a reasonable number of collectors
is higher than the VC air conditioning systems and in order to achieve a comparable en­
ergy consumption the area of collectors is extremely large. When studying the economic
performance, the investment cost of the collectors should be taken into account. How­

Discussion
The general results of the comparison are not favorable to the SECC in any of the param­
eters studied. In every location, the energy consumption of the system can be reduced
by the use of a SECC with a sufficiently large solar collector field; as Qaux can be reduced
to 0 due to the flexibility given by the storage system. The size of the solar collector
field needed to reduce the energy consumption to the level of that of a vapour compres­
sion AC unit is extremely large and when accounting for the investment cost of such an
amount of collectors, the actual cost of the cooling surpasses the vapour compression air
conditioning in every case.
The main difference between the locations when evaluating the energy consumption is
the massive difference in cooling demand.
The lowest demand is found in Copenhagen. This location also has the lowest amount of
yearly solar radiation and the lowest efficiency of the thermal collectors, therefore being
the location with the lowest solar energy generation per m2 in both PV and solar thermal
systems. On the other hand, lower temperatures on the condenser improve the COP of
the ejector and the vapour compressor unit. Despite Copenhagen being the location with
the highest energy price, the lower demand and lower performance of the solar subsystem
do not compensate the investment cost on a solar system in any case. Economically, the
best solution in Copenhagen is the conventional vapour compression air conditioner.
Barcelona presented the most interesting results. The climate in this location presents
temperatures and annual solar radiation between Copenhagen and Delhi. Interestingly,
lower temperatures on the condenser and therefore higher COP than Delhi compensate
for the lower solar radiation; and the amount of cooling obtained per m2 of PV panel
using the vapour compression cycle is higher than in Delhi. The conditions on cooling
demand and electricity price in Barcelona also makes the investment on PV panels have
a positive impact, reducing the EUAC up to a 16%. The ejector, however, is still not close
to the performance of the vapour compression cycle.
The high temperatures in Delhi throughout the whole year mean a high cooling demand
and therefore a larger number of solar collectors are needed to fulfill the heat demand on
the generator. The larger energy consumption in Delhi and lower electricity and natural
gas costs make Delhi the cheapest location of the three regarding the cost per kWh of
cooling in both ejector and vapour compression systems. These cheaper prices make the
investment in solar panels not as convenient as in Barcelona, despite the bigger demand
and better performance of the solar subsystems.
The results of sensitivity analysis show the importance of a correct design of the SECC
to maximize the performance of the system. The choice of the type of solar collectors,
for example, depends on the price of the collectors, the price of the auxiliary heat and the
climatic conditions. In the investigated case, the flat plate collectors are the best option in
every location when taking into account the savings in operation cost and the investment
cost of the collectors. Different assumptions on costs, however, may give the edge to the
evacuated tubes collectors, as this technology has a better performance, specially in cold
locations.
Another key aspect of the design of the system to consider is the use of an auxiliary heater
45
==PAGE:60==
to supply the necessary heat on the generator when the temperature of the tank is low.
This system is similar to the study from Pridasawas et al. [8]. Despite the natural gas
heater being cheaper alternative to the usage of electric heating for the generator, the
solution requires an oversized solar collector field and a large capacity for the auxiliary
heater to be comparable to the vapour compression air conditioning as shown by the
results from the year­round simulation from Pridasawas et al. as well as this study.
The alternative presented by Chesi et al. [7], using a vapour compression cycle and a cold
storage instead of an auxiliary heater would reduce greatly the heat losses of the tank and
allow a better usage of solar energy. In the chosen configuration of this study, the heat
losses and the curtailed solar energy are a huge factor in the excessive energy consump­
tion. While the energy consumption of these distinct configurations are not comparable,
the efficiencies and COP of both studies are similar.
Alternative configurations which make use of the ejector such as the mentioned study
from Chesi et al. or the configuration studied by Ahmadzadeh et al. [11] appear to have
better results than the chosen configuration for this project. According to existing literature
[5] [6], the absorption system is also a better alternative than the ejector for solar thermal
refrigeration, but a more detailed straight up comparison between both technologies could
be interesting due to the cheaper, more simple ejector.
This study can also be investigated further, for example, by adding to the comparison the
heating and domestic hot water demands in winter that could be supplied either by the
vapour compression air conditioning working as a heat pump or by the solar collectors
in the SECC presents interesting possibilities. This investigation could specially be in­
teresting in locations such as Barcelona, with high solar radiation and a high electricity
price.
Another interesting investigation regarding the ejector system could be a more detailed
evaluation on its use for trigeneration, which uses waste heat for heating and cooling typi­
cally involving an absorption chiller. In this case, the temperatures could allow for a higher
pressure in the generator, increasing the COP of the ejector cycle. The cheaper cost of
the ejector compared to the absorption chiller could give a competitive advantage to this
technology. The study should include a more detailed sizing of the heat exchangers and
the ejector as the operation of these components have a huge impact on the performance
of the ejector cycle.
While the performance of the ejector can be improved the use of PV panels and a con­
ventional vapour compression air conditioner still appears to be the better option from an
energy and economic point of view according to the results. The results show that the in­
vestment on PV panels can still a positive impact on the cost of the systems in Barcelona
and Delhi despite not considering any other electric energy consumption, any compensa­
tion for the excess energy nor any battery storage. While SECC could reduce the energy
consumption with respect to the vapour compression air conditioning, the area of col­
lectors and consequential investment would still be better used by PV panels in every
location.
Photovoltaic systems are shown to be a viable technology to decrease the electric con­
sumption with a positive economic impact. Its natural synergy with the vapour compres­
sion air conditioning, due to the allocation of the cooling demand usually coinciding with
the highest solar radiation, will further favour its implementation. Besides, the cost of
the system has decreased during the last decade and the efficiency and the knowledge
of such systems is still increasing. The system is positive not only for the economy of
46
==PAGE:61==
the user but also for the overall CO2 emissions and environmental impact and measures
should be taken in order to facilitate its implementation.

Conclusion
The global demand for cooling and air conditioning is expected to grow as global temper­
atures rise. In order to achieve a sustainable future, solutions for greener refrigeration
must be found. Investigation on efficiency of existing technologies, transitioning into low­
GWP refrigerants, etc. is vital. Since the demand of cooling is closely related to the solar
radiation, the combination of air­conditioning and solar energy is an interesting concept.
The goal of this work is to evaluate the performance of two solar­driven refrigeration strate­
gies: solar ejector cooling cycles (SECC) and PV­driven vapour compression air condi­
tioning using refrigerants with low global warming potential. A year­round simulation of
both systems is made in different sizes (5 kW, 20 kW and 300 kW) and locations (Copen­
hagen, Barcelona and Delhi) to study the effect of cooling demand, climatic conditions
and size on the energy and economic performance. The work includes a model in EES
of each of the components of the system to easy evaluate the energy consumption of the
systems and therefore the cost associated.
In this study, the ejector technology is shown to be far from the performance of the vapour
compressor systems in terms of energy consumption and also economic performance.
Despite the energy savings compared to the conventional vapour compression air condi­
tioning being plausible, this system would require an extremely oversized solar collector
field to match the consumption of the conventional air conditioner. Adding the PV­system
would further improve the performance of the vapour compression air conditioner in terms
of energy use but would also decrease the cost of cooling in locations with high cooling
demand.
In both technologies, locations with high solar radiation and able to keep low temperatures
at the condenser were favoured.
The effect on the results of different assumptions is also presented. The results show
that the desired temperature at the evaporator and the sizing of the heat exchangers are
important factors that greatly affect the performance of both ejector and vapour compres­
sion air conditioner. The sensitivity analysis shows that the performance of the ejector
depends greatly on the initial assumptions and it can be improved with the correct opti­
mization of the design. However, despite its potential, the solar­thermal technology is still
clearly outperformed by the vapour compressor air conditioner in every case.
This huge difference in the energy and economic performances between the vapour­
compression air conditioner and the SECC is further extended after the addition of the
PV system to power the vapour compression cycle, which had impressive results spe­
cially in Delhi and Barcelona.
While other possibilities could be investigated, the vapour compression air conditioning is
nowadays superior to any other technology. It is very likely that the increasing demand of
cooling all over the world will be met through the usage of this technology. Therefore, it is
very important to study further on the possibilities offered by these technology, increasing
the efficiency and the usage of natural, non­harming refrigerants.
