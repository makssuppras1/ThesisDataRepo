Abstract
In this thesis, it was assessed whether four pretreatments of amorphous and crystalline PET can
affect the enzymatic degradation at the optimal enzymatic assay using the enzyme LCCICCG.
Spectrophotometry was used to investigate product formation and enzyme activity after degrada­
tion of the PET. DSC was used to investigate thermal physical transformation characteristics of
the pretreated PET. The four pretreatments were (1) use of plasticizer, (2) mechanical grinding,
(3) partial chemical degradation and (4) use of surfactants. The pretreatment, use of plasticizer,
decreased the Tg to 62◦C of APET after soaking for one day at 64◦C with water as the plasticizer,
but the decrease in Tg did not have an effect on the enzymatic degradation. The mechanical grind­
ing increased the surface area of the PET and the particle size of 0.355 to 0.4 mm showed the most
positive effect on the enzymatic degradation. The partial chemical degradation, was a microwave
assisted glycolysis working at reaction temperatures between 71◦C to 142◦C at a timespan from
5 to 40 seconds, resulting in a more crystalline PET, which decreased the enzymatic degradation.
The surfactants, SDS, DDM and Tween­20, were used to charge the PET surface to increase the
attraction between PET and the enzyme and thereby increase the enzymatic degradation. The sur­
factant did not increase the enzymatic degradation.

Assignment text
Title: Intensification of enzymatic PET degradation by substrate pretreatment.
Background: Polyethylene terephthalate (PET) is a widely used polyester for food and drink
packaging due to its desirable properties of durability, low cost and low gas permeability. Plastic,
including PET, is also a major source of environmental concern, with an estimated 12,000 Mt of
plastic waste to be discarded by 2050. In 2016, a bacterium (Ideonella sakaiensis) was identified
that could use PET as a carbon source and contained a set of enzymes capable of hydrolysing
PET into its monomeric units. This sparked a wave of new research into enzymatic methods of
PET degradation with the goal of facilitating a novel, greener and sustainable process for the re­
cycling/removal of waste PET.
Pretreatment of substrates is commonly used in industrial processes, catalysed by enzymes, to
increase their efficiency. Previous studies have reviled that PET degrading enzymes are highly
dependent on the physical properties of PET. An enhancement of these properties, though pre­
treatment, could therefore be used to increase the activity of PET degrading enzymes.
Learning objectives:
• Obtain a clear understanding of the enzymatic degradation of PET, and the substrate related
properties affecting the activity of the enzymes.
• Demonstrate knowledge of protein chemistry and enzyme kinetics.
• Understand the theoretical and practical aspects of various analytical methods, used for en­
zyme assays and substrate characterization.
• Gain profound experience with experimental design and quantitative data handling. Be able
to communicate research results and theory in a written, visual, and oral forms.
• Obtain understanding of the relation between enzyme technology and the UN’s sustainable
development goals.
ECTS Points: 30
Type: Master Thesis
Start date: 2021­01­26
End date: 2021­06­25
Content:
• A Literature study on the prospects of enzymatic degradation of PET
• An assessment of various Physiochemical pretreatment methods of various types of PET
substrate (e.g. textile, film, post­consumer packaging), which can be used to enhance the
enzymatic degradation of PET. These include, but is not limited to:
– Plasticizers
– Surfactants
– Mechanical grinding
– Partial chemical hydrolysis
• An evaluation of the assessed pretreatment methods with respect to the following:
– Optimal pretreatment conditions
– Activity of PET degrading enzymes (temperature optimum, Vmax, maximal yield)
– Modification of the substrate (Xc, Tg etc.)
– Applicability in a large­scale set­up

Introduction
Plastic define the way we live today. They improve the quality of life for millions of people across
the globe by making our lives easier, safer, and more enjoyable, by being durable, water­proof,
and flexible materials (PlascticEurope, 2020). Plastics are light, low cost and have considerably
impacted the decrease of transportation costs and prolonging the shelf life of food (Upasen &
Wattanachai, 2018).
The major part of plastic packaging materials are short­lived, which get the packaging material to
become disposable plastic waste after a single use (Thompson et al., 2009). Consumers can refill
and thus reuse their plastic bottles at home, but in all these cases, the recycling of the plastic bottles
and containers does not continue for long (Francis, 2016). The success of a material is only as great
as the harmful environmental impact allows (Barnes et al., 2009).
1.1
Plastic Pollution
Plastics have become a major environmental concern as a result of their durability and resistance
to degradation. As a lack of alternatives, plastic waste is therefore stored in landfills (Salvador
et al., 2019). When plastics ends up in the environment, it will eventually have a detrimental effect
to terrestrial and marine ecosystems, and eventually to humans (Thompson et al., 2009). When
plastic is not sorted, it either ends up in landfills or is used for incineration (Williams et al., 2005).
1.1.1
Accumulatiom of Plastics
In 2019, 368 million tons of plastic were produced worldwide (PlascticEurope, 2020), and esti­
mates suggest that 150­200 million tons of the annual demand ends up in landfill or in the natural
environment (Geyer et al., 2017).
Plastics are nowadays accumulated in the environment, and it has become an enormous concern
(Barnes et al., 2009). Especially because of its ability to degrade into microplastics with a potential
toxic effect (Alimba & Faggio, 2019). This stresses the need for innovative approaches to plastic
management, including the possibility of utilizing microbial activities, such as enzymes, to use
plastic waste as a feedstock for biodegradation (Francis, 2016).
1.1.2
Marine Pollution
Several studies has shown that microplastics (<5 mm) have polluted remote areas on the earth
(Cincinelli et al., 2017) (Lusher et al., 2015) (Odobašić & Varrone, 2020). In the Arctic, microplas­
tics have been detected in marine waters (Morgana et al., 2018) (Tekman et al., 2020), sediments
(Bergmann et al., 2017), sea ice (Peeken et al., 2018) and snow (Bergmann et al., 2019). Be­
tween 100 and 1200 tons of plastics have been estimated to be floating on the surface of the Arctic

1.2
Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET)
PET was first obtained by Whinfield and Dickson in 1941 and showed a great promise as a fibre­
and film­making polymer (Rex Whinfield & Tennant Dickson, 1945). Later in 1953 PET was
produced in industrial scale (Feldman, 2008). It is one of the most available semicrystalline ther­
moplastic resin, mainly used as packaging materials, and the global production, consumption, and
waste generation rate have increased considerably ever since (Karagiannidis et al., 2008) (Al­Salem
et al., 2009). A thermoplastic is a plastic that can be melted when heated and hardened when cooled.
It can be reheated, reshaped and frozen repeatedly. It is indeed this property that makes PET easy
to recycle (PlascticEurope, 2020). In 2018 the annual global production of PET was 70 million
tons and are expected to grow as the demand increases (“PET Polymer ­ Chemical Economics
Handbook (CEH) | IHS Markit”, n.d.).
1.2.1
Application of PET
Today, PET is used in a variety of products. Their major application is as packaging film, although
they are widely used as an electrical insulator, filament and staple fibre and film tire cord. One
of the applications of PET has been blow­molding to produce bottles. PET has a high order of
regularities in the chemical and geometric architecture, which results in the PET matrices’ crys­
tallisability. (Mandal & Dey, 2019)
1.2.2
The Life Cycle of PET
The recycling of PET have, for the past few decades, been the focus for many researchers. That
kind of research is also motivated by changes in regulatory and environmental issues, such as
the UN SDG. (Al­Salem et al., 2009) Due to the large amount of PET waste produced, more
sustainable recycling or alternative materials are needed. In this regard, life cycle assessment
studies should prioritise the critical points in the production chain in order to limit the negative
effects. The overall goal is to develop technologies with lower environmental impact and more
efficient recycling methods, such as closed cycle recycling. (Gomes et al., 2019)

Properties of PET

1.2.4
Glass Transition Temperature of PET
The degree of crystallinity and the presence of water has an impact on the glass transition temper­
atures (Tg). The Tg is the temperature range, where the polymer substrate changes from a rigid
glassy material to a soft mobile material. The typical heat flow versus temperature plot at Tg is
shown in figure 1.2. (Humboldt Universitat zu Berlin, 2020)
Figure 1.2: A plot of heat flow versus temperature. Tg is a gradual transition that occurs over
the range of temperatures (Humboldt Universitat zu Berlin, 2020)
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) is a valuable method to determine Tg. It is important to
note that the transition occurs over a range of temperatures. The temperature in the middle of the
inclined area is taken as Tg (Humboldt Universitat zu Berlin, 2020). Amorphous PET has an Tg at
67 ◦C and crystalline PET has an Tg at 81 ◦C (Groeninckx et al., 1974).

1.3
Recycling and Recovery of PET
Condensation polymers including PET can be degraded to monomer units, feedstock, or monomer
recycling (Al­Salem et al., 2009). Condensation polymers are produced by a condensation reaction,
combining two molecules to a single molecule. (Ouellette & Rawn, 2015)
Plastic recycling is a tricky and complex term, because of the variety of recycling and recovery
strategies. Nevertheless, plastic recycling can be divided into four major categories, re­extrusion
(primary), mechanical (secondary), chemical (tertiary) and energy recovery (quaternary).
Primary recycling refers to the in­plant recycling of the waste material of controlled history, it
is a closed­loop recycling (Francis, 2016). This method pertains to the reuse of PET products in
their original structure. This recycling is more affordable due to the waste are only clean, uncon­
taminated, single­type waste (Dimitris S. Achilias, 2012). The downside of this process is the
restriction on the number of cycles for each product (Goodship, 2009).
The secondary recycling category is a mechanical process, where the product has lesser properties
and are referred to as downgrading. Within this procedure, only the thermoplastic polymers can be
recycled, as they are able to remelt as well as reprocessed into finish good (Chirayil et al., 2019).
Mechanical recycling of PET includes collection, separation, digestion, granulation of polymer
waste, before re­circulation into production (Kilinç et al., 2005). The PET are chopped into pellets
of suitable quality for production, afterwards the pellets are melted to create a new product (Chi­
rayil et al., 2019). The drawbacks of the technique are that the properties of the product lowers for
every cycle because of the low molecular weight of the recycled resin. The low molecular weight
is due to the existence of water and acidic contaminants (Williams et al., 2005). It is often this type
of recycling there results in the production of clothing. (Turukmane et al., 2018)
Tertiary recycling is known as the process in which polymers are chemically converted to monomers
or to a specific extent depolymerized to oligomers via a chemical reaction. Chemical recycling
of PET, includes methanolysis, hydrolysis, glycolysis, aminolysis, ammonolysis, photodegrada­
tion, and ultrasound degradation (Chirayil et al., 2019). Cho et al., 2013 discover the effect of a
microwave assisted glycolysis of PET. PET are cleaved by certain reagents, for example, water
in the case of hydrolysis or glycols in the case of glycolysis. Because of the high pressure and
high temperatures, all of these processes are very energy demanding. Some chemical reactions
use chemicals as a catalyst to increase the rate of the reaction without itself being changed. In a
biochemical reaction, that catalyst is an enzyme and the degradation are then a bio­degradation.
(Chirayil et al., 2019)

In quaternary recycling, waste plastic is converted into energy by incineration. Even though this

Thermomechanical recycling is the most used method to recycle PET, even though it results in a

1.3.1
Bio­degradation of PET
Virgin PET is primarily manufactured using fossil­based feedstocks. Due to the chemical structure,
these synthetic plastics show a sensitivity to some microbial enzymes capable of modifying or
degrading recalcitrant synthetic polymers. (Zimmermann, 2020)
Figure 1.3: PET degraded to TPA; EG; MHET, mono­(2­hydroxyethyl) terephthalate; BHET,
bis­(2­hydroxyethyl) terephthalate.
Researchers have discovered enzymes developed by nature to degrade biological material (eg.
Cuin) have also shown activity against PET due to the similarities in the bonds. Another enzyme
used to degrade PET are Ideonella sakaiensis (IsPETase), which specializes in growing on PET.
(Zimmermann, 2020)
Even though PET represents only a smaller part of the global solid plastic waste, it will be suitable
to be recycled in a biocatalytic process. Recent advances in the understanding of the mechanism of
enzymatic PET degradation by microbial polyester hydrolases are one of the advantages. Another
advantage is the very high recovery rate of PET beverage bottles due to efficient deposit return
systems in some countries, which provides access to a continuous feed of high purity plastic com­
posed of only PET after cleaning and removal of labels and caps. Enzymatic hydrolysis enables
the recovery of the monomers without downstream processes to produce new bottles, which could
minimize or even replace oil based PET in the future. Considering that many PET beverage bottles
are produced worldwide, an efficient biocatalytic bottle­to­bottle recycling could have a positive
impact on the circular plastics economy. (Zimmermann, 2020)

INTRODUCTION
Polymer chain fluctuates at temperatures higher than the Tg, at which point chain mobility is in­
creased in the amorphous phase, allowing improved access to ester links between repeat units. A
microbial attack for PET recycling will require both high catalytic turnover and kinetic stability
at temperatures above PET’s Tg for the enzyme (Shirke et al., 2018). The Tg value is lowered in
water by water molecules diffusing between polymer chains, which also will increase the chain
mobility (Kawai et al., 2019) (Y. Chen & Yang, 1998).
In general, the surface conditions (surface area, hydrophilic, and hydrophobic properties), the first
order structures (chemical structure, molecular weight and molecular weight distribution) and the
high order structures (Tg, melting temperature and crystallinity) of polymers have an important
role in their biodegradability (Tokiwa et al., 2009). The molecular weight is important for the
biodegradability because it determines many physical properties of the polymer. Increasing the
molecular weight decreases the degradability. The degree of crystallinity is a crucial factor affect­
ing biodegradability, since enzymes primarily attack the amorphous regions of the polymer. The
molecules in the amorphous region is loosely packed, making it more susceptible to degradation.
The crystalline part of the polymers is more resistant than the amorphous region. (Tokiwa et al.,
2009)
Figure 1.4: An illustration of a semi­crystalline polymer with both amorphous and crystalline
regions (grey areas). The amorphous parts are more accessible to enzymatic attacks. (Wei &
Zimmermann, 2017)
The chemical structures of aliphatic polyester [ester bond (­CO­O­)] are a typical plastic polymer
that show high potential for use as biodegradable plastic, owing to the susceptibilities to lipolytic
enzymes and microbial degradation, due to an exposed active site in a substrate binding groove
located at the protein surface (Tokiwa et al., 2009)(Wei et al., 2016).

1.4
PET Degrading Enzymes
PET materials are generally resistant to microbial attacks, but recently some PET­degrading en­
zymes have been identified (Tournier et al., 2020). The enzyme exhibit an exposed active site in
a substrate binding groove located at the protein surface. The degradation product was identified
as the mono(2­hydroxyethyl) terephthalic acid and the bis­(2­hydroxyethyl) terephthalate. The
mechanism of the degradation have been investigated and Joo et al., 2018 proposed a catalytic
mechanism for IsPETase degrading PET. The proposed mechanism included a united process be­
tween various amino acids around the active site on the ester linkage of the PET polymer, while
other amino acids were able to rotate and pull the degradation products from the ester cleavage reac­
tion away from the active site, leaving it accessible for introduction for another substrate molecule.
(Joo et al., 2018)
In addition, the use of surfactants for enzymatic degradation of PET has shown an increase in
enzyme activity. Alam et al., 2015 and Furukawa et al., 2018 have documented that surface coated
with surfactants can accelerate the enzyme degradation. They evaluated both non­ionic, anionic,
cationic, and zwitterionic syrfactants, which indicated that the charge of the ion had an effect on
the enzyme activity. Alam et al., 2015 reported an increase in activity of Rhizopus niveus lipase
by using a zwitterionic and a non­ionic surfactant. Furukawa et al., 2018 reported that surfactants
bearing long alkyl chains and negatively charged head groups increased the activity of PETase,
indicating that these surfactants bound to the PET increased access of the enzyme to the PET
indicated in figure 1.5.

PROBLEM DEFINITION
2
Problem Definition
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate different pretreatment methods of PET to improve the
enzymatic degradation. The thesis was divided into two parts. The first part of this thesis was a
determination of the standard reaction conditions for an enzymatic assay, used in the second part,
where the pretreatments were evaluated.
The four pretreatments to be evaluated are use of plasticizer, mechanical grinding, partial chemical
degradation and use of surfactants. The hypothesis for the use of plasticizer, is that a plasticizer
are able to lower the Tg and thereby will the reaction temperature be higher than the Tg of the PET
sample, which will increase the enzyme activity.
The hypothesis for the mechanical grinding is by increasing the surface area the binding sites is
more accessible, which will increase the enzyme activity.
The hypothesis of partial chemical degradation is reducing the chemical degradation time and com­
bining it with enzymatic degradation, the resource costs will decrease, but the recycling efficiency
would not be affected.
The hypothesis for the use of surfactants are that the surfactants will interact with PET surface as
the negatively charged head groups would be oriented toward the aqueous phase and would convert
the PET surface into a charged surface. LCC is a positively charged enzyme that could interact
with the negatively charged PET surface, resulting in an increase of enzyme activity.

Experimental Section
3.0.1
Substrate Preparation
Amorphous PET Sheets (GoodFellow Cambridge Ltd., Huntingdon, England) cat. no. ES303010
with a thickness of 1 mm were punched into plates Ø=6mm using a whole punch. Samples denoted
as APET were annealed for 5 minutes at 85◦C and subsequently quenched in ice water. Samples
denoted as CPET were annealed for 30 minutes at 115◦C and subsequently quenched in ice water.
3.1
Determination of Reaction Conditions
The first part of this thesis was a determination of the standard reaction conditions for an enzymatic
assay.
3.1.1
Optimal Enzyme Dosage
The concentration of the enzyme was found by inverse Michaelis Menten (iMM) kinetics. The
enzyme was LCCICCG with a stock concentration at 41.3 µM. The substrate concentration were
one APET plate. The buffer use was 50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 8, the reaction temperature were
65◦C using a Thermomixer comfort (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) and the reaction time were
60 minutes. The enzyme concentration interval from 20 nM to 300 nM. The data was handled
according to section 3.6. A concentration of 375 nM Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) (Merck,
Germany) CAS Number 9048­46­8, were afterward added to hinder the enzyme to bind to the
eppendorf tube in the iMM.
3.1.2
pH and Temperature Optima
The optima of pH and temperature was found with a central composite design (CCD) with 3 centre
points at the response surface methodology (RSM). Each point was made in triplicates. The limits
for the temperature was 60 and 80◦C and the limits for the pH was 8 and 10 using a 50 mM Glycine­
NaOH buffer. The reaction time was 60 minutes, the enzyme concentration was 250 nM and the
substrate was APET. SAS jmp (SAS, Cary, NC, USA) was used for statistical analysis (section
3.6) and graphical illustration.
3.1.3
Reaction Time
The enzyme activity was measured based on endpoints. A key assumption for measuring the ac­
tivity was that the initial reaction rate was constant. The product formation [µM] was thereby
measured for the reaction time 15, 30, 60, 120 and 240 minutes. The buffer used was 50 mM

Use of Plasticizers
It has previously been shown that by exposing PET to high humidity in a specific time. It was pos­

temperature at or above the Tg the enzyme activity will increase, due to the increase in mobility of
the PET.

in an aqueous environment, why this pretreatment method will not require increased water con­

for enzymatic degradation of APET and CPET.

5.1
Determination of Reaction Conditions
It would be expected that the iMM data would follow the MM kinetic. This was not the case when
no enzyme activity was observed at the low enzyme concentrations in figure 4.1. Due to the ma­
terial of the eppendorf tube, some enzymes would bind to the eppendorf tube instead of reacting
with the substrate. In principle, there would always be a certain amount of the enzymes that bind
to the eppendorf tube. The effect was only visualized at the low enzyme concentrations, as there
was not enough enzyme to degrade the substrate at the same time. Thus, it appears that the binding
affinity of the eppendorf tube was greater than for the substrate or that the concentration of binding
sites in the eppendorf tube was higher than on the substrate. Attempts were made to accelerate the
enzyme activity using the binding agent BSA (Figure 4.2). The blocking of the binding sites in
the eppendorf tube, did not work fully, indicated by a less steep relation between the low enzyme
concentrations in figure 4.2. It was from the Hill equation still possible to determine the Vmax and
the preferred enzyme concentration at Vmax. The downside of the effect of enzyme binding to the
eppendorf tube, was that the actual enzyme concentration at Vmax were being shifted to a higher
enzyme concentration. Making it necessary to use more enzyme to have the reaction at Vmax. The
enzyme concentration of 250 nM would thereby still result in the maximum activity in the given
conditions.
The pH and temperature optima were determined to be pH 9 and 70◦C. Tournier et al., 2020 reports
a reaction temperature of 72◦C and for LCC they use a pH 8. At 70◦C after 1 hour of reaction,
the reaction temperature is close to Tg and the mobility of the substrate increases and the substrate
changes from glass state to rubber state. The change in Tg for a reaction of 1 hour are shown in
figure E.2. Another aspect of the temperature optimum was the inactivation of the enzyme, but
with a melting temperature of LCC at 84.7◦C it will not be a problem (Tournier et al., 2020). The
pH value should be 9 because of the enzyme’s pI value and thus gives a slightly positive protein
to react with the negative parts of the substrate.
Tournier et al., 2020 evaluates the process of depolymerisation of PET with LCCICCG as the
enzyme at 72◦C and the data show a linear curve up to 3 hours approximately. Indicating that the
result in figure 4.4 where the regression were linear up to 240 minutes seems reliable. Making it
possible to only use endpoint measurements when reporting the activity.

5.2
Use of Plasticizers
5.2.1
Water as a Plasticizer
From Shirke et al., 2018 and Kawai et al., 2019 it was known that to increase the enzyme activity,
the reaction temperature had to be at or above the Tg. From Y. Chen and Yang, 1998 it was known
that by introducing water as a plasticizer in PET the Tg will decrease in time. A decrease in Tg were
observed due to soaking in water in figure 4.5a. When increasing in moisture content in PET, leads
to formation of hydrogen bonds with polymeric chains, increasing the distance between polymeric
chains. Hence, increases the free volume and decreases Tg. The formation of hydrogen bonds
seems to create a larger part of the PET to be rigid amorphous and less to be mobile amorphous.
(Omnexus, 2019)
From table 4 it was indicated that the distribution of the types of amorphous regions do not change
due to the decrease in Tg and the formation of hydrogen bonds. On the other hand, the enzyme
activity in figure 4.6, do not change by the time of soaking.
With optimal reaction conditions, the soaking time with water as the plasticizer, only has an effect
on the Tg. An iMM experiment was performed to investigate whether soaking would have an ef­
fect on the enzyme activity if the enzyme concentration was changed (Figure 4.7). Table 3 indicate
that the maximum activity, Vmax, were statistical the same for all soaking times. This experiment
indicate, as well as the experiment with optimal reaction conditions, that the soaking with water as
the plasticizer do no effect the enzyme activity.
With the soaking temperature at room temperature, the soaking time was 3 days before the decrease
in Tg were stable. A CCD was created, with the parameters soaking time and soaking temperature
to accelerate the decrease in Tg (Figure 4.8). The optima of the CCD were for soaking temper­
ature, 56◦C, and for soaking time, 1 day (Figure D.2). The interval of soaking temperature was
measured in a pre­experiment, to be sure that the high limit did not crystallise PET. Because of this
precaution, it seems like the soaking temperature keeps decreasing the Tg. In reality, the soaking
temperature will have a negative effect depending on the soaking time, when the soaking temper­
ature rises to around 70◦C. Figure D.2 shows that by increasing the soaking time, the crystallinity
increases too, which have a decreasing effect on the enzyme activity. The soaking time does not
have an effect on the Tg. The reason could be that the decrease in Tg were at the lowest after one
day of soaking, which was the low limit for soaking time. If the soaking time was changed to an
interval of 1 hour to 6 hours, the effect would be expected to be different, because it was known
from figure E.2 that after 1 hour in 65◦C the Tg has change to around 70◦C.

The experimental setup was created to see the effect of having a reaction temperature above and
below PETs Tg on the enzyme activity. The experiment was made with two different reaction
temperatures, 65◦C and 70◦C. The effect is shown in figure 4.9a. It was important that the high Tg
sample’s Tg always were above the reaction temperature and that the low Tg sample’s Tg always
were below the reaction temperature, which was indicated in figure 4.9b and 4.9d. To be sure that
the samples was identical except for the Tg, the crystallinity and the amorphous region was evalu­
ated in figure 4.9c.
The enzyme activity for the high Tg sample was higher than the enzyme activity for the low Tg
sample, to discuss the data some unpublished data from Thomsen, 2021 shown in Appendix E in
figure E.1. It was seen that PET after soaking has a lag phase. The assumption was when the
crystallinity increase, the lag phase increases as well. Even though a difference in crystallinity
could not be measured on the DSC, the actual crystallinity of the sample was a little higher, and
still high enough to see a lag phase in the enzyme activity. It can be discussed whether it was
in the surface itself that the PET plate had been crystallized, and because the entire sample was
measured by DSC, a change of crystallinity only in the surface cannot be detected. The enzyme
will degrade PET from the surface, and therefore a change of crystallinity in the surface will have
a higher effect on the enzyme activity. Another view were large standard deviations in the DSC
data and very small standard deviations in the enzyme activity. The measurement methods used
were less precise and therefore had difficulty separating the crystallinity from each other.
Another reasons why an increase in crystallinity could be expected was that the Tg value of the
low enzyme activity was below the reaction temperature, and it was known that the crystallisation
occurs when PET was exposed to temperature above the Tg value. This assumption thus seems
plausible, even if it could not be detected.
The high crystallinity of CPET affected the enzyme activity negatively compared to APET, indi­
cating that the enzyme activity depends on the crystallinity. In general, the results of using water
as a plasticizer indicate that only the crystallinity had an effect on the enzyme activity.

5.3
Mechanical Grinding
After grinding APET, the plate, which have the biggest size, have the most enzyme activity shown
in figure 4.11a. It was expected from Tournier et al., 2020 that by increasing the surface area, the
enzyme activity would increase. Looking at figure 4.11b the crystallinity of the grinded APET
were much higher than the crystallinity of the APET plate, indicating that the effect of the crys­
tallinity were higher than the effect of the particle size. When the surface area was increased, the
amorphous regions of PET would be more accessible, but when the PET was grinded mechani­
cally, the surface become more crystalline due to the mechanical stress the grinding adds to the
PET.
To confirm the hypothesis from Tournier et al., 2020, that by increasing the exchange surface of
PET by micronisation, the enzyme activity would increase. To do so another experiment with
grinding were made, where all PET was crystallised before grinding, making the crystallinity sta­
tistical equal (Figure 4.12b). This meant that the difference in activity was only due to the differ­
ence in particle size. The activity of 0.06 µmol/(min*mL) were measured at the second­smallest
particle size, where it could have been expected to be at the smallest particle size (figure 4.12a).
The reason for that could be impurities instead of some substrate. It could also be because a lot of
substrates was collected in a lump at the bottom of the eppendorf tube because the rotations were
not strong enough to loosen this lump, making it difficult for the enzyme to obtain.
Another factor of this decrease in enzyme activity between the particle size <0.355 and 0.355­0.4
mm could also be that when stopping the reaction with acid, the product precipitates and sinks to
the bottom where the substrate was. When working with that small particles, it can be very dif­
ficult to pipette only the product, making it possible that some of the volume that was analysed
with the spectrophotometer contained substrate, making the absorbance smaller. By lowering the
particle size, the surface area was increased as the amount of substrate was kept constant. Making
the degrading parts of the substrate more accessible.
It had been difficult to make conventional MM kinetics due to the small reaction volumes and the
large insoluble substrates. However, as the substrate has been grinded, it has become possible to

5.4
Partial Chemical Degradation
The data from the glycolysis showed that the partial degradation did not have an effect on the en­
zyme activity for the first 20 seconds, and then it had a negative effect, shown in figure 4.14a. This
was due to the increase in crystallinity of the substrate, indicated in figure 4.14b. After 20 seconds
of glycolysis, the reaction takes place below 100 ◦C, shown in table 1. This means that the reaction
temperature was still below the crystalline temperature, which for the samples processed in less
than 20 seconds was 133◦C, where the samples that had reacted for a longer time had a crystalline
temperature of 126◦C (Appendix G table 7). This therefore meant that when the reaction time was
above 20 sec, the reaction temperature was above Tg for a longer time, resulting in a decrease of
the crystalline temperature and an increase of the crystallinity in the substrate. The increase in
crystallinity showed a decrease in enzyme activity (Figure 4.14).
On the other hand, the substrate disintegrated during the glycolysis, so to assess the total degrada­
tion of the substrate the EG from the glycolysis in 40 seconds of 7 PET plates was analysed and
the product formation is shown in Appendix G in table 8. Table 8 indicates that the glycolysis had
a negative effect on the total degradation of APET, but a positive effect on the total degradation
of CPET. Some measurement was only performed as single measurements, making the increase in
product formation of 19 µM not statistical reliable, but only an indication.
5.5
Use of Surfactants
DDM and tween­20 are non­ionic surfactants and therefore do not have an attraction to the protein
(Figure 4.17a and 4.16). SDS was anionic and the charge for the protein was positive, therefore
an attraction was expected. The attraction does not have a positive effect on the enzyme activity
(Figure 4.15).
In Furukawa et al., 2018, the attractiveness was also investigated, but here a clear improvement
in enzyme activity was seen by the use of anionic surfactants and a positively charged protein.
Furukawa et al., 2018 also shows that the surfactants bound on the PET film increases the access
of the enzyme.
At an enzyme concentration of 30 nM, an inactivation of the enzyme was seen in the presence of
SDS. SDS has the ability to block the active sites at high SDS concentrations. This meant that
where low enzyme concentrations were used, there was proportionally more SDS than enzyme,

5.6
Overall Discussion
For the three pretreatment methods, use of plasticizer, mechanical grinding and partial chemical
degradation, where the structure of PET was affected by temperature or mechanical stress, the crys­
tallinity increased and that affected the enzyme activity negatively. After PET has been soaked and
Tg was lowered, it was not possible to apply other pretreatments affecting the structure of the PET
without changing the Tg again. The effect of soaking PET in neither EG nor water had a positive
effect on the enzyme activity, making it unfavourable for industrial scale.
On the other hand, PET where had been grinded has the ability to apply other pretreatments. It was
known from Tournier et al., 2020 that it was possible to amorphise the PET afterward, by melting
and then quenching the PET. By doing that, the pretreatment would be similar to the pretreatment
Carbios are using (Tournier et al., 2020). Carbios is a green chemistry company, who develops
biological and innovative processes to revolutionize the end of life of plastics and textiles. In 2019
Carbios applied the method described in Tournier et al., 2020 to degrade PET bottles by enzyme
degradation into new PET bottles. In November 2020 Carbios were able to produce the first clear
plastic bottles from enzymatically recycled textile waste. With this in mind, the pretreatment me­
chanical grinding would be suitable for industrial scale by the addition of extrusion.
From the partial chemical degradation the important point was that all chemical degradations works
at high temperatures, and at high temperatures PET will crystallize, and it has a decreasing effect on
enzymatic degradation PET became crystalline when it was exposed to temperatures above Tg for
a longer time. The higher the temperature, the faster the crystallisation takes place, which makes
this pretreatment not suitable for industrial scale. Making the resource costs decreasing, but the
recycling less efficient.
The use of surfactants did not have an effect on the enzyme activity. So to continue using surfac­
tants, would just make the enzyme degradation more expensive, which makes this pretreatment
not suitable for industrial scale.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
The parameters for the enzyme degradation, should as a conclusion be a reaction temperature of 70
◦C, a Glycine­NaOH buffer at pH 9, in a reaction time of 60 minutes, with an enzyme concentration
of 250 nM.
Of the four pretreatments, use of plasticizer, mechanical grinding, partial chemical degradation, and
use of surfactants, the pretreatment, use of plasticizer, did not show an effect on enzyme activity,
even though the soaking decreased the Tg by using plasticizers. It was not an advantage to use EG
as the plasticizer compared to water as a plasticizer. The theoretical knowledge about an increase
in enzymatic degradation by having a reaction temperature above PET’s Tg, have not been proven
to be right.
The partial chemical degradation will not increase the enzyme activity, due to high reaction temper­
ature, which in general increased in crystallinity and thereby decrease the enzyme activity. Thus,
it will not be possible to reduce the resource costs with the combination of chemical and enzymatic
degradation, without lowering the efficiency of an ordinary chemical degradation.
Furthermore, the pretreatment, use of surfactants, did not show an increasing effect on the enzyme
activity. The negatively charged SDS did not positively affect the surface of PET to get a better
interaction between PET and LCCICCG.
The only pretreatment showing a positive effect on the enzyme activity was the mechanical grind­
ing. By lowering the particle size, the surface area was increased, making the degrading parts of
the substrate more accessible. This pretreatment has some similarities to the ones Carbios uses,
which is the leading company in the market of enzymatically degrading PET both as bottles and
textiles.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
7
Future perspectives
This thesis has conducted knowledge about how three pretreatments of PET can affect the physical
properties and how four pretreatments of PET can affect the enzymatic degradation.
For future perspectives, it is important to assess that the increase in surface area has a positive effect
on the enzymatic degradation. The degree of crystallinity and the reaction temperatures above Tg
have a negative effect on the enzymatic degradation. And the ratio between the concentration of
surfactant and enzyme should be large (Surfactant « Enzyme).
Of the four pretreatments, only mechanical grinding had a positive effect on the enzyme activity.
This method are already used with an addition of the pretreatment, extrusion. In 2019 infinite
recycling technology of PET plastics developed by Carbios join the selection of the 1,000 sustain­
able and competitive solutions certified by the Solar Impulse Foundation. This label means that
the technology meets five of the SDGs adopted by the United Nations General Assembly with the
2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. The five goals are SDG6: Clean Water and Sanitation,
SDG 7: Affordable and Clean Energy, SDG 9: Industry Innovation and Infrastructure, SDG 11:
Sustainable Cities and Communities and SDG 12: Responsible Production and Consumption.
With this technology, it is possible to remove big parts of the plastics polluting the landfills and
oceans. By doing that, this technology will also meet SDG 14: Life below water.
