Summary
The aim of this thesis is to suggest and implement a method, of detecting and track a
Spherical object moving in 3D, using computer vision, followed by tests of the imple-
mentation showing its performance and limitations.
The main motivation for this thesis is the MBZIRC competition in 2020.
OpenCV is utilized in the solution. Most of the availbale litterature on this is found
online, where most of the litterature for this thesis has been procured. Other sources
has been litterature from DTU courses.
The implementation is done on a Raspberry Pi, and revolves around functions provided
in OpenCV using HUE color space thresholding and Hough Circle Transformation for
detection and Kalman filter for tracking.
Detection and tracking was implemented succesfully, although the code was not im-
plemented directly on a drone, which left certain tests undone. The tests showed that
image resolution of 640x480 or 320x240 could both be considered, however they offer
two different strengths, and it becomes a tradeoff between FPS and detection distance
and a final choice could depend on the circumstances during the challenge.

Nomenclature
Camera Holder: A special designed object used to contain the Raspberry Pi and
RaspiCam during the thesis.
Camera System: Reference to the camera coordinate system, with the camera at
origo.
Drone system: Reference to the drone coordinate system with the drone at origo.
X, Y and Z (Capital): Refers to a set of coordinates within the Camera system.
Detection: Knowing the presence of an object (possibly with some attribute infor-
mation)
Tracking: Maintaining the state and identity of an object over time despite detec-
tion errors, occlusions, and the presence of other objects.

as the weather and time of day can change how much light is introduced to the scene
quite alot.
The color space used is not limited to what the camera can produce, as operations
exists to change between different color spaces.
Two methods often chosen for color filtering in computer vision and similar software,
is HSV and HSL color spaces [Stone 2018].

3.3.2.1
Hough circles
The houghCircles function in OpenCV is based of the Hough Circle Transform.
It
describes and returns circles by 3 variables, xcenter, ycenter and radius of the detected
circle(s) described in pixels.
Figure 3.3: The green circle is the edge in a image from Canny in houghCircles. The red
points are example of edge points, from where a circle like the blue circles are formed. A
circle is estimated by finding a circle size, where a predefined minimum number of circle
intersections (votes) occur. A circle is detected only if it meets the votes criteria.
Figure 3.3 shows the basic principle of how houghCircles detect circular objects. By
drawing circles on the edge of pixel objects, it counts the number of interssections of
thos circles, if enough intersections occur, it accepts the object as a circle of the given
test size.
For unknown circle size, a range of circle sized are testet. Larger range means more
testing, which increases time taken.
Function inputs
Minimum distance between detections. This setting is a minimum pixel distance be-
tween two detections.
Canny Upper threshold sets the value that a pixel gradient must be higher than, in
order to be accepted as an edge.
Votes set the threshold for center detection. Circles that is not meeting the vote cri-
terium is rejected. .
Circles with higher votes will be returned as the first object [OpenCV 2015].
Canny upper threshold: Determines where the canny accepts an edge.

3.4
Kalman filter for tracking of single object
Even the best implementations of target detection may experience target occlusions or
detection of a false object. In the case of a camera sensor, occlusions may occur if a
object gets between the camera and the tracked object, or if the object moves outside
the field of view. Conditions such as light or background noise may also influence the
detection and in worst case cause a false positive detection, which would make the sys-
tem believe the tracked object is somewhere it is not.
The tracking can help improve the detection, by taking advantage of the predicted loca-
tion based on previous information regarding the position and movement of the target. A
suitable tracker will also be able to continue predicting position even when an occlusion
appears, even more than once, which will improve the robustness of the complete system.
To track the sphere, the first information needed is its position. Due to this, a tracker
cannot be utilize until first detection has been made. The position we get from the de-
tection and following position calculations. Based of the position changes, velocity and
acceleration could be estimated. These are ofcourse only estimations, and how good
these estimations would be would rely on the accuracy of the detection. These three
informations can describe the movement of the sphere. In small steps, position and
velocity alone could describe this aswell.
However, for this tracking task, it is not only the sphere that is moving. The cam-
era used for detection is mounted on a drone, which also moves, as it will attempt to
follow the sphere. So information regarding the drones movement, being velocity and
maybe acceleratation is also needed. The world position of the drone is, however, not
available in an accurate fashion. This is acceptable, as the sphere position is considered
relative to the camera. In this sense the camera is treated as position (0,0,0), and the
drone position is constant related to this.

3.4.2
Kalman filter
The Kalman Filter is the chosen method for tracking. It works on linear systems, but
can be extended to work on unlinear systems aswell.
The Kalman filter is a very usefull tracking tool in an envoirement where noice exists
and is usefull when combining information from different sensors. Only the last mea-
surement is used by the kalman filter for its prediction, which gives low memory costs.
In generel the kalman filter is a fast filter that takes little computational power, fitting
well to the Raspberry Pi.
The Kalman filter uses variances in calculating the potential new positions, defining
possible new positions as an area, making the computation needed less compared the
particle filter.
The kalman filter works under the assumptions:
• That the system is linear.
• That the variables are gaussian distributed.
To calculate a position estimate, the kalman filter takes into account the current state
and the certainty of this (in the covariance around the mean) together with known vari-
ables, such as speed and acceleration, and calculates a new set of mean and covariances
describing the new possible position..
By setting up the model to include camera system coordinates XYZ, The kalman
filter can predict the position even if the sphere is not detected in some frames, due to
occlusion or if it is beyond of the imageframe. This helps smoothen the process, and
potentially it could also make sure the sphere is not lost if it moves behind a pillar in
the arena.
3.4.2.1
Extended Kalman filter
The extended kalman filter deals with non-linear systems and include steps to linearize

3.4.4
Choose model
To choose a model suitable for the task, the system that the kalman filter must describe
must be considered.
It is desireable to keep the model as simple as possible, yet still gain the benefits from
having a kalman filter implemented to track the sphere.
3.4.5
Model type
Before choosing the model, it should be considered how the target sphere is expected to
move.
During the tracking, the tracking drone will fly behind the sphere. The exact movement
of the sphere is unknown, however some assumptions can be made.
• The sphere will not make abrupt changes in velocity in any direction.
• The sphere will make changes in direction, causing acceleration of the positions X,
Y and Z (Z will mainly be affected by the change of X or Y).
• Random movement should be expected as the ball is hanging in a string or similar
below a drone.
The models that will be considered are so called motion models.
The movement of the sphere could possibly be described as a nonmaneruvering motion
at each time instant of kalman prediction.
”A nonmaneuvering motion is the straight and level motion at a constant velocity, some-
times also referred to as the uniform motion. Loosely speaking, all the other motions
==PAGE:44==
26
3 Analysis
belong to the maneuvering mode.”Jilkov 2000
Or it could be assumed to maneuver, due to its change in flight path and random
movement within the image due to the envoiremental forces.
The link between the points X, Y and Z is only regarding the Z coordinate, which
changes if eighter of the other changes. This relation may be neglegted. This means
that each coordinate is uncoupled to the others.
It is also noted, that in the final implementation, the tracking drone will respond to
change in the sphere position, hence the acceleration of the sphere in the camera frame
should not be as extreme as the spheres acutal movement in the real world.
3.4.5.1
The constant velocity model
The constant velocity model (CV), a nonmaneuver target dynamic model.
Clearly, the velocity of each position is not constant as the flightpattern is not a stright
line, however, in this model acceleration can be treated as noise and the motion treated
as constant between frames.
Useful to model smooth target motion [Giorgio Grisetti and Burgard 2019] or when
the maneuver is small or random.
This is described as the simplest Coordinate-Uncoupled target maneuver model [Jilkov
2000]. It assumes that the coupling between the coordinates are small enough to be
neglegted in the model.
The model is also known as the white-noise acceleration model.
3.4.5.2
The constant acceleration model
The constant acceleration model (CA) is the second simplest model for Coordinate-
Uncoupled target maneuver.
This model is expanded from the Constant velocity model by including acceleration
of the position.
The model is useful to model target motion that is smooth in position and velocity
changes [Giorgio Grisetti and Burgard 2019].

3.4.7
Kalman filter behavior
For the Kalman filter to benefit, it is important that it behaves in a benifitial manner.
The goal is to have a filter, which will not respond too much to what is only measure-
ment noise, but at the same time react fast when a actual change in position happens.
If the filter reacts too much to measurement noise and a occlusion happens, the kalman
prediction will shoot of towards the direction that made the difference between the 2
last measurements. Instead the filter should ignore small changes within the expected
measurement variance, such that only larger differences in position alters the kalman
velocities.

Adaptive votes
Having a high properbility of a detection demands that votes is 20 or less. A value that
is too small will allow too many noise elements to be detected as a circle.
I have implemented a adaptiveness of the required votes.
When no circle is detected, votes is decreased by 1, therby increasing the chance of
detection for the next frame. This is based of the idea that a nonsuccesfull detection
is due to a bad color threshold, rather than the sphere being out of sight or similar.
However the votes is not to go lower than 10, in order to prevent bad detections.
When more than one circles are detected, the votes are then increased by 1 for the next
loop so better reject the worse detections.

5.2.3
Setting parameters of the Kalman Model
To get a well functioning model, the varables of the matrices Q, R and P should be
selected.
The kalman model should not over respond to the noise of the detection, but still re-
spond well to actual changes of the position of the detection.
As no there is no informations regarding the position of the first position, the P ma-
trix is initialised with a big gap.
PPre = 10
PPost = 0.01
The R matrix contains the measurement noise. Measurements are the position of the
sphere from the image detection, and the velocity from the drone. The accuracy from
the position detection should be within 5 cm in either direction, so this is set as the
measurement noise covariance value.
Nothing further is known regarding the drone velocity measurements, and as no flying
tests involving this will be done, they are set to 0.0001, and assumed accurate for any
simulating tests involving them.
The Q matrix contains the process noise covariance. The way that these have been
chosen has been mainly through tests, looking at the response of the kalman filter. For
the Sphere position, the process noise is assumed low. The position from the detection
is expected to provide truthfull position, and as the exact position is wanted at all times,
it does not seem usefull to take wind noise or similar into great account.
The process noise of the Drone velocities is set to 0.0001, just as the measurement noise.
The velocity process covariance is then set, based of kalman response tests. The se-
lected value ended with 0.75. Higher values makes the estimation close in faster to a
changed detection, but also introduce more overshooting. If the value goes lower, the
opposite is observed.

5.2.4.1
Setting houghCircles search limits
houghCircles is one of the major time consumers in the detection software. This is some-
thing that is unwanted, and any methods of reducing this should be investigated.
The main reason the houghCircles is as slow as it is, it due to its state of not know-
ing anything about the size of the object it is detecting. This changes when kalman
filter is introduced. By exploiting the predictions of X, Y and Z coordinates in the cam-
era framework, things such as the estimated sphere radius can be calculated, hence give
a estimation of how big the sphere is expected to be. With this knowledge, parameters
can be altered in the houghCircles that indicates the minimum and maximum search
radius. These minimum and maximum ranges should be determined by using the esti-
mated radius, and then finding a proper variance, such that no sphere is missed due to
a too narrow search option, but at the same time, it should be as narrow as possible to
reduce time consumption as much as possible.
KalmanSphereDistance =
√
KalmanZ2 + KalmanX2 + KalmanY 2
(5.1)
Once a distance estimate has been calculated, as in equation (5.1), the pixel radius of
the sphere is calculated by rewriting equation 4.1 into equation (5.2).
objectradius(pixels) = focallenght(mm) · realheight(mm) · imageheight(pixels)
(sensorheight(mm) · KalmanSphereDistance) ∗2
(5.2)
The limits in houghCircles can then be calculated using equation 5.2, by inserting
(KalmanRadius + DistanceRange) and (KalmanRadius −DistanceRange). Where
DistanceRange is the propesed search distance, expanded from the estimated distance.
When an occlusion happens, these limits could have part in solving this, such that
if a occulusion happens, the DistanceRange should be expanded, increasing the search
frame. This should be incremented at each occlusion until the search is discontinued
due to a loss of the ball.
The maximum hough radius should have a limit that fits the limit for how close hough
can detect the sphere. This area lies within the span of 15-20 cm distance from the
camera. By implementing such a limit in the software, errors spawning from the calcu-
lation in equation 5.2 can be halted. Say, if the sphere is within 50 cm of the camera,

radius is constantly smaller than the predicted.
Figure 5.6 shows the results of the initial implementation of the hough limits. It seems
clear that the kalman estimation is a little higher than the detected radius whenever
there is no change in the detection. This is due to the calibration used when calculating
the XYZ coordinates of the sphere. As the calibrated values are the input to the kalman
filter, the predicted radius is based of these, rather than the actual detected radius. To
fix this, the calibration value was added to the search limit calculation.
After the implementation of radius limits, the number of failed detections was reduced,
increasing the robustness of the system.
To further improve upon the robustness, an adaptive search limit was implemented,
based on wether the detection of each image is succesfull or not. Figure 5.7 on the fol-
lowing page shows a testresult of this implementation. In this test the distance searched
is increased by 25 cm in both directions for every occlusions up to 10. If 10 occlusions
occur the kalman filter is abandoned and max search limits is set. Every succesfull
detection works opposite and reduce the search distance by 25 cm, until the minimum
search range is reached.
As the sphere get further away, the frame between hough minimum and maximum search
get more narrow. At long distances, it becomes so narrow that it may obstruct the search
due to the larger variance in detected radius.

the Z velocity.

order to get a higher FPS. However a fast target may require more distance, which is
what the 640x480 resolution provides, but at a lower FPS.
Settings of the filters such as HSV values, votes and hough search frame is something
that can be tinkered with if the priorities changes.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A suggested solution has been made for detection and tracking of a moving object in
3D. In the special case of first detection, some thought has been made of how to do
this if the distance of implemented distance is not sufficient, however it has not been
implemented.
The detection of the sphere has been based of its color and its shape, which are the
attributes that define the sphere and makes it stand out from the envoirement. The
HUE colorspace is used for this with an implementation of Hough Circle transformation
to detect the circular shape. Certain filters has been implemented to reduce the risk of
false detections during tracking.
Taking advantage of the known size of the Sphere, the distance is calculated. Based of
the distance and the Sphere center position in the image, coordinates X, Y and Z are
calculated, which is the 3D coordinate of the sphere relative to the camera in metric
terms.
For tracking the Kalman filter is suggested. By taking advantage of the known sphere
position and drone velocities, the velocity of the sphere can be estimated by the filter.
The Constant Velocity motion model is the base of the suggested filter, meaning that
any alterations in the sphere velocity (acceleration), is treated as noise.
The detection and tracking is codependent, as the kalman filter provides limitations
for the detection and selection of the correct detection, thereby increasing robustness
and reducing timeconsumption. However, for the tracking to work well, the detection
required to be good.
Results show that resolutions of 640x480 and 320x240 provide FPS that can be con-
sidered for tracking, although the possible detection distance drops drastically every
time the resolution is lowered. Which resolution that should be used during the chal-
lenge, could very well depend on the actual velocity of the target sphere, and as every
setting is scaleable as the resolution is, it can be determined which one is suitable on
the spot.
The implementation was never implemented and fused directly with the drone controls,
as such there is still work to be done, but i have confidence that the suggested method
would get the job done.

7 Conclusion
7.1
Further work
7.1.1
Filter for object velocity
The method for choosing the correct detection relies on filtering the bad detections.
However, another way to increase robustness would be to implement multiple kalman
filters, so all detections can be tracked, and the velocity of them can be determined. If
their velocity is too low, too high or just does not match the current measured velocity
of the sphere, they can be deemed false.
Espicially when doing first detection, a background object should not be accepted to be
the sphere. A method of filtering this, should it get thorugh the already implemented
filters, could be to look at the kalman predicted movement of the object, and if the
object seems not to move, it would be discarded.
7.1.2
Other method to deal with image distortion
The method of undistorting the image is a slow process and if it could be done in a faster
way, it could potentially increase FPS by a great deal. As such, any method that may
remove this step, could improve robustness and FPS.
One possible method could be to use rotated rectangle with fincontours.
This esti-
mates the smallest possible rectangle that the pixel object can fit into. As the distortion
is always facing away from the center, it could be argued that of the rectangle, the small-
est dimension could go as being the sphere radius. If necesarry, a sphere center offset
could be calculated based of the distortion values and equation. This feature would
although only work well on a good circle in the thresholded image, where houghcircles
or finContours with minimum enclosing circle may detect a circle from a partial object.
Depending on the distance while tracking the sphere, the undistortion could be con-
sidered removed at long distances, as the accuracy here is already bad, and it might not
have a significant impact on the DTU drones ability to follow. This could be investigated
once the suggested implementation is programmed onto the drone.
7.1.3
Looking further into errors in position calculations
Further investigations of the error in coordinate calculations could potentially remove
the need for a calibration value.
If still no reason is discovered, the calibration of the Z value should probably be altered
to be a calibration of the calculated direct distance, as this is practically the source of
the Z calculation, and also the step that relies on the detected radius.
==PAGE:129==
7.1 Further work
111
7.1.4
Trim code
The number of images should be reduced, so each image is used for mulitple steps during
the detection. Just 2 images should be able to get the job done. This will reduce the
memory used by the Raspberry Pi.
7.1.5
Look into option of changing resolution midflight
Results clearly show that the 320x240 has a much better FPS, but it also shows that it
has a greatly reduced range.
It could be looked into how much time it takes to change resolution during midfligt,
or if it is possible to make multiple camera objects in the code, using the same camera,
and simply shift.
What i suggest to use this option for, is when it is time to actually catch the ball,
the increased FPS may prove very usefull as the DTU UAV closes in, as the low resolu-
tion is accurate enough up close. This way, the benefits of each proposed resolution is
utilized at different times of the challenge.
This suggestion is only of interrest, if the tracking resolution is higher than 320x240
up to the point of capturing the sphere.
