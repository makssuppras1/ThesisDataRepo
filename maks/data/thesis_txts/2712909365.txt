Master’s Thesis
Abstract in English
Understanding the interaction of molecules with light is the first step in many disciplines in chem-
istry, and could be a way to attain more direct control of chemical reactions, for example by inducing
dissociation of certain bonds. This thesis focuses specifically on the description of bond-dissociation
of a diatomic molecule, the HeH+-ion, modelled by the Morse potential in one and three dimensions
using laser light.
The first focus is the calculation of dissociation probabilities of the molecule interacting with
a laser field at an intensity of 7 · 1013 W
cm2 for different center frequencies in one dimension with
the intention of reproducing results by Wustelt et al.[1]. This is done through modification of a
preexisting, separate propagation-script constructed by Alexandre Paolo Voute so it uses the Morse-
potential as the model for the molecule and includes interaction with a field, which also makes the
potential time-dependent. The results show a decent agreement with the calculations by Wustelt
et al. and a maximum dissociation probability of 76.86% is obtained at a center frequency of the
laser corresponding to a period of 14 fs.
The theoretical framework and setup of the script is then expanded to include chirping of the
pulse, which makes it possible to reach a dissociation probability of 93.05% at a center frequency
corresponding to a period of 15.6 fs. The intensity is also decreased to 2 · 1012 W
cm2 , which results
in lower dissociation probabilities overall, but a more pronounced effect of the chirp, as the highest
dissociation probability without the chirp is found to be 0.587%, but with the chirp this increases
to 7.06%.
Finally, the calculations are expanded to three dimensions so rotation of the molecule is taken
into account. This requires derivation of the form of the time-dependent Schr¨odinger equation that
defines the foundation of the propagation by expansion of the wave function onto the spherical
harmonics. The script is modified accordingly to now handle a set of coupled equations instead of
just a single equation. The dissociation probability is then calculated in three dimensions for both
the high and low intensities. At high intensity, the dependence of the frequency is found to follow a
similar tendency as was found in one dimension, At low intensity the chirped and unchirped cases
are once again compared, and the chirp is found to again make a significant difference.

Introduction
The interaction of light and molecules is the basis of many different areas of chemistry. One can
look at this interaction from two angles, either in terms of how the molecule affects the light, as is
the basis of spectroscopy, or in terms of how the light affects the molecule. The focus of this thesis
is the second, examining how laser pulses can push a diatomic molecule to the point of dissociation.
Broadly speaking, the understanding of these interactions is part of the effort to attain higher
control over reactions, by influencing the reaction path taken through the use of light[3, p.234].
Understanding how to induce dissociation of bonds using light would be a step towards this.
The first part of the thesis focuses on reproducing results by Wustelt et al.[1] showing the
interaction of a short laser pulse at high intensity with the HeH+-ion, modeled with the Morse-
potential. In this study, the authors carried out both classical and quantum dynamical simulations
to determine dissociation probabilities of HeH+ for a range of center frequencies of the interacting
laser pulse. They also carried out experimental determinations of yields of the dissociation process
for different center frequencies, and saw good agreement between the experimental and numerical
results. I will be reproducing the results obtained by quantum dynamical simulations. For this, a
script is developed, based on previous programming work by Alexandre Paolo Voute, that propagates
the wave function describing the system of the ion interacting with the laser pulse through time.
The dissociation probability is then found by calculating the populations of the bound states of the
potential after the pulse has ended.
To further build on this, the laser pulse is modified to include the possibility of chirping, so the
central frequency of the pulse varies over time. This makes it possible to reach higher dissociation
probabilities at the high intensity, but also makes it possible to achieve significant dissociation
probabilities at lower intensities.
The rest of the thesis then deals with modifying the calculation setup to work in three dimensions
instead of one, so rotation of the molecule is also accounted for. This includes a reworking of the
time-dependent Schr¨odinger-equation in three dimensions to a set of coupled equations that each
can be treated with the one-dimensional framework, after a few modifications. The bound states
of the Morse oscillator with rotation included are not attainable analytically, but approximate
expressions can be found instead, so the dissociation probability can be determined in the same
way as for the one-dimensional case. The dissociation probability is however also determined by
a second approach, using the probability of the separation of the nuclei reaching values at which
the molecule is taken to be dissociated, so the approximate method can be compared to something.
Finally, the orientation and alignment of the molecule are examined by determining the expectation
values of cos θ and cos2 θ. These calculations are carried out in the high intensity case without
chirping and in the low intensity case, with and without chirping.
The thesis is generally a combination of theoretical derivations and construction of calculation
scripts in the program MatLab. The derivations are based in found literature, but have in many

Theoretical Considerations
Before any calculations can be carried out, a few things have to be clarified. First, the specific
form of the time-dependent Schr¨odinger equation that governs the behaviour of the system has
to be determined, which means determining the specific form of the Hamiltonian. In addition to
this, a method of actually tracking the behaviour as time passes is needed. The ultimate goal is to
determine dissociation probabilities, so a method for calculating these will of course also be needed.
2.1
A Few General Concepts in Quantum Chemistry
As a quick introduction, I here go over a few of the overall concepts of quantum chemistry that
will be relevant for the upcoming derivations and calculations. I will not go into great detail with
these, but the descriptions are loosely based on chapter 4 of McQuarrie’s Quantum Chemistry[14,
p.143-179].
The state of a quantum mechanical-system is fully described by the wave function, usually
denoted Ψ(r, R, t), where r and R denote the spatial coordinates of all the electrons and nuclei of the

2.5
Populations of Bound States and Dissociation Probability
In order to determine dissociation probabilities later on, the populations of the different bound states
must be found. The total population of all eigenstates will be one, so the dissociation probability
is logically one minus the populations of the bound states[2, p.2]. The populations can be found
by considering the expansion of the wave function onto the eigenstates of the Hamiltonian (both

2.6
Types of Transitions Between States
As the molecule interacts with the field, population will move up the ladder of bound states until
it reaches the unbound states and thus dissociates, but there are a few options for how these
transitions happen. The most likely is that the photon energy will match the difference between
two neighboring energy levels, and upon it being absorbed a transition from the lower level up to the
higher one will happen. At high intensities of the field, which will be one of the cases considered, it
is possible for two or more photons to be absorbed simultaneously and with their combined energy
result in a transition between states, including ones that are two or more levels apart. The higher
the intensity, the more likely this is to happen[15, p.1866-1867].
Another option is that a single photon has the energy corresponding to a transition between
states that are not neighbors, thus resulting in an overtone transition. These are however less likely
than the fundamental transitions between neighboring levels[15, p.1867][11, p.508].

4
Chirped Pulse
In the previous calculations the laser considered has had a constant center frequency over time,
and it was found that setting this frequency slightly lower than the frequency corresponding to the
transition from the ground vibrational state to the first exited state yielded the highest dissociation
probability, the explanation being that the difference in energy between neighboring vibrational
levels gets smaller the higher the states, until dissociation occurs. With this in mind, it might yield
even better results if instead of settling on one central frequency, it is set to decrease over time
in such a way that the frequency matches the transitions as higher and higher vibrational levels
become populated. This approach is known as ”chirping”[6, p.539], specifically downwards chirping
since the central frequency is decreasing with time. For the following calculations, I will be using a
linear chirp, meaning that the frequency varies linearly with time.

4.4
Lowering the intensity
The intensity of the fields used for the previous calculations is quite high, and although an improve-
ment of the dissociation probability is achieved by chirping, it is not a massive improvement. As
such, it would be interesting to see what happens if the intensity is lowered to a point where the
dissociation probability is not very large without the chirp, and whether a much bigger improve-
ment can be achieved with chirping. The idea is that-multi photon transitions will not be as big a
contributor, and so the system has to move up the ladder of bound states one by one. The chirp
accommodates this by having the frequency change as the system climbs higher and higher. It also
means that the calculations use an intensity that can more easily be achieved in a laboratory.

6
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, I have studied the dissociation of the HeH+-ion when interacting with
different laser pulses in both one and three dimensions. The goal was to numerically determine the
dissociation probability of the ion after interaction with the laser pulse by propagating the wave
function of the system over the time in which the interaction is taking place and then determining
the dissociation probability from the populations of the bound states. The molecule was described
as a Morse oscillator, for which the bound states can be determined analytically in one dimension
and decently approximated in three dimensions, at least for lower values of the rotational quantum
number. In three dimensions the dissociation probability is also determined as the probability of
the separation of the nuclei exceeding 10 ˚A where the molecule is deemed to be dissociated at
sufficiently long times after the pulse is over.
I have first reproduced results by Wustelt et al. to descent agreement for a high intensity laser
pulse, seeing the same tendency of the dissociation probability when varying the center frequency
of the laser. A maximum dissociation probability of 76.86% was achieved at a center frequency
corresponding to a period of TL = 14 fs using a laser pulse with an intensity of 7·1013 W
cm2 . To build
upon these results I have introduced a chirp to the pulse, allowing the frequency to vary over time
with the intention of having it follow the separation of the vibrational levels as higher and higher
levels are populated. With the chirp it was possible to achieve a dissociation probability of 93.05%
at TL = 15.6 fs and a chirp of crel = −0.16 relative to the center frequency and pulse length using
the high intensity. I have also done calculations at a lower intensity of 2 · 1012 W
cm2 , which show a
more pronounced effect of the chirp, taking the dissociation probability from 0.587% (at TL = 13.1
fs) to 7.06% (at TL = 15.5 fs and crel = −0.166). By examining the populations of the bound states,
it was also seen that the more pronounced effect of the chirp at lower intensities can be explained
by the populations of the bound states more systematically moving up the vibrational states one
state at a time.
For the three-dimensional calculations, it was found that similar magnitudes of the dissociation
probabilities could be achieved by lengthening the pulse by three times, to compensate for the
molecule not being aligned with the field at all times. With this, dissociation probabilities of 84.19%
and 84.12% were found using the approximate bound states and the probability of separation over
10 ˚A respectively, at TL = 14 fs for the high intensity case. The orientation and alignment of the
molecule were also examined by calculating the expectation values of cos θ and cos2 θ, which showed
that the molecule did have a tendency to align with the field, and stay aligned after the pulse, but
showed little preference for which way it was turned. The calculations were also run over a range
of TL-values, both with the three times longer pulse and the original pulse length, to see if the
tendency was the same as was found in one dimension, and this was seen to be roughly the case,
with both calculations showing many of the same features. For the low intensity, at TL = 13.1 fs
without chirping, the dissociation probabilities achieved were 0.0931% using both methods. Again,

the molecule showed some tendency to align with the field, but it was not as pronounced, and did
not persist after the pulse was over. The effect of including a chirp was calculated over a small range
of TL- and crel-values at the lower intensity and the dissociation probability was seen to generally
increase the stronger the chirp, with the highest value calculated being 1.63% at TL = 15.4 fs and
crel = −0.125, which was the highest crel-value considered in three dimensions, since the higher
chirping parameters used in one dimension resulted in some odd behaviour of the populations of
radial parts of the wave function.
In terms of building on the work done throughout this project, one avenue would be to improve on
the optimization of the laser pulse in terms of center frequencies and chirping parameters, which for
now have been determined by broad scans over the possible values of the parameters. It would make
these calculations less cumbersome if this search was done in a more ordered fashion, potentially
through the use of a genetic algorithm where a smaller set of random parameters could be tested,
and the best of them be combined through a series of successive runs to hopefully have them ’evolve’
towards the best possible parameters[28, p.479]. Aside from this, it would of course be exciting to
expand these calculations to molecules with more than one bond, to examine how interaction with
a laser pulse could be used to favour the breaking of one bond over another.
