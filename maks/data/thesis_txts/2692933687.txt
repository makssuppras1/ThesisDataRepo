Abstract
A numerical fluid dynamic model of a 17 [m3] bioreactor was constructed to investigate
industrial process conditions during lactic acid bacteria cultivation.
The primary
focus of the study was to quantify the pH spacial gradient in the tank, as well as the
effects on the resulting fermentation process. With the scope of simulating the pH
gradients in the bioreactor during fermentation, the numerical model should include
the mixing behaviour in the tank, the microbial growth leading to the acidification
of the system, as well as a model of the buffer system, in order to accurately predict
the pH.
A mixing simulation of the fermentation tank was performed in ANSYS CFX, with the
two studied impeller types, i.e. marine and rushton impeller. The rushton impeller
resulted in the lowest mixing time of 5-7 [s] compared to 32-35 [s] for the marine
impeller, depending on the turbulence model applied. The mixing simulation was
also performed with STARCCM+, which resulted in a comparable mixing profile for
the rushton impeller, and a different mixing profile for the marine impeller. Further
experimental validation of the model will indicate which solver provides the most
realistic solution.
The surface average wall shear was estimated between 28.7 and 36.7 [Pa] for the ma-
rine impeller and between 38.9 and 41.8 [Pa] for the rushton impeller, depending on
the turbulence model applied. However, the distribution of the wall shear on the im-
peller surface was different for the two impeller types, with the marine impeller being
more homogeneous, having the lowest maximum to average wall shear ratio, whereby
it is concluded the marine impeller is potentially less harmful to the microorganisms.
The buffer model was implemented to the mixing simulation to describe the pH in the
tank during a base pulse. Then the buffer model was combined with the microbial
kinetic model to describe the full behaviour of the fermenting system. The combined
model was successfully solved in MATLAB for the case of ideal mixing, however the
stiff nature of the mathematical system to be solved made the implementation of
the combined buffer and biokinetic model with the CFD model unsuccessful. Sev-
eral approaches were tested to solve the model without any successful outcome. As
an illustration to the limitations related with CFD and stiff mathematical systems,
possible adaptations to the model are discussed, that would facilitate the numerical
solving of the system.

Introduction
1.1
General Introduction
In the transition towards a sustainable society, solutions to reduce environmental im-
pact while maintaining life quality and economical growth are essential. Historically,
greenhouse gas emissions have been tightly correlated with economical growth. In
addition, the growing population and rising of life quality leads to an increase in the
global resource and energy demand. Hence the decoupling of greenhouse gas emis-
sions and economical growth is of utmost importance to reach a sustainable society.
Moreover, the contribution of human activity to global warming has an increasing
attention in terms of political legislation at national scales, e.g. taxation, ban of cer-
tain materials/processes; and at international scales, e.g. Paris Climate Agreement.
As a solution to this problem, new technologies offer alternatives to the traditional
methods, in terms of energy and material production, allowing for a decoupling of
economic activity and environmental footprint (1, 2).
Biotechnology is considered as one of the sustainable technologies contributing to the
ecological transition. Fermentation based manufacturing has reviled new perspectives
within several industries such as food and pharma, and is also gaining attention as a
substitute to fossil fuels, e.g. energy and bulk chemicals production. However being
an immature technology, many challenges relates to the industrial scales are yet to be
solved. Optimisation of the productions at larger scales is essential for the technology
to be competitive with traditional productions. And increasing the scale will increase
the benefits from the economy of scale, also improving the competitiveness of the
process.
One of the key challenges with industrial bioproductions is maintaining
homogeneous and desired process conditions throughout the fermenting volume.
In parallel, the exponential progress of computational power allows for more and more
complex computer simulations. Computer simulations have the advantage of being
more simple, safe and cheap with regards to actual experiments, especially in the
production scale. In this way, computer simulations work as a virtual laboratory to
explore industrial solutions.

the understanding of fluids we have today. Mathematical models have been proposed

Energy Balance (or Bernoulli’s Equation).

1.4
Computational Fluid Dynamics
Computational Fluid Dynamics, or CFD, is a method to find numerical solutions
to the governing equations of fluid dynamics. As mentioned in the previous section
(section 1.3), models for fluid dynamics have been proposed. The issue is that those
equations can be solved analytically, only for a few simple systems. So here, the
power of numerical solvers can play an important role. Indeed, it is possible to solve
==PAGE:28==
10
1 Introduction
the model equations numerically for any given geometry.
A numerical solution is a solution to the discretized form of the model equations. The
model equations are discretized both in the space and time dimensions, transforming
the partial differential equations to sets of algebraic equations. In this way, comput-
ers can be used to solve all the algebraic equations and provide a solution to the
discretized system.
The time dimension is discretized into time steps, meaning the time interval over
which the equations are desired to be solved is divided into time steps, where at each
time step the equations are solved. This means that more time steps, means more
equations and larger computational demand. The space dimensions are discretized
into volume elements, meaning the fluid domain is divided into smaller volume ele-
ments within which the equations are averaged and the flow variables are constant
(e.g. pressure, velocity vector..). This process is called the meshing, and is an essen-
tial part of CFD as the solution much depends on how the equations were discretized
(3).
Finally, when discretizing a flow problem with respect to both space and time, an
important parameter to consider is the Courant Number.

greater than one, information is moving through the fluid domain more than one

In this fist step, the geometry of the simulated fluid is to be constructed. Only the

1.4.2
Meshing
The mesh construction is an essential part of a CFD simulation as it dictates how
the model equations are to be solved. Essentially, the volume of the geometry is
discretized into smaller sub-volumes, called mesh elements or cells. In each element,
the model equations are to be solved in a discretized form. So the smaller the cells,
the finer the resolution. On the other hand, more cells means more equations to be
solved and increases the computational demand. A higher computational demand
means longer computational time, or requires a more powerful computer.
So the goal when constructing a mesh is to find the right balance between accuracy
of the results and computational demand. This balance is typically set by the user
or the person interested in the results, as it depends on the goal of using a CFD tool.
Some might be interested in having very accurate results, no matter the time it takes.
But in many cases, time is a concern and for CFD to be feasible, results should be
available within a certain time frame. This is particularly the case in the industry,
where the cost of having an engineer working on the project using an often expensive
CFD software, is taken into account.
In a three dimensional geometry, cell elements can have several shapes such as tetra-
hedrons (4 sided), pyramids (5 sided) and hexahedrons (6 sided). Tetrahedron meshes
are relatively easy to set up as they can typically be generated automatically. This is
one of its main advantages as it considerably reduces the time required to construct
a mesh, especially for more complex geometries. On the other hand, automatically
generated meshes tend to be of a lower quality and there is no control over the dis-

1.4.3
Definition of the flow problem
The next step in setting up a CFD simulation is to define the flow problem. This
is essentially selecting the equations to be solved, defining eventual parameters and
boundary conditions to the system. Based on the phenomena that is to be simulated,
the model equations are selected accordingly. Any flow problem typically requires the
momentum (equation (1.4)) and continuity (equation (1.1)) equations, as well as a
turbulence model (unless a laminar system is simulated). If the transport of a specie
is to be modelled, the specie transport equation (equation (1.31)) can be applied. The
heat equation can also be applied to model heat transport in the system, though is
not used in this project.
In order for the equations to be solved, some model parameters must be defined. For
instance when solving the momentum equation (equation (1.4)), the fluid density and
viscosity must be specified.
Spacial boundary conditions to the geometry boundaries must be specified, in order to
solve for a unique solution. The typical boundary conditions used in CFD are : Wall,
Inlet, Outlet, Opening, Symmetry and Rotational Periodicity. The wall boundary
condition can be either no slip wall, meaning the velocity of the fluid at the surface
of the wall is equal to zero in all directions, or free slip wall, meaning the stress of
the fluid at the surface of the wall is equal to zero. This is equivalent to no velocity
gradient near the wall, or no boundary layer. The inlet, outlet and opening bound-
ary conditions allow the fluid to pass through the boundary, only into the domain
for inlets, only out of the domain for outlets and both in and out of the domain for
openings. The symmetry boundary condition can be used to simulate symmetrical
systems by only simulating part of it. Rotational periodicity can be used in the same
way to only simulate a fraction of a system with rotational symmetry around an axis.
By using symmetry or rotational periodicity, it is possible to simplify the geometry,
by only simulating a fraction of it. This allows for a lower computational demand,
==PAGE:31==

but can be less realistic especially for turbulent systems that tend to behave in an
unsymmetrical, chaotic way.
Regarding the temporal dimension, simulations can be performed in a Steady State
manner (setting the time derivatives to zero) to provide a steady state solution of
the system (solution for t →∞with no change over time), or in a Transient manner,
where the time derivative is involved in the equations and provides a time dependent
solution of the system (models the flow over time).
In the same way as spacial boundary conditions, if the time derivative is involved
in the equations, a temporal boundary condition must also be specified. Typically,
it is formulated as an initial condition, the state of the system at the beginning of
the simulation. Both for steady state and transient simulations, a step time must
be specified. For transient simulations, the step time is the actual discretization of
time, while for steady state simulations the time step is nonphysical. It should be
noted that flow problems that have a stable oscillatory behavior as t →∞can not
be modeled using steady state simulations.
In CFD, moving parts can be modeled in different ways.
A moving wall can be
modeled by specifying the wall velocity, and setting the velocity of the fluid at the
surface of the wall equal to the wall velocity. This method allows for modeling of wall
movement without any deformation of the actual fluid domain. For rotating parts,
such as an impeller in a bioreactor, a common method is to divide the fluid domain
into two separate domains where on domain including the rotating part is rotating
(also called rotor domain) and the rest of the fluid domain is specified as stationary
(stator domain). A mesh is constructed in each domain and the two meshes are finally
connected using a mesh interface. The mesh in the rotating domain is then rotating
along with the walls, and the mesh interface connects the mesh elements from each
side of the interface, at each time step.
Finally, two methods can be used to model the rotating domain. The Frozen Rotor
method accounts for the rotation by adding a momentum source to the fluid at
the surface of a rotating wall, without taking into account the displacement of the
rotating domain. The Moving Rotor method, contrarily, does take into account the
displacement of the rotating domain. The main advantage of using the Moving Rotor
method is that the model does take into account the changing relative positions of
the elements in the rotating and the static domains. On the other hand, the Moving
rotor method is more computationally demanding and can only be applied to transient
simulations.
1.4.4
Solving and post processing
Once the CFD model is constructed, it is then simulated using the numerical solver.
The numerical solver solves the systems of discretized model equations by minimizing
==PAGE:32==
14

the Root Mean Square (RMS) of the residuals of each model variable. Typically, the
solution is considered as converged once the RMS of all residuals are and stay below
the pre-specified convergence criteria. Alternatively, a convergence criteria can be
set on the maximum residual, instead of the RMS. This allows for a more accurate
solution, but requires more iterations to reach the convergence criteria.
The require time to solve the CFD model depends on the the number of equations
to be solved and the available computational power. Meaning that increasing the
amount of mesh elements and the amount of step times to be solved, increases the
computational time. The value of the convergence criteria dictates how many itera-
tions are required to reach convergence, meaning a lowering the convergence criteria
will increase the computational time. Although a good initial guess may reduce the
computational time, as less iterations are required to reach the convergence criteria.
During the solving process, several errors can potentially occur. The different types
of errors are listed below (12).
• Round-off error occurs when the precision of the numerical solver is too low,
making the solution less accurate. This can be avoided by increasing the preci-
sion of the solver, but will increase the computational time.
• Iteration error occurs when not enough iterations have been performed to reach
a converged solution. this can be caused by the maximum number of itera-
tions being too low or the convergence criteria being too high. In both cases
the reached solution the solution may not actually be converged, meaning the
solution does not change by performing more iterations.
• Discretization error occurs when the constructed mesh is too coarse and is not
able to resolve the desired flow phenomena. In this case, there will be a sig-
nificant difference between the reached solution and the ”real / exact” solution
expected from a simulation with an infinitely fine mesh. Note that for transient
simulations, discretization errors may also occur with the discretization of the
time dimension. Indeed a too large time step may result in a poor resolution of
the flow. This can be solved by adjusting the time step such that the Courant
Number in all mesh elements is lower than 1.
• Model error occurs when model parameters and/or the models themselves are
not correctly selected. In this case, these is a deviation between the reached
solution and the ”real” solution that is expected, even though the residuals are
low, and that the solution is properly converged. A typical model error is the
choice of the wrong turbulence model, and can be solved by comparing several
models to analytical or experimental data, in order to assess which model best
fits the observations.
Finally after solving the CFD model, the results are yet to be presented in an under-
standable way. For example the model variables can be visualised on lines, planes or
volumes. It is also possible to compute overall system results such as the magnitude

1.5.1
Introduction to Fermentation Processes
Fermentation has been part of human culture for thousands of years, as traces of
fermented beverages were found dating from the Neolithic period, during the sev-
enth millennium BCE in China (13).
The basic principle of fermentation is the
conversion of chemical compounds through biochemical reactions taking place inside
microorganisms as part of their metabolism. Indeed, every living organism extracts
energy from its surroundings through biochemical reactions, resulting in the conver-
sion of some chemical compounds into others. Different microorganisms have different
metabolisms, and throughout the years, humans have discovered how to use specific
microorganisms to produce valuable products. A well known example of fermentation,
is the conversion of glucose to ethanol using yeast (Saccharomyces Cerevisiae) during
the production of alcoholic beverages.
The metabolic pathways used by the microorganisms much depends on the surround-
ing conditions. Indeed temperature, pH, access to nutrients and more generally the
chemical composition surrounding the cells may influence the metabolism of the mi-
croorganism, which influences the metabolite composition.
So when developing a
fermentation process, it is important to make sure the microorganisms are exposed
to the right conditions for the specific production that is desired.
It is important to mention that the recent discoveries within the field of genetic science,
allows for a wide range of biological optimisation that can improve fermentation
processes. For example making the microorganism more eﬀicient at producing the
desired compound, more eﬀicient at consuming substrate, or more resilient to the
production conditions (pH, higher tolerance towards toxic or inhibiting compounds).
In the modern industry, fermentation processes take place in bioreactors of various
designs, depending on the type of production. Bioreactors can be operated in three
ways: Batch, Fed-batch and Continuous (see figure 1.5). A batch is operated as a
closed system where all the raw materials are added to the bioreactor, along with
the microorganisms, at the beginning. And after the fermentation, the broth is col-
lected and processed. A Fed-batch is the same as a batch but with the difference
that feed (or a mixture containing substrate) is continuously added to the bioreactor
during the fermentation. Finally a continuous bioreactor is operated by continuously
adding feed as well as continuously collect the broth. Each setup has its advantages
and disadvantages, and are selected accordingly depending on the type of production.
In all above cases, apart from the bioreactor setup, some fittings are added to the
bioreactor in order to maintain optimal production conditions during the entire fer-
mentation. Indeed, during the fermentation biochemical reactions occur, changing
the chemical composition in the bioreactor. For instance, some microorganisms pro-
duce acidic or basic metabolites during the fermentation, thus changing the pH in the

Fermentation processes can be classified in two groups. Aerobic fermentation requires

and in some extreme cases, oxygen may even be toxic to the microorganism and so
an oxygen free environment must be maintained.
When considering industrial bioprocesses, the fermentation process, or upstream, only
includes the initial step of exponential biomass production. After the fermentation,
the broth containing a mixture of many compounds is then processed to separate the
product of interest from the rest of the broth. This step is call the downstream. The
design of the downstream much depends on the type of product and the requirements
for the final product. For instance, Bio-ethanol used for combustion may not require
a high purity, whereas a pharmaceutical product requires high purity, since it will be
used for human consumption.

1.6
Mixing in Bioreactors
Mixing is an essential parameter in many chemical and biochemical processes. Mixing
is the reduction of inhomogeneity in a system, being temperature, concentration or
phase. Materials and heat have two means of transport in a fluid, through diffusion
(conduction for heat) and convection. Diffusion transport is usually very slow, as
it depends on the concentration gradient and the diffusion coeﬀicient (according to
Fick’s Law) which usually have very small values. By introducing momentum into the
fluid, it creates convection, transporting material much more eﬀiciently than when
only relying on diffusion.
Generally, turbulence can be interpreted as convection
dissipating to smaller scales, and so more turbulence means more convection in the
small scales and thus better transport of material. So a general rule of thumb is that
increasing the turbulence in a system will improve the mixing.
In industrial scale production processes, mixing of liquids is usually performed using
stirred tanks. Both the type and configuration of the impeller(s) as well as the tank
geometry have an influence on the resulting mixing pattern. The two most simple and
common mixing patterns are the axial mixing pattern and the radial mixing pattern
(figure 1.7). These two mixing profiles are typically the type mixing profile expected
when using a marine impeller and a Rushton impeller respectively. Several impellers
can be placed on the shaft, and the resulting mixing will depend on the position of
the impellers. The rest of the tank geometry plays a role in the mixing. For example,
the use of baffles on the walls of the tank will contribute to increasing the turbulence
in the fluid.
The overall goal of mixing is to achieve identical process conditions throughout the re-
actor, and allows the entire process volume to operate at the desired conditions. This
is important for systems where the conditions have an influence on the process. In
the case of fermentation, the biochemical reactions carried out by the microorganisms
very much depend on the process conditions. Importantly, the extreme conditions at

The mixing in a stirred tank can be improved with different means. At first, the

Another way to improve the mixing without increasing the mixing power is by opti-
mising the mixing pattern in the tank, by changing the geometry and position of the
impeller(s), as well as adding baffles to induce turbulence in the fluid. The overall
shape of the tank also has an effect on the resulting mixing profile. The height to
diameter ratio, the size scale as well as the shape of the top and bottom of the tank
will influence the resulting flow profile.
Alternatives to the traditional stirred tank have been proposed in the industry. For
example, Bubble columns rely on the buoyancy of air bubbles in the tank to mix the
liquid phase.
One of the main challenges with optimising the mixing in industrial tanks is that the
tanks are in use most of the time, and planning experiments can be limited by the
schedule of the production. Also, changing pieces and features on large tanks can be
expensive, especially when not knowing if the change will improve the mixing. Here,
CFD can play an important role, working as a ”virtual laboratory” where simulations
of the system can be performed for many different configurations, and provide valu-
able knowledge about what experiments are worth investigating further.

time) that still provides satisfactory results.

3.1
Mixing Times and Flow Profiles
The mixing simulations were carried out successfully. The resulting flow profiles us-
ing the k −ϵ turbulence model can be seen on figures 3.1 and 3.2. The flow profiles
resulting from using the SST and Laminar flow models can be seen in appendix D.
Only based on the overall mixing profiles, the k −ϵ model provides the most realistic
flow fields. For the marine impeller (figure 3.1) the impeller pushes the fluid upwards,
as expected based on the rotational direction of the impeller. However the up-going
flow does not reach the top of the tank and is diverged towards the sides, where it
splits into two main circulation loops. When relating to the dimensionless concen-
tration profiles (figure D.5) It can be observed that the two lower monitoring points
(MP1 and MP2) located in the lower circulating loop experience high concentrations
during the mixing as the source point is also located at the bottom of the tank. The
tracer then slowly penetrates the upper circulating loop, which is translated to a
gradual increase in the concentration at the two top monitoring points. This mixing
behaviour it referred to as compartmentalised mixing, where two or more circulating
loops can be distinguished. In a compartmentalised flow, the mixing within a com-
partment is much faster than the mixing between different compartments. For the
case of the marine impeller, it can be observed that the flow velocity is higher in the
lower compartment relative to the upper compartment.
Regarding the rushton impeller (figure 3.2), the flow profile resulted in a large circu-
lation loop occupying almost the entire tank volume, and a small circulation loop at
the bottom center of the tank. This results in a much faster mixing where no distinct
concentration profiles are observed (figure D.6). However it is important to note that
none of the monitoring points are located within the smaller circulation loop, which
is why it can not be observed on figure D.6.
In relation to the mixing time and the log σ2 plots (figures 3.3 and 3.4), the marine
impeller resulted in the slower mixing time (30-35 [s]) compared to the rushton im-
peller (5-7[s]). This is in agreement with the large top circulating loop being observed,
where the fluid velocity is slower.

Apart from potentially disrupting cells, shear stress in the liquid is beneficial to a
certain extent. For aerobic fermentations, shear stress contributes to air bubble dis-
ruption, which improves the oxygen mass transfer, according to Doran (5, p. 157).
Shear stress can also contribute to disrupting cell aggregates, which has a large impact
on the rheological properties of the fluid. Chain-forming bacteria, like Streptococcus
spp., typically induce Non-Newtonean behaviour of the fluid (5, p. 139). The rheolog-
ical properties of the fluid influence the mixing: Non-Newtonean fluids tend to have
a lower apparent viscosity in the high shear regions and higher apparent viscosity in
low shear regions. This results in the development of stagnant, ”dead” zones away
from the impeller (5, p. 156). Experimentally, the benefit of shear stress was shown
by Arnaud et al. (20) where significant improvement in cell metabolism was observed
at intermediate shear stress levels, between 36 and 54 [Pa].
Overall, the wall shear on the impeller surface can be investigated using CFD, but
the actual amount of cells being impacted by the shear stress has not been estimated.
Here, a lifeline approach following the trajectory of individual cells (24) may provide
information about the shear stress conditions cells really experience during fermenta-
tion.
3.3
Alkali Mixing Simulation
As mentioned in the previous chapter, for the alkali mixing simulation, the pH is
determined based on the tracer concentration in the post processing, in Matlab. Here
for each time step, the equilibrium pH is determined based on the rate model, solved
with a stiff numerical solver (ode15s).
Stiff numerical solvers such as the ode15s in
MATLAB are specifically designed to solve stiff mathematical systems. A mathemat-
ical system is considered as stiff when changes in the solution occur over different
variable scales. For instance considering a time dependent solution stiff with respect
to time, the solution may be subject to very fast and very slow changes at the same
time. This poses a fundamental problem when solving the system numerically, as a
large total simulation time is required to visualise the slow changes in the solution
and a very small time step is to be used to resolve the fast changes. This often leads
to a high total number of time steps to be solved, hence more equations to be solved,
and a heavier simulation. Stiff solvers use adaptive time steps that have the ability
to decrease if a sudden change in the solution occurs, and increase when the solution
is monotonic.
In relation to the system investigated in this project, it can be noted that the acid
base reactions are very fast relative to the mixing time, suggesting that solving the
flow equations for the mixing time in parallel with the acid base reactions will result

At first, the model was implemented in ANSYS CFX with a simplified mesh of one

stiffness of the system was to reduce the time step, as well as the buffer reaction rates.
Reducing the difference between the rates of the buffer reactions and the microbial
growth will reduce the stiffness of the system. As long as the buffer reaction rates still
are much faster than the microbial growth, no significant impact should be seen on the
results. The source coeﬀicient tool in CFX was also used to fit the individual rates.
If the buffer reactions are fast relative to the time step, the solution diverges and
produces unphysical results. Fitting the simulation parameters (rates, time step) to
obtain a satisfactory result was unsuccessful. Taking into account the computational
time required to solve the one element mesh (±4 hours) the time step could not be
reduced more than 0.05 [s]. considering that the model is to be used on the full mesh,

lead to an unfeasible computational time ( several days of computational time for 1
second of simulation time). Here, the purpose of the study is not only to investigate
the microbial kinetics, in which case the simple homogeneous MATLAB model is
suﬀicient. The goal of the study is to quantify pH gradients during the fermentation
process, which requires a more advanced model taking into account the mixing in the
tank.
The implementation of the buffer model in CFX illustrated the limitations related to
CFD and stiff systems. Stiff systems require a fine tuning of the solver parameters to
be solved accurately in a reasonable amount of time. Tuning the adaptive time step
in CFX can be challenging and requires a lot of experience.
Some Adaptations can be made to the model to facilitate the solving process. At first
one could consider the buffer reactions as instantaneous relative to the slower rates in
the system, essentially reducing the buffer rate model to an equilibrium model that
would require solving algebraic equations, instead of solving the differential equations
of the reaction rates in a discretised way. Considering instantaneous buffer reactions
is a reasonable assumption, as the fast buffer reactions are not expected to have a
significant dynamic impact on the final results.
Another approach is to convert the CFD model into a compartment model. A com-
partment model is a simplified CFD model, where compartments are defined based
on the mixing profile, and considered as single elements, assuming homogeneous mix-
ing within each compartment. The volume of each compartment and the flowrate
between each compartment is estimated from the CFD model. The compartment
model can then be implemented in MATLAB where it can be solved with a stiff nu-
merical solver. Also, the fewer number of compartments relative to the initial number

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The objectives of the study was to construct a CFD model to simulate industrial pro-
cess conditions during fermentation. The mixing model was implemented successfully,
both in ANSYS CFX and STARCCM+. The two CFD software produced different
mixing profiles for the marine impeller, but similar mixing profiles for the rushton
impeller. With the ANSYS CFX simulation, the rushton impeller resulted in a lower
mixing time compared to the marine impeller. At this stage, experimental validation
of the model is crucial, as significantly different results were observed, when using
different simulation parameters, i.e. numerical solvers and turbulence models. Addi-
tionally, further investigation of the rheological properties of the fermentation broth,
as function of cell density, will contribute to a more realistic model.
The wall shear on the impeller surface resulted in different distributions on the marine
and rushton impellers, but have close average values. High wall shear regions on both
impellers may cause cell damage, however the marine impeller have a more homoge-
neous wall shear distribution, making it potentially less harming to the cells. It is
concluded that the wall shear may not be the most accurate parameter to investigate,
and a lifeline approach would provide more information about the real shear stress
conditions cells are exposed to during the fermentation. The lifeline approach uses
particle tracking to monitor the process conditions, and provides information about
the conditions cells experience during a life cycle.
The buffer model constructed to describe the pH was successfully implemented in
MATLAB, however the stiff nature of the system made it diﬀicult to implement in
the CFD model. Therefore, changes to the model must me made, in order to facili-
tate the solving process, and obtain significant results. The goal of the study was to
investigate and quantify pH gradients during fermentation, which requires a more ad-
vanced model than the ideal homogeneous case. A possible adaptation to the model
is to convert the CFD model into a compartment model, that can be solved with a
stiff numerical solver in MATLAB.
The goal of the study was also to propose potential optimisations to the system.
However using a model for optimisation purposes requires rigorous experimental val-
idation. A working simulation of the microbial kinetic model as well as the buffer
model would allow investigation of pH gradients in the tank with different control
==PAGE:78==

setups,i.e position of base addition point and location of monitor point used as con-
troller input. The setup leading to the lowest pH gradients would be considered as
the optimal control setup.
Apart from the control structure, other adaptations can be made to the process
to minimise pH gradients in the tank.
In general, better mixing will reduce the
gradients, and several adaptations can be made to improve the mixing. For instance,
the position and number of impellers, tank geometry and baffles disposition can be
adjusted to optimise mixing. Moreover, a higher buffer capacity will also contribute
to minimise variations in pH. Quantifying the impact of the buffer capacity on the
pH gradients could be performed with a full biokinetic and buffer model. In terms
of implementation of potential optimisations, some changes are easier to implement
in the industrial scale. For instance structural changes to the fermentation tank are
hardly feasible, whereas changing the control structure or initial buffer composition is
associated with fewer practical limitations. From the perspective of Christian Hansen,
this study provides valuable insights on the intrinsic limitations related to CFD and
stiff systems, as well as the alternatives to solve the system.
Future work on the model is necessary to model pH gradients in the tank, with the
kinetic and buffer models. A compartment model of the system can be constructed
and implemented in MATLAB, where it can be solved with the stiff numerical solver.
Moreover, experimental validation of the model is essential if the model is to be
further used to propose potential optimisations to the system.
