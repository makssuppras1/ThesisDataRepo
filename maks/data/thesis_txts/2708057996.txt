Abstract
The target of that project is to identify the performance of diﬀerent DCV systems that
obtain diﬀerent indicators as a variable controller for the ventilation system. Three dif-
ferent type of buildings (residential, oﬃce, classroom) were designed and conducted to a
sequence of simulation in IDA ICE software, for collecting the results. The outcomes for
the simulations are perceived and correlated driven by the requirements of the Indoor Air
Quality standards and the energy savings.
The simulations were carried out by implementing into the models 3 diﬀerent DCV sys-
tems. The three indicators that applies is the occupancy, the carbon dioxide and the
humidity.
The results showed that the CO2 indicator reported the highest reduction
regarding the energy fans around to 65% in a classroom. The DCV system based on
humidity addressed the a reduction of 19.98% in the dwellings. The air ﬂow rates that
controlled by the indicators noted decrease approximately to 20%.

Introduction
Investigations have shown that the vast majority of population that living in developed
countries allocate the 90% of their daily time either at the work-sites or inside home.
For this reason, arose the demand for society to be occupied with the improvement and
optimization of the buildings’ environment and assure a better Indoor Air Quality (IAQ),
hygiene and comfort for the people [1]. The role of ventilation systems, especially nowa-
days, is essential to achieve the aforementioned building requirements.
Along with the demands for IAQ, it is urgent to focus on the energy savings of the
constructions and the achievement of reducing the total energy consumption. According
to statistics, european buildings consume almost the 38% of total energy use, distributing
the 25% to residential buildings and 13% to non-residential buildings, respectively (Euro-
stat Regional Book, 2014).
The total energy use related to the non-residential buildings for heating, ventilation and
air-conditioning systems (HVAC) amounts to 50% [2].The target of EPBD requires all the
new constructions that will be built in the future, to meet the standards of nearly zero
energy buildings. This suggestion requires an amount of total energy use for ventilation
systems for oﬃces and educational (nZEB) buildings to be ranged around to 10-50%.[3]
An initial move before approaching the modiﬁcation of the HVAC systems is, to ensure
that the heating demand of the buildings is reduced.
This prerequisite of the nZEB
building for being energy eﬃcient can be achieved by the increment of the insulation
thickness and consequently, by increasing the air tightness of the building envelope [4].
Furthermore, this adjustments can be followed by dropping oﬀthe air change rate (ACH)
to residential and oﬃce buildings (5). Hence, in a residential building the initial air change
per hour was approximately 1.0-1 and nowadays has been decreased around to 0.5-1. [Chan
et al. 2003; Hodgson et al. 2000; American Society of Heating, Reftigerating, and Air-
Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) 2013b]
As it is noticed the performance of HVAC systems is crucial for meeting equally good
IAQ and reduced total energy use in the building.
The optimization of the ventila-
tion system is a fundamental starting point. The focus area must be the elimination of
pollutant emissions to the environment by controlling the ventilation air ﬂow rate. A
demand controlled ventilation system (DCV) with several indicators (C02, relative hu-
midity,occupancy, VOC) for measuring the (IAQ) requirements can achieve a reduction
to air ﬂow rates.
Moreover, a reduction to fan operation and heating/cooling energy
consumption can be accomplished.
The DCV system with a C02 sensor based and occupancy proﬁle is implemented widely.
The aim of this project is to apply diﬀerent cases of DCV systems with several sensors
into 3 diﬀerent type of buildings (residential, oﬃce, classroom). This plan can distinguish
the best indicator for each category of building that meets the requirement of ASHRAE
standards for IAQ [5] [6].
A constant air volume system (CAV) is created as a baseline for the further correlations
of the cases. The hypotheses of models are designed and simulated in the Swedish IDA
Indoor Climate and Energy ICE software according to the Building regulations (BR20)[7].

Part One: Building Regulations
The aim of this report is to develop several DCV strategies that can meet the requirements
of the buildings regulations by changing the indicators and conclude to the best optimal.
Thus, a delivered proposal is essential for illustrating the best indicators for each type
of building depending on the current demands that must be fulﬁlled. The IAQ strategy
is a complex of several parameters that must be disciplined and satisﬁed, as the source
control, ventilation and inﬁltration and/or air cleaning.[8]

2.2
Ventilation ﬂow rate
The pollutant emissions regarding the source control have been carried out with the suf-
ﬁcient choice of materials. The following strategy for fulﬁlling and meet the requirements
of the buildings is to design the ventilation ﬂow rates for diﬀerent type of ventilation
systems (natural, mechanical , hybrid). The estimation of the air ﬂow that is needed for
the buildings is essential to be estimated as it is crucial for the thermal comfort and the
indoor environment quality and the demand energy use, respectively.
The volume of air in combination with the time m3/h that the ventilation system needs
to be activated according to ASHRAE requirements determine the fan energy use and
the heating/cooling demand [6]. Subsequently, the modiﬁcation of the air ﬂow and the
choice of an eﬃcient HVAC system is prerequisite for achieving the best optimal and
sustainable design for the current case scenarios. For designing the ventilation ﬂow rates
for residential and non- residential building, must be considered the duration of occupied
and unoccupied hours.
According to standards there are three diﬀerent methods of calculation the ventilation
airﬂow rates of buildings [8]. In this project there are 3 diﬀerent type of buildings and
the ventilation system is designed based on the accordingly building category, activities,
occupancy and the emissions of the materials that contained in the construction [11]. As
the methods based on the preference of the designer, while the IAQ results diﬀer, the
applicable method must be reported. The aforementioned methods diﬀer whether the
building type is residential and non- residential.
2.2.1
Ventilation rates for non-residential buildings
In the following report there are 2 type of buildings that considered as non-residential
buildings (oﬃce, classroom). For dimensioning the ventilation rate that is needed ac-
cording to the recommendations of the standards, are assigned 3 diﬀerent methods of
application that are based on the corresponding criteria according to DS/EN 15251 [11].
The recommended methods are:
1. method based on person and building component;
2. method based on ventilation rate per person or per m2 ﬂoor area;
3. method based on mass balance and CO2.

Part Two: Demand Controlled Ventilation systems
The aim of the report is to deﬁne the best IAQ of the buildings by implementing several
systems types according to the air handling units. The initial step is to create a CAV
model for the diﬀerent type of buildings and use it as a base in order to correlate it with
the optimized systems. The various ventilation systems that are created are based on a
method called Demand Controlled Ventilation (DCV).
The necessity of designing a diﬀerent type of ventilation system than a constant ﬂow
(CAV) is created in matter of saving operational time and energy use. As the building is
not occupied the whole during day the whole day, because the occupancy varies, therefore,
the ventilation ﬂow rates must be ﬂuctuated respectively. The need of providing freshening
air in the rooms, in DCV systems, is based on the indicators that have been applied [12].
Furthermore, the abovementioned systems, by controlling the air ﬂow rates, that are
needed according to the standards criteria, participate to the reduction of the fan energy
use and the heating/cooling demand [4].
A DCV system can vary the needed airﬂow rates of the building by the existed sen-
sors which identify emissions of pollutants (carbon dioxide, humidity, VOSs etc.). The
controlling air ﬂow rate plan is achieved from a sequence of signals that encounter the
aforementioned emission rates. A (feed-back control) is the main strategy which ensures
the modulation of the demand air ﬂow rates according to the measurement of the air
pollutants and the IAQ and/or thermal comfort parameters [13].
This approach is more eﬃcient in regards of energy consumption and emissions load in the
rooms of the building. A graphical illustration, of the evaluation of DCV systems various
approaches and the energy use, is created by Wouters [14] and cited in the Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Diﬀerent DCV approaches and the energy use eﬀect.

4.5
Design of macros in IDA ICE
As the designing parts of every DCV system are, certainly, arranged with the required
settings to be applied in the IDA ICE the ﬁnal step is the most crucial. This step includes
the creation of dominant indicators for every case scenario of the demand controlled
ventilation system. This target is achieved through a schematic window, in the interface
of IDA ICE software, that allow to formulate macros controllers.
The application of macros control in the ventilation system, is the notiﬁer diﬀerence
between the several types of ventilation system regarding the indicator and sensors that
activate and deactivate the HVAC system. As it is demonstrated in Section3 about the
nature and function of DCV systems, it is mandatory to design a ventilation system that
is regulated in cooperation with a speciﬁc type of sensor. Thus, this step can be carried
out by designing a macros controller which receives signals from the sensor values (CO2,
RH, occupancy) and conclude to the operation of the ventilation system (on/oﬀ).
A sequence of steps are essential to be conducted for the ﬁnal formulation of the cor-
responding macro controller. The methodology that implemented to design the DCV
systems per indicator in IDA ICE software and is depicted to the Figure 4.8, Figure 4.9
and Figure 4.10, respectively.

Zone
From each zone of the room is derived the emissions according to the generation of carbon
dioxide, relative humidity, occupancy density of the models. The signals are inserted in a
P-controller system.
Setpoint
The role of the setpoints are to deliver to the system the range of the recommended values
of the IAQ requirements, for each emission rate . The corresponding signals are inserted
to the PI-controller, respectively.
PI-controller
The aforementioned type of controller is implemented to the models to modulate the IAQ
according to the received signals from "zone" and "setpoints". The synergy of the signals
end the required values of emission rates, is accomplished to maintain a good IAQ and a
thermal comfort to the buildings.
Schedule
The schedule proﬁle is implemented to determine the operation of the ventilation system
as a ﬁnal step of the schematic macro design.
Multiplier
The role of Multiplier is to deﬁne between the daily hours signals that are delivered and
detected from sensors and the operation schedule. Therefore, the ﬁnal signal that PI
controller provides is multiplied by the occupancy proﬁle to the ﬁnal ventilation signal,
to distinguish the demand period of time in the zones.
Output ﬁles
The output ﬁles is a key for having uniquely and individually results for the parameters
of interest. Therefore, each of the ﬁle can be determined, relative humidity behavior,
airﬂow in the zone etc. It depends of the pre detection that must be accomplished before
simulating for a long period of time.

6
Discussion
In the previous section several sequences of simulations were conducted. The project is
occupied with diﬀerent ventilation systems regarding the indicators, and how they behave
in various types of buildings. As it is mentioned, three indicators are applied in the DCV
systems and therefore, the correlation for selecting the more eﬃcient is obtained. The
DCV systems were based in models that have, initially, simulated and implemented by a
constant air volume ventilation system. The concepts that are essential to be discussed
are referring the ventilation rate of the system and the energy savings of the models.
6.1
Airﬂow rates
In the corresponding simulations the modulation of the air ﬂow rate according to the re-
quired need is estimated. For diﬀerent type of buildings and HVAC systems, was evaluated
the tendency of the supply air ﬂow rate of the room.
For the residential building an initial CAV system was implemented. The ﬁrst indicator
that the DCV system is based is the occupancy. In the occupancy results, regarding the
air ﬂow rates, it is addressed that the supply air ﬂow rate dropped for 3 hours in the
reduction value of 50%. Furthermore, the relative humidity and carbon dioxide indicators
reported a close tendency of reducing for every 1 hour, accomplishing an energy saving in
the system.
Moreover, the school building was investigated regarding the air ﬂow rate that requires
to fulﬁll the IAQ and thermal comfort standards. The ﬁrst indicator that is correlated
with the CAV model is the occupancy. The aforementioned DCV system addressed that
more than 75% of time the air ﬂow rate reported a reduction around to 20% (from 220
l/s to 160 l/s). The carbon dioxide reduced the air ﬂow rate around to 50%, while the
maximum setpoints are not met for a long period of time. The relative humidity keeps
the same tendency with the carbon dioxide, but not in a great reduction of air ﬂow rate.
The oﬃce room of the building is implemented with several HVAC type systems. The
initial design was with a constant air volume system and therefore with demand controlled
ventilation system with indicators. The occupancy and the relative humidity as indicators
reported the almost the same tendency. The air ﬂow rate was reduced for 3 hours totally
around to 50%. The biggest diﬀerence addressed by the carbon dioxide indicator that for
a long period of time did not meet the maximum set point of the standards, while it is
operated for less time than the other indicators.
6.2
Fans energy saving
The results from the simulations were conducted regarding the the diﬀerent type of build-
ings. The fans energy that required for the Air handling units to be operated is reported
for every category of indicator. For the residential building the base model of CAV is
determined by 367.7 kWh fan energy. The dwelling tested in Demand controlled venti-
lation systems. The ﬁrst indicator of occupancy delivered a fans energy of 311.2 kWh.
Furthermore, the carbon dioxide in the corresponding energy showed an amount of 352
kWh. As the last applied DCV indicator, humidity, required a 294.2 kWh energy for the
fans operation. Therefore, the DCV based on CO2 for the dwelling reported a 15.42 %
reduction in fans energy, while the occupancy enumerated a 4.26%. Thus, the humidity
indicator correlated with the base mode of CAV system illustrated a 19.98% reduction
into fan energies.

Conclusion
The aim of this report is to meet the requirements of demand controlled ventilation system
(DCV) by using several indicators. The indicator that is the most well reported in several
studies is the CO2. The reason is that provides the most eﬃcient results regarding to the
fulﬁlling requirements for the IAQ, thermal comfort and energy saving. In this project
a sequence of simulations were conducted to verify if the aforementioned indicator is the
best optimal. For that reason, two diﬀerent types of sensors were implemented in DCV
system. The former is the occupancy and the latter is the humidity. The correlation of the
indicators was conducted in three diﬀerent type of building (residential, oﬃce, classroom),
for achieving the best overview.
The models designed and carried out to several simulations regarding the air ﬂow rates
and the energy savings. The two aforementioned focused areas were chosen because it is
the main diﬀerences that a system gain when the DCV system is applied. The reduction
of air ﬂow rate that controlled by the indicators in the DCV systems provides for the
buildings energy savings and IAQ.
The results of the three diﬀerent type of buildings showed that the CO2 indicator was the
dominant in the most of the simulations. There is one case scenario that a DCV system
based on carbon dioxide is not the only good solution. In residential buildings the relative
humidity indicator reported a slightly more eﬃcient model regarding the energy saving of
the fans.
The fact that the carbon dioxide was not the best in this scenario, maybe it is occurred
in terms of the occupancy density. The aforementioned sensor indicates better behavior
where the range of occupants varies the most. This is also reported in the two other type of
building where, the occupants proﬁle varies through time and the results of the CO2 energy
savings are by diﬀerence more eﬃcient compare to others. conclude, carbon dioxide is
undeniable one of the best indicators regarding the demand controlled ventilation systems.
The collaboration with other indicators in certain scenarios maybe could be essential for
accomplishing the most eﬃcient ventilation system.

8
Future Potential
As the results of the simulations are carried out and discussed, there is a space for im-
provement for ﬁnding the best indicator for the demand controlled ventilation systems.
The conclusion of the projected reported the carbon dioxide indicator to be one of the
most eﬃcient in several type of building. There is still, a space of development in the
near future.
As an initial point, is to collaborate with a new indicator that of Volatile Organic Com-
pounds (VOC). Researchers have shown that a collaboration in diﬀerent type of buildings
(classrooms, residential etc.) can contribute to the optimization of IAQ. The reduction
on the energy use is not diﬀer in e great percentage with the aforementioned indicators.
Furthermore, as the conclusion of this report stated that there is no indicator that can
standalone in each type of building, there is space of cooperation between them. For ex-
ample, a collaboration of an indicator of carbon dioxide and occupancy can be implement.
Thus, a residential building can work eﬃciently in corporation of humidity and occupancy
DCV systems.
A supplementary case for though can be the implementation of several DCV systems in
multiple climates. For having a better overview of the eﬀectiveness of the systems diﬀerent
set-points must be investigated.
