Abstract
This thesis presents a techno-economic analysis of a hybrid offshore wind farm for hydrogen
production, focusing on the infrastructure required to transport energy from the offshore wind
farm to shore either as electricity or hydrogen. A user interface (UI) is developed to provide
a tool for researchers to systematically test different wind farm configurations. Four distinct
transmission configurations are evaluated: traditional HVAC and HVDC electric cables, hydro-
gen pipelines, and an emerging hybrid cable developed by the company Hellenic Cables capable
of transmitting both electricity and hydrogen. A Python model assesses these configurations
considering the transmission costs, Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE), Levelized Cost of
Hydrogen (LCOH), and a reliability study. The results indicate that hydrogen pipelines offer
the lowest LCOH up to a distance of 240 km. However, when both hydrogen and electricity are
desired as the final output, the hybrid cable provides the most economic solution up to 128 km.
Beyond that, combining an HVDC line with a hydrogen pipeline becomes more favorable.

1
Introduction
As part of the UN Agenda 2030, the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are pushing for
the decarbonization of the global economy. Specifically, SDG 7 [1] aims to ensure access to
affordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all, while SDG 13 [2] aims to take
urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts. In line with these initiatives, offshore
wind power and green hydrogen have become critical technologies in the transition to a low-
carbon economy. This thesis presents a techno-economic analysis of a hybrid offshore wind
farm capable of producing both electricity and hydrogen as a pathway for decarbonization.
The EU has developed several initiatives to increase the share of offshore wind in the energy
mix, with the goal of installing 86-89 GW by 2030 and 355-366 GW by 2050 [3]. A share
of approximately 85% of this capacity is expected to be installed in the North Sea, reaching
at least 260 GW by 2050, as signed by the North Seas Energy Cooperation (NSEC). This
collaboration is composed of nine European countries and aims to create an offshore energy
hub in the North Sea [4].
Increasing the production of green hydrogen is also part of the EU’s goals. Currently, each year
70 to 100 million tons of CO2 are produced by hydrogen generation [5]. In order to counteract
this, certain initiatives have been developed. The EU Hydrogen Strategy has the objective of
installing 40 GW of renewables linked to electrolysis by 2030 [6]. In addition, the REPowerEU
Strategy of 2022 sets the goal of producing 10 million tonnes and importing 10 million tonnes
of hydrogen by 2030. Renewable hydrogen is expected to cover around 10% of EU’s energy
demand by 2050 [7].
These initiatives highlight the importance that offshore wind energy and green hydrogen produc-
tion will play in the coming years, particularly in Power-to-X (PtX) applications. Combining
both presents a solution to several challenges in the energy transition, such as decarbonizing
sectors that are diﬀicult to electrify or providing grid stability by making the energy demand
more flexible. PtX can also help manage excess electricity production from offshore wind by
converting surplus energy into hydrogen, reducing curtailment [8]. Finally, hydrogen can be
used for other applications, such as energy for fuel cell vehicles, reactant in ammonia production
for fertilizers and steel manufacturing [9].

As of August 2025, there are no fully integrated, commercial hybrid offshore wind-to-hydrogen
power plants, as there is still a significant gap in research. Several pilot projects are being
developed, such as the Deep Purple project by the company TechnipFMC, which aims to test
a hydrogen production system onshore until the end of 2025, and use the results to scale the
system into an offshore wind farm [11]. In addition, the Dutch company PosHydon has built
a pilot test in the Dutch North Sea, 13 km off the coast of Scheveningen (The Hague). The
platform (Q13a-A) has already been built, and the integration of wind power is still pending.
The 1 MW electrolyzer is expected to produce 400 kg of green hydrogen per day [10].
In addition to these pilot initiatives, larger-scale projects are also being pursued, such as the
North Sea Wind Power Hub [12], aimed at transforming the North Sea into a major hub
for offshore wind power generation. Developed by a consortium of European countries and
companies, the project consists of the construction of artificial islands that act as points of
connection for offshore wind farms, facilitating the transmission of energy. Hydrogen plays a
crucial role in this initiative by enabling the storage and transport of excess wind-generated
electricity.
Considering the important role that green hydrogen will play in the future, identifying the most
technically and economically viable methods for integrating offshore wind with green hydrogen
production is essential.
1.1
Previous research
This section aims to explain the current state of research, in order to identify the gaps and
formulate a proper objective and research questions.
Extensive research has been done comparing different energy transmission systems from offshore
to shore, in particular between High Voltage Direct Current lines (HVDC) and High Voltage
Alternating Current lines (HVAC), such as ([13], [14]). By comparing their respective costs with
distance, these studies establish the average break-even distance at approximately 80 km, with
HVAC lines being more economical at shorter distances. Similarly, Patonia et al. [15] perform
a comparison between hydrogen pipelines and HVDC, highlighting how hydrogen pipelines are
generally capable of transporting a greater volume of energy, but are typically shorter than
HVDC lines.
Several economic models have also been developed for the different infrastructure elements,
such as the model developed by Xiang et al. [16] for the HVAC and HVDC lines, Dinh et al.
[17] for offshore hydrogen pipelines or Nakhai [18] for offshore substations.
Several studies have also researched the reliability of the different systems that constitute a
wind farm. For example, Warnock et al. [19] performed a study on failure rates of offshore
wind transmission systems, Ofgem [20] studied the availability of HVDC lines and Stefani and
Carr [21] developed a model to estimate failure rates of offshore pipelines.
In addition, there are some emerging technologies that have the potential to play an important
part in the future of green hydrogen production. The company Hellenic Cables has designed
a hybrid cable capable of transmitting both electricity and hydrogen using a single conduit,
aiming to reduce transmission costs [22]. Nevertheless, it remains a prototype, and remain a
novel solution with high potential but limited validation.
1.2
Research gap and objectives
Although the techno-economic feasibility of a hybrid offshore wind farm has been investigated
in detail in multiple studies, there are several gaps in the research that this thesis aims to fill.
Firstly, there is no established tool to compare different hybrid wind farm configurations.
Techno-economic models incorporate many different parameters that can have a significant
12
==PAGE:13==
Figure 2: Hybrid cable developed by Hellenic cables [22]
impact on the final results, and to have access to a standardized method would ease evaluation.
For this reason, the first objective of this study is to develop a mathematical framework to
allow researchers to systematically investigate different infrastructure combinations. A model
will be developed in Python to calculate the costs of the main infrastructure elements, and then
implemented in the form of a user interface (UI) to facilitate its practical use.
Secondly, current studies often consider the transmission of electricity and hydrogen as separate
processes that use different infrastructure: electricity is transmitted through cables while hy-
drogen is delivered through pipelines. However, the hybrid cable developed by Hellenic Cables
presents an innovative method to transport electricity and hydrogen simultaneously, which can
greatly reduce infrastructure costs. Despite this, given the novelty of this technology, there
is a lack of research comparing it with the other transmission systems, such as HVAC and
HVDC lines and hydrogen pipelines. Aiming to fill this gap, the second objective of this study
is to evaluate the techno-economic performance of the hybrid cable system in comparison with
conventional transmission alternatives.
Thirdly, there is a lack of research on the reliability of the hybrid offshore wind farm. While
failure modes and rates have been well-documented for conventional transmission systems like
HVAC, HVDC, and pipelines, their analysis in the context of a hybrid offshore wind farm
remains largely unexplored. For this reason, the third objective is to evaluate and compare the
reliability of the main transmission systems. Each system will be assigned a failure rate and a
mean time to repair (MTTR) and their impact on the LCOH will be assessed.
In summary, this thesis presents a techno-economic analysis and comparative study of various
infrastructure configurations for a hybrid offshore wind-to-hydrogen system. Given the inclu-
sion of the hybrid cable, emphasis will be placed on the modeling and analysis of different
transmission systems. Additionally, a reliability study is performed to evaluate their resilience.
The main economic indicator that will be used for comparison is the Levelized Cost of Hydro-
gen (LCOH, €/Kg-H2), although other parameters such as the Levelized Cost of Electricity
(LCOE, €/MWh) and the transmission costs (€/MWh) will also be analyzed.
The main question answered in this study is:
What is the techno-economic optimal infrastructure configuration of a hybrid offshore wind
farm?
In addition, the following sub-questions are addressed:
• What are the main system configurations of a hybrid offshore wind-to-hydrogen system?
• How do the different configurations impact the overall economic feasibility?
• What is the reliability of these configurations, and how does this affect their economic
viability?

Scenario description
Four scenarios were considered, each using a different transmission system of energy from the
offshore wind farm to the shore. In each scenario, 100% of the electricity is ultimately converted
into hydrogen, with the intention of making them comparable. However, the scenarios differ in
terms of where the electrolyzer is located (offshore vs onshore), what infrastructure is used to
transport the energy to shore (electric cable, hydrogen pipeline, and hybrid systems), and the
output flexibility that each infrastructure presents. While the hydrogen output is identical, the
techno-economic implications differ significantly.

lines.

3.9
Assumptions and limitations
The model was based on several assumptions that need to be considered. Firstly, the elec-
trolyzer was assumed to run on pure water.
Since in the Hydrogen Pipeline scenario the
electrolyzer is located offshore, this implies that pure water would have to be transported to
the offshore platform, which would significantly impact costs. A solution to mitigate this would
be to incorporate a desalinator in the system, but in turn it would carry an additional set of
considerations. For example, the percentage of purity of the water would have an effect on the
eﬀiciency of the electrolyzer, and the desalinator costs would also have to be modeled. This
was considered to be outside the scope of this study.
In addition, all costs were plotted for a range of 5 km to 240 km, in order to align with the
data range of the Xiang export cable model. The HVAC line costs were also fitted to a curve
to have a smoother interpolation of the values along the distance range.

5
Results
Before interpreting the results it is important to note that, although in all cases the output of
the wind farm is hydrogen, the transmission systems carry the energy onshore in different ways.
The Hydrogen Pipeline transports all the energy as hydrogen, meaning that there is no option
to use the energy as electricity onshore (unless it is converted back to electricity, which is not
considered in this scenario). On the other hand, the HVAC+Pipeline, HVDC+Pipeline and
Hybrid Cable scenarios allow for energy to be delivered onshore as both electricity and hydrogen
before it is ultimately converted into hydrogen by the electrolyzer. Therefore, each transmission
system offers a different level of flexibility in how the wind farm’s energy is ultimately utilized.
For these reasons, the results were analyzed from two perspectives. First, aiming to find the
optimal transmission system considering the lowest values for the transmission costs, LCOE
and LCOH. Second, aiming to find the optimal transmission system that offers the flexibility
to have both electricity and hydrogen onshore.
5.1

The CAPEX and OPEX of the wind farm were calculated for each scenario, as shown in
Figure 10. Transmission costs were the main factor increasing the overall costs with distance,
with the Hydrogen Pipeline being the most economical system. The reasons are discussed in

5.2
Transmission costs
The total transmission costs for the HVAC and HVDC lines, Hydrogen Pipeline, HVAC+Pipeline
and HVDC+Pipeline systems and Hybrid Cable have been plotted in Figure 11, normalized in

Figure 11: Transmission costs (HVAC, HVDC, Hydrogen Pipeline and Hybrid Cable)
The HVAC line was the most economical system for shorter distances due to its significantly
lower terminal costs compared to the HVDC, as seen in the cost breakdown of Figure 13.
However, the route costs escalate significantly because AC cables require more conductors and
suffer additional losses from the reactive component of current, skin-effect and induced currents
in the cable sheath and armor [31]. The HVAC line becomes uneconomical after a distance of
110 km. In contrast, the HVDC system has higher initial terminal costs due to the offshore
and onshore converter stations, but becomes more economical for longer distances. The HVDC
route costs are lower, and the overall costs scale more gently with length.
It is also important to note that the break even distance of 110 km between the HVAC and
HVDC lines is slightly higher than the average break even distance referenced in other studies,
around 80 km, such as in ([13]) and ([14]). This may be caused by the empirical nature of the
Xiang model. Nevertheless, their study is validated by other wind farms. The HVAC system is
validated against the 0.3 GW Capri–Torre Annunziata Interconnector in Italy and the 0.4 GW
Kriegers Flak Combined Grid Interconnector between Denmark and Germany. The HVDC
system is validated against the 1.0 GW ElecLink Interconnector between UK and France, the
0.7 GW Kontek2 Interconnector between Denmark and Germany and the 0.6 GW ELMED
Interconnector between Italy and Tunisia. In both cases, their results are considered to be
acceptable, and therefore the results of the model of this study are also considered to be valid.
The Hydrogen Pipeline becomes the most economical transmission system after a break even
distance of 73 km with the HVAC line. However, is is important to consider that the analysis
is limited to a range up to 240 km. For longer distances, the pipeline costs eventually surpass
the HVDC cable, as seen in the Dinh model in which this study is based, given the increased
costs of the compression stations, which are placed every 250 km.

Reliability study
As it has been previously mentioned, there is a lack of research regarding the reliability of hybrid
offshore wind farms for hydrogen production. This is an important aspect to consider because,
when a system failure occurs, repair times have a direct impact on the total operating hours and,
therefore, on the LCOH, (21). To complement the results of the previous section, a reliability
study was performed in order to assess the impact of failure of the different transmission systems
in the LCOH. As seen in Table 20, two parameters were assigned for each system: failure rate
and mean time to repair (MTTR) (Table 20).
The HVDC line parameters were obtained from the report produced by ENTSO-E et al. (2019)
[32], with a failure rate of 0.1 trips/100km·year (or 0.001 failures / km·year) and an MTTR
of 60 days (1440 h). The HVAC line parameters were obtained from Warnock et al. (2019) [19],
with a failure rate of 0.000705 failures/km·year, and from Ofgem (2012) [20], with an MTTR
of 65 days (1560 h).
For the subsea hydrogen pipeline, there is a lack of published real-world data. However, the
study by De Stefani et al. (2010) [21] performs an analysis on the failure frequencies for different
oil and gas pipeline models. The EGIG gas pipeline database was used as reference, since it’s the
one with the longest data span of that study, with a failure rate of 0.00037 failures/km·year.
There is also a lack of data on repair times for both hydrogen pipelines and generic gas pipelines.
For this reason, the MTTR was assumed to be the same as the HVAC line of 65 days.
For the combined systems of HVAC+Pipeline and HVDC+Pipeline, it was considered that the
electricity and hydrogen conductors are independent, and that in case of failure they can operate
individually without affecting each other. Therefore, they were assigned the same failure rates
and MTTRs as the previous systems.
Regarding the hybrid cable, there is also no reliability data available given the novelty of
the technology.
Since both the electricity and hydrogen are transported through the same
conductor, a failure in one system implies that the other needs to stop. In case of failure,
neither system can work. For this reason, the parameters were conservatively estimated to be
the 200% of the HVAC line and hydrogen pipeline to account for its novelty.
Two failures modes were considered: failure in the electrical transmission system and failure in
the hydrogen transmission system.

parameters on the LCOH. Three input variables were varied independently within realistic
ranges, while keeping all the other constant. The break even distance between the hybrid cable
and the HVDC+Pipeline from Figure 17 was selected to perform the sensitivity plots system,
at 128 km.

which are the number permitted by the IEA37Site code. As seen in 26, the LCOH decreases as
the number of turbines increases. This is because the costs are distributed over a larger amount
of energy production, decreasing the unit cost of hydrogen. The effect is more pronounced at
lower turbine numbers, such as the jump between 9 to 16 turbines. However, the rate of LCOH
reduction diminishes as the number of turbines increases. This highlights that beyond a certain
point, adding more turbines offers little economic benefit.

8
Conclusion
This thesis presented a techno-economic assessment of infrastructure configurations for con-
verting offshore wind energy to hydrogen. A Python model was developed integrating the main
infrastructure elements, including the wind turbines, foundations, energy transmission systems
and electrolyzers. A UI was developed to allow researchers to systematically vary the inputs
and test different configurations. In addition, the techno-economic performance of the Hybrid
Cable was evaluated in comparison with conventional transmission alternatives. A reliability
study was performed considering the failure rates and MTTRs for the different transmission
systems and their impact on the LCOH was assessed.
8.1
Research questions
The study answered the original research questions:
• What are the main system configurations of a hybrid offshore wind-to-hydrogen system?
The main system configurations were dictated by the energy transmission systems, includ-
ing the HVAC and HVDC lines, Hydrogen Pipeline, HVAC+Pipeline and HVDC+Pipeline
systems, and the Hybrid Cable. Each system introduced a different configuration in the
wind farm, such as the location and number of the electrolyzers. Each transmission sys-
tem also introduced different levels of flexibility in their final output regarding hydrogen
and electricity.
• How do the different configurations impact the overall economic feasibility?
The economic performance of each transmission system varied depending on the indicator
considered, with the costs increasing at different rates with distance. For the transmission
costs, the Hybrid Cable was the most economical system up to 198 km, followed by the
HVDC+Pipeline system. Considering the LCOE, the HVAC+Pipeline system was most
cost-effective until 110 km, followed by the HVDC + Pipeline system. When assessing
the LCOH, the Hybrid Cable proved most economical up to 128 km, again followed by
the HVDC + Pipeline system.
However, the flexibility to deliver both hydrogen and electricity was also taken into ac-
count. Under this criterion, the hybrid cable remained the most economical option up to
128 km, followed by the HVDC + Pipeline system.
• What is the reliability of these configurations, and how does this affect their economic
viability?
In case of hydrogen system failure, the Hydrogen Pipeline was the most cost-effective for
all distances. However, in the flexibility case, the Hybrid Cable was the most economic
choice up to 112 km, followed by the HVDC+Pipeline system.
In the event of electrical failure, the Hydrogen Pipeline was again the most optimal
solution for all distances. However, for the flexibility case the most economical option
was the Hybrid Cable up to 45 km, followed by the HVAC+Pipeline up to 107 km, and
then the HVDC + Pipeline system for longer distances.
• The main question was also answered:
What is the techno-economic optimal infrastructure configuration of a hybrid offshore
wind farm?
The Hydrogen Pipeline consistently showed the lowest LCOH in all cases, but the Hybrid
Cable offered the most flexible option to supply both electricity and hydrogen, particularly
44
==PAGE:45==
for shorter distances. Beyond these thresholds, HVDC+Pipeline configurations became
more cost-effective.
However, this thesis demonstrates that no single transmission system is universally op-
timal. Instead, the choice depends on many different aspects of the project, such as the
distance to shore, installed capacity, and desired flexibility in the output.
8.2
Future research
In Section 3.9 several assumptions were made that could be addressed in future research. The
electrolyzer was assumed to run on pure water, which could severely alter the results by adding
extra costs to the Hydrogen Pipeline configuration. The use of a desalinator could be considered,
but this would introduce further study paths, such as the effect of the purity of water in the
electrolyzer eﬀiciency and modeling the desalinator costs.
In addition, as was previously stated, the model takes approximately 3 minutes to compute
the costs for a wind farm of 64 turbines. This can hinder the evaluation of the wind farm
if multiple input parameters are to be tested. Therefore, an optimization of the code could
greatly improve the speed and the usefulness of the UI.
