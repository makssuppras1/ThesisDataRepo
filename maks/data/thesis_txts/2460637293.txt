Abstract
Honeycomb snadwich structures are widely used in aircraft application due to its lightweight and
good mechanical properteis. These structures are subjected to ground air ground pressure(GAG) cy-
cles throughtout its service time. However if the sandwich structure has disbond in core then this
GAG loading intensiﬁes the disbond propagation and therefore reduces the reliability of the struc-
ture. In this project an experimental approach to analyse the disbond propogation has been carried.
The project consists for designing and developing a inplane loading system so as to apply combined
GAG and inplane loading and observe the effect on disbond propagation. The combined loading was
carried ﬁrst time on the GAG test rig and with about 8000 cycles of combined loading carried during
this , it can be used as reference point for carrying future testing and improving test results further.

Introduction
Sandwich composite structures are important component in weight critical applications like aircraft,
high performance automobile, ships and wind turbines. A typical sandwich structure comprises of a low
stiffness core material and two high stiffness face sheet, see ﬁg.1. Because of its overall good mechanical
properties like high stiffness and strength-to-weight ratios and therefore are widely used as structural
components,especially within the aerospace industry. For aerospace applications the face sheets are
generally made of carbon ﬁber face sheets or a aluminum face sheet in combination with nomex or
metallic honeycomb cores. Nomex is considered at a good choice as it has high resistance to ﬁre. The
volume of honeycomb core materials typically consist of up to 95 percent enclosed air [1]. Because of
this sandwich panel is used in wings, rudders, cabins of aeroplane.
Figure 1: Sandwich panel made of epoxy honeycomb core and carbon face sheets [2]
Main advantages of a sandwich construction is it increases the bending rigidity or the ﬂexural rigidity.
As the size of core material is increased the face sheets are placed farther from the neutral axis which in-
creases the ﬂexural rigidity with minimum increase in mass. Because the young’s modulus and stiffness
properties for the face sheets is much higher as compared to core material all the normal and bend-
ing stress are taken up by the face sheets where as core carry shear stresses and separate and supports
faces against buckling and wrinkling. Although there are many advantages of sandwich panels for light
weight application there are also many drawbacks. For example sandwich panels may get damaged un-
der many different failure modes like face sheet wrinkling, core crushing, core shear failure, face sheet
and core separation called disbonding. In this study disbonding of face sheets and core is of interest and
so will be discussed in the next section. Disbonding occurs due to variety of reasons. One reason is the
2
==PAGE:7==
jump in stress in the sandwich panels through its thickness because of greater difference in the young’s
modules between the face sheet and core at the interface of the two material. As a result there is excess
shear stress at the interface which separates the face sheet away from the core and results in disbond-
ing. In aircraft application the disbond may occur due to other factors like in case of invented sandwich
panels the external factors like ambient pressure, humidity and temperature cause pressure difference
in the panel and develop stresses which results in disbonding.

Problem description
In 2005 airbus aircraft A310 experienced a failure in rudder. It can be seen in ﬁgure2. Similar incidents
have taken place in aerospace industry like in 1960s many spacecrafts had failure of sandwich struc-
tures [3]. Through investigations and many studies conducted to analyze and understand the cause and
nature of failure it was found that face sheet-core disbonding can result in damage propagation caused
by internal pressure changes in the core.
Figure 2: Rudder failure in Airbus A310 aircraft
At ground level the pressure inside the disbond cavity and outside is assumed as equal. This is because
of permeability due to which eventually equalization of pressure will take place [1]. When an aircraft as-
cends the ambient pressure decrease as it scales higher altitude. Consequenctly, when disbond is present
in an unvented the structure a pressure gradient is established between the entrapped air inside the dis-
bond cavity and the outside pressure of aircraft. As a result the sandwich panel bulges and so the disbond
propogates further. The phenomenon is explained in the ﬁgure 3. The red line denotes disbond inside
the sandwich panel, at ground level when pressure inside is equal to pressure outside no disbond takes
3

place. At altitude the pressure is not equal as shown in ﬁgure so the bulge is developed.
(a) Ground Level
(b) Crusing Altitude
Figure 3: Disbond damage due to GAG loads. [4]
In case of rudder of aircraft. The core and face-sheet propagation is not only the result of pressure differ-
ence but combination of aerodynamic forces (in plane loading) and the pressure difference.
2.1
Previous work on GAG test rig at DTU
After an agreement with Airbus, and associate professor Christian Bergreen from DTU the GAG test rig
was obtained in DTU structural lab. The ﬁrst project began in January 2017, it was carried by Søren
Bundgaard and was focused on designing, assembling and testing the GAG rig. Many components miss-
ing were designed during this project. Later on in 2018, work was carried to redesign ﬁxtures and test so
as to make the rig compatible with a 400 × 400[mm] sandwich panels provided by Airbus. The original
rig was for testing much bigger specimen of 800 × 800[mm] sandwich panels. Because of this added
complexities much time of the project was spent on redesigning the test rig. When the rig was ﬁnally
ready few test were conducted using the vacuum pressure only. Only a pilot test using combined loading
was conducted as a proof of concept but some failure in system caused the test to be cancelled.
As a part of Phd work of Arash Farsidi, a detailed numerical modelling was carried to develop disbond
fracture analysis tool so as to predict fracture and crack propagation for different loading and geome-
tries of disbond [5]. In their study unvented honeycomb core sandwich panels were subjected to both
ground air ground and in plane loading. The numerical results were used to validate experimental re-
sults from another study. The results of the numerical model were synchronous with the experimental
model. However due to some issues with the in-plane loading experimental facility in the test rig at DTU
the test could not be carried, however the GAG loading was running successfully.
Despite the successful completion of all previous works on the rigs, the rig had not been tested to its
full capacity. Therefore objective of this project is to understand current issues with the test rig, taking

necessary steps to rectify the issues which had occurred previously while testing and then to carry test
and validate the test results with the results from the Ph.d study carried by Arash Farsidi.

3.1
GAG test rig overview
The test rig is as shown in the ﬁgure 4. The rig consists of a red vacuum chamber inside which the spec-
imen in white can be seen. The vacuum pump attached to the chamber creates the low pressure in the
chamber simulating the high altitude of the aircraft and after predetermined cycle time the pressure in
chamber reaches the ground level pressure thereby completing a cycle of GAG loading. The specimen is
loaded by the actuator which is pinned on the top to the frame. The actuator has a capacity of 100KN of
linear force. The actuator transfer the load via a shaft or stroke rod to the specimen. Thus the load acts
as a in-plane load on the specimen which depicts the aerodynamic fores on the panel.

400 × 400 mm can be tested. All the ﬁxtures like towers, shelf, guides, base plate, are therefore design

even a small alteration in size of specimen will lead to many changes in all the associated components of

2. Alternative method for applying inplane load:
For applying the inplane load to the specimen care has to be taken so that the load does not direct
act on the honeycomb core. The honeycomb cannot sustain direct inplane load due to low stiff-
ness. Due to this it is important to apply the load on the face sheet directly and therefore transfer
the load along the length of specimen. For this in previous projects a wooden block was manu-
factured. The wooden block as shown in ﬁgure 6. According to the dimensions of wooden block
equivalent honeycomb was machined out. After machining the honeycomb the wooden block was
inserted in its place with slight clearance between the block and the core material so that no load
directly acts on the core. The block is glued to the inside walls of facesheet. The complete assembly
is shown in ﬁgure 6a.
(a) Wooden block between facesheets
(b) Damage due to failure of adhesive
Figure 6: Wooden insert between facesheets
When inplane load was applied on this specimen the wooden block slipped due to failure of adhe-
sive layer. Figure 6b shows how the failure took place. Due to this the wooden block damage the
8
==PAGE:13==
core instead of tranfering the inplane load via shear into the facesheets. The failure of transfering
load due to this method can be attributed to insufﬁcient bond layer between the the facesheet and
the wooden block. For this the machining of the core must be done with high precision in the area
where wooden block is to be inserted i.e the inside surface of facesheet must have no trace of the
honeycomb material.
Due to complexity in removing all the residues of honeycomb material it was decided to ﬁnd an
alternative approach to transfer the load along the facesheets. One approach is developed in this
project which is described in detail in later sections. Since the failure of the wooden block was
primarily due to adhesive layer failure hence it becomes important to understand the behaviour of
the adhesive in transfer of load due to shear. Hence a numerical and experimental study has been
carried in this project to design the joint and determine its strength.

Load Cell calibration
After the load cell was selected and required adapters were manufactured the load cell had to be cali-
brated before assembling into the test rig. calibration is necessary before start using any loadcell. This is
because loadcell are subjected to deterioration over a period of time due to many reasons like continu-
ous usage, mishandling, aging. Therefore calibration has to be carried to check whether the load values
are within the acceptable range of deviation.
For the purpose of calibration the load cell used was a 250KN calibration load cell. The maximum load
case for current test is around 40 KN and so it was necessary to use a calibration loadcell with higher load
capacity. For calibration the reference load cell and the loadcell to be calibrated are held together using a
short threaded rod. Both loadcells are gripped on the opposite hydraulic grips and the spacing between
the two loadcells is kept as small as possible to prevent buckling due to compression loading.
The accuracy of loadcell is different for various application. For most application an error value less than
±0.5 is considered to be acceptable. The plot below (ﬁg11) shows the error between the input value and
the output from the loadcell after ﬁnal calibration. It can be seen that for all the input value the desired
range of acceptable error.

height block was manufactured to the bellow remains within its stretchable limits. The drawing for the
height block can be found in appendix F . For assembling the height block an oval ring gasket has to be
ﬁtted between the stroke guide and the height block. The spring bellow can be screwed on top of the

Since the requirement for the material was that it should sustain the loading for the required number of

the available materials were ABS plastic and PLA plastic.
PLA or polylactic acid is a bioplastic, it is hard, strong with low thermal expansion properties. Whereas
ABS or Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene is tough and a bit ﬂexible material. Although both these materials
are similar and one can be preferred over the other depending on the application. However following
parameters were crucial for the testing purpose.
• Compression strength: Based on strength tests conducted by makerbot [9]. The compressive

printed at higher temperature and so while cooling the edge warping is common.
Due to above two reasons PLA was choosen as more applicable material.

7.1
Optimal overlap length Calculation
The overlap length previously stated was based on average shear stress in which simply adjusting the
overlap length until the shear stress is obtained within the allowable shear stress. However, many studies
prove that the once a certain overlap length is reached increasing the length furhter does not decrease
the shear stress. Hence this method may give unconservative results.
In the adhesive lap joint under shear loading the load is not completely uniform and so the shear stress
distribution is actually a tub shaped curve as shown below. The middle region is the elastic region and
the end regions are the plastic regions. Ones the load is applied if the shear stress exceeds the elastic
limits, plastic zones form at the joint ends. If the overlap length is sufﬁcient the shear stress trough will
be a zero at the middle of joint. Failure of joint occurs when the shear strain at the joint exceeds the
maximum shear strain. [14]. So sufﬁcient overlap length is fundamental for joint strength. For short
overlap joints once the adhesive elastic shear strain is exceeded the joint will fully became plastic and
thus it will be weaker. So the design a optimal overlap length load capacity method by Hart smith is
used. In this method the plastic zone are designed to carry entire load in addition to this elastic zone
allowances are added which gives sufﬁcient overall to carry the ultimate load and to ensure joint does
not fail. [15] [14]

to be cleaned with suitable solvent like isopropanol, acetone.
• Abrading: Abraded surface removes surface roughness and gives smooth and polished surface.

degreasing to remove all the dust from abrading.
• Application: As the surface is ready the glue must be applied on it quickly as the surface might get
contaminated again. The epoxy must be spread evenly on the given area as uneven thickness can
cause voids.
• Clamping: Ones the adhesive has been applied and the two adherents are put together the bonded
must be clamped so that some uniform force is maintained on the bonded area which results in
uniform bonding.
• Curing: The specimen is left for 20hrs at 23◦i.e the room temperature which is sufﬁcient to get a
strong bond.

DIC: setup and calibration
For measuring the outof plane deformation in the specimen a Aramis DIC system was used. Aramis is
non-contact 3d measuring system. It identiﬁes 3d points on the surface under inspection and tracks its
change in coordinated over a period of time. Therefore it is necessary that to track any specimen it must
have a pattern on it which DIC system can inspect. Depending on application pattern can be stocastic
pattern or reference patter. For this experiment Stochastic patterns are used because they are suitable to
measure deformations. Pattern are made on the face of the specimen which had disbond under it.
a
Setup
: For acquiring the digital image acquisition aramis 6M-Kowa 12.5 system was selected. For calibration
camera position and angles needs adjustment to ﬁt the measuring volume. The viewport of the chamber
is 350 × 350 mm . Therefore the measuring volume had to be limited to this value. From the chart for
6M system measuring volume was selected as (300×240)mm. Based on the chart the required measuring
distance is 368mm, slider distance of 124mm, camera lens lift of 0.49mm. Ones the distance is set to get
better resolution from camera the focus is adjusted till required quality of image is visible. The measur-
ing distance and camera(slider) distance was also slightly adjusted to get the best possible image .The
camera angle are already preset at 25◦and only changed for some speciﬁc applications.

1005mbar and so the vacuum chamber value was set at 180mbar.

Conclusion and Discussion
Based on the results obtained from the tests it was clear that the disbond propagation was not initialed.
The main objective of this project was to carry experimental analysis using combined load to see the
disbond propogate. Unfortunately the experiments weren’t that successful. From these experimental
results the model given by Arash were to be veriﬁed. According to Arash model at 40KN load and 1000
cycles disbond should have been propagated however in experiments it doesn’t seem to be the case. This
could be because the energy release rate in the modelwere minuted to about 60% of the critical ERR.
Despite the results from experiments the project have been a complete learning experience. Many de-
sign challenges were faced during the initial days of the project to make the rig functional to perform
GAG cycles.
Through project an introduction to previously lesser know concepts like science behind adhesive bond-
ing, additive manufacturing was obtained. Moreover, the biggest learning was planning and scheduling
and being prepared for unexpected event when working at a workshop or lab. Such a workshop expere-
ince in very crucial to gain in terms of future role as a engineer in similar situations in factories.
