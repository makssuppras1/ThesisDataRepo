Abstract
The oﬀshore wind turbine industry has been growing continuously since the ﬁrst wind farm was
installed back in 1991. A widely-used support structure for the wind turbines is the cost-eﬃcient
monopile, which is a simple pile driven into the seabed. However, an unprotected monopile is ex-
posed to the process of scour which can result in a large hole surrounding the pile and undermining
its structural integrity. To avoid the removal of the surrounding sediment from the seabed, scour
protection layers are placed around the monopile. These layers consist of large armour layer stones
and smaller ﬁlter layer stones, which are designed to protect the seabed from the ﬂow environment
and to prevent scour. However, observations of unexpected sinking of the scour protection layers
into the bed have been made from surveys. While many studies exist on the general stability of
the scour protection stones, the process leading to sinking of the stones is not as well understood.
Numerical modelling of the ﬂow dynamics leading to the sinking of the stones is a preferable al-
ternative to costly model tests, for improving the understanding of the processes leading to the
sinking.
This thesis will evaluate the applicability of numerical modelling of the ﬂow in scour protection
layers by use of the open source CFD software OpenFOAM. The scour protection will be modelled
as a porous medium, where the resistance eﬀect of the stones is modelled without resolving the
actual stone geometry.
The numerical model will ﬁrst be veriﬁed against known solutions for
simple ﬂows, and then validated by comparison of experimental and numerical results in the porous
medium, both under steady current and oscillatory ﬂow conditions. The sediment transport inside
the porous medium will be modelled using a classical sediment transport approach based on the
bed shear stresses.
It is found that in order to model the ﬂow inside the porous medium well, it is necessary to model
the medium with a gradual transition in porosity towards a value of one at the interface with the
free ﬂow. Application of a laminar ﬂow model will underestimate the bed shear stresses in the
porous medium, even though the eﬀect of turbulence is included in the modelled porous resistance.
It is therefore found necessary to apply a turbulence model in order to predict sediment transport
inside the medium. The standard k-ω-SST turbulence model is used with no modiﬁcations for
the porous ﬂow, and is found to overestimate the generation of turbulence kinetic energy, but still
provide a reasonable estimate of the bed shear stresses and the ﬂow inside the porous medium.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Oﬀshore wind turbines are becoming more and more popular with the increasing focus on sustain-
able energy production. Oﬀshore wind farms consist of a large number of wind turbines installed
together in an oﬀshore ﬁeld. Vindeby was the ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm and was installed in 1991
with a capacity of approximately 5 MW (4C Oﬀshore, 2015). It is located 1.5 to 3.0 km oﬀshore
in the Great Belt (Denmark), and was the ﬁrst of many wind farms to be installed globally in
the following years. Europe has since then remained the world leader in the development of wind
farms, and as of January 2014 the total capacity of oﬀshore wind farms in Europe has reached
6.562 MW (The European Wind Energy Association, 2014).
The oﬀshore wind turbines can be supported by an array of support structures such as gravity
based foundations, jacket structures and monopiles amongst others. The most commonly used
support structure is monopiles, as they are easy to install and cost-eﬃcient, and they can be used
in water depths of up to 30 meters. The monopiles range in diameters up to 6 meters, and are
hammered or drilled into the seabed to oﬀer vertical and lateral support for the wind turbine.
Unprotected monopiles are exposed to the process of scour, where the sediment surrounding the
pile is transported away from the pile by the ﬂow and a scour hole emerges around the pile. This
can undermine the structural integrity of the structure, and the scour process around unprotected
piles has therefore been studied extensively in the past, see e.g. Breusers and Raudkivi (1991) and
Fredsøe and Sumer (2002).
To protect the monopiles from scour, stone layers called scour protection layers are placed around
the pile. These often consist of both a ﬁlter layer of medium sized stones and an armour layer
of larger stones. The armour layer ensures stability under storm conditions, while the ﬁlter layer
should prevent the ﬁne sediment bed to be winnowed out of the scour protection in between the
large armour stones. However, unexpectedly large sinking of the scour protection stones into the
sediment bed have been observed and reported by Hansen et al. (2007) at the large wind farm Horns
Rev 1. Studies by e.g. De Vos (2008) and Lauchlan and Melville (2001) exist on the stability of
the stones in the scour protection, and studies on the sinking of scour protection around bridge
piers have been done by Chiew (2002) and Lauchlan and Melville (2000). These studies covered
empirical predictions on the failure mode of scour protections based on sinking and disintegration,
but did not assess the magnitude of the sinking.
The sinking of the stones can according to Nielsen (2011) be caused by either (1) damage to the
armour layer, (2) winnowing of the ﬁlter layer through the armor layer, or (3) removal of the base
sediment through the ﬁlter and armour layers. Hansen et al. (2007) showed that the the ﬁrst two
options were unlikely, while the removal of the sediment bed is possible. To describe this process, it
is necessary to have a detailed understanding of the ﬂow inside the scour protection layers to predict
the sediment transport governed by the ﬂow and bed shear stresses. This motivated experimental
investigations of the detailed ﬂow inside scour protection layers around a monopile in a steady
current by Nielsen (2011) and corresponding numerical modelling by Nielsen et al. (2013). The
measured ﬂow inside the scour protection was compared to results from numerical models by 3D
computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) simulations using the commercial CFD software FLOW-3D.
The scour protection was modelled both as spherical stones and as a homogeneous porous medium
without resolving the stone geometry. The results using the porous medium approach were not

good, as the measured velocity gradients of the ﬂow inside the scour protection were not captured
by the numerical model. The results were somewhat better when resolving the stone geometry as

This motivates the present work, which deals with numerical modelling of ﬂow and sediment

1.1
Objective of thesis
The aim of this thesis is to develop a numerical model which can describe the ﬂow within scour
protection layers accurately, and which can predict the bed shear stresses and relate this to sediment
transport inside the scour protection.
It is not within the scope of the thesis to predict the
quantitative sinking of the scour protection, but rather provide an accurate tool for predicting the
onset of sinking. The sediment bed will therefore be considered stationary, and the onset of sinking
will be related to mobilisation of the sediment bed by a sediment transport model. This will be
limited to a passive model where the sediment concentration doesn’t inﬂuence the ﬂow ﬁeld. The
scour protection will be modelled as a porous medium, since this simple approach is by far the
easiest one to use for engineering applications.
1.2
Methodology
The numerical modelling of ﬂow inside porous media can be approached either by commercial
software which is capable of solving ﬂow in porous media, or by use of open source software. While
commercial software is easier to get into and can produce quick results, the price is paid by the
closed source principle, where it can be diﬃcult to assess exactly which equations are solved to
produce the solution. Open source software is by deﬁnition much more ﬂexible and allows for
modiﬁcations of the code to implement new approaches in solving the problem, making it ideal
for research purposes. The previous attempts by Nielsen et al. (2013) at modelling the ﬂow in
porous media by the commercial software FLOW-3D have not proven to be satisfactory compared
to experimental results. Therefore it is chosen to use the open source CFD toolbox OpenFOAM
as a basis for the numerical modelling in this thesis.
With the ultimate goal of improving the existing numerical results for ﬂow in scour protection layers
around a monopile, a series of steps will be taken to get to this goal. The developed numerical
model for porous ﬂow will ﬁrst be veriﬁed and validated for simple ﬂows. Veriﬁcation is understood
as a check of whether the equations are solved correctly, while validation is a check of whether the
correct equations are being solved. The veriﬁcation will therefore consist of a comparison between
numerical results and known analytical or empirical solutions for free ﬂows, to ensure that the
model developed in OpenFOAM can solve the governing equations correctly. The validation on
the other hand, will serve as a check of how well the numerical solution based on the chosen porous
ﬂow equations corresponds to reality, based on comparison with experimental measurements.

2.1
Scour around a monopile
Monopiles are used for oﬀshore structures such as oﬀshore wind turbines and are typically used in
shallow waters with depths of up to 30 meters. They are hammered into the seabed, and as the
seabed is the only support of the structure, it is crucial that it remains so. However, the presence
of the monopile will induce complex secondary ﬂows around it as depicted in Fig. 2.1, which lead
to an ampliﬁed bed shear stress and resulting sediment transport around the pile. This process is
called scour and will with time develop a scour hole surrounding the monopile which can vary in
depth depending on the ﬂow conditions. The scour depth for a pile in a steady current will tend
towards an equilibrium depth of 1.3 times the pile diameter, while the depth will decrease for a
wave dominated ﬂow environment (Fredsøe and Sumer, 2002). This is quite a signiﬁcant depth, as
the monopiles for oﬀshore wind turbines ranges up to 6 meters in diameter.
The scour hole will in the extreme case undermine the structural integrity of the structure, but even
if this is not the case, a scour hole will eﬀectively increase the free length of the monopile, which
will aﬀect its eigenfrequency. This might cause the eigenfrequency of the monopile to coincide with
the wave loading frequency which can also lead to failure.
Scour protection layers are installed around the monopile to protect the pile against these failure
modes, as will be elaborated in Section 2.1.2. First, the ﬂow mechanics of scour will be explained
in the following.

Scour under steady current
For an incoming steady current, several secondary ﬂows will form around the monopile as illustrated
by Fig. 2.1 which shows both an unprotected monopile, and a monopile with surrounding scour
protection stones. These processes were described in detail by Roulund et al. (2005) and will be
brieﬂy accounted for here.

There are three eﬀects to take into account in relation to scour:
1. A horseshoe vortex is formed in front of the monopile, caused by the rotation of the incoming
ﬂow due to the boundary layer. The ﬂow undergoes a separation along the separation line S
in Fig. 2.1a, and rolls up and around the monopile.
2. Vortices are formed in the lee-wake of the monopile.
3. The streamlines contract around the monopile, leading to higher velocities adjacent to its
sides.
There is also a downﬂow in front of the monopile, which is driven by the deceleration of the ﬂow,
and adds into the rotation of the horseshoe vortex. The horseshoe vortex was found to be the most
important feature with respect to the development of the scour hole and it is therefore important
that the numerical model can capture this feature well.
Scour under wave action
When the ﬂow environment is dominated by waves motion, the Keulegan-Carpenter number, KC

As the diameter of monopiles for oﬀshore wind turbines is in the range of 2.5m - 6m, the wave

vortex and lee-wake vortices. For example a cylinder with D = 4 m under waves with the period
T = 12s, will require U1m > 2.33 m/s to satisfy KC > 7. This can be met under storm conditions,
but the scour under waves will generally be drastically lowered compared to under steady current
conditions. Scour under waves is therefore mostly relevant for slender monopiles.
2.1.2
Flow in scour protection layers
In order to avoid structural failure due to the scour process, scour protection which consists of
ﬁlter stones and larger armour stones is dropped around the monopile. The purpose of the stones
is to disrupt the scour process by protecting the sediment bed from the increased bed shear stress
from the ﬂow patterns in Fig. 2.1.
However, unexpected sinking of the scour protection layers has been observed at the oﬀshore wind
farm Horns Rev I, 20 km oﬀthe Danish west coast in the North Sea. The wind farm was installed
in the summer of 2002 and the scour protection stones adjacent to the monopile were found to have
sunk up to 1.5m under a control survey in 2005, (Hansen et al., 2007). This is quite signiﬁcant
compared to the thickness of the scour protection layers, which consists of a 0.5 m ﬁlter layer and
two layers of armour stones ranging in diameter from 0.35 to 0.55 m.
While scour around unprotected monopiles has been studied extensively, this unwanted sinking of
the armour stones is not as well understood. The goal is to understand this process and being
able to model it. This includes being able to model the ﬂow ﬁeld correctly and understanding
the mechanics of the sediment removal. The existing literature relevant to this understanding is
outlined in the following.

2.1.3
Modelling ﬂow in scour protection layers
There are two approaches to modelling ﬂow in scour protection layers:
1. Resolving the actual stone and pore geometry and solving the general ﬂow equations as
presented in Section 2.2 directly in the porous ﬂow. The ﬂow equations do not need to be
modiﬁed as they are only solved in the ﬂuid part of the scour protection layers. Standard
turbulence models can also be applied directly in the porous ﬂow.
2. Modelling the porous medium without resolving the actual geometry of the medium. To
introduce the eﬀect of the porosity into the ﬂow equations, they are averaged over the pore
volume and a resistance term is introduced to the momentum equation as in Section 2.3.
Turbulence models should also be be averaged over the pore volume to provide correct results.
The ﬁrst approach is more accurate as the actual ﬂow is modelled directly, but it is also much more
demanding - both in terms of computational resources and setting up the model mesh. It is most
suitable for ﬂow in between large stones as is the case for armour rocks in scour protection layers.
However, it is still necessary to have a representative amount of digital stones, i.e. stones which
have been scanned such that their 3D geometry can be modelled in a CFD simulation. It is also
necessary to arrange the stones in a natural manner by e.g. dumping them and using a physics
engine to determine the arrangement of the stones. The computational mesh of the model must
be very ﬁne in order to capture the exact stone geometry, if this approach is used.
Previous work by Nielsen et al. (2013) involved numerical modelling of porous ﬂow in scour pro-
tection layers using the commercial software FLOW-3D. The scour protection was modelled in

1. A convective term for the spatial velocity variations within the averaging volume. This can
be seen as a spatial analogue to the Reynolds stresses.
2. Pressure induced drag force acting on the porous media solids.
3. Frictional drag induced by viscous forces acting on the porous media solids.
The resistance is thus caused by turbulence as well as friction and form drag from the porous
media solids. Since the equation is derived from volume averaging, the spatial variations within
the pores cannot be modelled directly, and the eﬀects listed above are instead described by a single
resistance term Fi. Modelling of the resistance term Fi is elaborated in the next section.

2.4
Turbulence modelling
A solution of the Navier-Stokes equation Eq. (2.6) will in principle completely describe the ﬂow
including turbulent characteristics. For laminar ﬂows this equation is therefore solved directly as
there is no need to resolve any turbulent eddies. However, for a turbulent ﬂow this requires that
all scales of the turbulent eddies are resolved by the model mesh, which is not feasible due to the
immense computational demand. Since most engineering problems involve turbulent ﬂows, and
often in large scale, it is necessary to ﬁnd a way to include the eﬀect of turbulence in the numerical
models, without having to resolve the scale of the smallest eddies.
Several classes of turbulence models have been developed with this purpose, and each approach has
its limitations and applications. The most widely used approaches can be covered by the following
classes:

CHAPTER 2. THEORY
1. Direct numerical simulation (DNS)
2. Large eddy simulation (LES) and detached eddy simulation (DES)
3. Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) based models
These classes will be described brieﬂy, followed by a more thorough explanation of the chosen
RANS based k-ω-SST turbulence model.
2.4.1
Direct numerical simulations
Direct numerical simulation is not a true turbulence model, since the Navier-Stokes equations are
solved directly for ui and p. Given that the model resolves the entire spatial and temporal range
of turbulence, the Navier-Stokes equations will give the exact solution including ﬂuctuations from
turbulence. The mesh must be ﬁne enough to capture the smallest dissipative scale of eddies de-
scribed by the Kolmogorov length scale, and large enough to capture the largest energy containing
eddies. The time steps must also be small enough to resolve the time scale of the smallest eddies.
This sets very strict requirements on both the spatial and temporal resolution of the model. The
spatial requirements for the number of mesh points N 3 in a 3D mesh can be shown to grow
approximately with Re2.25, while the temporal requirements for the number of time steps can be
shown to grow with approximately Re0.75. The total number of operations for a simulation can
therefore be estimated to grow exponentially with Re3 (Rodi, 1992), which limits the use of DNS
for very small scale problems.

2.4.5
Turbulence in porous media
The turbulence inside porous media is important both with respect to the resistance it provides to
the mean ﬂow through energy losses, and with respect to the eddy viscosity which contributes to
larger bed shear stresses by the Boussinesq assumption as in Eq. (2.36). In reality, the turbulence
in scour protection layers is created by the geometrical disturbance which the stones provide to
the ﬂow. This extracts energy from the mean ﬂow, both due to the drag on the stones, the friction
on the stones, and the energy losses from the turbulence generated in the pores.
This thesis applies the standard k-ω-SST turbulence model for the porous ﬂow, without any
modiﬁcations as was done for the pore-averaged momentum and continuity equations. It will be
investigated in Chapter 5 whether this provides meaningful results, by comparison with measured
turbulence quantities inside stone covers. The generation of turbulence in the numerical model is
not caused by the geometrical disturbance from the stones, as they are not felt by the ﬂow. Only
a mean resistance is modelled, and the turbulence is therefore only caused by the shearing eﬀect
that the resistance has on the ﬂow, especially near the interface between the porous medium and
free ﬂow.
The resistance on the ﬂow introduced by the extended Darcy-Forchheimer equation (2.12) is cal-
ibrated based on empirical data for turbulent ﬂows. The eﬀect of turbulence on the resistance in
the porous medium should therefore already be included in the resistance term in the momentum
equation. The application of a turbulence model could therefore be reasoned to introduce an ex-
cess amount of turbulent resistance. It will therefore be investigated whether this eﬀect can be
neglected or must be taken into account, by comparison of turbulence and laminar model results
in Chapter 5.

Chapter 3
Numerical modelling
This chapter will ﬁrst explain the basic numerical principles underlying general computational ﬂuid
dynamics (CFD) software. The open source CFD software package OpenFOAM will be used for
the numerical work in this thesis, and the general functionality of OpenFOAM will therefore be
outlined. Modiﬁcations of the standard OpenFOAM tools have been implemented to handle ﬂow
in porous media according to the theory described in Section 2.3. The implementation of these
modiﬁcations will be also be addressed, but this chapter will not provide an in-depth explanation
of OpenFOAM, as it is quite a comprehensive CFD software package.
3.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics
CFD software is often used to solve partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs) because these equations
can be so complicated that no known analytical solutions exist.
One example is the Navier-
Stokes equation, also referred to as the momentum equation, which is coupled with the continuity
equation and solved for the velocity and pressure ﬁelds. As the Navier-Stokes equations have no
known analytical solution, numerical modelling is used to obtain an approximated solution at a
ﬁnite number of points which are deﬁned by a mesh. The ﬁrst step is therefore to discretise the
domain of interest, by creating a computational mesh inside it. The next step is to discretise the
equations in each cell of the mesh, and by using numerical schemes to do so, a linear system of
equations can be formed and solved for the numerical solution.

PISO Algorithm
The PISO algorithm is developed for unsteady ﬂow problems. A guessed pressure ﬁeld is ﬁrst used
to determine the velocity ﬁeld from the discretised momentum equations. The pressure equations
are then solved based on the estimated velocity ﬁeld, and a the diﬀerence between the new pressure
solution and the initial guess is used to further correct the results by iteration. The algorithm can
be broken into the following steps:
1. The momentum equation is solved for ui based on the pressure ﬁeld p from the previous time
step, or the initial condition if t = 0. This gives an approximation of the new velocity ﬁeld.
2. A new pressure ﬁeld is calculated based on the approximate solution of ui by a pressure-
velocity coupling equation.
3. A new velocity ﬁeld is calculated, such that it satisﬁes continuity based on the new pressure
ﬁeld.
4. Steps 2 and 3 are repeated until the error is below a given tolerance.
The reason that
iterations are necessary, is that even though the velocity ﬁeld is corrected in each cell based
on the new values of p, the correction of all the neighbouring velocities will give rise to a
second order error, which is handled by iteration.

OpenFOAM
OpenFOAM is a toolbox of numerical solvers and utilities, where the user can freely modify existing

3.2.2
Building a solver for ﬂow in porous media
The problems solved in this study involves both steady state and transient incompressible ﬂows.
The free surface will not be modelled, so a multi-phase solver is not necessary. The standard
OpenFOAM solver pimpleFoam is chosen as the basis for implementation of the porous media ﬂow
equations. pimpleFoam solves the general momentum equations and is intended for incompressible,
single-phase, transient ﬂows. It can therefore also be used to obtain steady state solutions by
ensuring the simulation time is long enough for temporal convergence. It is based on the PIMPLE
solution algorithm, which is a combination of the PISO algorithm as explained in Section 3.1.3 and
the steady state SIMPLE algorithm. The diﬀerence between the PIMPLE and PISO algorithm is
that while PISO solves the momentum equations once each time step, PIMPLE performs one or
more recalculations of the momentum equations, based on the corrected pressure and velocity ﬁelds
from the pressure-correction equations. However, in the present numerical models, the number of
corrections of the momentum equations is set to zero, such that the PIMPLE algorithm reduces
to the PISO algorithm.

In this chapter, the developed solver will be veriﬁed by comparison of numerical results with known

Validation of ﬂow in porous media
The developed solver for ﬂow in porous media has been veriﬁed for simple ﬂows in ducts and
channels. The next step is to investigate whether the theoretical framework for ﬂow in porous
media is actually correct from a physical standpoint. This is done by comparison of numerical
results with experimental results in the form of measured velocity proﬁles, turbulence energy levels
and bed shear stresses in porous media. The validation will be used to investigate whether the
use of a turbulence model together with the resistance coeﬃcients α = 500 and β = 2 introduces
too much resistance in the porous medium. The best way of modelling the transition between the
porous medium and outer ﬂow will also be clariﬁed based on the results, and ﬁnally the validity
of the porous medium approach is assessed with respect to modelling the bed shear stresses under
scour protection layers.
Experimental results by Sumer et al. (2001) will be used for validation of the model results for
ﬂow in porous media under a steady current, while results by Stevanato et al. (2010) are used for
validation of the model results under oscillatory ﬂow conditions. These experimental results consist
of measurements of velocity proﬁles, turbulent ﬂuctuations and in the latter case, also the phase
shift between the porous ﬂow and outer ﬂow. These experiments are conducted in experimental
set-ups with no signiﬁcant three dimensional eﬀects, and the numerical models will therefore be
set up as 2D models.
5.1
Flow in stone cover under oscillatory ﬂow
The basis of validation for oscillatory ﬂows is the experimental results by Stevanato et al. (2010)
for ﬂow in stone layers in an oscillatory u-tube environment. Measurements of the velocity and
turbulence energy proﬁles inside the stone covers are performed for respectively one, two and three
layers of stones. The velocity proﬁles are based on the pore velocity ⟨u⟩f, and are phase- and
pore-averaged based on measurements from twenty diﬀerent pores. The model will be used to
compare results from laminar and turbulence models with the measurements from three layers of
stones.
The stones from the experiment have a mean diameter of D50 = 36 mm, and the grain size
distribution has a standard deviation of 9 mm. The stone cover will be modelled as a uniform
porous medium with D50 = 36 mm and a porosity of n = 0.40. Sensitivity analyses are carried out
with respect to the inﬂuence of the porosity on the pore velocities and bed shear stresses beneath
the porous medium in Section 5.1.4 and 5.1.6 respectively.

Notes on laminar modelling
The laminar models have been found quite unstable when applying the second order accurate linear
upwind or central diﬀerencing numerical schemes for the divergence of the convective term in the
momentum equation (2.10). The instability is most likely caused by the fact that turbulence is
resolved directly by the laminar model when applying the higher order schemes. The ﬁrst order
upwind scheme has an inherent large numerical diﬀusion which has a smoothening eﬀect on the
solution, forcing a laminar behaviour of the ﬂow. The turbulence resolved by the model with the
higher order schemes is created at the interface between the porous medium and the free ﬂow,
where the diﬀerence in resistance has a shearing eﬀect on the ﬂow. When the ﬂow reverses, this
results in vortices being generated, as shown in Fig. 5.2 where the grey area is the porous medium.
Figure 5.2: Shearing of ﬂow at interface with porous medium in laminar models using higher order schemes.
Since no turbulence model is applied, the turbulence captured by the laminar model does not
dissipate. This causes the velocity and bed shear stress signal to ﬂuctuate increasingly with time,
eventually leading to an unstable simulation. The generation of vortices is not unphysical, since
the ﬂow is turbulent in reality. But seeing this eﬀect in a laminar model is only showing half the
picture, as only the large scale turbulence is captured. It is therefore not advised to use a laminar
modelling approach to model the turbulent ﬂow, although the porous resistance coeﬃcients are
calibrated based on turbulent ﬂows. The laminar results using the upwind scheme will still be
compared to results with a turbulence model for a qualitative evaluation of the validity of laminar
modelling inside the porous medium.

experimental results as it did in the laminar model with a transition in porosity in Fig. 5.9. The
agreement in the constant-velocity layer is again best at the phase values with a favourable pressure
gradient, ωt = 45◦and ωt = 90◦. Near the interface, the match is much better for all the phase
values in Fig. 5.13, except at the ﬂow reversal. The velocity is generally underestimated near the
interface, which can be due to several eﬀects:
 The body force in the numerical models is calibrated to match the experimental U1m at the
middle of the free stream ﬂow. This might be the wrong location compared to the measured
value from the experiments (it is not stated where it was measured).
 The numerically computed outer ﬂow velocity proﬁle might not correspond to the physical
proﬁle from the experimental results. No experimental results are presented in the outer
ﬂow for calibration. For the steady current validation in Section 5.2, it is found that the
modelled velocity proﬁle is not as uniform in the outer ﬂow as it was in the experiments (see
Fig. 5.24b). The same eﬀect may reduce the near-porous medium free stream velocity in
this model.
 The turbulence generated in the k-ω-SST model near the interface will create too large
resistance in this region, as the resistance term in the momentum Eq. (2.10) is already based
on resistance coeﬃcients calibrated empirically for turbulent ﬂows.
The results in the constant-velocity layer are largely unaﬀected by the application of a turbulence
model. This means that the generated turbulence (see Section 5.1.7) does not provide too much
resistance, even though the porous resistance coeﬃcients α and β in Eq. (2.13) and (2.14) already
contain the turbulent contribution to the resistance. The generation of turbulence near the interface
is much larger as shown later, and results in a signiﬁcant additional contribution to the porous
resistance. This might explain why the velocities near the interface are slightly underestimated in
Fig. (5.13), while they tend to be overestimated in the constant-velocity layer.
It is apparent from the presented velocity proﬁles, that it is necessary to implement a variation of
the porosity towards the interface with the free ﬂow, in order to model the ﬂow inside the entire
porous medium well. This is an even more important parameter than the use of a turbulence
model, but the turbulence model will make sure the porous ﬂow is modelled better near the
interface. Exactly how the porosity variation is to be modelled can be regarded as a calibration
and data-ﬁtting exercise, which is not done in this work.

5.3
Discussion of results from validation
The validation of the numerical model has shown that in order to model the ﬂow in stone layers by
a porous medium approach, it is necessary to model the porous medium with a transition towards
n = 1 at the interface with the free ﬂow.
With the porous medium modelled with a transition in porosity, the pore velocities inside the
constant-velocity layer can be modelled successfully with both laminar and turbulent ﬂow models.
The velocities correspond well with experimental results when the pressure gradient is favourable
with respect to the ﬂow direction, while the phase shift is too quick inside the porous medium at ﬂow
reversal. In order to model the velocities well near the interface with the free ﬂow, it is necessary
to use a turbulence model to capture the momentum exchange observed from measurements, and
to predict the phase lag inside the porous medium well.
The standard k-ω-SST turbulence model has been applied without any modiﬁcations for the
porous ﬂow. It is found to overestimate the turbulence kinetic energy inside the porous medium
by a factor of two, but still results in good predictions of the velocities in the medium under an
oscillatory ﬂow. In this case, the chosen porous resistance coeﬃcients α = 500 and β = 2 in Eq.
(2.13) and (2.14) are still valid, although they are calibrated to include the eﬀect of turbulence
on the resistance. The additional resistance from the turbulence model does not aﬀect the pore
velocities signiﬁcantly.
The numerical results for the steady current case did not yield good results, although the validation
data is not as complete in this case, and the mismatch inside the porous medium can be attributed
to an unsatisfactory modelling of the free ﬂow. The experimental data was based on a single layer
of stones, and to model this as a porous medium is to stretch the deﬁnition of porosity, as it might
be more appropriate to regard the stone layer as a bed roughness. Further validation for porous
ﬂow under steady current should be carried out, and this will also be done in the case of 3D ﬂow
in scour protection layers around a monopile in Section 6.1.
73
==PAGE:87==
CHAPTER 5. VALIDATION OF FLOW IN POROUS MEDIA
The modelled bed shear stress is in the correct order of magnitude when using the k-ω-SST
turbulence model based on the ﬁlter velocity gradient. This is likely because the eddy viscosity is
overestimated at the bed while the velocity gradient is underestimated when it is based on the ﬁlter
velocity. These two eﬀects cancel each other out, and reasonable results are obtained. It should
however be investigated in further work, whether the results are improved when pore-averaging
the turbulence model and using the pore-velocity gradient at the bed, as the physical basis of the
results is then more sound. It is not appropriate to use a laminar ﬂow model, as the contribution
from the eddy viscosity to the bed shear stress is then neglected, and the bed shear stresses are
then underestimated signiﬁcantly.
The bed shear stress is found to be quite sensitive to the porosity of the medium because this
inﬂuences the both the velocities and gradients hereof at the bed. It is also sensitive to the mesh
resolution at the bed, however to a smaller degree. This is because the boundary layer which would
theoretically form at the bed inside the porous medium is not resolved before the wall-adjacent
cells are as small as y+ = 6, which is below the intended range for the implemented turbulence
wall functions and results in a wrong eddy viscosity. This means that the velocity gradient is not
fully resolved, and consequently the bed shear stress will be dependent on the mesh resolution.
This poses a problem, because the bed shear stresses are reduced signiﬁcantly inside the porous
medium compared to the free ﬂow. If the mesh is uniform at the bed beneath the free ﬂow and
porous medium respectively, the diﬀerence in bed shear stress give a large diﬀerence in the y+
c
value of the wall-adjacent cells at the two locations. The veriﬁcation showed that especially the
bed shear stress is sensitive to small deviations of the wall-adjacent cell y+
c values, and it will be
hard to predict the stresses correctly both in the free ﬂow and porous medium simultaneously. It
is therefore recommended to either improve the wall functions or resolve the viscous sublayer to
avoid this problem.
It can be concluded that a transition in the porosity of the medium should be implemented at the
interface with the free ﬂow, and a turbulence model should be used in order to model the ﬂow well.
The bed shear stresses are in the correct order of magnitude when using the standard k-ω-SST
turbulence model and the ﬁlter velocity gradient at the bed, but further work should be done to
pore-average the turbulence model and use the pore-velocity gradient.

6.3
Discussion of results
The experimental results from Nielsen et al. (2013) oﬀer a more complete basis of validation for
porous ﬂow in the steady current case.
The modelled velocity proﬁles show that the porous
resistance is too large. The horseshoe vortex is generated and enters the porous medium, but is
weakened very quickly such that the return ﬂow in the porous medium is much weaker in the model
than in the experiments. The developed porous ﬂow model does show a better depiction of the
velocity proﬁles inside the porous medium than the results from FLOW-3D, and can be seen as
an improvement. The only concern is the calibration of the resistance in order to get the correct
magnitude of pore velocities inside the medium, since the chosen resistance coeﬃcients showed
good results for the 2D oscillatory validation case. The pore velocities were also underestimated
in the 2D steady current case, and it therefore seems that the porous resistance is too large solely
under a steady current.
The results also show an acceptable agreement with experimental results with respect to the
turbulent kinetic energy inside the porous medium. It is generally slightly overestimated, as it
also was in the 2D steady current validation case. However, it is worth noting that the turbulence
varies a lot between the diﬀerent cross-sections in the experimental results. When the measured
86
==PAGE:100==

velocities are low, the turbulence is also very low, while it is not as sensitive to the local velocities
in the numerical model. This can be attributed to the local generation in between the stones in
reality, while the turbulence is created at the interface in the model, and is transported down into
the porous medium by diﬀusion and is not as dependent on the local velocities. This could be
improved in the model by introducing additional dissipation inside the porous medium. However,
this should not be done as an isolated exercise - the entire turbulence model should ﬁrst be corrected
for porous ﬂow by pore-averaging of the transport equations.
The simulation of the ﬂow in scour protection layers around a monopile in an oscillatory ﬂow
also captures the generation of the horseshoe vortex. The bed shear stress ﬁeld inside the scour
protection resembles experimental results for the bed shear stresses around a free cylinder. The
bed shear stresses are much larger outside the porous medium, which results in a large bed load
transport outside the porous medium and compared to inside it.
The preliminary suspended
sediment transport model shows that this results in suspension of sediment outside the scour
protection layers and transport into the scour protection. Once the sediment enters the scour
protection, resuspension is not captured by the model due to the low ﬂow velocities and bed shear
stresses. Although the sediment transport model is not fully developed, this can be used to point
towards the fact that a classical sediment transport model might not be the right method to use for
predicting sinking of the armour stones. The results based on this method only show a tendency
for sediment transport into the scour protection layers. The local turbulence inside the pores varies
a lot, and this might be a more important factor for suspension and transport of sediment out of
the scour protection, than the bed shear stresses.
Now that the numerical model has been set up in the present study, further calibration of the
model should be carried out for ﬂow in scour protection layers. This should be done to investigate
how well the horseshoe vortex can be modelled, and whether a combination of a current and wave
environment will result in larger bed shear stresses inside the porous medium. It is a requirement
that the porous medium model can show a local net sediment transport out of the scour protection
layers, in order for this approach to be valid to model the sinking of armour layer stones.

Chapter 7
Further work
The developed numerical model has shown that it is possible to model ﬂow inside scour protection
layers reasonably well by the simple porous medium approach. In order to match the experimental
results better under steady current conditions, calibration of the porous resistance coeﬃcients and
the porosity variation near the interface with the free ﬂow should be done.
Turbulence model in porous media
The standard k-ω-SST turbulence model yields acceptable results with respect to ﬂow and bed
shear stresses for the given validation cases, although the turbulence kinetic energy is overestimated
inside porous media. The turbulence transport equations for k and ω should be pore-averaged as
the momentum equations have been, in order to provide a more physically accurate description of
turbulence in porous media.
Suspended sediment transport
While the developed model can provide a good description of the ﬂow and bed shear stresses in
porous media, the suspended sediment transport model is not fully developed. This part of the
model should be improved to handle the reference concentration cb correctly. The current model
applies this boundary condition for the concentration at the seabed, while it should in fact be
applied at the reference level zb two grain diameters over the bed.
One approach would be to move the model boundary at the bed up to the reference height zb
above the physical bed, and correcting the boundary conditions accordingly. This is sketched in
Fig. 7.1, with the modiﬁed boundary conditions for the velocity ﬁeld.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
A numerical model has been developed for ﬂow in scour protection layers with the goal of modelling
the ﬂow ﬁeld and bed shear stresses accurately, such that the sediment transport can be modelled.
This is developed as a ﬁrst step in the direction of modelling the sinking of armour stones which
is governed by the sediment transport.
The scour protection layers are modelled by a porous medium approach where the porous media
ﬂow equations developed by Jensen et al. (2014) are implemented in a numerical model using the
open source CFD software OpenFOAM. The results have been veriﬁed for both turbulent and
laminar steady current and oscillatory ﬂow problems without porous media.
The validation of the developed numerical model has shown that it is possible to model the ﬂow
inside scour protection layers well by the simple porous medium approach. The ﬂow in the constant-
velocity layer of the porous medium is largely independent of whether the ﬂow is modelled as
laminar or a turbulence model is applied. However, in order to model the porous ﬂow well in the
entire porous medium, it is found necessary to use a turbulence model and implement a transition
in the porosity of the medium towards a value of 1 at the interface with the outer ﬂow. The use of a
turbulence model does not result in a signiﬁcant overestimation of the porous resistance, although
the porous resistance coeﬃcients α = 500 and β = 2 are calibrated based on turbulent ﬂows, and
should therefore already cover the contribution of turbulence to the resistance.
The standard k-ω-SST turbulence model is used in the present work and is found to overestimate
the turbulent kinetic energy inside the porous medium. This results in a slight overestimation of
the bed shear stresses due to the increased eddy viscosity. The bed shear stresses are still in the
correct order of magnitude and can as such form the basis of a sediment transport model inside
the porous medium, using a classical bed shear stress based approach.
Results from 3D simulations of the ﬂow in scour protection layers around a monopile show the
expected formation of a horseshoe vortex which penetrates into the porous medium. However, the
porous resistance based on the aforementioned α and β is too large under steady current ﬂows,
and the return ﬂow in the porous medium is underestimated. The developed model can be used to
predict the bed shear stress ﬁelds under the free ﬂow and porous medium, and calculate the bed
load transport and reference concentration of sediment for the suspended load. The suspended load
transport model is not fully developed, but preliminary results show that the sediment transport
is mainly directed into the scour protection layers due to the large reduction of bed shear stresses
inside the porous medium compared to the free ﬂow.
Further calibration and development should carried out in order to evaluate whether the porous
medium approach can be used to model the sinking of scour protection layers. The current models
show a too large dampening of the horseshoe vortex in the porous medium, such that a net sediment
transport out of the scour protection is not observed.

Implementation of porosity variation in Open-

The preprocessing procedure for changing the porosity near the interface with the free ﬂow is as
follows:
1. The coordinates of the cell centres of all the cells in the domain are written to ﬁles ccx, ccy
and ccz.
2. Input for the interpolation is:
(a) The porosity of the medium n
(b) The vertical coordinate of the bed ybed
(c) The variation penetration factor λ
(d) The stone diameter D50 and number of layers nl
(e) The number of zones to describe the linear variation nvar.
3. The vertical coordinates yvar and y = yint are calculated from the input parameters.
4. The coeﬃcients a and b in the linear relation n = ay + b are determined from the boundary
conditions n = 0.4 at y = yvar and n = 1 at yint.
5. Each zone has the thickness ∆y = Lvar/nvar, and the lower and upper coordinate ylow,i and
yup,i for each zone i is determined.
6. The cellSet of the entire scour protection zone is read and each cell is matched with its
vertical coordinate read from ccy. The list is sorted and the cell indices for each zone are
determined from the limits ylow,i < y ≤yup,i
7. New cellSet ﬁles are written for each zone including the constant porosity zone. These ﬁles
deﬁne the cell indices included in each zone and will be read by the input ﬁle porosityZones
8. A new porosityZones ﬁle is written, where the entire scour protection zone is replaced by
the constant zone and nvar varying zones. This ﬁle deﬁnes the properties of the zones, and
implements the varying porosity based on ni = ayi + b where yi is the center value of each

Project plan 
This project plan serves as a guideline for the Master’s Thesis in order to ensure a well-defined project. The 
plan states the purpose of the project and the methods which will be used to get there. It is used as a 
guideline and can therefore be subject to change during the course of the project as it becomes apparent 
which problems will be possible and relevant to investigate. A Gannt chart for the project work tasks is 
appended this project plan.  
 
Objectives of project 
The primary purpose of the project is to be able to determine the bed shear stresses accurately in the 
complicated 3D flow within the scour protection layers around a monopile under wave action or a steady 
current. A simple sediment transport will also be developed based on a stationary bed. 
A secondary purpose is to investigate practical design considerations such as:  
1. The effect of the number of scour protection layers vs. the scour protection thickness 
2. The effect of a filter layer. 
3. The effect of combined wave and current environment. 
4. The effect of modelling the scour protection as a porous medium vs. resolving the individual stones 
using the Immersed Boundary Method (IBM). 
 
Methods 
The bed shear stresses and sediment transport will be determined by developing a fully 3D numerical 
model which can resolve the flow dynamics within the scour protection layers in detail. The numerical 
model will be developed in OpenFOAM with the goal of improving upon previous results by (Nielsen, et al., 
2010) obtained by use of the commercial software Flow-3D.  The sediment transport model will be based 
on a stationary bed.  
The model can thus serve as a qualitative design tool to assess whether sinking of the scour protection 
stones will be initiated. This is done by calculating the Shields Parameter based on the bed shear stresses, 
and comparing this with the critical threshold for suction of sediment. The model is to be used as a basis in 
further work to develop a model with a non-stationary bed, in order to predict the sediment transport and 
sinking of armour stones quantitatively. 
In order to work towards the final 3D model a number of work tasks are identified as following: 
1. Modelling a rectangular, porous caisson on a porous bed under wave action. 
a. This is based on an example case, which only calculates the pressure and velocity field. 
2. Reshaping of the porous caisson to a porous scour protection layer (trapezoid in 2D) 
a. This will be done by importing STL geometry as input for the OpenFOAM mesh.  
3. Implement a steady current. 
4. Model the scour protection with IBM instead of as a porous medium. 
5. Model the scour protection by a hybrid model. 
==PAGE:114==
6. Calculate bed shear stresses and implement sediment transport. 
After the theory has been implemented and validated (see next section) in the 2D model, it is to be 
expanded to 3D by the following steps: 
1. Convert the geometry from a 2D trapezoid to a 3D conical section shape of the scour protection. 
a. Both for porous medium, IBM and as a hybrid model. 
2. Implement a monopile in the domain. 
3. The final model is now established. 
 
Validation of results 
The model results must be validated before any big development is made, such that any problems will be 
caught as soon as possible and the results from the final model can be considered valid. This is done at the 
following stages: 
2D model: 
For both the porous medium, IBM and hybrid models of the trapezoidal 2D scour protection with no 
monopile, the following is validated:  
 
The velocity profiles inside and outside the scour protection layers 
o Steady current: comparison with measurements by (Sumer, et al., 2001, April)  
o Oscillatory flow: comparison with measurements (inside scour protection, measured at 
centroid of pores) by (Stevanato, et al., 2010)  
 
The bed shear stress via. the friction velocity.  
o Steady current: comparison with measurements by (Sumer, et al., 2001, April) in table. 
o Oscillatory flow: comparison with measurements over a period by (Stevanato, et al., 2010) 
3D model: 
The 3D model shall be validated also for both the porous medium, IBM and the hybrid model. Validation is 
done for the following, based on the model including the monopile: 
 
The velocity profile in the flow direction: 
o Steady current: sectional profile comparison with (Nielsen, et al., 2013) and (Nielsen, et al., 
2010) 
 
The general flow dynamics around a monopole and inside the scour protection layers. 
o Qualitative comparison with (Nielsen, et al., 2010). 
 
Horseshoe vortex 
 
Flow into scour protection at downstream edge 
o Counter rotating vortices in the leewake of the monopile.

The main result of the project will be a validated numerical model which can determine the bed shear

Appendix H
Numerical solver code
The code from the developed OpenFOAM solver is presented in this appendix. This includes the
main solving algorithm, and the the most important subfunctions it calls each time step.
The functionality of the main solving algorithm is as follows
1. A series of standard functions are called before simulation start, to build the mesh etc.
2. the ﬁle createFields.H is called. This sets up the ﬁelds of all the input and output variables
used in the simulation.
3. The simulation is started by a time-loop. Every time step, the following is called.
(a) The ﬁle UEqn.H is called. This sets up and solves the pore-averaged momentum equation
(Eq. (2.10)).
(b) The pressure-correction equations are iterated in pEqn.H.
(c) After the ﬁnal iteration, the ﬁle bedLoad.H is called. This calculates the bed shear
stress, non-dimensional bed load transport, and the reference concentration cb.
(d) The ﬁle updateSedimentC.H is called. This updates the boundary conditions at the
bed for the concentration of sediment, based on the cb values computed in the previous
step.
(e) The advection-diﬀusion equation (Eq.
(2.56)) is solved for the suspended sediment
concentration ﬁeld in the ﬁle CEqn.H.
The code is presented in the following.
