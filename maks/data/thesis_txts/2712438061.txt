Abstract 
When processing any algorithm, an electronic device consumes power. This power is 
dependent on the operation and the data used at that time. From that, we can acquire 
knowledge about any data and more importantly about secret data being processed by 
a chip. Following this, this project investigates how information can be leaked from the 
NIST Lightweight Cryptography finalist Ascon, thanks to what are called Power Analysis 
Attacks. The goal is to get as much information on the target in a way to discover the 
secret key. For this, Differential Power Analysis, Correlation Power Analysis and Simple 
Power Analysis are used on the Ascon reference implementation made compatible for 
the NewAE Chipwhisperer Lite. This would allow us to see if an attacker is able to break 
such an implementation with low budget and resources. It however did not result in 
breaking the Ascon encryption showing that it is not that easy to break through Power 
Analysis Attacks.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Raising the problem 
Over the past decades, thanks to the miniaturization and the power growth of electronic 
devices, we have seen the transition of our society toward a digital society become a 
reality. Nowadays, we find these devices almost everywhere, coming from our cars, for 
them to avoid collisions or simply inform the driver about the car condition, to household 
appliances. Electronic devices are bringing automation and help in our life. The progress 
made on them is such that we call them now “smart devices” since they can now suggest 
or make decisions about their tasks. 
One of the main improvements over the last year has been the Internet of Things (IoT) 
that link a number of these devices together through the internet in order to share 
information and thus work better together. This revolution is currently being developed 
and will lead to the industry 4.0 composed by a digital core. [1] 
These small electronic devices are getting more and more powerful following the 
Moore’s law and they tend to get smaller after each generation, while consuming less 
power and being a bit more environmentally friendly on that part. [2] 
However, they remain less powerful than other types of devices such as desktop 
computers, in terms of computational power or memory. This is an issue at first sight 
regarding the time they may take to perform a task so the optimisation needed for the 
programs to be developed on these targets. 
Unfortunately, another issue appears when we go past that one: their security. Indeed, 
having less resources available on these devices, lead to a need for changes in the 
security algorithm, especially the cryptographic algorithms in order to process 
encryptions and decryption of sensible data, first in an acceptable amount of time but 
also while respecting a certain amount of security. 
That is the reason why IoT security has been such a hot topic over the last years. The 
limitation of its hardware has led to a number of attacks, mainly the creation of botnets 
(a collection of devices ruled by the same one in order to perform a specific task) to 
perform Denial of Service (DoS) attacks. [3] 
To counter this trend and to assure a bright future for these devices with the appearance 
of quantum computers, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) [4] 
has created a contest called Lightweight Cryptography [5]. This will help finding a new 
standard for cryptographic algorithms that will be able to maintain a high level of security,

2 Background 
2.1 Encryption Algorithms 
Over the centuries and mainly during wars, confidentiality of messages has been 
needed to avoid unauthorized people or enemies to discover secret information. To 
ensure this, encryption has been used. Encryption is the process of transforming a piece 
of data into an incomprehensible message through different operations. The most well-
known examples are first the Caesar cipher used in the Antiquity consisting of a 
permutation of letters within the alphabet by a certain number [8] as we can see in Figure 
1 and second Enigma, infamously used during the Second World War by the German 
army, consisting of a permutation of letters using a secret message called a key and a 
table [9]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
However, with the development of computers and the digital world, encryption has been 
adapted to the new communication ways. Encryption algorithms have now replaced 
older encryption schemes and apply to bits of data. They follow this principle: the user 
inputs a message called a cleartext as well as a key into the encryption algorithm and 
get another message as an output, called the ciphertext as seen in Figure 2. This 
ciphertext is then considered secure and can be sent over a channel.

In addition to that, they assure other properties than just confidentiality. Indeed, new 
encryption algorithms allow users to assure integrity of the encrypted data as well as

2.2 Symmetric and Asymmetric Cryptography 
We differentiate two main types of cryptography: symmetric and asymmetric. The first 
one allows to use the same key for both the encryption and the decryption. It is fast but 
we need to store a key for all possible channels between users. On the other hand, 
asymmetric encryption uses a key pair which consist of a public key and a private key, 
one for encryption and one for decryption [11] [12], as we can see in Figure 3. In this 
case, we only need to store one key pair per user. However, the computation time is 
bigger with asymmetric encryption than with symmetric encryption. That is why in 
modern communications, asymmetric encryption is used to set up a symmetric key and 
then symmetric encryption is used between users.

2.3 Block Ciphers 
In the category of symmetric encryption, two classes of ciphers exist: stream ciphers 
and block ciphers. Stream ciphers encrypt bits or bytes one by one whereas block 
ciphers cut the plaintext in blocks of the same length and encrypt the whole block with a 
key of a fixed size [13]. When the size of the plaintext cannot be divided by the size of 
the block, padding functions are used. These functions complete the last unfinished 
block of data. The data used to complete the block depends on the algorithm used and 
can be related or not to the data in the plaintext. 
 
From this, different modes can be applied to operate the cipher. Each mode will 
complete different operations on each block. A few examples are Electronic Codebook 
(ECB) mode for the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) which encrypt all blocks 
separately and can be running multiple encryptions at the same time but is vulnerable 
to block replay attacks whereas Cipher Block Chaining (CBC) mode uses an Initialization 
Vector (IV) as well as a ciphertext feedback to avoid this attack. The drawback from that 
is that CBC cannot encrypt multiple blocks at the same time. [14] 
2.4 Cryptographic Sponge Functions 
Block ciphers as seen earlier take inputs of arbitrary length and give an output of fixed 
length related to the length of the input. Sponge functions on the other hand can use 
variable length input and give an output of variable length. They are built around a state 
of b bits composed by a bitrate of r bits and a capacity of c bits. The bitrate defines the 
size of the state we will add the plaintext on each time, in other words the size of the 
plaintext blocks, so the speed of the algorithm. The capacity defines the other bits, only 
used for the permutations, so the security of the scheme. 
 
The functions contain two phases: an absorbing phase and a squeezing phase. First, 
the absorbing phase starts by initializing the state to 0 bits. The plaintext is then cut into 
blocks of r bits which are XORed with the first r bits of the state. Between each XOR 
operation, a permutation function is done on the entire state. Afterwards, the squeezing 
phase output the first r bits of the state until the wanted sized is reached with the same 
permutation function between each output. The entire construction can be seen in Figure 
4.

Another version of the Sponge construction exists, called the Duplex construction, in 
which the scheme keeps XORing the plaintext with the state, performing the permutation 
function, taking the first r bits of the state as an output and continue doing the same 
operations until the wanted size is reached. [15] 
2.5 Hashing functions 
Hashing functions are irreversible functions that take an arbitrary length input and give 
back a fixed length output. The main property of these functions is that a small change 
in the input results in a big difference in the output. It is often used to verify the integrity 
of a data by reducing it to a smaller and easier form to compare. If the result of the hash 
is the same as the given one, we know that the data has not been modified. [12] 
 
The main type of attack used against hashing functions are collision attacks, which goal 
is to get the same output from two different inputs. As a result, to be secure, hashing 
functions must ensure some properties: 
- 
Pre-image resistance: difficulty to find the input resulting in the given hash. 
- 
Second pre-image resistance: difficulty to find another input giving the same hash 
than a give one. 
- 
Collision resistance: difficulty to find two inputs with the same hash.

3 Ascon Suite 
The Ascon Suite is a package of versions of the Ascon cipher, first finalist of the NIST 
Lightweight contest. It is an authenticated encryption with associated data (AEAD) 
scheme which means that it is safe against chosen-plaintext attack (the attacker 
provides the schemes with its own plaintext and gets the ciphertext from them). [17] To 
do this, the cipher refuses to encrypt the given plaintext if it does not fit rules given by 
the scheme. This way, only valid plaintext can be used and no information from the 
encryption can be taken out from an oracle. [7] 
3.1 Specifications 
Ascon relies on the Sponge construction and more precisely on the Duplex construction 
version of it. It provides 128-bits security and uses a 320-bits permutation (320 bits state 
split into five 64-bits registers words x0 to x4). Just like in the Sponge construction, it is 
defined by the key length and the rate r. In addition to that, two other parameters a and 
b are used to describe the number of rounds for the permutation function. 
 
As seen in Figure 5, the authentication encryption procedure takes the secret key K of 
k bits, a public nonce N of 128 bits, the associated data A of arbitrary length and a 
plaintext P of arbitrary length. It outputs a ciphertext C of same length of the plaintext 
and an authentication tag T of 128 bits authenticating both the associated data and the 
encrypted message:  
 
Ek,r,a,b (K,N,A,P) = (C,T) 
Figure 5: Ascon Encryption 
The encryption scheme takes a key, a nonce, associated data and a plaintext and outputs a ciphertext and a tag. 
 
The decryption and verification procedure verifies if the tag T is valid. If it is valid, it 
outputs the plaintext: 
Dk,r,a,b (C,T) = P 
Figure 6: Ascon Decryption 
The decryption scheme takes a ciphertext and a tag and outputs a plaintext if the tag is valid. 
Else it outputs an error sign. 
The Ascon Suite also features a hashing algorithm which can also be used as an 
extendable output function. An extendable output function, contrary to a hashing 
algorithm, gives an output of arbitrary length l [7]: 
Xh,r,a,b (M,l) = H         
Figure 7: Extendable Output Function 
The extendable output function takes a message and a length and outputs a hash. 
The focus within this project will be made on the encryption algorithm since it is the main 
feature of the Ascon suite.

3.2 Mode of operation 
Since Ascon follows the Sponge construction, it consists of an alternation between 
XORing data to the state and applying a permutation function to the state. The first 
difference with the Sponge construction is that instead of having a 0 bits state as the 
initialization vector (IV), Ascon’s one is made by concatenating a given IV with the key 
K and the nonce N. This new IV is then passed into the permutation function for a rounds 
(a stands here for the given parameter). The capacity bits are then XORed with a vector 
made from the key K prepended by 0 bits to pad it to the right size. 
 
After this initialization part, the Associated Data is absorbed in the state. To do that, the 
Associated Data is cut into blocks and each one of them will be XORed with the rate bits 
of the state, followed by a permutation of b rounds (b stands here for the given 
parameter). After the last permutation has been made, the capacity bits are XORed with 
a vector made from a 1 prepended by enough 0 bits to fill the required length. 
 
The third part of the encryption scheme is absorbing the plaintext P into the state. This 
is where the Duplex construction is used. Indeed, the plaintext is cut into blocks and for 
each one of them the algorithm XOR the block to the rate bits, output the new rate bits 
as a block of the ciphertext and finally apply a permutation of b rounds. At the end of 
this part, the ciphertext has entirely been output by the scheme 
 
The last part is the finalization. It starts with a XOR operation between the capacity bits 
and a vector made from the key K concatenated with 0 bits to fill the desired length. In 
addition to that, the permutation function is applied with a rounds. Finally, the key K is 
XORed to the capacity bits and the tag T is gotten from these bits. 
 
The decryption follows the exact same scheme except during the third part. Instead of 
XORing the plaintext to the state and outputting a ciphertext block, the ciphertext block 
is XORed with the state, the plaintext block is output and the ciphertext block becomes 
the new rate bits of the state. At the end of the finalization, the output tag is compared 
to the one given as a parameter. If they are the same, the plaintext is given. [7]

4 Side Channels Attacks 
While attacking cryptography, three main types of attacks exist [12] [18]: 
-    Attacking the cipher 
-    Attacking the key 
-    Attacking the cryptographic protocol 
 
In addition to that, we differentiate four types of cryptanalysis attacks [17]: 
- 
Ciphertext-only attack 
 
The attacker only has access to a list of ciphertexts. 
- 
Known-plaintext attack 
 
The attacker has access to a plaintext and its corresponding ciphertext 
- 
Chosen-plaintext attack. 
 
The attacker can obtain the ciphertexts for arbitrary plaintexts. 
- 
Adaptive chosen-plaintext attack 
The attacker can obtain the ciphertexts from other plaintexts than the one in the 
set. 
 
Side Channel attacks is the family of attacks focusing on gathering information from the 
implementation instead of finding weaknesses in it. This regroups multiple analysis such 
as timing or power consumption. This type of attack can be divided in two categories 
[19]: 
- 
Passive attacks: information is gathered by studying physical properties 
- 
Active attacks: inputs and/or outputs are modified to make the device work in 
a specific/abnormal way 
 
This family can also be divided into smaller categories [19]: 
- 
Non-Invasive attacks: only known and external interfaces are used. 
- 
Semi-invasive attacks: internal access is possible but the device should not 
be altered 
- 
Invasive attacks: the physical properties of the chip are irreversibly modified 
 
The focus will here be made on power consumption therefore on passive attacks. Indeed, 
each chip processing data consumes a different amount of power depending on the 
operation executed but also depending on the data processed. For example, processing 
the bit 0 or the bit 1 will have a different consumption. The same will happen when 
switching from a 0 to 1 or inversely as shown in Figure 14. From that, one can guess

we need to be able to measure, compute and take into account all these traces. Using 
the Behavioral level allows to get a result in an acceptable period of time. 
 
4.1 Simple Power Analysis 
Multiple approaches to Side Channel Attacks exist depending on the resources to 
execute the attack as well as the knowledge about the target. The first one, is Simple 
Power Analysis (SPA). It consists of interpreting the power consumption directly from 
the spectrum measured from the target. Its focus is to identify, within the traces, the 
operations done at a certain time by for example comparing them to traces of reference 
operations. After identifying the operations done, their power consumption has to be 
compared to theoretical consumptions to identify the processed data and thus discover 
the secret key. It is considered a time analysis because of its focus on the progress of 
the encryption along the spectrum. Since this does not represent a deep analysis of the 
power consumption, this approach is considered “simple” and does not need an 
important amount of power traces to be executed. This will be the attacker’s choice if 
provided with few resources. [23]

However, the attacker needs a good knowledge about the target. Indeed, the type of 
operations being processed must be known to be identified in the trace. The more 
information about the target, the better. 
 
In this perspective, the efficiency of the attack can be improved by creating templates. 
Templates help in characterizing the target by informing us about how operations are 
performed as well as statistical properties such as their mean or covariance which lead 
to more precise power models and an easier analysis of the traces by making the already 
existing templates more precise. [23] 
 
Once the characterization of the device has been done, the probability for each data to 
be processed at a certain operation (or time) is calculated. The highest probability 
indicates the most probable key.

operations at the same time. In this case, the power traces are mixing all of them at the 
same time and appear to be noisy. The more operations the processor will do

4.2 Differential Power Analysis 
A more efficient type of attack exists. The main focus with modern processors will be to 
erase the noise from the power traces. Differential Power Analysis (DPA) consists in 
applying statistical analysis method to the power traces in order to achieve this goal. 
This can be done by either using traces from the target performing non-cryptographic 
operations and compare it with the ones measured to erase the noise or by comparing 
specific statistical characteristics (means, variances, covariances…). What needs to be 
remembered is that the more samples we have, the more accurate the analysis will be. 
[24] 
 
DPA, in addition to being efficient with extremely noisy traces, does not need detailed 
information about the target. The main drawback being that a lot more power traces are 
needed and the computation time is largely increased.  
 
Its strategy is the following one: 
- 
The attacker chooses an intermediate result or vulnerable variable (output of 
the AES S-box for example) 
- 
Measures the power consumption of the device at this intermediate result  
- 
On the other side, the hypothetical intermediate results are calculated (what 
would be the output for all possible keys and sometimes all possible data as 
well) 
- 
From that the hypothetical power consumption for each key is gotten. 
- 
Finally the power traces measured are compared with all hypothetical power 
traces. The better the correlation or the statistical analysis, the most probable 
the data and key are the ones used.

It is then considered as a data analysis rather than a time analysis as SPA is. An 
important part of the analysis is the chosen vulnerable variable since it will designate the 
power model and thus the operation that needs to be performed as hypothetical 
intermediate results. Some of the most commonly used power models are the Hamming 
Weight and the Hamming Distance. The Hamming Weight is defined by the number of 
non-zeroes bits in a bit string while the Hamming distance is the difference of Hamming 
Weight between two bit strings. The former is used to approximate the power 
consumption of a data at a certain time whereas the latter is used to compare two 
consumption and thus the consumption needed to switch from one data to another. The 
better the power model, the more efficient the analysis and the less traces needed. [24] 
 
In addition to that, the needed operations and the difficulty of the analysis will depend 
on the implementation of the algorithm. Indeed, a hardware implementation of the cipher 
will be more difficult to model because of the complexity of its level (analogy level or 
behavioral level) than a software implementation even if this one will need more 
operations in each clock cycle and in this case will have more power traces so will need 
more memory. 
Once the choice of the right power model made, the right statistical analysis method 
needs to be chosen.  
 
4.2.1 Difference of Means 
The first analysis method is the difference of means. It consists in calculating the mean 
value of two sets that we want to compare and then the difference will tell us if the 
parameter differing between the two sets leads to an important change or not. In the 
case of side channel attacks and power analysis, the goal is to notice if the change in a 
key bit has an impact on the target’s power consumption. The set with the biggest 
change will lead us to the most probable key. [19] 
 
4.2.2 Correlation Power Analysis 
An even more efficient statistical tool that can be used is the Pearson correlation 
coefficient defined later in the report. 
 
This measures how likely two different sets are. In the case of Side Channel Attacks and 
power analysis, this will allow us to see which set has the closest power consumption to 
the measures done which will lead us to the most probable key. [24]

6 Differential Power Analysis on Ascon 
6.1 Method 
The first step in complex Side Channel Analysis is to find a vulnerable variable which is 
the variable that will be targeted by our attack [29] [30] [31] [32] This variable must 
depend on the secret we want to discover i.e, the encryption key. While looking through 
the Ascon scheme and its steps, we notice that the initialization part uses a vector and 
a S-BOX.  
 
This vector is composed by: 
- 
A known initialization vector, from bit 0 to 63 
- 
The secret key, from bit 64 to 191 
- 
A random known nonce, from bit 192 to 319 
 
This vector is then cut in pieces as followed to fit into the 64 5-bit S-BOXes: the ith S-
BOX receives as an input the ith bit of the initialization vector, the ith and 64+ith bits from 
the secret key and the ith and 64+ith bits from the random nonce (Figure 28). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
As a result, the output of the S-BOX depends on the key. We can then take a bit from 
the output (here the most significant bit since it is known) as a vulnerable variable and 
apply our analysis method. 
 
However, firstly, the Ascon model needs to be created. The model initializes the 
encryption parameters and from that the initialization vector, the secret key and the 
Figure 28: S-BOX inputs 
One bit of each register is taken to form the 5-bits input of the S-BOX. 
==PAGE:35==
Master Thesis Report 
Side-Channel Cryptanalysis of NIST Lightweight Ciphers 
35 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Nathan Meyer PAULET 
nonce to create the vector. It then cuts this vector as presented above and pass it 
through the S-BOX. 
 
Once that done and as explained earlier, the difference of means method is used to 
perform a Differential Power Analysis. It is used here to determine the effect of the secret 
key on the output of the S-BOX. To do that, multiple encryptions are performed with the 
same key but with random nonces and the corresponding power traces are measured. 
We then need to make a guess on the key bits, so 2 bits here and use the Ascon model 
to create a hypothetical leakage. 
 
A mask is then applied on the output of the S-BOX to isolate the vulnerable variable (one 
bit of the output). If the result is a one, the corresponding power trace is added to a first 
list. If this is a zero, the same thing is done but to a second list. We then have the power 
consumptions for ones and for zeros and can use the difference of means method to 
know if the key guesses have an impact on the repartition of ones and zeros as outputs 
(Figure 29). If the couple of key bits guess is right, then the consumptions should be 
sorted in the right lists, then the absolute value of the difference of means should be the 
highest of the ones calculated. 
Figure 29: Separating traces in two lists 
The S-BOX outputs are separated in two lists depending on the chosen bit. 
 
The result from these operations is the knowledge of the 2 key bits at the entrance of 
each S-BOX. From this, the secret key can be reconstructed, except for its last 8 bits 
that are XORed with a constant when during the AddRoundConstant operation from the 
SPN. Then this constant needs to be XORed back on these 8 bits to get the key back.

7.1 Method 
For Correlation Power Analysis, in addition to the Ascon power model, some other tools

As said earlier, the first task in the experiment was to implement the Hamming Weight 
model. This function, even if very small, is the key to the analysis. 
 
The second part of the experiment is the Pearson Correlation Coefficient and its

implemented. 
 
Finally, the analysis can start. In the one hand, once recorded, the power traces need 
to be characterized. This is why their mean and standard deviation were calculated. This 
represents the first set of data that will be used in the Pearson Correlation Coefficient. 
 
Then, the key bits need to be guessed. For that, the exact same setup as for the DPA is 
used: 2 key bits are estimated from the guesses “00”, “01”, “10” and “11”. For each of

- 
Mean: after calculating the correlations for a key guess, the mean of these is 
calculated. The highest mean lead us to the most probable key guess. 
- 
Maximum: after calculating the correlations for a key guess, the maximum of

8 Simple Power Analysis on Ascon 
8.1 Method 
Simple Power Analysis is usually used to reveal secret data such as secret keys by 
identifying operations executed by a device as well as the data processed through them 
using only a few power traces. 
 
However, in this study, this type of analysis will be used to identify operations within 
Ascon power traces. In fact, after recording power traces from encryptions, we will 
perform isolated operations that are similar to the ones used in the Ascon cipher and 
record their corresponding power traces. 
 
Thanks to these later traces, the patterns from the traces in the whole Ascon encryption 
traces can be recognised and then have a better knowledge on when and how each 
operation is performed by the Chipwhisperer can be reached. 
 
This can help in two ways. First, by improving the knowledge we have about the chip 
and in that case the template we have on it. Second, by having as a resource a set of 
operations performed without noise. This set can then be used in Differential Power 
Analysis to erase the noise from other operations and allow a better analysis of the 
power traces.  
8.2 Experiment 
After the issues encountered with both DPA and CPA, the focus was made on SPA to 
try to understand the power traces obtained and how the chipwhisperer is performing 
the encryptions. 
What was done each time for these experiments was to create new firmware for the 
chipwhisperer with the same kind of simpleserial communications as were used for DPA 
and CPA. A nonce and a key was sent to the firmware. 
The firmware then processes the data following the operations specified in the new 
firmware while the power traces are recorded each time. 
By adding or subtracting certain operations, the differences and how the behaving of the 
chipwhisperer changes from one operation to another could be seen. 
This would help in knowing what operations look like, when they are processed and how 
and if the chipwhisperer is making any optimisation.

9 Discussion 
Through this project, different Side Channels Attacks on the finalist candidate Ascon of 
the NIST Lightweight Cryptography contest were performed. To do that, milestones were 
defined in the first part of this report. 
Firstly the Ascon cipher was described, its versions and the properties of each one of 
these as well as its features and security measures. This was a mandatory step in the 
way to understand the Ascon cipher to better know what to attack within this cipher and 
how to do so. 
Once this knowledge acquired, Side Channels Attacks and mainly Power Analysis 
Attacks were defined and explained in order to understand the motivation and the reason 
behind the methods and tools used in this project. 
Later, the tools used were presented and a focus was made on the Chipwhisperer Lite, 
its properties, features and syntax, for it to play the role of the platform for the Ascon 
cipher as well as the way to process the data and do the analysis in the first part of the 
project. 
Then started the experimental part of the project. The first goal was to modify the 
reference implementation of the Ascon cipher to make it compatible with the 
Chipwhisperer Lite XMEGA architecture and to be able to call for encryptions, with the 
wanted parameters, from outside and to get back the measured power traces to perform 
the power analysis.  
This task succeeded resulting in an implementation giving back the right ciphertext and 
tag, verified with the Ascon reference list. In addition to that, the encryptions were 
performed within a pretty short amount of time. These results ensures that Ascon is a 
good candidate for such lightweight architecture. 
Once this tested, the analysis part was implemented and DPA and CPA were performed 
on our implementation. Unfortunately, these two attacks did not give good results. As a 
result, it was not proven that the method used could break the implementation. Therefore, 
that such an implementation could be broken using this low amount of resources. 
Following this, a list of tests, benchmarks and debugging were used to try to find the 
origin of these results. The main investigated reasons were the method used as well as 
the Chipwhisperer’s resources. However, the measures taken to counter these issues 
were not enough and the way the device was working was questioned.

implementation, we still managed to get information about the way the cipher is 
processed, thanks to SPA, important information that can be used in more complex

10 Future Work 
As mentioned previously, SPA would allow to gather more information about the device 
and its behaviour while performing the encryption algorithm. This can be used in different 
ways. 
Thanks to a deeper analysis with SPA, within the long power traces, the operations 
where the correlation with the data is supposedly the highest, could be identified. That 
could be for example a S-BOX or even a simple XOR operation within the S-BOX 
involving a key bit. 
This way, the performed DPA and CPA attacks would be more targeted and will only 
focus on a given window of the spectrum. 
Another idea would be to create templates of the device to perform more complex DPA. 
Indeed, once the traces can be characterised, another layer of calculation can be done 
on the data to make them compatible with a deeper statistical method of analysis. The 
analysis would take longer and would probably need more power traces but the results 
will be way better. 
On the other hand, the power model would be changed as well, in a way to only target 
a specific operation. This coupled with a windowed DPA or CPA would probably perform 
better. 
Finally, use a higher end Chipwhisperer or an oscilloscope coupled with another chip on 
which the Ascon cipher would be implemented could possibly allow us to get better 
measures of the power traces and then perform a better analysis.
