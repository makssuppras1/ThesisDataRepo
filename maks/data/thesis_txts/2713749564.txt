Abstract 
Many compounds within pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, and chemical building blocks contain fluorine. 
The incorporation of this atom into organic molecules, fluorination, serves as a tool to modulate their 
physical and chemical properties, such as stability, reactivity, lipophilicity, and acidity all with minimal 
steric perturbation, and can aid greatly in the identification of lead compounds (Fujiwara & O’Hagan, 
2014). In addition, fluorinated organic compounds (i.e., organofluorides) show higher thermal stability 
than their non-fluorinated versions. It is estimated that around 30% of all new approved drugs (Zhou et 
al., 2016), more than 50% of approved blockbuster drugs (Han et al., 2020), such as Lipitor® and Prozac®, 
and about 25% of licensed herbicides (Fujiwara & O’Hagan, 2014), such as Trifluralin and Flupoxam, 
present in their structure one or more fluorine atoms, with these compounds accounting for billions of 
dollars in annual sales. 
Due to these reasons, organofluorides have become highly valued within these industries, making their 
production and their detection very relevant processes. Nowadays, current production relies solely on 
traditional chemical synthesis, which is associated with harsh conditions, high energy demand, and 
sometimes pollutant solvents. Therefore, there is an urge to move towards bioproduction of these 
compounds, where selected bacteria are metabolically engineered to synthesize the organofluorides in 
the cells. Although there is a desire to change towards bioproduction, it is hampered by the catalytic 
efficiency of the fluorinase. While several methods exist to optimize enzymes, e.g. directed evolution or 
rational engineering, it remains difficult to set up screening methods for improved enzyme variants, and 
normally high-throughput screening methods of biocatalysts are only available for enzymes whose 
activity can be determined by the use of fluorescent substrates or products. Against this background, in 
this thesis a screening method for improved performance of fluorinases was designed, based on coupling 
growth of the host strain to the activity of the biocatalyst, so that enzyme variants with enhanced 
properties lead to higher fitness of the cells. This is achieved by implementing synthetic auxotrophies on 
the host strain which make the bacteria grow only when the fluorinase is expressed and active. An 
optimized protocol for growth-coupled in vivo screening of improved bacterial fluorinases was 
established: induction of the strains with auto-induction media, growth for 24 h at 30°C, washing of the 
cells to remove all possible methionine present from LB media or other sources, sealing of growth plates 
with foil, addition of antibiotic to the media, and growth in M9 with SAM (300 µM) and with NaF (10 mM). 
As for organofluorides detection, techniques like chromatography together with mass spectrometry (MS) 
are normally employed, but detection can be challenging due to lack of isotopic pattern, and high-
resolution MS is also utilized but availability creates hurdles for many laboratories (Zheng et al., 2020). 
Therefore, in this thesis, RNA aptamers capable of sensing fluorinated metabolites were designed, 
composed of a fluorophore and a metabolite binding domain which, thanks to the modular architecture 
that RNA aptamers present, can be adapted to detect different metabolites. Different circular and linear 
RNA aptamers with different expression systems were constructed as future biosensors. Efficiency of 
circular aptamers was not as high as expected, since, in bacteria, more endonucleases seem to be involved 
in RNA degradation. Therefore, circulation might not be a helpful strategy. Still, RNA aptamers are not as 
sensitive as commonly used protein biosensors, so strong promoters are needed for their expression.

Abstrakt (Dansk) 
Mange farmaceutiske og agrokemiske stoffer samt kemiske byggeklodser, indeholder fluor. Integrationen 
af dette atom i organiske molekyler, fluorinering, fungerer som et værktøj til at modulere deres fysiske 
og kemiske egenskaber, så som stabilitet, reaktivitet, lipofilicitet og surhed, alt sammen med minimal 
sterisk forstyrrelse, og kan bidrage markant til opdagelsen af bly-stoffer (Fujiwara & O’Hagan, 2014). Dertil 
kommer at fluorinerede organiske stoffer (organofluorider) udviser større termisk stabilitet end deres 
ikke-fluorinerede versioner. Det vurderes at omkring 30% af alle nye godkendte behandlingsmidler (Zhou 
et al., 2016), mere end 50% af godkendte ”blockbuster” behandlingsmidler (Han et al., 2020), så som 
Lipitor® og Prozac®, og omkring 25% af autoriserede ukrudtsmidler (Fujiwara & O’Hagan, 2014), så som 
Trifluralin og Flupoxam, indeholder et eller flere fluoratomer i deres struktur, med disse stoffer 
udgørende for milliarder af dollar i salg årligt.  
Af den grund er organofluorider blevet højt værdsat i disse industrier, hvilket gør deres produktion og 
opdagelse yderst relevante processer. Nu om dage afhænger produktionen udelukkende af traditionel 
kemisk syntese, hvilket er forbundet med strenge forhold, højt energiforbrug og sommetider forurenende 
opløsningsmidler. Der er derfor en længsel efter at skifte til biologisk produktion af disse stoffer, hvor 
udvalgte bakterier genmodificeres til at producere organofluorider i cellerne. Selvom der er et ønske om 
at skifte til biologisk produktion, er det begrænset af den katalytiske effektivitet af fluorinase. På trods af 
at flere metoder til at optimere enzymer eksisterer, f.eks. målrettet evolution og rationelt design, 
forbliver det svært at screene for forbedrede enzymvarianter, og normale højgennemstrømningsmetoder 
af biokatalysatorer er kun tilgængelige for enzymer hvis aktivitet kan bestemmes ved hjælp af 
fluorescerende substrater eller produkter. På denne baggrund, udvikles der i denne afhandling en 
screeningsmetode for forbedret ydeevne af fluorinaser, baseret på at forbinde vækst af værtstammen 
med aktiviteten af biokatalysatoren, så enzymvarianter med forbedrede egenskaber medfører øget 
fitness af cellerne. Dette opnås ved at implementere syntetiske auxotrofier i værtstammen, så 
bakterierne kun deler sig når fluorinasen udtrykkes og er aktiv. En optimeret protokol til vækstkoblet in 
vivo screening af forbedrede bakterielle fluorinaser blev etableret: Induktion af stammerne med auto-
induktionsmedie, vækst i 24 timer af 30°C, vask af cellerne for fjernelse af al sandsynlig tilstedeværelse af 
methionin fra LB-mediet eller andre kilder, lukning af vækstplader med folie, tilsætning af antibiotika til 
mediet og vækst i M9 med SAM (300 μM) og med NaF (10 mM). 
Til organofluorid-registrering anvendes der normalt teknikker som kromatografi kombineret med 
massespektrometri (MS), men registrering kan være udfordret af mangel på isotopiske mønstre, og 
højopløsnings MS anvendes også, men manglende tilgængelighed er en forhindring for mange 
laboratorier (Zheng et al., 2020). Derfor udvikles der i denne afhandling RNA aptamerer i stand til at sanse 
fluorinerede metabolitter, opbygget af en fluorofor og et metabolitbindende domæne, hvilket takket 
være den modulære arkitektur af RNA aptamererne kan tilpasses til registrering af forskellige 
metabolitter. Forskellige cirkulære og lineære RNA-aptamerer med forskellige udtrykningssystemer blev 
konstrueret som fremtidige biosensorer. Effektiviteten af cirkulære aptamerer var ikke så høj som 
forventet da der i bakterier lader til at være flere endonukleaser involveret i RNA-nedbrydning. Altså, er 
cirkularisering ikke nødvendigvis en god strategi. Dog er RNA-aptamerer ikke så følsomme som hyppigt 
anvendte protein biosensorer, så stærke promotere er nødvendige for deres ekspression.

Project purpose  
This project is divided into two subprojects with each their own final purpose. These goals are, firstly, to 
establish a new screening method for enhanced variants of fluorinase and related enzymes, but also with 
broad applications to a large variety of other biochemical reactions. Secondly, to design and establish RNA 
aptamer biosensors able to detect fluorinated metabolites in P. putida and related bacteria, but adaptable 
to detect other metabolites.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Fluorinated compounds 
Halogenated organic compounds (organohalides) are organic molecules that contain at least one halogen 
atom in their structure, which can be fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br) or iodine (I). Organohalides 
present a high economic value since they are widely used as pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, and fine 
chemicals (Zhou et al., 2016; Inoue et al., 2020; Ogawa et al., 2020), as well as ingredients of elastomers, 
adhesives, sealants, pesticides, refrigerants, fire-resistant oils, solvents, electrically insulating coatings, 
plasticizers, and plastics (Cros et al., 2022). One of the most abundant halogens in these compounds, 
especially within pharmaceuticals and pesticides, is fluorine (F). During the last decade, organic fluorine 
compounds (i.e., organofluorides) have had a profound impact on the development of bioactives for the 
pharmaceuticals market. According to more recent estimations on the presence of organohalides in the 
market, Cl represents a 57.4% followed by F with a 36.7%, whose predominant role is highlighted in the 
agrochemical sector (47% of the total contribution) (Ogawa et al., 2020) and in the pharmaceutical drugs 
sector (18% of the total contribution) (Inoue et al., 2020). It is estimated that up to 20% of the 
pharmaceuticals present in the market contain at least one F atom (Braun & Eicher, 2017), and that 30% 
of the leading 30 blockbuster drugs by sales contain, too (O’Hagan, 2010). 
Due to the small size of the F atom (covalent radius of 0.64 Å), the strong electronegativity of the C-F 
bond (3.98 Pauling), and the low polarizability of this bond, the introduction of F atoms into organic 
compound molecules changes the physiochemical properties of the actual compound, providing them 
with new properties. This is mostly done in agrochemicals and pharmaceuticals, where by substitution of 
hydrogen atoms or functional groups with F the acidity and lipophilicity of the compounds is modified, as 
well as being able to control conformational bias. Ultimately, these modifications often lead to an 
improvement on the biological and/or pharmacological properties of the compound. Another scenario in 
which addition of F improves some properties is exemplified in the replacement of methyl-arene (i.e., 
aromatic hydrocarbons) substituents by trifluoromethyl, which blocks potential oxidation sites in order 
to prevent undesired metabolic degradation/modification (Zhou et al., 2016). 
 
1.2. Fluorination reaction 
Owing to the unique properties of F, fluorination has become an essential part of industrial processes for 
the production of a variety of widely used compounds such as a wide range of pharmaceuticals and 
agrochemicals. However, production of organofluorines is almost exclusively carried out through 
traditional petro-chemistry. 
The traditional reactions carried out to produce organofluorines (and organohalides in general) are via 
environmentally hazardous processes, in which harsh reaction conditions are employed, and they rely on 
toxic reagents and solvents (Chrétien et al., 2005). These halogenation methodologies also show poor 
regioselectivity, low space-time yields, and formation of unwanted side products which requires tedious 
chemical separation methods and might also be toxic (Inkpen et al., 2013). 
In contrast, enzymatic halogenation (i.e., biohalogenation) offers an environmentally friendly alternative 
to circumvent most of the problems typically associated with traditional chemistry. Biological synthesis 
of organofluorines by employing engineered microbes as cell factories that perform the fluorination 
reaction is one of the main goals within this field. 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
The abundance of human-made organofluorines contrasts with their relatively low abundance in nature 
(Carvalho & Oliveira, 2017; Deng et al., 2004). 5′-Fluoro-5′-deoxyadenosine (5′-FDA) synthase, also called 
fluorinase (FlA), is the only known enzyme to catalyse the formation of the C−F bond. This enzyme, firstly 
identified in Streptomyces cattleya (Schaffrath et al., 2003), catalyzes the nucleophilic substitution of 
methionine for F− on the C5′ of the essential methyl donor S-adenosyl-L-methionine (SAM). Hereby, it 
generates 5′-FDA and L-methionine (L-Met) as products (Figure 1) (Zhu et al., 2007), with the former being 
the first committed product of the fluorometabolites biosynthesis pathway. Since the discovery of FlA in 
2003, only six other fluorinases have been reported in the literature, all of them sourced from 
actinomycetes (Deng et al., 2014; Ma et al., 2016; Sooklal et al., 2020; Pardo et al., 2022). 
 
 
Figure 1. Reaction of the fluorinase, with SAM and F- as substrates, and FDA and methionine as products. 
A major obstacle for biochemical synthesis of organofluorine is the low catalytic efficiency of the 
fluorinase. All fluorinases identified so far have low turnover rates that hamper biotechnological 
applications. For example, the fluorinase of S. cattleya catalyzes around 0.11 reaction per minute under 
ideal conditions (Wang et al., 2014). The enzyme found to catalyze this reaction with the highest turnover 
rate is a an archaeal fluorinase discovered from Methanosaeta sp. PtaU1.Bin055. After heterologous 
expression of this fluorinase in Pseudomonas putida, the 5′-FDA content (indicative of in vivo 
biofluorination) was 12-fold higher than with any other fluorinase gene studied previously (Pardo et al., 
2022). This fluorinase was also included in some experiments of this study. 
 
1.3. Traditional development of enhanced biocatalysts – In vivo directed evolution 
Nowadays, desired changes in the activity of enzymes is often achieved via directed evolution or rational 
engineering. Directed evolution is a robust method in which proteins with desirable functions are found 
through random mutations in the gene of interest, followed by selection of desired mutants. It requires 
no protein structure information and no a priori knowledge of the effects of specific amino acid 
substitutions. These are the main the main advantages of directed evolution. This method mimics natural 
evolution by imposing stringent selection and screening methodologies in order to identify proteins with 
optimized functionality, including genetic diversity, binding, catalytic properties, thermal and 
environmental stability (Bloom & Arnold, 2009). In this process, biological entities with desired traits are 
created through iterative rounds of genetic diversification and library screening. 
Within the directed evolution methodology, in vivo directed evolution allows for simple and robust 
identification of the enzyme of interest by coupling the activity of the enzyme with the survival of the 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
host organism (Nearmnala et al., 2021). In vivo directed evolution involves three key steps that are 
repeated until the desired protein features are achieved: (1) construction of random mutant libraries, (2) 
selection of evolved enzyme variants, and (3) verification of improved growth phenotypes (Figure 2). In 
the first step, a genetic library with many different variants of the gene encoding the enzyme of interest 
is cloned into plasmids. This genetic library can be produced, for example by error-prone PCR (EP-PCR) or 
base editing-mediated in vivo mutagenesis (Cravens et al., 2021). More details about genetic library 
generation are explained in section 1.5. Subsequently, the selected host organism is transformed with 
the genetic library, thereby each cell receives a different variant of the gene with specific mutations. In 
the second step, in order to select for the improved enzyme variants, the gene variants are expressed and 
the desired activity is screened for. In the third stage of the in vivo directed evolution cycle, the selected 
genes are isolated, expressed and purified to verify the improved phenotype. As mentioned before, in 
this methodology, coupling of enzyme activity to growth of the cells is normally employed, making a direct 
connection between genotype and phenotype, thus making it possible to isolate the gene(s) that code for 
the most desirable protein variant. These selected subsequently employed as template for the next round 
of mutagenesis, in case the enzyme of interest needs to be further optimized. Due to the randomness of 
this methodology, its utilization allows for the generation of a spectrum of proteins that can reach 
different optimum characteristics regarding its activity (Packer & Liu, 2015). 
 
 
Figure 2. The three steps carried out repetitively in in vivo directed evolution. First, mutagenesis of the gene of interest is 
performed via methods like Error-prone PCR in order to create variants with different point mutations. Secondly, screening of 
the originated variants by the selected acitvity assay so as to find the enzyme variant with the best features. Then the gene from 
which the improved protein is coded is isolated and amplify in order to undergo further cycles of mutagenesis until the optimal 
protein characteristics are achieved. Adapted from Packer & Liu, 2015.

limitations when it comes to screening the created genetic library, since this depends on the

product of the reaction. In the specific case of the FlA, fluoride has toxic effects on bacteria when present

product extraction (Markakis et al., 2020). Due to these problems, it may become difficult to screen for 
improved enzymes since the more efficient the catalyst is the more stress will be imposed onto the cells. 
This could become a problem when coupling the reaction of interest to a growth/survival phenotype, as 
the toxicity may counteract. In other types of reactions, the problem could lie on the interactions with 
the host’s metabolism, the transport through membranes, or the requirement to lyse the cells in order to 
perform an assay for the enzyme activity. 
 
1.4. In vivo screening of improved biocatalysts - Coupling enzyme activity to microbial 
growth 
While several methods exist to optimize enzymes, e.g., directed evolution or rational engineering, it 
remains difficult to set up screening methods for all the improved enzyme variants generated. 
Fluorescence-linked reactions are often used in literature to accomplish high-throughput screenings, but 
fluorescent substrates or products are only applicable in a few reactions. For most of the 
biotechnologically-interesting reactions, e.g., that of the fluorinase, no fluorescent analogue is available. 
Moreover, measurement of absorbance or fluorescence using flow cytometry or microfluidics allows 
high-throughput screening but still depends on the chemical nature of the product or biosensors to 
translate the product information into detectable signals. These screening methods are suitable for 
directed evolution of some enzymes but may not be applicable to diverse proteins without catalytic 
functions. That is why other screening methods that do not depend on fluorescence are being 
investigated, with growth-coupled analysis of the enzyme activity being a very interesting method 
employed in this project.  
One of the simplest ways to screen for a desired phenotype is to have this phenotype linked to the cells’ 
fitness. In growth-coupling strategies, better enzyme activity is translated into better growth of the 
organisms. Due to the need for the cells to have some essential molecules for their cell machinery to 
work, it is possible to reprogram cellular metabolism to engineer synthetic auxotrophs in a way that these 
only grow when the protein of interest is expressed and active (Chen et al., 2022). Auxotrophy is the 
inability of an organism to synthesize a compound needed for its growth, and so synthetic auxotrophs are 
organisms engineered to depend on this particular molecule for their viability (Lopez & Anderson, 2015). 
Synthetic auxotrophies-based selection of improved catalysts is very high throughput (109 variants), 
laborsaving, and minimal equipment is needed. Additionally, the selected protein variants can be directly 
employed with in vivo applications, which minimizes the influence of environmental differences between 
in vitro and in vivo selection. This strategy has proven to be successful and practical for directed evolution, 
not only for enzymes but also other proteins such as DNA-binding proteins (Taylor et al., 2001; Luo et al., 
2019; Casini et al., 2018; Russ et al., 2020). In this project, the strain employed to assess the fluorinase 
==PAGE:15==
5 
 
activity already contained several gene deletions as well as two genes knockins to create the synthetic 
auxotrophies. Genes involved in the production of an essential amino acid, methionine, were deleted.  
As indicated before, the fluorinase reaction produces methionine next to FDA (Figure 3). Methionine is 
synthesized from homocysteine through methionine synthase (MetE/MetH) (See Table 1). E. coli ΔmetE 
is auxotrophic for methionine, and it can only grow when methionine is supplemented to the medium 
(Mordukhova and Pan, 2013). The auxotrophy can also be complemented with cobalamin (vitamin B12) 
supplementation, as metH codes a B12-dependent methionine synthase. If the SAM transporter from 
Rickettsia prowazekii (RP076) (Tucker et al., 2003) is heterologously expressed in E. coli, the auxotrophy 
can also be complemented by supplementation of SAM, as E. coli K-12 (genotype of the strain employed 
E. coli BW25113) possesses a SAM-dependent methionine synthase (MmuM) (El-Hajj et al., 2013) (Figure 
3). El-Hajj et al. (2013) showed that the activity of MmuM is sufficient to allow growth of E. coli K12 ΔmetK 
with the SAM transporter on minimal media with SAM, while E. coli strains lacking mmuM and metK need 
methionine in addition to SAM supplementation, as the methionine biosynthesis is inhibited by the 
feeding of SAM. This peculiarity can be explained by the fact that E. coli cannot take up SAM, and 
therefore naturally never encounters this metabolic situation. Through deletion of mmuM and metE, 
which prevents methionine synthesis from homocysteine in the absence of B12, and the knock-in of flA 
(and variants thereof), the auxotrophy can be complemented by the fluorination reaction in the presence 
of F- and SAM (Figure 3). In conclusion, the auxotrophies can be complemented by either methionine or 
vitamin B12 supplementation or through the activity of the fluorinase, when SAM and F- are 
supplemented.

1.5. Creation of mutant libraries by error-prone PCR 
When the objective is to screen for improved biocatalysts, it is essential to firstly obtain a broad range of 
variants of the enzyme or protein that present different alterations in their amino acid sequence. In order 
to achieve this, mutagenesis on the gene coding the protein of interest is performed. This is a common 
process occurring in nature during DNA replication; however, it is accentuated when the genetic material 
is exposed to a mutagen (like UV rays) or to conditions that make this process less accurate, but still, it 
has lead evolution through time (Kumar & Singh, 2013). 
Generally, two methodologies are employed when performing mutagenesis on a genomic region: random 
and rational strategies. As the name implies, rational mutagenesis introduces mutations in specific areas 
of the coding sequence of interest often corresponding to essential parts of the protein such as the 
binding site or structurally important residues. One of the most common rational strategies uses synthetic 
DNA oligonucleotides containing one or more degenerate codons at positions corresponding to targeted 
residues, so several mutations are introduced in a very small region (Packer & Liu, 2015). Regarding 
random mutagenesis, this is a more commonly used method since the same mutations as with rational 
strategies can be achieved and no extensive knowledge of the catalytic site or structure of the protein are 
required. This is usually done by using chemical and physical agents that damage the DNA (not common 
for directed evolutions due to mutational biases), or by introducing random mutations into a specific DNA 
sequence via Error-prone PCR (EP-PCR) (McCullum et al., 2010). 
As mentioned before, genetic libraries are created by different mutagenic methodologies, such as EP- 
PCR, sequence saturation mutagenesis (SeSaM), synthetic production of libraries (with GG-based 
assembly), and Darwin assembly (simultaneous introduction of multiple mutations in distal parts of the 
protein) (Alejaldre et al., 2021; Cravens et al., 2021; Wong et al., 2004). As their names indicate, EP-PCR 
is based on a random procedure in which point mutations are introduced into a genetic fragment while it 
is amplified. In this project, only EP-PCR was employed so focus will only be put in that procedure. During 
the PCR of a given sequence, mutations will accumulate with each amplification cycle. To increase the 
mutation rate, Taq DNA polymerase is employed with its inherently low fidelity of 1.8 · 10-4 
errors/base/doubling due to the lack of proofreading activity. This value can be even further increased to 
10-3 by the addition of Mn2+, increasing Mg2+ concentrations, and the unequal use of dNTP (Wilson & 
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
Keefe, 2001; Potapov & Ong, 2017). Thanks to the fact that mutations will be introduced in each cycle of 
amplification, it is possible to control the number of mutations in the final library through the number of 
amplification cycles (McCullum et al., 2010). 
Screening of a genetic library can be limited by the transformation efficiency of the selected host 
organism. That is why, the gene library, cloned into plasmids, needs to be first transformed into a high-
efficiency transformation strain, so a plasmid purification can be made from the liquid cell culture, and 
then use this miniprepped plasmid to transform the selected host. Transformation is more efficient when 
a purified plasmid (supercoiled) is used than with a ligation mixture of the gene library together with the 
backbone plasmid, with the latter showing transformation efficiencies as low as 1–10% of the former. 
 
1.5.1. Library size and parameters calculations 
When constructing genetic libraries, appropriate statistical analysis is important, and this is even more 
relevant for the interpretation of data from these libraries. In order to create and evaluate these libraries, 
calculations of some parameters need to be made. The equations employed are useful in estimating the 
distribution of the mutations present in each variant, the degeneracy (D) of the library (number of distinct 
members among the transformants, i.e., number of independent clones), the frequency of a particular 
clone in the library, the probability that a particular clone is present, and the probability that a library is 
complete (Bosley & Ostermeier, 2005). Most often, library size corresponds to the total number of 
transformants (T) achieved during the library construction. However, if T > Dmax (maximum degeneracy of 
a library assuming an infinite number of transformants), then the library size equals the maximum 
degeneracy. Although these are important parameters to determine, it is much more informative to 
describe the library in terms of “significant” library members.

change in the amino acid sequence since some of these will code for the same amino acid as in the original 
sequence and, therefore, be synonymous. Consequently, it is important to also take into account the rate 
at which synonymous mutations occur. All the parameters calculated in the flA library created in this 
project are described below with their respective formulas and statistics (Bosley and Ostermeier, 2005;

RNA aptamers are RNA oligonulceotides capable of binding to specific targets with high affinity and 
specificity (Germer et al., 2013). These sequences can potentially be used as biosensors in order to detect

Although RNA aptamers present all these benefits, they are also more easily degraded than proteins or 
DNA due to the effect of the exonuclease. In order to make the RNA constructs more stable and increase 
their half-life time, circularization of the RNA is a common strategy employed, as no 3’ or 5’ ends are 
exposed to be acted upon (Guo et al., 2011). Circular aptamers are short sequences of artificial DNA or 
RNA capable of binding various molecules with high affinity and specificity, which are able to ligate from 
the 3’ end to the 5’ end due to their structure. The binding sequence used to detect the metabolite of 
choice is positioned in the center of the aptamer sequence, since it will be the circularized region. This 
sequence is flanked by a ligation sequence on each side, which are flanked by a ribozyme sequence on 
each side, and with a promoter for the polymerase at the 5’ end and the terminator in the 3’ end. Once 
the ribozyme sequences are cleaved after transcription, ligation sequences are joined by a ligase, forming 
thus the circular aptamer (more details about the structure are detailed in section 1.6.2).  
Regarding their benefits, circular RNA aptamers exhibit higher structural integrity and stability in 
comparison to linear nucleic acid assemblies (Kuai et al., 2017), since they resist endogenous 
exoribonucleases therefore producing high concentrations of the aptamer. Moreover, as stated by Litke 
and Jaffrey (2019), RNA-based biosensors can be expressed at high levels and utilized to sense metabolite 
concentrations in the cells. As for the disadvantages, if these aptamers were to be used as therapeutic 
drugss, excretion from the bloodstream by renal filtration would be a limitation as well as the control of 
the duration of action. More general problems of aptamers are their interaction with intracellular 
targets, the generation of aptamers using unpurified target proteins, aptamers cross-reactivity, and the 
automation of aptamer generation (Lakhin et al., 2013).

The design strategy followed in this project was adapted from Litke and Jaffrey (2019), where an 
expression system for achieving rapid RNA circularization was designed resulting in RNA aptamers with 
high stability and expression levels (Figure 4). Circularization is achieved without the co-expression of any 
additional protein or enzyme. To trigger RNA circularization, RNA transcripts are expressed containing an

this approach, protein-binding RNA aptamers that otherwise have minimal effects in cells become potent 
inhibitors.

µL was plated on LB plates with the appropriate antibiotic. The rest of the cells were centrifuged at 14,000 
rpm for 1 min, discarding supernatant, resuspending in 100 µL of LB and finally plating the remaining cells. 
Plates were incubated at 37°C ON. 
 
2.1.4. Growth in selection media – Autoinduction  
Cultures were first grown in LB to achieve high cell densities. Rapid growth to high cell densities is 
desirable for maximizing the yield and efficiency of producing target proteins, i.e., the fluorinase in this 
case. After having the precultures grow ON in LB media, strains were subsequently grown in minimal M9 
medium with the autoinduction media (glucose, glycerol and lactose) as well as methionine, so that cells 
were adapted to minimal medium before starting the selection (growth without methionine in medium). 
All the components of the media employed and the different solutions utilized are detailed in Table S2.  
Auto-induction (AI) media was chosen for these experiments in order to have mild conditions. To this end, 
glycerol, glucose and lactose were supplemented to the M9 medium. In this case, the auto-induction is 
based on the fact that lactose is not metabolized as long as glucose is present in the medium, but 
induction of the lac operon still occurs through an intermediate of the lactose metabolic pathway. Cells 
are induced and the genes under control of the T7lac promoter are expressed (either the GFP protein or 
flAMA37) (Studier, 2005). 
A volume of 10 mL of minimal M9 with AI media 1 (used in 50x) (see Table S2) supplemented with 
methionine (final concentration 50 µg/mL) and 20 µL Km was inoculated with 100 µL preculture in 
triplicates for each clone. These cultures were left growing at 25°C ON 250 rpm, and when an OD630 of at 
least 1 was reached, 200 µL of the negative control culture (Strain 1) were withdrawn and loaded into a 
==PAGE:25==
15 
 
96-well-plate (96WP) in order to measure OD630 and fluorescence to confirm growth and induction of the 
T7 expression system respectively. The optimal values would be around 1 and around 40,000, 
respectively, which would take normally 2 days to reach. In optimized conditions later on, an incubation 
temperature of 30°C was employed, resulting in OD630 of 1 and fluorescence intensity of around 70,000 
in one day. Moreover, Strain 1 cultures were pelleted in order to check for fluorescence. When OD630 and 
fluorescence intensity values of 1 and 40,000, respectively, were reached, 1 mL of each culture was taken 
up and washed with sucrose in order to get rid of all the methionine present in the media by centrifuging 
at 9,000 g for 1 min twice, and finally resuspending in 1 mL of M9 (without any carbon source nor 
methionine). OD630 was measured for all cultures using M9 as blank, with desired values of around 2-3.

2.1.6. Carbohydrate analysis of the medium 
In order to determine which carbon source from the AI media 2 was being consumed first by the cells 
during the growth-coupling experiments, and check whether this affected the cells’ growth, a growth 
experiment was conducted, in which samples for posterior HPLC measurement were taken up every hour. 
For this experiment, Strain 2 (flA mutant) and Strain 4 (positive control) were employed, and 2 conditions 
were tested: culture with methionine and FDA, and culture with methionine and without FDA. Media was 
prepared by inoculating 10 mL of M9 with AI media 2 (without glucose), 50 µg/mL methionine, 50 µg/mL

every hour, measure OD630 with the spectrophotometer, until cells reached stationary phase. At the same

is to make electroporation transformation as efficient as possible, after the USER reaction, 1 µL of T4 
ligase and 1 µL of T4-buffer were added to the USER mix and let incubate for 30 min at room temperature.

2.1.8. Gene silencing by Base editing with pBEC-Csy4 
The base editing technique with the plasmid pBEC-Csy4 is based on the creation of knock-out strains by 
inserting stop codons into the open reading frame of the gene of interest, and it can be used for P. putida, 
P. aeruginosa, E. coli and other bacteria (Tong et al., 2019). This CRISPR base editor strategy enables 
single-nucleotide resolution manipulations (C·G → T·A) with >90% efficiency. This method overcomes 
typical limitations of previous technologies and empowers engineering programs in Gram-negative 
bacteria that were out of reach thus far (Volke et al., 2022). 
The selection strain employed already has the gene ybdL knocked out, which prevents the oxidized 
version of methionine (4-methylsulfanyl-2-oxobutanoate) to take the NH3+ group from an L-α-amino acid 
and produce methionine again. However, there is another gene that could also be producing methionine 
in some level, the 2-iminoacetate synthase (thiH). This synthase catalyzes the radical-mediated cleavage 
of tyrosine to 2-iminoacetate and 4-cresol, it uses NADPH and SAM as substrates, and produces many 
compounds with methionine amongst them. Therefore, further silencing of thiH was performed. 
In order to design the single guide RNAs (sgRNAs) to silence the thiH gene, an online tool called CRISPy-
web (Blin et al., 2016) was employed. CRISPy-web is a user-friendly tool to design sgRNAs for the 
CRISPR/Cas9 system. By uploading your genome data, this tool allows you to interactively select a region 
of the genome to scan for possible sgRNAs. Two protospacer sequences for C -> T editing were selected 
according to the following criteria: 
• 
The “C” site to be edited should be in positions 3 to 9 in the suggested protospacer sequence 
• 
For maximum efficiency, there should not be a “G” in front of the “C” to be edited 
• 
Preferably, the introduced STOP codon should be towards the 5’ end of the CDS, checking that 
there are no in-phase start codons downstream that could allow the translation of a truncated 
but potentially functional protein 
Considering the selected spacer sequences, the GoldenGate_PrimerDesigner3.0 (Excel file) was able to 
provide us with the oligos for the Golden Gate assembly into the pBEC-UGI-GFP plasmid. The forward 
primers obtained from this design tool are thiH1-pos1-gg-for and thiH2-pos2-gg-for (Table S1), and the 
reverse primers are standard oligos employed for Golden Gate assembly which in this case were 
Pri_CDV_878_gRNA-Position1-gg-rev for the first spacer and Pri_CDV_877_pnCas9-BsaI-Csy4-Rv for the 
second spacer. 
sgRNAs were prepared by PCR amplification of the gRNA-Csy4 construct from plasmid pEX128-gRNAtemp. 
Reaction was composed of 1 µL of template plasmid (around 20 ng), 0.1 µL of Phusion Hot Start (HS) DNA 
==PAGE:29==
19 
 
polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific™), 0.3 µL DMSO, 2 µL of Buffer, 0.2 µL dNTPs 10 mM, 0.5 µL of each 
primer, and up to 10 µL with Mili-Q water. Goal was to insert 2 stop codons into the thiH gene, so 2 
different sgRNAs were being designed: for one, the primer pair thiH1-pos1-gg-for and 
Pri_CDV_878_gRNA-Position1-gg-rev were employed, and for the second gRNA, the primer pair was 
thiH2-pos2-gg-for and Pri_CDV_877_pnCas9-BsaI-Csy4-Rv. The thermocycler program is detailed in Table 
S4, with extension time of 15 secs and annealing temperature of 58°C. After verifying the PCR product by 
1% agarose gel electrophoresis, 1 μL of each sgRNA fragment was mixed in a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube and 
diluted 1:50 in Mili-Q water. Base-editing plasmid was constructed by Golden Gate Assembly according 
to the protocol outlined in Table S5. Reaction mixture was prepared by combining 2 μL sgRNA mix, 1 μL 
of 100 ng/μL pBEC-UGI-Csy4 (pnCas9-6-UGI-Csy4::Ptrc in Table 3), 1 μL 10X T4 ligase buffer (Thermo Fisher 
Scientific™), 0.5 μL of T4 DNA ligase (Thermo Fisher Scientific™) and of Eco31I restriction enzyme (Thermo

1% agarose gel electrophoresis were sent for sequencing to verify the assembly.  
Due to difficulties observed when cloning both sgRNAs into the pBEC plasmid, different strategies were 
followed: inserting just one of the two spacers, inserting both spacers as one whole fragment, and 
inserting a spacer as two complementary oligos. However, after performing all experiments and verifying 
ultimately the assembled plasmids by sequencing, cloning of any of the spacers was unsuccessful, 
preventing possible further thiH silencing.

3.2.2. Linear vs circular aptamers 
Aptamers can be designed in a linear or in a circular form, with the former approach being the simpler to 
create since no modifications need to be made to the RNA. However, as nucleic acids are prone to 
degradation by nucleases, linear aptamers present stability issues in vivo. Chemical modifications are 
typically performed to the aptamers to make them nuclease-resistant after they are created by in vitro 
selection or SELEX (Ellington & Szostak, 1990), like 3′-end capping with inverted thymidine (Guo et al., 
2011), conjugation with biotin (Dougan et al., 2000), 2′-F or 2′-NH2 ribose groups addition (Ni et al., 2017), 
phosphodiester linkage of DNA replaced with methylphosphonate or phosphorothioate analogue (Sacca 
et al., 2005), or oligonucleotide phosphodiester linkage replaced with triazole linkages (El-Sagheer & 
Brown, 2010). However, chemical modifications often require complicated organic synthesis, can reduce 
the activity of aptamers and produce undesired biological side effects (Kusser, 2000; Lee et al., 2005; 
Dellafiore et al., 2016). Furthermore, they are not feasible to introduce in vivo. 
Apart from chemically modifying the aptamers to render them nuclease-resistant, circularization of an 
existing linear aptamer is an attractive alternative for several reasons. First, a circular construct becomes 
completely resistant to exonucleases, a primary source for nucleic acids degradation, since they have no 
3’ or 5’ ends exposed to be acted upon (Guo et al., 2011). Second, circularization of a linear aptamer can 
increase its thermal stability, as well as being able to assist with aptamer internalization through cell 
membrane (Giusto et al., 2006). Moreover, circularization permits the use of natural/unmodified 
nucleotides thus avoiding potential toxicity associated with chemical modifications (Giusto & King, 2004). 
Finally, naturally occurring circular RNAs show half-lives that can be measured in days (Ashwal-Fluss et 
al., 2014), demonstrating the stabilizing effect of circularization.

4. Conclusions 
In this thesis, an optimized protocol for growth-coupled in vivo screening of improved bacterial 
fluorinases was established: induction of the strains with auto-induction media, growth for 24 h at 30°C, 
washing of the cells to remove all possible methionine present from LB media or other sources, sealing of 
growth plates with foil, addition of antibiotic to the media, and growth in M9 with SAM (300 µM) and 
with NaF (10 mM). Additionally, plating aliquots from the different incubated cultures and posterior 
colony PCR on metE, mmuM or yedA for verification of the strain. Subsequent cycles of selection must be 
performed with strains expressing different mutant fluorinases in order to discover enhanced 
biocatalysts. Moreover, long-term auxotrophy stability was verified since the selection strain was able to 
grow up to 8 days (minimum, since incubation was stopped afterwards). As for FDA toxicity, it was verified 
that FDA does not inhibit cell growth nor affects the carbon source consumption. Regarding the feasibility 
of this screening strategy, this could be improved once all possible sources of methionine contamination 
could be prevented. After overcoming the cross-contamination problems presented, the last in vivo 
assays carried out showed that the only impediment for acquiring an optimal screening method was the 
possible presence of methionine in the SAM aliquots. 
Regarding future prospects of this part of the thesis, focus should be put in the archaeal fluorinase 
(flAPtaU1), reported to present the highest reaction rate of all discovered fluorinases. Moreover, as for the 
flA mutant library (originated from pIP31), more rounds of EP-PCR should be done while changing 
different parameters in order to achieve a higher mutation rate. 
In this thesis, different circular and linear RNA aptamers with different expression systems were 
constructed as future biosensors to detect fluorinated compounds. In previous work, circular aptamers 
were only tested in mammalian cells, where they showed to be more stable. However, in bacteria, more 
endonucleases seem to be involved in RNA degradation since these were not working in vivo as efficiently 
as linear aptamers. Therefore, circulation might not be helpful. Generally, RNA aptamers are not as 
sensitive as commonly used protein biosensors, but probably strong promoters are needed for their 
expression since XylS-Pm system didn’t seem to be strong enough for the expression of these aptamers. 
As for future prospects, more in vivo testing with the aptamer containing the T7 system should be done 
in E. coli BL21, using IPTG as inducer and assessing different induction times. Moreover, further study of 
chemical modifications that can make the aptamers more stable could be done.
