Abstract  
Corporate biodiversity impact footprint has gained increasing attention due to the adoption of 
European Directive CSRD, which requires certain EU and non-EU companies to disclose their 
upstream, downstream, and direct impacts on biodiversity. However, this represents multiple 
challenges for businesses because of the complexity of the topic, the lack of standardised 
methods and metrics, and the lack of guidance of the CSRD and other corporate reporting 
guidelines. This study aims to create a framework for best- practices in corporate biodiversity 
impact assessment based on 1) legislative requirements, 2) metrics and methods suggested in 
scientific literature, and 3) feasibility for corporations covering only MRIO and LCA 
approaches.  To accomplish this, we conducted two literature reviews to understand the 
requirements and recommendations from CSRD and from the corporate biodiversity reporting 
guidelines TNFD and GRI, and to identify best-practices from the scientific community. 
Additionally, we performed a quality assessment to evaluate a selection of characterisation 
models in terms of their scientific robustness, applicability, documentation and transparency 
and stakeholder acceptance to try to answer objective 3.  Finally, we use an illustrative case 
study of a wind turbine manufacturer using an MRIO approach and compare two 
characterisation models that achieved a good score in our quality assessment: IMPACT World+ 
and Recipe 2016. The results of our literature review show that the choice of approach (process-
based LCA versus spend-base MRIO) and characterisation model can have a significant influence 
on the results. Based on our quality assessment we recommend companies the following 
characterisation models: Recipe 2016, IMPACT World + and LC-IMPACT. The results of the case 
study show that the choice of characterisation model has a significant influence on the 
contribution of midpoint impact categories to the biodiversity footprint of the downstream and 
upstream supply chain, and of the own operations. However, in this case, the choice of 
characterisation model does not affect the identification of product categories with the largest 
contribution to the biodiversity footprint of the upstream and downstream supply chains.

1 Introduction  
 
1.1 Corporate Biodiversity Reporting 
Destructive business practices are in part responsible for the current decline in nature and for 
the global mass extinction that our planet is experiencing. However, corporations also rely on a 
healthy planet to access resources and ecosystem services, either directly or through their 
supply chains (Zabey & Thissen, 2022). In fact, around half of the GDP at global level is dependent 
on nature for essential services and resources (World Economic Forum, 2020). 
Pressures for companies to disclose their impacts on biodiversity are increasing from customers, 
investors, and policymakers (United Nations, 2024), also as a response to the recently approved 
Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity framework (GBD) which sets global targets to halter 
biodiversity loss across the globe (Hughes & Grumbine, 2023). In the last years, multiple 
guidelines have been developed (or are still in development) to offer recommendations for 
corporate biodiversity reporting, such as the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), the International 
Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), the ISO/TC 331, The Task Force on Nature-related 
Financial Disclosures (TNFD) and the Science Base Targets for Nature (SBTN).  
At EU level, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), implemented in 2023, 
requires large European companies1, listed European SMEs (excluding micro-enterprises) and 
non-EU companies with significant market in EU, to assess financial risks and opportunities 
arising from sustainability-related issues, and to disclose their social and environmental impacts. 
The aim of the CSRD is to enhance the quality of corporate sustainability reporting to promote 
transparency and consistency of sustainability disclosures across EU companies, enabling 
investors, consumers and other stakeholders to access detailed sustainability information 
(European Commission, 2023; European Commission, 2024a).  The CSRD replaces the former 
Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) introducing stricter reporting requirements and 
covering more social and environmental aspects (Directive 2023/2772, Directive 2013/34/EU; 
European Parliament, 2022; European Commission, 2024a; European Commission, 2024b). 
Furthermore, the CSRD affects a broader set of companies. In total, around 50,000 companies 
are subject to this directive (PWC, 2024). Large European companies that are considered public-

1.2 The complexity of evaluating 
changes on biodiversity  
Although biodiversity is gaining increasing attention, it is an abstract and complex concept that 
entails different dimensions of nature (Bartkowski et al., 2015). Multiple definitions have been 
proposed in the literature, but the most widely used is the one adopted by the Convention on 
Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992), which describes biodiversity as: 
"The variability among living organisms from all sources, including, inter alia, terrestrial, 
marine, and other aquatic ecosystems, and the ecological complexes of which they are 
part; this includes diversity within species, between species, and of ecosystems." 
This definition emphasises the multidimensionality of biodiversity, encompassing genetic 
biodiversity (differences within species), species diversity (differences between species) and 
ecosystem diversity (differences between ecosystems) (Verones & Dorber, 2023).  
However, there are other alternative classifications of biodiversity proposed by the scientific 
community. For instance, Noss (1990) identifies three attributes of biodiversity: composition, 
structure and function. Composition refers to the variability and identity of different 
components of the ecosystems, such as genes, species, population, organism, etc; structure 
relates to the physical arrangement of an ecosystem; while function includes the variety of roles 
that organisms play in an ecosystem, for example nutrient cycling, energy flow or decomposition 
(Noss, 1900; Swigland, 2013). 
Another classification is proposed by Pereira et al. (2013), introducing the Essential Biodiversity 
Variables (EBV) classes as key areas of biodiversity change: genetic composition, species 
population, species traits, community composition, ecosystem structure, and ecosystem 
function. The definition for each EBVs class is included in Table 1. 
Marques et al. (2021) also highlight two other important aspects of biodiversity: ecosystem 
multifunctionality and species extinction. Ecosystem multifunctionality refers to the ability of an 
ecosystem to provide multiple functions or services simultaneously (Mouillot et al., 2011). 
Species extinction is understood as the irreversible loss of species at the global level due to

Following a life cycle assessment perspective, biodiversity change is identified as an endpoint 
impact category, since represents the final consequences of various pressures on the natural 
environment (Huijbregts et al., 2017; Benini et al., 2014). These pressures are called drivers of 
biodiversity change. There are five key direct drivers of biodiversity change: land-use change, 
climate change, invasive alien species, over-exploitation3 and pollution4. Indirect drivers of 
biodiversity change are pressures such as population growth or human resource use (IPBES, 
2019; Pereira et al, 2012; Millenium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005). 
Due to the different aspects of biodiversity, the multiple definitions, and its geographical 
variability, there are multiple methods and metrics proposed in the scientific literature for the 
assessment of biodiversity change. However, there is no globally acceptable metric that can 
capture all the dimensions of biodiversity change (Verones & Dorber, 2023). Instead, there are 
multiple metrics proposed by the scientific community. In fact, Burgess et al. (2024) have 
documented a total of 573 metrics just related to the measure of changes in terrestrial 
biodiversity. 
1.3. Research problem and aims  
While the CSRD represents a major step towards greater transparency on corporate biodiversity 
reporting in the European Union, it also entails several challenges for businesses. Biodiversity 
was not covered by the previous NFRD directive and is an unexplored area for most companies 
(Directive 2013/34/EU; Blanco-Zaitegi et al., 2024; Alder et al., 2018; Addison et al., 2018).  
 
3 Over-exploitation encompasses harvesting, logging, fishing and hunting activities (IPBES, 2019). 
4  There are different types of pollution sources, such as greenhouse gases, untreated waste and 
contaminants from residential areas, as well as industrial and agricultural activities. Eutrophication is one 
of the main negative effects of pollution on the natural environment (IPBES,2019; Pereira et al., 2013). 
IPBES (2025) also recognises acidification as another negative effect of pollution on the environment.

In addition, selecting the right measure of biodiversity impacts is not an easy task. The existence 
of a large number of tools, methods and metrics to quantify changes in biodiversity as well as 
the complexity of the concept itself and the different definitions and classifications proposed by 
the scientific community, could result in an overwhelming process for companies. Although 
companies can follow the recommendations of the guidelines and of the CSRD directive, these 
do not share a unified approach and there is a lack of standardised methods and metrics for 
corporate biodiversity reporting, even within the CSRD (Elliot et al., 2024).  
Therefore, businesses are in need of more detailed guidance to comply with legislation, to 
successfully report their impacts on biodiversity, and to define a transition plan to mitigate their 
impacts.  
Thus, the aim of this master’s thesis is to provide a framework for best-practice in corporate 
assessment based on 1) legislative requirements, 2) metrics and methods suggested in scientific 
literature, and 3) feasibility for corporations. 
To do this, the following objectives will be answered:  
1. Review the current legal requirements and guidelines for corporate biodiversity reporting 
and gain an overview of the level of reporting and of the methods and metrics that are 
applied.  
2. Critically review current methods for quantifying impacts on biodiversity included in the 
scientific literature to get an overview of the advantages and disadvantages of methods and 
metrics proposed in the literature, their application context, and implications.  
3. Develop a set of criteria to evaluate and suggest best-practice principles for corporate 
biodiversity reporting and assessment based on the legislation and scientific methods 
previously reviewed.  
4. Test the recommended approach from objective (3) using an illustrative case study of a wind 
turbine manufacturer using a spend-based approach.  
5. Provide recommendations for further operationalisation and implementation of the 
proposed approach. 
1.4 Scope of the study  
Corporate biodiversity reporting is a broad topic and encompasses different areas, such as the 
assessment of impacts, evaluation of risks and opportunities and development of a strategy to 
reduce the biodiversity footprint (Directive 2023/2772; GRI, 2024; TNFD, 2023a) Nevertheless, 
this project will only be concerned with the evaluation of impacts of companies on biodiversity, 
while the remaining topics are considered out of the scope.  
Numerous methods and metrics have been proposed in the literature to assess changes in 
biodiversity. Nevertheless, it is unrealistic to cover all these methodologies and metrics. Instead, 
in this study we focused only on methodologies that measure biodiversity change as an endpoint 
impact category. Specifically, we covered Multi-Regional Input-Output (MRIO) and Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) approaches which are typically used to trace biodiversity impacts across 
supply chains (Verones & Dorber. 2023; Marques et al., 2017). Environmental Impact 
Assessment (EIA) methods and other site-specific approaches were excluded from this study.

2 Methods 
To develop a framework for best-practices in corporate biodiversity assessment different 
methodologies were used (see Figure 1).  First, two literature reviews were conducted to (1) 
understand the legal requirements and recommendations from corporate biodiversity 
guidelines and legislation and (2) identify methods, metrics and datasets recommended in the 
scientific community. The reviews revealed that the CSRD and existing guidelines provided 
limited guidance on the choice of metric, approach (MRIO or LCA), and characterisation 
method.  While the scientific literature provides some indications on whether to use MRIO or 
LCA approaches to determine environmental flows across the supply chains in biodiversity 
impact assessments, it does not indicate which characterisation models should be preferred to 
translate environmental flows into impacts on biodiversity.    
To address this gap, we performed a quality assessment of a selection of characterisation models 
based on the following criteria: scientific robustness, documentation and transparency, 
applicability and stakeholder acceptance. The key learnings from the literature reviews helped 
us to build these criteria. Additionally, the learnings from the literature reviews and the quality 
assessment were used to develop a set of guiding principles for corporate biodiversity impact 
assessment, which were tested through an illustrative case study of a wind turbine 
manufacturer. In this case study, we determined the biodiversity impacts across the company’s 
value chain and own operations using a spend-based approach and comparing two of the 
characterisation models that achieved a high score in the quality assessment. The learnings from 
this case study were also used to redefine and improve the guiding principles, which are included 
in the conclusion of this report.

assessment from the scientific community. The search particularly focused on studies involving 
LCA and MRIO approaches due to their increasing use to assess corporate environmental 
impacts, such as carbon footprint (Emborg et al., 2025), and biodiversity impacts at product and

target papers focusing on MRIO methods.

2.1.2 Review of legislation and guidelines 
An extensive review of the current legal requirements as well as the guidelines for corporate 
biodiversity reporting was performed. The aim of this review was to investigate if there were 
specific methods and metrics that were mandatory or preferred to follow when reporting 
impacts on biodiversity.

2.2  Quality assessment of 
characterisation models 
The aim of this section was to define a quality assessment to evaluate the performance of 
characterisation models based on the following criteria: scientific robustness, documentation 
and transparency, applicability, and stakeholder acceptance. These criteria were adapted from 
JRC (2010) and Hauschild et al. (2011), which provide a framework for evaluating life cycle 
impact assessment (LCIA) characterisation models. Additionally, key learnings from our 
literature reviews were also used to build these criteria.  
Then we conducted the quality assessment on a selection of characterisation models. We used 
the research of Damiani et al. (2023) to identify endpoint characterisation models.  Damiani et 
al. (2023) reviewed a selection of LCA and beyond LCA end-point methods5  (including corporate 
biodiversity footprint tools) based on the coverage of different biodiversity aspects. In addition 
to the methods reviewed by Damiani et al. (2023), we also included in our assessment the 
GLOBIO4 model (both the terrestrial and aquatic models) (Janse et al., 2015; Schipper, 2020) 
since it has been integrated in multiple corporate biodiversity tools and case studies for 
biodiversity impact assessment (Lammerant et al., 2024).

2.2.1 Description of the evaluation criteria 
2.2.1.1 Scientific Robustness 
The criteria used to evaluate the scientific robustness of the selected methods were adapted 
from Damiani et al. (2023). These criteria include the coverage of ecosystems, coverage of direct 
drivers of biodiversity change, coverage of Essential Biodiversity Variables (EBV) classes, 
coverage of taxonomy groups and coverage of fundamental aspects of biodiversity, which 
Damiani et al. (2023) considered to be the measure of global species extinction and multi-
functionality. Additionally, as an outcome of our literature review, we added another relevant 
attribute to these criteria: the use of spatially differentiated characterisation factors. Note that 
the driver of biodiversity loss ‘pollution’ was further split into eutrophication and acidification, 
since these impact categories have been identified as main effects of pollutants in the natural 
environment (IPBES, 2025).  
2.2.1.2 Documentation and transparency 
The criteria for evaluating the documentation and transparency of the methods were adapted 
from Hauschild et al. (2011) and JRC (2010). Three aspects were assessed within this category 
First, the availability of the method's documentation to the public was evaluated. Second, it was 
determined whether this documentation was accessible in English. Third, we investigated if the 
characterisation factors (CFs) for the model were publicly available.  
2.2.1.3 Applicability  
The applicability criteria were adapted from Hauschild et al. (2011) and JRC (2010). First, we 
assessed the methods based on their coverage of economic sectors. Cross-sectoral models 
fulfilled this criterion, whereas methods targeted at a specific sector did not. 
Second, we evaluated whether the method could be fully integrated into the main MRIO and 
LCA datasets identified in our literature review (EXIOBASE and Ecoinvent). Models relying on 
input data not covered by these datasets—such as information on hunting or land 
fragmentation—did not meet this criterion. 
Third, we examined whether an additional modelling step was required to access the CFs of the 
models. Models requiring an extra step, such as the use of a GIS-based tool, did not fulfil this 
criterion. 
Additionally, we assessed the implementation of characterisation models into relevant LCA 
software, such as SimaPro and Sphera, as well as corporate biodiversity tools identified by 
Lammerant et al. (2024). Models already integrated into these LCA software or into corporate 
tools met this criterion. Furthermore, methods for which developers provided data packages 
that could be directly implemented into LCA software—without requiring data manipulation—
also fulfilled this criterion. 
2.2.1.4 Stakeholder acceptance 
The stakeholder acceptance criteria were adopted from Hauschild et al. (2011) and JRC (2010). 
First, we assessed whether the metrics derived from the models aligned with legislation and 
guidelines for corporate biodiversity reporting and were recommended by these frameworks. 
Second, we examined whether a public body was responsible for the method and actively 
maintained and updated it.

2.3 Case study  
2.3.1 Description of the virtual company  
To illustrate the usefulness of the framework, we built a virtual case study of an onshore wind 
turbine manufacturer company that we named ‘Green Turbines’. We assumed that Green 
Turbines had around 30000 employees and that its production and other main activities (such 
as research and development and sales) were located in Denmark and the United States of 
America (USA). During the financial year of 2024, the company sold a total of 3100 wind turbines, 
where 50% were installed in USA, 25% in Denmark and the rest 25% in Germany.  Figure 2 shows 
a representation of Green Turbines’ own operations, upstream and downstream supply chain. 
The company’s activities consist mainly of the manufacture of wind turbines, design and sales 
activities, transport of the wind turbines to the sites and installation of wind turbines. The usage, 
decommission and end-of-life treatment of wind turbines were part of the downstream supply 
chain, while the extraction of raw materials, and remelting and refining activities were part of 
the upstream supply chain. 
Green Turbines was subjected to the CSRD and wants to carry out a first screening of its 
biodiversity footprint (of own operations and value chain) of the the financial year 2024 and to 
identify hotspots. Due to the lack of guidance of CSRD and other reporting guidelines, the 
company was unsure of which methodology and data should be used for this assessment.

2.3.2 Biodiversity impact assessment  
To assess the biodiversity footprint of Green Turbines, we applied a spend-based approach using 
the MRIO database EXIOBASE v3.8 for the year 2022 (Stadler et al., 2018) to determine 
environmental flows across the value chain. MRIO calculations were performed in Python, 
following the consumption-based Leontief model (Leontief, 1987; Miller et al., 2009). We chose 
a spend-based approach as it allows for a quick screening of impacts, enabling us to focus more 
on the comparison of characterisation models. Specifically, we compared the results using two 
models that received higher overall scores in the quality assessment, Recipe 2016 and IMPACT 
World+, to identify potential differences in the outcomes. 
To integrate EXIOBASE with IMPACT World+, we used the IMPACT World+ v 2.0.1 extension for 
EXIOBASE (Agez et al., 2023), which relies exclusively on global CFs. To integrate Recipe 2016, 
we extracted the CFs from the Dutch National Institute for Public Health and the Environment 
(2016) and manually matched them to the environmental flows of EXIOBASE.  The 
‘Supplementary Materials II’ includes the calculations used to couple Recipe 2016 CFs to 
EXIOBASE environmental flows.  To ensure comparability, we also used global CFs for Recipe 
2016. 
Although IMPACT Word+ and Recipe 2016 use metrics derived from PDF to measure endpoint 
biodiversity impacts, their units are not directly comparable. While IMPACT Word+ employs the 
unit 𝑃𝐷𝐹∗𝑦𝑒𝑎𝑟∗𝑚2, Recipe uses 𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑐𝑖𝑒𝑠∗𝑚2 (Bulle et al., 2019; Huijbregts et al.,2016).  
Because of this reason, we could not compare the biodiversity footprint of our company in 
absolute terms.  
 
2.3.3 Scope and data assumptions 
To build this illustrative case study, we used publicly available data from wind turbine 
manufacturers, particularly from Vestas’ 2022 sustainability report (Vestas, 2023a) and Vestas’ 
annual report (Vestas 2023b). These sources were used to gather data regarding material and 
energy consumption, waste generated and financial information on properties, equipment, 
leases, etc., helping us to build the annual expenditure of our company (see Figure 3).  
Regarding own operations, we used Vestas’ information on scope 1 CO2 emissions and water 
withdrawal of 2022. Additionally, we estimated the land use of our company based on the land 
use from Vestas’ factories.  
Data related to downstream activities, such as end-of-life transport and waste treatment, was 
estimated from the LCAs studies of wind turbines (Vestas, 2022). The lack of clear guidance in 
the CSRD, TNFD, and GRI regarding scope created uncertainties about which downstream 
activities to include. For example, should the construction of transformer foundations in wind 
farms be considered, given that they are not manufactured by our company but are essential 
for wind turbine operation? Since we were unsure, we decided to include these activities in our 
scope.  
Similarly, uncertainty arose regarding the recycling of metals at the end of their life cycle. Since 
recycling generates revenue rather than costs for companies that create metal scrap, it was 
unclear how to model this in MRIO. We decided to exclude these recycling activities, assuming 
that the environmental burdens of the recycling process would be allocated to the company 
==PAGE:22==

purchasing the secondary steel. This applied to recycling activities at the end-of-life of the wind 
turbines, but also to the recycling of the metal scrap generated during Green Turbines 
manufacturing activities. Other waste treatment activities, such as landfill and incineration, 
were included in our scope, as waste generators typically bear the cost of disposal. 
We also accounted for the land use of wind turbines during their use phase as a downstream 
burden. This was estimated using a land surface requirement of 0.047 MW/km² (Rinne et al., 
2018). Additionally, we estimated CO₂ emissions from the transportation of end-of-life waste. 
Finally, physical units were converted into monetary units using price conversions obtained 
through Google searches. Also, to determine the country where the materials were purchased, 
we used sources such as the EC Dashboard's ‘Raw Materials Information System,’ which provides 
information on the top producing and exporting countries of specific materials (EC, 2025). 
More details on our assumptions and data sources can be found in ‘Supplementary Material III’.

3 Results 
3.1 Literature reviews 
3.1.1 Scientific literature review   
LCA and MRIO are recognised as suitable approaches for assessing biodiversity footprints at the 
consumption, product, and corporate levels (Verones & Dorber. 2023; Marques et al., 2017; 
Crenna et al., 2020). These frameworks have been also extensively used in corporate contexts 
to evaluate greenhouse gas emissions across value chains (Schmidt et al., 2022; Emborg et al., 
2025).  
Although through this review we have identified various case studies that focused on measuring 
consumption-based and production-based biodiversity impacts, such as Sanyé-Mengual et al. 
(2023) and Kyttä et al. (2023), no single case study at business level was found. Nevertheless, 
Damiani et. al (2023) and Lammerat et al. (2024) identified multiple biodiversity impact 
assessment tools intended for the business domain that are also based on LCA and MRIO 
approaches.  
To get a better understanding of these approaches, it is important to differentiate between 
background datasets or inventories and characterisation models. MRIO datasets and LCA 
inventories can be used to quantify environmental flows across complex supply chains. Then, to 
evaluate the biodiversity impacts of these environmental flows, an endpoint characterisation 
model must be applied (Crenna et al., 2020). Characterisation models consist of a number of 
characterisation factors that translate individual environmental flows into impacts on 
biodiversity, which are measured using a specific metric derived from the model (Guinee et al., 
2022). While MRIO approaches rely on spend-based (i.e., monetary) data and transactional 
flows across different sectors and countries, LCA approaches use process-based (i.e., physical) 
data (Emborg et al., 2025; Verones & Dorber, 2023). The environmental flows are then derived 
by either LCA, MRIO or a combination of both (hybrid approaches). Figure 4 represents the 
method architecture behind these approaches.

In our review, we identified case studies and tools that use LCA approaches to determine 
environmental flows across supply chains, such as Sanyé-Mengual et al. (2023). Other studies 
and tools apply MRIO approaches, for instance, the Biodiversity Impact Assessment Tool (IBAT) 
(CISL, 2020; Lammerant et al., 2024) and Wilting & van Oorschot (2017). Additionally, some tools 
combine both MRIO and LCA data, such as the Global Biodiversity Score (CDC Biodiversité, 2020; 
Lammerant et al., 2024). EXIOBASE (Stadler et al., 2018) and Ecoinvent (Ecoinvent, 2025) are the 
main datasets that were identified in our review.  
Regarding the characterisation model, some studies and tools use life cycle impact assessment 
(LCIA) models derived from the LCA context, such as Recipe 2016 (Huijbregts et al., 2016), LC-
Impact (Verones et al., 2020) or IMPACT World+ (Bulle et al., 2019) to translate environmental 
flows into impacts on biodiversity at endpoint level. This is the case of the IBAT tool (CISL, 2020; 
Lammerant et al., 2024). In contrast, other case studies and tools used GLOBIO (Janse et al., 
2015; Schipper et al., 2020) as characterisation model, such as Wilting & van Oorschot (2017) or 
the Biodiversity Footprint Methodology (BFM) (Van Rooij et al. (2016); Lammerant et al., 2024).  
Additionally, we can identify different methodological combinations:  
• 
Methods that combine LCA approaches with LCIA models (e.g., Sanyé-Mengual et al., 
2023) 
• 
Methods that integrate LCA datasets with the GLOBIO model (e.g., Sanyé-Mengual et 
al., 2023) 
• 
Methods that couple MRIO datasets with LCIA models (e.g., the IBAT tool from CISL, 
2020) 
• 
Methods that combine MRIO approaches with the GLOBIO model (e.g., Wilting & van 
Oorschot; 2017)

GLOBIO is a model that relies on geospatial and ecological data and was primarily developed for 
scenario modelling and for evaluating the effects of policy interventions. However, it has also 
been used for supply chain accounting (GLOBIO, 2025). It is important to note that GLOBIO does 
not directly translate environmental flows into endpoint impacts. Instead, it translates midpoint 
pressures (e.g., climate change or land fragmentation) into endpoint impacts. Therefore, 
approaches that use GLOBIO require an additional characterisation method to translate 
environmental flows into midpoint pressures.  Furthermore, certain midpoint pressures that are 
used in the GLOBIO model such as road disturbance or hunting are not considered in the LCIA 
characterisation models.  
While GLOBIO uses Mean Species Abundance (MSA) as its metric (GLOBIO, 2025), many 
biodiversity footprint LCIA models use the Potential Disappearance Fraction (PDF) (Damiani et 
al., 2023). MSA measures the mean abundance of original species in a disturbed environment 
relative to their abundance in undisturbed ecosystems, and it falls under the species population 
EBV class (Crenna et al., 2020). In contrast, PDF represents the ratio between species loss and 
species richness in a given area and falls under the community composition EBV class (Crenna 
et al., 2020). However, both MSA and PDF have limitations, as they only cover one of the six EBV 
classes, a common criticism raised by researchers (Marques et al., 2017; Crenna et al., 2020), 
highlighting that they do not fully capture the complexity of biodiversity. 
The choice of characterisation models and impact metrics can influence the outcomes of 
biodiversity impact assessments. For instance, Sanyé-Mengual et al. (2023) evaluated the 
biodiversity footprint of EU consumption using the same LCA inventory but applying different 
characterization models, including Ecological Scarcity 2013, IMPACT World+, LC-Impact, Recipe 
2016, Stepwise, two GLOBIO-derived approaches, and land-use-specific methods. Results 
demonstrated that the top three inventory processes contributing to the overall biodiversity 
impacts of EU consumption varied depending on the characterisation model used. Another 
example is the study carried out by Kyttä et al. (2023), which compares the biodiversity impacts 
of Finish diets obtained using two characterisation methods: Land use intensity specific 
biodiversity footprint (LUIS) and Habitat conservation and fragmentation (HFC). The outcomes 
of Kyttä et al. (2023) show that the choice of method can affect which products appear the most 
burdensome.  
According to the literature, an essential factor to adequately assess changes in biodiversity is 
the use of spatially differentiated characterisation factors and spatially detailed datasets. These 
are essential for capturing the variability of elements that affect biodiversity, such as climate, 
ecosystem type or species diversity (Verones et al., 2020; Marques et al., 2017). Marques et al. 
(2021) emphasise that although certain characterisation models have been highly regionalised, 
such as LC-Impact or IMPACT World+, more detailed spatially differentiated datasets are needed 
to fully make use of these developments.  
 On the other hand, Damiani et al. (2023) highlight the relevance of covering different elements 
of biodiversity: direct drivers of biodiversity loss, EBV classes, taxonomy groups, and 
incorporation of ecosystem multifunctionality and global species extinction aspects. The paper 
critically reviews a selection of LCA and beyond LCA methods based on the fulfilments of these 
elements. Although some of the methods have a higher coverage of biodiversity, such as LC-
Impact, IMPACT World+ or Recipe 2016 there is no method that can cover all aspects of 
biodiversity assessed by Damiani et al. (2023).  
==PAGE:26==

26 
 
Moreover, the selection of the dataset can also affect the impact assessment results. For 
instance, Steubing et al. (2022) identified substantial differences between the carbon footprint 
of products included in the LCA database Ecoinvent (Ecoinvent, 2025) and the carbon footprint 
of the matching products from the MRIO database EXIOBASE (Stadler et al., 2018) even though 
the same characterisation factors were used. For half of the matched products, their carbon 
footprint differentiated in a factor of two or more. These differences could be due to 
methodological differences underlying the dataset (for instance, the system boundaries and the 
approaches used to solve multifunctionality), differences in data, and choices made during the 
manual matching of products and processes.  
Additionally, Emborg et al. (2025) compare the use process-based LCA and spend-based MRIO 
to calculate the upstream GHG emissions under the category ‘Goods and Services’ in the 
petroleum industry using the same characterisation method. The outcomes show a difference 
of a factor four between the results of both approaches. Some reasons behind their findings are 
highlighted, such as: (a) the low sector granularity of the MRIO datasets compared to LCA data, 
(b) potential errors of assigning products to a ‘general’ product category, (c) price fluctuations 
and (d) due to the lack of information on products providers. The study strongly recommends 
the use of process-based LCA over MRIO when assessing impacts at the level of product group 
if detailed product-level data is available. Even though MRIO is less time-consuming and has 
better coverage of the supply chains, it also entails a decrease in accuracy of the results if it is 
compared to a process LCA.  
To enhance the accuracy of MRIO approaches, Moran et al. (2016), which investigates the 
suitability of MRIO methods for evaluating biodiversity footprints at the product level, 
recommend using hybrid approaches to increase the economic sectoral detail. Although MRIO 
approaches can be very attractive for enterprises due to their low cost and time efficiency 
(Moran et al., 2016; Emborg et al., 2025); adding key LCA information to improve the granularity 
of the data could lead to more accurate and reliable biodiversity impact assessment results 
without significantly increasing the work-efforts (Moran et al., 2016). 
3.1.2 Review of legislation and guidelines 
The outcomes of this review show that the CSRD presents a lack of guidance for the assessment 
of biodiversity footprints, leaving companies with significant flexibility. The only main “strict” 
requirement that this directive establishes is that companies must address impacts across the 
value chain (upstream and downstream) and impacts of own operations. Moreover, the CSRD 
does not offer specific direction regarding the choice of metric and method. However, the 
directive provides an overview of the type of data that enterprises can use for the assessment 
of their biodiversity impacts: (a) primary data collected in-situ, (b) secondary data from 
geospatial data layers and (3) model-based data.   
TNFD and GRI offer slightly more detailed guidance than the CSRD (see comparison in Table 7). 
TNFD aligns with CSRD and recommends the assessment of upstream, downstream and own 
operations impacts. Besides, the guideline provides a list of metrics as illustrative examples to 
measure changes in biodiversity. Some of these metrics are derived from specific methods. For 
instance, TNFD (2023b) explains that MSA is derived from the GLOBIO model and PDF from LCIA 
methods. In terms of data, TNFD recommends the use of primary data, such as internal business 
data or data collected from suppliers and customers. However, other data sources could be used 
including model-data, third-party data or proxy-data, such as LCA datasets, industry and 
government reports, and other peer-review and grey literature.

also gives as an example a set of metrics that could be used for the measure of impacts of

3.1.3 Key messages 
The scientific literature shows that the choice of characterisation model, metrics and data has a 
significant impact on the results of the biodiversity impact assessment (Steubing et al. 2022; 
Kyttä et al., 2023; Sanyé-Mengual et al., 2023; Emborg et al., 2025). Nevertheless, both 
legislation and guidelines present a lack of detailed direction regarding these aspects. The 
outcomes of the scientific literature indicate that both MRIO and LCA are suitable approaches 
for biodiversity impact assessment. While MRIO approaches are less time-consuming and 
provide good coverage of the supply chain, they lack economic granularity and are 
recommended to be used for a ‘first screening’ of the impacts (Emborg et al., 2025; Moran et 
al., 2016). Combining MRIO data with LCA data (hybrid approaches), helps to increase the 
accuracy and reliability of the results without adding much workload. In terms of accuracy, LCA 
approaches are preferred, however, they are in general more time demanding than MRIO or 
hybrid MRIO approaches (Emborg et al., 2025; Moran et al., 2016).   
In contrast, the gaps in the legislation and guidelines in terms of the choice of characterisation 
model and metrics are not covered by the best practices from the scientific literature.  Although 
Damiani et al. (2023) investigates the coverage of different biodiversity elements of a selection 
of LCIA and beyond LCIA methods and tools, this study does not consider the use of spatially 
differentiated characterisation factors, a relevant aspect identified in the scientific literature 
(Verones et al., 2020; Marques et al., 2017). In addition, other important aspects defined by JRC 
(2010) for the review of characterisation methods, are not assessed in this study, such as 
documentation and transparency, applicability and stakeholder acceptance. The EU Business 
and Biodiversity Platform has also developed a guideline to help companies choose the right 
tool for the assessment of their biodiversity impacts. Nevertheless, the methods behind these 
tools are not assessed in detail (Lammerant et al., 2024).  
3.2 Quality assessment of 
characterisation models 
The outcomes of the quality assessment of the characterisation models are shown in Table 8. 
Additionally, the ‘Supplementary Material I’ offers more detailed information on sources used 
for this assessment and on the results.  
As presented, none of the assessed models achieved a high score based on the ‘scientific 
robustness’ criterion, and only Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT, IMPACT World+, GEP, PBF, and GLOBIO 
received a medium score. This is because none of the models fully covered all aspects of 
biodiversity considered in our assessment. For instance, EcoScarcity 2013, BV, FDP, BioMass, 
HCF, LI, LUIS, FFP, and LUCI-LCA addressed only land use—one of the five drivers of biodiversity 
change—and focused solely on terrestrial ecosystems. Other models that target aquatic 
ecosystems, such as HCP, WT, FSR and EI, did not cover any of the direct drivers of biodiversity 
change. PBF was the only method that addressed the five drivers while GLOBIO addressed all 
except for invasive alien species. Recipe 2016, LC-Impact, IMPACT World+ and GEP only covered 
pollution, climate change and land use.  
==PAGE:29==

In terms of taxonomy groups, most models performed poorly. IMPACT World+ had the best 
coverage, addressing 15 out of 17 taxonomy groups, followed by GEP and PBF, which included 
16 groups, and LC-IMPACT, which covered 10. The remaining models addressed fewer than half 
of the taxonomy groups that were considered in our assessment. 
Additionally, most models focused on only one of the six Essential Biodiversity Variables (EBV) 
classes, either community composition (mainly frameworks using the PDF metric) or species 
population (primarily models using MSA as a metric). BV covered two EBV classes, FDP 
addressed three, and HCP covered four. 
All methods used spatially differentiated characterisation factors (CFs) except for Stepwise and 
FFD. In terms of fundamental aspects, only a few models measured global species extinction 
(HCF, GEP, WT, and FSR) and multifunctionality (FPD, LUCI-LCA, and GLOBIO). 
Under ‘documentation and transparency,’ most models met all criteria. However, BioMass, HCP, 
EI, and PBF did not fulfil this requirement because their characterisation factors were not 
publicly available.  
For ‘applicability,’ Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT, IMPACT World+, Stepwise, and EcoScarcity 2013 
satisfied all four assessment aspects that were evaluated.  BV, FPD, HCF, LUIS, and FSR covered 
only three attributes, as they were not integrated into relevant LCA tools or corporate 
biodiversity tools. The remaining models fulfilled two or fewer aspects. For instance, GLOBIO 
received a medium applicability score because it cannot be fully integrated with LCA and MRIO 
datasets. This is because the model uses certain input data, such as information on hunting and 
land fragmentation, that is not covered in datasets such as Ecoinvent or EXIOBASE. Additionally, 
an extra modelling step in a GIS-tool is needed in order to extract or aggregate GLOBIO’s CFs, 
lowering its applicability. A similar extra modelling step is needed for the GEP and WT 
characterisation models. Furthermore, methods without publicly available CFs failed to meet 
the requirements for ‘integration into relevant LCA or MRIO databases’ and ‘no additional 
modelling step for CF extraction and aggregation’ due to the lack of available data. All methods 
fulfilled the cross-sectoral criterion, except for LUCI-LCA, which is specifically designed for 
agricultural activities. Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT, IMPACT World+, Stepwise, EcoScarcity 2013, 
PBF, and GLOBIO were the only models integrated into relevant LCA or corporate biodiversity 
tools. 
For ‘stakeholder acceptance,’ five characterization models—Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT, IMPACT 
World+, GLOBIO, and EcoScarcity 2013—achieved a high score. These models are supported by 
a public body that continuously updates them and use a metric (MSA or PDF) included in TNFD 
or GRI guidelines. In contrast, Stepwise, BV, FPD, HCP, FFP, and EI did not meet these two 
attributes, receiving a low score. The remaining models achieved a medium score. 
Looking at the overall scores, Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT, and IMPACT World+ received a high 
score, while EI, LUCI-LCA, and BioMass scored low. The remaining models received medium 
scores. 
Finally, ‘scientific robustness’ proved to be the most challenging criterion to fulfil, as none of the 
models achieved a high score in this category. In contrast, ‘documentation and transparency’ 
was the most feasible criterion, with 16 out of 20 models receiving a high score.

3.3 Case study 
The results of the impact assessment show that the contribution of the upstream and 
downstream supply chains and own operations to the total biodiversity footprint is mostly equal 
regardless of the choice of the characterisation model (Figure 5).  Upstream impacts represent 
around 80% of the total impacts, while downstream impacts contribute to the other 20%. 
Impacts of own operations are neglectable when comparing them with the total impacts.

Regarding the contribution of different midpoint pressures, we can appreciate some differences 
when comparing the two models (see Figure 6 and Figure 7). In both approaches, climate change 
is the major contributor to upstream activities (52-53%), downstream activities (52-53%) and 
own operations (90-99%). However, there is a significant difference in the contribution of 
climate change to own operations, varying from 90% (using IMPACT World+) to 99% (using 
Recipe 2016).  In both approaches, acidification is the second largest contributor to upstream 
activities and downstream activities, but we can identify some differences in the results 
obtained by both approaches. In the case of IMPACT Word+ acidification contributes with 42-
45% to both downstream and upstream activities, while when we use Recipe 2016, acidification 
contributes with 35-36% to the upstream and downstream impacts.  Additionally, acidification 
is also the second largest contributor to own operations (8.3%) when we use IMPACT World+, 
while when using Recipe 2016 results show that acidification does not have a contribution to 
the impacts of own operations. 
Furthermore, we can appreciate some differences regarding the contribution of toxicity 
categories and land use. In the case of IMPACT World+, land use has a contribution between 1.5 
and 2.2% to the upstream, downstream and direct impacts. However, in the case of Recipe 2016, 
the contribution of land use is almost neglectable. Moreover, Ecotoxicity has a contribution of 
3.4% when using Recipe, to both upstream and downstream impacts. Nevertheless, the 
contribution of ecotoxicity when using IMPACT Word+ corresponds to 0.5% for both upstream 
and downstream activities. Additionally, photochemical ozone formation is considered an 
impact category in Recipe 2016, while this impact category is not taken into account in the 
IMPACT Word+ model. In the results obtained with Recipe 2016, we can see that photochemical 
ozone formation has a contribution of 7.5% to the downstream and upstream impacts. 
When looking at the contribution of different product categories to the upstream and 
downstream impacts, results are very similar when comparing IMPACT World + and Recipe 2016. 
Regarding upstream emissions, the product category ‘Basic iron and steel and of ferro-alloys and 
first products thereof’ has the largest contribution to both upstream and downstream 
operations representing more than 98.5% of the total impacts (see Figure 8 and Figure 9).

4 Discussion  
4.1 Implications of the framework  
The lack of clear guidance of the CSRD on biodiversity corporate reporting presents significant 
challenges for companies subject to this directive. These challenges include selecting 
appropriate methods and metrics to assess biodiversity impacts across their value chain and 
own operations. This framework addresses these gaps by providing detailed guidance on the 
selection of characterization models and background data. It incorporates learnings from 
scientific literature and includes a quality assessment of various characterization models, 
evaluating their scientific robustness, documentation and transparency, applicability, and 
stakeholder acceptance. By reviewing relevant guidelines and the CSRD itself, we ensure that 
the framework remains aligned with legislative requirements. Additionally, the framework is 
illustrated through a case study of a wind manufacturing company, demonstrating its practical 
application and hotspot identification.  
4.2 Limitations of the study  
This study presents the following limitations:  
• 
Although the outcomes of the scientific literature enchasing the use of spatially 
differentiated characterisation factors and LCA or hybrid approaches to increase the 
accuracy and reliability of the results, time constraints led us to adopt an MRIO approach 
with global characterisation factors for our case study. This decision may have 
implications, such as potential oversimplification of biodiversity impacts, which could 
also affect the comparison of the results obtained using IMPACT World+ and Recipe 
2016. 
• 
While the illustrative case study demonstrates the applicability of the framework, its 
outcomes may not fully reflect real-world conditions. The lack of real data introduces 
uncertainties, particularly in assumptions related to translating physical units into 
monetary values. Additionally, the lack of clear guidance of the CSRD, TNFD, and GRI 
regarding the scope created uncertainties about which downstream activities to 
include. Thus, some sensitivity analysis should have been carried out to understand, for 
instance, the implications of including or excluding the foundations and transformers in 
the scope of the assessment. Moreover, a more exhaust investigation should have been 
done about how to model recycling activities in a spend-based approach.  
• 
In our case study, we did not investigate the implications of using different metrics, since 
both IMPACT WORLD+ and Recipe 2016 use metrics derived from PDF. Since many 
corporate biodiversity footprint tools included GLOBIO as characterisation model 
(Lammerant et al., 2024), which uses MSA, it would have been relevant to include also 
GLOBIO in our biodiversity impact assessment.

5 Conclusion  
This study presents the following conclusions:  
• 
The CSRD, TNFD and GRI leave companies with significant flexibility when carrying out 
the assessments. The only ‘strict’ requirement of CSRD is that companies must assess 
their impacts across their upstream and downstream supply chains and own operations. 
MRIO and LCA are suitable approaches to comply with the regulations and guidelines.  
• 
The review of the scientific literature shows that the choice of a characterisation model 
can have an impact on the results (Steubing et al. 2022; Kyttä et al., 2023; Sanyé-
Mengual et al., 2023; Emborg et al., 2025). In our case study, we can see that the choice 
of characterisation model leads to some differences regarding midpoint pressures 
contribution but does not influence the identification of product categories with the 
largest biodiversity footprints.  
• 
The literature review also showed that LCA process-based approaches are preferable 
over MRIO to evaluate the flows across value chains (Emborg et al., 2025). Although the 
MRIO approaches can be attractive to companies due to their low cost and low time-
consuming, are also subject to uncertainties due to price fluctuation and due to their 
low sectoral granularity (Emborg et al., 2025). Adding key LCA information to MRIO 
datasets could help to improve the accuracy and reliability of these assessments without 
significantly increasing the work efforts.  
• 
The quality assessment of characterisation models shows that Recipe, IMPACT World+ 
and LC-IMPACT are the preferred methods from the selection of models that we 
evaluated in terms of scientific robustness, documentation and transparency, 
applicability and stakeholder criteria. Also, this evaluation shows that none of the 
methods successfully covered all the relevant aspects of biodiversity.  
• 
The results of the case study show that steel and iron materials have the largest 
contribution to the upstream and downstream impacts, while climate change is the 
largest midpoint pressure contributor to upstream, downstream and direct impacts. 
This could lead to defining a transition plan for the company, such as using less carbon-
intensity steel or replacing steel with materials that have a lower carbon footprint.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:37==
Development of a framework for Corporate Biodiversity Footprint 
 
37 
 
In addition, the following guiding principles are suggested for corporations:  
• 
Both LCA and MRIO approaches are suitable to determine the environmental flows 
across the value chains. Although MRIO approaches are less time demanding and could 
be more attractive for companies, also present a lack of sectoral granularity which could 
affect the accuracy of the results. Hybrid approaches could add more accuracy without 
significantly adding more work effort. Finally, in terms of accuracy, LCA approaches can 
be also more time-demanding for corporates.  
• 
Based on our results, we recommend the use of the following characterisation models: 
Recipe 2016, Impact World+ and LC-IMPACT.  
Moreover, we also make the following recommendations for future studies:  
• 
We advise carrying out a real case study comparing the characterisation models 
recommended in our assessment (Recipe 2016, LC-IMPACT and IMPACT World +) by 
incorporating also spatially differentiated characterisation factors to investigate if, 
under these conditions, the choice of characterisation model has an impact on the 
results.  
• 
Additionally, another aspect that could be investigated is how the use of an MRIO affects 
the results of a business-based study, compared to a LCA approach and a hybrid 
approach.  
• 
Since in our case study we did not investigate fully how the choice of different metrics 
affects the results (as stated in Chapter 4), it could be interesting to compare the results 
of a business case study using also the GLOBIO model. On top of this, other approaches 
and metrics could have been investigated.  For instance, Burges et al. (2024) recommend 
businesses to complement the use of metrics such as MSA or PDF with metrics that are 
based on features measured at the level of an individual component of biodiversity, such 
as the Species Threat Abatement and Restoration (STAR). Thus, it could be interesting 
to extend the scope of this study and include other approaches and metrics. 
Finally, we suggest the following recommendations for policymakers:  
• 
Our literature review showed the choice of metrics, characterisation models, and 
datasets can influence the results of biodiversity impact assessments (Steubing et al., 
2022; Kyttä et al., 2023; Sanyé-Mengual et al., 2023; Emborg et al., 2025). Moreover, 
our study shows that biodiversity footprint in terms of absolute values might not be 
comparable across companies, because of the choice of different characterisation 
models. Thus, developing a standardised methodology and metrics is essential to ensure 
the comparability of biodiversity impacts across corporations.  
• 
Additionally, the CSRD, TNFD and GRI do not indicate which activities should be 
considered in the assessment of impacts across the value chains and own operations. 
Thus, more detailed guidance in terms of scope is also needed.
