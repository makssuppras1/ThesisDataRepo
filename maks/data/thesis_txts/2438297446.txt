Summary
The seismic vulnerability and assessment of reinforced concrete silos, with the use of fragility
curves, is studied in this project. An in depth literature review explains the seismic behavior
of silo structures and how fragility curves can be used for seismic risk assessment. A suitable
suite of strong ground motions is chosen and justiﬁed. Then a ﬁnite element model of the
structure is presented and the uncertainties in the model parameters are treated. Nonlin-
ear time history simulations are run to obtain the response of the structure under seismic
excitations and Probabilistic Seismic Demand Models are build to estimate the demand re-
sponse of the structure. Next, Limit states according to the literature are used to develop
the fragility curves of reinforced concrete silos. Lastly, the results are discussed and future
work is proposed.

1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Industrial facilities are used for the manufacture, storage and production of goods. They
are the main component of factories, thus their importance is huge for the normal operation
and continuation of the production line.
In this project, a speciﬁc type of industrial facility is studied which are the silos. Silos are
these industrial facilities that are used for the storage of any type of bulk materials, so they
can be seen in various industries used to store for example grains, sand, coal, cement etc.
They have cylindrical shape and can be constructed from diﬀerent materials such as rein-
forced concrete and steel, while they come in diﬀerent sizes in terms of height and diameter
based on the needs of each speciﬁc industry and the material they need carry. Their impor-
tance is very critical and thus they should be designed to operate under both typical loads
such as the vertical loads and the wind loads but also for more extreme loads such as loads
from earthquakes in seismic prone regions.
Their response under seismic loads is what this report investigates. It has been observed
from the past, that earthquakes can create severe damages to silos [12, 37] which can lead to
economic losses due to their immediate aﬀect in the production line as described above, but
also risk the safety of humans working near them. So, a safe and robust structural design
needs to provide seismic protection for silos and guarantee their safe operation throughout
their lifetime.
So far, the typical design guidelines propose a design based on two limit states, the service
and the ultimate. The ﬁrst deals with the response of the structure under normal opera-
tional conditions and speciﬁes limits for the displacements, deformations, crack widths etc.
The ultimate limit state deals with the ultimate capacity of the structure and veriﬁes that
the structure is safe from collapse under external loads. These two limit states even though
they are very useful and easy to implement for engineers, they lack a more detailed per-
formance description of the structure. When there are seismic loads present in the design
(structures in seismic prone areas) then the need of a more detailed design scheme is even
more necessary. This approach of design is called "Performance Based Design" and is being
used for many years especially for buildings and other structures such as bridges. However,
not many research has been done to implement performance based design techniques in
other structures such as silos and the main reason for that, is that in order to use this kind
of design approach one has to deﬁne damage states for the structure under investigation.
Only until recently some research has been carried to create damage states for reinforced
concrete silos under seismic excitation [50, 23]. Nevertheless, except from the fact that the
research done on the topic is very limited, the fragility assessment of reinforced concrete
silos can be updated or re-evaluated taking into account more parameters that can aﬀect
the seismic assessment in order to provide a more accurate representation of the fragility risk.
With this in mind, it can be understood that there is a big opening for research on the
topic of seismic fragility assessment of reinforced concrete silos. Along these lines, the main
objectives of the current project can be summarized below:
– The importance of the careful selection of a strong ground motion suite and how it
aﬀects the response of the structure under investigation will be highlighted.

– A numerical model will be presented for the analysis and attention will be given on

2. Literature Review
2
Literature Review
In the present chapter, a detail description of the previous research that has been done on the
topic of the project is presented. More analytically, the seismic behavior of silos is shown
in the ﬁrst section in order to give the reader a good idea of how structures of this kind
behave under earthquakes, what are the main issues that need to be addressed as well as
what needs further investigation in terms of their response under seismic hazards. Then,
the topic of seismic risk assessment is presented to the reader which constitutes the basis of
fragility analysis, followed by examples of fragility curves in diﬀerent structures.
2.1
Seismic behavior of Silos
Earthquakes introduce both horizontal and vertical loads to the structures. The vertical
loads are often neglected because they are weak compared to the horizontal but also they
are counter-acted by the self weight of the structure so they do not create signiﬁcant damages
to the structures. This is also the case for silos, namely that the horizontal seismic loads
are critical in design. Horizontal loads introduce big bending moments and shear forces at
the base of the silo. These forces introduce high stresses that can create severe damages and
even make the silo collapse.
One of the best ways to explain the damages on silos due to earthquakes is to study previous
earthquake events. At the 2010 Chile earthquake [37] damages on silos after the earthquake
were studied and some very useful conclusions were made. First, silos with openings suﬀered
bigger damages and cracks comparing with silos with either small or stiﬀened openings,
meaning that the openings on the silos could be very critical in their response. Another
ﬁnding of that study was the diﬀerent failure mechanisms observed at the silos with both
ﬂexural-shear failure type and also ﬂexural cracking failure type being observed. Very use-
ful ﬁndings can be found from the 2003 Algeria earthquake [2]. In that earthquake, it was
observed that the silos that suﬀered the most severe damages where the ones carrying the
most material at the time of the earthquake indicating that the higher the mass of the
silo from the material, the higher the possibility of being damaged. In terms of the type
of damage on the silos, severe concrete crushing, steel rebar fractures and buckling, lack
of reinforcements and partial sliding of the external concrete shell were observed. All the
aforementioned failure types are also described in [12] where diﬀerent failure types as well as
loading conditions of silos are presented and discussed. Of particular interest are the cases
of the Kocaeli and the Duzce earthquakes in Turkey in 1999 were it was also observed that
silos with 85 % of the volume of the ﬁlling material showed severe damages compared to
silos with 25 % of the volume of the ﬁlling material where there were little to no damage
on the silo structures, coming in agreement with the study of the 2003 Algeria earthquake [2].
One of the most important aspects in the investigation of the seismic behavior of silos under
seismic excitations is the response of the ﬁlling material and how it interacts with the silo
structure. There is a wide range of literature that supports that the dynamic behavior of
the ﬁlling material introduces additional pressure on the silo walls, creating an interaction
similar to the soil structure interaction that is observed at the foundation of structures.
The most common way to model this interaction between the ﬁlling material and the silo
structure is to model the granular material using a diﬀerent constitutive model than the
silo structure and then introduce a friction interface between the two. The most preferable

constitutive model and the one found in most in the literature for the ﬁlling material is
hypoplasticity [31], while the constitutive model of the silo depends on the material of the
structure (reinforced concrete of steel) and also the type of the analysis required (elastic or
plastic). The preferable type of friction for the interface is the Coulomb friction. All the
above are investigated in [38], where a reinforced concrete silo is modelled in two diﬀerent
ways, one with a hypoplastic model for the ﬁlling material and a friction interface and one
where there is no interaction between the ﬁlling material and the silo, but 80 % of the mass
of the granular material is assigned to the sides of the walls of the silo. The results show that
lower stresses are developed when the interaction is taken into account. The same result is
obtained for silos constructed out of steel as shown in [39].
The inﬂuence of soil structure interaction at the base of the silos under earthquake loading
has also been investigated by some researchers. That was done in [13] where a 3 degree of
freedom ﬁnite element model was tested taking into account both the grain-structure as well
as the foundation soil-structure interaction, the most interesting ﬁnding was that by running
fully elastic analysis for both the ﬁlling material and the silo, the results were very close to
the ones obtained from analytic simpliﬁed solutions in the literature.
As far as the design codes are concerned, Eurocode 8-4 treats structures like silos, tanks and
pipelines against seismic excitations [15]. EN1998-4 provides two methods to design silos
under earthquakes. The ﬁrst one suggests that 80 % of the ﬁlling material to be included
as eﬀective mass when analyzing the silo for a seismic analysis. The second method which
is more accurate, suggests that an extra pressure has to be added on the silo walls with a
magnitude of between 75 % and 95 % of the total mass [46].
2.2
Assessment of Seismic Risk
Seismic risk refers to the risk of damage from a seismic event to a structure or system. It has
been deﬁned as the potential economic, social and environmental consequences of hazardous
events that may occur in a speciﬁed period of time [5]. The earthquake event by itself how-
ever can not fully describe the risk of a particular structure. For example, a seismic-proof
designed building in an earthquake prone area is at lower risk than a non seismic-proof
building in an area with lower seismicity.
In order to assess the seismic risk for a particular structure, several ways can be found. For
example, in order to determine the seismic risk for an urban area, [47] proposes a two level
risk assessment procedure where in the ﬁrst level, each building of the city is inspected from
the street, and on the second level, structural parameters are inspected from the ground level
inside the building. This two level risk assessment approach was tried in Istanbul after the
1999 Duzce earthquake and it gave good results regarding the buildings that had extensive
damages and could risk the safety of the occupants and the people in a close proximity.
Another way to assess the seismic risk is with computer programs. For example, HAZUS
software [20], is a software that can model diﬀerent hazards such as hurricanes, ﬂooding and
earthquakes and can estimate their risk in terms of economic losses, structural damage, etc.
However, n order to get the risk assessment for every structure existing in an area with high
seismicity every structure needs to be analyzed separately. This can be extremely expensive
and not possible to be done. A tool to overcome this is to calculate the seismic fragility or
seismic capacity for a type of structure (for example n-storey buildings) or a component of
a structure (for example bridge piers). That way the seismic risk assessment becomes easier

2.3
Seismic Fragility Curves
Seismic fragility curves are tools for assessing the vulnerability of a particular structure or
component of a structure, and oﬀer a means of communicating the probability of damage
over a range of potential earthquake ground motion. A big amount of research can be found
on fragility curves for structures such as buildings, bridges, dams and nuclear plants, while
at the end of the section the fragility analysis research that has been done for silos will be
presented along with the goal of this report.
Buildings are the type of structures that have gathered the biggest amount of research when
it comes to fragility curves. Several researchers have developed fragility curves for diﬀerent
types of buildings in terms of height, material and also location of the building. In [44],
fragility curves for mid-rise reinforced concrete buildings in Istanbul are derived. Two dam-
age states are used, yielding and collapse. With the use of regression analysis, the study is
extended and fragility curves are derived for buildings with higher number of storeys leading
to the result that the fragility results change signiﬁcantly due to the number of stories of the
building. Also for the city of Istanbul, [16] developed vulnerability (fragility) curves, this
time however deﬁning four damage states, slight damage, moderate damage, heavy damage
and very heavy damage. These damage states are similar to the ones found in the American
codes (FEMA). Similar research has been done for other cities, for example in [18] fragility
curves are developed for masonry and reinforced concrete buildings in Catania city in south-
ern Italy. Again here the same damage states as in [16] are used. In [29] fragility curves for
reinforced concrete buildings in the region of Greece can be found where the main diﬀerence
with the previous reports is that in this one the damage states are not related with the
actual damage on the components of the building but are connected with the cost required
to repair the structure after the seismic event.
Fragility curves for bridges is another category of structures where extensive literature in
the topic of fragility analysis can be found. Typical work of fragility analysis is found in [7]
where fragility curves are developed for four diﬀerent types of bridges in moderate seismic
zones in the USA. The ﬁnding of this research was that the most vulnerable bridge types are
the multi-span simply supported and multi-span continuous steel-girder bridges. However,
what makes the investigation of the vulnerability of bridges more complex than buildings is
the fact that bridges can come in diﬀerent sizes and types and also the bridge components
may have a greater inﬂuence in the behavior of the bridge superstructure, so sometimes it
is beneﬁcial to develop fragility curves for each bridge components separately. This can be
seen greatly in [3]. Here, a component level approach is used that counts for all mayor con-
tributing components in a highway bridge. It is shown that a bridge system is more fragile
than any of the individual components and it is risky to base the fragility analysis of the
entire bridge system in the fragility of the bridge piers alone. A component level study is
also being done in [48] where the inﬂuence of the Abutment-Embankment stiﬀness is stud-
ied and it is shown that the probability of failure at a system level is diﬀerent when the
abutment fragility is studied separately, comparing with the convectional fragility analysis
procedures. A study of fragility curves for retroﬁtted bridges can be found in [27], where
the impact of retroﬁt measures in the overall vulnerability of the bridge is studied before
and after the retroﬁt measures, the results show that a speciﬁc retroﬁt measure may have

positive, negative or no impact on the whole bridge system and has to be studied separately.
Other type of structures that fragility curves have been developed for are dams. Fragility
curves for these types of structures are of huge importance since a failure in a dam can
risk the lives of thousands of people and also create enormous social and economical losses.
Fragility curves for dams have been done from many researchers, for example in [49] the
Bluestone Dam on the New River in West Virginia was studied and it was concluded that
sliding along the dam–foundation interface is likely if the dam were to be subjected to an
earthquake with a magnitude above a certain limit. Four limit states were used for this study
deﬁned as: Material failure—concrete (at the neck of the dam), Material failure—foundation
(at the toe), Sliding at the dam–foundation interface and Deﬂection of the top of the dam
relative to the heel.
It can be seen until now that a very important component of the fragility analysis which is
the limit states can be deﬁned in diﬀerent ways depending on the type of the structure, but
also on the area the structure is build and the costs of repairing the damages.
Not much research has been done for the fragility analysis of silos. One of the reasons is
that speciﬁc damage states for silos have not been derived. Only recently some researchers
proposed some damages states for reinforced concrete silos. Tuska et. al. [50] presented
damage states by using nonlinear Incremental Dynamic Analysis (IDA). These IDA curves
were developed for several strong ground motions in terms of base shear and maximum top
displacement of the silo, then by using the mean IDA curve, ﬁve diﬀerent damage states
were deﬁned for reinforced concrete silos. Similar approach was followed by Guo et. al. [23]
were damage states were deﬁned in a similar way and then fragility curves were developed
with the same ground motions that were used to deﬁne the damage states. However, in his
deﬁnition of damage states in [23] no justiﬁcation was presented as to why only a speciﬁc
IDA curved was used to deﬁne the damage states, while also in the fragility analysis only a
limited number of analyses were used to obtain the demand displacements and no uncertainty
treatment of the model parameters was done as everything was deﬁned is a deterministic
way.
The goal of the current project is to develop fragility curves using the damage states deﬁned
by Tuska et. al. [50] and using a wide range of strong ground motions to cover a wider
range of applicability of the fragility curves. Also, in this project the uncertainties of the
parameters are taken into account in the derivation of the fragility curves.

3. Methodology
3
Methodology
The purpose of this chapter is to give to the reader a deeper understanding of what fragility
curves are and how they are implemented in this project. Fragility curves are a statistical
tool representing the probability of exceeding a given damage state (or performance) as a
function of an engineering demand parameter that represents the ground motion (for example
the spectral acceleration). Depending on what are the damage states used, fragility curves
are developed for each one of them. An example of fragility curve for two damage states is
shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Example of fragility curve for two damage states as a function of the
Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA)
There is not a single way to construct fragility curves. The analytic fragility functions which
are the most accurate were used in this project, however other ways to construct fragility
curves are discussed. In the ﬁrst sections, empirical fragility curves are explained highlighting
their advantages as well as their drawbacks. Then, the analytic derivation of fragility curves
is shown along with the main components required to construct them followed by how all
these are used for the present case to obtain fragility curves for reinforced concrete silos.
3.1
Empirical Fragility Curves
Empirical fragility curves, as the name suggests are build from previous experience from
actual earthquake data. This type of curves exist for some well known strong ground mo-
tions such as the Loma Prieta and the Northridge earthquakes in west USA where empirical
fragility curves for bridge structures were developed by some researchers [1, 14]. The Kobe
earthquake in Japan [45] is another earthquake for which empirical fragility curves for bridges
were developed. In order to create empirical fragility curves inspection has to be done in
the structure investigated to assess the damage after earthquake. This damage is assessed in
terms of damage states and then for every structure, the damage state speciﬁed is related to
a ground motion parameter such as the peak ground acceleration (PGA). In order to obtain
the probability of exceedance for each damage state and develop the full curves, probabilis-
tic tools are used. These probabilistic tools can be regression analysis [1] or the Maximum
Likehood Method [45].
7
==PAGE:16==
3. Methodology
The big advantage of empirical fragility curves is the ease of their derivation. However many
drawbacks come with this type of fragility curves. The ﬁrst one is that in order to build
empirical fragility curves the structures have to be inspected after a signiﬁcant earthquake
which is not realistically possible for many structures.
This means that only structures
of high importance are investigated (such as big bridges) which by itself creates another
problem which is the limited number of structures of the same type investigated bringing an
uncertainty to the result. Another drawback is that the post-earthquake assessment done
by experts can be subjective which means that results from diﬀerent seismic events and thus
diﬀerent assessment are diﬃcult to be compared.
3.2
Analytic Fragility Curves
A way to build fragility curves for every case and when data from previous earthquakes or
post-earthquake assessments are not available is to derive them analytically. A vast ma-
jority of literature exists where analytic fragility curves for diﬀerent types of structure are
presented and some of them were reviewed in Chapter 2. Here an explanation is going to be
given on how analytic fragility curves are derived.
If the damage states for a structure are related to the structural capacity (C) and the response
of the structure to the structural demand (D), then the fragility or probability of failure for
a given level of ground motion intensity measure (IM) can be described as [27]:
Fragility = P[D ≥C|IM]
(3.1)
According to [53], this probability has shown a good ﬁt when modeled as a lognormal prob-
ability so it is a safe assumption to model it that way. To estimate the seismic demand and
capacity, probabilistic seismic demand models (PSDMs) need to be developed which require
analytic modeling and simulations.
These simulations can vary from elastic or inelastic spectrum analysis, nonlinear static anal-
ysis and nonlinear time history analysis. For this project nonlinear time history analysis
was used which even though is the most computationally costly method happens to be the
most reliable of all the other methods [45]. When a nonlinear time history analysis method
is chosen, the steps to follow in order to derive fragility curves are presented below.
The ﬁrst step is to select a suite of strong ground motions that describes well the purpose
of the study. If for example the fragility analysis deals with a speciﬁc geographic area, then
the strong ground motions have to be selected from previous earthquakes in the same loca-
tion, while if a more general fragility analysis is to be done, like for example the one in this
project, then the selection of strong ground motions needs to be more general and include
earthquakes from diﬀerent locations with diﬀerent characteristics. Also, for every case the
strong ground motions need to cover a wide range of parameters like the epicentral distance,
the magnitude, the peak ground acceleration (PGA) etc. The selection of the strong ground
motions for the current study is presented in Chapter 4.
As the name suggests, for a nonlinear time history analysis, a nonlinear numerical model
is required. The ﬁnite element method is used to create a model that has to represent the
true behavior of the structure under the seismic excitations. Material, as well as geometric
nonlinearities are taken into account when necessary. The ﬁnite element method for the
8

reinforced concrete silos is presented in Chapter 5 where the constitutive laws for the mate-
rials are described as well as other parameters that have to do with the computational model.
After the numerical model is build, the structural properties such as the material strengths,
geometric data, damping ratios etc. are treated probabilistically using a sampling technique.
This is done N times generating N identical but statistically diﬀerent structure models. Each
model is put together with a diﬀerent strong ground motion and a nonlinear time history
analysis is done for each one of them having at the end N diﬀerent analyses.
For each
analysis the response of interest is taken (this response can be the displacement, curvature,
ductility etc.) and used to derive the Probabilistic Seismic Demand Models using a statistical
estimation technique such as regression analysis or the Maximum Likehood Method. Linear
regression was used in [9], for a study about buildings, and the estimate of the median
demand were calculated as:
Sd = a · IMb
(3.2)
where, IM is the intensity measure (for example the PGA or the spectral acceleration), Sd
is the median value of the seismic demand and a, b are regression coeﬃcients. By taking the
natural logarithm on both sides, Equation 3.2 can be written in a transformed space as:
ln(Sd) = ln(a) + b · ln(IM)
(3.3)
By using a transformed space, the derivation of the regression coeﬃcients becomes easier.

3.3
Summary
A summary recap of the procedure to derive analytic fragility curves is given below.
– Select N number of strong ground motions that need to be representative of the problem
investigated and contain a wide range of earthquake parameters.
– Create N numerical models of the structure under investigation. Use a sampling tech-
nique to generate the modeling parameters that are not treated in a deterministic way
and pair each of these set of parameters with a diﬀerent strong ground motion for every
analysis.
– Run nonlinear time history analysis for each one of the N simulations and get the
responses of interest for every simulation
– Gather all the responses and plot them versus the intensity measure in order to estimate
the structural demand. A parameter estimation technique should be chosen (regression
for example) to build the Probabilistic Seismic Demand Model from which a, b are
estimated to get the median demand Sd along with its dispersion βD|IM.
– Use an empirical, experimental or analytic approach to obtain the limit states for the
structure along with the dispersion of the damage state (βC).
– Calculate the Fragility Curves by use of Equation 3.6

4
Selection of Strong Ground Motions
In the current Chapter, the strong ground motions that were used in the simulations are
selected. First, the relevant parameters that are important in the simulations are explained.
Next, the strong ground motions that were chosen are presented. Finally, the seismic exci-
tations are categorized based on their frequency content and their spectra are shown.
4.1
Important Parameters of Strong Ground Motions
The parameters that were taken into account in the selection of the strong ground motions
were:
– The earthquake magnitude
– The Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA)
– The epicentral distance
– The soil type at the location of the measurement
All the above parameters have direct impact on the structural response of structures and
were chosen in a way to accurately represent the structure response. More speciﬁcally, the
earthquake magnitude is a measure of the strength of the earthquake which means that
seismic events with low magnitude will fail to excite adequately the structure analyzed. A
measure that also describes the power of an earthquake is the Peak Ground Acceleration
(PGA) and is very useful in earthquake engineering since it has units of acceleration which
makes it easy to read and describe a ground motion. The measurement of the ground motion
needs to be in a near distance of the earthquake event. This is expressed in the epicentral
distance which is the distance between the earthquake event and the location of the mea-
surement. Finally, the soil type at the location of the measurement can greatly inﬂuence the
response and can not be neglected.

from Asia, Europe and Americas and can be used to derive fragility curves for reinforced
silos build everywhere and not in a speciﬁc seismic prone area.
Another very important aspect that needs to be taken into account when selecting ground
motions is to avoid using recordings from the same seismic event but from diﬀerent recording
stations. When several recordings from the same seismic event are used, the analysis is not
very accurate as these recordings, even though from diﬀerent recording stations, are simi-
lar to each other and inﬂuence the results [30]. Thus, for this study it was decided that no
more than 10% of the total strong ground motions selected come from the same seismic event.

can be seen along with their characteristics in Table 4.2.
4.3
Frequency Content of the Strong Ground Motions
A very useful tool in earthquake engineering is the use of the response spectrum. By using
a response spectrum, the performance of a structure can be analyzed, if it is assumed that
the structure behaves like a simple oscillator with known frequency. This assumption is very
reasonable for many types of structures since it is the ﬁrst dynamic mode that dominates the
response, thus, the frequency of the ﬁrst mode can be used along with the response spectrum
to evaluate the performance of the structure. Spectra can be constructed with displacement,
velocity and acceleration spectral amplitudes. The reinforced silo analyzed in this project
has a natural period at the ﬁrst mode equal to T = 0.2s and at this range it is the spec-
tral acceleration that dominates the response [8], so the pseudo-acceleration spectra are used.
A typical pseudo-acceleration response spectrum can be seen in Figure 4.1. From this exam-
ple it can be seen that indeed, in the lower frequency range the pseudo-acceleration dominates
the response.
The period at which maximum spectral energy (maximum Pseudo-Acceleration) is concen-
trated is called the predominant or peak period.
By comparing the peak period of the
spectrum with the natural period of the structure a good indication can be made on how
much the structure is going to be excited from the earthquake. Of course the spectral ac-
celeration is not enough to tell the whole story of the performance of the structure, but

Numerical Model
In the present Chapter, the numerical model used for the simulations is presented and
described. Topics such as the constitutive models for the materials and other parameters
taken for the ﬁnite element analysis will be discussed. The model used was developed by
Tuska et. al. [50] and the reason this model was chosen was that it was the model used for
the generation of the damage states for reinforced concrete silos in the same study [50], so
it would make the fragility analysis even more accurate if both the damage states and the
fragility curves are derived from the same numerical model.

is allowed after contact in order to model the cyclic behavior under seismic loading more
accurately. Regarding the friction between the sand material and the silo walls and base,
Coulomb friction is used with the coeﬃcient of friction depending on the sand properties

The silo is fully ﬁxed at its base, while symmetry boundary conditions are used on the
symmetry cut. The seismic excitation is inserted in the model as a boundary condition of
acceleration at the base nodes. The seismic load is acting on the big side of the model parallel
to the symmetry line in order to make symmetry work and the loading has a duration equal
to the duration of the strong ground motion.
5.2
Constitutive Laws of Materials
Of great importance when running nonlinear time history analyses (or any nonlinear analysis)
are the constitutive laws of the materials of the model. In contrast with elastic analysis
where only the modulus of Elasticity and the Poisson ratio are enough for the simulation, in
a nonlinear analysis the inelastic response of the materials is required. For this model, the
concrete, the reinforcement as well as the sand material are all modelled inelastically and
their constitutive laws are described below.

5.2.3
Hypoplastic Constitutive Law for Granular Materials
The preferred material law to model the ﬁlling material of silos from diﬀerent researchers is
hypoplasticity [50, 26, 23]. Hypoplasticity is a particular class of incrementally non-linear
constitutive models, developed speciﬁcally to predict the behaviour of soils [34]. The ﬁrst
model of hypoplasticity was developed by Kolymbas [31] but the model was reﬁned by von
Wolﬀersdorﬀ[52] and is the one that is most used and called the standard hypoplastic model.
The model is composed of two constitutive laws that describe the behavior of a soil sample
in a conﬁned compression test and a triaxial test respectively [21]. The diﬀerence with other
plasticity models is that in hypoplasticity the stress-strain relation is not composed of elastic
and inelastic part. The stress-strain curve in the two soil tests (conﬁned compression and
triaxial) is nonlinear and is described by a rate equation in diﬀerent increments. By calcu-
lating the stress and the strain rate, with integration the stress-strain relation is obtained.
The hypoplastic model for granular materials has eight parameters -φc, hs, n, ed0, ec0, ei0, α,
β-and their calibration was done by [25]. Here, the procedure to determine these parameters
will be brieﬂy described [34].
– The critical friction angle φc determines the resistance of a granulate subjected to
monotonic shearing in critical state. Drained or undrained triaxial tests as well as
simple or direct shear tests can be done to determine it.
– The granulate hardness hs along with the exponent n can be calculated directly from
oedometric loading curves, Figure 5.7.
The parameter n controls the curvature of the oedometric curve while the parameter
hs controls the overall slope of the curve.
– The limit void ratio ed0 is a lower bound void ratio ed0 = emin, ec0 = emax is the void
ratio in a critical state at zero pressure and both ed0 and ec0 are determined through

6. Uncertainty Treatment of Model Parameters
6
Uncertainty Treatment of Model Parameters
There are diﬀerent types of uncertainties when one runs computer simulations in order to
analyze a real life problem. In the case of seismic fragility analysis uncertainties can be
found on the strong ground motions, as well as on the numerical simulation of the structure.
In order to overcome the uncertainty of the strong ground motions, a careful selection of
seismic events was done in Chapter 4 that covers a wide range of earthquake parameters
and thus reduces the uncertainty. In this Chapter the uncertainty of the model parameters
is treated.
For this study, 70 simulations were used to derive the fragility curves for R/C silos. When
one wants to treat the uncertainty of the model parameters, diﬀerent set of model parameters
has to be assigned for each one of the 70 simulations. In order to chose the values of each
set of parameters a sampling technique is required. First, the sampling technique used is
presented and described to the reader, while next each of the model parameters that was
treated probabilistically is presented, namely the concrete strength, the steel strength, the
ﬁlling sand material, the damping ratio of the R/C structure, the damping ratio of the ﬁlling
material and the coeﬃcient of friction between the ﬁlling material and the silo walls.
6.1
Monte Carlo Simulation
The Monte Carlo simulation is a way to account for risk in decision making and quantitative
analysis and can be used as a sampling technique to construct computer experiments. What
is required in a Monte Carlo simulation is the Probability Distribution Functions (PDF)
along with their Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDF) for each of the parameter that is
to be treated probabilistically and also a random number generator. The procedure is done
as follows:
– First, the distribution type of each input parameter is deﬁned in terms of Probability
Distribution Functions (PDF) and Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDF). All types
of distributions can be used for example Normal, Logarithmic, Exponential etc. An
example of a Normal Distribution PDF and CDF is shown in Figure 6.1.
Figure 6.1: Normal distribution probability density and cumulative distribution
functions for a mean value µ = 160 and standard deviation σ = 40 [17]
– Then, a random number generator will generate N random numbers (where N in our
case is the number of simulations and thus diﬀerent sample pairs) in the range of 0

and 1 and for each one of them the value of the simulated sample is found. This is
best illustrated in Figure 6.2
As it is obvious, the higher the number of random number generation N, the more precise
the Monte Carlo Simulation will be and the uncertainty of each parameter is reduced since
more values of the simulated sample space are chosen. The major drawback of this sam-
pling technique is that it requires many simulations which is not possible when nonlinear
time history analyses are run since the computational cost is very high. The number of 70
simulations run in this project is very small to describe accurately the uncertainty of each
parameter and a much higher number of simulations is required.
In order to overcome the drawback mentioned above, diﬀerent sampling rules can be applied
in the Monte Carlo Simulation to derive diﬀerent sampling techniques. In this project the
so called Latin Hybercube Sampling technique was used which is a reﬁned version of the
standard Monte Carlo Simulation and has been used from other researchers in the past to
treat model uncertainties when deriving fragility curves [6, 3, 48].
6.2
Latin Hybercube Sampling Technique
The Latin Hybercube Sampling technique is a reﬁned version of a Monte Carlo simulation
and is widely used to generate random samples of parameter values. As the name suggests,
it is based on the Latin Square Design which is an ancient puzzle where you try to ﬁnd out
how many ways Latin letters can be arranged in a set number of rows and columns if each
symbol appears only once in each row and column. An example of a Latin Square is shown
in Figure 6.3

A hypercube is a cube with more than three dimensions implying that this technique can be
used in multiple dimensions.
The method was ﬁrst developed by McKay in 1979 [35] and is widely used for structural reli-
ability studies [42]. The big diﬀerence between Latin Hybercube Sampling and the standart
Monte Carlo sampling is that in LHS the Cumulative Distribution Function is ﬁrst divided
in N equal ranges (N is the number of sampling pairs needed) and in each of these ranges
a random number is picked in order to obtain the sample value, while in standard Monte
Carlo simulation the CDF is not divided in parts and the N points are randomly picked. An
illustration of the LHS is shown in Figure 6.4.

In a study from Olsson [42] it was shown that by using LHS, the processing time can be
reduced up to 50 % in comparison with Monte Carlo sampling making the LHS the optimal
method in cases where the computational cost is high.
6.3
Blocking of parameters
In certain cases, when either the distribution of a parameter is not known, or the parameter
is inherently diﬀerent from the other parameters treated probabilistically (for example un-
certainty in the geometry of the structure), a statistical tool called blocking can be used.
Blocking, as the name suggests, divides the sample into blocks in each one of which a diﬀer-
ent parameter is used. A good example of when blocking is needed is the case of geometry
uncertainty treatment in a numerical model. When one wants to investigate the inﬂuence
of the structure geometry in a simulation, diﬀerent models with diﬀerent geometries can be
used with blocking. Each block will contain a set of simulations (which will be a part of the
total simulation sample space), and for each block a diﬀerent numerical model in terms of

==PAGE:40==
6. Uncertainty Treatment of Model Parameters
geometry is used.
For the present study, blocking was used to model the uncertainty in the sand material. As
explained in the previous Chapter, ﬁve diﬀerent sands were used in the simulations. With a
total of 70 simulations, each one of the blocks should contain 14 diﬀerent simulations with
the same sand material. Blocking does not aﬀect the sampling of the rest of the parameters
as a unique set is used in each of the 70 simulations. This way, the uncertainty of the sand
material is inserted in the analysis without having to use a sampling technique.

6.4.1
Concrete Material Uncertainty
For the concrete material, the ultimate compressive stress σcu was assumed uncertain. Ac-
cording to Choi 2002 [6] the concrete compressive strength follows a Normal Distribution.
For a concrete class of C20/25, the mean value is equal to µ = 33.8 MPa and the standard
deviation is σ = 4.3 MPa.
6.4.2
Steel Material Uncertainty
Similarly with the ultimate compressive strength of the concrete, the yield stress of the steel
reinforcement was also treated in a probabilistic manner. According to the Probabilistic
Model Code [28], reinforcing steel follows Normal Distribution and for a steel category S400,
the mean value is equal to µ = 460 MPa and the standard deviation is σ = 30 MPa.
6.4.3
Uncertainty in the Damping Ratio of the R/C Silo Structure
As described in the previous Chapter, due to the non-linearity of the simulation, a low value
of damping ratio is used. According to a study from Fang et. al. (1999) [19] in a study for
tall buildings, he concluded that the damping ratio follows a Normal Distribution. Thus, it
was assumed for this study a damping ratio with a mean value equal to µ = 1.5 % and a
standard deviation equal to σ = 0.5 %.
6.4.4
Uncertainty in the Damping Ratio of the Filling Sand Material
For the same reasons as for the damping of the R/C silo structure, the ﬁlling material is as-
sumed to have the same damping ratio distribution with the same mean value and standard
deviation. The reason that it is modelled as a diﬀerent parameter is in order to account for
the possibility the two material not having the same damping ratio.

7.2
Selection of appropriate Intensity Measure
As has been seen so far, it is of great importance to accurately describe a seismic motion in
order to get a good correlation between the seismic excitation and the structure response.
In fragility analysis, it is the intensity measure that describes the earthquake motion and in
this section diﬀerent intensity measures are going to be discussed based on their practicality
and eﬃciency.
There are several intensity measures that have been used in the past for fragility analysis.
The most used and well known is the Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA). The PGA was
used as intensity measures in FEMA’s loss assessment program HAZUS [22]. This intensity
measure depends only on the earthquake motion and not on the structure characteristics.
If one wants to use an intensity measure that containts information about the structure
under investigation, then spectral measures (for example Spectral Displacment, Spectral
Acceleration) can be used. A study done by [33] on the performance-based design of bridges
showed that for an intensity measure to be optimal it should be eﬃcient, where eﬃciency
was measured by the standard deviation of the logarithm of the demand model, which is
the amount of variability in the demand parameter for a given intensity measure. They
concluded that the most eﬃcient intensity measures tend to be the spectral quantities based
on the fundamental period of the bridges. In this study, two diﬀerent intensity measures were
used for the generation of the fragility curves, namely the Peak Ground Acceleration and
the Spectral Acceleration at the fundamental period of the reinforced concrete silo. Their
pros and cons are presented below.

Spectral Acceleration as Intensity Measure
Another widely used intensity measure is the spectral acceleration at the fundamental period
of the structure. This intensity measure, in contrast with the Peak Ground Acceleration,
contains information about the dynamic properties of the structure and can show higher
eﬃciency than the PGA with better ﬁtting of the response demand around the mean.
The main drawbacks when using the spectral acceleration as an intensity measure are dis-
cussed. Firstly, the spectral acceleration is not as practical as the PGA which is readily
available for every ground motion, but the pseudo-acceleration needs to be constructed ﬁrst
to obtain it. Another disadvantage is that the fundamental period of a structure is found
after modal analysis assuming fully elastic behavior, however in a nonlinear analysis (like in
this project) the structure very often enters the inelastic range and thus the fundamental
period of the structure is not the same because the structure loses stiﬀness. This means that
the spectral acceleration at the fundamental period does not always shows the true picture

As it was described in Chapter 3, the fragility of a structure or a component is the proba-

intensity measure parameter that describes the ground motion. The structural demand and

8.1
Limit States for R/C Silos
Only marginal research exists regarding damage states for reinforced concrete silos. Even
though performance based design is a practise used for many years, not a lot of study has
been done on silo structures. Limit states for structures or components can be deﬁned using
expert judgment where experts assess the structure after a major earthquake and deﬁne
damage states, experimental methods that still rely on analysts and experts but the main
diﬀerence is that in experimental methods the damage states are quantiﬁed and based on
the physics on the structure and ﬁnally analytic methods that include nonlinear incremental
dynamic (IDA) or nonlinear static (pushover) analyses and deﬁne diﬀerent damage states.
Only analytic damage states have been deﬁned for reinforced concrete silos, Tuska et. al.
[50] deﬁned diﬀerent damage states using an Incremental Dynamic Analysis along with a
suite of strong ground motions and a nonlinear ﬁnite element model. Guo et. al. [23] used
a similar approach with an Incremental Dynamic Analysis with a diﬀerent suite of ground
motions to descibe the damage states for reinforced concrete silos. For this study the damage
states deﬁned from Tuska et. al. are chosen to be used for two main reasons:
– The numerical model used to develop the damage states from Tuska et. al. [50] is
very similar as the one used in this study to develop the Probabilistic Seismic Demand
Models
– The strong ground motion suite used for the IDA in Tuska et. al. [50] is a sub-part of
the 70 strong ground motions used in this study
Due to those reasons it was judged that the damage states from Tuska et. al. [50] will be in
agreement with the demand responses obtained and thus more accurate fragility curves will
be developed.

its probability of exceedance does not reach its maximum value, for earthquakes with high
pseudo-acceleration at the ﬁrst natural frequency of the structure high fragility probability
that the damage state will be exceeded exists. The extensive damage state curves looks very
similar with the one in Figure 8.3 while the collapse damage state curve has very low values
of fragility.
8.3
Impact of the Intensity Measure on the Fragility Curves
A comparison between the fragility curves using PGA and the ones using the spectral accel-
eration is made. As it was pointed out also in Chapter 7, the ﬁtting of the structural demand
power model plays a major role on the accuracy of the fragility curves. The PSDM with the
spectral acceleration has vastly better ﬁtting of the measured response values around the
median comparing to the PSDM with the PGA which means that the fragility curves using
the spectral acceleration describe the true nature of the problem more accurately. The bad
ﬁtting of the PSDM with the PGA can be also observed from the slight damage fragility

curve where it does not take its maximum value even at high values of PGA due to the high
uncertainty inserted from the bad ﬁtting of the PSDM.
Another interesting conclusion from the fragility curves is the very low probability of ex-
ceedance of the collapse damage state when the spectral acceleration was used.
This is
something that was expected since in the deﬁnition of the damage states from Tuska et. al.

real Peak Ground Acceleration) that brought the structure to collapse, whereas normal un-
scaled strong ground motions were not able to reach that damage state. In contrast, slightly
higher values of fragility for the collapse damage state fragility curve were given when the
PGA was used which is explained on the bad ﬁtting of the PSDM with the PGA.

9. Conclusion and Future Work
9
Conclusion and Future Work
The seismic vulnerability assessment of reinforced concrete silos was studied in this project.
Using a numerical analysis, a suite of strong ground motions and running time history
analyses, the fragility of reinforced concrete silos was assessed in the form of fragility curves.
The fragility curves were build both for the Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) and the
spectral acceleration at the fundamental natural period of the structure, as intensity measure.
The main ﬁndings of this project are presented below.
– The selection of the ground motion suite for a fragility analysis is of high importance.
The earthquake motions should contain a wide range of characteristics for the study
to be more accurate. The frequency content of the seismic excitations has also to be
taken into account in the selection of the ground motions as it has a big inﬂuence on
the response of the structure.
– For a nonlinear numerical analysis a proper nonlinear numerical model is required in
order to describe the true behavior of the structure as accurately as possible. The
constitutive models chosen to describe the material nonlinearities should be suitable
for cyclic loading conditions when time history analyses are used.
– For an accurate fragility analysis, the uncertainty of the modeling parameters have
to be considered. Diﬀerent sampling techniques exist in the literature, but the Latin
Hybercube Sampling technique seems to be the most appropriate for fragility analysis
studies of this type.
– Two intensity measures were used in this study, the PGA and the spectral acceleration
at the fundamental frequency of the structure. The spectral acceleration is the one
that ﬁts the response results better around the median and creates more accurate
representation of the demand response of the structure. The PGA has bad ﬁtting of
the response results, but its main advantage is its practicality as it is easier for the
reader to use and understand.
– Four damage states are identiﬁed for reinforced concrete silos, namely Slight, Moderate,
Extensive and Collapse and fragility curves are developed for each of those damage
states.
It is concluded that the Slight damage state is very likely to be exceeded
even for average to weak earthquakes. The moderate damage state is very likely to
be exceeded for strong earthquakes, while the extensive damage state has a medium
probability of exceedance for strong earthquakes and small probability of exceedance
for average and weak earthquakes. Finally, the collapse damage state is very unlikely
to occur even for very strong ground motions.
Limited research exists on the seismic vulnerability of silos. To the author’s best knowledge
there are several topics that are worth to be investigated regarding the seismic behavior of
silos. Some of these topics are presented below.
– Inﬂuence of Soil-Structure interaction in the seismic response of reinforced concrete
silos.
– Seismic fragility analysis for reinforced concrete silos with diﬀerent geometries in terms
of height and diameter as well as diﬀerent type of ﬁlling material.
49
==PAGE:58==
9. Conclusion and Future Work
– Inﬂuence of imperfections in the response of reinforced concrete silos under seismic
excitations. Imperfections could include openings on the silos or other geometric im-
perfections. Also, the reinforcement layout plays a big role on the response of R/C
silos and diﬀerent reinforcement design could be investigated.
– Seismic fragility analysis of silos made by diﬀerent material than reinforced concrete
such as steel made silos.
– Impact of earthquake sequences on reinforced concrete silos. In this study only the
main-shock of an earthquake was used to excite the structure. A study can be done were
not only the main-shock but also the for-shock and the after-shock of the earthquake
excite the structure and the fragility of the silos can be re-evaluated.
