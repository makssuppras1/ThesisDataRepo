Abstract
For players in the deregulated energy markets, forecasts are vital when deciding
on bidding strategies. In a 2008 analysis Tryggvi proposed to use a two step approach
for making forecasts for the regulating price in Nord Pool’s Western Danish price area
(DK1). First, one estimates the probability of regulating occurring, and then one gives
an estimate of the regulating price conditioned on regulating occurring. Additionally,
he ﬁnds that the regulating price depends on the wind penetration.
This approach was tested on current data from the energy market in DK1. Further,
it was tested whether wind penetration still have an eﬀect on the regulating price.
Of the classiﬁcation models tested, Support Vector Machines are found to out-
perform Logistic Regression models in terms of accurately predicting both up and
down regulating. Of the conditional estimators tested, Quantile Regression models
were found to outperform Hidden Markov Models both for up and down regulating.
Further, the combined use of these models was demonstrated.
Comparing results of models including various combinations of explanatory vari-
ables, moderate evidence was found that wind have an important impact on the reg-
ulating price in DK1.
It was not possible to ﬁnd evidence that supports a claim
that wind penetration contains information that is not captured by other wind based
variables.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
With the reliance on electronics across virtually all areas in the private and public sector,
keeping a steady power supply is vital to any society. In Denmark this is achieved through
international multimarket processes.
In Denmark two of the important electricity markets are the Elspot market, also called
the Spot market or the Day Ahead Market, and the Regulating Market.
The bulk of
electricity in the nordic countries are traded through the Elspot market which works by
scheduling trading for the coming day. In the regulating market actors make energy bids
which are activated to cover imbalances that arise at delivery time. Both of these markets
are managed by the organization Nord Pool.
Of these the Elspot market have received signiﬁcant more academic attention. 135 results
are found for the query “ ‘Spot Price’ Nord Pool” in the database ‘Findit’, which is managed
by the Technical University of Denmark. In comparison the regulation price is relatively
underexplored in academia - with only 3 results appearing when “ ‘regulation price’ Nord
Pool” is queried into the same database.
Additionally, the contined trend of Danish energy
production to be increasingly based on larger shares of renewable energy resources causes
an associated loss of control over power production which might be expected to inﬂuence
the regulation price. For these reasons this work will be an analysis of the regulation price.
This thesis will attempt to replicate and reﬁne the results of the analysis done by Tryggvi
(2008[1]) using recent data. In this analysis Tryggvi proposed that the regulating price
could be modeled by a two-step approach. First, one estimated the probability of regulating
occurring, and then one estimated a spread of quantiles conditional on regulating happening.
A major ﬁnding of this work was the signiﬁcant inﬂuence of wind penetration, deﬁned as
the energy produced by wind farms divided by the total energy produced, on the regulating
price. As this ﬁnding now is a decade in the past, one might suspect it possible that actors in
the Danish energy market take this factor into consideration when making bids, potentially
eliminating the eﬀect of this variable. As such, the focus of the subsequent analysis will be
particularly on wind penetration, however it will also explore whether this could be replaced
by other variables, that likewise attempt to capture the eﬀect of wind on the Danish energy
market.
Improvements to the models proposed by Tryggvi will focus on exploring not just the
eﬀect of potential predictors, but the interactions of these variables and, when appropriate,
diﬀerent numbers of knots in splines. Additionally, as one might expect the market to contain
multiple diﬀerent states, a Hidden Markov Model is implemented and the performance of
this model is evaluated with respect to a quantile regression model.
The thesis is structured in the following way. In chapter 2 a description of the market is
given which aims to give the reader an understanding of what the regulating price is, and the
major eﬀects that inﬂuence the regulating price, both in terms of incentives in the system
and of how the production of energy, especially renewable energy, inﬂuences the price. In
chapter 3 a description of the available data is given as well as a brief validation of this data.
A mostly visual exploration of which of these might contain informational value is given in
chapter 4 which also contains a ﬁrmer deﬁnition of the target variable. An overview of the
most important mathematical theory used in the work of this thesis is given in chapter 5,
and the method and details for how these are used are described in chapter 6. The results
for speciﬁc models based on the theory and method chapters are given in chapter 7. Lastly,
chapter 8 contains a discussion of the ﬁndings.
To limit the scope, the thesis will focus on one area within Denmark, namely the price
area called DK1, which is described in the following chapter. This will not be an issue in

2
Market Description
The following section is intended to give the reader an understanding of the Nordic electricity
market.
The physical structure of the market, the interconnected characteristics of the
Nordic markets, the incentive system for the relevant players, and the nature of the energy
sources used all inﬂuence the regulation price, and thus a basic understanding is necessary
to understand the decisions made through this project as well as later results. This chapter
examines each aspect in turn.
2.1
Physical Market
The vast majority of electricity trading in the nordic countries is done through Nord Pool.
Nord Pool is a conglomerate with members being the Nordic Transmission System Op-
erators(abbreviated TSO) which are the organisations that govern the national electricity
systems. The local TSO in Denmark is called Energinet. Nord Pool was started in 1995 as
part of a wider deregulation process in Norway and has since expanded to cover a large part
of northern Europe. For more information on the history of Nord Pool see [2].
The areas in which Nord Pool operates are separated into price areas. Denmark contains
two such price areas which are split along the Great Belt. The price area west of the Great
Belt is called DK1 and the price area to the east is called DK2. This thesis is limited to DK1
in order to reduce the computational load, though it is quite possible that the approach is
suitable for other districts as well. One of the multiple advantages of Nord Pool is that it
allows any single price area to have access to more energy sources than the price area would
have locally. For instance, while Denmark has no water reservoir, the access to hydropower
from Norway can reduce the ﬂuctuation in price.
Nord Pool manages several physical delivery markets for power trading. One such market
is called the day-ahead market, which is sometimes referred to as the Elspot market or the
Spot market. In this market, all actors specify the purchase and sale volumes for the next
day in 5 minute increments, by noon on the day before electricity is delivered, and spot
prices are set on a market equilibrium model, taking into account all Elspot bids made in
the entire Nord Pool market. This interconnected nature then allows one price area to have
its energy needs covered by another price area while global system balance is maintained.
Since the market closes at noon and electricity is traded for the following day, bids are made
with a time horizon of 12-36 hours. In order to be allowed to trade on this market an actor
need to either be a Balance Responsible (described in section 2.2) or have a contract with
a Balance Responsible.
A snapshot of the movements in some of the cross-price area connections are shown with
red lines in Figure 1 which also contains the local Elspot price for that hour. DK1 is directly
connected to Norway(NO2), Sweden(SE3), Germany(DE), Eastern Denmark(DK2), and, as
of 2019, the Netherlands(NL).
As the physical capacity for transferred energy is limited, it is frequently reached. When
the ﬂow of energy exceeds the capacity of transfer connections, price areas end up with
diﬀerent energy prices.
All electricity producers within a price area are paid an equal
amount for the energy on this market and similarly all consumers pay the same price. One
example of the mechanics of this can be seen in Figure 1. The equivalent Elspot price (shown
with black numbers) in DK1 and DK2 implies that transfer capacity between these price
areas has not been reached. The higher price in DK1 compared to NO2 implies that transfer
capacity between DK1 and NO2 has been reached which is shown by directional arrows.

that drastic, many power using devices require a relatively constant voltage and thus would
stop working or work incorrectly. For this and other reasons it is desirable to keep the power

The cheapest of these methods uses the interconnectedness of Nord Pool to alleviate
some of these deviations by allowing price areas to easily assist each other. For example, if
there is a deﬁcit in DK1 and a surplus of energy in NO2, energy can be transferred from
the latter to the former, which reduces costs for both price areas. In many cases this either
is not feasible or is not suﬃcient to cover the full deviations, and other methods have to be
used.
For this reason Nord Pool oﬀers another market, referred to as the Intraday Market or
the Elbas Market. In this market individual oﬀers are made and deals happen if two oﬀers
align. This market closes 45 minutes before delivery time. In this way actors might be able
to solve their personal energy imbalance issues without the intervention of the local TSO.
A third market that is often used is called the Regulated Power Market. This market
is explained more thoroughly in Section 2.3 but the brief explanation is that in this market
participants put in bids that Nord Pool may or may not activate to cover existing imbalances.
These bids are similar to future contracts in that they grant the relevant TSO the right to
activate these bids from the participants should deviations occur. Depending on the direction
of the system imbalance bids are activated to increase or decrease the total energy and this
is referred to as up and down regulating respectively. Participants in the regulated power
market commit to being able to deliver the energy of their bids with a 15 minute warning.
Conceptually, placing a bid grants the relevant TSO the right but not the obligation to buy
or sell electricity on behalf of the Balance Responsibles.
2.2
Balance Responsibles Parties
Load Balance Responsibles (LBR) and Production Balance Responsibles (PBR) are com-
panies or organizations that pool oﬀers of load and production respectively. If an LBR
or PBR does not hit their projections (for instance if customers use more electricity than
projected or if wind stillness cause wind energy producers to produce less electricity than
anticipated) after the Intraday Market closes, they are obligated to cover their deﬁciencies
through buying (or selling) electricity through the regulated market. The schema for pricing
is diﬀerent for LBR and PBR. For more information on Balance Responsible parties see [4].
The price that a PBR pays for their imbalance depends on whether their contribution to
the overall system imbalance increases or decreases the system imbalance. If they contribute
to the overall imbalance(e.g. they use more electricity than expected and the overall balance
is lacking in energy) they must use the Regulated Power Market price. If their contribution
instead diminishes the overall imbalance(e.g. they use less electricity than expected and the
overall balance is lacking in energy) they must sell (or buy) excess power on the Elspot price.
These four scenarios can be seen compared in Figure 2. Here it is clear that they stand to
lose proﬁt from contributing to the system imbalance compared to selling the same power
at the Elspot Market. However, they do not gain or lose anything from having personal
imbalances which aid in reducing the overall system imbalance. This two price model was
chosen on the assumption that PBRs have greater control of their production and thus
would easily be able to exploit the scheme used for LBR.
LBRs, on the other hand, always pay and receive the regulation price for their imbalances.

2.3
Regulation price
As the regulation price deviates from the Elspot price in a way that generally is unfavorable
to the Balance Responsibles, the approach that is normally used ﬁrst to alleviate deviations
is to try to ﬁnd price areas that have imbalances in opposite directions. When this is not
feasible, either because the transfer connections might be at or near full capacity or the
imbalances might be bigger than the transfer capacity, regulating bids gets activated to
cover the deviations.
The group of activated bids consists of the bids which collectively allow the existing
imbalance to be equalized and which individually deviate the least from the Elspot price in
the direction of the imbalance. Except for bids that are activated through special regulation
(see below) all bids that are activated in the regulating power market are paid the same
price per megawatt which is the price of the bid that deviates the most from the Elspot
price. Bidders are able to specify whether they allow part of their bid to be activated or
whether their bid has to be fully activated or not activated. As a toy example imagine a
Elspot price of 46¿/MW, a price area imbalance of an excess of 100 MW after cross-price
area equalization has been attempted, and a down regulating bid list consisting of 3 bids,
one at 60 MW at 45¿/MW, one at 40 MW at 44¿/MW, and one at 50 MW at 43¿/MW.

In this case the ﬁrst two bids are activated and the two bidders buy the power at 44¿/MW
with this price being called the regulating power price.
In the above example it was assumed that the energy could be transferred to the activated
bidders, however just as cross-price area cables are limited in capacity so are cables in the
local grid. In some instances an area might have an excess or deﬁcit of energy and the
relevant cables might not be able to sustain the required speed of energy movement to allow
for this imbalance to be covered by the bidders that normally would have been activated
to cover it. One important example for DK1 is that excess wind production in Germany
is often exported to Denmark in signiﬁcant quantities, leaving a signiﬁcant excess of power
in southern Jutland, which cannot be moved to Fynen or northern Jutland fast enough.
In this case bids that ordinarily would not get activated in Southern Jutland might get
activated, still prioritizing bids with the smallest deviation from the Elspot price. This
process is called special regulation and, while it does have an impact on regulation price,
such as by eliminating excess power or through removing bids from the pool of accessible
bids, the regulating power price does not directly depend on bids activated through special
regulation.
2.4
Bidder incentives
Since the price paid for any activated bid is the same for all activated bids and bids closest
to the Elspot price is activated ﬁrst, there exist incentive to bid as close to the Elspot price
as possible in order to ensure activation. Simultaneously it is in the bidder’s interest that
the regulation price is as extreme as possible and thus it is in the bidder’s interests that the
most extreme activated bid deviates as much as possible from the Elspot price.
From a decision theoretical perspective, if all bid makers cooperated, they could agree
on making extreme bid prices. However, it is likely that some would defect (bid lower than
other bidders) when there is no way of punishing defectors. This is additionally compounded
in that collaborating in this fashion would have negative connotations for most players from
Nordic countries. In such a repeated prisoners dilemma[5] with no punishment for defectors,
one would expect repeating defects and thus bid prices to trend towards the Elspot price.
The above however does not account for the cost to produce the power associated with the
bids. The cost of producing energy, and thus the minimum price at which an up regulating
bidder can earn proﬁt, is strongly tied to the type of energy source used to produce the
energy.
As such, the pattern found by sorting the regulating bids from cheap to expensive for any
given delivery time might be expected to approximate a step function with the placement
of bids being strongly inﬂuenced by which power source was used to produce their energy.
Therefore, it is relevant to outline the underlying nature of the relevant energy sources used
to power DK1.

2.5.1
Wind power
Wind power plants use wind to provide mechanical energy by moving big turbines. Gen-
erating power this way is only eﬃcient at certain speeds and with the wind hitting the
turbines within a certain range of angles[7]. The power output by a windmill is positively
correlated with moderate wind speed, but with diminished returns at certain higher wind
speeds, converging eventually to 0 or even negative correlation at extreme speeds.
A severe limitation of wind energy is that the wind speed and direction, and thus the
associated power output, is diﬃcult to predict for the time horizons involved in the before-
mentioned markets. This leads to a lack of control in power output. Since a large proportion
of the power produced in DK1 comes from wind, one would expect this to be a major
contributor to variation in the regulation price.
The price of producing wind energy lies primarily in the construction and maintenance of
the windmill farms, since wind as a fuel source is free. Additionally, wind power is subsidized
by the Danish government, further reducing price. This results in a very low activation price
for bids using wind energy. It follows that regulating bids based on wind power would be
expected to be among the ﬁrst bids to get activated when up regulating is needed.
2.5.2
Solar power
As with wind power, sun is a free fuel when available, and the majority of the cost associated
with solar plant farms are in the construction and maintenance. While solar power lacks
control in ways that are similar to wind power, the times during which solar power and wind
power are available are diﬀerent. This can help mitigate some of the inherent variance in
each of the individual power sources.
2.5.3
Thermal power
Most traditional power plants work by a controlled burning of fuel in order to transform
thermal energy into kinetic energy. While there is an important distinction between many
forms of bio fuel, nuclear power and fossil fuels in terms of the environmental impact, they
do not diﬀer structurally in terms of how the conversion into power is handled and the degree
of control that is available to the energy producer. Compared to wind and solar power, the
fuel for thermal power plants is costly, but the control of the power output is independent of
variations in for instance weather conditions. As such, one might expect thermal power bids
to be activated when other bids are not available, and that the regulation price at least in
some circumstances, could be inﬂuenced by the price of the cheapest thermal fuel available.

2.5.4
Hydropower
Hydropower uses the potential energy stored in water reservoirs to generate energy by allow-
ing the water to ﬂow downwards, transforming the potential energy into kinetic energy. As
with solar and wind power, the majority of the cost lies in construction and maintenance.
However, compared to the previously discussed power sources, the fuel for hydropower,
namely water that arrives through rivers, has unique characteristics related to the price.
While water, like sun and wind, does not have a cost associated with producing or trans-
porting it, each reservoir has limited quantities of water storage.
Since the electricity price changes it is in the best interest of a reservoir to release water
to produce electricity when the price is high and hold onto water when the price is low, and
thus there is an opportunity cost associated with releasing water at a suboptimal time. At
the same time there is a constant inﬂow of water and it is in the reservoir owners interest to
release water even when the price is low if the alternative is releasing water without earning
anything due to reaching reservoir capacity. Reservoir managers must then reach a balance
to maximize proﬁt.
Hydropower is one of the cheapest energy sources and this is especially true during the
spring during which snow and ice melt, signiﬁcantly increasing the rate of reservoir reﬁlling.
As such DK1 often imports cheap power from Norway, especially when wind energy is sparse.

In many cases the explanatory relationship between the metrics examined and the target

5.6.2
Selection strategies
Once a family of models, a full set of potentially explanatory variables and legal combinations
and manipulations with all possible combinations, has been selected, and a process to include
those variables in the models has been selected, a modeler must decide on how to select the
best model. In the following I will refer to the V (r)
(full), with r omitted when the meaning is clear
from context, as the search space, and I will call two models close if they can reach each other
with a low number of individual changes. A method that is guaranteed to give the optimal
model is to estimate parameters for all models in V(full). However, in most cases |V(full)| is too
large for this to be computationally feasible. Instead the most common approaches taken
for selecting the best model follow some variation of the following framework.
1. Select an initial model. Often this model is either an empty model, a full model, or a
model based on prior domain knowledge.
2. Make a number of candidate models by searching (some part of) the search space close
to the current best model.
3. Through some criteria decide which is the best model between the current models
under consideration. If this model is not better than the last best model stop. If not,
let this be the current best model and return to step 2.
Page 39 of 76
==PAGE:40==
5
THEORY
Depending on the modelers domain knowledge and how well understood the ﬁeld is, the
modeler might a priori select a few ’likely best models’. Conversely, they might instead lean
heavily on some numerical score.
Well-studied approaches that rely heavily on a numerical score and follow the above
framework include
 Forward Selection: In this approach one (1) starts from a small model, possibly the null
model, (2) generates candidate models that are equal to the current best model with
one additional term added, and (3) selects the best model from the list of candidates.
 Backwards Elimination: In this approach one (1) starts from the largest possible
model, (2) generates candidate models that are equal to the current best model with
one term eliminated, and (3) select the best model from the list of candidates.
 Bidirectional Selection: This approach combines forwards selection and backwards
elimination in step 2. In this approach one (1) starts from some initial model, (2)
generate two sets of candidate models, one with models equal to the current best
model with an additional term as in forward selection, and one with models equal to
the current best model with a term elimination, and (3) select the best model from
the list of candidates.
 Evolutionary Algorithm: Rather than examining the search space close to the current
best model, each candidate model is generated such that it has a non-zero probability
of reaching any point in the search space. In this approach one (1) generates an initial
model, often randomly, (2) generates some number of candidate models such that
there is a non-zero chance for each candidate model to reach any point in the search
space while placing the bulk of the probability mass centered in the region close to
the current best model(s), and (3) selects with some probability the best performing
model as the current best model.
Forward selection is in most cases the most computationally eﬃcient approach since
initial models with a low number of parameters are generally faster to estimate and it only
checks in one direction. In a convex search space for the variables forward selection will ﬁnd
the globally best performing model. However, in most cases forward selection gets stuck in
a local optimum. Additionally, forward selection might include a variable that early in the
iterative selection process performed well, but was later explained by other variables with
no way to eliminate that variable. Compared to forwards selection, backwards elimination
has a higher chance of overﬁtting, though in some cases is able to reach better models due
to the initial model holding more information about the variables.
Since bidirectional selection, also sometimes called bidirectional elimination[27] and step-
wise selection[28], estimates parameters for models in both directions before taking a step, it
is usually more expensive than forward selection and backwards elimination. As it is able to
remove parameters whose informational value is better captured in a combination of other
variables or add values, which later in the process are informational, it in most cases is able
to reach a better model than forward selection or backwards elimination.
Evolutionary Algorithm is a varied class of algorithms. However in most cases it holds
that since the candidate models have a non-zero probability of reaching any point in the
search space, it has a non-zero chance at any point to reach the global optimum.
The
probability for candidate models to change across any axis is commonly called the mutation
rate, and this rate is usually low enough that candidate models are likely to be close to the

current best model(s). If an algorithm always selects the best model between the current
best model and the candidate models it is called elitist. Some non-elitist algorithms will
accept a worse candidate model with some probability in order to allow it to get out of a
saddle point.
5.6.3
Selection criteria
A well studied selection criteria is Aikaikes Information Criterium(AIC) which consists of
two terms; one term that rewards models that predict the data well and one term that
penalizes complexity through the number of parameters. In some cases it is not possible
to use a straightforward AIC. Two such cases are Support Vector Machines and Quantile
Regression.
In SVM the separation by along hyperplane does not lend itself to an easy selection
criteria. In [29] Bierman and Steel propose a two step process for variable selection for
SVM. They use an inner criterion to select the best model between models with the same
number of explanatory variables and an outer criterion to decide these within-group best
models. For outer criterion they ﬁnd success in choosing the locally best model with the
least number of positive support vector coeﬃcients.
Similarly for QR, the asymmetric loss function (Eq. 29) is not a probability and can
thus not directly be used in an AIC framework. Neither the estimator (Eq. 30), the sum
of absolute reliability scores (Eq. 33), or the CRPS (Eq. 34) contain a penalty term for
parameters. Therefore, an approach that seeks to optimize for one of those metrics would
likely end up overﬁtting. In [11] Koenker suggests an alternative estimator that is achieved
by adding a lasso term to the estimator in Eq. 30, yielding the estimator

splined variable. If all or none of these parameters approach zero the solution is trivial.
However, if some of the parameters go to zero and others do not, one must choose an
appropriate fraction of required non-zero parameters for the splined variable to be included.

Method
This section describes how the results are achieved by describing how the data is used, how
each model type was implemented, and the choices that were made in this process.
To estimate models the dataset was split into 3 subsets, a training dataset consisting of
50% of the data used for parameter estimation, a testing dataset consisting of 25% of the
data used to select between models when using a black box approach, and the remaining
25% used for result evaluation and validation. This was done through uniform sampling
without repetition. Reproducibility of the data segmentation was achieved by using the r
function set.seed() which sets the seed for the random number generation. The seed needs
to be a large number and in this case was set to 9876543.
The set of explored variables consisted of the forecasted variables described in section
3.1, {W, C, P, T(con)} in addition to their combination {WP, WS} and the 3 seasonal values
{H, M, D} for a total of 12 variables.
6.1
Logistic Regression
In this section the models of logistic regression models will be presented. The form of these
models are described in Section 5.1 though in these cases the explanatory variables included
are not speciﬁed.
Initially a gradient based likelihood method described in section 5.1.1 was manually im-
plemented using a learning rate change,α in Equation 10, of 0.05. While this implementation
did achieve good results, it was computationally slow. Attempts at optimization were made,
however none of these led to a signiﬁcant increase in speed.
One approach for increasing speed was to search for pre-optimized R estimation methods,
and the glm() function from the stats package[30] using family=binomial was found to
increase computation speeds by orders of magnitudes. This implementation uses the GLM
framework introduced in section 5.1.2.
The axis explored was all variables under consideration, their pairwise interactions, and
splines of both variables and interactions with legal number of knots K = {0, 3, 4, · · · , 15}, to
allow for non-linear informational value. For seasonal values and the interactions consisting
only of these, periodic cubic splines were used, since this reﬂected their seasonal behaviour.
For non-seasonal variables, their interactions, and their interactions with seasonal values,
that is the interaction of one seasonal variable and one non-seasonal value, cubic natural
splines were used. As such, the total number of possible combinations, using Eq. 60, is
around 2 · 1016, and an exhaustive search of the search space is infeasible. Instead an algo-
rithm was implemented that closely follows the bidirectional selection processes described in
section 5.6.2 using AIC as the selection criteria and parameter estimation performed using
GLM.
To ﬁnd the optimal number of knots in each spline, the initial implementation only
explored the immediate search space, that is knew = kold ± 1 in each spline. In this way,
for each variable in the current best model, two candidate models were generated. These
were identical to the current best model with the number of knots in the spline function
for one variable changed such that an integer was added to or subtracted from respectively
the original number of knots for that variable. However, as it was early discovered that the
search space for optimal number of knots was non-convex, a stochastic process was used
that instead added and subtracted a normally distributed scalar to the old number of knots
for each spline respectively.

Parameters were estimated by the rq() function from the quantreg package[34] and the

The bidirectional model selection was done as described in Algorithm 3 considering all

7.5
Combining the models
One way for balance responsibles to use these models in combination is to allow for the
best classiﬁcation model to give an estimate of the probability of regulating happening and
if it is deemed likely enough, make an estimation of RPD(Dir),t conditional on regulating
happening. The following will show the results of this combined approach using the last
25% of the dataset as the validation set, spanning from the 27th of July 2018 to the 10th of
September 2019.
The best classiﬁcation model in either direction was the support vector machine that
contained all variables.
For up regulating, the best model for modeling the conditional
RPD(UP) was either the model found by the bidirectional selection M(BS) (Eq. 76) or the
one which was like the Tryggvi model (Eq. 71) with Wind Penetration substituted with
Wind Share M(WS) depending on the evaluation criteria.
As such, a framework which
combines the best SVM model with M(BS) and a framework that combines that best SVM
model with M(WS) is tested. The reference model always predict that the RPD will be the
same as at the time of prediction. As all predictions in this section are made at 15:00, 3
hours after Elspot closure, this prediction takes the form of a straight line which changes
every 24 hours.
Point estimates were made for each model. This estimate was equal to 0 when the SVM
predicted no regulating and equal to the average of the conditional quantile distribution
when the SVM predicted regulating. A snapshot of this combined framework is shown in
ﬁgure 25. Here the point estimates are shown with dots of the associated colour, and bars
show the range from the 5th percentile to the 95th percentile, thus giving a 90% conditional
conﬁdence interval for when activating happens.
Blue and green indicate the combined
model that uses M(WS) and M(BS) respectively as the conditional estimator respectively.
The observations are shown as black dots.
The mean squared prediction error(MSE) was calculated for the two combined models
as well as the reference model. The coeﬃcient of determination, denoted R2, was calculated
in sample for the two combined models. These can be seen in Table 12. The model using

8
Discussion
In this section the results are discussed, major observations are summarized, and an expla-
nation is given of how the described models could be used in practice.
The best model found using Logistic Regression managed a hit rate of 70% and 61% for
up and down regulating respectively. Both of these models were signiﬁcantly biased towards
predicting no regulating, with the up regulating having the particularly low sensitivity of
only 14%. The corresponding hit rates for the best Support Vector Machines were 72% and
67% with the increase being driven exclusively by a signiﬁcant increase in sensitivity.
The best model in Quantile Regression outperformed the Hidden Markov Models on
both reliability score and CRPS, and Hidden Markov Models performed signiﬁcantly worse
than even a fully endogenous quantile model.
In the best performing models for each model family, variables containing information
about the wind energy was often included. Table 14 summarizes which of the best models
in each model type included the three wind-related variables. ‘Y’ in position ij in the table
indicates that variable j was included in the best performing model for model type i for
j = {Wind(W), Wind Penetration(WP), Wind Share(WS)}. ‘N’ in position ij shows that
variable j was not included in that model. If there are two best performing models, due to
one model performing best on the ﬁrst evaluation metric and another performing best on
the second evaluation metric, ‘N’ and ‘Y’ are shown for both model with & separating them.
As such ‘Y&N’ indicates that the variable was in one but not the other model and ‘N&N’
indicates that the variable was in neither of the models.

All 3 wind based variables were included in the best models for the SVM. As the local
search space around the best performing model of this model type was not searched and
since the resulting parameters are not easily interpretable, no further conclusions can be
drawn other than that this model outperformed the competitors. The inclusion of Wind
Penetration as the only wind based variable in the best performing Quantile Regression
model for up regulating but not for down regulating suggests that Wind Penetration have
more information about up regulating than down regulating.
At least one wind based variable was included in each of the best models. This might be
interpreted as moderate to strong evidence for wind having an eﬀect on the RPD that is not
yet fully captured by existing models used by the actors in the market. The fact that wind
penetration does not dominate wind share could be interpreted as weak evidence for wind
penetration not holding the same explanatory value as it did at the time of Tryggvi’s 2008
analysis and consequently, could potentially be replaced with other wind based variables.
Page 64 of 76
==PAGE:65==
The inclusion of the transferred energy between Germany and Denmark in the quantile
regression model found with bidirectional selection might be taken as evidence for this
transfer connection to be more inﬂuential to the down regulating price than other transfer
connections. However, since this model was not the best performing model on any metrics
this can only be taken as very weak evidence.
It is of concern for the quality of the results, that the Nord Pool data was unable to pass
simple data validation, and this may lower our conﬁdence in the accuracy of the results.
The classiﬁcation models seem to consistently outperform the models used by Tryggvi
even for models of the same form. This can potentially be attributed to better prognoses,
as this project uses prognoses made by Energinet, or it could be attributed to changes in
the market over the last decade.
In the example for how these models could be used, the combined models outperformed
the reference model, and visual inspection of the snapshot of their predictions seem promis-
ing. The R2 of best best of these models was 0.11 for up regulating and 0.28 for down
regulating.
The conditional quantile estimate can be used to make a point estimate, however the
real use of it is in its ability to capture the range of likely behaviour for the conditional reg-
ulating price. For instance a balance responsible may be interested in preemptively covering
potential loses, such as by increasing/decreasing production or through the Elbas market,
when the 95th quantile is extreme and the probability of regulating is high. Additionally,
the expected cost per MW of personal imbalance may be calculated by averaging over the
predicted quantiles and multiplying with the probability of regulating. This expected cost
can be directly compared to the cost associated with preemptively covering a potential
imbalance, thus allowing precise cost-beneﬁt analysis.
An alternate use of the combined models are the use for simulation. By simulating the
relevant explanatory variables, this combined model can be used to create simulations that
capture the overall behaviour of the regulating price market. Given that the fully endogenous
quantile model performed surprisingly well in both up and down regulating, it might even
be possible to make simulations without the need for assumptions on the underlying values,
if the simulator have access to a suitable probability distribution.
In summary, the best hit rate found for regulating prediction was 72% and 67% for up
and down regulating respectively and this was achieved by using Support Vector Machines.
It was not possible to ﬁnd evidence to support the claim that Wind Penetration carry
unique informational value not captured by other wind based variables, such as Wind Share.
Moderate evidence was found for wind having a signiﬁcant impact on the regulating price
and consequently, that the impact of wind on the regulating price is not fully captured by
models currently used by actors in the regulating price market. The best combined models
had an R2 of 0.12 and 0.28 for up and down regulating respectively.
In Tryggvi’s analysis it was shown that the classiﬁcation models can be improved further
by only training the models on a subset consisting of the most recent data instead of the
entire dataset.
In this way parameters were estimated for each prediction rather than
parameters being ﬁxed scalars. It is expected that this approach would similarly improve
the above models.
While Hidden Markov Models performed poorly, more research is required in order to
fully dismiss this group of models. As an improvement was observed when the search process
was given more computational power(i.e. decrease the tolerance for convergence), it’s quite
possible that a Hidden Markov Model that performs better than a Quantile Regression model
can be found with suﬃcient computational power. Further, allowing exogenous variable to

Due to the interconnected nature of the Nordic power markets a model that incorporates
information from multiple price areas might yield improved results. For instance, it is plau-

A market that have not been explored in this analysis is the eﬀect of the Elbas market
