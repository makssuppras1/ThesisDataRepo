1. Abstract 
When sequencing microorganisms from an environmental sample, a metagenomic approach 
has proved to be an efficient method for quickly identifying species. However, there is still 
room for improving and finetuning the method. The most error-prone step of the metagenomic 
approach is binning – the process of correctly grouping assembled sequences into bins so 
that each bin represents one species. To overcome this limitation, single-cell (sc) 
metagenomic sequencing has been proposed as a candidate for overcoming these limitations.  
Sc-metagenomics builds on a metagenomic methodology, Though, rather than extracting 
genetic material from the sample directly, all cells are isolated from the sample, encapsulated 
into drops for DNA amplification, and are sequenced individually. Through these additional 
steps we can apply a co-assembly strategy. The main advantage of single-cell sequencing 
after encapsulation is that the co-assembled contigs can be mapped back to the individual 
cells, resulting in a per-cell coverage matrix. With this new insight, the binning step now has 
the potential to produce higher quality single-amplified genomes (SAGs) for classification. 
A wastewater sample from Bangladesh spiked with a mock community was used to investigate 
the sc-metagenomic methodology’s advantages. We used a coverage matrix file gained from 
the co-assembly with the binning program MetaBAT 2 as it has the capacity to intake this new 
information. However, this resulted in worse quality bins than when the drops were simply 
treated as a metagenome. With the assumption that MetaBAT 2 was not developed to 
optimally bin single-cell co-assembled contigs, we developed a manual binning method 
through compositional clustering. This method consists of using the coverage matrix as well 
as a tetramer frequency table and utilizing them for grouping contigs through different 
clustering algorithms, such as k-means, and hierarchical clustering. After applying these 
methods, we observed that higher quality bins were produced compared to MetaBAT 2 and 
the conventional metagenomic approach, demonstrating that single-cell metagenomic 
sequencing has the potential for gaining higher quality SAGs, and therefore, better 
taxonomical classification compared to more traditional methods.

2. Introduction and Background  
2.1 Single-Cell Metagenomics 
Within the last few decades, the advancement in computational power has allowed for a 
significant improvement in the field of genomics, specifically in the speed and complexity at 
which genomic data can be analysed. This advancement has resulted in more accurate data 
interpretation which leads to better detection of rare genetic variations, as well as a deeper 
understanding of evolutionary traits between species. Two methodologies which are important 
in the field of genomics are single-cell sequencing and metagenomic sequencing, each with 
their individual strengths and limitations. 
Single-cell sequencing allows for the insight into the complete genome of an organism, 
although this methodology requires isolation of the organism cell, and may be affected by 
incomplete or uneven coverage during amplification techniques. Metagenomic sequencing 
eliminates the need for cell isolation since it has the advantage of analysing an entire 
community of organisms’ genetic content at once through a single sample. Although it avoids 
the need for cell isolation, the metagenomic methodology requires an additional step which 
consists of binning assembled contigs into complete metagenome-assembled genomes 
(MAGs), otherwise known as bins. The main issue arises during this step as current binning 
tools have been observed to have difficulty in producing high quality bins, where the quality is 
determined by completeness and contamination of the genome. [1] Single-cell (sc) 
metagenomics is a proposed methodology which combines the strengths of single-cell 
sequencing and metagenomic sequencing with the aim of overcoming each individual 
methodologies’ limitations.  
The methodology of sc-metagenomics builds on the metagenomic methodology with the 
addition of an encapsulation step. As mentioned, metagenomic sequencing’s main limitation 
is correctly distinguishing and comparting assembled contigs into their genome of origin. Sc-
metagenomics attempts to overcome this limitation by encapsulating all cells found in the 
metagenomic sample into individual capsules, named drops. Each drop is put through DNA 
amplification and sequenced individually. At this point, it is possible to assemble the reads 
from each individual drop into single-amplified genomes (SAGs) with the intent of identifying 
every species encapsulated.

However, the likely possibility of low read-counts in the drops can lead to fragmented genomes 
with low read depths, resulting in unreliable or impossible taxonomical classification of the 
species found in the sample. A co-assembling strategy should be used instead to avoid this 
issue by taking all the drops’ reads and assembling them together with the aim of producing 
higher quality SAGs. [1] 
2.2 Co-Assembly and Binning 
In short, a co-assembly strategy consists of combining all the reads from each drop into a 
single input file and assembling them as if they would be a standard metagenomic sample. 
This will result in higher read depths and potentially less fragmented genomes, although it will 
also result in losing track of which reads came from which drop. To overcome this, the gained 
contigs can be mapped against the individual drop reads. This method of mapping the contigs 
back to drop reads (described further in the Methods section titled BBMap) results in a file 
containing the mean and variance of base coverage depths, which will be referred to as the 
coverage matrix file from this point on. 
An important step required after using co-assembly is to correctly bin the contigs into 
genomes, similarly to when using metagenomic sequencing. It is crucial for this binning step 
to be reliable and accurate since it should not undo the efforts of encapsulation and co-
assembly. As mentioned previously, the main limitation of metagenomic sequencing is 
correctly binning the contigs into complete genomes. In the case of using sc-metagenomics, 
the binning step can make use of additional scaffold information which is the coverage matrix 
gained from mapping the scaffolds back to the drop reads. The binning tool MetaBAT 2 is an 
ideal tool for this since it operates using contig, or scaffold, tetranucleotide frequency (TNF) 
with the option of also using the coverage matrix as input for binning. However, MetaBAT 2 is 
designed for binning contigs gained from a standard metagenomic assembly, and using the 
coverage matrix file as input might affect its binning performance. Therefore, this project will 
also investigate in developing its own binning method in the hopes of overcoming this potential 
limitation. 
CheckM2 is a tool which can evaluate the quality of SAGs and MAGs, and thus, it is a good 
method for evaluating the binning methods utilised in this project. Once the quality of the SAGs 
gained during the binning process have been quality checked by ChecM2, a taxonomical 
classification tool named GTDB-Tk can be used to classify which species are found in the 
given metagenomic sample.

2.3 Sc-Metagenomic Pipeline 
This project aims to create an optimised pipeline which can perform all the mentioned 
computational steps post encapsulation and sequencing, where the input would be any 
number of FASTA files gained from sequenced drops, and the output would be an overview of 
complete genomes of the organisms found in the sample. The project’s main investigative goal 
is to optimise the pipeline, specifically the binning step, in its ability to produce as many high-
quality bins, or SAGs, from the given sample as possible. This is achieved by creating a 
baseline for performance using the binning tool MetaBAT 2, a well-established binner in 
standard metagenomic methodologies.  
MetaBAT 2 groups contigs using their tetranucleotide frequency (TNF) information, although 
it is also able to take the mentioned coverage file gained from mapping contigs back to the 
original reads. In a traditional metagenomic methodology, this would result in a file consisting 
of a single column of values denoting how well each contig maps back to the reads. In the 
case of using co-assembly, the contigs can be mapped back to each drop, resulting in a file 
where each column represents a drop, creating a coverage matrix file. Using the same idea, 
we aimed to create our own binning method with the hopes of producing a larger number of 
high-quality SAGs. This was achieved by exploring different clustering algorithms which would 
use the same TNF and coverage data from the assembled contigs and clustering them into 
bins. The two main clustering algorithms explored in this project are hierarchical clustering 
and k-means clustering. 
2.4 Datasets 
Two different genomic datasets are used to assess the various steps of this pipeline. The first 
is a simple, pure culture dataset consisting of the two species Escherichia coli and Bacillus 
spizizenii, previously known as B. subtilis subspecies spizizenii. This dataset, referred to as 
the pure culture sample through the project, was used mainly as a starting point for assessing 
whether clustering algorithms could be applied to the scaffold TNF and coverage data to obtain 
clear groupings of the two species.  
The second dataset is a wastewater sample from Bangladesh. Since this is an environmentally 
obtained sample, there is no previous information on the species found within.  Therefore, the 
sample, referred to the sewage sample throughout this project, has also been spiked with a

The main objective of this project is to design and construct a pipeline focused on maximizing 
high-quality bin output. With this focus in mind, the final version of the pipeline is aimed to 
implement the best performing binning tactic tested on the sample data. The remaining steps 
and structure of the pipeline are the same throughout and described in this section.  
The pipeline is designed to take FASTQ files of the 
post-sequencing drop reads as input and conclude 
with an output file containing a list of the 
taxonomical classifications of the organisms found 
in the sample. The pipeline is also designed to

3.1 Snakemake 
For the development of the pipeline centred around this project, we chose to use the workflow 
management system named Snakemake, mainly due to its straightforward syntax which has 
led it to be a popular choice in bioinformatic pipelines. The Snakemake workflow is written in 
a comprehensible, python-like syntax file named Snakefile, which is then executed through a 
command line prompt. Each step of the workflow developed during this project is written within 
this file, where several individual scripts are called and executed as the pipeline runs. These 
steps are referred to as Rules in the pipeline. 
Snakemake provides several useful features, such as the ability to define how many cores 
(CPUs) to use for each step, which in turn allows for automatic parallelisation of multiple steps 
for maximum efficiency. Beyond this, it is the first system to support multiple variables, named 
wildcards, which are automatically imbedded into the naming of input and output files. In other 
words, the same pipeline can be run on two different datasets simultaneously through its use 
of parallelisation, allowing for more efficient data analysis. Another useful feature is the ability 
to benchmark individual steps so that a separate log file is saved. The file contains multiple 
measuring parameters such as the memory usage, computational time, and real time of which 
an individual step has used, which allows us to compare the efficiency of the different 
workflows contained in this project. 
Once a pipeline has been developed, Snakemake also has the useful option to implement 
user defined variables which can be specified when executing the pipeline in a command line 
format. In this project’s case, the pipeline will have the option for the user to determine the 
minimum number of reads in a drop for it to be included in the coverage matrix, as well as the 
minimum base pair length of a contig to be included in the coverage matrix. Such a feature 
will allow for future users to use the pipeline without the need to edit the Snakefile itself. [4]

3.3 metaSPAdes 
The first step of every workflow in this project is collecting the reads from each individual drop 
and combining them into a single FASTA file. This is done using the command ‘zcat’ and 
written as it’s individual rule in the Snakefile. This FASTA file is then used as the input for the 
rule which will contain the co-assembly step, where we chose an assembling tool named 
metaSPAdes for this task. The SPAdes assembler was originally a tool designed for de novo 
assembly with focus on single-cell sequencing data and cultivated microbial isolates. Although 
in the years following its development, SPAdes has evolved to include additional assembling 
strategies, including metaSPAdes which is a variety of the tool designed for the assembly of 
metagenomic data. [5] MetaSPAdes is a great candidate for the co-assembling step, since it 
was developed to assemble metagenomic data which is what our sample contains and has 
been shown to be the overall best-performing assembler when compared against other 
metagenomic assemblers. [6] 
MetaSPAdes assembles the individual reads into contigs through a series of different 
algorithmic steps. Firstly, it performs k-mer counting, a method for estimating the abundance 
of unique k-mers of various sizes. This step is crucial in overcoming the challenges that come 
with metagenomic data such as closely related species or varying abundance of species 
across the sample. Using the information gathered during k-mer counting, metaSPAdes then 
constructs a de Bruijn graph which represents the overlaps within the k-mers of the reads. 
From this point, the graph is put through a simplification step before becoming the blueprint 
used to build contigs which are the final output by the tool. If the input reads are paired-end, 
which is the case for the data used in this project, metaSPAdes is able to take the contigs and 
further assemble them into scaffolds, based on their paired-end read information. [7]

Each drop’s reads is mapped against the file containing the scaffolds output by metaSPAdes,

bioinformatic tools from the collection named SAMtools. [9] The entire drop mapping step and 
with several BAM file outputs is done through a custom written script using the mentioned 
tools and embedded into the pipeline as a single rule. 
Each individual BAM file is then combined to create the mentioned coverage matrix file using 
‘jgi_summarize_bam_contig_depths’, a script developed as a part of the MetaBAT 2 package 
for the purpose of combining information of several BAM files. From this point in the project, 
the coverage matrix, where the columns represent each drop and the rows represent each 
scaffold gained from our co-assembly, is same file used for the different binning algorithms 
tested for a better comparison between methods.  
3.5 MetaBAT 2 
As previously mentioned, MetaBAT 2 is a well-known binner used for metagenomic research, 
and in this case, a great baseline for comparing the results of our developed clustering 
algorithms against. The tool is typically used to bin contigs, or scaffolds, gained from a 
standard metagenomic assembly, so the project aims to illustrate whether it can perform just 
as well when using the additional information gained from co-assembling our dataset. 
MetaBAT 2 is designed to bin contigs or scaffolds using solely their tetranucleotide frequencies 
(TNF), or with the additional base coverage depth file, therefore, MetaBAT 2 is tested under 
three different conditions. The first condition is binning using only the TNF information from 
the scaffolds, which the tool itself is designed to acquire in its pipeline. The second condition 
is equal to a metagenomic methodology use of MetaBAT 2 where it makes use of the TNF 
information as well as a coverage file which only has one column, the sample’s reads against 
each scaffold. The third condition is one which examines whether MetaBAT 2 can make use 
of the additional information in the coverage matrix file, by using TNF information as well as

3.6 Binning with R 
The main efforts of this project became centred around creating an algorithmic binning 
strategy able to outperform the binning tool MetaBAT 2 when dealing with sc-metagenomic 
data. Although R is not the most efficient language, RStudio is chosen for developing the 
binning methods tested as it has a huge variety of already developed packages designed for 
handling and clustering data. An additional advantage is that it has the option of both writing 
and documenting scripts, using files named R Markdown, which allow for effortless 
combination of code and narrative text. Beyond this, it also includes the option of printing a 
report with an overview of the code, including tables and graphs, of how the user’s input data 
was analysed. [11] 
The idea behind the binning process is to be able to take the coverage matrix gained from a 
co-assembly, as well as the TNF information of the gained scaffolds and combine them with 
the aim of grouping the scaffolds into bins using compositional data analysis and clustering 
methods. Before clustering, the coverage matrix and TNF data are read, and all zero values 
are replaced using the package zCompositions which has the function ‘multRepl’ and 
transformed individually using centre log ratio (CLR), using the package Compositions, so that 
the sum of each row is equal to one and the mean is equal to 0. After that, the transformed 
coverage matrix and TNF are adjusted in weight and combined into a single data frame. The 
adjustment in weight is in other words, how much influence each factor has when clustering 
the data. In the coverage matrix, each column represents the reads of each drop of the data 
so that there are as many columns as there are drops in the data frame. The TNF data frame 
has 132 columns, a constant number of unique tetramers.  
The two clustering algorithms which are explored are hierarchical clustering and k-means 
clustering which will use the combined TNF and coverage matrix data for clustering the 
scaffolds.  
3.7 CheckM 2 
The original version of CheckM is a tool built for assessing the quality of microbial genomes 
recovered from single cells or metagenomes. It estimates the quality of input bins by using a 
set of highly conserved marker genes to determine their completeness and contamination, 
both of which are given to the user as a percentile. Completeness is determined by identifying

these marker genes in the given bin and identifying the fraction of them present. 
Contamination is then the fraction of identified marker genes belonging to a different species 
or multiple copies of the same marker gene. [12] 
CheckM2 builds on its original version of identifying marker genes by implementing machine 
learning for prediction of completeness and contamination. It has since demonstrated to 
outperform CheckM, making it the better choice for bin quality assessment. Beyond this, 
CheckM2 has the option of predicting completeness and contamination using both its new 
machine learning method and the original CheckM algorithm, with an additional parameter 
indicating which of the two the tool evaluates to be the most accurate method for the bin. This 
option, called using the additional command ‘--predict’, is used in the pipeline for more reliable 
quality indicators. [13] 
When using CheckM2, the bins which are shown to have a completeness score nearing 100% 
with no contamination implies that they contain contigs that together make up complete 
genomes of the sample’s species without any genomic data from other species. Both 
parameters are uniquely important for this project in terms of maximising high-quality SAGs, 
and each attempted binning method will have multiple bins with varying completeness and 
contamination ratings. Therefore, a scoring system is built for an easier comparison between 
methods.  
Firstly, the completeness and contamination of each bin should be combined into a single 
value. Bins with high completeness are the main goal of the pipeline, but not if they have high 
contamination. Thus, the contamination value must bear a higher weight when calculating this 
score. Specifically, we chose for the contamination to have a fivefold increased weight, 
resulting in the following equation:

3.8 GTDB-Tk 
The Genome Taxonomy Database Toolkit (GTDB-Tk) is a tool designed for the taxonomical 
classification of microbial genomes. It provides accurate and comprehensive taxonomy of 
genomes by using the Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB), hence the naming of the tool, 
by using genomic information as well as phylogenetic analysis of the given data. GTDB-Tk 
has proven to be an efficient classification tool able to provide accurate taxonomical

5. Discussion 
The main goal of this project was to design a pipeline which could take multiple input FASTA 
files containing reads of encapsulated species from a metagenomic sample and utilise a co-
assembly strategy for taxonomical classification of the species found in the sample. The focal 
point of the pipeline’s development was to guarantee that the binning step could be tested and 
improved in such a way that would not counter the advantages of using encapsulation and co-
assembly. The first step was to establish a baseline with MetaBAT 2 so that any developed 
improvements could be compared against a well-known tool within the metagenomic field. 
Using the pure culture sample as a starting point for designing a clustering method, we 
observed through a principal component analysis that using the scaffolds’ TNF the coverage 
matrix combined information allowed for a clear clustering of the two species found in the 
sample. We then used MetaBAT 2 with varying degrees of scaffold information input  to assess 
whether it’s resulting bins matched the clusters we observed through PCA. Figures 2-6 
illustrate that according to our PCA clusters, MetaBAT 2 performed best when binning with 
only the TNF information. Additionally, it performed the worst when it had both TNF and the 
coverage matrix information where it could only create one bin of E. coli scaffolds. This was 
unexpected since we theorized that inputting more information given on the scaffolds will only 
improve the accuracy of the binning step. This gave a clear motivation for the project to seek 
our own binning algorithm, developed for the purpose of utilising the full information of the 
coverage matrix combined with TNF.  
We decided on testing two different clustering algorithms for developing our binning method, 
hierarchical clustering, and k-means clustering. Since hierarchical clustering has a variety of 
options for distance and linkage algorithms, we ran multiple heatmap analysis with different 
combinations of distance and linkage methods. We observed that all combinations agreed the 
resulting clusters of the two species, which is likely due to the simplicity of the pure culture 
sample. At this point, it is worth noting that a more complex dataset with around five different 
species could potentially be a better option for testing the different distance and linkage 
combinations. Still, the combination of using Euclidean distance combined with ward.D2 
linkage produced the clearest differentiation between both clusters. As for k-means clustering, 
we also chose for it to use the Euclidean distance method for two main reasons. Firstly, using 
a Euclidean distance method is the standard algorithm for k-means, also known as naïve k-
means. Secondly, using the same distance method as for hierarchical clustering allows for a 
more just comparison between the two algorithms.

From this point, the sewage sample was used to evaluate our two clustering algorithms. 
MetaBAT 2 was again used as a baseline with three varying degrees of scaffold information 
where we observed that it performed best when only given the scaffolds TNF with no additional 
coverage information, the same outcome we witnessed with the pure culture sample. We 
chose MetaBAT 2 as the baseline since it has been confirmed to be a trustworthy binning tool, 
so it is unexpected that it would perform less than optimally when given the additional 
coverage matrix information; although, that may be the reason for its shortcomings. MetaBAT 
2 was designed to bin contigs and scaffolds that have gone through a standard metagenomic 
assembly. Using it for the purpose of binning co-assembled data and inputting a coverage 
matrix file, rather than the usual single-column coverage file, is possibly the reason it to not 
perform as expected. In other words, it is not that MetaBAT 2 has not been a trustworthy too, 
but rather that it has been utilised with a dataset it was not designed for. Still, this further 
motivates the project to have developed its own binning step.  
Before testing both clustering algorithms, a PCA dimension reduction was performed on the 
data to reduce computational efforts. The number of dimensions to reduce the data to was 
chosen by visualising how much of the sample data was explained by the first 100 principal 
components. In the end, we chose to reduce the dimensions down to 50 since it would 
represent over 85% of the data. As a future prospect, a more extensive approach would be to 
choose a variety of dimensions and comparing them. As an example, one could reduce the 
dimensions down to 25, 50, 75, and 100, and compare how significant of an impact each 
reduction has on the quality of the resulting bins. 
The first tested clustering algorithm was hierarchical clustering where we observed that it got 
a binning method score of 899.35 which was slightly higher than MetaBAT 2’s best score of 
875.86. Overall, the hierarchical clustering had 15 bins of at least medium quality compared 
to MetaBAT 2 which at its best had 17. On the other hand, hierarchical clustering had 4 HQ 
bins which was double of MetaBAT 2 which could be argued for as a better result since the 
main aim is to get as high-quality bins as possible. K-means clustering was the next tested 
algorithm which managed to have a binning method score of 929.06, which is an even better 
score than hierarchical clustering. It produced the same number of MQ bins but had 5 HQ 
bins, higher than hierarchical clustering. 
We could assume that k-means clustering is the better choice for implementing into the 
pipeline based on these results, but to be certain of its potential advantage, more runs were 
tested with different coverage matrices where the number of drops and scaffolds to create it 
were varied. Seen in Tables 4 and 5 are the results of these runs, where there is further 
evidence that k-means clustering is superior to hierarchical clustering. Seven out of all nine

A secondary purpose of assessing the different algorithms with varying drop and scaffold

The final aspect of this project was to find a method which would replace testing a range of k-

methods were tested on the various runs of k-means clustering, but we observed that neither 
of the two methods managed to make a convincing argument for the best k-value. This means 
there is much room for improvement in terms of finding a better way for finding the k-value in 
the clustering method to produce the best bins in terms of quality.

6. Conclusion 
Overall, the main goal of this project was to develop a pipeline which could intake several 
FASTA files containing reads from individually sequenced species and output high quality bins. 
When comparing the gained bins from the hierarchical and k-means clustering methods to the 
established baseline using MetaBAT 2, the project was successful in outputting bins with 
improved quality. On that note, whether the quality of the bins is good enough is the next big 
question. This project managed to show that the clustering methods used to bin scaffolds 
together is viable, but there is still room for improving this method. The main shortcoming of 
the project was not being able to implement a suitable method for finding the optimal k-value, 
seeing as the elbow and silhouette methods did not work as expected. Finding such a method 
would improve the pipeline’s results by finding the greatest number of high-quality bins in a 
more computationally friendly way. 
For the future, the first step would be to look further into different methods which can find the 
optimal k-value for a clustering algorithm. Beyond that, there is much room for further testing 
the steps of the pipeline. The best place to start would be to test the pipeline’s performance 
on multiple different datasets, preferable of varying complexity to test the limits of the 
developed binning method. The results of this project are promising in the aim of outputting 
high quality bins, but they are limited to the dataset used during this project, therefore the 
pipeline can only improve from further testing on more environmental metagenomic samples.
