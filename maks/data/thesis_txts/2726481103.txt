Abstract
This thesis investigates the solid biomechanics of the human heart using computational
modelling and simulation techniques. Two left ventricle models were studied: a simple
symmetric model created in Solidworks and a more realistic model created from CTA
data using 3D Slicer. A finite element model was developed for each left ventricle geom-
etry to study its mechanical behaviour under different loading conditions in COMSOL
Multiphysics 6.0. The simple symmetric model exhibited less complex mechanical be-
haviour compared to the more realistic model, suggesting the importance of considering
heart geometry in mechanical analyses. Furthermore, the simulations suggest that the
shape and motion of the heart during the cardiac cycle have a significant influence on the
distribution of stress.
Overall, the findings of this thesis provide important insights into the solid biomechan-
ics of the human heart, highlighting the need for accurate modelling techniques and the
consideration of realistic anatomical structures. These findings have implications for the
development of more accurate models of heart function and diseases.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The study of the biomechanics of the human heart is a critical area of research in the
field of cardiovascular health. This field aims to understand the mechanical properties
and behaviors of the heart, including its structure, function, and performance, in order
to develop improved diagnostic and therapeutic strategies for heart conditions. A better
understanding of the biomechanics of the heart can lead to the development of more
effective medical devices, such as artificial hearts and heart valves, and improved surgical
procedures. Furthermore, advances in this field can also inform the development of new
pharmacological treatments for heart conditions and improve the prognosis for patients
suffering from heart disease.
The study of the solid biomechanics of the human left ventricle has gained considerable
attention in recent years due to its crucial role in the functioning of the heart.
The
left ventricle is responsible for pumping blood throughout the body, and understanding
its mechanical behaviour is essential for improving our understanding of cardiovascular
disease and developing effective treatments. The field of soft tissue biomechanics became
a significant area of research in the 1960s, thanks to the rise of nonlinear mechanics, the
development of finite element methods, and the rapid advancement of computer science
during that time. The left ventricle is responsible for pumping oxygenated blood to the
rest of the body, and any dysfunction can lead to heart conditions such as heart failure.
Studying the biomechanics of the left ventricle through numerical simulation can provide
insight into its behavior under different conditions and help in the development of better
diagnostic and therapeutic methods for heart conditions.
1.2
Thesis objective
The objective of this master’s thesis project is to create numerical simulation models of the
human heart to study the biomechanics of the left ventricle. This involves several steps,
including the analysis of Computed tomography angiography (CTA) data, segmentation
of the ventricular geometry, creation of a mesh representation, assignment of material
properties based on a literature study, and simulation of deformations under different
loading conditions using COMSOL Multiphysics. The overall goal is to use numerical

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter will describe the heart’s functional anatomy, discussing the structure and
function of the heart, including its chambers, valves, heart wall composition and car-
diac cycle. It will also explore the state-of-the-art models used to describe the passive
myocardium and its behaviour under various conditions. In particular, it will focus on
the finite element method, widely used to simulate the mechanical response of the heart.
Overall, this chapter will give a comprehensive understanding of the heart’s anatomy and
physiology and the latest techniques for modelling and analyzing its behaviour.
2.1
The human heart
2.1.1
Functional anatomy
The heart is the main organ of the cardiovascular system, it is composed of blood vessels
and it behaves as a muscular pump that regulates the blood supply throughout the body.
It consists of four pumping chambers: the right and left atria and the right and the left
ventricles, as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
The right atrium (RA) and the right ventricle (RV) receive oxygen-poor blood from
the body via the vena cava and pump it to the lungs through the pulmonary valve and
pulmonary arteries. The left atrium (LA) and the left ventricle (LV) receive oxygen-rich
blood from the lungs via the pulmonary veins and pump it to the rest of the body through
the aortic valve and the aorta [15].
The blood flow direction is controlled by four heart valves, ensuring that the blood
flows in only one direction through the heart. The mitral and tricuspid valves, also known
as the atrioventricular valves, are situated between the atria and ventricles. These valves
prevent blood from flowing back into the atria. They are supported by the papillary
muscles, which extend from the walls of the ventricles, and the chordae tendineae, which
connect to the valves. The papillary muscles are situated inside the ventricle and prevent
the valves from inverting or collapsing. In addition, the pulmonary valve controls the
blood flow from the right ventricle of the heart to the lungs, while the aortic valve regulates
the blood flow from the left side of the heart to systemic circulation.
Together they
contribute to regulating the movement of blood out of the ventricles.

2.1.1.1
The Left Ventricle
The left ventricle is a cone-shaped chamber, considered the primary pump for systemic
circulation. It is situated posterior to the right ventricle, and it is surrounded but isolated
from the right ventricle by the interventricular septal wall.
As illustrated in Figure 2.2(a), the LV is composed by the base, which is the widest
and uppermost part of the chamber, and forms the bottom of the left atrium. It is located
closer to the back of the body and is the point of attachment for the big vessels - the
aorta and pulmonary artery. The apex of the LV is the lowermost and pointed end of the
chamber, which forms the tip of the heart. The apex is located more towards the front
of the body and is where the heart’s muscular contractions are most forceful. The free
wall of the LV is the wall that separates the LV from the surrounding structures, such
as the left atrium and the great vessels. The free wall is thicker than the other walls of
the ventricle and is responsible for generating the force needed to pump blood out of the
heart and into the systemic circulation.
Although both ventricles pump an equal amount of blood, the LV has a thicker muscle
layer compared to the RV, as illustrated in Figure 2.3. The reason behind that is that
the LV needs to generate more pressure to pump blood through the long systemic circuit
with higher resistance. The RV, on the other hand, has less pressure requirement as the
pulmonary circuit has less resistance and is shorter.
The interior of the left ventricle is characterized by several structures that work to-
gether to regulate blood flow through the heart, these are illustrated in Figure 2.2(b). The
walls of the left ventricle are lined with muscular ridges and columns known as trabeculae

2.1.2
The heart wall
The wall of the heart is composed by three separate layers: the epicardium, the my-
ocardium, and the endocardium, as illustrated in Figure 2.4(a). These three layers are
embryologically the same as the three layers found in blood vessels, which are tunica
adventitia, tunica media, and tunica intima, respectively [2].
The endocardium is the innermost layer and its function is to line the inside of each
heart chamber. This thin membrane is made up of a combination of a 2D network of
collagen and elastin and a single layer of endothelial cells that acts as a bridge between
the blood and heart wall [15].
The myocardium is the middle and functional layer of the heart, responsible for en-
abling the heart to pump blood. It is composed mainly of myocytes arranged in locally
parallel muscle fibers, surrounded by an extracellular matrix comprised largely of types I
and III collagen. The alignment of the muscle fibers changes according to their location
within the heart wall, as illustrated in Figure 2.4(b). This allows the left ventricle to con-
tract in several directions simultaneously [29]. The left ventricle can contract effectively
due to the variable orientation of its myocardial fibers. The muscle fibers located adja-
cent to the endocardium are longitudinally aligned, resulting in longitudinal shortening.
In contrast, the mid-wall muscle fibers have a circular orientation around the short-axis,
and their contraction leads to radial shortening causing the ventricular cavity’s diameter
to decrease. The muscle fibers near the epicardium have an approximately 60° orientation
in relation to the mid-wall fibers, and their contraction results in a twisting motion of
the entire ventricle. This twisting contraction is referred to as circumferential shortening,
with the basal segments rotating clockwise and the apex rotating counterclockwise [29].
The epicardium is the outer layer, a thin and serous layer composed mainly of collagen
and elastic fibers. The heart is also surrounded externally by a serous membrane called
pericardium, which protect the heart and reduce the friction between the heart and the
surrounding tissues.

2.1.3
Cardiac Cycle
The cardiac cycle refers to the sequence of pressure changes and rhythmic pumping ac-
tions that occur in the heart during one complete heartbeat. These changes in pressure
within the heart cause the movement of blood through the different chambers of the heart
and throughout the body [21].
The cardiac cycle is made up of two main stages: systole and diastole. Systole occurs
when the ventricles contract and pump blood out of the heart, while diastole occurs when
the ventricles relax and fill with blood. Figure 2.5 shows pressure and volume trends
registered in the left side of the human heart.
Atrial contraction (systole) leads to an increase in LA pressure, causing the mitral
valve to open and the LV to fill. The rise of the LV pressure causes the onset of ventric-
ular systole. The mitral valve closes when LV pressure is greater than LA pressure. The
aortic valve opens when LV pressure surpasses the aortic pressure, leading to blood being
expelled and a rise in aortic pressure. After the peak of pressure reached during systole,
the pressure in the two ventricles starts to drop. Diastole is composed of four stages:
isovolumetric relaxation, fast filling, reduced filling, and atrial contraction. During isovol-
umetric relaxation, the heart valves are closed and pressure increases in the atria. When
atrial pressure surpasses that of the ventricles, the atrioventricular valves open, causing
blood to quickly flow into the ventricles in the fast-filling stage. The ventricles continue
to relax, initially lowering pressure but eventually rising as blood flows into the passive
ventricles through the open atrioventricular valves. This marks the end of diastole and
the point of maximum volume and internal pressure in the ventricles.
End-diastole is a crucial moment in the cardiac cycle and it affects the left ventricle’s

2.2
State of the art
Over the last 30 years, different models describing the passive mechanical properties of
the myocardium have been proposed. These models encompass different mathematical
expressions, microstructural findings, and test circumstances to which they are related.
Constitutive relations refer to mathematical equations or laws that describe the relation-
ship between the mechanical properties of a material and its response to applied forces
or stimuli. These relations are essential in modelling and predicting the behaviour of
materials and structures under different loading conditions.
The simplest representation of the myocardium is the isotropic model. Fuchs et al. [35]
introduced the first nonlinear isotropic model for myocardial tissues, which has the same
structure as the Mooney-Rivlin model utilized in rubber elasticity [40]. Isotropic models,
such as neo-Hookean, Mooney-Rivlin, or Yeoh models can provide accurate representa-
tions of the mechanical behaviour of the myocardium under certain loading conditions.
However, they do not fully represent the anisotropic behaviour of the myocardium.
Anisotropic models assume that the mechanical properties of the material vary with
direction. The ventricles of the heart, consist of a helically organized myocardial band
with oblique, overlaying myocardial sheets. The orientation of the collagen fibres and
the direction of the muscle cells varies throughout the myocardial band, as illustrated in
Figure 2.4(b), allowing the blood to be ejected effectively from the ventricle, and making
the myocardium highly anisotropic [8].
The first anisotropic models proposed were the transversely isotropic models. Humphrey
and Yin proposed a transversely isotropic model, it was the first anisotropic invariant-
based model that took account of the fibre structure (represented by the second term
which includes the invariant I4) [17]. After that Humphrey et al. (1990) developed an ad-
ditional transversely isotropic model, also utilizing the invariants I1 (isotropic invariant)
and I4 (anisotropic invariant). More recent studies of myocardial microstructure (Dokos
et al. (2002)) demonstrated through simple shear tests on passive ventricular myocardium
from pig hearts that the myocardium tissue has three different material responses in each
of the three material directions, so it can be considered an orthotropic material.
The study of the solid biomechanics of the LV is a rapidly evolving field, with new
advancements being made every year.
However, despite these advancements, there is
still much to be learned about the mechanical behaviour of the LV. Further research is
needed to improve our understanding of the LV and to develop effective treatments for
cardiovascular disease.

The Finite Element Method
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a numerical technique used to approximate so-
lutions to partial differential equations that describe physical systems. This method is
used to solve problems for which it is not possible to derive an analytical solution and
involves dividing a continuous structure into smaller, manageable parts (elements) and
using mathematical equations to describe the behavior of each element. The results from
all the elements are then combined to obtain an overall solution for the entire system
[5]. The geometry of some of the possible 3D elements that can be used are illustrated
in Figure 2.7. The nodes, located at the edges of the elements, connect the elements and
==PAGE:24==

determine the geometry and degrees of freedom of the system. They also represent the
explicit position for determining the field variable in the finite element model [28].
To solve a problem using FEM, information about the geometry of the system, ma-
terial properties, boundary conditions, element type, and mesh density is required. The
geometry of the system includes information about the size, shape, and arrangement of
the physical components. Material properties include mechanical properties of the ma-
terials such as elasticity and density.
Boundary conditions include information about
the loads and constraints applied to the system. Element type and mesh density include
information about the type of finite elements used in the model and the density of the
mesh used to discretize the system.
Once all of this information is inputted into the finite element analysis software, the
software solves the equations of equilibrium at each node and combines the results from
all the nodes to obtain an overall solution for the entire system.
In relation to the study of the heart biomechanics, the FEM is used to model and
simulate the mechanics of the heart and its response to various physiological conditions.
The FEM can be used to study the deformation and stresses within the heart during
different phases of the cardiac cycle and to analyze the behaviour of heart tissues and
structures under different conditions, such as diseases or injuries.
These simulations
can provide valuable insights into the mechanics of the heart and help in the diagnosis,
treatment, and understanding of cardiovascular diseases.

CHAPTER 3
Modelling
The focus of this research study is to examine the mechanical behaviour of the human
heart’s Left Ventricle (LV). This chapter provides a comprehensive explanation of the
modelling process. A symmetric simple 3D model and a realistic one have been created.
Starting with a simple symmetric model can provide a solid foundation for analyzing the
biomechanics of the LV before progressing to more complex models. The symmetric model
was created in SOLIDWORKS considering average LV dimensions, while the realistic 3D
model of the LV was reconstructed from cardiac data acquired through CT angiography
(CTA). The CTA images supplied three-dimensional coordinate data from multiple views,
including sagittal, lateral, and axial views, which were utilized to construct a 3D heart
model. The LV models were imported into COMSOL Multiphysics 6.0, where material
properties were incorporated into the LV geometry and a finite element analysis was
conducted.

patient’s bloodstream before performing a CT scan.

4.2
Boundary conditions
In this study, LV biomechanics was investigated using the Solid Mechanics interface in
COMSOL. Various boundary conditions were selected to accurately model the mechanical
behaviour of the LV and simulate its response.
The myocardial tissue is modelled as an elastic material with nonlinear strain-stress
behaviour, commonly referred to as a hyperelastic material. The transversely isotropic
model from Humphrey et al.
(1990) is selected for this study, as it is a widely used
and validated model for myocardial tissue [22]. The constitutive relations presented in
equations 2.4 and 2.7 from Section 2.3 are implemented in COMSOL. These relations take
into account the anisotropic nature of the myocardium, which means that the material
properties vary with the direction of the fibres and behave differently in the longitudinal
direction compared to the radial and circumferential directions.
4.2.1
Boundary load and constraints for the
steady-state simulation
For the boundary load of the first simulation, constant pressure was selected. The orien-
tation of the load is defined by the normal to the boundary, and the load is considered
positive when directed towards the solid. The load is only applied to the inner wall of
the left ventricle. To simulate the pressure exerted on the inner wall of the left ventricle
during diastole and systole, a boundary load of 1 kPa and 15 kPa is applied, respectively.
An overview of how the load is applied to the internal ventricular wall is illustrated in
Figure 4.3.
Furthermore, to avoid undesired rigid body displacement, and to constrain the move-
ment of the model, the motion of the ventricular base was suppressed using the Roller
node. The Roller applies a roller constraint as a boundary condition, which restricts the
displacement in the direction perpendicular to the boundary while allowing free movement
in the tangential direction. This boundary condition ensures that the ventricular base
does not move in a manner that would affect the accuracy of the simulation results, but
allows the ventricle wall to expand in the radial and circumferential direction. Compared
to other constraints such as the Fixed constraint, which completely restricts the motion of
the base in all directions, the Roller constraint provides a realistic representation of the
motion of the ventricular base and avoids unrealistic stress concentrations in that area.
In reality, the ventricular base is not completely fixed and, despite its stiffness, it is still
able to move in the tangential direction due to the flexibility of the surrounding tissues.

an additional COMSOL simulation is run. To get accurate results and effectively capture
the stress distribution during ventricular contraction, the following boundary conditions
were applied in this case:

straint, meaning it is constrained from any displacement or movement in all directions.
This serves the purpose of preventing excessive movement or displacement of the model
when cyclic pressure is applied from the outside. It is a reasonable approximation since
the base of the LV is where the mitral valve is attached, and the mitral valve annulus is
a fibrous and rigid ring-shaped structure, which contributes to the overall stiffness of the
base [38].

natural process of ventricular inflation and expansion, the pressure was ramped up with
time until 1 second, after which it was maintained at a constant value. This approach
enabled the LV to remain pressurized when subjected to external pressure. The pressure

In studying the biomechanics of the two LV models through an FE study, the meshing

elements, and it is used to perform the finite element analysis. The mesh for this geometry
was performed using the integrated meshing function available in COMSOL and defined
by selecting tetrahedral elements, which are considered the best choice to discretize com-
plex geometries. In COMSOL different sizes of the meshes elements can be chosen, the
available choices are: ”Extra coarse” ”Coarser”, ’Coarse’, ’Normal’, ’Fine’, ’Finer’ and
’Extra Fine’. The element size of the mesh should be small enough to capture the impor-

Solver
For this project, both parametric sweep and time-dependent studies were performed in
COMSOL. A parametric sweep is a feature that allows to vary one or more parameters
in a simulation. In this project, this feature has been used to vary the pressure values
to resemble the physiological cardiac changes from diastole to systole. COMSOL auto-
matically generates multiple solution files, each corresponding to a different value, and
then the results can be post-process to analyze how the simulation results change as the
parameter is varied. A time-dependent study was also performed in COMSOL to analyze
and track how the solution evolves over time. Both studies are set with a direct and fully
coupled solver, using PARADISIO with default settings.

5.2
Principal Stretch
While Cauchy stress components represent internal forces within a material, principal
stretch components describe how the material deforms under these forces. The principal
stretches are obtained by finding the eigenvalues of the stretch tensor, with the correspond-
ing eigenvectors giving the orthogonal directions [9]. A principal stretch greater than 1
indicates lengthening, equal to 1 means no change, and less than 1 implies shortening of
the segment along that direction.
5.2.1
Symmetric model
Figure 5.11 displays the values of the principal stretches λ1, λ2, and λ3 as function of
the radial coordinate for diastolic and systolic pressure in the symmetric model. Figure
5.11(a) shows the plot for the diastolic pressure. The maximum value is the first principal
stretch λ1 equals to 1.35 at the internal ventricular wall, indicating an increase in the
length of the segment in the circumferential direction during deformation. The second
principal stretch λ2 has a nearly constant value of around 1.05, corresponding to the axial
direction where there is no significant change in length. The third principal stretch λ3 is
approximately 0.7 at the internal wall, indicating that the ventricular wall gets thinner
in the radial direction when inflated. Notably, the most significant stretches are observed
at the inner wall, where values furthest from 1 are registered.
Figure 5.11(b) depicts the outcomes for the systolic phase, revealing that all three
principal stretches deviate from the value of 1 more compared to the diastolic stretches
plot. The most significant stretch, λ1, reaches a value of 2.4 at the inner wall, implying
that the length has more than doubled. This stretch is associated with the deformation in
the circumferential direction. The second principal stretch λ2 denotes the deformations
along the axial direction and maintains a nearly constant value of 1.7 throughout the wall
thickness. On the other hand, λ3 characterizes the deformation along the radial direction,
and it has a value of 0.2 at the inner radius, indicating that the LV has undergone a
compression to one-fifth of its original size in the radial direction.
5.2.2
Realistic model
Figure 5.12 illustrates the principal stretches as a function of the radial coordinate for
diastolic and systolic pressure in the realistic left ventricular model. The plot in Figure
5.12(a) represents the values for the diastolic pressure. The first principal stretch λ1 has
the highest value, which is about 1.3 at the internal ventricular wall, indicating an in-
crease in the length of the segment in the circumferential direction during deformation.
The second principal stretch λ2 shows a decreasing value as well, starting from 1.15 at
the inner wall. This means that there is a small deformation also in the axial direction.
The third principal stretch λ3 has a value of 0.7 at the internal wall, suggesting that the
ventricular wall is compressed in the radial direction during deformation.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This project aims to investigate the biomechanics of the LV, a finite element implementa-
tion of a transversely isotropic constitutive relation in COMSOL Multiphysics is presented.
The mechanical behaviour of the ventricle was analyzed under diastolic and systolic pres-
sure for both the symmetric and natural geometric models. Additionally, the temporal
variation of stress on the realistic LV during applied contraction was investigated. In
this section, the results obtained in the present study are discussed and compared with
relevant findings from the existing literature.
6.1
Stress Tensor and Intramural Stress
The results illustrated in Section 5.1 indicate overall that the LV wall experiences much
higher stresses during the systolic phase compared to the diastolic phase. The order of
magnitude for every normal component between Figures 5.2, 5.7 and Figures 5.4, 5.9 is
around 6000% higher. Stress is a physical quantity that describes the magnitude of forces
that cause deformation, so it was expected that for higher pressure values applied, the
stress increases accordingly. This could have important implications for the development
of LV-related pathologies, and further investigation is warranted. However, the stress
distributions observed during systole and diastole are similar, indicating that the wall’s
mechanical behaviour is consistent across the cardiac cycle.
Intramural stress results presented in Figures 5.3, 5.5, 5.8, and 5.10 reveal that all the
examined stress components exhibit a decrease in magnitude from the inside of the wall
to the outside. This observation indicates that the inner wall of the left ventricle experi-
ences higher stress compared to the outer wall. This outcome aligns with expectations,
considering that the forces generated by the blood flow in this simulation only act on the
internal surface of the LV, with no external forces applied. Similar observations regarding
the decrease in stress magnitude with radius have been made in previous studies: by
Arnab Pali et al. (2017) [24] using personalized bi-ventricles models, by Humphrey et al.
(1989) [14] who shows stress induced by varying conditions, and by Ghista and Hamid
(1977) [12] who developed LV model from a single plane cineangiocardiogram. The stress
values reported in [24] for diastole are: σrr= -1.7 ± 1.47 kPa, σϕϕ= 4.6 ± 3 kPa, and
σzz= 1.8 ± 0.66 kPa. These values are consistent with the results in Table 5.1. The mi-
nor variation can be attributed to slight differences in geometry and material parameters
==PAGE:65==
6.2 Stress distribution over time
53
employed. Furthermore, consistent with the findings of the present study, these previous
investigations consistently demonstrate that the intramural stresses near the midventri-
cle, specifically σϕϕ and σzz, exhibit positive values, with the hoop stress (σϕϕ) having the
highest magnitude. Conversely, the radial stress (σrr) is observed to be negative. This
alignment with the results of the current study further strengthens the agreement about
the distribution of intramural stresses within the left ventricular wall.
It is important to highlight a significant distinction between the two models employed
in this study. Typically, the distribution of stresses in a ventricular wall is influenced by
various factors, including mechanical and material properties, as well as geometric con-
siderations. However, comparing Figure 5.1 and Figure 5.6, it becomes evident that the
geometry of the models mainly dictates the distribution of von Mises stress. Although a
uniform stress distribution has been an appealing theory of optimal myocardial function
([4], [11]), the findings of this study suggest that stress distributions can vary significantly
along the longitudinal axis of the ventricular wall. This observation aligns with previous
research ([13], [34], [20]), which has demonstrated the noteworthy impact of object ge-
ometry on stress outcomes. Consequently, this implies that the outcomes derived from
the natural model, used for evaluating the mechanical properties of the LV hold greater
relevance when compared to the results obtained from the symmetric model, particularly
in terms of their correlation with LV physiology. Furthermore, the studies cited above
exhibit the same order of magnitude in the stress distributions reported for the diastolic
configuration, but the stress values associated with the systolic phase differ by one order
of magnitude in [13] compared to the findings of the present study. This discrepancy can
likely be attributed to different applied pressure values (14 kPa in [13] versus 15 kPa in
the present study), differences in the geometric model used, and a different transversely
isotropic constitutive relation.
Principal stretches were investigated as well, showing that as the ventricle undergoes
inflation and the wall becomes thinner, the predominant deformation occurs in the circum-
ferential and radial directions. Additionally, as expected, during the systolic phase, the
vessel experiences even greater deformation, especially in the radial and circumferential
directions, compared to diastole. Consequently, the principal stretch values deviate even
further from 1 in systole, due to the more significant stretching experienced by the LV
wall during this phase. In the realistic model, λ1 increased from 1.24 ± 0.03 to 1.85 ±
0.11, λ2 increased from 1.08 ± 0.03 to 1.51 ± 0.09, and λ3 deviates from 0.75 ± 0.04 to
0.36 ± 0.04.
6.2
Stress distribution over time
The principal stress distribution over time in the realistic LV model, particularly at the
apex, base, and midventricle, during simulated ventricular contraction has provided valu-
able insights into the mechanical behavior of the left ventricle. The results show variations
in stress magnitudes over time, following the cyclic trend of the applied load. At the apex,
where the external load is higher and the stress is mainly compressive, the stress com-
==PAGE:66==
6.3 Model limitations
54
ponents exhibited expected behavior during the cardiac cycle. During systole the stress
magnitudes increased, reaching their peak values. Conversely, during diastole the stress
magnitudes decreased, returning to relatively lower levels.
Moving towards the middle and base regions of the LV, the stress distribution over
time is characterized by a higher variability from one cycle to another and an overall
higher standard deviation. This behaviour can be primarily attributed to the irregulari-
ties present in the geometry of those areas and the distribution of the load across them.
In real LV the middle and base regions exhibit greater geometric complexities compared
to the apex, as they encompass structures such as the papillary muscles and trabeculae.
These anatomical irregularities can influence the distribution of stress within the LV wall
during ventricular contraction. A recent study investigated the influence of trabeculae
and papillary muscles on blood flow and wall stress by comparing detailed and smoothed
LV models reconstructed from magnetic resonance images (MRI) of ex-vivo human hearts
[18]. The study found that trabeculae and papillary muscles significantly alter blood flow
by increasing intra-ventricular pressure drop and reducing shear stress on the ventricular
walls. Specifically, papillary muscles and trabeculae resulted in a reduction of wall shear
stress in the LV of about 23.5% to 66.7%. This finding provides insight into the reason for
the existence of such endocardial structures and how they impact the stress distribution.
The stresses were also evaluated transmurally in the three locations considered. At
the apex and middle regions of the LV, there is a notable trend of decreasing stress from
the inner to the outer layers. However, the apex exhibits a higher variation in stress
distribution across its thickness. This is primarily due to the geometric characteristics,
which lead to a greater concentration of forces on the inner surface of the apex. Conversely,
at the base, where it is fixed in all directions, the stress tends to increase going from
the inside to the outside.
This is because the fixed boundary condition restricts the
displacement and deformation at the base, causing a concentration of stress in that region.
Overall, the stress variations within the LV depend on the load distribution, boundary
conditions, and structural response, leading to different stress behaviors at the apex,
middle, and base regions.
Similar studies have reported similar findings ([1], [19], [10]). However, a direct com-
parison of stress magnitudes is not appropriate in this case.
The reason is that the
applied motion, boundary conditions, and geometry vary significantly across different
studies, which leads to variations in stress magnitude. Therefore, it is crucial to consider
the specific context and conditions of each study when interpreting and comparing stress
results.
6.3
Model limitations
In both the LV models presented certain aspects of the model are simplified to optimize
performance and reduce computational complexity. Smoothing is applied to the LV geom-
etry obtained from CTA data, and some elements such as the papillary muscles, chordae
tendineae, coronary arteries, valves, and connective tissue are not represented in the model.
==PAGE:67==
6.4 Future work
55
This is common practice when creating 3D LV model, however, the oversimplification of
the model can limit the realism and the accuracy of the results.
Additionally, the geometry was simplified to a single layer representing the myocardium
with a homogeneous thickness. This simplification assumes that the myocardium is the
primary contributor to LV biomechanics, disregarding other layers like the endocardium
and epicardium.
Assuming a homogeneous thickness simplifies the analysis, although
it overlooks variations in thickness across the LV wall.
While this simplification aids
tractability, it may limit the accuracy in capturing regional mechanical properties or
specific pathological conditions that manifest differently in various LV regions.
Another aspect that can be improved in the study is the representation of the blood
lumen environment and the hemodynamics. Currently, only the forces exerted from the
inside of the vessel and a simplified external contraction load are modelled. However,
it is important to consider the influence of other factors, such as the surrounding tis-
sue, the presence of the other chambers and fluid dynamics, on the behaviour of the
LV. The current approach represents a simplified approximation compared to the com-
plex hemodynamic conditions present in the heart. By including more realistic motion
and considering the vessel’s interaction with its environment, a more comprehensive and
realistic understanding of the biomechanics can be achieved.
6.4
Future work
Further research and development of the present study can enhance the realism and
accuracy of the models used and expand their capabilities.
Currently, the models focus primarily on the left ventricular soft tissue, but expanding
to a biventricular model would provide a more comprehensive representation of the heart’s
mechanics, such as in [6] and [3]. A biventricular model includes both the left and right
ventricles, enabling the investigation of interactions and functional relationships between
the two chambers. Additionally, incorporating the valve structures, such as the mitral
and aortic valves, would further enhance the model’s realism. Including these components
would allow for the analysis of valve mechanics and their influence on left ventricle function.
Another potential direction for future research is to incorporate more realistic motion into
the left ventricle models, for example, by prescribing movement based on cardiac CT scans,
which provide detailed information about the dynamic behaviour of the heart during the
cardiac cycle. Furthermore, investigating the influence of various parameters, such as
tissue stiffness, fibrosis, or changes in wall thickness due to cardiac disorders such as
myocardial infarction or cardiomyopathy, can help determine their impact on left ventricle
mechanics. By systematically altering these parameters in the model and analyzing the
resulting changes in stress distribution and ventricular function, a more comprehensive
understanding of the role of these factors in cardiac disorders can be gained.
==PAGE:68==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The project focused on developing numerical simulation models to investigate the solid
mechanics of the human LV. Through the investigation of previously published literature,
the constitutive relations, boundary conditions, model dimensions and material properties
were determined and implemented in COMSOL. In conclusion, the present study has re-
vealed valuable insights into stress distribution and how the ventricular wall deforms with
the applied pressure. The stress distributions demonstrate to remain consistent through-
out the cardiac cycle, indicating consistent mechanical behaviour. The geometry plays a
significant role in stress distribution, and findings from the natural model can be consid-
ered more relevant to LV physiology. Further research can improve the models used by
expanding to biventricular representation, including valve structures, incorporating real-
istic motion, and investigating various parameters, thereby advancing the understanding
of left ventricle mechanics and their association with cardiac disorders.

APPENDIX C
Project Plan
C.1
Introduction
The project plan will provide a summary of the learning objectives and intended outcomes
of my Master’s thesis, as well as a time plan for the project. The project will be conducted
at DTU in the department of Biomedical Engineering during the spring semester of 2023.
The project duration is 5 months, accounting for 30 ECTS points. It will start on January
2 and concludes on June 2. The supervisor for the project is Associate Professor Marie
Sand Traberg.
C.2
Project Tasks
The purpose of this master’s thesis project is to create a numerical simulation model of
the human heart to study the biomechanics of the left ventricle.
The first step is to analyze computed tomography angiography imaging (CTA) data
of the left ventricle. Next, a segmentation of the ventricular geometry from the imaging
data will be performed. This can be done using manual or automated techniques. Once
the ventricular geometry has been segmented, a mesh that represents the surface of the
left ventricle will be created. This mesh will be performed in Comsol Multiphysics, a
specialized software that generates a finite element mesh based on the segmented geometry.
After a literature review and experimental studies, the most suitable material properties
will be assigned to the elements in the mesh. Finally, Comsol Multiphysics will be used
as finite element analysis software to simulate the deformation of the left ventricle under
various loading conditions. This will allow the study of the biomechanics of the ventricle
and the understanding of how it functions under different conditions.
C.3
Project Objectives
The objective of this project is to develop a numerical simulation model of the left ventricle
to study the solid biomechanics of cardiac muscle tissue, gain a deeper understanding
of the mechanisms that govern the behavior of the heart, and how these mechanisms
contribute to the overall performance and function of the heart.
