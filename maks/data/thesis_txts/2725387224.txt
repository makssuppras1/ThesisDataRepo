Abstract
The main focus of this thesis lies in exploring the benefits of a robotized electric vehicle charging solution. As
such, a feasibility study takes the central part, with various scenarios being presented, together with their eco-
nomical impacts. A company which has an already existing product in the form of a robotic arm (Autofuel ApS)
represented the industrial partner that helped shape this study.
The thesis follows an analytical methodology that was developed through trial and error and heuristics. After
introducing the broader context, the problem characteristic is formulated. The methodology is then presented,
together with a discussion on its possible applications. Afterwards, different scenarios are explored. Going further,
their impacts and simulations are analyzed and validated in another section. The last part deals with conclusions,
future perspectives and decisions taken by the company.
The motivation behind this thesis came from the need of blending technological aspects with a hands-on market
research. This approach could offer new insights into bringing the automatic charging field closer to reality. It is
the intent of this project to spark future interest for a framework that can be easily replicated.

Introduction
When talking about the fundamental goals of the field of robotics, there are quite a few which deserve to be
mentioned. From finding optimal solutions for repetitive tasks to solving various projects that would otherwise put
human life in danger, research in this domain is clearly as versatile as humans are able to make it. The noteworthy
subdomain of robotics which will be explored throughout this thesis is automatic electric vehicle charging.
In this regard, there are various approaches when addressing the named topic. From human behaviour, power
grid stress, environmental impact and going as far as economic feasibility. If progress is to be pursued, a holistic
approach would need to take into account these various components and try to come up with a solution.
In broader terms, the impact of automated robotic refueling can be one of the necessary small steps towards
achieving a more sustainable and climate change mitigating solution. As figure 1 shows, there is much progress to
be made in the energy sector and especially in the transport sector - the latter being the focus of this thesis.
Figure 1: Global GHG emissions by sector (2016) - Courtesy of Our World in Data [1]
For even more perspective, some really ambitious initiatives include making the EU the first carbon-neutral
continent [2]. As such, one can also glimpse at some estimations that indicate an increasing relevance of the EV
sector [3]. Moreover, figure 2 shows the estimated new EV sales. These are clear indicators of increasing relevance
for the topic at hand.
However, as optimistic and ambitious such a plan can sound, only a grassroots approach would maximize its
chances of success in the actual context. Understanding the companies that can aid in making this transition happen
is key if we want to accelerate the process and make it as smooth as possible. That is why an actual company which

1.1
Potential advantages
The solutions which will be explored could be further expanded to other industries, but for the sake of narrowing
down the focus of the thesis, the EV sector was the one in the foreground. Any insights into this domain can prove
useful in the long term from multiple points of view.
Firstly, an automated robotized solution would fit perfectly in a future dominated by autonomous vehicles. From
charging to driving, parking and completing tasks, it would come as a natural consequence that the whole chain
would be devoid of human labour. Costs could be cut down and convenience in terms of time could be on the rise.
Other long term benefits that come into play are the ones related to safety and increased productivity, alongside
more time & resources that could be used for pursuing other technological advances.
Secondly, stress on the power grid will become an increasing issue in the years to follow. A whole restructuring
of urban infrastructure could be avoided through smart optimization techniques provided by a robotized charging
solution. One of these optimizations can be the scheduling of energy-efficient charging times. It can also be the
most cost-effective option. The ability to postpone charging may come in aid when dealing with power surges and
market volatility. An efficient allocation of resources would greatly act as a buffer against unforeseen circumstances
as well.
Another point to be made is the increased convenience for customers (with hassle-free charging and reduced idle
times). Standardization of such solutions would also help in the direction of time saving (e.g. standardizing how
charging nozzles are locked for different EV models) - another discussion point that could be framed as an advantage.

Last but not least, enhanced usage of existing charging equipment can also be mentioned. A robotized solution
would make the best use of infrastructure that is already in place (a single robot could serve multiple cars under
the right conditions). Besides, it would allow for easier installation and fewer chargers needed for future projects.
The discussion can be expanded towards dealing with really costly fast chargers in the industry.
Being in its infancy, such a solution would be costly in the beginning. Its efficiency in various scenarios depends
on its costs. Nevertheless, the question of installing robotized equipment which lowers upfront costs will become
relevant for some cases: superchargers, freight vehicle industry, highly customizable chargers and 24/7 operating
chargers. At the same time, it would not apply to the entire industry, with basic chargers not always needing a
robotic arm to be operated. Some cases it would not apply to: single household chargers, low power chargers (for
which the investment in a robotized solution would not break even) or rarely used chargers in remote areas.
Some questions which had arisen were: would there be ways to optimize how efficient charging these vehicles can
be? what are the limitations of this? Although not easy to answer, these considerations helped shape the direction
of the thesis.
This suite of solutions could help municipalities, energy distributors, gas stations and governmental agencies in
avoiding a complete rework of the electricity distribution grid. Secondly, it could solve the issue of sustainability,
by accessing the power grid in favourable conditions and when electricity is cheap. Less energy used in peak hours
means a greener way of consuming. Thirdly, it can direct Autofuel’s plans of expansion into the domain of electric
vehicle charging. By providing some valuable insights into the accessible market perspectives, it can determine if
one path might prove significantly more valuable to follow than another one. A feasibility study that can lay the
groundwork for similar patents in the future.
1.2
Literature review
This chapter aims to select the main topics of interest that will shape the information presented in the thesis.
In this sense, a thorough work of research, selection and summary of relevant papers has been carried out. It starts
with the design and possible functionality of robotic arms. It then proceeds with the need of path planning and
minimizing the resources necessary to undergo automatic refueling problems. Going further, it covers the power grid
stress of electric vehicle charging infrastructure. Sustainability is the last topic, with a focus on general behaviour
and consumption patterns in a day-to-day scenario.
The problems this research can solve stem from different points of view. Firstly, it can provide solutions to the
problem of power grid stress, which according to [5] has already started to be a major issue in terms of infrastructure
capabilities and demand-supply balance. The topic is a hotly debated one, with [6] making an argument for giving
priority to public charging station such that grid impacts are controlled and minimized. More similar approaches,
in line with the scope of this thesis, were mentioned in [7] and [8]. They argue, amongst others, for a smartly
managed and more structured grid.
The main paper on which this research was built upon is [9]. It is a 2021 study and it closely resembles what
Autofuel already has sold on the market. Figure 3 illustrates the prototype of the authors’ 6 DOF robotic arm. As
stated before, it is the intent of this thesis to pick up on the conclusions from this paper and try to enhance them.
One of the most relevant mentions is the acknowledgement of the authors about the necessity of a cost benefit
analysis of this type of product in a real life situation.

Consideration points
Some consideration points which should be kept in mind for these types of solutions could be:
• Large initial investment could be a deterrent towards small and medium enterprises which would aim towards
an automated solution (Autofuel’s robotic arm is currently priced at roughly 100.000e/ arm)
• The volatility of energy prices plays an important role in the amortization period of the automated solution,

• Reducing power grid stress comes at the expense of a continuous effort of observing and tweaking the way in

Methodology
3.1
Theory - special case of a scheduling problem
With the robotic arm in mind, the main scope for all the scenarios was to find a suitable business model and
explore its possibilities in relation to the questions asked in the beginning. The main focus began shifting towards
the scheduling problem. The main concern was to optimize the time frame in which vehicles are charged. This was
strongly correlated with the electricity prices, as well as the real life need of having a certain percentage of charge
when leaving a facility. An algorithm had to be designed in order to account for the hours during a day when
charging could occur.
Secondly, the window charging problem came into discussion. It asks the question of when it becomes optimal
to charge the vehicles: both from an economical, as well as power grid stress POVs.
3.2

1. Assess whether a scenario is worth pursuing using Autofuel’s robotized solution
2. Explore the basic economic feasibility of said scenario, taking into account all known costs and externalities

4. What decisions would make the most sense by further simulating adjacent scenarios
5. Finding an optimal dimensioning considering random SoC levels and caps
6. Reassess the cost benefit and draw conclusions based on the simulations

5.2
Algorithm choice
The requirements of this solution were quite straightforward: it would be designed for an indoor parking lot.
This meant that the scenario was pre-mapped. It did not need to change. At the same time, the distance covered
was quite small, making a less computationally intensive algorithm more suitable. The candidates for this algorithm
were these 3 main ones: A*, Djikstra and Greedy.
As per the conclusions observed in [21], A* can find the shortest path better than Greedy. Among its weaknesses,
the authors list the fact that it needs complex data (a straight line distance towards the end node). In this specific
case, A* became the one which resembled the most the real life situation of a parking lot, which does not require a
high degree of complexity.
The A* algorithm [22] combines shortest path and heuristic searching. It is considered a best-first algorithm,
with each cell in its configuration space being evaluated by the value:
f(v) = h(v) + g(v)
(3)
In equation 3, h(v) represents the heuristic distance (Manhattan, Euclidean or Chebysev) of the current cell
towards the goal state. g(v) represents the length of the path from the initial state until the end state, considering
all the cells passed through. Whenever a new cell is reached, the algorithm evaluates each adjacent cell taking into
account f(v). The chosen one has to have the lowest value of f(v).

Charging time simulation over 1000 cases
Vehicles arriving with random SoCs were simulated over 1000 cases. This has the scope of accounting for real

to introduce a new dimension of the feasibility study: accounting for the robotic arms’ delays in movement. This
proved beneficial in the further NPV study of the scenario which will be detailed below.

start to add up and impact the feasibility study quite significantly.

Validation of Results
Even if the thesis worked with numerous assumptions, the robustness of the final curves show a turning point
which was expected when a company wants to buy a certain number of robotic arms. The shape of the curve could
have been predicted from the initial assumptions, since a penalty is applied if the fault rate is deemed too high. On
the other hand, the algorithm confirmed the drop in usefulness the more we increased the number of robotic arms
after this turning point - figure 35.
In terms of the simulations present in the freight vehicle chapter, there were numerous checks and validations of
results. One which was important and reinforced the initial feasibility linear correlation presented in figure 26 was
the fact that the algorithm would produce an error for 100 trucks and only 7 robotic arms. Without any restrictions
fed into it, it would simply not have enough time to charge each and every one of them in the extreme case that
they all arrived depleted back to the garage. That changed the acceptable number of robotic arms to minimum 8 if
one wants to ensure the complete charging of the whole fleet. Of course, this was no hard restriction, as companies
might negotiate a fault rate which would be acceptable to them in order to buy less robotic arms in the beginning.
This was only to prove that the model can generate a minimum number of robotic arms that could do the tasks
according to the required specifications.
Another check worth noting was the one in place when dealing with the fault rate. As the threshold figures [32,
33] show, the 0% fault rate applies from certain points onwards. The further away they strained from this 0%, the
harsher the hard cap was on the NPV calculation.
The sensitivity of the simulations could be further analyzed by looking at the final simulation - figure 35: when
comparing the usual price chart (colored red) with the increased price chart (colored blue), one can notice the
difference in slope that comes between 16 and 17 robotic arms. That can be explained due to how the algorithm
calculates the cumulated savings in electricity - this being a central component of the NPV.
On the same note, a robustness check shows that a 20% in price variation could make a difference of roughly
500.000e/10 years, or an approximate 40% increase in savings.

Discussion, Summary and Conclusions
8.1
Wider perspectives of the technology
The perspectives presented in this Thesis touch only the tip of the iceberg when it comes to future research.
As with everything, it could not fulfill the requirements of a completely comprehensive study. Nevertheless, some
interesting future perspectives of this technology were identified, which could make the case of continued research
into this area:
• Possibility of developing a complementary algorithm with reinforcement learning. This could enhance power
grid usage when fed with models of prediction for electricity prices.
It could further enhance the whole
scheduling issue, as well as maximize savings for charging when electricity is cheapest.
• In the context of a fully automated cycle of transport, the findings of this Thesis might prove beneficial to
explore for other scenarios as well, like a fleet of fully autonomous vehicles with close to 0 human intervention
• For the long term, once the robotic arm solution becomes a mature technology, its implementation cost will
decrease and its applications could expand towards normal chargers as well. This could happen due to its
lower initial costs.
• Future standardization and power grid improvements might be appealing to other companies to try and enter
this ever-growing domain and trying to build variations of a robotic charger - it could drag prices down and
spark innovation through competition
• Another future proof aspect of this solution would be the possibility of Vehicle to Grid charging - an automated
solution with the present algorithm could give back power to the grid once there is no need for it from the
trucks, further enhancing the economical aspect
8.2
Data sources, error sources and other uncertainties
Data for this research was mainly extracted from the last 2 years. That could be both a constructive approach
(as the past 2 years have seen huge fluctuations in prices across all domains), as well as destructive (in the future
prices might stabilise and the numbers presented here could be rendered obsolete). The data is based on public
announcements, as well as publicly listed company insights into the domain.
An important aspect of this paper was the high number of uncertainties and their impact. Simulations can
reflect theory up to a certain point, but if the preliminary assumptions are skewed, so will the results become. That
is why initially, an approach of simulating 100 times each figure was adopted. Throughout the thesis, through trial
and error, even though the computation time increased significantly, it was observed that 1000 simulations would
affect the numbers from previous 100 simulations quite significantly. This was a fortunate finding in the first quarter
of the simulations, so not everything had to be redone. Higher numbers were also tested (5000, as well as 10.000
iterations), but the trade-off in computational time was minimal compared to the accuracy of 1000 simulations.
Also, as this is a novel project in a niche domain (at the time of writing, there have not been identified other
projects aimed in this exact direction), it can be difficult to draw conclusions from hypothetical and paper-described
research instead of real-life tests.
Towards the final steps, a mistake was spotted in the way the charging curve was simulated - figure 20. It
surfaced when a more precise simulation (a double check) which was meant to be closer to 100 minutes of 0-100%
charging time actually ended up in 115 minutes of charging time. Investigating, the error was laying in an ill defined

8.3
Summary
As this paper was done in collaboration with Autofuel, their valuable input throughout the whole process was
kept in high regard. Initially, Autofuel had a more proeminent focus on small, personally owned EVs. Midway
throughout the thesis, after assessing opportunities from other external partners and taking into account the re-
search presented to them up to that point, they started exploring the idea of a freight truck scenario more and more.
The idea of using the solution for a bus fleet was also entertained at some point in time. However, the duration
of the research, combined with the fluctuating focus of the company towards achieving a profitable, yet sustainable
option, made it out of scope for this specific paper.
The solutions, market perspectives and simulations presented in this paper could be replicated in other domains
as well. The scenarios, even though focused on a local level, could have a high degree of replicability in similar
parts of the world which aim for the transition towards an automated charging solution.
Towards the end, they have used the knowledge from this thesis to simplify their decisions when trying to pursue
one scenario or another. They admitted that they would be further interested in collaborating and even asked for a
third scenario: industrial high power electric vehicles used in harbors. It would have followed the same methodology,
but accessing information would have depended way more on Autofuel, as online resources proved really scarce in
an incipient phase of assessment.
8.4
Conclusions
Comparing the simulations, their weaknesses and strong points proved close to the initial assumptions. Even
though, at times, the values were not exactly as expected (e.g.: initial no. of robotic arms vs final one OR initial
NPV for charging the whole fleet vs final NPV one), an argument can be made in favour of this thesis: it worked
with the most conservative values available and sort of ”worst case scenario” in terms of economics, so a lot of room
for improvement was left from the numbers presented here. This applies for the following areas: average lifetime
of the batteries (chose the minimum period of 10 years compared to the average of 12 years), average battery cost
(per kWh) and average improvement in battery life (due to optimized partial charging).

What business case proves the most suitable and sustainable (which is the most feasible according to each case
study)? The short answer is, as always: it depends. The simulations and figures presented in this thesis have
by no means a fully comprehensive outlook on the various scenarios explored. One way in which this could be
implemented would be to start trials with just a quarter of the fleet being automatically charged and the other half
being manually charged. Some timeframe (6 months to a year) can pass and then the situation can be reassessed
and compared to the numbers presented here. It all depends on the expectations of the contracting parties (in this
case ARC and Autofuel) and their willingness to compromise.
Another tricky part could be the fact that even though a solution might prove sustainable and environmentally
friendly, it might not always make sense from an economical POV, so a trade-off has to be decided going forward.
Even though this thesis was made in collaboration with a company, that does not always have to be the case. One
could also argue that new governmental funds aimed towards a green transition might render this solution viable
for municipalities or at least help companies which want to enter this domain.
On another note, the impact of this solution could have also been modeled in the sense of the power grid stress
a bit more. It could also benefit from technologies that could allow a V2G charge, as described before.
Addressing the interim shift change is another important issue in this thesis. If a contractor would not experience
the big amount of power drawn when trying to address it, then the numbers here would change quite significantly.
It was in the scope of this thesis to see how a balance can be achieved while not straining the systems in place.
Another point to be made would be the fact that this thesis presents the price of electricity for the individual
consumer. However, an industrial consumer can negotiate different prices and would have a progressive charging
scheme. Having an algorithm to help with consumption would be beneficial to say the least in such a scenario.
Something that can be learned from this thesis is the possibility of bringing technological innovations closer to
reality. It has the potential to draw attention towards implementing real life scenarios through its hands-on ap-
proach of the market. It can also act as a bridge for future collaborations between institutions or industry partners
that wish to switch to an automated charging solution.
The results can be applied to a plethora of other areas and the algorithm present in this thesis is by no means
restricted to EVs. The main scenario (freight truck) explored can always be adapted with new battery capacity
values, charging time schedules, as well as number of robotic arms.
