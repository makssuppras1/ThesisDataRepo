A. Abstract 
Energy efficiency and its related costs for operations of agitation and aeration in bioreactors 
are an important consideration in process economics. This report reviews the research 
conducted on the characterisation of the energy efficiency in bioreactors, which is a key 
process parameter in the industry due to its economical character. This was accomplished 
by investigating two stirring systems in the same 611 litre vessel. The first stirrer is a classic 
six bladed Rushton turbine stirrer setup, the second is a stirring setup that makes use of 
rotating jet heads (RJH), often used for tank cleaning purposes. 
 
For both systems, the oxygen mass transfer was investigated with kLa-values (mass transfer 
coefficients) as the prime parameters. These values were obtained by making use of the 
glucose oxidase method in a non-coalescence medium. The specific power input window 
for the Rushton turbine setup ranged between 0.12 kW/m³ and 1.18 kW/m³, for the rotary 
jet head setup this ranged between 0.38 kW/m³ and 2.57 kW/m³. In the overlapping zone 
of the power windows, no difference between the two systems could be found for the 
oxygen mass transfer coefficients. Only a stagnation in increase for the kLa could be 
distinguished for the higher power inputs in the RJH setup. To quantify this stagnation, the 
energy efficiency was calculated based on the kLa-values and the power measurements 
done during the experiments. 
The evidences from this study suggest that for both systems the energy efficiency [kg 
O2/kWh] could be correlated to P/V-.60. This negative exponential correlation gives an 
explanation for both the higher kLa values found in the Rushton turbine setup that reached 
a lower specific power than the RJH, and the stagnation of the increase of the kLa values 
for the RJH.

1 Introduction 
Products originating from fermentation are being used in almost every aspect of daily life. 
These products can be found in feed, fuel, food, industrial chemicals and pharmaceutical 
products. In 2015 the global fermentation market exceeded 55 million tons and the 
expectations for 2020 are that this is going to further increase to be around 70 million tons. 
This quantity is mainly attributable to the fermentation processes for ethanol and methanol 
production, but the huge demand for organic acids in the plastic and fibre industries will for 
the future have the highest impact on the growth of the global fermentation market. 
Another factor, in the expansion of this business, is the stringent regulations from the EPA 
and the European Union to positively stimulate initiatives aiming for sustainable production 
processes. Furthermore, the general public demand more and more environmentally 
friendly products. On the other hand, there are some factors that restrict the growth of this 
industry (Grand View Research, 2016; De Gelder, 2015; Deloitte The Netherlands, 2014). 
Besides the supply of raw material, the most important factor is the efficiency of the 
equipment used and the ability to scale up the process (Deloitte The Netherlands, 2014). 
The importance of the efficiency of the production equipment in the biotech industry, and 
more specifically the energy efficiency, will in the future only increase due to the rising 
energy demand and energy prices (U.S. Energy Information Administration, 2016; 
Sieminski, 2016). 
 
1.1 The Biotech industry 
When optimizing the efficiency of the production equipment, a thorough understanding of 
the whole production chain and its setting need to be acquired. This goes from 
understanding of the industry to understanding the setting of the company and eventually 
the different processes. In a competitive market, from a wastewater treatment plant to 
chemical plants that ferment glucose to succinic acid, every company tries to add value to 
a certain raw material (Figure 1.1). By doing this, the company can generate revenue and 
invest again in its development. A trending way to do this is by creating functional or 
organizational units that are responsible for new business development (NBD). Rather than 
only doing research for new product development in the existing market, the traditional 
focus of an R&D department, the NBD will also deal with (new) business activities along 
the whole value chain (Daubenfeld et al., 2014).

Figure 1.1: Value chain with specific added values for renewable feedstock (Grotkjær, 2015). 
In the development of these new activities, the technical feasibility of the project is a key 
factor. Accordingly, a techno-economic analysis should be conducted when a change in the 
plant setup is anticipated. As an example, one can consider the bio-based succinic acid 
production (Figure 1.2), first of all, a few parameters can be defined to describe the 
fermentation performance; product yield, productivity and concentration. These are 
respectively the amount of product formed per amount of carbon source [g/g], the space-
time yield [g/(L.h)] and the titer of product [g/L]. Later in the process, the production 
process can be refined by looking into the following cost reducing parameters; medium cost 
and utility chemicals [$/MT], by-product formation [%purity] and stability of microorganism 
[%successful fermentation]. These parameters can be used for a clear and understandable 
communication between decision makers, engineers and scientists inside a company. 
(Grotkjær, 2015) 
 
Figure 1.2: Production process from glucose to succinic acid (Grotkjær, 2015). 
==PAGE:15==

The aforementioned parameters are based on the fact that the production of bio-based 
succinic acid is a fermentation process. Vaswani (2010) defines this process as “an energy-
deriving reaction in eukaryotic or prokaryotic organisms, which converts a simple carbon 
source into various alcohols”. For the succinic acid production, this means that glucose will 
be converted to succinic acid. Multiple pathways can be followed to accomplish this, 
however, in the presence of oxygen (aerobic conditions), the following biomass formation 
is assumed on a C-mol basis: 
 
𝐶𝐻2𝑂+  0,398 𝑂2  →0,574 𝑋(𝐶𝐻1,8𝑂0,5𝑁0,2) +  0,426 𝐶𝑂2 
(1.1) 
The chemicals in this equation are normalized to one carbon atom (Grotkjær, 2015). 
Vaswani (2010) investigated the economic feasibility for an 83 million Lb/yr bio-based 
succinic acid plant. Here it can be concluded that the feedstock is the biggest variable cost. 
For the investment cost of the fermentation equipment, the compressors accounts for 
10.7% of the investment cost. The compressor cost originates from the fact that, besides 
glucose, the organisms also need oxygen to produce succinic acid (equation (1.1)). This 
oxygen will be introduced in the form of a compressed air mixture, and for this reason, 
these vessels are often called gas-liquid contactors. 
After the injection of the air, there exist numerous methods to improve the transfer of 
oxygen from the air bubbles to the liquid. A few of those are increasing the pressure over 
a fermenter, enriching air with pure oxygen and increasing the power input for agitation 
and aeration. Shields and Kao (1994) showed that a cost reductions of 27.8% of the 
aeration and agitation cost is possible, just by having a good understanding of the necessary 
power input and corresponding effects for aeration. However, one should keep in mind that 
the total variable cost for agitation and aeration might only account for 0.5-1% of the total 
variable production cost per kg product. Nevertheless, because of the high influence of the 
aeration and agitation system on production processes with a high oxygen uptake rate, it 
has a big influence on the production yield. Because of this, case studies were found in the 
literature where a better aeration and agitation system are implemented. Herein cuts up to 
25% of the total production cost per kg of product were reported (Ryu and Oldshue, 1977; 
Soderberg, 1996; Shields and Kao, 1994; Vaswani, 2010).  
1.2 Outline of thesis 
In previous work at DTU Chemical and Biochemical Engineering, a glucose oxidase method 
for mass transfer characterization in a bioreactor was further developed (Israelsen, 2016). 
This method will here be used to investigate the power efficiency in bioreactors. To do this, 
two different stirrer setups are used that rely on a total different principle to accomplish the 
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
transfer of oxygen to a liquid. The first setup is a traditional mechanical radial stirrer, the 
six bladed Rushton turbine. The second setup is the IM10 Rotary Jet Mixer, based on 
rotating jets. 
 
Chapter 2 gives a review of the literature that supports this thesis. The theory of oxygen 
mass transfer will be discussed as well as how oxygen mass transfer is achieved in practice. 
Further on, attention is paid to different measuring methods of the oxygen mass transfer 
coefficient and to the representation of the efficiency of this oxygen transfer. 
 
Chapter 3 gives an overview of the two setups used in the experiments. Besides this, the 
operational method for the glucose oxidase is described and how the data of the runs were 
interpreted. To end this chapter, the method for the power estimation is elaborated for 
both the setups. 
 
Chapter 4 presents the results found during the experiments and compares the both 
setups based on the energy efficiency. Furthermore is the sensitivity of the kLa value on the 
driving force discussed and the need for a robust controller strategy. 
 
Chapter 5 and 6 gives an overall conclusion with some perspectives for future work on 
the three major topics of this work; the GluOx method, the RJH and the energy efficiency.

2 Theory 
2.1 Gas-liquid mass transfer 
A general representation of aerobe microbial reactions, such as the succinic acid production, 
can be given as follows (Villadsen et al., 2011a): 
 
𝑆𝑢𝑏𝑠𝑡𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑒 (𝑆) + 𝑂2 →𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑑𝑢𝑐𝑡 (𝑃) + 𝐶𝑂2 + 𝐵𝑖𝑜𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠 (𝑋) 
(2.1) 
The substrate for these reactions is present in the form of a carbon source. For a closed 
system with constant volume, the reaction rate and the production mass balance can be 
seen as: 
 
𝑟= 𝑘[𝑆]𝑛[𝑂2]𝑚 
(2.2) 
 
𝑑[𝑃]
𝑑𝑡= 𝑘[𝑆]𝑛[𝑂2]𝑚 
(2.3) 
With k the rate constant and [S], [O2] and [P] the concentrations of respectively the 
substrate, oxygen and the product. Since the rate constant, k, is independent of the 
concentrations of any reactants, it is a measure of the intrinsic reaction rate.  
However, in practice, for most aerobic systems the intrinsic reaction rate of the microbial 
system appears to be bigger than the actual reaction rate. This can be explained by the 
rates of transport processes which can be slower than the aforementioned intrinsic reaction 
rates. 
In case of a large-scale aerobic fermentations, the transfer of oxygen in the gas phase to 
the liquid phase is the most vital part of the operation. As a result of the relatively slower 
transfer rates, especially during fast growth. Pinches and Pallent (1986) describe the fall of 
oxygen concentration for a xanthan production with a Xanthomonas campestris strain due 
to a high oxygen demand during the growth phase. An oxygen concentration of 5-10% 
saturation is generally accepted as the critical oxygen concentration for yeast and bacteria 
(Pinches and Pallent, 1986; Shuler, 2002). This oxygen concentration can be maintained by 
a good understanding of the rate of oxygen transfer into the fermentation broth. 
Therefore, the discussion of the mass transfer in this thesis will be especially focused on 
the transfer of oxygen from the gas phase to the liquid phase. One should keep in mind 
that the transfer of other reactants (carbon dioxide, methane, etc.) or the transfer between 
other phases (solid fixed enzymatic phase and liquid substrate phase) or both are also 
important in specific cases (Villadsen et al., 2011b; Ryu and Oldshue, 1977), but these are 
not further discussed in this work.

2.1.1 Oxygen mass transfer 
The path that an oxygen molecule has to take can be seen as a sequential number of steps, 
with each their corresponding resistances, (Figure 2.1) until it reaches the place where it 
will be consumed. 
 
Figure 2.1: Steps during the oxygen transfer from a gas bubble to the cell (Garcia-Ochoa and 
Gomez, 2009). 
For the improvement of the efficiency of oxygen transfer from the gas phase to the liquid 
phase. The rate limiting phenomenon must be identified among these steps. A mass 
transfer equation will be introduced for describing the transfer of oxygen from a bubble to 
the bulk liquid. Whitman (1962) states that this could be done with his two film theory of 
gas absorption. Herein Whitman described the flux through each film as the product of the 
driving force with the mass transfer coefficient (Figure 2.2): 
 
𝐽𝑂= 𝑘𝐺(𝑝𝐴−𝑝𝐴𝑖) = 𝑘𝐿(𝑐𝐴𝑖−𝑐𝐴) 
(2.4) 
With JO the gas flux (mol/(m²s)) through the interface (i). kG and kL are the mass transfer 
coefficients for respectively the gas film and the liquid film, p is the partial pressure of a 
certain gas, A, in the bubble and at the interface and cA the dissolved gas concentration at 
the interface and in the bulk liquid of the same gas component A (Whitman, 1962). 
 
Figure 2.2: Two film model with the partial pressure and concentration profiles. This picture was 
adopted from Mann (2009). 
==PAGE:19==
7 
 
However, studies have shown that the concentration gradient in the liquid film isn’t linear 
and is by itself a combination of 2 layers where in the first layer (near the gas film) the 
diffusion dominates and in the second layer (near the bulk liquid) the turbulent advection

2.2 Equipment 
In an industrial setup, the gas bubbles size and shape, and thus the oxygen mass transfer, 
will depend on the different forces acting on the bubbles injected into a liquid. Three major 
groups of gas-liquid reactors can be found in the industry (Figure 2.3).  
 
Figure 2.3: Gas-liquid contactors. From left to right: Stirred tank, bubble column and airlift reactor. 
This picture was adopted from Doran (1995). 
The first group is the bubble column, it is characterized by its simplicity and its high 
height:diameter proportion (typically between 6:1 and 12:1 (De Gelder, 2015)). This gas-
liquid contactor, in its simplest form, only consist of the vessel itself and a sparger. The 
second group is the airlift reactor, this is basically a bubble column with an inner tube, 
called the riser. This riser will induce a structured recirculation pattern with higher local 
liquid velocities compared to the bubble column. The last group is the stirred tank. This 
tank has a smaller height:diameter (typically between 1:1 and 3:1 (De Gelder, 2015)) 
proportion compared to the previous gas-liquid contactors. The biggest difference, 
however, is the introduction of a mechanical device to move the liquid around, the stirrer. 
For each of these reactors a few factors exist that influence the oxygen mass transfer, the 
focus in this part will be on the sparger and the stirrer. 
2.2.1 Spargers 
The sufficient supply of air in a reactor is the first step in optimizing the oxygen mass 
transfer. This will take place through a device called a sparger. The main goal of the sparger 
is to disperse the gas (usually air, in the form of bubbles) through the vessel with an as 
high as possible specific surface area and distributed over the vessel to maximize the mass 
transfer rate. On the other hand, the sparger still has to be able to handle aeration rates 
large enough to provide a certain mass transfer rate. This is because an insufficient transfer 
==PAGE:22==
10 
 
may cause the failure of the process, a reduction in the yield, or production of undesired 
side products (Takahashi et al., 1992). 
 
Figure 2.4: Three groups of sparger types (Zehner and Kraume, 2000; Kulkarni and Joshi, 2011). 
Numerous spargers designs are known, but most of them fall under these three categories: 
nozzles (A), perforated plate, tube or ring spargers (B) and sintered spargers (C) (Figure 
2.4). The categories differ in opening size and the number of openings. In the literature, 
different researchers have proven that the design of the gas sparger has an influence on 
the mass transfer of oxygen for bubble columns (Ohkawa et al., 1987; Jin et al., 2006), 
however, for airlift reactors this dependency seems to only be the case for low volumetric 
gas inputs (Contreras et al., 1999; Merchuk et al., 1998). Š ijač ki et al. (2013) found that, 
compared to a single orifice sparger and a perforated plate, the mass transfer was the 
highest when using a sintered plate, but only for low superficial gas velocities (ug < 
0.03m/s). This can be explained by the fact that the sintered plate will produce the smallest 
and abundant bubbles. These bubbles will be held in dispersion more easily and will be 
depleted of oxygen when the superficial gas velocity increases. These bubbles will as a 
result of this act as a sink and mix with the fresh bubbles (Š ijač ki et al., 2013; Al-Masry and 
Dukkan, 1997).

2.2.2 Agitators 
In stirred vessels, the dispersion of oxygen is a combination of the action between the 
sparger and the stirrer, where for airlift reactors and bubble columns this is just the sparger. 
This is because, in this case the bubble size and dispersion will depend on the relative rate 
of stirring and on the gas flow (Figure 2.5). Consequently, the mechanism of bubble 
formation in the sparger is of secondary importance compared to the effect of the rotor 
blade or the impeller (Doran, 2013). In the trail of the impeller, bubble break up will occur 
due to the mechanical stress. This also implies that the average bubble size in the tank 
bears few similarities with those formed by the sparger (Luo and Svendsen, 1996). 
 
Figure 2.5: Gas-liquid dispersion in an agitated vessel (Varley and Birch, 1999). When stirrer speed 
(N) increases with a constant gas flow rate (QG) the system will go through three stages. First, the 
stirrer will be flooded with gas, related to a very low power input from the stirrer. Second, the gas 
bubbles are driven out horizontally, but no recirculation under the stirrer occurs. In the last stage, 
the gas bubbles are fully dispersed and there is a good gas recirculation. The same steps are seen, 
but in reverse order, when the stirring speed is held constant and the gas flow rate increases. 
So the design and the location of the gas sparger to the stirrer has not been considered as 
critical as in non-agitated reactors (Ulbrecht and Patterson, 1985). However, this is only 
true when looking at ring spargers with a diameter smaller than the impeller itself and 
within one impeller diameter of the turbine plane (Birch and Ahmed, 1997). The sparger 
design remains important for its role in introducing air bubbles to the stirrer. In view of this 
fact, the sparger should have a diameter bigger than the stirrer diameter for a Rushton 
turbine. For an up- or down-pumping propeller, a sparger equal to the stirrer diameter is 
recommended. Then an optimum gas transfer rate will be established now that the 
maximum amount of air will be introduced right into the output stream of the impeller 
(Sardeing et al., 2004). In this output zone, that goes from the impeller tip to 1.3 or 1.4 
times the impeller outer radius (Figure 2.6), shear forces are the strongest and almost all 
of the bubble break up will take place in this zone (van't Riet and Smith, 1973; Parthasarathy 
et al., 1991).

2.2.3 Alternative gas-liquid contactors 
Ordinarily, in gas-liquid contactors, the stirring system starts from the same principle: a 
rotating mechanical stirrer and/or a stationary sparger. However, since 2004, a patent exists 
for a system that is based on a totally different system: rotating jets (Hummer, 2004). 
This rotating jet head (RJH) is based on the cleaning system used in large oil-tanks by 
sprinkling water with powerful jets. It consists of a main body that can horizontally rotates 
around its axis and four jets that rotates in a vertical plane. These movements are facilitated 
by the liquid that’s running past a turbine which is connected through a gearbox with the 
moving parts (Figure 2.8). The two rotations take place at a different speed and after 43 
rotations of the main body and 45 rotations of the jets, a full pattern is completed.

In the last decennia, multiple protocols were developed to measure the mass transfer 
coefficient. They either make use of the oxygen mass transfer rate (OTR) or the oxygen 
mass balance, that takes also the oxygen uptake rate (OUR) into account;

This oxygen uptake rate can be caused by chemical or biochemical reactions that use the 
dissolved oxygen in the given liquid.  
 
Further on, the most important methods will be presented. However, prior to presenting 
the different methods, one should take into account that for all the following methods the 
assumption of a well-mixed gas- and liquid-phase is made. Accordingly, a constant mass 
transfer coefficient is expected over the whole gas-liquid contactor. When this isn’t the case, 
modelling of the different phases might be needed (Jones, 2007; van 't Riet and Tramper, 
1991). 
 
Except for a few more exotic methods, all the oxygen mass transfer measuring methods 
can be divided into two groups. The first are those who are tied directly to the oxygen 
consumption by the bio-reaction and the second are the reaction controlled methods that 
require the addition of chemicals during the execution of the measuring method (Villadsen 
et al., 2011b). 
2.3.1 Bio-reaction based methods 
In the first category, the direct method and the dynamic method are the most commonly 
used methods. When making use of the direct method, the molar in- and outflow of oxygen 
molecules is monitored. By measuring the partial pressures or the oxygen concentration in 
the airstreams, the oxygen transfer rate to the liquid can be found. This will be achieved 
when the measurement are done with a stable oxygen profile in the liquid (

airstream. Consequently, the kLa can be calculated if the dissolved oxygen concentration is 
measured and if the oxygen saturation concentration can be calculated. This method can 
be applied in-line in existing fermentation plants, even for real-time feedback, but can be 
very costly if the different probes aren’t already present in the plant. Another big drawback 
of this method is that, when applied to a small vessel, the difference between the measured

values in the in- and outflow will be small, which increases the error. When applied to a 
larger vessel, the pressure differences in the tank become too big to assume a 
homogeneous saturation concentration. Modelling of the gas and liquid phase might then 
be necessary. Mukhopadhyay and Ghose (1976) tried to solve the first problem by depleting 
the tank for oxygen by adding sulfite to the broth. This will however, have the same 
disadvantages as the sulfite method that will be discussed further in this work (van 't Riet 
and Tramper, 1991; Villadsen et al., 2011b; Mukhopadhyay and Ghose, 1976). 
For the dynamic method, either the air supply will be sequentially shut down and resumed 
or the oxygen saturation concentration can be stepwise changed by changing the pressure 
or the oxygen concentration in the air supply. The last option is only effective when no 
oxygen consumption takes place in the reactor. By plotting the dissolved oxygen 
concentration over time, the OTR can be found from the difference between the change of 
oxygen concentration over time (dcO2/dt) and the OUR (Figure 2.10).

The dynamic method can be applied in real fermentation processes. However, available DO 
sensors have a response time close to the characteristic time for mass transfer which means 
that the measurements can be influenced by the sensor dynamics. This is especially the 
case with the Clark electrode: the mass transfer in the boundary layer of the electrode will 
be a matter of great concern when working in viscous systems or working with low fluid 
flow velocities. In the literature, a general rule of thumb can be found. For electrodes with 
a response time of 2-3s, measurements up to kLa = 0.1 s-1 are acceptable for this method 
(Van't Riet, 1979; Tribe et al., 1995). Another problem associated with this method is when

oxygen concentration. In this case, either the logarithmic mean driving force as defined

taken into account. In general, the turning point for when it is needed to use this logarithmic 
mean driving force lays around a vessel height of 1m (van 't Riet and Tramper, 1991; 
Gogate and Pandit, 1999).

these methods, the use of model liquids is required to maintain specific reaction conditions. 
Due to this requirement, the results are hard to translate to real fermentation broths, but 
on the other hand, it makes the ideal results for comparing them with other cases in the 
literature. Three different reaction controlled methods will be discussed.

2.4 Oxygen transfer efficiency 
Efficiency can be defined in various ways. In order to compare the efficiency with other 
literature, the definition of the efficiency has to be clear. This is also the case for aeration 
systems. In this work, the energy efficiency as defined by Albæk et al. (2012) will be used. 
Besides this, an introduction to other forms of efficiency will be given in the following 
paragraphs. 
 
The first oxygen efficiency evaluation method is by studying the kLa or the OTR. These 
parameters can be corrected for site-specific conditions to the standard oxygen transfer 
rate. Herein, the OTR will be rescaled to conditions of 20°C, 1 atm and zero salinity. 
However, since this unit is highly dependable on the specific settings, any comparison only 
based on this should be avoided (Henkel et al., 2011; Wagner and Popel, 1998) 
 
Secondly, the efficiency can also be defined as the quantity of oxygen absorbed per volume 
unit, called the “specific oxygen absorption” [g O2/m³]. Since this especially gives 
information about the capability to dissolve oxygen into the broth at a steady state and not 
about the rate, this is mainly used when looking into the difference of a tank design where 
factors as depth and pressure play a role (Wagner and Popel, 1998; OTT System, 2000). 
 
Another, more frequently used parameter is the aeration efficiency or the “specific oxygen 
transfer efficiency” (SOTE). This parameter shows the percentage of oxygen diluted in the 
water per cubic meter [%/m³]. This parameter is already accepted and broadly used in 
systems for wastewater treatment. These systems are defined by low oxygen consumption 
rates. Because of this, variations in oxygen consumption don’t have to be taken into account 
(Tumeo and Stephens, 1996; Wagner and Popel, 1998). 
 
The energy efficiency is the ratio of the oxygen transfer rate to the specific power required. 
This parameter characterizes the economics of an aeration system better than the other 
parameters. Especially, when plotted against the OTR, this parameter gives a good 
indication for companies who are interested in achieving a certain OTR and who want 
knowledge about the power cost that accompanies this process for a certain setup (Albæk 
et al., 2012; Schügerl, 1993). Consequently, besides the measurements of the oxygen 
transfer rate, a good measurement of the power draw has to be made. Therefore, the 
different measuring methods for power consumption will be discussed in the following 
paragraphs.

2.4.2 Power measurement methods 
2.4.2.1 Electrical energy measurements 
The first measuring method is based on the electrical consumption of the stirrer motor and 
can be found by the following equation when using three-phase AC motors: 
 
𝑃= √3 𝐸𝐼𝑐𝑜𝑠𝜙 
(2.24) 
Here, the factor cosφ is called the power factor and this is a technical specification of the 
motor that is known for full load operations. However, the main challenge is the calculation 
of this power factor with other loads. 
The power consumption of the motor can also be found directly by using a wattmeter. But, 
in both cases, this will still give a picture of the total energy consumption, including the 
different losses in the motor, the gearbox (if present) and losses due to friction. While this 
might be an important value for economical purposes, for process design or scale-up this 
value isn’t valuable to make conclusions or prediction. It is considering that losses can take 
up to 70% of the total power measured at laboratory and bench scale. In industrial settings 
this method might still be used due to the high costs of other sensors or the technical 
feasibility of the sensors. The latter applies especially to the dynamometer for larger vessels. 
In these industrial settings, the real power draw into the liquid will then be approximated. 
This will be done with the use of correction factors for the energy losses and by doing extra 
measurements without any liquid (van 't Riet and Tramper, 1991; Ascanio et al., 2004; King 
et al., 1988) 
 
2.4.2.2 Calorimetrical 
The first law of thermodynamics and the law of conservation of energy states that, for 
isolated systems, a change in the internal energy is equal to the work done on the system 
plus the heat supplied to the system (Cohen and of Pure, 2008).

thermal energy. Typically, several thermistors are placed at different spots in the tank to 
have an average temperature of the whole vessel. When the temperature is logged over

Besides the liquid volume (Vl), the liquid density (ρl), the specific heat of the liquid (cl) and

equivalent liquid volume (Ve) needs to be defined that takes the heat capacity of the stirrer, 
shaft, coils, wall, etc. into account. Secondly, heat interactions with the environment (ΓHL) 
have to be taken into account for the power calculations. This can be done in the form of

reaction needs or gives to the liquid. Especially for smaller scale setups (<1 m³) the heat 
losses through the wall are relatively large which makes this method inaccurate.  
Oosterhuis and Kossen (1981) performed calorimetric measurements in a 25 m³ tank for 
demonstrating the heat losses across the vessel wall. Herein, they found an energy loss of 
1% of the total power input. However, note that in these experiments a vessel with a 
cooling jacket was used that was only filled with air. This helped with keeping the tank 
isolated. Other disadvantages of the calorimetric method are that high sensitivity 
thermistors are needed (± 0.1°C) and experiments need to be done over a relatively large 
time period since it takes a lot of energy to increase the temperature of the liquid. Since 
this method has more disadvantages than advantages, this method isn’t that commonly 
used (Oosterhuis and Kossen, 1981; van 't Riet and Tramper, 1991; Ascanio et al., 2004; 
Carreau et al., 1992).

2.4.2.4 Torque meter 
Another method to measure the power draw into the liquid is to measure the torque. The 
power draw can again be calculated with equation (2.27) where the vector product of the 
force and the lever arm is equal to the torque (M). 
A torque meter consists of an axis part, usually with a smaller diameter. The principle of 
this measurement is then to quantify the distortion in the axis. This can be done optically, 
magnetic/electrically or by a strain gauge. An example of optical measurements was 
published by Ascanio et al. (2004) who used a photo elastic coating for a full-field indication 
of the strain.

resistance of a conductor depends on its geometry. When the strain gauge is placed on the 
stirrer shaft and connected in a Wheatstone bridge setup (Figure 2.13), the resistance can 
be accurately correlated to the strain and thus the torque.

4.4.2 pH stabilization 
When starting these experiments, a pH set point of pH 5.5 was chosen. Being that the 
optimum activity of glucose oxidase is situated around this pH (Wohlfahrt et al., 2004) and 
this would give a more constant activity when the pH changes. However, when conducting 
the experiments at this set point, it was hard to reproduce the experiments. The gained 
results seem to have a higher kLa value at the end of the day (Figure 4.10). To make sure 
this wasn’t the degradation of the feed, samples were taken over time and the concentration 
was determined by titration. In these experiments, no difference in concentration was 
found. But when looking at the pH profile over time (Figure 4.11), evidence of pH buffering 
of the liquid can be seen. As a result of this, a set point of pH 7 was chosen for the 
experiments conducted after this. This set point was chosen due to the fact that a pH had 
to be chosen with an as big as possible gap with the pKa value of 3.7 for GluA (OECD, 
2004) and with enough activity of the enzymes to perform the conversion.

same day (24/10/2016). The set point for the experiments was pH 5.5. 
A second issue is that the used controller must keep the pH steady, also in a vessel where 
mixing is not optimal. In these experiments, an on/off-controller was used. Compared to 
using an advanced controller, it is assumed that this on/off controller resulted in bigger pH 
fluctuations. This, in combination with the need to work around a pH that wasn’t the 
optimum pH for the enzymes, resulted in even bigger oxygen fluctuation. This is a result of 
the bigger dependency of the activity of the enzymes on the pH at this higher pH (Figure 
4.12).

5 Conclusion 
This study was set out to characterize the power efficiency in bioreactors. To do this, two 
setups were chosen, a six bladed Rushton turbine setup and a Rotary Jet Head setup. The 
specific power draw for agitation and aerations, as a function of the volumetric oxygen 
mass transfer, was studied for both systems. 
 
The glucose oxidase method was utilized for the kLa measurements. With this method a 
good estimation of the kLa was found, especially in cases with lower kLa values, but still 
enough power input to be a well-stirred system. For higher kLa values, a relatively high 
feeding rate was necessary what implicated a high feeding rate. This higher feed demand 
has some practical implications that make this method harder to use for high kLa values. A 
few other drawbacks of this method were revealed during the experiments, such as the 
buffering capability of GluA and the high pH dependency of the activity when running the 
experiments at a higher pH. A robust pH control strategy has to be employed, for this 
method to be reproducible.

6 Future work 
In this section, suggestions for future research will be given. Three different topics will be 
discussed: Research to improve the GluOx method, research on the RJH and research on 
the efficiency. 
 
For the glucose oxidase method, the pH stability is the most important factor that needs 
more research. For this, the design of an robust control strategy needs to be considered or 
a calculating method to take the buffering into account when working at a lower pH. Both 
methods will result in experiments with a more stable enzyme activity over time and thus 
a more stable dissolved oxygen profile. 
 
Considering the RJH, it might be interesting to look deeper into the hypothesis of having 
an efficiency drop for the oxygen mass transfer at high power inputs. If this is the case, the 
study whether there is a correlation between this upper limitation and the distance to the 
vessel wall could be an interesting subject for further research. Mixing time studies at higher 
power inputs could be conducted or flow simulation models can be useful to get an 
indication on this topic about the presence of this limitation. 
 
All the measurements done in the context of this work were conducted to define the energy 
efficiency. For the energy efficiency, a general correlation could be seen for both systems. 
However, in further research, this correlation should be tested to see if it still holds when 
being used on a different scale. Especially for cases where gradients of oxygen 
concentration will be present such as high tanks or very viscous mediums. 
Another interesting field of study for the industry could be to connect the energy efficiency 
to productivity. The question to be asked is: is the increase in productivity through a power 
input increase able to financially compensate the higher cost of energy? Especially keeping 
in mind that a higher power input also implicates a lower energy efficiency.
