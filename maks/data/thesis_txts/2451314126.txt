Sustainable design and operation of residential buildings. An integrated
approach towards energy efﬁciency and thermal comfort for housing in heating
dominated climates
by Carlo MANCARI
Climate change, increasing energy prices and evolving energy policies are driving
the building codes towards more and more stringent energy frames. To comply with
the regulation criteria it is of fundamental importance that the behaviour of a build-
ing as a consequence of its characteristics is studied since the very early stages of the
design. This approach requires a close collaboration of the multiple stakeholders in
a multidisciplinary team, so that the different aspects, needs and challenges can be
targeted since the very beginning. An integrated design has already proven to bring
great advantages not only to the outcome of the projects, but also to its time-frame
and economy.
This thesis examines the main features to evaluate in the early stages of a build-
ing design, namely massing and orientation, thermal envelope and thermal mass.
Each of these topics is studied and discussed starting from up-to-date literature re-
view and integrating it with complementary simulation-based analyses. The out-
come of the study is then applied to a residential building in Copenhagen, being
currently designed by Henning Larsen Architects.
The focus is kept on passive strategies and design choices that improve the per-
formances of a building prior to the use of active systems. Such performances are
evaluated in terms of energy demand, in particular for space heating, and of ther-
mal comfort. Economical considerations are presented alongside. Danish BR20 and
EN15251:2007 are the main benchmarks for the evaluation of the results.
The outcome quantiﬁes the potential for energy savings and improvements in
thermal comfort for each of the examined measures and optimisation strategies, pro-
viding a knowledge base for the realisation of low energy residential buildings.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Contextualising the study
Residential buildings have roots more than 10000 years deep: since the history of
men evolved from nomadic to sedentary, the ﬁrst homes meant to last were built.
They were made of wood and branches, stones, mud and clay bricks - simple struc-
tures with the essential purpose of creating a shelter from the outdoors. Materials,
shape and characteristics would widely vary depending on the geographical loca-
tion, or more precisely on the local availability of resources and the climatic condi-
tions.
In the years, the building techniques improved, guaranteeing more and more
livability to the inhabitants, and the methods to increase indoor thermal comfort
evolved alongside.
Local geographical characteristics strongly affected the evolution of buildings,
both under the climatic point of view (like sun availability, precipitations, wind and
temperature proﬁles) and for the abundance or scarcity of particular building mate-
rials. In addition, technological development and socio-cultural aspects like tradi-
tions, economic conditions and local history of architecture played a signiﬁcant role.
Across countries and centuries there was however a common underlying con-
ception of what a house should provide: good living comfort, aesthetical satisfac-
tion, high habitability and affordability. Historically, these have been the core ideas
around which architects, engineers and builders developed their projects, and out of
which dwellers evaluated their quality.
Scarce attention was paid to the consequent depletion of resources, consump-
tion of energy and emission of pollutants into the atmosphere, all direct effects of
the construction and the operation of a building. These factors received a smaller,
peripheral attention, mainly because of meagre awareness or negligence on environ-
mental issues, but they are today well acknowledged and studied.
According to the International Energy Agency, the 22% of the global ﬁnal energy
consumption is attributable to dwellings [1]. This makes the residential sector one of
the three more consuming ones, together with industry (37%) and transport (29%).
Despite energy efﬁciency measures reduced the energy intensity of this sector com-
pared with the past decades, the increasing rate at which new houses are built kept
the energy use rather stable over the last years (see Figure 1.1).

1.2
The Integrated Design Process
The best answer that can be given to the call for energy savings in the residential
sector is the Zero Energy Building (ZEB), in its different conceptions and deﬁnitions.
The main idea behind it is achieving an energy balance where the negative terms
equal the positive ones. In other terms, the overall energy demand is completely
covered by self-generation through renewable energy technologies, most frequently
from solar and geothermal sources. Whether the building exchanges energy with the
grid or not, whether the balance is in terms of energy or exergy, whether the «Zero»
in the balance equation itself is obtained at all points in time, or yearly, or over the
lifetime of the building, the fundamental points in the realisation of a ZEB are the
same, and can be summed up into two main steps:
• Reduction of energy demand through passive solutions and efﬁcient systems;
• Supply of clean energy through on-site renewable energy systems (RES).
The generation side depends on the local characteristics of the project, and mainly
relies on solar harvesting or ground-coupled systems.

1.3. Why this thesis?
3
As the surface where the RES can be installed (usually the building’s roof or facades)
is limited and the required investment cost is signiﬁcant, it is important that the de-
mand of the house is reduced through efﬁciency measures. The limit for energy use
is generally imposed by national standards; however, the Energy Performance in
Building Directive of 2010 states that all buildings realised from 2020 on have to be
Nearly Zero Energy Buildings. To fulﬁl this requirement, the designers need to think
about a building as a whole, unique system composed of all the different and many
parts that play a role in the overall functioning and in its energy balance.
"Systems thinking" is a design attitude that considers all the possible interconnec-
tions among the components of a building, and requires the synergy of specialists
of different ﬁelds (architecture, construction, heating, ventilation, plumbing...). It is
the collaboration of these ﬁgures that makes the quality of the building high enough
to reach a zero or nearly-zero energy balance: if all the professional ﬁgures concur-
ring to the project sit together and work jointly from the very beginning, each design
choice can ideally be taken as the optimum or best-compromise across the different
aspects and systems, allowing functionality and efﬁciency to be brought to higher
levels. This is what is called Integrated Design Process.
This design strategy opposes to the traditional "linear" process in which all de-
sign steps were decoupled and seen as individual. This prevented the full exploita-
tion in the synergies that intertwine the architectonic elements of the building, its
systems and the overall performances, most probably leading to higher operation
costs and worse outcome. The linear design method was more suitable for the for-
mer building codes that mainly followed a prescriptive approach, specifying prop-
erties of a solution rather than the expected performances of it. However, this ap-
proach is now out-to-date as most of the building regulations and standards evolved
towards the performance domain [5].
When all the actors in the design team cooperate and coordinate their common
efforts since the very beginning of the project, their individual knowledge can be
fully harnessed and put together, ensuring that each design choice is taken as the
best compromise across the different elements that, altogether, allow the realisation
of a sustainable building. A broad range of parameters is included in the analysis,
and their evaluation is carried out in parallel under each ﬁeld’s point of view.
1.3
Why this thesis?
This comprehensive and synergyc process expresses its potential especially in the
early stages of the design, where the stakeholders have the largest freedom of choice.
If the knowledge of different specialists is coordinated and intertwined since the
very beginning of the project, not only the quality of the outcome is more likely to
be superior, but also timeline and costs can be reduced.
As learned during the course of studies, the processes that play the main roles in
the early building optimisation can be summarised into three key steps.
The ﬁrst one relates to the preliminary planning, when the overall volumes of the
building are deﬁned. Shape, proportions and orientation are studied and combined
in the so-called massing process. This phase determines, ﬁrst of all, the ratio between
thermal envelope and internal volume or ﬂoor area: in other words, the proportion
between heat ﬂows and necessary, "useful" heat. In addition, in a combination of

dimensions, proportions and orientation, the solar availability is deﬁned. In this
stage it is important that the heat ﬂows resulting from the architectural planning
are understood, so as to minimise losses and enhance passive heating and daylight
availability. Therefore, not only the external shape, but also the internal room layout
has to be taken into account.
The subsequent step involves the characterisation of the thermal envelope, re-
sponsible for losses and gains of heat.
Especially when the fenestration area is
addressed, it is very important that energy consultants assist architects to balance
winter and summer regimes. In fact, if very large glazings can improve the pas-
sive heating, they can also provoke discomfort for too high temperatures during the
warm months. This is the case, for example, of the residential building Redmolen
1.19, whose study is addressed in part of the thesis.
Yet the design of the transparent envelope is depending, thus inseparable, from
that of the opaque envelope. Thereby an accurate study of the thermal character-
istics should be carried out on the entire envelope, aiming to optimise yearly and
life-time performances.
Lastly, the design team should consider, alongside the heat ﬂows, also the heat
accumulation, and in particular the quantity and rate of heat being stored and re-
leased to the inside. As will be discussed, internal walls can have sufﬁcient thermal
mass to inﬂuence the thermal inertia of a building. Since the ﬁrst stages of the de-
sign inertial phenomena should then be understood and exploited in favour of the
energy efﬁciency through the study of thickness and materials of such walls.
Because of the complexity of the relationships between these steps, the early
stages are often designed with sub-optimal solutions. The building sector could
beneﬁt from literature that provides a range of architectural choices and a set of
corresponding energy implications. The stakeholders of a building project would
then be aware of the impact of the many decisions on the overall performance of the
building. Improving the performances means reducing the impacts related with its
realisation and operation, the required time and the derived economic effort.
Such literature work is what this theses attempts to achieve: a tool for a compre-
hensive optimisation in those stages that have the largest potential, a piece in the big
picture of a sustainable present capable of maintaining the environmental balance in
the future.

Chapter 2
Goal and Structure
The underlying intention behind this thesis is to support, develop and pull together
the current practices for the design and realisation of high performing buildings, in
the attempt to facilitate the spread of sustainable housing solutions. The reduction
of the energy demand through the passivity of the building is the main focus of the
following discussions, on the basis of the potential of massing the building, charac-
terising its thermal envelope and exploiting the inertial dynamics.
The early phases of the design are what this work intends to explore, in order to
create an inherently well performing groundwork, prior to the choice of the active
systems that will be used to achieve and maintain a comfortable indoor climate.
The motivation for writing this thesis lies on two personal involvements. The
ﬁrst is a feeling of urge for rapid changes in how energy is consumed and how
intense are the consequent impacts, hand-in-hand with the belief that sustainable
buildings can bring a concrete help in the reduction of the human environmental
footprint.
The second originates by the understanding, gathered along the university stud-
ies, of the importance and complexity of the integrated, holistic design process. With
such an approach it is complicated to ﬁnd compromises that satisfy all the needs and
requirements for a building (in particular architectonic features and energy perfor-
mances). The complexity and lack of awareness of the mutual inﬂuences across the
many design choices can lead to intrinsic inefﬁciencies and sub-optimal results. The
early stages need to receive particular attention in the very ﬁrst steps of the project
for two main reasons:
i) important synergyes can be exploited in these stages, creating a solid ground-
work for the realisation of an efﬁcient building;
ii) overlooking the mutual inﬂuences of different sides of the design (here, archi-
tecture and energy efﬁciency) may require to modify the design in a later stage,
where the changes are more complicated and more expensive to implement.
Consequently, this thesis was shaped so as to help the different professional ﬁg-
ures involved in the building design to jointly take early-stage decisions during the
realisation of «green» buildings.
The discussion is divided into two parts: the ﬁrst one, which comprises the Chap-
ters 4, 5 and 6, identiﬁes and discusses the fundamental steps in the early-stages
design process, in the attempt to present strategies to increase the overall building
efﬁciency. These three chapters will be organised so that:

Chapter 3
Methodology
The work carried out in this thesis follows a descriptive and applicative integrated
approach applied to the early stages of the design of a multi-storey dwelling. The lo-
cation of the building is assumed to be Copenhagen; however, the achieved results
and conclusions can be considered generally valid wherever a heating-dominated
proﬁle exists, on the qualitative point of view. The research begins with the identi-
ﬁcation of what the steps of the passive design are, and consequently seeks for and
studies the inﬂuencing parameters in each stage. As will be discussed in more de-
tail in the following, these elements are analysed through both literature research
and individual work, which is mainly conducted with the help of the EQUA’s build-
ing simulation software IDA ICE. For the deﬁnition of the reference buildings or for
the evaluation of the energy performances, the current Danish Building Code BR18
is taken as benchmark. The quality of the indoor environment is instead assessed
through the parameters and thresholds suggested by the standard EN 15251 (2007).
3.1
Thesis strategy
The research and original work carried out cover multiple topics, namely massing
process, thermal envelope and thermal mass. As anticipated in Chapter 2, these top-
ics are at ﬁrst studied in Part I and then applied in a case study in Part II, of which a
complete description is provided in Section 3.4.
The most effective strategy for the realisation of Part I of the thesis was thought
to be an individual examination of the three arguments, each in a dedicated chapter.
Every chapter follows the same structure, i.e.:
i) the starting point is a literature review, where the up-to-date knowledge is
tackled and presented;
ii) from the analysis of the existing literature, gaps or lacking research are high-
lighted;
iii) starting from the knowledge acquired from the review and from the individual
academic experience, the literature is integrated with personal research. The
investigations are carried out with the help of simulation tools. For consis-
tency across the three chapters, the same simpliﬁed reference model is used,
on which more can be read in Section 3.3.
Part II follows the same order as Part I, implementing the ﬁndings of the thesis
to the study case, the Redmolen 1.19. With this strategy, each chapter of Part II
addresses the corresponding topic from Part I..

and EN 13791, LEED, BREEAM and others, its reliability makes it a widely used
simulation software especially in the Nordic Countries. Version 4.8 was used for the
simulations; the chosen location for the simulations is Copenhagen/Kastrup, as here
is located the closest weather station.

3.4
Case study
The Redmolen 1.19 is a residential building being currently designed by Henning
Larsen Architects, under the energy consultancy of Rambøll Group A/S. It is part of
a complex of four buildings, of which two will be used for ofﬁces and the other will
host a hotel. It develops on 6 ﬂoors, and has a ground area of 1536 m2.
Figure 3.2: Nordhavn district and the position of the Redmolen, in
the red circle.
The designated location is Mariehamngade, Copenhagen, in the emerging dis-
trict of Nordhavn (see Figure 3.2), where energy efﬁciency is the key word of each
project. The Redmolen, not to be outdone, will comply with the stringent bound-
aries of the BR20 building code. To do so, the yearly primary energy use should
not exceed 20kWh/m2. A primary energy factor of fp,DH = 0.6 will be used for the
heating demand, covered by district heating; a fp,el = 1.8 will be considered for all
the electric loads.
3.4.1
Current state of the project
The Redmolen 1.19 is, under the architectural and structural points of view, in a well-
developed stage, although still subject to changes and improvements. The geometry
of the building is shaped and the housing units are deﬁned, together with the size,
position and features of all the individual rooms. However, as the design is not the
ﬁnal one, Henning Larsen Architects requested to refrain from showing renderings
and images of the building.
As the design was handed out to Ramboll A/S for the energy consultancy, some
problems were encountered. In fact, because of the very large fenestration area and
of the relatively large solar heat gain coefﬁcient of the glazing units, major overheat-
ing problems arose. In particular, three bounds set by the architectural design can
be seen as responsible for thermal discomfort:
i) the current size of the windows needs to be kept constant or nearly unchanged;
ii) muntin bars, especially in the central part of the windows, should be avoided
to create a continued, undisturbed view on the outside. This makes the most
of the windows not openable, hindering the use of natural ventilation;
==PAGE:30==
12
Chapter 3. Methodology
iii) the glazings need to be fully colourless and transparent, to limit their impact
on the view. As the visible transmittance varies according to the the solar heat
gain coefﬁcient (cf. Eq. 5.8 in Section 5.2), the g-value cannot be freely de-
creased. A lower bound of 0.6 is set for the visible transmittance, thus the solar
heat gain coefﬁcient cannot be smaller than 0.3.
Furthermore, only a fraction of the windows are protected by direct solar radia-
tion in the summer months. Balconies provide shading to the glazings underneath,
and their size was calculated so as to avoid the sun rays to hit the windows during
all summer. However, more than half of the total fenestration area is unshaded, and
other ﬁxed or operable shadings are not planned because of aesthetic concerns.
As a consequence, overheating occurred for more than the 25% of the hours of
the Test Reference Year, with temperatures exceeding 27◦C from March till October.
The solution suggested by Ramboll was an increase in the mechanical ventilation
ﬂow rate from 0.3 l/(sm2) to 3.2 l/(sm2) during the warmest months. Good temper-
ature control was then achieved, but the energy used by the air handling unit was
too high to comply with the regulatory frame of BR20.
When the building was taken as a study case for this thesis, the request of Hen-
ning Larsen Architects was to ﬁnd a solution to mitigate the overheating issues. The
answer was found mainly in the implementation of venting panes on top of the cur-
rent windows. With this measure the overheating hours reduced by around 85% in
the critical zones. The starting point for the analyses carried out in this thesis was
therefore subsequent to the implementation of the venting panes.
More concerning the venting panes can be found in Appendix B. A description
of the current performances of the Redmolen 1.19, as given by Henning Larsen Ar-
chitects and prior to any change, is provided in Appendix A
3.4.2
Modelling approach
The Redmolen 1.19 was modelled on the dynamic building simulation software with
a high level of detail and precision. The geometries of the building were retrieved

sponded to the architectural design, including size and position of the windows.
The model was then completed with all the necessary characteristics, listed be-

Subsequently, the efﬁciencies of the systems were modiﬁed from the default val-
ues so as to match the resulting energy use with the data provided by Ramboll A/S.
Once the model was calibrated, it was edited to realise two different models to
be used for the analysis of Part II. The simulations of the Redmolen were run on two
different levels:
i) single-apartment model on the critical apartment, i.e. the A06 in the South-
West corner. This model was used for running the ﬁrst simulations, allowing to
perform the necessary analysis in a brief time thanks to the reduced complexity
of the model. The ﬂoor area of the A06 is 125.7 m2;

Chapter 4
Orientation and Massing processes
The very beginning of the design phase is represented by an overall architectural
programming of the building, that includes the analysis of the site and of the general
characteristics that deﬁne the building shape. In this stage, massing, solar orienta-
tion and design of the ﬂoor plan layout have to be considered concurrently because
of their interconnection.
The commissioning body often plays a role in the deﬁnition of these parameters,
providing the contractor with indications and boundaries that the building has to
satisfy. Also, especially in cities, position, size and shape of the land plot and stylistic
coherence with the surrounding buildings are likely to impose some constraints on
the size and shape of the building itself.
Apart from these restrictions, it is up to the design team to deﬁne the geometries
and volumes of the building.
4.1
Planning the internal spaces
A bottom-up approach centred around the functionality of the spaces is an effec-
tive practice for the planning and design of performance-oriented buildings. This
method starts from the small-scale organisation of the housing units and translates
it into a large-scale deﬁnition of the building massing. An effective programming
allows to arrange the internal zones in the most advantageous way for daylight and
solar gains exploitation, and allows improvements in thermal control according to
the purposes of each room.
The main idea behind this approach is to keep in mind when and how the rooms
are occupied, what heat gains they are subject to, and what the position and the irra-
diance of the sun is at that time; understanding and harnessing the interconnection
between use and location of the spaces can lead to signiﬁcant advantages in terms
of energy use and thermal comfort. Passive solar heating, shading and daylight ex-
ploitation are the central matters to be targeted, but reducing the lenght of pipes and
ducts and fostering natural ventilation air ﬂows should be kept in mind as well.
In general, common best-practices in the thinking of the internal layout are:
• Living rooms are often the most used and the largest spaces, thus a maximisa-
tion of daylight availability and passive solar heating can lead to signiﬁcant
advantages in terms of energy savings. Southwards disposition and glazed
surfaces enhance the use of daylight and harness the solar heat gains to reduce
the energy use in winter. However, solar control should be planned by means
of shadings and/or solar control coatings to avoid excessive temperatures in
the summer months.
==PAGE:38==
20
Chapter 4. Orientation and Massing processes
• Bedrooms should ideally be located so as to avoid solar gains in the late part
of the day; northern or eastern exposure are both appropriate as allow a good
temperature control.
• Kitchens receive a large amount of heat gains from the appliances (ovens, cook-
ing stoves), therefore additional solar heat from west facing windows could
lead to overheating issues in the late afternoon hours, when they are usually
in use. In addition, proximity with dining rooms and with technical rooms (to
reduce ducting and piping lenght) should be sought.
However, the short timing to which the use of kitchens is limited allows for a
higher ﬂexibility in its positioning.
• Utility spaces such as technical rooms, laundries or bathrooms can be planned
on the northern side, as they can provide a buffer zone from this coldest side of
the house and the most occupied areas. In addition, windows are not necessar-
ily required in such rooms, and minimising the glazed surface on the northern
facades can bring a signiﬁcant beneﬁt to the energy balance of the house. As
for the kitchens, a short distance between bathrooms or laundries and the tech-
nical room allows higher distribution efﬁciency and reduced life-cycle costs.
Especially in tall buildings, a vertical juxtaposition of these rooms can give
place to substantial energy and material savings and to improved DHW sys-
tems performances.
Applying these criteria to the characterisation of the planimetry results in some
boundaries to the overall shape and the solar orientation of the dwelling; the mass-
ing process of the building should therefore start from individual housing units and
combine them in such a way to obtain the highest degree of compactness of the
whole building.
4.2
Building massing
The process of massing deals with the establishment of the building’s overall size,
shape and volumes. It is a common conception that this process is to be based on
aesthetic factors, yet the architects need to be aware that the consequences of their
choice in this design stage reﬂect considerably on the energy performances of the
building [12], [13].
A careful development of the massing of the building has energetic relevance be-
cause it inﬂuences the heat-exchanging area of the building through its compactness
ratio, it affects the availability of free solar energy and of wind-driven ventilation air-
ﬂows and has an impact on the amount of renewable energy that can be generated
on-site.
4.2.1
Literature review
Building shape and energy use
There is a considerable number of studies on the relation between shape and energy
use, proposing different approaches to the optimisation of the building shape with
respect to its performances and impacts. In the most recent research, generative de-
sign appears to be a common practice in this process. Most often this method is
applied through the development of genetic algorithms that, given a set of variables
(lenght and number of building sides being the most recurring ones) and constraints,
==PAGE:39==
4.2. Building massing
21
generate optimised solutions with respect to the objective function. Usually the opti-
misation is carried out with respect to life cycle costs, environmental impact, heating
loads or energy use in general [14].
Conventionally, a ﬁrst architectural design is created and a model to evaluate its en-
ergy performance is realised; alternative designs are then generated according to a
set of parameters and bounds, modelled for energy simulations and analysed.
Hemsath and Bandhosseini [15] presented a methodology to evaluate the build-
ing form with respect to its energy consumption; a genetic algorithm was used to
produce a number of variations in the height-to-lenght proportion, the volume kept
constant. For each parametric variation, further variations in the quality of the ther-
mal envelope were carried out, and it was seen that the massing of the building
could have a larger impact than the materials used in the envelope on the energy
performance of the building; this, however, depends on the climatic location of the
building.
Luisa Caldas [16] suggests the application of a Generative Design System for
three-dimensional building shaping in the cold climate of Chicago, considering day-
light factor and energy losses. Massing, aspect ratio, surface-to-volume ratio and
building orientation proved to be the most signiﬁcant drivers for the studied per-
formances; in particular, it was seen that with higher compactness both the average
daylight factor and the heat losses decrease. Thus the optimisation of the building
shape leads to different results depending on what variable is taken into account.
However, as the space heating is often responsible for a higher share of the to-
tal energy demand than the artiﬁcial lighting, for the sake of reducing the energy
demand of the building it is preferable to optimise the massing for heat loss reduc-
tion. This is generally achieved with a low shape factor, deﬁned as the ratio between
thermal envelope area and internal volume.
Also, if the room layout is already planned for allowing sufﬁcient daylight ac-
cording to the criteria of 4.1, the heat-losses-wise optimisation of the building shape
will not prevent the building from receiving sufﬁcient daylight.
Wang et al. [17] realised a generic optimisation approach that aimed at min-
imising life-cycle costs (LCC) and life-cycle environmental impacts (LCEI), and they
found out that, in general:
• rectangular shapes have better performances, with aspect ratio that varies de-
pending on the objective function. A value of around 1 reduces the construc-
tion costs and the impacts related to the use of materials, as a square section
minimises the external envelope surface; rectangular shapes with the long side
facing South (aspect ratio of 0.4) yield better energy performances;
• the orientation of the x-axis of the building mostly converged to an azimuth of
0◦especially for rectangular-based shapes;
• a wall tilt angle of 75◦, leaning inwards, proved to reduce both initial and
energy-related running costs. This is explained by a reduction of 17% in the
roof area and an increase of only the 3% in the wall area, that decrease the
need for materials and the heat exchange with the outdoor environment;
Parameters like window-to-wall ratio, type of window, insulation thickness, over-
hangs and structural materials were optimised as well, but they are disregarded here
as they fall outside the purpose of this section. The limit of Wang’s study is the

constraint imposed to the ﬂoor shape, that was allowed to be either rectangular or
L-shaped.
One year later the same authors published a paper discussing a polygonal ﬂoor
shape optimisation algorithm they developed, where the number of sides was given
as input (in their case study, ﬁve) and the lenght and orientation of each side was op-
timised [18]. Again, LCC and LCEI were used alternatively as objective functions,
and did not always lead to the same results, showing that the optimum for one in-
dicator can be different from the optimum for the other. However, in general a large
difference in results was seen between sub-optimal and optimised ﬂoor shape, con-
ﬁrming the necessity to carefully design the building shape to achieve high energy
performances.
The rest of the available literature about optimisation of the building massing, es-
pecially for the minimisation of energy use, relies on the analysis of the shape factor.
This quantity is a measure of the compactness of the building. Generally speaking,
buildings with smaller shape factor (more compact shape) have a smaller surface
through which the heat can be lost, thus they tend to use less heating energy.
The shape factor can be calculated as a ratio of the thermal envelope surface to the
heated volume (A/V) or as the ratio between the thermal envelope surface and the
total ﬂoor area (A/A). As pointed out by Lylykangas [19], the former is not always
a good indicator of energy efﬁciency, and the use of the latter should be preferred as
a guideline in architectural design.
However, the impact of the shape factor is strongly dependent by several aspects,
the main ones being climatic characteristics, WWR and thermal transmittance of the
envelope.
As an example, Danielski, Joelsson and Fröling [20] found a difference of 11-14%
in the heat demand when, for a highly insulated residential building in a Nordic
climate (Tavg = 8◦C), the shape factor (A/A) changed by the 70%. This dependency
is more pronounced when the thermal losses are higher: when the global U value
was increased from 0.1 to 0.3 W/(m2K), the difference in heat demand increased by
the 20% for the same change in shape factor.
Building shape and energy generation
The dualism between the geometric characteristics of the building and its behaviour
is not only valid under the energy use point of view, but it applies also in terms of
on-site generation with renewable sources. The inﬂuence of the process of building
massing on its generation potential, especially for solar energy harvesting, was in-
vestigated by numerous studies, where different shapes and orientations were tested
and compared.
The potential of roofs and facades for solar energy exploitation in a urban con-
text was studied by Compagnon, who presented a generalised method to identify
the most suited portions of facades and roof where to locate generation technologies
[21]. In this way, different proposals for building massing or urban planning could
be quantitatively compared; however, being the impact on the proposed geometries
on the energy demand of the building overlooked, this method does not necessarily
suggest the holistically optimal conﬁguration of volumes.
==PAGE:41==

Caroline Hachem et al. [22] broaden the analysis from the individual building
to the neighbourhood design, assessing the inﬂuence over photovoltaic generation
and use of solar heat gains of housing shape (rectangular, L-shaped and V-shaped
geometries), site layout and density of buildings in the site.
The main ﬁnding was that the result of an optimisation for solar energy generation
varies from the optimum in terms of energy efﬁciency: L-shaped or V-shaped build-
ings lead to higher production from photovoltaic technology, with beneﬁts that vary
depending on proportions and rotation angle of the side wings. Also, the optimal
shape depends on the site layout, therefore the relationship among site and building
shape cannot be generalised but has to be contextualised according to the character-
istics of the building site.
Youssef’s paper [23] on the determination of the ideal building shape and orien-
tation for maximising the integrated photovoltaics generation relies on a parametric
variation from an original, simple massing. The space of solutions depends on the
PV technology considered, and both the outcome of the optimisation process and
the related costs vary from type to type . However, his ﬁndings highlighted how the
analysis for building massing needs to comprehend PV generation and energy use
altogether, as optimising for the former may reﬂect negatively on the latter, and the
mutual effects cannot be neglected.
The literature review presented so far illustrates the bond that exists between the
overall massing of the building and its behaviour, in terms of both energy consump-
tion and generation potential. Signiﬁcant and exhaustive qualitative assessments of
the building performances exist in these two respects separately, but little research
publications can be found on the integrated evaluation of a given building geometry
under both points of view at the same time. Yet, as the shape optimisation leads to
different results if the minimisation of the energy demand or the maximisation of the
photovoltaic generation are taken as objective function, it is possible that a threshold
exists, in the shape factor of a building or in its aspect ratio, after which the matching
of consumption and generation is not achievable.
In this very early phase of the project the choice taken by the design team can
ease or complicate, and possibly even hinder, the achievability of a zero energy bal-
ance.
In the attempt to ﬁll this gap, a simulation-based analysis of different build-
ing shapes was performed starting from a square-base reference case that was ma-
nipulated to achieve various conﬁgurations respecting the same ﬂoor area. Four
rectangular-base buildings with different aspect ratios are the alternatives included
in this study, that aims at understanding what the best conﬁguration is considering,
altogether, heating and cooling demands, lighting energy use and balancing of the
loads by on-site photovoltaic generation.

a signiﬁcant share in the second and a smaller fraction in the ﬁrst.
The fourth category is met only for the building with an aspect ratio of 1/4, and in
particular the discomfort is caused by overheating above the set point of 26◦C, even
though the operative temperature never reached 27◦C. Checking the heat balance
of this model it was seen that this small overheating is caused by solar radiation
that, despite the presence of shadings, provokes heat gains particularly in the ﬁrst
afternoon hours of the spring: here, the sun path is still low enough to limit the
effectiveness of the shadings. In the other buildings the smaller total glazed surface
allowed to avoid this issue.
By analysing the yearly plot of the Predicted Mean Vote it can be seen that, for
all four shape conﬁgurations, the main cause for lower comfort categories is repre-
sented by the perception of a sense of cold. As the operative temperature is never
dropping below 19.8◦C, but the PMV in winter months gets lower for smaller aspect
ratio, the reason can probably be found in the thermal asymmetry caused by cold
radiation from the windows.
4.2.3
Conclusions
Conﬁrming what was found out from the presented literature review, the simula-
tions showed how the shape of the building affects its energy performances under
several points of view. It was possible to conclude that:
- Larger aspect ratios, i.e. more compact massing, reduce the shape factor of the
building, thus decreasing the total size of the surfaces responsible for the heat
losses. As in high-rise buildings the roof and ground ﬂoor represent a minor
fraction of the total envelope, the relationship among aspect ratio and space
heating energy use is nearly linear, provided that the window-to-wall ratio
and the characteristics of the glazing units are the same. From the most to the
least compact shape, a difference of more than 10% exists in the energy use
for space heating. Furthermore, considering the potential reduction of glazed
surface on the north side allowed by narrow conﬁgurations, larger heat saving
potentials arise.
- Compact shapes play against efﬁcient daylight distribution and reduce the ef-
fectiveness of natural ventilation; however, an accurately planned room layout

can mitigate the impact of the overall building shape on the use of daylight.
Moreover, natural ventilation should not be taken as a decisional parameter as
in cities the outdoor air quality is often poor, thus discouraging this practice in
favour of mechanical solutions.
Overall, the reduction in lighting energy demand allowed by narrow shapes is
outweighed by the larger space heating consumption.
- The cooling demand shows a digressive trend for decreasing aspect ratios,

- As reducing the aspect ratio of a building yields improvements under certain

Thermal envelope
This section deals with the characterisation of the building envelope, that delimits
and protects the indoor environment from the outside. Apart from being structurally
sound and aesthetically pleasant, its main purpose is to regulate the ﬂows of heat
and moisture into and out of the building, and its mass can have an inﬂuence on
the phase shifting of the thermal loads. This last point will be the focus of the next
chapter, thus it will be left out from this section.
There are two main features to consider when evaluating the quality of a thermal
envelope: its thermal transmittance and its air tightness.
The thermal transmittance (U, [W/(m2K)]) expresses the heat ﬂow across one
square meter of surface with a unitary temperature difference, thus when the area of
the envelope and the temperature gradient across it are known it reveals how much
heat is lost or gained.
Heat ﬂows through the envelope of a building occur also by means of inﬁltra-
tions and exﬁltrations, uncontrolled and unintentional air ﬂows caused by leakages
and pressure differences. Their magnitude depends on several factors: the driving
force is typically a pressure gradient between the indoor and outdoor environments,
whether caused by differences in temperature or by wind colliding with the enve-
lope, or in most situations by a combination of the two [25].
In high-rise buildings, stack effect can play a major role in the establishment of in-
ﬁltrations and exﬁltrations: as the temperature difference across the building height
creates buoyancy phenomena, a pressure difference is created from the inside to the
outside of the building, forcing the air out of the envelope from the top and in at the
bottom [26].
The characteristic of the thermal envelope that describes its ability to limit these un-
desirable air ﬂows is the air tightness, deﬁned as the leakage airﬂow rate at a refer-
ence pressure, often 50 Pa, and typically measured in Air Changes per Hour (ACH)
or in m3/h.
The reduction of inﬁltrations and exﬁltrations from the thermal envelope is not im-
portant solely in terms of heat transfer: also the moisture that is carried through the
leaking air is of relevance for the long-term performance of the building material
and for the quality of the indoor environment.
The way the building envelope can be composed and characterised, and what
are the important aspects where designers should focus their attention, is going to be
discussed in the next pages, divided into two sections: the ﬁrst concerns the opaque
envelope, whereas the second addresses glazed surfaces.

5.1
Opaque envelope
The term «opaque envelope» is used to generally indicate external walls, roof and
ground ﬂoor. Even though their composition is different because of the diverse
structural roles they have, an overall common structure can be identiﬁed, composed
of a structural layer and an insulating layer. As the ﬁrst is mainly designed out of a
structural set of choices, the pages of this section solely cover the discussion of the
latter.
As a foreword, two types of insulation exist for the opaque structure, namely
bulk and reﬂective.
Bulk insulation restricts the convective and conductive heat transfer across the
shell of the building, and its performance depends on the thermal resistance it can
achieve. This is a function of the material, more precisely of its thermal conductivity
(λ, [W/(mK)], and of the thickness of the insulation layer. Neglecting the direct
proportionality between temperature and conductivity, the thermal resistance for a
given thickness is an inherent property of the material, thus any value can be reached
by adjusting the insulation layer in thickness.
In contrast, reﬂective (or foil) insulation acts on the radiative thermal exchange.
It generally consists of a thin layer of aluminium supported by backing materials
such as hard foam, polyethylene ﬁlm, plastics or cardboard, and needs to face a
layer of air where the heat can be bounced back [27]. Oppositely to bulk insulation,
the thermal resistance of a reﬂective insulator is not a function of the material itself,
but varies depending on its positioning, the cleanness of its surface and the type of
climate the building faces. This type of insulation is generally more suitable to hot
climates where the heat needs to be kept outside.
For this reason, and because of the generally better performances allowed by bulk
insulation materials, foil insulation will not be treated hereinafter.
When the insulation of the opaque components is assessed, there are three the
parameters to take into account: the position of the insulation layer in the stratigra-
phy of the wall, its thickness and the type of material used.
5.1.1
Position of the insulation layer
The insulating material can, generally speaking, be arranged in four different ways.
i) Internal placement gives the highest protection of the insulator from the atmo-
spheric agents, but the structural material suffers from highest thermal stress
(temperature swings, expansion/ contraction phenomena). In addition, ther-
mal bridges assume a signiﬁcant weight and the exploitation of the thermal
mass is limited;
ii) External placement limits the thermal bridges by creating a more continuous
coating all around the shell of the building. As the structure, i.e. the most
capacitive layer, is in contact with the indoors, its thermal mass is more effec-
tively activated, thus it can absorb excess heat gains better;
iii) Central placement gives an even distribution of the insulation in the slab, as-
suming part of the characteristics of the other two conﬁgurations;
iv) Combining the above three is sometimes practised too to achieve compromises
between different qualities, with beneﬁts that would be presented below. In
==PAGE:51==
5.1. Opaque envelope
33
this case, the insulation material is distributed within the slab in different lay-
ers. Note that, as the thermal resistance of the wall is a summation of the
individual s/λ of each layer, for a given material and total thickness the over-
all R-value does not change from having one layer of thickness s1 or x layers
of thickness s1/x.
Kossecka and Kosny [28] studied, for six different wall conﬁgurations, the in-
ﬂuence of the insulation layer position over the heating and cooling loads and the
total energy demand of a one-storey building in six different climatic areas. The
walls varied only for the reciprocal position of concrete and insulation, maintain-
ing the same thermal resistance (R=3.21 m2K/W) and capacity (C=330 kJ(m2K). The
study revealed that for all the heating-dominated areas the best yearly performance,
deﬁned by the lowest overall energy use, was achieved when the insulation was
placed either on the external side of the wall - this case yielded the minimum heat-
ing and cooling loads too - or between two concrete layers where the internal one
had around twice the thickness of the external. The scenario having internal insula-
tion had the poorest performance in both heating- and cooling-dominated regions,
with a difference in total energy use of around the 3% from the best case.
Ozel and Pihtili [29] describe the analysis of 12 different wall conﬁgurations in
eastern Anatolia (average yearly temperature of 8.7◦C), with one or multiple insu-
lation layers placed in the internal, central and/or external part of the slab. The
thermal transmittance and capacity of the walls were, again, constant across the dif-
ferent cases, and the ﬁnite-difference, transient-based modelling of the problem gave
as optimal conﬁguration a complex assembly. Rather than a unique insulation coat,
three layers of smaller thickness were placed in the inside, centre and outside of the
wall. Here, the optimum was intended as the solution allowing the highest time lag
and smallest decrement factor, with the implicit assumption that this would lead to
the lowest loads and energy demand. The difference between this study and the
aforementioned one of Kossecka and Kosny reveals that optimising the inertial be-
haviour of a building does not necessarily mean minimising its energy use.
Similar research was carried out by Al-Sanea and Zedan [30], who investigated
the number of insulation layers in a vertical slab. For equal thermal resistance and
capacity, the wall with three separate layers was found to bring substantial improve-
ment to the case with a unique insulation layer: the time lag was increased from 6 to
12 hours, the decrement factor was about 10 times lower. The heating and cooling
peak loads were both reduced by 20%, resulting in an yearly demand 3.2% lower for
space heating and 1.6% lower for space cooling.
The three typical slab assemblies, i.e. with external, internal and middle insula-
tion, were analysed by Ozel in [32], who compared them in terms of heating load, in-
ertial shifting and amplitude reduction: that the differences in performance were far
greater in terms of time lag and decrement factor than in heating load. However, the
heat transfer equations used for the calculations overlook the thermal bridges, that
for the different insulation conﬁgurations can assume signiﬁcantly different magni-
tude and lead to considerable deviations in the energy use.
After carrying out this literature review, three details were observed:
i) it was seldom speciﬁed if the linear loss coefﬁcients were changed to take into
account the different thermal bridges arising from changing the position of the
insulating layer;

5.1.3
Conclusions
Starting from the review of research papers, the four possible strategies of position-
ing the insulation in the wall stratigraphy have been studied and integrated through
simulation-based analysis. It was found that the position inﬂuences the energy per-
formance as it affects heat load, peaks phase shifting and energy use as recapped
hereafter.
- As the heavy (structural) material is placed towards the inside of the slab, the
heat storage is facilitated with positive impact on the reduction of the heat load
peaks, thus decreasing the need for energy to preserve the comfort tempera-
ture.
- Thermal inertia especially beneﬁts radiative heating systems, whereas when
convective heating is used (i.e. with radiators instead of ﬂoor loops) a faster
response of the room leads to energy savings, thus external or central insula-
tion become favourable.
- External insulation proved to yield the best performance, followed by split,
central and internal insulation. This trend applies to both kinds of heating
system, despite being more pronounced for radiant ﬂoor heating.
- Using intermittent or continuous heating strategies lead to the same trend
when the four insulation positions are compared; however, intermittent heat-
ing allows some energy savings when a low-inertia envelope is used.
- The lowest the thermal transmittance, the more signiﬁcant the difference in
energy use across the four analysed insulation strategies. In this regards, the
size of the U-value is however more important than the position of the insula-
tion and the consequent availability of inertial phenomena. In fact, the worst
case studied for the wall with U=0.14 W/(m2K) still allowed a lower energy
demand than the best case of the wall with U=0.18 W/(m2K).
All things considered, the best performing wall is the one with the lowest thermal
transmittance and external insulation.
Turning the attention to the quantity of insulation, it was understood that the
thickness of the insulating layer has to be optimised taking into account both the
long-term running costs and the initial investment cost relative to the insulation ma-
terial. In the choice of the latter, both performances and speciﬁc cost have therefore
to be weighted, as a better performing but more expensive material is not necessar-
ily preferable to a cheaper one having higher conductivity.

5.2
Glazings
When turning the attention to the transparent envelope components, thermal trans-
mittance and air tightness are not sufﬁcient to fully describe their characteristics;
two optical properties have to be introduced:
- the Visible Transmittance (Tvis or VT), i.e. the percentage of light in the visi-
ble spectrum that, when colliding with the glazing, is able to pass through it.
Visible transmittance is relevant for the quantity of daylight it allows indoors,
thus its inﬂuence on the energy balance of the building regards the electric
consumption for artiﬁcial lighting;
- the Solar Heat Gain Coefﬁcient or Solar Transmittance (g or SHGC), i.e. the
ratio of the total solar radiation striking the window that is transmitted as heat
to the inside rooms. If on one side high solar gains can reduce the need for
active heating in the cold season, it is also true that in summer they provoke
overheating issues and, therefore, require energy for space cooling.
It is well acknowledged that the characterisation of the transparent envelope has
a signiﬁcant, direct inﬂuence over the performances of buildings, thus a careful plan
in this regards can bring substantial variations in their yearly energy balance [44].
Architects and engineer need to agree under two main points, namely the overall
size of the glazed surface, often quantiﬁed through the window-to-ﬂoor or window-
to-wall ratios, and the type of the window itself, that is to say its thermal and optical
characteristics. Thermal transmittance, Visible transmittance and Solar heat gain
coefﬁcient are strictly related one another, as one of these qualities inﬂuences the
others.

Regarding the energy balance of a window in cold countries, a controversy ex-

and thermal transmittance values to reduce heat losses without compromising the
visual comfort towards the conception of glazings as a source of free heat [51]. With

in cold, northern climates but also in southern European climates [52]. Nonetheless,
attention has to be paid, when trying to maximise the solar gains, not to cause over-

be achieved with relatively high g-values and the use of shadings [53], as will be
discussed later on in Section 5.2.2.

Thermal comfort
The use of shadings emerged as essential for the control of the indoor temperature
during the summer months. Prior to their implementation in the models, a cooling
energy of up to more than twice the heating energy was observed in some cases,
despite the signiﬁcant difference in the lenght of heating and cooling seasons. As a
consequence, the beneﬁts of larger sizes and g-values were hindered when the yearly
balance was considered.
The IDA ICE Ideal cooler unit used for the simulations does not allow the tem-
perature to exceed the set-point, thus a realistic mechanical ventilation system (with
Tair,supply = 18◦C) was implemented to check overheating issues. Excessive tempera-
tures were not avoidable in the majority of the shading-free cases with a high g-value
glazings; the solar heat gain coefﬁcient property was proving to be the most inﬂu-
encing characteristic over the indoor temperatures, in accordance with the ﬁndings
of Kull for south-facing windows [48]. Furthermore, for similar value of solar trans-
mittance, the glazing units having lower thermal transmittance resulted in higher
cooling energy demand, as a consequence of the lowest possibility of internal heat
gains to escape the thermal envelope.
This behaviour was observed also when shadings were in use: as the effect of the
incident solar radiation was strongly reduced, it was the ability of the window to re-
tain or dissipate heat to have the largest inﬂuence on the indoor climate. Despite be-
ing below the overheating threshold of 27◦C, the highest temperatures with low-Ug
windows arose mainly in the evening hours, when highest internal heat gains from
the occupants and the equipment occurred. Conversely, with higher Ug the highest
temperatures were detected in the central hours, when the outdoor temperatures
were higher and conductive heat transfer caused a rise in the room temperature.

5.2.3
Conclusions
The main outcome from this section is that glazings can achieve a net positive en-
ergy balance over the heating season in cold climates. This objects the previous
thesis that, no matter the characteristics of a glazing, a windowless wall was always
performing better than an equivalent case having whichever fenestration area. It
exists in fact a range of thermal transmittance and solar heat gain coefﬁcient combi-
nations for which the presence of a window can reduce the space heating consump-
tion. However, it has to be remarked that this is true solely for south-facing walls,
whilst all other orientations result in net losses of heat. For residential buildings in
heating-dominated climates, windows with very low Ug value (0.6-0.7 W/(m2K))
give the best performances, requiring less and less energy as the g-value increases,
and allow for more ﬂexibility in the choice of the window size.
Shadings proved to be of fundamental importance for maintaining thermal com-
fort in the summer months without excessive cooling energy use. When the awning
was implemented, for small g-values negligible differences arose from increases in
the fenestration area. This difference was however gaining more importance when
higher solar transmittances, especially if the Ug value was small.
For a given solar heat gain coefﬁcient, the lowest thermal transmittance performed
more poorly in summer, as the heat gains accumulated in the room could not escape
to the outdoors. However when the whole year is taken into account, low Ug values
are still preferable.

Thermal Mass
In the previous sections the early-stage factors that inﬂuence the energy balance of
a dwelling have been discussed. Speciﬁcally, emphasis was laid on what character-
istics affect the ability of a building to gain and retain heat and improve its solar
accessibility. However, as anticipated at the very beginning of Chapter 5, there is an-
other property of notable relevance in the dynamic analysis: the ability to accumulate
heat within its thermal mass.
In the most general sense, the thermal mass of a structure describes its ability
to store and sequentially release heat [64]. As shown in Figure 6.1, massive build-
ings allow to reduce and, mainly, shift the effect of external temperatures and in-
stantaneous internal and solar loads on the internal environment. If planned so as
to integrate and work together with the shape and orientation of the building, its
insulation, fenestration and installation of shading devices, thermal mass lays the
groundwork for the realisation of a passive or low energy building.
Figure 6.1: Two effects of thermal mass: shifting and damping of the
temperature peaks.
Two factors are of central importance to determine the thermal mass of a mate-
rial:
- Speciﬁc heat capacity (Cp, [J/(kgK)], i.e. the ability of the material to store heat;
==PAGE:68==
50

- Density of the material: the heavier the structure, the more heat it can accumu-
late.
The direct combination of Speciﬁc heat capacity and density, multiplied by the thick-
ness of the material layer, gives information about how much heat can be stored, for
unitary temperature difference, in a square meter of a wall, ceiling or ﬂoor.
Other two characteristics inﬂuence the quality of a material as heat sink: one
is the thermal conductivity, as it has an inﬂuence on the rate and effectiveness of
the heat ﬂows through the element. As an example, let’s consider timber (Cp =
1600J/(kgK), λ = 0.13W/(mK)): despite the high Cp, its performance as thermal
mass is poor because of a too low thermal conductivity, that limits the speed with
which thermal storage occurs. The second one is the characterisation of its surface,
in terms of texture and colour, as this can enhance or hinder the accumulation and
release of heat in and from the wall [65].
Before discussing in deeper detail how the right amount and disposition of ther-
mal mass can be obtained, some literature review will present why this property is
interesting as an efﬁciency and thermal comfort measure for sustainable buildings.
6.1
Thermal mass and energy use
The inﬂuence of thermal mass on the energy balance of residential buildings is still
subject of controversy and open discussion. In fact, both increases and savings in the
energy use with incremented thermal mass are reported by different authors, espe-
cially during the heating season. These discrepancies arise from the fact that, as will
be discussed afterwards, the effects of thermal mass strongly depend on a number
of other design choices, thus making it difﬁcult to reach a generic conclusion [66]. By
contrast, energy savings in the cooling season are almost unanimously documented,
provided that nighttime free cooling is performed [67, 68, 69, 70].
Focusing on the heating season, it is not possible to achieve general statements
as «The thermal mass of concrete in buildings [...] reduces heating energy consump-
tion by 2–15%» [71], as too many variables play a role in combination with thermal
mass.
As revealed by studies such as Karlsson et al.’s [72], where thermal mass was varied
by changing the thickness of a concrete layer in the inner part of the wall, ther-
mal inertia can possibly both ameliorate or deteriorate the space heating energy use,
depending on other characteristics of the building. If intermittent heating is used,
lightweight structures showed to be preferred, in line with the previous ﬁndings of
Bojic and Loveday [73]. If signiﬁcant solar gains are allowed as heat source, a dou-
bling in thermal mass (from 1500 kJ/(m3K) to 3000 kJ/(m3K)) cuts around 3% of
the heating demand. Due to the simplicity of the models used, however, this value
should not be taken as a quantitative fact. When no solar gains occurred, no differ-
ence in heating energy use arose from the heavy- and the light-weight buildings.
This last ﬁnding suggests that, as claimed by Rodrigues [74], thermal mass has a
small inﬂuence on the inertial phenomena if the thermal conditions are kept nearly
constant throughout the day: temperature swings are of fundamental importance
for the activation of thermal mass, and without them no or negligible beneﬁts can
be achieved. This means that a re-thinking of the control systems should be car-
ried out to fully exploit the inertial behaviour of a building. In fact, the develop-
ment of controllers led to increased stability and minimal ﬂuctuations around the
set point, but this strongly hinders the capability of the structure to accumulate an

emit heat. Braun draws attention to this issue in a study where he made an attempt
to optimise the zone temperature set-points for allowing the use of thermal mass.
Quoting his research paper, «The ultimate solution would be to employ an optimal
controller that determines a trajectory of zone temperature set-points by minimizing
an integral cost function evaluated using models for transient cooling requirements
and air conditioning equipment performance, utility rate information, and weather

development are still necessary.
From the above discussion it emerges that directly relating the thermal capacity
of a building with its energy demand is not possible, nor a generic statement about
the convenience of building massive dwellings can be stated. However, other con-
siderations of relevance for the energy-related economy of a building exist.
Heat emission system: as massive buildings suffer less than lightweight ones from
outdoor temperature shifts, a smaller heating or cooling power is required to cope
with low or high temperature peaks. This property allows high thermal mass build-
ings to reduce the size of heat emission/removal systems, with beneﬁts on the in-
vestment cost [77], and to have them running at design capacity for most of the
time, with positive consequence on their efﬁciency [78, 79]. In addition, the use of
low temperature heating and high temperature cooling are facilitated, as sudden
and intensive power outputs are not likely to be necessary [80].
Energy ﬂexibility: especially with the growth of renewable generation, demand
ﬂexibility becomes a central issue in the energy management. The contribution of
thermal mass to provide ﬂexibility to the building sector can be well understood
thinking about cold spells. During very cold, short-timed periods the space heat-
ing demand is likely to soar, causing peaks that can be difﬁcult and expensive to
tackle. Yet if heat is stored within the mass of the building the thermal comfort can
still be ensured even without the operation of active heating systems, thus shifting
the heat demand towards off-peak hours. Demand-Side Management becomes here
very important [81], and its potential in thermal mass has been widely studied and
acknowledged [82, 83, 84, 85].
As emphasised by Le Dréau and Heiselberg, who compared a poorly insulated
house (U · A = 179W/K) with a recent, well-performing one (U · A = 62W/K),
insulation is a key factor in the amount of time a building can maintain comfort
temperatures [86]. After a complete switch-off of the heating system, they found
that 2-5 hours of autonomy were achieved for the ﬁrst dwelling, whereas the second
maintained the comfort temperature for more than 24 hours.
To increase the performance of thermal mass as a modulation of the heating de-
mand, actively triggering its storage and release mechanisms is possible. Different
strategies exist: surface activation, air ﬂow through cavities, water pipes, and elec-
tric heating cables, recognised as the most effective activation means, were compar-
atively analysed by Olsthoorn et al., who stressed their advantages and limitations
in a very comprehensive publication, cf. [87].
Foteinaki et al. [88] investigated the potential of preheating and heat storage in
the thermal mass during low-demand periods, for residential buildings in Copen-
hagen. The strategy for enhancing the accumulation of heat in the structure relied on
increasing the room set-point for 2, 4 or 6 hours before the shutdown of the heating
system. Good load shifting results were reported, varying as a function of insu-
lation level, properties of the structural material, relative size of internal/external

Life-cycle analysis: from a life-cycle impact point of view, the position receiving
more support in literature argues that massive structural material should be min-
imised [89, 90]. In fact, as pointed out by Shaﬁgh et al. [91], concrete is the most
widely material used to achieve high thermal mass, but it is agreed that its intensive
production process heavily penalises the LCA of a building [92, 93].
Pajchrowski et al. compared under an LCA point of view four single-storey
dwellings having the same geometrical characteristics [94]. Two of them were made
out of concrete and two of timber, and for each construction material one case was a
traditional house and the other an all-electric passive building. It was seen that pas-
sive buildings, being generally more sophisticated, have largest embodied impact
(around 20 environmental points higher in both cases) than traditional ones in the
construction stage. Also, in this phase the impact of wooden houses amounted to
around half of the environmental points of the equivalent masonry houses. There-
fore the incidence of concrete over the lifetime impact of a residential building was
seen to be very high, calling into question the advantages of massive structures when
looking at the building’s entire life span.
However, some remarks are needed. Firstly, the foundations and the roof of the
house were responsible for the majority of the embodied energy, but in the case of
multi-storey buildings the share would clearly decrease.
Then, due to the assumed location of the analysed buildings, the Polish energy mix
was taken into account. Being the share of renewables as small as the 2%, with a 90%
of generation from coal plants, the embodied impact in the materials production and
in the operation stages are very high.
The analysis should therefore be contextualised on a country-by country scale to
consider the diversities in the energy mix and in the related emissions. However,
the matter of balancing beneﬁcial and damaging effects of the use of massive mate-
rials was raised, and still remains an open debate. It should be taken into account
that thermal mass, if obtained with environmentally impacting materials, should be
carefully assessed in the design, and should be coupled with strategies for effectively
exploiting it, otherwise its negative impacts could outweigh the advantages.
Summarising the literature review on thermal mass in conjunction with the en-
ergy use of a building, 6 points are identiﬁed, namely:
i) it is not possible to declare in absolute terms whether thermal mass is a bene-
ﬁt or not: complex interactions and multi-sided matters have to be taken into
account, such as type of heating, cooling and ventilation systems, solar access
and temperature proﬁles, occupancy and appliances schedules, walls compo-
sition and other characteristics of the buildings;
ii) due to the shaving of the temperature peaks, the size of the heating and cool-
ing systems can be smaller, with positive effect on the investment cost of the
building;
iii) as a consequence, the systems are more likely to have a working point always
close to their nominal one, with beneﬁts on their efﬁciency. Also, the use of
low temperature heating and high temperature cooling is facilitated;

6.2
Thermal mass and comfort
Because of the relationship of the inertial phenomena with other aspects of the build-
ing and the climatic characteristics of its location, predicting the level of comfort of
a building out of a simple relation with its thermal mass cannot be achieved. How-
ever, it can be stated that appreciable advantages can be obtained when massive
structures are used.
Ponechal studied the change in operative temperature in both lightweight- and
heavy-structured buildings where a 10 hours shutdown of the convective heating
system was simulated, in response to outdoor temperatures between −4◦C and
+1◦C [95]. The dry bulb temperature fell from the set point of 21◦C to 17.7◦C and
16.3◦C for the heavy and light thermal masses respectively. When a scenario with 35
hours of shutdown was investigated, the temperature drift from the set point was
of 4.5◦C and 6◦C for the two cases. On the other side, after the restart of the heating
system the set point was achieved in a shorter time with a lightweight structure.
Arcuri et al. [96] compared intermittent radiant and convective heating systems
with different thicknesses (from 0 cm to 30 cm) of the heat accumulation layer in
the slabs. It was found that higher thermal mass generally corresponds to an in-
crease in thermal comfort, and that radiant heating responds better when massive
structures are used. Here, the temperature drop after the shutdown of the heating
system is slower and less pronounced with ﬂoor heating, because it can activates
more thermal mass. Also, the mean radiant temperature is always higher than with
radiators, thus beneﬁting the operative temperature perceived by the occupants. In
the same study, it was seen that whilst strong difference arise from increasing from
0 cm to 8 cm the heat accumulation layer, only minor advantages resulted by in-
creasing this layer from 8 cm to 30 cm. Substantial difference in the thermal comfort
with different thermal mass appeared also in the cooling season: overheating was
almost completely eliminated when a heat storing layer of at least 3 cm was used,
while mean air temperature reached a maximum of 32◦C when no thermal mass was
included.
However, it is important to highlight that the effectiveness of thermal mass on
both winter and summer behaviour of a dwelling depends on the whole building
and its installed systems: it is therefore important that the performance of thermal
mass in a building is always contextualised to the characteristics of building itself. In
support of this argument, Gauthier et al. claimed that «whilst the likelihood for more
stable internal temperatures may lead to the assumption that heavyweight buildings
are more thermally comfortable, this will certainly not always be the case and is de-
pendent upon the interactions between the thermal mass and heating/ventilation

54

systems, controls, scheduling and the external environment» [97]. The research car-
ried on by the authors was comparing 26 lightweight and heavyweight buildings
with different characteristics, spread over 5 European countries with different cli-
mates. The survey-based study revealed that heavyweight buildings were perceived
as more comfortable, with the odds of subjects feeling thermally neutral or in com-
fort being 1.5 times higher than for light structures. Out of this study, a direct re-
lationship can be recognised between thermal capacity and comfort sensation. This
link is not dependent on the season, but it has more weight for warmer climates
and was revealed to depend on the ventilation system too. Humid, oceanic climates
(London, Götheborg, Malmö, Halmstad) revealed a minor dependence of thermal
capacity on comfort.
Although only little literature exists on thermal mass beneﬁts during the heat-
ing season, numerous studies investigate its impact of thermal mass on the comfort
during summer conditions [98, 99, 100], especially when coupled with natural night
cooling. Here, the hours of overheating of a building can be strongly reduced or
even eliminated, with an effectiveness which grows proportionally to the day/night
temperature swing. This is becoming more and more important also in heating-
dominated countries, where the tendency of building airtight and over insulated
and the realisation of large glazings are likely to cause a new demand for cooling.
Indeed, the European Directives 2010/31/EU [101] and 2012/27/EU [102] indicated
a proliferation of cooling and mechanical ventilation systems in the European coun-
tries. A well thought use of thermal mass could provide sufﬁcient removal of excess
heat in the warmest months, avoiding overheating without large energy use or with-
out the installation of active cooling devices. In fact, as stated by Dunster out of the
study of the increasing trend for insulation and air tightness and of the increase of
heatwaves due to global warming, in the UK lightweight home will likely need me-
chanical cooling from around 2020, compared to 2060 for heavyweight homes [103].
As a recap of this literature-based research, three main advantages of thermal
mass were identiﬁed for the improvement of thermal comfort:
i) The reduction in the amplitude of internal temperature swings reduces the
Predicted Percentage of Dissatisﬁed because of the lowest internal temperature
change rate;
ii) In the event of sudden external temperature drops, a higher decrement factor
results in a limited decrease of internal temperatures, thus providing better
comfort;
iii) High thermal mass, especially in combination with external insulation, helps
maintaining the internal surface temperature of the walls close to the set point,
thus minimising thermal discomfort for radiant asymmetry.
6.3
Effective use of thermal mass
This last section of the chapter deals with how thermal mass can be achieved, and
to what extent it should be realised. A ﬁrst part, based on the knowledge acquired
through literature review, presents the common means to provide thermal inertia to
the building; a simulation-based study of the most advantageous time lag for resi-
dential buildings will follow.
==PAGE:73==
6.3. Effective use of thermal mass
55
To begin with, two mechanisms exist to passively activate the inertial mecha-
nisms of the building fabric.
i) Convective coupling relies on the variations in the air temperature close to the
surface of the wall to alternate the accumulation and release of heat. As the
external walls are yielding better performances when insulated by outside (cf.
Section 5.1.1, the thermal mass is likely to have a reduced dependency from
the outside air temperature swings, and rather be activated by changes in the
internal conditions;
ii) Radiative coupling works so that changes in the surface temperature govern the
heat storage phenomena in the slab. When radiative coupling takes place, not
only the characteristics of the material of the slab, but also its surface colour
have an impact on the performances of the thermal mass.
Means for actively triggering the thermal mass exist, relying for example on wa-
ter ﬂows in embedded pipes, as in the case of Thermo-Active Building Systems
(TABS), or in electric wiring, as previously mentioned in 6.1. However, as they are
not passive solutions, they fall outside the boundaries and purposes of this thesis,
and will not be explained further.
With these two mechanisms in mind, two ways of employing materials to act as
thermal mass exist, namely direct and diffuse storage.
Direct storage is realised when the thermal mass receives direct solar radiation,
thus being heated on the surface. The increase in surface temperature drives a pene-
tration of heat within the material, where it is stored until the radiative and convec-
tive mechanisms release it back to the room [104].
Diffuse storage relies on a sparse placement of massive elements in the room, that
accumulate heat through both convective and radiative exchanges. Furniture is a
typical example of diffuse storage, and although the heat accumulation is usually
slower, it can be distributed into several elements and proved to be effective in
the increase of the room thermal capacity [105, 106]. As an example, Wolisz et al.
compared, in a simulation-based analysis, an empty, massive-structure room with a
lightweight but heavily furnished one. After a preheating time followed by a switch-
off of the heating system, the two rooms could maintain the thermal comfort for a
similar number of hours [107]. However, relying on furniture to obtain thermal mass
is not advisable as it strongly depends on the subjectivity of the dwellers. It is there-
fore a better strategy to design walls, ceilings and ﬂoors in such a way they can
provide the rooms with the needed thermal capacity.
Entering now the discussion of how the building fabric can be designed so as
to yield the desired inertia to shift the external temperature swings, two main key
concepts can be identiﬁed.
i) As anticipated in Section 6.2, the relationship linking the amount of thermal
mass with the inertia of the building is not linear. Arcuri et al.’s comparison
of 9 different thicknesses of the concrete layer showed that, whilst noticeable
differences in the inertia exist when 1 cm, 3 cm, 5 cm and 8 cm are compared, an
increase between 8 cm and 30 cm does not bring substantial beneﬁts [96]. This
is valid both in heating and cooling regimes, and ﬁnds a further conﬁrmation
in the ﬁndings of Ghoreishi and Ali [108]. A graphical representation of this
relationship is shown in Figure 6.2, where a ﬂoor loop is used for space heating.

It is therefore possible to say that the amount of massive surface, rather than
its volume, is of importance to provide inertia to the building.
ii) As found out by Foteinaki et al. [88], the main contribution to the inertial
behaviour of a building comes from the internal slabs, namely partition walls,
ﬂoors and ceilings. The external walls absorb heat, but they do not release it
back to the indoors: rather, they tend to dissipate it towards the outside.
Bringing together these two concepts, it can be suggested to realise thermal mass
relying on the internal slabs. In fact, these proved to be more effective than the ther-
mal envelope for the heat charge/discharge cycles, and their total surface is gener-
ally larger than that of the external walls.
However, the choice of material and thickness of ﬂoors and ceilings is bound to
structural parameters, and can be inﬂuenced by the presence of ﬂoor heating sys-
tems. Furthermore, ﬂoors can be covered with carpets or furniture, and ceilings are
not exposed to direct sunlight, making them less effective as thermal mass [].
Designing the internal walls for thermal mass can thus be the best option to en-
hance the inertial phenomena of the thermodynamics of the building. They can
behave as both diffuse and direct storage, and have less restrictions in the use of ma-
terial. In addition, their typical thickness of around 10 cm matches rightly with the
above mentioned literature, provided that the construction material has sufﬁcient
heat capacity. Within this constraints, different thermal capacities can be realised by
using different construction materials.

6.5
Conclusions
Although the advantages of thermal mass for the energy use still do not allow gen-
eralisations, its implementation in a model located in Copenhagen proved to allow
energy savings both in the heating and cooling seasons, together with a better indoor
environment. In particular:
- During the cold season, especially with radiant heating systems, the indoor
temperature ﬂuctuations were not substantive and the internal gains repre-
sented only a small fraction of the energy balance. Therefore, the thermal mass
had reduced effect, but still it represents an advantage as it allows loads shift-
ing, a smaller sizing of the systems and a higher efﬁciency in their operation.
- In summertime the heat storage and re-radiation mechanisms are important
features in the performance of a building, provided that night cooling is ap-
plied. If carefully planned and integrated in the building, thermal mass can
avoid the need for an active cooling system. Yet solar shadings are essential in
the avoidance of overheating, and should be implemented in the design.
- Especially when designing for passive heating, thus with large south-facing
windows, thermal mass is of great importance for the temperature control in
the middle seasons. Despite the radiation is not very powerful yet, in this pe-
riod the shadings are not likely to be effective, due to the small solar height
angle. Solar gains could therefore represent a problem, but heavy-weight in-
ternal walls showed to provide a good temperature control.
- The simulations suggested the existence of a correlation between the amount
of thermal mass and the optimal insulation thickness. Thermal transmittance
and thermal capacity should therefore be evaluated jointly with the help of
dynamic simulation tools.
- As it is especially the solar energy gain to activate the heat accumulation in the
building materials, a relation between the thermal mass and the area and/or

Chapter 7
Redmolen: Orientation and
Massing processes
The Redmolen 1.19 is a well suited case study for the comparison of the thermal
performance of a housing unit with different orientations. Its shape is such that the
apartments are planned differently depending on their position: both living rooms
and bedrooms oriented in the four cardinal directions are present. The bathrooms
are always in the internal part of the apartments, thus never directly in contact with
the thermal envelope. In this way the zones exposed to daylight are free to be used
for living areas, but mechanical air extraction becomes unavoidable. The kitchen
is never separated from the living room: the heat gains from the appliances spread
over a large ﬂoor area decreasing their impact on the heat balance.
On the other side, the complex shape of the dwelling makes it complicated to
directly relate to the massing process discussed in Chapter 4, which applies better to
simple geometries.
Especially thanks to the large glazed area, the free energy from solar radiation
can be exploited vastly with an effectiveness that depends on the orientation of the
single zones. On the other side, overheating can cause larger or smaller problems
depending on the exposition of the rooms to the outside.
At ﬁrst, the inﬂuence of the orientation of zones and housing units will be dis-
cussed, addressing both energy efﬁciency and thermal comfort.
Then, a massing analysis of different parts of the building will be described.
7.1
Zones layout
In order to verify the impact of the position of a given type of room on its perfor-
mances and, mostly, on the thermal comfort of the occupants, several zones of the
building with different orientations were compared. The models used for the analy-
sis had venting panes for natural ventilation, as this measure, although not included

amount and rate of heat released by the structure, that changes the response of the
building to the external climate. With heavy internal partitions, during a cold spell
the thermal mass is capable of providing more heat to the zones by convection and
radiation than with a lighter one. Two are the consequences of this fact:
i) less power is required by the heating devices, resulting in a lower energy de-

working hours, bricks 2020 h/y and L/W concrete 2150 h/y.

Section 6.4.2 was observed. Here, for increasing thermal capacity of the internal
walls the need for cooling is larger. The difference between the different cases is
however small, with a percentual increase of 3% only from the lightest to the heaviest
construction material.
The inversion of the tendency is mainly attributable to the larger width of the
internal walls of the Redmolen compared to that of the reference room of Part I. Be-
cause of a combined effect of thermal capacity and conductivity, a portion of the heat
stored in the days with highest free gains cannot be released. As a consequence, the
heat builds up within the thermal mass and hinders the capability of the structure to
reduce and shift the thermal load. Conversely, when cooling is performed the walls
release heat and behave as a further heat load.
This result highlights the difﬁculty of reaching a generalised evaluation of the
beneﬁts of thermal mass, already pointed out in Chapter 6.

Chapter 10
Conclusions
The results on energy use and thermal comfort obtained in the second part of this
thesis showed a good correspondence with the earlier analyses. The Redmolen 1.19,
especially because of its highly passive behaviour, was an interesting and well suited
case study for the application of the ﬁndings of Part I. The highlights from the results
of the last three chapters are here summed up and commented, and can provide gen-
eral suggestions to consider during the early stages of the design of a building.
Massing and orientation processes
The compactness and regularity of the shape, studied by analysing separately the
North and the South wings of the Redmolen 1.19, had a larger inﬂuence on the heat-
ing and cooling demands than the one observed in Section 4.2.2. This difference can
be explained by the difference in shape: the Southern side of the Redmolen 1.19 has
also more complex volumes extruding from the building. Consequently, both heat-
loosing area and lenght of thermal bridges are increased. These factors showed to
have a larger impact on the energy demand than the aspect ratio alone. Two con-
clusions can be drown from this result. On one side, the aspect ratio of a building
is a poor indicator of its future performance: the ratio of envelope area and heated
volume would give more information and predict the energy demand better. On the
other side, when the solar gains represent a large share of the heat balance, smaller
aspect ratios extending on the East-West axis allow to realise larger windows on the
South facade. This would foster the passive heating and reduce the energy use.
However, because of the thick, externally-placed insulation layer, and of the
availability of solar gains, both sides of the building have a very small energy use
for space heating. The Southern wing, because of its more complex shape and the
higher number of edges, has however larger heat losses from thermal bridges, but
this does not prevent this section of the building from having a mostly passive be-
haviour throughout the heating season.
This fact can be generalised by saying that, with a good thermal insulation, the
architectural team can design the shape of a building with many degrees of freedom.
The main focus should be kept, rather than on the compactness of the volumes, on
the availability of solar gains. Shading strategies should be studied and realised so
as to prevent direct exposure to solar radiation in summer without hindering the
passive heating in the cold months. A good example is given by the balconies on
the South-exposed facades of the Redmolen, whose lenght was calculated such as to
shade the glazings underneath only in the summer period. Such a solution, which is
a good example of integrated design, can strongly reduce (in this case, almost halve)
the cooling demand: as cooling, or ventilation, require electric energy (fp=1.8), the
primary energy demand can be largely inﬂuenced by the way the building receives
==PAGE:108==

solar radiation throughout the year.
The way the layout of the building is arranged into housing units, and especially
the orientation that the difference type of rooms have within each apartment, yielded
small differences in thermal comfort but good energy saving potentials. Apart from
the heating demand, strongly reduced when solar gains can be better exploited, the
differences in natural lighting suggest that lighting energy and the distribution of its
usage along the day are inﬂuenced by the solar orientation.
Thermal envelope
Similarly to what noticed in Part I, the position of the insulation layer plays an im-
portant part on the heating energy. External insulation is recommendable, not only
as it yields the smaller demand, but also because it allows to considerably reduce
the size of the heating devices. The advantages are therefore double: on one side,
yearly savings are allowed by the smaller energy use; on the other side, the smaller
design power of the heating units allows reductions in the investment costs.
The thermal transmittance of the external walls is one of the most important fea-
tures in the overall energy balance. Small values are advisable and necessary to
comply with the standards. However, precise calculations should be performed to
avoid over-insulation. From the original insulation thickness of the Redmolen 1.19,
suggested by Ramboll A/S to comply with the regulation, a reduction of 1/3 was
obtained by the Life-Cycle Cost optimisation, suggesting that the LCC method (or
similar alternatives) can strongly improve the life-time economy of a building. The
yearly energy demand was low enough to fulﬁl the energy frame also after the in-
crease in thermal transmittance.
Especially in passive regimes, the windows play a central role in the perfor-
mances of a building. The presence of an optimum size, understood in Part I, was
here conﬁrmed. This size depends on the thermal transmittance of the glazing and
on its solar heat gain coefﬁcient. The suggested approach to follow by the energy
engineering design team would be to select a minimum g-value out of the desired
visual properties of the glazing, then to chose a U-value depending on the fenes-
tration area desired by the architects, so as to reach the minimum energy use. The
combination of optical and thermal properties that yields the best energy perfor-
mance should be targeted However, a compromise between aesthetics (number, size
and position of the windows) and energy performance should be seeked by the de-
sign team. Because of the current tendency to over-insulate and to realise very air-
tight envelopes, summer performances should be considered on a par with winter
behaviour, so as to avoid the risk of shifting the energy use from heating towards
cooling and/or mechanical ventilation.
In this respect, natural ventilation should be included in the design. Carefully
designed venting strategies showed to have a large impact on the thermal comfort.
Because of their potential for reducing overheating, they give more ﬂexibility to the
architectonic design, allowing to increase the fenestration area (beneﬁting, up to a
certain threshold, both heating demand and natural lighting) without compromis-
ing thermal comfort.

Thermal mass
The main advantage of thermal mass was seen, in the Redmolen 1.19, in the im-
provement of thermal comfort in summer. Consistently with the results of Part I,
especially with highly insulated envelopes the thermal mass also reduced heating
energy, maximum heating power and hours of heating.
Overheating was largely reduced when using heavy materials for the internal
walls, showing that the increase of fenestration area to exploit better the solar gains
is acceptable in summer regime if sufﬁcient thermal mass is present. However, in
some critical zones the regulation limit of 25 hours per year above 27◦C could not be
satisﬁed regardless of the thermal mass.
Large differences in the embodied impacts would arise instead from the choice of
the used materials. In the Redmolen 1.19, the beneﬁts of using concrete rather than
other materials would not justify the increase in embodied energy and emissions.
The amount of thermal mass, however, is not a decision depending only on the
calculations of energy engineers. It is also the result of building techniques and
structural calculations. If more sustainable materials could be chosen, the environ-
mental beneﬁt of the alternative should be evaluated against the thermal beneﬁt
allowed by the use of concrete.
