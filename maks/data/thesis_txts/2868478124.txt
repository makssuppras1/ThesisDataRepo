Abstract
This study applied the TAIL rating scheme to evaluate indoor environmental quality (IEQ)
across four oﬃce buildings, focusing on thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, indoor air quality
(IAQ), and luminous comfort. The buildings, varying in architectural design, construction year,
and DGNB certiﬁcation status, included two located on the DTU Lyngby campus (non-certiﬁed)
and two in Metropolitan Copenhagen (DGNB gold-certiﬁed). Both objective measurements and
employee surveys were conducted to capture IEQ conditions and occupant perceptions.
The assessment revealed that all buildings received an overall IEQ classiﬁcation of level IV, with
thermal and luminous comfort consistently demonstrating the poorest performance. Despite
di"erences in building typologies and certiﬁcations, no signiﬁcant variations in overall IEQ
were observed, although speciﬁc components showed sensitivity to performance ﬂuctuations. In
addition to the original TAIL method (Method 2), two alternative methods were introduced
to assess overall IEQ at both building and oﬃce levels. Comparative analysis showed minimal
di"erences, with Method 2 providing greater sensitivity to oﬃce-level variations.
A combined subjective and objective TAIL assessment was conducted to evaluate IEQ
comprehensively. Subjective assessments highlighted discrepancies with objective evaluations,
emphasizing
the
importance
of
integrating
occupant
feedback
for
a
comprehensive
understanding of IEQ. Seasonal comparisons between fall and spring measurements suggested
that
repeated
assessments
across
all
seasons
may
not
be
necessary,
with
targeted
measurements during critical periods recommended. Future research should focus on reﬁning
the TAIL framework by incorporating subjective data and further evaluating the feasibility of
the proposed methods for enhanced IEQ assessment.

Introduction
In contemporary society, the substantial amount of time individuals spend indoors is well-
documented. Survey data reveals that people spend approximately 90% of their time indoors
during the summer, increasing to 96-98% in the winter [1], with a signiﬁcant share spent in
oﬃce environments. A study further reports that 65% employees spend 4 to 8 hours at their
workplace, while 32% spend over 8 hours daily [2].
In environments where people spend such a signiﬁcant amount of time, maintaining optimal
indoor climate conditions is crucial, as it impacts human health, well-being, and, particularly,
productivity and performance in the workplace.
This is ensured through a good indoor
environmental quality (IEQ), deﬁned as ’The quality of a building’s environment in relation to
the health and well-being of its occupants, encompassing aspects of design, analysis, and
operation that contribute to energy-eﬃcient, healthy, and comfortable buildings.’ [1]. IEQ is
comprised of four key domains: thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, indoor air quality (IAQ),
and luminous comfort [3], which will be elaborated upon in the following sections.
1.1
Thermal environment and comfort
In indoor environments, thermal comfort, recognized as the most extensively studied domain [4],
signiﬁcantly inﬂuences occupant satisfaction. ASHRAE Standard 55 deﬁnes thermal comfort
as ’a state of mind that reﬂects satisfaction with the thermal environment, determined through
subjective evaluation’ [5]. Stated di"erently, thermal comfort is experienced when surrounding
temperature aligns with an individual’s thermal preferences. The primary factors inﬂuencing
thermal comfort include metabolic heat production i.e. human physical activity, clothing level,
and environmental parameters such as air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air humidity,
and air velocity. The interaction of these factors can lead to equivalent thermal sensations across
di"erent combinations [6].
To predict overall thermal sensation and discomfort levels, the EN ISO 7730 standard
introduces a method for calculating the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) and the Predicted
Percentage of Dissatisﬁed (PPD) [7]. The PMV index provides an estimated average thermal
sensation perceived by a group of individuals exposed to the same environmental conditions,
utilizing a seven-point thermal sensation scale ranging from -3 (cold) to +3 (hot) with 0 being
neutral.
Based on this index, a quantitative PPD value can be derived, representing the
proportion of occupants experiencing thermal dissatisfaction.
In studies of parameters a"ecting thermal comfort, air temperature is considered the most
inﬂuential due to its direct inﬂuence on human performance [8].
A study emphasized that
increased temperatures lead to thermal discomfort, which subsequently has a negative impact
on performance [9]. Berglund et al. [10] and Niemelä et al. [11] both observed a decrease

in workplace performance when indoor temperatures exceeded 25◦C. Similarly, Lan et al. [12]
examined human performance across three temperature settings: 17◦C, 21◦C, and 28◦C. The
results showed that the performance decreased at both lower and higher temperatures, leading to
greater e"ort by occupants to maintain productivity in thermally uncomfortable environments.
Furthermore, Kakitsuba and White [13] concluded that optimal thermal comfort and sensation
are achieved when air temperatures are higher in the morning and lower in the afternoon, as this
pattern is consistent with the body’s natural circadian rhythm of core temperature regulation,
which rises in the morning and stabilizes later in the day.
Establishing a speciﬁc indoor air temperature range for a thermally comfortable environment
can be challenging, given the inﬂuence of di"erent factors including gender and age. Several
studies indicate that female occupants tend to report lower levels of satisfaction compared to
males in indoor environments, both in terms of overall IEQ and, especially, regarding thermal
environmental conditions. [4], [14].
1.2
Acoustic environment and comfort
The second main domain of IEQ is acoustic comfort, deﬁned as "a state of contentment with
acoustic conditions" [15].
With the growing trend of adopting more open and diverse
environments, such as open-plan oﬃces, an increase in acoustic problems has been observed
[16]. Parameters such as background noise level, clarity of conversation, echo, and acoustic
privacy have a direct impact on occupants’ acoustic comfort [17].
In indoor environments, occupants perceive any undesired sounds as noise [18]. E"ective noise
control in oﬃce spaces is essential, as poor acoustic conditions can result in employee
dissatisfaction and decreased productivity.
The indoor acoustic environment is a"ected by
both external and internal sound sources.
External noise may encompass air and land
transportation,
whereas
internal
noise
typically
includes
human
conversation,
oﬃce
equipment, or other operational machinery [4].
A global survey revealed that 27.9% of 82315 oﬃce workers complain about noise in their
workplace [19]. In particular, speech unrelated to task can contribute to physiological stress
and increased heart rate ﬂuctations [20].
The sound intensity can be determined based on measurements of sound pressure level (SPL)
over short and long periods and frequency. A study demonstrated that noise exposure for 30
minutes had no signiﬁcant e"ect on occupants’ sense of discomfort, whereas after 120 minutes,
a considerable impact was observed [21]. The level of discomfort is a"ected by the intensity
and frequency of the sound. Unexpected or irregular noises, such as nearby conversations or
doorbells, tend to cause greater disruption compared to steady background noise at a constant
frequency [22].

1.3
Indoor Air Quality and comfort
Indoor air quality (IAQ), along with thermal comfort, has a major impact in determining
occupant comfort, well-being, and productivity [21]. These factors are closely linked to overall
health, emphasizing their importance in built environments. Indoor air comprises a mix of
outdoor pollutants inﬁltrating through ventilation systems - natural or mechanical - and
indoor contaminants originating from various sources.
These include emissions speciﬁc to
activities associated with the building’s use as well as general sources such as construction
materials, furnishings, and electronic systems (e.g., cooling and heating systems). The indoor
air is weakened by a wide range of chemical substances such as particulate matter (PM),
volatile organic compounds (VOCs), as well as gases such as carbon monoxide (CO), carbon
dioxide (CO2), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), ozone, and radon, and biological entities like fungi,
bacteria, and pollen [23], [24].
Relative humidity (RH) is also a key factor in deﬁning indoor air quality. Fang et al. and Toftum
et al.
have conducted studies demonstrating that relative humidity signiﬁcantly inﬂuences
occupants’ perception of indoor air quality. While people tend to prefer cool and dry air (for
freshness), this preference can lead to negative e"ects such as irritation and itching of the eyes
and nose [25]. In contrast, high humidity levels increase biological growth such as mold and
bacteria. These organisms cause an increased risk of developing chronic allergies and respiratory
issues, such as asthma [26].
Furthermore, several studies have indicated the potential impact of global warming on the level
of air pollution. Concentrations of pollutants and pollen have demonstrated a parallel increase
with the number of people experiencing allergy symptoms [27]. Furthermore, poor indoor air
quality, can lead to chronic health conditions, referred to as building-related illnesses (BRI).
Among these, Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) emerges as one of the most frequently observed
health issue [21]. SBS is a condition where building occupants experience health symptoms
without a speciﬁc cause or illness, which tend to improve once the occupants leave the building.
These symptoms include throat, eye, and nose irritation, as well as sneezing, headaches, and
dizziness [28].
This highlights the importance of maintaining optimal indoor air quality in occupied
environments, such as oﬃces and classrooms, to reduce health risks, maintain productivity
and overall well-being.
1.4
Luminous environment and comfort
In a visual environment, factors such as glare, artiﬁcial lighting, and the amount of daylight
are inﬂuencing occupant comfort [3]. Numerous studies further emphasize that the lighting
signiﬁcantly impacts the health of occupants in an oﬃce building, as well as their alertness,
attention, and overall mood [29]. Thus, luminous comfort holds signiﬁcant weight as the fourth
domain of indoor environmental quality.

The European Commission has highlighted that buildings account for 40% of the total energy
consumption of the EU [30], while electric lighting accounts for 20-30% of overall electricity
usage in oﬃce buildings [31].
This signiﬁcant share has captured the interest of building
designers, who focus on optimizing daylight utilization to reduce energy consumption and
improve sustainability. Daylight, deﬁned as the natural illumination of indoor environments
delivered through windows, can be determined using a static metric known as the Daylight
Factor given in percent (DF). The factor is calculated as the ratio of indoor illuminance at a

Despite the fact that the daylight is considered the most preferred lighting source among
people [33], Boyce et al. [34] highlight that daylight may cause challenges, such as glare and
distractions. Consequently, the strategic integration of daylight into building designs is crucial
to balance its beneﬁts with the need for optimal luminous performance.
1.5
Expanding on previous research
The four IEQ domains are deﬁned by various parameters as outlined in the previous sections.
These include air temperature, background noise level, RH, CO2, formaldehyde, PM2.5, radon,
DF, and illuminance. Quantitative benchmarks for assessing these IEQ parameters are provided
by international standards, including ASHRAE Standard 55 [5], ISO 7730 [7], and EN 16798-
1 [35].
Speciﬁc threshold values and guidelines for these parameters are established by the
standards, ensuring consistent and reliable evaluations across di"erent building environments.
This master’s project focuses on evaluating these IEQ domains including IEQ parameters within
oﬃce environments. The study builds upon two prior projects conducted during the spring: (1)
’Rating of IEQ in Buildings on DTU Campus Lyngby with the TAIL and Employer Satisfaction
Questionnaire’ by Ida Birk Feldskou and Lina Fiil Præstegaard [36], and (2) ’IEQ in Buildings
at DTU Campus Lyngby’ by Dania Tareq Pharaon [37]. Utilizing a similar methodology and
analytical framework, this project expands the scope to include oﬃce buildings with previously
assessed indoor environmental quality, aiming to perform a comparative analysis with a focus on
seasonal variations and measurement durations. Additionally, the study incorporates di"erent
oﬃce building types located in Metropolitan Copenhagen to broaden the evaluation.

2.1
Introducing TAIL scheme for rating IEQ
Although various standards, guidelines, and certiﬁcation schemes have their approaches and
thresholds, a universally accepted and e"ective method to assess IEQ in buildings has yet to
be established. To address this gap, the TAIL rating scheme was introduced as part of the
energy certiﬁcation method developed by the European ALDREN project [38]. The project,
known as ALliance of Deep RENovation in buildings, was launched in the years from November
2017 to October 2020, with the primary objective of integrating IEQ parameters into energy
performance assessments, demonstrating that renovation can be cost-e"ective in terms of energy
savings.
Additionally, the intention was to support the process of deep energy renovation,
initially in oﬃce buildings and hotels, ensuring that the IEQ of the renovated buildings was
not compromised and met the health and well-being requirements outlined by international
standards and guidelines [38].
The rating scheme involves an assessment of the four main components of IEQ and an overall
IEQ in the building. These four components are Thermal environment, Acoustic environment,
Indoor air quality (IAQ), and Luminous environment, collectively known as TAIL and
graphically illustrated in Figure 2.1.
The quality level of each of these four components is
ranked by color codes: green represents the highest and most desirable level, yellow indicates
a medium and elevated level, orange denotes a moderate level, and ﬁnally, red represents the
lowest and undesirable level.
Once each component has been assigned a quality level, an
overall IEQ level, represented by a Roman numeral in the center of Figure 2.1, can be
determined. Similar to the color coding, the rating is divided into four levels: I represents the
highest and most desired level, II indicates a medium and elevated level, III denotes a
moderate level, and IV represents the lowest and undesirable level. The quality levels of the
four components and the overall IEQ in Roman numeral are consistent with the European
Standard EN 16798-1 [35], which speciﬁes the indoor environmental input parameters for the
design and assessment of the energy performance of buildings.

2.2
TAIL vs. other rating schemes
Various rating schemes have been developed to assess IEQ in buildings. In the following, the
TAIL framework will be compared with the IEQ-Compass and DGNB In-Use (Drift) approaches.
The IEQ-Compass is a tool designed for a holistic assessment of IEQ, developed by Larsen et
al. [45]. Its primary purpose is to emphasize the importance of IEQ, promoting healthier and
more comfortable buildings.
The assessment tool evaluates four equally weighted IEQ
components: thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, IAQ, and visual comfort. Each component
includes four parameters, with the fourth parameter assessing the occupants’ ability for
personal control over IEQ. The parameters within each component are weighted di"erently,
depending on their relative inﬂuence on the overall evaluation of indoor climate and comfort.
This approach di"ers from the TAIL rating scheme: In TAIL, each IEQ component consists of
one or more parameters, and the overall component rating at the oﬃce level is determined by
the lowest achieved level among its parameters.
A major di"erence between the IEQ-Compass and the TAIL rating scheme lies in their
approaches to data collection. While the TAIL assessments are based on objective, real-time
measurements, IEQ-Compass relies exclusively on qualitative observations conducted during
on-site building inspections, supplemented by analyses of blueprints and Building Information
Modeling (BIM) data, when available. This methodology allows assessments to be completed
within approximately three hours. However, these assessments are conducted by a group of
Indoor Environmental Quality in Oﬃce Buildings Using TAIL Rating Scheme
Page 10 of 85
==PAGE:18==
experts relying on their subjective judgments, which can weaken the objectivity of the tool.
Consequently,
the lack of real-time measurements in the IEQ-Compass may limit its
objectivity and precision, raising the question of whether this approach is suﬃcient for
accurately assessing the IEQ of buildings.
DGNB In-Use is a certiﬁcation system developed by the Deutsche Gesellschaft für Nachhaltiges
Bauen (DGNB), designed to evaluate and optimize the performance of existing buildings [46].
Among its three core categories, social quality is assessed through one of its key criteria, indoor
climate (SOC1-B), which accounts for 10% of the total score. The IEQ assessment focuses on
two primary components: thermal comfort and IAQ, which speciﬁcally evaluate temperature,
CO2 concentration, and RH, respectively. Data collection in DGNB In-Use relies on existing
operational data and documentation. Measurement data are collected from both summer and
winter to capture seasonal variations in temperature and humidity. While the primary focus is
on these components, acoustic comfort and visual comfort can be included as supplementary
components, awarding additional points. This ﬂexibility allows for a tailored assessment that
adapts to the speciﬁc requirements of each building.
In comparison to TAIL, DGNB In-Use operates within a shorter time frame as it leverages
existing data to evaluate buildings, whereas TAIL relies on real-time sensor measurements
across all four components (excluding simulated daylight factor), resulting in a longer time
frame. However, this raises questions about the quality and availability of existing data used in
DGNB In-Use. Real-time measurements, as employed by TAIL, may provide a more accurate
representation of the current IEQ in a building. Furthermore, DGNB In-Use evaluates each
IEQ parameter individually, and the ratings are summed to determine the overall IEQ score,
which lacks the strict robustness of TAIL.
The major di"erence between the two rating schemes lies in their cost: TAIL is cost-free,
whereas DGNB In-Use requires a fee. From an economic perspective, TAIL may represent a
more feasible option, particularly since the two schemes demonstrate signiﬁcant similarities,
di"ering only in a few key aspects.

4.3.1
Sensor collocation
In such experimental scenarios, it is crucial to perform cross-calibration of the sensors to ensure
their accuracy and reliability. Signiﬁcant discrepancies could indicate faulty or non-functional
sensors, potentially due to error sources such as a damaged sensor or incorrect calibration.
Ideally, a specialized collocation experiment should be conducted, wherein both measurement
devices and lab-grade instruments are placed in the same environment to generate and measure
various levels of the parameters of interest. The obtained results can then be used to compare
the performance of the measurement devices against the lab-grade instruments as a reference,
allowing for adjustment of the device readings.
In this project, conducting an experimental setup with lab-grade instruments was not completely
possible; therefore, a collocation calibration among the measurement devices was conducted
instead. As the devices had been utilized prior to the measurement period, cross-calibration
was conducted only after the measurements were completed in the buildings. 16 Atmocubes
were placed in the same room, positioned near each other, and measurement were made over
four-day period, during which the parameters temperature, CO2, RH, PM2.5, light, and sound
were systematically varied.
Subsequently, the data for each parameter from all Atmocubes was plotted against the average
value for each measurement interval (1 minute). This approach provided a clear insight into the
di"erences and consistency among the various devices, and identiﬁed Atmocubes that revealed
signiﬁcant deviations. Particularly for temperature and RH, signiﬁcant deviations were observed
and examined further. For sound pressure levels measured in decibels (dB), the logarithmic
mean was calculated and compared to the measurements from individual Atmocubes to verify
the accuracy of the calibration.
Since the measurements obtained from the Atmocubes were not compared with lab-grade
instruments, two HOBO data loggers (model U12-013) were instead collocated in the same
room during the same measurement period as the 16 Atmocubes.
This setup enabled the
comparison of temperature and RH to further investigate signiﬁcant deviations. The primary
purpose of using the HOBO data loggers was to serve as an independent third-party reference
and to facilitate a comparison between di"erent measurement devices.
Following the collocation experiment, the data was collected and analyzed.
For each
parameter, all Atmocubes measurements were plotted against the HOBO data logger.

approach:
two Atmocubes were identiﬁed - one with the highest number of data points
slightly below the HOBO data logger values and one with the highest number of data points
slightly above.
The average value from these two Atmocubes was calculated and used to

Discussion
6.1
Potential sources of errors
IEQ in four oﬃce buildings was assessed utilizing the TAIL rating scheme. This assessment
included the evaluation of the IEQ components thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, IAQ, and
luminous comfort, based on the IEQ parameters incorporated in the TAIL framework. For
this purpose, measurements were conducted over the course of one week in each building. In
such assessments, uncertainties in the results may arise due to potential sources of error in the
measurements, which are evaluated in the following sections.
The Atmocube device was deployed to measure the IEQ parameters of interest in the TAIL
framework. Determining the optimal location of the device created a challenge due to limited
desk space.
Consequently, the devices were placed on a nearby empty table, in the corner
of desks, or occasionally near electronic oﬃce equipment.
Additionally, the devices needed
to be located at a distance to ensure that breathing did not a"ect the measurements. Since
this was not a laboratory experiment conducted in a controlled oﬃce environment, preventing
employees from moving the device or engaging in behaviors that could inﬂuence measurements
was challenging. Such behaviors included opening windows more frequently or being too close to
the device. A preliminary analysis of device placement was conducted prior to the measurement
period. However, further coordination with relevant employees would be recommended. This
would involve selecting a speciﬁc desk in advance of the measurement period and arranging
with the corresponding employee to ensure that space is available for device placement. This
approach would allow the devices to be positioned at an optimal distance from the seated
employee, thereby enhancing measurement accuracy.
Collocation calibration of the 16 devices was conducted after the measurement period due to
the unavailability of the devices. Signiﬁcant parallel o"sets in both temperature and RH were
observed.
This highlights the importance of calibrating devices before the measurement
period to assess whether a factory reset is needed using the Atmocube conﬁguration tool [55].
Furthermore, these ﬁndings raise the question of whether low-cost monitors provide suﬃcient
accuracy for assessing IEQ and their overall suitability for this purpose. A study [56] suggests
that low-cost monitors are particularly e"ective at capturing long-term variations in IEQ
compared to lab-grade instruments.
This emphasizes the value of representativeness over
precision, as measurements conducted over a longer period could more accurately reﬂect IEQ
conditions in contrast to the precision of a single measurement conducted by the lab-grade
instruments.
Nevertheless, these monitors should be regarded as complementary tools that
provide practical and cost-eﬃcient solutions.
Based on these considerations,
future
measurements obtained from low-cost monitors should be systematically validated against
Indoor Environmental Quality in Oﬃce Buildings Using TAIL Rating Scheme
Page 44 of 85
==PAGE:52==
laboratory-grade instruments to enhance the accuracy of the results.
All buildings were assigned an overall quality level of IV, the lowest possible rating. Speciﬁcally,
thermal and luminous comfort were consistently rated at level IV across all buildings, with a
few notable exceptions. Building 115 demonstrated a higher thermal comfort level at III, and
Building 220 achieved a level III rating for luminous comfort. Despite di"erences in the year of
construction and architectural features between Building 115 and the other buildings, the overall
IEQ results remained insigniﬁcant. Interestingly, Building 115, expected to underperform in
thermal comfort due to its older construction, actually outperformed newer buildings. This
discrepancy is likely due to improperly adjusted mechanical ventilation and glass facades that
lead to room overheating.
Furthermore, no signiﬁcant di"erences were observed between non-certiﬁed and certiﬁed
buildings. DGNB In-Use requires recertiﬁcation every three years [57]. Given that Buildings
A1 and A2 were certiﬁed in 2013 and 2019, respectively, they may not accurately reﬂect the
conditions today. This emphasizes the importance of conducting regular and comprehensive
assessments of IEQ to ensure continued occupant comfort and productivity. The TAIL tool
o"ers an e"ective and cost-eﬃcient solution for this purpose, as it can be implemented
without incurring additional costs.
For thermal comfort, incorporating air velocity as an additional parameter may provide a
more comprehensive understanding of the perceived drafts reported by occupants. Regarding
luminous comfort, testing varied illumination ranges across di"erent seasons could help
identify the underlying causes of inadequate lighting conditions.
Additionally, glare was
frequently cited in subjective responses, indicating that glare measurements should be
considered, when suﬃcient time and appropriate equipment are available.
Furthermore,
observations revealed that the CH2O sensors captured data at speciﬁc
concentration levels, and PM2.5 sensors consistently recorded low concentration levels.
To
verify sensor accuracy,
recalibration using the Atmocube conﬁguration tool would be
recommended, along with cross-calibration against a laboratory-grade instrument.
In Building A2, measurements were conducted on two out of four ﬂoors, as carpet replacement
on the remaining ﬂoors resulted in odors that could have inﬂuenced the results. The TAIL
methodology recommends conducting measurements in at least one oﬃce per ﬂoor, raising the
question of whether the collected data adequately represent the overall IEQ of the building
compared to a scenario in which all ﬂoors were assessed. Nevertheless, signiﬁcant variations in
the results are not anticipated, given the uniformity in ﬂoor layout and function.

lower-performing oﬃces. Consequently, the overall IEQ assessment of the building may appear

lower-performing oﬃces, as it evaluates each oﬃce independently. Method 3 follows a similar

for Method 1 to smooth out variations when every oﬃce should ideally provide an optimal
indoor environment?
From a practical perspective, Methods 2 and 3 may o"er greater utility for existing buildings,
as their assessment of individual oﬃces enables the identiﬁcation of targeted improvements,
ultimately enhancing overall IEQ. In contrast, Method 1 may be more appealing to building
owners, as it presents a more favorable representation of IEQ, whereas oﬃce occupants are
likely to prefer Methods 2 and 3 due to their direct exposure to lower-performing spaces. This
consideration introduces the necessity of integrating subjective and objective TAIL assessments
to establish a more comprehensive evaluation of overall IEQ classiﬁcation. Ultimately, it is not
merely the building that is exposed to poor IEQ, but also its occupants, who spend hours within
these environments. To address this perspective, the following section introduces a combined
subjective and objective TAIL assessment, bridging the gap between quantitative metrics and
occupant experiences.

Threshold values for thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, IAQ, and luminous comfort are deﬁned
by standards such as ASHRAE 55, ISO 7730, and EN 16798-1. These thresholds for objective

This

representative of the general population.
Consequently, it invites reﬂection on whether the

of the occupants in that building [58].
Even when IEQ conditions comply with established threshold values, occupants may still
experience discomfort - or conversely, perceive the conditions as acceptable despite objective
measurements exceeding the threshold values. This phenomenon, as observed in the combined
subjective and objective TAIL classiﬁcation in this study, emphasizes the critical importance
of incorporating subjective assessments. Consequently, a subjective population can be deﬁned
to represent the speciﬁc building, ensuring that their preferences and comfort needs are
integrated into IEQ assessments.
These observations do not suggest that threshold values for objective measurements are
inadequate; rather, they highlight the importance of subjective assessments in identifying the
need for localized adjustments within these thresholds.
For instance, a study on oﬃce

perceived IEQ among female occupants, reinforcing the necessity of integrating subjective
assessments as a fundamental component of building IEQ evaluations.
Therefore, accurately deﬁning the occupant group within a building, along with their speciﬁc
needs of comfort, is crucial. Considerations such as gender composition, age distribution, and
other demographic contrasts should be incorporated into the assessment.

From a critical perspective, subjective assessments come with several potential limitations.
Survey responses are often shaped by individual preferences and sensations, sometimes driven
by strong opinions, which can lead bias and result in an inaccurate reﬂection of the actual IEQ.
Moreover, certain IEQ parameters are challenging to sense, emphasizing the importance of

6.4
Further Work
The TAIL methodology proves to be an e"ective tool for assessing IEQ in buildings. One of its
key strengths lies in its ﬂexibility, allowing for the seamless integration of additional parameters
as needed. For example, if occupants frequently report discomfort due to drafts, air velocity
measurements can be incorporated into the thermal comfort assessment. Similarly, if subjective
feedback highlights acoustic issues, such as echo, including reverberation time measurements
can enhance the evaluation of acoustic comfort. In cases where other speciﬁc issues are reported,
relevant parameters can be integrated to identify potential sources of discomfort.
Therefore, it is recommended to conduct subjective assessments as an initial step, thereafter
using the results to inform the selection of additional parameters when performing the objective
assessment. This integrated approach enhances the accuracy of IEQ evaluations while providing
a comprehensive assessment of the indoor environment, capturing both objective measurements
and the lived experiences of building occupants.

7
Conclusion
This study applied the TAIL rating scheme to assess the IEQ in four oﬃce buildings, with a
focus on thermal comfort, acoustic comfort, IAQ, and luminous comfort. Two of the buildings,
without DGNB certiﬁcation, were located on the DTU Lyngby campus, while the remaining
two, with DGNB gold certiﬁcations, were situated in the Metropolitan Copenhagen.
The
buildings di"ered in key characteristics, including variations in construction year, oﬃce layout,
and architectural design. Furthermore, a survey was conducted among employees to capture
their perceptions of IEQ within the buildings.
The assessment results revealed that all buildings received an overall IEQ classiﬁcation of level
IV. Two IEQ components - thermal comfort and luminous comfort - consistently demonstrated
lower performance across all buildings. Thermal comfort was rated at level IV in Buildings
220, A1, and A2, while luminous comfort received the same rating in Buildings 115, A1, and
A2. Despite the diversity in building features and certiﬁcation status, the results revealed no
signiﬁcant variations in overall IEQ performance among the buildings. This highlights that while
overall IEQ may remain una"ected across di"erent building typologies, speciﬁc IEQ components
are generally more sensitive to ﬂuctuations in performance.
In addition to the original TAIL method (Method 2), which determines IEQ classiﬁcation at
the oﬃce level, two alternative methods were introduced: Method 1, which evaluates IEQ at
the building level, and Method 3, which evaluates IEQ at the oﬃce level with incorporation
of additional analytical steps. Comparative analysis of these methods revealed no signiﬁcant
di"erences in overall IEQ classiﬁcation. However, luminous comfort exhibited a higher rating
when assessed using Method 2 in Buildings A1 and A2. This ﬁnding emphasizes that while
Method 1 potentially underestimates lower-performing oﬃces, Method 2 and 3 o"er greater
sensitivity to variations at the oﬃce level, enabling targeted improvements.
Survey responses were utilized to develop two di"erent proposals for subjective TAIL
classiﬁcation, both of which revealed discrepancies when compared to the objective TAIL
classiﬁcation. Proposal 2, when combined with the objective TAIL derived from Method 2,
resulted in a ﬁnal overall IEQ classiﬁcation of level IV for all buildings. Furthermore, thermal
and luminous comfort were consistently rated lower in the objective TAIL across all buildings.
Conversely, IAQ received lower ratings in subjective assessments for all buildings, except for
Building A1, where subjective and objective classiﬁcations aligned. These outcomes highlight
the
importance
of
combining
subjective
and
objective
TAIL
assessments
to
gain
a
comprehensive view of IEQ in buildings. Subjective data captures occupant experiences that
may not align with standardized measurement thresholds.
For Buildings 115 and 220, the TAIL classiﬁcations obtained using Method 1 were compared

with data from a previous study [36]. This study was conducted in the spring season, using
the same methodology but with a measurement duration of 2.5 days. The comparison revealed
poorer luminous comfort in Building 115, while in Building 220, acoustic comfort was poorer for
fall measurements. RH was the only parameter rated lower during spring measurements. These
ﬁndings suggest that repeated measurements across multiple seasons may not be necessary for
all IEQ parameters. Instead, measurement strategies can be optimized by focusing on critical
seasonal periods where environmental conditions are most likely to vary signiﬁcantly.
Future research should expand on these ﬁndings, particularly by integrating subjective
assessments as an integral component of the TAIL framework for comprehensive IEQ
evaluation in the buildings. Additionally, further investigations are recommended to evaluate
the feasibility of the three methods and to explore potential discrepancies between IEQ
ratings at both the building and oﬃce levels.
