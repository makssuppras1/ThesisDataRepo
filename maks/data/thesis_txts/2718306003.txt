Abstract 
 
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the difficulties that Manufacturing Design companies 
face when they try to change initiatives and become more sustainable. My focus is on the net-zero 
transition process, in which I identify, highlight, and discuss current problems associated with a lack 
of knowledge or understanding about how to make such a transition effectively within this specific 
field. With this focus, the aim is to contribute to the practical understanding of how sustainability can 
positively affect a company's value, such as by taking advantage of benefits like access to new 
markets, customer loyalty, supply chain security, and stronger brand value. The need of implementing 
sustainable initiatives in design companies is essential to face the challenge that sustainability 
presents for businesses and how far they want to take sustainability with their internal plans. In this 
case, sustainability covers a wide range of practices from applying new strategies to the company’s 
plans, decarbonizing the supply chain, reducing energy consumption to switching to renewable 
energy sources to strategic management initiatives, that can be adopted to help to achieve the balance 
between environmental sustainability and growth. 
 
Chapter 1 
1.0 Introduction 
 
In 2015, the Paris Agreement was signed by 195 countries with the objective of strengthening 
the global response to climate change and keeping global temperatures from rising 2 degrees Celsius 
above pre-industrial levels by 2100 (UNFCC, 2018). If we want to achieve this goal, innovation is a 
must. The world must switch from using fossil fuels to renewable energy sources, which is necessary. 
Climate change is a global issue that's being discussed more and more in both scientific journals and 
social media platforms. While the agreements made in the Paris Agreement offer hope, it will be a 
large and complex transition. According to (Andriosopoulos & Silvestre, 2017, p. 376), there are 
many uncertainties at the societal, financial, and institutional levels that have created challenges and 
impeded a specific progress to the environmental commitments from design companies. Although 
there is ample scientific evidence available, these uncertainties make it difficult to properly implement 
policies correctly. Through an extensive literature review, this thesis will uncover what strategies 
might be the most effective when transitioning to a more sustainable approach to manufacturing 
design companies. Additionally, it will highlight key areas for improvement in existing manufacturing 
design practices, identify potential obstacles on the part of businesses, and provide businesses with 
alternatives on how they must start their sustainability journey or improve existing plans. The first 
chapter investigates and clarifies the scope of the problem, the thesis objective, the research questions, 
and the motivation to pursue the research topic.  
The second chapter introduces the executed methodology, which includes the research 
approach and design, whilst illustrating the data sources, which include a distinction between 
secondary and primary data. Chapter three provides an extensive literature review, which is broken 
into four segments. To begin, I will cover the concept of sustainability and its theories, including a 
working definition, general concepts, and the reasons for and value-adding factors of sustainable 
development. Secondly, I discuss on how manufacturing design companies can capture the value of 
Sustainability. Thirdly, I will explore possible methods of integrating specific initiatives into 
company plans. Finally, I will understand how companies should go about facilitating this transition. 
In chapter four, the procedure of the executed qualitative research is described and the findings of the 
analyses of general expert interviews and the analysis of the selected literature are stated.  
Lastly, recommendations and discussion are developed, followed by a final conclusion.

1.1 Objective 
The objective of this thesis is to highlight the issues related to the sustainability journey in the 
Manufacturing Design companies and determine under what conditions the transition to net-zero can 
be facilitated. What must be emphasized is that global cooperation is required for the net-zero shift, 
especially as a shared goal with comparable aims would not be equally felt by everyone. Today, many 
industries are moving in the direction of greater sustainability, with more and more firms including 
sustainability techniques at all levels of their organizations, operations, business processes and try to 
implement them (Chofreh & Goni, 2017). The focus of this research project is to better understand 
how Manufacturing Design companies are progressing with their net zero goals and what the main 
obstacles are to achieving them as well as focusing on the main SDGs related to this specific topic. 
To gather data on which methods design companies use to be sustainable and the indicators they have 
met, this study will utilize various methods including interviewing company representatives and 
reading company’s sustainability reports to understand how they interact within their production. By 
comparing the different approaches taken by these companies, it is possible to see which ones are 
closer or further away from their targets. By investigating this topic, I hope to bring some new 
developments to the table. The research will bring the thesis to outline the efforts that should be 
explored at the global level to achieve this desired balance between our needs and preserving the 
future, through the integration of policies that connect the environment, economy, and society. The 
study behind this thesis indicates that a global net-zero transition is essential to stemming the tide of 
climate change and that this will require a concerted effort from design companies as well as all 
sectors of society. 
1.2 Problem Statement 
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), a United Nations organization that 
examines scientific evidence pertaining to climate change, agrees with the idea that emissions from 
population growth, economic development, land usage, and technological and political choices are 
all contributors to an increase in greenhouse gases (IPCC, 2014). This research project will look into 
the biggest challenges manufacturing design companies face when making the move towards 
sustainability and how it can be managed for net-zero operation. The aim is to determine which 
transition plan and management of sustainable practices will prove most successful. The research 
further discusses the need to strive toward a specific environmental and sustainable plans and what 
manufacturing design companies might follow to kick off an important change within their business. 
The reason why the study was carried out is to highlight real problems present in the current 
manufacturing design industry and how to possibly tackle them by applying sustainable plans. The 
research provides an analysis of what sustainability means for the companies involved in the study, 
and how to make sure that these organizations take the right direction and follow specific 
sustainability plans. These organizations are facing real issues daily and what they must understand 
is that developing sustainable plans, would allow them to mitigate environmental damage and ensure 
long-term success. Achieving a sustainable environment and growth is possible if the company 
prioritizes and integrates specific strategies that connect the environment, economy, and society, 
creating an internal business value in the process. Since the term "sustainability" encompasses 
environmental, social, and economic sustainability, this refers to a business' ability to preserve natural 
resources, support a thriving workforce and community, and generate enough revenue to sustain itself 
financially in the long run. McKinsey & Company's research (2022) of 100 design firms found that 
sustainability is a priority concern for CEOs. This was evidenced by the fact that sustainability had 
been integrated into the companies' business practices, there was a formal strategy to handle such

1.3 Greenhouse Gas (GHG)  
Returning to the problem of climate change mentioned bove, in 2015 the Paris Agreement was 
reached. This set the world on a path to begin fighting climate change. Regulations have been put in 
place, investors are putting pressure on businesses, and customers are becoming more aware of their 
impact. Businesses are responding with public commitments to reduce emissions in all aspects of 
their operations. These include a big category of emissions (GHG), including Scope 1 and Scope 2 
emissions, which are direct and indirect emissions respectively (WRI, 2020). Today, however, an 
increasing number of companies are also pledging to reduce their Scope 3 emissions. This includes 
all other indirect emissions from supply chain activities, such as transportation and consumption of 
products. Reducing Scope 3 emissions is essential to mitigating climate change, and businesses that 
make this commitment will be well-positioned for the future.  
The Paris agreement is a legally binding international agreement on climate change. Its main 
goal is to limit the global warming below 1.5 degrees Celsius, compared to the pre-industrial levels 
(UNFCCC, 2015). The GHG Protocol standards are aimed at the private and public sectors, value 
chains, products, cities, and policies. As the world's most widely-used greenhouse gas accounting 
tool, the GHG Protocol has been adopted by many companies and organizations in the previous 3 
years. Achieving the net-zero for Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions is a formidable challenge for many 
companies, especially those who are using energy and working in resource-intensive sectors, such as 
heavy manufacturing industries. For design and manufacturing companies, tackling Scope 3 presents 
an additional layer of complexity, including a difficult carbon-accounting and tracking practices that 
needs to see companies collaborating with their customers, supply networks, and industry groups, and 
of course the difficulty of keeping stakeholders engaged in a complex and continuous change effort.  
Over the last years, many organizations have made efforts to reduce their own emissions, but 
what they often discover is that their direct emissions are mainly generated by their supply chain 
networks. Based on the last report of (CDP, 2019), supply chain emissions are 5.5 times higher than 
operational emissions. This suggests that businesses must work with their suppliers and customers to 
take meaningful action on GHG reduction. By taking a supply chain approach, businesses can engage

1.4 Gap in knowledge 
 
The transition to the net-zero is a daunting task, and many companies are struggling to figure 
out how to effectively and efficiently implement net-zero initiatives. It is important that we shift our 
focus from NDCs to the implementation gap, as stated in the IPCC's Working Group III report for 
policymakers (Ming et al., 2021). The Paris Agreement requires companies to concentrate on the 
conditions necessary for closing these gaps. There are many gaps that exist right now which make it 
difficult for companies to achieve a net zero transition. One such gap is the lack of understanding of 
how to effectively and efficiently implement net-zero initiatives. Another gap is the lack of knowledge 
about the most effective strategies for reducing emissions during production and extraction of raw 
materials for manufacturing organizations. Additionally, there is a lack of consensus on what 
constitutes a 'net-zero' company, and what specific goals and targets should be set in order to achieve 
this status.  
Furthermore, there is a lack of awareness of the benefits of becoming a net-zero company, 
which makes it difficult to convince companies to make the switch. All of these gaps need to be 
addressed in order for companies to successfully achieve a net zero transition. Implementing 
sustainable plans in companies could help reduce emissions so that a jurisdiction can achieve its 
climate targets. This includes policy formulation, which concerns creating climate policies aimed at 
reducing emissions to match adopted climate goals. This policy starts with sustainable infrastructure 
and contains three key pillars to the investment strategy: first, utilizing the opportunities of low-
carbon; climate-resilient infrastructure; secondly, changing finance for enablement of change; thirdly 
and most importantly, getting rid of fossil fuel subsidies while installing a carbon tax, which would 
then increase market motivation for low-carbon investment.  
 
Finally, while it is known how some theories could play out beneficially, research has not yet 
determined how to integrate these theories or the necessary conditions for companies to experience a 
successful transition.

1.5 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 
 
It is important to focus on specific Sustainable Development Goals related to the topic 
discussed in the thesis to understand both the focus of the study and the direction of the thesis. The 
reason why SDG7, SDG 12, and SDG13 are important for manufacturing design companies is that 
they have a responsibility to ensure that the goods they create are produced in an efficient way, 
minimizing their environmental impact.  
The Sustainable Development Goals, birthed at the 2012 United Nations Conference on 
Sustainable Development in Rio de Janeiro, superseded the Millennium Development Goals with the 
intention of fighting global poverty (SDGs, n.d.). The principles behind SDGs align with those set 
forth in the Paris Agreement to establish a uniform standard and practical target for all nations 
concerning carbon emissions. SDGs are important because they provide guidance on how to achieve 
a net zero transition in an efficient and effective way. They also emphasize the importance of 
international cooperation in order to successfully combat climate change. Without these goals, 
companies would be left to their own devices when it comes to figuring out how to achieve a net zero 
transition. Additionally, the SDGs provide a framework for measuring progress toward a net zero 
future (The 17 Goals, 2019). This is important because it allows countries and companies to track 
their emissions and set targets accordingly. Finally, the SDGs raise awareness about the importance 
of becoming a net zero company and the benefits that come along with it.  
 
Figure 2, shows the most relevant SDGs for climate change and the net zero transition that are 
involved in this study are the:  
o SDG 7: Affordable and clean energy 
o SDG 12: Responsible consumption and production 
o SDG 13: Climate action 
Goal 7 is important because as the human population continues to grow, the demand for cheap 
electricity does as well. However, the toxic dependency seen with fossil fuels is creating drastic 
changes in the current climate (UNDP, 2019). The second goal that has a relation to this thesis is goal 
12. This goal states that responsible consumption and production are about using resources in a 
sustainable way. It includes reducing waste and pollution, making products and services more 
environmentally friendly, and using less water, energy, and raw materials. The third goal is number 
13, which states that not just one country is affected by climate change and that action is necessary 
through international cooperation with common policies and regulations to stop irreversible 
consequences.  
This frame of thinking correlates to the underlying theme that is seen in this thesis, which is 
that the net zero transition is a global effort and no country or company can do it alone. Additionally, 
the SDGs provide an important framework for thinking about and addressing the issue of 
sustainability. They are relevant to policymakers because they provide guidance on how to create 
policies that balance the economic, environmental, and social dimensions of sustainability. These 
goals emphasize the importance of international cooperation and stakeholder engagement in 
addressing this complex issue. The recent research made by Deloitte Company has shown that 
companies that have made progress by applying the stated SDGs to their strategies and made the 
switch to net zero have seen improvements in employee morale and engagement, as well as increased 
creativity and innovation (Deloitte, 2019).

1.6 Relevance 
 
The topic under study is related to the current sustainability policies being developed by the 
frontrunners United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), and the 
European Union, that advocates the change. NDCs, or National Determined Contributions, are the 
basis for international emissions reduction and climate change adaptation policies. According to the 
Paris Agreement (UNFCCC, 2015), each signatory country is required to communicate and maintain 
a list of NDCs that it intends to achieve. Parties shall take measures within their own countries to 
achieve the objectives of such contributions. This is relevant because it concerns what governments 
of different countries are doing to help companies of all working fields to reach goals decided on in 
the Paris Agreement. Many policies that have been created, and are currently being worked on by 
various governments, focus on reducing carbon emissions. The research conducted on climate 
change, global warming and ways to renewable energy sources has led to new discoveries without 
which we would be unaware of the severity of human impact on the environment (NASA, 2018). The 
IPCC's projections are constantly being updated as new information arises and develops past findings. 
Additionally, models are always changing since they can only predict the future to a certain extent; 
unforeseen events may take place which would impact these predictions. To make sure that near-zero 
emissions materials and products become mainstream in sectors with a lot of public consumers, such 
as the manufacturing design companies, we need large-scale public procurement commitments. These 
obstacles stand in the way of progress for these manufacturing design companies: complex product 
alterations and industrial process changes, sourcing greener materials is tough, or they have to do 
major transformations to their business practices, organization, and culture.

2.0 Methodology 
 
A systematic literature review was conducted in this thesis to identify current research related 
to this topic and to determine whether previous studies have found any evidence of a correlation 
between the net-zero journey and manufacturing design companies. Systematic literature reviews are 
forms of literature reviews that allow for more comprehensive explorations of given topics by using 
specific methodologies to collect and analyze all relevant research (Watson, 2017). The main aim of 
this thesis is to investigate how design companies can adopt sustainability strategies in their 
operations and meet the goals of net-zero impact. To do this, existing theories and methodologies 
were used to inform the strategies. The research methodology section of the thesis discussed methods 
for gathering data, including primary and secondary sources. Sustainability strategies refer to 
practices and approaches used by design firms that focus on environmental protection, social 
responsibility, and economic efficiency, all important elements for ensuring a company’s longevity. 
Sustainability strategies help to minimize a company’s environmental impact and preserve resources 
for future generations. It is essential that manufacturing design companies take this into consideration, 
especially if looking to remain competitive in the long-term. Through analyzing existing literature 
and conducting interviews with experts operating in the field, the thesis will identify specific practices 
that can be incorporated into sustainability strategies.  
 
When conducting my research on the specific terms  “net-zero transition” and “main barriers 
& sustainability”, “ decarbonization” and “green strategies in manufacturing design industry”, I used 
DTU Findit1, Scopus and Google Scholar to find pertinent information. To ensure that only relevant 
works were included in the final results list, I filtered out all irrelevant material not related to specific 
manufacturing design field. This enabled me to explore further into the issue and identify potential 
models for implementation. By doing so I was able to single out useful sources that could help me 
address the problem at hand. Such a thorough search process has allowed me to gain a deeper 
understanding of zero transition and barriers of sustainability in manufacturing design.

3.1.1 The 3 Pillars of Sustainability 
According to the British industrialist (Elkington, 1997), the concept of sustainability has been 
around since the late 20th century, when it was first introduced by him. He mentioned that businesses 
should aim beyond their own survival to make societies healthier and environments cleaner for 
everyone involved with them this middle ground he called “the triple bottom line” (Gupta, 2020). 
Sustainability was then framed as a multidimensional concept composed by three distinct dimensions: 
economic, environmental, and social; often referred to as the “triple bottom-line (Magon et al. 2018). 
1. Economic development  
Economic development is probably the simplest form of sustainability. The economic 
sustainability of a business is dependent upon it being profitable and producing enough revenues to 
continue operating in the market. The challenge with this form, however, achieving equilibrium 
between making money at any cost versus generating profit that aligns with other elements such as 
environmental protection or social responsibilities has been challenging for many companies who are 
attempting to balance these two priorities without sacrificing one over another else’s needs. There are 
various ways to measure economic sustainability, such as assessing profitability and ability to 
innovate, as well as looking at external factors like overall economic health, market demand, and 
government policies (Vachon & Mao, 2008). Achieving a balance between making money and 
upholding social and environmental responsibilities is challenging but necessary in order to create 
long-term prosperity while protecting our planet and its people. Sustainable economic development 
seeks to do exactly this - finding ways to promote growth without sacrificing other important aspects 
of life. It is an ongoing challenge that requires an integrated approach from businesses and regions if 
we wish to build a more sustainable world for the future.

2. Environmental protection  
Environmental sustainability is crucial in the modern world. It includes reducing our carbon 
footprints, cutting down on water usage, using decomposable packaging, and getting rid of wasteful 
processes in the supply chain journey. Many of these practices are not only cost-effective but also 
good for the environment. There are several ways in which companies can improve their 
environmental and social sustainability. For example, they can invest in renewable energy sources, 
promote green practices within the organisation, and support employees in developing sustainable 
lifestyle habits. Companies can also push for more environmentally responsible behaviour among 
their suppliers and customers. They might, for example, provide discounts to consumers who use 
recycled materials or incentives for choosing environmentally friendly transportation methods. The 
aim is to develop a long-term business strategy that reduces the company’s environmental effect while 
increasing its profit margins. By doing so, businesses can play a key role in protecting our planet and 
its inhabitants for future generations. (Eizenberg & Jabareen, 2017). Active social sustainability is 
also obtained when an organisation actively supports the preservation and creation of skills, as well 
as current or future generations. 
 
3. Social development  
Social development is related to the way companies treat their own employes. Companies that 
succeed in their social development are those with a commitment to ethical and sustainable practices 
within the organization, among its employees and stakeholders, as well as throughout the community. 
This can be accomplished through offering more comprehensive benefits packages for your team's 
wellbeing. Companies often use various approaches to promote social development. Some 
organizations concentrate on providing generous benefits while others prioritize ethical and 
sustainable treatment of their personnel, stakeholders, as well as the local community. Nevertheless, 
regardless of which approach is taken, investing in social development is essential for a company's 
prosperity (Reckmann, 2019). A company must first identify its key stakeholders and assess their 
needs and expectations. It is also important to define what social responsibility means to the company 
and how it can be achieved. Once these objectives are clear, a plan can be put into action. After 
formulating a plan, communication should be a top priority in order to build trust with employees, 
stakeholders, and the community. Companies that prioritize social responsibility not only improve 
their own operations but also make an impact on the world around them. In today’s business 
environment, social responsibility is no longer optional - it is essential.

3.2 International Standards and Frameworks in Design Manufacturing 
Manufacturing design organizations, to manage their environmental impacts have adopted 
different ways to measure the triple bottom line and it is using the ISO (International Organization 
for Standardization, n.d.) certificates. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) is a 
non-governmental organization located in Geneva, Switzerland. This organization work to develop 
consensus-based voluntary technical standards by coordinating the efforts of groups of experts 
representing different national standard institutes. 
 
Organizations seeking to demonstrate their commitment to sustainability can also receive 
certificates such as ISO 26000 which focuses on social responsibility, ISO 14064-3 which 
encompasses greenhouse gas emissions, or ISO 14001 which is an environmental management 
standard that helps businesses in the development and implementation of their environmental 
requirements. These certifications enable companies to demonstrate how they are reducing their 
impact on the environment while contributing positively to society as well. 
 
o ISO 14064-3 is an international standard that provides detailed guidance on the development 
and implementation of a Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Verification and Validation System. This 
system allows organizations to measure, verify, and report their GHG emissions in 
accordance with specific requirements. The standard also outlines how to ensure accuracy in 
the data reported by providing independent third-party verification or validation of GHG 
emissions inventories. The framework is incredibly useful for manufacturing design 
companies because it allows them to accurately measure and report their GHG emissions. 
Additionally, ISO 14064-3 provides organizations with a framework for developing systems 
that allow third-party verification or validation of their inventory reports. These measures 
help create transparency and trust between buyers and suppliers while also ensuring that 
customers can make informed decisions when buying from the organization. Ultimately, 
using ISO 14064-3 helps manufacturing design companies demonstrate their commitment to 
reducing emissions (ISO, 2019). 
 
o ISO 14001 is an environmental management standard that was created by the International 
Organization for Standardization (ISO). It's designed to help businesses in the development 
and implementation of their environmental requirements. The goal of implementing the ISO 
14001 standard in an organization's environmental policy is to improve performance, meet 
compliance obligations and achieve objectives. This norm can be applied to any company, 
regardless of size or industry (ISO, 2019). 
 
o ISO 26000 is the first international standard for social responsibility, and it helps 
organizations measure their compliance with the triple bottom line. ISO 26000 is a guidance 
standard that helps organizations understand and communicate their social responsibility, 
providing them guidance with it. It was developed by ISO, the International Organization for 
Standardization, and guides how organizations can operate in a socially responsible way 
(ISO, 2010.). Ultimately, the goal of ISO 26000 is to promote sustainable development by 
encouraging businesses and organizations to adopt socially responsible practices and is 
intended to apply to all organizations, regardless of size, sector, or location (ISO, 2010).

3.3 The value of Sustainability  
With the business case for sustainability strengthening, an increased number of companies are 
looking to profit from it. But what is the value of sustainability? With the purpose of answering the 
research question: "How manufacturing design companies measure progress in their sustainable 
plans towards the net zero goal?", specific articles were investigated to gain insight on the issue 
surrounding the ways of how companies capture the value of sustainability and develop knowledge 
to answer the overarching research question.  
When discussing the value of sustainability, we must first consider how it helps to meet a 
company's goals outlined in their mission statement or strategic framework. There are as well some 
reasons why companies may not be capturing the full value of sustainability. One reason is that many 
companies view sustainability solely as a cost-saving measure, rather than as an opportunity to create 
value. As a result, they often fail to invest in sustainable practices that could generate long-term 
benefits (McKinsey & Company 2021). Companies should think about the advantages that 
incorporating sustainability into their operations gives them over competitors who have not done so. 
Additionally, design companies should look at how their sustainable plans will benefit the 
communities they are involved in as well as how these initiatives might impact their brand image and 
reputation. Although some businesses only see sustainability as a method for cost-saving, not 
understanding its full potential, other organizations may recognize it as an opportunity to create long-
term economic value (Rentizelas, Tolis, & Tatsiopoulos, 2009, p. 887). The exact value of 
sustainability relies on each individual business's perspective; however, one thing is certain, 
companies are becoming more aware of its importance. As the business case for sustainability 
continues to strengthen, we can expect more and more businesses to strive towards capturing its value.

3.4. Sustainable Business Model

company while simultaneously creating value for society. A sustainable business model considers the

ways. However, one of the most effective methods involves incorporating sustainability into your 
company's core values and mission statement; this strategy emphasizes that sustainability should be

3.4.4 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) 
 
The concept of sustainability is often used interchangeably with corporate social responsibility 
(CSR). However, there is a distinction between the two terms. CSR refers to the voluntary actions 
taken by businesses to address environmental and social issues. Sustainability, on the other hand, 
encompasses all aspects of a business’s operations from its supply chain to its product design with 
the goal of minimising its impact on the environment and society (Morimoto et al., 2013). Corporate 
social responsibility knows as well as (CSR), is the integration of business operations and values, 
whereby companies manage their businesses to be socially and environmentally responsible. CSR 
focuses on the sustainability of a company’s actions and its impact on society, environment, 
customers, suppliers, employees, and other stakeholders. Its goal is to create long-term value for all 
stakeholders by focusing on environmental and social improvements. Given that, integrating external 
engagement through CSR is an important way to build trust with stakeholders while also creating 
shared value that can benefit everyone involved in the process. In other words, companies are 
adopting a more comprehensive approach that includes understanding stakeholder needs as well as 
taking responsibility for their decisions (Bryson J. et al., 2015). CSR is important for manufacturing 
design companies because it promotes a holistic approach to decision-making that takes into account 
the needs of all stakeholders, including customers, employees, suppliers, and the environment. As 
environmental sustainability and corporate social responsibility become more important taking an 
Integrated External Engagement approach helps manufacturing design companies ensure that their 
decisions are based on a strong collaboration between all stakeholders, with each stakeholder’s needs 
==PAGE:29==

their impact on society and the environment but also how they can create shared value by investing 
in green initiatives and ethical labor practices. As a result, these companies can build trust with their 
stakeholders while also driving positive change toward an eco-friendly future (Saeidi, et al. 2015, p.

3.4.5 Integrated External Engagement (IEE) 
The integration of external engagement into everyday business must be a successful move for 
manufacturing design companies that are looking at addressing environmental, social and governance 
issues. This does not mean that they need be philosophical about it or even involve themselves in ethical 
discussions; instead, a more practical approach will enable them to be more focused on recognizing what 
matters most when doing good while also making money and gain value. Manufacturing design companies 
are related to integrated external engagement because it is important for them to build relationships with 
their stakeholders and ensure that they understand their needs and expectations, as well as respond 
accordingly. In addition, engaging in IEE helps manufacturing design companies become more sustainable 
by creating shared value among their stakeholders, managing risks effectively, and improving the 
company's reputation. This involves all forms of communication and interaction between the business and 
its stakeholders, including dialogue, consultation, transparency, and accountability. Furthermore,

3.5.2 Sustainability and Operations Management (SOM) 
 
Sustainable Operations Management (SOM) is a methodology that focuses on the development 
of environmentally sustainable operations and processes. It enables companies to reduce their 
environmental impacts while also improving efficiency, productivity, competitiveness, and 
profitability (Singhal, 2005). SOM involves a comprehensive approach to sustainability that considers 
both the upstream and downstream impact of operations on the environment in order to minimize 
pollutant emissions and waste. The reason why SOM can help organizations solve complex 
sustainability challenges is because it provides a comprehensive framework for managing their 
operations in an environmentally responsible manner. Operations Management is the process of 
managing the daily operations of an organization in order to meet its strategic objectives (Kander, 
Jiborn, Moran, & Wiedmann, 2015, p. 431). 
Through activities such as planning, scheduling and controlling, organizations can utilize 
Sustainable Operations Management (SOM) to address complex sustainability challenges. SOM 
provides a comprehensive framework for companies to identify improvements, develop strategies for 
reducing waste and emissions, monitor their performance over time, and collaborate with stakeholders 
from different parts of the supply chain. By utilizing SOM, organizations can transition towards net 
zero emissions while protecting the environment and reducing their carbon footprint. Additionally, 
operations managers must ensure that the company's day-to-day operations are sustainable, and any 
improvements also consider sustainability. This can be a difficult task, as there may be competing 
objectives like efficiency or cost-savings. Sustainability requires a long-term mindset while 
Operations Management looks to optimize in the short term, but both strive to increase effectiveness 
and efficiency of an organization (Gunasekaran, Irani, & Papadopoulos, 2014, p. 806). By 
incorporating sustainability into Operations Management and other aspects of business, companies 
can reduce their environmental impact while still accomplishing their goals. To move toward a more 
sustainable future, collaboration across industries is key and SOM promotes this effort. Through 
leveraging effective Operations Management practices businesses can effectively combat climate 
change.

3.6.1 Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
 
The design and manufacturing sectors are dynamic, competitive, and currently facing a 
transition to further increase the environmental sustainability and circularity of their products. 
However, determining which sustainability criteria should be prioritized is still debatable. This is due 
to the numerous variables found in design products' life cycles and the unique properties of each item. 
In this context, this chapter explains what a Life Cycle Assessment is.  
Life Cycle Assessment, often known as (LCA), is a method for assessing the environmental 
consequences of a product or service throughout its entire lifecycle, from raw material extraction to 
production, usage, and disposal. Design companies frequently use LCA to assess the sustainability of 
their offerings. The tool comes in both a model that can be imported into an LCA software (for 
experts) and as a spreadsheet document (for non-experts or quick analyses). Users have the ability to 
select diverse impact categories (e.g., global warming, depletion of resources, transportation, etc.) 
and weighting schemes according to what the company prioritizes (Curran, 2016, p. 359). In this way, 
the decision maker can easily compare and prioritize different options, based on different 
sustainability criteria. The tool has been applied to four main types of European furniture (i.e., office 
chairs, sofas, beds, and chest of drawers), considering three different options for each product 
category (i.e., traditional production, use of certified wood and recycled materials). Some sensitivity 
analyses have been also performed to understand how the results change by varying some impact 
categories and weighting schemes, to deliver an accurate calculation on the impact categories 
emissions (Jacquemin, Pontalier, & Sablayrolles, 2012, p. 1028). 
By understanding the potential environmental impacts of their products and services, 
companies can make informed decisions about how to reduce these impacts and in which part of the 
production process or product itself. LCA can also help companies to identify areas where they can 
improve their sustainability performance. For example, a company may find that most of its 
environmental impacts come from the production phase of its product life cycle. As a result, the 
company may decide to focus its efforts on reducing these impacts, through measures such as reduced 
energy use or implementing their production processes. In this way, LCA can be a valuable tool and 
important initiative that design companies must apply to improve their sustainability performance. 
Since LCA is an iterative methodology, to make it work well, companies must require full data 
transparency from their suppliers and manufacturing to greatly help support the credibility of the 
assessment and make it easier to constantly update the data to provide accurate and reliable 
information to support decision-making (Bjørn A. at al., 2020). LCA is a complex methodology that 
requires validation to ensure the credibility of the information reported and exchanged. This process 
can be expensive for companies as well, depending on company size. 
In the framework of the Paris Agreement, the manufacturing design and furniture sectors can 
therefore contribute to the goal of limiting the global warming to 2 degrees Celsius by 2050. However, 
the main problem is that the environmental impacts/benefits coming from the use of wooden products 
are not always easy to quantify. Attention should be paid more to the environmental impacts

Decarbonization can be achieved through a variety of means, including carbon capture and storage

3.7.3 Green Supply-Chain  
Scope 3 emissions make up a large percentage of all emissions from different sectors. 
According to (McKinsey & Company, 2022), companies reported the percentage of Scope 3 
emissions from B2C industries is between 93 and 99 percent. By saying this, we can realize that 
transport-related emissions from the supply chain are a common opportunity to reduce carbon 
footprints. The green supply chain is an essential part of a company’s sustainability plan. It involves 
considering the environmental impacts of each step in their supply chain and working to make them 
more efficient, reduce waste, or use renewable resources (Linton et al., 2007). Green supply-chain 
management does not only refer to transportation but it covers all aspects of the production process. 
This includes reducing, reusing, and recycling raw materials, waste management, energy efficiency, 
renewable energy sources such as solar power, water conservation, and more.  
The manufacturing industry is one of the most intensive users of natural resources plus energy 
and has a large environmental footprint (World Economic Forum, 2021). By introducing green 
practices in the supply chain, manufacturers can reduce their environmental impacts. Companies 
should assess the impact of their current production methods, find ways to increase efficiency, and 
introduce new methods for reducing emissions. This could include optimizing routes for 
transportation, switching to renewable energy sources, or utilizing more efficient packaging processes 
(Ahmad & Rehan, 2022). It is vital to utilize sustainable practices when sourcing materials and 
services from suppliers as part of green supply-chain management. Companies should take into 
account the environmental impact of their products when determining which supplier to use, such as 
those who employ renewable energy sources or offer low environmental impact materials. To 
decarbonize transportation between factories and companies, a comprehensive solution must be 
implemented. This includes utilizing efficient pathways such as direct charging, investments in 
renewables energies, synthetic fuels for transport and other clean energy alternatives where possible. 
If no other options are available, then hydrogen or electro fuels can be used.    
 
To conclude this section, supply-chain decarbonization is a vital element for companies that 
want to remain competitive, gain an advantage over their competition and boost the value of their 
sustainability initiatives. It is not an easy task to implement, however; common challenges such as a 
lack of expertise and resources, a lack of knowledge on how to do it and resistance from stakeholders 
can present difficulties. Nevertheless, investing in green practices and sustainable sourcing can result 
in significantly diminished carbon emissions and improved efficiency by 2050 (Grabinski et al., 
2020). Thus, despite the challenges that accompany its implementation, supply-chain decarbonization 
is key for manufacturers looking to reduce their environmental impact.

3.8 Circular Economy 
 
The circular economy is an alternative to the traditional linear economy, in which we take 
resources, use them to create products, and then dispose of them once they are no longer useful. It’s 
a closed-loop system, in contrast to the traditional linear “take-make-waste” model, where we extract 
resources, make products, use them for a short time, and then dispose of them. Consumers have access 
to products that are built to last, and they can feel good about knowing that their purchase is not 
contributing to environmental degradation. Businesses save money by using fewer virgin materials 
and by reducing waste. And finally, the environment wins because we are no longer over-consuming 
resources and creating mountains of waste (Sillanpää & Ncibi, 2019). There are many benefits to 
moving to a circular economy. By keeping resources in use for longer, companies can reduce the 
reliance on virgin materials, which saves energy and reduces pollution and by recycling and reusing 
materials instead of discarding them, they can save money and reduce waste, showing a clear benefit 
not only for the environment, but also for the businesses. As mentioned before, circular economy in 
sustainability means sustainability through a closed loop system, in which there is no waste or 
emissions.  
This can be done through a variety of methods such as using recycled materials, renewable 
energy or simply using less resources overall (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017, p. 757) 
==PAGE:39==
The Net-Zero Transition 
39 
There are many ways to make the transition to a circular economy, but some common and important 
steps that all design and manufacturing businesses must take are mentioned below: 
 
o Increase the use of recycled materials: Using recycled materials instead of virgin materials 
is one of the easiest ways to move to a circular economy. Not only does it reduce pollution 
and save energy, but it can also be cheaper than using new materials.  
 
o Increase the use of renewable energy: Renewable energy is another key part of a circular 
economy. By using renewable energy, businesses can reduce their reliance on fossil fuels and 
help to combat climate change. 
 
o Use less resources overall: One of the best ways to move to a circular economy is to simply 
use less resources overall. This can be done by increasing efficiency, using alternative 
materials, or simply redesigning products to use less material. Making the transition to a 
circular economy is not easy, but it is essential if we want to create a sustainable future for 
our planet.  
 
In order to transition into a circular economy, it is important that businesses find the right 
strategy that suits their specific needs and goals. However, no matter what approach is taken, there 
are always going to be advantages for the business by making this switch. To put it simply, companies 
can show concern for environmental issues by changing over to a sustainable model which doesn't 
require new resources constantly. By increasing the use of recycled materials, utilizing renewable 
energy sources, and reducing overall resource consumption, businesses can reduce their 
environmental footprint while also saving money in the long run. Such an approach allows companies 
to reap both social and economic benefits as they move towards greater sustainability (Herrero-Luna, 
et al., 2022, p. 65)

production and consumption system, which can be reused or recycled - a concept known as cradle-
to-cradle. Reuse and recycling of these materials helps conserve resources and protect the 
environment, but only if done correctly as improper disposal can lead to contamination and other 
environmental issues. Adhering to best practices for reuse and recycling of industrial grade materials 
ensures minimal impact on our planet. Ideally, production-consumption systems should be designed 
with a sustainable circular economy in mind during product development; however, this is often not 
the case. Talking about the limitations of recycling, some materials and products cannot ultimately 
be recycled due to a lack of waste management infrastructure. Companies must therefore redesign

such items to avoid generating unnecessary waste or consuming energy unnecessarily (Gullino M., 
2017). Benefits from an integrated materials point of view may provide new possibilities for devising 
effective strategies, technologies, and sustainable policies for a functioning circular economy as well 
as new business models (Bocken & Van Bogaert, 2016). 
 
3.8.2 Relation between Sustainable Agile Principles and Blockchain 
The integration of blockchain technology with sustainable initiatives and sustainable agile 
methods can be beneficial in many ways. Blockchain-based applications are well-suited for creating 
transparent, secure, and efficient networks that enable companies to make decisions faster. As 
mentioned before, blockchain provides a decentralized platform through which companies can better 
manage their processes, ensuring accuracy of information as it passes from one party to another 
(United Nations SDGs, 2019). At the same time, the use of agile methodology within organizations 
helps to promote a more adaptive approach to decision making and the design of sustainable 
initiatives. By using agile methods, companies can take advantage of various contextual data points 
to inform decision making, including customer feedback or insights from market research (Shore, 
2019). Blockchain and Agile principles have similar goals in mind, but they differ in the way they 
achieve these objectives. Blockchain technology enables networks of computers to reach a consensus 
on transactions without a central authority or third-party intermediary. On the other hand, Agile 
principles emphasize frequent communication, collaboration, and iterative development cycles that 
allow teams to rapidly respond to changing customer demands (Kurpjuweit, et al., 2019). 
3.8.3 Active performance management 
 
As public awareness of sustainability has increased, so has the demand for sustainable design 
solutions. This has made it necessary for firms to find methods of measuring and enhancing their 
efficiency. One such method is performance management. Active performance management can be 
very crucial in achieving successful implementation and optimization of sustainability plans and net 
zero progress. It enables organizations to observe, evaluate, and adjust their environmental initiatives 
continuously while looking out for new opportunities to grow further. Through the collection and 
analysis of data regarding resource utilization, energy consumption, employee involvement activities, 
supply chain operations procedures, waste administration concerns and more; businesses are able to 
create benchmarks for monitoring transformations over time (Saratun, 2016, p. 84). 
Furthermore, active performance management can be employed to determine the degree of 
success that a company's sustainability initiatives have had in terms of reducing their carbon footprint. 
By inspecting results on an ongoing basis with this system, firms can fine-tune their plans and make 
strides towards attaining net zero emissions. To facilitate sustainable growth going forward, 
organizations must incorporate a more useful and practicable Sustainable Business Model (SBM) into 
their operations. This would benefit them by identifying further advancement opportunities (Heaton, 
2019). Active Performance Management (APM), which may prove more likely to produce lasting 
improvements compared to past techniques, is a new approach to efficiency that can aid industrial 
design companies better comprehend performance and seize permanent gains. This strategy is 
composed of three distinct areas: Design & Operation, Project Management Office (PMO), and 
Governance. Each of these components has its own set of goals and plans to create lasting sustainable 
solutions for businesses (Folan & Browne, 2005, p. 663).

3.9 Managing the Net-Zero Transition 
The focus of this section is to answer the research question: “How can the sourcing strategy 
for a manufacturing design company be improved?” In order to get a better understanding of potential 
problems with these companies' current sourcing strategies, recent scientific reports and articles were 
consulted. A manufacturing design company needs to make sure that they have a sourcing strategy in 
place to ensure they are sourcing the necessary materials and products that are required for their 
designs. This includes sourcing renewable energy technologies as well as sustainable materials and 
packaging. In addition, it is important to focus on local sources whenever possible in order to reduce 
carbon emissions from transportation (Apostu, Panait, & Vasile, 2022, p. 71358). Additionally, it is 
also beneficial for the company to find ways to partner with other companies that share similar values 
and goals when it comes to sustainability, such as large technology firms or wind turbine 
manufacturers. By taking these steps, the manufacturing design company can ensure that its sourcing 
strategy is in line with its sustainability goals (Lin, 2020, p. 723). The study looks into how to maintain 
a competitive edge while transitioning towards net-zero emissions and an efficient, low-carbon 
emission supply chain. Through interviews and existing literature, data was gathered regarding 
potential steps that leaders may need to take as well as activities to support the transition. The analysis 
found that design and manufacturing companies are the primary sources of competitive advantage in 
manufacturing. Nowadays, competition has shifted to the supply chain which is connected with a 
different level of product utilization. As such, there is a three step process for achieving the net-zero 
objective: establishing a baseline, defining a pathway, and overseeing implementation and tracking.   
o Establishing a baseline: 
A company's carbon footprint can be reduced by first determining an accurate baseline of 
emissions from all sources in its value chain. This includes Scope 1 emissions, which come directly 
from the firm's operations and are usually easy to detect at the plant level, as well as Scope 2 
emissions, which are tied to electricity, heat, cooling and steam purchased. Lifecycle analysis or 
input-output analysis can also be used for assessing Scope 3 emissions outside of a firm's own 
operations. After establishing the initial baseline, strategies can then be formulated on how to reduce 
those emissions (McKinsey & Company, 2021). Calculating the expected benefit from such measures 
is important when considering expenses related to offsetting costs and potential risks and regulatory 
adjustments associated with improved ESG practices. Establishing a clear baseline is a necessary first 
step for any business hoping to improve its environmental performance. 
 
o Target definition and program setup:  
 
The target definition and program setup involves setting the desired outcome for a company's 
sourcing strategy. This should include both short-term and long-term goals, such as reducing overall 
emissions or increasing sustainability initiatives throughout the supply chain. Once these goals have 
been set, it is important to develop and implement a program that can help achieve them. This includes 
analyzing the current sourcing strategies of all suppliers, identifying any areas where improvements 
can be made, establishing new supplier partnerships, ensuring compliance with environmental 
regulations and standards, developing strategies for procurement optimization, and continual 
monitoring of progress (McKinsey & Company 2022). Finally, it is important to develop effective 
metrics for evaluating the success of the program. These should include both quantitative and 
qualitative measures that track progress towards goals such as reducing emissions or increasing 
==PAGE:43==
The Net-Zero Transition 
43 
sustainability initiatives. The metrics should be regularly reviewed in order to make any necessary 
changes or adjustments to ensure the program meets its objectives (Kaminski, 2021). 
 
o Implementation and tracking: 
When it comes to putting a new plan into action, design businesses often turn to their various 
departments for help. To monitor progress and ensure successful completion, an initiative-tracking 
system is usually necessary. For instance, factories might focus on decreasing fugitive emissions, 
energy usage and garbage while procurement teams can be tasked with finding renewable energy 
sources and setting decarbonization goals for vendors that they partner with. Research and 
Development should prioritize the development of low-carbon product designs while sales teams 
should strive to advertise these products and lead customers towards reduced-carbon alternatives 
(Pinderb, 2016).

5.0 Discussion 
 
According to the findings of the interviews, the major issues that were identified are the lack 
of commitment to carbon emission reduction across supply chains, the lack of expertise, lack of 
knowledge and to conclude, a resistant mindset. Manufacturing companies that are considering 
implementing sustainability plans require incentives and support in order to make it a feasible 
investment. Unfortunately, this is often not available from the government due to limited resources 
or lack of commitment and an adequate guidance on how best to pursue sustainable practices in 
manufacturing. Without access to the necessary information and resources, it becomes difficult for 
companies to make informed decisions about their operations. As a result, many companies are unable 
to invest in sustainability initiatives despite wanting to do so.  
This lack of incentive policies from the government hinders progress towards achieving a more 
sustainable future. Without access to incentives for sustainability plans, many manufacturers struggle 
to provide adequate resources for these plans or may even choose not to pursue them at all. This limits 
the potential for reducing emissions and promoting sustainable practices within manufacturing 
operations, which could ultimately have negative impacts on the environment. In regards of this, it is 
==PAGE:47==

clear that without adequate incentive policies from the government, manufacturing design companies 
face difficulty implementing sustainability plans and making progress towards a more sustainable 
future. It is essential that governments recognize their role in providing incentives for such initiatives 
in order to facilitate the reduction of carbon emissions and promote a more sustainable future. The 
lack of expertise in the field is another problem highlighted by our interviews, as many are unaware 
of what actions they can take in order to become more sustainable. As such, companies need to invest 
in training their staff and hiring experts who can guide them along this journey. Additionally, there is 
a lack of knowledge and awareness about sustainability among both employees and consumers alike, 
meaning that businesses have an even greater responsibility for educating both parties on the 
importance of sustainable practices. Lastly, a resistant mindset was observed among many companies 
which prevent them from making the necessary changes to become more sustainable. Companies 
must be willing to make necessary sacrifices in order to reduce their carbon emissions, and this 
requires a shift in mindset which is often difficult for many businesses. 
 
5.1 Answering the research problem 
 
The research problem attempted to identify the major issues that hinder progress towards a 
more sustainable future for design manufacturing companies. Through interviews with stakeholders 
and a detailed literature research, it was found that the main problems faced by these companies were 
the absence of assistance from supply chain associates, the complexity of tracing carbon dioxide 
emissions during production and difficulty in committing to sustainability initiatives without help 
from suppliers. Additionally, there is also a challenge in keeping their workforce informed about 
sustainability matters. The lack of incentive policies from government and organizations, lack of 
expertise in this field and lack of knowledge among both employees and consumers alike create 
further obstacles for these companies as they strive to reach a more sustainable future.  
Consequently, if organizations and the government do not provide sufficient incentives or 
support, in addition to increasing education on sustainability topics, design manufacturing companies 
will find it hard to move forward toward a net zero goal, with a more sustainable future without those 
actions in place.

promoting technological innovation, or advocating for policy shifts. Companies are aware of the 
potential barriers to success, but they lack direction on how to confidently overcome these hurdles.

such initiatives. Companies often don’t know where to start when it comes to developing sustainable 
strategies, nor do they understand enough about the technologies available to be able to make 
informed decisions. Design companies may also lack confidence in their ability to measure the 
effectiveness of these efforts, or even how sustainable programs could affect their bottom line. The 
key challenges mentioned above are identified and recommendations in form of corrective actions

relationships and creation of transparency in supply chains through technologies such as blockchain.

factors, drivers and benefits and challenges in a practical context. Therewith, the study assists global 
acting companies within the implementation of Sustaiability strategies through providing a wide list 
of some of the best practices of strategies and critical factors to achieve the net zero goal. With the 
above strategy in mind, companies can make strides towards net zero emissions by focusing on three 
core fronts: reducing their own operational footprints through energy efficiency and renewable energy 
usage, investing in green technologies such as renewable energy sources, and engaging with suppliers 
and customers who are taking steps towards sustainability. With collaboration among these three 
fronts as well as dedicated funding for design companies, we can move closer to achieving a truly 
sustainable future. Only then can we unlock a world of possibilities and make our planet a better place 
for all.
