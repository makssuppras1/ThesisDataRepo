Abstract
Current 3D scene segmentation methods are heavily dependent on manually annotated 3D training
datasets. Such manual annotations are labor-intensive, often lack fine-grained details, and the
low volume of available data restricts models to a fixed set of categories. Consequently, models
trained on this data struggle to recognize object classes beyond the annotated definitions, failing
to generalize to unseen environments. In contrast, abundant datasets exist for 2D, enabling 2D
foundation models to demonstrate strong generalization and impressive zero-shot abilities. While
recent approaches have successfully leveraged these 2D models for 3D supervision, they rely on
RGB-D sequences, effectively restricting training to datasets with aligned depth information.
Addressing these limitations, this work presents an enhanced framework for class-agnostic
3D instance segmentation. The method employs a three-stage training pipeline that utilizes 2D
foundation models to generate high-quality pseudo-labels for partial point clouds. These labels
are then used to supervise a native 3D model, avoiding the noise introduced by heuristic merging
procedures and completely eliminating the need for manual 3D annotations.
A critical innovation introduced here is the removal of the RGB-D dependency in the initial
training stage. By relying solely on RGB images for supervision, the framework unlocks the capa-
bility to train on internet-scale 2D data. Despite these reduced sensor requirements, experiments
on the ScanNet++ dataset demonstrate a performance improvement of at least 6% over baselines
that utilize depth data. A supplementary study further analyzes the scalability and data efficiency
of this RGB-only approach.

1.1
Problem Statement
1. INTRODUCTION
Semantic segmentation is a fundamental computer
vision task that enables the identification of spe-
cific object classes within a scene, serving as a cor-
nerstone for applications ranging from augmented
and virtual reality to robot navigation [1, 2, 3, 4]
and autonomous driving [5].
Building upon this foundation, instance segmen-
tation introduces the complexity of distinguishing
between individual occurrences of the same object
class, a critical requirement for interacting with
cluttered real-world environments. Taking this
concept further, open vocabulary instance segmen-
tation [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11] allows systems to clas-
sify unique object instances into categories and
freeform text not encountered during training, en-
abling generalization beyond predefined training
sets. Which is essential for real world application.
However, open-vocabulary models often struggle
to generate high-quality instance masks.
This
project specifically focuses on class-agnostic 3D
instance segmentation [12, 13, 14, 15]—a tech-
nique that identifies distinct object instances in
3D scenes, regardless of their semantic class or
whether they were encountered during training.
This research aims to complement and enhance re-
cent advances in open-vocabulary 3D recognition,
such as the works of Jiang [6] and Peng [8], by pro-
viding the robust segmentation masks necessary
for comprehensive scene understanding.
1.1. Problem Statement
Current methods for 3D scene understanding typ-
ically focus on semantic [16, 17, 18, 19] or in-
stance segmentation [20, 21, 22] of predefined
classes. This limitation stems primarily from the
scarcity of labeled datasets for 3D applications,
which severely constrains their generalization ca-
pabilities and applicability in real-world scenarios.
In contrast, 2D foundation models, such as
CLIP [23] and SAM [24], demonstrate remarkable
generalization capabilities, largely attributed to
their training on vast image collections. 3D models
have yet to replicate this success, primarily due to
the absence of comparable large-scale 3D datasets.
To bridge this gap, 3D architectures should ideally
learn directly from the extensive repositories of
available 2D imagery [25, 26, 24, 27].
Applying 2D foundation models to 3D scenes is a
logical progression, as a 3D scene can be projected
to a collection of 2D views. This allows for the
leveraging of foundation models to achieve 3D
scene understanding without relying on manually
labeled data.
A
pioneering
attempt
in
this
direction
is
SAM3D [28], which utilizes SAM[24] to gen-
erate 2D labels and projects posed RGB-D im-
ages into 3D space. However, multi-view pro-
jections often contain conflicting information, in-
troducing significant noise during label merging.
Consequently, this has led to a swath of SAM-
based heuristic merging rules for posed RGB-D
data, all attempting to reconcile these inconsistent
views [29, 30, 14, 15, 13].
In addition to data scarcity, inference speed
presents a significant challenge, particularly in
multi-view scenarios where computational de-
mands are high [29, 28, 20, 30].
Segment3D [31] made significant progress in ad-
dressing these issues. By utilizing SAM [24] to
automatically annotate RGB-D frames and project-
ing these predictions into 3D space, the method
introduced a strategy to train a native 3D model
directly on partial point clouds. This approach
offers two distinct advantages: it maintains high
inference speeds by processing native 3D data, and
it eliminates the need for manual labels by lever-
aging SAM. Furthermore, this strategy decouples
the training process from strict multi-view depen-
dencies. By learning directly from partial point
clouds, the model treats each RGB-D frame as an
independent sample, effectively eliminating the
requirement for temporally coherent sequences or
overlapping views during training
However, this approach does not fully resolve
the data scarcity issue. Crucially, the framework
remains dependent on RGB-D data—sequences
paired with hardware-aligned depth scans.
This reliance on physical depth sensors prevents
the model from utilizing standard internet images,
which typically lack depth information.
Con-
sequently, training remains confined to limited,
domain-specific captures rather than diverse, un-
aligned image collections. This thesis proposes
a method to eliminate this hardware dependency.

1.4
Structure of the Paper
By substituting physical depth sensors with state-
of-the-art Monocular Depth Estimation [32] and
introducing Model-Guided Iterative Refinement
(Section 3.4), this work aims to advance the Seg-
ment3D [31] framework. The objective is to en-
hance segmentation mask quality and, fundamen-
tally, to enable the training of native 3D models
on arbitrary, RGB-only image datasets.
1.2. Research Question
This research explores the following question:
Can the reliance on physical depth
sensors in 3D segmentation training
pipelines be eliminated by integrating
foundational depth and segmentation
models, thereby unlocking the capa-
bility to train on RGB datasets?
The objective opens up 3 sub questions:
1. Can monocular metric depth estimation,
match the efficacy of physical sensors for
creating valid training data from single im-
ages?
2. Does a bidirectional, Model-Guided Iter-
ative Refinement process between the 3D
model and the 2D foundation model reduce
the noise inherent in pseudo-labels, outper-
forming the static projection methods used
in current baselines?
3. Does the proposed sensor-less pipeline al-
low for effective training on non-sequential,
unaligned 2D datasets (e.g., ImageNet [25]),
and how does this diversity compare to train-
ing on limited domain-specific scans?
1.3. Contribution
This research advances the field of class-agnostic
3D instance segmentation by:
1. Elimination of RGB-D Dependencies: The
training pipeline[31] is extended to support
RGB-only inputs by replacing physical sen-
sors with monocular metric depth estimation
(Section 3.3.2), enabling the use of arbitrary
2D datasets.
2. Introducing
depth
confidence
(Section
3.3.3):
An uncertainty-aware weighting
scheme is introduced to mitigate noise in
synthetic depth maps, improving the quality
of the partial point clouds used for training.
3. Evaluating Model-Guided Iterative Refine-
ment (Section 4.5): An empirical evalu-
ation of a bidirectional 2D-3D feedback
loop determines that the computational cost
of Model-Guided Iterative Refinement cur-

4. Evaluating internet-scale training (Section

These contributions collectively address the criti-
cal data sparsity bottleneck in 3D vision, provid-
ing a pathway toward foundation models that can
learn 3D geometry directly from standard internet
images.

2. RELATED WORK
2.1. 2D Foundation Models
2D foundation models have revolutionized the
field of AI through extensive pre-training on mil-
lions of images. These models generalize effec-
tively beyond their training distributions, enabling
zero-shot predictions in novel scenarios without
the need for task-specific fine-tuning.
2.1.1. 2D Instance Segmentation
Instance segmentation involves identifying and de-
lineating distinct object instances within the same
semantic category. The field has been significantly
advanced by the Segment Anything Model (SAM)
family [24, 33, 34]. SAM established a new stan-
dard for class-agnostic, zero-shot instance segmen-
tation, trained on 1.1 billion masks it exhibits ex-
ceptional generalization capabilities, while accept-
ing diverse prompts (points, boxes, text). This
research utilizes the original SAM architecture to
generate the required pseudo-labels.
2.1.2. 2D Monocular Depth Estimation
Monocular depth estimation is the task of pre-
dicting a depth map from a single RGB image.
This field is categorized into relative depth estima-
tion [35, 36], which focuses on structural consis-
tency, and metric depth estimation [37, 38, 39, 32],
which predicts absolute real-world distances. Of
particular relevance is the state-of-the-art Depth
Pro [32], which is utilized in this framework to
generate accurate depth maps from standard 2D
imagery.
2.1.3. 2D Open-Vocabulary Tasks
Open-vocabulary recognition refers to the ability
to identify objects based on free-form text descrip-
tions rather than a predefined set of classes. A piv-
otal breakthrough in this domain was CLIP [23],
which achieved remarkable zero-shot classifica-
tion by aligning visual and textual representations
within a high-dimensional feature space. Numer-
ous downstream tasks [40, 41] have since inte-
grated CLIP to enable open-vocabulary capabili-
ties.
2.2. 3D Closed-Vocabulary Instance Segmenta-
tion
While progress in 2D segmentation has been rapid,
3D segmentation has historically lagged behind.
This disparity is primarily driven by the scarcity
of high-quality labeled 3D datasets. Consequently,
most existing approaches remain restricted to
closed-vocabulary settings, detecting only a fixed
set of predefined classes [20, 21, 22], thereby lim-
iting their utility in diverse, real-world environ-
ments.
2.2.1. Mask3D
Mask3D [16] adapts the Transformer-based
paradigm [42], previously used in 2D instance seg-
mentation by Mask2Former [43], to the domain
of 3D point clouds. Departing from traditional
approaches that rely on point-grouping or voting
mechanisms, Mask3D employs learnable instance
queries that are decoded to class-labels and binary
masks.
The architecture utilizes a sparse convolutional U-
Net backbone based on the MinkowskiEngine [44]
to extract a multi-scale hierarchy of features, cap-
turing both coarse semantic context and fine-

grained geometric details. Central to the model
is a set of instance queries, which are initialized
to be spatially distributed across the point cloud.
These queries undergo iterative refinement through
a stack of Transformer decoder layers via two pri-
mary mechanisms:
1. Masked Cross-Attention: This constrains
queries to extract features only from vox-
els located within their currently predicted
mask region, thereby filtering out irrelevant
context.
2. Self-Attention: This allows queries to rea-
son globally and interact with one another,
effectively suppressing duplicate predictions
for the same object.
Finally, a Mask Module operates at each decoding
step, performing a dot product between the refined
queries and high-resolution point features to out-
put the final binary instance masks and semantic
classes C. Making it able to predict a closed set
of |C| classes.
2.3. 3D Open-Vocabulary Instance Segmenta-
tion
To bridge the gap between 2D and 3D capabilities,
recent research has focused on extending 2D open-
vocabulary success into the 3D domain. Prominent
methods [45, 6, 7, 8, 9] typically achieve this by
leveraging CLIP [23], projecting its rich semantic
features from 2D images directly into 3D space to
enable language-driven 3D scene understanding.
However, while these models excel at semantic
recognition, they often struggle to generate pre-
cise, tight bounding boxes or high-fidelity instance
masks. This project addresses this geometric lim-
itation by focusing specifically on class-agnostic
3D instance segmentation.
2.4. 3D Datasets
Standard benchmarks for indoor 3D segmentation
are exemplified by ScanNet [46] and its deriva-
tives. While ScanNetv2 offers 1513 scenes, it
lacks semantic granularity. This is addressed by
ScanNet200 [47], which expands the semantic
categories, whereas the high-fidelity ScanNet++
stands as a rigorous benchmark specifically for
fine-grained segmentation. The present study pri-
marily utilizes ScanNet and ScanNet++ for train-
ing and evaluation. Other notable datasets include
Matterport3D [48] and S3DIS [49] for indoor en-
vironments, and KITTI-360 [50] or nuScenes [51]
for outdoor domains.
Despite these resources, the aggregate number of
diverse 3D scenes remains in the high thousands,
making data scarcity the fundamental bottleneck in
the field. Synthetic generation techniques, such as
Structured3D [52], attempt to address this but in-
evitably introduce domain gaps between synthetic
and real-world environments. Alternatively, ap-
proaches like Agile3D [53] focus on streamlining
the annotation process; however, these methods
remain constrained by the significant shortage of
suitable raw 3D datasets available for annotation.
2.5. Segment3D
Segment3D [31] serves as the architectural back-
bone of the present study. Fundamentally, the
method introduced a novel strategy to train a na-
tive 3D model directly on partial point clouds,
effectively decoupling 3D training from the need
for dense, manually annotated 3D datasets.
The architecture employs a native 3D model based
on Mask3D, see Section 2.2.1. This design enables
the generation of multiple mask proposals, from
which the highest-scoring candidates are selected
to facilitate multi-mask outputs.
The training process begins by utilizing SAM [24]
to generate pseudo-labels for RGB images. These
images are paired with depth information, allow-
ing for their projection into 3D space as partial
point clouds. These structures function as ego-
centric point clouds, spatially constrained by the
camera’s pinhole projection field of view. The 3D
model is pre-trained on these partial point clouds,
resulting in a network highly effective at segment-
ing view-dependent, single-perspective 3D data.
To extend this capability to complete scene ge-
ometry, the pre-trained model is subsequently ap-
plied to full (360◦) point clouds. High-confidence
predictions from this inference step serve as ini-
tialization points for a DBSCAN[54] clustering
algorithm. This clustering step refines the segmen-
tation masks, generating improved labels which

3. METHODOLOGY
3.1. Model Architecture
The present study adopts the Segment3D architec-
ture [31], derived from Mask3D (Section 2.2.1).
While the core design is preserved, the architecture
is adapted for class-agnostic instance segmenta-
tion.
To achieve this, the semantic classification head is
modified to predict a single class (|C| = 1). This
output functions as an objectness score, determin-
ing whether a query represents a valid object or
background. Consequently, the multi-class seman-
tic loss is replaced by an objectness loss Lobj.
3.1.1. Query Refinement and Mask Generation
Consistent with Mask3D, learnable instance
queries are initialized via Farthest Point Sam-
pling (FPS) with Fourier positional embeddings.
These queries undergo iterative refinement through
Transformer decoder layers, attending to point
cloud features and other queries across multiple
scales.
Final binary masks are generated by computing
the similarity between refined query features and
backbone point features. The resulting heatmaps
are passed through a sigmoid function and thresh-
olded at 0.5 to yield the binary mask M.
3.1.2. Training and Loss Function
Training is supervised using lifted SAM-generated
pseudo-labels.
Following Mask3D, bipartite
graph matching establishes correspondences be-
tween unordered predictions and ground truth via
the Hungarian method [55].
A key distinction in this framework is the han-
dling of unassigned queries.
Unlike Mask3D,
which utilizes an auxiliary "no-object" class for
unmatched queries, Segment3D optimizes these
solely through the objectness score. The total loss
L is formulated as:

3.2. Three-Stage Training
The proposed method progresses through three
distinct training stages, illustrated in Figure 1.
Stage 1: Pre-Training on Partial Point Clouds.
Inspired by Segment3D [31], the pipeline begins
by utilizing SAM to generate 2D pseudo-labels.
These are projected into 3D space to create anno-
tated partial point clouds, which serve as the su-
pervision signal for pre-training. This initial phase
yields a native 3D model capable of segmenting
partial point clouds.
Stage 2: Model-Guided Iterative Refinement.
To enhance the model, the second phase devi-
ates from the original framework by employing
a Model-Guided Iterative Refinement loop. The
process operates cyclically: SAM initially queries
the 3D model, after which the 3D model queries
SAM. This recursive interaction progressively im-
proves the pseudo-labels, boosting the model’s
performance on partial point clouds.
Stage 3: Full Point Cloud Fine-Tuning. Finally,
to bridge the domain gap between partial and full
geometry, the method follows Segment3D [31] by
generating predictions on full point clouds. High-
confidence outputs are selected as self-supervision
signals, enabling the model to generalize to full
scene geometry. The ultimate result is a native
3D model capable of processing full point clouds,
achieved entirely without manual annotation.

3.3.2. Depth Estimation
The feasibility of employing estimated depth for
3D training rests on the observation that pixel-wise
alignment between RGB and depth modalities is
often more critical than absolute geometric ac-
curacy. Standard regularization techniques, such
as elastic distortion, demonstrate that 3D models
possess a degree of robustness to, and may even
benefit from, geometric deformations. Conversely,
misalignment between segmentation masks and
depth maps causes severe projection errors, partic-
ularly for distant points where minor offsets result
in significant spatial deviations.
Consequently, Monocular Depth Estimation is
identified as a viable strategy. While this approach
inevitably introduces a geometric bias, it guaran-
tees the necessary alignment for effective projec-
tion. To implement this, the metric depth estima-
tion model Depth Pro [32] is employed. Experi-
ments indicate that relying on model-predicted fo-
cal length yields suboptimal scaling; therefore, this
parameter is explicitly extracted from the camera’s
intrinsic matrix rather than inferred (see Figure 3).
A quantitative evaluation of Monocular Depth Es-
timation versus sensor ground truth is presented in
Section 4.3 Table 3.

3.4. Stage 2: Model-Guided Iterative Refine-

address these limitations, a Model-Guided Itera-

this proposed label refinement process operates
completely independently of inter-image relation-

3.5. Stage 3: Full Pointcloud Fine-Tuning
Stage 3 adopts the self-supervised fine-tuning strat-
egy from Segment3D [31] to bridge the domain
gap between partial pointclouds and full point-
clouds. The fundamental insight enabling this
approach is that the model utilizes a native 3D
architecture, which processes pointclouds indis-
criminately, regardless of whether they are partial
or full. However, to adapt the model to the specific
geometry of full scenes without manual labels, a
self-training process is required.
First, inference is performed on the complete point
cloud to generate candidate masks. To ensure that
only high-quality predictions are utilized as super-
vision, a confidence score c is computed for each
instance. This score is derived from the per-point
heatmap h, calculated as the dot product between
the instance query features Fq and the point-level
backbone features F0 (following Mask3D):
h = Fq · F0
(12)
The mask confidence, denoted as cmask, is defined
as the mean probability of the points where the sig-
moid activation σ(h) exceeds 0.5. Incorporating
the binary is-object classification probability cobj,
the final confidence score is formulated as:
c = cmask · cobj
(13)
Finally, predictions with a score c exceeding
a threshold τc are selected as initial seeds for
DBSCAN[54]. Following the implementation of
Segment3D [31], DBSCAN is applied to spatially
expand these seeds. This refinement closes ge-
ometric gaps caused by occlusions, resulting in
denser and more accurate masks compared to us-
ing raw model predictions alone.
These refined masks subsequently serve as pseudo-
ground truth for fine-tuning. A critical modifica-
tion during this stage is the exclusion of the binary
object classification loss Lobj, leaving only the
mask loss Lmask to be optimized. This constraint
prevents the model from being penalized for valid
objects that were effectively detected but filtered
out by the high-confidence threshold.
The impact of this stage and DBSCAN[54] refine-
ment are discussed in Section 4.7 and the impor-
tance of dataset alignment can be found in Section
4.6.1.

4.1.1. Dataset
All experiments employ either the ScanNet200
or ScanNet++ instance-level datasets, utilizing
official training and validation splits to ensure
reproducibility.
For ScanNet++, two training
scenes (dfac5b38df, 00dd871005) and one vali-

file corruption and GPU memory constraints.
To match the Segment3D baseline, every 25th
frame was sampled from ScanNet, yielding 76320
training and 21372 validation frames. ScanNet++
frames were similarly downsampled to produce
matching frame counts.
Input resolutions differ between datasets: ScanNet
experiments use 640×480 RGB-D frames, while
ScanNet++ experiments use 640×480 RGB and
256×192 depth frames. Since point density varies
per frame, comparisons are aligned by the number
of views rather than total point count. This pre-
vents direct comparison between the two datasets.
However, ScanNet++ likely retains richer seman-
tic information per frame, as higher downsampling
factors reduce inter-frame overlap.
4.1.2. Training Curriculum
The model follows the three-stage training proto-
col outlined previously.
Stage 1: Pre-Training on Partial Point Clouds.
The model trains on pseudo-ground truth partial
point clouds (Section 3.3). Training runs on two
NVIDIA L40 GPUs or two V100-32GB GPUs
for approximately 60 hours, using a learning rate
of 2 × 10−4, a batch size of 16, and 100 instance
queries over 20 epochs.
Stage 2: Model-Guided Iterative Refinement.
The model is fine-tuned on the refined pseudo-
labels generated in Section 3.4. This phase retains
the Stage 1 hyperparameters and proceeds for an
additional 10 epochs, requiring approximately 30
hours.
Stage 3: Full Point Cloud Fine-Tuning. First, the
full point cloud dataset is generated as described
in Section 3.5. The DBSCAN[54] clustering step
runs on the CPU, requiring approximately 80 GB
of RAM for full scenes. Subsequently, the model
is fine-tuned on full ScanNet scenes. This stage
uses a batch size of 8 and a learning rate of 2×10−4
on two NVIDIA A100-80GB GPUs for approxi-
mately 20 hours, consuming approximately 140
GB of VRAM when using a voxel size of 2 cm.
Due to limited computational resources, a modi-
fied Constrained Setup was employed for select
experiments undergoing Stage 3 training. As de-
tailed in the Appendix Section 7.3, this configu-
ration results in an approximate 27% reduction
in performance gain compared to the baseline.
Consequently, results utilizing the Constrained
Setup should be interpreted as relative compar-
isons rather than absolute performance ceilings.
4.1.3. Comparison Metrics
Performance assessment varies by training stage.
Stage 1 models are evaluated on partial point
clouds (Partial) generated from the validation
split. To ensure consistency, these validation point
clouds are constructed using the exact genera-
tion method employed for the training set, includ-
ing the same depth and label sources. Although
the specific validation data changes between par-
tial point clouds experiments based on generation
parameters, these metrics indicate how well the
model captures the training distribution.
To assess final performance, all models are ad-
ditionally evaluated on the ScanNet++ full point
cloud (Full) validation split.
Performance is measured using Average Precision
(AP) at IoU thresholds of 0.25 (AP25) and 0.50
(AP50). Additionally, the mean AP is reported,
averaged over the IoU range [0.5:0.95:0.05]. Vali-
dation is performed at epoch 20 for Stage 1, epoch
10 for Stage 2, and epoch 50 for Stage 3.

4.6.3. Training on ImageNet
Experiments demonstrate that scaling stage 1 train-
ing improves performance, likely due to the ben-

Additionally
modern depth estimation now rivals or surpasses
ground truth sensor accuracy, motivating an exper-

pseudo-labels, estimated depth, and partial point
clouds, as described in Section 3.3.
To leverage ImageNet’s high diversity while main-
taining a comparable performance on ScanNet++,
a mixed training strategy is employed. ImageNet
samples undergo filtering for minimum resolu-
tion (640×460) and maximum depth (<25 meters),
yielding 63356 frames. These are combined with
76320 ScanNet++ frames to form the final training
set.
Due to the unreliability of EXIF data, intrinsics for
ImageNet frames are generated using fixed focal
lengths. This approach normalizes scene scale,
preventing macro photography from degenerating
into meaningless voxels during voxelization while
preserving semantic content. Finally, while the
standard random rotations and translations are ap-
plied, no explicit pose information is utilized due
to the absence of extrinsic data.

5. FUTURE WORK
The research presented in this thesis opens up sev-
eral promising avenues for further investigation,
ranging from data optimization and benchmarking
to architectural advancements for handling seg-
mentation ambiguity.
5.1. Data Quality and Training Refinements
Several strategies could be employed to enhance
the quality of the supervision signals and the train-
ing curriculum.
First, the confidence estimation mechanism could
be extended to incorporate the mask scores pro-
vided by SAM. Currently, these scores are dis-
carded; however, integrating them into the loss
weighting could further suppress low-quality
pseudo-labels and focus learning on reliable in-
stances.
Second, a rigorous comparison between "raw" and
"locally normalized" depth confidence metrics is
required (see Section 3.3.3). This work priori-
tized localized confidence to target object bound-
aries. However, this focus inadvertently excluded
raw confidence metrics, which may offer superior
noise filtering in highly uncertain environments
like ImageNet [25].
Finally, strict adherence to the Segment3D [31]
self-supervision protocol in Stage 3 remains to
be validated. It is probable that training strictly
with the original execution patterns would yield
performance gains, suggesting the current results
do not yet represent the method’s upper bound.
5.2. Scalability and Benchmarking
The primary objective of this thesis was the im-
provement of the training pipeline, evaluated
against rigorous baselines. However, given the
inherent scalability of the approach, future work
should assess its competitiveness against state-of-
the-art non-native 3D segmentation methods. Now
that the pipeline is established, the focus can shift
from ablation to maximization; leveraging larger
2D datasets would determine if this method can
surpass current benchmarks when scaled.

5.3. Architectural Scalability
As the proposed method constitutes a model-
agnostic training curriculum, it is inherently scal-
able regarding architecture. The current backbone
could be replaced with modern Transformer based
models, such as Point Transformer V3 [66]. In-
vestigating the pipeline’s efficacy with these state-
of-the-art architectures would help decouple the
contributions of the training strategy from the un-
derlying model capacity.
5.4. Addressing Segmentation Ambiguity
A fundamental challenge in segmentation is the ar-
bitrary definition of an "object," with datasets vary-
ing between whole entities [47] and constituent
parts [67]. The current use of the MGM, which
merges regions into maximum-size masks, en-
forces a fixed granularity that limits generalization.
Two distinct strategies are proposed to address
this:
Multi-Mask Prediction: Instead of collapsing
SAM pseado-labels, the architecture could be
adapted to generate multiple masks per query, sim-
ilar to SAM [24] or PointSAM [68]. This would
preserve the full scene hierarchy, capturing both
sub-parts and whole objects simultaneously.
Semantic Guidance: Alternatively, ambiguity
could be resolved through semantic guidance in
the pre-training step moving beyond rigid dataset
definitions. A simpler and less effective solution
could be integrating open-vocabulary methods,
such as OpenMask3D [9] or OpenScene [8] in
the inference step, text prompts could dynamically
"steer" the model to select the mask depth that
matches the user’s semantic intent.
6. CONCLUSION
This thesis presented a framework that eliminates
the dependency on physical depth sensors during
the initial pre-training phase. Experimental val-
idation demonstrated that monocular depth esti-
mation serves as a robust alternative, surpassing
the quality of low-resolution sensor data in indoor
environments.
By removing the reliance on RGB-D sensors,
this approach theoretically facilitates training on
internet-scale 2D imagery. To preserve this scala-
bility, a bidirectional Model-Guided Iterative Re-
finement strategy was introduced to consolidate in-
formation across unrelated frames, thereby avoid-
ing the reliance on multi-view consistency found
in other methods. However, adapting to the high
variance of diverse datasets like ImageNet remains
a challenge, necessitating further optimization of
the training process to fully capitalize on this po-
tential.
Most notably,
despite decoupling the Seg-
ment3D [31] pipeline from RGB-D dependencies,
this work established a lower bound performance
improvement of 6% in Average Precision (AP)
compared to depth-dependent baselines. Conse-
quently, this research validates a scalable, data-
efficient path toward generalizable class-agnostic
native 3D segmentation, effectively circumventing
the scarcity of annotated 3D datasets.

7.1. Depth confidence estimation techniques
All methods evaluate confidence by assessing the stability of the output, under the assumption that
high stability corresponds to high confidence.
7.1.1. Method 1: Feature-Based
This method examines the magnitude of variance across different features of the decoder, scaling
them to the output dimension. However, the features exhibited different scales, resulting in incoherent
outputs. Whether due to implementation errors or inherent design limitations, this approach was
discontinued in favor of Method 2, which demonstrated greater potential.
7.1.2. Method 2: Multi-Scale
This method examines stability across scale transformations. This is achieved by passing RGB images
of varying sizes to the model and analyzing the pixel-wise variance of the depth output across all
instances. The output uncertainty is highest around corners, as expected, and increases with depth.
This behavior aligns with a projection-based camera model, where distant objects occupy fewer pixels,
providing the model with less information and thereby affecting depth precision.

7.3. Computational Constraints
To strictly follow the Segment3D [31] protocol for Stage 3, the model requires training for 50 epochs
with an effective batch size of 8 full 3D point clouds. This baseline was successfully reproduced using
two A100-80GB GPUs to maintain the required batch size.
However, due to limited compute availability and cluster prioritization, the experiments for this work
utilized a modified setup comprising two L40 and two A40 GPUs. Consequently, the effective batch
size was reduced to 4. Regarding hyperparameters, it was found that doubling the learning rate while
halving the batch size yielded the best results for this configuration.
