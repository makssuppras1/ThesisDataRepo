Summary 
This project aims to validate a floating wind turbine model converted from the WindIO ontology to 
the HAWC2 file format—an essential step in integrating HAWC2 into an optimization framework. By 
ensuring compatibility, HAWC2’s advanced modeling capabilities can enhance the framework’s 
ability to optimize turbine designs for performance, reliability, and cost efficiency. 
The project focuses on the initial phase of HAWC2 integration by verifying the conversion process 
developed by DTU. Specifically, it ensures that key turbine components—including the rotor-nacelle 
assembly, tower, platform, and mooring systems—are accurately translated into HAWC2 while 
maintaining consistency with their WindIO definitions. To benchmark the validation, the project 
uses the IEA 15MW reference turbine and the UMaine VolturnUS-S reference platform, both widely 
recognized in academic research for their realistic design challenges. 
The primary objective is to confirm that the WindIO converter accurately represents the structural 
and dynamic characteristics of the reference model in HAWC2. This validation is crucial for enabling 
the optimization framework to confidently utilize HAWC2 for complex floating wind turbine systems, 
ultimately supporting more reliable and comprehensive design optimizations to advance sustainable 
energy solutions.

2.3 
Project objective 
This project aims to validate a floating wind turbine model converted from the WindIO ontology to 
the HAWC2 file format—a vital step in integrating HAWC2 into an optimization framework. 
Seamlessly converting and verifying these models is critical because HAWC2, an aero-servo-elastic-
hydro coupled simulation tool, provides advanced capabilities for analyzing the dynamic behavior of 
floating wind turbines. By ensuring compatibility with the optimization framework, HAWC2’s 
detailed modeling capabilities can enhance the framework’s ability to optimize turbine designs for 
performance, reliability, and cost-efficiency. 
The literature review underscores the growing demand for robust optimization frameworks that 
incorporate accurate and detailed modeling tools. HAWC2’s broader integration is currently 
hindered by the lack of an automated and validated conversion tool capable of translating turbine 
definitions from standard ontologies like WindIO. This project addresses that gap by verifying the 
conversion process developed by DTU, ensuring that turbine components—including the rotor-
nacelle assembly, tower, platform, and mooring systems—are accurately translated into HAWC2 
while maintaining consistency with their WindIO definitions. 
The project uses the IEA 15MW turbine [8] and UMaine VolturnUS-S platform [9] as benchmarks 
due to their prominence in academic research and their representation of realistic design 
challenges. By comparing the converted WindIO model to a validated baseline HAWC2 model, 
incremental analyses identify discrepancies, refine the conversion process, and build trust in the 
results. The focus is on verifying that the WindIO converter replicates the structural and dynamic 
characteristics of the reference model accurately. This ensures the optimization framework can 
confidently use HAWC2 for complex floating wind turbine systems, enabling more reliable and 
comprehensive design optimizations to advance sustainable energy solutions.

HAWC2 aeroelastic code and theory 
3.1 
Beam theory 
HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code, version 2), developed by the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU), is a specialized aeroelastic simulation tool primarily used for 
analyzing the dynamics and performance of wind turbines in the time domain. The software 
leverages beam theory to model the structural behavior of turbine blades, towers, and other 
slender structures under dynamic loading conditions. This section provides an overview of the 
theoretical framework for the beam representation in HAWC2. 
3.1.1 Fundamentals of beam theory 
Beam theory is a foundational element in structural mechanics, used to describe the behavior of 
slender, elongated structures under various loading conditions. In HAWC2, the beam elements are 
typically modeled using Timoshenko beam theory. Unlike the simpler Euler-Bernoulli theory, which 
assumes that plane sections remain plane and perpendicular to the neutral axis, Timoshenko 
theory allows for cross-sections to deform due to shear forces. It also accounts for rotational inertia 
which becomes important in dynamic analysis of slender structures. This allows HAWC2 to simulate 
the structural dynamics of wind turbine components (e.g. blades and tower) with greater accuracy 
compared to classical beam theory [12]. 
3.1.2 Beam representation 
In HAWC2, each body (such as a blade or tower section) is further divided into sub-bodies or finite 
beam elements. Within the global multibody model, the sub-bodies are treated Timoshenko beams 
which increases the flexibility of the model because it allows for local deformation while keeping the 
computational effort manageable. However, the deformations within a single sub-body are 
assumed to be small in HAWC2. This allows for the linearization of strain-displacement 
relationships and simplified calculations of deformations and stress, which is convenient from a 
computational perspective. 
Bodies are connected through constraints or joints, which enforce relationships like fixed 
connections or relative rotations. The Timoshenko beam theory governs the behaviour within 
individual sub-bodies, while constraints handle the interactions between the larger bodies 
(aggregated sub-bodies). The result is a good balance between accuracy (due to the inclusion of 
shear deformation and rotational inertia) and computational efficiency without losing global 
nonlinear effects [11]. 
3.2 
Aerodynamic model 
The aerodynamic model in HAWC2 is a framework designed to simulate the forces acting on wind 
turbine rotors. It uses a Blade Element Momentum (BEM) formulation to predict how the rotor 
interacts with the surrounding airflow, enabling accurate performance analysis of wind turbines 
under various conditions. The model simplifies the complex 3D interactions of wind and blades into 
manageable 2D problems by operating at two distinct levels. Each level focuses on a specific aspect 
of the aerodynamic forces, working together to create a comprehensive understanding of rotor 
behavior [11].

3.2.1 Rotor aerodynamics  
At this level, the aerodynamic model focuses on the behavior of the entire rotor and its interaction 
with the airflow. The rotor extracts energy from the wind, causing the airflow to slow down—a 
phenomenon fundamental to wind turbine operation. This process is described using momentum 
theory. Additionally, the presence of the rotor introduces variations in the airflow near it, such as 
axial induction (airflow slowing along its direction) and tangential induction (swirling motion 
imparted by the rotor’s rotation). 
The rotor is divided into concentric annular sections, each representing a blade element. The 
aerodynamic forces on these blade elements are computed independently, reducing a complex 3D 
problem into simpler 2D problems. This approach, based on the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) 
formulation, combines blade element theory (aerodynamic forces on blade elements) with 
momentum theory the relationship between rotor forces and airflow momentum changes. The 
resulting model calculated the axial forces (slowing the wind) and tangential forces (causing rotor 
rotation). 
3.2.2 Airfoil aerodynamics 
This level examines the 2D aerodynamic forces acting on the airfoil sections that make up each 
blade element. Forces are categorized into steady and unsteady components. Steady forces occur 
under stable conditions and include lift (perpendicular to the relative wind) and drag (parallel to the 
relative wind), see Figure 3-1.  
 
Figure 3-1 Aerodynamic loads on a blade section [11] 
In contrast, unsteady forces arise from dynamic changes, such as wind turbulence, blade pitch 
adjustments, or rotor rotation interacting with varying wind conditions.  Unsteady aerodynamics 
require advanced modeling to capture effects like dynamic stall, where there’s a delay in lift and 
drag forces during rapid angle-of-attack changes, and wake interaction, where airflow disturbed by 
one rotor part affects another. These models rely on airfoil-specific data—typically obtained from 
wind tunnel experiments or computational simulations—such as lift and drag coefficients and 
corrections for Reynolds and Mach number effects[12].

3.2.3 Integration of levels 
The two levels of the aerodynamic models are strongly interconnected to predict the forces acting 
on the wind turbine rotor. The first level provides global flow conditions, such as induction factors, 
that describe how the rotor alters the airflow. These conditions serve as inputs for the second level, 
which calculates the aerodynamic forces on individual blade elements based on the localized flow 
conditions. This integrated approach enables HAWC2 to simulate wind turbine performance under a 
wide range of operating conditions with high accuracy. 
3.3 
Hydrodynamics and mooring system 
3.3.1 Hydro element 
HAWC2 models hydrodynamic forces using the hydro element module, which is specifically 
designed for analyzing offshore wind turbine platforms such as monopiles and jackets. The module 
defines structural properties, including mass, stiffness, and damping, in line with the general beam 
modeling framework used in HAWC2. 
Hydrodynamic forces are calculated based on Morison’s equation, making the approach suitable for 
slender structures where the wavelength is significantly larger than the beam dimensions.

When the structure is slender, the force exerted on the structure can be decomposed into 
contribution from fluid inertia and drag. The inertia coefficient depends on the geometry of the 
structure and the added mass effect, typical values range from 1.5 -2.0 for cylindrical members in 
water. Rectangular cross sections often have even larger. The drag coefficient depends on the 
geometry, surface roughness and Reynolds number. A typical value for a smooth circular cylinder 
with relatively low Reynolds number (laminar flow) is 0.8-1.0 but this can vary significantly 
depending on the roughness and flow regime. The Reynold’s number is a dimensionless quantity 
that helps predict the flow behaviour of a fluid, indicating whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.  
As a result, hydro elements can model the hydrodynamics of floating platforms, provided the 
structure remains slender enough for Morison theory to dominate and diffraction effects can be 
neglected. This assumption is further explored in Section 5.4.3. The method offers a 
computationally efficient and relatively simple framework, making it well-suited for integration into 
the WindIO conversion process.

3.3.2 Import WAMIT 
For advanced hydrodynamic analysis, HAWC2 supports the integration of results from external 
potential flow solvers like WAMIT. WAMIT uses boundary-element methods to model complex 
wave-body interactions, focusing on diffraction and radiation effects [9]. These effects are crucial 
for accurately representing hydrodynamic forces on floating structures, as diffraction accounts for 
wave alterations when they encounter large structures, and radiation describes the waves 
generated by oscillating bodies in the water. 
Diffraction theory addresses how waves change when they interact with an obstacle, such as a 
floating platform. When waves hit a structure, some are reflected, and others are diffracted around 
the object, altering the wave field behind it. This effect leads to significant wave forces on large 
structures and is crucial for predicting the dynamic behavior of offshore platforms. Radiation 
theory, in contrast, explains how a moving structure in water radiates waves that propagate away 
from it. As the body oscillates, it displaces fluid, generating new waves. These radiated waves 
contribute to damping effects and energy loss, which are important for understanding how the 
structure responds to wave excitation. The added mass and damping effects due to radiation are 
essential for accurate dynamic simulations of floating platforms. 
WAMIT computes the added mass and damping matrices that account for the inertia of displaced 
water and the energy radiated away by the structure. These effects are integrated into HAWC2, 
significantly improving the accuracy of platform dynamics simulations, especially in complex wave 
environments. This integration helps simulate the real-world behavior of floating wind turbines and 
other offshore platforms under wave conditions. While the WAMIT-based approach provides a more 
detailed representation of hydrodynamic forces, it comes with increased computational demands 
and setup complexity. As a result, the WindIO converter, introduced in Section 4.5, currently 
focuses on simpler Morison theory for representing the hydrodynamics of floaters. Morison's 
equation is less computationally intensive and works well for slender structures, but it does not 
fully capture diffraction and radiation effects. 
Future developments aim to integrate diffraction and radiation effects directly into the WindIO 
converter, allowing for more comprehensive hydrodynamic modeling. This will improve the ability 
to handle more complex scenarios, enhancing the simulation of wave interactions with floating 
structures and improving the design and performance of offshore wind turbines systems. 
3.3.3 Mooring system 
The mooring system module implements the equations of motion of a mooring line element. Via 
the mooring module, a user can specify and define two main components, that univocally define 
a mooring system: the mooring lines themselves (named elasticbar) and a set of constraints 
where the mooring line can be fixed either to the global reference system, to another node of the 
HAWC2 structure, or to another mooring line, to generate more complex mooring geometries [12].

WindIO and optimization frameworks 
4.1 
General 
Optimization frameworks are vital for advancing the design and performance of floating offshore 
wind turbines, as they enable systematic exploration of design parameters to achieve cost-effective 
and reliable solutions. A key requirement for such frameworks is the availability of parametric 
models that can efficiently represent the various components and interactions in an FOWT system. 
This section discusses the importance of parametric models, the role of the WindIO ontology, the 
HAWC2 converter, and the verification process in enabling robust optimization frameworks for 
floating wind energy systems. 
4.2 
Parametric models  
Parametric models are foundational for optimization frameworks as they allow the systematic 
variation of design parameters while maintaining consistency across different simulations. These 
models enable automated workflows, reduce manual intervention, and facilitate the exploration of 
high-dimensional design spaces. For FOWTs, where aerodynamic, hydrodynamic, and structural 
interactions are tightly coupled, parametric models are essential to: 
› 
Streamline the Design Process: By parameterizing key variables, designers can efficiently 
iterate on turbine configurations to evaluate performance under various environmental 
conditions. 
› 
Enable Advanced Optimization Techniques: Parametric models integrate seamlessly with 
gradient-based methods and machine learning algorithms, enabling rapid convergence toward 
optimal designs. 
› 
Support Multidisciplinary Analysis: These models provide a consistent framework for 
integrating aerodynamic, structural, and hydrodynamic analyses, which is critical for 
addressing the complex interactions in FOWTs. 
Despite their importance, developing parametric models for fully coupled aero-elastic-hydro 
systems in floating wind has been challenging due to the complexity and variability of these 
systems. Traditional modeling approaches often lack the flexibility or interoperability required for 
comprehensive optimization studies. 
4.3 
WindIO ontology - standardization  
The offshore wind industry relies on a variety of aeroelastic software tools to model turbine 
behaviour and performance. Each tool incorporates different modelling assumptions, numerical 
methods, and input-output structures, which can lead to discrepancies in results. The ability to 
compare models across these diverse software platforms is crucial for ensuring consistency, 
validating findings, and gaining comprehensive insights into turbine and floater performance. 
To address this need, the WindIO ontology has defined two standardized frameworks for system 
engineering multi-disciplinary analysis and optimization (MDAO) of wind turbines and wind plants. 
These frameworks standardize the inputs and outputs for aeroelastic modelling, enabling a

The WindIO ontology addresses the challenges of creating parametric models by providing a 
standardized framework for defining and exchanging data. The key advantages include ensuring 
that data can be exchanged and interpreted correctly across different software platforms and the 
structured approach simplifies the integration of parametric models into automated optimization 
workflows. 
4.4 
HAWC2 integration 
The Wind Energy with Integrated Servocontrol (WEIS) project, developed by the National 
Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), is an open-source, user-friendly software stack designed to 
enable controls co-design (CCD) optimization of floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs). WEIS 
integrates multiple NREL-developed tools, including the Wind-Plant Integrated System Design and 
Engineering Model (WISDEM), OpenFAST, and ROSCO, to perform multi fidelity co-design of wind 
turbines. [13] 
The primary goal of WEIS is to provide the offshore wind industry and research communities with a 
flexible framework that allows for the concurrent design of both the physical aspects of FOWTs and 
their control systems. This integrated approach aims to capitalize on the inherent coupling between 
system dynamics and controller behavior, leading to more optimized and cost-effective wind 
turbine designs. [14] WEIS operates by utilizing a multi fidelity library of models built on the 
foundations of WISDEM and OpenFAST. Researchers at DTU aim to integrate HAWC2 as a 
compatible software within this framework, as illustrated in Figure 4-1, in addition to OpenFAST. To 
achieve this, the HAWC2 software must first achieve full compatibility with the WindIO ontology.

4.5 
WindIO to HAWC2 conversion 
DTU has recently developed a Python package designed to convert WindIO-defined YAML files into 
various software formats. Currently, the converter primarily supports generating HAWC2 files, with 
plans to extend compatibility to other software formats in the future. As detailed below, the 
package's coordinate system converter is adaptable for use with additional tools. The aim is to 
ensure a reliable translation of turbine (rotor nacelle assembly, tower, platform, and mooring) 
definitions from the WindIO ontology into ready-to-use HAWC2 files, enabling seamless integration 
into optimization frameworks like WEIS. 
One motivation for developing this converter arises from the frequent challenges associated with 
data format conversion, particularly those stemming from errors or misunderstandings in 
coordinate systems and their transformations. In the case of converting WindIO to other formats, a 
significant portion of the complexity lies in the coordinate system conversion process. If each 
format converter independently handles coordinate transformations, it limits the reusability of the 
transformation logic for conversions to other software formats. 
By decoupling coordinate system transformations from individual format converters and handling 
them upstream, the transformation can be reused across multiple tools, even if they share the 
same coordinate system but differ in data structures. This approach not only improves efficiency 
but also makes the coordinate transformation process more explicit. Consequently, format 
converters can focus solely on mapping WindIO entries to the required input/output format 
simplifying their design and implementation [15]. The converter leverages the AESOpt base classes 
to facilitate the translation of wind turbine simulation data into the HAWC2 format. AESOpt, a 
library developed by DTU provides a structured framework for handling various data formats in 
wind energy simulations [15]. The converter inherits from these clases, allowing it to standardize 
data representation and manipulation. This promotes code reusability and maintainability, as the 
converter leverages existing functionality provided by AESOpt. 
4.6 
Validation and verification 
The WindIO converter represents a significant advancement in automating the translation of 
WindIO ontology into HAWC2 files. By streamlining the conversion process, it saves time, reduces 
manual effort, and minimizes potential human errors during model creation. However, ensuring its 
practical applicability and reliability requires a robust verification process. The validation of the 
Baseline model, manually created for HAWC2, is not part of this project, as it has already been 
established. Instead, this project focuses on verifying the converted HAWC2 models against the 
validated reference model. The goal is to ensure that the models generated by the WindIO 
converter accurately replicate the structural and dynamic characteristics of the turbine and floater 
defined in the reference model. Any discrepancies in the converted model could lead to errors in 
analyses, affecting the integrity of subsequent simulations and design decisions. 
The verification process highlights areas where the converter might fall short, such as missing 
parameters or inaccuracies in the translation process. These insights are critical for iterative 
improvements and future feature expansion. A verified and reliable tool will be more readily 
adopted by the wind energy community, supporting the automation of workflows in turbine and 
floater modeling. By focusing on verification rather than re-validation, this project assesses the 
performance of the WindIO converter and contributes to its iterative development. A thorough 
verification process ensures the tool meets the high standards required for advanced wind turbine 
modeling and sets a strong foundation for its broader adoption in the field.

5.4.2 Baseline model 
The Baseline model was cloned directly from the GitHub repository [16], which developed the 
HAWC2 model as part of the IEA Wind Task 37 initiative. All necessary files to create a functional 
HAWC2 model are included. However, the UMaine report [9] references the OpenFAST software, 
which necessitated a range of additional modeling decisions to replicate the same response in the 
HAWC2 model. Unfortunatley, these decisions are not fully documented in the GitHub repository.  
One notable discrepancy is that the tower mass in the HAWC2 model is larger than what is 
specified in the UMaine report [9] (see Section 6.2.3 for details). Since this difference is relatively 
minor in its contribution to the global model, further investigation is considered beyond the scope 
of this project. Despite this difference, the Baseline model is considered validated and is used as 
the reference for this project. 
 
 
 
Figure 5-5: Baseline model at max thrust condition 
The following modeling decisions have been made for this project: 
› 
Blade properties: The blade is modeled using the original beam model, and the fully populated 
matrix from the blade element cross-sectional aerodynamic properties is not utilized. 
› 
Wind properties: A steady, uniform wind is applied, without incorporating wind shear or 
turbulence effects. 
› 
Tower shadow method: The "potential_2" method is used to model tower shadow effects, 
where the shadow source moves and rotates with the tower coordinate system [12]. 
› 
Aerodynamic rotor specifications: Normal blade element momentum (BEM) theory with 
dynamic induction is employed. The default tip loss correction method (Prandtl) is applied. The 
MHH Beddoes dynamic stall model is used for capturing unsteady aerodynamic effects [12]. 
› 
Hydrodynamic properties: Water density is assumed to be 1025 kg/m³, and gravity is set at 
9.8066 m/s². 
Unless otherwise stated in Section 5.4.3, the WindIO model utilizies the same modelling decisions.

Verification of the WindIO conversion   
6.1 
General 
Aero-servo-elastic-hydro coupled models, such as those used in wind turbine modeling with 
HAWC2, are inherently complex due to the interplay between aerodynamic, structural, and 
hydrodynamic forces. To manage this complexity, a stepwise approach was adopted to 
systematically reveal and address uncertainties. 
Section 6.2: The investigation began with analyzing the structural properties of each system 
component.  
Steady-state simulations were conducted in the next steps under simplified conditions—neglecting 
servo control and using a uniform wind field without shear or turbulence. 
Section 6.3: This step involved fixing the first tower top node in space to isolate the rotors aero-
elastic behavior. Initially, stiff blades were used to focus solely on the aerodynamic properties, 
which are critical to the model’s accuracy. Blade flexibility was then reintroduced to study 
aeroelastic interactions.  
Section 6.4: The next step fixed the tower bottom in space, allowing the tower’s structural 
dynamics to contribute to the simulation while using flexible blades, as the rotor dynamics had 
already been investigated. 
Section 6.5: In the final step, no constraints are added to evaluate the floating system in its 
entirety. This allowed for the assessment of interactions among all components in the coupled 
system. 
This methodology provided a structured framework for analyzing each component’s influence on 
the coupled system, facilitating a clearer understanding of the overall performance when 
comparing the WindIO model and the Baseline model.

overall aerodynamic performance at this blade position is generally similar between the two models 
as shown by the magnitudes on the y-axis. However, the WindIO model exhibits an amplification 
which is out of phase from what is observed in the Baseline model.

Figure 6-8: stiff blade - aerodynamic force from 180-200sec 
The observed difference in aerodynamic output may be physically accurate, reflecting variations in 
the two modeling approaches. However, this was not considered important for the purpose of this 
project and not investigated further. To provide a broader perspective, the mean values of selected 
rotor signals from 125–200 seconds were plotted in  Figure 6-9. It can be seen that the

6.5 
Floating system 
The final step in verifying the WindIO model involves removing external constraints to evaluate the 
floating system. As discussed in Section 5.4.3, the hydro elements representing the floater in the 
WindIO model are based solely on Morison theory and the pontoons cross-section are modeled as 
cylinders rather than rectangles. The Baseline model incorporates more complex hydrodynamic 
information from potential theory (WAMIT). This section aims to assess the current performance of 
the floater and identify key areas for improvement in the implementation of hydrodynamic 
modeling within the WindIO converter. 
6.5.1 System equilibrium 
As an initial investigation, the system equilibrium was evaluated without environmental loads, 
revealing several issues. The WindIO converter exhibited an error in the sign convention for the X 
and Y end positions in the mooring line definitions. Further, the mooring line fairlead positions or 
connection points on the floater are located approximately 2m within the outer surface of the radial 
column rather than on the surface as on the Baseline model. This discrepancy should have a minor 
impact on a chain line with 850m unstretched length. More importantly, the axial stiffness and 
hydrodynamic coefficients of the mooring lines generated by the WindIO converter were found to 
be significantly larger than those in the Baseline model. This discrepancy was traced to the YAML 
input file rather than the converter itself. For this project, the WindIO model's mooring system was 
adjusted to allow meaningful comparisons of other floater characteristics. The sign convention for 
the end line positions was corrected, the axial stiffness and hydrodynamic coefficients were aligned 
with the Baseline model.

6.5.3 Steady state wind 
Time-domain simulations were conducted under steady wind conditions to investigate the total 
system response. In the maximum thrust case, illustrated in Figure 6-17, the floater response 
reveals key insights that warrant further discussion. Several sources of damping are absent in the 
WindIO model, which is evident in the observed time series. 
First, the viscous damping is underestimated due to the pontoons being modeled as circular 
members, as discussed in Section 6.5.2. Additionally, radiation damping—representing the energy 
lost to radiated waves—is not captured by the WindIO model, which relies solely on Morison theory. 
For this floater with relatively slender members and simple geometries, it is plausible that the 
difference in viscous damping is the main contributor here. As observed in Figure 6-17, the 
Baseline heave oscillations are damped within three cycles whereas the WindIO model has a small 
damping ratio such that oscillations do not significantly reduce in magnitude over time. Focusing on 
the heave again, the WindIO pontoon was modelled with a diameter of 9.61m and a drag 
coefficient of 0.8, see Appendix C. Referring to Table E-1 of DNV-RP-C205 [17] indicates that the 
rectangular pontoon would have a Cd closer to 2.5. Therefore, the WindIO model is going to 
drastically underrepresent the amount of viscous damping in the heave direction. With more time, 
it would have been interesting to test a larger Cd on the WindIO model to see if the discrepancy in 
damping was purely viscous or it also had a component of that captured by potential theory. 
Furthermore, a noticeable difference in surge motion suggests additional work is needed to verify 
the accuracy of the mooring system conversion. Since this simulation excludes waves and currents, 
the aerodynamic thrust is the only external load acting on the floater. Figure 6-18 confirms that 
both models are subject to identical external forces. However, differences in added mass and 
damping lead to varying dynamic behaviors. Despite these differences, both models should 
converge on the same surge offset if their mooring systems were comparable. Encouragingly, the 
models demonstrate strong alignment regarding pitch behavior, driven by the overturning moment 
from the aerodynamic thrust, which verifies several hydrostatic characteristics of the floater. 
From Section 6.5.1, it is timely to note that both models achieve an equilibrium pitch of -1.43 
degrees without environmental loading. Under the applied loading scenario, the aerodynamic thrust 
generates an overturning moment, resulting in a pitch of approximately 5.8 degrees. This outcome 
aligns with quasi-static hydrostatic calculations for this loading.

7 
Conclusion 
7.1 
General 
This project successfully investigates the conversion of a floating wind turbine model from a 
WindIO ontology to the HAWC2 file format. By employing a stepwise verification approach, this 
report considers the alignment of structural, aerodynamic, and hydrodynamic characteristics 
between the manually created Baseline model and the automatically generated WindIO model.  
Key outcomes include: 
› 
Structural and Aerodynamic Properties: The blades aerodynamic properties were verified but 
discrepancies in the blade structural properties were identified. The WindIO converter must be 
investigated further to identify and resolve the error, see Section 6.2.2. 
› 
Hydrodynamic Performance: As discussed in Section 6.5.2 and 6.5.3, the WindIO model is 
constrained by its limitation to represent the floater using only cylindrical members. It is 
plausible that, with accurate drag and added mass coefficients for the rectangular member, 
the WindIO model could have matched the Baseline model more closely in terms of 
hydrodynamic performance. This assumes the floater is sufficiently slender, minimizing the 
influence of diffraction and radiation effects. 
› 
Mooring system: Errors in the mooring system conversion were partially resolved, but further 
refinements are needed to improve the accuracy of the model, see Section 6.5.1. 
Overall, the WindIO-to-HAWC2 conversion process has proven robust, establishing a foundation for 
further development and integration into optimization frameworks like WEIS. Addressing the 
identified limitations will enhance its utility and reliability for floating offshore wind turbine design. 
7.2 
Future work 
The identified issues with the blade structural properties and the mooring system are likely 
straightforward to resolve once further investigated. Ideally, the WindIO ontology will soon be 
updated to support the definition of rectangular cross-sections, as this represents a relatively 
simple improvement that could enhance hydrodynamic modeling accuracy. Incorporating more 
accurate drag and added mass coefficients for key floater components would further advance the 
model’s reliability. 
It is important to note that modeling the entire floater with Morison elements has inherent 
limitations, particularly for non-slender geometries or more complex floater designs. For integration 
into an optimization framework, a more robust and flexible approach would involve incorporating 
radiation and diffraction hydrodynamic effects. This should be considered as a priority for future 
development.
