Abstract 
One of the things holding back second-generation biofuels such as bioethanol is economics. Currently, it is 
not commercially viable to produce bioethanol. This is partly due to difficulties with the enzymatic break 
down of plant matter into short and easily fermentable sugars. A process that in the future could be 
enhanced by Lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases (LPMOs). A group of endo-acting enzymes divided into 
the Auxiliary Activity (AA) groups of enzymes in the CASy database. They have been shown to increase the 
efficiency of exo-acting glycoside Hydrolases, which are often used in second generation bioethanol 
production. This makes LPMO’s an interesting candidate for second generation biofuel production. 
The importance of LPMOs in the digestion of polysaccharides was investigated in the known LPMO 
producer Aspergillus nidulans. This was done by attempting to delete entire AA families and evaluate their 
growth on different substrates. Out of 10 AA7 encoding genes, only A. nidulans strains with deletion of one 
or two AA7 genes was validated and tested. No impact was seen in the one growth test, requiring further 
research to elucidate the importance of LPMO genes on the metabolism of A. nidulans. 
It was shown that CRISPR plasmids constructed as in (Nødvig et al. 2018) can delete entire genes in non-
homolog end joining deficient A. nidulans strains. This is done by inducing double strand breaks outside the 
targeted genes via Cas 9 guided by sgRNAs encoded on the plasmid’s splicing cassette. The gene is then 
deleted by homologous end joining made possible by a provided oligonucleotide. 
Dansk resume 
En af de ting der holder anden generations biobrændsler tilbage så som bioethanol, er at de ikke er 
økonomisk rentabelt på kommerciel skala. En vigtig faktor er den enzymatiske nedbrydningen af svært 
nedbrydelige plastmateriale til korte let fermenterbare sukkere. En proces der muligvis i fremtiden kunne 
blive forbedret af lytiske polysaccharid monooxygenaser (LPMO’er). En gruppe endo-aktive enzymer fordelt 
på nogle af Auxiliary Activity (AA) grupperne i CASy databasen. Nogle LPMO’er har vist sig at forbedre 
virkningen af exo-virkende glykosid hydrolaser, enzymer ofte bugt i produktionen af anden generations 
bioethanol. Dette gør dem til interessante kandidater i bioethanol produktion. 
Vigtigheden af LPMO’er i nedbrydningen af polysaccharider for den kendte LPMO producent Aspergillus 
nidulans blev undersøgt. Dette blev forsøgt gjort ved at slette hele AA familier og undersøge deres vækst på 
forskellige substrater. Det lykkedes kun at lave og validere A. nidulans stammer med deletioner af et eller 
to AA7 gener ud af i alt 10 AA7 kodende gener i svampen. Ingen forskel i svampens vækst kunne observeres 
i det ene vækst eksperiment der blev lavet. Yderligere undersøgelser er krævet for at belyse vigtigheden af 
LPMO gener i A. nidulans metabolisme. 
Det blev vist at CRISPR plasmider konstrueret som i (Nødvig et al. 2018) kan fjerne hele gener i A. nidulans 
stammer, der ikke kan udføre ”non homolog end joining”. Ved at forårsage dobbelt streng brud udenfor det 
valgte gen via Cas 9, guidet af sgRNA sekvenser indkodet på plasmidets splidsnings kassette. Brud svampen 
kan kun reparere via ” homologous end joining” ud fra et designet stykke oligonukleotid. Resulterende i 
total fjernelse af genet efter reparationen af kromosomet.

Aim 
The aim of this project is to use the genetic editing strategy first presented in (Nødvig et al. 2018) to delete 
entire LPMO AA families in A. nidulans by using CRISPR plasmids with splicing cassettes containing sgRNA 
sequences for targeting and deleting multiple AA genes simultaneously. The aim is furthermore to assess 
the impact that the deletion of AA families will have on A. nidulan’s ability to depolymerize and metabolize 
various kinds of biomass with relevance to biofuel production. 
Introduction 
It has been increased urgency in recent decades to reduce our greenhouse gas emissions in order to 
combat climate change. In combination with dwindling reserves of fossil fuels such as crude oil, it is 
increasingly clear that we will need more climate friendly and cost-effective alternatives (Naik et al. 2010). 
One such alternative is biofuel made from plant material. As biofuel  fixates CO2 in their growth, and the 
CO2 that is released again when the fuel is burnt results in no net increase (Zeng et al. 2011). Biofuels are 
divided into families depending on what type of biomass is used in their production. First generation 
biofuels are made of food stuffs such as corn, seeds or sugarcane. They are easy to convert into fuel but are 
in competition with the use of biomass as food. This results in some moral dilemmas and competition with 
food prices (Martin 2010). Second generation biofuels are based on plant materials that are not used as 
food. It could be agricultural or forestry waste such as straw, sawdust or wood trimmings. It can also be 
plant matter grown specifically for the production of combustibles. This type of biomass often consists of 
high amounts of celluloses, hemicellulose and other hard to digest polysaccharides, that cannot be readily 
fermented into example bioethanol (Sims et al. 2010). They need to be pre-treated with enzymes in order 
to depolymerize the material into easily fermentable sugars. This is a challenging and lengthy process that 
is not commercially feasible with currently used enzymes (Naik et al. 2010) (Carriquiry et al. 2011). This is 
where Lytic Polysaccharide Monooxygenases (LPMOs) could be interesting. LPMOs are a grouping of 
families of enzymes that can cleave different polysaccharides in an endo-acting fashion by opening up the 
polysaccharide chains for further degradation by other enzymes such as exo-acting cellobiose 
dehydrogenase(Beeson et al. 2015). This could potentially reduce the cost of producing second generation 
biofuels (Beeson et al. 2015). One producer of LPMO enzymes belonging to multiple families under CASy 
Auxiliary Activity (AA) is the model fungi Aspergillus nidulans (Lombard et al. 2014) (Nekiunaite et al. 2016). 
In this project, entire AA families will be attempted to be deleted. This will be done to evaluate what impact 
this will have on A. nidulans’ ability to utilize different carbon sources from plant materials such as starch 
and xylan. This will be done using the strategy presented in (Nødvig et al. 2018) to create CRISPR plasmids 
that, with the addition of a oligonucleotide, can delete multiple AA encoding genes simultaneously.

Theory 
Biofuel production 
With the increasing problems with climate change, lead on by the emission of greenhouse gasses, it is 
becoming more and more urgent to decrease the emission of such gasses if we are to reduce the negative 
impact we are having on our planet(Murphy 2015). One way of reducing CO2 emissions is to replace fossils 
fuels with fuels that are made from biomass, so-called biofuels. When plant biomass is used to produced 
biofuels, the carbon stored in the fuel is released back into the atmosphere as CO2. Plants gets all their 
carbon from CO2 in the atmosphere, carbon that is released when the fuel is burnt. Because the time 
between trapping and re-release is so short compared to fossil fuels, no net increase of CO2 is seen in the 
atmosphere. However, the energy generated by fossil fuels in the production of the biofuel still does. 
Normally, biofuels are divided into different generations based on the biomass that is used(Sims et al. 
2010). 
First generation 
First generation biofuels are made from products that are normally considered food stuffs such as corn, rice 
or grain. These are the easiest to use since they are often made from easily digestible and starch-rich 
biomass that often doesn’t need mush processing before being fermented into ethanol. It could also be 
plant oils which easily can be extracted from seeds (Martin 2010)(Willem H. van Zyl et al. 2011). The big 
disadvantage to these types of fuels are that they are made from food stuffs and might drive up food 
prices, potentially making some foods less available to some populations(Martin 2010).  
Second generation 
Second generation biofuels are made from biomass that is not regarded as food stuffs, such as straw, 
wood, husks or other kinds of non-edible plant matter(Kerckhoffs & Renquist 2013). The biomass typically 
comes from agricultural waste products or rapidly growing plants grown for the purpose of making 
biofuel(Sims et al. 2010). The problem with making second-generation biofuels is that the biomass often 
consists of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin that are very hard to depolymerase into easily fermentable 
sugars(Sims et al. 2010). Extensive pre-processing steps involving prolonged exposure of the biomass to 
mixes of enzymes are often needed. However, even then the cellulose components can be hard to convert 
to easily fermentable sugars(Sims et al. 2010). The advantages on the other hand is that biomass can be 
rapidly produced and doesn’t have to compete with food production since agricultural waste from food 
production can be used(Sims et al. 2010; Martin 2010).  
Third generation 
Third generation biofuels utilize biomass created by specially engineered organisms that are made for 
optimized biofuel production. One such example is genetically engineered algae that can produce 
biodiesel(Ghasemi et al. 2012). Such an approach has the advantage of being able to utilize water covered 
areas for biofuel production. It is also estimated that algae in the future might be up to 30 times more 
effective compared to the same area of land in creating biomass for biofuel production. This could 
drastically reduce the area needed to produce biofuel. The disadvantage is that more research is still 
needed to harness the theoretical production potential of these organisms(Ghasemi et al. 2012).

LPMOs and biofuel production 
Cupper dependent lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases, or LPMOs, are a group of enzymes that can 
degrade a wide array of polysaccharides via oxidation. Substrates for LPMOs include cellulose, chitin, xylan, 
xyloglucan, glucomannan, lichenin, β-glucan and starch (Quinlan et al. 2011) (Chylenski et al. 2017). LPMOs 
are produced by many species of fungi and some bacteria, as can be seen by the number of protein 
sequences coding for LPMOs in the CAZy database, see figure 1. In nature, they help their producers with 
the depolymerization of polysaccharides. They behave in an endo-acting manner and, for example, are able 
to attack crystalline cellulose to oxidize cellulose fibrils creating breaks in the cellulose strands. These nicks 
in the strands open up the cellulose for the depolymerization of other enzymes that degrade cellulose in an 
exo-acting way. This leads to a more effective and synergistic degradation of cellulose and liberation of 
sugars for the LPMO producer to metabolize(Nekiunaite et al. 2016) (Quinlan et al. 2011).  
They do this via a single active cupper atom in the enzyme’s active site. LPMOs are also depend on oxygen 
and an electron donor to oxidize its substrate. An example of one such donor used by many fungi is 
cellobiose dehydrogenase (CDH)(Beeson et al. 2015). LPMOs attack cellulose by attacking either the β1 or 
β4 glucan bonds in polysaccharides (Agger et al. 2014). Depending on which bond the different LPMO 
enzymes can oxidize, they are categorized into three types. Type 1 oxidizes at the C1 position, type 2 at the 
C4 position and type 3 can attack either C1 or C4(Forsberg et al. 2014). Figure 1 and 2 shows how different 
enzymes can attack cellulose and how LPMOs can help make additional ends available for exo-acting 
cellulases. 
This makes them interesting with regard to second generation biofuel production since historically, it has  
been hard to breakdown cellulose from agricultural waste into fuel production using only conventional 
cellulases(Andlar et al. 2017)(Chylenski et al. 2017). It has been seen that LPMOs used in conjunction with 
other cellulases can have a synergistic effect that increases the degradation of starches and cellulose 
resulting in an increased release of sugars(Chylenski et al. 2017). This makes LPMOs interesting with regard 
to  i.e. the pre-treatment of biomass in biofuel production since LPMOs might be able to prime the material 
for degradation by exo-acting enzymes(Nekiunaite, et al. 2016a)(Chylenski et al. 2017).

Families of LPMO enzymes 
LPMOs are classified in the Auxiliary Activity (AA) family in CAZy database(Lombard et al. 2014). They are 
further divided into different sub families based on similarities in their protein sequence (Lombard et al. 
2014). As can be seen in figure 3, LPMO families are found in almost every branch of the tree of 
life(Lombard et al. 2014). They are divided in AA families 7, 9, 10, 11 and 13 in the CAZy database(Lombard 
et al. 2014).

different enzyme families among different organisms from the CAZy database(Lombard et al. 2014; Nekiunaite, Abou Hachem, et al. 
2016).

Because they are divided into families based on protein sequences, members of the same families often 
display similar properties with regard to function and substrates(Lombard et al. 2014). AA9 was formerly 
classified as GH61 in CAZy. AA9 is an LPMO family only found in some species of fungi (Nekiunaite, et al. 
2016). They are reported to be able to cleave cellulose at the C1, C4 and even in some instances C6 
position(Lombard et al. 2014). They have been proven to boost cellulase activity when mixed with other 
cellulases and are used in commercially available enzyme mixes such as Cellic CTec3 from Novozymes 
(Nekiunaite, et al. 2016). 
AA9 enzymes are found in some fungi including A. nidulans. There exist AA9 enzymes which belong to all 
three types with regard to cutting site. In A. nidulans the production is highly regulated and changes 
depending on what media the fungi are grown on. It is mainly thought that the enzyme family is used by A. 
nidulans to break down plant cell membranes since they all are active on cellulose. All AA9s produced by A. 
nidulans also showed to be tagged with a short secretion amino acid tag in the end, suggesting that the 
enzymes are secreted out of the cell to work on the surrounding media. This implies that they are used to 
provide nutrients for the fungi (Jagadeeswaran et al. 2016a).  
AA10 family enzymes are predominantly found in bacteria. They can be divided into two subclasses; those 
that can cleave chitin and those that cleave cellulose. The vast majority of AA10 enzymes can only cleave at 
the C1 position placing them in the type 1 category (Nekiunaite, et al. 2016) (Hemsworth et al. 2014). 
AA11 enzymes use starch and chitin as their substrates. They were only recently discovered and have only 
been studied to a limited degree (Nekiunaite, et al. 2016) (Hemsworth et al. 2014).  
AA13 enzymes found in A. nidulans have shown activity against starch. They have been shown to cleave at 
the C1 position of starches. They are believed to be a part of some organisms’ way of depolymerizing starch 
into easily digestible sugars. In A. nidulans, a huge increase in the production of AA13 enzymes has been 
seen when fungi are grown on a starch rich medium(Lombard et al. 2014)(Nekiunaite, et al. 2016). 
The AA7 family is not well studied. They are classified as glyco-oligosaccharide oxidases and 
chitooligoccharides in the CAZy database(Lombard et al. 2014). They are found in some fungi, and 10 AA7 
encoding genes have been identified in A. nidulans (Nekiunaite, et al. 2016) . 
Fungi 
As we know, fungi are all around us and come in many different forms that, to the naked eye, can seem 
unrelated. From single celled yeasts that can be mistaken for large bacteria, to the mold found on old 
bread, to the large edible fruiting bodies of some basidiomycetes such as the portobello mushrooms from 
Agaricus bisporus(Wood & Goodenough 1977)(Moore et al. 2011). But it is not only on the outside that the 
kingdom Fungi shows great diversity. AT the molecular level, fungi show a great ability to produce different 
biological active compounds/secondary metabolites. These metabolites have a wide variety of abilities such 
as: adapting their environment to fit them better, poisoning their competitors or breaking down 
compounds to release nutrients the fungi can utilize in their metabolism(Moore et al. 2011)(Samson et al. 
2010). 
This vast variety of compounds can be both a bane and a blessing for us. On one hand, fungi are estimated 
to account for around 11% of annual crop destruction in the entire world(Moore et al. 2011). One dire

Repair of double-strand breaks in fungi genomes 
When a double-strand DNA break occurs on one of the chromosomes of a fungi, it must be repaired before 
the chromosome can be copied or transcribe genes at the break site. There are two different methods the 
fungi can utilize to do this. The first is non-homologues end joining (NHEJ) where the break is repaired 
without the assistance of a homologous sequence. This process  often leads to errors, since there is no 
template to ensure that no errors occur during the repair of the break (Miura et al. 2012). 
The second method is to use an existing non-broken sequence that spans the break area, called 
homologues repair. This can be done in two ways: homologous joining (HJ) or synthesis-dependent strand-
annealing (SDSA). Both start with degrading the 5’ strand, forming 3’ single strand tails. HJ involves the 
invasion of the tails into the intact double-strand sequence, annealing the broken ends to the intact 
sequence and elongating them. The process is finalized with the formation of two Holliday junctions that, 
depending on how they are resolved, can lead to crossovers between the repaired and intact sequences, as 
in homologous recombination (Miura et al. 2012). 
 
SDSA similarly involves the invasion of the intact double-strand. One end invades and is elongated with the 
intact complementary strand as a template. The elongated strand is liberated and can bind to the tail of the 
sequence at the other end due to the elongation. The missing pieces can then be inserted, and the strands 
ligated, creating a repaired sequence with any nucleotides that were lost in the break restored identically 
to the repair sequence. In figure 5, the different methods are shown step by step (Miura et al. 2012).

CRISPR Cas9 
CRISPR Cas9 systems provide many archaea and bacteria with a form for adaptive immune system that 
enables them to fight viral infections(Yang & Patel 2017). Before the discovery of CRISPR/Cas systems it was 
believed that bacteria and archaea only possessed innate immune systems. But with the discovery of 
CRISPER/Cas 9 systems it has been elucidated how some of these single celled organisms can use these 
systems to protect them against foreign genetic material(Choi & Lee 2016). This is done via CRISPR arrays 
consisting of 21-40 base pairs long palindromic repeats between 20-40 base pairs long non-repeating 
sequences called protospacers, giving rise to the name CRISPR (Clustered regulated interspaced short 
palindromic repeats). The protospacers consist of sequence heterologous to a sequence found in a previous 
invader (Choi & Lee 2016). The whole CRISPR array is expressed as one long transcript that is cut by 
endogenous occurring RNases. They form pieces consisting of one repeating section and one protospacer. 
Together they form a sgRNA unit (Nødvig et al. 2015). A series of genes coding for Cas enzymes can be 
found close to the CRISPR arrays, see figure 6.  
 
Figure 6 Model of a CRISPR locus. An example of a CRISPR locus showing how the different components can be organized with the 
two main parts: the CAS Genes encoding, the Cas enzyme and the CRISPR array which can be transcribed and then cut to form 
sgRNA sequences that can guide the cutting action of Cas enzymes(Sorek et al. 2008). 
One Cas protein, Cas9, is an endonuclease that can use sgRNA to form a complex where the sgRNA acts as a 
guide to find a sequence that is an inverse repeat of the protospacer. When the  Cas9 complex finds the 
target sequence and makes a double strand break at the end of the targeted strand, herby degrading the 
invading genetic material (Sorek et al. 2008). Sometimes, when a bacterium or archaea is infected with a 
phage but survives, the genetic material of the phage will be degraded and sometimes a piece can be 
integrated into the end of the CRISPR array, hereby giving the cell protection against phages with the same 
sequence present on their genome. 
In addition to the sequence in the protospacer, Cas proteins also need a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), 
which is a short sequence usually just downstream of the protospacer, in order to be able to bind the 
invading genetic material properly. This limits the targeting capability of Cas enzymes to some extent. The 
PAM is different for different Cas enzymes found in different organisms. For example, the PAM for the 
much studied Cas9 type II protein from Streptococcus pyogenes that requires the PAM NGG (Karvelis et al. 
2015) figure 7.

Even with this limiting factor, it is still possible to use CRISPR-Cas9 in almost any place in a given genome. 
Due to the very precise and targeted cutting action of the Cas9 complex, there is only a very small risk of 
off-target cutting since it is unlikely that the protospacer sequence will be present in other areas of a given 
genome. This makes the CRISPR-Cas9 a valuable tool in gene editing since it enables very specific cutting at 
almost any desired location on a given genome (Nødvig et al. 2015). 
This makes gene editing easier than with other older methods such as TALENs or zinc-finger. 
 
USER cloning 
Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent, or USER for short, is an enzyme mix offered by New England Biolabs that 
can excise uracil from DNA by making a single strand break at the excision site. This is done by a 
combination of the enzymes Uracil DNA glycosylase (UDG) and Endonuclease VIII (Kaas et al. 2009). The 
DNA glycosylase excise the uracil while leaving the backbone unbroken, and the Endonuclease makes a 
single strand cut at the site where uracil was excised, see figure 8. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
Mikkel Sloth Rasmussen s123715 Master Project Biotechnology 19/2/2018                                         20 of 84  
 
 
Figure 8 Picture shoving the cutting action of USER enzyme mix. Picture shows the cutting action of UDG and Endonuclease VIII. UDG 
removes the uracil and the endonuclease makes a single strand cut of the backbone where the uracil was situated (England New 
Biolabs® 2018).  
This allows for the creation of single strand overhangs on i.e. PCR products containing uracils situated near 
the end. This makes it possible to create sticky ends that can be used in cloning(Kaas et al. 2009). By doing 
this and making sure that the overhangs do not have the same sequence, it is possible to dictate the 
direction that the PCR fragment will be inserted into an open reverse complementing USER cassette. USER 
cassettes are small sequences that can be enzymatically cut to get single strand overhangs with different 
sequences. By engineering vectors with USER cassettes on them, it is possible to open the vectors and allow 
for DNA fragments with reverse complimenting single strand tails to be integrated into the cassette without 
the use of ligase(Nour-Eldin et al. 2010). One of the most common USER cassettes is PacI, Nt.BbvCI 
cassettes that are double strand cut by PacI and then nicked by Nt.BbvCI which forms the needed single 
strand ends. These expression vectors with USER cassettes can then be used in conjunction with PCR 
fragments with ends that are USER compatible to facilitate the cloning of the fragments into the plasmid. 
The plasmid can then carefully be transformed into competent E. coli where the DNA backbone breaks can 
be ligated, leading to a plasmid with no breaks and containing the PRC fragment(Nødvig et al. 2018). Figure 
9 is an illustration of how USER cloning works and the processes involved in inserting a fragment into a 
Plasmid vector containing a USER cassette.

LPMO deletion with CRISPR plasmid strategy 
Strategy summary 
All LPMO genes on the list provided by Majid Haddad Momeni were mapped by their chromosomes and 
divided into sets depending on the individual gene’s location and AA family. For each set, a plasmid 
containing the following was made: 
1. A splicing cassette containing an sgRNA sequence that can be liberated by RNases and direct Cas9 
to cut at the ends of one or two LPMO genes. 
2. An auxotrophy marker for selection when transforming into A. nidulans 
3. Ampicillin resistance for selection when plasmids are ligated and amplified in E.coli 
4. AMA 1 witch to help sustain the plasmid but which makes it unstable and can be lost after 
transformation into A. nidulans. 
5. The gene coding for Cas 9 protein to delete LPMO encoding genes 
Plasmids were created from fragments made with PCR using an existing CRISPR plasmid as fragment. All 
fragments were made to be USER compatible. After the plasmids were assembled, ligated and amplified in 
E. coli, they were purified and transformed into A. nidulans strains deficient in non-homologous end joining 
with oligo nucleotide for homolog repair. Transformants were validated for correct deletion and grown on 
minimal media with the necessary supplements to get the transformant to lose the plasmid which allows 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
Mikkel Sloth Rasmussen s123715 Master Project Biotechnology 19/2/2018                                         29 of 84  
 
for the same auxotrophy marker to be used for multiple deletions. Additional deletions could then be made 
by re-transforming with different plasmid to target other LPMO genes. 
On figure 15 an example of a gene deletion using this strategy can be seen. 
It has been speculated that it would be possible to combine multiple splicing cassettes to form cassettes 
containing sgRNA for the deletion of additional LPMO genes with a single plasmid. The new plasmid 
cassettes could then be combined with other new cassettes and thereby form cassettes with even more 
targets. However, due to time constraints the strategy was never able to work. 
The plan was to create transformants with multiple LPMO deletions in the same AA family that could then 
be crossed, hopefully creating offspring with the entire AA family knocked out. The strains lacking one or 
potentially more AA families could then be grown on different test media to elucidate the impact that the 
deletions would have on the fungi’s growth. Unfortunately, due to time constraints and trouble validating 
transformants, only transformants with one or two AA7 genes knocked out were tested.

Splicing sgRNA cassette 
By making use of the A. nidulans endogenous occurring RNase Z and P it is possible to make a single sgRNA 
splicing cassette that that can be processed by the two RNases to liberate multiple pieces of sgRNA from a 
single piece of RNA figure 13.  
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Mikkel Sloth Rasmussen s123715 Master Project Biotechnology 19/2/2018                                         35 of 84  
 
 
Figure 13 Model of sgRNA liberation. RNA digestion by RNase Z and P liberates multiple pieces of sgRNA from a single RNA 
transcript. Thus enabling multiplex gene CRISPR targeting based on a single splicing cassette (Nødvig et al. 2018).   
This enables multiplex CRISPR gene editing from a single RNA transcript (Nødvig et al. 2018)by creating a 
CRISPR plasmid with a single sgRNA splicing cassette that can produce a single large transcript containing 
multiple sgRNA sequences situated between identical rRNA sequences. When the transcript is digested by 
the RNases, the sgRNA containing the protospacers are liberated and can be used by Cas9 to create the 
desired double strand breaks (Nødvig et al. 2018). The breaks can only then be repaired via homolog repair 
since the strains used are deficient in non-homologues end joining. This is done by using the designed 
oligonucleotide which results in total gene deletion due to the repair (Nødvig et al. 2018).  
Primer and oligo design 
The same general strategy as was used in (Nødvig et al. 2018). Protospacers were found on the genome of 
A. nidulans from AspGD (Arnaud et al. 2018) in pairs of two, one forward and one reverse for each gene to 
be deleted. One in each end of the targeted gene. The sequences consisted of 20 base pairs situated just 
upstream from the PAM sequence needed for binding and cutting of the Cas9 enzyme used. Figure 14 
below shows an example of a protospacer PAM and gene sequence illustrated in CLC. 
 
Figure 14 Model of PAM, protospacer and oligo in one end of an AA coding gene. Showing the start of the LPMO AA7 gene with 
annotation ANIA 02648 on AspGD (Arnaud et al. 2018) along with a chosen protospacer sequence, oligo part and PAM sequence.  
==PAGE:36==

Each protospacer was also chosen so it contained an A and a T with 8-15 base pairs between them. 
Primers a designed so that the forward primer (direction of the chosen protospacer) starts with the A 
followed by the sequence between the A and T. The T is replaced with a U followed by the rest of the 
protospacer followed with GTTTTAGAGCTAGAAATAGCAAGTTAAA. A reverse complement primer was made 
in the same manner but ending in TGCATCATCCGTGAATCGAAC. This results in primers that can be used to 
make USER compatible fragments and that can self-assemble in USER cloning, see figure 11. 
An oligo nucleotide sequence was designed for each gene to be deleted. Each oligo contains 20 base pairs 
just outside both cutting sites of Cas9 from a given primer set. This results in a 40 base pair long sequence 
that can be used to repair the chromosome after Cas9 have cut, using homologous end joining (Nødvig et 
al. 2018). Oligoes are also designed so the protospacers aren’t present after the deletion is made. See table 
9 for examples of primers and oligo designed for one gene. See figure 15 for an illustration of a deletion 
example.

USER cloning was carried out in a thermal cycler to precisely manage temperatures. After transformation

stored at -20°C for later use. A small portion of the liquid E. coli cultures were saved to be added to the 
plasmid collection. After validation of the plasmids, cryo-stock of the liquid cultures were made and stored 
at -80°C for later use.

Getting A. nidulans to lose unstable AMA 1 plasmids 
It is possible to use the same auxotrophy marker in multiple transformations in the same strain by getting 
the fungi to lose the plasmid in between transformations. This was done by adding the AMA 1 gene which 
makes the plasmid unstable in the fungi. The plasmid is retained as long as the fungi is grown on minimal 
media without supplements. Losing the plasmid would result in the fungi being unable to grow. However, 
when the fungi are grown on minimal media with the amino acid/acids it cannot produce without the 
plasmid, there are no selective pressure forcing the fungi to retain the plasmid. By growing the fungi on 
such plates and by taking samples in at edge of the fungi growth zone, it is possible to collect spores and 
mycelium that lack the plasmid contained in the original A. nidulans strain. Since fungi grow from the 
center out, mycelium that have lost the plasmid will continue to grow outwards. This results in a wedge like 
zone in the mycelium which has lost the plasmid. Figure 27 A shows such mycelium wedges and figure 27 B 
shows a test to see if the mycelium and spores in the wedges have lost the plasmid thereby and the ability 
to make the required amino acid/acids.

Combining Splicing Cassettes 
By combining the splicing cassettes from different finished plasmids, it should be possible to make one 
large splicing cassette that can target all the genes that the parent plasmids can. This was attempted by 
using the two finished parent plasmids as templates in three different PCR reactions in order to produce 
three fragments that can then be assembled into one large splicing cassette. A cassette that then can be 
inserted into an empty USER cassette vector. The primers used in the first PCR were CSN348 and the last 
reverse primer used to originally create the first parent plasmid, using the parent plasmid as a template. 
The second used a forward primer that matched the reverse primer of the parent in the first PCR and a 
reverse primer that complements the forward primer of the second parent plasmid in the third PCR. The 
template for the second PCR were PAC 902. This created a fragment that works as a linker between the 
fragments created in the first and third PCR. In the third PCR, the forward primer was the second forward 
primer used in the creation of the original second parent plasmid. With CSN790 as the reverse primer, and 
the second parent were used as template. See figure 28 for a model of the fragments. Once these 
fragments are purified they can be inserted into an empty CRISPR vector via USER cloning and transformed 
into competent E coli.

Discussion 
The strategy of (Nødvig et al. 2018) designing and creating the plasmids containing splicing cassettes 
proved very reliable and relatively fast. Over just a few weeks, it was possible to create a finished CRISPR 
plasmid that could target one or two genes. Creating the fragments was easy, and the PCR reactions had a 
high success rate of producing usable fragments. Purification of the fragments with kit (Zymo Research 
2018) worked well. The assembly of the splicing cassettes using USER cloning and the subsequent 
transformation into competent E. coli worked well and provided a high number of transformants on the 
plates. Amplification and purification of the plasmids using commercial kit (Zymo Research 2018) was easy 
and provided reliable results. This resulted in the successful construction of plasmids for all sets. 
Validation of the plasmids using restriction analysis with BspE1 as the cutting enzyme showed correct band 
lengths for samples in all sets. Correct plasmids were saved both as purified plasmids and in E. coli for later 
plasmid production. 
Validating the sequencing of the cassettes on the plasmids ensured that they appeared to be correct and 
did not merely have the right length.  
Because of the use of the reverse primer, the data at the start of the splicing cassettes were of low quality 
which resulted in some base pairs in the first protospacers could not be verified in most plasmid. Since all 
data of a decent quality was correct it is assumed that the rest was also correct. It would still have been 
prudent to have had a sequencing where a forward primer with a binding site before the start of the 
cassette was used. This would have provided better data in that area and would have increased the 
strength of the validation. 
Transforming the plasmids into the recipient strains NID 109 for PAC 331 and NID 108 for PAC 330, proved 
inconsistent between transformations and batches of protoplasts. It is unlikely that this is because of the 
plasmids since they were validated. The oligos needed for the homolog repair were resuspended in water 
and kept at -20°C after arrival. Therefore, it is unlikely that they were the reason. The most likely reason for 
why the transformations at times worked and at others didn’t were the protoplasts. One reason could be 
because protoplasts are very fragile. They can therefore easily be killed if they are handled too roughly or 
stored incorrectly. Storing them in the fridge for two weeks resulted in zero transformants in one 
experiment. Another problem could be that some batches might not have been competent, meaning that 
they were unable to take up the oligonucleotide and plasmid during transformation. This resulted in few or 
no transformants on positive and negative control plates as well as transformation plates. 
The trickiest part of the project was validating the transformants with tissue PCR. Some initial results were 
achieved using another protocol with GC buffer, DMSO and MgCl but the success rate was very poor 
approx.20%, see figure 34 in appendix. Switching to a CxL buffer initially gave better results (see figure 25) 
but there was a consistent problem with getting all bands to appear in samples and control. Towards the 
end of the project, tissue PCR started to get smudgy (see figure 32) with no bands showing. Other labs in 
the building had the same problem, so all reagents were replaced. New reagents still resulted in no bands 
but without the smudge (see figure 33). At the end, only transformants from set 3 and set 10 were 
validated using tissue PCR because of the trouble with getting it to work. Normally, black pigment found in 
many fungi such as A. niger can interfere with the polymerase. But since all transformants were white or 
yellow, this was not the case. One way of achieving a better success rate would be to extract genomic DNA 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
Mikkel Sloth Rasmussen s123715 Master Project Biotechnology 19/2/2018                                         55 of 84  
 
from the transformants and use that as template in a validation PCR. But that was not possible due to the 
time constraints of the project. 
The failed attempts to combine splicing cassettes from different sets turned out to be because of the 
primers used since all designed forward primers contained the same tail in addition to the protospacer and 
the same is true for the reverse primers. All fragments made with the primers would therefore contain 
those sequences resulting in finished plasmids with the tail sequences regularly spread out in the cassettes. 
This resulted in the multiple bands seen on figure 29. The reverse tail (TGCATCATCCGTGAATCGAAC) caused 
the problem on AB fragments in figure 28 and the forward tail sequence 
(GTTTTAGAGCTAGAAATAGCAAGTTAAA) on the EF fragments in figure 28. Since the linker CD fragment in 
figure 28 is made with PAC 902 as a template this problem did not occur, see figure 29.  In order to get the 
correct fragments, new primers consisting of only the protospacers and maybe a few nucleotides on the 
tails (to adjust melting temperatures) should be used. 
Since a validated yellow transformant for set 6 was never obtained the crossing of transformants with 
different visible phenotypes were never carried out. It was therefore never tested if the green offspring of 
such a crossing could be made which had the deletions from both parent strains. 
Only one fungal growth experiment was carried out to see the impact of deletions. Results of this 
experiment were poor due to contamination and multiple colonies growing on some of the plates. It was 
difficult to verify whether deletion strains NID2379 – NID2383 showed impaired or different growth patters 
compared to the control NID 109. A difference in growth patterns or speed could indicate a change in the 
fungi’s ability to metabolize the different nutrient sources in the test media. Further tests are needed to 
see if the growth of the deletion mutants differ from the control. However, since A. nidulans can produce 
33 different LPMO genes, of which 10 are AA7, with only one or two deleted genes in tested strains it 
stands to reason that the impact on growth will be minimal. This is because there are so many other genes 
in fungus that have products that carry out or have very similar function. The impact of so few deletions 
would likely be small or nonexistent since fungi can compensate by increasing the expression and secretion 
of other AA enzymes. 
The overall strategy proved successful at deleting two AA genes with a single CRISPR plasmid cutting at four 
separate places in the A. nidulans genome and then subsequently getting the fungi to repair the double 
strand breaks using the provided oligonucleotide. Transformation rates fluctuated suggesting a need to 
further refine either the protocol or its execution of. It was also possible to get the transformants to 
subsequently lose the plasmid, enabling an iterative deletion process. A process in which the same 
auxotrophy markers can be used in the same strain to delete multiple AA genes a few at a time.

Conclusion 
It was not possible to get any transformants that had entire AA families deleted due to time constraints 
stemming from problems validating the transformants. It was possible to use the strategy presented in 
(Nødvig et al. 2018) to construct CRISPR plasmids that with the addition of a oligonucleotide could delete 
one or two AA genes at a time in A. nidulans. It was also possible to get the transformant to subsequently 
lose the plasmid. Which enabled the time-consuming deletion of a few AA at a time in an iterative process 
with the same markers. A process that can be used to create transformants with entire AA families deleted 
over time.  
It was not possible to combine multiple splicing cassettes using the attempted strategy. It might be possible 
if different primers were used for the PCR reactions. 
Only four different correct transformants could be validated: NID2380-2381 for set 3 transformants 8 and 
9,  NID2379 and NID2382 for set 10 transformants 2 and 3. 
No difference was seen in NID2379 – NID2383s’ ability to utilize the tested substrates in the test carried out 
in comparison to the control. 
Further work is needed to elucidate the importance and characterization of LPMO AAs in A. nidulans. Both 
with regard to what substrates the fungi can degrade but also if any of the LPMO’s it produces could be 
applied in future biofuel production.
