ABSTRACT 
Among the renewable energy sources, the popularity of photovoltaics has been growing 
in the recent years. The photovoltaic community has recently regarded the use of nano-
pillars as an interesting way to use the expensive and high efficiency III-V semiconduc-
tor materials without increasing the cost of the photovoltaic devices too much. These 
nanopillars present an increased photon absorption compared to the most common pla-
nar architectures used in solar cells. Among these materials, GaAsP presents several 
characteristics that allow for the growth of the pillars directly on a silicon substrate fa-
cilitating the fabrication of tandem solar cells.  
 
In this project, the absorption of GaAsP nanopillar solar cell on a silicon substrate was 
analyzed in detail by finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) simulations of the reflec-
tance. The goal was to optimize the geometrical parameters that maximize the absorp-
tion, obtaining that a large height, a dimeter of about 300 nm and a pitch of 600 nm are 
the optimal values. 
 
Then a fabrication process for the positioned nano-patterned silicon substrate that was 
necessary for the growth of the nanopillars was developed in the cleanroom of Danchip. 
 
Finally, optical characterization of the samples was made to analyze the device perfor-
mance of the GaAsP solar cell by measuring the reflectance with the integrating sphere 
and the goniometer.  The samples characterized showed agreement with the simulations.

INTRODUCTION 
Around 78% of the worldwide total energy generation is done using fossil fuels like oil, 
coal or gas [1]. This method of energy production is associated with such issues like the 
global warming effect that has a negative impact on the environment and even on hu-
man health. Another main issue of the fossil fuels is that they are not evenly distributed 
throughout the world. Therefore, controlling the areas that are rich in fossil fuels is of 
paramount importance for countries and it also leads to armed conflict and political in-
stability. 
 
There is an increasing need for renewable energy sources that can provide clean and 
abundant energy and also compete in the world market with other sources of electricity 
generation. 
 
Among the renewable energy sources, the popularity of the solar power has been grow-
ing in the recent years due to its reliability and low maintenance. According to the U.S 
Energy Information administration, in 2012 there were 3.5 million of megawatt-hours of 
electricity generated by photovoltaics panels. In 2015 that amount grew to 23.3 million 
MWh [2]. Solar is also closing the price per kilowatt-hour with other sources, with a 
68% reduction in the cost of $/MWh from 2010 to 2015 [3]. 
 
In Europe, the trend is very similar. From 2005 to 2013, the photovoltaic growth rate 
was an impressive 65%, the highest among renewable energies [4].Countries like Den-
mark reached its government goal of 200 MW of photovoltaic capacity planned by 2020 
already in 2012 [5]. 
 
The main compound in solar cell manufacturing without doubt is silicon. It is one of the 
most abundant elements in the crust of the Earth, which also quite robust, cost-efficient 
but, however, not free of drawbacks. One of the major ones its lower energy conversion 
efficiency, which happens due to the indirect bandgap, compared with other III-V semi-
conductor materials which possess the direct bandgap. 
 
To create a successful technology for solar cells, a way to combine the good properties 
that some of the III-V semiconductor materials offer with the well-established and de-
veloped silicon manufacturing process must be achieved. However, this merge of tech-

nologies presents several problems such as different thermal expansion coefficient and 
lattice mismatch between the III-V semiconductor compounds with high efficiency and 
the silicon substrates. 
 
In this thesis, the optimization, fabrication process for the array of nanopillars and char-
acterization of a GaAsP solar cell in silicon substrate is presented. By combining a sili-
con substrate solar cell with Gallium arsenide phosphide nanopillars it is possible to 
overcome the lattice mismatch problem because the small area of contact at the inter-
face allows the stress induced due to lattice mismatch to be relaxed within the first 
monolayers[6][7][8]. 
 
By using this method, it is possible to create a GaAsP nanopillar solar cell, which is 
grown directly on top of a silicon substrate that contains a p-n junction. This combina-
tion allows manufacture of the GaAsP-Si tandem solar cell, in which part of the incident 
photons will be absorbed by the GaAsP nanopillar solar cell and the rest of the photons 
that go through, are absorbed by the silicon solar cell underneath. 
 
The Si substrate used for the fabrication of the tandem solar cell contains a p-n junction 
that was made by high n-type doping on a p-type silicon substrate. The substrate also 
has the <111> crystallographic orientation instead of the most common <100> because 
this crystallographic direction is necessary to allow the nanopillars grow perpendicular 
to the substrate [9][10]. If the nanowires are grown on a substrate with the orientation 
<100>, they tend to grown in four different directions and have an angle of approxi-
mately 35º with the silicon substrate, making them unsuitable for the process. 
 
Furthermore, a layer of approximately 30 nm of silicon dioxide was grown on the sub-
strate by thermal oxidation. This layer and its subsequent pattern are a fundamental part 
of the nanopillar growing process. 
 
The material for the nanopillars is the compound gallium arsenide phosphide that has 
been selected because of their unique compatibility with silicon solar cells. This materi-
al is created as a combination with different proportions of gallium arsenide and gallium 
phosphide.  
 
The nanopillars can be grown from the previous hole-patterned layer of Si02 by molecu-
lar beam epitaxy. The first step is the creation of the core nanopillar, a vertical p-doped 
gallium arsenide phosphide that will be the center of the radial nanopillar solar cell. 
After this step, a vertical shell is grown around the core. This shell is a compound of 
two internal layers. The first one, closer to the core, is made of intrinsic gallium arse-
nide phosphide (i-GaAsP) and the second one, surrounding the latter one, is made of n-
type gallium arsenide phosphide with the goal to form a p-i-n junction in the radial di-

In a planar p-n junction, charge separation and collection happen in the same direction 
that the incident light. Most of the photo-generated carriers are collected only if they are 
a diffusion length away from the p-n junction because the diffusion length indicates the 
average distance that a carrier can travel before undergoing a recombination process. 
 
One of the main features of the radial p-n junctions is that the carrier collection and sep-
aration are perpendicular to the light collection hence shorter carrier collection distances 
are possible.  The photons of the incident light are absorbed in the axial direction while 
the carriers, the pairs of electron-holes generated, are collected radially which allows the 
carriers to diffuse for a shorter distance before undergoing into a recombination phe-
nomenon [11]. 
 
The Gallium arsenide phosphide nanopillar solar cells bandgap can be adjusted by 
changing the percentage of GaP or GaAs used to create this ternary compound. Howev-
er, logically this band gap value can only be adjusted between the value of the bandgap 
for GaAs, which is 1.43 eV at 300 K and the value of GaP, which is 2.26 eV at 300 K. 
With a proportion of 80% GaAs and 20% GaP, the value for the band gap obtained was 
1.7 eV which is the value selected by the researchers. 
 
Theoretically, the efficiency of the tandem solar cell created by the combination of the 
silicon solar cell acting as a substrate with a bandgap of 1.1 eV and the nanopillar solar

33 and 43% [12][13], As a result it is able to compete in the market with multi-junction 
solar cells that have a similar efficiency but with the big advantage over the previous 
ones that the amount necessary for the expensive III-V semiconductor materials is min-
imum [13] which results in a very attractive option for the manufacturers of commercial 
solar cells. 
 
However, in order to reach the maximum theoretical efficiency, the individual sub-

1.1 
Formulation of the problem 
 
The creation of a tandem solar cell with the fabrication method proposed by the compa-
ny GASP Solar presents several advantages that can be exploited to create a competitive 
product. It has been already proved by them that the nanopillar fabrication method al-
lows for fabrication of nanopillar without the need of gold. This is that is very advanta-
geous because gold can diffuse easily and introduce defects on the solar cells.  Also they 
proved that the theoretical efficiency of the pillars can exceed the Shockley’s limit of 
efficiency because the optical cross section of the nanopillar is bigger than the geomet-
rical cross section and this acts a build-in concentrator.  However to achieve the effi-
ciencies predicted by the theory in the real world, the individual components the forms 
he tandem solar cell must be optimized.  
 
This Master’s project will try to answer some questions regarding the optical optimiza-
tion of the GaAsP solar cell: 
 
-Which is the optimal diameter of the nanopillars that minimizes the reflectance above 
the desired bandgap? 
-Which is the optimal distance between nanopillars to minimize the reflectance? 
-How relevant is the height of the nanopillars? 
-Which is the best tiling pattern for the nanopillars? 
-Which is the best combination of these geometrical parameters for our purpose?  
-How is the detailed process of fabrication of the substrate that allows growing the posi-
tioned nanopillars to create the GaAsp solar cell? 
-How close are the results of the simulations compared with measures of real nanopil-
lars in the laboratory? 
 
 These questions will be answered in the following chapter of the Master’s project.

1.2 
 Solar cell theoretical background 
 
Solar cells convert light into electricity by photoelectric effect [14]. If an incident pho-
ton hits the semiconductor material with energy above than that of the band gap, it will 
be absorbed and a pair of electron-hole will be created. The electrons and holes generat-
ed in this way are metastables and only last for a time equal to the minority carrier life-
time before they undergo a recombination process. When this recombination occurs, the 
generated electron-hole pair is lost and no current can be generated within the solar cell. 
 
To avoid this recombination, a p-n region is necessary. This region is the union of a 
positively doped silicon or p-type silicon. This is created by doping silicon with accep-
tor impurities. One of the most widely used acceptor dopants for silicon is boron. In the 
p-type, the Fermi level is closer to the valence band. 
 
An N-type semiconductor is created in a similar way, introducing donor impurities into 
the silicon semiconductor. The most popular dopant for an n-type semiconductor is 
phosphorus. In the n-type, the Fermi level is closer to the conduction band. 
 
When these two regions are put together, electrons migrate from the n-type semiconduc-
tor to the p-type semiconductor and the opposite happens to the holes. This happens 
until the system reaches it thermodynamic equilibrium and the Fermi level in both re-
gions is equal. 
 
This alignment of the Fermi levels, induce a “bending” of the valence and conduction 
band in the junction and an electrical field is created due to potential gradient. This re-
gion is called Depletion region.  Electrons excited within this region will migrate to the 
n-type area of the semiconductor and the opposite occurs to the holes. Therefore, spatial 
separation of the carriers occurs in the p-n junction.

According to Shockley and Queisser, the limit for the efficiency for a single p-n junc-

One way to overcome the efficiency limit of 30% for silicon semiconductors is to com-
bine this technology with nanostructures of different semiconductor materials. These 
nanopillars have several characteristics that make them very attractive for solar cell ap-
plications.  For the direct band gap semiconductor materials, those nanowire exhibits 
resonances similar to those described by Mie theory, resulting in a build-in light-
concentration effect [16] and have the advantage of the radial p-n junction that allows 
distributing carrier collection and light absorption into two orthogonal directions [17]. 
 
1.3 
Outline of the thesis 
 
This thesis covers the design and characterization of a nanopillar solar cell. The project 
is divided into three major areas that are covered in detail in each of the following chap-
ters. 
 
In chapter 2, the design of the nanopillars is covered. This design is driven by three ma-
jor parameters that affect the performance of the nanopillars: diameter, height, and 
pitch. The goal here is to minimize the reflectance of the array of nanopillars for the

energies above the bandgap. This optimization was carried out using FDTD simulations 
and selected the optimal parameters for the growth of the nanopillars.

orientation with a thin layer of Silicon dioxide on top in which an array of nanoholes

Finally, in chapter 5 a conclusion of the results obtained during the project is presented 
and future areas of interest for the project will be addressed.

2.3 
Yee’s Algorithm 
 
This FDTD algorithm uses second-order central differences to solve the time-dependent 
Maxwell’s equation in its partial difference form. This method was firstly proposed by 
Kane S. Yee in 1966 [20]. 
 
The algorithm “method of operation” can be expressed as follows:  
1. Replace the derivatives in Maxwell’s equations by finite differences and discretize 
time and space so fields are staggered in space and time. 
 
2. Solve the resulting finite difference equations to get “new updated equations” that 
express the future electric and magnetic fields in terms of the past fields. 
 
3. The change in the E-field in time is dependent on the change in the H-field across 
space. Therefore, evaluating the E-fields one time-step into the future as they are 
known now. 
 
4. Evaluate the magnetic fields, one time step into the future so they are known now.  
 
5. Iterating the E-field and H-field updates results in a marching-in-time process. It is 
necessary to repeat the previous steps into a leapfrog scheme until the fields are 
obtained over the desired duration.

2.6 
Geometrical Optimization of Nanopillar Array 
 
The influence of the geometrical parameters on the optical absorption of the nanopillar 
array plays a fundamental role. It has been proved by many researchers that an array of 
nanopillars with well-defined geometrical parameters achieved much higher absorption 
than their thin film counterpart with the same thickness [23]. 
 
The geometrical parameters considered in our simulations are the diameter of the nano-
pillars, the center to center pitch and the height. A good engineering design of these 
parameters is important to maximize the absorption. 
 
To be able to select the most convenient growth parameters for the nanopillar growth, 
several simulations were made.  Those simulations were based on performing a sweep 
of one of the parameters to investigate while keeping the other two fixed. 
 
As it has been mentioned above, the final device consists of a GaAsP solar cell in com-
bination with a Si substrate solar cell. However, in the simulation process, only the part 
absorbed by the GaAsP nanopillar is considered for the optimization. GaAs0.8P0.2 has a 
bandgap of 1.7 eV [8], that correspond to a wavelength of 730 nm, so all the parameters 
selected have the objective to maximize the absorption from 400 nm to that value.

2.11 
Chapter Conclusion 
 
In this chapter, the response of the reflectance to the variation of the three main geomet-
rical parameters was studied.  
The study was conducted using FDTD simulation created by the Lumerical software.  
This software solves Maxwell’s equation in differential form and calculates the future 
fields at each point based on the previous ones by an iterative process.  This method is 
very precise but it has the drawback of consuming a lot of memory.  
 
Due to the limited amount of memory available and the long time of the simulations, 
symmetric and anti-symmetric boundary conditions were selected instead of the period-
ic ones and also the 30 nm SiO2 was removed from the simulations. The selection of 
these parameters reduced the computation time a lot without reducing the accuracy of 
the simulations.    
 
From the studied parameters, it is recommended that the height of the nanopillars is 
large (around 2 m), in order to have the minimum reflectance from 400 nm to 600 nm. 
However, the variation of this parameter is less significant than variations on pitch and 
diameter because the reflectance variation is small at variations of heights (no peak dis-
placement presented in the desired range). 
 
The value of the center to center pitch is another important geometrical parameter which 
is worth considering. And it is seen from Figure 2-13, that it has a local minimum of the 
reflectance at 600 nm pitch for a fixed wavelength. It is also seen from figures 2-11 and 
2-12 that the pitches that present the lower reflectance over the studied range are 500 
nm and 600 nm. It is also important to note that, as it has been mentioned above, that 
the holes which define the pitch of the pillars are created by e-beam lithography in the 
==PAGE:37==
 
Chapter 2. Design of Nanopillar Array  
35 
silicon substrate (this process will be explained in detail in chapter 3) and it can be made 
with great accuracy. The other two parameters which are the diameter and height of the 
pillars are created by the collaborators of the project during the epitaxial growth and 
they are much harder to control accurately. 
 
The diameter of the pillars is also taken into consideration in order to select the optimal 
parameters for the growth of the pillars. It is clearly seen from Figure 2-8 and 2-9 that a 
smaller diameter presents lower reflectance over the wavelength range of interest. The 
diameter value of 270 nm seems to have a local minimum for the reflectance at 650 nm 
and from that value, reflectance increases with diameters increasing as well.  
 
Finally, angular dependent measures of the reflectance are performed. For a fixed pitch 
and diameter, the reflectance of the nanopillar seems to be quite independent of the an-
gle of the incident source. A big reflectance peak was detected during this analysis. Fur-
ther investigation about this phenomenon varying diameter and pitch conclude that oc-
curs due to resonances in the unit cell due to the nanopillar structure.

3.3 
Spin coating and wafer preparation  
 
The first step in the fabrication process is to select the wafer for the substrate. In this 
case, a 4-inch Si wafer with crystallographic orientation <111> was selected. As men-
tioned above, this crystallographic orientation is very important and allows a perfect 
vertical growth of the nanopillars. 
 
A 30 nm silicon dioxide layer was grown on top of the silicon by dry thermal oxidation. 
SiO2 is a highly hydrophilic material and is very important to remove the thin layer of 
moist that forms on top of it, to assure a good adhesion of the photoresist during the 
spin coating process. To do that, the wafer was pre-baked for 10 minutes at 150 °C. 
 
After this step the wafer was spun coated using a spinner (LabSpin6) to create a layer of 
photoresist. In this case the photoresist used was CSAR AR-6200.09 (AllResist) diluted 
1:1 in anisole. This is a positive-tone resist that presents high sensitivity and allows for 
resolutions down to the sub-10 nm range. This resist is composed of an electron sensi-
tive material that alters its solubility by means of chain scission, when exposed to the 
electrons and allows for selective removal of the exposed part during the development 
process.

3.4 
Electron-beam lithography 
 
In this part, the process of defining the pattern on the wafer is explained in detail. The 
exposure is made with an electron-beam writer inside the cleanroom facilities at 
Danchip. The writer used is JEOL JBX-9500 EBL, a spot electron beam type with ac-
celerating voltage up to 100 kV, which can define structures in the sub-10 nm range and 
presents a maximum writing field of 1mm2 without stitching problems. 
 
For the creation of the patterned array with this tool, it is necessary to follow certain 
steps in order to prepare the files necessary for software that controls the electron beam 
writer. 
 
 BEAMER is the name of the software that converts the different files in something that 
the e-beam writer can read. To do that, a magazine file (mgn-file) must be created. This 
==PAGE:43==
 
Chapter 3. Nano-Patterned Substrate Fabrication  
41 
file contains all the information about the desired pattern to write, important parameters 
like for instance the dose or the current that are applied during the writing and infor-
mation about which cassette to write among all the ones that are loaded in the machine. 
 
The magazine file previously mentioned is composed of three different files that must 
be created separated and then compiled into the magazine file. 
 
First of all, a v30-file is created containing information relative to the design of the pat-
tern. This design was carried out using the L-edit software and the exported into a GDS 
format. Then this GDS file is later converted into the corresponding v30-file using 
BEAMER. 
 
The next step is to create the schedule file (sdf-file) with all the parameters for the writ-
ing. This file is created using the software SuperEdi. 
The last necessary file is the job deck file (jdf-file) which specifies what pattern to use 
and in which cassette must be written. This file is also created with SuperEdi software. 
All these files are included in appendix A. 
 
The pattern design by L-edit is one of the most important tasks during the electron-
writing. For the goal of this project perfectly round holes were necessary for the correct 
growth of the nanopillars.  
 
A large and dense array of holes like the one needed for this project are very high in

3.4.1 
HEIMAP and virtual chip marks 
 
The electron-beam writer JEOL JBX-9500 EBL has a system which focuses the beam at 
the corresponding height for the correct focus and application of the dose. The subrou-
tine HEIMAP is implemented during the initial calibration of the e-beam writer prior to 
the exposition. This subprogram measures the height across the wafer on a defined array 
position that is specified by the user during the calibration process. It measures a 5 x 5 
matrix of points in the X and Y directions which has to cover the entire wafer surface. 
Then the system takes the average height and uses this value for writing all across the 
wafer. This is the most common form of height calibration. However, it is not suitable 
for high currents or large height variation.  
 
==PAGE:47==
 
Chapter 3. Nano-Patterned Substrate Fabrication  
45 
Sometimes this subprogram fails to provide accurate measurements of the height. For 
example, in cases where the wafer is bended and the height is not uniformed across it or, 
like in our case, when small chips cover the surface rather than a big pattern area. 
When the height is not measured correctly on the chip of the wafer, it primarily affects 
the stitching of the main fields (1 mm x 1 mm) and also the subsidiary fields (4 m x 4 
m). 
As mentioned above, when the pattern consists of several small chips distributed across 
the wafer surface, it is more accurate to measure the height in the relevant locations for 
our sample. Therefore, virtual chip marks must be included. These marks are virtual 
alignment marks that are introduced in specific locations, usually on top of the designed 
structures. The software uses these marks instead the HEIMAP subprogram and in 
consequence, the height map provided is more accurate in those places. This infor-
mation has to be encoded into the jdf-file.

3.6 
Reactive ion etching  
Reactive Ion Etching (RIE) is used to transfer the pattern of nano-holes into the 30 nm 
SiO2 layer. The goal is to etch about 25-28 nm of the silicon dioxide layer with RIE and 
to remove the last few nm by using wet etching. This way, the final holes are much 
cleaner and fewer impurities are found on top of the exposed silicon substrate. It is very 
important to assure a correct growth of the nanopillars because contamination and parti-
cles on the bottom of the holes cause a severe harm in the yield of the nanopillars.  
Reactive Ion etching is a type of dry etching that uses chemically reactive plasma en-
hanced by an electrical field to remove material deposited on the wafers. The plasma is 
generated in a vacuum because increases the mean free path of the ions. A typical RIE 
system consists of a vacuum chamber with two parallel plates in which the plasma is 
generated by an alternating electrical field. The electrons are accelerated and hit the 
walls of the chamber where they are absorbed. However, some of these electrons will 
hit the wafer and the surface will build up a negative charge. Now the positive ions are 
directed to the wafer platter by a large voltage difference created between the plates. 
These ions react chemically with the materials in the sample but they can also sputter 
some material. This process allows for a vertical delivery of the ions which result in a 
very anisotropic etch profile.

The results of this etch test showed a significant non-uniformity of the etching across 
the wafer, being more pronounced on the edge of the wafer. This non-uniformity across 
the wafer surface is a well-known process that has been reported by other users of the 
RIE2 system in Danchip. It also showed inconsistency on the etch rate itself. It is be-

ions accelerated by the potential difference between the plates. This effect was in-
creased after several etch tests because the created polymers filled up the chamber and 
therefore the deposition rate of these polymers was increased. And as a consequence of 
that, the etch rate was reduced. This inconsistency in the etch rate is a big problem be-

the pre-condition process that was done on a dummy wafer prior to the etch rate test was 
completely removed. This pre-condition step contributes to the formation of polymers in 
the chamber and it does not present any additional benefits. Also, the aluminum carrier 
in which the sample was originally placed was removed to match the conditions of pre-
vious reports using a very similar recipe[27]. Finally, the most important consideration 
was to perform a very long cleaning process that lasted for 1 hour before the first etch 
test, and also very long cleaning steps of 30 minutes each in between each of the tests 
performed to assure that the chamber was clean of polymers at the beginning of each 
test. The extra-long cleaning step of 1 hour at the beginning of the process assured to 
remove any contamination in the chamber generated by other users with different mate-
rials. In addition, it was very convenient due to the reparations that the RIE2 systems 
underwent during that period of time.

The measurements showed an etch depth of around 14-15 nm even though the aimed 
depth was around 25-28 nm. There are many possible explanations for this difference in 
the etched depth. 
First of all, differences related to the aspect ratio. RIE etch rate is dependent on aspect 
ratio and pattern geometry. Even for the same recipe, the etch rate of narrow trenches or 
small holes is slower than that of large diameter holes or wider trenches[28]. 
Also, all the etch rate tests were made using thermally oxidized wafers. The value of the 
etch rate in the real sample may be slightly different due to the CSAR resist layer. Some 
polymers from the resist composition may be etched or sputtered away during the pro-
cess and deposited on the sample and, as a consequence of that, the etch rate may be 
reduced. 
==PAGE:59==
 
Chapter 3. Nano-Patterned Substrate Fabrication  
57 
 It was also considered that the crystallographic orientation of the substrate can affect 
the properties of the silicon dioxide grown on top. Therefore, it was expected that 
<100> and <111> substrate would produce oxides with different properties, that can 
have different etch rates. However, it was found in the literature that crystallographic 
orientation is a property of the substrate only, and it does not affect the structure of the 
oxide once it is grown. That means that thermally grown oxide on a <100> substrate has 
the exact same properties as the one grown in a <111> substrate[29]. Thus, this consid-
eration was discarded. 
Finally,  some considerations about the AFM measurements themselves, that were like-
ly to have a big impact on the measured depth of the holes. First of all, the bottom of 
each hole is not completely flat which is a big indicator of the fact that the tip of the 
AFM could not reach the bottom of the hole and the depth is therefore, not correct. Also 
the “unidentified polymer” that was present on the surface of the wafer and inside the 
holes could interact with the tip of the AFM and provide a false depth measurement. On 
top of that, there are other effects commonly associated with AFM measurements like 
tip folding that can have negative impact on the depth.  
It was believed that, even though inside the holes the etch rate was slower and the depth 
was probably less than the expected 28 nm, the measurements made by AFM were not 
very trustworthy. It was due to the small diameter of the holes and the fact that the pro-
files did not show a flat bottom. The evidence to support this idea was the fact that a 
very similar recipe had already been tested successfully in rather similar condition [27].

Then the 4” wafer was soft-baked at 90 ºC for 90 seconds. After that, the wafer was

Since the samples were out of the Clean room for dicing, an ultrasonic soap treatment in 
DI water and Triton x100 for 10 minutes had to be done before bringing the samples

before being blow-dried with N2.   
After the dicing, a standard cleaning process (RCA) was done to remove any impurities 
formed during the previous steps.  The cleaning process was divided into two steps 
called standard clean 1 and standard clean 2 (SC-1 and SC-2 respectively). 
SC-1 was used to remove light organic compounds, particles, and metals. The SC-1 
contains H2O, NH4OH (29%), and H2O (30%) with a volume ratio of 5:1:1. The sam-
ples were dipped into SC-1 at 70 ºC during 10 minutes. 
The SC-2 is used for removal of alkalis, residual metals and metal hydroxides. It is 
composed of H2O, HCl (37%), and H2O2 (30%) with a volume ratio of 5:1:1. The sam-
ples were also dipped into SC-2 at 70 ºC during 10 minutes. 
Traditional preparations of standard RCA cleaning include hydrofluoric acid (HF) but it 
was eliminated from our preparation to avoid unwanted SiO2 etching. The RCA process 
works by oxidizing the surface of the sample by H2O2 and then removing the generated 
oxide by the effect of acids and bases. By the combination of oxide generation and sub-
sequent removal, the sample surface can be cleaned.

Chapter conclusion 
In this chapter, a fabrication method to define an array of nanoholes patterned on a sili-
con substrate with SiO2 layer is presented in detail. CSAR resist is tested for its high 
sensitivity and sub 10-nm resolution. Electron beam lithography is used to define the 
array of nanoholes into the resist. It was found out that the dose as high as 550 C/cm2 
was necessary for the correct development of the structures. It was noticed that the most 
important feature for the correct exposure was to create an accurate height map that 
would allow to perfectly focus the beam on the surface. During this process it was dis-
covered that the subroutine HEIMAP used by default during the calibration process of 
the electron beam gun was not accurate enough in our case. Therefore, the introduction 
of virtual chip marks was necessary to create an accurate height map.  The inspection of 
the nanoholes by SEM and AFM showed a hole diameter very close to the one selected

method is its long writing time and an expensive equipment that traduces to into low 
throughput which makes the process unsuitable for mass production.  
The patterning was completed with other common techniques in micro and 
nanofabrication, for instance, Reactive Ion Etching or wet etching. The challenge with

CONCLUSION 
This chapter provides a summary of the findings in this project. 
 
5.1 
Results 
 
In the first part of this project, FDTD simulations were performed to analyze the absorp-
tion by studying the reflectance of a tandem solar cell composed of a GaAsP nanopillar 
solar cell and a silicon solar cell that acts as a substrate of the previous one. The focus 
of the project was only on the GaAsP nanopillar solar cell, with a range of interest form 
400 nm to 730 nm due to the bandgap of 1.7 eV of the GaAsP solar cell. 
 
Simulations sweeping a big range of diamters, pitches, and heights were performed to 
analyze the reflectance of the nanopillars and therefore to choose the set of optimal pa-
rameters that minimize the reflectance.  
The results obtained show that a large height of the nanopillars reduces the reflectance 
compared with a small height. Nevertheless, the height parameter was the less relevant 
of the studied ones. 
 
The diameter of the nanopillars play a more important job in the reflectance obtained. 
Relatively small diameters (around 300 nm) seemed to have less reflectance compared 
with large ones. At increasing values of the diameter, the reflectance approaches the one 
of the planar substrate.  
 
The center to center pitch was also an important geometrical parameter. The best results 
obtained for this parameter was on structures with 600 nm of pitch.  
 
The tiling pattern of the nanopillar array was also considered. Based on the work of 
Mariani et al. it was concluded that the tiling of the nanopillars has no significant effect 
on the absorption. For simplicity, the tiling pattern selected for the simulations and the 
fabrication part was a square array. 
 
Finally angle dependent measures of the reflectance were carried out. A fairly inde-
pendent reflectance with the incidence angle of the source was observed. 
==PAGE:85==
Chapter 5. Conclusion 
83 
In the second part, the fabrication of a substrate by electron beam lithography to pro-
duce a positioned array of nanopillars was demonstrated.  The selected resist was CSAR 
because of its high sensitivity and resolution. High uniformity and repeatability of the 
diameters of the holes were achieved. The introduction of the virtual chip marks during 
the electron beam exposure was necessary because the patter designed was composed of 
small chips. Therefore, a local measure of the high map was necessary to achieve uni-
formity and avoid stitching problems. It was also discovered that in order to achieve 
consistent etch rates in the RIE, it was required to apply long clean processes and re-
move the precondition steps. This part was crucial in our project due to the small thick-
ness of the layer in which the etch was needed.  
 
The last part of the project was to characterize the samples with the nanopillar arrays 
measuring the reflectance using the integrating sphere and the goniometer. 
 
Measurements of the reflectance were performed on the sample with higher yield of 
nanopillars. The SEM characterization of the samples showed parameters far away from 
the optimal ones selected based on the simulations. Therefore, new simulations were 
carried out to provide a more accurate result. 
 
The measurements showed that the pitch of 600 nm had the lower reflectance as pre-
dicted by the simulations. It also showed independence of the incidence angle of the 
light source as it was also predicted by the simulations.  
 
The new simulations showed a certain grade of agreement with the measurements. 
Some peak shifts in the simulations with respect to the real measurements were also 
observed. These shift could be explained by the low yield of the nanopillars, the large 
diameter variation, and height variation.  
 
 
 
5.2 
Further work 
 
In order to improve the work presented in this project, characterization of samples with 
high yield and geometrical parameters close to the ones designed is recommended. In 
addition electrical characterization and measures of Voc, Isc, fill factor and efficiency is 
necessary to create a competitive product that can compete in the global photovoltaic 
market
