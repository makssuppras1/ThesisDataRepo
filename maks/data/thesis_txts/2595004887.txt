Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to assess the potential of exploiting the increased ﬂexibility in the Nordic
electricity market by installing an ice tank, that, controlled by an NMPC algorithm, can curtail the
refrigeration system in a supermarket, hence equivalently increasing the ﬂexibility of the refrigeration
systems energy consumption.
The approach consists of three main parts. Initially, we develop three stochastic state-space models of
increasing complexity to describe the dynamics of the compressor capacity in the refrigeration system
as a function of the mode of the ice tank. We do not succeed in including nonlinearities on the state
equations, but do indeed improve existing models. From thereon, we outline key markets within the
Nordic electricity market and on top of these, we derive two optimal control problems, for scheduling
the mode of the ice tank over a prediction horizon. The two OPCs assume that the ice-tank can act
as price-taker on only the day-ahead market or both the day-ahead and the regulation power market,
respectively. These two parts culminate in the deﬁnition of two NMPC algorithms.
The controllers are evaluated over short as well as long term in-silico simulations comparing the
performances across the three models and the number of allowed mode cycles. The simulations show
that a relative saving around 11.5 % is achievable in the day-ahead market while participating in both
markets have the potential of saving close to 14 %. The studies indicate that even higher savings can
be expected in real physical tests. Finally, no signiﬁcant diﬀerences across neither the model choices
nor number of switching times were found.
Lastly, we assess the perspectives in replacing the optimizer in the NMPC algorithms with an oﬄine
trained ANN invoking a vast reduction of time spent in the online phase. Taking the low amount of
training data and network tuning into account, the networks show excellent performances. Regarding
the day-ahead market, the network performs just as good as the original NMPC, while the performance
only decreases slightly when we participate in both markets.

Summary (Danish)
Målet med denne afhandling er at vurdere potentialet i at udnytte den øgede ﬂeksibilitet i det nordiske
elmarked ved at installere en istank, der, reguleret af en ikke-lineær model-prædiktiv regulator, kan
aﬂaste kølesystemet i et supermarked og dermed også øge ﬂeksibiliteten af kølesystemets energiforbrug.
Tilgangen består af tre hoveddele. Til at begynde med udvikler vi tre stokastiske state-space modeller
af stigende kompleksitet til at beskrive kølesystemets kompressorkapacitets dynamik som en funktion
af istankens tilstand. Vi lykkes ikke med at inkorporere ikke-lineariteter i state-ligningerne, men vi
formår at forbedre eksisterende modeller. Derfra redegør vi for nøglemarkeder under det nordiske el-
marked og udleder, baseret på disse, to optimal-reguleringproblemer, med de formål at skemalægge
istankens tilstand over en prædiktionshorisont. De to optimal-reguleringsproblemer antager, at istan-
ken kan agere som pristager på henholdsvis udelukkende day-ahead-markedet og på både day-ahead-
og regulerkraft-markedet. Disse to dele kulminerer i deﬁnitionen af to ikke-lineære model-prædiktive
regulatorer.
Vi evaluarer regulatorne over kort- såvel som langsigtede in-silico simuleringer, hvormed præstationerne
over de tre modeller og antal tilladte status-cykler kan sammenlignes. Simuleringerne viser at en relativ
besparelse på omkring 11,5 % er mulig på day-ahead-markedet, mens det at agere på begge markeder,
har potentiale til at opnå relative besparelser på omkring 14 %. Dog peger studierne i en retning af,
at endnu større besparelser kan forventes i virkelige fysiske eksperimenter. Endeligt er der ikke noget,
der peger på, at der er signiﬁkant forskel mellem hverken modelvalg eller antal tilladte cykler.
Til slut vurderer vi perspektiverne i at erstatte optimeringsmodulet i model-prædiktionsregulatoren
med på forhånd trænede kunstige neurale netværk til drastisk at reducere tiden brugt i online-fasen.
Taget i betragtning hvor lille et træningssæt, der anvendes, samt hvor lidt netværkene bliver justeret,
præsterer disse fremragende. Vedrørende day-ahead-markedet præsterer netværket lige så godt som
den originale regulator, mens præstationen kun falder en smule, når der deltages i begge markeder .

Chapter 1
Introduction
As a part of the global rollout of the green transition these years, the thirst for optimal utilization
of renewable energy sources is ever-growing. In the period 2004 to 2018, the proportion of consumed
energy extracted from renewable sources had an increase by almost double, from 9.6 % to 18.9 %
[Eur]. Additionally, more or less binding but ambitious, climate goals are taking their entrance on
the political scene. That is agreements like the EU 2030 goal of cutting its greenhouse gas emissions
by at least 40 % compared to 1990 [Cou14], the Danish climate bill of cutting down with 70 % in
the same period and target 100 % in 2050 [oG19] and an EU goal of at least 27 % renewable energy
consumption by 2030 [Cou14].
Hence, studies and development of technological tools to optimize
national and global energy consumption is of large interest.
A paradigm shift in energy production
These political progressions in how we conceive energy production and consumption entails an increase
in the share of energy produced from renewable sources rather than traditional biofuel. However, in
contrast to the black energy, our control of the production is limited by the amount of wind blowing,
sun shining, etc.
That is, as our dependence on renewable energy sources increase, so does our
vulnerability to unbalances between the demand side and production side in the energy ecosystem.
The most obvious and heuristic way to solve the imbalance problem would be to simply store all
surplus energy generated in massive batteries generating an enormous amount of energy that can
be used whenever needed. However, neither does such batteries yet exist nor does it seem like an
aﬀordable solution [Rev18].
Optimal control of supermarket refrigeration systems
One way to address this problem is to explore the possibilities of moving the ﬂexibility from the supply
side (where we have traditionally been able to up- and down adjust the production) to the demand
side. This ﬁeld of research experience great interest these days, whereas especially ﬂexible heating of
buildings, is being well studied [ALZ+16] [LDH16]. Another large player in the omnipresent energy grid
is the supermarkets, that are absolutely crucial in our perception of social infrastructure. In Denmark,
roughly 2 % of the annual energy consumption is consumed by the around 4,500 supermarkets spread
over the country. Besides heating, lightning of the buildings, substantial shares of the energy is used
for cooling [HLJ11] that can account for up to 50-80% of the total energy consumption [HLSJ11]. A

2.1
The system of interest and data acquisition
The focal point of this thesis is a refrigeration system in a supermarket to which an ice tank has been
installed. The refrigeration system is driven by a controller that monitors the temperature in the
refrigerator while being in charge of controlling the workload, that the compressors needs to deliver
to maintain a stable and desirable temperature.
In short, a refrigeration system works in the way, that a refrigerant (liquid) is sent around in the
system removing heat from a low-temperature object while rejecting it at higher temperature. In this
way heat can be transported eﬃciently from within the object, that is to be cooled down, to outside.
The journey of the refrigerant is denoted the refrigeration cycle. Depending on where in the cycle the
refrigerant is located, it can either be liquid or gas.
In the following key aspects of the refrigeration cycle are elaborated.
• The refrigerant enters the evaporator as a low-pressure cold part liquid part vapour. Here it picks
up heat from the surrounding air becoming a low-pressure warm gas, leaving the surrounding
air colder than before.
• The refrigerant enters the compressor, which, using electrical power, increases the pressure of
the gas. Hence also the temperature increases.
• The refrigerant enters the condenser which is located outside. In the same way as in the evap-
orator, yet with a change of sign, the refrigerant loses temperature to the colder air outside.
The refrigerant leaves the condenser as a high-pressure regular temperature gas. The colder the
outside temperature is, the colder the refrigerant will become.
• Under normal circumstances, the refrigerant ﬁnally passes through the expansion valve that only
allows small amounts of refrigerant to pass through at the time. This lowers the pressure of the
refrigerant and causes it to become part liquid, part vapour. At a lower pressure, the refrigerant
also boils at a lower temperature. Hence, the refrigerant easier evaporates thus undergoing the
phase change to low-pressure warm gas. This ends the cycle, that can now start over.
However, as it can be seen from Figure 2.1, the cycle that we consider in this thesis also contains
an extra component; A sub cooler between the condenser and the expansion valve. The sub-cooler is
able to cool down the refrigerant even more than what is achieved in the condenser, hence emulating
an extreme cold outside temperature. This emulation provided by a well isolated ice tank located

2.2

2.2.1
A brief introduction to Grey-Box Modelling
Grey-box modelling is the compromise between black box and white box modelling. In Black-Box
modelling we solely use the structure of the data to derive a model. That is, we do not take the world
that the data origins from into account. If the amount of data is large enough, this often leads to
==PAGE:28==
8
Modelling the Ice Tank
very strong models in terms of describing phenomenons that are close to the data used. However,
the parameters in the model will not be interpretable and the model would not have any physical
meaning. Furthermore, it can be very hard to extrapolate from such models, since they are tightly
connected to the data domain. In White-Box modelling, we build the model from the ﬁrst principles.
This ensures, that the model applies widely and that we understand every aspect of this. However,
those can be extremely hard (or impossible) to both derive and to identify due to measurement errors
and noise the process of interest. In this thesis, we consider Grey-Box modelling. Grey-box modelling
is the compromise between the two other approaches in the sense that we provide a model of the
dynamics of interest (white box), but deduce the model parameters from the available data taking
random noise in the process and the measurement errors into account (black box). In practice, the
modelling consists of a set of stochastic diﬀerential equations describing the continuous dynamics in
the system as well as a set of observation equations. We then estimate the parameters in the model by
the available data. This way, we obtain a compromise between prior physical knowledge of the system
and shaping the model from the data.
2.2.2
Overall model deﬁnition
In the following section, we present three diﬀerent models of varying complexity and levels of physical
insight. We want to model the process as a continuous-discrete stochastic state-space system. That
is,
dx(t) = f(x(t), u(t); θ) dt + g(x(t), u(t); θ)) dω(t),
(2.1a)
y(tk) = h(x(tk)) + vk.
(2.1b)
Here y is the measured compressor capacity in percent, x is some underlying state describing the
system and u is the controllable input to the system. The measurement error on observation k is
assumed to be normally distributed with mean zero and some variance, vk ∼Niid (0, Rk). Since the
measurement tool is the same for all observations and the data acquisition time spans a relatively
short amount of time, it seems fair to assume that the precision is unchanged over time. Thus we
assume that R = Rk for all k. ω is a standard Brownian motion introducing some random behaviour

2.3
Parameter estimation
An estimate of the parameters in the model is the set of parameters that was most likely to have
generated the data observed given the model. We consider the method outlined in [KMJ04] that
takes advantage of the Continuous Discrete Extended Kalman Filter (CDEKF). This ﬁlter is originally
developed to continuously estimate the value of underlying states in nonlinear models, ﬁnding the best
trade-oﬀbetween what is expected by the model and what is actually observed. However, [KMJ04]
suggests that this procedure can also be used to estimate the likelihood of the observed data given the
model and parameters. The likelihood is then maximised over the parameter space.
2.3.1
Continuous Discrete Extended Kalman Filter
CDEKF is an iterative ﬁlter, that in each iteration computes a prediction of how the system is going to
evolve and then performs an update of the current estimate of the values the system’s states based on
the prediction and an observation. If the model is linear and the diﬀusion function, g, is time-invariant,
the process is Gaussian and hence, the evolution of the states from one time step to another, can be
completely characterized by its mean and covariance. We deﬁne the CDEKF by linearizing f and g
around the trajectory ˙x = f(x(t), u(t); θ) obtaining a linear SDE from which one-step predictions of
the mean and covariance can be obtained.
At each time step, tk, the ﬁlter produces a one-step prediction of the mean and covariance matrix of
the states at tk based on the estimate of these at tk−1. Hence, the mean can be used as a predictor
of the current state while the covariance is an expression of how certain we are about this prediction.
Afterwards, we hold these predictions against the observations, yk, to compute an estimate of the
current states and the associated covariance matrix.
In the following, we describe the mean and
covariance estimation procedure.

2.5.2
Second order model
We now expand the model with another state to better capture the oscillations there are clearly seen
around what we called steady states in the previous model. It is well known that the compressors
in refrigeration systems are controlled by microcontrollers with the responsibility of keeping the right
temperature in the refrigerators. This is most likely a kind of PID controller, since these are the far
most common for industrial application [Ben01].
PID controller are in general likely to suﬀer from over/under shooting [Ben01] resulting in the con-
trolled variable to perform damped oscillations around some value until the controlled variable ﬁnds
rest at the set point. When inspecting the data it seems very likely that this is the case; Every time
the regime changes, we see some damped oscillations.
To imitate this behaviour, we develop a model, where the drift part in both regimes is damped
harmonic oscillations. We then try to estimate the parameters that would correspond to the physical
parameters in the corresponding physical damped spring system.

Chapter 3
The Nordic Electricity Market
Trading of electrical power in and between Nordic and Baltic regions, Germany, France, the Nether-
lands, Belgium, Austria and the UK is handled by the power exchange Nord Pool AS owned by
Euronext and the Nordic and Baltic Transmission System Operators (TSOs)[Poo19]. The exchange
cover 15 interconnected price areas. The TSOs are in charge of one area each, and the one with respon-
sible of operating in Denmark is called Energinet.dk. The TSOs are responsible for securing balance
in the power grid and security of supply as well as a well-functioning competitive markets [Ene09].
The power prices are determined by supply and demand [Poob]. As a result of the green transition
evolving right now, the amount of power supplied from renewable sources are vastly increasing [Eur],
which implies much more uncertainty in the power grid across Europe than earlier. This challenges the
TSOs in terms of securing the balance and security of supply. Equivalently, the power price becomes
much harder to predict, since we (even with the state of art weather models) are not able to perfectly
predict e.g. the wind and thus the amount of electricity produced by wind turbines. In the following,
we brieﬂy introduce and discuss four markets operated by Nord Pool and the TSOs that together play
a big role in securing a balance between supply and demand.
Normally, end power users like a supermarket are the last in the electricity food chain, and thus
cannot participate directly in the markets outlined below, but must instead purchase power through a
supplier at their sell price [Pooa]. In this thesis, we assume that the ice tank controller is price taker
in the markets. For a single supermarket this infeasible due to the consumed volume, but connecting
multiple supermarkets on a national level, this is indeed conceivable.
3.1
Day-Ahead Market
The Day-Ahead market is an across Europe electricity market facilitating day to day electricity trading.
It is by far the biggest market and more than 70 % of the total northern Europe electricity consumption
is traded here. The market works in the following way. Every day at 10.00 CET the market opens.
Until 12.00 CET sellers publish bids in terms of the power volume they are willing to sell at what
price. Oppositely, buyers publish their bids in terms of how much power they want to purchase and
at what price level they willing to pay [Ene19a]. No later than 13:00 CET, Nord Pool matches the
oﬀers and bids. As a result of this matching process, prices for each hour during the next 24 hours are
derived. The prices are settled as the price intersection between the ordered bids from demand and
supply side with respect to the current capacity in the grid. That is, everybody in the same local areas
receives the same ﬁnal price though diﬀerent price levels were bided [Poob]. We denote this price the
spot price. The next day the procedure repeats itself.

Even within a small country like Denmark, the price can vary because diﬀerent areas are connected
diﬀerently in the grid across Europe. In this thesis we consider the prices from the area denoted in
the market as DK1 [Ene16]. This refers to the western part of Denmark [Ene20a]. Additionally, all
presented price data has been extracted from Energinet.dk’s market database [Ene20b].
In Figure 3.1, a 48-hour spot price example can found. For now, we only consider the spot price. It
is easy to see how the price varies heavily throughout the day. These changes are due to the dynamic
market governed by the supply and demand bids. E.g. around hour 17 the price intersection between
the bids was located very high. This happens when the demand-side participants are very willing to
purchase relative to how inclined the production-side participants are to sell. This is likely to happen
in hours where the need for power is large. In contrast, the prices are quite low in the night hours,
where the demand side probably don’t need as much power as the production side wants to sell.

Though buyers and sellers have agreed on a speciﬁc amount of production and consumption, based on
the expected needs and weather forecasts, the production side obviously can never guarantee that the
actual amount of produced power will match this, due to e.g. prediction errors in the weather forecast
models. Likewise, the demand-side player cannot be sure how the exact amount of needed power will
unfold. Hence, there is a risk that either too much or little power is produced in a given hour. But it
is critical to assure balance in the power grid, since we currently do not have eﬃcient power storing
tools at hand to store signiﬁcant quantities of excess power [Rev18]. On the other hand, deﬁcit power
will course a fault in the system. Since the social infrastructure heavily built upon the expectation
that it is always possible to get power, this would be fatal.
In short, it is crucial that buyers and sellers can adapt throughout the day by changing there original
day-ahead schedule.
3.2
Intra-day Market
The ﬁrst tool applied in the strive of balance in the grid is the Intra-Day market operated by Nord
Pool, that opens when the day-ahead market closes. Here, electricity is traded just like securities. That
is, in contrast to the day-ahead market, the seller and buyer settle on a price unique for the given
trade instead of everybody a receiving a common price. The intra-day market closes 45 minutes prior
to the hour of operation and can thus adapt to changes in forecast models and expected consumption.

3.3 Regulation Power Market
35
3.3
Regulation Power Market
Simultaneously with intra-day market, it is also possible to bid on a third market: The regulation
power market. This is one of tools used by the TSOs to secure balance in the grid if the intra-day
trading is not suﬃcient. The regulation power market is a bidding market in the sense that market
players submit how much and at what cost they are willing to deviate from there plan in case of
imbalances. Like the intra-day market this is possible until 45 minutes prior to the hour of operation
[Ene17]. The market is shared among the TSOs in Norhern Europe and the bids are aggregated within
the Nordic Operational Information System (NOIS). The TSOs then assess if a grid imbalance of a
signiﬁcant volume is expected. This governs three scenarios. Since this thesis considers a demand-side
problem, we present the scenarios from a consumers point of view and refer to the footnotes for the
production-sides point of view.
• Down regulation: There is a surplus in the produced power. Market participants can commit
to increase their power consumption at a lower price level than the spot price.1
• Status quo: There is no signiﬁcant imbalance in the grid.
• Up regulation: There is a power deﬁcit in the grid. Market participants can sell power (that
is still virtual at this stage) acquired in the day-ahead market at a higher price than the spot
price.2
Depending on which of the three scenarios and the volume of the imbalance, regulation power bids
are activated. By activation, we mean that the TSOs buy or sell the bidded volume of power. That
is, if the TSOs expects a surplus of 5MWh, the n bids with the lowest price necessary to accumulate
a volume of 5 MWh is activated. They all recieve the same price pr MWh for selling there repsective
volumes of power. The price is settled at the highest of bidding prices such that everybody recieves a
price at least as good as they bidded. If status quo is expected, no bids are activated.

the amount produced electricity and the scheduled production. To ensure ﬂexibility in the grid, the

problem, we will only discuss the ﬁrst case.
In short, the balancing power market ensures that one can simply use more or less power than notiﬁed.
The balancing power market, however, is not a bidding market like the day-ahead and regulation power
market. In this market, one simply has to pay/sell at a predeﬁned price. The price is set using the
One-price-model which simply states, that the imbalance is settled at the regulation power price from
the given hour of imbalance [Ene17]. In there is no regulation the player receives the spot price.

4.1.3
Approximations of piecewise constant functions
As a very last consideration before establishing the full OCP, we will discuss how the piecewise constant
functions in the problem can be approximated by smooth approximations.
Since the prices jumps at each change of hour, q(t) it s piecewise constant function with discontinuities
at each whole hour t = 0 mod 60. That is, it is discontinuous at every whole hour and ﬂat otherwise. In
short, most optimization algorithm relies on an iterative process computing/approximating gradients
and Hessians of the object function in a point updating this point based on the these.
The ﬂat
and discontinuous structure of the price function heavily challenges this procedure. First of all, the
derivates of the problem is undeﬁned at these discontinuities implying that approximations of the
==PAGE:64==

derivatives in these points will not make sense.
Secondly, very superﬁcially phrased, the way the
algorithm approximates gradients and Hessians can be interpreted in the way, that the algorithm is
detecting how appropriately small changes in the optimization variable aﬀect the objective function.
But if the price function is completely ﬂat, it will not be able to see any diﬀerence in the objective
function for small changes of the input; It will not be able to discover e.q. lower price structures in
the neighbouring hour. Thus, the piecewise constant structure of u is most likely to challenge the
algorithms.

4.3
Deﬁning a Nonlinear Model Predictive Control Loop
To facilitate the optimal control system, we construct an NMPC loop as the one sketched in Figure
4.5.
At 12:00 each day, the system estimates the current state of the system based on a system
model. The key component in a closed loop NMPC is the ability to adjust the model based on system
observations using a CDEKF. In this project, we have chosen to let the system use the predictions
from the previous day as the current estimated state. This makes it an open loop NMPC. This is, in
practice, equivalent to one huge OCP divided into sub-problems for each day. This decision has been
made on behalf of the fact, that despite an observation correction of the one-step prediction, the used
models would return so fast to the previous ODE solution relative to the time scale, that there most
likely would be no diﬀerence in the results. The only situations where this is expected to great impact,
would be if the model believed that the system was in the idle regime whilst it, in reality, was in
melt or the opposite for a longer period. Due to the way the process is deﬁned and the deterministic
inﬂuence from the input, this should never occur. For industrial application, though, a CDEKF most
likely would indeed be installed for model corrections.

One of the strengths of an (N)MPC is the diﬀerence between the prediction horizon and the evaluation
horizon. The fact that we have a model for the system, allows us to predict the state of the system at
the next time step (and further on!). That is, when ﬁnding the optimal input to the system, we also
take the predicted future into account using our model, instead of limiting ourself to only look at the
near future. This way, one, to some extend, secure one-self, that a greedy optimization algorithm will
not risk everything by e.g. using all the stored ice, to get a low cost in the coming day. Instead, it will
always have to keep Day 2 in mind when deciding how much should be gambled throughout Day 2.
4.3.1
Solving the OCPs
Based on the current state of the system, the NMPC solves the upcoming 48 hours based on one of
the two OCPs discussed above using ipopt. We consider a single shooting method in the sense that
in the optimization algorithm, we solve the integral over the entire prediction horizon each time the
objective function is evaluated and simply impose the linear constraints previously discussed. As the
complexity of the process increases, this might be computationally ineﬃcient due to the possible high
number of required objective function evaluations. [DBDW06] suggests a method for faster solutions
to nonlinear optimal control problems. This, they denote Fast Direct Multiple Shooting. The method
divides the prediction horizon into N consecutive intervals linked together by boundary constraint for
==PAGE:72==
52
Optimal Control of Switching Times
each sub-problem. This way the problem is transformed into a system of N equations, that can then
be solved parallelised to decrease the required computation time.
Since this is not a study of computational eﬃciency, we stick to the single shooting method. The prob-
lem is solved using the nonlinear optimization framework ipopt (Interior Point Optimizer) presented
and derived in [WB06]. The method implements an interior point line search ﬁlter method to solve a
given constrained nonlinear Program (NLP). One can either provide the analytic or approximated ﬁrst
and second order derivates to ipopt. The ﬁrst OCP is phrased in such a way, that is allows explicit
computation of the analytic derivative as described in [EWD03]. However, this is rather cumber-
some involving computing a costate to the cost function by solving a backward diﬀerential equation.
Furthermore, this only applies to the simple case and not the extended one acting on both markets.
Instead we use the numeric framework cppad (C++ Automatic Diﬀerentiation) that, in brief, es-
tablishes a way to compute the gradient and Hessian analytically in terms of a graphs of symbolic
operations [Bel13] [GW00]. Provided the objective function implemented as a C++ algorithm, cppad
delivers the necessary derivatives, that can then be fed to the algorithm applied by ipopt.

5.2.1.1
Optimal number of switches
Intuitively, one would except that, with a perfect optimizer that always ﬁnds the global minimum, the
more possible switching cycles the better. This is because all solution with ms switching times are
contained in the manifold of solutions with ns switching times when ns > ms since the optimizer is
always allowed to, in practice, collapse a mode by creating a melt or idle period with inﬁnitesimal
lengths.
Since we cannot guarantee that the optimizer ipopt ﬁnds the global minimum or even
converges within a feasible amount of iterations we cannot be sure that this technique in practice will
be accomplished. Such an iteration limit, 200 iterations, has been found necessary to apply such that
results could be collected in a feasible amount of time. By inspecting the optimzation path, it has
been veriﬁed that the algorithm often converges or at the maximum iteration only considers parameter
adjustments with a maximum size of a few minutes or seconds. This however, could easily mean that
the optimizer has found a bad local minimum due to over parametrization of the problem. Hence this
comparison. Unfortunately, it has not been possible to extract the optimization history from ipopt
and thus we cannot substantiate these claims by quantiﬁcation.

5.2.2
Day-Ahead Market combined with Regulation Power Market
We now continue to the case where we extend the idea from [BGH+20] to letting the controller act
on both the day-ahead and the regulation power market. Recall that this means that the controller
can buy a certain amount of power in a given hour based on one set of switching times, but at the
end of the day, distribute the switching times diﬀerently to exploit the ﬂexibility in the market. We
denote the optimal solution to the OCP (4.44) the optimal switching times, τ ∗. Recall, that this
solution consists of a set of switching times dictating how to buy power on the day-ahead market and
a set describing how we are really going to control the ice tank. We denote the ﬁrst the optimal spot
switches, τ ∗
s and the latter the optimal true switches, τ ∗
r .
The results are structured equivalently to the section above, initially evaluating the diﬀerence between
a diﬀerent number of allowed switching cycles, followed by a long-term evaluation of the controllers.
Before evaluating the performance, we visualize an example of how the new controller distributes the
switching times with respect to the price structure. In Figure (5.13), an optimal solution suggested
by ipopt using the ﬁrst-order model is shown. We see how the controller highly exploits the ﬂexible
electricity prices. E.g. from hour 42 to 48 the spot switches (orange) makes sure that a lot of electricity
is bought on the day-ahead market (be dictating idle), while the true switches (green) tells the ice
tank to be in melt mode which uses less power. Hence, the system uses less power than bought
on the day-ahead market, which can then be sold at a higher price than the purchased due to the
up-regulation period. The opposite pattern unfolds in the down-regulation period from hour 18 to 22.
Here the two sets of switching times collaborate to buy a lot less power than actually needed, such
that the remaining power can be bought at a cheaper price in the regulation power market.

Final remark on the results

when prices are high. For the Combined problem, surplus power is consistently bought on the day-
ahead market at up-regulation hours and vice versa in down-regulation hours. Though the optimization
problem has great insight in the exact expected costs, the solutions follow the price structure so sharply,
that one could suspect that the same solution (even without violating the constraints!) could have
been achieved manually. This work, however, constitutes a baseline that can be extended to consider
systems that exploit the ﬂexibility in the electricity market even further and plausible in ways, where
one could not reproduce the optimal solutions manually. Finally, one of the aims of mathematical
modelling is, besides achieving results of greater quality, to automatize tasks that previously required
human interaction to solve. The results deﬁnitely show that both thing are indeed accomplished.

In the previous chapter, it was clear how solving the problems of ﬁnding optimal switching times
is a complex task consisting of a vast number of rather cumbersome function evaluations leading to
long computation times. In this chapter, we address the perspective of signiﬁcantly decreasing (in
practice neglecting) the time spent in the online phase using an Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN).
The literature contains a lot of work concerning applying this idea to a vast number of scientiﬁc
computation problems in diﬀerent shapes. [MHEB19] presents (among other) a method for evaluating
solutions to nonlinear time-dependent PDE-constrained optimization problems, while [AKL05] strive
to solve the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation associated with control inputs. Common to them,
is that they both solve their respective problem using their respectively traditional methods multiple
times and then use the solutions, in some way or another, to train an ANN.
In this chapter, we apply this idea to the problem of estimating optimal switching times, by learning
a regression model, that by a relatively small amount of computations, can generate a set of switching
times based on the current system observation and a set of electricity market prices. That is, in this
proof of concept, we wish to move the majority of the computational work connected to estimation
switching times, from the online phase to an oﬄine phase, that has learned the structure of the
solutions prior to application.
Initially, we, very brieﬂy, elaborate on the main concepts of ANNs and discuss how this can be applied
to compute optimal switching times. From thereon, we present how training data is acquired and
develop a number of networks.
Eventually, we present an evaluation of how the ANNs performs
compared to actually solving the optimization problems using ipopt.
6.1
Outline oﬀthe two phases
As described we want to split the online phase used so far into two:
Oﬄine phase: We solve the problem of interest a large number of times, collecting all the solutions
and associated inputs provided to the optimizer.
We then train a neural network to imitate the
behaviour of the optimizer by learning it to reconstruct the optimal switching time solutions based
on those inputs. As seen in Chapter 5, solving the optimization problem can potentially take a long
time, why the oﬄine possibly will be rather extensive.
==PAGE:94==
74
Artiﬁcial Neural Network for Switching Times Scheduling
Online phase: In the NMPC framework, we replace the optimizer with the trained neural network
from the oﬄine phase.
The time it takes to generate a solution is now so short, that the online
computation time can in practice be neglected.
We will denote this framework Neural Network Predictive Control (NNPC) and in the following brieﬂy
describe how a neural network regression model can be built for supervised learning of the switching
time problem.

PyTorch implementation and data acquisition
The development of a supervised ANN consists of three main objectives. These are:
1. Collect labelled data used to train the network.
2. Deﬁne the architecture of the network and implement the model in a framework for training
such.
3. Construct a loss function that responsible for evaluating how well a given set of solution predic-
tions matches the true ones and establish of a training algorithm based on this.
In this section, we brieﬂy touch upon the key considerations within each objective.

then not guaranteed that the output of transformation is within [0, 1], but this is not crucial at all,
since we do not expect to face prices that are several magnitudes larger than the ones in the training
data. A properly trained and regularized network should be able to handle small deviations from the
range of the training data.

the variance has been tested with less success than Min-Max-Normalization. This technique is referred
to as z-score-normalization in [HKP11].

Training implementation
The model is implemented using the python library PyTorch. This framework both allows for an
implementation of the architecture as well as building a training loop. Like cppad, the framework uses
automatic diﬀerentiation to compute the gradient of the loss function with respect to the parameters in
==PAGE:99==

the network [PGM+19]. The training loop deﬁnes an iterative method of minimizing the loss function
by running through the training samples multiple times. PyTorch provides an easy way of building
a training loop, that searches for optimal parameters using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). SGD
is close to gradient descent in the sense that we solely use the gradient of the loss function to decide
the direction of the next step. However, instead of computing the gradient over the whole training
set, MT , in each iteration, a random set of sampled, MB ⊂MT , called mini-batch is drawn. Then
the gradient is computed only over this mini-batch and the parameters are updated according to this.
A new (but diﬀerent) batch is then subsampled and used to take a new step. This is repeated until all
samples in MT has been seen. This cycles is denoted as one full epoch. This routine is repeated until
either convergence or we reach a chosen maximum number of epochs [Bot10]. SGD is an extremely
frequently used method used in deep learning to avoid local minima in the loss function or at least
increase the chance of escaping these, by introducing noise in the gradient induced chancing the com-
binations of input all the time.
How parameters are updated given the gradient, the size of the mini-batches and multiple other
hyperparameters are often a crucial part of ﬁne-tuning an ANN. In this proof of concept, we use a
mini-batches of size 4, at learning rate (step size) of 0.001 while we use the built-in optimizer rmsprop
[TH12] to decide the parameter updates based on the gradient.

6.2.4.1
Inwoking constraints on Neural Network solutions
When using the neural network for predicting the switching times, we face the problem, that this is
unconstrained. That is, the chronological ordering of the switching times as well as the maximum time
allowed in melt cannot be guaranteed. These constraints can only be learned from the training data.
However, when using this predictor in the NNMPC algorithm, it is required that solutions are feasible
such that the models can actually be evaluated and such that we achieve a fair comparison with the
constrained solutions from ipopt. Lastly, if this was to be implemented in a real-world application, it
obviously would be required to satisfy the maximum melt time constraints.
To challenge this problem, we allow ourself to ﬁlter out cycles that are in the wrong order by simply
removing τmelt,k and τidle,k if τidle,k < τmelt,k. Likewise, we remove τmelt,k+1 and τidle,k if τmelt,k+1 <
τidle,k. That is, the actual number of regime cycles can be fewer than 6 after this ﬁltering
Afterwards, we check if the ﬁltered ANN solution violates the maximum melt constraint. If this is
the case, we reduce all melt regime lengths in the solution proportionally to their lengths, such that
the total melt time constraint is satisﬁed.

some broader perspective structure of the solutions as a function of the prices than the what ipopt

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Before we conclude on the presented work, recall the main objectives:
Assess the potential of exploiting the demand side ﬂexibility in the electricity market,
by developing a fast Nonlinear Model Predictive Control on a refrigeration system, that
can consider one or multiple markets at once, solving the optimal switching times for
system mode changes, evaluating the performance through in-silico experiments.
We found that it was indeed possible to develop a stochastic state-space model for the compressor
capacity in the refrigeration system improving the performance of the ﬁrst-order model presented
in [BGH+20] by including more states imitating higher-order state derivates. However, we did not
succeed in incorporating nonlinearities in the state equations, but only in the observation equation.
This was most likely due to limited physical knowledge of the system, a too short data time series and
a non-clever distribution of the inputs in data generation.
That said, the developed models performed rather well in terms of predicting the system dynamics as
a function of the mode changes of the ice tank. However, it was also clear from the residuals that the
models can still be improved substantially in terms of describing the internal regime dynamics and
the decrease when switching from melt to idle. Through in-insilico experiments, it has become clear,
that the model development was only of modest success, since realizations from the developed SDE
showed more random behaviour than we would have expected from the process based the available
data. A behaviour that indeed turned to be challenging the evaluation of the developed NMPCs.
Regarding the NMPCs, we did succeed in establishing two model-based constrained optimal control
problems with nonlinear objective functions as well as implementing these in the optimization frame-
work ipopt. We saw a clear connection between the heuristically intuitive optimal switching times
and the ones suggested by ipopt. This coherence has implied easy validation and interpretation of
the solutions, however, also raises questions whether how crucial it is to utilize a high amount of
computational resources to produce a solution that could have been obtained manually by inspecting
the price structure.
By a comprehensive evaluation of the three developed models, the number of allowed regime cycles
and the smoothness of the objective function, we can conclude that signiﬁcant savings certainly can be
achieved by installing the ice tank. Relative savings just above 11 % were achieved by solely purchasing
electricity on the day-ahead market, while also considering the regulation power market showed long
term savings close to 14 %, with indications of even higher saving in a physical test. We did not
manage to detect signiﬁcant performance diﬀerences, neither when comparing the models nor when
evaluating the number of regime cycles. However, by evaluating the performance without diﬀusion in
the models, we did see small signs, that Model 2 might have the potential of outcompeting the two
==PAGE:108==

other in a physical test.
Finally, we delivered a well-performing proof of concept regarding using an Artiﬁcial Neural Network
to increase the speed by which the complex optimization problems can be solved. By only 80 training
and 20 testing samples governed by ipopt solution and a minimum of eﬀort in architecture design
and hyperparameter tuning, we were able to train well-performing networks solving both outlined
problems. When considering only the day-ahead market, the NNMP performed just as good as the
MNMPC over a 100-day simulation realizing a saving of 11 % in our in-silico environment. Regarding
participating in both the day-ahead and the regulation power market, the NNMP did perform a little
worse than the ipopt solver by only saving just above 9 % compared to 13.5 %. Nevertheless, this
should be considered as very successful, taking into account how complex this problem is compared
to the very little amount of data and complexity of the network.
We conclude, that the ice tank installation indeed has showed vast potential of decreasing the costs
associated with the refrigeration system in a supermarket by exploiting the ﬂexibility in the electricity
market. It is clear that the models used in the NMPC indeed can be improved and will possibly show
even better results if applied in the real world. Indeed we can conclude that the work in this thesis
points in a direction, from where stronger models and even more sophisticated ways of taking part in
the electricity market can be developed in to improve how the uncertain electricity market is used and
thus the degree, by which, we manage to comply the urgent needs for replacing fossil fuel by renewable
energy source.

Chapter 8
Future work
In the following, we suggest four approaches for the further development of the work presented in this
thesis.
Predicting the Regulation Power Prices
In the thesis, we deliver an almost end to end framework for optimal utilization of the ice tank for
exploit the ﬂexible electricity market. However, we do build the work on top of one rather unrealistic
assumption, which is perfect knowledge of the regulation power prices 48 hours in advance.
To
complete the end-to-end framework accurate prediction of the relevant prices would be necessary.
[JPN+12] suggests a two-step methodology for predicting the Spot prices accounting for wind power
predictions. In [Wer14] multiple state-of-the-art solutions are reviewed and discussed. In would make
sense to look into these methods in terms of predicting the electricity power prices complete the
end-to-end framework developed in this thesis.
Combining Stochastic Price Predictions with the NMPC
Assume that we are not able to achieve deterministic price predictions that are satisfyingly accurate,
but only stochastic models of these using one of the tools presented in [Wer14]. A method to achieve
optimal scheduling based on a stochastic price model could be to realize n price time series from this
model. Solving the OCP n times, one time using each price realization, we achieve n switching time
suggestions; A distribution of switching times. Based on this distribution one could decide on a regime-
switching strategy. For high values n, this method would require a vast amount of computational
resources using an optimizer like ipopt. However, using a properly trained ANN, we can achieve all
solutions with very little amount of work. Hence, the combination of stochastic price models and the
ANN would indeed be a topic of future interest,
==PAGE:110==
90
Future work
Participating in the Regulation Power Market as Bidder
[BMNMS20] develops and evaluate an MPC application controlling a wastewater treatment plant to
exploit the Nordic electricity market as in this thesis. However, this controller also has the possibility
of bidding on the attractive special regulation market. That is, the controller is not quarantined of
getting activated to the bidded price. [BMNMS20] shows that savings close to 30 % can be achieved
under special conditions. Investigations of the possibilities of applying this methodology to the ice
tank installation should be carried out.
Model improvement
Based on the model development presented in the thesis, we are left with a set of models far from
perfect.
As also touched upon in the thesis, there a nonlinearities in the dynamics that are not
captured. The ﬁrst task to accomplish this is to collect more data based on a better experimental
design. From a modelling point of view, introducing exogenous input in the state equations combined
with deeper knowledge of the physical properties of a refrigerations system should be considered.

Code guide
The code used to construct all results presented in the thesis is assembled in a Github repository that
can be accessed at https://github.com/madsobdrupjakobsen/master_thesis.
The code is divided into two main directories: modelling/ and switching_time_optimization/.
In the following we describe their content and purpose.
