Abstract
In this thesis it has been investigated whether negative thermal expansion (NTE) of chiral
materials is related to phonon pseudoangular momentum (PAM). Several chiral mate­
rials with different thermal expansion (TE) properties have been studied: Selenium, α­
quartz, β­quartz, α­cristobalite and α­ZrW2O8. Anisotropic Grüneisen models were com­
bined with ab initio density functional perturbation theory (DFPT) calculations. The mode
Grüneisen parameter determines the contribution to TE of each phonon mode. No corre­
lation between mode Grüneisen parameters and phonon PAM was found. Modes which
have PAM are circularly polarised about an axis. No causalities explaining how circular
polarisation of atoms or phonon PAM can cause NTE or abnormal coefficients of ther­
mal expansion (CTE) have been found. Only indirect effects were found such as larger
degrees of freedom of the phonon modes and lower average energies of polar modes.
Phonon modes which have a total polarisation larger than the average total polarisation
were found to have a large contribute to TE per mode. The just mentioned modes have
lower energies on average, which may explain the large contribution to TE per mode as
Grüneisen parameters are inversely proportional to phonon energies. Phonon PAM can
be considered an extra degree of freedom as eigenvectors which have a net PAM are not
allowed if phonon PAM is not. Phonon PAM may facilitate modes with strong vibrational
TE mechanisms which would have been prohibited or restricted in structures of higher
symmetry.

Resume
I dette speciale er det blevet undersøgt, om der er en sammenhæng mellem negativ
termisk ekspansion af chirale materialer og fonon pseudoangulært moment. Udvalgte
chirale materialer med forskellige termiske ekspansionsegenskaber er blevet undersøgt:
Selen, α­kvarts, β­kvarts, α­cristobalit og α­ZrW2O8. Ab initio beregninger er blevet an­
vendt sammen med anisotropiske Grüneisen modeller. Den enkelte fonontilstands bidrag
til termisk ekspansion er bestemt af fonontilstandens Grüneisen parameter. Der er ikke
fundet nogen korrelation mellem Grüneisen parametre og pseudoangulære moment af
fonontilstande. Tilstande, der har et pseudoangulært moment, er cirkulært polariserede
omkring en akse.
Der blev ikke fundet nogle kausalsammenhænge, der kunne fork­
lare, hvordan cirkulære vibrationer af atomer eller pseudoangulært moment kan bidrage
til negativ termisk ekspansion eller abnormale termiske ekspansionskoefficienter. Kun
afledte effekter blev fundet, så som at fonontilstandene har større bevægelsesfriheds­
grader og lavere gennemsnitlige energier af polære fonontilstande. Fonontilstande, der
har en større total polarisering end den gennemsnitlige totale polarisering, bidrager rel­
ativt meget til den termiske ekspansion. Grüneisen parametre er omvendt proportionale
med fononenergi. De netop nævnte tilstande har gennemsnitligt lavere fononenergier,
hvilket kan forstærke deres bidrag til den termiske ekspansion. Fonon pseudoangulært
moment kan betragtes som en ekstra bevægelsesfrihedsgrad, da fononegenvektorer kun
kan have et pseudoangulært moment forskelligt fra nul, hvis fonon pseudoangulært mo­
ment er tilladt. Fonon pseudoangulært moment kan facilitere fonontilstande med stærke
vibrationelle termiske ekspansionsmekanismer, som ikke ville have været tilladte eller ville
have været begrænsede i strukturer med en højere grad af symmetri.

Introduction
The first material discovered to exhibit isotropic NTE over a wide temperature range was
α­ZrW2O8 [1]. Several prominent inorganic NTE crystals crystallises in chiral crystal struc­
tures, including α­ZrW2O8 [2]. Considering the general rarity of chiral inorganic crystals
[2], the question arises: whether there is a link between NTE and the chirality of the crystal
structures? Chiral crystal structures do not have inversion symmetry [3]. In the presence
of time reversal symmetry, the absence of inversion symmetry allows for phonons which
are circularly polarised and have quantized PAM [4]. It is investigated whether phonon
PAM is related to NTE of chiral materials.
Phonons are lattice vibrations and polar phonons are lattice vibrations which have non­
zero circular polarisation. E.g. a polar phonon could have all the atoms in the lattice
orbit about the same axis. Net circular polarisation of atoms results from phonon eigen­
vectors which are forbidden by symmetry at generic q­points in achiral structures. The
phonon modes of chiral structures can be viewed as having an extra degree of freedom
[2]. Polar phonons have a distinct handedness and can be distinguished as having ei­
ther right­ or left­handed circular polarisation and are hence chiral in that sense. Similar
phonons of the opposite chirality do not simply mirror each other, which means that a pair
of polar phonons may influence the physical properties of the crystal differently. Phonon
branches with the same energies but opposite chirality do exist and are related by time­
reversal symmetry, such that a chiral branch from say Γ to X is of the opposite chirality
as the branch from Γ to X′ with the same energy [2]. As the same phonon of opposite
chirality exists, it is not expected that the sign of the polarisation can influence the physical
properties of the material and is considered arbitrary. Degenerate phonons may only dif­
fer by the chirality and the net PAM of degenerate pairs must be zero, such phonons are
allowed for chiral as well as achiral structures. It follows that chiral phonons are limited to
the center and edges of the Brillouin zone (BZ) for achiral structures [2]. Chiral phonons at
the center and corners of the BZ have been observed in monolayer hexagonal lattices [5].
The lack of inversion symmetry lowers the symmetry of chiral structures and breaks the
degeneracy of branches, which would have had higher degeneracy if inversion symmetry
had been present. Breaking the degeneracy results in branch splitting i.e in branches
being shifted down and up in energy with respect to each other. TE is proportional to
the Grüneisen parameters of the phonons, which in turn are inversely proportional to the
phonon frequencies [6]. The branches shifted down in energy are expected to contribute
more to TE than the branches shifted up in energy.
This thesis follows the methods applied in the paper on α−BiB3O6 by Romao [2]. Both
the chirality of the phonon modes and their contribution to NTE of α−BiB3O6 were inves­
tigated. It was found that the lowest energy acoustic phonon branch from Γ −→C2 was
chiral and contributed to NTE of α−BiB3O6. The acoustic branch of opposite chirality did
not contribute to NTE. In this thesis several different structures are investigated. Firstly,
the elements: Si and Se, are investigated. Se has a chiral crystal structure whereas Si is
achiral. Si is investigated because 4 SiO2­phases are investigated too. Quartz and Cristo­
balite are two distinct crystalline forms of SiO2 with different TE properties. Both Quartz
and Cristobalite are polymorphs with an α­ and β­phase. Quartz and Cristobalite have in
common that the TE is smaller of the β­phase than of the α­phase. α­quartz and β­quartz
are both chiral. α­quartz is naturally occurring and exhibits positive thermal expansion
(PTE) [7] whereas β­quartz exhibits NTE in the temperature range from 600­800◦C [8].

Lattice vibrations
The first thing to consider when describing a crystal structure is the equilibrium positions
of the atoms in the lattice. Crystal structures can be represented by Bravais lattices de­
scribing the translational symmetry of crystal structures and a basis of 1 or more atoms
describing every discrete point in the lattices. Every point in a lattice can be described
by: r(n1, n2, n3) = n1a + n2b + n3c, where a, b and c are the primitive lattice vector and
n1,2,3 are integers.
Classically, at zero K the atoms are considered to be at rest. Thermal expansion re­
sults from temperature effects changing the average bond lengths and/or angles within
the lattice. The kinetic energy of the atoms increases upon heating and the atoms start
to vibrate around their equilibrium lattice positions. The vibrations can be described as
elastic waves. Elastic waves are described by the displacements of the atoms, us, which
depend on the wave vectors, q, defining the propagation direction of the waves and the
angular frequency: ω, describing the time development of the waves.
us = uei(q·r−ωt)

of the forces and to depend on the relative displacement of any two atoms in the crystal,
and can therefore be described by Hooke’s law.

Thermodynamics
Lattice dynamics are fundamental to describe thermodynamic properties of crystal struc­
tures hereunder TE and phase stability. The stable phase of a compound is the phase at
which the Gibbs free energy is minimum:
G = H −TS = E + pV −TS
(3.1)
At constant pressure the free energy consists of two contributions: one from the enthalpy
and one from the entropy. E is the internal energy, commonly, named U. Thermal vibra­
tions are for many solids the primary origin of entropy. The dynamics of crystals must be
understood to develop temperature dependent quantitative models of crystal behaviour
[10].

3.2
The Quasi Harmonic Approximation
If the anharmonic interactions are reasonably small the harmonic picture can be modi­
fied to account for the anharmonic interactions. Within the quasi harmonic approximation
(QHA), the anharmonic interactions are assumed to give rise to a dependence of the
phonon frequency on the structure of the material e.g on the volume of the material. By
assuming the temperature dependence of the phonon frequencies as being only indi­
rect through the volume dependence on temperature, thermodynamic properties can be
calculated within the HA by using modified frequencies [10]. Classically, changing the
volume of the structure corresponds to changing the spring constants between the atoms
and thereby modify the oscillations of the atoms. Alternatively, the phonon frequency de­
pendence on temperature can be calculated by considering direct interactions between
phonons, which is called the renormalised phonon theory [10]. The QHA is a reasonable
approximation in a wide temperature range up to temperatures close to the melting point,
where higher order terms in eq. 3.2 become important [13].
The nature of lattice waves is dispersive which is not considered within QHA [14]. Phonons
have a finite life time as they collide with each other and interact. The anharmonic terms
are a result of different phonon interactions. Within the formalism of second quantisation
the anharmonic terms have physical interpretations in which phonons are created and
annihilated. The third order anharmonic term corresponds to a phonon spontaneously
decaying into two lower energy phonons or two phonons colliding, resulting in the creation
of a phonon of the combined energy. The fourth order anharmonic term corresponds to all
possible interactions where 4 phonons are involved. The higher order anharmonic terms
correspond to interaction with one more phonon involved every time the order is increased
by one. There are two conservation laws for the processes. Conservation of energy and
the change in wave vector must be equal to a reciprocal lattice vector. As a result the
direction of the overall wave vector can be changed as well as the flow of energy. All of
which is dealt with within the renormalised phonon theory [10].
In this study the anharmonicity of the total energy is accounted for by calculating phonon
frequencies under different stresses, which result in different volumes. It is assumed that
the phonon frequencies are only dependent on volume, and it is the volume which is tem­
perature dependent. The CTE are proportional to the mean Grüneisen parameter, which
in turn is proportional to the partial derivatives of the phonon frequencies with respect to
volume. All of which will be explained in section 3.4

3.4
Grüneisen Models
Grüneisen proposed a model to describe TE of isotropic crystalline solids in 1912 [6].
Grüneisen related the contribution of the different phonons of a material to the TE of the
material by considering the volume derivative of the phonon frequencies.
Grüneisen parameters can be calculated from experimental measurements by measuring
the change in phonon frequency at variable pressure using inelastic scattering techniques
or from theoretical calculations using ab initio methods such as DFPT. DFPT allows for
explicit investigation of the phonon modes and thereby the mechanisms of TE.
The original Grüneisen model and variable­pressure measurements can not account for
material anisotropy [15]. TE of isotropic or cubic structures is considerably simpler than
that of structures which lack cubic symmetry. Cubic crystals expand at the same rate in
different direction, while crystals which lack cubic symmetry expand at different rates in
different directions which is termed thermal expansion anisotropy [15]. Firstly, the original
Grüneisen model will be reviewed. Thereafter an extension of the original model will be re­
viewed which incorporates a coupling between elastic anisotropy and thermal expansion
anisotropy required for non­cubic crystal families.

3.4.2
Anisotropic Grüneisen Models
Different models have been proposed to extend the original Grüneisen model to treat ther­
mal expansion anisotropy. Barron and Munn [18] replaced the volume perturbation of the
phonon frequency by uniaxial Lagrangian strain perturbations. Choy et al. [19] proposed
a model closer to the original Grüneisen model neglecting direct treatment of the elastic
anisotropy but still considering strain perturbations. Recently, Romao [15] proposed a
Grüneisen model considering stress perturbations instead of strain perturbations.
Anisotropic Grüneisen models are difficult to test experimentally due to challenges in­
volved in applying uniaxial perturbations. Ab initio calculations of phonon band struc­
tures allow for theoretical prediction of anisotropic thermal expansion when combined with
anisotropic Grüneisen models. The model of Barron and Munn [18] has shown mixed re­
sults between qualitative and quantitative levels of accuracy. Both the stain and stress
perturbation models consider the strain, the difference is that the stain is defined as the
response to an applied stress for the stress perturbation model. The stress perturbation
model has been found to improve the accuracy of the description of TE of materials with
unusual elastic properties [15].
Furthermore, the stress perturbation model has the advantage that the CTE along a de­
sired axis depend on the Grüneisen parameters and Young’s modulus along the same di­
rection. The lack of cross terms makes the interpretation easier for the anisotropic model
proposed by Romao [15] than the model proposed by Barron and Munn [18]. A con­
sequence of the stress perturbation model is that NTE can not occur without negative
Grüneisen parameters [15].

Vibrational Thermal Expansion Mechanisms
It is the anharmonicity of 3N­dimensional potential that determines the TE of a give struc­
ture. At finite temperatures each atom vibrates around its mean position, ⟨Ri⟩. The ampli­
tude of the vibrations will increase with increasing temperature. In an anharmonic potential
⟨Ri⟩and the interatomic distances become temperature dependent.
3 simple vibrational mechanism at the atomic level are known to cause TE when con­
sidering pairwise interatomic potentials. The first mechanism is bond stretching and is
responsible for PTE. It is rather intuitive. It is modelled by anharmonic pair potentials
between atoms vibrating along a line. The asymmetry of the potential causes the mean
distance between the atoms to increase as the amplitude of the vibrations increases be­
cause the potential is less steep when increasing rather than decreasing the bond length,
see fig. 3.2. Bond stretching is the most important mechanism for PTE and reveals that
it is the anharmonicity of the potential which causes thermal vibrations to cause TE.

effects are rotation of units within the structure e.g tetrahedra. The rotations correspond

potential anharmonicities causing PTE [21]. Transverse vibrations can lead to coupled
rotations of polyhedra which may cause NTE, depending on the rotation. In structures
with distorted polyhedra, the polyhedra tend to become less distorted as the temperature

4.1.1
The Adiabatic Approximation
The adiabatic approximation relies on the difference in time scale of the motion of elec­
trons and nuclei. The adiabatic approximation states: the electrons do not undergo tran­
sitions between stationary states. Due to the low mass of the electrons compared to the
nuclei the electrons will instantaneously follow the motion of the nuclei while always re­
maining in the same stationary state of the electronic Hamiltonian. Electrostatic coupling
of the electrons and the nuclei alters the stationary state of the electrons over time. The
nuclei follow the dynamics of the system but the electrons will remain in the ground state,
if the electrons are in the many body ground state to begin with. A consequence of the
approximation is that transitions between electronic stationary states are not allowed to
occur in the absence of an external electromagnetic field.

5.3
Symmetry Requirements: Phonon Pseudoangular
Momentum
The last question arises considering phonon PAM: when is PAM allowed by symmetry?
Coh [4] has recently classified materials into 5 symmetry classes in which 3 classes al­
low for phonons with angular momentum at generic non­symmetric points. A generic
non­symmetric point is away from high­symmetry points, lines and planes. Generic non­
symmetric points are important as thermal expansion relies on the increase in the popu­
lation of phonons across the entire BZ.
The restriction to generic non­symmetric points results in a quite simple argument. At
high­symmetry parts of the BZ other symmetries must be considered, when determining
whether polarisation of the phonons are allowed. It is assumed that there are no accidental
symmetries present. If a crystal has inversion symmetry: P, the operation of inversion
transforms a given phonon into a phonon with the direction of the linear momentum: q,
reversed but leaves the angular momentum: l, unchanged: P(l, q) →(l, −q). Time
reversal symmetry: T , changes the sign of both l and q: T (l, q) →(−l, −q). If the
combined symmetry of time reversal symmetry followed by inversion symmetry: PT , is
present then the operation results in a change of the sign of l but leaves q unchanged:
PT (l, q) →(−l, q). PT is the only symmetry which leaves q unchanged at a generic non­
symmetric point of the BZ. If PT is present then l must be zero and the phonons must not
have angular momentum. If PT is not present quantized phonon angular momentum is
allowed.
All materials can be described by which of the following three symmetries are present: T ,
P and T P, which results in 5 groups. Neither of the 3 classes allowing for phonons with
angular momentum have the combined symmetry: T P. The 3 classes are distinguished
by having neither T nor P present or having just one of the two symmetries present.
At high symmetry point (HSP)s it may be possible to find a basis where degenerate
phonons have angular momentum even though the symmetry T P is present [4]. If phonon
angular momentum is allowed at generic points, there may be HSPs, at which additional
symmetries do not allow for phonon angular momentum.
The dynamics of ions are typically described within the Born­Oppenheimer approxima­
tion, which is the case for DFPT. The motion of the ions are determined by the force
constant matrix, as explained in chapter 2. For materials which lack P and T P, phonon
angular momentum originates within the Born­Oppenheimer approximation, as some of
the degenerate phonon bands will become non­degenerate as the force constants will
no longer be equal by symmetry. The force constant matrix will have broken P and the
phonon eigenvectors will have angular momentum at generic non­symmetric points in the
BZ. The classical equation of motion used within Born­Oppenheimer approximation is only
approximate, as the true dynamics of nuclei is quantum­mechanical. The approximation
can be improved by including the velocity­force constant matrix, which can be consid­
ered as a Lorentz force acting on a charged particle in an effective magnetic field. The
velocity­force constants are the second derivative of the energy with respect to atomic
28
Thermal Expansion
==PAGE:39==
displacement and atomic velocity. The force constant matrix is time reversal symmetric
as the second derivative of the energy with respect to the displacement is invariant un­
der time reversal. Whereas the velocity force constant matrix changes sign under time
reversal as the velocity changes sign. For materials with lack T and T P, phonon angular
momentum originates from the velocity force constant matrix. For materials which lack P,
T and T P the phonon angular momentum originates from both the force constant matrix
and the velocity force constant matrix. Materials which lack T have not been investigated
and the velocity force constant matrix have not been calculated for any structures [4].
5.4
Definition of Chirality
Chiral structures are non­superimposable onto their own mirror images. The classical
criterion of chirality is the non­superimposability of the two enantiomers, which are the two
related structures. All crystals which crystallises in one of the 65 Sohncke space groups
are chiral [33]. 22 of the Sohncke space groups are chiral. The chiral space groups
consists of 11 pairs of enantiomorphous space groups. All chiral structures are defined
by having two distinct enantiomorphic states that are interconverted by space inversion
[3]. As the operation of space inversion converts a chiral crystal into its enantiomer, chiral
crystals do not contain the operation of space inversion. Chiral crystals which contain time
reversal symmetry must allow for phonons with PAM at generic non­symmetric q­points.
Both chiral and achiral structures can be defined by their symmetry groups: The symmetry
group of a chiral structure contains only symmetry operations of the first kind: rotations,
translations and screw­rotations, and none of the second kind: inversion, reflections, roto­
inversions, and glide reflections. Achiral structures contain equal numbers of symmetry
operations of the first and second kind [3]. Whether phonon PAM is allowed for achiral
structures depends on which symmetry operations of the second kind are contained in
the symmetry group and on whether time reversal symmetry is present.
Chiral crystal structures have been investigated because it is guaranteed that phonon spin
PAM is allowed as long as time reversal symmetry is present. All of the structures investi­
gated exhibit time reversal symmetry. Se, α−, β­quarts and α−cristobalite belong to chiral
space groups, whereas α­ZrW2O8 does not but is a chiral crystal too. Si, β−cristobalite
and ZrV2O7 are achiral.

6.1
Convergence studies
Accurate phonon calculations are of outermost importance for this study. In principle a
convergence study of phonon frequencies should be performed. It is not feasible to per­
form a convergence study of phonon frequencies as calculations of phonon frequencies
are computationally very demanding, especially for large structures. Convergence studies
of the pressure are carried out instead. The phonon frequencies depend on the volume
of the material which depends on the pressure. The convergence studies of the different
structures are shown in appendix A.1, B.1, C.1, D.1 and E.1.
Later on stress perturbations will applied to the crystal structures because the changes in
phonon frequencies with respect to strain are needed to calculate the Grüneisen param­
eters of the phonon modes. A convergence of the pressure within 0.005 GPa is desired
to ensure that the later applied stress perturbations will be well defined.
The ground state calculations of many of the initial structures resulted in large pressures.
Especially, for the 4 SiO2 structures for which pressures of approximately 10 GPa were
calculated. Initial relaxations were carried out to ensure that the phases would not go
through phase transitions during the relaxations.

mechanisms of TE can be explained.
At Γ the AC modes are in phase translations of the atoms. Following the paths from Γ

7.1.5
Summary of Silicon Analysis
The TE of Si has been successfully predicted from 0­340 K. It is the transverse AC modes
which are responsible for NTE at low temperatures. The NTE modes cause atoms to
vibrate in directions transverse to the directions of the bonds between the corresponding
atoms. Transverse motion is known as the bond tension effect and is known to cause
NTE. The PTE at high temperatures was observed to be due to bond stretching.

The largest expectation values of circular polarisation are about the z­axis, which is the

Circular polarisation is a measure of the circularity of the motion of the atoms about a
given axis. It is no surprise that the modes along the Γ­A path exhibit circular polarisation
about c because Γ­A coincides with the c­axis. The pairs of transverse modes have the
atoms move around in circles within the ab­plane, orbiting with opposite handedness.
Spin PAM of the phonons is allowed at generic non­symmetric q­points of the BZ because
the crystal structure of Selenium does not have the combined symmetry: PT . The phonon
spectrum consists of the high symmetry paths and accidental symmetries may be present
within these paths. Accidental symmetries may not allow for phonon spin PAM. E.g all the
modes are non­polar at the HSPs and the polarisation about z is zero along the paths:
M­K­Γ and L­H­A. The path Γ­K is within the ab­plane, the path lies along the direction
of chains across the unit cell going from the origin to a+b in real space, see fig. 7.10.
This path can be rotated into itself by rotating the crystal 180◦about the (a+b)­direction.
z is rotated into ­z. This rotational symmetry combined with time reversal symmetry does
not allow phonons to have PAM about the z­axis. The time reversal symmetry operation
results in: T (l, q) →(−l, −q). The rotational symmetry does not change the sign of l
but changes the sign of the z­component, therefore there must be no polarisation about
z as the combined symmetry operation results in: (l, qz) →(−l, qz). Polarisation is still
allowed about the a(x)­ and b­axis. The same argument hold for the path: A­H, which is
parallel to Γ­K, as q3 is rotated into ­0.5. The paths M­K and L­H are parallel to the b­axis.
The b­axis is parallel to another direction of chains. The same argument holds for these
two paths as for the two other paths, as a similar rotation about the b­axis exists.

7.2.6
Conclusion
The theoretical model correctly predicted PTE of the ab­plane and NTE of the c­direction.
There is good quantitative agreement with experimental measurements of αcc. The model
predicts that αaa reaches a constant value at 100 K, whereas the experimental measure­
ments increase. NTE of the c­direction was found to be due to the elastic coupling be­
tween the ab­plane and the c­direction. Low energy modes have the largest numerical
Grüneisen parameters and dominate TE of Selenium. Visual inspection of the phonon
dispersion spectrum showed that modes with large Grüneisen parameters had changing
inter­chain distances in common. The phonon mode analysis did not reveal a relation­
ship between PTE or NTE and phonon PAM. Modes of opposite handedness did not have
the same TE properties within the sampled part of the BZ. No correlation was found be­
tween the expectation value of circular polarisation and the mode Grüneisen parameter.
The quantitative analysis showed that the polar modes did contribute relatively more to
NTE of the c­direction than the non­polar modes, independently of the polarity criterion.
NTE of the c­direction was found be increasingly dominated by polar modes at higher
temperatures. The polar modes were found to have lower average energies, which may
explain the numerically larger Grüneisen parameters. Furthermore, exclusion of modes
which have the opposite sign of γij,ν,q than the majority of the modes increased the con­
tribution to TE of the polar modes per mode. No causality between TE and phonon PAM
was found. It is likely that circular vibrations of atoms are energetically favourable and
therefore have low energies and large numerical Grüneisen parameters.

8.1
Stability of the SiO2­phases
The β­phases are high temperature phases. DFT calculations are performed at 0 K per
definition and unstable phonons are expected to appear in the phonon dispersion spectra
of the β­phases. Unstable phonons will appear in the phonon spectra as branches with
negative energies and corresponding imaginary frequencies. The β­phases will have
dynamical instabilities, which means that corrective atomic displacements will lower the
potential energy of the phases, i.e the phonons with negative energies. Imaginary modes
can lead structures to other phases through continuous atomic displacements and lattice
deformations. It follows that imaginary modes can be used to study displacive phase tran­
sitions. If there is no energy barrier the phase transitions can happen spontaneously. The
negative phonon branches in the dispersion spectra of the β­phases will lead to transi­
tions to the dynamically stable α­phases. Large displacements stabilise high temperature
phases at higher temperatures [13].
It is not possible to calculate the thermodynamic properties of crystal structures with imag­
inary frequencies. The aim is to compare the TE properties and the phonon modes of the
α­ and β­structures. One stable phonon spectra of each phase must be achieved to com­
pare the phonons of the different phases, as unstable phonons lead to phase transitions
and it is not the phase transitions which are studied in this thesis. It is not necessary to
calculate the phonon frequencies at different temperatures as it is only the changes in
frequencies with respect to applied stresses which are needed for the Grüneisen mod­
els. The Gibbs free energy determines the stability of the phases and depends on both
pressure and volume. Following Welche et al. [21] the structures were expanded by intro­
ducing negative pressures to stabilise the β­structures. The expansion of the structures is
discussed in appendix C.4. For both β­structures it was necessary to introduce a negative
pressure of ­4 GPa to stabilise the structures. A pressure of ­4 GPa resulted in expanding
β­quartz by 3.2 % and β­cristobalite by 2.9 %.

tional TE mechanism while investigating the modes of α­quartz. It has been decided to
move on to ZrW2O8 and only present the key points of the quantitative analysis of the
SiO2­phases. The calculated Grüneisen parameters of the β­phases did not reproduce
the TE properties of the β­phases and the emphasis will be on the α­phases. A last com­
ment on the phonon dispersion spectra of the SiO2­structures: they are key results in
their own right and are presented in appendix C.5. The spectra may be used to gain a
better understanding of the α­β phase transitions and the vibrational TE mechanisms of
the α­phases.

8.5
Conclusion
The CTE of quartz and cristobalite have been predicted by theoretical models based on
isotropic and anisotropic Grüneisen models and DFPT calculations of the phonon fre­
quencies. The predicted CTE of α­quartz were in good agreement with experimental
measurements, especially, at low temperatures. The predicted CTE did reproduce the
known TE properties of α­cristobalite. The high temperature β­phases were unstable at
0 K and were stabilised by introducing a negative pressure of ­4 GPa. The Grüneisen pa­
rameters did not reproduce the known TE properties of the β­phases. Investigations of the
vibrational TE mechanisms of the phonon modes of α­quartz did not reveal a relationship
between phonon PAM and TE properties of the modes. No correlations have been found
between the expectation values of circular polarisation and the Grüneisen parameters of
the phonon modes. It was found that highly circularly polarised modes are not important
when considering TE of the SiO2­structures as their contribution to TE is negligible. Many
modes exhibit a degree of circular polarisation and polar modes do contribute to TE of the
chiral SiO2­structures.

ZrW2O8
ZrW2O8 is a quite remarkable framework material. ZrW2O8 was already discovered to
exhibit NTE over a wide temperature range in 1996 by Mary et al. [1]. ZrW2O8 exhibits
NTE from 0.3 K to 1050 K at which temperature ZrW2O8 decomposes. The cubic symme­
try of ZrW2O8 persists over the entire stable temperature range. Below 430 K the space
groups is P213, which is chiral. A phase transition occurs at 430 K. Above 430 K the space
group becomes Pa¯3, which is achiral [1]. Only the low temperature phase: α­ZrW2O8, is
investigated in this thesis. The α­ZrW2O8­structure can be seen in fig. 9.1 and the recip­
rocal lattice in 9.2. ZrW2O8 is a highly flexible structure [1]. The magnitude of the CTE
qualifies ZrW2O8 as high expansion ceramics, which is rather unusual as ZrW2O8 ex­
hibits NTE. α­ZrW2O8 is a well­ordered structure. The structure contains corner­sharing
ZrO6 octahedra and two crystallographically distinct WO4 tetrahedra. Every tetrahedron
has a dangling oxygen bond. The high temperature phase: Pa¯3, is disordered with an
occupancy of 50% of one of the oxygen sites, which has significant oxygen mobility [20].

gives rise to the tension effect. The potential is expected to be harmonic transverse to
the direction of the bonds because the chemical environments close to the bridging O­

positions of atoms in harmonic potentials are constant. The displacement of the O­atom
increase the actual bond length during the vibration and a restoring force results. The
tension in the bonds increases as the volume increases causing the restoring force and
the frequency to increase and γν,q becomes negative. If the motion is not just transverse
but circularly polarised about the direction of the bond, there will still be a restoring force
in the direction of the equilibrium position of the atom, i.e the center of the circle, and γν,q
still becomes negative. As more than one TE mechanism is present within each mode, it
is not possible to address which of the mechanisms are dominating the TE of the modes
by simply visualising the modes. The two mechanisms described above were recurring
for the strong NTE modes.

Conclusion
The CTE of ZrW2O8 have been successfully predicted from 0­300 K. The majority of the
modes had a small but non­zero total polarisation. Modes for which the total polarisa­
tion is larger than 0.6 account for a negligibly small part of the TE of ZrW2O8. A large
total polarisation is only observed close to Γ at low phonon energies. |γ| decreases as
the total polarisation increases for modes which have a total polarisation above 0.6. For
modes which have a total polarisation below 0.6 there is no correlation between γ and the
total polarisation as the spread of the data is too large. A non­zero total polarisation of
the phonon modes is only allowed because the combined symmetry: PT , is not present
for chiral structures which have time reversal symmetry. The low expectation values of
circular polarisation of the strong NTE modes, for which γν,q < −10, were observed to
be due to the atoms being circularly polarised about different directions. For the strong
NTE modes circular or elliptical transverse motion of the bridging O­atoms was recurringly
observed as well as circular or elliptical coupled motion of the W1­ and O4­atoms. Trans­
verse motion of atoms with respect to directions of bonds is known as the tension effect
and causes NTE. The coupled motion of the W1­ and O4­atoms was observed to bring
the O4­atoms closer to form W­O­W links known from denser ZrW2O8 structures. If a
net polarisation was not allowed the coupled motion of the O4­ and W1­ atoms would be
more restricted and the circular polarisation of all the atoms would have to exactly cancel
each other out. The majority of the NTE modes within the phonon spectrum had large
re­normalised expectation values of circular polarisation of the dangling O­atoms. It is not
the modes which have a large total polarisation which dominate TE of ZrW2O8 but low
energy NTE modes which have a total polarisation below 0.4.

Discussion and Outlook
There are many sources of error to consider when performing theoretical calculations of
CTE as many approximations are needed. TE is determined by the entropy dependence
on volume [17]. Only vibrational TE mechanisms have been examined because vibra­
tions are the primary origin of entropy for many solids [10]. Non­vibrational mechanisms
may contribute to the entropy of solids and are sources of error [17].
Excitations of lattice vibrations, also called phonons, have been calculated within the HA.
It is the anharmonicity of the potential which is responsible for TE. The anharmonicity of
the potential has been accounted for by introducing stress perturbations and calculating
the dependence of phonon frequencies on volume. The resulting Grüneisen parameters
are calculated at 0 K. Within the formalism of second quantisation the anharmonicity of
the potential is interpreted as phonon interaction. Constant Grüneisen parameters cor­
respond to assuming that the strength of the phonon interaction is constant and that it
is only the phonon occupancy which is temperature dependent. The elastic properties
are calculated at 0 K too. The temperature dependence of elastic properties has not
been dealt with. Elastic properties of materials are temperature dependent e.g the bulk
modulus of ZrW2O8 is highly temperature dependent. The CTE depend on the phonon
occupancy and are extrapolated from 0 K as the Grüneisen parameters and volume are
calculated at 0 K. The HA is only valid for small atomic displacements [10]. The atomic
displacements increase as the occupancy of phonon modes increases with temperature
and the errors increase too. Only convergence tests of total energies and pressure have
been performed. Convergence test of elastic properties and phonon frequencies should
have been performed as well. The Grüneisen models must capture the most important TE
mechanisms as comparison with experimental measurements is in quite good agreement
with the theoretical predictions of Si, Se, α­quartz, α­cristobalite and ZrW2O8.
The aim of this thesis was to investigate whether there is a relationship between phonon
PAM and NTE of chiral materials. The PAM of the modes was quantified by calculating
the expectation values of circular polarisation of the modes. The contribution to TE of
each mode is determined by its Grüneisen parameter: γν,q. No correlations between
the size or sign of γν,q and the expectation value of circular polarisation were found. An
exception is observed for a subset of modes in ZrW2O8, for which |γν,q| decreases as the
total polarisation increases. All modes within the subset have a total polarisation above
0.6 and coincide with the transverse polar AC modes. A correlation is observed because
only a few paths with similar properties are examined. The spread in the data is too large
to observe any correlations if more than a few similar paths in the RS at similar energies
are examined. There is no correlation between the total polarisation and the Grüneisen
parameter for the low energy modes in Se and the chiral SiO2­structures.
Polar modes do contribute significantly to TE of the investigated chiral materials, as many
modes have non­zero expectation values of circular polarisation. The modes which have
a total polarisation above 0.6, 0.7 or 0.9 were found to contribute with a negligible small
part of the TE of Se, the chiral SiO2­structures and ZrW2O8, respectively. The contribu­
tion to TE from polar modes which have a larger total polarisation than the average total
polarisation was found to be large per mode. The average energy of polar modes is lower
than that of all the modes and may explain the large contribution to TE per mode because
the Grüneisen parameters are inversely proportional to the phonon energies. Modes in
Se and ZrW2O8 which have a small total polarisation tend to have the opposite sign of γν,q

larger. It can not be concluded that the occurrence of polar modes results in larger or

The TE mechanisms of the modes which have the largest numerical Grüneisen param­
eters in Se and ZrW2O8 were investigated. Vibrations of atoms towards atoms in neigh­
bouring chains were recurring for the strongest TE modes in Se. NTE of the c­direction
was found to be due to the elastic coupling between the ab­plane and the c­direction.
Circular or elliptical transverse motion of the bridging O­atoms and circular or elliptical
coupled motion of W­ and dangling O­atoms were recurringly observed for the strongest
NTE modes in ZrW2O8. Transverse motion of the bridging O­atoms gives rise to the ten­
sion effect, which causes NTE. Coupled motion of O­ and W­atoms brings O­atoms closer
to form W­O­W links known from denser ZrW2O8 structures and is likely to cause NTE.
The strong NTE modes in ZrW2O8 had small expectation values of circular polarisation.
No relationship between TE and phonon PAM was found from investigating the modes in
Se and ZrW2O8. No causality explaining how circular polarisation of atoms can cause TE
was found either. It can not be the PAM of the modes which causes TE because no corre­
lation was found between the two. It is not possible to assign the contribution to TE among
different vibrational mechanisms if more than one mechanism is present within the same
mode. This is especially a problem for structures with large unit cells. Originally, it was
found that the tension effect causes NTE by performing calculations on an artificial mate­
rial with harmonic atomic pair­potentials thus eliminating the bond­stretching mechanism
[17] [56]. To gain a better insight into circularly polarised vibrational mechanisms, it is pro­
posed to study simple models or artificial systems in which other vibrational mechanisms
are eliminated. Such investigations may answer the question of whether circular motion
of vibrating atoms or a net polarisation can cause TE. It is difficult to imagine how circular
motion can cause TE. TE depends on the average positions of atoms and the average
position of a circle is its center. The anharmonicity causing TE may come from something
else than the circularity of the motion e.g. bond­stretching for elliptically polarised atoms
or the tension effect for atoms polarised about directions of bonds. It follows that circular
polarisation combined with other vibrational TE mechanisms may cause TE.
For crystal structures which do not allow for phonon PAM, the circular polarisation of all
the atoms have to exactly cancel each other out as no net PAM of the modes is allowed.
No net polarisation limits the degrees of freedom of the modes and the motion of the
atoms is more restricted. Phonon PAM allows for a more wide range of modes as the
circular polarisation of the atoms do not have to cancel each other out. Phonon PAM is
only allowed at generic non­symmetric q­points if the combined symmetry of time reversal
symmetry followed by inversion symmetry: T P, is not present. Chiral structures do not
have inversion symmetry. It is the low symmetry of chiral structures which allow for phonon
PAM and increases the degrees of freedom of the phonon eigenvectors i.e the motion
of the atoms. The low symmetry of chiral structures may facilitate modes with strong
vibrational TE mechanisms which would have been prohibited or restricted in structures
of higher symmetry. Whether these modes contributes to NTE or PTE must depend on
the anharmonicity potential. Whether these extra degree of freedom are important must
depend on the crystal structure. The symmetry of a structure can be lowered by other
means than not having inversion symmetry, which increases the degrees of freedom of
the phonons as well. It may be the low symmetry of prominent NTE materials that is
important and not the chirality of the materials allowing for phonon PAM. Low symmetry
structures may allow for low energy modes with large Grüneisen parameters which would
otherwise have been prohibited or restricted by symmetry requirements.

Conclusion
Several prominent NTE materials are known to be chiral. If time reversal symmetry is
present, chiral materials are allowed to have quantized phonon PAM. It has been investi­
gated whether NTE of chiral materials is related to phonon PAM.
Anisotropic Grüneisen models enable one to assign the contribution to TE of each phonon
mode in every crystallographic direction. Anisotropic Grüneisen models were combined
with DFPT calculations of phonons to predict the CTE of several different materials. The
predicted CTE of the crystalline forms of Silicon, Selenium, α­quartz, α­cristobalite and α­
ZrW2O8 were all in reasonable agreement with experimental measurements. Especially,
at low temperatures. The β­phases of Quartz and Cristobalite were successfully sta­
bilised by introducing negative pressures, thereby expanding the structures. The calcu­
lated Grüneisen parameters did not reproduce the known TE properties of the β­phases.
The TE properties of the modes in the α­ and β­phases were not compared.
Both phonon modes at generic q­points and at q­points along high symmetry paths were
found to have non­zero values of phonon PAM for all the investigated chiral materials.
The phonon PAM of the modes was quantified by calculating the expectation values of
circular polarisation of the modes. No correlation between the expectation values of cir­
cular polarisation and Grüneisen parameters of phonon modes was found. Polar phonon
modes have non­zero expectation values of circular polarisation. Similar polar modes of
opposite handedness were observed to have different Grüneisen parameters, but no cor­
relations were found. Polar phonon modes were found to contribute significantly to the
CTE of the chiral structures. It can not be concluded that polar modes contribute to larger
negative or positive CTE than that of structures which do not allow for phonon PAM, as
no correlation was found between phonon PAM and TE. The most polar phonon modes
were found to contribute with a negligible small part of TE of the investigated materials.
On the other hand, polar modes were found to have lower energies on average and con­
tribute relatively more to TE than non­polar modes. The relatively large contribution to
TE from polar modes might be explained by the on average lower energies as Grüneisen
parameters are inversely proportional to phonon energies.
Phonon PAM can be considered an extra degree of freedom as eigenvectors which have a
net PAM are not allowed if phonon PAM is not. It is the absence of the combined symmetry
of time reversal symmetry followed by inversion symmetry, which allows for phonon PAM.
Chiral structures do not have inversion symmetry. Lowering the symmetry of a structure
by other means than not having inversion symmetry increases the degrees of freedom of
phonons as well. The low symmetry of chiral structures may facilitate modes with strong
vibrational TE mechanisms which would have been prohibited or restricted in structures
of higher symmetry. Such as the case of α­ZrW2O8, for which the important vibrational
NTE mechanisms are fluctuations towards a denser structure and phonon PAM allows for
a more wide range of modes fluctuating towards the denser structure.
No causalities explaining how circular polarisation of atoms or phonon PAM could cause
NTE or abnormal CTE were found. Only indirect effects were found such as larger degrees
of freedom of the phonon modes and lower average energies of polar modes.

SiO2­structures were relaxed at a pressure of 0 GPa. Respecting the symmetry of the
structures, both shape, volume and atomic positions were allowed to relax. The results
are shown in table C.3. The absolute energies are meaningless as the reference point for

The β­structures must be stabilised to be investigated. Within Abinit anisotropic stresses
can be defined which are needed for the anisotropic Grüneisen models. The β­structures
must be stabilised somehow within the DFT formalism. The phonon frequencies are tem­
perature dependent and positive for dynamically stable phases. It is not necessary to
calculate the phonon frequencies at different temperatures as it is only the change in fre­
quencies with respect to applied stresses which are needed for the anisotropic Grüneisen
models. Other methods must be used to calculate phonon spectra at different tempera­
tures as the temperature is 0 K within the DFT formalism by definition. In this thesis, all
which is needed is to compare one stable phonon spectra of each structure.
The stable phase of a compound is the phase with the lowest Gibbs free energy given
by eg. 3.1, as described in the beginning of chapter 3. Temperature effects can not be
handled within the formalism of DFT as DFT calculations are performed at 0 K. The Gibbs
free energy of the calculated structures are: G = E +pV , as T=0 K. The α­ and β­phases
will co­exist when the Gibbs free energy per SiO2 unit of the two phases are equal. The
aim is to introduce stresses corresponding to different pressures and calculate the volume
and electronic energy of the 4 SiO2­phases. It is expected that the β­structures will have
lower Gibbs free energies than the corresponding α­structures at some negative pressure
and become stable. The four structures were relaxed and the results are shown in fig.
C.7. As the β­structures are of higher symmetry than the α­structures a full relaxation
will result in a transition to the β­structures, if the Gibbs free energy of the β­structures
become lower than that of the α­structures. The higher symmetry phases are sub spaces
within the lower symmetry phase spaces. The relaxations are constrained is such a way
not to break the symmetry of the structures, therefore it is possible to find the ground
state of unstable phases as long as the energy landscape allows for a local minima to be
found. The β­structures had local minima and therefore the ground state structures could
be calculated without expanding the structures.

D.4
New Measure of Circular Polarisation
The re­normalised circular polarisation of the modes about the different axes have been
calculated for: the individual atoms, the 3 elements and different subsets of atoms. The re­
normalised polarisation of each of the 44 atoms has not been shown. The disadvantage of
calculating sums of atoms is that the polarisation of atoms with the opposite handedness
cancel each other. Only the results for sx are shown. The trends are the same for sy and
sz which they must be because the crystal structure is cubic. sx of the different elements
are shown in fig. D.7. Both the vibrations of the W­, O­, and Zr­atoms result in a degree
of circular motion about the x­axis of some of the branches. The O­atoms have larger
re­normalised magnitudes of sx than the W­ and Zr­atoms. The expectation value of the
circular polarisation is the result of the amplitude and the degree of circular movement of
the vibrations. The relative amplitudes are contained in e(q) and O is the lightest element
and larger amplitudes are expected. The handedness of most of the branches below 10
meV are the same for the W­ and O­atoms but the magnitude is different. The handedness
of the Zr­atoms are different from that of the W­ and O­atoms for some of the branches.
The polarisation of different elements do not tend to cancel each other.
