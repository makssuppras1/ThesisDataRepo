Abstract
Urban wet-weather discharges (WWD) from combined sewer overﬂows or separate sewer
outlets may contain important loads of micropollutants (MP) that exhibit toxic stress
on aquatic organisms in the receiving water bodies during storm events. Due to the
high spatial and temporal variability of these discharges, a comprehensive monitoring of
resulting MP concentrations in surface waters is often not feasible. Instead, screening
models can be an eﬃcient tool for a preliminary risk assessment in order to identify river
stretches that are critically impacted by WWD.
In this thesis, a simple chemical fate-model has been developed for this purpose,
modelling concentrations of MP that can be expected during or after storm events in
Danish surface waters. Both dilution and removal of MP due to environmental processes
such as biodegradation or settling in receiving water bodies were taken into account. The
most relevant fate processes and model parameters for 39 diﬀerent MP were determined
via sensitivity analysis and the model was tested on a case study of three Danish rivers.
It was found that for most MP the environmental removal processes are too slow to
play a role in their fate considering the short residence times in Danish rivers in the
range of hours to a few days, and that dilution is the predominant dissipation process.
It was therefore concluded that for most MP, a simple river dilution model is suﬃcient
to allow for a robust risk assessment, especially since the parameters for the fate model
can only be estimated with high uncertainties. However, for heavy metals the developed
fate model provides an added value, since it allows to calculate the dissolved fraction of
their total concentrations in surface waters that is decisive for their toxicity and hence
their environmental risk assessment.
The application of the model for a risk assessment across Denmark identiﬁed a high
risk of exceedance of environmental quality standards, particularly for heavy metals and
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Predicted concentrations (50th and 90th percentile)
exceeded the thresholds of at least one substance in 64% and 75% of the modelled river
points, respectively, 10% and 63% by a factor > 10. These ﬁndings indicate problematic
impacts of WWD on Danish water bodies in terms of acute toxic stress and should
therefore be further investigated through more detailed models or monitoring campaigns
at the aﬀected river stretches.
Finally, the model was applied for a management scenario analysis. It was investigated
whether a sewer separation as a potential mitigation approach can reduce the impact of
WWD in terms of MP concentrations in the receiving water bodies. The results show
that while the input of mainly wastewater originating substances could be reduced, the
risk of exceeding environmental quality standards was increased for most investigated
MP. Unless the run-oﬀcollected in separate sewers is treated before being discharged
into the water body, sewer separation is therefore not a recommended mitigation option.

1. Introduction
Over the past years, urban run-oﬀhas come more into focus as an important source
of micropollutants (MP) impacting the water quality of receiving water bodies (Wicke
et al. 2021; Gasperi et al. 2014; Zgheib et al. 2012).
It has been estimated that MP
loads from urban stormwater conveyed into surface waters via combined sewer overﬂows
(CSO) and stormwater outlets (SWO) from separate sewers can be in the same order of
magnitude as those from waste water treatment plants (WWTP) or even considerably
higher, depending on the MP (Wicke et al. 2015). Consequently, the mitigation of these
wet-weather discharge emissions is essential to achieve a good chemical status in the
receiving water bodies (RWB) which is the goal of the European Water Framework
Directive for 2027.
In Europe, wet-weather discharges are currently mostly regulated through a source-
oriented approach, i.e. on the level of individual CSOs/SWOs via uniform emission limit
values (ELV) in terms of discharge volume or frequency (Dirckx et al. 2011). However,
these have shown to not correlate well with the acute impacts of the discharges on
the RWB, such as acute toxic eﬀects from MP (Engelhard et al. 2008). Consequently,
there is a need for a more eﬀect-oriented approach in the regulation of wet-weather
discharge where the predicted resulting concentrations of MP in the RWB are taken into
account. This requires a holistic assessment of the cumulative impact of the discharges
on the RWB, in order to be able to set scientiﬁcally based requirements at the base of
future further regulations for wet-weather discharges, as well as to allow for stormwater
management at a strategic level rather than at the level of individual CSOs/SWOs
(Jensen et al. 2020). As monitoring campaigns are very time- and resource-consuming
and not suitable to assess these impacts on a national scale with high resolution for local
catchments, models can serve as eﬀective tools to predict concentrations of MP in the
recipients. Integrated urban wastewater and stormwater system (IUWS) models have
been used for single local applications to assess the impact of stormwater discharges
on MP concentrations in the recipients and identify cost-eﬀective mitigation options
(Vezzaro et al. 2014). However, the set-up of these models is complex, as they require
calibration for a big set of parameters, and hence time- and resource-intensive data
collection, which makes them inadequate for the application on a big scale (Schellart
and Rieckermann 2019). Instead, more simple and easy-to-set-up models are needed
which can be used to screen for potentially critically impacted spots and corresponding
SWOs/CSOs.
Then at a second stage these river stretches or SWOs/CSOs can be
assessed in more detail through IUWS models or speciﬁc monitoring.
Within the scope of a previous Master thesis (Scarpellini 2021), a simple spatially
explicit dilution model was developed to predict the impact of CSO emissions on
1
==PAGE:14==
Danish surface waters.
It is based on open data on modelled river ﬂows from the
Danish National Water Resource model, modelled CSO discharges from the Danish
Environmental Protection Agency, and estimated event mean concentrations. Two cases
were assessed: First, only the local dilution at CSO discharge points was considered
for calculating resulting concentrations in the recipients, without taking into account
eventual accumulation of MP loads along the river basin.
Second, the cumulative
impact of all CSO discharges within a river basin was estimated in a worst-case
scenario, where all CSOs of one river basin would overﬂow in sequence, thus using
the cumulative MP load of all upstream discharges for the modelling of surface water
concentrations.
This model allowed to screen for critical discharge points leading to
the exceedance of Environmental Quality Standards (EQS). However, extremely high
modelled concentration values were observed for some MPs that were assessed as not
plausible (Scarpellini 2021).
As this was the case for MPs such as heavy metals or
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH), which are expected to be mostly associated to
particles, it was concluded that the simple dilution model does not properly reﬂect the
MP’s fate within the surface water, but removal processes such as sedimentation need to
be taken into account. Furthermore, in order to improve the robustness of the modelling
results, it was proposed to use a stochastic emission modelling approach to reﬂect the
high temporal variability of wet-weather discharges, and to include propagation of
model uncertainties, expressing results in terms of risk of EQS exceedance. Finally, the
model only considered wet-weather discharges from CSOs, although for a comprehensive
ecological risk assessment the discharges from SWOs should also be taken into account,
since they represent another important MP source during storm events. Therefore, this
thesis aims to implement these suggested improvements and develop a simple chemical
fate-model to assess short-term impacts of MP from both CSOs and SWOs on Danish
streams, based on the existing simple screening model developed by Scarpellini (2021).
Spatially explicit river fate modelling has been done extensively to predict MP
concentrations on a river catchment or even continental and global scale (Grill et al.
2016; Oldenkamp et al. 2018; Font et al. 2019; P. D. Anderson et al. 2004; Kehrein
et al. 2015).
However, these models focus on MPs conveyed into the river systems
through WWTPs, or take total urban MP emission loads estimated from spatial
consumption data as an input, and have not been used yet to model speciﬁcally the
pressure of wet-weather discharges on recipients. The latter have certain characteristics
distinguishing them from WWTP discharges: While for mostly wastewater originating
substances such as pharmaceuticals or personal care products (PPCP) the assumption
of a constant emission at constant concentrations seems reasonable, the variability of
stormwater emissions questions the applicability of the steady-state-approach of these
models. For wastewater originating substances, relatively constant concentrations can
be expected in the river basins, hence their risk is usually assessed in terms of chronic
exposure (e.g. Grill et al. 2016). In contrast, urban run-oﬀmay lead to short-time peak
concentrations during rain events such that the assessment of acute exposure also plays
a role (Spahr et al. 2020; Wicke et al. 2021). In this case, long-term removal processes
2
==PAGE:15==
such as biodegradation that typically have half-lives of several days in surface water
(Monteiro and Boxall 2010; Souza et al. 2020) will play a less important role for the risk
assessment. In addition, since the aim of the simple screening model is also to assess
impacts on a local scale for short river stretches or small creeks at a high resolution,
residence times will be shorter than in the existing fate models at the scale of river
catchments or continental and global scales, decreasing the potential for signiﬁcant
MP removal through slower processes. These distinctions may have implications both
in terms of the general applicability of this fate modelling approach to wet-weather
discharges, and in terms of the inﬂuence fate modelling has on the modelled impact of
wet-weather discharges on the RWB.
The objectives of the thesis are therefore the following:
1. Development of a model to assess the impact of wet-weather discharges
on the receiving water bodies in terms of chemical status, taking into
account in-stream dissipation of MP due to environmental fate pro-
cesses. It is investigated whether the mentioned spatial fate modelling approach
used for MP from WWTP discharges can be reasonably applied for assessing the
impact of wet-weather discharges in Denmark at a high resolution without intro-
ducing requirements for extensive data collection. In contrast to the simple dilution
model developed by Scarpellini (2021), not only discharges from CSOs, but also
stormwater discharges from separate systems are considered in order to assess the
cumulative impact of all wet-weather discharges.
2. Determination of the most important model parameters via sensitivity
analysis. For instance, it is investigated which removal processes are crucial for
which MP.
3. Assessment of the inﬂuence of considering fate processes on the resulting
concentrations in the RWB. It is evaluated whether including fate modelling
in the simple screening model and therefore increasing its complexity is justiﬁed,
considering the general uncertainty of the results.
4. Application of the model for ecological risk assessment. The model is used
to identify river stretches with a high risk for exceeding EQS due to wet-weather
discharges. The high temporal variability of wet-weather discharges as well as the
uncertainty of parameters is taken into account using a stochastic approach with
Monte Carlo simulations.
5. Application of the model for management scenario analysis. Within a
hypothetical case study, the applicability of the model for planning purposes is
investigated.
The fate modelling is ﬁrst implemented and tested for three Danish rivers with dif-
ferent characteristics. Subsequently, it is applied to all Denmark for the ecological risk
assessment.

2. Background
2.1. Characteristics of wet-weather discharges and their impact
on the chemical status of receiving water bodies
Wet weather discharges result from the runoﬀgenerated from impervious areas during
storm events. There are two types of sewer systems to collect urban runoﬀthat lead to
diﬀerent types of wet-weather discharges: In combined sewer systems, the runoﬀenters
sewers together with wastewater from households or industries and is normally treated
in wastewater treatment plants. However, during heavy rains, the hydraulic load often
exceeds the capacity of the sewer system and/or the WWTP, leading to combined sewer
overﬂows (CSO) that discharge the mixture of untreated wastewater and stormwater
directly into the receiving water body. The alternative is a separate sewer system where
stormwater is collected separately from wastewater. This avoids overloading of sewers
and WWTPs during storm events, so no untreated wastewater is discharged into the
RWB. The stormwater itself however is always discharged directly or only after a certain
treatment into the RWB via stormwater outlets (SWO). Consequently, although average
concentrations of most pollutants are lower in SWOs than in CSOs, the total volume
discharged untreated to the RWB is higher and therefore also the total pollutant load
may be higher in separate than in combined sewer systems. (Vezzaro et al. 2018)
Wet weather discharges can carry a wide range of pollutants that pose a risk to the
RWB. In the past, the focus was mainly on traditional pollutants such as suspended
particles and nutrients that lead to oxygen depletion or eutrophication (Borchardt and
Sperling 1997; Even et al. 2004). However, during the past decade the impact of wet
weather discharges on the chemical status of the water bodies has gained attention
(Gasperi et al. 2014; Wicke et al. 2015). Chemical substances that are problematic due
to their toxicity, bioaccumulation and/or persistence even at low concentrations in the
range of µg/L or ng/L are called micropollutants (MP) (Schwarzenbach et al. 2006).
The term covers a wide range of chemical compounds, including pharmaceuticals and
personal care products (PPCP), household and industrial chemicals, pesticides and bio-
cides, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) and heavy metals. Micropollutants enter
stormwater via the wash-oﬀfrom urban surfaces such as PAH originating from combus-
tion processes from highways or biocides from facades (M¨uller et al. 2020; Burkhardt
et al. 2012), and CSOs also contain wastewater originating substances such as PPCP
(Gasperi et al. 2012). Their occurrence and concentrations depend on the properties of
the catchment such as land use, as well as on rainfall variables such as the magnitude and
duration of the rain event and the duration of antecedent dry-weather periods (Wicke
et al. 2021; Maniquiz et al. 2010), since they inﬂuence the accumulation and wash-oﬀpro-
4
==PAGE:17==
cesses of MP. Some MP such as pesticides also show an important seasonality (Wicke
et al. 2015; Helmreich et al. 2010). Therefore, MP concentrations in wet-weather dis-
charges generally exhibit a high variability, across sites and events as well as within
events. For instance, nicotine from cigarette buts shows a so-called ﬁrst-ﬂush behavior
with high concentrations in stormwater at the beginning of a storm event, while with in-
creasing duration, concentrations decrease due to progressing elution (Wicke et al. 2015;
Roder Green et al. 2014).
Consequently, monitoring of MP in wet-weather discharges and determining represen-
tative concentrations is costly and time intensive. Concentrations of MP in wet-weather
discharges are mostly reported as event mean concentrations (EMC) that are determined
from time-, ﬂow- or volume-proportional sampling. Mutzner et al. (2021) estimated that
at least 6 or 7 EMC need to be measured per site for CSOs and SWOs, respectively,
in order to obtain a reliable estimate of the site mean concentration. This would be
needed in order to assess whether a discharge point exceeds potential emission limit
values (ELV). However, wet-weather discharges are currently not consistently regulated
in the EU and Denmark, as outlined in the next chapter. For the impact of wet-weather
discharges on the RWB not only the conveyed pollutant loads, but also the characteris-
tics of the RWB itself, such as upstream water quality and the dilution potential, play an
important role. For instance, for small rivers in highly urbanized areas, wet-weather dis-
charges may contribute signiﬁcantly to the ﬂow in the RWB, resulting in high pollutant
concentrations during rain events, while big rivers with good upstream water quality
will be less impacted (Vezzaro et al. 2018).
2.2. Regulation of wet-weather discharges within the EU and
Denmark - current status and challenges
The EU Water Framework Directive (WFD) sets the basis for the regulation of wet-
weather discharges within the EU (European Commission 2022).
It aims to achieve
good ecological and chemical status of water bodies by 2015 or latest 2027. In terms of
chemical status, this means that the Environmental Quality Standards (EQS) for chem-
ical substances need to be met, expressed as maximum allowable concentrations (MAC-
EQS) or annual averages (AA-EQS) that must not be exceeded (EU Directive 2013/39
2013). For achieving this target, a combined approach on the source and the eﬀect side
is promoted: source control strategies such as Emission Limit Values (ELV) and Best
Available Techniques (BAT) for point sources, as well as Best Environmental Practices
(BEP) for diﬀuse sources need to be implemented as basic measures, while additional
measures are required if on the eﬀect side the EQS are not met. The implementation of
the WFD is based on catchments for which so-called River Basin Management Plans are
established in cycles of six years, reporting the current status and setting targets for the
future, including programmes of measures. This approach aims to address the issue of
surface water quality in an integrated way looking at the cumulative impact of human
activity, independent of political or administrative boundaries. (European Commission
2022)
5
==PAGE:18==
However, this catchment-based, combined approach is often not reﬂected in the im-
plementation of the WFD and the resulting regulations for wet-weather discharges on a
national level.
In Denmark for instance, objectives and measures for the River Basin Management
Plans are formulated on a national level, but the actual implementation in terms of
regulating wet-weather discharges is subject to the municipalities (Jensen et al. 2020).
They are responsible for the issuance of discharge permits. However, there is no con-
sistent handling of these permits, especially for separate stormwater discharges, since
the statutory orders that accompany the River Basin Management Plans remain very
vague, stating that the discharge must not prevent the recipient from meeting the EQS.
As a consequence, municipal authorities have to derive the requirements to be made in
the permits in terms of ELV, BAT and BEP themselves in order to ensure compliance
with the EQS in the recipients. This poses a challenge, since setting scientiﬁcally sound
requirements in the discharge permits would require an assessment of the cumulative im-
pact of wet-weather discharges to the recipients (Jensen et al. 2020). A similar situation
can be seen in other European countries: In Spain for instance, 14 diﬀerent watershed
authorities with diﬀerent CSO and stormwater discharge permits and sizing criteria exist
(Schellart and Rieckermann 2019).
In Denmark, all wet-weather discharges from combined as well as separate systems
need to be registered in a national database (punktkildedatabase - PULS), where mu-
nicipalities report yearly discharges in terms of volume, P, N and COD load which are
estimated by using mathematical models (Vezzaro et al. 2019). However, uncertainties
of these estimates are high (at least 150-200% for a single discharge event), and there
is no such emission inventory in terms of chemical pollutants. In addition, monitoring
of the chemical status of the recipients in Denmark is poor. Hence, there is no holistic
assessment of pressures and eﬀects at the catchment scale which hampers the setting
of discharge limits at a strategic level, rather than at the individual outlet point scale
(Jensen et al. 2020). This has also been criticized by the report of the European Commis-
sion on the implementation of the WFD within the Member States in the context of the
Second River Basin Management Plans. It claims that although Denmark does address
point sources in its Programmes of Measures a.o. aiming at reducing the discharge from
stormwater overﬂows, there is no assessment of how eﬀective these measures will be at
improving the chemical status of the recipients (European Commission 2019).
Also in most other European countries the regulation of wet-weather discharges is
currently mainly source-focused, i.e. based on uniform limit values at the source instead
of an eﬀect-based approach on the basis of EQS in the recipient (Dirckx et al. 2011).
Only few countries implement CSO regulation approaches solely based on EQS, e.g. the
United Kingdom. Such a site-speciﬁc regulation based on the status of the local recipient
needs integrated modelling studies of the sewer network, waste water treatment plant,
the river ﬂow and its water quality with extensive data requirements (Schellart and
Rieckermann 2019).
A general challenge for the regulation of wet-weather discharges by emission limits
is their short-intermittent nature making it diﬃcult to correlate impacts with emission
6
==PAGE:19==
based parameters that are easy to determine. Common design criteria for CSOs are
a minimum dilution of the dry weather ﬂow, i.e.
a throttle ﬂow limitation, and a
maximum annual overﬂow frequency, but also limits for discharge volume, discharge
ﬂow, loads or concentrations at the discharge points can be deﬁned (Vezzaro et al. 2018).
An investigation by Engelhard et al. (2008) showed that while long-term impacts on
the receiving water resulting from discharge loads (hydraulic load, eutrophication and
persistent pollutants) can be described well by volume based parameters, no suitable
parameter was found to correlate with acute impacts such as oxygen depletion or acute
toxic impacts. Although overﬂow frequency based indicators showed no good correlation
to any impacts, several countries base their design norms for CSOs on this parameter,
also Denmark (Dirckx et al. 2011).
Overall, the current gaps and challenges in the regulation of wet-weather discharges
highlight the need for the implementation of more holistic, eﬀect-based approaches. To
this end, simple assessment tools for local regulators to estimate the cumulative impact
of wet-weather discharges on the recipient need to be developed.
2.3. A simple screening tool to assess the impacts of combined
sewer overﬂows on freshwater
In a previous Master thesis, Scarpellini (2021) developed a simple dilution model in or-
der to assess the impact of CSOs on Danish rivers in terms of chemical status regarding
micropollutants. It is based on open data of modelled streamﬂows and annual CSO
discharges in Denmark, and on typical MP concentrations in CSO discharges which
were collected in another study (Mutzner et al. 2022).
The model was developed to
estimate MP concentrations resulting from the contribution of CSOs in Danish rivers.
The concentrations in each river, discretized into river points at a 100 m resolution,
were calculated assuming a simple dilution of the MP load from the CSOs by the river
discharge. Two cases were assessed: First, only the local MP loads discharged at the
respective river points were taken into account for the calculation of the resulting con-
centration (see equation 1). Second, the cumulative impact of the CSOs was assessed
by assuming an accumulation of the MP load of all CSOs upstream the respective river
points, i.e. the concentration at each river point was calculated taking into account the
upstream concentration (see equation 2). The latter approach corresponds to a worst
case scenario where it is assumed that once the pollution plume carrying the MP load of
an upstream CSO passes the next CSO, that one overﬂows as well, and so do all CSOs
downstream, leading to the maximum possible accumulation.

the conservative assumption of an accumulation of all CSO discharges along a river, no
environmental processes leading to an in-stream dissipation of the MP concentrations
were considered in this simple screening tool. These are expected to be particularly
relevant for MP with a high tendency to sorb to suspended particles, which can then
be removed via sedimentation from the water column. This is the case for most heavy
metals and PAH.
In order to improve the robustness of the results of the screening model, Scarpellini
(2021) therefore proposed to develop the model further to include environmental fate
processes. The development of such a fate model is the aim of the present work.

3. Modelling procedure
The development, implementation, testing and application of models follows commonly
certain general steps (c.f. Jørgensen and Fath 2011; Jakeman et al. 2006) which have
also been followed in this thesis. Figure 1 summarizes the performed steps that also
form the structure of this report.
After the deﬁnition of the modelling purpose and the mapping of the modelling context
including the identiﬁcation of the basis and prerequisites of the model and a system
description, a literature review on existing spatial river fate models was performed.
From this knowledge collection, the model structure was developed deﬁning the equations
and parameters of the model. Subsequently, the parameter values for the model were
determined and the micropollutants to be investigated were selected. With these steps,
the ﬁrst objective of the thesis, the model development, was achieved.
A sensitivity analysis and an analysis of the results of a test model run with selected
rivers were performed in order to assess the inﬂuence of considering fate processes on
the resulting concentrations in the RWB (thesis objectives 2 and 3). Finally, the model
was applied for the risk assessment across all Denmark and the management scenario
analysis to address the objectives 4 and 5 of this thesis.
It was not possible to perform a model calibration and validation of the model, since
no representative measurement/monitoring data is available on MP concentrations in
Danish rivers during and after rain events that could be used to compare to predicted
concentrations from the model.

4. Model development
4.1. Deﬁnition of modelling purpose
The model aims to assess the impact of wet-weather discharges on the chemical status
in terms of MP in the receiving water bodies in Denmark. Speciﬁcally, the model should
allow i) to identify river stretches across Denmark that have a high risk to exceed EQS
due to MP loads conveyed by wet-weather discharges and hence to design, prioritize, or
optimize monitoring programmes and mitigation strategies, ii) to assess the cumulative
impact of wet-weather discharge points and identify critical ones in order to facilitate
regulators setting scientiﬁcally sound requirements in the respective discharge permits,
and iii) to evaluate diﬀerent management/control strategies via scenario comparison.
To this aim, the model should provide spatially explicit estimates of MP concentra-
tions in the RWB that can be expected during or after rain events resulting from MP
inputs from wet-weather discharges, and compare them to the respective EQS. Since
wet-weather discharges exhibit a high variability, the probability of EQS exceedances
should be assessed via stochastic modelling.
4.2. Modelling context
Prerequisites and deﬁnition of the system
Building on the work of Scarpellini (2021), the basis of the model is the open
data on monthly streamﬂow and volume estimates of wet-weather discharges in Den-
mark.
The former is available from the Danish Hydrological Information and Pre-
diction system (Hydrologisk Informations- og Prognosesystem - HIP, accessible via
https://hip.dataforsyningen.dk). It provides the results of the hydrological model cal-
culations from the National Water Resource Model, which uses data on the entire hy-
drological cycle to calculate depth to groundwater, soil water content in the root zone,
discharge, inﬁltration and groundwater ﬂow. Average monthly discharge data is avail-
able for all Danish streams at a 100 m grid resolution, for all years since 1990. The data
can be obtained as a spreadsheet with the coordinates of the discretized river points, a
corresponding ID indicating the name of the river and the location along the river, and
the respective discharge. Volume estimates of wet-weather discharges can be obtained
from a database managed by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (PunktU-
dledningsSystem - PULS, accessible via https://spildevandsdata.dk/) for the record of
emissions from point sources. There, municipalities have to report model-based esti-
mates on annual discharge volumes of all CSO and SWO structures in Denmark. The
11
==PAGE:24==
data can also be obtained as a spreadsheed containing the coordinates of the discharge
points and the respective discharge volumes.
Hence, no source/emission model is needed and the system consists only of the mod-
elled river points given by the HIP dataset and the wet-weather discharge points from
the PULS database releasing the MP loads to the river points. The system comprises
thus only the river network, and the model should therefore replicate the release of the
MP load to the river, their dilution, and the environmental fate processes leading to
an in-stream decay of the MP load. The model to be developed is thus in principle a
classical river fate model.
Spatial and temporal scale
The model should serve as a screening tool to identify river stretches with critical impacts
from wet-weather discharges across Denmark, i.e. at a big spatial scale. However, it
should also provide a high spatial resolution in order to assess the impact of single
discharge points and assist municipalities in the regulation, planning and management
of these. As the open streamﬂow data from the HIP model serves as a basis for the
model, the same 100m grid resolution should be adopted.
As a screening tool, the model is not meant to predict time-resolved MP concentra-
tions in the river network, but to assess the risk of EQS exceedances due to wet-weather
discharges during a rain event under speciﬁc assumptions. For instance, it should allow
to model maximum MP concentrations in a worst-case scenario regarding streamﬂow
conditions, the magnitude of the rain event and the accumulation of wet-weather dis-
charges.
The temporal scale hence comprises single rain events causing wet-weather
discharges.
4.3. Prior knowledge collection
In the following section, diﬀerent types of environmental fate models are brieﬂy discussed,
focussing on spatially explicit fate models instead of simple box models, as the former
are required for a spatial risk assessment aimed by the screening model. Also, previous
studies using spatially explicit river fate modelling for assessing the risk of MP are
presented that served as archetypes for the fate modelling implemented in this thesis.
4.3.1. Existing spatial river fate models
Diﬀerent approaches to model river water quality exist, diﬀering signiﬁcantly in their
complexity and parameter requirements and hence in their scope of application. They
can be divided into two main types, multi-media and single-media fate models.
Multi-media fate models model the processes within and the transport between dif-
ferent environmental compartments such as water, sediment and atmosphere.
They
normally model rivers as serial continuous stirred tank reactors (CSTR) with multiple
compartments, for each solving mass balances with transport and reaction terms. This
representation of the diﬀerent compartments is usually required either for high-resolution
12
==PAGE:25==
applications at a small scale where a detailed description of transport processes between
media is needed to compute concentrations in the water phase at suﬃcient accuracy, or
if the spatial distribution of chemicals is being investigated explicitly in diﬀerent media,
however at a coarser resolution. Examples are the Integrated Urban Wastewater and
Stormwater system model (e.g. Vezzaro et al. 2014), used at the small spatial scale of
urban areas, and STREAM-EU, the Spatially and Temporally Resolved Exposure As-
sessment Model for EUropean basins (Lindim et al. 2016), which has been applied at
the scale of river basins. Spatially explicit multi-media fate models are usually dynamic,
i.e. time-resolved models and thus used when there are important temporal variations
of discharges and emissions. However, along with the description of various media, this
makes them very parameter- and computation intensive.
A simpliﬁcation can be made by modelling only one compartment and treating trans-
ports to other media as losses, assuming that feedback ﬂuxes are negligible. This as-
sumption is reasonable if a substance is mostly emitted to the modelled compartment,
leading to a negative concentration gradient towards other media (Pistocchi et al. 2010).
Spatially explicit single-media fate models assume advection to be the dominant dilu-
tion mechanism. Rivers are modelled as one-dimensional plug ﬂows, where in-stream
dissipation of an emission load E is modelled through a ﬁrst-order decay function using
stream velocity u and distance x from the emission source as the parameters determin-
ing reaction time, while the river discharge Q determines the dilution (see equation 3)
(Pistocchi et al. 2010). The ﬁrst-order removal rate constant k lumps all transformation
processes and losses due to transport to other media.

4.3.2. Estimation of hydrological parameters in river fate models
Some of the mentioned river fate models such as GREAT-ER (Kehrein et al. 2015) or
PhATE (P. D. Anderson et al. 2004) require the direct input of spatial data about river
and catchment geometries and river attributes such as discharge, depth and velocity
for each modelled stream segment, i.e. they rely on external models estimating these
parameters or on existing databases. However, especially for models at big scales, this
data is diﬃcult to obtain. The models by Oldenkamp et al. (2018), Grill et al. (2016), and
Font et al. (2019) therefore calculate the necessary hydrological parameters from data
that can be obtained easily from Geographic Information Systems and open databases.
Streamﬂow data is usually calculated from run-oﬀaccumulation (Font et al. 2019; Grill
et al. 2016). For the estimation of river velocity and depth, two main approaches have
been used that are described below.
Hydraulic geometry regressions
Leopold and Maddock (1953) showed that river width, depth and velocity vary with
discharge as simple power functions of the form x = αQβ. These relationships hold
true both for varying discharges at a single cross-section and for discharges with the
same frequency of occurrence (e.g. mean annual discharge) at diﬀerent cross-sections
along a river. It was also shown that although the intercept α of the log-log plots varies
greatly among rivers, the slope β is in a similar order of magnitude even for rivers
with great diﬀerences in their width to depth ratios. Especially for the width-discharge
regression, β seems to be quite consistent between basins. Typical values for β are 0.5 for
the regression of river width, 0.4 for depth and 0.1 for velocity (Leopold and Maddock
1953).
Despite the variations among rivers, studies have also applied these types of regressions
to approximate river width, depth and velocity across river basins on continental scales.
For instance, Allen et al. (1994) developed empirical equations to estimate these river
characteristics from bankfull discharge across the U.S.. The corresponding equation for
river velocity with α = 1.07 and β = 0.1035 was used by Grill et al. (2016) in their
spatial single media fate model for a Canadian river basin.
Manning’s equation
Oldenkamp et al. (2018) and Font et al. (2019) used Manning’s equation to calculate
river velocity. It relates river discharge Q, hydraulic radius R, cross-sectional area A,
channel slope J and Manning’s roughness coeﬃcient n:

4.4. Deﬁnition of model structure
4.4.1. General approach
For the present model, the steady state single-media approach was used. Although it
has been applied only to model the fate of MP from continuous emissions so far, this
approach was considered to be suitable for modelling the fate of MP originating from
variable wet-weather discharges in this case due to the following reasons:
The aim of the simple screening model is not to predict time-resolved concentration pro-
ﬁles during rain events, but to estimate maximum concentrations that can be expected
from the accumulation of wet-weather discharges.
Its approach is therefore also not
a dynamic, time-resolved one, but similarly to a spatially explicit steady state model,
single values per location are computed. That is, the conservative assumption of an
accumulation of all wet-weather discharge loads along a river corresponds in principle to
the steady state assumption of the single-media fate models presented above.
The single-media approach also allowed to keep the simple model structure from the
model by Scarpellini (2021), since no additional compartments are modelled, but the
model boundaries are conﬁned to the modelled river points.
Moreover, since the aim of the model is to provide a simple screening tool without exten-
sive data requirements for model parameterization, it was aimed to keep its simplicity as
far as possible. Modelling the river as a one-dimensional plug-ﬂow (c.f. equation 3) does
not require complex parameterization, the only additional parameters which are needed
compared to the simple screening model by (Scarpellini 2021) are river velocity and the
MP speciﬁc removal rate constants. The former can be estimated as explained above,

the latter can be calculated from the physico-chemical properties and reported half lives
of the MP retrieved from public data bases, as will be explained in the following sections.
Thus, the general approach of the fate model developed in this thesis is an extension
of the dilution equation used in the simple screening model (c.f. equation 2) by a decay

4.4.2. Modelling of lakes
Due to the high hydraulic residence time in lakes, no signiﬁcant short-term impacts
of wet-weather discharges on MP concentrations in the lakes are expected. Instead, it
was assumed that a constant equilibrium concentration is established in the lakes as
a result from the annual MP load discharged into the lakes from upstream.
Hence,
lakes were modelled as CSTRs, with the cumulative annual MP load from CSOs and
SWOs upstream broken down to a constant input (EWWD), and with the same removal
processes taking place in the lakes as in the rivers.

other side, the input of MP from other sources, particularly the constant input from
WWTPs, is not taken into account. Nonetheless, this approximation was considered
a good compromise in terms of complexity and it can provide a ﬁrst estimate of the
order of magnitude of expected pollution levels in the lakes resulting from wet-weather
discharges.
4.4.3. Modelling of environmental fate processes
To model the diﬀerent environmental fate processes that lead to an in-stream decay of
the MP, general principles of environmental chemistry and fate modelling as for exam-
ple described in the classic work of Schwarzenbach et al. (2003) and the approaches of
existing single-media fate models have been reviewed. A selection of the equations to be
used in the model implementation was made taking into account the data availability for
the process parameters and the general structure of the model. The diﬀerent removal
processes needed to be described in terms of (pseudo-)ﬁrst-order kinetics to be able to
summarize them in a lumped overall ﬁrst-order removal rate constant k to be used in
the decay term of the principal model equation 9.
The processes considered in this model are partitioning, settling, volatilization, hydrol-
ysis, photolysis and biodegradation. It is assumed that the removal through volatiliza-
tion, biodegradation, hydrolysis and photodegradation only aﬀects the truly dissolved
fraction of a compound, while settling removes the particle-bound fraction. The overall
removal rate constant is therefore calculated according to equation 11. The equations
used to determine the partitioning behaviour and the removal rate constants of the dif-
ferent processes for the diﬀerent MP are described in detail in annex A, together with
the results of the literature review presenting the approaches of existing fate models.

4.4.4. Modelling of TSS and DOC concentrations
Wet-weather discharges constitute an important source of particulate matter (total sus-
pended solids - TSS) as well as dissolved organic carbon (DOC) which aﬀect the MP’s
partitioning in the river. Therefore, diﬀerent to existing river fate models, the concen-
trations of TSS and DOC were not assumed to be constant, but variable in space. TSS
concentrations were modelled from the input through CSOs and SWOs similarly as the
MP concentrations, with only settling as a removal process. It was assumed that there
is also a natural background concentration of TSS in the surface waters below which the
concentration does not fall, therefore total TSS concentration at each river point was
calculated as the sum of the concentration modelled from the CSO and SWO inputs and
the background concentration.
For the DOC, no removal process was considered, the DOC concentrations were hence
simply modelled as the mixing concentration of the DOC load from CSOs and SWOs
and the background concentration in the river. The resulting concentrations of TSS and
DOC were then used as an input for the fate modelling of the MP.
4.4.5. Model implementation
The model was implemented in Python 3. It was set up in a modular form, with a
main script where the substances for the screening are deﬁned and that loads all other
modules when needed: a river module setting up the river data, and the river fate module
modelling the MP concentrations. The latter furthermore calls a function that calculates
the removal constants for each river point.

5. Parameterization
5.1. River discharge
The discharge data from the HIP model of the year 2019 has been used. In order not
to overestimate dilution of wet-weather discharges in the rivers, a conservative approach
using the discharge data of the month with the lowest ﬂow was taken, in 2019 this holds
true for the month July. It must be noted that by using average monthly ﬂows, increased
river discharge during rain events is not considered. However, in rural catchments where
the rivers usually originate, runoﬀis generated with a slower response compared to
urban areas (Vezzaro et al. 2018). Hence, using the average monthly discharge is a rea-
sonable assumption, also representing a rather conservative estimate in terms of dilution
potential.
In contrast, the contribution of the CSOs and SWOs themselves to total river discharge
during rain events cannot be neglected. Especially for small rivers in highly urbanized
areas, wet-weather discharges may contribute signiﬁcantly to the total ﬂow (Vezzaro
et al. 2018). Therefore, the total ﬂow was calculated as the sum of the average monthly
discharge and the cumulative sum of all CSO and SWO discharges upstream. However,
due to the high hydraulic residence times in lakes, the total ﬂow downstream of a lake
was assumed not to be impacted by wet-weather discharges from upstream, i.e. it was
set equal to the average monthly discharge of the river.
For few river stretches, the HIP dataset lists negative discharges for July 2019. Since
this would lead to the calculation of negative concentrations by the model, the total ﬂow
at the respective river points was arbitrarily set to 0.001m3/s.
5.2. River velocity and depth
River velocity is the parameter determining the residence time in each river section and
is therefore essential for the fate modelling. However, it is not a model output of the
HIP model and therefore needed to be estimated. The same holds true for river depth,
which is needed for the calculation of several removal rate constants. Diﬀerent attempts
have been made to derive river velocity as well as river depth from available data.
If cross-section data is available, velocity can be calculated as the ratio between
river discharge and cross-sectional area. HIP provides cross-section data under ”rand-
betingelser” (boundary conditions), however only few municipalities have reported cross-
section data of their rivers to the HIP and the coverage was not suﬃcient to calculate
river velocity for the application of the model to all Denmark. Therefore, the follow-
ing two main approaches have been tested for estimating river velocity and depth from

available data.
Manning’s equation
It was tested whether the approach using the Manning’s equation as proposed by Pistoc-
chi and Pennington (2006) for continental scale modelling described in chapter 4.3.2 is
also applicable to Danish rivers at the smaller scale and higher spatial resolution. River
velocity and depth were calculated according to equation 6 and 7 from river discharge,
channel slope, river width, and a single value for Manning’s roughness coeﬃcient of
0.045sm−1/3.
Two options to get an estimate for the channel slope at each modelled river point were
tested: Once, a digital elevation model (DEM) at a 25m resolution was obtained from the
European Union’s Earth observation programme Copernicus (Copernicus 2011), which
was upscaled to a 100m resolution raster and subsequently transformed by applying
the GDAL slope raster analysis tool in QGIS to a slope raster with the same resolu-
tion.
Then, the slope raster was sampled by the river points.
The other time, the
DEM from the Danish Agency for Data Supply and Infrastructure (Dataforsyningen,
https://dataforsyningen.dk/data/928) at a 0.4m resolution was used and directly sam-
pled by the river points to calculate the slope as the diﬀerence in elevation between two
riverpoints divided by their distance.
River width was calculated from the regression by Pistocchi and Pennington (2006)
mentioned above (see equation 8). Since this regression was established from data for
big European rivers and hence its applicability to Danish small creeks is questionable,
an attempt was made to ﬁnd a regression speciﬁcally for Danish rivers. To this end,
the river width at the modelled river points was calculated directly from the WFS layer
”Vandloebskant” from the Dataforsyningen QGIS Plug-in which consists of a line layer
with the river shorelines of parts of the Danish river network (c.f. annex B.2). The line
layer was converted to a point layer (with a distance of 1m between points) and the river
shoreline points closest to the modelled river points were determined via the ”Distance
to nearest hub” tool. Only the ones with a maximum distance of 20m to a river point
were taken. Then, a processing algorithm was used to get the distance between these
shoreline points and the closest point in the shoreline points layer under the condition
that this point was not from the same line (S¸ahbaz 2020).
Hydraulic geometry regressions
Although Leopold and Maddock (1953) stressed that the power-law equations relating
river width, depth and velocity with discharge are only applicable for downstream cross-
sections with the same frequency distribution of discharges, these have been also applied
across river basins at higher scales (Allen et al. 1994; Pistocchi and Pennington 2006),
as already mentioned in chapter 4.3.2. It seemed therefore reasonable to try to ﬁnd a
regression for river velocity and depth as a function of discharge for Danish rivers.
A representative data set was obtained from a report of the Danish Environmental Re-
search Institute (Danmarks Miljøundersøgelser - DMU) of the year 2000 which provides

river velocity and depth under the modelled conditions where CSOs and SWOs con-

5.3. Lakes
For the parameterization of lakes, the global HydroLAKES database has been used,
which provides the shoreline polygons of all global lakes with a surface area of at least
10 ha together with attribute information such as surface area, shoreline length, and
estimates of average depth, water volume and residence time (Messager et al. 2016). To
determine which of the modelled river points from the HIP model lay within lakes, the
point layer of the river points was intersected with the lake polygon layer on QGIS,
and the respective estimates of lake depth, volume and residence time were extracted.
Individual uncertainties of the attribute estimates may be high, as these were predicted
using a geostatistical model based on surrounding land surface topography (Messager
et al. 2016). However, due to the lack of an open database providing these parameters
for Danish lakes with a higher accuracy and at a higher resolution, they have been used
in the model to provide a ﬁrst estimate of MP fate in lakes.

5.5. Environmental parameters
Sedimentation rate
According to Trapp and Matthies (2012), empirical values for the sediment growth are
negative (i.e. there is erosion and no net deposition) for rivers with a high slope, between
0 and 1 mm/a for slowly and normally ﬂowing rivers and between 1 and 3 mm/a for
very slow rivers and average lakes. In the model by (Oldenkamp et al. 2018), the default
value from the USEtox 2.0 model (Fantke et al. 2017) for the annual sediment growth
rate of 8.6 · 10−11m/s = 2.7mm/a was taken, while for the application of the GREAT-
ER model to the fate modelling of zinc in river Ruhr, H¨uﬀmeyer et al. (2009) used a
sediment growth rate varying between 0.5-5 mm/a depending on whether it was in river
reaches, lakes or drinking water reservoirs.
For the model in this thesis, only a diﬀerentiation between rivers and lakes was made,
and annual sediment growth rates of 0.5 and 3 mm/a were assumed, respectively.
To calculate the sedimentation rate from the annual sediment growth rate (c.f. equa-
tion 24), the same default values for the porosity of the sediment and the mineral density
of the sediment were used as in the model by Oldenkamp et al. (2018), i.e. 0.8 L/L (Pa-
terson and Mackay 1995) and 2.1633 kg/L (Fantke et al. 2017), respectively.
Concentration of suspended matter and dissolved organic carbon
For the TSS background concentration in rivers and lakes, a default value of 15 mg/L
was assumed. The same value was used in the model by Oldenkamp et al. (2018) based
on Humbert et al. (2011), and it is also the default value proposed by European Com-
mission - Directorate General for the Environment (2011) to derive EQS based on total
concentration from EQS based on dissolved concentration. A similar TSS concentration
of 10.29 mg/L was measured in the Danish river Usserød ˚A by the Riverscapes campaign
(Ribaucourt 2019).
For the organic fraction in suspended solids, the default value from the USEtox 2.0
model (Fantke et al. 2017) of 0.1 was used. This is close to the value of 0.085 adopted by
Ribaucourt (2019) in their model of Usserød ˚A according to the mean of values measured
by Kronvang et al. (2003) and Hnaˇtukov´a et al. (2009) in agricultural and urban streams.
The chosen value also lies within the typical range of 0.02-0.2 according to Schwarzenbach
et al. (2003).
For the DOC background concentration, a default value of 5 mg/L was taken which
has been used in the model by Oldenkamp et al. (2018) according to a report on river
Rhine (Asselman 1997).
This value lies in the range of other measured values, e.g.
2.9 mg/L in River Ruhr in Germany (Heß et al. 2004), 4 mg/L in Lake Greifensee
in Switzerland (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003), and an average of 7.2 mg/L in ten Danish
catchments situated in the Horsens Fjord area on the eastern coast of Jutland (Mattsson
et al. 2009).

5.6.2. Concentrations in wet-weather discharges
As mentioned, MP concentrations as well as TSS concentrations in wet-weather dis-
charges are highly variable. Mutzner et al. (2022) determined probability distributions
for MP concentrations in wet-weather discharges from datasets of measured concentra-
tions at 63 sites worldwide (Germany, US, Switzerland, France, Australia and Denmark)
with 506 monitored events and more than 42’000 observations. For a ﬁrst assessment,
the 50th and 90th percentiles of the distributions were used as constant concentrations
to calculate the MP load in the CSO and SWO discharges. The values can be found in
table B.1 in the annex.
Measured TSS concentrations in wet-weather discharges compiled by Vezzaro et al.
(2018) range between 13 and 1433 mg/L in CSOs and 0.006 and 6143 mg/L in SWOs.
For a ﬁrst assessment, the mean eﬄuent concentration of 260 mg/L for CSO discharges
and the median eﬄuent concentration of 68.1 mg/L for SWO discharges was used.
Data on DOC concentrations in wet-weather discharges is scarce.
In Australian
stormwater samples, measured DOC concentrations were in the range of 1-20 mg/L,
with most samples around 5 mg/L (Tang et al. 2013). Reemtsma et al. (2000) reported
DOC concentrations of 34+/-11 mg/L in combined sewer overﬂow in Berlin. In lack of
more representative data, these values of 5 mg/L and 34 mg/L were used as discharge
concentrations of SWOs and CSOs, respectively.
5.6.3. Data for physico-chemical properties and fate of micropollutants
Data on physico-chemical properties of the MP and their environmental fate was mostly
obtained from the open chemistry database PubChem at the U.S. National Institutes of
Health (Kim et al. 2021) which integrates the information of several databases, a.o. the
Hazardous Substances Data Bank (HSDB). It provides information on physico-chemical
properties and summarizes experimental data on partitioning coeﬃcients and half lives
of degradation processes.
For the organic micropollutants, the reported data for molar weight, dissociation con-
stant, vapor pressure, Henry’s constant, octanol-water partitioning constant (Kow), em-
pirical organic carbon-water partitioning coeﬃcients (Koc) and half lives for the removal
via biodegradation, photolysis and hydrolysis were retrieved. Especially for biodegrada-
tion, attention was paid to use values determined under conditions similar to expected
environmental conditions, i.e. in surface water. If several values or a range of values was
reported, the minimum and maximum value were retrieved. For partitioning constants,
the logarithmic mean of the reported maximum and minimum values was calculated,
and for removal rate constants, the minimum value was used according to the conserva-
tive approach. Reported half lives were converted to removal rate constants under the
assumption of ﬁrst-order kinetics. If no information on a particular removal process was
available, this process was assumed to play no role in the fate of the respective MP, i.e.
the removal rate constant for this process was assumed to be zero.
If no empirical Koc was available, it was estimated from the Kow via the QSAR from

The model was tested and the inﬂuence of the fate modelling on predicted concentrations
was evaluated on only a selection of three Danish rivers. This reduced the runtime of
the script and the gained information to an extent which would allow a detailed analysis
of the results and explanation of observed eﬀects.
The selection aimed to include rivers with diﬀerent characteristics:
• Big river: Guden˚a is with 149km the longest Danish river (Ovesen et al. 2000)
and has a relatively high discharge with an average of 9.71m3/s in 2019. Only
some stretches are aﬀected by wet-weather discharges with a total of 29 CSOs and
114 SWOs discharging a cumulative annual volume of 0.22 · 106m3 and 2.0 · 106m3
in 2020.
• Highly regulated urban stream: Harrestrup ˚
A is only 20km long and repre-
sents a highly aﬀected urban recipient ﬂowing through the western greater Copen-
hagen area. It is highly regulated and receives important wet-weather discharges,
with stormwater making up 0-6 times the base ﬂow in an annual variation (Eriks-
son et al. 2007). Its average discharge was 0.26m3/s in 2019. 33 CSOs and 42
SWOs are along its course, discharging cumulative annual volumes of 1.3 · 106m3
and 2.7 · 106m3 in 2020.
• Peri-urban stream: Mølle ˚
A was chosen to allow for a comparison with the
results of Scarpellini (2021), who tested the model on this river.
It is a small

(Blichfeldt and Skodborg 2018), allowing to analyze the impact of those on the
results. 40 CSOs and 54 SWOs discharged cumulative annual volumes of 0.54 ·

Diﬀerent model runs were performed in order to assess the inﬂuence of considering
fate processes on the resulting concentrations in the RWB: In addition to a run of the
fate model for the selected rivers, a second run was performed where the fate module
was shut oﬀ, i.e. the overall removal rate constant for all MP was set to zero and hence
only dilution was considered. In order to evaluate whether including fate modelling in
the simple screening model is justiﬁed considering the uncertainty of the results, the
impact of parameter uncertainty on the model results was examined at the example of
the parameter river velocity. Hence, the model was run again applying an uncertainty
factor to the river velocity in both directions, i.e. one model run was performed dividing
the velocity estimates by the uncertainty factor, and another run multiplying them with
the uncertainty factor.
The modelled concentrations were analyzed for each MP in two ways: The risk quotient
(RQ), i.e. the quotient of the modelled concentration and the EQS, was calculated, and
the percentage of river points exceeding the EQS, i.e. where RQ > 1, was determined.
In the case of the heavy metals where the decisive EQS is expressed in terms of dissolved
concentrations, RQs and exceedances were calculated both for total concentrations using
the EQS in terms of total concentration if available, and for dissolved concentrations
using the respective dissolved EQS. To this end, the dissolved fraction at each river point
was retrieved from the model and multiplied with the total concentration in order to
obtain dissolved concentrations. The acute EQS, i.e. MAC-EQS (c.f. table 4) was used
for the calculation of risk quotients and exceedances, since the modelled concentrations
are to be expected in the rivers only during or shortly after rain events.

7. Model application
Two diﬀerent applications of the developed model were demonstrated: On the one hand,
a classical risk assessment to identify river stretches critically impacted by wet-weather
discharges was carried out. This corresponds in principle to the application demonstrated
by Scarpellini (2021), but assessing not only the impact of CSOs, but of all wet-weather
discharges including SWOs, and considering the environmental fate of MP. In addition,
the applicability of the model for management purposes was shown, i.e. its use as a ﬁrst
step to identify possible solutions for critically impacted river stretches.
7.1. Risk assessment across Denmark
The risk assessment was carried out for all rivers across Denmark that receive discharges
from CSOs or SWOs. These are 1791 rivers out of a total of 2867 rivers that are modelled
by the HIP.
Given the high uncertainty of the model parameters of the fate model and the high
variability of MP concentrations in wet-weather discharges, the developed fate model
was extended by a stochastic modelling approach to propagate these uncertainties to
the model results: Monte-Carlo simulations were performed where parameters as well
as discharge concentrations were sampled from their estimated distributions. The mod-
elling results could therefore be presented as a risk of EQS exceedance, i.e. the number of
simulations where a river stretch exceeds the EQS divided by the total number of simula-
tions is interpreted as its probability to exceed the EQS. The details of the Monte-Carlo
simulations are described below.
However, due to computation time constraints, a risk assessment using Monte-Carlo
simulations was only feasible for two MP. One heavy metal and one organic MP were
chosen based on the results from the case study for this assessment. To obtain at least
an idea of the magnitude of critically impacted river stretches across Denmark for the
other MP as well, a single model run using the default parameters was also performed for
all MP and all Danish rivers as a preliminary risk assessment. In the case of the heavy
metals, the risk quotients were calculated once more from the dissolved concentrations
and the decisive EQS expressed as dissolved concentrations.
Monte-Carlo simulation set-up
Based on the study of Delli Compagni et al. (2020b) who used a Monte Carlo approach
to model the fate of pharmaceuticals in integrated urban wastewater systems, 1000
simulations were performed for each MP and random parameter combinations were
36
==PAGE:49==
obtained through random sampling from their probability distributions. The parameter
probability distributions were arbitrarily assumed to be uniform or triangular, depending
on the data availability.
If a minimum, maximum and mean value were available, a triangular distribution was
used with these values. If two values were found, the parameter was considered to be
uniformly distributed between the two values. In case only one value was found, a trian-
gular distribution was set up with the reported value as mode and the expected range of
variation from the reported value as extremes of the distribution. Most environmental
data can be assumed to follow a skewed distribution, particularly when mean values are
low, variances large, and values cannot be negative (Limpert et al. 2001). Hence, most
parameters were assumed to be lognormally distributed and therefore, distributions were
based on the logarithm of the reported values. All varied parameters and their assumed
probability distributions are summarized in Table 6. Since the KDOC and Kaw of organic
MP are calculated by the model, the expected range of these parameters was based on
the uncertainty of the estimation method according to the authors of the used empirical
equations, assuming that the physicochemical properties (Henry constant KH, vapour
pressure and Kow) are reliable. According to Burkhard (2000), the 95% conﬁdence limits
of his predictive relationship for deriving KDOC from Kow are within a factor of 20. In
the case of the Kaw, using the enthalpy of vaporization to extrapolate KH to lower tem-
peratures introduces the most uncertainty, since this will overestimate the enthalpy of
the air-water transfer for large, apolar compounds and consequently underestimate KH
at the lower temperature (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). Therefore, the KH was sampled
from a uniform distribution using the KH at 25°C as the maximum and the extrapolated
KH at 15°C as a minimum of the distribution.
For river velocity and depth, an uncertainty factor was used to account for the uncer-
tainty of the estimates from the hydraulic geometry regression. At each simulation, this
uncertainty factor was sampled from a triangular distribution with a mode of 1 and the
expected maximum uncertainty as the extremes, which were arbitrarily set to 10 or 1/10.
All estimates of river velocity were then multiplied by this uncertainty factor. Since at
a constant river discharge and width, river velocity and depth are inversely proportional
to each other, the estimates of river depth were consequently divided by the same factor.
To account for the temporal variability of MP concentrations in wet-weather dis-
charges, these were also sampled randomly for each CSO or SWO from the lognormal
probability distributions of EMC estimated by Mutzner et al. (2022). Similarly, the TSS
and DOC concentrations in CSOs and SWOs were sampled randomly from triangular
distributions with reported minimum, mean/median and maximum values (Vezzaro et al.
2018; Reemtsma et al. 2000; Tang et al. 2013). For the calculation of the equilibrium con-
centrations in the lakes however, median or mean concentrations were used to calculate
the annual MP, TSS and DOC loads.
The results of the 1000 simulations were then summarized in terms of 50th and 90th
percentile concentrations for each river point, and the probability of EQS exceedance
was calculated as the quotient of simulations exceeding the EQS divided by the total
number of simulations.

7.2. Management scenario analysis
The applicability of the model for planning purposes was investigated by using it to
compare the current impact of wet-weather discharges to Mølle ˚A to a hypothetical
management scenario.
Separate sewer systems are generally regarded as the standard solution for urban
drainage systems and therefore normally implemented in recently constructed munici-
palities (Shishegar et al. 2018). The separation of combined sewer systems in older mu-
nicipalities is often brought forward as a mitigation strategy to reduce combined sewer
overﬂows, for instance in the US (USEPA 1995a; USEPA 1995b). However, several stud-
ies have questioned the supposedly better performance of separate systems in terms of
limiting the pollution load released into the receiving waters (Brombach et al. 2005; Li
et al. 2010; Mannina and Viviani 2009). Though, their analysis was based mostly on
conventional pollutants.
Therefore, the model was used to investigate whether a separation of the sewer system
in the catchment of the river Mølle ˚A would reduce or increase the impact of wet-weather
discharges on the river for the diﬀerent micropollutants. To this end, the model was
run for a management scenario where all CSOs have been transformed to SWOs. The
annual discharge volume of these was estimated via the same methodology that is used
by municipalities to estimate discharge volumes for the PULS database (Level 1 model-
based estimation of wet-weather discharges, according to Vezzaro et al. 2019), using the
eﬀective annual rainfall and the impervious area of the catchment (c.f. equation 15).
The annual precipitation and impervious area of the catchment of each former CSO
were obtained from the PULS database, and an initial loss of 0.6mm and a hydrological
reduction factor of 0.8 were assumed.

8.2. Analysis of removal rate constants and determination of
sensitive parameter uncertainties
In order to understand the diﬀerences in the concentration proﬁles and the behaviour
of the diﬀerent MP, the removal rate constants determining their fate and half lives in
the river are analyzed in this section, identifying the most important processes for the
diﬀerent MP. Also, the sensitivity of the removal rate constants to the uncertainty of
the diﬀerent input parameters is examined, addressing research question no. 2.
8.2.1. Removal rate constants
Table 7 shows the removal rate constants for the selected MP for each removal process
as well as the resulting overall removal rate constant and half life, calculated for a de-
fault river depth of 0.5 m, the TSS and DOC background concentrations of 15 and 5
mg/L, respectively, and the adopted sedimentation rate in rivers calculated from the
sediment growth rate of 0.5 mm/a. It is important to note that all constants are re-
lated to the total concentration, they describe the overall removal taking into account
the partitioning, i.e. the rate constants for biodegradation, photolysis, hydrolysis and
volatilization have already been multiplied by the dissolved fraction of the MP. This also
means that all constants depend on the TSS and DOC concentration: The higher the
TSS concentration, the higher ksed, but the lower kbio, kphoto, khyd and kvol. The higher
the DOC concentration, the lower the overall removal, since a higher fraction of MP is
bound to DOC and hence inert to removal according to the model. Here, the removal
rate constants were calculated for the TSS and DOC background concentration, but it
has to be kept in mind that local concentrations can be signiﬁcantly higher due to the
wet-weather discharge contributions. Nevertheless, Table 7 can give a ﬁrst impression
of which removal processes are the most important for each MP, even though they vary
45
==PAGE:58==
depending on the local conditions. It can also indicate for which MP half lives are short
enough so that a signiﬁcant removal within the river can be expected.
It can be concluded from the results that hydrolysis does not play a major role in the
fate of the selected MP. Biodegradation is an important fate process for only few MP,
namely Bisphenol A, Diuron and Ibuprofen. Volatile compounds for which volatiliza-
tion is an important fate process are Mercury, 4-tert-Octylphenol, para-Nonylphenol,
Benz[a]anthracene, Fluoranthene and Chlorpyrifos. Photolysis plays a major role for
some PAH, as well as for the pesticides Carbendazim, Cypermethrin, Imidacloprid and
Pentachlorophenol, and the PPCP Diclofenac, Estradiol and Estrone. For all heavy met-
als except Mercury, most PAH, and DEHP and PFOS, removal through sedimentation
is expected to be the most important fate process.
The range of the overall removal rate constants and the respective half lives is
immense. While for some MP such as Anthracene and Diclofenac, the half life is in
the range of hours and consequently, signiﬁcant removal along a river stretch can be
expected, other MP such as Uranium, DEHP or PFOS show expected half lives in the
order of years and therefore, removal within the river will be negligible. Even for the
longest river in Denmark, a rough calculation assuming an average velocity of 0.4 m/s
over its length of 149 km results in a travel time from the source to the mouth, i.e. a
maximum residence time of about 100 h. This means that all MP with longer half lives
cannot be expected to be signiﬁcantly removed in rivers, but only in lakes with higher
residence times.

8.2.2. Sensitivity of the removal rate constants to parameter uncertainty
Figure 11 illustrates the sensitivity of the overall removal rate constant k to the diﬀerent
environmental and MP-speciﬁc parameters and their uncertainty or variability. Since
diﬀerent removal processes dominate the overall removal rate constant of the diﬀerent MP
and the value ranges of the MP-speciﬁc parameters vary (c.f. annex B.4), the deviation of
k with the parameters depends highly on the MP. Overall, the least sensitive parameters
of the model are the hydrolysis rate constant and the decadic beam attenuation coeﬃcient
α, for which k varies maximum by a factor of 2, followed by wind speed and DOC
concentration. The DOC partitioning constant, the air-water partitioning constant, the
biodegradation rate and wind speed are sensitive parameters only for few MP. For the
overall removal of the heavy metals and PAH, sedimentation velocity, followed by river
depth is the most sensitive parameter, although for some, TSS concentration and Kd are
even more important. For many PAH, the uncertainty of the photolysis rate also has
an important inﬂuence on their overall removal. Since most pesticides and PPCP are
mainly removed by photolysis, the uncertainty of the photolysis rate, followed by water
depth, DOC concentration and the decadic beam attenuation coeﬃcient α play the most
important role. In annex D, the relative deviation of k with the parameter uncertainties
is shown for the diﬀerent MP per substance group to allow an easier identiﬁcation of the
most important parameters for each MP.
For most MP, k varies up to a factor of 5 if the uncertainty or variability of parameters
is considered, and for some MP even more, up to factor 16, particularly for the parame-
ters TSS concentration, suspended solid-water partitioning constant Kd and photolysis
rates. This has to be kept in mind when interpreting the results of the model if de-
fault parameters are being used to model concentrations, since the high uncertainty in
the overall removal rate constants will directly translate into high uncertainties of the
predicted concentrations and risk quotients.
Additionally, it should be kept in mind that this is only a ﬁrst assessment of the
sensitivity of k to parameter uncertainty, since parameters were only varied one-at-a-
time due to limitations in computing capacity and time. However, the sensitivity to a
parameter will often be not linear and depend on other parameters. For instance, the
sensitivity of k to partitioning constants will increase if TSS and DOC concentrations
are higher. Therefore, it is recommended to eventually conduct a Global Sensitivity
Analysis varying all parameters (e.g. see approach used by Vezzaro et al. 2011).

8.3. Results of the case study
8.3.1. Results of the model test run
In this section, the results from the model run on the three selected rivers of the case
study are analyzed. First, the results are analyzed on the example of three micropol-
lutants with diﬀerent characteristics in terms of sources and environmental fate: Di-
49
==PAGE:62==
clofenac, Pentachlorophenol, and Copper. Diclofenac is almost exclusively discharged
via CSOs, while Pentachlorophenol is only present in the eﬄuent of SWOs. Both have
relatively short half lives of few hours. Copper enters the water body via both CSOs and
SWOs, although with a higher concentration in SWOs, and it has a very low removal
rate constant with a half live in the order of months. The concentration proﬁles of these
MP along the rivers have been plotted and are interpreted in the following, taking into
account the characteristics of the three diﬀerent rivers of the case study.
Peri-urban stream: Mølle ˚A
Figure 12 shows the results for Mølle ˚A. The upper two
diagrams illustrate the boundary conditions that are the input to the model: the annual
discharge volumes of CSOs and SWOs, their contribution to the total river discharge
and the presence of lakes are plotted over the length of the river in order to illustrate the
load and the expected dilution of wet-weather discharges. Below, the concentration pro-
ﬁles of TSS and DOC as well as MP concentrations are shown. MP concentrations are
expressed in terms of risk quotients (RQ), i.e. modelled concentrations divided by the
respective MAC-EQS. Both 50th and 90th percentile concentrations in the wet-weather
discharges have been modelled.
Under the assumption of an accumulation of all CSO and SWO discharges, their contri-
bution to the river discharge is immense for Mølle ˚A, with the total discharge during the
rain event reaching more than 5 times the average monthly discharge in July at some
river points. However, the impact is mostly limited to the river stretch downstream of
kilometre 4 due to the presence of the lakes: wet-weather discharges upstream a lake
were modelled to not contribute to the river discharge downstream a lake and therefore,
no signiﬁcant accumulation of these additional discharges is observed. This seems realis-
tic, since lakes are not expected to be signiﬁcantly impacted by short-term wet-weather
discharges due to their big volume and long residence times. The important equalizing
eﬀect of the lakes is also evident for the concentration proﬁles of both TSS/DOC and the
MP which peak due to the CSO and SWO input, but decrease signiﬁcantly in the lakes.
This becomes particularly clear between the ﬁrst two lakes, Farum Sø and Furesø, where
all concentration proﬁles exhibit a sharp peak which is mitigated due to the dilution and
removal potential of lake Furesø.
The diﬀerent dynamics of MP with diﬀerent half lives and from diﬀerent sources
(CSO/SWO borne) can be observed by comparing the concentration proﬁles of the three
diﬀerent MP. Since the ﬁrst 5 kilometres of Mølle ˚A receive only SWO discharges, no
Diclofenac is present in this river stretch. Pentachlorophenol on the other hand shows a
peak at the discharge point followed by a decrease in concentration. This decrease can
be mostly attributed to dilution, since no great diﬀerence can be observed between the
curve of Pentachlorophenol and Copper, although Copper has a signiﬁcantly higher half
life. The SWO discharges also dominate between Farum Sø and Furesø, since the peak
of the SWO borne MP is more pronounced than of Diclofenac. However downstream of
Lyngby Sø, the last of the three lakes, where the input of CSOs dominate, Diclofenac
shows a much steeper increase in concentration than Pentachlorophenol. The decreases
in concentration that can be observed between kilometre 4 and the river mouth can
50
==PAGE:63==
again be attributed mainly to the dilution, since they inversely follow the curve of total
discharge. However, on the very last 5 kilometres, a slight decrease in the concentra-
tion of Diclofenac can be observed even though the total discharge remains relatively
constant, and in the case of Copper, this decline is much less pronounced. Hence, this
decrease can be attributed to the environmental fate modelling.
Highly regulated urban stream: Harrestrup ˚A
Figure 13 shows the results for Har-
restrup. In the case of Harrestrup, there are no lakes along its course that mitigate the
impact of the wet-weather discharges. Their contribution to the total discharge is even
higher than for Mølle ˚A, the resulting total discharge during the modelled rain event
is 30 times the average monthly ﬂow in July, which justiﬁes its designation as an open
sewer channel. Harrestrup exhibits a very unequal distribution of CSOs and SWOs along
the river. Roughly the ﬁrst 10 km of Harrestrup only receive SWO discharges, while
between kilometre 10 and 15, CSO discharges predominate. This is clearly reﬂected in
the concentration proﬁles: Pentachlorophenol and Copper show very high concentrations
due to the SWO discharges in the ﬁrst half of the river, but are then diluted by the CSO
discharges, while Diclofenac shows a reverse proﬁle, peaking in the second half. Since
TSS and DOC concentrations are much higher in CSOs than in SWOs, they show a
similar picture as Diclofenac. Similarly as in Mølle ˚A, no signiﬁcant decrease due to
environmental fate processes can be observed, the predominant dissipation process is
dilution.
Big river:
Guden˚a
In the case of Guden˚a, the many lakes from kilometre 40 on
downstream limit the impact of wet-weather discharges both in terms of hydraulic
load, i.e. contribution to total discharge, and MP concentrations (c.f. ﬁgure 14). In
the ﬁrst 40 kilometres however, the inﬂuence of the environmental fate modelling is
apparent for the ﬁrst time: Comparing the concentration proﬁles of Diclofenac and
Pentachlorophenol with Copper or even the TSS and DOC concentrations, the former
two show a notable exponential decrease in concentration while the latter only decrease
slightly. On the one hand, this is probably due to the longer river stretch and therefore
longer residence/reaction time compared to Mølle ˚A and Harrestrup.
On the other
hand, DOC concentrations are signiﬁcantly lower, hence reducing the attenuation of
light within the water body which increases the photolysis rates of both Diclofenac and
Pentachlorophenol.
In summary, the model results of the test run show that the model behaves as ex-
pected, since the obtained concentration proﬁles appear reasonable and trends can be
explained by the input parameters. The RQs show that in all three rivers of the case
study, both Copper and Diclofenac exceed the respective MAC-EQS in large parts, only
Pentachlorophenol remains below a RQ of 1.

centration, while the dissipation due to environmental fate processes seems to be only

The inﬂuence of partitioning
Until now, only total concentrations have been consid-
ered. However, it has to be noted that not necessarily only the dissipation of the total
concentration is inﬂuencing the impact of the MP on the RWB, but also the partitioning
itself can aﬀect the bioavailability of the MP and therefore its ecotoxic impact. For heavy
metals, this is reﬂected by the fact that the decisive EQS are expressed in terms of dis-
solved concentration. Hence, the RQ of the dissolved concentration over the respective
EQS is the more important parameter for risk assessment in this case. This parameter
could not be determined in the simple dilution model, since partitioning and therefore
dissolved concentrations were not modelled, therefore if available, EQS expressed as to-
tal concentrations were used to calculate RQs. The advantage of the developed fate
model is that dissolved fractions at each modelled river point can be easily retrieved as
an additional model output, which allows to calculate the RQ in terms of dissolved con-
centrations. Figures 17 and 18 show the same diagrams as before, this time only for the
heavy metals and in the case where fate modelling is considered, the RQs are calculated
from the dissolved concentrations and the EQS expressed as dissolved concentration.
Interestingly, looking at dissolved concentrations does not necessarily reduce the RQ
signiﬁcantly, for many heavy metals the diﬀerences to the model results were fate mod-
elling was not considered are not signiﬁcantly higher than it was when the RQ was
calculated from total concentrations. For Cadmium, Lead, Nickel and Zinc there are
even some river points where the diﬀerence is negative (c.f. Figure E.17 in Annex E.2
plotting the relative diﬀerence in RQs from the two model runs for a better comparison),
i.e. the RQ is higher if partitioning and fate modelling is considered than if concentra-
tions are predicted from the simple dilution model. This can be explained by the fact
that in some cases the dissolved fraction is higher than the quotient of the EQS expressed
as dissolved and total concentration. For instance, the dissolved fraction of Zinc at the
minimum TSS and DOC concentration (background concentration of 15 and 5 mg/L,
respectively) is 0.32, while the quotient of dissolved and total EQS is 5µg/L
20µg/L = 0.25. This
shows that the decisive EQS expressed as a dissolved concentration is not necessarily
respected if the EQS expressed as a total concentration is met, which means that the
use of the latter for risk assessment is less protective. However, for those metals where
no EQS expressed as a total concentration was available, considering partitioning makes
a great diﬀerence in their risk assessment. For instance, for Barium, Cobalt, Selenium
and Tin the median RQ is reduced by 40% and more, if fate modelling and partitioning
is considered and hence the RQ can be calculated from dissolved concentrations instead
of total concentrations.
In terms of EQS exceedance, using dissolved concentrations instead of total con-
centrations for the risk assessment makes the greatest diﬀerence for Chromium, Lead
and Nickel (c.f. Figure 18), which only showed a minor diﬀerence in the fraction of
river points exceeding EQS when fate modelling was considered and EQS exceedance
was assessed in terms of total concentrations. For Lead, the diﬀerence in the fraction
of river points exceeding the EQS is even higher than 20%.
This shows that even
though for Lead the RQ is sometimes underestimated if expressed in terms of total
concentrations, using the RQ in terms of dissolved concentrations overall reduces the
58
==PAGE:71==
percentage of river points exceeding EQS. This can be explained by the fact that
high concentrations of MP often come along with higher concentrations of TSS and
DOC, since TSS and DOC are also present in elevated concentrations in wet-weather
discharges. Therefore, at those river points exhibiting particularly high RQ in terms of
total concentration, these will be reduced if they are calculated from dissolved concentra-
tions, since the dissolved fraction decreases with increasing TSS and DOC concentration.

In summary, the results from the case study show that the fate modelling has a
non-negligible inﬂuence on the predicted EQS exceedance for some MP, reducing the
fraction of river points exceeding EQS by up to 20%. However, the results must be
treated with care due to the high uncertainty of the model parameters, notably of river
velocity and the sensitive parameters for the removal rate constants (c.f. sections 8.1
and 8.2). In order to evaluate whether including fate modelling in the simple screening
model is justiﬁed considering the uncertainty of the results, hereafter the sensitivity of
the results in terms of EQS exceedance due to the parameter uncertainty is examined
at the example of the parameter river velocity.

The inﬂuence of lakes
The independence from river velocity of all other MP’s EQS
exceedances is surprising.
An obvious explanation would be that the reduction in
EQS exceedance due to the fate modelling is only caused by reduced concentrations
in the lakes, since the MP removal in lakes is independent of river velocity. To verify
this hypothesis, the fraction of river points exceeding EQS has also been calculated
excluding those riverpoints that lay in a lake, for the model run without fate modelling
and the default with fate modelling for comparison. The results are shown in Figure
20. Indeed, for many MP the diﬀerence in fraction of river points exceeding EQS if fate
modelling is considered disappears if lake points are excluded. This is the case for most
PAH, 4-tert-Octylphenol, DEHP, Carbendazim and Estrone. And for most other MP,
the diﬀerence becomes much smaller. An exception are only some of the heavy metals,
notably Chromium, Lead and Nickel, where the diﬀerence between the results with vs.
without fate modelling even increases if lake points are excluded. Here, another factor
61
==PAGE:74==
must be responsible for the independence of the reduction of EQS exceedances from
river velocity: the partitioning. If the fate module is shut oﬀ, EQS exceedances can only
be calculated from total concentrations. Therefore, if the dissolved fraction is lower than
the quotient of the EQS expressed as dissolved concentration over the EQS expressed
as total concentration, the risk quotient will be reduced, even if no overall removal of
the respective MP happens. This seems to be the cause for the important reduction
of EQS exceedance of Chromium, Lead and Nickel in the rivers due to the fate modelling.

These results conﬁrm what has already been observed from the concentration proﬁles:
For most MP, the removal through environmental fate processes is only signiﬁcant in
lakes with high residence times. However, the calculated concentrations in lakes are not
acute concentrations during the modelled rain event, but a rough estimate of average
62
==PAGE:75==
equilibrium concentrations resulting from the annual MP load conveyed by wet-weather
discharges. They were calculated via a very simplistic approach, primarily in order to
have an estimate for the initial MP concentrations in rivers downstream a lake, but the
lakes themselves are actually not the target of the screening model, whose focus is to
model acute concentrations in rivers that can be expected during rain events. If the
impact of wet-weather discharges on lakes speciﬁcally was to be assessed in terms of
chronic concentrations, the MP loads from other sources, especially continuous ones like
WWTPs could not be neglected, but would need to be added to the annual MP loads
from wet-weather discharges. Consequently, in terms of risk assessment, the resulting
concentrations should also be assessed via the chronic AA-EQS instead of the MAC-
EQS.
However, with the focus the model currently has, the results question whether including
fate modelling in the simple screening model and therefore increasing its complexity is
justiﬁed. If looking only at rivers, at least the fraction of river points exceeding EQS is
about the same for most pollutants regardless of whether environmental fate processes
were considered or not. This ﬁnding together with the aforementioned high uncertainty
of the fate model parameters and consequently the results reveals that the developed
fate model does not generally add much value compared to the simple screening tool
developed by Scarpellini (2021). For most MP, the complexity of the simple screening
tool seems to be suﬃcient to allow for a robust risk assessment. It is only for those MP
where the partitioning plays a role for their risk assessment, i.e. the heavy metals, that
the fate modelling provides an added value, since it allows the calculation of dissolved
concentrations and hence a risk assessment using the decisive EQS expressed as dissolved
concentrations.

an annual discharge volume of only 3085m3/a, but with an impervious area of 138.7ha,
which leads to a high discharge if this CSO is converted to a SWO. On the other hand,
there is a big CSO with an annual discharge volume of 106417m3/a about 7km down-
stream lake Furesø with an impervious area of 124.3ha. In the latter case, the DEHP
load conveyed by the CSO is higher than if it was converted into a SWO, therefore, from
that river point on, DEHP concentrations are higher in the baseline scenario.
A boxplot (c.f. Figure 31) of the risk quotients using 50th percentile concentrations was
created to compare the concentrations of the two scenarios for all micropollutants and to
assess overall which scenario performs better in terms of water quality control. It shows
that the risk quotients are signiﬁcantly reduced for Tin, Cypermethrin, Diﬂufenican,
Diclofenac, Estradiol, Estrone and Ibuprofen, and EQS exceedances could be avoided
by sewer separation for Cypermethrin, Diclofenac and Estrone. However, for the other
compounds, risk quotients are higher in the management scenario and EQS exceedances
would be more frequent for the heavy metals, PAH, 4-tert-Octylphenol, Carbendazim
and Diuron. It can therefore be concluded that under the assumptions of the model
in terms of rain characteristics and discharge concentrations, and for the investigated
pollutants, a simple transformation of the combined sewer system in the catchment of
Mølle ˚A into a separate system would not be an eﬃcient management strategy, since
in addition to high costs it would lead to more EQS exceedances of many MP in the
receiving water body.
A sewer separation can only be regarded as reasonable if the
run-oﬀcollected in the separate sewers is treated before being discharged into the water
body.

9. Outlook
9.1. Future improvements of the model
In the course of this thesis, several constraints of the model were identiﬁed that could
be alleviated through further improvements of the model which are discussed in this
section.
Consideration of tributaries
One constraint that has been identiﬁed is the fact that
in the model, each river with its own ID or name deﬁned by the HIP model has been
modelled independently, i.e. conﬂuences of rivers have not been considered. This means
that MP loads being conveyed from tributaries are not accounted for, and MP concentra-
tions in rivers with tributaries are consequently underestimated. Hence, the assumption
of an accumulation of all wet-weather discharges during a rain event is actually only
implemented partially in the model, since it is restricted to single rivers instead of catch-
ments. In a further development of the model, the routing of the rivers should therefore
be taken into account, for instance the distance of the last river point to the sea could
be checked for each river to identify tributaries which could then be assigned to the next
closest river point via QGIS. However, this would require to deﬁne diﬀerent levels of
rivers, e.g. level 1 discharging into level 2 discharging into level 3 which would need to
be modelled one after the other to calculate the MP input load for the rivers in the next
level, leading to an immense increase of complexity of the model structure. Apart from
that, it is questionable whether this would be really necessary, since the assumption of an
accumulation of all wet-weather discharges within a whole catchment is very unrealistic,
and therefore the assumption of an accumulation only along individual rivers might ac-
tually be a good compromise in terms of a conservative but realistic assessment. Wicke
et al. (2015) argue that a reasonable aggregation depends on the research question, since
depending on the problem, total loads at the catchment level or acute concentrations on
site may play a role. Hence, as long as the developed model is used for assessing acute
concentrations on site, and is not extended to assess the chronic stress that wet-weather
discharges put on water bodies by calculating average concentrations from annual pol-
lutant loads, the current implementation of the accumulation worst-case scenario can
probably be supported reasonably. However, this remains to be further investigated,
taking into account for instance the size of the tributaries in question and the typical
geographical extent of rain events.
Consideration of other MP sources
Another constraint to keep in mind when inter-
preting the results of the model in terms of a risk assessment is the fact that for the
76
==PAGE:89==
prediction of MP concentrations and EQS exceedances in the rivers, only MP loads from
wet-weather discharges have been considered, neglecting any other sources. For instance,
WWTP eﬄuents might contribute signiﬁcantly to the total MP load, especially during
low-ﬂow conditions in summer. For instance, in the case of Usserød ˚A, WWTP eﬄuent
represents up to 67% of the dry-weather ﬂow in July (Vezzaro et al. 2018). The respec-
tive MP load leads to a background concentration of MPs where the contributions of
wet-weather discharges add up during a rain event. Especially for waste-water born MPs
such as pharmaceuticals and personal care products that are not eﬃciently removed in
WWTPs, these loads are probably relevant for risk assessment. The same holds true for
other MP sources, such as rural run-oﬀ, which might contribute to the MP load through
e.g. leaching of pesticides. At least the input from WWTPs could be implemented easily
to improve the model, since their discharge volumes are reported in the PULS database
as well.
Assessment of model assumptions
It has to be kept in mind that many assumptions of
the model have been made more or less arbitrarily, e.g. the selection of the return period
of the archetype event deﬁning the CSO and SWO discharge ﬂows, or the month of the
selected river ﬂow data. These have not been varied even in the Monte Carlo simulations,
but were regarded as ﬁxed boundary conditions deﬁning the conservativeness of the
screening. However, these parameters have an important inﬂuence on the results and
these should be interpreted accordingly.
Depending on the research question or the
application, diﬀerent assumptions could be reasonable.
It is therefore recommended
that for future applications of the model, the inﬂuence of these assumptions on the
results are assessed in detail.
Consideration of intra-event variability
Even if stochastic modelling, i.e.
Monte
Carlo simulations are performed to account for the variability in wet-weather discharges
through the sampling from concentration distributions, it must be stressed that as long
as EMC are being used, no intra-event variability is considered. For instance, ﬁrst-ﬂush
eﬀects might lead to much higher short-term concentrations in wet-weather discharges.
Hence, for MP where ﬁrst-ﬂush eﬀects are known to occur, this should be considered in
the future.
Toxicity of transformation products and chemical mixtures
Finally, well known ﬂaws
in chemical risk assessment also apply to the application of the model in this thesis:
Firstly, considering the dissipation of organic chemicals due to transformation processes
in risk assessment implicitly assumes that the transformation products are less con-
cerning, i.e.
less toxic, than the parent compounds.
Although this may be true in
the majority of cases, there are also substances where transformation increases toxicity
to aquatic organisms (Boxall et al. 2004). For instance, in the case of anthracene and
diclofenac their photodegradation products have been found to be more toxic to test
bacteria and algae (Brack 2003; Schulze et al. 2010). However, it could be shown in this
work that transformation processes play a rather negligible role for the fate of MP from
77
==PAGE:90==
wet weather discharges. In this respect, this constraint seems not to be so relevant in
this case.
A second general challenge in risk assessment is the assessment of the toxicity of chemical
mixtures. In the model application in this thesis, risk quotients have been calculated
and assessed individually for the diﬀerent MP. However, the toxic stress of individual
chemicals can add up if aquatic organisms are exposed to mixtures, particularly if they
exhibit a similar mode of action, which often is the case for substances belonging to the
same group, i.e. PAH. A common approach to deal with this eﬀect is the toxic unit or
concentration addition approach, where the risk quotients of substances with a similar
mode of action are summed up to estimate the toxicity of the mixture (European Com-
mission - Directorate General for the Environment 2011; Backhaus and Faust 2012).
This approach could be easily implemented to improve the risk assessment of the model.
9.2. Possible further applications of the model
In addition to the possible improvements of the model, further aspects are discussed
below that could be investigated in the future building on this thesis.
Assessment of the impact of wet-weather discharges on the MP load of the sediment
in Danish streams and lakes
In the model developed in this thesis, only the impact
of wet-weather discharges on acute aquatic MP concentrations is assessed. However,
most investigated MP have a high suspended solid-water partitioning coeﬃcient, i.e. are
expected to be mostly particle bound. If these particles settle in rivers and lakes after
rain events, it can be expected that they contribute signiﬁcantly to pollutant loads in the
sediments (Vezzaro et al. 2018). Since the developed model models the partitioning of
the MP, it can theoretically also be used to estimate sediment concentrations originating
from wet-weather discharge loads and compare them to the quality criteria for sediments
deﬁned in the Danish legislation (BEK 439 19/05/2016 - Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet,
2016). To this end, the total particle bound MP loads being discharged from CSOs and
SWOs over a year could be calculated and by combining them with annual sediment
growth, estimates of MP sediment concentrations could be derived.
Application to Europe
The model has been developed on the basis of spatial data on
river ﬂows and wet-weather discharge volumes that are publicly available in Denmark.
The transferability of the model to other countries with diﬀerent data availability could
be investigated in future work. For instance, the work of Quaranta et al. (2022) could
serve as a basis for an application of the screening model to whole Europe. They de-
veloped a hydrological model to estimate pollution from CSOs at the regional scale and
applied it to urban areas in the European Union representing almost half of the EU28
population. The model only requires input data on population and rainfall, the imper-
vious area of catchments, and on 6 aggregated CSO descriptors. If the model is not
calibrated and default parameters for the combined sewer characteristics such as storage
and maximum conveyance are used, the accuracy of the predictions is still expected to
78
==PAGE:91==
be within an order of magnitude. The output of the model is a time series of overﬂow
volumes and the ratio of dry weather ﬂow volume and runoﬀwith the same resolution
as the input rainfall series. However, the model has only estimated total pollution loads
based on the runoﬀand untreated dry weather ﬂow volumes emitted by CSOs so far,
i.e. the dilution and status of the receiving water bodies are not taken into account
and hence, acute MP concentrations in the water bodies resulting from the overﬂows
have not yet been predicted. Hence, the model developed in this thesis could serve as a
blueprint to extend the model developed by Quaranta et al. (2022) to model acute MP
concentrations resulting from CSOs across Europe. The necessary river ﬂow data could
be obtained for instance from the dataset FLO1K created by Barbarossa et al. (2018)
which provides annual predictions of annual mean and highest and lowest monthly mean
ﬂow at a resolution of about 1 km.

10. Conclusion
Following up on the objectives of this thesis comprising the development, testing and
application of a simple chemical-fate model to assess the impacts of micropollutants from
wet-weather discharges on Danish streams, the following conclusions were derived:
• Development of a spatial chemical fate model based on open data: The
fate modelling approach which has so far been used only for assessing the im-
pact of constant emission sources such as WWTP could be applied for variable
wet-weather discharges in the chemical-fate model due to the assumption of an
accumulation of all CSOs/SWOs along a river, which was made for the conser-
vative assessment. However, it turned out to be a challenge to obtain reasonable
estimates for river depth and velocity, the two most important spatial parameters
of the river fate model, from open data. Due to the lack of a better alternative, a
hydraulic geometry regression estimating the parameters from river discharge was
used, even though a comparison to measured data revealed unsatisfactory accuracy
and hence high errors must be assumed.
• Determination of the most important model parameters and assessment
of the inﬂuence of fate modelling on the results: An analysis of the calcu-
lated overall removal rate constants of the diﬀerent MP and the test run of the
model for three Danish rivers with diﬀerent characteristics revealed that for most
MP the environmental removal processes are too slow to play a role in their fate
considering the short residence times in Danish rivers and that dilution is the pre-
dominant dissipation process. Hence, signiﬁcant removal can be expected only in
lakes with high residence times. Since MP concentrations in lakes are expected
not to be impacted by short-term emissions such as wet-weather discharges and
are therefore not the target of the screening model, whose focus is to model acute
concentrations in rivers resulting from wet-weather discharges, it is questionable
whether including fate modelling in the screening model is justiﬁed, considering
additionally the high uncertainty of the model parameters. Instead, for most MP,
the complexity of the simple dilution model seems to be suﬃcient to allow for a
robust risk assessment.
However, it has to be noted that not necessarily only the dissipation of the total
concentration is inﬂuencing the impact of the MP on the RWB, but also the par-
titioning itself can aﬀect the bioavailability of the MP and therefore its ecotoxic
impact. For heavy metals, this is reﬂected by the fact that the decisive EQS are
expressed in terms of dissolved concentration. This parameter could not be de-
termined in the simple dilution model, since partitioning and therefore dissolved
80
==PAGE:93==
concentrations were not modelled, therefore if available, EQS expressed as total
concentrations were used to calculate risk quotients. However, the results of the
model test runs show that for some heavy metals, using dissolved concentrations
for their risk assessment does make a diﬀerence and hence, the fate modelling pro-
vides an added value in these cases. Since for this application only the partitioning
plays the decisive role and not the sedimentation, the fate model could be simpli-
ﬁed accordingly. Also, if only partitioning is modelled as a fate process, there is
no more need for extimates of river depth and velocity.
• Application for ecological risk assessment across Denmark: A stochastic
modelling approach with Monte Carlo simulations taking into account the vari-
ability of wet-weather discharges and uncertainty of parameters was performed
for two MP, Lead and Benzo[b]ﬂuoranthene. The results show that the median
probability of exceeding the MAC-EQS during or after rain events of the river
points of all Danish rivers receiving wet-weather discharges is higher than 75%
for Benzo[b]ﬂuoranthene. In the case of Lead, the median probability of EQS ex-
ceedance is less than 5%, but there are also river points which exceed the MAC-EQS
for Lead in all simulations. For all other MP, a single model run was performed
as a ﬁrst risk assessment, showing high risk quotients and hence EQS exceedances
for most PAH and heavy metals. These ﬁndings indicate serious impacts of wet-
weather discharges on Danish water bodies in terms of acute toxic stress and should
therefore be further investigated through more detailed models or monitoring cam-
paigns at the aﬀected river stretches. Ultimately, solutions need to be implemented
to mitigate the MP emissions from wet-weather discharges, particularly since CSO
discharge volumes are expected to increase due to climate change leading to a rise
of frequency and intensity of storm events (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al. 2013).
• Application for management scenario analysis: In the application of the
model for planning purposes, sewer separation was investigated as a potential
mitigation approach to reduce CSOs at the example of a hypothetical case study
of the river Mølle ˚A. The baseline scenario, i.e. the default run of the model was
compared to a scenario where all CSOs were transformed into SWOs. It was found
that although sewer separation lead to a reduction of concentrations and hence
of EQS exceedance for mostly wastewater originating MP such as Diclofenac, for
the majority of compounds the risk quotients increased and EQS exceedances were
more frequent for most heavy metals, PAH, 4-tert-Octylphenol, Carbendazim and
Diuron. It was therefore concluded that a simple transformation of the combined
sewer system in the catchment of Mølle ˚A into a separate system would not be an
eﬃcient management strategy unless the run-oﬀcollected in the separate sewers is
treated before being discharged into the water body. These ﬁndings are in line with
previous studies that question the utility of sewer separation in terms of pollution
reduction. Instead, nature-based solutions and compact treatment systems to treat
CSOs have been identiﬁed as the most promising approaches for the mitigation of
CSO emissions (Botturi et al. 2021).

A. Modelling of environmental fate processes
A.1. Relevant processes
Transformation processes as well as transfer processes to other media may lead to a
decrease in concentration of micropollutants within a water body (Schwarzenbach et al.
2003).
The relevant transfer processes to other media are water-sediment exchange or
volatilization to the atmosphere. In single-media models other compartments such as
the atmosphere or sediment are not modelled. Therefore, the transfer of micropollutants
to other media is described as a removal process with a removal rate proportional to the
net ﬂux of the compound to the other media which is estimated from the local envi-
ronmental parameters and the compounds’ physico-chemical properties, for instance its
partitioning behaviour (see sections A.2, A.3 and A.4).
The partitioning of a compound is an important concept in environmental fate model-
ing, describing the compound’s equilibrium concentration between diﬀerent phases, such
as water-air or water-suspended solids. The ratios of these equilibrium concentrations,
expressed through partitioning constants, determine how strong a compound’s tendency
is to volatilize and to sorb to suspended particles or sediments. The partitioning there-
fore aﬀects the removal of compounds both by transfer and transformation processes,
as some processes only aﬀect the dissolved fraction of the compound, while others only
aﬀect the sorbed fraction (see section A.2).
For organic compounds, the most important transformation processes include bio-
transformation, hydrolysis and photodegradation (Bavumiragira et al. 2022). These are
mostly modeled as ﬁrst-order decay processes with experimentally determined rate con-
stants (see sections A.5, A.6 and A.7).
Unlike organic contaminants, metals cannot be degraded, therefore they can only be
removed from the water phase through transfer processes. However, their speciation
governed by chemical transformations such as redox-reactions or methylation, as well
as complexation inﬂuences their solubility and therefore precipitation processes, and
their tendency to sorb. This speciation is mainly controlled by pH and redox-conditions
(Bourg and Loch 1995; Briﬀa et al. 2020). Since these processes are very complex and
depend on the local environmental conditions, fate models usually do not simulate them,
but neglect precipitation and use single partitioning constants determined under typi-
cal environmental conditions for modeling of sorption and consequent removal through
settling of particles (H¨uﬀmeyer et al. 2009; Vezzaro et al. 2010; J. D. Allison and T. L.
Allison 2005).

been used in existing river fate models to implement them are described in detail, as
well as the chosen approach for the model which is the subject of this thesis.

A.3. Water-sediment transfer processes
As described by Schwarzenbach et al. (2003, Chapter 23.2), the transfer of a compound
between water and sediment is governed by three processes:
1. Settling of particles
2. Adsorption and desorption processes to the sediment i.e. diﬀusive exchange with
the sediment layer
3. Resuspension and resettling of particles
The settling of particles removes the sorbed fraction of the compound from the water
column to the sediment.
The latter two processes result from the fact that sediment and water are normally
not in equilibrium. This is due to diagenetic processes that change the physicochem-
ical properties of the sediment, which leads to a diﬀerent partitioning between water
and sediment than between water and freshly sedimented particles. Moreover, if the
aqueous concentration has not been constant over time, the older sediment reﬂecting
the historical exposure will not be in equilibrium with the present concentration in the
water column (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). Also, removal processes such as biodegrada-
tion may take place within the sediment and therefore alter the sediment concentration
of the compound (Oldenkamp et al. 2018, Supporting Information). Consequently, the
concentration of the compound on the sediment particles will be diﬀerent from the equi-
librium with the aqueous concentration in the water column, and this concentration
gradient results into a diﬀusive ﬂux between water and sediment. Similarly, the resus-
pension of particles from the sediment leads to adsorption or desorption to or from the
resuspended particles to reestablish the equilibrium with the concentration in the water
column. These ﬂuxes depend on the compound’s concentration in the sediment layer, its
dissolved fraction in the water column, its sediment-water partitioning and the diﬀusive
transfer velocity or the resuspension rate, respectively (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). The
overall sediment-water exchange velocity as a combination of the two described processes

A.5. Photolysis
Direct photolysis is a process where the absorbance of photons by a molecule and its
associated excitation leads to a transformation of the molecule through cleavage of cova-
lent bonds. Compounds that absorb light at wavelengths that reach the Earth’s surface,
i.e. within 290-600nm, may be susceptible to undergo direct photolysis. The ability
to absorb photons depends on the chemical structure of the compound. While most
alcohols, acids, ethers, esters, and aliphatic hydrocarbons do not absorb natural light,
compounds exhibiting moieties with delocalized p electrons, such as aromatics, are prone
to absorb solar radiation. (Bavumiragira et al. 2022)
The rate of transformation of a compound by direct photolysis depends not only
on its light absorbance, but also on the absorption eﬃciency determining how easily
the excited molecule will undergo a chemical transformation instead of going back to
its ground state. This is expressed by the quantum yield, calculated by the ratio of
transformed moles to the moles of photons absorbed. Hence, the photolysis rate is the
product of light absorbed by the molecule and the quantum yield. (Bavumiragira et al.
2022; Schwarzenbach et al. 2003)

A.6. Hydrolysis
Within abiotic chemical reactions, hydrolysis plays the most important role in the envi-
ronmental fate of compounds in surface waters, as the reaction partners - water molecules
and its ions - are most abundant (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). However, hydrolysis only
aﬀects compounds with hydrolysable functional groups such as esters or amides. For in-
stance, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) are inert to hydrolysis (Bavumiragira
et al. 2022).
Hydrolysis reactions are pH-dependent, as they are catalyzed by hydronium or hy-
droxide ions whose concentration changes with pH. Hence, the reaction rate constant
can be described as the sum of the neutral, base and acid catalyzed reaction as a function
of pH (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003):
khyd = kA · [H+] + kB · [OH−] + kH2O · [H2O] = kA · 10−pH + kB · 10pH−14 + kN
(46)

et al. 2015). The rate constants kN, kA and kB for the individual neutral, acid and

constant pH (mostly pH=7). Therefore, Oldenkamp et al. (2018) directly use khyd in

A.7. Biodegradation
The rate of microbial transformation of MP depends on the chemical structure of the
compound, i.e. its biodegradability, as well as on numerous environmental factors, such
as the microbial community and their abundance, temperature, nutrient availability, pH,
water ﬂow, and salinity (Bavumiragira et al. 2022). Since it is generally too complex to
consider all these parameters, biodegradation is usually modelled as a simple ﬁrst-order
decay using experimentally determined biodegradation rate constants kbio reported in
literature (e.g. Oldenkamp et al. 2018; Vezzaro et al. 2014). This approach was also
adopted in the present model.
However, a study which compiled biodegradation rate constants in groundwater and
soil of a large set of MP available in literature found that they vary over more than three
orders of magnitude for the majority of compounds (Greskowiak et al. 2017). Only weak
correlation with temperature or redox conditions was found, and no correlation at all with
biodegradation probabilities or predicted rate constants obtained from QSAR models. It
was concluded that other site-speciﬁc factors such as the microbial population inﬂuence
biodegradation rates which makes it hard to extrapolate rate constants determined at
one site for local or catchment scale modelling. According to the authors, this should
only be done considering their large uncertainty by stochastic modelling approaches.
