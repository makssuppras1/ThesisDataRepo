Abstract
Oxygen transfer in stirred tank bioreactors has been a subject of continuous re-
search for over a century. Yet, the field remains full of contradictions, largely due to
the system-dependent nature of gasÔÇôliquid mass transfer and the complex interplay of
physical and operational variables. Among these, temperature has traditionally been
underestimated or treated as a secondary factor often overshadowed by more ÔÇ£tunableÔÇØ
parameters like agitation or aeration.
This thesis narrows the focus to a specific but underexplored question: How does
temperature affect the volumetric mass transfer coefficient kLa and eventually the-
oretical Oxygen Transfer Rate OTR in bench-scale bioreactors?
Contrary to the
longstanding assumption that higher temperatures hinder oxygen transfer due to re-
duced gas solubility, this work investigates whether the simultaneous improvements in
diffusivity, reduced viscosity, and enhanced bubble dynamics can in fact offset or even
surpass this limitation. A detailed literature review is followed by taking a shot in
the development of a mechanistic model built from first principles. Experimental val-
idation was attempted using gassing-out method with high-speed oxygen probe across
45 combinations of operating parameters for distilled water and a repetition of the 45
combinations in a salt solution.
Agitation and aeration rates were varied as part of the design of experiments, to
create a robust dataset that reveals the underlying trends attributable to tempera-
ture. The results highlight that in no salinity(coallescing medium) or low-salinity(non
coallescing medium), well-mixed systems, increasing temperature can significantly en-
hance kLa, particularly at higher operating ranges relevant to thermophilic processes.
However, this enhancement is counteracted by the simultaneous decline in oxygen sol-
ubility with temperature, leading to a compensatory effect. As a result, the overall
oxygen transfer rate (OTR) remains relatively steady or slightly declines across the
studied temperature range.

1
Introduction
Oxygen transfer plays a fundamental role in aerobic bioprocesses, influencing cell growth,
metabolism, and product formation. Efficient oxygen delivery is particularly crucial in
stirred tank reactors (STRs), where the mass transfer rate must keep pace with the bio-
logical oxygen demand to ensure process stability and productivity. The volumetric mass
transfer coefficient, kLa, is a key parameter used to quantify the effectiveness of oxygen
transfer in these systems. It integrates the liquid-side mass transfer coefficient kL and
the specific interfacial area a, both of which are influenced by the liquid medium, reactor
design and operating conditions.[1][2]
Among these parameters, temperature holds a particularly significant yet often un-
derexplored role. Conventional wisdom has long suggested that lower temperatures are
preferable due to the higher solubility of oxygen in cooler liquids. However, this perspective
may overlook other temperature-driven mechanisms that enhance oxygen transfer, such as
reduced viscosity, increased diffusivity, and altered bubble dynamics. These effects become
especially relevant in the context of thermophilic bioprocessing, which is gaining interest in
many industries such as waste treatment, metallurgy, and the biopharmaceutical industry
due to the potential for higher productivity.[3][1] Industrial leaders are actively exploring
the feasibility of operating at elevated temperatures, and a more rigorous understanding
of how temperature truly affects kLa is needed to guide such initiatives.
The present study investigates how temperature, in conjunction with changes in coalles-
cence (via altering salinity )and operating parameters such as aeration rate and agitation
rate, affect kLa and OTR in a 3 L bench-scale stirred tank reactor. While multiple vari-
ables are examined, the principal focus is on temperature, specifically its direct and indirect
effects on oxygen mass transfer under controlled hydrodynamic conditions.
To this end, the thesis can be compartmentalised in three parts. The first part consisted
of a literature review of all temperature-driven factors affecting oxygen mass transfer in
stirred tank bioreactors. The second part was dedicated to experimental work, where vari-
ations of the static gassing-out method were used to evaluate kLa and subsequently OTR
under a wide range of operating conditions. Dissolved oxygen was monitored using a high-
speed optical sensor, and results were analyzed using linear and nonlinear approaches.The
final part involved the development of a semi empirical model attempting to predict kLa as
a function of temperature based on mass transfer principles and semi empirical correlations.
The ultimate goal of this work is to provide experimentally grounded insight into how
temperature influences oxygen transfer efficiency, especially in STR systems. This under-
standing can inform better process design and scale-up strategies in biotechnology and
pharmaceutical manufacturing, especially for thermophilic systems.

2.3
Temperature effect on physicochemical properties of water
GasÔÇôliquid oxygen transfer in bioreactors is governed by both mechanical conditions and
intrinsic physicochemical properties of the liquid medium. While reactor geometry and
agitation determine turbulence and mixing, the degree at which they do depends on the
properties of the media such as , viscosity, solubility, surface tension all of which are af-
fected by temperature and critically shape the mass transfer coefficient kL and interfacial
area a. Temperature, decreases oxygen solubility but simultaneously increases diffusivity
and decreases viscosity, often resulting in a net gain in kL [3]. Salts can reduce solubility
through salting-out but may also suppress bubble coalescence, increasing a [11]. Inter-
facial tension influences bubble size, with lower tension promoting smaller bubbles and
enhanced surface area . Turbulence further aids mass transfer by renewing the boundary
layer and distributing bubbles [10]. Together, these factors interact to define the systemÔÇÖs
overall oxygen transfer capacity. Below lies a description of each of the most impactful
physicochemical properties and their relation with kLa.

2.3.3
Temperature Effect on Surface Tension
At higher temperatures, the surface tension of water decreases, which reduces the stabi-
lizing force between adjacent bubbles. This promotes bubble coalescence, leading to fewer
and larger bubbles, and ultimately a reduction in the total gasÔÇôliquid interfacial area a.[16]

reducing resistance to deformation from turbulent forces. This leads to the formation of
smaller bubbles and can increase the total interfacial area a, unless coalescence mechanisms
override the breakup process.[16]

Temperature effect on Diffusivity
Diffusivity, particularly in gas-liquid systems, is a strong function of temperature. As tem-
perature increases, the thermal motion of molecules intensifies, leading to higher diffusion
rates. For dilute systems like oxygen in water, the temperature dependence of the diffusion
coefficient is relatively well-documented. [18]

correction from a reference value at 20┬░[19]C:

Coalescence Mechanism
When two gas bubbles approach one another, thin films of liquid form between them. These
films are initially stabilized by hydrodynamic forces, creating dimples due to deformation.
As the bubbles get closer, the film thins and the dimples eventually disappear, forming
a flat film interface. The film continues to drain until it becomes unstable and ruptures,
allowing the bubbles to merge into a larger one. The capillary pressure driving this process
depends on surface tension and bubble curvature. This thinning can be slowed by repulsive
forces such as disjoining pressure, which may delay or prevent coalescence. In systems
without additives, frequent bubble collisions typically result in coalescence unless balanced
by surface or electrostatic forces. Salts or surfactants are often used to stabilize bubbles
by altering surface forces and interfacial tension. Coalescence is important for gasÔÇôliquid
mass transfer because larger merged bubbles reduce the total interfacial area.[22][23]

Effect of Salt on the Liquid-Side Mass Transfer Coefficient, kL
The presence of salt in aqueous systems exerts a multifaceted influence on the liquid-side
mass transfer coefficient, kL, primarily by altering the fluidÔÇÖs physicochemical properties.
Most notably, the addition of salts such as MgSO4 increases the dynamic viscosity ┬Á of the
liquid while simultaneously decreasing both the diffusivity D of oxygen and its solubility
CÔêù. All of these parameters are tightly interwoven with the magnitude of kL.However these
changes are significant only at higher concentrations of salt.[11][18][28]
Experimental studies consistently report a net decrease in kL with increasing salt con-
centration. For instance, Harriott and Hamilton (1965) observed a reduction in oxygen
mass transfer rates with the addition of NaCl, attributing it to decreased diffusivity . Sim-
ilar findings were echoed in bioprocess applications, where electrolyte-rich broths exhibited
diminished kL values relative to distilled water systems [18].
17
==PAGE:23==
An important nuance, however, is that this decrease in kL does not scale linearly with
salt concentration. Rather, the suppression follows a nonlinear trend, often characterized
using Sechenov-type models for gas solubility and StokesÔÇôEinstein approximations for dif-
fusivity suppression. At low to moderate salt concentrations (e.g., 1ÔÇô2 wt%), the impact
on kL is modest, but becomes more pronounced at higher salinity levels.[11]
Furthermore, electrolyte type plays a critical role. Multivalent salts such as MgSO4
exert a stronger effect than monovalent ones like NaCl, due to their greater influence on
water structure, viscosity, and ionÔÇôdipole interactions. [18][11]
In conclusion, salt acts to suppress the liquid-side mass transfer coefficient primarily
by increasing viscosity and reducing oxygen diffusivity. [11]
Effect of Salt on the Interfacial Area, a
Salt affects interfacial area mainly through their effect on coalescence.Numerous studies
have demonstrated that certain salts can significantly suppress coalescence, thereby reduc-

a heat jacket for temperature control and sensors for pH and dissolved oxygen monitoring
are often integrated. The STRÔÇÖs geometry is defined by key parameters such as tank diam-

2.4.2
Aeration
Aeration plays a pivotal role in oxygen mass transfer by determining the volume and dis-
tribution of gas introduced into the bioreactor. Its effect is manifested primarily through
changes in the interfacial area (a) and, to a lesser extent, the liquid-side mass trans-
fer coefficient (kL), ultimately influencing the overall volumetric mass transfer coefficient
(kLa).[27]
As the aeration rate increases, a larger number of gas bubbles are introduced into the
liquid phase, leading to a higher total interfacial area available for mass transfer. This is
particularly impactful in systems with low agitation, where the interfacial area is primarily
governed by the bubble population rather than turbulent breakup. Moreover, increased
gas hold-up contributes to prolonged bubble residence time, enhancing the contact time
between the gas and liquid phases. However, the benefit is not linear; at excessive aeration
rates, bubble coalescence near the sparger, flooding, or gas channeling may occur, limiting
the effective rise in a.[27]
In terms of kL, aeration alone contributes modestly since molecular diffusion at the
bubble-liquid interface remains limited by the relatively stagnant liquid film. However,
increased aeration does promote mild convection and turbulence around rising bubbles,
thinning the boundary layer and marginally increasing kL. The synergistic effect of aeration
and agitation becomes apparent when both are applied simultaneously: rising bubbles
increase gas holdup and interfacial area, while impeller-driven turbulence enhances renewal
of the liquid film and increases bubble breakup thereby boosting kLa significantly as shown

2.5.2
Dynamic gassing out method
The dynamic gassing-out method is a widely applied technique for determining the volu-
metric oxygen mass transfer coefficient kLa in systems where biological oxygen consump-
tion is present. Unlike the static method, which assumes no cellular activity, the dynamic
method enables real-time assessment of kLa during active fermentation or cultivation pro-
cesses. It is particularly valuable in scaling studies and process optimization, where mea-
surements must reflect actual operating conditions .[2]
In this method, the bioreactor is initially operated under standard aeration until the
dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration reaches steady state. Aeration is then temporarily
stopped, allowing microorganisms to consume oxygen and reduce the DO level. Once a

resumed, and the reoxygenation curve is recorded using a fast-response DO probe. The
recovery phase is used to estimate the oxygen transfer rate, while accounting for the oxygen
uptake rate (OUR).[2]

2.5.3
Pressure step Method
The pressure-step method is a less commonly used but theoretically robust approach for
determining the volumetric oxygen mass transfer coefficient (kLa). It involves abruptly
increasing or decreasing the pressure inside a sealed bioreactor to induce a shift in the
saturation concentration of dissolved oxygen 5ÔÇô10% of CÔêù) The resulting change in the
measured DO concentration over time reflects the systemÔÇÖs response to the altered driving
force for mass transfer. By analyzing the DO decay or rise kinetics, kLa can be estimated
using first-order models.
This method is particularly useful in pressurized systems or
reactors with minimal gas exchange with the environment. .However, because the pressure
shift alters the oxygen concentration in both the bubbles and the liquid simultaneously,
the method does not account for non-ideal mixing effects, potentially limiting its accuracy
in poorly mixed systems. [2]

2.5.4
Sulfite Oxidation method
The sulfite oxidation method is a chemical technique for determining kLa based on the
rapid, non-biological consumption of oxygen. It involves the oxidation of sodium sulfite in
the presence of a cobalt catalyst, which reacts immediately with dissolved oxygen:
Na2SO3 + 1
2O2 ÔåÆNa2SO4
Because the reaction maintains the dissolved oxygen concentration at near zero, the
rate of oxygen transfer into the liquid becomes equal to the rate of oxygen consumption. As
such, the oxygen transfer rate can be calculated based on the amount of oxygen consumed,
typically determined via titration of the oxidized product. This method offers high repro-
ducibility and eliminates the complexities of biological systems, but it can overestimate
kLa due to altered hydrodynamics and bubble behavior in the sulfite solution. It is best
suited for clean, controlled lab conditions where biological interference is absent [2][31]

Design of Experiments (DoE)
The initial plan was to encode three values in each range(-1,0.1) which would be the two
extremes values and one middle value. Therefore a possible 33 = 27 number of experiments
should be performed for distilled water and an additional 27 experiments for the salt
solution which would more closely resemble process water. It was later decided that to
capture temperature trends more accurately since the range is quite large an additional
2 intermediate points should be added at 30 and 60 degrees. That raised the amount of
experiments to5x3x3 = 45for the distilled water and 45 more for the salt solution for a
total of 90 experiments or 270 repetitions of the gassing out method. It was therefore
concluded that it would be more conducive to perform a subset of the total experiments
and predict the results of the rest using a smart DOE.
At the initial planning stage, a Box-Behnken Design (BBD) was considered to efficiently
explore the effects of three independent variables temperature (ÔùªC), agitation speed (RPM),
and gas flow rate (L/min) on the volumetric oxygen mass transfer coefficient (kLa) similarly
to the work of Lone 2018.. BBD is a response surface methodology that allows estimation
of quadratic models while requiring fewer experimental runs compared to full factorial
designs. It avoids corner points of the design space, making it safer for sensitive systems
like bioreactors.[26]
As an alternative to reduce experimental workload, a Random Forest regression model
was also explored as an alternative strategy. This machine learning approach can capture
nonlinear interactions between variables and was intended to predict missing or unmeasured
kLa values based on a subset of experimental data.[36]
However, initial attempts using both BBD and Random Forest yielded poor predictive
performance.The original plan of the current thesis was to attempt to replicate the lab
experiments in the pilot scale using identical P/V and vvm values. However it was later
decided to perform all runs and not predict values using DOE to establish reliable lab
scale results, a time consuming endeavor which prevented replication of the experiments
at larger scale within the time scope of the thesis.

A mechanistic exploration of kLa begins with the classical two-film theory, which models
the liquid-side mass transfer coefficient as:
kL = D
╬┤
where D is the molecular diffusivity of oxygen in the liquid phase and ╬┤ is the effective
thickness of the stagnant liquid film adjacent to the gas-liquid interface.As temperature
increases Diffusivity increases.Moreover the liquid film thickness decreases since the vor-
texes that affect the thin layer dissipate closer to the film since viscocity is lower.So by the

parcels at the interface, offering a more mechanistic foundation for modeling mass transfer
under turbulent conditions.

Discussion
5.1
Discussion on Oxygen Solubility
The measured maximum DO values across temperatures were consistently lower than
their theoretical solubility limits at elevated temperatures (60ÔÇô70┬░C). This discrepancy
can largely be attributed to the operational limits of the optical oxygen sensor , which is
rated for reliable performance only between 0 and 50┬░C [34]. Beyond this range, the fluo-
rescence lifetime of the sensor dye becomes nonlinearly affected by temperature, leading to
inaccurate phase-shift readings and systematic underestimation of dissolved oxygen levels.
As temperature increases, the decay time of the luminescent dye shortens, potentially ex-
ceeding the detection resolution of the photodetector, which in turn introduces error into
the lifetime-to-concentration calibration curve . These issues are compounded by the fact
that oxygen solubility itself decreases with temperature, reducing signal amplitude further
and making accurate detection more difficult.[34]
Despite these limitations, the calculated kLa values remain valid, as they are primarily
derived from the rate of change in DO over time rather than absolute DO values.If the
overall oxygenation curve maintains its shape and time constant, the slope used to deter-
mine kLa remains accurate even if the upper and lower DO limits are offset. This makes
the method robust against systematic calibration errors,as long as recalibration is done at
each set of experiments performed at different temperature.
5.2
Discussion on kLa and OTR results in Distilled Water
The experimental results for distilled water showed a clear increase in kLa with rising
temperature across all agitation intensities, consistent with theoretical expectations. At
each power input per volume (P/V) level, kLa values began at relatively low levels at 20┬░C
and increased steadily up to 70┬░C. The upward trend became more pronounced with higher
agitation speeds, as shown in the linear regression slopes, suggesting that the temperature
sensitivity of kLa increases with mixing intensity.
This trend aligns well with what was previously found by scientists in the field[17]
[21][26][3]as well as with the underlying theory.[1][5][9][10] [21]According to the penetra-
tion theory, the liquid-side mass transfer coefficient kL increases with decreasing dynamic
viscosity (┬Á) and increasing diffusion coefficient (D). Both of these temperature-dependent
parameters facilitate faster renewal of the liquid at the gas-liquid interface. Additionally,
the interfacial area a is known to increase with agitation due to bubble fragmentation,
and its theoretical dependence on parameters such as surface tension (¤â), gas holdup, and
density (¤ü) supports this behavior as also shown in equation 26.
On the other hand, the oxygen transfer rate (OTR), computed as OTR = kLa ┬À CÔêù,
remained largely stable or even slightly declined across the temperature range. This is
expected due to the opposing influence of oxygen solubility (CÔêù), which decreases with
temperature. The increase in kLa is therefore counterbalanced by the reduction in CÔêù,
particularly in well-mixed systems where probe readings are assumed to be accurate and
67
==PAGE:73==
reflective of bulk conditions.[39][25][3][26]
Overall, the results support the hypothesis that operating at elevated temperatures in
distilled water enhances (kLa), while the net oxygen transfer rate (OTR) is governed by
the trade-off between increased (kLa) and reduced solubility.
5.3
Discussion on kLa and OTR results in Salt Solution
In saline water, the overall temperature trend of kLa remains similar to that observed in
distilled water: kLa increases with temperature across all agitation intensities. However,
two important deviations must be noted.
First, the absolute values of kLa are generally higher in saline conditions. This increase
is consistent with theoretical expectations that the interfacial area a plays a dominant
role. The addition of salt can suppress bubble coalescence, leading to smaller bubbles and
higher gas-liquid interfacial area. While the diffusivity (D) may slightly decrease in saline
solutions, and viscosity (┬Á) may increase, the overriding effect of enhanced a appears to
dominate, resulting in improved mass transfer.
However, at higher temperatures, the linearity of the kLa increase breaks down. This
deviation could be explained by a temperature-induced disruption in the anti-coalescence
effect of salt. At elevated thermal conditions, surface tension gradients and bubble dy-
namics may override the stabilizing influence of salt, leading to increased coalescence and
reduced effective interfacial area a.[11][28][18]
An alternative and increasingly plausible explanation is related to measurement arti-
facts. The consistent pattern of deviation across multiple runs and temperatures suggests
that the optical oxygen probe may become less reliable at higher temperatures, since it
is stated in the pyrobench manual that its use is intended for temperatures below 50
C.However if that is true this nonlinearity should have appeared in the distilled water
graphs as well
5.4
Discussion on Synergistic effects of temperature with agitation on
interfacial area
The influence of temperature on the gasÔÇôliquid interfacial area, a, is not straightforward
and appears to depend heavily on the reactor type and the dominant bubble dynamics
mechanisms. In the absence of mechanical agitation as is typical in bubble columns exper-
imental studies have consistently shown that increasing temperature leads to a reduction
in interfacial area. This is primarily attributed to a decrease in liquid viscosity and sur-
face tension with temperature, which in turn accelerates the drainage of the liquid film
between bubbles, facilitating coalescence and leading to the formation of larger bubbles.
Larger bubbles possess a lower surface-area-to-volume ratio, thereby reducing the total
interfacial area.
In a study on oxygen transfer in coalescing systems (pure water), it was reported
that interfacial area decreased by approximately 20% when temperature increased from
68
==PAGE:74==
20┬░C to 50┬░C [40]. Similarly, another study on bubble column reactors demonstrated a
systematic decline in a with rising temperature, unless salts or surfactants were added above
the critical concentration necessary to suppress coalescence [18]. In such non-coalescing
systems, the interfacial area may remain relatively stable or even slightly increase with
temperature.
However, this temperature effect behaves differently in mechanically agitated systems
such as stirred tank reactors (STRs). Here, the bubble size and corresponding interfacial
area are dominated by the hydrodynamic forces generated by the impeller. Under such
forced convection, increasing temperature reduces the liquidÔÇÖs viscosity, which allows the
impeller-generated turbulence to break up gas bubbles more efficiently. As a result, the
interfacial area may actually increase with temperature despite the coalescence-promoting
effects of reduced viscosity and surface tension.
This highlights a key distinction: in systems with external agitation, the mechanical
energy input can overcome thermal effects that promote coalescence.[27][20]
5.5
Discussion on Semi-Empirical model
It is important at this point to discuss the thought process behind the theoretical model
in the current thesis.The mechanistic model developed in this study is rooted in a first-
principles approach, starting from foundational mass transfer theory. The formulation for
kL was derived from the penetration theory, due to the fact that it is better in explaining
transient behaviour , modified to apply to STRs. Similarly, the expression for interfacial
area a was constructed from semi empirical relationships tied to bubble diameter and gas
holdup in STRs. The first part of this attempt resulted in an equation connecting kLa with
the physicochemical properties described in theory plus the operating parameters.
Then,by systematically substituting expressions for temperature-sensitive properties as
functions of temperature( dynamic viscosity ┬Á(T), density ¤ü(T), surface tension ¤â(T)ÔÇ× and
diffusivity D(T),) we ultimately derived a comprehensive expression for kLa as a function
of T, P
V and vg, eliminating direct dependence on the underlying fluid properties.This final
model is dependent solely on operating parameters .Contrary to the common power law
model ,where the exponents are substituted only on empirical fitting,this model is derived
by theoretical principles and semi empirical correlations that apply for STRs.
Concerning the models accuracy as a whole when compared to the experimental values
it showed an mean absolute deviation percentage of 12% which mean it accurately pre-

Conclusions
This study explored how temperature, affect oxygen mass transfer in stirred tank reactors in
distilled water and with the addition of salt, through both experimental trials and theoretical
modeling. The main conclusions drawn from this work are:
1. The volumetric mass transfer coefficient kLa was observed to increase linearly with temper-
ature across all conditions tested. This trend was consistently confirmed by experimental
data, relevant literature, and also supported by the mechanistic model, which captures the
temperature dependence through fluid properties like viscosity, diffusivity, and surface ten-
sion.
2. The oxygen transfer rate (OTR) stayed constant or slightly decreased in some cases as
temperature increased. This result fits with theory, since OTR depends on both kLa and
the oxygen solubility CÔêù. As kLa goes up, CÔêùdrops, leading to a balancing effect. The
outcome was clearly reflected in the data and agreed with findings from the literature.
3. The use of salt (in this case, magnesium sulfate) raised kLa values across the board. This
effect is most likely due to salt suppressing bubble coalescence, leading to increased interfacial
area a. However, this benefit appears to break down at higher temperatures, possibly because
thermal effects begin to override the stabilizing influence of salt, allowing coalescence to
return.
4. An alternative oxygen probe should be considered in future work. ThereÔÇÖs a chance the ob-
served deviation in oxygen solubilities (or mean max DO values) at high temperatures may be
due to limitations in the probeÔÇÖs accuracy at temperatures higher then its intended use.This
could also explain the non linearities for the salt solution graphs at higher temperatures.An
alternate probe should be used that can operate in a wider range of Temperatures
5. The effect of temperature on gasÔÇôliquid interfacial area (a) is highly dependent on reactor
type and mixing conditions. In non-agitated systems such as bubble columns, increasing
temperature reduces promotes coalescence and decreases total interfacial area.In contrast,
in mechanically agitated systems like stirred tank reactors (STRs), the reduction in viscosity
with increasing temperature enhances impeller-driven bubble breakup. As a result, the in-
terfacial area may remain stable or even increase with temperature, despite the simultaneous
coalescence-promoting effects of reduced viscosity .This phonemena can be partly explained
by tables 12 and 13 where temperature-agitation synergy becomes apparent.Future work is
needed to experimentally verify this difference in interfacial area behaviour between agitated
and non agitated systems .
6. The proposed model demonstrated satisfactory predictive capability, with an overall mean
absolute deviation of 12% from experimental values across tested conditions. While it suc-
cessfully captured the increasing trend of kLa with temperature, the model consistently un-
derpredicted absolute kLa values compared to both experimental measurements and LeeÔÇÖs
model, particularly when using the isolated temperature correction factor f(T).

Future perspective
As mentioned in the discussion and conclusion there appears to be some synergy between
temperature and agitation that changes the interfacial area behaviour in STRs where agi-
tation is present compared to bubble columns where agitation is absent.To experimentally
validate the proposed synergy between agitation and temperature on interfacial area a,
a follow-up study could introduce two additional agitation conditions: 1) a non-agitated
system (0 rpm) to mimic a bubble column, and 2) a low-rpm condition just above 0 rpm,
sufficient to create bulk circulation and vortexing but not intense enough to induce bubble
breakup.
In the 0 rpm case, temperature would affect a primarily through viscosity-driven co-
alescence, as expected in classical bubble column systems. The low rpm setup, however,
may accelerate bubbles upward through impeller-generated vortexing without causing sig-
nificant breakup, effectively increasing their rise velocity ug and reducing residence time,
while still allowing coalescence.
To connect this transition to theory, a bubble imaging probe (e.g., high-speed camera )
could be installed in the reactor to directly observe bubble size distributions. If the theory
is correct, one would expect to see relatively large bubbles at 0 rpm, slightly smaller but
still coalesced bubbles at low rpm, and a marked shift to fine dispersion once turbulence
induced breakup takes over at higher speeds.
In regards to temperature , in the no and low rpm settings one can expect increasing
bubble size and decreasing a with increasing temperature.
In the higher rpm settings(the settings of this study) ,one can expect to see the opposite.
Decreasing bubble size and increasing a with increasing temperature
If bubble size distribution is measured using a camera probe, it also becomes possible
to estimate the interfacial area a by combining the Sauter mean bubble diameter with
an appropriate estimate of gas holdup . Given that kLa is already experimentally deter-
mined, the liquid-side mass transfer coefficient kL can be back-calculated . This opens the
door to analyzing how kL and a vary independently with temperature, and to comparing
each experimentally derived term to its theoretical prediction. In particular, kL can be
benchmarked against penetration theory or two film theory based models, while a can be
compared with semiempirical correlations that incorporate power input and bubble size.
In the case of salt-containing media, measuring how fluid properties such as dynamic
viscosity , density , oxygen diffusivity , and surface tension change with salt would allow
to capture the full effects of salt on system and not just its effect on preventing coalescence
Finally, the experiments should be repeated at pilot scale. Multiple DO and temper-
ature probes could be installed at different heights to assess temperature gradients and
non-ideal mixing zones. Additionally, using a single bottom-mounted impeller in a tall
reactor would create a system with hybrid hydrodynamics:STR-like conditions near the
bottom and bubble column behavior in the upper part. Then the experiments should be
repeated but with the addition of impellers at different heights that lead to uniform mixing.

Final thoughts
As previously stated, the existing literature on oxygen mass transfer is highly fragmented.
Experimental studies can be conducted in a variety of systems such as stirred tank reac-
tors, bubble columns, or aeration tanks. Additionally, the working media used can range
from distilled water to fermentation broths, process water, or even tap water. Theoretical
investigations also differ significantly, each beginning from distinct foundational assump-
tions.Some follow the two-film theory, others the penetration model, and many adopt
semi-empirical fits . As a result, science and practical engineering start to diverge: What
becomes clear is that each system must be approached with its own tailored strategy if
results are to be trustworthy.This leads to two main points.
First, if industrial relevance is the end goal, then scale becomes a limiting factor.
Experiments conducted in laboratory-scale vessels may be insightful, but they are not
very representative. Any future investigation aiming for application should be shifted to
a pilot-scale system, which more closely mirrors real operating conditions. Only at this
scale can meaningful agitation, aeration, and temperature phenomena be noticed which
are otherwise misrepresented at small scale.
Second, the composition of the working media must reflect what is actually used in in-
dustrial practice. Attempts to mimic real broths by gradually adding components to water
are fundamentally flawed. Each ingredient added not only changes the fluid properties but
does so in nonlinear and often complex ways. The moment more than a few ingredients are
introduced, the complexity increases exponentially. Viscosity, surface tension, solubility,
and density interactions can no longer be predicted . Thus, while the idea may be mentally
stimulating, it is of little practical utility.
A more realistic direction would be to measure both kLa and OTR as a function
of temperature in a pilot-scale reactor using actual process media whether it be a cell
culture broth or a fermentation slurry. For complex media, empirical data remains the
only dependable route.
