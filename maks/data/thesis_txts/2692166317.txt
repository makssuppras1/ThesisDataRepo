Abstract
In a conventional structural design process the structural material is chosen based on intuition
rather than actual calculations of the structural performance. The aim of this Master’s thesis
is to expand the solution space and provide knowledge about diﬀerent design options in the
early design phase. This is achieved by implementing a material-independent model using an
integrated dynamic model. This approach seeks to connect the structural calculations to the
design of the geometry in a single model which can handle diﬀerent materials. The structural
and the geometrical parts are connected by Grasshopper, a visual programming environment.
In the thesis, the following materials are considered: structural steel, reinforced concrete and
mass timber. The material properties and the structural veriﬁcation of the materials are quite
diﬀerent, but this thesis rather pursues ﬁnding the similarities in the structural design procedure
between these materials. The thesis focuses on frame structures and particularly the design of
beams and columns according to the applicable Eurocodes.
To validate the implemented framework the performance of the structural veriﬁcation is tested
for each material.
It has been found that the structural veriﬁcation works as expected for
beams and columns subjected to a single section force, which is often the case in braced frame
structures. To validate the design ﬂexibility and precision of the framework, two case studies
are carried out. The case studies show that the developed tools can design frame structures
in diﬀerent materials and with diﬀerent structural layouts. Finally, it is showcased how the
framework through automation can generate multiple diﬀerent design options, which supports
an evidence-based approach to structural design in the early design phases.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Often major design choices is made based on intuition, rather than on the basis of analysis and
informed decision making. This is also the case for structural design.
Design decisions made at early design stages can highly impact the ﬁnal outcome of a project and
are usually the most important design decisions of a project[4]. These decisions are often very
inﬂuential on key drivers of a project, such as cost and material quantities, imposing constraints
on the project and thereby shaping later decisions during the design process.
In these early phases changes are relatively easily made, but limited information is available.
This is in contrast to later phases where every change is diﬃcult and expensive. Typically,
architects make most of these early decisions. However, the presence of structural engineers at
the early phases is key since it gives the structural engineers an opportunity to include structural
optimization in the conceptual design stage. This can lead to smart decisions on the conceptual
design by providing guidance when the design is still ﬂexible.
However, most often structural analysis and optimization happen too late to have any signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the design. This can result in expensive project changes and reduced solution space.
The variations in later phases are fairly small compared to variations in the early design phases,
and the impact of these decisions on the key drivers is relatively small.
Despite the relevance of the conceptualization step there is a general lack of computational
support for the structural engineer during early decision-making. Most current tools available
for the structural engineer are often built for the stages at the end of the design process and are
intended for detailed engineering analysis or documentation purposes rather than for structural
design. These tools do not help the designing structural engineer support the early design stage
on a conceptual level, since these tools are mainly aimed at precision rather than ﬂexibility and
generation of alternative design options [5][6].
Another problem in the conceptual design phase is the lack of compatibility between tools
for architects and engineers.
These tools are often not interchangeable and the conversion
of information may take a lot of time, slowing down the entire design process. Most of the
software used by the architect are often limited to geometry while the structural engineer’s
analysis software is adapted to speciﬁc structural tasks at a very detailed level. Without good
compatibility the structural engineer has to interpret the information given by architects and
remodel the geometry from the architect with structural properties.
As a result, it is not
possible to work on a design simultaneously. This may lead to a gap in the understanding of
the project between the structural engineers and the architects leading to delays in the workﬂow
and consequently is responsible for coordination errors and the necessity for rework followed by
increased cost [7].

CHAPTER 1.
INTRODUCTION
1.1.
PROBLEM STATEMENT
In recent years new and powerful tools for digital modelling have been developed and intro-
duced to the architectural design process. Computational parametric design, in which design
parameters can be embedded to algorithms, is probably the most relevant subject in this new
era.
Parametric design involves designing parameters which in turn deﬁnes a model. This allows the
exploration of design options not possible through a conventional design process. One of the
main software used for parametric modelling is Grasshopper, a visual programming language
(VPL) that runs within the modelling software Rhino. An advantage of this parametric tool in
comparison to other similar tools is its wide range of available plug-ins which are able to perform
tasks going beyond geometric manipulation.
One of these plug-ins is Karamba3D, which is a structural analysis tool. Karamba is able to
perform ﬁnite element analysis inside the Grasshopper environment by enabling the conversion
of geometric elements into ﬁnite elements, thus bridging the gap between architectural and
structural design [8]. In this way changes to the parameterized geometry are followed by real-
time structural analysis. This has the potential to reduce manual work and iteration process in
a conventional design process.
One of the ﬁrst decisions made in a structural design process is the choice of structural material.
This choice aﬀects later decisions to a great extent since structural materials have vastly diﬀerent
properties. When parametric design is used in structural modelling it is almost always used in
the scope of a design with a speciﬁc material and structural system. This means that very early
in the design the design space has been greatly reduced.
1.1
Problem Statement
The purpose of this thesis is to describe and develop a tool in Grasshopper that can approximate
the primary structure of a building independent of the structural material used. This thesis
focuses on the materials: structural steel, reinforced concrete and mass timber, for the design of
frame structures with beams and columns as the primary load bearing members.
Aided by parametric design, the goal is to integrate geometry creation, typically performed
by architects, and structural analysis, typically performed by structural engineers, in a single,
ﬂexible environment with quick feedback. The motivation is to create a tool that can be used
in cooperation between architects and structural engineers which is capable of both geometric
and structural design.
Based on this motivation, the questions that this report will attempt to solve are:
• Is it possible to develop a model that provide the ﬂexibility and precision required for
structural design in early design stages?
• Can it be ﬂexible enough to handle structural design independent of material choices?
• Can it deliver relevant feedback to support the selection of a structural model?
1.2
Structure of the Thesis
The work of this project is divided into two books. The ﬁrst one, namely this report, is the
thesis which also includes the appendices. The second book, Framework Manual, consists of a
guide to the developed tools. It explains the details of the developed components and aims to
to explain how to use the tools.

Chapter 2
Method
The primary design method used for this thesis is the Double Diamond model developed by the
British Design Council [9]. The model consists of 4 phases, which help you ﬁrst deﬁne the right
problem and then ﬁnd the right solution, see Figure 2.1.

right problem
Finding the
right solution

Define
Develop

Figure 2.1: The Double Diamond model. The discover phase is characterised by divergent thinking investigating a broad
range of ideas and opportunities. The deﬁne stage involves the evaluation and selection of ideas. In the develop phase,
tools are developed to solve problems or user needs identiﬁed in the previous phases [9].
The discover phase of this thesis consists of conversations with architects and engineers who
both supplied useful but diﬀerent desires and ideas for what a potential tool should do. Further
it was deﬁned what is required of an early structural design primarily based on requirements
from diﬀerent standards.
In the research of the project diﬀerent concepts and computational tools that might aid the
development of the tool has been investigated.
The development of the tool has been an iterative process with regular discussions with structural
engineers and architects about the development of the tool throughout the project. Towards
the end of the project, the tool has been tested on both structural engineering students and
structural engineers, which has provided useful feedback for creating the ﬁnal version of the
tool.

Chapter 3
Project Basis
This chapter establishes the decisions made based on the ﬁrst phases of the design method
leading to the development of the tool.
Based on the discussions with architects and engineers the primary needs on both sides have
been determined. From an architectural perspective, the main concerns of the structural design
relates to the spaces inside the building. Architects want ﬂexibility of the ﬂoor plans, which
is constricted by placing internal structural columns. On the other hand, the structural engi-
neer is concerned with the structural performance of the structure. The structure must satisfy
requirements according to diﬀerent standards regarding diﬀerent limit states. In this project
it is decided to focus on the bearing capacity of members in the ultimate limit state and the
deﬂections of members in the serviceability limit state.
The requirements for a tools for the early structural design results in a clash between ﬂexibility
and precision of the tool. A validation of the precision of the tool is presented in Chapter 5
which concerns veriﬁcation of structural members. Further, the precision of the developed design
algorithm is showcased in a case study, see Section 6.1. The ﬂexibility of the tool is presented in
Chapter 7 and in a case study, see Section 6.2. The developed tools are presented in Chapter 4.
3.1
Software Tools
In this section the tools used to develop the framework are described. These tools all work inside
the same visual programming language environment.
3.1.1
Rhinoceros 3D
Developed by McNeel & Associates, Rhino(ceros) [10] is a 3D computer graphics and computer-
aided design (CAD) application software. It enables the generation of any kind of geometry and
is a popular tool ased by architects. However, in the developed framework, Rhino is primarily
used as a visual platform of designs made in Grasshopper.

CHAPTER 3.
PROJECT BASIS
3.1.
SOFTWARE TOOLS
3.1.2
Grasshopper
Grasshopper is a visual scripting language (VPL) and environment for Rhino (developed by
Robert McNeel & Associates). Using a VPL, designers can explore generative design solution.
Grasshopper works by giving the user the ability to connect components that carry out speciﬁc
tasks in a sequential order. It is fully embedded in the Rhino modeller and is able to use its
graphical capabilities. Grasshopper provides a broad range of additional plug-ins by third-party
developers, where the main plug-ins used in thesis are explained below.
Karamba3D
Karamba [11] is a parametric structural engineering tool developed by Clemens Preisinger in
cooperation with Bollinger und Grohmann ZT GmbH. It features a wide set of tools to perform
ﬁnite element analysis for buildings and design of steel structures according to Eurocode 3:
Design of steel structures. [8]
Beaver
Beaver [12] is a plugin for the Grasshopper environment, which allows parametric analysis and
design of timber structures according to Eurocode 5: Design of timber structures. Beaver was
conceived at the Polytechnic School of the University of São Paulo - Brazil, and developed by
Eng. Márcio Sartorelli and Eng. João Tavares Pini. Beaver is composed by a series of Grasshop-
per components that work based on section forces and displacements provided by Karamba or
another FEM plug-in for Grasshopper. In extension to a FEM plug-in, structural data can be
fed to Beaver for speciﬁc timber ULS and SLS analysis. [13]
Anemone
Anemone [14] is one of a few diﬀerent plug-ins that allow you to add looping within Grasshop-
per which is otherwise restricted to a “left to right data stream” rule which is mandatory in
Grasshopper. This is useful in iterative processes where iterations depends on previous ones.
Ladybug Tools
Ladybug [15] is an environmental plug-ins for Grasshopper to help designers create an environmentally-
conscious architectural design.[16] The reason that this plugin is used in this project is that it
contains a component Ladybug ﬂy that allows you to cycle through all input options connected
to the component.
3.1.3

DESIGN OF GEOMETRIC MODEL
4.1
Design of Geometric Model
The typical procedure in Grasshopper to build a generative algorithm is to connect components
to build a algorithm designed speciﬁcally for one pattern. This means that one must create a
parametric model that ﬁts a speciﬁc structure. It may take a long time to set up this algorithm.
The programmer must consider numerous preconditions and plan his work systematically. Mod-
elling with a parametric design algorithm is a slow process if the elements needs to be modelled
one by one and the structure is not repetitive. Tasks such as drawing a line for an element
cannot be done using the mouse and drawing from one point to another. Instead it needs to be
done by programming to keep the model dynamic. According to the thesis of Mäenpää it took
approximately 200 hours to set up the model for a steel warehouse [24].
Thus, one could ask whether the time is well spent creating the parametric model in the ﬁrst
place since it is only applicable to one exact structure. What if the model is changed in a way
that is outside the capabilities of the parametric model? Of course, parts of the of the framework
may be reused in a new design, but additional programming has to be made by the user of the
algorithm. Early-stage design is an ambiguous and intuitive process that requires the use of
highly ﬂexible design method and this approach is only ﬂexible to a certain degree.
Instead of manually designing an algorithm for a certain structure, in this project it is proposed
to use a more ﬂexible tool that by design rules can model diﬀerent structures based on the input
parameters. The concept is described below, while the developed toolbox is described in further
detail in the Framework Manual.
The approach is to transform input zones into spatial zones determining where to place struc-
tural elements. The main input of the tool is a spatial zone which constitutes the design space of
the structural model, see Figure 4.4. The ﬁgure shows how the structural design space is subdi-
vided into smaller cells in a 3-D rectilinear grid, or as described in the following, a cell complex.
The geometry of a structural model is generated by applying properties to the individual cells.
To create the cell complex a topological model is needed. A Grasshopper plug-in for this pur-
pose is Topologic [25]. Topologic contains a software modelling library enabling hierarchical and
topological representations of architectural spaces and buildings through non-manifold topology.
Topologic implements the classes: vertex, edge, wire, face, shell and cell which can describe a
single 1-, 2- or 3-dimensional entity. In addition to those the concept of a cell complex is in-
troduced, which is a contiguous collection of cells and is non-manifold [26][27]. A property of
being non-manifold is that it can contain internal geometry. For this project that property is
important, since it is necessary to describe the bonds between neighboring cells. However, this
plug-in is not used in this project since it currently runs very slow when creating larger cell
complexes. Instead, a simpler topological tool for designing structures in a 3-D rectilinear grid
is implemented. Of the classes implemented in Topologic, the following will be used: vertex,
edge, face, cell and cell complex. Of these classes the most important is the cell, since structural
design routines will be run at this level. A cell is deﬁned as a closed collection of faces, bounding
a 3D region. Each of the topological classes is composed of topologies of the previous class.

4.2
Creating Loads
A big part of structural modelling is determining the loads acting on a structure and how they
are transferred through the structure. The loads acting on a structure, which are considered in
this thesis includes permanent load, from the self weight of the structural and non-structural
elements, imposed load, wind load and snow load. Some of these can be generated automati-
cally based on the geometry on the structure. Both wind load and snow loads can be generated
based on the exterior shape of the building, while the self weight of structural elements can be
modelled by gravity loads. However, some loads have to be determined depending on the usage
of the building such as the imposed loads that depend on what the ﬂoor areas are used for.
Additionally, only the primary structural elements are modelled, meaning that other parts are
not given by the gravity load of the structure and have to determined.
As part of the plug-in implemented for this thesis procedures are implemented for 1) determining
wind loads acting on a structure and 2) applying loads on the structure from a load plan. These
procedures are described below. The way dead loads and imposed loads are handled in the
model is through load plans imported from an Excel spreadsheet containing information about
loads acting on diﬀerent areas and values of the dead and live loads. This load library is loaded
into Grasshopper and connected to the assembly of the ﬁnite element model.
The wind loads designed by the developed components are calculated according to Eurocode 1
part 1-4 [20]. In the Eurocode the wind loads acting on a structure are only described for a few
regular building shapes. This is also what the implementation of the wind load design in this
project does and may only be reliable when designed with these simple geometries.

In Karamba there is no in-built method to create load combinations, which means that only load
cases can be created. There is two diﬀerent approaches that can be taken to solve this problem.
The ﬁrst approach is to deﬁne the load combinations as load cases. This way the design loads
with partial coeﬃcients are calculated before the analysis. The other approach is to analyze the
load cases and use superposition of the results for the load cases to get total the displacements
and stresses for each load combination.
The advantage of the ﬁrst approach is that it also works for nonlinear analysis where superpo-
sition of load cases cannot be applied. Additionally, it works well with optimization algorithm’s
from Karamba, which optimizes based on the input load cases, which in this case are the load
combinations. The advantage of the other approach is that results from diﬀerent types of loads
can be distinguished. This is e.g. required for the calculation of long-term deﬂections of timber
elements.
In this thesis the ﬁrst approach is taken. Two components have been implemented to do this.
The ﬁrst component generates load combinations for the Ultimate Limit State (ULS) and the
Serviceability Limit State (SLS) based on Eurocode 0 [17].

4.3.
PREPARATION FOR FINITE ELEMENT ANALYSIS
4.3
Preparation for Finite Element Analysis
Elements
For translation from the geometric model to the FE model the elements are distributed into
diﬀerent sets (beam sets and shell sets). Thus lines and surfaces are assigned to diﬀerent sets
depending on their location and direction, e.g. so that diﬀerent types of beam and columns can
be distinguished. Diﬀerent sets are created to take diﬀerent releases and loads on elements into
account in addition to the diﬀerent cross sections.
In Karamba two main types of elements can be modelled; beams and shells. Beam elements can
be created by choosing the primary inputs consisting of a line, an id and a cross section. The
lines from the geometric model are sorted by the type assigned from the beam sets. Similarly,
a shell can be created by choosing a mesh, an id and a cross section. The surfaces from the
geometric model are sorted by type, so that ﬂoors and walls get assigned diﬀerent cross sections.
Joints
In addition to the creation of elements the connections between them have to be determined.
This thesis considers the design of frame structures, which can be classiﬁed into rigid frames
and braced frames. Both of these types of frames will be analyzed in the thesis.
The stiﬀness of the rigid frame is provided mainly by the bending rigidity of beams and columns
that have rigid connections. When rigidly connected, a joint has to transfer three section forces
(one axial force, two shear forces) and three moments (one torsional and two bending moments).
Depending on the type of material such full connections are sometimes (e.g. for wood) hard to
achieve, costly and bulky. A solution to this problem consists in introducing hinges and use a
braced frame. Braced frames are composed of beams and columns that are hinged with bracing
to resist lateral loads. The resistance to lateral forces in obtained through both horizontal and
vertical bracing so that columns can be designed as pure compression members. In a braced
frame beams and columns carry vertical loads, and the bracing system carries the lateral loads.
The hinges are created by setting the stiﬀness of the degree of freedom to zero. The diﬀerent
beam identiﬁers created are used to deﬁne diﬀerent hinges for diﬀerent types of bar elements.
Beam releases are designed using one of two systems, either ﬁxed or simply supported, in an
attempt to design either a rigid frame or a braced frame. Using the ﬁxed support approach
no releases are deﬁned. On the other hand, using the simple support approach all elements
will be designed to be equivalent to simply supported. However, diﬀerent releases are designed
depending on the location and type of beam element.
The design rules for hinges are designed taking stability into account. A ﬁnite element analysis
requires that every node must be stable in all 6 degrees of freedom. Adding too many hinges at
a node may result in mechanisms that must be addressed.
4.3.1
Apply Loads
In this thesis all loads are applied as mesh loads acting on surfaces of the structure. The mesh
loads are then distributed to groups of elements speciﬁed on which equivalent loads shall be
generated. By deﬁning diﬀerent ids for the elements, it can be chosen which type of elements
to apply the loads to.
The procedure for calculating nodal loads and uniformly distributed
beam loads from surface loads consists of the following steps: First Karamba3D calculates the
resultant load on each face of the given mesh. Then the resultant load of each face gets evenly
distributed among its vertices.

DESIGN OF STRUCTURAL MEMBERS
4.4
Design of Structural Members
The design of structural members in diﬀerent materials follows diﬀerent design procedures since
each material is designed according to a diﬀerent Eurocode. In this section the design procedures
and assumptions used for all three materials are described. The main focus in this thesis is the
design of beam elements.
4.4.1
Beam Elements
For structural design of beams it is necessary to know their buckling length. Karamba3D has
an algorithm to approximate it. Starting from the endpoints of an element, proceeding to its
neighbors, it is tracked when a node connects to more than two elements. When that happens it
is determined as as endpoint of an element. The total length between the two endpoint is then
used as the buckling length of the element. In case of a free end the buckling length is doubled.
For cases of system buckling, which would be the case for a rigid frame, this approximation does
not lie on the safe side [29].
Steel
The calculation of the utilization of steel beams is made using Karamba. The procedure for
calculating utilization [30] is in accordance with Eurocode 3 [22] taking buckling and lateral
torsional buckling into account. An elements lateral torsional buckling length is calculated in
the same way as for conventional buckling. The superposition of diﬀerent cross section forces
works according to procedure 2 (see annex B of EC 3). For lateral torsional buckling the elastic
critical moment is calculated according to the procedure from NCCI: Elastic critical moment
for lateral torsional buckling [31]. Another assumption made using Karamba is that a reduced
eﬀective area is not considered for class 4 cross sections.
In this thesis it us assumed that every cross section acts as a class 3 cross section, thus calculating
the design resistance and forces elastically. This is on the safe side for class 1 and 2 cross section,
but on the unsafe side for class 4 cross sections, since the reduced eﬀective area is not taken into
account.
The performance of Karamba for structural steel design has been extensively researched by
Mäenpää in his master thesis[24].
In the thesis it has been studied how accurate Karamba
calculates utilization of steel beams by comparing the results to another structural analysis
software. It has been assessed that Karamba is capable to design steel structures well, though it
is argued that if factors that uses moment diagrams are used it can cause diﬀerences compared
to more accurate design, which is the case when the critical design criteria is lateral torsional
buckling.
Timber
The utilization of timber beams is calculated using the Beaver plug-in in accordance with Eu-
rocode 5[23]. Material properties, along with geometric properties such as cross section geometry
and buckling lengths must be provided, which can either be retrieved from Karamba. The eval-
uation of the nominal strength, modiﬁcation coeﬃcients for load duration, service class and
material uncertainties are considered [13].

DESIGN OF STRUCTURAL MEMBERS
Reinforced Concrete
Currently, no plug-in exists for the structural veriﬁcation of reinforced concrete (RC) beams
and columns. As part of the toolbox developed in this an algorithm is implemented for the
design of a beam and another for the design of a column according to Eurocode 2[21] and based
on methods from Betonkonstruktioner efter DS/EN 1992-1-1[32]. Calculating the utilization of
a RC cross section is a more complex task than a steel or timber cross section. Since it is a
composite material the design depends on both the size and cross section and the amount of
reinforcement. This means that many design options can be viable and an optimal cross section
for a given load is harder to deﬁne.
4.4.2
Shell Elements
In this project shell elements are used for the design of slabs and for shear walls. In Karamba
shell elements can only be connected to beam elements through the nodes in the model. If a
single element is used for each beam in the model, the shell will consequently not be connected
to the beams as shown in Figure 4.13. This means that beam elements must be designed with
nodes corresponding to the edges of the mesh for the shell to be connected to a beam. Another
issue is modelling shell joints. Currently, only nodal joints can be created in Karamba making
it very diﬃcult to create shell hinges and thereby diﬃcult to create one-way span and single
span layouts. However, line joints are currently a work in progress in Karamba and will be part
of the next version of the plug-in [33]. Since it is not desired to split the beams into many
smaller segments and because of the lack of line joints, a diﬀerent approach is taken to model
slabs. The two diﬀerent functions of the slabs, namely transferring horizontal loads through
membrane action and vertical loads through plate action, are created in diﬀerent ways in the
framework. The slabs are modelled as membranes with a very high stiﬀness and no mass for
the sole purpose of transferring horizontal loads to the vertical stability members. Using this
approach all loads acting on the slabs including the self-weight are instead modelled as mesh
loads transferred directly to other load carrying members. To determine the self-weight of the
slabs approximate calculations are used.

4.5
Cross Section Optimization
In a conventional design the process of determining a good cross section size is comprised of
preliminary sizing based on rules of thumb followed by veriﬁcation of the member. This is an
iterative process where the cross sections may have to be changed manually many times.
In a parametric environment this procedure can be automated. For steel cross sections Karamba
has an Optimize Cross Section component that automatically selects the most appropriate cross
section taking the load bearing capacity of a family of cross sections into account according to
Eurocode 3. The component determines the cross section of each element in such a way that
their load bearing capacity is suﬃcient for all load cases. In statically indeterminate structures
the section forces depend on the stiﬀness of the members, which necessitates an iterative process.

The procedure to is similar to the procedure from Karamba, but more naïve. For this procedure
to work the cross sections must be ordered from the weakest to the strongest cross section. The
cross sections are optimized by two conditions, namely the utilization U and displacement u of
the elements. For each element it is checked whether U is above or below a target utilization
Ugoal. Additionally it is checked whether the displacement is above or below a ratio between
the span of the element L and a limiting value vlim. If either criteria is not satisﬁed the next
(larger) cross section in the list is chosen. On the other hand, if U and u are below Ugoal and
vlim a smaller cross section will be chosen.

The limiting value depends on the material and element type, as there can be diﬀerent re-

depend on what is agreed on in a speciﬁc project and are thus set as input parameters. Examples

The procedure is not guaranteed to converge towards a solution. One reason is that the elements
aﬀect the loading of the structure and changing the cross section changes the gravity load on
the model. Additionally, the cross section of an element will switch back and forth between the
two sizes closest to Ugoal. If one of these values is outside the tolerable error level the structure
may have elements oscillate between two cross sections and not ﬁnd a suitable solution if the
utilization of the elements are not in sync, i.e. some elements will have an unsatisfying cross
section for an iteration, while some other elements will for the next iteration. Therefore, a limit
for the maximum number of iterations is set. This should be set at least high enough such that
each element can get through the list of cross sections, since the cross section can only change
to the next in the list for each iteration.
This approach, of course, has its drawbacks. In the approach using the Karamba optimization
component the entire optimization loop is contained inside a single component and is a lot
more eﬃcient for several reasons. In addition to probably needing more iterations, the second
approach can be problematic because the entire script is run again for each iteration. It is much
easier to avoid unexpected errors when only running the loop inside a single component designed
for that purpose instead of running a loop over the entire script which can result in unpredictable
behaviour.
Another issue is that it is one dimensional. It does not know which capacity check is exceeded,
only if it is exceeded or not. This is ﬁne for steel cross sections since they typically come in
increasing sizes where the next cross section is always stronger than the previous. However, for
timber and concrete structures, where a rectangular cross section is used, the cross section can
either be strengthened by making it taller or wider. Depending on the cross section forces acting
on a member it might be more beneﬁcial to either change the height or the width. Since this
is not possible one has to ﬁx a dimension of the cross section. This can either be by selecting
a ﬁxed dimension in one direction or by ﬁxing it relative to the other dimension for a constant
height to width ratio. This is done in this project where a ﬁxed height to width ratio is used for
timber beams.

Alternatively, an optimization algorithm that takes both dimensions into account can be used.
This can be achieved by looking at the utilization in diﬀerent directions and strengthen the
direction utilized the most. If a beam is aﬀected by a moment about the y-axis of the beam
it makes most sense to add height to the beam and vice versa if the moment is in the other
direction. This way, if the utilization in one direction is larger, this direction is chosen and has
its size increased.
4.6
Comparison of Models
The motivation of this project is to be able to compare structural models in diﬀerent materials
in the early design. Until now the workﬂow has explained how to automate the design of a
single structure. With that set-up only one model can be created at a time. It is conceivable
that at some point the operator will stop the design process of ﬁnding the optimal model. It
could be due to bias for a preferred material, time restraints or simply that more unusual design
proposals are not even considered at all. In this section it will be explained how to set up a
framework where diﬀerent models can be created automatically. Thereby, a more informed and
evidence-based decision can be made based on the analysis of a much larger solution space.
Diﬀerent approaches to ﬁnding the best model can be taken. Two diﬀerent methods have been
considered in this project. One approach is to use an optimization algorithm to ﬁnd the best
solution for each material. Grasshopper has a native tool for optimization called Galapagos
Evolutionary Solver, which utilizes a genetic algorithm as described by Mitchell [36].
However, the algorithm works like a black box where only the ﬁnal result is shown. Thus, the
solution does not say anything about how diﬀerent solutions compare. Therefore, it is uncertain
what would be the consequence of changing a value of a part of the genome, e.g. changing
the grid dimensions. Consequently, one may look at the solution and think that it is the best
possible solution without considering other solutions that might be better solutions given slightly
diﬀerent selection criteria.
Alternatively, one can cycle through all combinations of values and save the result of each
model. This approach means that all the data is available and the selection process takes place
subsequently. Thereby, one can change the criteria with which the models are evaluated and end
up with a new best model. An issue with this approach can be the vast amount of combinations
to look through both computationally and for the later analysis. Therefore, it is important to
limit the design space such that the amount of data is ﬁtting.
This approach is used in the project. A ﬂowchart of this set-up is shown in Figure 4.17. The
ﬂowchart is an expansion of the Anemone ﬂowchart where the Ladybug ﬂy component is used
to iterate through input sliders in Grasshopper.

COMPARISON OF MODELS
Another question is what criteria the models are compared based on. In this project a simple
criterion is used; the total amount of material for each of the three considered primary structural
materials. Using this criterion all mass of a material is lumped together, e.g. whether it is
reinforcement steel or structural steel.
The resulting mass of each model can then be used for analyses based on diﬀerent metrics.
In this thesis analyses will be carried out based on the cost and the global warming potential
(GWP) of the structure. These can simply be achieved by multiplying the mass of the structure
with a factor for each of the materials, either a factor for the cost or GWP per unit of a material.

5.3
Summary
In this chapter the design of the primary structural elements used in the project has been eval-
uated. Karamba is a well tried tool used in many real construction projects. It’s reliability has
further been conﬁrmed in this chapter. Beaver is a tool recently developed, yet the results show
that it performs well for the ULS veriﬁcation of timber cross sections. This is supported by a case
study by Pini [8]. At the moment, no plug-in exists for the design of reinforced concrete cross
sections. The implementation of components for reinforced concrete beam and column veriﬁ-
cation have been validated by hand calculations, but further testing is needed for more reliability.
The design of slabs in the framework are based on very rough estimates from diﬀerent sources
[2][34]. The approximate thicknesses based on these sources have been compared to hand cal-
culations, where it is found that the deﬂection of a slab that determines its thickness. For both
RC and CLT slabs it was found that the estimates are more on the safe side for longer spans.

Discussion
When developing a tool you want to make it ﬂexible, precise and user friendly. However, these
criteria are often contradictory, which means that one has weight each of them to ﬁnd a balance.
The discussion of this thesis will address how these criteria can be used to evaluate the developed
framework. They are chosen from a compilation of criteria to be used for assessment of a model
in a review by Negendahl. In the review he discusses diﬀerent modelling methods and their
integration into the early design phases [1].
Both architects and engineers tend to favour tools that provide a user-friendly graphical inter-
face [1]. The developed framework runs in the Grasshopper environment, requiring that the user
understands the concepts behind a visual programming language (VPL). The use of VPL’s are
more common among architects than engineers, which is unfortunate, since the intended user of
the tool is a structural engineer. The reason is that it has lots of settings that change the struc-
tural behaviour of the model, which means that the user must have a moderate understanding
of this topic. In the future structural engineers might be more inclined towards VPL or we may
see architects with a solid structural comprehension; a hybrid practitioner, who acts as both an
architect and an engineer [1].
For an experienced Grasshopper user the developed tool can be seen as very user friendly while
at the same time very customizable since the developed components are designed as a plug-in
intended to work with other third party plug-ins in the Grasshopper environment. Another
option for the more skilled Python programmer is to add their own custom extensions to the
tools.
The tool is designed to be generic to have the ﬂexibility to design structures with diﬀerent
materials and layouts. The ﬂexibility comes at the price of lots of input options which is not
very user friendly. Default values have been created to mitigate this issue, but this can also be
problematic because the user might not examine what diﬀerent options do, even though it may
be of great importance.
The ﬂexibility also comes with at a cost for precision. To a certain extent it might be viable
to extent the framework to creating additional components and procedures for more detailed
design, but it is near impossible to achieve the level of customization that a manual design in a
dedicated FEA software is able to achieve. Even in that case, one has to reﬂect if it has then
lost its original purpose, namely being a quick and ﬂexible tool. The developed tools of this
project have been developed in the scope of early design phases, where a high level of detailing
is not needed.
Another thing to consider is calculation time. In the project it is opted for both a fast calculation
and modeling time. The precision of the calculations are inversely correlated to the calculation

later stages but engineers are often forced to use them at this stage even though it may be really
time consuming because no good alternative exists (or one that they know of). It might also be
that they are unable to invest the time required to get acquainted with a new tool. Structural
engineers tend to be conservative, not only in regards to the veriﬁcation of structures, but also
with new tools. Why change something if the current method works?
8.1
Future Work
The framework and tools presented in this thesis do not attempt to show that it is a ﬁnished
product; rather it tries to showcase the possibilities enabled by the approach.
Some of the ﬂaws of the developed framework is that only considers utilizations and deﬂections.
Requirements that are important for the design of one or more of the materials, such as the design
of connections for timber or the calculation of crack widths for concrete, are not considered in
the framework. This would add complexity to the material-independent approach and may be
more suited for the design of a single material. An additional plug-in could be created for each
of these material speciﬁc requirements and be used with the tools developed in this projects.
One of the biggest strengths of the framework is its ability to quickly generate multiple design
options. In the implementation it has been seen that it can create problems to go against the
natural ﬂow in Grasshopper by using loops. A better solution would be to internalize the loops
inside components such as the Optimize Cross Sections from Karamba, with the obvious solution
being to make it as an extension for this. This would require a full comprehension of how the
plug-in is coded, but fortunately a scripting guide to Karamba exists [41].
When generating many designs one may run into calculation time issues. If it takes 30 second to
generate a design, generating a 1000 takes many hours and may restrict the quick design process.
A solution to this is to use parallel computing, where the data can be split into many streams.
The amount of streams could potentially be as many as the number of designs generated, but
not more, since the optimization of the individual structure is an iterative process with each
iteration dependent on the previous one. It might not be a good to create that many streams,
since it also takes time to set up the script handling the diﬀerent streams. Thus, it is once again
a balance between saving time of modeling vs calculation. An obvious split would be one for
each material which with three materials, potentially could make the calculation three times as
eﬃcient.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
The primary aims of the project has been to develop a tool to aid the cooperation between
architects and structural engineers and provide support for comparison of diﬀerent design options
independent of material in the early design phases. The tool has been devised to be both as
ﬂexible and user friendly as possible.
In the project an integrated dynamic model has been created by combining geometric and cal-
culation models through a visual programming environment. The use of an integrated dynamic
model enables a wide and customizable range of visual feedback for the user of the tool, while
values describing the structural performance are simultaneously available. Aimed at the early
design stages, this allows to perform structural tasks in an environment that increasingly more
designers are familiar with.
The use of an integrated dynamic model also provides support
for the multiple iterations a design goes through at the early design by providing an almost
instantaneous feedback to design changes.
The development undertaken in this project has resulted in a successful implementation of a
tool that can design a structure independent of material choices. The developed framework
considers the diﬀerent structural design processes that exist for each of the three examined
materials, namely steel, reinforced concrete and timber and designs the structure accordingly.
Furthermore, automatic generation of multiple models is implemented in the framework. Ulti-
mately, the frameworks supports the ambition of a structural design process with a larger solution
space, where solutions can be chosen by a combination of design and structural performance.
In the study the performance of the tool has been evaluated by comparison to structural models
from real projects through case studies. It has been found that the tool is capable of generating
multi-material models and optimize the cross section sizes of the structural members. In one
case study it has been found that the developed framework resulted in a model with a reduction
of between 29% and 45% compared to the baseline model when measuring the total mass of
the structural material considering diﬀerent maximum utilization ratios. However, since the
tool is developed for such a wide application, it is uncertain how it performs on structures very
diﬀerent from the ones examined in the thesis. Therefore, it is concluded that more testing
of the tool should be carried out on models with various shapes, dimensions and materials for
further veriﬁcation.

Interview 
Del 1: 
Konstruktionsdesign af bygninger 
Hvordan designes det statiske system? 
1. Hvilke ting skal der tages højde for og hvad er vigtigst? (f.eks. arkitektens ideer, installationer, pris) 
De nævnte emner prioriteret i den listede rækkefølge, og desuden bygherrens eventuelle krav og 
ønsker som første prioritet sammen med arkitektens ideer, undergrundens beskaffenhed (pæle, 
brøndfundering eller direkte fundering), og CO₂-aftryk/bæredygtighed som desværre nok for det 
meste prioriteres under pris, og tidsplan (f.eks. ift. insitubeton kontra betonelement). 
 
2. Hvordan vælges et grid? 
Det er for det meste bundet op på facadeudtryk, så typisk er det arkitekten, men kan også være 
forankret i hensigtsmæssige spændvidder på det valgte statiske system. 
 
3. Hvordan vælges det stabiliserende system? 
Det er en integreret del af svaret på spørgsmål 1 
 
4. Hvilke forskelle er der mellem design af bygninger i en og flere etager? 
Det er mest et spørgsmål om lastens størrelse på bygningsdelene i bunden, og så kan de lave 
bygninger nogen gangen henføres til konstruktionsklasse KK1 (uden krav om certificeret statiker), 
og ved bygninger over 5 etager skal den statiske behandling udføres iht KK3 eller KK4, hvor KK4 er 
med tredjeparts kontrol. 
Og ved høje slanke bygninger er det nødvendigt at eftervise bygningen vha FEM-programmer, for 
at håndtere konstruktionens stivhed. Ved almindelige bygninger i KK2 er det sjældent nødvendigt 
eller hensigtsmæssigt, medmindre der er tale om en bygning med et bærende system i stål. 
 
5. Hvilke forskelle er der mellem design i beton, stål og træ? 
Det er vidt forskellige verdener, beton er typisk skivebygninger, stål er typisk stangsystemer med 
bjælker søjler og afstivende afkrydsning, træ kan være begge dele men typisk med kortere 
spændvidder (undtaget limtræsrammer med stor nyttehøjde) 
 
Hvordan evalueres konstruktionen i starten af et projekt? 
6. Hvordan verificeres elementer i SLS og ULS? 
Spørgsmålet ikke forstået, det skal holde brudmæssigt og overholde gængse deformationskrav – 
generelt dimensioneres typisk med lidt reserve i de indledende faser.  
 
a. Hvilke kriterier er de vigtigste? 
Prisen er ofte bestemmende, så oftest leveres desuden et anlægsoverslag på 
byggeudgifterne. ULS skal selvfølgelig altid være overholdt, men der skal også gerne være 
styr på deformationer og revnevidder 
 
7. Hvornår benyttes hhv. regneark, FEM-modellering eller andre redskaber? 
Det er ret individuelt efter hvilke programmer den enkelte er vandt til at bruge, men grundreglen er 
'keep it simpel' i de indledende faser. 
==PAGE:122==
 
8. Bruges der typisk forskellige modeller til lastnedføring og til stabilitet? 
Det er ikke ualmindeligt, men dog uhensigtsmæssigt mht de stabiliserende vægge.  
Der er dog som regel ikke stabiliserende vægge og søjler, hvor der i sagens natur kun er behov for 
lodret lastnedføring 
  
9. Hvad er en god udnyttelsesgrad? 
ULS på 80-90 % i det færdige bygværk synes jeg er et godt design, i de indledende faser dog helst 
ikke over 70-80%. Det forudsætter en er sikkerhed for, at de påførte laster er retvisende for resten 
af resten af bygningens levetid.  
Det kan dog godt være bæredygtigt at overdimensionere bygningen, for at tage højde for ukendt 
fremtidig anvendelse, så bygningen kan "genbruges" til andre formål.   
SLS på 90-99% i det færdige bygværk synes jeg er et godt design – det er oftest dimensionsgivende, 
og kan nogen gange give ULS udnyttelsesgrader på ned til 50-60%, og det er bare ærgerligt. 
 
10. Hvad bliver den bestemt af? (guidelines, regler eller andet) 
Belastning, styrke, partialkoefficienter, og grænseværdier for deformationer, alt sammen defineret 
i normerne, inklusive dynamiske svingninger som også er normbegrænset. 
Det kan også være bygherre (eller bygherrerådgiver) har skærpede krav.

Del 2: 
Case Study, EAT 
1. Hvad skal bygningen bruges til? 
Det er et industrikøkken til Københavns Kommune, der skal kunne producere 12.000 kuverter om 
dagen, til skoler og institutioner. 
  
2. Hvilken type konstruktion er valgt? (materialer, statisk virkemåde) 
Arkitekt og projektleder valgte en let bygning med taghældning – jeg havde først tænkt det som en 
traditionel betonkonstruktion med fladt tag. 
Så det blev en let bygning i stål, med stabiliserende tværgående rammer, á la sportshal/grisestald. 
Bygherren havde ingen styrende ambitioner om en mere bæredygtig træbygning, hvilket nok heller 
ikke ville være hensigtsmæssigt i en rent-rums-bygning.  
Det statiske system er valgt sådan, for at minimere søjleantallet i bygningen, og eliminere 
stabiliserende afkrydsning indvendigt i bygningen.  
Spædretning af de lette facade- og tagpartier på langs af bygningen er styret af de langsgående 
ovenlysbånd. 
Det er deformationskrav til rammerne der har været styrende for ståldimensionerne i rammerne.

mindre end to dage på at definere laster og sætte forskellige mulige scenarier op mellem

2. ændring fra to til en etage var efter færdigt design med beskrivelse, tegninger, statisk
