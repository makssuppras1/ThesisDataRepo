ABSTRACT  
  
Car sharing is one of the most promising instance of the emerging sharing economy. As such, businesses 
and individuals are developing different models for car sharing. Meanwhile, the current transportation 
sector is under pressure. This due to the higher demand for personal mobility, the changing climate 
and the increasing urbanisation. This thesis considers the future of car sharing in Denmark in relation 
to the current transportation modes by analysing the development of different types of car sharing 
organizations, the transportation system, and related policy. Semi-structured interviews were 
conducted with different car sharing organizations active in Denmark and a Danish congressman. For 
the analysation and discussion of the findings regarding the position and potential of car sharing, the 
multi-level perspective is applied. While for the outlining of the unique characteristics of each car 
sharing operator, the building blocks of the business model canvas were used. The results indicate, 
regarding the multi-level perspective, that the concept car sharing has not found a momentum as such, 
meaning every car sharing operator represents its own niche innovation. Having different motivations 
for participating in the car sharing businesses assure differences varying from stimulating and 
educating about the sharing economy to just make money. Both round-trip and free-floating practises 
are gaining ground and this is expected to remain growing. Important for car sharing businesses to 
expand and possibly be disruptive to the current transportation sector, is to find supportive actors in 
the socio-technical regime. At this moment, local policymakers are convinced of round-trip car sharing 
but not yet free-floating car sharing. On the other hand, national policy makers are adopting a wait-
and-see approach; not intervening in the car sharing until necessary and speculating that self-driving 
cars may disrupt the system. Whereas car manufacturers and rentals are gradually getting into car 
sharing, and the public transportation is a slow operating bureaucracy. Meanwhile, the landscape 
pressures come from climate change and sharing economy, both creating more opportunities for new 
car sharing operators. What the future holds for car sharing in Denmark is quite uncertain; car sharing 
is upcoming and the supply is extensive. Even though, there is no momentum yet, this does not mean 
car sharing is not able to disrupt the transportation sector. However, car sharing does need a socio-
technical regime actor to embrace the concept so it can make the step to become disruptive to the 
Danish transportation sector. Nevertheless, car sharing is here to stay and will interfere and ensure 
their position within the transportation system sooner rather than later.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 RESEARCH ENVIRONMENT 
Sharing goods and services, the so-called sharing economy, has been growing over the past years. At 
the same time, the demand for smarter mobility is growing as well. The sharing economy model linked 
to mobility arose from dissatisfaction with the available transportation as well as the environmental 
burden it comprises. Car sharing therefore is one of the bigger ‘products’ created by the evolving 
sharing economy. Within the last years, the car sharing market has grown exponentially. Car sharing 
can be defined as: ‘the enabling of, short-term as well as long-term, access to car-based mobility 
without the user owning the vehicle (Le Vine, Zolfaghari & Polak, 2014). The general perception of 
car sharing is that it is convenient, economically beneficial, environmental friendly and, has a high 
service level. As more and more people become aware of the effects their lifestyle has on their 
surrounding environment, consumer behaviour changes alongside, however still rather slow. Despite 
this, the car sharing practises keep on coming and research is praising.  
If these are the general features of car sharing, why is car sharing not the main way of transportation 
these days and what will be the future perspective? By now, the benefits of car sharing have been 
investigated for several years and the same goes for consumer behaviour regarding their (potential) 
participation. If car sharing is as promising as it seems, is there a business model that is the right fit 
for the future or is there a need for something else? 
1.2 RESEARCH OBJECTIVE 
The main research objective of this thesis is to gain insights into the future development of car sharing 
practises in Denmark, influenced by several entrepreneurial initiatives, existing organisations as a 
niche and the socio-technical regime and landscape. A greater focus is placed on the definition of car 
sharing as a niche, by assessing the business and operational models, the positioning of car sharing 
within mobility and the indication of the necessary properties for future expansion of car sharing in 
Denmark. This ultimately presents future scenarios for the development of car sharing in Denmark. To 
accomplish this research objective, the Business Model Canvas building blocks (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 
2010) have been used as well as the multi-level perspective on transitions by Geels (2007). This defines 
the characteristics of a business and positions the businesses within the current environment, 
respectively. Interviews with six car sharing operators in Denmark and a congressman from the Danish 
parliament, were carried out to determine the position and transition car sharing is subject to.

To begin with, an extended literature review is carried out to gain insights into the environment and

and future development, including recommendations for practices as well as regime actors.

2 LITERATURE REVIEW 
Several studies have been conducted regarding car sharing practises, varying from the opportunities 
and benefits car sharing comprises for cities and environmental, to the determination of customers and 
their behaviour. What car sharing has to offer seems clear, however it has not become mainstream yet. 
Therefore, the focus in this thesis is on the position and developments regarding car sharing in 
Denmark and this literature review covers the landscape and regime developments, including research 
about opportunities sharing economy, transition of the transportation system, business models, users, 
policy and future trends. 
2.1 SHARING ECONOMY 
The increasing interest in the sharing economy and therefore car sharing can be accredited to the 
exponential growth and innovation within digital technology (Roh, 2016; Csonka & Csiszár, 2016) 
and the increasing importance of the environment (Geels, 2012). Arising sharing concepts have a 
benefit for the environment in the sense that fewer resources will be used, because less objects must 
be produced (PwC, 2014). Due to these two aspects, the opportunities for the sharing economy keep 
on growing (Frenken & Schor, 2017; Hasan & Birgach, 2016). In 2016, Martin assigned the catalysing 
sharing concept to the success stories from Silicon Valley of Airbnb and Uber. These successes have 
assembled this new participatory concept and brought media, ICT, industries, venture capitalists and 
entrepreneurs together. Businesses participating in the sharing economy are subject to a fast-changing 
market. The sharing economy is a disruptive business model innovation driven by digital technologies, 
as well as a major commercial opportunity for entrepreneurs, companies, industries and countries 
(Martin, 2016).  
The size of the current sharing market is uncertain. However, for 2025 PWC (2014) estimated that the 
five key assets of the sharing economy could be held accountable for 2.23 trillion Danish kroner of the 
revenue worldwide, with car sharing as one of the five key contributors. To put future car sharing into 
perspective, it should be determined how the sharing economy will evolve. The sharing economy has 
been defined as both a “neoliberalism on steroids” mentioned by Morozov (2013, para 3) and as a 
remedy for a hyper-consumerist culture (Richardson, 2015). The contrasting definition shows the 
strong interrelatedness of economy and culture, which can be attributed to the necessity of consumer 
and/or user participation. This duality can also emphasise the need for government reaction and 
political shift (Martin, 2016). Therefore, challenges arise from several underlying aspects of the car 
sharing future (Dowling & Kent, 2015). 
As the term sharing economy is used frequently but inconsistently, clarification of the concept is 
necessary, as it is a floating signifier for a diverse range of activities (Richardson, 2015). For the sake 
of this thesis, some definitions will be combined to have room for future development with the eye on 
the car sharing practise. In a recent study (Frenken & Schor, 2017) a determination of the sharing 
economy and related forms of platform economy is expounded. Per Frenken and Schor (2017) a 
sharing economy is defined as; ‘consumers granting each other temporary access to under-utilized 
physical assets (“idle capacity”), possibly for money’. Three key elements within the sharing economy 
are; peer-to-peer interaction, temporary access and physical goods. In addition to this, Roh (2016) 
found that the sharing economy mode can be divided into three main types: product service systems, 
redistribution markets and collaborative lifestyles. Combining these two types describing a sharing 
==PAGE:10==

economy allows this thesis to consider the evolvement of car sharing practices without excluding future 
combinations within the overall sharing concept. Car sharing belongs in the category product service 
system and within the category different types can be distinguished.   
In Denmark, the population that took part in any aspect of the sharing economy measured 2014 and 
2015 has tripled. Where in 2014 only 2% used someone’s object, in 2015 this increased to 6%. The 
percentage of people sharing their objects with others arose from 1% to 3% (Nordea, 2016). There is 
a percentage of Danes that do not take part in the sharing economy having varying reasons. The biggest 
group is not willing to share their own objects with strangers 48%, there is 15% who fear uncertainties 
the legal responsibility, 5% says it is too much effort to participate, 7% does not give a reason for their 
non-participation. The remaining 25% of has different reasons for not participating but are not 
incorporated in the research conducted by Nordea (2016). This means that the opportunities for the 
growing sharing economy rise, however the population of Denmark is not yet convinced about 
participation in the sharing economy. At the same time, policymakers have difficulties grasping the 
variety of sharing purposes and more and more sharing practises come into existence, therefore it is 
necessary to consider the future of a concept like car sharing.  
2.2 TRANSITION OF THE TRANSPORTATION SYSTEM 
Where sharing economy concepts play part in reducing the extraction of resources (PwC, 2014), the 
current transportation system is built on the extraction of fossils and contributes significantly to CO2 
emissions and the rise thereof (Geels, 2012). About a quarter of the CO2 emissions can be attributed 
to the global transportation sector, where 75% is due to car and truck emissions (International Energy 
Agency, 2009). Statistics show, that the world transport energy use per road passenger has doubled 
from 1971 till 2006, With a growing worldwide population and corresponding greenhouse gas 
emissions, new challenges have arisen in urban environments, as well as for planning and for 
administration (Ferrero, Perboli, Vesco, Musso & Pacifici, 2015). The transportation system and society 
are intrinsically linked with one another, as via this everything becomes accessible (Ferrero et al., 
2015).  
“The consequences of predominant use of individual vehicles in car-centric cities are well known: 
congestion, noise, higher energy, shortage in parking, inefficient land use, pollution, waste and 
climate changes” (Ferrero et al., 2015, p.1)    
  
Transitions like these, take a long time to evolve and comprise different actors and social groups who 
will need to alter simultaneously (Geels, 2012). All different actors have another focus when looking 
at these rising problems, as liberal economists trust on the market solving the negative externalities, 
in this case the need for transportation and climate change related issues (Morozov, 2013). Whereas 
engineers approach the problem as inefficient transportation system which is highly polluting (Geels, 
2012), and politicians try to comply with the environmental goals set international treaties and many 
other opinions and viewpoint from different parties are recognised. However, the increasing demand 
for mobility in dense areas, with the expectancy of two thirds of the world's’ population living in cities 
(International Energy Agency, 2009), ideally all would pursue an integral solution. The problem is not 
only the infrastructure and its corresponding system, it compiles multiple challenges, actors and 
multiple levels require attention and coordination (Hodson & Marvin, 2010). Loads of examples exist 
of cities try to improve their public transportation services by adding more and more bus, metro and

2.3 CAR SHARING  
Nowadays, there is no contradicting the fact that cities suffer from air pollution, atmospheric haze, 
congestion and parking problems (Qu, Yu & Yu, 2017). Several transport operators have tried to solve 
these problems through restrictions and rules. However, the mobility demand is high and the supply 
has to meet that demand (Qu et al., 2017). Car sharing, in contrast to the traditional way of car usage, 
has the potential to be a part of the solution (Le Vine et al., 2014). The concept of car sharing is simple, 
an individual has the benefits of using a car while avoiding the costs and the responsibilities (Shaheen, 
Sperling & Wagner, 1998). For consumers, there are several motivations to participate in car sharing, 
and the planet profits from more car sharing (Böcker & Meelen, 2017). When consumers get cheaper 
access to a car, they become less dependent on ownership. Therefore, the production of cars declines 
and results in environmental benefits (Frenken & Schor, 2017). The average privately owned car stands

However, research conducted by Csonka and Csizár (2016) noted that car sharing cannot be successful 
when isolated from other transportation modes. 
Future development of car sharing practises can close the gap between public transportation and 
private car ownership disappears, ideally. Wagner, Brandt and Neumann (2015) found that car sharing 
relies on synergies with public transit, where it can provide the additional flexibility the transportation 
sector needs. The future changes within mobility demands are predicted by Földes and Csizár in 2016 
by the usage of flexibility and number of passengers as the most important indicators. As they 
determined that the overall goal is to satisfy the mobility demands in the most personal way. The 
prediction of Földes and Csizár (2016), considers the possibility of (partly) autonomous vehicles and 
the paper includes the emerging telematics-based car sharing. The results show, taken all travellers 
into account, a shrinking group of walkers, the bikers stay the same, but the current drivers, some 
walkers and passengers become one group of passengers in the future. This trend is offsetting already, 
whereas young people between 18 and 29 are less likely to use private motorised transportation modes. 
At the same time, the share in public transportation and non-motorised transport is growing among 
this group (Csonka & Csiszár, 2016). 
At locations where walking, cycling and public transportation are reasonable options, vehicle 
ownership is low and parking spots are vacant, car sharing has been successful (Kent & Dowling, 2013). 
Thus, car sharing is now concentrated in high populated areas where the service and attractiveness of 
public transport is high. Interlinking these findings with the numbers found by Nordea (2016), the 
future in accordance with Földes and Csiszár (2016) seems rather far away for Denmark. However, as 
predicted, everyone will be a passenger eventually conveys the impression of the extinction of 
ownership by individuals. On the other hand, Haustein and Nielsen (2015) considered car sharing 
users in Denmark and categorised them somewhat into households with and without a private car, 
aligned with the fact if they have a car sharing membership.

2.4 ENTREPRENEURS AND BUSINESSES  
Contemporary entrepreneurs use the Silicon Valley successes as a model for creating their own success, 
as Uber and Airbnb both made the transition from start-up towards multi international corporation 
within 5 years (Martin, 2016). It is probable that entrepreneurs will continue to develop the sharing 
economy and turn into sustainable and profitable business. Even though the associated business 
models are not without dispute (Cohen & Kietzmann 2014). For businesses involved in car sharing, it 
Is a necessity to revise their business models to better cater to consumer preferences and the rapid 
changing market (Wagner et al., 2015). These days, car sharing looms as an alternative transportation 
mode, the competition is growing as more and more operators appear. Which increases the motive for 
innovation and differentiation between new businesses (Ferrero et al., 2015). Previous research lacks 
the connection between the differences of car sharing models connected to their business models 
(Remane, Nickerson, Hanels, Tesch & Koble, 2016). For some analysis is it useful to find linkages 
between these two different types of models, however there is an absence of studies linking business 
models of the service to the development and operational models (Ferrero et al., 2015). Remane et al. 
(2016) enabled an adequate classification for car sharing models using the most relevant dimensions 
and characteristics by a taxonomy listing. Which can be the base for the exploration of business models 
interlinked with their car sharing practises.

2.4.1 TAXONOMY OF CAR SHARING MODELS  
Several attempts have been done in order to categorise the variety of car sharing models, all based on 
different characteristics. An extended exploration of different business models regarding car sharing 
has been conducted by Shaheen and Cohen (2013), as the market is growing fast, this categorisation 
missed out several new types that came into existence. Another way of defining the different models 
is done by Cohen and Kietzmann (2014) as they categorized the car sharing business models on 
characteristics based on the established framework called BMfSs, which focuses on sustainability. Most 
of the attempts to define the different practises starts with the division between who is sharing with 
whom, namely these main business models that can be distinguished are (1) Business-to-Consumer 
(B2C) and (2) Peer-to-Peer (P2P). The models can be split into other business models; however, the 
superordinate will be one of the models. Every company makes its own adjustments to their business 
model to meet the users’ needs (Cohen & Kietzmann, 2014). All models usually charge users either for 
the operating time or the distance covered and in some cases, or a combination of those.  
Business-to-consumer, a company positions vehicles throughout a specified area, very often a city. Cars 
can be picked up and used whenever a user wants, therefore the time that the car stands idle is 
constricted to a minimum. However, it is important to note that services like this can either be a free-
floating model, roundtrip model or the station model. Free-floating meaning the used vehicle can be 
left behind on the street, where roundtrip users collect the car and when they are done, they should 
leave it at the point of collection and the station is something in between. Cars can be used for single 
trips but must be parked, by the company, at the designated places. Very often a company organised 
like this, tries to make agreements with governments to deliver the highest service for their users in 
terms of parking and road use (Cohen & Kietzmann, 2014). 
Peer-to-peer, this model relies on online platforms where individual car owners can connect to 
potential drivers which can rent or borrow available cars. Self-evident is that such car use is limited to 
only roundtrips. If P2P sharing is arranged amongst a set group of people, who all own the car partially, 
it is called fractional ownership.

2.6 EMERGING TRENDS  
It is a clear likelihood that the mobility systems will change significantly within the next ten years, as 
McKinsey & Company (2016) pointed out several trends within mobility that can assure this severe 
change. One of the three changes are mentioned to be shared mobility, the other two mentioned are 
electrification and autonomy. Combinations of the trends are insurmountable. The developments 
within the urban areas will heavily effect the changes of mobility systems (McKinsey & Company, 
2016).  
In 2013, Shaheen and Cohen examined four future trends for car sharing which they found emerging, 
the expansion of vehicle sharing can again be allocated to the business models mentioned earlier, B2C 
or P2P. First, large car sharing operators have expanded worldwide and became more mainstream. 
The researchers forecasted successful global expansion with unification and procurement included. 
Therefor growth driven operators will stay market leader of members and vehicles for car sharing. As 
a second trend, rental car companies enter the car sharing market by shortening the minimal rental 
period. In addition to this, auto manufacturers entered the market as well. Not only as a car sharing 
operator but they provide car sharing technology, and they form business partnerships with operators 
as vehicle supplier. The third introduced trend is the increase of one-way car sharing, meaning the 
start point of the journey made with a shared car does not have to be the finish. The fourth trend is 
assigned to a growing private car sharing market, which can be achieved in four different ways, shared 
ownership, traditional P2P, hybrid P2P and P2P platform. Traditional car sharing enables individuals 
to use a private owned car temporarily, while with a hybrid P2P individuals join an organisation that 
consists of private owned cars and organisation owned cars, in scattered locations. A P2P platform 
functions as a marketplace and facilitates the exchange between owners and users. 
These four opposed trends arose from the assumption that car sharing will be a growing business and 
the previously mentioned trends will drive the car sharing market towards a more competitive market. 
New business models will be invented to live up to the rapid changing market of car sharing, Shaheen 
and Cohen (2013) predicted more niche markets to be served by car sharing in 2015. Simultaneously 
to the rapid growth of the car sharing market, more industry cooperation in several fields as insurance, 
parking public policy, data management, technology and alternative fuels shall appear (Shaheen & 
Cohen, 2013; Földes & Csiszár, 2016). For the future development of car sharing practises, the 
emphasis can shift from decreasing the amount of private owned vehicles [x households] by replacing 
them with shared ones to a more specific focus on target audience (Clawson, 2014; Barth & Shaheen, 
2014). Tony Lynch quotes in Forbes ‘car sharing doesn’t work’, and he found a simple explanation to 
endorse this statement (Muller, 2015). People find it difficult to arrange car sharing related practises 
with strangers, such as payment and the trust issues. Therefore, he proposed car sharing to be a 
solution for commuting for example, people to the same company or industrial suburb. According to 
Lynch, it is key to find a common interest in the travel. Addressing essential travels and closed groups 
could make car sharing a bigger success in the future (Muller, 2015). The previously mentioned idea 
for the exploration of car sharing is somewhat affirmed by Martin et al., (2010), as in that research 
found that a possible target group for car sharing can be households owning two cars. The existing 
research focusses mostly on general car sharing practises, the aim is to replace private owned cars by

2.6.1 FUTURE PERSPECTIVE FOR CAR SHARING   
Car sharing must be as flexible as possible to attract and retain users, the challenge that arises is the 
spatial and temporal discrepancy between vehicle supply and demand (Wagner, 2015). In addition to 
this, Csonka and Csiszár (2016) found the success is contingent on the availability of other 
transportation modes. It was determined that there is user potential in Denmark for car sharing in the 
current state (Haustein & Nielsen, 2015). If car sharing is to be a possible alternative transportation 
mode, changes need to occur. Three mechanisms for people changing practices are defined by Kent 
and Dowling (2013), which enable better insight in the rise of popularity of car sharing. Firstly, the 
development of the involved elements fundamental for car sharing, such as materials, meanings and 
competences. Secondly, developments of people already engaged in this practise. Finally, the 
interconnectedness of car sharing with other practises.  
According to the extended literature review of the evolving car sharing practises, several points attract 
attention that need further exploration. More and more car sharing initiatives come into existence, 
either initiated by entrepreneurs or established firms, unfortunately possible users do not seem to 
change their habits simultaneously. Besides, cooperation with other transportation modes could assure 
a bigger success for car sharing practises and the order of disruptiveness car sharing can accomplish 
by the extensive development the mobility service is exposed to due to climate concerns and the 
growing demand of personal mobility.   
The research performed in this thesis explores different car sharing practises and their corresponding 
business models to look into the development and the future of car sharing in Denmark. From a 
comprehensive literature review, the active car sharing businesses in Denmark have been categorised

model canvas building blocks and how this afterwards can be positioned in the multi-level perspective 
on transitions.

3.1 GATHERING THE DATA 
3.1.1 CHOOSING THE METHOD 
In qualitative research, several methods are applicable: namely structured, semi-structured, 
unstructured, and informal interviews as well as focus groups. For this study, semi-structured 
interviews were done to get a complete response and more in-depth information (Harrell & Bradley, 
2009). Interviews allow to react upon answers, to receive the preferred information. In this thesis, 
there is a focus on comparing the cases as well as focus is on gaining insights in each individual case. 
Even though, the thesis contains a small sample size in numbers, a semi-structured interview method 
is most suitable to fulfil the comparison purpose and the individual information.  
The structuring assured the coverage of theoretical questions that were significant in order perform 
the intended analysis, whereas the semi-structured left space for more supplementary questions and 
the exploration of new relevant insights useful for following interviews as not everything was foreseen 
from the beginning. Which left changing the list of questions during and after individual interview, to 
improve the questioning and make the most efficient use of time with the respondent.      
Limitations of conducting semi-structured interviews, most importantly are their social construction 
and therefore every conversation is influenced by the interaction between the interviewer and 
respondent (Pole & Lampard, 2002). Where the research done beforehand determined, on behalf of 
its own preconceived ideas, what relevant questions and answers are (Kvale, 1996).

Before conducting the interviews, an extended literature review has been completed to ask the relevant 
questions for answering the research questions and make the comparison between the different 
companies. To prepare the interviews, the questions were grouped in different themes that were direct 
or indirectly connected to the research questions. Because of the semi-structured interviews, it made 
more sense to ask the questions regarding the path and answers that were guided by the respondents. 
This so that the interviews became more of a conversation which made it easier for the respondent to 
answer freely and engage in the topic and aim of the research.  
As the respondents are originally Danish, whereas the interviewer is Dutch, the interviews were 
conducted in English. Misunderstandings from both parties had to be taken into account, clarification 
of questions was necessary in order to obtain the answer as meant by the interviewer. After every 
interview, the structured questions were somewhat adjusted thus the next interviews were conducted 
with increasing efficiency.

4.1.2 POSITIONING WITHIN THE TRANSPORTATION SYSTEM 
The strategy and thoughts towards other transportation modes have been made clear, where the public 
transportation services are interlinked with the usage of shared cars. LetsGo brought forward that car 
sharing is “an essential partner of public transportation”, this can be assigned to their customer segment. 
The users that utilize the car for non-daily transportation need to go around daily as well. Here arises 
the necessity of good public transportation, bike and pedestrian facilities. LetsGo’s service lends itself 
best for leisure and business trips, as the round-trip model demands a car to be parked at the 
designated parking space, if not the user pays for the whole period that the car stands idle. Therefore, 
car sharing is complementary to public transportation, LetsGo declares that the dependency goes both 
ways, if this is to be the alternative for private owned cars. When there is no attractive alternative to 
buying a car, cars will be bought. To push the usage of shared cars, loyalty programs and integration 
within the public transportation planners is key in order to reach the envisioned customer segment. 
Another important aspect that is brought forward, to compete with private owned cars, is peoples’ 
mind-set. However economic benefit and easy accessibility are two big triggers for people to 
participate.

4.3 SNAPPCAR  
Snappcar is a P2P car sharing company, established in 2011 in the Netherlands by two entrepreneurs. 
Recently it has entered the Danish market due to acquisition of minbildinbil, a former car sharing 
company. Snappcar is a platform that connects people who want to use a car to car owners not using 
their car. The cars rented through Snappcar can be used throughout Europe, while the users are insured 
and everything is taken care of regarding the car use. Currently Snappcar Denmark has around 20000 
renters and 2000 car owners on their platform, Snappcar’s goal is to grow in Copenhagen and 
afterwards enter other cities in Denmark, to prove the concept of P2P car sharing as an international 
trend. Recently Europcar bought 20% in Snappcar, this makes automatic access possible soon.  
4.3.1 BUSINESS MODEL 
The customer segment Snappcar serves is divided into the renters and the car owners, where the 
renters are usually in their twenties and early thirties. Car owners are from their late twenties up to 
the age of fifty. 60% Of the users have a higher educational level and they mostly live in apartment 
buildings. When looking at the data from the Netherlands, where Snappcar has been operating for 5 
years, the age group is more diverse and the amount of people with higher education is 85%.  
The value proposition Snappcar has is is divided into three points. The primary one is the economic 
incentive, offering people a cheaper car rental than the already established rental companies and on 
the side of the car owners it is to earn something extra. On top of that, the socio-economic context is 
of high importance to their service. Snappcar emphasises sharing between peoples instead of amongst 
companies and in this way the inequality between people decreases. The second value proposition is 
the building of a community, the importance of being part of a society and contributing to the sharing 
economy. While Snappcars users share goods, the users meet people they would not meet without 
sharing, this can have a positive influence on the mind-set towards strangers. The third value 
proposition is the environmental benefits that occur while sharing cars.  
The channels in order to reach out to new users and create brand recognition are in the first place 
social media. As a part of their value is to build communities, social media is a channel where actual 
communities can be built. Competitions, blogs, updates and articles related to the sharing economy 
and the environment are posted regularly on their website and in Facebook groups. Another action 
Snappcar Netherlands takes is to facilitate meetings with car owners, the Snappbasidor program, 
where they discuss the perks of being a car owner on the platform and exchange information based on 
their experiences. The way Snappcar generates revenue is by taking 5% of the car owner's side and 
10% of the renters’ side, while the car rental price gets determined by the car owners themselves. 
Community management is the most important key activity, making sure the customers are satisfied 
and have nice experiences. When something happens, it is Snappcar’s duty to take the user by the hand 
and lead the user through the processes, to make sure the user feels like everything is taken care off. 
Apart from offering the car owners a nice experience, the renters are offered helping hand as well. 
Manual matching is the action Snappcar takes to make a car owner aware of the fact there are requests 
awaiting confirmation. It is highly important to make sure possible renters have a nice experience as 
well.  
  
Snappcar mentioned the focus on flexibility, as Snappcar is a platform that arranges transactions

4.5 SPIRI 
Spiri is one of the several concepts that has been rolled out by a group of entrepreneurs, initially they

service, Spiri aim to out rule the city buses as they are known now.  
4.5.1 BUSINESS MODEL 
In order to describe Spiri’s customer segment, it is divided into two phases. For the first stage where

The passenger seats will be occupied by people with higher incomes that now take taxis, use Uber and 
other services. People owning a car for fun and status, will not make use of Spiri. The next stage is 
where Spiri will operate with self-driving micro busses, everyone will be their customer, as nowadays 
most of the private owned cars serve the mundane parts in people's lives. The value proposition is 
cheap mobility for everyone. As Spiri is still in the concept phase, channels are not defined. As revenue 
stream, only the customers that are passengers in the car will pay for their ride. The price of one ride 
will be affiliated to the price customers would likewise pay for a bus ticket.  
The key activities are important for offering the envisioned service Spiri has, in the first place, a 
corridor of stations throughout the city needs to be planned/placed anticipated by mapping the places 
with the highest demands. Secondly, the technology of the cars should be up to date meaning technical 
maintenance and physical maintenance, such as cleaning.   
  
The flexibility Spiri will offer is endless in term of usage as well as economically. In use, when the 
network of Spiri will be connected to personal data, the autonomous vehicle will be smart and know 
exactly when a customer needs a car. The aim is to program and connect personal devices to the car 
service, so the car knows peoples schedule and can seamlessly anticipate on their behaviour.  
For the environment, Spiri it’s autonomous electric micro busses will ideally replace the public 
transportation in cities.

Albertslund delebil is a relatively small cooperative working with a round-trip model, currently owning

4.6.1 BUSINESS MODEL 
The customer segment Albertslund delebil serves are people who do not have access to a car for doing

should do with their new booking system. Albertslund delebil just switched to LetsGo’s booking system

searching for a key box somewhere near the car parking, which can be opened with a universal key all

When it comes to the company’s environmental goals and motivations, mentioned is the fact one 
shared car replaces five to six private owned cars and the offered cars all have a high fuel efficiency. 
Albertslund delebil would like to have half of their fleet electric already, but as the municipality is not 
collaborating with designated parking spots with chargers, it is difficult for them to accomplish this

5.1 NICHES  
To answer the first sub question: How do the car sharing business models relate or differ from each other 
as a niche innovation? Different building blocks from the BMC make clear what the significant 
differences and similarities are between the different CSOs in Denmark and the classification of car 
sharing as a niche will be placed in the MLP.   
  
Value proposition 
Each of the car sharing organisations represent their own niche as they differ in their business model 
building blocks and the way those correlate with each other. Even though nearly all organisations have 
the same value proposition, the customer segments differ. All value propositions have, in essence, that 
they will provide a mobility solution for everyone who is in the need for a car without owning one, 
with the economic incentive as the most important attraction factor (Shaheen et al., 1998; Böcker & 
Meelen, 2017). The environmental aspect is not necessarily part of each value proposition, and neither 
is there an environmental incentive for most of the customers to join car sharing. However, the value 
proposition of the P2P operator is strongly related to the sharing economy concept, and so are their 
customers. By sharing goods, the wealth will be better redistributed and the resources will used more 
effectively (PwC, 2014; Richardson, 2015). Whereas this value has not been mentioned by any other 
CSO than the P2P, which highlights the creation of communities as a value proposition. Their mission 
is not only to get people into car sharing, but get people willing to be part of other concepts within the 
sharing economy to change people’s mind-set towards sharing.  
 
The environmental benefits car sharing can offer, does not have a high importance for all CSOs as this 
was only mentioned by half of the respondents. However, it cannot be defined if this has something to 
do with the way they operate. It is not distinguishable which category of CSOs is more environmentally 
friendly than the other, this can be assigned to the way they came into existence.   
The differences are highly visible between round-trip providers without EVs in their fleet and fleets 
consisting partly of EVs as well as totally. Almost all CSOs mentioned the fact that a shared car possibly 
replaces 5-6 private owned cars, as answer to their environmental meaning, whereas literature often 
mentions a much higher level of replacement (Martin et al., 2010), this can be explained by the vehicle 
member ratio. This ratio for Denmark was 42 in 2015, as defined by Frost & Sullivan. Some conclusions 
can be drawn based on this number, however this is not fair. With the addition that environmental 
concern is not why they attract customers, but it is mentioned as a reason for customers to be loyal to 
car sharing when they have obtained a good experience with it. With this good experience, the 
expectation is they will stay and for future expansion of the car sharing customer base.

Customer segment 
The customer segment the CSOs serve are divided in different age groups, travel behaviour and trip 
purpose, income and educational background and other motivations. The travel behaviour is one 
measure for defining the customer segment. The round-trip operators have customer using their cars 
for leisure, this varying from functional trips to IKEA and such to multiple day trips. The aim for this 
customer segment corresponds to the private car use in Denmark, where the leisure car usage 
represents the biggest group with 36% (Jensen, 1999). The everyday car driver, i.e. for work purpose, 
is the second biggest group with 33%. This group of possible customers gets tackled by the free-floating 
services as well as some round-trip operators. This customer gets attention because of the parking 
issues the daily car user encounters, every day again (Ferrero et al., 2015; Kent & Dowling, 2013; Qu 
et al., 2017).  
  
One round-trip operator sees businesses as potential clients, the collaboration between companies 
using car sharing during working hours will result in more leisure use of shared cars as well by 
employees who got used to the concept. Companies can gain from car sharing services as well, 
particularly economic benefits in purchasing, maintenance and parking licenses and the fact that, 
company cars stand idle after working hours and in the weekend. The free-floating operator aims for 
business clients as well, for the same reasons as mentioned before, however this is for shorter distances 
within the designated area they operate in. When comparing the ages of the users, it is observed that 
free floating car sharing attracts a younger customer base, likewise the P2P, for renting with twenties 
to early thirties, whereas the round-trip operators serve an older customer base around thirty-forty 
years of age. In this age, the car owner participating in P2P belongs to this category as well, this can 
be explained by the intended trip purpose, whether doing day trips or work appointments. The relative 
young users can be explained by the fact they have never owned a car themselves before as well as the 
prioritization of other high cost investments. In Denmark, it is primarily the urban citizen who 
participates in car sharing, but this can be assigned to the supply. Another point to note is the education 
level of the participants, this is middle to higher educated, which can relate to the high density of 
educational institutes in cities as well as to how much more environmentally aware urbanists are. 
Again, this is not the first motive of joining car sharing.    
  
There is an unreachable customer according the CSOs, this is the car user that drives for pleasure i.e. 
which owns an expensive sports car or sees car use and driving as their hobby. This again corresponds 
with the group of 6% car users which is named the passionate car driver (Jensen, 1999). However, in 
the future can customer be persuaded into car sharing with a more varying fleet, with a so called 
premium car collection which is recently applied by one of the round-trip operators, that is owned by 
a car manufacturer. The P2P service can theoretically serve this segment as well, if car owners like to 
share their extraordinary cars. However, the numbers on sharing economy participants in Denmark 
(Nordea, 2016) are not at a preferred level yet as 48% of the people are not willing to share their own 
goods. However, both people sharing objects to someone as well as sharing someone's objects tripled 
the past few years. This is of importance for P2P sharing, but not so much for B2C sharing, as the 
research did not determined whether people are willing to share goods owned by a company.

Membership  
The bigger round-trip companies have applied a membership structure, three round-trip operators offer 
three different types of memberships. In general, the three types are subdivided based on user type 
and travel behaviour. Conversely being a free floating or P2P customer, you only pay for the time you 
use the car. Immediately committing to a membership, is not exactly attractive. In order to attract new 
customers, a trail without any commitment would be preferred. The differences in memberships, on 
the other hand, enhances the variety in customer base, which is flexible and user friendly so everyone 
can find a subscription which assures both, low economic burden and high mobility service. Another 
revenue stream mentioned, is to put the price for taking a ride has to be at the same level as the prices 
for public transportation. By comparing all these different options, and when mentioning that money 
is the most important incentive for people to join, it seems to be an easy choice between the different 
operators. While this has not led to one overpowering car sharing practise, making the membership 
differences no significant attractive factor. 
  
 
Channels 
Social media, such as Facebook and LinkedIn were named as channels to reach new customers, 
however according to one round-trip operator is this not enough for getting new customers and better 
name recognition. The usage of these media platforms is interconnected with their customer segment, 
in terms of age and educational level, Facebook and LinkedIn respectively. Several operators mention 
the market machines from the bigger companies as important channels to attract more people and 
spread the word about car sharing amongst people. Which insinuates that campaigns to promote car 
sharing, have a positive effect on the niche. On the other hand, promoting on something other than 
social media in order to reach a much broader audience seems something to consider for each CSO. 
Newspapers and magazines, or just campaigning around cities, would reach the customers they do not 
have yet. 
  
Key activities 
Their key activities are highly interconnected with the value proposition, however all have their 
functional and operational activities such as maintenance, cleaning, managing their fleet. Another 
important activity is the data management, user data analysis (Shaheen & Cohen, 2013; Földes & 
Csiszár, 2016, Martin, 2016) which enhances the user friendliness, in terms of car stations, availability 
of cars and accessibility. Based on the company's motivation and vision for now and the future, their 
key activities differ accordingly from educating people about the sharing economy by the P2P operator 
to trying to get people replacing their ‘second’ car for a shared car as a bigger operator aims for. The 
origin of the operators underpins their vision and key activities. As found by Martin in 2016, due to 
the development of the sharing economy several concepts have been brought together, varying from 
entrepreneurs, venture capitalists, media and ICT. This is what happened with the car sharing 
organizations grown from entrepreneurial initiatives and at the same time established businesses 
stepping in the sharing concept, which boosts the concept of car sharing and it supports a fast 
expansion of practises. Thus, making the CSOs all different from the base demonstrated by their key 
activities connected to the value proposition. On the one hand, we have the bigger round-trip CSO 
without environmental purposes trying to replace the second car and on the other side of the spectrum 
the P2P CSO whose key activity is to educate customers and others about sharing and environmental 
benefits. These assets, strongly relate to the motivation for stepping in to the business. Motivations are

Flexibility 
Flexibility is another asset, which was not directly mentioned as value proposition, however the 
importance of flexibility when offering a car sharing service was one of the most important measures 
mentioned. Even though all CSOs have a different view on flexibility and the actual content of such. 
Different important are attributed to flexibility, in literature was found flexibility as an important asset 
of car sharing which could fill the gap between car ownership and public transportation (Wagner et 
al., 2015). This in a way that personal mobility can meet the demand of a growing number of 
passengers (Földes & Csiszár, 2016). At the same time, a survey showed that there is a strong 
correlation between flexibility, public transportation and private car ownership (Beirão & Sarsfield 
Cabral, 2007). The advantages and disadvantages of the two transportation modes have been 
researched, public transportation has the disadvantage of lacking flexibility while private vehicles have 
the advantage of having the flexibility. However, the terminology of flexibility differs and is sometimes 
unclear. In this thesis, the broadest sense of the word has been investigated on in order to include all 
answers  
Depending on their customer segment, the flexibility is explained differently by each CSO. As the 
cooperatives understood flexibility as the accessibility of a car for a very low price, flexibility in 
expenditure. Both, for customers not having the burden of buying and maintaining a car as well as the 
car prices of their services. Whereas the bigger companies highlight the flexibility in choice of cars, 
they offer a car for every occasion. On the other hand, the accessibility drops because not all cars are 
available at every spot, they all have designated parking or collection spot. This linked to the companies 
having a single purpose car, the accessibility is extremely high, but there can be limitations in the 
utilization.   
  
User friendliness 
Another issue of car sharing which is named frequently while talking about flexibility, is the car parking 
spots. Where every shared car has its own designated parking space, using a shared car means that 
you do not have to search for the car and more important you do not have to search for a parking spot 
when you finished the trip and on top of that, the parking does not cost anything. The difficulty of 
finding parking spots and the costs that come with parking a car are a big disadvantage of using a 
private car (Beirão & Sarsfield Cabral, 2007)  
For the business users of car sharing, they would like to use the car to go to appointments and back 
without losing time for searching for parking spots. As a city like Copenhagen assigns parking spaces 
to shared cars, this inconvenience parking space hunt is taken away from users. This is strongly 
correlated to the flexibility of the business user which mentioned by one while another respondent 
uses the same argument but for private usage. However, there is confusion about these shared parking 
spots, as it might happen a free floating shared car parks on the designated space and on top of this, 
the municipality of Copenhagen does not recognise free floating car sharing as car sharing. The free-

spaces of licenses. Hereby comes that in Aarhus, parking spots reserved for either EV cars or shared

Wrap-up 
The niche innovations try to touch upon all different actors in the socio-technical regime, therefore it 
is difficult to determine an exact momentum. Car sharing as a concept has expanded into several 
different business models and operational models, and each concept affects another or several socio-
technical regime actors. Whereas they are comparable to a certain content, what they offer is a high 
service mobility without the burden of owning a car. This however, seems the only thing they have 
exactly in common. The diversity within car sharing models even allowed other entrepreneurs to touch 
upon the concept by changing it slightly and become a new niche, now or in the future, like Spiri. 
Where Geels MLP theory mentions that transitions do not always succeed, either because niches fail to 
build up sufficient momentum or changes in the existing regime do not give the opportunity for niches 
to interrupt it. However, this is not applicable for car sharing; indeed, the concept ‘car sharing’ has not 
found a momentum as such, yet this does not mean that car sharing models cannot break through and 
challenge and change the transport sector as we know it now. This process appears to be taking place 
in a manner that deviates from MLP theory, because the variation in niches keeps on expanding.  
  
The external influences on the niches in this case are coming from the landscape and the regime, 
namely the business opportunities the sharing economy and climate change bring, as well as local 
policy as a supporting factor. Entrepreneurs and established companies start working with the car 
sharing concept and come up with various applications. However, the elements never became aligned 
and did not came together as one dominant design.

Policy  
The policymakers are a key element for the success rate of the car sharing practises in Denmark, the 
actions taken by government and municipalities have significant impact on every CSO. As it has been 
determined that car sharing offers significant benefits for citizens and cities when it becomes a merit 
good (Cohen & Kietzmann, 2014). 
Whereas Copenhagen has rolled out a policy and goals towards car sharing in the city, Aarhus, as the 
second largest city in Denmark, stays behind. Therefore, CSOs cannot find a big foundation in the 
mobility practises in Aarhus. One CSO withdrew from Aarhus because the lack of support, where 
another one removed the EV cars away from there, due to the lack of charging stations. In this way, 
the local governments influence the car sharing practices in areas, as this loops back to the CSO and 
possible other start-ups. Especially as Copenhagen implements a new policy regarding the support of 
car sharing practises, but only the round-trip operators will benefit from these new measures, as the 
Copenhagen municipality does not recognise free floating car sharing as such. Even though, it took 
some time for the CSOs and the policymakers to get on the same page and get the politicians convinced 
about the positive impact car sharing will have on a city as Copenhagen. The opportunities for such 
sharing concepts grow as the local policymakers are supportive. All CSOs mention the same issue, 
parking spaces, the lack of them. As round-trip operators have a car parked at a designated spot in 
order to be found by customers, it is of high importance that those spots are not being taken by private 
owned cars, otherwise the accessibility drops and results in negative user experience. The influence 
policy has on the expansion of car sharing is significant, as they can assure the most beneficial parts of 
car sharing for customers, difficulties with parking and costs thereof as mentioned earlier.   
This is only local policy, whereas the national policymakers have other goals and different interest to 
defend.   
 
On a national level, the attitude towards the sharing economy and e.g. car sharing is different and 
more reluctant. A wait-and-see attitude is more applicable for the national policymakers, when it comes 
to sharing practises. The sharing economy is something that is something difficult to grasp, the strategy 
towards this is live with it for a while and interfere when it goes out of hand. However, the sharing 
practices rather see this changing, and two possible options can be adopted. One being that some space 
around the sharing practises is given but with boundaries, the other one is for policymakers to adopt 
a real position on the sharing economy concept. The P2P car sharing will not be in danger, as has been 
said the money that is earned by renting out a personal vehicle is not something a person can live from. 
It seems here that the concern policymakers have around the sharing economy is based on economic 
incentive. It seems like the overall ideology of the Danish government is the typical neoliberal 
standpoint, where the market will solve itself. However, when it comes to external influences that put

User preferences 
In order to position car sharing in the transportation sector, people's’ habits and preferences are highly 
important. When there are no customers, there is no market. Whereas the quality of life these days the 
gets more attention than before, this enhances changes in the surrounding environment. Danish people 
are rather reluctant when it comes to sharing (Nordea, 2016), however the market growth of car 
sharing operators is inevtiable (Frost & Sullivan, 2016; Cohen & Kietzmann, 2014) and this can be 
seen in the expansion of the different types of car sharing practises around Denmark. As every niche 
serves its own customer segment, there are still a lot of boundaries to overcome in order to attract the 
majority. This been said, research conducted earlier was always based on either taking private cars or 
taking the public transportation offered. While car sharing can be perceived as flexible especially when 
compared to public transportation (Kent & Dowling, 2013). However, the mobility as a service, as 
offered by CSO has not been the focus in this duel. Whereas people mention advantages and 
disadvantages for taking the public transportation and for taking their own car, car sharing can some 
of these disadvantages of public transportation and car ownership take away. Disadvantages of public 
transportation such as: too crowded, wasting time, lack of comfort, time uncertainty, lack of control, 
unreliability, long waiting and the lack of flexibility. The disadvantages of car ownership become 
==PAGE:49==

eliminated when using shared cars, namely: costs, difficulty and lack of parking, parking costs. While 
various benefits from public transport and car ownership remain: low costs, less stress, travel time and 
flexibility, rapidity, comfort, private space, music, safety respectively. The perks of driving yourself or 
the benefits of being a passenger, remain. Car sharing can offer similar services like car ownership 
without the burden of owning a car, as it also comprises some advantages of traveling by public 
transportation but without the burden of being a dependent passenger. 
  
 
As mentioned earlier, big groups in society are not ready to start sharing and therefore the focus of the 
CSO is on a younger and educated audience, because that target group has the tendency of joining. 
Here, the different approach from a P2P operator to emphasise the community building and educating 
about the sharing economy and environmental concern, is something in order to persuade people into 
car sharing without only naming the economic benefit.

Public transportation  
For everyone not owning a car, a couple of years ago, you would have been designated to the public 
transportation system. However, car sharing tries to fill up the gap there is between public 
transportation and car ownership and the increasing demand for personal mobility (Wagner et al., 
2015; Kent & Dowling, 2013; Földes & Csiszár, 2016; Qu et al.,2017). The CSOs position themselves 
all slightly different regarding public transportation. Whereas several research indicate car sharing will 
have a burden on public transport and at the same time it should go hand in hand with car sharing for 
a successful completion (Csonka & Csiszár, 2016; Wagner et al., 2015). Free floating car sharing is a 
replacement for bus, bike and walking trips by several actors.  
 
It seems that the car sharing operators are not sure about how to take position with regards to public 
transportation, contrasting things have been said. Mentioned by one is that the public transportation 
is a big competitor, however they did try to collaborate with several public transportation services. 
This in terms of covering ‘the last mile’ transport, for instance from trains stations to the destination. 
While trying to roll out such pilots and ideas, other CSOs mention that they can possibly stand alone 
and public transport is not something they have to connect with.  
 
These different views are interlinked with their customer segment, value proposition and they position 
themselves. If the aim is people doing day or multiple day trips with a car, they are not directly linked 
to the public transportation. However, there must be kept in mind that people that need a car for the 
weekend or trips, also have to go around town and further every day, this will be done by public 
transportation, by foot or by bike. In this sense, all car sharing practises are directly or indirectly 
dependent on the available other transportation modes including public transportation. Even though, 
the car sharing organisations as well as the public transport do not seem to be convinced yet. When 
talking about the free-floating service for instance, they see a future where car ownership gets ruled 
out by all other services including theirs, so they are very aware of the fact that public transportation 
plays a significant role in accomplishing this, even though the public transport is a competitor.  
All these contrasting views about the dependence or independence of car sharing on public transport 
services, do not make it easier for car sharing as a concept to interfere and change the current 
transportation system and therefore determine the future development and the direction car sharing 
shall take.   
  
Private car ownership and the car industry 
In some cases, car sharing tackles the private car, to some content. Again, this is highly interlinked 
with the service they offer and who they address. Whenever some mention to try to replace the ‘second’ 
car in a household, and other have the ultimate goal of eliminating private owned cars. However, 
people are still very attached to their own car and not willing to share as been determined before. The 
question is here, is if the goal is to out rule private owned cars on the longer term, because when this 
==PAGE:51==
 
45 
is the case all car sharing operators should aim for the same goals in order to accomplish a certain 
change. The biggest obstacle for a CSO is getting more customers on board, as the overall mind-set 
towards car ownership has to be changed somehow or other benefits of new services have to be 
convincing enough for people to reconsider owning a car. However, more and more car manufacturers 
enter the market of car sharing, so the industry that enhances car ownership is able to play a role in 
the transition. It seems as if the car industry is adopting car sharing faster than most other regime 
actors. Either in terms of buying into car sharing organisations, like has happened in Denmark by now 
or car manufacturers enter the market themselves.    
  
Technology  
Another socio-technical regime is technology, there is already determined that without the innovation 
and technology, car sharing would have never been possible in the way it exists now. The fast changing 
and innovative techniques and data gathering, all ensures the best service possible for car sharing 
practises as well as for other transportation services. By means of the fast-developing technology, 
personal mobility can be offered easier and the CSOs use this for making their service as preferred and 
MaaS is a concept where different actors are looking into, in order to deliver the best possible mobility 
service. Whereas science constantly comes up with new and different solutions for the aforementioned 
problems as CO2 emissions due to cars, congestion, air pollution and so on. Which offers opportunities 
for entrepreneurs to step in and come up with real life solutions.  
There is also another upcoming technology trying to come into the market, the self-driving car. The 
future perspective of autonomous vehicles is perceived differently by all CSOs, whereas some see it 
happening and a combination would make car sharing even easier. Others are more reluctant towards 
the self-driving solution. It is not the question if, but when the self-driving technology is good enough 
to be adopted by all different actors. Highly dependent on what and where the self-driving technology 
will be applied, it will have its effects on car sharing. Unsure if it is going to be an advantage or the 
disadvantage of car sharing.  
  
Wrap-up  
The regime actors all fulfil a significant role in the transition of the current transportation sector. It 
does not seem too important to change the transportation system significant, as no actor yet has 
adopted the car sharing as the solution or made it possible for the car sharing concept to interrupt the 
current market as we now know. Also, the attitude CSOs have towards the other transportation modes 
is varying, whether they are dependent on public transportation or not, research shows they have to 
be in co-existence or collaborations between public services and car sharing. However, the problems 
car sharing can solve do not have the top priority for possible game changers apparently. While the 
national government just waits for the autonomous car to disturb the current transportation system, 
round-trip car sharing initiatives get support from local governments. Therefore, it seems like it will 
be difficult for car sharing to take over an entire country, as if now only cities seem cooperative and 
eager. As Denmark is perceived as a country with high environmental awareness there is a difference 
between people as environmentally aware, compared to car users as acting like environmental aware.

5.3 SOCIO-TECHNICAL LANDSCAPE 
The transportation system these days is inherent to change, and there are factors stabilising and 
destabilising this status quo. Car sharing is here to destabilise and challenge this status quo. Whereas 
the destabilizing factors are in favour of car sharing, the stabilizing factors not so much.  
 
Sharing economy 
First, the growing sharing economy is a change in the landscape which the transportation sector 
encounters in all different modes of transportation as well as in people's daily lives. The sharing 
economy business model offers many commercial options for entrepreneurs, established companies, 
industries, cities and even countries. Therefore, the sharing economy can be perceived as 
“neoliberalism on steroids” as mentioned by Morozov (2013, para 3.), however the benefits for society 
grow as more and more people start participating in sharing because resources will be used more 
efficiently as well as the wealth gets distributed equally by increasing the accessibility of goods which 
might have never been in someone's reach. This can be seen in the increasing amount of car sharing 
practises, this growing trend has been determined by several consultant companies and researchers in 
the past years (PwC, 2014; Martin, 2016; Shaheen & Cohen, 2013). Even though it has been 
determined by Nordea (2016), Danes are not willing to share their own goods, nothing has been said 
about sharing products or services which belong to companies or platforms.  
  
More and more car sharing organisations have entered the market in Denmark with car sharing 
business models, all having different background and motivations for stepping in. This underlines the 
predictions by Frost & Sullivan (2016) as well as the forecasted trends by Shaheen and Cohen in 2013. 
The various motivations were; high interest in the sharing economy and the division of wealth by a 
P2P; a spin-off of a big car rental company which decided that conventional car rentals as we know 
now, will no longer exist in the future; small groups of entrepreneurs who decided that owning a car 
by each individual is not necessary if we are able to share; and a duo which was very unsatisfied with 
the current available services to receive a high level of service in mobility; and another start-up is ready 
to challenge the status quo by offering flexible and modern urban transport solution.   
  
The connection between society and the transportation system is something which has been 
determined by Ferrero et al., (2015), and sharing practises are linked to both concepts as the problems 
that come along with high level of service within the transportation system partly because of private 
car ownership. Whereas private car ownership offers a high level of flexibility and service in someone’s 
life, as when sharing these private owned cars, the level of service is still high without having everyone 
to buy a car. 
This is something P2P car sharing benefits from, a platform that offers an opportunity for car owners 
to share and renters to use a vehicle. For the P2P operator, adds something extra to society in terms of 
sharing. Even though car sharing is their first interest, educating people about the sharing economy is 
a strong second value proposition. The interest in customer behaviour and the sharing practises in 
general makes, them a game changer in the bigger picture around sharing economy concepts.  
  
Sharing in this way, will be more beneficial when more and more people start participating, as 
resources will be used more efficiently as well as the wealth gets distributed equally by increasing the 
accessibility of goods which might have never been in someone's reach. The sharing economy already 
==PAGE:53==
 
47 
offers loads of opportunities for businesses to start, while on the other hand the government has an 
actual say in this. However, that is not something they do, the Danish policymakers did neither took a 
stand in the sharing economy concept nor left sharing concepts some freedom. The strategy here seems 
to just wait and see, which is not beneficial for sharing economy concepts in general as well as for 
some car sharing practises.  For all CSOs go that, they offer the personal mobility for customers not 
having the financial aids or space to own a private car. People are not designated to the public 
transportation and can have the highest level of service when necessary, without having the burden, 
and this is made possible by the rise of the sharing economy.  
  
Climate change  
The growing concern about the environment, also has its effect on the transportation sector. As 
Denmark has to meet certain goals in terms of the reduction of CO2 emissions as well as Copenhagen 
that has set their own goal, namely to be CO2 neutral by 2050 (Nielsen et al., 2015). This puts pressure 
on the current regime actors and at the same time it creates opportunities for entrepreneurs and 
business to come up with ideas and concepts that help to meet these goals. Even though people joining 
car sharing do not have and environmental motive for joining, nor all CSOs have that, it still has a 
positive effect on these goals by limiting the number of cars in a country as well as the fuel consumption 
by CSOs having electric vehicles in their fleet. Lowering the CO2 emissions asks for a change in the 
current transportation and this has to be accomplished one way or the other.   
  
Urban planning 
The way cities and the landscape is carefully planned and developed, is definitely in favour of car users. 
More asphalt was what we needed, now more and more urban planner realises that the quality of life 
became more important to citizens. At the same time, our current transportation system is causing air 
pollution, traffic, CO2 emissions and inefficient use of dense areas. Nowadays, a change towards more 
space for bikes is preferable and already recognised by Danish cities. However, the streets are still 
designated for cars, either for parking or for driving. Even though it has been determined in research 
that cars stand idle 95% of the time, 
However, more research in the urban planning in favour of car sharing has to be conducted in order 
to grasp a fair overview on the vision and plans.  
  
Wrap-up 
The sharing economy and climate change concern, are the two bigger destabilising factors, while the 
more social/cultural habits are stabilising the current transportation. For example, the preference for 
having your own car in order to go wherever you want whenever you want; the urban interior is 
structured and build up on the idea of car ownership; cars are some sort of status symbol; and the 
rising demand of personal mobility. However, with the opportunities given by the shared economy and 
the pressure that the changing climate is putting on the current transportation system, there is a lot 
for car sharing to gain as car sharing can offer some solutions.

5.4 SOCIO-TECHNICAL ‘RECONFIGURATION’ 
Regarding the MLP developed by Geels, the niche innovations will come to a momentum and at the 
same time it will disrupt the socio-technical regime and will find its spot. However, discussing the 
niche innovations, all car sharing, but all significantly different, there is no momentum to be found. In 
the line of expectations, it does not seem that there will be one car sharing practices that will break 
through as several actors in the regime start to adopt different kinds of car sharing services. Therefore, 
the MLP does exactly work for car sharing as a practise, the model allows to look into all different 
levels and actors influencing the status quo as well as the niche innovations, which solutes in an extra 
part in the MLP. As the varying car sharing practises are getting picked up by several regime actors, 
collaborations between specific regime actors and car sharing practises or parts of them emerge. Not 
one of the regime actors is going to be replaced by car sharing, as it seems now because of the different 
goals and motivations the CSOs have individually.   
The concept of car sharing is omitted by national policymakers, they are waiting for the autonomous 
vehicle to disrupt the transportation system. While the CO2 emission reduction is high on their agenda, 
no steps are taken regarding neither investing in the public transportation system nor making car 
ownership less attractive. There is a big dissension amongst all regime actors, which makes it possible 
for several niche innovations to interfere with the status quo as some actors are encompassing while 
others are reluctant. Whereas the landscape actors immediately influence the niche innovations as well 
as the fast inclusion of several car sharing practises by various actors. The landscape actors like climate 
change and the sharing economy have effect on the regime actors, but are adopted by some earlier 
than others. This assures the realignment of regime actors adapting niches faster than others, and 
therefore changing faster in another direction compared to before car sharing practises.   
Based on all the discussed in this chapter, Geels MLP does not work completely for car sharing, so I 
propose to make an adjustment at the stage where new configuration breaks through, as this is fed by 
more than one niche innovation instead of one momentum, as can be seen in figure 5. Where the socio-
technical regime actors do not adopt one niche, but where different actors adopt different niches in 
order to adjust, by the influence and pressure from the socio-technical landscape.

5.5 FUTURE SCENARIOS 
By answering the two sub questions in the aforementioned, the main research question: How can car 
sharing be disruptive to the current transportation sector in Denmark? can be addressed. While the future 
of car sharing is highly uncertain, several future outcomes are feasible, based on the results and the 
discussion about the ruling regime and landscape actors. These findings are used to create future 
scenarios, where it will be assumed that demand for car sharing will grow, following the predictions 
made earlier (Frost & Sullivan, 2016; BCG, 2016). This can happen either because of people’s 
involvement in sharing and hereby reducing the use of natural resources, or the economic profitability 
might be convincing enough for potential users. 
  
The technology developments that will make everything possible in the transportation sector are 
evident, e.g. cars on alternative energy and autonomous cars. Most CSOs indicated, that innovations 
like those will inevitably disrupt the transportation sector (Seba, 2016), but if and when cars become 
autonomous it is more likely that they will promote car sharing business models (Roberts, 2017).

Beyond these technological innovations, the three possible scenarios discuss how the socio-technical 
regime could change through other actors such that car sharing disrupts the current transportation 
sector. It is important to note, that based on earlier findings, various car sharing practises will continue 
to exist. They all represent a unique business, as has been determined in the discussion. If this results 
in a future where the different niches are not being driven towards a standard design, car sharing will 
remain a scattered concept and will not likely be disruptive. 
  
For car sharing to disrupt the current transportation system, three possible scenarios are explored:  
  
1. Policy-makers give preference-  local policymakers seem supportive towards car sharing in 
cities; 
2. The car industry takes action- car rental and car manufacturers are interested in car sharing as 
a practise and are already connected to several operators; 
3. Public transportation integration: the car sharing operators making overtures of collaboration 
with public transportation services, as result of the acknowledgement of direct or indirect 
dependency. 
5.5.1 POLICYMAKERS GIVE PREFERENCE 
This scenario describes a city wherein all cars are shared, whether they are privately owned or owned 
by car sharing organisations. Policymakers become convinced of the fact that car sharing is what their 
city needs to address congestion, planning, and quality of life objectives. The greater goal is brought 
in by local governments to reduce the parking spots by 80% and repurpose these grey, asphalt areas 
to parks and other leisure places. In the beginning, policymakers are hesitant regarding the free-
floating service, and are not rewarding these services in town. Policy makers realise that car sharing is 
in line with the goals they want to accomplish. This allows users to use an available car and leave the 
car again at a random space. When citizens have the need to return, or go somewhere else, a quick 
look on their phone is enough in order to determine where and which car is available. Whenever 
someone wants to go for a daytrip or multi-day trip, a car can be booked on beforehand and customers 
will be matched to other customers to fill up the car to that destination. Unless customers like their 
privacy, they can decide for a supplementary fee to book the whole car.  
Local governments will assure and take responsibility for having enough cars available in good 
collaboration with the CSO. Therefore, citizens are incentivised (or otherwise rewarded) to share their 
privately-owned car. Car sharing organisations will keep on expanding with new services and new 
products, and healthy market competition keeps costs low and transport affordable for the citizens.  
  
In this scenario, everyone has access to all cars available- ideally it would be a case where all people 
can access all cars. However, when looking at the current difference in car sharing practises, it is 
conceivable that various car sharing organisations will persist, all offering the same thing: flexible and 
cheap personal mobility. While on the other hand, all have unique services and customer segments. 
P2P platforms will be there for all private car owners to offer their cars on, as this is being promoted 
by local governments.  
Eventually, private cars will be banned from cities and only shared cars can be utilized within the 
urban area. However, before banning cars from urban areas, city governments will have everything in 
place for offering the highest possible mobility standard. In strong collaboration with local 
==PAGE:57==
 
51 
governments, car sharing will be the mainstream way of transportation in cities. Also, when all cities 
apply such measures, they all will be connected later and disrupt the total transportation system in 
Denmark. 
5.5.2 CAR INDUSTRY TAKES ACTION 
The interference of the car industry in car sharing around Denmark is undeniable, as the car sharing 
concept is a threat to car manufacturers and car rental companies. The car rental demand will drop till 
the minimum, as car sharing offers a much more specific service. Meanwhile, the car sharing concept 
makes cars accessible for every budget, and there is no need of buying a private car. For the car 
manufacturers to maintain their market position, a total change in core business will occur. Either by 
taking over existing car sharing organisations or by buying into them, starting their own sharing 
concept also has potential. As no manufacturer will lose business and it is strong position, business will 
change from offering a product, e.g. a car, to offering a service, e.g. car access. People will have to take 
prescriptions on car access, and they can be loyal to their preferred brand, as every manufacturer will 
offer this mobility service. Going to the car dealer to purchase a prescription instead of an actual car, 
will be the future for car manufacturers.    
  
The process of changing the market is already put in motion, with the development that Hertz entered 
the car sharing market as a round-trip operator with multi-purpose cars and was taken over by Volvo. 
Meanwhile, Europcar bought into Snappcar for 20% in June 2017 and already has deals with other 
CSOs.  
Every car manufacturer will compete in the car sharing market, and no single car will be sold to a 
specific individual. The resources and power that car manufacturers and car rental companies can 
bring to the car sharing market, will result in the disappearance of the entrepreneurial initiatives car 
sharing entails. At the same time, the evolvement of technology making car sharing easier and more 
accessible is resulting in the most controversial services when it comes to technology and data 
management.  
  
No niche will become the rule of thumb as the smaller entrepreneurial based companies will be either 
be bought or will vanish because the manufacturers will steamroll the small operators. At the same 
time, the car industry offers car sharing deals to businesses, companies and industrial sites, this all 
made to measure and on big scale. The future here will hold all car manufacturers and car rental 
companies together offer all car sharing services, either round-trip, free-floating and station based as 
well. Whereas they compete with each other, like they do in the current transportation sector, but not 
by means of tangible cars but by offering mobility as a service to everyone in various price segments.  
  
5.5.3 PUBLIC TRANSPORTATION INTEGRATION   
After long negotiations, between car sharing operators and the different public transportation modes, 
they both have agreed on the fact they are highly dependent on each other. And car sharing now fills 
the gap between the public transportation and private car ownership. For the public transportation, it 
is necessary to change, because of the discomfort that is perceived by users. Public transport keeps on 
losing users to private car ownership and to car sharing operators. However, both sides are dissatisfied 
with this development. Car sharing as well as the public transportation want to attract the same users. 
==PAGE:58==
 
52 
The growing demand for high personal mobility is something the public transportation cannot live up 
to, while car sharing can.  
  
Car sharing will become part of the online travel planner, if a user decides to go somewhere all 
available options are shown. Every time one plans a trip, car sharing will be included in the possible 
options for completing the travel. Then it is up to the user to pick which proposed route it takes, the 
fastest, cheapest or shortest. These routes consist of all different transportation modes, which of course 
can be selected by the user. Car sharing is totally integrated within the public transportation system 
and can be paid in the same way as the others by checking in and out. Depending on the willingness 
to pay a higher price, more and more various cars will become accessible. Busses already belong to the 
history, minibuses and shared cars fill up this gap. Not only the cars are shared, also the rides are 
shared, like this is mainstream in public transportation. Phone applications show where, which and 
how many people take certain cars and trips. Meaning that the options for shortest and fastest road 
are real-time connected, this makes the public transportation system with car sharing integrated in 
their service, the most efficient and only way of traveling around.  
  
Private car ownership will be obsolete soon, the busses, metros, light rails and trains have expanded 
significantly. The car sharing operators linked themselves to various public services. More car sharing 
services come into practise, all serving specific transportation needs. Therefore, the public 
transportation will become the mainstream way of transporting, made possible by the inclusion of car 
sharing.   
  
Wrap-up 
All technology developments and future innovations, have the possibility to accelerate all three 
scenarios however it is dependent on what technology and which scenario fits best to the innovation.  
As it looks right now, first the electric car will become mainstream hereafter follows the self-driving 
technique. Within a future where car sharing is a mainstream transportation mode, both innovations 
will enhance the endless possibilities and benefits car sharing encloses. This goes for all types of car 
sharing, whether it is P2P or B2C, round-trip or free-floating. Even though round-trips will change as 
the car will do a round-trip and not necessarily the customer.

6 CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In order to conclude on the potential car sharing has to disrupt the transportation sector in Denmark, 
first car sharing operators have been determined as niche innovations. What can be noted here, is the 
multi-level perspective theory by Geels provides a great guidance by defining and positing all socio-
technical regime and landscape actors involved in the transition car sharing can carry out.  
However, it can be seen that until now the niche innovations about car sharing did not result in a 
momentum, one best practise or a rule of thumb. All operators serve their own customer by offering a 
distinctive, in greater or lesser degree, service with different aims, whether this is to out rule car 
ownership, public transportation or the ‘second’ car. It even seems possible more of car sharing 
practises will come into existence, this all is influenced by the socio-technical regime actors that have 
something to with the current transportation system and car sharing as a concept. Regime actors 
decide, conscious or unconscious, which and how and even if the car sharing concept can be a 
disrupting factor for the transportation sector in Denmark. 
 
As now the regime and landscape broaden the possibilities for car sharing as a concept, governance 
cannot be measured by the same yardstick when it comes to car sharing. Local and national governance 
have to be divided, when looking into the case of car sharing as both have other visions. The national 
attitude is to wait and see, which goes for disruptive technologies as well as for all sharing economy 
concepts, whereas the local authorities are more likely to participate on this fact changing concept and 
use it in their advantage.  
 
As there is some resistance towards the sharing economy, whether it is a new business model to make 
money with or if it is the solution for hyper consumerism. As car sharing is beneficial for the 
environment, as well as for people's economy and it enhances a social advantage. All car sharing 
concepts in practise in Denmark have positive impacts on the environment by means of either the fact 
that one shared car is replacing 6.6 private owned cars, however not all operators are in the game for 
the environmental benefits car sharing offers. At the same time, people get more freedom in their 
economy when no big expenses have to be done, without giving in on comfort and mobility. By the 
economic benefit, it is redistributing wealth which is a social benefit car sharing entails as well as some 
educating some operators do to teach people about the sharing economy in general.  
This being said, even if there is a reluctant attitude towards the sharing economy either by policy 
makers, because it is seen as the neoliberalism on steroids, it still has benefits for society, as well as 
being the remedy for a hyper-consumerism culture. Or even if there is a certain reluctance in attitude 
and mind-set about sharing other people's car and possibly not owning their own car any longer, the 
future potential of car sharing is high. By bigger pressures coming from the climate matters, there is 
the need for a transition in the transportation system, one way or the other and the sharing economy 
together with the climate concern, and the growing demand for a high level of service in personal 
mobility, enhance great opportunities for new businesses.  
Especially car sharing will benefit from these landscape changes, with the influence of technology and 
favourable policies, an expansion of car sharing will arise. Even though other actors might be unwilling 
and hesitant in making space or investing in car sharing as it is not important enough for them to 
transform the transportation system. Unlike Geels approach for transitions, no gap is created by the 
regime actors for car sharing to fit in and fill up this gap.  
==PAGE:60==
 
54 
Therefore, will car sharing be merged in other practises and replace parts of them, partly or in its 
entirety. Like is proposed in the three possible future scenarios, uphold by the policymakers, car 
industry and public transportation. The most likely pathway to disruptiveness, is the car industry takes 
action, as came through in this thesis. Policymakers and the public transportation services are rather 
slow in the adaptiveness and their attitude is neoliberal, where the market will just solve itself. 
Unfortunately, this will mean the end for smaller practises, start-ups and other initiatives, as they will 
be merged into the car manufacturers and the car industry will maintain their monopoly in the 
transportation sector. Without offering cars as a product, but mobility as a service.   
 
The concept car sharing is not there yet, but the future seems rather bright, whether technology 
interruptions take place or not. To obtain a more precise and representative research, I recommend to 
include more respondents of every company, as well as all actors should be represented. In order to 
come up with a better future perspective it is preferable to investigate within the car industry and 
within the public transportation services about their attitude towards car sharing and their future 
strategy. Another important socio-technical actor, the users and market preference, has to be 
investigated more in depth, as most research is focussed on either shared economy on P2P base or on 
car ownership versus public transportation usage. For car sharing practises, I think it is smart to look 
in possible collaborations either with each other, municipalities or public transportation.
