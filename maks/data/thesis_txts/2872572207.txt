Abstract
Modern IQF tunnel­freezer lines station 4­6 attendants at the in­feed conveyor to pull
apart poultry pieces that stick together. The task is repetitive, costly, and hygiene­critical.
This thesis examines whether a collaborative robotic system can fulfill this role without
compromising throughput or product quality.
A laboratory­scale prototype combined a Schneider Modicon M262 PLC, a six­axis Lex­
ium Cobot, a Robotiq 2F­85 adaptive gripper, and a 2­D colour camera vision system that
segmented contact points and published real­time pick­and­place poses. Eight scenarios,
involving a static belt and motion at 1 cm/s and 4 cm/s, with two to four poultry pieces,
were executed over 36 trials.
The cell achieved an overall success rate of 83%, 100% when the belt was stopped,
87.5% at 1 cm/s, and 75% at 4 cm/s. Pick­coordinate error remained within the 3 mm
positioning target after a single affine calibration, confirming the viability of vision­guided
manipulation at line speeds.
With a turnkey price of approximately 15 million ISK for the system, the economic assess­
ment indicates that the investment is recovered in about two years when it replaces two
in­feed attendants, who earn roughly 1,900 ISK per hour.
Limitations include testing at 20°C with Play­Doh rather than poultry, intermittent Modbus­
RTU drops to the gripper, and insufficient conveyor width. The study demonstrates that a
hygienic, PLC­integrated cobot system can reduce labour and ergonomic risk. It outlines
an industrial upgrade path, including the IP69K Schmalz mGrip, a 3­D laser profiler, and
overhead gantry mounting, to transition the concept from prototype to the factory floor.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In late 2024, KAPP, an Icelandic technology company that specializes in refrigeration
services, production, and service of equipment for fisheries, aquaculture, and other in­
dustries [2], along with other investors, acquired the bankrupt estate of another Icelandic
technology company called Skaginn 3X [3].
Among the machinery Skaginn 3X manufactured and the newly formed entity, KAPP Sk­
aginn, will continue to manufacture is the IQF Tunnel Freezer. The IQF Tunnel Freezer
is a two­in­one crust and core freezer that can handle a variety of food products.
In front of the IQF Tunnel Freezer is a conveyor belt that feeds products into the freezer.
To prevent products from freezing and sticking together, 4­6 people stand next to the
conveyor belt and manually separate the products. The project KAPP Skaginn has pro­
posed is a concept design and feasibility study of a collaborative robotic system located
at the beginning of the conveyor belt. The system should effectively replace 2­4 people
by identifying where products are in contact on the conveyor belt and using a robotic arm
to separate them. The product this project will address is poultry, specifically.
1.2
Problem Statement and Research Question
In front of the IQF Tunnel Freezer, 4­6 people are typically stationed to separate poultry
pieces on a moving conveyor belt. This manual process is time­consuming, expensive,
and prone to reduced quality or throughput when operators experience fatigue or become
inattentive. Over time, relying on a large team for such a repetitive task raises safety
concerns and imposes significant labour costs on poultry­processing facilities. Further­
more, inconsistent product spacing before freezing can result in decreased yield if pieces
stick together during the process or in suboptimal freezing outcomes that compromise the
product’s quality.
This thesis aims to investigate whether and how a collaborative robotic system can auto­
mate the separation of poultry pieces without compromising production speed or quality
standards. The key question to consider is: How can a collaborative robotic system
be designed and implemented to effectively and efficiently separate poultry prod­
ucts on a conveyor belt in front of an IQF Tunnel Freezer? This question is founded
on the hypothesis that advanced machine vision, coupled with a robotic arm, can reduce
the required manual labour by half or more while maintaining throughput. To explore
this hypothesis, the project will investigate strategies for detecting poultry pieces that are
stuck together, determining how to manipulate them without damaging or contaminating
the product, and integrating these functions with existing hardware, such as the freezer
unit and conveyor system. Additional considerations include hygiene requirements, food

==PAGE:16==
safety standards, and the seamless coordination of robotic control and other supervisory
elements.
Exploring this question involves considering several critical points:
• Sensing and Localization. Machine vision must reliably identify points of contact
between poultry pieces and do so in a variable environment.
• Robotic Manipulation. Once located, the robot arm needs a suitable end effector
and motion plan to separate clumped pieces, all while respecting constraints on
speed, product delicacy, and cleanliness.
• System Integration. Any proposed solution should interface smoothly with the IQF
Tunnel Freezer, conveyors, and broader production lines, adhering to critical safety
and hygienic standards for food handling.
A successful robotic solution could reduce labour costs, streamline operations, and miti­
gate the risk of contamination or injury associated with human intervention by automating
a repetitive and ergonomically challenging task. This has the potential to improve product
consistency and reduce waste, benefiting both producers and customers. More broadly,
demonstrating the feasibility of a collaborative robotic system in this role could inform sim­
ilar automation efforts in other stages of food processing, where careful handling and safe
human­robot interaction are particularly crucial.
This study holds relevance that surpasses the boundaries of a single production line. By
evaluating design requirements, hardware trade­offs, and food industry standards, this
thesis will provide a detailed framework that highlights both the potential and challenges
of integrating collaborative robotics into the poultry processing environment.
1.3
Objectives and Scope
The primary objective of this thesis is to design, develop, and evaluate a collaborative
robotic system capable of separating poultry products on a conveyor belt in front of an
IQF Tunnel Freezer. By integrating a Schneider Electric M262 PLC and a Schneider Lex­
ium Cobot, the solution aims to reduce manual labour requirements, maintain or improve
throughput, and ensure safe human­robot interaction. In pursuit of this objective, the the­
sis will investigate strategies for machine vision­based product detection and localization,
as well as robotic manipulation of poultry and seamless coordination between hardware
and software subsystems.
More specifically, the project will:
• Explore and compare suitable sensor or camera options to detect instances where
poultry products are in contact, thereby guiding the robotic arm to separate them.
• Explore and compare suitable end­effectors or grippers that can carefully grab sen­
sitive poultry products.

• Develop a proof­of­concept prototype, focusing on integrating the Lexium Cobot with
the M262 PLC and an appropriate end effector.
The scope of this thesis is limited to designing a system that can operate effectively on
a conveyor belt feeding into the IQF Tunnel Freezer, with poultry as the primary product.
It encompasses hardware selection, software integration, and the creation of a working
prototype at a reduced scale. Full­scale industrial deployment and broad comparative
tests with other robotic arms or PLC brands fall outside the scope. Furthermore, the
project does not involve an in­depth redesign of the existing IQF Tunnel Freezer; instead,
it will focus on creating a complementary robotic system that can be seamlessly integrated
into the current setup.
1.4
Limitations
Though this project benefits from a relatively open scope, the project owner has estab­
lished a few key constraints. First, the programmable logic controller (PLC) must be the
Schneider Electric M262, which is already in use with the IQF Freezer system.
Second, the robotic arm must be the Schneider Lexium Cobot, a choice that similarly cur­
tails comparison across a broader range of robotics solutions. While such uniformity can
streamline integration, it does reduce opportunities to evaluate other potentially valuable
hardware or to incorporate non­Schneider technologies.
Third, the system under investigation must be a collaborative robotic system, meaning it
must be designed and certified to operate safely in shared workspaces with human per­
sonnel. This excludes the use of standard industrial robots that require significant physical
barriers or safeguarding measures, even if such robots might offer different performance
or cost benefits. While the results of this thesis might ultimately suggest a broader range of
solutions, the project’s mandate confines current exploration specifically to collaborative
technology.
Beyond these specified requirements, the project faces no other significant limitations.
Nonetheless, reliance on a single manufacturer’s ecosystem imposes restrictions on both
hardware choices and software tools. Adaptation might be cumbersome if features or
performance surpass what is available in the Schneider suite. On the other hand, by
accepting these stipulations, there is no need to justify or defend component selections.
This enables the project to align with established company standards, ensuring compat­
ibility while focusing efforts on the feasibility and optimization of poultry product handling
to improve efficiency.

Analysis
2.1
Robots, Collaborative Robots and Automation
A robot, as defined by the Cambridge Dictionary [4], is ”a machine controlled by a com­
puter that is used to perform jobs automatically.” Robots have long been a symbol of
technological advancements, representing the intersection of mechanical engineering,
electronics, and computer science. Depending on the context, robots can operate with
varying degrees of autonomy, from simple pre­programmed machines to systems that
make real­time decisions using artificial intelligence. Today, robots are an integral part of
the field of automation.
Automation refers to the use of machines to perform tasks traditionally carried out by
humans, or to execute functions that would otherwise be impossible. Unlike basic mech­
anization, automation involves self­regulating systems operating with minimal human in­
tervention. Originating in the automotive industry in the 1940s, the term gained popularity
to describe the growing use of automatic controls in manufacturing. Automation relies
heavily on computer technology and feedback control systems, enabling processes to be
performed with high precision and autonomy [5].
According to Intel, there are seven main types of robots [6]:
1. Autonomous Mobile Robots (AMRs): Navigate environments independently us­
ing sensors and onboard processing for real­time decision­making.
2. Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs): Follow fixed paths and often require super­
vision, mainly used in logistics and manufacturing.
3. Articulated Robots: Robotic arms with multiple rotary joints, ideal for welding,
packaging, and material handling tasks.
4. SCARA Robots: Compact arms optimized for high­speed, vertical assembly and
precision tasks in confined workspaces.
5. Humanoids: Robots with human­like forms and functions, often used in customer
service and information roles.
6. Cobots: Collaborative robots designed to work safely alongside humans, assisting
in repetitive or hazardous tasks.
7. Hybrids: Combine features from multiple robot types (e.g., AMRs with arms) to
handle complex operations with enhanced versatility.
Among the various categories of robots, collaborative robots (cobots) are of particular
relevance to this thesis. Collaborative robotics refers to systems in which humans and
robots work in close proximity to accomplish shared tasks. Unlike traditional automation,
Collaborative Robotic System for Poultry Processing
5
==PAGE:20==
it emphasizes physical closeness and functional cooperation, even if not performed si­
multaneously. The core idea is that humans and robots contribute within shared spaces,
with robots functioning autonomously during collaboration [7].
Collaborative robots are rapidly transforming industries by enabling safe, flexible automa­
tion in environments where traditional industrial robots may not be suitable. Designed to
work alongside humans, cobots enhance productivity without sacrificing safety. They are
particularly valuable for small­batch, high­mix operations that require frequent changes
and close human interaction. This unique ability of cobots to operate safely close to hu­
mans while adapting to dynamic environments makes them particularly well­suited for the
food processing industry.
2.2
Collaborative Robotics in Food Processing
As outlined in the previous section, cobots are emerging as a key enabler of automation
in the food industry, where flexibility, safety, and hygiene are essential. Tasks involving
raw meat and poultry remain challenging due to product variability, the need for delicate
handling, and stringent sanitary requirements. Cobots, especially when paired with vision
systems, offer a promising solution by combining human­like adaptability with robotic pre­
cision.
In a 2021 article [8], Dawson discusses the advancements and challenges of integrating
robotics into poultry processing, particularly in unstructured environments like the front
end of processing plants. While robots have effectively handled repetitive tasks such as
case packing and palletizing, automating processes involving items of varying shapes
and sizes, like individual poultry parts, remains a challenge. To address this, the Georgia
Tech Research Institute is developing an intelligent cutting system that captures images
of each bird, predicts the location of bones and tendons, and calculates a unique cutting
trajectory, thereby maximizing yield and value.
In their 2022 mini­review [9], Xiao et al. discuss the integration of machine vision systems
in food detection, emphasizing its role in enhancing food quality and safety. The authors
highlight that traditional methods of food inspection, primarily reliant on human sensory
evaluation, are often subjective and inefficient. Machine vision systems, equipped with ad­
vanced image acquisition tools such as high­definition cameras, X­ray, thermal imaging,
and magnetic resonance imaging, offer a more objective and efficient alternative. These
systems can capture detailed information about food products, including size, shape, and
surface colour, thereby improving monitoring accuracy and reducing human error.
While collaborative robotics and machine vision have shown great promise in upstream
food processing, their role becomes equally vital just before the freezing process. Consis­
tent product orientation and spacing in poultry processing are crucial for ensuring uniform
freezing and product quality. This is especially important in IQF systems, where products
must be evenly distributed on the conveyor belt to prevent clumping and freezer ineffi­
ciencies. The following section introduces the IQF Tunnel Freezer, highlighting its role

2.3
IQF Tunnel Freezer
The IQF Tunnel freezer is a two­in­one crust and core freezer with freezing coils, powerful

System Architecture
3.1
Overview of the Proposed System
As outlined in previous chapters, the proposed system aims to automate a task currently
performed by 2­4 human operators positioned along a conveyor belt. A critical mechan­
ical consideration in this design is the method of mounting the robotic arm. This thesis
evaluates two distinct mounting configurations.
The first configuration, illustrated in Figure 3.1a, involves positioning the robotic arm be­
side the conveyor belt on a platform, mimicking the stance of a human operator. The
second configuration, shown in Figure 3.1b, suspends the robotic arm from a custom­built
overhead frame directly above the conveyor belt. Note that for clarity, the vision system
components, such as the camera and sensors mounted above the belt, are not depicted
in these figures but would be located at the beginning of the conveyor belt directly above
it.
Each configuration presents unique advantages and disadvantages. The first configura­
tion, a side­mounted platform, is more straightforward and more cost­effective to install.
It also offers easier maintenance and accessibility for troubleshooting. However, its pri­
mary limitations include a reduced working range, which limits the robotic arm’s operation
to approximately 50% of its maximum reach, and an increased risk of collisions, as the
arm may extend into adjacent walkways. In contrast, the second configuration, which
involves mounting the robotic arm on an overhead frame, maximizes the arm’s range of
motion by enabling 360­degree access to the conveyor belt. This setup also improves
safety by eliminating the risk of the arm encroaching on pedestrian pathways. The trade­
off, however, is the need to construct a robust, load­bearing steel frame, which introduces
higher initial costs and greater structural complexity.

3.3
System Description
3.3.1
Safety System
The collaborative robotic system is built on the premise that no dedicated safety controller
is added to the line. Instead, every required function is executed on the same Schnei­
der Electric Modicon M262 PLC that governs the IQF tunnel freezer. By extending the
freezer’s existing emergency stop circuit and fieldbus channels to the robotic subsystem,
the project avoids duplicating hardware, simplifies validation, and ensures that a single
safety hierarchy supervises the entire machine.
As stated in ISO/TS 15066:2016, the operator shall be able to stop the robot at any time
with a single action or have an unobstructed means of exiting the collaborative workspace
[52]. The cobot’s safety­rated power­and­force­limiting sensors ensure compliance, so
the arm stops as soon as an operator restrains it, and by emergency­stop buttons posi­
tioned on the machine. Only when the robotic arm is mounted at the overhead steel frame,
as shown in Figure 3.1b, does the operator have an unobstructed means of exiting.
3.3.2
Functional Description of the System
The system’s functionality can be divided into four main subsystems: the vision system,
the PLC, the cobot, and the gripper. The vision system detects all objects on the conveyor
belt and finds objects touching or overlapping. After that, it finds an available spot to move
either of the touching objects to and returns transformed coordinates to the PLC. The PLC
has a queue for storing all pick and place coordinates and a timestamp. The timestamp is
used to calculate the displacement of the objects on the conveyor belt. Every PLC cycle,
the queue is verified and updated. The PLC also handles the pick and place sequence for
the cobot. It first dequeues the top element of the queue and sends the pick coordinates
to the cobot. When the cobot has moved to that position, the cobot’s location is verified,
and a close command is sent to the gripper. When the gripper is closed, the PLC sends
the drop coordinate to the cobot, which moves to the drop coordinates. When the cobot’s

two operations. Fastening the Lexium Cobot arm to the steel table that forms the base of
the prototype cell and attaching a Robotiq­2F­85 adaptive gripper to the arm’s tool flange.
Cobot Arm Mounting
Four holes were drilled and threaded in the existing steel table inside the prototype cell,
allowing the arm’s base plate to be bolted down securely. No additional interface plates
or reinforcements were needed.
Gripper Mounting
The Lexium wrist terminates in a standard ISO­9409­1, 50 mm M6 tool flange, identical
to Universal Robots and most other collaborative manipulators [56]. The Robotiq­2F­85
gripper shares this pattern so that it could be bolted on directly with M6 screws [29]. No
bespoke adapter plate or shimming was required.
Conveyor Belt and Vision Camera Mounting
DTU provided the conveyor belt, which came fully assembled. Therefore, it only needed
to be placed in the correct spot inside the prototype cell. Furthermore, the vision camera
was strapped to a clamp overhanging the conveyor belt.
4.1.3
Electrical Integration and Wiring
The prototype’s electrical architecture integrates three of the four subsystems: the Lex­
ium Cobot (with its controller and gripper), the vision system and workstation, and the
Schneider M262 PLC. Two independent 230 VAC supplies are used. One is dedicated
to the cobot and its controller, while the other supplies a 230 VAC/24 VDC power source
for the PLC and gripper. Communication among the devices relies on four primary signal
buses. Figure 4.2 shows the overall electrical architecture and wiring.
Power Distribution
The prototype is powered from two standard 230 VAC wall sockets, with each socket
dedicated to a different part of the system. The first socket supplies power to the Schnei­
der Lexium Cobot Controller via a standard mains lead. Inside the controller, an internal
power supply converts the 230 VAC to 48 VDC and sends it directly to the robot arm
over the factory cable. The second socket feeds a custom DIN­rail power supply that
converts 230 VAC to 24 VDC. This 24 VDC line runs to both the Schneider M262 PLC
and the Robotiq two­finger gripper, which share the same terminal block on the DIN­rail.
Because the current drawn by the PLC and gripper is well below the capacity of the labo­
ratory’s circuit breaker, no extra fuses or breakers were added inside the prototype. This
simple two­socket arrangement keeps the high­power robot arm on its dedicated circuit
and provides the low­voltage devices with a clean and separate 24 VDC source.

Safety Provisions
Safety on this prototype relies on a few basic measures rather than a full engineered
system:
• Physical cage: The entire cell sits inside a safety cage with a closed gate. During
normal operation, the gate stays shut, so no one should be inside the robot’s reach
zone while the system is powered.
• Emergency stop for low­voltage devices: A red emergency­stop button is built
into the custom DIN­rail mount. Pressing it removes all 24 VDC, which instantly
powers down the PLC and the Robotiq gripper. This stop only affects the low­voltage
devices.
• Emergency stop for the cobot: The Lexium Cobot Control Stick has an emergency­
stop button that cuts power to the cobot controller and, in turn, the arm.
No additional safety functions, such as door interlocks or light curtains, have been de­
signed for this prototype. The setup is used only for supervised laboratory testing, where
the cage remains closed and a trained operator is present to hit either stop if needed.

Vision System
As mentioned in Table 4.1, the camera of choice for the prototype was an onn. webcam
with up to 1440p at 30fps, built in ring light and auto focus [55]. That camera was specifi­

No industrial­grade cameras were considered for the prototype due to cost and added
complexity.
The whole image processing algorithm was built in Python using OpenCV and NumPy.
OpenCV is an open­source computer vision and machine learning software library orig­

OpenCV Foundation [57]. NumPy is a general­purpose array­processing package, capa­
ble of working with multidimensional arrays at high performance [58]. The pyModbusTCP
package was used to communicate with the PLC.

steps are:
1. Detect every chicken breast.
2. If any two touch, choose the piece to move.
3. Plan an in­frame drop target that clears the cluster.
4. Send both the pick and place coordinates to the PLC.

Control and Software Architecture
Figure 4.4 shows a simplified flowchart of the PLC Sequence. After the start­up sequence,
the PLC waits for coordinates from the vision system. When coordinates are received, the
robotic arm moves to a resting position. In most cases, the arm is already in the resting
position, so it moves directly above the pickup location. As the speed of the conveyor belt
is constant, the length of the robotic arm’s movement is calculated using the Pythagorean
theorem. The speed of the arm from the current position to the pickup position is then
calculated using the constant speed of the conveyor belt as a reference on when the
pickup object is in the same place.
When the robotic arm is positioned above the pickup location, it moves directly down to it
and closes the gripper. The robotic arm moves directly above the drop­off position when
the gripper is closed. When that position is reached, the arm moves directly down to
the desired drop­off position and opens the gripper. Lastly, when the gripper has been
opened and the object placed back on the conveyor belt, the robotic arm moves back
to the resting position and waits for new coordinates from the vision system, essentially
closing the loop.
Due to the sequential nature of the process, the PLC source code, written in structured
text, is set up as two switch cases. The first one handles the vision handshake, and the
second one controls the robotic arms’ movement. The complete PLC source code can
be found in Appendix B.

5.1
Design Results
5.1.1
Robot Mounting
Chapter 3 proposed two geometries: a side­mounted pedestal (Option 1) and an overhead
steel frame that cantilevers the cobot above the belt (Option 2). Option 2 solves the two
pain points called out.
• Full 360° reach enables the Lexium Cobot to serve larger conveyor belts, allowing
for a smaller version of the arm to be chosen and effectively reducing the system’s
cost.
• Safer human traffic as the arm never swings into the walkway, conforming with
ISO/TS 15066:2016.
The tradeoff is the fabrication and installation cost of the steel frame.
5.1.2
Gripper
Three soft grippers were shortlisted. The Schmalz mGrip was selected because it is the
only candidate that meets all food­hygiene requirements, carries an IP69K rating, swaps
fingers in under thirty seconds, and still achieves 120 picks per minute when driven at five
bar. Competing devices from OnRobot and Robotiq either lacked wash­down protection
or ran too slowly. The pneumatic mGrip does impose compressed­air demand, about
five normal litres per pick, but no other option matched its combination of speed, hygiene
compliance, and gentle handling.
5.1.3
Vision System
All five candidates can identify the ridge where two poultry pieces touch, yet the final
choice must balance reliability on a wet, reflective conveyor with hygienic integration and
straightforward commissioning. Two screening rules, therefore, frame the decision:
• Depth data over grey­scale data.
Prototype trials with an earlier 2­D camera
showed that intensity­only imaging misses are harder to work with and not as reli­
able. Although the Cognex In­Sight 3800 is a far more capable 2­D unit than the one
used in the prototype, the fundamental limitation remains: depth cues are essential.
• On­board processing over host­based processing. Keeping segmentation in­
side a sealed sensor head avoids the extra cabinet, cabling, and wash­down val­
idation that an external PC or GPU demands. This criterion rules out the SICK
Ranger3.
After applying those filters, three self­contained 3­D profilers remain: Cognex In­Sight
3D­L4000, SICK TriSpector 1000, and LMI Gocator 2400. Each meets the throughput
and hygiene requirements. However, the LMI Gocator 2400 is selected because its wider
Collaborative Robotic System for Poultry Processing
51
==PAGE:66==
field of view accommodates both narrow and larger belts that the production conveyors
are expected to be in future lines. This flexibility simplifies mechanical integration and
leaves room for throughput upgrades without changing the vision hardware.
In short, the LMI Gocator offers the needed 3­D accuracy, provides onboard feature ex­
traction, and, thanks to its generous optical coverage, best fits the IQF Freezer conveyor
width range.

Discussion
6.1
Limitations of the Study
• Environmental realism: Tests ran at 20 °C, not the 0­4 °C, high­humidity zone in
front of an IQF freezer. Condensation on lenses and silicone may alter detection
and gripping.
• Product variability: Play­Doh was used to mimic chicken for the study. While it
could be molded into shapes that looked like chicken, it lacked the structural integrity
of an actual chicken, whether boneless or bone­in.
• Single Pick Algorithm: The developed vision system algorithm only took into con­
sideration a single instance of the pick­and­place sequence.
6.2
How well did the prototype meet the requirements?
In chapter 2, Tables 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4, and 2.5, several requirements are put forward for
the system. The main requirements are: The system shall be able to detect every point
where two poultry pieces touch, supply millimeter­accurate pick­and­place coordinates,
and pick and place objects according to the coordinates on the moving conveyor belt.
Detection and Localization
After increasing the vision threshold, every test image in Stages 3­4 was segmented cor­
rectly, even with inconsistent laboratory lighting, thereby conforming to the requirement
FR­1. The simple two­dimensional affine transformation delivered coordinates with an
accuracy well within the 3 mm required by requirements FR­2 and PR­2.
Pick­and­Place Sequence Time Performance
As required in the requirement PR­2, the pick and place sequence shall not take longer
than 2 seconds for every 10 cm the chicken pieces need to be moved. During the pro­
totype testing, that requirement could not be satisfied due to the size of the conveyor
belt used in the prototype. However, the short distance the pieces were moved showed
promising speed, which also confirms the maximum speed stated by Schneider Electric
for the robotic arm. The same can be said about requirement PR­1 and FR­3.
Interface and Integration
Requirements IR­1 and IR­2 posted in Table 2.3, Interface and Integration Requirements,
are passed. The system used both Schneider Electric’s M262 Motion Controller and the
Lexium Cobot. However, requirements IR­3 and IR­4 are not met in the prototype, as
the vision system uses neither an FDA nor an EU Food­grade compliant camera, nor is
it in any way suitable for an industrial system. The Robotiq gripper used in the prototype
is also not FDA or EU food­grade compliant. However, the Schmalz mGrip and the LMI
Gocator 2400, chosen for the overall design, both meet these requirements.

Safety and Regulatory Requirements
The prototype met the requirement SR­1 as the PFL sensors at every joint of the robotic
arm were active during all testing. Requirement SR­2 could not be met in the prototype,
as no integration with the IQF Tunnel Freezer was available.
6.3
Root Causes of the Prototype’s Failure Rate
The leading cause of failure in the prototype testing was the loss of communication with
the Robotic gripper. It is not clear at this point what caused the communication loss, but
likely, the cable used between the gripper and the PLC or connection points on the terminal
block were faulty. One failure was due to the PLC not verifying the robot’s location. The
PLC reading of the robot’s target position was 1 mm off. This can be fixed by adding an
accepted range of points around the expected location, rather than only accepting the
millimeter­accurate position.
6.4
From a Prototype to an Industrial System
Three main things must be done to go from the working prototype to an acceptable indus­
trial system.
1. Gripper Upgrade: As shown during prototype testing, the Robotiq 2­F­85 is un­
suitable for this type of an industrial system. The Schmalz mGrip should be used,
as it conforms to hygienic standards and has been proven to work effectively with
raw poultry. It also simplifies the communication with the PLC as it uses digital and
analog signals for actuation instead of less robust bus communication.
2. Vision System Upgrade: The vision system must be upgraded from the two­dimensional
experimental vision system developed in this prototype to a more robust 3­dimensional
industrial system conforming to the requirements’ IP ratings. The LMI Gocator 2400
proved to be the best option.
3. Robotic Arm Mounting: The arm shall be mounted on a steel gantry overhanging
the conveyor belt. This takes advantage of the entire range of motion of the robotic
arm as it would be able to work 360 degrees compared to 180 degrees when it is
mounted next to the conveyor. This also eliminates the risk of the arm swinging into
a person walking next to the conveyor belt, making the overall system safer to work
around.
Furthermore, the system can be set up in Schneider Electric’s Machine Expert Twin ap­
plication, where a digital twin can be tested with larger conveyor belts at greater speed if
necessary.

7
Conclusion
This thesis demonstrates that a collaborative robotic system can reliably separate touch­
ing poultry portions on the in­feed conveyor of an IQF tunnel freezer, thereby reducing
manual labour while safeguarding throughput and food quality. Over the course of five
months, the work progressed from a requirements analysis through hardware trade­offs,
digital design, and the development of a laboratory­scale prototype. The key outcomes,
implications, and the next steps are summarised below.
Proof­of­concept performance
The prototype, built around a Schneider Lexium Cobot, Modicon M262 PLC, a Robotiq 2F­
85 gripper, and a webcam­based 2­D vision script, achieved an overall 83% success rate
across eight test scenarios. It performed flawlessly when the belt was stationary, and still
passed 87.5% of trials at 1 cm/s and 75% at 4 cm/s, even with two to four articles in view.
After a simple affine calibration, the vision system delivered pick­and­place coordinates
with a target accuracy of 3 mm. These results confirm the core hypothesis that millimetre­
level localisation and gentle robotic manipulation are feasible at food­industry line speeds.
Compliance with functional requirements
All functional requirements (FR­1 ­ FR­4) and most performance requirements (PR­1,
PR­3) were met; only the 2 s/10 cm cycle­time target (PR­2) could not be proven, as the
laboratory conveyor was too short to run a full­speed move. Interface requirements IR­1
and IR­2 were satisfied by basing the cell entirely on Schneider hardware, while hygienic
requirements IR­3 and IR­4 were deferred to the industrial design stage.
Root causes of residual errors
The dominant failure mode, 67% of all misses, was a momentary loss of Modbus­RTU
communication between PLC and gripper, causing the gripper to close late or not at all. A
single instance of a 1 mm pose­verification mismatch also revealed the need for tolerance
bands rather than single­point checks.
Prototype limitations
Three constraints temper the laboratory results:
• Tests ran at 20 °C, not the 0­4 °C, high­humidity freezer in­feed environment.
• Play­Doh surrogates lacked the mechanical properties of real bone­in or boneless
chicken.
• The vision algorithm handled only one pick per camera frame, leaving multi­clump
resolution for future iterations.
Path to an industrial system
Bridging the gap to a factory­ready solution requires replacing the experimental gripper
with an IP69K Schmalz mGrip, upgrading to a 3­D blue­laser profiler (LMI Gocator 2400)
for speckle­free segmentation at up to 5 kHz, and installing the cobot on an over­belt
Collaborative Robotic System for Poultry Processing
59
==PAGE:74==
gantry to unlock its complete 360° workspace and remove swing­radius hazards. Each
recommendation aligns with the original requirement tables and can be validated in a
digital twin before physical roll­out.
Economic Viability of the System
The cost analysis indicates that the collaborative robotic system yields a simple payback
of approximately two years in high­ and mid­wage poultry­processing markets. Even with
conservative upkeep allowances, the system outperforms manual labor, confirming its
commercial relevance in KAPP Skaginn’s traditional customer base. Conversely, in low­
wage regions, the payback stretches toward a decade. Here, adoption must be justified
by secondary drivers such as hygiene standards, labour scarcity, or night­shift avoidance.
Overall, the system is economically attractive wherever wages exceed roughly 1.900 ISK.
Future research
Immediate extensions include multi­instance pick scheduling to increase success rates
on densely packed belts, condensation­robust optics tests in a chilled, humid chamber,
and closed­loop force control to accommodate bone­in pieces.
Final reflections
The study demonstrates that a tightly integrated vision­guided collaborative robot can con­
sistently and gently separate raw poultry within the spatial, hygienic, and safety constraints
of modern processing lines. By automating a repetitive and ergonomically challenging
task, the proposed system promises to reduce labour by up to 50% and create a modular
template for similar applications across protein processing. While further engineering is
required for industrial hardening, the results provide a robust technical foundation that
KAPP Skaginn and its partners can confidently build upon.
