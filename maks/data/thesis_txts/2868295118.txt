Abstract
BrightôÙfield microscopy is a widely used, nonôÙinvasive imaging technique, yet its perceived
limitations in depth resolution have historically restricted its application for detailed 3D
analysis. This thesis explores the potential of deep learning to unlock hidden structural
and functional information within brightôÙfield microscopy volumes by translating them into
fluorescent counterparts. The aim is to quantify and enhance the utility of brightôÙfield
imaging for biological research, particularly in evaluating the growth and behavior of canôÙ
cer cells and organoids.
Through the development of inôÙhouse deep learning architectures, including 3D UôÙNet
and 3D Deeplabv3, and the design of specialized datasets, this work demonstrates the
feasibility of brightôÙfieldôÙtoôÙfluorescent image translation. A systematic evaluation, highôÙ
lights the importance of both quantitative metrics and qualitative visual analysis in ensurôÙ
ing biologically meaningful results. The findings reveal that brightôÙfield microscopy, when
paired with advanced computational methods, can provide valuable insights traditionally
reserved for fluorescent imaging.
This study showcases the potential of brightôÙfield microscopy as a scalable, costôÙeffective
alternative for 3D biological imaging, offering new possibilities for functional screening and
personalized medicine. Future work will focus on expanding dataset diversity, exploring
advanced architectures, and refining the integration of deep learning for quantitative bioôÙ
logical analysis.

Introduction
Recent advancements in microscopy and machine learning have revolutionized the field
of biomedical imaging, enabling researchers to extract unprecedented levels of detail from
cellular and tissueôÙlevel structures. Among these, 3D imaging techniques such as brightôÙ
field and fluorescent microscopy provide complementary insights into the organization
and behavior of cells and organoids.
Traditionally, brightôÙfield microscopy has not been considered a true 3D modality, as it
lacks the depth resolution inherent to techniques like confocal microscopy. Without optiôÙ
cal sectioning or depthôÙspecific illumination, brightôÙfield microscopy has historically been
viewed as incapable of capturing quantifiable depth information. In this traditional perôÙ
spective, brightôÙfield image stacks are seen as mere snapshots of cellular morphology
across different focal planes, offering limited insight into fine 3D structural details.
However, a novel perspective is emerging, one which this thesis adheres to. Research
has demonstrated that brightôÙfield microscopy indeed contains latent depth information
that can be harnessed with the aid of deep learning models such as UôÙNets. These modôÙ
els, designed for tasks like translating imaging modalities, have revealed that brightôÙfield
microscopy can provide meaningful depth resolution, challenging traditional assumptions.
For example, AIôÙdriven approaches have shown that nonôÙinvasive, labelôÙfree brightôÙfield
microscopy imaging can yield much richer insights than previously thought, effectively
capturing 3D structural details when paired with advanced computational techniques [2].
This shift in perspective underpins the motivation for this work and sets the foundation for
exploring the untapped potential of brightôÙfield microscopy in 3D imaging.
1.1
Cellular organization imaging
The imaging of cellular organization is a cornerstone of modern biological research, proôÙ
viding critical insights into the structure, function, and behavior of cells and tissues. AdôÙ
vancements in microscopy techniques have enabled researchers to explore cellular proôÙ
cesses with unprecedented detail, driving breakthroughs in fields such as oncology, deôÙ
velopmental biology, and regenerative medicine. Among these techniques, brightôÙfield
and fluorescent microscopy have emerged as two complementary modalities, each with
its own unique strengths and limitations [1].
1.1.1
BrightôÙfield microscopy
BrightôÙfield microscopy is one of the most widely used imaging techniques in biological
research. It relies on simple illumination, where light passes through a sample, and conôÙ
trast is generated by the natural absorption or scattering properties of the specimen. This
method is particularly effective for observing cell morphology, tissue structure, and genôÙ
eral cellular features without requiring fluorescent labeling, making it ideal for nonôÙinvasive
imaging. However, its reliance on intrinsic contrast often makes it challenging to distinôÙ
guish specific features in complex samples [3].
BrightôÙfield imaging has been a cornerstone of biological research for decades, providing
a quick, costôÙeffective method for researchers to visually assess and analyze biological
samples. The images produced are similar to those viewed through a simple laboratory
microscope, which has been a staple of biological research for over 50 years. However,
brightôÙfield images are notoriously hard to quantify and segment, and they have traditionôÙ
ally been regarded as lacking 3D information.

1.1.2
Fluorescent microscopy
Fluorescent microscopy has revolutionized biological imaging by enabling researchers to
visualize specific molecules within cells and tissues with remarkable specificity and resoôÙ
lution. By tagging samples with fluorescent dyes or proteins, this technique makes it posôÙ
sible to observe dynamic processes, structural details, and molecular interactions that
are otherwise invisible under traditional light microscopy. It has become indispensable
for studying cellular functions, identifying protein localization, and understanding comôÙ
plex biological mechanisms [4]. However, fluorescence microscopy comes with several
limitations, including the requirement for advanced instrumentation and timeôÙconsuming
sample preparation. Furthermore, significant phototoxicity and photobleaching can disôÙ
rupt samples, creating a tradeoff between data quality and the time scales available for
liveôÙcell imaging.
Additionally, the number of simultaneous fluorescent tags is inherently restricted due to
spectrum saturation and potential impacts on cell health, limiting the number of parallel laôÙ
bels for joint imaging. In contrast, brightôÙfield microscopy techniques, such as brightôÙfield,
phase contrast, and differential interference contrast imaging, offer a lowôÙcost, labelôÙfree
alternative with reduced phototoxicity and simplified sample preparation. While fluoresôÙ
cent microscopy remains the gold standard for specific molecular imaging, the rapid adôÙ
vancements in brightôÙfield imaging, supported by deep learning models, suggest a growôÙ
ing potential for these methods to complement or even replace fluorescent techniques in
certain applications. By leveraging the strengths of both modalities, researchers aim to
achieve a more comprehensive understanding of biological phenomena while minimizing
experimental challenges [2].

Data Pipelines
In this section of the project, the focus shifts towards data pipelines, their significance
for our case, and how they are utilized in this project. Data is the lifeblood of any sucôÙ
cessful AI project, and a wellôÙengineered data pipeline is the key to harnessing its power.
Data pipelines are complex chains of activities that manipulate data where the output of
one component becomes the input of the other, thereby allowing smooth and automated
flow of data from source to destination [12]. This automated system acts as the engine,
seamlessly moving data through various stages and transforming it from raw form into
actionable insights. It starts with data collection, gathering information from the source.
The pipeline then tackles the raw data, performing cleaning and transformation. This
step removes inconsistencies and irrelevant information and transforms the data into a
format suitable for analysis, visualization tools, machine learning (ML) or deep learning
(DL) models [13].
Following this small introduction to data pipelines, the nature of our data, their origin, and
the process of ingesting them from the source (microscope server) to the target platform
(Google Cloud Storage of Metsystem) will be thoroughly analyzed.
2.1
Data Source
Primarily, the confocal microscope [14] which is used to image spatially aligned brightôÙfield
and fluorescent images is located in a laboratory, inside the Bio Innovation Institute [15],
where the BioStartup is located . After each experiment, the data are being stored in a
server connected to the microscope. This server is not allowed to be on the Internet for
security reasons. If a researcher wanted to get their hands on the data, they could connect
to a desktop outside the laboratory which has a LAN connection to the aforementioned
server. Yet again this desktop does not belong to the BioStartup and cannot have access
to the internet. In an effort to tackle the situation above, the BioStartupôÇs main computer
was moved close to the laboratory. The computer then was connected to the server via
LAN and to the Ethernet of the building accordingly. With this connectivity the computer
had access to both the serverãs data and a stable connection to the internet as well.
Informatively, the Ethernet connection was estimated to provide an average uploading
speed of 0.8ôÙ1 Gigabyte per second, which is essential for the loading data pipelines.

3.1
CNNs
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a class of deep learning models specifically
designed to process structured gridôÙlike data, such as images. They excel in imageôÙ
related tasks by automatically learning spatial features like edges, textures, and shapes
through convolutional operations, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering.
CNNs are inherently translationôÙinvariant, making them robust to variations in input, and
their layered architecture enables the extraction of both lowôÙlevel details and highôÙlevel
concepts. By capturing nonlinear relationships over large image areas, CNNs signifiôÙ
cantly outperform traditional machine learning methods in tasks such as classification,
segmentation, and imageôÙtoôÙimage regression. This combination of automated feature
learning, spatial awareness, and versatility has established CNNs as a cornerstone of
modern computer vision and image processing applications. [2].
The core for learning these spatial features, e.g. edges, corners, textures, or more abôÙ
stract shapes, are manifold and successive transformations of the input data (convoluôÙ
tions) on different spatial scales (e.g. via pooling operations). This facilitates identifying
and combining both lowôÙlevel features and highôÙlevel concepts [26].
CNNs have been shown to outperform other stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart models in basic image recogôÙ
nition and have been used in biomedical imaging for a wide range of tasks including image
classification, object segmentation, and estimation of image transformations [2]. These
are some of the key motives for the author of this thesis to implement variations of CNN
architectures, in order to translate 3D brightôÙfield volumes to 3D annotated fluorescent
volumes.

3.3.1
Model architecture
For this project, a 3D adaptation of the DeeplabV3 architecture was developed to address
the unique challenges of volumetric image to image translation. The proposed model
combines the strengths of the Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) module, a hallmark
of DeeplabV3, with the ability to process 3D input data such as volumetric medical imôÙ
ages. This architecture is designed to capture multiôÙscale contextual information while
maintaining computational efficiency. Below is a detailed breakdown of the architecture:
1. Backbone: Feature Extraction
The backbone of the proposed architecture is a custom convolutional neural netôÙ
work tailored for 3D data. It consists of a sequence of 3D convolutional layers inôÙ
corporated with Instance Normalization and ReLU activation functions. The feature
extraction process starts with a 3D convolutional layer with a large receptive field,
followed by progressively deeper layers with increasing channel dimensions. Each
convolutional operation reduces the spatial resolution while extracting hierarchical
features, enabling the model to capture both lowôÙlevel and highôÙlevel representaôÙ
tions of the input data.
2. ASPP3D Module: MultiôÙScale Context Capture
At the core of the model lies the ASPP3D module, which is an extension of the
ASPP module from DeeplabV3. This module employs parallel branches of dilated
3D convolutions with different dilation rates (6, 12, and 18) to capture multiôÙscale
contextual information. In addition, a global average pooling branch is incorporated
to provide global context. Each branch outputs features that are concatenated and
fused to form a rich representation of the input data.
The ASPP3D module is particularly wellôÙsuited for 3D data, as it effectively captures
patterns across varying spatial scales and volumetric structures. The global average
pooling branch ensures that even the largest structures in the input are represented,
while the dilated convolutions focus on capturing finer details.
3. Decoder: Feature Refinement

==PAGE:30==
To refine the segmentation output, a simple decoder was added to the architecture.
The decoder consists of 3D convolutional layers with Instance Normalization and
ReLU activations. These layers reduce the dimensionality of the ASPP output and
project it into the desired number of segmentation classes. While not as complex as
the decoder in DeeplabV3+, this module provides an additional level of refinement,
improving the accuracy of segmentation boundaries.
4. UpôÙsampling and Output
The final prediction is generated by upôÙsampling the decoder output to match the
original input dimensions. This is achieved using trilinear interpolation, which enôÙ
sures smooth and accurate spatial scaling. The combination of the ASPP3D modôÙ
ule and the decoder allows the model to produce highôÙquality predictions, even for
small objects and fine boundaries, without excessive computational overhead.
Unlike the original DeeplabV3 [24], which was designed for 2D image segmentation, the
proposed model extends the framework to 3D volumetric data. This adaptation involves
replacing 2D convolutions with 3D convolutions throughout the network, allowing it to capôÙ
ture spatial relationships in three dimensions. The inclusion of the ASPP3D module and
a lightweight decoder ensures a balance between computational efficiency and segmenôÙ
tation accuracy.
Our aim is to train this inôÙhouse developed architecture with 3D datasets as the ones
described in the Data section of this paper.
We intent to investigate the advantages
of this architecture for applications involving 3D data, such as medical imaging, where
the segmentation of cells, tissues and organoids, requires fineôÙgrained detail and multiôÙ
scale context. By leveraging the ASPP3D module and a tailored backbone, we desire
to demonstrate strong performance in translating volumetric structures while maintaining
computational feasibility.

3.4.3
Loss Functions
A fundamental learning process for Machine learning and Deep Learning algorithms and
models is the loss function. The loss function is a mathematical process that quantifies
the error margin (difference) between the predicted output of a model and the actual target
value (ground truth). It serves as a measure of the model error or performance during
training [35]. The goal of the model is to minimize the loss function, which is done by
adjusting its weights and biases through a process called backôÙpropagation combined
with an optimization algorithm. Given an artificial neural network and an error function,
the method calculates the gradient of the error function with respect to the weights of the
neural network [36].
During the modelsã development phase, an array of loss functions, namely Mean Squared
Error, Huber (Smoothl1), and SSIM, were employed to train and evaluate the deep learnôÙ

anisms that influence the training process and subsequent imageôÙtoôÙimage translation
performance.
1. Mean Squared Error Loss (MSE): MSE is perhaps the simplest and most comôÙ
mon loss function. It is computed as the squared difference between the modelãs
predictions and the ground truth, averaged across the entire dataset. The MSE will
never be negative, since we are always squaring the errors. The MSE loss funcôÙ
tion formula is similar to the one described in the metrics section. Its most profound
advantage is ensuring that our trained model has no outlier predictions with huge
errors, since it puts larger weight on these errors due to the squaring part of the funcôÙ
tion. One drawback of MSE is that if our model makes a single very bad prediction,
the squaring part of the function magnifies the error [37].
2. Huber Loss (Smooth L1): Huber Loss offers the best of both worlds by combining
the benefits of MSE (penalizing large errors) and MAE (robustness to noise and
