Abstract
The heterogeneous setting of glacial deposits consisting of a mixture of sediments including low-
permeability fractured tills embedded and underlain with higher permeability sand lenses/layers,
are frequently encountered in Denmark and are especially problematic when contaminants are
spilt at the surface of such environments.
Although low-permeability deposits like clay tills
were in the past viewed as geological boundaries to underlying aquifer systems, fractures can
facilitate fast vertical transport of contamination to the underlying aquifers and the low perme-
ability matrix can store contaminants, contributing to the long-term persistence of groundwater
contaminant plumes. Additionally, the presence of connected sand stringers and horizontal frac-
tures can also facilitate preferential contaminant flow in a horizontal direction. It is important
to consider these processes when assessing the risk to underlying aquifers at contaminated sites.
The method that is traditionally used for risk assessment and was also applied in this study
was transect method for determining contaminant mass discharge. The method was used at
a contaminated site located in Vassigerød, North Zæland, where contamination consisting of
chlorinated solvents was detected in a complex heterogeneous setting of glacial deposits.
The study consisted of site investigation to characterize the contaminant source, flow and
transport through the subsurface. This part of the study revealed important information about
the geological setting of the site. First of all the clay till was reinterpreted to be sandier or even
sand till, and secondly, a stratigraphic unit of ice-marginal deposits was delineated between the
upper supra-glacial deposits and bottom out-wash sand aquifer. Based on this finding, some of
the well screens, which were prior believed to be screened in the clay till layer, were actually
found to be located in the unit of ice-marginal deposits. Based on the measured and estimated
hydraulic conductivities, the transport in this unit was found to be horizontal. These findings
were incorporated into a new hydrogeological model, which was used together with contaminant
extent and mass characterization, for the formulation of conceptual site model.
Contaminant mass discharge was determined in both horizontal and vertical direction. Two
vertical transects located in the source area revealed heterogeneity of contaminant mass discharge
in the ice-marginal deposits and that transect method, in this case, could have missed some
volumes of higher contaminant concentrations. Additionally, the screens used for groundwater
sampling were partially screened in two layers with different hydraulic conductivities, which could
also affect the measured concentrations in the groundwater samples. The use of this method
could, therefore, be improved, with a finer sampling grid of monitoring wells at the transect.
At the same site, vertical control planes were placed to estimate vertical contaminant flux
through a fractured matrix of melt-out till in the supraglacial deposits. Since the source area was
paved, one scenario used estimated value of net-infiltration for quantifying mass flux. The second
scenario was based on the observed vertical hydraulic gradient between the supraglacial deposits
and ice-marginal unit, and this parameter was used to estimate vertical flux. Comparison of
the two scenarios revealed contaminant mass discharge values that differed by two orders of
magnitude, with the estimations using vertical hydraulic gradient being significantly higher.

Abstract
This showed that at the investigated site, quantifying vertical contaminant mass discharge is
challenging and sensitive with respect to deciding on which parameter to use for calculation. The
use of wrong parameter could result in under- or overestimation of the risk at the contaminated
sites.
Finally, the comparison of horizontal and vertical contaminant mass discharge revealed that a
large amount of contamination was discharged from the fractured melt-out till to the underlying
layer of ice-marginal deposits and out-wash sand aquifer.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Groundwater presents the largest freshwater resource in the world and anthropogenic ac-
tivities resulting in pollution from domestic, agricultural or industrial sources are putting
it at risk. In EU groundwater is included in the Water Framework Directive, setting goals
with regards to preventing and controlling its contamination (Hinsby et al., 2008).
Glacial deposits present a complex mixture of deposits with different hydrogeologic
properties (Stephenson et al., 1988) and are found at many contaminated sites in Denmark
and other parts of Northern hemisphere, which is why contaminant fate and transport in
these settings have been widely investigated in the past (Damgaard, 2012; C. M. Chris-
tiansen, 2010; Manoli et al., 2012; Kessler et al., 2010; McKay et al., 1993). In glacial
deposits contaminants will be preferentially transported within these high permeability
zones like natural fractures, sand lenses and stringers. However these high permeability
bodies may only present a small percentage of the total formation. Hence the zone with
lower hydraulic conductivity, such as clay matrix can store significant amounts of contam-
inants, resulting from diffusion, sorption and slow advection in the matrix. This stored
mass can after depletion or removal of the source, serve as a long term source, where the
retarded mass will undergo back-diffusion from the clay matrix to the underlying aquifer
(Parker et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2015; Falta and Wang, 2017).
Contaminant mass discharge estimations can serve as important information when for
example, assessing the risk of the contaminant plume to the downstream water supply,
evaluating the efficiency source zone remediation technologies or quantifying natural at-
tenuation at contaminated sites (Einarson and Mackay, 2001). The most straightforward
traditional method for estimating contaminant mass discharge is measuring concentra-
tions and groundwater flux along control transects perpendicular to the groundwater
flow direction. Needless to say, different method for gathering such data exist (Interstate
Technology Regulatory Council, 2014). Direct methods include point scale sampling from
fully screened or multilevel wells, and integral groundwater investigation approach, where
mass discharge is determined with a pumping test. Placement of the control planes; verti-
cal and horizontal direction, sampling of representative samples and assigning sub-areas
in the transect are limitations of the transect method.
The transect method was a subject of several research papers, where the uncertainty
of mass discharge estimations was quantified (Kübert and Finkel, 2006; Troldborg et
al., 2010; Troldborg et al., 2012). Overall, high heterogeneity of the subsurface can cause
severe limits for measuring contaminant mass and mass discharge (Guilbeault et al., 2005).

1 Introduction
The factors that are found to affect spatial heterogeneity of the mass flux in the control
plane include spatial distribution of contaminants in the source zone, specific conditions
controlling the contaminant discharge from non-aqueous phase into groundwater and
spatial and temporal variations of flow field (Kübert and Finkel, 2006). The accurate
mass discharge estimates using traditional methods are therefore limited by sampling
density in the control plane and conceptual model uncertainty (Troldborg et al., 2010;
Troldborg et al., 2012).
Indirect approaches for determining mass discharge consist of solute transport mod-
els. A modeling tool predicting vertical transport from a source zone to an underlying
aquifer was recently developed, which can be used for risk assessment in the fractured till
(Chambon et al., 2011).
An essential part of the risk assessment is a conceptual site model which combines in-
formation on the subsurface conditions, both geological and hydrogeological, contaminant
source, contaminant distribution and transformation processes (Sara, 2003; Thomsen et
al., 2016). The construction of such a conceptual model is based on the data collection
from various sources, which can be challenging due to the lack of data, misinterpretation
of the site conditions as well as because of the simplified interpretations of the complex
geology and hydrogeology, contaminant distribution and fate processes (Thomsen et al.,
2016).
This paper aims to improve understanding of the contaminant mass discharge through
glacial deposits using the traditional transect method and applying it at a contaminated
site consisting of heterogeneous clay till geology.
1.2
Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to identify and assess different approaches of determining con-
taminant mass discharge through heterogeneous subsurface consisting of glacial deposits.
This is achieved by:
1. Performing a site investigation for the characterization of contaminant source, flow
and transport through glacial deposits
2. Building a geological and hydrogeological model in order to develop conceptual
understanding of the flow, transport and contamination at the site
3. Estimating contaminant mass discharge using different calculation methods and an
analytical model
The contaminated site that is investigated is located in Vassingerød, North Zealand
in a typical clay till geology.

2.1
Glacial deposits
Large amounts of ground-water resources in Denmark are covered by glacial deposits,
which were primarily deposited during the repeated glaciations while the Weichselian
glaciation (Houmark-Nielsen, 2004).
Glacial deposits describe a mixture of sediment
types with different hydrogeological properties and hydraulic conductivities varying by
several orders of magnitude (Klint and Gravesen, 1999). These material include poorly
sorted and stratified sediments, like tills, proglacial and supraglacial deposits addressed
as diamictons. Another group of glacial deposits are glaciofluvial and glaciolacustrine
deposits, wich have been deposited by lakes in association with glacial activity, or by
streams linked to glacier ice (Stephenson et al., 1988). The classification, related to the
deposition processes and hydrogeological properties of the two groups of deposits was
briefly summarized in the literature review (Putrih-Jeza, 2017).
The mode of deposition during the re-glaciation is an important feature affecting:
- sorting
- stratification
- surface form
- texture of the deposits
- presence of fractures
- grain size of the material
- consolidation
- lateral continuity
- potential to transmit water

Glacier stagnation when the glacier reaches the maximum extent, the advance stops.
The debris-covered dead ice zone starts to melt and releases melt-out water and
material, described as meltout basal till.
Glacier retreat The dead ice-cores subside slowly, causing slumping and collapsing of

due to the irregular melt-out and complex mixture with till, they are discontinuous.

timeter to many meters, surrounded by low-permeability tills. During the ice-cores’
collapse and the material from the ice above, normal faulting can facilitate normal
fault and extension fractures.

The transport and fate of the contaminants, which are released in the subsurface is
governed by complex physical and chemical processes, including advection, dispersion,
diffusion and sorption. In low permeability media, matrix-diffusion is an important pro-
cess to be considered. It takes place when there is abrupt difference in concentration
between transmissive zones (sand layers or fractures, e.g.) and zones with comparatively
low permeability (clay tills, e.g.). In the initial phase after the contaminant release, the
contaminants are preferentially transported through the layers of higher hydraulic per-
meability. With time, the dissolved contaminants start diffusing from zones of higher
concentrations to zones of lower ones. This is the result of concentration gradients be-
tween high and low permeability zones. After some time, the concentration difference can
be displaced or the contaminants are removed from the high permeability zones. This can
lead to release of the contamination trapped in the low permeability zone, which is the
result of back diffusion process and presents a challenge to remediation efforts (Chapman
and Parker, 2005).

risk assessment, the key parameters controlling the flow through porous media have to
be understood. The physical characteristics that are important to understand in this

Hydraulic conductivity K is defined by the soil-type. If well sorted, soils have high K
and can be used for groundwater extraction, whereas unsorted deposits (glacial tills, e.g.)
have a varying composition, which makes it difficult to determine K (Svensson, 2014).
Several methods exist for estimating K. Indirect measures are based on grain size
distribution. Field methods that give estimations of K of the soil directly adjacent to
the screened part of the well include pumping tests and slug tests. Vienken and Dietrich
(2011) compared different empirical formulas for determining K from grain size analysis
data and compared those results with directly measured K, using direct push slug test,
where estimated K from grain size analysis were significantly smaller than estimates from
slug tests. The general conclusion of the research was, that the estimation of K from
grain size data is a suitable method for the initial assessment, but not sufficient for high-
resolution assessment of hydraulic conductivity variations.

2.3.2
Slug test
Slug tests are simple in situ methods for determining hydraulic parameters of the aquifer
by water level observations in a single well (Rosenshein and Bennett, 2013). Compared
to indirect methods, the measurements are assumed to be more accurate, since hydraulic
conductivity is measured in-situ. The basic principle of these tests is a rapid removal or
addition of water to create a change in pressure or fluid head. Results are later analyzed by
using different interpretative models, depending on the theoretical assumptions regarding
aquifer characteristics and hydraulic typology.
These tests are cheaper and faster than other methods such as pumping tests, can
easily be implemented, require short test duration, there is no need for post-treatment
of a large volume of extracted contaminated water and the disturbance of the aquifer
flow system is relatively low (Yeh et al., 2008). Additionally, performing a series of slug
tests in a relatively closely spaced wells can give a more detailed information about the
horizontal and vertical variations in hydraulic properties at a site, compared to pumping
tests (Butler, 1997).
The important parameter for realistic aquifer properties estimation from slug tests
data, which was discussed in the literature is the insufficient well development. It re-
sults in drilling-induced disturbances and can impact the slug test results (Butler, 1997).
Specifically, during the drilling of the well a large amount of drilling debris is generated,
consisting of fine material fixed in the proximity of the well, having different hydraulic
properties than the subsurface formation (either lower or higher K). The presence of well
skin can be recognized by comparing slug test results with other data, which was, in this
case, the K values from grain size distribution. Additionally, the dynamic-skin effect can
be seen observing the shape of the early-time displacement curve. Secondly, it is advised
to compare the theoretical and measured values of the initial displacement values. For
example, in small-diameter wells, the radius of the casing will have an effect, since the
velocity in the casing can be great enough to produce additional head losses that can also
affect the analysis of response data (Butler, 1997).
For the purpose of this study two types of slug tests were used: the slug-in and
the vacuum slug test (Bjerg et al., 1992). Further information about both methods are
summarized in Appendix B.

It was recognized that the complexity of the fractured subsurface presents a challenge
in terms of field measurements that can identify geometric and hydraulic properties of
this kind of media (Neuman, 2005). The contaminant mass flux through fractured media
can be estimated by using the values of contaminant concentrations from wells installed
in fractured media and depth average groundwater flows (Klammler et al., 2016)). Field
method for determining contaminant mass discharge through fractures has been devel-
oped by Klammler et al. (2016). The tool is designed in a similar was as passive flux
meters, however it is applicable for un-screened wells in rock fractured aquifers. These
kind of settings have different fracture geometry and distribution than fractured low-
permeability media like clay tills.
Alternatively to field methods, modeling tools have been developed for assessing the
risk of contamination leaching through fractured tills.
Even though data on fracture
geometry and location is usually unavailable, fractures can be taken into account using
different kinds of models. Firstly the concept of equivalent porous media with equivalent
conductivity and porosity can be used(Chambon et al., 2011). This method only accounts
for advection and dispersion when simulating contaminant flow through featured media,
and does not account for the contaminant exchange between matrix and fracture (Jør-
gensen et al., 2004). Discrete fracture models are also available, where fracture network
is simplified by using a single fracture surrounded by a semi-infinite matrix, where the
relevant processes controlling the contamination are all included in the model. Most re-
cent model has been developed by Chambon et al. (2011) which takes into account the
advection through the fracture, exchange between the fracture and the matrix, sorption
and molecular diffusion in the low permeability matrix (Chambon et al., 2011). In ad-
dition, they allow simulation of different source geometry and history, including source
overlying fractured media of known or unknown duration, as well as secondary source sim-
ulated as homogeneous concentration in the porous matrix, when source is not defined
historically or geometrically. The comparison of the simulation results using both the
equivalent porous media and discrete fracture model showed that the discrete fracture
tool is more consistent with the observed fracture transport mechanisms than equivalent
porous media tool (Chambon et al., 2011). Additionally their simulation results were
consistent with the field observations (Chambon et al., 2011). It should be mentioned

The methods that are available and have been used for measuring contaminant dis-

can be applied at a point-scale within a screened well (Kueper et al., 2014) and later used

Additionally integral pump tests can be used,
which give a mass discharge estimate by pumping water using fully screened wells from
the contaminated aquifers.
This method is not further discussed in this thesis, since
pumping large amounts of water from heterogeneous media with low permeability zones
such as clay tills is not suitable.
The passive flux meters consist of permeable, sorbent pack which can be used in the
transect of screened wells perpendicular to the groundwater flow (Hatfield et al., 2004).
The measurement is based on the principle of depletion of tracer mass in the packer as a
result of groundwater flow through the passive flux meter by which groundwater flux can
be calculated. The contaminant flux is calculated from the contaminant mass captured
on the sorbent. This method was successfully applied at different contaminated sites
(Guilbeault et al., 2005; Johnston et al., 2014; Brooks et al., 2008). In all cases this
method was proven beneficial in providing more detailed information on spatial patterns
in contaminant mass discharge at the source control plane.
The traditional method for calculating mass discharge is using multilevel samplers
or the regular groundwater sampling in the transect of screened wells at multiple loca-
tions and depths, together with estimated groundwater flux based on measurements of
hydraulic conductivity and local hydraulic gradient (Guilbeault et al., 2005).
An important stage of risk assessment and preforming mass discharge estimations is
developing of a conceptual model of the site, where one contaminated site can be equally
well characterized with different site conceptual models(Thomsen et al., 2016; Troldborg
et al., 2012; Troldborg et al., 2010). It was recognized that an important feature to con-
sider is the uncertainty concerning the conceptual site model, resulting from lack of data,
miss-interpretation of the site conditions, simplifications included to characterize com-
plex processes such as heterogeneous geological and hydro-geological conditions, complex
contaminant source geometry and transformation processes, e.g. degradation.

4.2.2
Grain size distribution
Sediment samples for grain size distribution were collected during the drilling and stored
in closed plastic bags. Samples were 400 to 500 g and covered each stratigraphic layer.
Grain size distribution was carried out in April 2017 through sieving and laser diffraction
method in laboratory of DTU Environment. The sieving procedure consisted of three
parts:
1. The samples were dried in the oven at 60◦C for 3 days to remove the water content
and then, washed in a fine-grained sieve to remove the fraction smaller than 0.063
mm (silt and clay). The fine fraction was collected in a suspension flask.
2. Sieving the coarse fraction (above 0.063 mm), which required again drying that part
of the material and weighing the sample and later using a standard set of sieves
(2.0-1.0-0.5-0.355-0.250-0.180-0.125-0.090 and 0.063 mm), to obtain percentages of
material passing through each sieve. The weight of the fraction remaining on each
sieve was recorded. The fraction smaller than 0.063 mm remaining in the bottom
pan was added to the suspension flask.
3. Determining the particle size distribution from below 0.063 mm to 0.002 mm. This
was done using laser diffraction method with a Mastersizer2000 device, available at
DTU Civil Engineering. The wet samples were shaken before adding the sample in
the sample dispersion unit, where samples were prepared and after delivered to the
optical bench, in which the scattering pattern of the particles was captured, and
the size of the particles was calculated. The device then reported the fractions of
grain sizes between the 0.063 mm to 0.002 mm.

The data obtained during the two sets of slug tests collected with the data logger at the

the pressure values in the falling phase of the test from the initial static pressure value
established after reaching equilibrium. This data was than plotted with time to obtain
input data used for estimating K using AQTESOLV®.
As already mentioned, important feature that has to be considered during interpre-
tation of slug test data is well filter pack around the screen of the well, which might
affect the test results. Geological settings with moderate to low K will have lower per-
meability than the sand or gravel pack around the well (Butler et al., 1996). Drainage
and resaturation of the well filter pack can significantly influence the results from the
slug test. Therefore, for slug-in tests the theoretical volume of water required to raise the
water level to the actual obtained displacement level was calculated to obtain information
about the volume of water draining from the well filter pack.

of abrasion during the lodgement and deformation processes, while basal melt-out till has
a higher content of medium sand to very coarse silt (Klint et al., 2010). In this case the
precise differentiation of these two sub-glacial deposits was not possible. However, the
grain size analysis indicates subglacial sand till.

were chosen. The representative hydraulic conductivity values for the layers were chosen

conductivities as the measured ones. Additionally, they were obtained for all the defined
layers at the site.
Based on the findings from grain size analysis re-evaluated local geological model was
made (See Figures 5.6, 5.7 and 5.8, with Figure 5.5 showing the respective transects at
the filed site). The subsurface at the investigated site is assumed to consist of 4 geological

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
6.1
Site investigation and development of the site
conceptual model
In this report, the site investigation of a contaminated site with a glacial till setting
resulted in the re-interpretation of the local geological and hydro-geological model. A
site conceptual model was developed based on the findings. Re-interpretation was done
by thorough characterization of the geology, based on particle size distribution, direct
and indirect measurements of hydraulic conductivity, borehole log interpretations and
soil sample descriptions, and literature knowledge concerning glacial deposits.
The existing geological model which was based on borehole log data alone indicated a
relatively complex coupled clay-till sand lenses overlaying a sand aquifer system. During
the re-interpretation of the geology, the site was identified as originating from a dead-ice
environment, with a very complex subsurface stratigraphy.
The grading curves obtained with the sieving analysis, and values of hydraulic con-
ductivity using both, the slug tests and the empirical equations based on grain size
distribution, suggested: (a) the content of clay in the subsurface is generally low and the
fine fraction is higher in silt, (b) the grading curves indicate a silty-sand flow till layer
present in the upper part of the bottom out-wash sand, (c) the hydraulic conductivities
in the subsurface range from 5 · 10−7m/s to 2.5 · 10−5 m/s.
Compared to the literature values for clay tills found in Denmark, ranging from 3 ·
10−11m/s to 1.3·10−4m/s (C. Christiansen and Wood, 2006), this site is identified as being
at the high end of these scale. This indicates that what was interpreted as clay till is, in
reality, more sandier or even sand till/sediment. The re-interpretation of the subsurface
geology at the site revealed the layer of ice-marginal deposits, which was of a different
type than indicated in the existing borehole logs. Some of the well screens are located in
different layers than indicated in the existing borehole logs. Specifically, well B402, B404
and B408, which were prior believed to be screened in clay till layer were found to be
located in ice-marginal deposits. This finding is important for the understanding of the
transport processes, since the ice-marginal deposit layer has a much higher conductivity
than the till matrix above and therefore facilitates transport of contaminants in the
horizontal direction.
Based on the literature knowledge concerning this type of setting, it is assumed that
the melt-out till and sub-glacial till matrix embedded with discontinuous sand and gravel
lenses overlying the ice-marginal flow till is fractured which facilitates vertical flow. How-

ever, this assumption is subject to uncertainty, since 1) field observation of fracture the
network was not possible, 2) fractures that are formed in dead-ice environments are as-
sumed to be random, making it challenging to estimate the degree of natural fracturing
in these deposits (C. Christiansen and Wood, 2006; Klint and Gravesen, 1999); there-
fore, fracture parameters were estimated using literature values for fracture networks in
sub-glacial tills.
As mentioned, hydraulic conductivity values were used in the interpretation of ge-
ology. However, it is important to take into account that 1) the estimated hydraulic
conductivities based on particle size distribution are representative for matrix hydraulic
permeability, and not bulk hydraulic permeability of unconsolidated material (Campbell
et al., 1990); 2) the soil samples that were analyzed for grain size distribution were taken
for each representative layer (interpreted by Orbicon’s contractor, that is Jysk Geoteknik),
however it would be more suitable if the samples were taken in 0.5 or 1 m intervals to ob-
tain more consistent depth discrete particle size distribution curves; 3) the screens, where
hydraulic conductivity was estimated with slug tests, were located in two permeable units
(ice-marginal deposits and bottom out-wash sand), meaning that the resulting K values
possibly reflect a mix of the two layers and not the K of individual layers.
Observing the water levels in the ice-marginal deposits and the out-wash sand aquifer,
the groundwater isopotentials indicate a south-western flow direction. This estimation
was made using wells located in the area of the contaminant source, and additionally two
wells from the neighbouring sites (See Figure 5.4, right). However, the contamination
appears to be spreading in a more southern direction (See Appendix L, Figure L.1). It
would be beneficial if some wells were placed further down gradient and southeastern of
the site to assist in a more accurate delineation of both the groundwater flow direction
and the plume delineation.
The extent of the contamination was analysed based on the PID response and soil
concentrations in the fractured supra-glacial deposits, consisting of melt-out till and sand
and gravel lenses. Based on the data, the absence of DNAPL at the site was assumed, since
the contaminant concentrations were below 1% of the solubility of TCE. Additionally,
contaminant mass in the source zone was estimated using the soil measured concentrations.
It is possible that higher concentration soil volumes within the melt-out till layer were
missed during characterization (Kueper et al., 2014; Interstate Technology Regulatory
Council, 2003).

plane 10 m downstream from transect 1 appears to vary less. This could be explained
by the effect of the local hydrodynamic dispersion smoothing the spatial distribution
of the contamination (Troldborg et al., 2010). However, this could also be because the
significant volume of the plume with elevated contaminant concentrations was missed,
resulting from large spacing between the monitoring points of well B421, B422, B413 and
B423. B423.

6 Discussion
6.3
Vertical mass discharge through glacial deposits
To estimate the vertical dissolved contaminant mass discharge from the fractured melt-
out and sub-glacial till to the underlying ice-marginal deposits and out-wash sand aquifer,
horizontal control planes were placed on the bottom of the sub-glacial tills. Two scenarios
regarding two different approaches of calculation of contaminant mass discharge were
used. Hydraulic conductivity of melt-out till and subglacial till was assumed equivalent
to estimated hydraulic conductivity using grain size distribution (5.010−7 m/s). Since
the complex contaminant distribution in the source area was acknowledged, different
sizes of the control planes were used (transect 3 and 4). Transect 5 was used to estimate
contaminant mass discharge from sand lenses in the supra-glacial deposits to the ice-
marginal deposits. The net infiltration rate through the fractured media was: 1) assumed
to be 0.05 m/year, since infiltration in the area is limited because of the paved surface; 2)
assumed using bulk hydraulic conductivity and measured the average vertical hydraulic
gradient of 0.6. The resulting mass discharge gives two values which differ by two orders
of magnitude as seen in Figure 6.1. (Transect 3: 0.08 kg/year and 15 kg/year respectively,
transect 4: 0.003 kg/year and 0.53 kg/year, respectively, and transect 5: 0.0001 kg/year
and 0.024 kg/year). Transect 3 and 4 yield the highest contaminant mass discharges,
resulting from the highest concentrations found in the fractured melt-out till in the area
of these transects. The two scenarios, using two different approaches for determining
vertical groundwater flux, yield contaminant mass discharges that differ by two orders of
magnitude. This shows how sensitive the conceptual site model and consequently mass
discharge estimation in the vertical direction are with respect to selecting the parameters
determining vertical groundwater flux. Though the area is paved, meaning assumption
of low net infiltration seems reasonable, the mass discharge estimation can significantly
increase when using vertical hydraulic gradient.
Comparing these values with the horizontal mass discharge through the ice-marginal
deposits and bottom out-wash sand, it can be assumed that significant amount of contam-
ination has been discharged to the underlying deposits from the fractured melt-out and
sub-glacial till. Secondly, these results indicate the effect of using different parameters
for determining net-infiltration, where approximating net infiltration and disregarding the
presence of vertical hydraulic gradient can have a significant effect on the mass discharge
estimates in the vertical direction. This can consequently affect the risk assessment for
the contaminated site, where risk can be easily underestimated or missed. With regards
to vertical contaminant discharge, an analytical model simulating contaminant transport
through fractured deposits is recognised to be more suitable for estimating leaching con-
centrations. However, it is also prone to uncertainties in the case of unidentified physical
properties of the fractured geological system. Additionally, none of these methods ac-
count for the contaminant mass discharge through horizontal fractures which are also
possible in glacial deposits.

6.4 Identified challenges in determining mass discharge in glacial deposits
67
6.4
Identified challenges in determining mass discharge
in glacial deposits
The application of the transect method for estimating the contaminant mass discharge
in a complex heterogeneous subsurface of glacial deposits was insightful, by means of
understanding why this method does not suffice to accurately determine the contaminant
mass discharge. First of all, the placing of the control planes where the contaminant mass
discharge can be measured is challenging, because it requires a detailed knowledge about
the subsurface geology and contaminant distribution in the subsurface. Placing of the
horizontal transects can yield over or underestimated contaminant mass discharges since
it is challenging to accurately determine the vertical contaminant flux through fractured
low permeability media. Secondly, since glacial deposits are heterogeneous, it is difficult
to accurately delineate different geological units. The intricacy of the geology contributes
to an additional complex contaminant distribution in the subsurface, in which case the
transect method can result in sampling non-representative samples of groundwater and/or
soil and consequently underestimating contaminant mass discharge. Thirdly, assigning
dedicated sub-areas to each sampling point in the transect is also challenging.
6.5
Suggestions for future research
It was acknowledged by Troldborg et al. (2010), that their tool for quantifying the un-
certainty of mass discharges from contaminated sites can be used for conceptual model
testing and can give an indication of areas where additional data collection or investiga-
tion should be preformed. Using this kind of tool could improve capturing much of the
plume complexity and provide sufficient sampling density of sufficient spatial resolution
in the heterogeneous subsurface of glacial deposits.
Additionally, it would be beneficial to develop new methods for measuring vertical
groundwater and contaminant flux through fractured low permeability media that could
be implemented in the traditional mass discharge measuring approach such as transect
method. Passive flux meters installed in a well are designed for characterising the hori-
zontal component of groundwater and contaminant flux (Hatfield et al., 2004). A concept
that could be considered is developing a similar approach to passive flux meter installed
in a horizontal control plane using inclined or horizontal wells.
Horizontal wells are
commonly used in other fields, e.g. oil industry, but are relatively scarce in environmen-
tal field (Brown Jr et al., 2014).
When applied, however, horizontal wells have been
proven beneficial for sites where permanent constructions or landfills are preventing the
application of vertical wells, and in remediation, using air sparging and vent extraction
(Zhan et al., 2001). Installation of such wells is expensive, however, it can provide a
better surface-area contact with contaminants (Zhan et al., 2001). Further research and
method development combined with field application could be a topic for future work.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was the identification and the assessment of different
approaches in determining contaminant mass discharge through heterogeneous subsurface
of glacial deposits, which are frequently encountered at contaminated sites in Denmark.
The contaminated site at Vassigerød situated in a typical clay till setting was investigated
and studies on site geology and hydrogeology, groundwater flow and contaminant soil
and groundwater chemistry in the underlying glacial deposits were included to build the
conceptual site model. The used approach for determining contaminant mass discharge
consisted of application of traditional transect method, by incorporating both vertical
and horizontal control planes through in the contaminated source area.
The field investigation of the contaminated site resulted in re-interpretation of the
geological model. It was concluded that the layers of glacial deposits originate from the
dead-ice environment and are very complex. After re-evaluation of the available data on
geology and detailed analysis of hydraulic conductivities using two different approaches,
the clay till was re-interpreted to being more sandier, thus to sand till. Additionally,
the presence of ice-marginal deposit layer between the upper supraglacial deposits and
bottom outwash sand aquifer was identified. This finding was important since this layer
was assumed to facilitate horizontal transport of contaminants, as opposed to clay till
layer, where vertical component would be more significant.
On the basis of the findings of geology and hydrogeology at the site, the contamina-
tion extent and mass was evaluated using observed contaminant concentrations in soil
and groundwater. This allowed proposing a new conceptual site model, which was used
for determining contaminant mass discharge using the transect method. Two transects
were set in the source area, one in the proximity of the source zone and another some dis-
tance downgradient. This revealed that the contaminant mass discharge varies in space
and that the monitoring wells with large spacing between each other, might have missed
some volumes of elevated contaminant concentrations, underlining the need for a finer
sampling grid for determining contaminant mass discharge through glacial deposits. Ad-
ditionally, three horizontal control planes were placed in the source zone to evaluate the
vertical contaminant mass discharge through fractured meltout and subglacial till layer.
The application of estimated net-infiltration to determine the vertical groundwater flux
resulted in relatively low contaminant mass discharge estimations. Since the presence
of vertical hydraulic gradient was observed at the site, this was in comparison also used
for contaminant mass discharge, which yielded contaminant mass discharge that was two
orders of magnitude higher.
Based on the results and comparison of different transects it was observed that sig-

Additionally, it was observed that using different approaches of
