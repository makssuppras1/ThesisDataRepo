Abstract
The increase in oil demand and decrease in primary recovery of the reservoirs have forced
industry to focus more on secondary and tertiary oil recovery methods. Water ﬂooding is one
of the common secondary recovery techniques where water is injected into the production
zone of the reservoir to increase the oil production.
Propagation of the surface separating two immiscible ﬂuids under their motion in a porous
medium plays a signiﬁcant role in water ﬂooding and diﬀerent methods of Enhanced Oil
Recovery (EOR): surfactant ﬂooding, microbial EOR, methods based on mobilization and
relocation of reservoir ﬁnes (smart water ﬂooding). There have been a lot of attempts to build
numerical models which can describe the multiphase ﬂow in the porous media accurately.
However, the classical models of two-phase ﬂows in the literature do not account for the
interface between the ﬂowing phases.
The purpose of this project is to study the generalized Buckley-Leverett model accounting
explicitly for the motion of the interface. We have developed a new numerical algorithm for
unsteady state ﬂows, to solve the system of equations and ﬁnd the saturation proﬁle in 1D
simulation. The eﬀect of diﬀerent parameters such water-oil mixing, water-oil interface and
capillary-viscous ratio on the displacement front, which are neglected in traditional models,
are studied. The results of computations show much sharper displacement fronts and more
piston-like displacement than predicted by the classical Buckley-Leverett scheme accounting
for capillary pressure, even if the value of capillary pressure is rather large.

Introduction
The increase in oil demand and decrease in primary recovery of the reservoirs have forced
industry to focus more on secondary and tertiary oil recovery methods. Water ﬂooding is a
secondary recovery technique where water is injected into the reservoir to increase the oil
production. Surfactant ﬂooding and microbial Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) are methods
in which the motion of interface plays an important role. There have been a lot of attempts
to build numerical models which can describe the multiphase ﬂow in the porous media ac-
curately. However, the classical models of two-phase ﬂows in the literature do not account
for the interface between the ﬂowing phases. The purpose of this project is to study the gen-
eralized Buckley-Leverett model accounting explicitly for the motion of the interface. We
have developed a new numerical algorithm by which the saturation proﬁle is predicted in 1D
unsteady state ﬂow. Furthermore, the eﬀects of diﬀerent parameters such water-oil mixing,
water-oil interface and capillary-viscous ratio on the displacement front, which are neglected
in traditional models, are studied.
In this chapter, the previous works on models describing ﬂuid ﬂow in porous media and their
shortcomings are reviewed. The importance of our study is also highlighted.

Multiphase ﬂow in porous media has wide range of application in ﬂuid dynamics, gas stor-
age in geological formations, ground water pollution, and oil and gas recovery. Due to the
importance of ﬂuid ﬂow in porous media, researchers have studied problems in this ﬁeld for

The eﬀect of interfacial area
The interface between phases plays an important role in two phase ﬂow problem but in the
extended Darcy’s law this eﬀect is neglected. In the classical two-phase ﬂow model, pa-
rameters like relative permeability and capillary pressure depend only on saturation and the
eﬀect of saturation hysteresis and interfacial area is not considered. Hassanizadeh et al. [2]
demonstrated that using extended Darcy’s equation to describe two-phase ﬂow in porous
media is scientiﬁcally unacceptable. They suggested new equations of motion in which the
speciﬁc interfacial area is considered in the governing equations. They conclude that a rela-
tionship between capillary pressure, interfacial area and saturation might exist which led to
elimination of saturation hysteresis.
Dalla et al. [3] and Gladkikh et al. [4] suggested diﬀerent approaches to measure interfacial
area between phases experimentally to determine the range of applicability of the interfacial
area coeﬃcients in models in the literature .
Reeves and Celia [5] performed simulated immiscible displacement experiments to estimate
how interfacial area between phases change during drainage and imbibition in a two-phase
ﬂow system (Figure 1.1). Their results show that a functional relationship could be consid-
ered between capillary pressure, water saturation and interfacial area. The results of their
network model simulation supports the hypothesis of Hassanizadeh et al. [2].

Another limitation of two-phase Darcy’s approach is that pressure gradient and gravity are
the only driving forces considered for the ﬂow of each ﬂuid and the possibility of having
additional driving forces such as momentum transfer between phases which is known as
viscous coupling is neglected [6].

that will signiﬁcantly aﬀect the motions of the neighboring ﬂuid particles.” Several studies
show that the eﬀect of viscous coupling has been known from 1950’s. Yuster was the ﬁrst

Dullien and Dong [12] found the transport coeﬃcients in the coupled equations of two-phase
ﬂow experimentally with the assumption that the pressure gradient in one of the two ﬂowing
ﬂuids is equal to zero. Their assumption does not represent real case of two phase ﬂow since
they assumed one ﬂowing phase and one stationary phase. As a result, they were unable
to ﬁnd net contribution of viscous coupling to water or oil ﬂow. Reviewers suggest viscous
combination of steady and unsteady state displacement with concurrent and countercurrent
ﬂow [6].
Bensten [13] investigated eﬀect of momentum transfer between ﬂuid phases on eﬀective
mobility in conjunction with fractional ﬂow theory. His results showed that neglecting vis-
cous coupling between ﬂuid phases has signiﬁcant impact on the shape of eﬀective mobility
curves.
Ayodele and Bensten [14] performed numerical simulation of interfacial coupling phenom-
ena in two-phase, cocurrent ﬂow in porous media. The aim of their work was to verify if
the inclusion or non-inclusion of the interfacial coupling eﬀect has any signiﬁcant impact on
the physics of ﬂow. Modiﬁed set of transport equations gave a good description of horizon-
tal two-phase cocurrent ﬂow in porous media. Their results showed that viscous coupling
eﬀects are insigniﬁcant in cocurrent ﬂow for small values of viscous forces. Otherwise, it
would have a greater eﬀect on the physics of ﬂow and should be considered.

The eﬀect of non-equilibrium capillary pressure
When two immiscible ﬂuids ﬁll the pore space of the reservoir rock, they compete for a
place on solid surface. The ability of a liquid to wet or spread over a surface is known as
“wettability”. The angle which the liquid interface makes with the solid is called “contact
angle (𝜃)”. Contact angle is usually measured in the dense phase. i.e. for the case of oil

1.1.2
New meaning to Buckley-Leverett equation
As discussed in section 1.1.1, for describing two phase ﬂow in porous media, coupling ver-
sus no-coupling has always been a debate. Rose realized that although diﬀerent models have
been suggested for improving extended Darcy’s law based on viscous coupling eﬀect, there
have been less focus on considering this approach to Buckley-Leverett equation.1 He em-
ployed the relationships derived from the general theory of non equilihrium thermodynamics
to Buckley-Leverett formula for two-phase, isothermal ﬂow of homogeneous, incompress-
ible ﬂuids. His system of equations have four transport coeﬃcients which are complicated
functions of saturation, saturation distribution and history of saturation change. The diago-
nal coeﬃcients are representative of ﬂux of each phase based on mechanical energy drive,
while the cross (coupling) coeﬃcients show how the ﬂuxes are aﬀected by motion of neigh-
boring immiscible phase. Rose concluded that Buckley Leverett formula can be both applied
to recovery processes based on classical relative permeability theory, as well as recovery
processes governed by theory of coupled transport processes. In conclusion, it has been
suggested that it worths to perform additional laboratory measurement to evaluate transport
coeﬃcients of the coupled model in order to have a more accurate model in which all factors
have been considered [21].

2.1
Porous media
A porous medium is a solid with a regular or random shape containing void spaces (pores),
either connected or unconnected. The pores contain a variety of ﬂuids such as air, water, and
oil. If the pores contain a large volume and are well connected, the porous media has a high
porosity and permeability. The porosity is deﬁned as the ratio of the pore volume to the total
bulk volume of the porous media and permeability is the ability of porous media to conduct
ﬂuids through interconnected pores of the rock.
Porous materials are classiﬁed according to the size of the pores. Materials with sizes less
than 2 𝑛𝑚are called micropores, while materials with pores between 2 𝑛𝑚and 50 𝑛𝑚are

2.2
Immiscible displacement
The simultaneous movement of two or more immiscible ﬂuids (such as water and oil) is called
immiscible displacement. The ﬂuids are separated by boundaries, called interfaces, across
which discontinuities in density and pressure exist. Although some components in the ﬂuids
might diﬀuse in both phases, the ﬂuids are called immiscible as long as the sharp interface
between them is maintained. In other words, the existence of the interfaces is characteristic
of what are called immiscible ﬂuids [24].

tion forces. The driving force can be a change in chemical potential, temperature, pressure
diﬀerence or gravity force. While the friction forces between two components or phases is
proportional to the diﬀerence in velocity of each individual phase [31].
MS theory can be applied in wide range of subjects from transfer in gas and liquids to transfer
through solid porous media. Shapiro developed two approaches for investigating the motion
of two phase ﬂow in porous media under steady state condition based on MS theory. These
approaches are named as a general approach and an approach by considering the moving
interfaces that is described in the following subsections.

Numerical solution
In order to solve the set of equations numerically we need to replace the derivatives by ﬁ-
nite diﬀerences. In this study, we have two independent variables, 𝑇and 𝑋, that need to
be discretized. Two numerical methods known as explicit and implicit methods can be used
to solve time dependent ordinary and partial diﬀerential equations. In explicit method, the
solution at next time step can be obtained by only the knowledge of the values of the current
time, while in the implicit method the solution can be found by solving an equation involving
both the current state of the system and the later one. Implicit methods need more compu-
tation time but they suﬀer less numerical instabilities, while explicit methods are faster but
they require much smaller time intervals in comparison to the implicit method to avoid nu-
merical instabilities [33]. Therefore, it is necessary to check the stability condition when
using the explicit method. In this study, we have solved the set of equations explicitly and

other words, bvp4c adds points when the error estimate is too large and it reduces number

The mixing parameter (a) is deﬁned as characteristic ratio of thickness of cluster branch to
pore size [15]. Figure 5.5 shows a simpliﬁed schematic example of porous media with dif-
ferent values of mixing parameter. Assuming a cylindrical pore geometry, mixing parameter
equal to one (a = 1) means that one capillary is ﬁlled with water, and the adjacent capillary
is ﬁlled with oil and so on so forth. In other words, thinner clusters of oil and water exist in
the porous media and this leads to higher interaction between oil and water which we simply
refer as the absolute mixing case.
When the mixing parameter is equal to six (a = 6), oil and water exist besides each other
as packs of six capillaries which lead to a weaker interaction between the phases. In other
words, the branches of the clusters become thicker, and the inﬂuence of water-oil clusters
becomes weak making the mixing occur less than the previous case.

Conclusions and future work
We have implemented a new algorithm for solving the governing ﬂow equations of a gen-
eralized Buckley-Leverett model which describes one-dimensional two-phase immiscible
ﬂow under unsteady state conditions in porous media considering the eﬀect of the motion
of a separating surface. This model has some advantageous over traditional methods since it
considers force interactions between the ﬂowing phases as well as velocity of the water-oil
interface as an additional variable. Furthermore, the phase pressure diﬀerence is assumed to
be diﬀerent from static capillary pressure due to dynamic eﬀects of the ﬂow.
Since the system of equations in our model are highly nonlinear combination of higher
derivatives of 𝐹and 𝑠with regard to 𝑥, it is diﬃcult to ﬁnd the analytical solution to the
governing equations. Therefore, the results of saturation proﬁle from our model were com-
pared with analytical Buckley Leverett solution to validate our numerical model. The three
main parameters introduced in the new approach were mixing parameter (a), interfacial area
eﬀect (𝜀𝐴), and capillary-viscous ratio (𝜀𝑐). The results showed that decreasing the mix-
ing parameter leads to thinner water and oil cluster branches, stronger drag force between
phases and more piston-like displacement. It was also found that mixing parameter has the
most signiﬁcant eﬀect on changing the shape of the displacement front to a much sharper
one.
Due to high capillary number in chalk, the numerical studies based on traditional models
could not predict the experimental behavior of water-oil displacement which is observed to
be piston-like. The results from our model showed that the eﬀect of 𝜀𝐴is dominant over
==PAGE:77==
62
capillarity eﬀect especially when capillary-viscous ratios are in ranges around one which is
quite large.
Introducing new terms such as motion of interface as well as mixing parameter in the cou-
pling coeﬃcients, and the inﬂuence of water-oil interfacial area might be the reason which
make the prediction of model more realistic to what is observed in the experiments on chalk.
If such statement is valid, we might be on the right track to ﬁnd a solution to describe the
water-oil displacement in chalk more accurately.
In our model, the data for equilibrium water-oil interface was used; however, the values of the
water-oil interfaces under non-equilibrium conditions are diﬃcult to measure experimentally
and lack of such data in the literature causes some limitations in our model. The next step in
developing this approach could be to derive new sets of equations for the three-dimensional
case considering the motion of interface and to further optimize the parameters in small
scale. One may also consider implementing IMPES method to avoid the problem associated
with the stiﬀproblem mentioned in section 5.6.
