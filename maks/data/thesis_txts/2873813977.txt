Abstract
This thesis investigates how different input methods influence player performance, perceived
challenge and enjoyment in a gamified mobile-style anime music quiz. While the initial scope was
to explore interaction design more broadly, the research focus was refined and narrowed to now
compare recognition-based input such as multiple Choice (MC) with recall-based input such as
filtered dropdown (DD) in order to determine which format offers a better balance between speed,
accuracy, engagement and satisfaction.
The theoretical background combines cognitive psychology and game design models, drawing
on Self-Determination Theory, the Octalysis Framework, and Bartle’s Taxonomy of Player Types.
Recognition tasks are generally faster and less cognitively demanding than recall tasks, while mod-
erate challenge can enhance motivation and enjoyment, especially for certain player types.
To
examine these concepts, an online experiment was conducted where participants completed two
quiz blocks. One using MC and one using DD. The experiment was within-subject, so every par-
ticipant got to experience in, but in different orders. Objective performance data such as response
time and accuracy were recorded automatically via Supabase, while subjective measures such as
motivation, satisfaction, perceived challenge, preferences and demographics were collected through
a survey.
Due to missing or mismatched participant IDs, the data were organized into three datasets:
Supabase-only (n = 40), Survey-only (n = 31) and Merged (n = 17). Power analysis indicated that
the Supabase dataset met the sample size required to detect medium effects, whereas the merged
dataset did not, limiting sensitivity for smaller effects.
Results showed that MC enabled significantly faster and more accurate responses compared to
DD. However, survey feedback indicated that many participants valued DD for its greater challenge
and sense of reward. These findings suggest that MC is well-suited for casual or mixed-skill play,
while DD may be preferable for competitive or expert contexts.
This study connects cognitive theory with practical game design and can serve as a foundation
for future research on input methods in music-based quiz games, including testing other answer
formats and involving a wider range of player groups.

Introduction
2.1
Mobile gaming
Mobile games have become a leading form of entertainment, largely because smartphones are a
necessity for most people. Therefore their accessibility and the wide variety of available games mean
there is something to suit almost everyone’s interest. In 2024, mobile games is making up almost
half of total revenue in 2024 (about $92 billion) within the global games market and is continuing to
grow each year (1). The popularity of mobile games came when the apple launched the iPhone and
the App store in 2008, which transformed and revolutionized the whole mobile gaming industry. The
highly simplified and intuitive touchscreen-based user interface on the iPhone greatly increase the user
experience and engagement, therefore pathing the way of mobile gaming. The mobile gaming market
transformed with a new generation of cellular networks such as 3G, 4G and now 5G enabling richer
graphics, further complex games, real-time social interaction and multiplayer experiences (4). Within
this modern mobile gaming landscape, trivia-style mobile games especially benefit from this due to its
short session formats and social engagement, with top titles achieving higher retention rates through
competitive and community-based features such as GeoGuessr.
2.2
Trivia games
All of the above explains why, trivia games have seen some significant growth, especially knowledge-
based trivia games. Knowledge-based trivia games have slowly become popular by leveraging real-time
competitive elements to enhance user engagement and retention. Games such as Geoguessr have proven
how interactive mechanics, social competition and exploration-based gameplay can turn simple trivia
knowledge games into highly engaging experience that retains players. The unique way of playing
knowledge based trivia games by figuring out the current location and point the location out from
a map, makes it very intuitive and engaging for the users. The success of these elements is evident,
when looking into the numbers of GeoGuessr World Cup 2024, where it attracted almost 300.000
peak viewers, an average of 61.2k viewers over 26 hours of airtime and 1.6 million hours watched from
all kinds of streaming platforms (5). It managed to get these numbers without having the biggest
GeoGuessr influencer ”Rainbolt” cover the event. The promising numbers of GeoGuessr paves the

However, while many trivia games have evolved significantly over time, niche knowledge-based

engagement and satisfaction in this specific area. While you may ask, why would anyone want to look

The Anime Industry
The anime industry has become a major cultural and economic force worldwide, as it has grown
from an interest of niche communities to a global phenomenon captivating audiences of all ages and
backgrounds. As of 2024, the global anime market is valued at approximately USD 34 billion with
projections that suggests a growth which will exceed USD 60 billion by 2034. The rate is growing at a
Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 9.8% (6). The main cause of this growth has been due to
the rise of social media, which allows fans of the anime shows to interact and engage with each other
in online communities. The increased connectivity also means an increased demand of anime culture
globally. This means all aspects of anime, hence the larger market size in television, film, streaming
platforms, merchandises and last but not least music. Streaming services like Netflix, Crunchyroll,
and Funimation have expanded global access to anime, which fuels the market growth. The US has
been a front runner for most global phenomenons, hence the US numbers of the market will reflect the
global market of anime. When looking into the numbers of the US anime market alone, it is expected
to grow from USD 2.21 billion in 2023 to USD 5.84 billion by 2030 (7).
Anime’s appeal spans a wide demographic, attracting audiences of all age groups and backgrounds,
while previously only popular among younger audiences. Now, it has started to gain traction among
the older audiences, especially those who grew up with it during the 1990s and 2000s. As the fanbase
has matured, the anime medium has followed along and the content has shifted towards being more
diverse, mostly in the genre area to appeal to a broader audience. The genre can range from action and
fantasy to sports and drama, even to something as mundane as romance and slice of life. This diversity
has broadened the appeal of anime in general, hence the shift of it being a niche medium to being
among the front runners of media consumption. This expansion in genre spectrum and connectivity of
the users towards the anime brand has increased the appeal of anime. This has created more potential

2.5
Research question & Initial scope
Despite the increasing popularity of trivia games and the growth of the anime industry, anime
music quizzes have yet to fully utilize the gamification aspects and interaction design to enhance user
engagement. While platforms like Anime Music Quiz have established a dedicated player base, their
web-based format and limited interaction features have left significant potential untapped.
This study seeks to explore the role of interaction design in enhancing user engagement and satis-
faction in real-time, gamified mobile anime music quizzes. The research aims to answer the following
question::
How does interaction design influence user engagement and satisfaction in real-time,
gamified mobile anime music quizzes?
By analyzing successful game mechanics from trivia games such as GeoGuessr and integrating
them into an anime music quiz, this study aims to enhance the user experience through optimized
and engaging input methods, feedback loops to help the players further improve on their gameplay,
and multiplayer dynamics for the social aspects. The research will follow a human-centered design
approach, beginning with a literature review on gamification, cognitive science, and interaction design,
while also conducting a preliminary field study at Jpopcon. This will help out with the design of the
quiz and align it with user needs and preferences. The findings from this research will contribute
to the broader and better understanding of how interaction design can improve user engagement in
mobile quiz games, specifically those within somewhat niche fandoms like anime communities.

3.1
Gamification and Motivation Theories
3.1.1
Self-Determination Theory
Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a widely recognized psychological framework that helps us
understand human motivation, which explains why people act and engage in certain activities. SDT
also explains the distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, where intrinsic is when the
activity itself is rewarding, while extrinsic is when you engage in an activity for the reward. SDT also
emphasizes that motivation is not only just external rewards, but it’s about fulfilling the three basic
psychological needs, which are the following:
1. Autonomy: A need to feel in control of their life, decisions and action, hence individuals with
the autonomy need fulfilled will perceive their actions as self-chosen rather than controlled, which
in turn makes them feel more satisfied and motivated.
2. Competence: A need to feel capable and effective when during tasks, hence individuals with the
competence need fulfilled are motivated from experiencing skill mastery and achieving success.
3. Relatedness: A need to feel connected with others, hence individuals with the relatedness need
fulfilled are motivated from social interactions and being part of a community.
In the context of gamification, SDT is very valuable for the design of game mechanics, since
motivation is build upon the three psychological needs. When those needs for the players are satisfied,
they are more likely to be more motivated, leading to greater engagement and satisfaction.
The
opposite can happen, when those needs are not fulfilled, which often leads the played frustrated,
disengaged and unmotivated leading to less motivation and engagement.
3.1.2
The Octalysis Framework
The core drives in gamification can be explained from the Octalysis framework, which is a model
used to analyze and design gamification systems (3). It consists of eight core drives, that explains
the motivational factors that keep users engaged, which can be used for the purpose of creating an
engaging and user-centered experience. This does not only apply to websites and apps, but can also
be applied to product, workplace, marketing and life itself, as all those aspects can be gamified to
9
==PAGE:11==
make the users feel engaged. These drives are the key factors that motivate people to act and respond
to different experiences. They include:
1. Epic Meaning & Calling: A sense that makes the user feels like, they are part of something
greater than themselves or/and they believe, they are the chosen ones to take that action.
2. Development & Accomplishment: A sense that makes the user feel like, they are developing
skills, progressing and overcoming challenges, hence making them feel accomplished.
3. Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback: A sense that makes the user feel like, they can
express themselves or/and engage in a creative process, where they can repeatedly try new things
or different combinations and then see the results of it.
4. Ownership & Possession: A sense that makes the user feel like, they are owning something
or/and having something in control, hence wanting to improve upon it.
5. Social Influence & Relatedness: All social elements like mentorship, social acceptance and
feedback, companionship, competition and even envy.
6. Scarcity & Impatience: A sense that makes the user feel like, they want something due to
the fact, it is exclusive, rare or unattainable.
7. Unpredictability & Curiosity: A sense that makes the user feel excited and engaged of the
uncertainty of the next event, and the user will therefore pay more attention to it.
8. Loss & Avoidance: A fear of missing out (FOMO) of the user is avoiding of something negative
happening like the loss of previous work or if they don’t act, they will not be able to attain it in
the future.
3.1.2.1
Other aspects of the framework
The framework can be divided into left brain (extrinsic motivated) and right brain (intrinsic
motivated) core drives. If you are extrinsic motivated, you are motivated, because you want to obtain
something, which can be a goal, a product or anything unattainable. On the opposite site, if you are
intrinsic motivated, you are motivated, because the activity itself is motivating. This entails activities
like using your creativity, hanging out with friends or feel the suspense of the unpredictable.
10
==PAGE:12==
The framework can also be divided into a top core drives (white hat gamification) and bottom
core drives (black hat gamification). The white hat gamification entails a higher sense of meaning as
it makes the user feel like achieved and they can express their creativity, hence making them feel very
good and powerful. On the other hand, the black hat gamification entails a sense of uncertainty and
a fear of loss, because you will struggle to attain the things you can’t have, hence always making the
user feel bad.
Figure 1: Level 2 Octalysis. Screenshot from the gamification book by Chou, Y. (2016) in page 41
The next level of the octalysis framework is the level 2 octalysis, which uses the framework in
four different phases of a player’s journey within a gamified system. An example of how a player’s
journey will look like is the illustration above, which shows the eight cores of the frameworks within a
timeline with the four experience phases. In the discovery phase, the player is most likely curious and
it is seeking the unpredictable to get a new experience, hence in the figure, it is very skewed towards
that core drive. During the onboarding phase, a lot of the core drives will be fulfilled and the most
prominent core drive is the Development & Accomplishment, where the player learns and often feels
achieved, as they embark on new challenges and conquerer them. Then in the scaffolding phase, the
player might be motivated due to social elements or/and wants to achieve the goals, they couldn’t
achieve in prior phase, hence the player is mostly extrinsic motivated. In the end, the player might
continue due to not wanting to lose their current status and achievements, hence the motivation ends
up fulfilling the most criteria of black hat gamification.
3.1.2.2
Bartle taxonomy of player types to the framework
The level 2 octalysis is not the final level, as the level 3 octalysis explores the four phases between
all kinds of players by applying the Bartle taxonomy of player types, which is a game design model
11
==PAGE:13==
of Richard Bartle, where each phases will look different for the four player types. The four players
consists of the Achievers, Explorers, Socializers and Killers. The achievers want to master everything
within the game system, hence their motivation factors are challenges and progression. The explorers
want to explore what the world has to offer and experience it, but not necessarily wants to overcome
all the challenges. The socializers want to interact and having a conversation with other people, hence
their primary goal is to build friendship. The killers want to both strive to the top and beat everyone
in the process to achieve it and furthermore get admired by everyone else.
3.1.2.3
Why the Octalysis Framework?
Other gamification models exist, such as the Points-Badges-Leaderboard (PBL) Triad or the
Mechanics-Dynamics-Aesthetics (MDA) framework. The problem with both of those framework is
that they tend to focus narrowly on game structure rather than user motivation. An example of this
is the MDA framework, which has been used for the purpose of analyzing and categorizing gamifica-
tion models in education in a research paper by Kusuma et al. (2018), but it isn’t as user centric.
The other framework is the PBL Triad, which is the simplest and is considered the first building
blocks of gamification, but it doesn’t go beyond points, badges and leaderboards (33). . Both of these
framework have their purposes, but won’t be used, as the frameworks are not user/player-centric.
The Interaction Design Foundation, which is very UX-centric foundation, highlights that effective
gamification should go beyond points and badges. Therefore the focus should be on meaningful user
motivation and engagement (34). This perspective supports the choice of the Octalysis Framework
in this thesis, as it centers on eight core motivational drives that address both intrinsic and extrinsic
factors.
Nicole Lazzaro’s 4 Keys 2 Fun framework was also considered due to its focus on emotional drivers
like challenge, novelty, social play and meaningful impact. However, while useful for understanding
broad emotional goals, it offers limited guidance for connecting specific features to motivational triggers
(35).
For these reasons, this thesis adopts the Octalysis Framework. It provides a detailed view of both
positive and negative motivators and works well alongside SDT, which another framework used in this
thesis. This makes Octalysis particularly useful for examining the nuanced motivations behind features
observed in existing platforms like Anime Music Quiz (AMQ) and AniGuessr. For instance, AMQ’s
12
==PAGE:14==
discord community, in-game chat systems and cooperative modes align with social influence/related-
ness, while AniGuessr’s daily challenges draw on unpredictability and loss avoidance and unlocking
skins draw on ownership. This alignment enables a consistent analysis of current features and explains
the design decisions for the prototype developed in this research.
3.1.3
Emperical Research on Gamification
In the context of gamified mobile applications, a study by Bitri´an et al. (2021) researched how
gamification elements influence user engagement. Their research used the psychological framework
called the ”Self-System Model of Motivational Development (SSMMD) and used in the context of
SDT. It suggests that motivation is influenced by how each person perceive their ability to fulfill
their psychological needs of competence, autonomy and relatedness. This is how the research iden-
tified the three primary categories of game elements that consisted of achievement and progression-
oriented elements, social-oriented elements and lastly immersion-oriented elements. Achievement and
progression-oriented elements included the features like points, levels and badges to provide some
sense of accomplishment within the users. The social-oriented elements had mechanism such as leader
boards and social sharing to further enhance community interaction and competition. The last cat-
egory was the immersion-oriented elements, which are components such as story telling, thematic
design and audio-visual elements that enhances the user experience and the emotional connection to
the app. The study found a correlation between the three categories of game elements with the three
basic psychological needs and found that those elements will thereby increase engagement and lead to
positive marketing outcomes such as higher usage intention and favorable app ratings (29).
Drawing from the study by Bitri´an et al.
(2021), the design of gamified anime music quizzes
can integrate the same categories of game elements to fill the same psychological needs and therefore
enhance user engagement. This is done by introducing features like scoring systems, levels and badges
to motivate users to continue playing and improve their performance within the achievement and
progression-oriented elements. Furthermore, introducing features like sharing custom music quizzes
and achievements, incorporating leader boards and multiplayer feature to increase social interaction
for the social-oriented elements. Finally, introducing features such as utilizing thematic app design
or providing visuals to the audio for deeper connection to the quiz. Incorporating these elements
makes the quiz engaging in a way that aligns with how anime fans enjoy music, supports different
13
==PAGE:15==
player motivations, and encourages continued participation rather than one and done use. While these
theories explain why players stay motivated, they do not address how users interact with the game
interface.
3.2
Interaction Design in Gaming
In the previous subsection, gamification frameworks were used to explain what motivates users to
play. This subsection focuses on interaction design principles, which describe how to make the game
easier to use and support the player’s cognition. When looking into interaction design in games, it
involves structuring player input, how players receive feedback, and how the system responds to create
a smooth and enjoyable experience. Good interaction design removes unnecessary options and avoids
cluttered interfaces, which helps reduce frustration and makes the game feel more immersive.
3.2.1
Norman’s principles & Nielsen’s heuristics
A useful theoretical foundational are the Norman’s principles, which are the following (36):
• Affordances: Features of UI elements that show what actions are possible (pressable buttons
to go to a new page)
• Signifiers: Visual or audio cues that clearly indicate how they are used (Labels on buttons or
grayed out buttons that are unusable)
• Feedback: Immediate response to user actions (color change or audio cues when clicking on
wrong or right answer button)
• Mapping: Natural relationship between the controls and its results (Swiping from the left to
right on a page, should move the page to the right)
• Constraints: Limit available actions to prevent errors (Grayed out buttons that prevent the
user to proceed further)
These principles work together with Nielsen’s usability heuristics, especially the distinction between
recognition and recall. The full set of Nielsen’s heuristics is summarized in the Table 1, which are all
considering when making the prototype of the app (37).

3.2.2
Continuous and immediate feedback
Building on these principles and heuristics, research on mobile game UX design by Pichlmair et

during short play sessions. It also stresses learnability, meaning players should understand the interface
without long instructions, and prioritizing intuitive interaction that leads to low cognitive load. Those
aspects are important to ensure that controls feel natural and do not overwhelm the user. These
principles are particularly useful for trivia games where players need to focus on the content, which
is to identify songs, rather than figuring out how to interact with the interface to keep the friction to
minimal (32). Therefore the iteration process should make use of user/player-centric design methods
to match features with how players actually want to use it.
3.2.3
Gesture patterns in mobile gaming
As previously mentioned, a recent study by Li et al. (2021) looked at how players use gestures
when playing mobile games by collecting and visualizing their touch patterns. The study found that
players often use the parts of the screen that are easiest to reach with their thumbs and that beginner
and expert players use different gesture patterns. It also showed that sometimes players interact with
the interface in ways that the game designers did not expect, such as tapping in areas that were
not originally planned for the main actions (31). For a quiz game like the anime music quiz in this
thesis, these findings mean that answer buttons should be placed where they are easy to reach with
one thumb, be large enough to avoid mistakes, and spaced so players can tap quickly without errors.
Designing the layout to match natural touch behavior makes the game easier to use and can also help
players feel more comfortable and motivated to keep playing.

Input methods impact on motivation and satisfaction
Building on the importance of feedback and gesture friendly layouts, the choice of input method
itself becomes a central interaction design decision for mobile gaming. For a mobile quiz game, this

choice directly affects not only speed and ease of use but also player motivation and learning outcomes,
hence why this topic can be particularly interesting to further look upon such as multiple choice
against filtered drop-down input that the current anime music quizzes utilizes like Anime Music Quiz

the song name themselves, which can support deeper learning but may feel slower and less convenient

3.2.5
Cognitive theories relevant to game interaction
3.2.5.1
Flow Theory
Flow theory describes a psychological state of deep involvement and enjoyment that occurs when
a person’s skill level is well-matched to the challenge of a task. According to Csikszentmihalyi, “The
best moments usually occur when a person’s body or mind is stretched to its limits in a voluntary
effort to accomplish something difficult and worthwhile” (39, p. 15). In this flow state, individuals
experience focused concentration, a sense of control and motivation, often leading to a sense of loss in
time. According to Csikszentmihalyi to achieve in a state of flow, it requires clear goals, immediate
feedback and balance between challenge and skill. The person in the state of flow needs to know, what
exactly needs to be done with quick and informative response of their actions. The task should not be
easy as it could lead to boredom or way too hard as it causes stress and anxiety. In game design, flow
theory is frequently applied to maintain player engagement by dynamically adjusting the challenge
according to the player’s skill level, ensuring that the player is always motivated or engaged.
17
==PAGE:19==
3.2.5.2
Cognitive Load Theory
Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) explains how the limits of our working memory affect how well we
can process and learn information (? ). The working memory has a limited capacity, hence why too
much information presented or an overly completed task can cause decrease in performance due to
cognitive overload. CLT describes three types of cognitive load, which is the intrinsic load, which is
about how difficult the task or matieral is. Extraneous load, which is how the information is being
presented that cause extra unnecessary difficulty and lastly, the germane load, which describes the
mental effort used to process and understand the content. In game design, that means managing
cognitive load is a crucial factor to maintain player performance and engagement. Therefore in game
design, it is important to remove unnecessary distraction by simplifying interfacte interactions which
then lowers extraneous load. Another factor to keep in mind for game design is to keep challenging
the player according to their skill level to optimize intrinsic load. In the context of germane load, it
could be the mental effort the players invest into building strategies to recognize or remember songs
faster, like remembering patterns from previous quiz questions.

3.3.1
GeoGuessr
GeoGuessr is a browser-based geography quiz game launched in 2013 that builds upon Google
Street View. The mobile app was released for Playstore and App Store in 2021 (25). Players are
dropped into a random location on Earth and must figure out their position by exploring the 360°
environment and placing a marker on a world map. Points are awarded from 0 to 5000 based on the
accuracy of the guess, with additional gameplay modifiers such as time limits, restricted movement or
zoom disabled, which adds layers of challenge and replayability.
18
==PAGE:20==
Over time, GeoGuessr has evolved into a feature rich platform offering multiple modes, including
solo play, head-to-head duels, daily challenges, team battles, and thematic quizzes like world capitals or
UNESCO heritage sites. The core gameplay loop emphasizes visual exploration, pattern recognition,
and geographic deduction, often under time pressure.
From an UX standpoint, GeoGuessr excels by offering a clean, responsive interface that supports
rapid switching between two core views: the Street View environment and the top-down map. In a
study by Berners-Lee (2023) (9), expert players such as GeoWizard were observed rapidly alternating
between these views to identify contextual clues like vegetation, architecture, or signage, and test
hypotheses on the map. These “semiotic moves” as the study calls them, form the cognitive and
interactional backbone of the game—enabling players to shift smoothly between searching, guessing,
and verifying.
This interplay between observation and action is key to GeoGuessr’s appeal and retention. Its
interaction model supports fluid engagement and rewards deductive thinking, curiosity and spatial
memory, which appeals strongly to Bartle’s player types of Explorers and Achievers. According to
the Octalysis framework, it also activates motivational drives such as Unpredictability & Curiosity,
Development & Accomplishment and Social Influence, which is especially prevalent in competitive
modes.
Although GeoGuessr is not anime-themed, its interaction design and motivational structure offer
valuable insights for designing engaging quiz experiences. The game shows how simple controls, quick
feedback, and regular daily rewards can keep players coming back. Moreover having game elements
that intrigue the player types the game is intended for. These qualities are particularly relevant for
anime music quizzes, where users often engage in short sessions, expect immediate feedback and are
motivated by personal mastery and fandom-based connection. This makes it a useful example for
understanding how to build fun and repeatable experiences in anime-themed quiz games, as this is
among the most successful trivia-based knowledge game of them all.
3.3.2
Anime Music Quiz (AMQ)
AMQ launched in 2014 as a browser-based social quiz for anime fans. Created by Danish developer
Rasmus Egerod Leth, it challenges players to identify anime opening, ending, and insert themes using
only audio clips (11). Over the next decade, AMQ grew through community-submitted song lists,

==PAGE:21==
in-game ranked lobbies, and community hosted tournaments through platforms like Discord. It runs
on an HTML5 front-end with real-time WebSocket connections. In 2023, Leth founded Next World
Games ApS to formalize development and hosting (13), and the community supports the platform via
Patreon and cosmetic purchases (12). Today, AMQ offers solo, private and public rooms with in-game
chat, global and friend-only leaderboards, and seasonal events proving that a lightweight, web-native
codebase plus strong community contributions can scale into a robust social music quiz.
How the game works is that at the start of each round, AMQ displays a “Waiting. . . buffering”
message as the next 20-second audio clip loads via WebSockets. Once the snippet is ready, playback
begins and during the guessing phase it shows a large countdown timer in the top half of the browser
window, and in the replay phase it switches to the corresponding anime video. Directly below sits
the answer interface—either a four multiple choice panel or an auto-complete dropdown that accepts
romanized and English titles—while each player’s avatar is arrayed along the bottom side of the
browser. A persistent chat panel on the right supports real-time banter and coordination. Immediate
visual feedback is delivered through colored avatar borders and expressions: a red outline and sad
avatar icon mark incorrect answers, whereas a green outline and happy avatar celebrate correct guesses,
which can be seen in figure 2. The host can configure game parameters such as format, song type,
genres, playlist size, guess time and can run solo games or multiplayer lobbies (10). This tight coupling
of audio cue, input control, and instant, emotive feedback reinforces the rapid listen–guess–confirm
rhythm that keeps players engaged.

3.3.3.1
Background and Motivation
AniGuessr was originally created as a side project inspired by AMQ and the minimalist design
inspired by Lolidle. The game was created by the French game developer duo, Miki & Nuktorn and the

appeal to anime enthusiasts. Unlike AMQ, which leans heavily into competitive multiplayer music
identification, AniGuessr aimed to be more casual and accessible. It started with image-based quizzes
with character and anime screenshot recognition and later expanded to include music-based questions,
though the latter are less frequently played due to practical limitations such as lack of headphones
and some users skip opening and ending songs.
3.3.3.2
Interaction Design Approach
Simplicity was a core tenet of AniGuessr’s interaction design. The developers intentionally built
the platform as a single-page application to minimize navigation complexity and enable fast, repeatable
game sessions lasting roughly 10 minutes, which can be seen in the appendix. The features were kept
lightweight and had no no nested menus, minimal settings and an one-click game start option. This
design philosophy ensured accessibility for both casual users and mobile players, the latter of whom
represent approximately 30–35% of the user base, despite it being a browser based game, which was
initially intended for mostly PC based players. The daily-reset mechanic (synchronized to French

continuous content generation. How the game makes the game fresh every time is by making the daily

also play prior days’ daily challenges, which ensures that it doesn’t punish players that can’t play the
game daily.
3.3.3.3

AniGuessr employs various gamification strategies in line with SDT and the Octalysis Framework:

progress tracking.
• Social features such as score-sharing and optional profile linking (e.g., to MyAnimeList), sup-

These design choices cater especially to Socializers and Achievers, as defined in Bartle’s Tax-

3.4
Discussion
The review of literature and case studies reveals a strong relationship between motivational design
and interaction principles in shaping the user experiences in trivia-style games. Gamification frame-
works such as Self-Determination Theory and the Octalysis Framework highlight that engagement
often depends on satisfying psychological needs like competence, autonomy, and relatedness. These
frameworks explain why features such as streaks, daily challenges, and unlockable content are success-
ful, which is to motivate players to return back to the game and foster a sense of progression. The case
studies GeoGuessr and AniGuessr demonstrate how simple, repeatable gameplay loops supported by
daily resets can lead to long-term retention without requiring complex mechanics.
Interaction design principles add another dimension to this understanding by focusing on how
users physically and cognitively interact with a game. Norman’s concepts of affordances, signifiers and
feedback, combined with Nielsen’s heuristics of recognition versus recall, emphasize the importance of
minimizing friction and ensuring clarity in user interfaces. This is especially relevant for mobile quiz
games, where players expect short, low-effort sessions and immediate feedback, but where some may
also seek deeper mastery over time.
Comparing existing trivia games reveals a gap in how input methods are handled. Platforms like
Anime Music Quiz (AMQ) and AniGuessr favor filtered dropdown input, which support memory recall
and can deepen learning of song titles but often feel slower and less suited for casual play. In contrast,
widely used trivia apps such as Kahoot rely on multiple-choice input, which reduce cognitive load and
increase accessibility but may encourage guessing rather than real knowledge. This trade-off between
recognition-based input for convenience and recall-based input for challenge emerges as a key design
decision that affects both user satisfaction and motivation.
Broader trends in mobile gaming reinforce this tension. As outlined in the introduction, mobile
players prefer experiences that are quick to access, easy to understand, and enjoyable in short ses-
sions. In contrast to long-term retention, which requires to create an experience that gives a sense
of accomplishment and community. Moreover to support the claim, in the Research Methodology

==PAGE:27==
chapter, field observations and surveys on the users were conducted. Furthermore, case studies like
GeoGuessr show that balancing exploration and reward can lead to habitual play, while AniGuessr
demonstrates how even niche quiz games can build loyal audiences through daily engagement loops
and simple interaction flows.
Taken all this in together, these insights suggest that the most crucial factor for this thesis is under-
standing how different input methods, particularly multiple-choice versus filtered dropdown, impact
the balance between accessibility, cognitive challenge, and motivational appeal. While gamification
elements can drive return play and social connection, the way users input answers directly shapes the
shape and depth of the quiz experience. Building on these findings the research has narrowed and
refined the scope to examine the following question:
How do multiple-choice and filtered dropdown input methods impact player engagement
and perceived challenge in gamified mobile-style anime music quizzes?

4.2
Build-Measure-Learn
Figure 5: The Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop (image taken from (14))
The Build-Measure-Learn loop model (BML) is a iterative methodology within UX and lean devel-
opment, which involves a cycle of continuous creation and testing of ideas, validating hypotheses and
adapting based on results (14). The process aims to determine whether a feature or concept should
be kept, reserved or pivoted away from. The steps involved for the model are listed as following and
will be applied to the making of the anime music quiz prototype:
• Step 1 – Plan: Before building, the concept needs to be clearly defined. After the concept has
been defined, the hypotheses are formulated based on prior research and user insights, which
could stem from surveys and observations etc. In the case of the thesis, one of the hypotheses
would be about which input method better supports user satisfaction.
• Step 2 – Build: A Minimal Viable Product (MVP) is then developed to test the hypotheses.
The MVP showcases the core features and functionalities of the app like the input methods in
this thesis. With each iteration, the MVP can be expanded or refined based on user feedback.
30
==PAGE:32==
• Step 3 – Measure: The data is collected from prototype testing. The collected data are both
quantitative such as completion time and qualitative such as user satisfaction. The results will
be compared and evaluated against the hypotheses.
• Step 4 – Learn: The findings are used to decide the next steps, which is to either preserve
the current iteration, refine the prototype or pivot to a totally different approach. The cycle is
repeated until it aligns with user needs and goals.

was highest in trivia activities that rewarded knowledge sharing and allowed for friendly competition
among friends. All of this matches the motivational traits seen in Bartle’s Socializers and Achievers.
An example of this was one of the larger activities which is shown in Figure 6. This particular
event activity demonstrates how music recognition can be used to create a shared experience among
attendees, where players engage both competitively and socially in real time. This figure is important
because it visually represents how fan communities already interact with music-based games in physical
settings, offering insight into design opportunities for digital quiz formats.
Watching how players
worked together and reacted to familiar songs by humming, singing along, or simply enjoying the
moment, gave some ideas for how to design digital interactions. It showed that features like quick
feedback, easy participation, and social elements can make the experience more engaging.
These
observations help explain how similar ideas could be used in an anime music quiz app to create an
experience that players want to return to and play repeatedly.

Interestingly, this raises an important question about how different input types align with player

Even with the small sample size, the results match what was observed at the convention. People

deeper knowledge testing will guide the prototype design, especially when comparing multiple-choice
and filtered dropdown input in later user testing.

4.5.2
Prototype 2: Cognitive science experiment
The second prototype, which is a high-fidelity functional prototype was used in a formal experiment
to evaluate how two different input methods, multiple-choice and filtered dropdown, influence player
engagement and perceived challenge in a mobile anime music quiz. The aim was to link design decisions
36
==PAGE:38==
to cognitive science concepts such as cognitive load, decision-making speed and motivation.
4.5.2.1
Experimental design
The experiment followed a within-subjects design, which means that every participant will expe-
rience playing both input methods. This allowed for a direct comparison between conditions while
reducing the influence of individual differences in skill or anime music knowledge. It also allowed for
the amount of participants to be less.
4.5.2.2
Hypotheses
• H1 (Time): Mean completion time per song differs between MC and DD.
• H2 (Accuracy): Accuracy rate differs between MC and DD.
• H3 (Experience): Motivation and satisfaction ratings differ between MC and DD.
• Additionally, an exploratory question was included, which was the following: Do participants
perceive different workload or challenge between the two input methods?
4.5.2.3
Design, Controls & Variables
Each condition consisted of the same control factors, which were the 20 distinct song clips with
the song playing from the beginning to the end. There were no time limits, so the players were able
to take their time to guess the songs. The scoring rules and visuals were held constant. Furthermore,
the counterbalancing was considered as the trials for the participants were randomized, so the order
was either of multiple-choice (MC) and then filtered dropdown (DD) or vice versa.
• Independent variable: Input method MC vs DD).
• Dependent variables: Player engagement, perceived challenge and quiz performance (score
and completion time).
• Control factors: Control factors: the same set of quiz questions, scoring system and visual
style.
• Covariates: Self-reported familiarity of anime musicu quizzes and self-identified Bartle’s player
type.

4.5.2.4
Materials
A survey made from Google Forms were given to the participants that included the function
prototype. The functional prototype was built in Flutter and deployed in Netlify. It included two
modes corresponding to the two input methods. The same set of 20 songs was used in both conditions.
Data were logged automatically to Supabase, recording participant ID, order of conditions, song ID,
trial index, correctness, response time and set-level performance summaries (mean RT & accuracy).
4.5.2.5
Participants
A total of 31 participants took part in the experiment according to the survey. They were recruited
from anime-related communities mostly from Discord, personal contacts and social media channels.
Participants were required to be at least 18 years old or to have parent/guardian consent if younger and
to have a familiarity with anime music quizzes or good anime knowledge. The study was minimal risk
as no sensitive personal data was collected. All answers were anonymous and every participant got a
random generated participant ID, unless they themselves write their ID. Furthermore the participants
could withdraw at any time. The participant’s ID that did not complete all 20 questions will still be
used, but a separate result of those that completed all of it will also be showcased.
A power analysis using G*Power 3.1 for a within-subjects, two-tailed paired t-test with α = .05
and 80% power showed that n = 34 participants were needed to find a medium effect size (dz = 0.5)
(40). The Supabase-only dataset (n = 40) was above this number, but the merged dataset (n = 17)
was below it. This lower number in the merged dataset made it harder to find smaller effects, which
is why the results in Section 5.3.14 are reported separately for each dataset.
4.5.2.6
Consent & Ethics
As the study was conducted entirely online and involved minimal risk, consent was obtained
through the introduction at the start of the survey stating that. In the introduction, the participants
were shortly briefed and informed about the study’s purpose, procedures and their right to withdraw
at any time. If they continue the survey they consent to participating to it. No identifying information
was collected, and all responses were stored anonymously through randomly generated ID. Data was
used solely for the purposes of this study and handled in accordance with standard ethical research

==PAGE:40==
practices (38).
4.5.2.7
Procedure
The experiment followed these steps:
1. Participants received an online survey form made from Google form.
2. The participants were informed about the study’s purpose, procedure and their right to withdraw
within the survey.
3. They then have to complete the pre-task question before proceeding the test.
4. Participants were randomly assigned to start with either the multiple-choice or the filtered
dropdown version of the quiz by just pressing start, when ”Randomize order” was turned on. In
case of them being assigned, they would be told beforehand to turn it off.
5. After playing the both sets of multiple choice and filterend dropdown, the participants completed
post-quiz questions about their engagement and perceived challenge.
6. At the end of the survey, the participants were invited to provide open feedback and tell their
prefered input method.
4.5.2.8
Exclusions
Participants with obvious inattentive play was excluded which could be captured from unrealisti-
cally fast responses such as a combined mean response time of ¡1000 ms). All exclusions were logged
inside supabase, as those data got flagged from the system.
4.5.2.9
Data collection
The data were collected automatically by the prototype to Supabase. The data that was col-
lected included participant ID, trial (MC or DD), song ID, trial index, correctness (TRUE/FALSE)
and response time (ms). Set-level summaries, which are either DD or MC set, were also recorded
and stored such as mean response time and accuracy percentage. Engagement, satisfaction, work-
load and challenge ratings were collected through the Likert-scale from the surveys in Google Forms.

==PAGE:41==
Finnally, optional open-ended comments were gathered and the participants preferred input method
were gathered for qualitative insights.
4.5.2.10
Analysis Plan
The goal of the analysis was to compare the two input methods (MC vs. DD) for performance,
experience and perceived workload. For each dependent variable, a paired-sample t-tests were planned
to address if there was a difference between the two methods in a within-subject experiment. If the
data was not normally distributed, a Wilcoxon signed-rank test would be used instead. Effect sizes
for significant effects would be reported using Cohen’s dz with 95% confidence intervals.
Performance was measured by mean response time for each block and percentage of correct answers.
Experience-relates measurements such as motivation, enjoyment and satisfaction as well as workload
measures such as mental demand and challenge were calculated as mean scores of their respective
Likert scale items.
Order effect were examined using independent-samples t-tests to compare the
difference in scores between the conditions (MC–DD) for participants who played in the AB vs. the
BA order.

4.5.2.11
Cognitive science link
In the context of this thesis, flow theory is relevant for understanding how different input methods
can shape the balance between challenge and skill, which influences the engagement for different player
types. Multiple-choice inputs are expected to lower memory retrieval demands, as correct answers are
visible among the options. In contrast and from a germane load perspective, filtered dropdown inputs
require active recall and targeted search. This may increase cognitive load and perceived difficulty, but
can also encourage deeper processing and long-term retention by forcing players to actively recall song
and anime titles, whereas multiple-choice may focus more on quick recognition rather than active
learning and skill. According to cognitive load theory, these difference can impact engagement by
either enhancing the sense of challenge or causing frustration, depending on the player’s preferences
and skill level.

5.3.1
Prototype 2
5.3.1.1
Desgin
The interface for Prototype 2 was deliberately optimized for mobile-style interactio to enable
all devices to be used for testing.
A fixed mobile viewp and touch-friendly controls ensured that
participants, whether on mobile or desktop, experienced the same interaction style. This approach
aligned with the narrowed research question, which focused on mobile-style quiz mechanics rather
than strictly mobile, which the initial research question was.
The design maintained the visuals and structure on both input methods. This meant that elements
such as the audio playback controls, song clip length, scoring rules, and overall layout remained
constant. All of this was to ensure that input method was the only functional independent variable.
Ideally, The prototype incorporated clear feedback cues which including answer confirmation and
score updates to align with flow theory principles such as immediate feedback and clear goals, but due
to internal pilot runs, even a short, floating SnackBar drew attention away from the gameplay, audio
and the next input, when tested on mobile devices. A solution was not found due to time constraints.
Therefore, the prototype did not incorporate instant feedback cues whenever an answer was made
during the quiz. Instead, performance feedback was provided only at the end of the whole test with
the results summary screen. Since all 20 songs were unique, learning effects were not a concern. The
decision was therefore to omit instant feedback to avoid biasing perceived challenge or engagement
and ensuring differences between input methods were clearer to interpret.
5.3.1.2
User journey of the prototype
The user journey was kept simple and linear:
1. Start screen with instructions.
2. Play through of one input method block (10 songs).
3. 10 seconds break with countdown
4. Immediate transition to the second block (10 songs) with the alternative input method.
5. Results screen with performance data
51
==PAGE:53==
6. Post-game survey capturing engagement, satisfaction, workload, and perceived challenge
This straightforward flow gave minimal complexity in navigation and kept the focus on the main
game loop, while reducing extraneous cognitive load.
5.3.1.3
Development
Prototype 2 was implemented as a high-fidelity, fully functional web application using Flutter to
ensure consistent performance across both mobile and desktop browsers. It only tested the features
of input methods.
Two separate input modes were developed within the same application. Multiple-Choice, which
displayed four possible answers for each song, on right answer and three distractors, allowing partici-
pants to make quick recognition-based selections. For the Filtered Dropdown, the search field function
was powered by Supabase’s partial-match search, which allowed the results to appear even with typ-
ing errors or incomplete titles to help reduce frustration, while keeping the recall challenge intact. In
order to balnace the order of input method, a randomization system was implemented. Half of the
participants started with MC then DD (AB order), and the other half started with DD then MC (BA
order). Flutter’s “randomise order” feature handled this automatically.
The backend was also handled by Supabase, which recorded all gameplay events automatically.
For each trial, the following data were logged: Participant ID, Condition (MC or DD), Song ID and
trial index, Response time in milliseconds, Correctness (0 = incorrect, 1 = correct) and mean response
time and accuracy percentage of the block.
Google Forms survey was used to collect feedback about enjoyment, motivation, satisfaction, work-
load and perceived challenge. It also asked for basic demographic information, familiarity with anime
music quizzes, and self-identified player type. The prototype link was placed directly in the survey so
participants could move easily from the instructions, to the game, and finally back to the feedback
form in the survey.
Before the launch, the prototype was tested to make sure that the audio clips played and looped
correctly. The switching between MC and DD block worked without problems and all gameplay data
was recorded correctly in Supabase. All of it was to ensure that the prototype was ready for the
experiment.

Analysis of the merged dataset shows that response times were significantly faster for MC than
for DD. Specifically when looking at t(16) = -3.68, p = .002, dz = -0.89. The degrees of freedom is
therefore 16, hence t(16) due to 17 participants and the numbers indicate a large effect size in favor
of MC. This suggests that participants could select answers more quickly when using the recognition-
based MC format compared to the recall-based DD format.
Accuracy was also significantly higher for MC when looking at t(16) = 2.25, p = .039, dz = 0.55,
which reflects a medium effect size. This difference implies that the MC format not only reduced
the time required to respond but also increased the likelihood of correct answers. Such a pattern is
consistent with cognitive theories that recognition tasks typically impose lower memory demands than
recall tasks, thereby improving both speed and accuracy.
Overall, the merged dataset results support the two hypothesis (H1 & H2) that recognition-based
selection (MC) is less cognitively demanding than recall-based entry (DD), since there is a difference
between MC and DD in both accuracy and time. However, the relatively small sample size (n = 17)
limits the ability to generalize these findings to smaller effects, which is why they are interpreted
alongside the larger Supabase-only dataset.

6
Conclusion
This thesis started with the broad goal of exploring how interaction design influences user engage-
ment and satisfaction in real-time gamified mobile anime music quizzes. As the project progressed
delving into the related works and the state of art, the scope became more focused on a specific aspect
of interaction design, which is: how different input methods affect player performance, challenge, and
enjoyment in an anime music quiz game. This focus made it possible to study the topic in more depth
while still connecting back to the original aim of understanding how design choices shape the player
experience. The main comparison was between recognition-based multiple choice and recall-based
filtered dropdown input formats to see which provided a better balance between speed, accuracy, and
engagement.
The background and theory chapters explained that recognition is usually faster and easier than
57
==PAGE:59==
recall, but that challenge can also be an important part of player enjoyment. The experiment tested
these ideas in a real gameplay setting, using both gameplay data and survey feedback.
The results showed that MC was faster and more accurate than DD, supporting the idea that
recognition needs less memory effort. However, the survey showed that many participants enjoyed DD
because it was more challenging and rewarding. This connects to the idea in the introduction that
not all players value speed or accuracy the same. This is due to the fact that some prefer a bigger
challenge, even if it means slower performance and accuracy.
The data was split into three datasets: Supabase-only (n = 40), Survey-only (n = 31), and
Merged (n = 17) datasets. This is because not all participants completed the survey or gave a correct
participant ID. The Supabase dataset met the sample size from the power analysis for detecting
medium effects, but the merged dataset did not, which made it harder to see smaller effects. Still,
both datasets gave the same main result: MC is more efficient, while DD is more challenging.
For game design, the results suggest that both methods have benefits. MC works well for casual
play and mixed skill levels. DD can be used for a more competitive or expert mode where the challenge
is higher. A mix of both could give variety and keep players engaged for longer.
This study had some limits, such as the small size of the merged dataset, the specific group of
participants, and the range of music used. Future research should use larger and more diverse samples,
include other input methods, and look at the long-term learning effects of each method.
In summary, this research links theory about recognition and recall with real game design choices.
It shows that the best input method depends on the type of experience the designer wants to create
and what the target audience prefers. These findings can serve as a foundation for future studies
on input methods in gamified mobile-style environments, helping guide both academic research and
practical game design.
