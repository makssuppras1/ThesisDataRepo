Abstract
Optimal pharmacokinetic (PK) sampling is essential for informed decision­making in early
drug development, particularly when selecting appropriate doses for subsequent studies.
However, traditional methods often require extensive sampling, making them resource­
intensive and ethically challenging due to the high number of animals involved. This the­
sis introduces an automated, model­based R workflow leveraging nonlinear mixed­effects
modeling via nlmixr2 and optimal experimental design through PopED. The workflow inte­
grates prior knowledge using a curated library of population PK (popPK) models derived
from preclinical animal studies, improving sampling efficiency by incorporating similarity
metrics to select representative models and to import variability priors.
The developed methodology streamlines study designs, enabling robust estimation of PK
parameters with fewer resources. It also enables researchers lacking specialized com­
putational expertise to generate optimized PK study designs, reducing repetitive tasks
such as extensive communication between researchers and computational specialists.
Additionally, the documented workflow ensures ease of updates and maintenance, pro­
moting continuous improvement and expanding its applicability as new data enrich the
popPK model library. There is considerable potential to broaden this approach further by
integrating existing human popPK data, enabling a transition from preclinical to clinical
studies. Consequently, this approach improves the efficiency of early drug development
while aligning with sustainable and ethical research practices.

Introduction
Drug development is a complex, multi­stage process that involves collaboration across
biology, chemistry, pharmacology, engineering, and related disciplines. At each step,
success depends on informed decision­making, from discovery and screening to clinical
evaluation and regulatory approval. A persistent challenge is to develop safe and ef­
fective medicines while using time, resources, and experimental subjects efficiently and
responsibly.
Pharmacokinetic­pharmacodynamic (PKPD) analysis provides a quantitative framework
for linking dose to drug exposure and exposure to drug response in a particular animal,
supporting decisions throughout development [1, 2]. The most critical decisions supported
by PKPD analysis are the selection of suitable doses for new compounds, balancing ef­
ficacy and safety. It combines pharmacokinetic (PK) models, which describe how drug
concentrations change over time, with pharmacodynamic (PD) models, which relate those
concentrations to observed effects (Figure 1.1).
Figure 1.1: PKPD framework illustrating the relationship between dose, drug concentra­
tion, and drug effect through PK and PD models.
An essential part of PKPD analysis and drug development is designing studies that effi­
ciently capture drug concentration profiles with as few samples as possible, balancing
robust data needs with ethical, economical, and practical considerations.
Traditional
approaches, however, have often involved extensive sampling, leading to increased re­
source demands and use of laboratory animals.
Figure 1.2 outlines a typical early drug development workflow and highlights stages where
model­based sampling optimization is especially valuable.

A typical project begins by identifying a disease of interest and a therapeutic strategy
based on relevant biology [3]. This leads to target identification and the selection of can­
didate lead compounds (gate 1). Iterative compound screening follows, where efficient
PK sampling is critical for ranking candidates and selecting a lead compound (gate 2).
Once selected, the lead compound undergoes detailed PKPD analysis in animal models
to assess safety, efficacy, and toxicity. These data guide subsequent development and
allow early elimination of suboptimal candidates, which reduces overall cost and attrition
[4].
Despite advances in modeling and optimization, many early­stage studies still rely on
suboptimal or manual sampling designs, often overlooking variability between subjects
and existing knowledge from previous studies. Modeling and simulation strategies, par­
ticularly those based on population PK (popPK) models, have emerged as powerful tools
to address these challenges by quantifying variability, guiding the selection of sampling
time points, and improving the efficiency of PK data collection. However, implementing
model­based designs often requires specialized computational skills, creating a technical
barrier for many scientists and limiting the routine application of these approaches in early
drug development.
Aim and scope
To address this barrier, this thesis presents a user­friendly tool that enables scientists
without specialized computational expertise to apply advanced model­based methods for
optimizing PK sampling schedules in early drug development.
In the initial design cycle, the tool assumes sufficiently similar PK across candidate com­
pounds, with preliminary screening data serving as justification. A single sampling sched­
ule is therefore applied across candidates for lead compound selection. For subsequent
lead compound characterization, the tool leverages a library of popPK models constructed
from established compounds in common laboratory species (e.g., rat, mouse, dog) to de­
sign new in vivo studies. This incorporates prior knowledge and species­specific vari­
ability, as quantified from previous studies, on the assumption that such variability ap­
plies to new compounds in the same species. As the model library expands, each new
study further enhances this shared resource, supporting the long­term sustainability of
the approach. Throughout, the tool generates optimized sampling schedules designed to
support robust parameter estimation while minimizing sampling and animal use.

1. Lead compound selection, generating a shared sampling schedule to enable robust

2. Lead compound characterization, generating a compound­specific schedule by com­

3. First­in­human (FIH) study, generating a compound­specific schedule by using sta­

This thesis implements and validates the first two cases and outlines the framework for
the third. While focused on these applications, the tool is generalizable to other contexts
where optimal PK sampling is required.

ter 6 concludes with a summary of findings and suggestions for future work, including
extensions to clinical study design.

2
Population Pharmacokinetics
This chapter provides an introduction to population pharmacokinetics (popPK), focusing
on its role in describing variability in drug behavior and its relevance to study design in drug
development. The purpose is to establish a foundation for understanding the methods and
concepts relevant to the work presented in this thesis.
2.1
Introduction to population pharmacokinetics
Pharmacokinetics (PK) describes the movement of a drug through the body, from ad­
ministration to elimination. Models are used to characterize these processes, varying in
complexity depending on factors such as the route of administration, sampling techniques,
study design, and whether the modeling is performed at the individual or population level.
Traditional PK models focus on individual subjects, often under controlled conditions, and
do not capture the full extent of variability observed in real­world populations. In contrast,
popPK models describe drug behavior across a population, accounting for differences
between individuals as well as within the same individual over time [5].
To accurately model this variability, nonlinear mixed­effects (NLME) models are commonly
used in popPK studies. ’Nonlinear’ refers to the relationship between drug concentration,
model parameters, and independent variables (such as time), which is often not linear.
The ’mixed­effects’ framework combines fixed effects, which represent the typical popu­
lation parameters, with random effects, which account for variability at different levels [6,
7].
Variability in PK is generally categorized into inter­individual variability (IIV) and residual
unexplained variability (RUV) [5], with inter­occasion variability (IOV) increasingly recog­
nized as a distinct source of variability [8]. IIV refers to differences in PK parameters
between individuals due to factors such as genetics, age, weight, organ function, and
environmental influences.
RUV encompasses unexplained variations, including intra­
individual variability (random fluctuations within the same individual over time), measure­
ment errors, and model misspecifications. IOV accounts for differences in a patient’s
pharmacokinetic parameters across different occasions [5, 6, 7, 8].
There are three major components in popPK models. The first two are the structural
and statistical models, which in most cases together form the base model. The struc­
tural model describes the drug’s concentration–time profile as it moves through the body,
while the statistical model accounts for variability around the structural model [7]. The
third component is the covariate model, which explains part of the existing variability in
PK parameters by identifying relationships between subject characteristics and these pa­
rameters. Typically, covariate model building is initiated from a base model consisting
only of the structural and statistical components. However, in some cases, covariates are
incorporated into the base model when strong prior knowledge supports their inclusion
[9].
2.2
Structural model
The structural model describes how drug concentration in the body changes over time.
To achieve this, various PK modeling approaches can be used, including compartment
models and physiologically based pharmacokinetic (PBPK) models. Among these, com­
partment models are widely applied [6], describing drug concentration–time profiles using

terconnected compartments, each corresponding to a theoretical space where the drug

2.3
Statistical Model
The statistical model is incorporated into the popPK model to account for variability both
within individuals and across the population. Here, variability is categorized into three ma­
jor sources: inter­individual variability (IIV), inter­occasion variability (IOV), and residual
unexplained variability (RUV). Each of these sources adds a layer to the structural model
presented in Equation 2.1, introducing the corresponding variance terms.
2.3.1
Inter­individual variability
Inter­individual variability (IIV) describes how an individual’s parameter values differ from
the population values. In nonlinear mixed­effects (NLME) models, IIV is modeled using
random effects that modify the individual’s model parameters. Each parameter is asso­
ciated with a random effect ηP,i, representing the deviation of individual i from the typical
population value θP . IIV is incorporated into the model as:
Pi = g(θP, ηP,i)
(2.25)
where Pi is the parameter value for individual i, θP is the population­level estimate, and
ηP,i is the individual­specific random effect. The function g defines the parameterization.

2.3.3
Residual unexplained variability (RUV)
Residual unexplained variability (RUV) accounts for differences between observed and
predicted concentrations that remain after accounting for structural model components,
covariates, and IIV or IOV. It captures sources such as measurement error, model mis­
specification, and intra­individual variability.
RUV is incorporated by defining an observation model that relates the prediction f(t, θ, ηi)
to the observed data yij through an error term. Commonly used RUV models include
additive, proportional, and combined additive­proportional formulations [13].
Additive error model
Assumes constant variability across all concentration levels:
yi = f(t, θ, ηi) + ϵi
(2.33)
where ϵi ∼N(0, σ2)
Proportional error model
Assumes that variability scales with the predicted concentration:
yi = f(t, θ, ηi) · (1 + ϵi)
(2.34)
where ϵi ∼N(0, ω2)
Combined additive and proportional error model
Captures both constant and concentration­dependent variability:
yi = f(t, θ, ηi) · (1 + ϵprop,i) + ϵadd,i
(2.35)
where ϵprop,i ∼N(0, ω2) and ϵadd,i ∼N(0, σ2)
2.4
Covariate model
Covariates are commonly included in pharmacometric models to account for predictable
sources of IIV, particularly in parameters such as clearance and volume of distribution.
Including covariates can improve model fit and predictive performance, and may also
reduce RUV [14].
Covariate analysis can further support drug development decisions, such as identifying
relevant patient subgroups or informing dosing strategies [15]. Covariates may be in­
cluded as categorical or continuous predictors and are typically selected based on prior
knowledge of the drug, drug class, or relevant physiological factors [7].
Covariate modeling was not conducted in this project. The discussion is therefore kept
brief and is intended only to acknowledge its relevance in the broader context of pharma­
cometric analysis.

3.1.4
Estimation methods
nlmixr2 currently supports four estimation methods for parameter fitting in nonlinear mixed­
effects (NLME) models: nlme, FOCE, FOCEi and SAEM. Each offers different advantages
depending on model complexity, data structure, and computational requirements.
1. nlme
The nlme method, based on the nlme R package [16], uses a first­order linearization
around the population mean and minimizes a generalized least­squares criterion
rather than a likelihood­based objective function. It assumes normally distributed
random effects and is typically suitable for simple models with dense data [18]. As
this method was not used in the present project, it is not discussed further.
2. FOCE (First­order conditional estimation)
FOCE linearizes the model around the conditional mode (empirical Bayes estimate)
of the random effects, rather than assuming they are zero as in nlme. This improves
accuracy, especially when IIV is moderate or data per subject are sparse. The log­
likelihood is approximated using the Laplace method.
3. FOCEi (FOCE with interaction)
FOCE with interaction (FOCEi) extends FOCE by accounting for interaction between
IIV) and RUV, which improves estimation when residual error is not independent of
individual variability. Like FOCE, the log­likelihood is approximated via the Laplace
method.
4. SAEM (Stochastic approximation expectation–maximization)
SAEM is a simulation­based algorithm that treats random effects as latent variables
and uses stochastic simulations to approximate the expectation step of the expec­
tation maximization (EM) algorithm [19, 20]. It is well­suited for highly nonlinear
models, differential equations, and sparse or unbalanced datasets. In nlmixr2, the
log­likelihood is approximated using the Laplace method after the stochastic EM
convergence.

PopED
PopED is an open­source R package for model­based optimal experimental design in
pharmacometrics. It enables users to optimize design variables such as sample times,
dose levels, and number of subjects to maximize study information [21, 22]. The opti­
mization framework within PopED consists of three major components: model definition,
PopED database creation, and design optimization. For the resulting design to be infor­
mative and efficient, each part must be followed by systematic evaluation and potential
refinement.

lower bound on the variance of parameter estimates, known as the Cramér­–Rao lower
bound [21, 23]. This approach builds on earlier work by Foracchia et al., who demon­

Optimization algorithms
The method argument in PopED specifies the optimization algorithms used to explore the
design space. PopED supports several built­in methods, which can be applied individually
or in sequence. A short overview of a selection of these algorithms is provided below:
1. Adaptive Random Search (ARS): A global stochastic optimization method that
explores the design space by introducing random perturbations. The step size is
adjusted dynamically based on recent performance to balance exploration and ex­
ploitation.
2. Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS): A gradient­based local optimizer
from the quasi­Newton family. It uses approximations of the Hessian matrix to find
local optima efficiently.
3. Line Search (LS): A simple local optimization strategy that iteratively adjusts pa­
rameters along a direction to optimize the objective function.

Methods
This chapter describes the implementation of the sampling design tool developed in this
project. It introduces the modeling frameworks, the model library and similarity­based
logic used to obtain variability priors, and the optimization strategies used to support effi­
cient experimental design in early drug development.
4.1
Overview of methodology
Figure 4.5 outlines the overall workflow. Raw concentration–time data are cleaned, ex­
plored, and passed to model development, where nonlinear mixed­effects (NLME) models
are fitted in nlmixr2 and refined until they meet predefined diagnostic criteria. Accepted
models are stored in a popPK library for reuse in design optimization.
Two optimization workflows follow. In the lead compound selection workflow, PopED uses
fixed­effect parameters representing target PK properties (e.g. terminal half­life) to opti­
mize a shared sampling schedule across candidate compounds. In the lead compound
characterization workflow, PopED combines a structural model from the selected lead with
a statistical model drawn from a representative popPK library model of the same species,
selected based on compound similarity. Both workflows involve building a design space,
optimizing sampling times, and evaluating results against precision targets, repeating if
needed with increased sample size.
This chapter is organized accordingly: data preparation (Section 4.2), model development
(Section 4.3), model library and similarity mapping (Section 4.4), and optimal design work­
flows (Section 4.5). Section 4.5 presents the characterization workflow first, followed by

4.3.1
Initial parameter estimation
Before defining the initial model, initial parameter estimates must be provided, as required
by all estimation methods listed in Section 3.1.4. These are typically obtained from non­
compartmental analysis (NCA) [29], manual inspection, or literature values. In this project,
the process was automated using separate approaches for one­ and two­compartment
models, as described below.
Initial estimates for one­compartment models
To obtain reasonable initial estimates for one­compartment models, a linear model was
fitted to the log­transformed concentration–time data. This yielded the intercept C0, repre­
senting the initial concentration, and the slope, corresponding to the negative elimination
rate constant −k. Based on these values, the initial population parameter estimates for V
and CL were calculated using Equations 2.5 and 2.7, respectively.
Initial estimates for two­compartment models
Due to the biexponential nature of two­compartment models, initial estimates were ob­
tained using nonlinear least­squares fitting, specifically the Levenberg–Marquardt algo­
rithm [30]. The R function nlsLM() was used to fit the data, based on the analytical
solution for the concentration in the central compartment shown in Equation 2.13. The
function returns the coefficients A and B, which represent the intercepts of the distribution
and elimination phases, and the rate constants ω and β, corresponding to the distribution
and elimination rates. Figure 2.6 illustrates the biexponential fit used to estimate these
parameters.
Once these values were obtained, the initial population parameter estimates for the central
compartment, volume of distribution (Vc) and CL, were calculated using Equations 2.17
and 2.18. The peripheral volume (Vt) and intercompartmental clearance (CLd) were then
derived from Equations 2.23 and 2.24.

4.3.2
Model fitting and refinement
For each cleaned and formatted dataset, model development followed a consistent work­
flow. The process began by fitting a one­compartment model with a random effect on
CL, even when the log­transformed concentration–time profiles exhibited biexponential
behavior. In such cases, a two­compartment model with a random effect on CL was also
evaluated.
Before fitting each model, initial parameter estimates were provided to ensure conver­

in Section 4.3.1, depending on the compartment structure.
Once these initial models had been fitted using the nlmixr2() function (as shown in List­

Model performance was initially assessed using individual prediction plots, which display
observed concentrations alongside both population and individual predictions over time.

tify major model misfits or inconsistencies before continuing refinement.
Model evaluation was based on two sets of criteria (defined in Section 4.3.3): one to
assess whether a model was statistically and numerically reliable, and another to compare
competing models.
The adequacy of individual models was evaluated using:
I. Relative standard error (RSE) on parameter estimates below 30% [31]
II. Shrinkage below 25% for all retained random effects [32]
Only models meeting these requirements were considered for further comparison.
Model comparison was based on:
I. A decrease in objective function value (∆OFV > 3.84), indicating a significantly
improved fit
II. A lower Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), favoring simpler models that balanced
fit and complexity

4.3.3
Evaluation criteria
The final model selection was based on both quantitative and qualitative evaluation crite­
ria, outlined below. These criteria were used to assess the adequacy of individual models
and to compare competing alternatives.
Model evaluation included statistical measures such as the likelihood ratio test (LRT)
using the objective function value (OFV), relative standard error (RSE), shrinkage, and
Akaike information criterion (AIC). Graphical diagnostics, specifically observed vs. pre­
dicted plots, residual plots, and visual predictive checks (VPCs), were used to complement
the numerical assessments. This section defines each criterion and explains how it was
applied to assess model fit, stability, and predictive performance.
Relative standard error
RSE quantifies the precision of parameter estimates by expressing the standard error as
a percentage of the estimate itself. It is calculated as:

In this project, RSE values reported by nlmixr2 were reviewed for all fixed effects to assess

were considered acceptable.

The AIC is commonly used for model selection across many fields, including pharmaco­

AIC = OFV + 2 · np

where OFV is the objective function value and nP is the number of estimated parameters.
AIC combines a measure of fit with a penalty for model complexity, favoring models that
achieve adequate performance with fewer parameters. Lower AIC values indicate a more
favorable balance.
In this project, AIC was used to support model refinement by comparing alternative models
that were already considered adequate based on other diagnostic criteria. It was espe­
cially useful when evaluating non­nested models, where the LRT could not be applied.
Observed vs. predicted plots
Observed vs. predicted plots are used to visually assess model performance by compar­
ing predicted concentrations to observed data.
In this project, two commonly used plots were included:
• PRED vs. observations: to evaluate the agreement between population predic­
tions and observed concentrations.
• IPRED vs. observations: to assess how well individual predictions match the ob­
served concentrations.
Here, PRED and IPRED refer to population and individual predictions, respectively. An
ideal model produces data points that align closely with the line of identity (y = x), indi­
cating that both fixed effects and IIV are well captured.
Residual plots
Residual plots help identify potential model misspecification by examining the distribution
and structure of residuals. Ideally, residuals should be randomly scattered around zero,
with no apparent trends or structure.
The following residual plots were included in this project:
30

• Time vs. IWRES: to detect time­related trends in residuals.
• Pred vs. IWRES: to identify trends related to predicted concentrations.
Here, IWRES refers to individual weighted residuals. Patterns in these plots were in­
terpreted as signs of model inadequacy, prompting further refinement when necessary.
In particular, the residual plots were used to evaluate the adequacy of the RUV model.
A well­specified RUV model was expected to produce residuals that were symmetrically

heteroscedasticity were observed, alternative error models (e.g., additive, proportional,

Visual predictive checks
VPCs are simulation­based diagnostics used to evaluate a model’s ability to reproduce
the central trend and variability of observed data over time [37, 38].
To construct a VPC, the time axis is divided into bins, and the 10th, 50th, and 90th per­
centiles of the observed data are calculated within each bin. These are plotted as dashed
lines to represent the empirical distribution.
Model predictions are generated through Monte Carlo simulation, where a large number
of datasets are simulated using the same model and study design (e.g., dosing regimen,
sampling schedule). For each time bin, the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles are calculated
from the observed data and plotted as curves. From the simulations, 90% prediction
intervals for the same percentiles are constructed and displayed as shaded areas.
In this project, VPCs were used to assess the predictive performance of each model. A
good fit was indicated when the empirical percentiles fell within the simulated prediction
intervals, without persistent deviations over time.

Model library and similarity mapping
In order to obtain an optimal sampling design for a detailed PK study (e.g., lead com­
pound characterization), prior knowledge is required about both the structural and statis­
tical models of the compound in question. However, this information is often not readily
available, as reliable estimates of variability typically require extensive sampling and a
relatively large number of subjects. In preclinical settings, such data are usually derived
from animal studies, where variability can differ notably across species. To address this, a
similarity assessment was constructed to extract variability from the most representative
model in a predefined library of popPK models, developed as described in Section 4.3.
This library accounts for both compound and species, enabling selection of relevant vari­
ability priors.
Discussion of one­compartment models is omitted here, as none of the resulting models
in the predefined library followed a one­compartment structure.

Evaluating the optimality loop
Each optimization cycle generated a candidate sampling schedule, calculated the FIM,
and converted it to RSEs. A design was accepted only when the RSE for every param­
eter fell below 30%. If any RSE exceeded this limit, the schedule was enlarged by two
sampling times, new PopED inputs were created, and the optimiser was run again. The
loop stopped as soon as all RSEs satisfied the 30% target.

LS (ARS+LS). Each method was applied using four optimality criteria: A­optimality, D­
optimality, log D­optimality (lnD­optimality), and RSE­optimality.

Genetic algorithm (GA) and BFGS methods were initially considered but excluded due
to their incompatibility with discrete design variables. PopED automatically skips GA op­

sampling times were defined over a discrete grid. Similarly, BFGS is a gradient­based
method that requires continuous, differentiable variables and is not applicable in discrete
optimization problems. Only methods compatible with the discrete design space were
retained for evaluation.

6
Conclusion and Future Perspectives
The primary aim of this thesis was to implement a user­friendly tool that allows scien­
tists who lack computational skills to build defensible pharmacokinetic (PK) sampling de­
signs early in drug development. By integrating an expanding population PK (popPK)
model library, similarity­based priors, and optimization routines, the workflow supports
efficient, ethically sound, and sustainable sampling strategies. An initial screening step
applies a simplified, shared sampling schedule to quickly identify and exclude candidate
compounds, thereby reducing unnecessary animal use. Once promising leads are se­
lected, the tool enables detailed characterization by designing compound­specific, opti­
mized sampling schedules that ensure robust parameter estimation while maintaining eth­
ical study design. As more existing and newly collected data from these optimal designs
are incorporated, the ever­expanding model library strengthens cumulative knowledge
and sustainability for future studies.
Key findings
First, a curated popPK model library was assembled using single­dose (IV) PK datasets
from preclinical animal studies across multiple compounds and species. For each unique
compound–species pair, an individual popPK model was built and carefully documented,
currently restricted to a two­compartment IV structure. Modeling each compound–species
combination separately allows the library to capture interspecies PK differences, and its
design allows straightforward addition of new models as further data become available.
Second, a quantitative similarity metric was implemented to reliably identify which library
model’s PK behavior most closely matches that of a new compound. For each assess­
ment, only models from the same animal species as the new compound are considered,
ensuring that species­specific physiological differences are respected. This enables the
use of credible priors for inter­individual variability (IIV) and residual variability (RUV) once
a lead compound is selected for further study, supporting robust and biologically relevant
analyses.
Third, two complementary optimization workflows were automated. The lead compound
selection workflow produces a single schedule that can be applied across multiple candi­
date compounds and relies only on target fixed­effect parameters; designs are accepted
when they meet predefined criteria for terminal half­life error, extrapolated exposure, and
a minimum number of flexible samples (Figure 4.8). The lead compound characteriza­
tion workflow then refines the sampling schedule for a selected compound by coupling
its structural model with variability priors from the library, iteratively optimizing until all pa­
rameter precision targets are met (Figure 4.5). Combined with the popPK model library
and similarity metric, these workflows provide an integrated platform for key PK sampling
decisions prior to first­in­human (FIH) trials (Figure 1.2).
Contributions to the field
The principal contribution of this work is a documented R workflow that integrates ad­
vanced nonlinear mixed­effects (NLME) modeling with optimal design techniques, en­
abling researchers to obtain an optimal sampling design using a single script without writ­
ing custom code. By consolidating the process into a streamlined and reproducible work­
flow, it reduces turnaround time and delivers statistically robust sampling designs while

ensuring complete traceability of each step. Importantly, this framework also allows do­
main experts to contribute modeling and design insights without requiring direct, ongoing
coordination, supporting more efficient and collaborative study development as needs or
datasets evolve.
Limitations
This workflow’s model library is limited in scope, as it is based exclusively on single­dose
IV data and, after substantial data cleaning, contains only two­compartment IV bolus mod­
els with sparse individual observations. The small number of compounds, limited models
per compound, and few subjects per model reduced the ability to identify variability and
prevented robust evaluation across diverse PK scenarios. As a result, both the imple­
mentation and assessment of the workflow are restricted to this narrow model class, and
applicability to broader or more complex settings remains uncertain. Time constraints
mainly affected the appearance and user interface of the tool, as enhancements such as
a user­friendly dashboard could not be developed within the project scope.
Future work
Several developments could strengthen both the scope and the usability of the workflow:
Broaden the model library Incorporating additional structural models such as three­compartment
kinetics and dosing routes that include absorption like oral or subcutaneous (SC) ad­
ministration would widen the tool’s applicability. Each new model class should be
matched with corresponding logic in the optimization scripts and in the similarity
assessment.
Enrich model metadata At present the information file that accompanies every library
model records only whether RUV interacts with IIV. Adding fields for covariate ef­
fects, dosing route, sampling window constraints and other model­specific flags
would be a clean and maintainable way to set more arguments automatically and
on rational grounds.
Refine the similarity metric The current metric relies on fixed­effect estimates for the
new compound. A next step is to redesign it in close consultation with domain
experts, combining PK profiles with chemical or biological descriptors so the tool
can still choose the right variability priors when parameter estimates are uncertain.
A richer metric will become increasingly important as the model library grows and
candidate matches increase.
Expand evaluation criteria A richer set of stopping rules with separate thresholds for
fixed effects and random effects or user­defined limits on specific parameters would
give researchers finer control over what counts as an acceptable design. Litera­
ture suggests, for example, a 30% percent standard error for fixed effects and 50%
percent for random effects[7]. Exposing such targets as editable inputs would add
flexibility.
Develop a human model library Creating a popPK model library based on clinical hu­
man data could extend the tool’s applicability to first­in­human (FIH) trial design.
Once a suitable human model library is assembled, the workflow could be used
directly for optimizing clinical study designs, facilitating a smoother transition from
preclinical to early clinical phases. This aligns with the translational research objec­
tive of increasing clinical trial success rates by improving continuity and informed
decision­making across development stages [41].

Create a shared dashboard and extensible R package Build an interactive Shiny dash­
board with two panels. One panel lets laboratory scientists run design searches
without opening an integrated development environment (IDE). The second panel
allows contributors to store example workflows, modeling guidelines, and shared
notes, building a living pharmacometrics knowledge base. The dashboard will rely
on a companion R package that contains both the optimization routines and utilities
for model development. New functions and logic can be added as the model library
grows, keeping the package and the dashboard in step.
Pursuing these lines of work will transform this proof of concept into a versatile platform
that advances with new methods and an expanding model library. By making robust
model­informed design accessible to laboratory researchers regardless of computational
background, the tool supports more efficient and targeted PK studies, while reducing un­
necessary animal use and cutting costs associated with excessive sampling. In making
these capabilities broadly available, the workflow has the potential to accelerate and im­
prove drug development.
