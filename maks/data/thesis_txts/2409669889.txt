 IV 
Abstract 
According to the Hue Heat hypothesis, colors affect the human perception of temperature, 
making the same environment seem warmer in warm hues and cooler in cold hues. The 
aim of this study was to investigate the potential correlation between the correlated color 
temperature (CCT) of LED lighting and the perceived temperature. If proven true, the 
concept of the hypothesis can support an expansion of the temperature range building 
occupants find comfortable and help save energy for heating and cooling. 
The study was based on experiments exposing 44 human subjects to different CCTs at 
optimal thermal summer and winter conditions. Both the immediate effect and the effect 
after a 30-minute period with constant CCT exposure was studied. Additionally, the 
impact of aging and exposure to light from a PC monitor was studied – areas not 
sufficiently uncovered previously, but essential for the implementation of the potential 
results in the real world. 
The results indicated a correlation between the CCT of lighting and the thermal 
perception but were not statistically significant. As such, the hue-heat hypothesis was not 
confirmed. For an operative temperature of 24.5 °C the results indicated that by adjusting 
the CCT an expansion of the comfort temperature interval of 1.8 °C can be achieved. The 
effect of the CCT on the thermal perception decreased after the 30-minute period with 
exposure to a constant CCT. The results from the PC experiments were similar to the 
other however the CCT extremes had weaker impact on the evaluation of the thermal 
environment. Light exposure to high CCT resulted in the most perceptible influence of 
age.  
The correlation between the CCT and the perception of the visual environment were 
studied as well. Despite a constant illuminance level, the light was perceived as being 
brighter, preferred darker and causing more glare with increasing CCT.

1   INTRODUCTION 
==PAGE:13==
1. Introduction 
 
 13 
It seems to be a commonly accepted idea that reddish colors are associated with warmth 
while bluish colors are associated with coldness. This color-temperature relation is known 
as the hue-heat hypothesis (Bennett & Rey 1972). According to the hue-heat hypothesis 
not only the perceived temperature of objects is affected by this phenomenon, 
environments are too. An environment with light in the red end of the visual spectrum is 
perceived as thermally warmer than the exact same environment with light in the blue 
end of the visual spectrum. Thus, an expansion of the temperature range building 
occupants find thermally comfortable might be possible by changing the color 
temperature of the lighting. A confirmation of the hue-heat hypothesis implies energy 
savings as heating and cooling can be reduced. 
The influence on thermal sensation by color must be of a psychological nature since color 
has no obvious influence on the human heat balance (P. O. Fanger et al. 1977). Through 
human experiments in test facilities at the Technical University of Denmark the aim of 
this project was to test the hypothesis that the correlated color temperature of modern 
LED-lighting affects the perceived temperature.  
1.1 Context 
The Danish funding organization ELFORSK supports research and development 
targeting energy savings. This master thesis is part of an ELFORSK funded project and 
is a collaboration between two departments at the Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU), Department of Civil Engineering and Department of Photonics Engineering and 
the Danish Building Research Institute (SBi). With the official title “Warm or Cool? 
Lights influence on thermal comfort” the project started in March 2016 and will end in 
December 2018. The project phases are illustrated in the timeline in figure 1.1.

Field tests will be conducted in office spaces at DTU Risø in the beginning of 2018.

December 2018. 
1.2 Objectives and delimitation 
The objective of this study was to quantify the correlation between correlated color 
temperature (CCT) of LED lighting and thermal sensation of humans around optimal 
thermal comfort conditions for summer and winter. In addition to the evaluation of the 
immediate effect of CCT on the thermal sensation, the prolonged effect of exposure to a 
constant CCT was studied. Further, the influence of light exposure from a PC monitor 
and the impact of aging was studied.  
The experiments were designed to represent typical office work and thus the design 
criteria were based on standards for offices in Denmark. Daylight was excluded from the 
experiment to reduce the number of influencing variables; thus, only the effect of the 
CCT of artificial LED lighting was evaluated.

1.3 Project phases 
The activities in this master project can be divided into five phases. 
Phase 1: Literature study. Initially, experience and knowledge from previous studies 
testing the hue-heat hypothesis were gathered. Essential theory regarding artificial 
lighting and thermal comfort was examined as well as the requirements of the Danish 
Building Regulations (The Danish Ministry of Economic and Business Affairs Danish 
Enterprise and Construction 2015) on these issues.  
Phase 2: Preparations of experiments. The experimental protocol was developed based 
on the experience of previous studies. The test room was calibrated ensuring the indoor 
environmental aspects during the experiments. Further, the lighting scenes were 
developed and tested, questionnaires were developed and test subjects were recruited and 
briefed.  
Phase 3: Execution of experiments. The experiments were carried out over a three-week 
period (week 44, 45, 46, 2017) with a total of 25 experiment sessions. 
Phase 4: Data processing and results. After the experiments, all the data was organized 
and converted from answers to data. The data was manually fed into the computer and 
graphs were made to communicate the results. General tendencies were found and 
statistical analyses were made to bring clarity to the significance of the results. 
Phase 5: Documentation and discussion. Finally, the results were processed and 
analyzed. The experimental protocol was discussed in light of study objectives and how 
the experiment protocol should be adjusted for the field experiments.

2   THEORY 
==PAGE:17==
2. Theory 
 
 17 
The indoor environment is of significant importance in a modern world where people on 
average spend 87 % of their time indoors (Klepeis et al. 2001). It influences our health, 
performance and productivity (Geng et al. 2017; Sundell 2001), making investments in 
good indoor climate important for companies and society, as neglecting the issue can 
have negative implications on performance in various manners. Since the first Danish 
building regulation in 1961 (Danish Transport Construction and Housing Authority 2016) 
regulations and standards have continuously been brought up to date to improve the 
indoor environmental quality (IEQ) and avoid harmful compounds in building materials. 
Ever since the oil crisis in the 1980’s the energy performance of buildings has been an 
area of interest. Optimizing energy performance has affected almost every component 
inside buildings that have continuously been improved. This development has caused that 
lighting in the future will be dominated by light emitting diodes (LED). The advantages 
of LED lighting are high efficiency, controllable CCT and potentially good CRI.   
Four main aspects are included in the evaluation of the IEQ; acoustic, atmospheric, 
thermal and visual conditions. Noise level and reverberation time are factors related to 
the acoustic environment. The atmospheric environment depends on the air humidity, 
odor, particles and the concentration of gaseous pollutants. The thermal environment 
depends on the temperature and velocity of the air, and mean radiant temperature. Finally, 
light intensity and color, contrast and glare are factors in the visual environment.  
The theory chapter is divided into two subchapters necessary to understand the hue-heat 
hypothesis, a visual and a thermal subchapter. Since no daylight is present in the test 
facilities and thus excluded from the laboratory experiments, the visual environment is 
only concerning artificial lighting. 
2.1 Lighting 
Lighting is electromagnetic radiation visible to the human eye. With a wavelength of 380-
780 nm visible light represents only a small part of the electromagnetic spectrum. 
In sunlight light every color of the rainbow is represented. In 1666, Newton discovered 
that sunlight with a prism can be separated into its constituent colors (Eliasson & 
Nørretranders 2015) which is illustrated in figure 2.1.

Light can be explained in both waves and particles. This is called the wave-particle-
dualism of light (Fontoynont et al. 2004). Essentially, particle theory can explain the 
emission and absorption of light and wave theory can explain the propagation of light 
(Young et al. 2014). 
2.1.1 Human perception of light 
In the human eye light is transferred via receptors to electrical impulses going into the 
brain through the optic nerve. 
 
The outermost layer of the eye-ball, sclera, protects the eye and maintains the eye shape 
under pressure. Light gets through a bulge, transparent, part of the sclera, called the 
cornea. The amount of light entering the lens is regulated by the size of iris. The ciliary 
muscle controls the focal length by changing the shape of the lens. After the lens, light

travels unaltered through a transparent vitreous humor to the retina (Fontoynont et al.

Rod and cone are the names of two classes of photoreceptor cells. Rods are light sensitive 
and responsible for night vision and motion detection. Rods is the dominant receptor cell 
everywhere in the retina, except the center of the eye, the fovea. The fovea is the 1.5° 
center of the visual axis and is populated with cone receptors only. The cone receptors 
register color of the light entering the eye under daylight conditions. Further, cones are 
divided into three photoreceptor types with the ability to receive different wavelengths. 
The L, M and S cone type is responsible for the red, green and blue color. When light hits 
these photoreceptors electrical impulses are transferred through the optic nerve to the 
brain in which they are turned into visual information (Boyce 2014). 
2.1.1.1 Vision and aging 
As people age, changes in the eye occur. The speed and range of motion of the iris 
decrease with increasing age, likewise is less light reaching the retina, the close-up focus 
ability is reduced, more scattered light is reaching the retina and more stray light is 
generated inside the eye. The consequences of these changes are a vision characterized 
by: 
- 
Reduced absolute sensitivity to light 
- 
Reduced visual acuity  
- 
Smaller visual fields 
- 
Reduced contrast sensitivity 
- 
Greater sensitivity to glare 
- 
Reduced color discrimination  
- 
Difficulty moving from bright to dark conditions suddenly 
- 
Difficulty distinguishing dark colors 
Starting in the early adulthood these changes increase in form and magnitude as people 
get older (Boyce 2014). The ability to discriminate color has been examined on subjects 
from 20-79 years of age at different illuminance levels by  Knoblauch et al. (1987) using 
the Farnsworth-Munsell 100 hue test. A perfect score in this test produces a perfectly 
shaped circle, but as the number of errors increases the circle becomes larges and more 
ragged. An extract of the test results is shown in figure 2.3. It shows that the ability to 
discriminate colors reduces with increased age, especially at low illuminance level.

2.1.1.2 Adaption 
The human visual system can operate in a wide range of luminous environments from a 
dark night (0.0001 cd/m2) to a sunny beach (20,0000 cd/m2). To cope with this wide range 
of retinal illuminances the visual system continuously changes its sensitivity. This 
process is called adaption and involves three different changes: 
Change in pupil size: The amount of retinal illumination can be increased by dilation or 
constriction of the iris. For young people the diameter of the pupil can range from about 
2-8 mm and decreases with age and is about 3-6 mm at the age of 50 years. Iris 
constriction

Neural adaption: Neural processes account for the majority of the transitory changes in 
sensitivity of the eye at luminances below 600 cd/m2. The neural adaption happens 
continuously and fast (less than 200 ms.) in electrical lighted environments. 
Photochemical adaption: When the retinal irradiance changes, a series of photochemical 
reactions must be accomplished before equilibrium is attained. The cone photoreceptors 
have the shortest adaption time and will achieve maximum sensitivity in 10-12 minutes, 
while the rods will require 60 min to achieve their maximum sensitivity (Boyce 2014).

2.1.2 Light quality 
Quality of light depends first on the actual light quality from the light source with two 
main factors CCT and CRI and second on the uniformity of the distributed light and 
control of glare.  
2.1.2.1 CCT 
Correlated color temperature is measured in Kelvin and is a relic from when the 
incandescent bulb was the standard. The CCT corresponds to the hue from a black body 
radiator. A black body radiator will radiate different color light at different temperatures 
(Lenk & Lenk 2016). A candle light is a black body radiator with a color temperature of 
1800 K. An incandescent bulb is too, it burns at 2700 K and appears colder than a candle. 
However, it is still defined as a warm light given the reddish color. A white light with a 
CCT at approx. 4000 K corresponds to neutral while CCT’s above 5300 K correspond to 
a cold, blueish light. An overview of the connection between CCT and color appearance 
is presented in table 2-1.

2.1.2.2 CRI 
The CRI describes how well a light source reveals the true color of an object. The 
maximum CRI-score is 100 and is found in an ideal natural light source e.g. sunlight, a 
candle and an incandescent bulb. The CRI-score is based on an evaluation of the light 
looking at eight pastel colors then comparing with a black body illuminant at similar CCT, 
as colors in natural light is the reference (Rea 2000). The appearance of an object is 
determined by the light reflected from it. Thus, an object reflecting radiation at a approx. 
530 nm wavelength appears green to the human eye (Eliasson & Nørretranders 2015). 
This green object will not have a natural green appearance if the light does not feature a 
complete color-spectrum.  
CRI quantifies the color rendering ability by one number the rendering average (Ra). 
Visually, the quality of a light source can be revealed by dividing the light up into its 
different colors with a spectrometer (see figure 2.4).

It is possible to create white light with only three colors. In a light without the full 
continuous spectrum of colors, the human eye is unable to see the true color of the 
illuminated object.

when Japanese Dr. Nakamura developed green and blue LED (El-Zein 2013). Today it is

2.2 Thermal comfort 
Thermal comfort is according to DS/EN ISO 7730 (2006) “… that condition of mind 
which expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment”. Thermal satisfaction is 
subjective and is achieved differently to each human being. Thermal neutrality is closely 
related to thermal comfort and is the condition at which neither a warmer nor cooler 
environment is preferred. However, individual preferences affect thermal neutrality as 
well as the climatic region. Further, thermal neutrality is not preferred by all people as 
some feel most comfortable with a slightly warm or cold sensation. This variability makes

3   PREVIOUS STUDIES 
==PAGE:33==
3. Previous studies 
 
 33 
Before 1926 when the first study investigating the hue-heat hypothesis, the correlation 
between color appearance and perceived temperature was described as being “a 
widespread belief” by Mogensen and English (1926). This first study tested the 
hypothesis by paired comparison of two differently colored cylinders. The subjects were 
to judge which seemed the warmer by looking, firmly grasping the cylinders 
simultaneously for a second, then lay both hands on a grey surface and judge. 25 subjects 
were tested in the experiment, but no correlation was found.  
Another three American studies were performed without confirming the hue-heat 
hypothesis. Houghten et al. (1940) projected light onto a screen and had two male subjects 
20 years of age looking at the screen. Red and blue filters were used in front of the white 
light to create three scenes. The subjects judged their thermal sensation in three scenes, 
but no correlation was found between the screen color and their thermal sensation.  
Neither Berry (1961) or Bennett & Rey (1972) found any acceptance of the hue-heat 
hypothesis. Berry used 5 different colored lighting and Bennett & Rey tested subjects 
wearing colored goggles. 
The first study supporting the hue-heat hypothesis was carried out by Fanger et al. in 
1977. The preferred ambient temperature of 16 subjects was tested at two different noise 
levels and two differently colored environments. The noise levels of 40 dB(A) and 85 
dB(A) did not affect the thermal preferences, but subjects preferred 0.4 ˚C higher ambient 
temperature in the extreme blue environment compared with the extreme red. The 
illuminance level was measured to 150 lx for the blue light and 190 lx for the red.  
Three years later Greene & Bell (1980) had 144 subjects in a room with blue, red or white 
painted walls. The color did affect the subjects’ mood but the not the preferred 
temperature.  
When Winzen et al. in 2014, after 33 years without documented studies on the hue-heat 
hypothesis published their study, they introduced LED light technology for the first time 
in testing the hypothesis. In an aircraft cabin with a constant temperature of 22.2 ˚C two 
blue and two yellow light colors were tested. The room temperature was perceived 
warmer in the yellow light than in the blue light. In addition, the blue light induced that 
the air quality was perceived higher and subjects felt more alert.  
Up till today from Winzen’s study in 2014, another six studies concerning the hue-heat 
hypothesis have been performed. In most of the studies colored light have been used 
==PAGE:34==
Human Perception of Lighting 
 
 
34 
(Vitali et al. 2014; Albers et al. 2014; Huebner et al. 2016; Baniya et al. 2016). Two 
studies applied the color to an object like Mogensen & English (1926), but Ho et al. 2014 
projected color to first an object and second the subjects’ hand and Ziat et al. 2016 by the 
use of virtual reality. Actually, Ho et al.’s (2014) results opposed the hue-heat hypothesis, 
as an object at constant temperature was judged colder with a hand projected with red 
light vs. blue light. Their explanation to this result was the brain’s interpretation of the 
expected temperature of objects. Thus, a red hand’s thermal sensation of the objects is 
colder vs. the blue hand (Ho et al. 2014) 
Baniya et al. (2016) did not confirm the hypothesis when testing 16 subjects in a room 
with constant air temperature, air velocity and relative humidity. The illuminance was 
kept constant at 500 lx and only the CCT of the LED-lighting in the room was adjusted 
in 14 steps from 2700 K to 6200 K and the subjects filled in questionnaires at 2700 K, 
4000 K and 6200 K. They concluded that the subjects thermally felt most comfortable in 
an indoor workplace with a CCT of 4000 K. 
The latest study was conducted in spring 2017 by Kühl, Rosenbech & Gudmandsen. They 
had 44 subjects in an experiment with CCT as the only variable. As they expected, the 
best chances of approving the hypothesis at the extremity of the thermal comfort zone, 
three temperatures were examined with 19 ˚C and 27 ˚C as primary and 22 ˚C as a 
reference with only half the experiments conducted at this test temperature. At each 
temperature, subjects were exposed to CCT between 2700 K and 6200 K at a rate 
unidentifiable for the human eye. They found no correlation between CCT and thermal 
perception at 19 ˚C and 27 ˚C but at 22 ˚C the hue-heat hypothesis was confirmed.

The results and reflections from the previous studies formed the basis of the methodology 
for the experiments in this project. The practical aspects are described in the following

4.1 Test conditions 
The experiment was carried out at optimal temperatures for both summer and winter 
according to DS/EN ISO 7730 (2006). The operative temperature was kept constant at 
either 22 ˚C (heating season) or 24.5 ˚C (cooling season) in each experimental session. 
Chapter 4.5 describes the HVAC-system and how the indoor environmental parameters 
were kept at a constant level through the experiments. Thus, the only parameter that was 
varied was the CCT of the light. The longer-time effect to a constant color temperature 
was tested at CCT extremity in a 40-min period starting and ending with a subjective 
questionnaires and performance test.  
The experiments started and ended at an intermediate CCT in order to create a natural 
setting to prevent the test subjects from being too aware the lighting. To eliminate the 
impact caused by time of exposure to the results, two lighting animations were created 
balancing order of exposures. It either went from intermediate-to-high-to-low-to-
intermediate (HL) or in reverse from intermediate-to-low-to-high-to-intermediate (LH) 
with intermediate being a CCT of 4019 K, low 2674 K and high 6087 K (Casambi values). 
The luminous flux was controlled to ensure the requirement from DS/EN 12464-1 2011 
was met giving an illuminance of approx. 500 lx at table height. Chapter 4.4 describes 
the lighting conditions. 
As this experiment was expected to be the final laboratory experiment before the field 
experiments, designing a real-life scenario formed the basis for the selection of operative 
temperature and illuminance level. Since the test environment was a simulated office 
space, a further step towards a real-life scenario was introducing PCs in the room. Thus, 
one of the experiments was evaluating how the light from PC-monitors affected the hue-
heat effect from the general lighting in the room. The four variations of the experiments

4.3 Test subjects 
People from other climates may have other preferences than the Danish concerning 
temperature and lighting. To reduce variation caused by geographical difference in the 
perception of thermal environment and lighting, all test subjects were recruited based on 
originating from the northern part of Europe. In addition, all test subjects were tested for 
color blindness before the experiment using the Ishihara color perception test (H.V.W. 
1918) (see Appendix B). 
Recruitment of test subjects happened through posters on campus and social media. The 
senior group was unique as the people recruited for this group were older and were all 
office employees.

4.6.1 Acclimatization   
To ensure a uniform metabolic rate of the subjects from the start of the experiment and 
to eliminate residual heat from biking, walking et. subjects arrived 30 minutes prior the 
experiment. Acclimatization took place in a room similar sized and adjacent to the test 
room, but with daylight. The temperature was controlled to match the conditions in the 
test room. In this period, the clothing level was adjusted to fit with either summer 
conditions (1.0 clo) or winter conditions (0.5 clo). Further, before the experiment, the 
test subjects completed a personal questionnaire regarding their sensitivity, their 
eyesight and whether they were suffering from common symptoms like headache, 
blocked nose etc. (see Appendix E for this questionnaire).  
4.6.2 Questionnaires 
During each experimental session, the subjects were asked to fill out five identical 
subjective questionnaires timed to show the immediate and longer-time effect of the 
exposure to different CCT. The 20 questions mainly concerned the subjects thermal and 
visual experience, though a few questions about alertness, noise and air quality were 
added to make the purpose of the study less obvious. The design of the questionnaire was 
based on the recommendations from DS/EN ISO 10551 (2001) and questions on the 
subjects thermal and visual experience were organized in a four-step structure starting 
with a subjective evaluation, the level of comfort, preference and finally acceptability.

4.7 Data processing  
The data obtained in the experiments can be divided into four categories; questionnaires, 
performance tests, body and skin temperature and environmental parameters.  
The experiment was composed of five rounds of questionnaires, round one, three and four 
were used for evaluation of the immediate effects of the CCT and round one-two and 
four-five were used to evaluate the longer-time effects of a constant CCT (see figure 4.22 
and 4.23 for experimental timeline).  
The terminology of the four experiment types is as follows; the title ‘22 °C paper’ and 
‘24.5 °C paper’ cover the 2-hour experiment with questionnaires and performance tests 
completed on paper, the ‘PC’ title covers the 2-hour experiment at 22 °C with 
questionnaire and performance tests completed on PC and the title ‘senior’ covers the 1-
hour experiment similar to the 22 °C paper, but with subjects belonging to the older age 
group and without the two longer-time exposures at constant CCT. The term “longer-
time” is used for the part of the experiment evaluating the effect of constant CCT 
exposure for 30 minutes. 
4.7.1 Questionnaires 
All answers were coded into numbers in Microsoft Excel in a sheet containing answers 
to the 20 questions with the associated information regarding experiment type, subject 
age, gender, the individual thermal- and visual sensibility, calculated clothing insulation 
value, measured air and operative temperatures, relative humidity, measured body- and 
skin temperatures.  
First, simple plots and summary statistics were constructed to understand the structure of 
the raw data and afterward, statistical analyses were carried out for each question. All 
analyses were carried out separately for the four experiment types and investigated the 
effect of CCT on the immediate effect and longer-time effect, both with the questionnaire 
output as the dependent variable.

the mixed effects model, both stochastic and deterministic variables were included, while 
in the multinomial logistic regression model only deterministic variables were included.

7   DISCUSSION 
==PAGE:99==
7. Discussion 
 
 99 
The aim of the study was to quantify the correlation between CCT of lighting and thermal 
perception of humans around thermal neutrality for summer and winter conditions. The 
results and the experimental procedure is discussed in this chapter with a few reflections 
to the pending field study. 
7.1 Outcome 
7.1.1 Thermal perception 
Question 1 assessed the subjects’ thermal perception at optimal thermal conditions for 
the summer and winter conditions. The hue-heat hypothesis was not verified as the 
statistical analyses did not support that CCT affected the human perception of the thermal 
environment. An indication of an effect in accordance with the hue-heat hypothesis was 
however found in several of the results. The effect of longer-time exposure to a constant 
CCT on the thermally related questions was not entirely clear, but the thermal perception 
went towards neutral. In the PC experiments, the CCT showed less effect on the thermal-
related questions with the high CCT being most tolerable. Senior subjects were generally 
less comfortable peeking at high CCT and felt cooler regardless of CCT. 
7.1.2 Brightness perception 
A significant correlation was found between CCT and the perception of lighting in line 
with the theory of spatial brightness. The lighting with a constant illuminance of 500 lx 
was perceived as being brighter with increasing CCTs, which was as expected and in 
correspondence with findings of Fotios et al. (2015). The senior subjects perceived the 
lighting in the same way as the younger subjects and thus perceived the lighting as being 
brighter from 2674K to 4019 K, but when the CCT changed from 4019 to 6087 K no 
change was found in how the lighting was perceived by the senior subjects. This might 
be explained by a decreasing ability to see the blue light with age (Boyce 2014). In the 
PC experiments, the lighting was perceived as being bright regardless of the CCT. After 
longer-time exposure to constant CCT, the effect on the brightness perception decreased. 
This can be explained by chromatic adaption (Boyce 2014). The lighting was still 
perceived as brighter at 6087 K after the longer-time exposure; a finding contrary to the 
study by Boyce and Cuttle (1990). This suggests a potential for reducing the illuminance 
level without compromising the perceived lighting. 
7.1.3 Perceived air quality 
==PAGE:100==
Human Perception of Lighting 
 
 
 
100 
The air quality was expected to be perceived fresher with increasing CCT (Kühl 2017; 
Rosenbech & Gudmandsen 2017; Winzen et al. 2014).  No significant correlation 
between CCT and perceived air quality was found, but the experienced air quality 
assessed in question 7 indicated a correspondence as expected as the air was perceived as 
being fresher with increasing CCT. The air quality was generally perceived better at 22 
°C than at 24.5 °C. However, the CO2-concentration was higher in the 24.5 °C 
experiments. The reason for this could be a poorer air distribution because of the higher 
temperature and the fact that the diffuse inlet and the exhaust were in the ceiling. The 
results on air quality from the PC experiments were inconsistent, while the senior subjects 
found the air quality most acceptable at 4019 K and stuffier at 6087 K. 
7.2 Experimental design 
The design of the experiments in this project was based on experiences and 
recommendations from previous studies. The experimental phase of this study gave rise 
to discussion and subsequent reflections on how to design future experiments 
investigating the hue-heat hypothesis, elaborated in the following paragraphs.  
7.2.1 Experiment conditions  
The operative temperature was ideal for summer and winter conditions and the duration 
of the experiments was made as short as possible still featuring CCT extremes and 
evaluating the effect of longer-time exposure to constant CCT. Two hours of sedentary 
work adversely affected the thermal state of the subjects and several subjects afterward 
expressed that they felt cold during the experiments. The test results from the thermally 
related questions reveal a clear tendency of test subjects feeling colder towards the end 
regardless of CCT. This presents an unwelcome and inexpedient distraction. To avoid 
this, initiatives should have been made during the experiments to maintain a constant 
metabolic rate, i.e. having the test subjects stand or move around at certain intervals.  
The illuminance level of 500 lx on the working plane (table height) seems reasonable as 
it is the recommended minimum value for office work (DS/EN 12464 2011) and the test 
subjects’ lighting perception was centered around neutral as seen in figure 5.22. 
According to Christensen & Støttrup (2017) higher lighting intensity would cause a 
bigger impact of the CCT and with an illuminance of 1000 lx, the hue-heat hypothesis 
was confirmed in a similar study by Kühl (2017), Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017). 
==PAGE:101==
7. Discussion 
 
 101 
With lighting fixtures directed towards the wall, an increased illuminance on the wall in 
front of the subjects would be possible while still maintaining 500 lx at table height and 
a uniform lighting distribution in the room. These fixtures are called ‘wall washers’ and 
could possibly enhance the effect of lighting in the test room but could be useful in the 
field experiments as well.  
To achieve the greatest range between CCT minimum and maximum, values from the 
initial lighting measurements were used when the lighting animations were created. The 
lighting fixtures consist of a warm-white and cold-white LED and at minimum CCT the 
cold-white part of the lighting fixture turns off and that switch was noticeable. This glint 
might have caused increased attention to the lighting and could have been avoided by 
having a CCT of 2800 K in Casambi as the minimum value.  
The effect of the CCT of the general lighting in the test room was decreased to a great 
extent in the PC experiments. For future experiments, the CCT of monitor light can be 
controlled in correspondence with the CCT of the lighting in the room. This might induce 
effects of the CCT equal to the paper experiments or even greater.  
The assessment of the air quality may have been biased by the connotations of a small 
room with 4 old stationary computers in yellowish plastic. The air quality was assessed 
the least acceptable in question round one for the PC experiments. Having newer, slimmer 
and cleaner monitors and keyboards inside the test room and removing the computer 
outside of the room could reduce conception of the air quality. 
7.2.2 Subjects 
The gender distribution was close to equal and thus the only improvement would be a 
better age composition with an equal representation of all age groups. Providing subjects 
with clothing would ensure more uniform clo-values closer to 0.5 and 1.0. A group of 
color blindness subjects would enable an investigation of the actual impact of this 
disability.  
7.2.3 Experimental procedure  
The break between each experimental session was one hour and was determined to ensure 
enough time to stabilize the room to the nominal operative temperature and a low CO2-
concentration for the next session. Since three experimental sessions of a two-hour 
duration were conducted per day with one-hour breaks in between, the subjects 
==PAGE:102==
Human Perception of Lighting 
 
 
 
102 
acclimatized in an adjacent room. The acclimatization room had supply air similar to the 
test room, however, subjects may have spent a part of the actual experiment adjusting 
from one climate to another. Alternatively, subjects should acclimatize in the test room, 
still another 30 minutes in the test room do not seem to be a good idea as subjects 
expressed that they got bored, cold and sleepy. The subjects getting bored and sleepy may 
have caused them to get unfocused in the answering and therefore monotonously repeated 
their answers.  
Overall, the experiments had an equal distribution of the two lighting animations (LH and 
HL), but an unbalanced distribution was present for the 22 °C and 24.5 °C paper 
experiment with six HL and three LH animations for the 22 °C experiments and vice 
versa. Due to this, it was necessary to include an interference factor in the statistical 
analysis, making it more complex.  
7.2.4 Questionnaire 
The subjects were encouraged to ask during the introductory meeting if they had doubts 
about words or sentences in the questionnaire. Still, only a few of the subjects had English 
as their primary language and with no language requirements, the possibility of 
misinterpretation of the questions was present. Alternatively, the questionnaires should 
have been translated to the native language of each subject which would, however, induce 
some interpretations as different understanding and inflection of the words apply in 
different languages. 
For the essential questions, a continuous scale was used in anticipation of more precise 
results. But in spite of careful instructions and encouragement to use the scale in-between 
the preset marks, test subjects tended to often tick off at the preset marks. An alternative 
scale with fewer preset marks would minimize this but would then, in turn, introduce 
issues with subjective interpretation of the scale. 
7.2.5 Data processing 
The manual process of converting the answers from a tick in a box or on a line into data 
by typing them in might possibly have induced some errors. Outliers were reevaluated 
and thus some errors were corrected.  
The large extent of data forms the basis for statistical analyses to a greater extent than 
performed in this study. Due to the great comparability, a combination of the result from 
==PAGE:103==

 103 
this study and the previous study by Kühl (2017), Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017) will 
increase the potential outcome. 
7.3 Field-experiments 
Going from laboratory experiments to field experiments entails less control of the 
environment and the introduction of new variables. The field experiments should be 
planned so they are comparable with the lab experiments. Experience on the effect of 
some of the factors is obtained in this study and the previous study by Kühl (2017), 
Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017) and some are still uncharted. The most important 
parameters in the pending field study would be daylight, time of exposure, changing 
metabolism and light exposure from PC monitor and mobile phone. As CCT of the ceiling 
light will be the only independent variable controlled by the project group in the next 
stage of this PSO project, it is important to register all other relevant parameters including 
daylight, artificial lighting besides the ceiling lighting, thermal parameters and air quality. 
The clothing level must be registered as well as the metabolic rate; both parameters are 
expected to have a larger variation in the field experiments. The mean age of the subject 
group is expected to be higher than for the main part of the lab experiments and thus 
experience from the ‘senior experiments’ in this project might be useful. The operative 
temperature is expected to be similar to this project but with larger variations.  
The first field experiments will be conducted at DTU Photonics offices in Risø with 
modern and centrally controlled LED-lighting. Since the occupants will be at work, the 
questionnaire must only occupy the subjects for a couple of minutes and thus the 
questionnaire must be reduced to essential questions. As the occupants, usually work for 
8 hours it is a great opportunity to extend the investigation of the longer-time effect of a 
constant exposure.

8   CONCLUSION 
==PAGE:105==
8. Conclusion 
 
 105 
The aim of this study was to investigate the human perception of lighting by examining 
whether the correlated color temperature (CCT) of LED lighting affects the perceived 
temperature – the hue-heat hypothesis. Additionally, the prolonged effect of CCT was 
studied as well as the impact of age and light form PC monitors. The latter two factors 
have never been studied before in this relation, but they are essential to carrying the 
potential effects to the real-world.  
The correlation between the CCT of lighting and the thermal perception was not 
significant and thus the hue-heat hypothesis was not confirmed. The result did, 
nevertheless, show indications in accordance with the hypothesis. However, a 0.52 
difference on the seven-point scale was found for an operative temperature of 24.5 °C 
corresponding to an operative temperature difference of 1.8 °C. Thus, a CCT of 2674 K 
at 23.6 °C should cause the same thermal sensation as a CCT of 6087 K at 25.4 °C. After 
the 30-minute exposure to a constant CCT, the thermal perception went towards neutral, 
indicating a weaker correlation between CCT and thermal perception, possibly due to 
chromatic adaption. The hue-heat hypothesis was studied with nine human subjects with 
an average age of 44 years to evaluate the effect of age by comparing their responses with 
those of a younger subject group. No significant correlation between the CCT and the 
thermal perception was found, but the elder subjects found the thermal environment less 
comfortable and cooler. The correlation between CCT and the thermal perception when 
subjects were exposed to light from a PC monitor throughout the entire experimental 
session was slightly weaker but quite similar. 
There was a significant correlation between CCT and the perception of brightness as the 
light was perceived as being brighter, preferred darker and causing more glare with 
increasing CCT. When comparing extreme CCTs, a difference of 1.47 was found on the 
seven-point scale even though the illuminance was constant at 500 lux. To obtain an equal 
brightness perception of light with a CCT of 2674 and 6087 K the illuminance must be 
adjusted by 21 %. After longer-time exposure of constant CCT, the effect decreased 
slightly. For the visually related questions, the senior group responded like the younger 
subjects at CCTs from 2674 to 4019 K, but an increased CCT above this showed no 
increased effect. In the PC experiments, the light was perceived as being bright regardless 
of the CCT despite less experienced glare in the PC experiments.  
==PAGE:106==
Human Perception of Lighting 
 
 
 
106 
Several parts of the conducted experiments indicated correlations between CCT of LED 
lighting and perceived indoor environment. If the correlations persist in practice, 
implementation in buildings with controllable LED lighting will lead to energy savings. 
The hue-heat hypothesis was however not definitively confirmed as the results were not 
conclusive. To quantify potential energy savings field experiments should be conducted 
which will include daylight, the effect of which was eliminated in this study but will 
have an influence in buildings in practice. By including the impact of aging, exposure to 
light from PC monitors and the prolonged effect of CCT on thermal perception, this 
study adds value to previous research. Because these factors have now been examined 
in a controlled environment their effect can be taken into account when natural lighting 
is introduced. This creates a solid foundation for testing the hypothesis in buildings in 
practice. 
This study has shown how an increased CCT leaves room to reducing the illuminance 
without compromising human brightness perception, thus lowering energy consumption. 
This bodes well for practical applications in a time where energy efficiency in buildings 
and construction is on the agenda.
