Abstract  
The current thesis presents a case of the multiperiod petrol station replenishment 
problem (PSRP), which is a variant of the inventory routing problem (IRP). The 
delivery of several petroleum products from a depot to a set of stations is investigated, 
over a monthly planning horizon. The product delivery is performed by a heterogeneous 
fleet of trucks that are equipped with flowmeters, a feature that to the authorвҖҷs 
knowledge was never investigated for the PSRP. Since the problem is related to the 
vendor managed resupply policy, the size and the timing of the deliveries must be 
determined, ensuring that no station will experience any stockout. The goal is to 
minimize the total traveled distance, over the given planning horizon. At this purpose, 
three strategies are proposed, whose development involves the use of three problems, 
that are, the Travelling Salesman Problem, the Set Partitioning Problem and the Truck 
Loading Problem. The first strategy investigates a delivery plan of single-station trips. 
The replenishment of the stations is performed when their stockout is impending. The 
second strategy allows the trucks to visit up to three stations per trip. Specifically, after 
planning the trips to stations with impending stockout, a maximum number of two 
stations is examined to be included in the same trips by means of a detour. The last 
developed strategy is based on the second strategy, but additionally allows to consider, 
in a single trip, two stations whose stock is running dry. The three developed strategies, 
after being tested for a real life-instance, provided feasible and consistent results, which 
were further evaluated for different key performance indicators.  
Keywords: inventory routing problem, petrol replenishment, vendor managed 
resupply, truck loading problem, heterogenous truck-fleet

1 Introduction 
Nowadays, more and more companies highlight the importance of logistics 
management for creating customer value. Customer value can be created not only by 
the features of the product itself but also by the quality of logistics service. Product 
availability, timeliness, and consistency of deliveries are some of the elements of 
logistics service that play a crucial role in customer satisfaction. A policy used by many 
companies for value creating logistics is the vendor managed resupply. Vendor 
managed resupply policy refers to an agreement between a customer and a vendor in 
which the customer delegates the inventory management to the supplier (Coelho et al. 
2014). In particular, the supplier monitors customersвҖҷ inventories and decides about the 
timing and the size of the deliveries, ensuring that no stockout will occur to any of the 
customers.  In this way, the vendor saves distribution and production costs by 
coordinating efficiently the deliveries to the customers, and the customers do not have 
to dedicate resources to inventory management (Campbell et al. 1998). Most of the 
companies that have adopted this policy are related to the petrochemical, industrial gas, 
and soft drink industry.  
The problem related to the vendor managed resupply policy is called the Inventory 
Routing Problem (IRP). The inventory routing problem integrates two basic 
components of the logistics value chain: inventory management, and transportation 
(Campbell and Savelsberg 2004). The aim of this problem is to jointly determine the 
timing and the size of the deliveries, as well as the routes that must be followed to visit 
the customers. The ultimate goal of this problem is the minimization of the long-term 
distribution costs, without allowing any stockout to occur to the customers. Stockout - 
or dry run - refers to an event in which the demand for a product exceeds the current 
inventory. The IRP can be considered as a variation of the well-known Vehicle Routing 
Problem (VRP). The vehicle routing problem deals with a single day in which the 
customers place orders and the supplier assigns the orders to vehicles and designs the 
associated routes for visiting these customers. The aim of this problem is the 
minimization of the total traveled distance in that given day. On the contrary, the IRP 
is typically concerned with the repeated distribution of a variety of products from a 
warehouse to a set of customers over a long horizon. The problem is periodic, in the 
sense that customers are not necessarily served at each day of the horizon but rather a 
specified number of times. Furthermore, the customers do not specify any visit dates 
and delivery amounts. Depending on the customersвҖҷ inventory status, the supplier is the 
one who decides how much to deliver to which customers, each day of the planning 
horizon.  
This work concerns a special variant of the IRP, that is, the Petrol Station 
Replenishment Problem (PSRP). Given a depot and a set of petrol stations, the aim is 
to optimize the delivery of several petroleum products from the depot to the stations, 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
over a given planning horizon. More specifically, for each day of the planning horizon, 
it must be specified which stations to be visited, how much of each product should be 
delivered to these stations, how to load the products into the trucks and how to design 
the vehicle routes. Each station may contain many tanks of varying capacities, demand 
patterns and required products. The routes are typically carried out by a heterogenous 
fleet of trucks of varying capacities and compartment configurations. Therefore, the 
PSRP can be viewed as an IRP with additional constraints associated with the proper 
allocation of the petroleum products into the truck compartments and stations with 
varying tank features (Cornillier et al. 2008). Typically, the goal of the PSRP is either 
the minimization of the total traveled distance or the maximization of the net profit by 
minimizing the labor costs, over the planning horizon.  
Determining a distribution strategy in PSRP that minimizes the total traveled distance 
over a given planning horizon is the problem addressed in this thesis. Based on a real-
life instance, three strategies are proposed for the multi-period PSRP, considering the 
periodic nature of the problem. Specifically, given a set of tanks which are running out 
of stock at each day, a number of trucks is designed to replenish them. The planned 
amount of fuel to be delivered determines the day they must be replenished again. 
Provided that the trucks are not fully loaded, they can be considered for refueling other 
tanks, whose stockout is supposed to occur earlier than the new stockout of the empty 
tanks. These tanks are located either in the same stations along with the empty tanks, 
or in other geographically nearby stations. Their dry run is shifted at the same time 
period or after the new stockout of the empty ones. Thus, the next visit to the stations 
is delayed, saving transportation costs in the long run. 
The structure of the thesis is organized as follows. In the next chapter, a brief review of 
literature related to inventory routing and petrol replenishment problems is provided. 
In chapter 3, the PSRP is defined and all the parameters involved in the problem are 
explained thoroughly. The chapter 4 describes three mathematical models that play an 
essential role for developing the three proposed strategies in chapter 5. Chapter 6 
reports the numerical results, based on the provided real-life instance, while in the last 
chapter conclusions and future work are provided.

2 Literature Review 
The Inventory Routing Problem has been extensively studied for more than 30 years. 
The first published articles mainly proposed variants of models designed for the VRP 
and heuristics that took into account customersвҖҷ inventory costs (Coelho et al. 2014). 
One of the first papers was published by Bell et al. (1983), who investigated a stochastic 
IRP case for an industrial gasesвҖҷ producer. The objective was the maximization of the 
distribution profit over a long horizon, considering transportation costs, and ensuring 
that the customer inventory levels are always met. Dror and Levy (1986) integrated 
previously studied VRP heuristics into the solution of a weekly IRP. They generated an 
initial solution to a VRP, by monitoring the capacity of the trucks and the inventory 
status of the customers. An exact algorithm for the IRP was developed by Archetti et 
al. (2007), for investigating a delivery plan of single-customer trips. They proposed a 
branch-and-cut algorithm, taking into account the inventory holding costs from both 
customers and supplier. The algorithm was able to solve instances of up to 50 customers 
in a three-period horizon and 30 customers in a six-period horizon in less than two 
hours. Popovic et al. (2012) focused on the multi-product IRP. Specifically, the 
problem consisted of multiple fuel types that had to be delivered to a set of customers 
by one truck of many compartments. For finding the solution, they developed a MILP 
model, as well as, a fast heuristic based on neighborhood search in order to handle large 
size of instances.  
A problem closely related to the current project was investigated by Campbell and 
Savelsberg (2004). They described a multiday IRP involving the vendor managed 
resupply policy. The problem concerned the repeated distribution of a single product 
from a depot to a set of customers by a homogeneous fleet of trucks without time 
windows. For each day of the planning horizon, the set of customers to be visited, the 
delivery amounts to these customers and the design of the routes were specified. The 
objective was the minimization of the vendorвҖҷs average distribution costs without 
causing stockouts at any of the customers. For solving the problem, they developed a 
two-phase approach embedded in a rolling-horizon framework. The planning phase 
consisted of long-term decisions over a predetermined planning horizon, while the 
scheduling phase consisted of detailed decisions only for the first few days of the 
planning horizon. Specifically, in the planning phase, an Integer Program (IP) was 
developed to determine the customers to be served and their delivery amounts for each 
day of the planning horizon, considering the proximity among the customers. Since the 
IP program was hard to be solved, they excluded from the model the non-urgent low-
usage customers located far from customers who required delivery in the next few days. 
In the scheduling phase, they employed routing and scheduling heuristics to construct 
the set of delivery routes for the first few days, specifying the departure times and the 
delivery sequences for each truck. Computational results demonstrated the 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
effectiveness of this approach since it managed to solve a dataset of 100 customers in 
less than ten minutes. 
One of the first articles related to PSRP was published by Brown and Graves (1981) 
who described a highly automated, real-time dispatch system regarding a petroleum 
product distribution network in USA. The network included more than 80 bulk 
terminals and a heterogeneous fleet of 300 tank trucks. The bulk terminals were the 
storage point for up to 16 petroleum products. Each truck was divided into up to six 
compartments of varying capacities, and it was not equipped with a flowmeter, i.e. each 
compartment could be used for refueling only one tank. The dispatch system took into 
account constraints regarding customer delivery times, special equipment requirements, 
capacities of the truck compartments etc. Brown and Graves investigated the delivery 
plan of single-customer trips in the presence of time windows. They developed an 
integer program in order to assign the customer orders to trucks for each day. The 
objective was the minimization of transportation costs, keeping the workload 
distribution balanced each day. Specifically, they defined a penalty for those trucks 
which either exceeded or fell short of the allowable working hours per day. 
Some years later, Brown et al. (1987) developed a computerized assisted dispatch 
system for Mobile Oil Corporation, similar to ones of Brown and Graves (1981). The 
network included 120 bulk terminals and 430 non-identical tank trucks. The adopted 
dispatching procedure combined human solutions and heuristics and it was an extension 
of the ones of Brown and Graves since more than one customer was allowed to be 
visited in each trip.     
Avella et al. (2004) studied a petroleum distribution network involving a warehouse 
and a fleet of heterogenous vehicles of varying compartment sizes without time 
windows. The purpose was to optimize the delivery of fuel products from the 
warehouse to a set of fuel pumps in a single period. To simplify the truck loading 
procedure, they assumed that the truck compartments could not be partially filled with 
products. Therefore, each time the trucks left the depot, the compartments should be 
either fully loaded or completely empty. The objective was the minimization of the total 
travel cost, given a set of fuel pumps to be visited and their associated delivery 
quantities. To solve the problem, they proposed a fast packing/routing heuristic and a 
branch-and-price algorithm based on a set partitioning formulation to find the exact 
solution. Computational results showed that the exact algorithm managed to solve test 
problems of 25 clients and six trucks in less than five seconds and the solution quality 
was about 15% better than the heuristic one.  
Cornillier et al. (2007) proposed two strategies for the single-day PSRP in order to 
determine the least costly delivery routes given a set of stations that must be 
replenished. The stations were served by a heterogenous fleet of trucks of varying fixed 
costs without the presence of time windows. A limit of two visits was imposed on each 
trip, as well as on the duration.  Given the set of stations that must be visited, all the 
possible subsets of stations that form routes were found, and the route costs were 
calculated solving the traveling salesman problem. Furthermore, an algorithm for the 
Tank Truck Loading Problem (TTLP) was developed in order to allocate properly the 
delivery amounts into the truck compartments. For each truck, the algorithm firstly 
==PAGE:15==
5 
 
checked whether the truck was able to carry out each route, then the delivery volumes 
were loaded into the truck compartments by means of a sequential allocation process. 
The first proposed strategy solved firstly the tank truck loading algorithm considering 
the cheapest possible truck for each route, and then the final set of routes was chosen 
by means of a matching problem. On the contrary, the second strategy solved the 
matching problem firstly having chosen the cheapest truck for each route and then the 
TTLP determined whether the delivery amounts could be loaded into the trucks. The 
computational results indicated that the second strategy was far superior to the first one. 
The computing time of the second was only 0.28% of the first one since only few TTLPs 
were solved under the second strategy.  
Cornillier et al. (2008) also developed a heuristic for the multi-period PSRP. The 
objective was to maximize the total net profit, by delivering several petroleum products 
to a set of petrol stations, over a given planning horizon. Each petrol station contained 
a number of underground tanks which were served by a limited heterogeneous fleet of 
trucks of varying capacities and compartment configurations. The trucks were not 
equipped with flowmeters. Five crucial procedures were involved in the problem for 
determining the delivery amounts for the stations each day. The routing and truck 
loading procedures were similar as the ones proposed in Cornillier et al. 2007, 
considering the stations whose at least one of their tanks was running out of stock. 
Knowing the final set of routes, as well as all the available trucks that could carry out 
these routes, the route packing procedure was implemented, in which the trucks were 
assigned to routes with as ultimate goal the maximization of the net profit. Specifically, 
the procedure took into account all the sales revenues and regular and overtime costs 
by assigning routes to trucks, under normal and overtime working time limits for each 
trip. When the duration of a trip was exceeding the overtime limit, the look-back 
procedure was enabled and iteratively moved the visit of some stations to the previous 
time periods. On the contrary, when the duration of a trip was less than the normal time 
limit, the look-ahead procure considered other stations whose stockout occurred in the 
next periods. Computational results showed that the proposed heuristic was far superior 
in relation to a previously published algorithm. 
After one year, Cornillier et al. (2009) proposed a MILP formulation for the single-day 
PSRP with time windows. The aim was to optimize the delivery of several petroleum 
products to a set of stations using a limited heterogeneous fleet of trucks without 
flowmeter. The objective was the same as Cornillier et al. 2008, that is, the 
maximization of the net revenues considering regular and overtime costs. The main 
difference from their previously published papers was that trucks could visit up to four 
stations, as well as the determination of the departure time for each truck trip, respecting 
stationsвҖҷ specific time windows. Due to the large number of stations in the mathematical 
model, two heuristic procedures were proposed in order to reduce the variables in the 
model. In particular, the heuristics were implemented in advance before solving the 
mathematical model in order to reduce the number of candidate routes. The first 
heuristic consists of preselecting promising arcs, while the second one consists of route 
preselection based on geographical decomposition. Computational results confirmed 
the efficiency of the proposed heuristics. The first heuristic managed to solve small 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
instances of up to 15 stations, while the second succeeded in finding near-optimal 
solutions even for large instances. 
A truck loading problem was studied by YГјceer and Г–zakГ§a (2010). The problem was 
concerned with the proper allocation of a variety of products into a truck in order to 
deliver them to a given set of destinations. The truck was divided into many 
compartments of varying capacities while the products consisted of solid packages of 
several sizes. Since the products were not liquids, they could be mixed together in the 
same compartments. YГјceer and Г–zakГ§a developed a MILP model in order to 
determine the delivery amount of each product type, respecting the capacity of the truck 
compartments and meeting the required demand of each destination. The objective was 
the maximization of the replenishment time for the given route of destinations, provided 
that the demand was satisfied until the next delivery. Since the model was hard to be 
solved, they proposed a fast heuristic divided into a main algorithm and two 
subalgorithms. The main algorithm searched for a replenishment time, the first 
subalgorithm found a feasible solution for a given replenishment time by solving the 
weighted distribution problem of the MILP model, while the second one checked 
whether the solution was optimal. Computational results showed that small instances 
could be solved in a fraction of a second, guarantying a solution 82% of the optimum. 
The present thesis investigates the delivery of several petroleum products from a depot 
to a set of petrol stations, over a horizon of one month. Each station consists of many 
tanks of varying features, such as product requirements, usage patterns and capacities. 
The delivery is made by a fleet of heterogenous trucks of varying capacities and 
compartment configurations. The trucks are equipped with flowmeters, therefore the 
delivery amount of more than one tank can be loaded in each compartment. The 
developed strategies take into account for replenishment, not only the tanks that are 
getting empty at each day, but also other tanks whose stockout occurs in the next few 
days. The purpose is to push the next visit to the stations as far as possible, by 
determining the right delivery amounts to be loaded into the trucks. In this way, the 
total transportation costs are minimized in the long run.

provided by AMCS, an IT company which corporates and designs software for waste 
and recycling companies. The aim is to optimize the delivery of several petroleum 
products to a set of stations located in Denmark by a limited heterogeneous fleet of 
trucks, over a planning horizon of 30 time periods. Each time period represents one 
day. The goal is to develop a strategy so that the total traveled distance is minimized 
over the planning horizon, without causing stockouts to any of the stations.

a number of stations exactly once, according to the delivery schedule. Once all the 
stations have been served, the truck returns to the depot and may do another round-trip.

unloading procedures are assumed to occur instantly. An example of a daily schedule 
for a given truck is presented in figure 3.3. The delivery schedule of the truck considers

of the stations is divided into two different round-trips. Stations 1 and 2 are visited in

the first trip, while stations 3 and 4 in the second one. Initially, the truck is located in 
the depot, where the planned amounts for the tanks associated with stations 1 and 2 are 
loaded into the truck compartments. Then, the first round-trip is carried out. Once 
stations 1 and 2 have been served, the truck returns to the depot and is loaded again 
with products in order to serve stations 3 and 4.  
Concerning the tanks that are planned for replenishment each day, their associated 
stations must be visited, and an efficient delivery plan should be made so that the 
traveled distance is minimized. Three limitations must be taken into consideration: 
вҖў Stations opening hours 
вҖў Stations qualification demands  
вҖў Trucks availability 
The first limitation concerns the time period the stations are open. Although the depot 
and the majority of stations are open 24/7, some stations may not be available for 
replenishment in some periods of the planning horizon or for some hours in a period. 
The delivery task becomes more complex, especially when the tanks of these stations 
are running out of stock in these periods. Whether a station is closed all day, the visit 
must be made in one of the previous days, whereas if the station is closed for some 
hours in a day, the replenishment can be performed either in one of the previous days, 
or in the current day, provided that the station can be visited within its time window 
before the stockout.  
The second limitation concerns the number and the type of vehicles that are capable to 
visit the stations. As referred previously, the number of trucks that can serve a station 
is determined based on its qualification demands. Although most stations can accept all 
trucks, there are stations which accept a specific type of truck, restricting not only the 
number of available trucks, but also the way the routes are constructed. It is not possible 
to construct a route which includes stations whose qualification demands do not match, 
since the truck type must be accepted from all stations. For example, suppose that there 
are stations 1 and 2 to be visited, whose qualification demands are Tractor and Rigid 
respectively. The stations cannot be included in the same route, since station 1 can be 
served by trucks 3706 and 3720 while station 2 by trucks 3721 and 3723.  A more 
complicated example is shown in figure 3.4 where three stations are supposed to be 
visited. Although station 2 accepts all types of trucks, there is a mismatch between the 
qualification demands of station 1 and station 3. Station 1 can accept only Rigid trucks 
while station 3 only Tractors. Therefore, the stations cannot be all included in the same 
route, and the alternative routes in this case are presented as follows:

The last constraint concerns the trucksвҖҷ availability. Each truck is available limited 
hours each day and the round-trips must be made within a specified time window.  In 
case a truck is available all day, it must return to the depot in the meantime for shift 
change. Thus, apart from the stations time windows, the delivery plan must consider 
the time windows of the trucks as well. 
Considering the stationsвҖҷ qualification demands and the time windows of both trucks 
and stations, the design of a proper plan which reduce the total travel distance over the 
planning horizon is a very demanding and complicated process. For this reason, it is 
assumed that the stations are open 24/7, as well as that the four trucks can do unlimited 
round-trips each day and with no need to return to the depot for shift change. In this 
way, no time windows are taken into account in the problem, and the route construction 
is determined based only on the distances and the qualification demands of the stations.  
All in all, the assumptions of the problem are summarized as follows:  
вҖў The products are available at the depot in unlimited amounts. 
вҖў Each station is visited at most once per day. 
вҖў Tank usages are deterministic. 
вҖў The usage of each tank is deducted from the stock at the end of the day while 
the refill amount is added to the stock at the beginning of the day.  
вҖў All trucks have the same constant speed while fixed and variable costs are not 
involved in the problem (ex. labour costs). 
вҖў The truck loading and unloading procedures occur instantly. 
вҖў The stations and the depot are open 24/7. 
вҖў Unlimited working hours of trucks, without considering shift changes. 
Therefore, each truck can do unlimited round-trips each day.  
вҖў Each truck is always able to carry the minimum delivery volumes for every 
single-station trip.  
A delivery plan should be made in order to determine the tanks to be refueled and the 
stations to be visited for each day of the planning horizon. Based on the considered 
stations, many potential routes can be designed which can be possibly carried out by 
more than one truck. Focusing only on finding the routes that minimize the traveled 
distance on each day does not imply that the total traveled distance is minimized overall. 
If the trucks are not able to deliver the optimal amounts to the stations, the stations have 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
to be visited again in a short time, increasing the total traveled distance in the long run. 
Therefore, the choice of routes is directly related to the amounts delivered to the 
stations. Since the trucks are not identical to each other, the delivery amounts that can 
be loaded in each available truck for each alternative route must be examined, in order 
to find the optimal plan that minimizes the total traveled distance over the planning 
horizon.  
All in all, the delivery plan must jointly determine for each day: 
вҖў The set of stations to be visited and the set of tanks to be refilled 
вҖў The delivery quantities for the tanks  
вҖў The way the products are loaded into the truck compartments  
вҖў The set of delivery routes  
вҖў The assignment of routes to available trucks 
Therefore, in the next chapter three related problems are introduced, that are used as 
subproblems in the proposed strategies. The first two problems find the set of delivery 
routes that minimize the traveled distance in a given day, while the third problem 
determines the proper refill amounts to be delivered to a set of tanks, as well as, how 
these amounts are optimally allocated in the compartments of a given truck.

DistMatrix.csv and TimeMatrix.csv files respectively.

4 Mathematical Models  
In this chapter, three mathematical models are presented which are involved as 
subproblems for the proposed strategies in chapter 5. The first model in section 4.1 is 
the travelling salesman problem which finds the shortest route, given a set of stations. 
The set partitioning problem in section 4.2, selects the set of routes that minimizes the 
total traveled distance, given a set of stations that must be visited only once. An 
algorithm related to the truck loading problem is developed in section 4.3. Given a truck 
and a set of tanks that must be replenished, the algorithm determines the delivery 
amounts and the way the products are loaded into the truck, respecting the capacity of 
the truck compartments and meeting the tanks demand till the next delivery. The goal 
of this model is to push the dry run of these tanks as far as possible by maximizing their 
delivery volumes. The first and the third problem are involved in all the proposed 
strategies, while the set partitioning problem is used only in strategy 3.

4.3 Truck Loading Problem 
The loading task is the most challenging part of this project since the delivery amounts 
loaded in the trucks affect the time period the tanks must be replenished again. Given a 
truck and a set of tanks that must be refueled, the purpose is to develop a model in 
which the refill amounts are determined in such a way so that the dry run of these tanks 
occur as late as possible. In this way, the next visit to their associated stations occurs 
late as well, saving transportation costs in the long run. A simplified case of the truck 
loading problem has been studied by YГјceer and Г–zakГ§a (2010) as referred in chapter 
2.  
However, in the current project the design of a model is more complex due to the 
limitations regarding the trucks and the tanks features. Each truck is subdivided into 
many compartments of limited capacities, and each compartment can hold the amount

of only product type. The number of product types considered in a truck must not 
exceed the number of compartments, otherwise it is not possible for the truck to perform 
the trip.  The current problem deals with a total number of six product types and all 
trucks consist of at least six compartments, so all product types can be carried by any 
truck. However, the number of considered product types affects the potential amounts 
that can be loaded in the truck, since each compartment has limited capacity. 
Consequently, the more product types are included in the truck, the less space is

4.3.2 Limitations of the Truck Loading Model 
Solving the truck loading model, two limitations were detected in the model, which 
prevent some of the tanksвҖҷ delivery quantities to be maximized. Although the 
replenishment time of the trip рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ is maximized, the replenishment times of some tanks 
are not maximized individually. This is due to the restrictions described previously, that 
are, the limited capacity of the trucks and the tanks. 
The first limitation is detected when the delivery amount of a tank is equal to its 
maximum delivery volume (рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—= рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—) and its replenishment time is equal to the tripвҖҷs 
one (рқ‘Ўрқ‘—= рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ). The objective function (рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ) cannot be further increased. As a result, 
the refill amounts of the other tanks are based on the tripвҖҷs replenishment time, even 
though there is free space both in the truck and the tanks (рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—< рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—). For example, in 
figure 4.9, the refill amount for tank 1 in compartment 1 has reached its maximum refill 
limit (рқ‘Ҙ1 = рқ‘Ҹ1) and its replenishment time defines the tripвҖҷs replenishment time (рқ‘Ў1 =
рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ). The refill amounts of the other tanks (2 вҖ“ 5) are not maximized since they are 
determined based on the tripвҖҷs replenishment time by equation 4.7. 
 
 
Figure 4.9 First limitation in the truck loading model 
 
The second limitation is identified when some of the compartments in the truck are 
fully loaded by one product. The tanksвҖҷ delivery volumes related to this product cannot 
be further increased, if all the other compartments have been assigned to other products. 
If the replenishment times of these tanks are equal to the tripвҖҷs one (рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ), the refill 
amounts of the other tanks are not maximized (рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—< рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—), leaving free space in the 
compartments. The example in figure 4.10 presents this case. There are four different 
products and four compartments in the truck.  The tanks 1, 2 and 3 require product 1 
and their refill amounts have fully loaded the compartment 1, while the other 
compartments have been assigned by the other products. The replenishment times of 
tanks 1, 2 define the tripвҖҷs replenishment time, while for tank 3, its replenishment time 
is greater than the tripвҖҷs one (рқ‘Ў3 > рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ) since its refill amount cannot fall below the 
minimum delivery volume рқ‘Һ3. The refill amounts of the other tanks are not maximized, 
since they are determined based on the replenishment times of tanks 1 and 2.

4.3.3 Algorithm for the Truck Loading Problem  
For removing these limitations, an algorithm for the truck loading problem (TLP 
algorithm) was developed, which maximizes not only the tripвҖҷs replenishment time but 
also the replenishment time of the tanks individually. The algorithm includes a modified 
version of the model in section 4.3.1 which is executed many times till all tanksвҖҷ 
delivery volumes have been maximized. Each time the model is solved, the algorithm 
detects the tanks whose replenishment time is equal to the objective function and whose 
refill amounts have either reached their maximum delivery volume (рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—= рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—), or they 
have fully loaded some of the truckвҖҷs compartments. The replenishment time and the 
delivery volumes of these tanks have been maximized, and therefore they are excluded 
from the model in the sense that their variables рқ‘Ҙрқ‘— and рқ‘Ўрқ‘— are set as constants in the 
model without affecting the objective function of the model. Then, the model is solved 
again, and the procedure continues in the same way until all tanksвҖҷ delivery volumes 
have been maximized.

from set рқҗө. The algorithm for the truck loading problem is presented as follows.

The algorithm is divided into four steps. The first step initializes the sets рқҗҙ, рқҗө and the 
parameters рқ‘ҹрқ‘—, рқ‘ҷрқ‘— and рқ‘ҡрқ‘қ. Since the TLP model is not solved yet, the sets рқҗҙ and рқҗө include 
all the considered tanks and products, respectively (рқҗҙ= рқҗҪ, рқҗө= рқҗј), and the parameters 
рқ‘ҹрқ‘—, рқ‘ҷрқ‘—, рқ‘ҡрқ‘– are initially set to zero. In step 2, the truck loading problem is solved. The 
differences in comparison with the model in section 4.3.1 are the constraints (4.16), 
(4.17) and (4.18). The first two constraints set the variables рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—, рқ‘Ўрқ‘— equal to рқ‘ҹрқ‘—, рқ‘ҷрқ‘— for the 
tanks that have been removed from set рқҗҙ. The constraints (4.18) set the amounts that 
have fully loaded some of the truck compartments as constants (рқ‘ҡрқ‘қ) for the products 
which have been excluded from set рқҗө. In the first iteration, the obtained solution is the 
same as the one in the model in 4.3.1 since no tanks or products has been excluded from 
the model yet. 
The steps 3 and 4 are related to the limitations described in section 4.3.2 and they are 
implemented if there are more than one tank in set рқҗҙ, otherwise the algorithm exports 
the results. Each time the model is solved, the algorithm in step 3 checks whether there 
is any tank in рқҗҙ whose refill amount рқ‘Ҙрқ‘— is equal to its maximum delivery volume рқ‘Ҹрқ‘— 
(рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—= рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—) and its replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— is equal to the objective function рқ‘Ў (рқ‘Ўрқ‘—= рқ‘Ў). If 
exists, the tank is removed from set рқҗҙ and its associated refill amount рқ‘Ҙрқ‘— and 
replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— are stored in parameters рқ‘ҹрқ‘— and рқ‘ҷрқ‘—, respectively.  Then the truck 
loading model is solved again, in order to maximize the replenishment times of the 
remaining tanks in рқҗҙ. 
The fourth step examines the second issue, that is, the case when a product has fully 
loaded at least one compartment. If the replenishment times of the tanks in рқҗҙ related to 
that product are equal to the objective function, or their refill amounts are equal to the 
minimum delivery amount рқ‘Һрқ‘—, they are excluded from set рқҗҙ. Furthermore, the values of 
their associated variables рқ‘Ҙрқ‘—, рқ‘Ўрқ‘— are stored in parameters рқ‘ҹрқ‘—, рқ‘ҷрқ‘—. The product is removed 
from set рқҗө and its amount is stored in parameter рқ‘ҡрқ‘қ. Provided that there are still tanks 
in set рқҗҙ, the truck loading model is solved again, otherwise the results are exported.  
After the algorithm has been completed, the replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— with the minimum 
value defines the tripвҖҷs replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘Ўрқ‘ҹрқ‘–рқ‘қ. Furthermore, since the values of the 
replenishment times are decimals, they are rounded down to the nearest integer in order 
to explicitly represent the periods after which the tanksвҖҷ stockout occurs. For instance, 
if the replenishment time of tank рқ‘— is 4 вүӨрқ‘Ўрқ‘—< 5, the tank requires replenishment after 
four periods.

5 Proposed Strategies 
Three different strategies are proposed for solving the PSRP. Given the initial stock 
level of the tanks, the strategies start from the first day of the planning horizon, and 
iteratively construct delivery plans for each day. The aim of these heuristics is to 
minimize the total traveled distance over the planning horizon of 30 days, and maintain 
a feasible solution with a вҖңsafeвҖқ level of stock.  
5.1 Strategy 1 
The first proposed strategy forms the basis for developing the other strategies. For a 
given day, the stations planned to be visited are those which contain at least one tank 
that is dry, i.e., it is running out of stock. A routing plan is developed, in which each 
route includes the visit of only one of these stations. Each route can be performed by 
many trucks, depending on the qualification demands of the station associated with the 
route. Being the trucks not identical to each other, all the available trucks are examined 
for each route. The final choice of the trucks is based on the amounts that they can 
carry, so that the next visit to the stations occur as late as possible (figure 5.1).

Overall, strategy 1 consists of six steps, that are iteratively executed for each day and 
are summarized as follows.  
1. Find the replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— and the minimum and maximum delivery volumes, 
рқ‘Һрқ‘—, рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—, for each tank. If there is any tank whose replenishment time is zero, the tank 
is planned for refueling, and its associated station must be visited. 
2. Route Construction: For each station to be visited, design a route and find its 
traveled distance and time. Furthermore, determine the truck types that are capable 
to carry out this route. 
3. Truck Loading Procedure: Examine all the available trucks that can carry out each 
route. For each truck, determine which tanks must be considered for refueling in 
order to find the new replenishment time of the station associated with the route.  
4. Examine the replenishment of other, not considered tanks from the stations to be 
visited, provided that the trucks are not fully loaded. 
5. Truck Selection: For each route, select the truck which provides the maximum 
replenishment time of the station associated with the route. 
6. Stock Update: Update the stock level of each tank, by deducting its current 
consumption and adding the refill amount if the tank has been replenished.

All in all, the advantage of this strategy is the maximization of the stationsвҖҷ

for replenishment not only the tanks that are running out of stock in the current period,

5.2 Strategy 2 
Strategy 2 is an extended version of strategy 1, since it examines the possibility of 
including more than one station on each route. Specifically, based on the initial routes 
constructed for the dry stations, a maximum number of two stations is examined to be 
introduced on each route, allowing a small detour (figure 5.6). A route consisting of 
more than three stations is avoided due to the limited capacity of the trucks. Having 
determined the new replenishment times of the dry stations, as done in strategy 1, the 
stations to be added must contain tanks which require replenishment before the new 
stockout of the dry stations. The aim is to create a delivery plan, in which the tanks of 
the added stations are replenished in a way that their stockout is shifted at least in the 
same period as that of dry stations. The resulting routes with detour must not increase 
the traveled distance significantly compared to the initial routes. Furthermore, delaying 
the stockout of the added stations saves traveled distance in the long run.

вҖў The qualification demands of the stations must match each other, so that the route is

вҖў The new station must be located close to the route, preferably near the dry station,

вҖў The new station must contain tanks which require replenishment before the new 
stockout of the dry station. These tanks must have enough space in order to be 
refilled with such amounts, so that their new stockout will occur at least in the same 
time period as that of the dry station. 
вҖў There must be at least one truck that is able to deliver all the required amounts to the 
stations so that the replenishment time of the new station to be equal or greater than 
that of the dry station.  
As referred previously, a maximum number of two stations can be included in the 
routes. The detour stations are dynamically added one by one in each route, examining 
firstly the station which is closest to the dry one and ensuring each time that the above 
requirements are met.  
Overall, strategy 2 consists of nine steps that are iteratively executed for each day and 
are summarized as follows.  
1. Find the replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— and the minimum and maximum delivery volumes, 
рқ‘Һрқ‘—, рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—, for each tank. If there is any tank whose replenishment time is zero, the tank 
is planned for refueling, and its associated station must be visited. 
2. Construction of single-station routes: For each dry station, design a route and find 
its traveled distance and time. Furthermore, determine which truck types are capable 
to carry out the route. 
3. Truck Loading Procedure: Examine all the available trucks that can carry out each 
single-station route. For each truck, determine which tanks must be refueled in order 
to find the new replenishment time of the station associated with the route. 
4. Construction of two-station routes: For each single-station route, construct all the 
possible two-station routes, by adding in each of them a new station by means of a 
detour. 
5. Select the two-station routes that offer the greatest distance savings, and determine 
the available trucks that are capable to carry out these routes. Delete the single-
station routes that include dry stations that now belong to the selected two-station 
routes.  
6. Repeat steps 4 and 5, by examining the introduction of one more station on each 
two-station route, by means of a detour.   
7. Examine the replenishment of other, not considered tanks from the stations to be 
visited, provided that the trucks are not fully loaded. 
8. Truck Selection: For each route, select the truck that provides the maximum 
replenishment time of the stations associated with the route.  
9. Stock Update: Update the stock level of each tank, by deducting its current 
consumption and adding the refill amount if the tank has been replenished.

5.3 Strategy 3 
The third strategy shares the approach of strategy 2, i.e., a maximum number of two 
stations can be introduced on the routes that contain one dry station, allowing a small 
detour. The main difference in this strategy is that two dry stations can also form a 
route. Specifically, the routing plan for each time period might have routes that contain: 
вҖў Only one dry station  
вҖў One dry station and a maximum number of two detour stations 
вҖў Two dry stations 
Adding a further station in the routes that contain two dry stations is avoided, due to 
the limited capacity of the trucks. Considering all the possible subsets of dry stations 
that potentially form routes, strategy 3 focuses on choosing the set of routes that 
minimizes the daily traveled distance, by using the Set Partition Problem developed in 
section 4.2. Then, based on the selected routes, the truck loading procedure is applied 
in a similar way as described in strategy 1, in order to determine the tanks to be refueled. 
For the routes which contain only one dry station, a maximum number of two detour 
stations is examined to be introduced, following the same approach as in strategy 2.  
The steps followed for each time period are summarized as follows: 
1. Find the replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— and the minimum and maximum delivery volumes, 
рқ‘Һрқ‘—, рқ‘Ҹрқ‘—, for each tank. If there is any tank whose replenishment time is zero, the tank 
is planned for refueling, and its associated station must be visited. 
2. Routes Construction: Considering the stations that must be visited, find all the 
possible subsets of stations that might form routes. Each subset must contain at 
most two stations. Exclude the pair subsets whose stationsвҖҷ qualification demands 
do not match. For each subset, design a route and find its traveled distance, time 
and the order with which the stations are visited. Furthermore, determine the truck 
types that are capable to carry out each route. 
3. Routes Selection: Knowing the traveled distance for each route, select the routes 
that minimize the daily traveled distance, by solving the Set Partitioning Problem.  
4. Truck Loading Procedure: Examine all the available trucks that can carry out each 
route. For each truck, determine which tanks must be considered for refueling. 
5. Construction of two-station routes with detour: For each single-station route, 
construct all the possible two-station routes with detour in which a new station is 
added to each of them. 
6. Select the two-station routes with detour that offer the greatest distance savings and 
determine the available trucks that are capable to carry out these routes. Delete the 
single-station routes that include dry stations that now belong to the selected two-
station routes with detour. 
7. Repeat steps 5 and 6, by examining the introduction of one more station on each 
two-station route with detour. 
8. Examine the replenishment of other, not considered tanks from the stations to be 
visited, provided that the trucks are not fully loaded. 
==PAGE:59==
49 
 
9. Truck Selection: For each route, select the truck which provides the maximum 
tripвҖҷs replenishment time. 
10. Stock Update: Update the stock level of each tank, by deducting its current 
consumption and adding the refill amount if the tank has been replenished. 
 
Initially the replenishment time рқ‘Ўрқ‘— and the minimum and maximum delivery volumes 
рқ‘Һрқ‘—, рқ‘Ҹрқ‘— are calculated for each tank. If there is any tank рқ‘— whose replenishment time is 
zero, it is added to set рқҗ·рқ‘Үрқ‘–, and the associated station is added to рқҗ·рқ‘Ҷ set (step 1). Then, 
in step 2, all the possible subsets of the stations included in рқҗ·рқ‘Ҷ are created, where each 
subset contains a maximum number of two stations. For instance, if there are four 
stations that must be visited, i.e. рқҗ·рқ‘Ҷ= {1, 2, 3, 4}, then the number of possible subsets 
is ten: 
 
рқ‘Ҷ= {(1), (2), (3), (4), (1, 2), (1, 3), (1, 4), (2, 3), (2, 4), (3, 4)} 
 
In this set, the pairs whose stationsвҖҷ qualification demands do not match are excluded 
from set рқ‘Ҷ. Based on the remaining subsets, the routes are constructed and the traveled 
distance, time and the order with which the stations are visited are found using the 
routing heuristic described in section 4.1. Furthermore, the truck types that are capable 
to carry out the routes are determined. Knowing the routes and their distances, in step 
3 the Set Partitioning Problem is solved, in order to select the set of routes that 
minimizes the total daily traveled distance, including the stations in set рқҗ·рқ‘Ҷ only once. 
Then, the heuristic in steps 4-10 works in the same way as in steps 3-9 of strategy 2. 
The truck loading procedure is implemented for each available truck on each route in 
order to determine which tanks must be considered for refueling (step 4). Then, a 
maximum number of two stations is examined to be added on the routes which contain 
only one dry station, according to the detour requirements described in strategy 2 (steps 
5-7). The allowable length of detour for each route is determined by parameter рққҖ, in the 
same way as in strategy 2. After having determined the final set of routes рқ‘… and all the 
potential trips рқ‘Ү, the refueling of other, not considered tanks from the stations to be 
visited is examined for each trip, provided that there is still available space in the trucks 
(step 8). Then, in step 9, the trip whose replenishment time is the maximum is chosen 
for each route. Whether more than one trip (truck) provides the same maximum 
replenishment time for a given route, the one that delivers the maximum refill amount 
is chosen. Based on the chosen trips, the stock level of the tanks is updated (step 10). 
All in all, the advantage of this strategy is that two dry stations can also form a route, 
which may further reduce the total traveled distance in the planning horizon. 
Concerning the routes consisting of two dry stations, these are chosen without having 
examined whether the minimum delivery volumes can be loaded on the available 
trucks. This can cause infeasibility issues, as a result, the early termination of the 
heuristic without finding any final solution.

6 Results and discussion  
Chapter 6 presents the results from the developed strategies, based on the provided real-
life instance. As mentioned in chapter 3, the instance consists of 59 stations and 195 
tanks in total. The total number of product types required by the tanks is six, while the 
refill amounts are delivered to the stations by a heterogeneous fleet of four trucks. The 
optimization problem concerns a time horizon of 30 days. The three proposed strategies 
were developed in Python 3.6 language, while the mathematical models were solved by 
CPLEX solver. The coding part of the strategies is provided separately in three different 
files (Strategy1.py, Strategy2.py, Strategy3.py). Furthermore, for comparison reasons, 
a simple strategy was developed, in which only the dry tanks are replenished at each 
day of the planning horizon. As in strategy 1, each route includes the visit of only one 
station. The strategy is named as вҖңStrategy 0вҖқ and the coding part is provided separately 
in Strategy0.py file.   
For assessing the efficiency of the strategies, the main key performance indicator taken 
into account is the total traveled distance. The total traveled distance for each strategy 
is calculated by summing the traveled distance of the trips carried out in the planning 
horizon. The strategy that produces the minimum traveled distance over the planning 
horizon is preferable. However, other indicators are taken into consideration.  
The chapter is divided into three sections. In section 6.1, the parameter рққҖ is tested in a 
range of values in order to identify which values offer the minimum traveled distance 
for strategies 2 and 3. Based on the optimal рққҖ values, in section 6.2, detailed results and 
discussion are provided, for each developed strategy. Finally, in section 6.3, the 
consistency of each strategy is checked, by testing two modified versions of the 
provided real-life instance.

6.3 Consistency of the strategies 
For checking the consistency of the strategies, two new datasets were tested for each 
strategy based on the real-life instance. The daily usage is increased by 2% for all the 
tanks in the first dataset, while by 5% in the second dataset. A heuristic must react to 
the change of datasets in a consistent manner. As the usage is increased, the stockouts 
will be more frequent, therefore the number of trips will increase, leading to an 
increased total travel distance. This is the logical reaction that the heuristics are 
expected to produce, without any infeasibility issues. As one can see from figures 6.13 
and 6.14, higher usages lead to a greater traveled distance and time, and the solution of 
the strategies reacts to the increments in a proportional way, without any infeasibility 
issues.

7 Conclusions and future work 
The current work is concerned with the Petrol Station Replenishment Problem (PRSP), 
which is a variant of the Inventory Routing Problem (IRP), associated with the vendor 
managed resupply policy. Specifically, it investigates the delivery of several petroleum 
products from a depot to a number of stations, consisting of tanks of varying features. 
Based on a provided real-life instance, the goal is to minimize the total traveled 
distance, over a monthly planning horizon. This work is inspired by similar literature 
studies, from which it mainly differs for the fact that the non-miscible products are 
delivered by a heterogeneous fleet of trucks equipped with flowmeters. The use of 
flowmeters guarantees a more flexible truck loading procedure, at the cost of increasing 
the complexity of the problem.  
Three strategies were developed in order to minimize the total traveled distance, 
incorporating three subproblems, that are, the Travelling Salesman Problem, the Set 
Partitioning Problem and the Truck Loading Problem. Strategy 1 considers only the 
replenishment of stations which contain tanks that are running out of stock, by refueling 
one station per trip. Apart from refueling the tanks that are dry, the replenishment of 
other tanks is also considered, which belong to the visited stations. The delivery plan 
of strategy 2, apart from the dry stations, considers in the same route also the 
replenishment of other stations, which are geographically close and whose tanksвҖҷ dry 
run occurs earlier than the new stockout of the dry stations. Strategy 3 follows the 
approaches of the previous strategies, but additionally allows to consider two dry 
stations in a single trip. For comparison reasons, a strategy 0 was further developed, 
which is a simplified version of strategy 1, and whose purpose is to replenish only the 
dry tanks each time they are running dry. 
The main indicator taken into account, when evaluating the performance of the 
strategies, is the total traveled distance. However, other indicators were also considered 
in the comparison, with the purpose of drawing more valid conclusions. In particular, 
the total traveled time, the daily workload distribution, the total and daily number of 
round-trips, the trucksвҖҷ average unused space, and the computing time. These indicators 
have been used to analyze and evaluate the solution of the strategies with respect to the 
real-life instance. 
Strategy 0, in spite of providing the fastest solution, is the weakest strategy, since all 
the analyzed indicators exhibited the worst performance. Strategy 1, as being an 
upgraded version of strategy 0, provided a clearly better overall performance, but still 
exhibited a great total traveled distance, thus a great total travel time and a large number 
of carried out trips. Strategy 2, by considering for each trip the possibility of adding a 
maximum number of two detour stations, exhibited a further considerable reduction of 
the total traveled distance. All the indicators are remarkably improved, except for the 
==PAGE:76==
66 
 
daily workload distribution and the computing time, which are the worst among the 
four strategies. Strategy 3, by taking advantage of the possibility of including two dry 
stations into a single route, exhibited the best overall performance for all the indicators. 
Specifically, it provided the shortest traveled distance, the best distribution of the daily 
workload, the lowest percentage of trucksвҖҷ unused space, after a moderate 
computational time. Moreover, strategies 1-3 successfully resolved datasets with 
different tank usages, proving to be consistent, by delivering feasible and reproducible 
solutions. 
All in all, the solutions provided from strategies 2 and 3 are superior over the other two. 
However, strategy 3 provides a slightly better performance on the most important 
indicator, that is, the total traveled distance, as well as in all the other considered 
indicators. Furthermore, the high oscillations in the daily workload, as well as the high 
computing time, make strategy 2 less attractive than strategy 3. Finally, among all the 
developed and tested strategies, strategy 3 is the one that provides the best performance 
overall. 
Regarding the future improvement of strategy 3, it would be beneficial to choose the 
final set of routes by checking in advance whether there is at least one truck that is able 
to carry out each route consisting of two dry stations. As referred in section 5.3, these 
routes are chosen without previously examining whether the minimum delivery 
volumes are feasible to be loaded into the trucks. As a result, this may cause infeasibility 
issues and the early termination of the heuristic. Additionally, the introduction of a 
further station, on the routes consisting of two dry stations, could be examined.  
Concerning the initial assumptions made in chapter 3, the stationsвҖҷ opening hours, as 
well as, the trucksвҖҷ working hours could be taken into consideration. This would help 
the implementation of these strategies in reality. Specifically, the Truck Selection step 
in the developed strategies could be replaced with a heuristic or a MILP model, that 
takes into account all the available trucks for each route. This heuristic must be able to 
assign the trucks to the routes in order to maximize the replenishment time of the 
stations, taking into account the limited working hours of the trucks.
