Summary
The goal of this thesis is to determine the eﬀect of the 1996 National Firearms
Agreement (NFA) legislation in Australia on ﬁrearm related suicides and
homicides.
Many studies have previously been done regarding the NFA-
legislation.
The method and approach that is taken in this thesis is described and ex-
plained in Chapter 1. In Chapter 2 we look through some results and methods
chosen from some previous studies. The time series data[1] that is used in
the ﬁrst model structure is introduced in Chapter 3. The raw data including
the ﬁrearm suicide and homicide and non-ﬁrearm suicide and homicide. This
data is used to model each time series using a simple ARMA [29] approach
in Chapter 4 and in Chapter 5 to apply Multivariate ARMA [27] approach.
In Chapter 6 some historical events are introduced into the model structure
such as World War 1 and 2 and the Great depression as intervention functions.
This approach is used to correct for sudden drop or rise in the data that might
skew the model estimation for later periods. Intervention functions are also
used to simulate the eﬀect of the NFA-legislation with constant, initial and
accumulative response functions. To be able to simplify the models they must
be considered to be stable before any reduction can take place. The stability
is determined in Chapter 7 by examining the residual covariance matrix and
how it develops with the increasing number of iterations. The stable models
found in Chapter 7 are reduced and simpliﬁed with a reducing method ex-
plained in Chapter 8. After the model reduction it was clear that the higher
order models gave more precise estimation than the ﬁrst order models, as
expected. However, since the higher order models become very complex very
quickly it was decided to include exogenous time series to try to improve the
lower order models. Unemployment rate, inﬂation and top 1% income share
data was used as the exogenous time series and are introduced in Chapter
9. The exogenous time series are used to deﬁne and compute new models in
Chapter 10, with the exogenous time series un-lagged and lagged by one year.
In Chapter 11 top 5% and top 10% income share are used instead of top 1%
to estimate which of the time series are better suited for the model structure.
It turns out that ﬁrst order ARMA model with exogenous time series with
un-lagged top 5% income share gives the best estimation compared to top 1%
and 10% income share. In Chapter 12 the models we have deﬁned in Chapters
8 and 11 are compared visually.
==PAGE:4==
ii
The best model from the comparison in Chapter 12 is explained and analysed
in Chapter 13 and the eﬀect of the legislation on the suicide and homicide
data are explained and determined. The ﬁnal conclusions is that the rate
of ﬁrearm suicides decreased after the NFA-legislation, while the non-ﬁrearm
suicide increased of around the same amount. The evidence indicates a total
method substitution in suicides. The eﬀect on the rate of ﬁrearm and non-
ﬁrearm homicides is non-signiﬁcant.
The ﬁnal conclusion and results are
explained in details in Chapter 15.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In April 1996 there was a mass shooting in Tasmania that shook the whole
nation of Australia where 35 people were massacred. Less than two weeks
later the Australian police ministers council [9] wrote and enforced a legisla-
tion called National Firearms Agreement (NFA). The NFA legislation banned
all semi-automatic, self-loading and pump action long-ﬁrearms. The legisla-
tion also limits the possibility of acquire any kind of ﬁrearms.
That was
done by introducing waiting period after purchasing the ﬁrearm (at least 28
days), which would potentially reduce the danger of purchasing ﬁrearm while
in rage or depression. Also everyone that wants to buy any ﬁrearm has to
have a genuine reason for the purchase and the use, i.e. sporting shooters,
hunters etc. Many other rules and regulations were enforced with the NFA
legislation, only the most signiﬁcant changes are listed here. Since previously
legal ﬁrearms were now illegal there was established a twelve month national
amnesty. Allowing gun owners of previously legal ﬁrearms to get a compen-
sation for the returned ﬁrearms which would then be destroyed. The ﬁrearms
buyback took place from early October 1, 1996 till September 30, 1997 [9].
Over that period more than 640.000 [6] illegal ﬁrearms were surrendered and
destroyed.
There have been a lot of articles and papers written about the NFA legisla-
tion in 1996 to determine the eﬀect on ﬁrearm related deaths. Most of these
researches use the same data for four diﬀerent time series, the rate of suicide
with and without ﬁrearms and the rate of homicide with and without ﬁrearms.
The time periods that are used in each paper varies in some cases but most
of the cases only use a small part of the available data when the paper is
published. Diﬀerent methods of analysis were also used in each case. From
these researches there have been a lot of diﬀerent speculation on the eﬀect of
the legislation. None of the cases used exogenous data or multivariate time
series that might improve the model. We will discuss the literature reviews
in more details in next Chapter 2.
The approach that will be used in this paper is to implement a multivariate
ARIMA and ARIMA-X analysis for available data that has been used in previous
papers, using marima package [27] for the statistical programming R [25].
==PAGE:11==
2
Diﬀerent multivariate models will be introduced using diﬀerent time periods,
include intervention regression variables to count for large events that might
eﬀect the data through history of Australia for the period of the data series.
Exogenous data series are also used to see whether the quality of the estima-
tion can be improved. Using this the preferred model after model comparison
we will evaluate the eﬀect the NFA legislation had on the death rate of suicide
and homicide with more consistency than before.

Chapter 2
Literature review
There have been a number of studies around the NFA legislation, we will look
at most of them where we will be considering the methods, data and data pe-
riods used and their conclusions. The studies that we will be looking at range
from the one of the ﬁrst study by Carcach [7] in the year 2002 and the newest
study was published in the year 2015 by Jeanine Baker and Samara McPhe-
dran [4]. Jeanine Baker and Samara McPhedran have published substantial
number of papers over the years regarding the NFA legislation, speciﬁcally [5],
[21], [18], [20], [19] and [4]. There were some other papers written since the
legislation were enforced in 1996. The additional papers we will be looking at
here are written by Peter Reuters and Jenny Mouzos [26], Ozanne-Smith et
al. [24], Chapman et al. [8], Christine Neill and Andrew Leigh [23] and [17],
Helen Klieve et al. [14] and Wang-Sheng Lee and Sandy Suardi [16].
Most of the papers are using the rate of suicide and homicide with and with-
out ﬁrearms except for the ﬁrst study Carcach al et. [7], which only used the
homicide data. Carcach al et. split the data into three periods, the ﬁrst from
1989-1996 pre-legislation the second period is from April 29 - May 4 1996
which is the the period just after the mass murder. And the third period is
May 5 1996 - June 30 1999 (the last available data). Carcach al et. [7] used
daily observation and analysed using moving average intervention model giv-
ing the conclusion of that there was no eﬀect on the homicide data. Reuters
and Mouzos [26] used longer period after the legislation i.e. 1996-2001. Giving
a diﬀerent results than (Carcach al et.). Reuters and Mouzos concluded that
there had been a sharp decline in ﬁrearm homicide while the rate of suicide
did not decline post-legislation using the data on the period from 1980 - 2001.
Ozanne-Smith et al. [24] used Poisson regression model to compare the rate
of ﬁrearm related deaths for Victoria state to the rest of Australia. Legis-
lation similar to the NFA was enforced only in 1988 in Victoria state which
made (Ozanne-Smith et al.) able to determine the eﬀect of that legislation
as well compared to the rest of Australia on diﬀerent periods of time. Their
ﬁndings indicate a signiﬁcant downward trend of all ﬁrearm related deaths
between 1988 and 1995 in Victoria state when the whole of Australia enforced
the ﬁrearms legislation and caught up with with Victoria’s strict legislation.
==PAGE:13==
4
These ﬁndings alone are a good indicator that such a ﬁrearm legislation does
have a positive eﬀect. Ozanne-Smith et al.’s conclusions on the eﬀect of the
NFA legislation were dramatic reduction in overall ﬁrearm related deaths in-
cluding the rate of ﬁrearm suicides using data from 1979 - 2000.
Chapman et al. [8] used yet another method which was negative binomial
regression analysis of change in ﬁrearm death rates and comparison of trends
before and after the legislation. Chapman et al. conclusions showed a posi-
tive eﬀect on ﬁrearm deaths particularly suicides while the legislation had no
eﬀect on the total homicide rate using data from 1979 - 2003.
Study from Baker and McPhedran (2007) [5] is quite interesting since they
use time series models similar to those that are presented in this thesis. They
use Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model to predict
future values for rate of homicide and suicide with and without ﬁrearms.
The period before the legislation 1979 - 1996 is used to make model with
ARIMA(1,1,1), which is used to predict the time period after the legislation
(1997-2004) and compared with the observations. This method showed no
eﬀect of the NFA legislation on rate of ﬁrearm and non-ﬁrearm homicide, and
the rate of ﬁrearms suicide decrease after the legislation.
McPhedran and Baker (2008) [21] analysed four previous studies ([7], [24],
[8] and [5]), from the consistency of the results from diﬀerent methods. They
concluded that the NFA only aﬀected ﬁrearm suicide.
Neill and Leigh (2008) [23] used the ARIMA modeling like Baker and McPhe-
dran (2007) [5]. Neill and Leigh recreated the model that Baker and McPhe-
dran that used ARIMA(1,1,1) model on a period from 1979 - 1996, and com-
pared it to ARIMA(1,1,1) model using the period from 1915 - 1996. They
note that the small sample period of the time series might be biased. Since the
homicide and suicide rates in the period 1970-1980 is very high compared to
the historical average, model that included that period could underestimate
any downward trends in ﬁrearm death rates post-NFA. Using a longer time
period indicates a much larger eﬀect of the NFA than when a shorter time
series are used. Where the model estimates on average of 250 fewer ﬁrearm
deaths per year after the legislation which is almost double the number using
the setup from Baker and McPhedran with period of 1979-1996.
There is no evidence of a method substitution in homicides or suicides accord-
ing to the conclusions from [23] but with available evidence, it leans towards
the conclusion that the NFA legislation did in fact reduce gun related deaths.
==PAGE:14==
5
In article from McPhedran and Baker [18] they question the quality of the
rate of suicides data from Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) [1], where it
is believed that there had been incorrect classiﬁcation of suicide as accidental
deaths. Thus, making it possible to believe that there might have been more
ﬁrearm suicides than shown by the ABS which could eﬀect all previous con-
clusions.
Klieve al et. [14] used negative binomial regression similar to (Chapman et
al. [8]) as well as looking at trends in suicides rates before and after the NFA
legislation. This study analysed the eﬀect on Queensland in the period 1990
- 2004 and Australia 1968 - 2004, since there was a ﬁrearm legislation set in
Queensland in 1990 introducing "cooling oﬀperiod" after buying a ﬁrearm.
The conclusion was that there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the rate of
ﬁrearm suicide pre- and post- NFA in Queensland, while there was signiﬁcant
diﬀerence for the Australian data. There appears to be some drop in both
suicides rates in Australia and Queensland after NFA even though the drop
in Queensland did not appear to be signiﬁcant where the rate of suicide was
declining with similar rate in Queensland after the NFA legislation.
Wang-Sheng Lee and Sandy Suardi [16], used the whole data set given from
1915 to 2004 to maximize the sample size of the time series. The log trans-
formation (just as Neill and Leigh (2008) [23]) is then used to keep the death
rate prediction positive. The natural logarithm of the time series shows a
downtrend in both suicide and homicide from around 1985. This continuing
downward trend might have masked any eﬀect of the NFA, and a visual anal-
ysis of the plots give no clear sign that the NFA had any eﬀect. However,
from the non-ﬁrearm homicide and suicide plots there has been an upward
trend from 1985 but the non-ﬁrearm suicides decrease after around 1996. The
model that is used is ARIMA (Autoregresive and Integrated Moving Aver-
age) model. The use of the model accounts for the eﬀect of the NFA with
approach based on unknown structural break tests. This is done to avoid the
need to choose a particular date that deﬁnes the pre- and post-periods of the
legislation in order to detect possible lagged impact of the legislation. Using
three diﬀerent tests to detect a signiﬁcant breakage in the data series, Lee &
Suardi [16] found no evidence of a break around the NFA legislation for any
of the time series. Lee & Suardi [16] concluded that there was little evidence
to suggest any signiﬁcant eﬀects on ﬁrearm homicide and suicides, and there
appears to be no method substitution.
Andrew Leigh and Christine Neill [17] used a period from 1968 - 2006 with
the time series, resulting in a conclusion similar to previous studies where the
==PAGE:15==
6
change in ﬁrearm homicide and suicide is very large. The conﬁdence interval
of the non-ﬁrearm homicide and suicide was too large to be able to rule out
any possible method substitution.
Samara McPedran and Jeanine Baker [20] tested the impact on youth suicides
of a cost-intensive Australian policy such as the NFA by looking at a struc-
tural breaks in diﬀerent age groups, using Zivot-Andrews and Quandt tests.
Lee & Suardi [16] used the same logic for the overall suicides and homicide
and found no breaks around the NFA legislation. McPedran and Baker used
three diﬀerent age group to analyse, 15 - 24, 25 - 34 and 35 to 44 years of age
using data period of 1907 - 2007 and 1979 - 2007. The result conﬁrmed the
conclusion from Lee & Suardi [16] that there were no signiﬁcant structural
break in any of the age groups using the long time period. For the shorter
time period (1979-2007) there was only one age group (35 - 44) around the
NFA legislation where a trend break in ﬁrearm suicide were found at 1997.
Other methods (Poisson regression, negative binomial regression and simple
linear regression) did not support that there were any signiﬁcant change in
the rate.
Samara McPhedran and Jeanine Baker [19] questioned the quality of the
counting and identiﬁcation of the data from 2004 and onward. They state
that the ABS have been under-counting ﬁrearm homicide since the year 2004
according to the Australian Institute of Criminology (AIC). This is due to
coding procedures used since 2004 where ABS data did not take into account
coroners cases that were "open" at the time of annual ABS data collection.
The National Coronial Information System states that the actual number of
suicides from early 2000s onwards may be in the order of 200 - 400 deaths per
year higher than ABS data indicated. This could possible aﬀect the newer
data 2004- onward.
The most resent study I found is from Jeanine Baker and Samara McPhedran
[4], using the newest reliable data 1979 - 2010 [1]. The ARIMA(2,1,1) and
ARIMA(1,1,1) models were used by modeling the period pre-NFA legislation
and using these model to predict the post-NFA period. There was no sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerence in the observed and predicted ﬁrearm homicide. However,
observations for the other time series ﬁrearm suicide, non-ﬁrearm suicide and
non-ﬁrearm homicide were signiﬁcantly lower than the predicted values from
the models. McPhedran & Baker mention that the other social, cultural and
economic factors may indeed inﬂuence the rate in ﬁrearm and non-ﬁrearm
deaths.

Chapter 5
Multivariate ARMA model using
the whole data set (1915-2014)
Generally since we have data up to 2014 we would like to make a model using
as much of the series as possible. In last section we used data from 1915
- 1996 to estimate the period pre-NFA. In this section we are interested in
comparing a ARMA model from the previous section to model that is made
by using the whole data set (1915 - 2014). To make the model comparable
we will use the simple ARMA process in R [29] to see how we can improve
the model. The order of the ARMA process is the kept the same as in last
chapter ARMA(1,1). We will deﬁne a multivariate model as well and compare
these three models, and evaluate if the introduction of multivariate ARMA
[27] model will give any potential beneﬁts.
The following plots show the
estimations for the three models (See. Figure. 5.1). We deﬁne the ﬁrst two
models by using the tseries package [29] in R for the two diﬀerent time
periods 1915 - 1996 and 1915 - 2014.
For the multivariate model we use the marima package where we deﬁne and
run it as following: First we need to deﬁne the model, the AR and MA order
are set to 1, and the number of timeseries we are using kvar is set to 5. The
rem.var is the number of variables that are not considered in the model. We
set indep = NULL since we want the time series to have inﬂuence on each
other [27].
After we have deﬁne the model structure we are able to call the marima
function in R [27] where we set the range of our data. The means = 1 in-
dicates that all variables are means adjusted, if means = 0 no adjustment
is made. The pattern for the AR and MA terms from the model deﬁnition
is used. Check and plot are set to FALSE where the Check is used to print
out some information from the model input, and plot can be useful when
to determine the stability using "trace" or "log.det" which plots the trace
or log(determinant) of the residual covariance matrix versus iterations gener-
ated. Number of iterations are set to 50 as default. The penalty parameter
is set to 0 so there is no automatic model reduction. The result of the model
structure is shown in Figure 5.1, the model is deﬁned and computed with
code in Appendix A.7 & A.8.

Chapter 8
Model reduction
For each of the model from the marima package we are able to produce a
matrix of the p-values for each variable. We will go through all the models
and determine which of the factors appear to be signiﬁcant and which are
non-signiﬁcant. For this we will use a signiﬁcance level of 5%. It is clear from
the ﬁrst lag for each model that there are some non signiﬁcant variables. For
the model reduction to be fast and eﬀective the following codes are used to
deﬁne and run the models in R and reduce the model by changing the pattern
where the p-value is non-signiﬁcant. When one eﬀect is removed, the eﬀects
of the other variables can change so the model will be computed every time a
variable is removed to compute new matrix for the p-value. This is repeated
until only the signiﬁcant variables remain.
To be able to reduce the models, they must converge and be considered stable
before the reduction takes place. We will use the models from last chapter
that converged (Model1, Model2, Model3, Marima21, Marima22, Marima42
and Marima34)
The process chosen to use when reducing one parameter at a time is that
we will proceed from the highest lag down. The ﬁrst thing is to evaluate a
marima model. From that model we can look at the p-value matrix for the
variables for all lags in AR parameters and MA. In each loop we will look at
the highest order lag and ﬁnd the maximum. If the maximum is less or equal
to the critical p-value we will proceed to the next lag and repeat the process.
After each reduction in AR and MA the model pattern is updated and new
marima model is computed. This process is repeated until all non-signiﬁcant
variables have been removed. The functions that are used to perform this can
be seen in Appendix A.2, A.3, A.4 and A.5. Figure 8.1 shows the estimation
of the reduced models that are of order AR(1) and MA(1) while the higher
order models are plotted in Figure 8.2. One model of ﬁrst order marima model
and one higher order model will be chosen and compared with other models
in later chapters.

Chapter 14
Discussion
Using exogenous data series to improve the models as we have done here like
Baker McPhedran [4] mentioned in their study is clearly a way to be able to
use lower order models to estimate and analyse the eﬀect of the legislation on a
diﬀerent level of accuracy than anyone has done before, to my knowledge. Our
ﬁndings conﬁrm some of the conclusions from previous studies even though
there is still room for improvement especially regarding the use of exogenous
data series and social factors. There were clear dependency of some of the
exogenous time series that are used in this study. The evidence for the eﬀect
of the NFA legislation in 1996 on the ﬁrearm suicide are quite clear. Even
though the downward shift could be a combination of other factors as well
(such as suicide prevention programs), it can not be ignored that all the
evidence point towards the conclusion that the NFA legislation contributed
to that eﬀect of rate of ﬁrearm suicide dropped around -1.2 per year few years
after the legislation (See. Eq.13.4 and Figure. 13.2). This result agrees with
most of the previous papers that concluded that the ﬁrearm suicides were
aﬀected by NFA legislation ([5], [19], [4], [8], [24], [23], [17], [14] ).
There is little evidence of the eﬀect of the NFA legislation (1996) found in
the model 13.5 on the rate of homicide only appears to have decreasing eﬀect
after year 1999, Figure 13.4 shows that the eﬀect is not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
from zero. Interestingly the inﬂation rate in Australia had signiﬁcant eﬀect
on the ﬁrearm homicides, since as the inﬂation decreases the rate of ﬁrearm
homicides decreases as well. There are some studies that suggest a eﬀect on
the ﬁrearm homicide from the NFA- legislation but that is very controversial.
Most of the studies that do ﬁnd any eﬀect mention that it is in most cases
either non signiﬁcant or quite small ([26], [24], [8], [23], [17]).
The rate of non-ﬁrearm suicide have been a bit of a mystery since the legis-
lation was enforced in 1996, there have been many controversial conclusions
from many of previous studies. The results from the model (See. Eq. 13.7)
indicates a upward shift of 1.7 the ﬁrst year after the NFA legislation and
increases to around 2.1 the year after and decreases onward, and converges
to around 1 after the year 2006 as we can seen in Figure 13.6. However when
the eﬀect from the ﬁrearm and non-ﬁrearm suicide (total suicide) is combined
the eﬀect is non-signiﬁcant few years after the legislation which would mean
a lot method substitution (See. Figure. 13.7). There is a large positive cor-
==PAGE:68==
59
relation between the unemployment rate as well as from the top 5% income
share on the rate of non-ﬁrearm suicide, that is if unemployment rate or top
5% income share increases so does the non-ﬁrearm suicides. The signiﬁcance
of the unemployment rate is not surprising, but the magnitude of the esti-
mated parameter making the eﬀect from the unemployment rate very large.
The signiﬁcance of the top 5% income share was also quite interesting and
giving that the top 5% income share has been increasing gradually over the
last 30 - 35 years, this correlation is very alarming. Looking at Figure 13.5
of the observation over the period of 1915 - 2015, it is quite clear that the
rate of non-ﬁrearm suicides has been increasing as it appears that we have a
permanent upward shift. Since the period post- NFA is quite consistent and
is not decreasing with the rate of ﬁrearm suicides we have to conclude that
the evidence of a method substitution are to strong to ignore. By limiting
access of ﬁrearms the legislation had a decreasing eﬀect on ﬁrearm suicides
but increased the non-ﬁrearm suicide through other methods to some degree
at best. There were two studies that showed some eﬀects on the rate of non-
ﬁrearm suicide out of these studies considered ([14], [4]). Non of them could
conﬁrm any method substitution, some speculations were made where this is
quite hard to prove but again the evidence from our model strongly supports
method substitution. The top 5% income share has increasing eﬀect on the
rate of non-ﬁrearm homicide, that is if the top 5% income share increases the
non-ﬁrearm homicide increases as well (See. Eq. 13.8). However the top 5%
income share has been increasing over the last 30 - 35 years while the rate
of non-ﬁrearm homicides have been decreasing since before the NFA legisla-
tion (See. Figure. 13.8). The accumulative intervention function counter the
increase in the top 5% income share after the legislation. This accumulative
eﬀect however, is non signiﬁcant as we can see from Figure 13.9 where the ef-
fect is shown with its standard deviation. From these results we conclude that
the legislation had no eﬀect on the non-ﬁrearm homicide which is consistent
with earlier studies.

Conclusions
To sum up the conclusion on the eﬀect of the NFA legislation, it is clear that
NFA had decreasing eﬀect on the rate of suicides but appears to have in fact
increased the non-ﬁrearm suicides trough method substitution. So the over-
all eﬀect of the legislation might not be as great as previous studies had us
believe. The eﬀect of such a ﬁrearm legislation maybe however be under es-
timated since the ﬁrearm suicides in Australia are relatively small portion of
the over all suicides. The legislation had no eﬀect on ﬁrearm and non-ﬁrearm
homicide.
