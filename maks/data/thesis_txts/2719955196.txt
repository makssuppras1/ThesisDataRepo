ABSTRACT 
 
Many manufacturing companies maintain a traditional product-oriented business model in which the 
provision of the tangible product is the source of the customer value. In the last years, there has been a 
rising interest in transitioning towards a product-as-a-service approach due to the demands of 
customers for more efficient and personalized solutions, higher competitiveness and faster 
technological advances, however not all companies tie this together with providing added value 
through the integration of products and services. Literature shows that Product/Service-Systems (PSS) 
are great enablers for achieving a balance amongst customer value and economic performance. This 
thesis aims to contribute to the research on how the theoretical methodologies for early stages of PSS 
can be adopted in the industrial environment from a customer value and economic performance 
perspective, having 3Shape, developer and manufacturer of dental lab and intraoral scanners, as a case 
study.  
The scarce existing investigation on practical application led to the thesis goal: to develop a support 
that will facilitate the early stages of design of the optimal PSS for the medical device industry in 
terms of customer value and economic performance, to provide a systematic decision making 
framework and promote the application of PSS in the medical device sector. Through literature and 
empirical data it was investigated how to support the development process of a PSS in the medical 
device industry. The identified key findings and gaps were used for the prototyping of several 
iterations of the support, which purpose is to provide guidance and knowledge, through learning and 
doing. The support was tested with stakeholders of the case company in order to assess its application 
and recognize limitations and possible improvements. Finally, the support showed a successful 
fulfillment of the requirements and objectives through application and evaluation with the 
collaborating company

INTRODUCTION 
 
Many manufacturing companies to this date have their attention set on increasing production through 
traditional product revenues. A shift in demands, environmentally and socially, is being reflected on 
the product-oriented business models companies are currently using. Traditional business models 
stimulate the consumption patterns of society, leading to an unmanageable use of resources which the 
planet is not ready to supply (UNEP, n.d.). There is a demand for using resources more efficiently 
without compromising the customers satisfaction. On the other hand, competition is increasing, 
technological improvements are being generated faster and consumers are asking for greater product 
differentiation and added value to meet their changing needs (Diehl et al., 2015; Pieroni et al., 2016). 
Moving towards a result-based business model can respond to customer requirements with more 
customized and cost-effective offerings(Xing & Ness, 2016). From an economic perspective, the 
added value obtained from services can generate high gross margins and increase recurring revenue, 
while gaining a stronger relationship with customers and creating customer loyalty (Deloitte 
Switzerland, n.d.). 
 
Product/Service-Systems can be the answer to battle these changes. A PSS is defined as innovation 
strategies where instead of focusing on the value of selling physical products, one focuses on the 
value of the utility of products and services throughout the product’s life period (A. R. Tan et al., 
2007).The transition towards a new business model consisting of an integration of products and 
services involves a need for methods and tools which support all the design and development phases 
(Cavalieri & Pezzotta, 2012). Additionally, there is a lack of systematic methods and tools 
supporting design and development of PSS and practical approaches making it hard for industries to 
adopt this type of business model (European Union’s Horizon, 2016) . 
 
3Shape, a Danish manufacturing company in the medical device industry, is experiencing this change 
in demand. 3Shape’s mission is to bring dentists and technicians together to deliver the best possible 
patient experience. Holding true to their philosophy, they are interested in improving their product-
centered offerings to move towards a user focused business model that provides added value, without 
compromising their economic performance. This means expanding their portfolio by developing 
integrated products and services, both existing or new. 
 
Having observed changes in demand and in our socioeconomic system, and with 3Shape as a case 
study starting from an initial strategic enquiry, the thesis aims to explore how a medical device 
manufacturer can deliver solutions through PSS in terms of customer value and economic 
performance. The investigation is initiated with the following introductory research questions, that are 
explored in the Research Clarification: 
 
1. How can PSS enable competitive advantage for enterprises? 
2. Why is there a need for PSS in the medical device industry?

Design Research Methodology 
 
The methodology used for the consecution of this project is Design research methodology (DRM). 
DRM (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009a) is a systematic and structured approach to the examination 
of design and design-related phenomena. It aims to gain an understanding of the design process, as 
well as generate knowledge and insights that can inform the development of new designs and design-
related products and services. 
 
The DRM consists of four stages: 
1. Research Clarification (RC) 
2. Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
3. Prescriptive Study (PS) 
4. Descriptive Study II (DS-II) 
 
It is not a linear process, but rather involves a lot of iterations between phases. A detailed explanation 
of each of the four phases follows: 
 
 
 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
Research clarification  
This initial stage involves clearly defining the research question and objectives, which guide the 
research process and establish the scope and focus of the study. This includes conducting an extensive 
literature review and identifying the background, context and problem statement of the research. 
 
Descriptive study I  
This stage involves collecting data through various means such as interviews, surveys, observation, 
and case studies. The data is analyzed to identify patterns and trends, and to gain insights into the 
design process and the factors that influence it. The objective of this stage is to understand the current 
state of the design problem, and to identify areas for improvement. 
 
Prescriptive study  
This stage involves the development and evaluation of design solutions. It includes the 
conceptualization and prototyping of design solutions, as well as the assessment of their potential 
impact and effectiveness. The objective of this stage is to identify the optimal solution or set of 
solutions that addresses the design problem. 
 
Descriptive study II 
This stage involves the implementation and evaluation of the design solution. It includes monitoring 
the performance of the design solution, and assessing its impact on the target users, as well as its 
impact on the organization and the environment. The objective of this stage is to ensure that the 
design solution meets the requirements and objectives established in the previous stages and to adjust 
if necessary.

How can PSS enable competitive advantage for companies?  
  
Today, the pace of the globalization is speeding up. The manufacturing sector is experiencing a 
deindustrialization process due to productivity changes and a transition in demand towards services 
(OECD, 2007). 
The concept of “moving up the value chain” is gaining popularity among manufacturers. This is to 
shift their productive activity into products and services that generate high value-added and intensive 
knowledge(Baines et al., 2007). This added value has always been associated with fabricated material 
products. However, in recent years a new vision of the elements that cause this added value has 
arised, lying in the utilization and not in the product itself. By providing services on top of the 
available products manufacturers can differentiate their offerings and respond to customer needs in a 
more customized way. Other factors contributing to this service-oriented approach pushing 
companies’ competitiveness are design, quality and technological improvements and continuous 
innovation (Mont, 2002). Providers may be more open to introducing new procedures and 
technologies by including added services to products to increase user readiness and, consequently, 
enhance market penetration and increase the customer base (Yip et al., 2015). 
 
A Product/Service-System has the potential to lead to the consecution of these actions. A PSS 
provides intense knowledge on product, process and user to achieve customer satisfaction and 
economic viability (Baines et al., 2007; Mont, 2002). 
 
Product/Service-System  
 
A standardized definition of the PSS concept does not exist in literature despite the great popularity 
and contribution to the field. A review of PSS definitions was carried out to identify the elements 
which compose a PSS.   
 
The identified key elements are  
 
- 
A PSS is the combination of a tangible product and an intangible product (service)  
- 
Realized in a network in which two sides are equally important, producer and consumer. The 
producer network englobes both the internal and external stakeholders (suppliers and service 
providers). Communication and collaboration inside the ecosystem starting at the early stages 
of the PSS design until operation is very important to achieve customer relationships.  
- 
Under an infrastructure which enables value delivery through supporting facilities, logistics, 
tools, software and hardware, and consequently achieve an increase in the value of the PSS 
providers’ recourses and differentiation from competitors (Sarancic, Pigosso, Colli, et al., 
2022).

Most authors classify PSS in 3 main categories. (Tukker, 2004), divides the three categories within a 
scale of tangible product content and intangible product content, as seen in Figure x. Depending on 
the amount of each of them the PSS is placed in one or another category: Category A, category B and 
category C.  
 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
 
Figure 1. Categories from (Tukker, 2004) 
  
Category A is product-oriented services, which is based on sales of products, but with extra added 
services, e.g. maintenance contracts, advice and consultancy.  
Category B is use oriented services, midway in the scale between tangible and intangible product 
content. The main element is not selling the physical product but providing its availability. Meaning, 
the ownership can stay with the provider and the product is made available in various forms, 
sometimes shared by several users, e.g. leasing, renting/sharing and pooling.  
Category C is result oriented services, where provider and consumer agree on a result and no physical 
product takes part. The result can be delivered through activity management, pay-per-service unit or 
as a functional result.  
 
The more to the right on the eight types of PSS, the lower the dependance on the physical product, 
and the higher the complexity of aligning the offering with the customer needs. The demands become 
less concrete and, although the freedom of decision of the provider increases, so does the difficulty of 
defining the elements that will satisfy the customer (Tukker, 2004).  
 
Baines presents this model of business through the example of a photocopier. Traditionally, the 
manufacturer sells the photocopier. This is the pure product-based value category, in which the 
ownership is transferred to the user and follows a common mass production process. A photocopier in 
a regular household would spend more time on standby than in use. Meaning, a great amount of 
resources and energy is not being taken advantage of as the overall usage is much lower than the 
products full potential (EllenMacArthurFoundation, n.d.). Moving along the categories in Figure 1, in 
a product-oriented PSS, the photocopier can be offered together with a product related service or 
advice, e.g, maintenance course. This can impact positively in the lifetime of the product and the use 
phase by reducing resource consumption. There are different alternatives of use-oriented PSS 
offerings: product lease (where the photocopier is uses exclusively by the customer but has no 
ownership of it), product sharing/renting (where the photocopier is owned by the customer and 
shared, or it is owned by the provider and rented to the customer) and product pooling (where the 
photocopier is used simultaneously by various users). Like in the previous category, these cases often 
include maintenance in the offering, prolonging the lifetime of the product. Moreover, the product is 
given a higher and better use. In result-oriented PSS, (Tukker & Tischner, 2006) propose 
outsourcing, functional result and pay-per-service. In outsourcing, a third party has the ownership of 
the photocopier and provides a product related service. In a functional result, the provider of the 
service delivers a specific result (the print of x copies). In pay-per-service, the customer pays for the

output of the product according to the use level (x amount of money is paid per printed copy). The 
authors show that result oriented PSS contributes to cost reduction in operations and prompts 
innovation. Finally, the pure service, which provides pure service-based value, where the physical 
product is non-existent (the user would be able to get a copy of a document out of nowhere).  
 
Why is there a need for PSS in the medical device industry?  
  
Literature demonstrates the benefits of PSS development, however there is scarce research in respect 
to systematic design and development of PSS. Specifically in the medical device sector, more 
practical case studies are required to explore the advantages of PSS implementation. Various 
implementations of PSS are evaluated as successful in the industrial sector and, however this does not 
seem to raise participants in a broader scenario (Mittermeyer et al., 2011). 
 
Currently the methods used for designing PSS are, in great part, coming from traditional product 
development. A PSS brings with it more complexity, translating into new required dimensions to the 
development process. The input in the traditional product development of design requirements is not 
enough in a PSS solution (Mittermeyer et al., 2011).   
 
The process for product development consists of the following main phases: (1) Problem 
identification, (2) Design input, and (3) Solution. It begins with the identification of the problem. An 
analysis of the situation takes place to define the problem statement. Next, conceptualization of the 
product for which design input is required. Finally, the development of the final solution that will be 
delivered to the customer. The overall phases for development of a product or a service are the same, 
however, the activities performed in each of them vary as the characteristics of a tangible element and 
an intangible element are different. For a traditional product development, the design input focuses 
mainly on the technical requirements of the product. On the other hand, PSS development covers the 
functional and performance requirements, considering the requirements of the physical product. It is 
relevant to get knowledge about the final use for the customer, as the delivered solution to the 
customer is the result or the use of the product rather than the operation or ownership of the product as 
in the traditional business. It is clear that new dimensions are needed for the development processes of 
PSS.   
 
The medical industry is highly traditional, following this traditional development, waterfall approach 
which aggravates lead time from ideation to launch (Sheppard, Kouyoumjian, et al., n.d.). This 
framework is being used for the development of both products and services, designing services on top 
of products. The need for a framework that designs integrated products and services and considers the 
characteristics and requirements this involves has not been contemplated in the past. 3Shape is an 
example of a medtech company which is using traditional product development processes for the 
design of their products and services. There is a need for a new development framework with tools 
that are suitable for the characteristics and requirements of a PSS (Mittermeyer et al., 2011). 
 
In a service based economy, satisfying individualized customer needs play a vital role rather than 
focusing on mass-production and consumption. Customers are more interested in availability or 
capability rather than purchasing physical artefacts (Vasantha et al., 2012)

Transitioning from a product oriented to a use or result oriented system can bring enormous benefits 
to the medical industry. Yip et al. highlighted that the means to achieve PSS offerings in health 
manufacturers is an important field of investigation, despite this, more guidance is needed for action 
to be taken.  
 
Given the intrinsic characteristics of the medical device industry there is a need for change of the 
existing situation: a need for reducing costs, a need for a better communication and cooperation 
between actors in the network, and a need for improved value. The following subsections elaborate on 
each individual element.  
  
A need for reducing costs  
 
Cost competitiveness is an important parameter for enterprises as it can be a positional advantage for 
the provider (Pagoropoulos, 2017). Reduction of costs and resources while maximizing the outcome is 
very relevant in the globalization situation that industries are suffering (Mittermeyer et al., 2011). 
Cost competitiveness is an important characteristic of PSS solutions, which have high potential of 
revenue creation. For example, as (Mittermeyer et al., 2011) explains, products and services have 
many possible configurations, one same product can be combined with several different services, 
benefiting from reusability. Nevertheless, the providers of PSS offerings are not the only actors to 
benefit from its capabilities, customers can be supported by the providers to gain productivity 
improvements and to reduce costs in the utilization phase of the offering (Pagoropoulos, 2017).  
The medical device sector is a high resource consumptive industry in which customers are required to 
invest a high economic volume at acquisition which are then followed by occasional, on demand, 
smaller fees.

A need for a better communication and cooperation between actors in the network  
 
The healthcare industry experiences an added complexity due to several characteristics: (1) Its 
innovative features, taking it towards specialization (Mittermeyer et al., 2011), (2) the regulated 
market which it is bound to. Integration is an important value proposition to achieve facing this 
specialization. PSS provides an integration of product and service that deliver value for the user’s 
needs. On the other hand, the healthcare sector involves multiple stakeholders who may have 
conflicting interests and requirements. These are both external (customer, end user, suppliers) and 
internal (stakeholders from within the company). For instance, the actors that are part of the medical 
device field: customers (e.g. hospitals) must comply with public procurement laws and the end-users 
(e.g. doctors, technicians) have a lower responsibility on the decision-making process to buy the 
products and services (Fargnoli et al., 2018) (Yip et al., 2015). Through PSS, the relationship between 
provider and customer comes closer together. The knowledge gap between physicians and product 
developers in the health industry is commonly quite wide. By intensifying communication and 
cooperation an improvement in the quality of the solutions will be attained (Yip et al., 2015).  
 
(Tukker, 2004) highlights the capability of PSSs to respond to user needs. The product the customer 
benefits from is the result, which must fulfill the customer requirements. For this to be achieved it is 
important to receive customer feedback and collect usage data. This exchange of knowledge should be 
made during the development and performance phases, early enough in the process for the needs to be 
defined correctly and actions to be taken (Mittermeyer et al., 2011).  
 
Among actors of the industry there is also a need for methodologies and tools to visualize this 
complex and broad actor network, together with their needs, relationships and contradictions. A better 
communication is achieved having an overview of the dependencies and functions of the stakeholder 
map involved in the provision of a PSS (Mittermeyer et al., 2011).  
 
A need for improved value 
  
Empirical analysis with employees of the case company, 3Shape, shows an increase in the utilization 
of recurring revenue models. By offering a service to the customer the provider obtains in return a 
recurring economical compensation and the customer an enhanced and more personalized user 
experience. This intangible product is difficult to reproduce by competitors, for which the provider 
will also gain a competitive advantage (Yip et al., 2015). The value does not lie exclusively in the 
quality of the product but in the use of the product and the customer activities associated with it. (A. 
Tan et al., 2006).  By integrating services with the physical product companies have the opportunity to 
innovate on a continuous basis, improve in design and quality and adapt the offering to the customers’ 
needs in a more personalized way (Mont, 2002).  
This aligns to what is found in literature. Authors suggest stakeholders to move from the focus of 
growing sales and maintaining margins to aiming for improving value for users, in this case patients.  
Value meaning the achieved outcomes for the patient against the cost of achieving them. PSS design 
promotes information sharing, training and, in general, integrated and multidisciplinary care through 
the delivery of added value and not merely the volume of product sales (Xing et al., 2017).  
 
Discussion of Literature review 
 
Product-Service/Systems provide a transition from selling products to selling value through service 
and performance. The systematic literature review shows how this can bring a competitive advantage 
for companies. On the other hand, another great characteristic is the environmental benefits it brings. 
The medical device industry, as many other sectors, are a big contributor to resource consumption and 
waste production. This can be decoupled from value creation and economic growth through PSS 
design (Kjaer et al., 2019).   
 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
In the literature review, the classification of PSS is presented according to a scale from product 
content to service content. The more to the right in the scale you place your offering, the higher the 
value delivered to the customer. A pure service-based solution will have the lowest environmental 
impact out of the whole scale of PSS, having in the extreme opposite a traditional manufactured 
product (Tukker, 2004).  
 
We can see how some authors include sustainability in the concept of PSS (Baines et al., 2007), and it 
was initially considered to approach the design of the support from a Triple Bottom Line perspective, 
taking the updated concept of it, the Triple Bottom Line nested view (Block & Paredis, 2019): 
everything is embedded in the environment, society exists within the environment and economy is a 
byproduct of society. However, the main drivers for 3Shape are customer value and economic 
performance for which, it has been decided to leave environment and social sustainability out of the 
scope of the present project. The value of sustainability within 3Shape is low. Despite the growing 
demand from workforce of introducing environmental practices to a larger extent in the company, the 
business strategy does not hold sustainability as a priority as for today. Actions are starting to be 
defined slowly, however, the support that will be designed has to be applicable for the current 
situation of the company, and its usability and usefulness will be best if aligned with the business 
strategy of the user.

Conclusions of Literature review 
 
Based on literature and empirical studies, it can be concluded that there is a need for PSS in the 
healthcare industry, and the medical device field in particular, as PSS can help deal with obstacles and 
difficulties in this environment. It is an innovative industry where product and its features are 
continuously improving. However, the competitors rapidly adopt them making differentiation very 
price bound instead of feature bound. PSS allows to deliver a bundled solution of products and 
services, where the services can act as the competitive asset in the market, providing added value. It 
consists of a complex and wide network of actors. Having a stronger, closer and knowledge intensive 
relationship with suppliers and customers would make it easier to understand user needs and define 
and adapt the offerings accordingly. The nature of the products of this industry makes the cost of the 
product very high, both for providers and customers. Developing and testing the technology, 
complying with regulatory affairs of health and safety and high expenses in new products are 
contributors to a high initial investment for the customer. Laws and regulations can also be an 
obstacle in the development process, innovation and technological advances. It is an area that, 
specifically in the medtech industry, has to be considered when moving from a traditional product 
development process to a PSS desgin process. PSS allows different Business Models like reusability 
or pay-per-use, in which resources are allocated in a greater part to the outcome and not the product 
itself to deliver added value to the user.

Literature shows a limited application of service-oriented business models in the context of capital

In the medical sector 
 
The medical industry experiences change constantly, impacting on the customer needs and use 
scenarios (Philips, n.d.-a). The flexibility and customization PSS business models provide (Mont, 
2002; Tukker, 2004) is a great advantage for the changing requirements. The medical sector has a 
wide actor network, from which all stakeholders have different needs and wishes (Yip et al., 2015). 
Requirements should be defined by introducing all stakeholders early in the design process, including 
customers (Mittermeyer et al., 2011). This is something Philips adopts in its design processes as it is 
key for a successful solution implementation. 
 
The Healthcare-as-a-service (HaaS) concept has been brought to life by Philips and VPS Healthcare. 
This type of model allows hospitals to have access to different functionalities on a subscription basis, 
without having the ownership and the high initial expenses (Philips, 2020). Back in 2020, a North 
American hospital, Jackson Memorial hospital, established a partnership with Philips in which an 
application of this model is provided. Enterprise Monitoring as a Service is developed in 
collaboration, which holds a scalable patient monitoring solution that provides 24/7 support, a 
reduction of the cost of care and an improved customer experience, both for the patient and the 
hospital staff. Some of the services that Philips provides the hospital with are data sharing, 
identification of clinical workflow optimization opportunities and training(Metzke, n.d.). The 
customer pays a per-patient fee and all software and hardware upgrades, as well as networking 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
solutions is responsibility of Philips, oppositely to a traditional hospital business model(Philips, n.d.-
a). 
 
Another example of a HaaS application is retail healthcare; small kiosks or pods in retail areas for low 
severity health services, consultations and preventive care services. This concept is starting to be 
explored with a higher interest after the phenomenon of COVID-19(Philips, 2020). A case study is 
being performed within a program of the United States Veterans. Philips provides remote patient 
monitoring solutions through stations placed across the country, bringing healthcare closer to rural 
areas. Veterans will be able to have regular check ups with their clinicians through secure, virtual 
appointments (Philips, n.d.-b). 
 
Some of the benefits of HaaS are the flexibility of a subscription based pay-per-patient, an 
improvement in patient experience, an increase in staff satisfaction and an improvement in the health 
of society overall(Philips, 2020). These are the key elements that conform the Quadruple Aim. This 
approach stands by the improvement of the healthcare system by proposing four areas of performance 
to the health institutions: improving the care experience, improving the health of society overall, 
reducing the healthcare cost per capita and improving the worklife of care providers (Bodenheimer 
& Sinsky, 2014). This concept is an evolution of the Triple Aim (Berwick et al., 2008), which 
disregards the satisfaction of the healthcare workers (Bodenheimer & Sinsky, 2014). This is a 
primary element to consider as there is a direct relationship between patient satisfaction and 
workforce satisfaction. If the clinician is overworked, their satisfaction will be low, which will impact 
on their performance and, hence, impact on the patient’s satisfaction (Bodenheimer & Sinsky, 
2014).

Existing processes and methods for the design of PSS  
 
As identified in the Research Clarification, PSS design suffers of a limited availability of 
methodologies for the development of PSS, specifically for the early stages of design (Sarancic, 
Pigosso, Pezzotta, et al., 2022). Some options are found, however do not achieve a comprehensive 
level (Cavalieri & Pezzotta, 2012). PSS design methodologies scarcely count with a practical 
approach, which is a limitation to adopt PSS for industrial cases (Baines et al., 2007) (Vasantha et al., 
2012). Many prevalent PSS design methodologies lack comprehensive and actionable guidance for 
practitioners, and clear and structured process flows, making their practical implementation 
challenging. Additionally, methodologies often miss detailed design activities and checkpoints that 
are crucial for successful implementation in real-world scenarios (Tran & Park, 2016). 
 
In regards to the industrial application, PSS design process models are often specific to a particular 
industry or company, which may mean that they are not transferable in an easy way to other sectors. 
They difficulty that raises from this is the development of a generic PSS design process model 
applicable throughout different industries or companies. 
 
A review of PSS design methodologies is made in order to understand the existing literature for the 
PSS arena. First (1) a number of criteria have been chosen for the methodology selection, to then (2) 
select the methodologies to review. The chosen criteria are the following:  
 
1. Should be applicable in B2B environments. 
2. Should be highly referred to in PSS community. 
3. Should be applied and demonstrated through industrial examples. 
4. Should have consideration of early-stage design.

The concept of IPS² (Industrial Product-Service Systems) was introduced (Welp et al., 2008) in the 
context of PSS design. They proposed that IPS² considers any combination of product and service 
offerings to meet the specific needs of customers. To support this approach, they presented a model-
based method for generating various concept models in the early stages of development, which allows 
for the integration of different solution elements across various levels of abstraction and from 
different development perspectives (Vasantha et al., 2012).  This approach is referred to as 
"heterogeneous IPS² concept modeling" and is aimed at providing more versatile and sustainable 
solutions for customers. 
 
The heterogeneous IPS^2 concept modelling provides a great practical approach as it consists of a 
process flow which details the specific activities to be done throughout. It is especially usable for use-
oriented PSS offerings, although also contemplated its use for product-oriented and result-oriented. 
One of its shortcomings is the lack of the presence of all the lifecycle stages of the product (Tran & 
Park, 2016). 
 
The second methodology in Table 4 is a comprehensive framework for the examination of various 
dimensions in PSS design. Four crucial dimensions are identified to be taken into account when 
designing PSS: value proposition, product life cycle, activity modeling cycle, and actor network. They 
proposed a methodology that includes steps for considering these dimensions and argued that these 
elements are essential for comprehending how current products and systems function, and for 
identifying areas where improvements may be made. This framework is beneficial for gaining an 
understanding of the design elements of a PSS, as well as for identifying areas where the dimensions 
may be better aligned. 
 
As the framework depicts in its representation, the lifecycle of the product is one of the key elements 
of this methodology, hence it considers from production to disposal. It consists of a flow of the four 
dimensions, however, does not specify on the necessary design activities and mentions but does not 
==PAGE:29==
 
28 
discuss in detail the specifications of the integration between product and service (Tran & Park, 
2016). 
 
(Aurich et al., 2006) proposed that adapting existing product design processes to account for the 
unique characteristics of technical services can lead to greater acceptance within an organization. 
They argued that using modular reference process models can assist in developing a shared 
understanding of design and manufacturing among all involved parties, and can help the identification 
of any differences due to geographic or cultural factors, as well as the size of service partners or 
customer demands. 
 
This methodology makes available a process flow, as well as the detailing of the activities through the 
flow. Although it covers the integration of product and service, it does not include the concepts of 
business model, organizational structure, and stakeholders. Nor when the user should be involved in 
the process or the implementation of feedback loops (Tran & Park, 2016). 
 
(Shimomura et al., 2007) proposed a technique for simultaneously and cooperatively designing 
service activities and products during the early stages of product design. It is given the name of 
Service Explorer. They posited that by integrating the design of services and products, customer value 
can be optimized by considering the effects of cooperation, alternatives, and complements. They 
introduced a unified approach for representing human and physical processes in service activities, 
using customer-centric parameters (RSPs) to depict changes in customer state. They also proposed a 
view model for handling functions and attributes of RSPs. The team argued that the lowest-level 
functions are associated with tangible entities such as hardware, people, and software. This approach 
aims to provide a more comprehensive and efficient design process, taking into account the 
customer's perspective and the interplay between service and product design 
 
As the rest of the selected methodologies, the Service Explorer provides a process flow with the 
detailing of the activities. As well as design checkpoints and a detailed explanation of the integration 
between product and service. It also covers and details in a systemic level the stakeholders, delivery 
channels and business model, leaving out the business model (Tran & Park, 2016). It provides a 
very thorough explanation of the transition from customer needs to requirements to functions (Kimita 
et al., n.d.) 
 
As the literature shows, no comprehensive or generic framework for early stages of PSS design is 
available and there is no generic process model for PSS design installed in a practical context. It is 
proven that although the methodologies cover many gaps, there is a low success rate of delivering 
profitability for companies when making use of these (Baines et al., 2007; Neely, 2008). (Sarancic, 
Pigosso, Pezzotta, et al., 2022) proposes a generic process model for early stages of design for 
companies to provide a solution to this void. The methodology consists of a three phased process, 
consisting of (1) strategic planning, (2) exploring opportunities and (3) PSS concept development, 
separated by decision-gates. In each of these phases, a workflow of activities are described 
(identification, analysis, definition, selection and refinement), covering all entities of early design 
stages of a PSS, which are grouped in (1) Business model, (2) network of actors, (3) requirements, (4) 
functions, (5) offerings, (6) structure and (7) plan for implementation. For each of the clusters the 
input, output, possible tools and constraints are defined, serving as a guide for the users of the process. 
The process also considers the innovation type of the PSS to design, which will determine in which 
phase the company should start in the process. Figure 10 illustrates the process and its components. 
The Generic process model will be used as a starting point for the design of the support in the 
Prescriptive Study.

Business Model 
 
A Business Model (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2020) is a conceptual framework that describes how an 
organization creates, delivers, and captures value. In the context of PSS, a business model describes 
the ways in which the PSS is marketed, sold, and supported, as well as the revenue streams that it 
generates. 
A PSS business model typically includes elements such as the target market, value proposition, 
revenue streams, distribution channels, customer relationships, and key resources and activities .

Some specific elements of a business model tool for a PSS include: 
 
1. Product ownership: Differently to traditional Business Models, PSS Business Models take 
into account the possibilities of product ownership. The archetypes defined in the Research 
Clarification cover the product ownership associated to each of them: product ownership is 
kept with the customer for a product-oriented PSS, in a use-oriented different options are 
leasing and renting, whereas, on the other extreme, for a result oriented the provider owns the 
product.  
2. Partners: A PSS often involves partnerships with other organizations to create, deliver, and 
support the PSS. The business model tool should identify the key partners and describe the 
nature of the partnership and how they contribute to the PSS. 
3. Services: Integration of products and services is a key characteristic of a PSS, such as 
providing maintenance and repair services, or providing training and consulting services. The 
business model tool should identify the different services that are provided and how they 
contribute to the PSS. 
4. Lifecycle management: A PSS often involves managing the whole lifecycle of the product 
and service, from the design to the disposal. The business model tool should identify the 
different steps and activities that the PSS takes to achieve this goal. 
 
A business model tool for a PSS should be able to effectively capture and convey the unique elements 
of a PSS, such as the integration of products and services, the role of partners, and the ownership of 
products. Additionally, it should be able to demonstrate how these elements interact and contribute to 
the overall value proposition of the PSS for customers, the company, and society, as well as its 
alignment with the organization's overall strategy.

QFD/Value Analysis 
 
Quality Function Deployment and Value Analysis are two powerful methodologies that can be 
integrated to optimize the design and development of a PSS that meets customer needs while 
maximizing value and minimizing costs. 
 
QFD is a customer-centric approach that aims to translate customer requirements into specific 
technical characteristics of a PSS. It involves a series of matrices and diagrams that organize and 
prioritize customer needs, and then map them to the design characteristics of the PSS. By using QFD, 
organizations can ensure that the PSS is designed to meet the needs and expectations of customers, 
resulting in increased customer satisfaction (Haber et al., 2020). 
VA, on the other hand, is a structured approach to identifying and eliminating unnecessary costs and 
increasing value in a PSS. It involves a systematic examination of the functions of the PSS and the 
costs associated with each function, with the goal of identifying ways to reduce costs without 
compromising performance or quality. By using VA, organizations can ensure that the PSS is 
designed and developed in a cost-effective manner, resulting in increased value for customers and the 
organization(Leber et al., 2014) . 
By integrating QFD and VA, organizations can develop a PSS that meets the needs of customers 
while also maximizing value and minimizing costs. This approach can help organizations to achieve a 
better balance between cost, quality and customer satisfaction, resulting in a more competitive PSS in 
the market.

Performance management has been recognized as an important area for measuring effectiveness 
and efficiency of activities. Most of the companies are largely using performance measurement to 
assess the progress of their processes to meet quantifiable goals and objectives in terms of time, 
cost and quality. The initial performance measurement frameworks were traditionally focusing 
mainly on financial indicators. As time has passed, researchers have proposed different 
frameworks, models and tools for designing performance measurement based on non-financial 
indicators like quality, customer satisfaction and innovation (Bititci et al., 2012). 
 
The BSC (Balanced Scorecard) (Kaplan & Norton, 1996) is a tool which provides a balance of the 
financial indicators for companies, allowing the transfer of the company goals to an operational level. 
The following four perspectives are included in this tool: 
1. Financial perspective: the strategy of aiming for growth, profitability and risk mitigation from 
a shareholder’s perspective. 
2. Customer perspective: the strategy for creating value and differentiation from a customers 
point of view. 
3. Internal business process perspective: the strategic priorities for business processes that create 
customer and shareholder satisfaction. 
4. Learning and growth perspective 
These four perspectives consider both tangible and intangible assets, which combination is a source 
for competitive advantage. Studies show this approach is appropriate for a PSS context (Chirumalla et 
al., 2013).

Financial 
 
It is challenging to estimate the financial potential of PSS offerings (Sarancic, Pigosso, & 
McAloone, 2022). The PSS characteristics increase the cost of production of the offering and of 
retaining the ownership while having the uncertainty of the duration and amount of return on 
investment. Providers also have the fear of acquiring risks that were assumed by the customer in the 
product business model situation (European Union’s Horizon, 2016). Hence, the providers are 
uncertain of the possible revenue and, moreover, have limited knowledge of costs. There are no 
history records of revenues and costs in 3Shape of PSS. The calculation of revenue and costs are 
made for traditional product development. The question is, what are the elements involved for a PSS? 
Cannibalization is another possible barrier, meaning experiencing conflict with offerings the company 
already has in the market. 
 
Social 
 
One of the main challenges is considered to be cultural. The provider must change the business model 
structure, moving from product thinking to system thinking. On the other hand, the customers mindset 
is positioned at acquiring the ownership of the product instead of fulfilling their needs through the 
result the product provides.  
 
It is a rather novel concept of which many capital goods manufacturing companies don't have 
experience about, meaning lack of knowledge and capabilities in the PSS area (Sarancic et al., 2021), 
in terms of service design methods and tools to evaluate and implement PSS design. The readiness of 
both company and the user to accept a PSS is a sub-factor of this barrier. A limitation to overcome 
this barrier is the culture of the company.

Needs of medical device industry  
 
It is observed that there is a gap in 3hape’s Product Roadmaps. The product development processes 
focus their attention on the operational use of the product. The product is launched being the central 
piece of the business model. Instead, to transition towards a product-as-a-service business model the 
customer must be placed in the center, considering the full lifecycle of the product associated to the 
needs of the user. 
 
3Shape requires a clear definition of roles and responsibilities, for all functions to have knowledge of 
their own activities as well as the dependencies with the rest in order to improve cross-functional 
communication. Increasing knowledge sharing can bring the functions closer and dissolve the “silos”. 
A centralized development process which is fully supported with training, knowledge sharing and best 
practices can tackle the key challenges 3Shape is facing. 
 
Cross-functional communication should be incentivized, as well as the creation of cross-functional 
teams. Not only this, the teams should be composed by individuals who are interdisciplinary, noy only 
experts in one concrete subject but who have knowledge and skills in different areas to have a more 
holistic view of the full design process (Sheppard, Edson, et al., n.d.). 
 
The changing and personalized needs and requirements of users and the rest of actors have to be heard 
throughout the full time period of the design of the PSS. Voice of the Customer studies, as well as 
collecting data from the product usage is a complete source of data. The data collection needs to be a 
combination between qualitative research and quantitative, such as analyzing warranty service 
agreements (Sheppard, Edson, et al., n.d.).  Learning, testing and iterating are the only way to deliver 
a product/service-system that will fulfill all stakeholders needs (Sheppard, Kouyoumjian, et al., n.d.). 
It is proven that prototyping early, “quick and dirty” and iteratively moves companies quicker and 
more effectively towards the design of a product that satisfies customers wishes (Sheppard, Edson, et 
al., n.d.) 
 
The classical product engineering and systems used for current business models do not support PSS 
design or facilitate knowledge acquisition and re-use (European Union’s Horizon, 2016). Companies 
like 3Shape require a structured process comparable to current systems, without compromising 
flexibility, to allow the revision of requirements and functions throughout the full process. The 
support should incorporate all phases of the products lifecycle and the correspondent actors from 
beginning to end of the early design stages.

PSS can be divided into four groups based on the connection among these regarding the PSS

delivery. It may be a process connectivity, meaning changes to be made to the existing processes due

study are one of the many contributors to the definition of the requirements for the support. 
 
Conclusions 
For RQ 1, an investigation of both existing PSS offerings and processes and tools that support their 
design took place.  
RQ1.1 revealed that PSS have started a successful path in the medical device industry. Big medical 
companies are already offering solutions through, for example, the Healthcare-as-as-service model 
that range from product-oriented offerings like standard support to result oriented, were the ownership 
of the product stays with the provider and the customer (i.e. hospitals, clinics…) purchases a pay per 
patient subscription in the archetypes of a PSS. For 3Shape and companies within its competitors, the 
spectrum of available typologies of PSS to their customers is not as wide. Practically only product-
==PAGE:44==
 
43 
based services are offered by the providers. Use oriented and result oriented are unexplored areas. 
Having examples from companies like Philips, watching their benefits and success, shows that there is 
a great opportunity for companies like 3Shape to also move in that direction. It shows that a PSS 
approach can contribute to pursuing the Quadruple Aim.  
 
Through RQ1.2 it was discovered that the design of PSS suffers of a lack of approaches, specifically 
for the early stages of PSS design, consisting of planning and conceptualization (Sarancic, Pigosso, 
Pezzotta, et al., 2022). PSS design methodologies scarcely count with a practical approach, which is a 
limitation to adopt PSS for industrial cases (Baines et al., 2007; Vasantha et al., 2012). The early

In RQ2.1, both barriers and drivers of PSS were explored through an exercise performed internally in 
3Shape. One of the drivers which was rated with a high value was to enable flexibility in delivering 
customized offerings to customers, among others the relationship between the speed of progress of a 
PSS and the evolution of customer needs, the availability of upgradability of the offering,… For 
example, PSS design enables the delivery of new added value, through services, on top of the same 
product overtime. This input is translated into the following requirement: Perform feedback loops 
overtime to constantly learn about customer needs and usage to enable a customized and flexible

From RQ2.1 it was identified that one of the top drivers for PSS for the interviewees from 3Shape was 
to understand and improve customer and network relationships and develop loyalty. In this was 
customer retention, and consequently the customer base, would grow. Oppositely, a detected 
challenge was the difficulties of identifying customer needs before developing new value 
propositions. To be able to have a bigger knowledge of what needs their customers have, 3Shape 
could take advantage of the enhanced relationships with the network of actors, including customers, 
that PSS design enables. The requirement found is: Involve customers early in the process to identify 
user needs (V.O.C). 
 
As discovered in the Research Clarification, companies are using product development methodologies 
also for the design of Product-Service Systems. (Aurich et al., 2006) suggests to replace 
methodologies used for the design of technical services with systematic design processes, without 
moving far away from familiar processes for the company. In RQ1.3, the existing processes in 3Shape 
are analyzed, being an example of how companies use traditional product development processes. The 
requirement can be set as: The new installed process should be comparable with the current 
traditional product development processes that are currently being used in the company. 
Another finding in the Research Clarification was the heavily regulated market that the medical 
device industry is. Given that 3Shape operates in the medical device industry, it is crucial to prioritize 
compliance of regulations to be able to, first of all, produce and distribute the offering as they have to 
be approved by regulatory bodies, and secondly, to ensure safety and improve health of the end users. 
The elements to take into consideration when designing a PSS to comply with regulations are defined 
in RQ1.3. A requirement should be to: Consider regulations as a part of the process of designing PSS 
offerings. 
 
As found in the Research Clarification, two of the elements that make up a Product-Service System 
are the product and the service. Consequently, companies that deliver PSS offerings will perform 
processes that involve both the physical part and the intangible part, requiring the collaboration and 
communication of different departments within the organization (A. Tan et al., 2006). Currently, as 
collected in RQ2.2, 3Shape works in silos. Being aware of the roles, responsibilities and dependencies 
would improve the collaboration and communication between teams. The requirement is stated as: 
Inform of the integration across the different levels of the company’s development activities. This

R1.2 exhibits the lack of a practical approach of PSS design in the industry (Baines et al., 2007;

implementation of a project are interested in having an understanding of the detailed steps of the used 
methodology. This includes describing in detail the activities to perform, providing its outputs (Tran

Given the required implication of many different actors of the organizational structure due to the

for a complete understanding of the tasks. The requirement that supports the above mentioned 
requirement is: Provide handover checklist at the end of every activity when the output of the activity 
is given to a different department. 
 
Evaluation is a relevant topic in PSS design. Having an evaluation step in the process of PSS design, 
before its launch can enable decreasing risk and uncertainty (Tran & Park, 2016). Setting gates at 
the end of every phase in the process ensures you are moving into the next phase with a complete and 
valid output, which turns into the input of that next phase, and will positively impact on the efficiency 
of the development process (Sarancic, Pigosso, Colli, et al., 2022). The requirement can be stated as:

Introduction 
 
In this section, a support is developed to cover the findings identified in the Descriptive Study I. First, 
the development process of the support is described. Next, the definition of the final support and how 
it should be used is presented. Finally, an evaluation plan of the support, which will be performed in 
the Descriptive Study II, is defined. 
 
Development of the intended support 
 
The goal and target group of the support are outlined as a result from the findings from the 
Descriptive Study I. 
 
 
As the goal of the support explains, the support should serve as a tool for the company to learn about 
PSS development and then do.  
 
The starting point for the development of the support is the current product development process 
followed by 3Shape, described in the Descriptive Study I. As the Generic process model of David 
Sarancic is designed with the intention of providing a generic model for early design stages of PSS 
design, this project’s support is developed on top of it. Its usability is tested, taking into account the 
gaps found in the Descriptive Study I for its adaptation to the requirements of the support of this 
project. The development of the support was performed in three iterations, as shown in Figure 20. 
The first iteration consisted of the instantiation of the Generic process model (Sarancic et al., 2023) 
and the detailing of information that cannot be covered in the diagram in excel sheets. The iteration 
ended with a feedback session in 3Shape to evaluate the usefulness and usability of the design. The 
second iteration starts with the list of considerations from the feedback session. The main aim in this 
iteration is to integrate the pieces into one interactive and practical framework. The selected tool to 
prototype the framework in is Power BI, with the integration of Visio, SharePoint and other Microsoft 
365 apps. Finally, in the third and last iteration, structuring and final definition of the support is done. 
A clear distinction between the learning and the doing module is created, expanding on the doing 
module which had not been detailed in the two first iterations. 
 
Goal 
 
To provide guidance and knowledge, as well as to serve as a practical platform for the early 
stages of PSS design, to deliver an optimal PSS concept in terms of customer value and 
economic performance. 
 
Target group 
 
All organizational actors involved in the early stages of PSS design: Top management, 
project managers, R&D, Regulatory Affairs, After-Sales, Market Insights, Commercial 
Excellence, Production & Supply Chain, Finance, Legal, IT.

Iteration 2 
 
With the provided feedback of the first iteration, it was clear that integration between the parts was 
needed. The diagram was considered the central piece of the support, however it did not visualize the 
full picture to achieve the goal. A more interactive and unified support would help fill in these gaps. 
Alternatives for ways of solving the weaknesses found in the first iteration were contemplated and 
finally it was decided to use tools from the Microsoft 365 package, specifically Power BI, Visio and 
SharePoint, and a smaller contribution of Power Automate and Power Apps. These were tools that 
already made part of 3Shape’s toolbox, for which it was accessible and known, if not by all 
employees, by a lot. 
 
Visio is used to create custom diagrams and visuals to help users better understand data. Power BI is 
used to create interactive dashboards and reports that can be used to analyze and gain insights from 
data. SharePoint provides a platform to store and share data, allowing teams to collaborate on projects 
and access data from any location. 
 
The diagram of swimlanes from the first iteration was moved from PowerPoint to Visio to be able to 
import it into Power BI and integrate it with the data in the excel sheets. In this way, the data could be 
displayed in the type of visualization that is most suitable for the purpose (donut chart, bar chart, 
list…) and not as raw data in an excel. To provide a complete guidance for the users when performing 
an activity, it would be helpful to provide tool templates for each activity. Templates for several of the 
activities were defined and integrated into the Power BI report. A link to the template, an excel 
document for example, was added to the screen of activity details. The templates that have been 
provided are for the activities of Cost calculations, TCO, for the definition of actor needs, 
requirements and functions, QFD for PSS and for the PEIs definition. A detailed explanation of each 
and the reasons why these three have been chosen can be found in the next section, in the description 
of the framework. Moreover, the milestones were separated from the activities and included in a 
timeline, in the swimlane of top management. This provides a clear and independent view of the 
milestones, without losing the relationship with the activities as it is stored in the back end and 
visualized in a chart.  
The integration between Visio and Power BI provides users with the ability to quickly and easily 
access and analyze data to make informed decisions.  
 
Additionally, as point 3 of the feedback from 3Shape covers, a different way of visualizing the 
deliverables was asked for. A list of the deliverables and the corresponding description would not be 
practical enough for users. By including the data of the deliverables to a SharePoint list users could, 
besides having access to the information, have access to a template of the deliverable for better 
guidance and applicability. The combination of these three tools, with the contribution of Power Apps 
and Power Automate would achieve an integrated and interactive platform in which the users could 
navigate through all the necessary data to understand and perform the early stages of PSS design, all 
separated by a click.

The level of dominance of an activity and to be able to prioritize them was point 4 in the feedback 
session of iteration 1. The criteria chosen to assess the dominance of an activity are the following and 
the reasons why they were selected are explained: 
 
- 
Business value/impact on project: if the activity wouldn’t be performed or would be 
performed incorrectly, how impactful would it be in the project? How important is the 
outcome of the activity for the business? 
- 
PSS specific: if the activity is PSS specific it will require more attention from 3Shape at least 
at the beginning since it is a new area for them.  
- 
Resource intensive: in terms of time and manpower. This is relative. The amount of resources 
required for an activity influence on its dominance. 
- 
Critical path: if the activity belongs to the sequence of activities that form the longest route to 
complete the project. 
- 
Alignment between actors: The level of collaboration required between actors, both internal 
and external, increases the dominance of an activity as it requires more effort than activities 
which only have one responsible and non or low alignment needed. 
- 
Severity of risks: how severe is for a risk to occur in the activity? 
- 
Difference between 3Shape and PSS process model: how different are the activities from the 
current development processes in 3Shape, both product and service, to the activities in the 
designed process model for PSS development? 
A matrix of the activities and the criteria is defined. The activities are assessed according to the 
criteria in a scale from 0 to 3, 0 being the lowest and 3 the highest value. The PSS specific criterion 
was already collected in the iteration 1, hence it can be inserted in the Focus Activities matrix. The 
critical path can also be taken from the Visio diagram, scoring the activities which are part of the 
critical path with a 3 and the rest with a 0. The difference criterion values can also be extracted from 
Table 13. The rest of the criteria have to be evaluated in this step as the information has not been 
analyzed in previous steps. The complete Focus Activities matrix is presented in Appendix H.

The Framework 
 
As a result of the iterative development process described in the previous section, the final version of 
the support is presented in the following section. 
The support is a framework for the early stages of PSS development focusing on customer value and 
economic performance. The framework will serve both as a theoretical platform to learn about the 
process and as a practical tool in the actual early development stages of PSS projects. 
 
The framework is built in Power BI, Visio and SharePoint, which integration provides the 
visualization of all relevant information the user needs to achieve the goal: design the optimal PSS 
from a customer value and economic performance perspective, both learning about the process and 
performing an early stages PSS project. It can be said that the framework is designed as an interactive 
application and it is a working prototype, in which users can navigate but also put into practice 
straight away. 
 
The application is divided into two modules: (1) the learning module, named PSS School, where the 
users of the support can find all the necessary elements to be trained on the early stages of PSS 
design, and (2) the doing module, the Projects area, in which users can perform projects and monitor 
their performance.

Roadmap 
 
The second part of the support is a roadmap designed to guide the company through a sequential path 
of recommended actions. As explained in RQ1.1 and 1.3, 3Shape is currently using traditional product 
development processes and its Business Model is designed mainly towards the transfer of the 
ownership of the physical product to the customer. Hence, 3Shape not only requires a guided mapping 
of steps to take for the implementation of the Framework but also the necessary changes at a company 
level for it to be used for its purpose: to deliver Product/Service-Systems, not only products and 
services as being done currently.  
 
There are four stages the company would pass to reach the full adoption of the Framework. 
1. Discovery: Exploration of the current state of the company and the envisioned state. Begin to 
discover the PSS arena in the high level of the organization and its potential. 
2. Initiation: Planning and preparing for the introduction of PSS and the early stages PSS 
Framework. 
3. Consolidation: The new process is up and running and starts being used for real-case projects, 
with the involvement of customers. Monitoring and analysis of performance. 
4. Optimization: Continuous improvement of processes. Keep improving the collaboration 
between product and service functions and optimize the organizational structure to facilitate 
this. 
Each stage is composed of a set of actions the company will go through, and these are clustered into 
different areas: 
1. Strategy: The actions the company will have to perform to define and follow the long-term 
plan to achieve the desired state, including goals and objectives. All three levels of strategy 
==PAGE:80==
 
79 
(corporate, business and functional) should be contemplated as the envisioned concept for the 
future should be aligned with the business units’ goals and the day to day work of the 
employees to achieve these. 
2. Process: Activities to be done for the different parts of the Framework to be put into action. 
There are some activities the company should do for the Framework to be usable. 
3. Training: The early stages PSS design Framework is a new tool for the company, as well as 
PSS is a novel concept on itself. The stakeholders involved in any of the stages of the 
lifecycle of a PSS will need to receive education on what a PSS offering is, how it is different 
from the designing and selling of a physical product and the elements and tools necessary for 
its design. A training plan will be performed, which counts with three levels. Level 1 is a 
basic training, everyone is obliged to do it and should be maintained in time with recurring 
trainings. Level 2 is a higher training level to achieve the trainer badge in order to be 
responsible for level 1 trainings. Finally, level 3 is the highest training, to achieve the expert 
badge and become the trainer of the trainers of level 2. 
4. Actors’ involvement: The internal and external stakeholders that are involved in each of the 
stages of the roadmap. In the initial steps, only the higher management will be part of the 
actions and decisions, however, as time and maturity move along, the rest of actors are slowly 
introduced into the process. The actors involved in the path are the organizational structure 
involved in designing PSS, partners, suppliers, 3Shape resellers and customers. 
Discovery 
 
In the Discovery stage the vision of the company is reviewed. 3Shape is already taking steps towards 
integrating services more with the physical product, as well as exploring recurring revenues Business 
Models. The first step to take is to analyze their existing Business Model (Frederiksen et al., 2021), 
defined in RQ1.3. A SWOT analysis can be performed to evaluate the company’s strengths, 
weaknesses, opportunities and threats. By looking at their competitors the company can discover gaps 
and strengths of what is already in the market. This will provide an exploration of the current situation 
and guide the company to reflect on the desired vision, mission, values and goals for PSS Business 
Models.  
 
Once having analyzed the current Business Model, the findings are used for the next step, the PSS 
archetype assessment. In the Research Clarification the three main categories of typologies or 
archetypes of PSS have been described: product-oriented, use-oriented and result-oriented. The 
company will assess the readiness for the archetypes in the current state and for the envisioned state. 
A tool developed by (Frederiksen et al., 2021) provides a thorough evaluation of the suitable 
archetype for a company, based on the provided answers to some questions regarding PSS 
characteristics. Besides, the tool provides a combined view of the readiness assessment and the vision 
assessment for the company to reflect on the results. The responsible in this stage is top management. 
 
Initiation 
 
The Initiation stage introduces the concept of PSS to the rest of the organizational structure. In this 
stage, an increase of awareness about service business is started inside the company, the performance 
and evaluation indicators are introduced and the PSS Base is created. First of all, the project managers 
receive training on the concept of PSS and how the Business Model is adapted for it. The definition of 
the new Business Model is made, considering the vision and archetype assessment of the Discovery 
stage.  
 
All characteristics of a PSS are introduced, emphasizing on the differences with the design of physical 
products. For example, it is important to explain the workflow of understanding user needs, 
translating them into requirements and finally transforming them into functions, not only considering 
the technical specs, to fulfil the user needs. It is also relevant to stress the consideration of all phases 
==PAGE:81==
 
80 
of a product’s lifecycle for the design of the PSS from its the early stages. Additionally and related to 
this last point, the introduction of the “whole cost”, or hidden costs when calculating the costs of the 
PSS. Subsequently, all functions involved in the early design of PSS are included into the trainings.  
 
In parallel to the training, different processes are performed. All roles and responsibilities that are 
defined in the support, in the RACI chart, should be reviewed with every one of the functions to 
ensure the allocation of resources is correct.  
 
The terminology of activities and deliverables should be reviewed to align with 3Shape terminology. 
As Requirement #1 from the Descriptive Study I indicates, the support should be comparable to the 
current situation of 3shape for the users of the support to be able to get familiarized with it with the 
lowest effort and in the shortest time, leaving the complexity to the understanding of the content, not 
the terminology. Of course, new terms specific to PSS design that 3Shape will adopt should maintain 
its original state, adding them to 3Shapes existing terminology.  
 
In this stage, performance and evaluation indicators are introduced. A list of performance and 
evaluation indicators has been provided in the support, however the company should review and 
include further indicators. Having an ad hoc performance and evaluation indicators list for the 
company will ensure that the PSS design is aligned with their goals and objectives, and that they have 
the necessary data and metrics to evaluate the PSS's performance over time in terms of customer value 
and economic performance.  
 
Finally, a Pilot test using the support. This is the activity that separates the initiation phase and the 
consolidation phase. Once the basic training has been performed and the users are familiarized with 
the different parts of the system, it is time to test the full system together. It will consist of a 
qualitative study in which all involved functions participate and test out the support by performing a 
dummy project. 
 
Consolidation 
 
The main strategic changes in this stage are the repositioning in the value chain, the customer 
involvement and data collection from voice of the customer and usage of products. 
 
In this stage the external stakeholders involved in the design, development and sales and distribution 
processes are introduced: partners and suppliers and resellers, the connection between 3Shape and the 
customer. These stakeholders must also be educated in the PSS arena, one of the main new elements 
is their extended activity focus, not only limited to the manufacturing, sales and distribution of the

Discussion 
 
The support consists of two parts:  
1. The early PSS design Framework that provides guidance and decision making to all functions 
involved in the early stages of PSS design supports from the planning of a PSS project to the 
evaluation of the PSS concept. 
2. The Roadmap for the company to have a full overview of the steps required to transition from 
a product-oriented Business Model to a PSS Business Model, being the early PSS design 
Framework one of the steps. 
First, it is important to note that the early PSS design Framework is not implementable without the 
first two steps defined in the Roadmap being performed. The company must review its vision, by 
analyzing the current Business Model, which is defined in RQ1.3, and assessing the PSS archetype. 
Only then the company will be ready to pass the Discovery phase and move on to Initiation, where the 
designed early PSS design framework comes into action. 
 
The starting point for this phase was the Generic process model designed by David Sarancic. In DSI, 
some gaps were identified in the Generic process model when evaluating the Requirement 
specification, as presented in Appendix D. The support fills in those identified gaps in the following 
way. 
 
The RACO chart, as well as the structuring of the main diagrams in swimlanes corresponding to the 
involved functions, presents the roles and responsibilities, as well as the communication and 
collaboration flows among functions. The allocation of these roles and responsibilities was performed 
together with 3Shape employees, however, representatives of more functions could have been 
involved. 
 
The tool is flexible and customizable. In the case that changes were made to the organizational 
structure in the future (once 3Shape reaches that step in the Roadmap and assesses the situation), these 
could be modified in the designed tool, as well as reallocating the activities to different functions. 
 
The four different levels that the tool provides (level 1: PSS overview diagram, level 2: detailed 
diagram, level 3: activity details, level 4: tools) provides a holistic view, while, at the same time, 
being detailed enough on an activity level regarding inputs, outputs and tools. 
 
The economic factor has been given a bigger role in the designed support as opposing to the Generic 
process model. The later was lacking more depth on this topic. It is especially important to achieve the 
goal of this support as it should assess the economic performance of the PSS concept. 
 
Conclusions 
 
This section collects the description of the development of a support that should face the challenges 
and achieve the requirements found in the Research Clarification and in the Descriptive Study I. The 
objective of the support was to provide guidance and knowledge, as well as to serve as a practical 
platform for the early stages of PSS design, to deliver an optimal PSS concept in terms of customer 
value and economic performance. The result was a two-parted support. On one side a framework 
through an application in which the users can learn about the early stages of PSS design and the 
decision making rules for Gates, Milestones and Deliverables and also do and track their own and 
others process of a real project to finalize with the delivery of the optimal PSS concept. And, on the 
other side, a Roadmap for the company to receive guidance on the timing and actions of the 
implementation of such a tool, starting from the first step to take for a transition towards a PSS 
Business Model.

Evaluation of the support 
 
The support was used and evaluated individually by three stakeholders of the design process from the

As mentioned in the outlined evaluation plan presented in the Prescriptive Study, the Application 
Evaluation is in charge of the evaluation of the usability of the support. While using the tool, the 
interviewees are asked to think-out-loud as they are guided through the different pages. They are also 
encouraged to provide suggestions for changes and improvements. Once the users have explored all

degree of usability of the support regarding a set of established criteria. The criteria that have been 
evaluated are:  
 
1. Utility: usefulness in facilitating the early stages of design of the optimal PSS, in terms of 
both teaching and guidance  
2. Consistency: level of consistency between the different levels proposed (PSS overview, 
detailed overview, activity overview) and between the activities 
3. Completeness: degree to which all necessary entities or activities are present in the model. 
4. Scope: level to which the model covers the whole early-stage design 
5. Precision: Accuracy of the definition of the content of the process model. 
6. Simplicity: could the model be represented more simply? 
7. Clarity: Difficulty of understanding the model.

to usefulness. This is to have a minimum indicator of this variable, before proceeding with the

Fortunately for medtech companies like 3Shape, and partly unfortunately, I could say, for society, 
there will always exist a demand for medical related technologies. The medical device industry is a 
growing sector, and with it also 3Shape. The demand for digital dentistry solutions is firm and 
increasing, proportionally to the amounts of competitors and new entrants. To confront these threats 
3Shape must look towards the future.  
 
Already action is being taken at a strategic level. Innovation is a great part of 3Shape, expanding it 
even more is a priority for the company. Investing in research and innovation in the outcome and 
utilization, beyond the physical product technical specifications can provide differentiation and, in all, 
a great competitive advantage. 
 
Exploring the Business Model of recurring revenues in software, hardware and services to a higher 
extent is in the plans of the company. This is a first step to diving into the PSS archetypes assessment

spectrum of product-oriented to result-oriented. Being a first mover in the industry can bring along

The Roadmap offers a clear step-by-step guidance for 3Shape to move forward with its strategic

Impact of support 
 
The provided support will serve as a guidance and training tool in the PSS area, as well as a practical 
tool that can be incorporated to their current system. It will provide the steps to move from a product-
thinking to a function-based thinking and guide them through the process of identifying needs and 
transforming them into functions to fulfill these, through the implementation of different tools. The 
guidance is extended to a project level, the support facilitates decision-making throughout the process 
of the early design stages, providing clear direction of completion of deliverables and passing of 
Gates.  
The support will enable 3Shape to move away from the silos structure towards more cross-functional 
teams, in which collaboration and communication is strengthened. The roles and responsibilities will 
be decided with an holistic perspective, and all information will be accessible by all actors through the 
support. 3Shape users can view not only what activities they are responsible for but also which 
functions they share responsibilities with, at what point in time of the early stages processes and who 
you are involved in for the hand-overs. 
 
Through the support, 3Shape will deliver solutions with an increased customer value and economic 
performance. The support facilitates a toolbox to teach and ease the necessary activities to do so. For 
example, it promotes the involvement of customers in the design process from very early on and along 
the full process to take advantage of feedback loops, and it presents the concept of hidden costs or 
“whole cost” through the Total Cost of Ownership tool. 
 
The creation of a “PSS Base” as part of the Roadmap will enable knowledge sharing throughout the 
company. A space were historical data of the PSS development of the company is collected, and for 
users of the support to learn from it, add to it or modify it. This will have a positive impact and can 
also be transferable to other parts of the company (only physical products, SW…)

Lessons learnt 
 
A company of the size and outreach of 3Shape, and adding to it the particularities as a medical device 
company, is found to be a very complex system. Writing a master thesis can be a complex and 
challenging task on itself. The complexity is further increased when working in collaboration with a 
company, as there are additional considerations and constraints to take into account. 
 
As one of the learning objectives refers to, it is essential to gain an understanding of the context to be 
able to give answer to the defined research questions. Working with a practical case, in collaboration 
with a company, implies analyzing the current situation and current processes and methods used, in 
this case for product development. Achieving this overall understanding is incredibly resourceful. 
First of all, the understanding of a new terminology. Secondly, the available information is so wide 
that it requires a good analysis and evaluation to limit the scope and narrow down the broadness. A 
good and early guidance from the company for a first delimitation is a good tip. 
 
Communication and collaboration with the company is key to be able to validate and compare the 
insights found in the literature review to the real-life situation in the industry. The collaborators in the 
company have shown exceptional availability and willingness throughout the full duration of the 
thesis for interviews, feedback sessions, Milestone presentations or any other meeting related with the 
project. However, it is important to plan the interventions, in quantity and content, as each individual 
has their own responsibilities to attain. Having an answer to the questions (1) what do I want to 
achieve with the meeting and (2) what will I use the outcome for and what research question will it 
help me answer, will help take the decision of the amount of time needed, the participants that should 
be involved and the content of the meeting. Time is valuable, both for the one who is writing the thesis 
and the ones who are collaborating in it. 
 
One of the biggest challenges in collaborating with a company is balancing the needs and goals of the 
organization with the academic requirements of the thesis. There have been some compromises that 
have had to be made in order to align the research with the company's objectives, while still 
maintaining the integrity of the academic work. Balance out the academic perspective and the 
practical view of the project, without forgetting about your own wishes and expectations. 
 
The voice of the customer is very valuable. This is something that has been applied to the support of 
the project for the design of PSS solutions and that also makes sense for the elaboration of any 
research project. As mentioned, this project has involved a lot of communication and collaboration 
with stakeholders, mainly internal people from the collaborating company, 3Shape, who are the users 
of the designed support. However, through the research and design of the model for the early stages of 
PSS design it was shown that the customers and end users of the PSS offering early involvement is a 
key piece for achieving customer value and economic performance. Including these stakeholders in 
the analysis of the project would have been a “nice to have”, to analyze a different point of view. 
Field studies with dentists, hygienists and patients in clinics could have been another approach to 
include in the research. The voice of the customer has been analyzed through the NPS study 
performed by 3Shape, in which the participants are customers of 3Shape. However, firsthand data 
would have been a nice contribution to the findings and a different experience to take home as well. 
Indirect users are also a good source of information. 
 
Design process (DRM): contributors and limitations 
 
The methodology used for the realization of this project has been the DRM. The principle that has 
been used, taken from the DRM, is to change the existing situation into the desired situation through 
design. This methodology has provided a structure to the thesis and has helped managing the 
complexity of the design process. Firstly, by formulating introductory questions in the research 
Clarification and giving answer to them. These raised more questions (RQ1, RQ2, RQ3), which were 
answered along DSI (RQ1, RQ2) and DSII (RQ3). Having defined a Research Plan in the RC of what 
==PAGE:95==
 
94 
research questions would be answered and in which phases they were expected to be answered, 
provided a great structure and guidance throughout the whole process. Besides, the flexibility of the 
DRM allowed the revision of these questions during the first stages of the thesis as the knowledge in 
the topic expanded and the questions raised and narrowed.  
The availability of data was high, both in literature and from the collaborating company. This 
improved the quality of the answers to the research questions and to the obtained results, but it also 
increased the complexity of the thesis. 
The involvement of the stakeholders from early on in the thesis was very beneficial for clarification of 
needs for the support and to co-design it. Feedback sessions of the prototypes of the support enables 
the identification of strengths and weaknesses of the support, and indications on how to improve the 
support to better fulfill the user’s needs. 
In the DSII, RQ3 was answered by evaluating the usefulness and usability of the support. This is a 
step in the DRM process that was found very valuable for the project, as it gave insights into how 
useful the support actually could be in practice. Having this step was also very positive for the value 
of the support. By achieving a successful outcome of the evaluation of the support, the potential of the 
support to be implementable in the company will be empowered, and the potential resistance of the 
collaborating company to implement it will diminish. 
 
Although communication and collaboration with stakeholders throughout the complete process is very 
valuable and beneficial for the design process, it also takes a significant amount of time. It requires 
recruiting and coordinating with the participants, as well as making a plan for the session and for how

The designed support has had a quite broad scope, as the thesis goal regarded the full early stages

an overview of the full process as the activities alone do not provide a PSS concept. 
 
Allocating more time to the testing and evaluation of the support in the Descriptive Study II would

using the designed support would have provided an evaluation of the usability. In this way, both 
usefulness and usability would have been assessed. This step has been included in the roadmap 
proposed to the company as it is an essential step to take before implementation.

CONCLUSIONS 
 
The objective of this thesis was to investigate how early PSS design can adopt a practical 
approach to deliver customer value and increased economic performance in the medical 
industry. The Design Research Methodology has been followed to structure the project, 
providing a systematic and iterative guidance throughout four phases. The thesis started with 
the Research Clarification in which PSS was identified as an enabler of competitive 
advantage for companies. Some of the advantages are added value in the utilization, 
customized offerings enabling differentiation from competitors and better customer needs 
fulfillment, and design, quality and technological improvements. It was also observed how 
the characteristics of a PSS could give response to the needs in the medical device industry, 
mainly reduction of costs, better communication and cooperation between actors and 
improved value. Despite the potential of PSS in the sector, scarce research in the practical 
approach of PSS in the medical device sector is performed. Hence, the goal of the thesis was 
to develop a support that will facilitate the early stages of design of the optimal PSS for the medical 
device industry in terms of customer value and economic performance, to support decision-making. 
 
The following are the research questions and sub-research questions that were investigated 
throughout the phases of the Thesis to reach the goal: 
 
RQ1 What is the existing situation of PSS within the capital good sector, specifically in the medical 
device industry?  
  RQ1.1 What PSS offerings exist in the capital goods sector, specifically in the medical device 
industry? 
  RQ1.2 What processes and methods are currently used for PSS design? 
  RQ1.3 What gaps exist in the product development processes and tools in 3Shape? 
RQ2 How can the design of the most optimal PSS in the medical industry be supported?  
  RQ2.1 What are the challenges and how can we encounter them?  
  RQ2.2 What are the requirements and how can we achieve them?  
RQ3 How useful and usable is the support for designing the optimal PSS? 
 
 
RQ1 What is the existing situation of PSS within the capital good sector, specifically 
in the medical device industry?  
 
The insights achieved from the answer to these questions were used for the design of the support that 
would facilitate the early stages of design of the optimal PSS for the medical device industry in terms 
of customer value and economic performance. 
 
RQ1.1 What PSS offerings exist in the capital goods sector, specifically in the medical device 
industry? 
 
A first study found in literature made to a sample of companies from the capital goods sector revealed 
that a limited application of service-oriented business models exists in this context. Their main 
revenue streams come from the product sales and not all services in the PSS archetype are explored. 
The most common offered services are basic services (documentation, repair, spare parts, basic 
training), while advances services are only sometimes offered (maintenance contracts, product 
upgrade, remote monitoring), falling especially in the archetype of product-oriented PSS. 
When investigating PSS offerings in the medical device industry, it was observed that big medical 
companies like Philips Medical are offering solutions that range from product-oriented offerings like 
standard support to result oriented, were the ownership of the product stays with the provider and the 
==PAGE:100==
 
99 
customer (i.e. hospitals, clinics…) purchases a pay per patient subscription. For 3Shape and 
companies within its competitors, the spectrum of available typologies of PSS to their customers is 
not as wide. Practically only product-based services are offered by the providers. Use oriented and 
result oriented are unexplored areas.  
In general, the existing application of PSS remains in the product-oriented domain within the medical 
device sector, and the capital goods sector more broadly. However, some companies are already 
experimenting in the areas of use-oriented and result-oriented. Hence, there is an opportunity for PSS 
design to break into the industrial practice of medical devices, like 3Shape. Having examples from 
companies like Philips, watching their benefits and success, shows that there is a great opportunity for 
companies like 3Shape to also move in that direction. It shows that a PSS approach can contribute to 
pursuing the Quadruple Aim.  
 
RQ1.2 What processes and methods are currently used for PSS design? 
 
It was discovered that the design of PSS suffers of a lack of approaches, specifically for the early 
stages of PSS design, consisting of the planning and conceptualization phases. Several methodologies 
for PSS design were identified and described. It was concluded that they are not suitable for industrial 
application. Besides, methods and tools were investigated (e.i, Business Model Canvas, TCO, KPIs, 
QFD, Value Analysis) and were found suitable for the design of PSS solutions that provide customer 
value and a high economic performance. 
 
RQ1.3 What gaps exist in the product development processes and tools in 3Shape? 
 
3Shape generates revenue primarily through the sale of its products and services: scanners, software, 
and accessories. The ownership of the product is transferred to the user. They offer complementary 
service agreements. Given their Business Model, 3Shape follows a traditional product development 
process for the release of their products to the market and, separately, after-sales processes for the 
development of the subscription memberships. In combination with the development processes, an 
overlay has recently been implemented in 3Shape, the Product Development Framework. Its main 
objectives are to increase cross-functional collaboration within teams, and to provide a more 
structured and transparent guidance to manage projects from beginning to end. It is concluded that the 
product development and the service development are distanced, both in terms of communication 
(space) and collaboration (time: service development happens once product development is ready). 
 
 
RQ2 How can the design of the most optimal PSS in the medical industry be 
supported?  
 
The insights achieved from the answer to these questions were used for the design of the support that 
would facilitate the early stages of design of the optimal PSS for the medical device industry in terms 
of customer value and economic performance. 
 
RQ2.1 What are the challenges and how can we encounter them?  
 
Findings from literature and empirical studies were compared regarding the challenges that are 
involved for introducing PSS solutions, in order to support the process of PSS development. 
Moreover, challenges that 3Shape is facing as an organization were collected as they impact directly 
on the introduction of PSS solutions, and consequently on the design of the support.  
The main challenges obtained from literature and empirical studies were assessed in a workshop in 
3Shape from which the main challenges were found to be: 
1. The nature of the product (amongst others regulations) 
2. Difficult to offer standard services and track and compare performance 
==PAGE:101==
 
100 
3. Difficulties of identifying customer needs before developing new value propositions 
4. IT set up is currently designed for traditional product development, it will require unknown 
changes 
5. Operation processes 
6. Challenging to estimate financial potential of PSS solutions 
7. Uncertainty of possible revenue and limited knowledge of costs 
8. Lack of knowledge and capabilities in PSS area (ex. to drive PSS dev. process) 
9. Market adoption risk 
It was found that 3Shape is facing increased organizational complexity, as well as increased product 
complexity. Its key challenges are a lack of holistic perspective, weak cross-functional collaboration 
due to silos, specifically in the design of products and services which is not done in parallel, and 
prioritization of tasks. A centralized development process which is fully supported with training, 
knowledge sharing and best practices can tackle the key challenges 3Shape is facing. 
 
 
RQ2.2 What are the requirements and how can we achieve them?  
 
The requirements to design the early stages of design of a PSS were identified through 
literature and empirical studied. Both customer needs and company needs were found. 
As the results from an NPS study performed by 3Shape it was concluded that the top areas of value 
for 3Shape customers regard the functionality of the product, while services are categorized as main 
influencers for detractors. The product should be integrated with services that provide the added 
value. Moreover, it is found that currently perceives customer experienced is high during promotion 
and onboarding and declines throughout the lifecycle of the customers activities. There is a potential 
to encourage 3Shape to expand the customer’s perception of value to the activities of the customer 
during the use of the product. 
 
3Shape requires a clear definition of roles and responsibilities, for all functions to have knowledge of 
their own activities as well as the dependencies with the rest in order to improve cross-functional 
communication. 
A structured process that defines deliverables and milestones, described in a way so that the users of 
the support understand what actions they have to take is needed. 
A process comparable to current systems, without compromising flexibility, and incorporating all 
phases of the products lifecycle and actors from beginning to end of the early design stages. 
A particular element of a medical device industry like 3Shape is regulations. They can significantly 
impact on the design of a PSS, differently from a traditional product, and this has to be reflected in the 
designed process. 
 
RQ3 How useful and usable is the support for designing the optimal PSS? 
 
An evaluation plan was performed in the Prescriptive Study I to evaluate the support in the 
Descriptive Study II, providing an answer to this research question. The evaluation plan consists of 
two phases, an Application Evaluation and a Success Evaluation. Due to the limitation of time for the 
completion of the thesis and the amount of time needed to perform a test of the full process that the 
support holds, it was only possible to perform the Application Evaluation. A Thinking-out-loud test 
was facilitated with 3Shape representatives of the three main functions who would be users of the 
support, followed by a questionnaire. It was concluded that that the designed platform supports 
guidance and decision making from the initiation of a PSS project to the design of a concept - the 
early stages of PSS design – in terms of usefulness. This was mainly due to the practical approach it 
provides without compromising the detailing of all necessary elements to design a PSS, including 
actor network, Business model, lifecycle stages, costs, customer requirements and processes. It also 
enabled the users to identify the offerings with highest customer value and economic performance. 
==PAGE:102==
 
101 
The users provided some suggestions to improve the usability of the support, the most popular being 
to provide training of the support among the final users of it in order to get familiarized with it and 
break down its different levels, reducing its complexity. To confirm that the support fulfills its goal in 
the practical context, the support must be evaluated in the real day-to-day work of 3Shape.
