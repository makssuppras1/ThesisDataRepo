Abstract 
Cancer is an ever-changing disease that is treated by a variety of methods that often lead to harmful 
side effects. Antibody-drug conjugates are an emerging and rapidly growing class of therapeutics 
that consist of an antibody and a cytotoxic drug that are connected by a linker moiety. The current 
antibody-drug conjugates in use today still suffer from drawbacks, including insufficient specificity 
and premature drug release, giving rise to non-selective toxicity. 
In this thesis, two novel sulfamidase-cleavable linkers, Linker 1 and Linker 2, have been 
investigated. The linkers' sulfamate group contains an enzymatic recognition site for sulfamidase 
enzymes which are overexpressed in certain forms of cancer, including breast cancer. Therefore, the 
linkers are designed to provide specific release inside cancer cells in which sulfatases are 
overexpressed, and this prevents premature release of the drug and lowers systemic toxicity.  
The linkers are designed to release their drug payload via an intracellular ring closure, which 
ensures a rapid and efficient release. Furthermore, they allow for hydroxy-functionalized drugs to 
be attached, whereas current linkers only allow for attachment of amine-functionalized drugs.  
A synthetic strategy has been investigated and the reaction conditions optimized for synthesis of 
Linker 1. However, the drug conjugation was not optimized although a successful strategy was 
identified. with a model drug attached. For Linker 2, a synthetic pathway follows well established 
chemistry. The attachment of a model drug will allow the linkers to be used in future enzymatic 
studies to study the rate of drug release.

Resumé 
Kræft er en mangfoldig sygdom og mange forskellige behandlingsmetoder bliver brugt i et forsøg 
mod at bekæmpe den. Med disse behandlingsmetoder opstår der ofte skadelige bivirkninger. 
Antistof-lægemiddel konjugater er en fremspirende form for kemoterapi, der består af et antistof og 
et cellegiftigt lægemiddel, som er forbundet gennem en linker. De antistof-lægemiddel-konjugater, 
der bruges i dag, har stadigvæk ulemper, heriblandt manglende specificitet og for tidlig frigivelse af 
lægemidlet, hvilket fører til giftige bivirkninger. 
I dette speciale beskrives to nye sulfamidase-kløvbare linkere, der er blevet udviklet. Linkernes 
sulfamatgruppe indeholder en del, der kan genkendes af sulfamidase-enzymer, som er overudtrykt i 
visse former for kræft, herunder brystkræft. Derved giver linkerne mulighed for specifik frigivelse 
af lægemidlet og derigennem afværges for en tidlig frigivelse af lægemidlet. 
Linkerne er designet til at frigive deres lægemiddel gennem en intramolekylær ringlukning, der 
sikrer hurtig og effektiv frigivelse af lægemidlet. Desuden muliggør linkernes design, at lægemidlet 
kan påsættes gennem en tilhørende hydroxygruppe. Dette er en fordel, da nuværende linkere kun 
tillader påsætning af lægemidlet gennem en tilhørende aminogruppe. 
En syntesestrategi er blevet undersøgt og reaktionsbetingelserne optimeret for at opnå Linker 1, og 
en syntese egnet til at opnå Linker 1 med et modellægemiddel påsat er blevet identificeret. For 
Linker 2 er en syntesestrategi, der følger veletableret kemi, blevet identificeret. Ved påsætning af et 
modellægemiddel vil linkerne kunne bruges i enzymforsøg for at undersøgning kløvning af 
modellægemidlet.

1 Introduction 
Cancer is a non-communicable disease classified as an abnormal growth of cells resulting in an 
assault on and destruction of surrounding healthy tissue. WHO reports nearly 10 million deaths 
caused by cancer in 2020 alone, making it a leading cause of death worldwide. Additionally, nearly 
20 million new cases of cancer were recorded in 2020 [1].  
Primarily, the objective in the treatment of cancer is to cure it or to prolong life and improve quality 
of life for the patient. The most common treatment methods are surgery, chemotherapy and 
radiotherapy [1]. Often a combination of two or more of these methods is needed to combat the 
cancer. Unfortunately, the cytotoxic drugs used in chemotherapy today are non-selective, killing 
healthy cells alongside cancerous ones, which results in serious side effects. This led to research 
aiming at treatments with higher selectivity, hereby avoiding unselective toxicity of chemotherapy 
drugs. Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have revolutionized the field of cancer treatment. An 
ADC consists of a highly selective antibody (1) attached to a drug that is extremely cytotoxic (3) by 
a linker (2) that tethers the two together; see Figure 1.1 below. 
 
Figure 1.1: An ADC consists of three parts: (1) a monoclonal antibody with high selectivity towards an antigen, (2) a linker that is 
stable in circulation and (3) a cytotoxic drug that is released upon internalization in the target cell. 
Both the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) have 
approved 10 ADCs; two more have been approved by the FDA alone, and five are now in late 
clinical trials, while there are more than 80 ADCs in active clinical trials as of writing [2], [3]. 
ADCs are of great interest, as they solve the non-specificity of toxic drugs by utilizing the high 
selectivity of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs).

1.1 Project aim 
The antibody in an ADC provides a certain degree of selectivity; however, the target antigen is also 
present in healthy, rapidly dividing cells. Therefore, an extra degree of selectivity is needed to limit 
off-target toxicity and improve the safety of the ADC. 
The aim of the project is to develop and synthesize a sulfamidase-cleavable linker that allows for (i) 
attachment of maleimide to allow conjugation onto a mAb, (ii) a site for attachment of a cytotoxic 
drug and (iii) a sulfamidase recognition site. Furthermore, the linker should be stable in circulation 
to avoid premature release and off-site toxicity. In addition, the linker should selectively and safely 
deliver a cytotoxic agent to the target cell and, lastly, should show efficient cleavage and release of 
the cytotoxic agent upon internalization.   
Two novel linkers were investigated. The linkers share common moieties: a sulfamate group 
(marked in blue in Figure 1.2 below), a secondary amine for drug attachment through a carbamate 
(marked in orange) and an azide handle (marked in green), for attachment of a maleimide used in 
later drug attachment. Sulfamidase will hydrolyze the sulfamate group, releasing a primary amine, 
which will undergo a spontaneous intramolecular cyclization and formation of a five-membered 
ring (see Figure 1.3).  
 
Figure 1.2: Two novel linkers: Linker 1 and Linker 2. 
In Figure 1.2 above, the linkers are shown without the drug or maleimide attached, as these were 
not introduced during this thesis. It is worth highlighting that these linkers allow for attachment of 
hydroxy-functionalized drugs, whereas current linkers only allow attachment of amine-
functionalized drugs. This will further broaden the scope of ADCs in cancer treatment. 
Furthermore, when a maleimide group is attached, it allows site-specific conjugation onto a 
monoclonal antibody via cysteine thiol groups. Once the linker has been internalized into the target 
cell, it is processed by sulfamidases that hydrolyze and cleave the S-N bond in the sulfamate group, 
causing an intramolecular ring closure, as illustrated in Figure 1.3 below.

1.2 Cancer 
Cancer cells grow more rapidly than normal cells and can spread to the rest of the body via the 
bloodstream or lymphatic system and cause further damage. The spreading of cancer to other parts 
of the body is known as metastasis, and it is the primary cause of death from cancer. Cancer occurs 
as a result of a wide variety of factors, and it is important to remember it is not one disease: rather, 
many different types of cancer exist. Nevertheless, certain hallmarks are used to distinguish and 
define the core characteristics of cancer as a disease [4]. 
Hormone-dependent cancers make up the majority of cancer occurrences around the world. Of these 
cases, the most common are prostate cancer and hormone-receptor-positive breast cancer. In breast 
cancer, one of the growth factor receptors that used for identification of the cancer is a human 
epidermal growth factor receptor (HER2) [5]. 
 
1.2.1 Breast cancer 
Breast cancer is cancerous cells growing in breast tissue. It is metastasis if the cancer spreads to the 
lymph nodes adjacent to the breast and distant metastasis if the cancer spreads to other organs of the 
body. If a person dies from breast cancer, it is due to distant metastasis [6]. Although breast cancer 
treatment and therapies are improving steadily, the global disease burden of female breast cancer is 
enormous. The incidence of female breast cancer is higher each year, a trend ascribed to a 
combination of population growth and increased awareness leading to more frequent diagnosis [7]. 
Of the nearly 20 million new cases of cancer recorded in 2020, 2.3 million of those cases were 
breast cancer. It is the most prevalent form of cancer in women, with 685 thousand deaths in 2020 
worldwide [6]. WHO estimates that “if an annual mortality reduction of 2.5% per year occurs

4 
 
worldwide, 2½ million breast cancer deaths would be avoided between 2020 and 2040.” [6]. 
Clearly, research aimed at improving treatment of breast cancer and cancer as a whole is vital. 
 
1.2.2 Treatment of cancer 
As mentioned above in section 1, the three main treatment options for cancer are surgery, 
chemotherapy and radiation. These are often used in combination with each other, one usually being 
adjunctive therapy to the main treatment. This is to ensure that the cancer is killed as effectively as 
possible, since it is estimated that 99% of cancer cells in a tumor must be killed for remission to 
occur [8]. 
Surgery is utilized to remove the cancerous tissue: it is the oldest form of cancer treatment. It 
remains the most common treatment for most cancers, but cancer surgeries are often highly 
challenging. For an optimal outcome, cancer surgery requires a team of specialists due to the 
complexity and heterogeneity of the disease. Furthermore, studies show that surgery, in conjunction 
with other cancer treatments, improves the outcome for the patient in both quality of life and 
mortality rates [9]. 
Radiotherapy is the use of external or internal radiation to kill or slow cancer cells by damaging 
their DNA. This has side effects, as it damages the nearby healthy cells as well, an intrinsic toxicity 
that often first manifests itself years after treatment [10].  
Treatment of breast cancer was primarily surgery and radiation throughout the 20th century, until 
chemotherapy for early breast cancer became established in the 1980s [11]. The rest of this section 
will focus on chemotherapy, as this is the main topic of this thesis. 
 
1.2.2.1 Chemotherapy 
Surgery and radiotherapy were the primary treatments for cancers, until researchers saw a halt in the 
rise in cure rates in the 1960s. These rates had reached 33% before they stagnated due to, among 
other factors, micrometastases. This led to the advancement of chemotherapy, i.e. the use of drugs 
against cancer. In the early 1970s, researchers DeVita, Moxley and Frei created combination 
chemotherapy programs to treat advanced Hodgkin’s disease. The complete remission rate for 
patients jumped from almost 0% to 80% in the 1980s, spurring on the field of chemotherapy 
through to today [12]. Chemotherapy is currently used in neoadjuvant, adjuvant and palliative 
==PAGE:21==
                   
5 
 
treatments, most often as part of a multimodal treatment plan that includes a combination of 
chemotherapy drugs. The types of drugs used vary depending on the type of cancer, the stage of 
metastasis and previous treatments (if any). They include DNA-targeting drugs, microtubule-
targeting drugs and topoisomerase inhibitors [1], [12]. 
 
1.2.2.2 Toxicity 
The goal of chemotherapy is to kill cancerous cells. Chemotherapy mainly targets and is aimed at 
disrupting various phases of the cell cycle, and because cancer cells are rapidly dividing cells, this 
disruption will greatly affect their development. Unfortunately, this also kills rapidly dividing 
normal cells such as cells in hair follicles, bone marrow, and the epithelial lining of the small 
intestines, giving rise to toxic side effects such as hair loss, myelosuppression and gastrointestinal 
issues. Other common side effects are neuropathy, fatigue and nausea [13].  
The non-specific toxicity of chemotherapy drugs is a major drawback, as it limits the drug dose that 
can be used to treat the patient. The therapeutic window is a term used to indicate the dosage range 
within which administration of a drug is safe, and it is described as the ratio between the maximum 
tolerated dose (MTD) and the minimum effective dose (MED). For chemotherapy, the therapeutic 
window is relatively narrow, which makes effective treatment more difficult. Researchers have 
attempted to circumvent this by using multimodal drug treatments, combining drugs with different 
modes of action, often with additive or synergistic effects [8]. 
 
Figure 1.4: ADCs show promise in widening the therapeutic window, making cancer treatment safer for the patient. Figured inspired 
by [8], [14].

Toxicity to the patient remains a major issue, however, so a strategy to broaden the therapeutic 
window is sorely needed. To do this, the MTD must be increased and the MED decreased, as 
illustrated in Figure 1.4 above. Increasing the MTD involves using a drug that is selective towards 
the target and does not affect healthy cells, while decreasing the MED involves increasing the 
potency of the drug, making it effective at a lower concentration [14].

2 Antibody-drug conjugates  
First-generation ADCs showed major limitations due to reduced efficacy for a number of reasons. 
The target-specific mAbs were of murine origin and therefore elicited an immune response in 
patients, culminating in a faster clearance of the ADC [15]. Furthermore, a limited knowledge of 
linkers and drug conjugation resulted in unstable linkers and a reduced potency of the conjugated 
drug compared with the parent drug [8]. Improvements were developed, including better methods to 
conjugate drugs without sacrificing efficacy and the development of humanized antibodies in order 
to lower – or eliminate – an immune response [16]. In the following sections the main components 
of ADCs are discussed, starting with the overall mechanism of action of an ADC. 
 
2.1 Mechanism of action  
To design an efficient and safe ADC, it is necessary to understand the mechanism through which 
ADCs kill cancer cells. The mechanism of action is illustrated in Figure 2.1 below. ADCs are 
introduced into the bloodstream intravenously in order to avoid degradation of the antibody and 
circulate in plasma. The antibody binds an antigen on the cancer cell surface, resulting in 
internalization via receptor-mediated endocytosis. The lysosomes in the cell fuse with the late 
endosomes containing the ADC. The linker is then cleaved, and the cytotoxic drug is released into 
the cytosol, resulting in cell death [17]. 
 
Figure 2.1: Mechanism of action for ADCs. 
==PAGE:24==
                   
8 
 
In essence, the antibody is the vehicle that transports the drug, while the linker is essential in 
ensuring that the drug remains bound to the antibody while circulating in the bloodstream. It is 
crucial that the drug is not released until it has been internalized to avoid toxicity. Additionally, it is 
important that the linker is effectively cleaved upon uptake in the target cell. The mechanism of 
release for the cytotoxic drug varies depending on the type of linker used, and the resulting cell 
death occurs from varying mechanisms that depend on the type of drug used. These factors are 
critical for the clinical success of the resulting ADC [18]. 
 
2.2 Monoclonal antibodies 
Monoclonal antibodies are the part of ADCs responsible for selectivity towards cancer cells. They 
are large glycosylated proteins that are comprised of two heavy chains and two light chains. 
Disulfide bonds connect the chains together, and a hinge region connects the heavy chains; see 
Figure 2.2 below. They have two specific regions called the variable domain and the constant 
domain. The constant domain determines what type of antibody it is. Antibodies are divided into 
five major classes: IgM, IgG, IgA, IgD and IgE. Immunoglobulin G (IgG) is the most common 
class and is subdivided into IgG1, IgG2, IgG3 and IgG4 [19]. In ADCs, IgG1 and IgG4 are the most 
used, with IgG1 being the most abundant [20]. 
 
Figure 2.2: General structure of a monoclonal antibody. 
==PAGE:25==
                   
9 
 
The variable domain is the region that recognizes specific antigens and binds to them, resulting in 
internalization. The variable domains must be specific and have high affinity for the relevant 
antigen. Today mAbs are produced in a fermenter using a mammalian cell line, usually Chinese 
Hamster Ovary cells, although other cell lines are also used [21].  
The antibody used should produce little or no immunogenicity from the host, which is why fully 
human or humanized antibodies should be used, although chimeric antibodies are still used in some 
ADCs on the market: e.g. brentuximab vedotin (Adcetris®) uses a chimeric mAb [22]. 
 
2.2.1 Selection of antigen 
For an antibody to be useful in the treatment of cancer, a target antigen must be expressed in high 
amount on the tumor cells, and there should be little or no expression in healthy tissue. It is 
estimated that there must be at least ten thousand antigens per cell to ensure selective delivery of the 
cytotoxic payload [8], [17]. Another important aspect of choosing the appropriate antigen is that the 
antigen should be expressed on the cell surface and available to circulating ADCs. For example, the 
ADC Adcetris® targets the CD30 antigen, which is overexpressed on the surface of myeloid cells in 
patients with acute myeloid leukemia [20]. 
Ensuring proper internalization of the ADC into the cancer cell is vital. However, it has been found 
that internalization may not always be necessary for therapeutic effect [23]. The ADCs trastuzumab 
deruxtecan (Enhertu®) and trastuzumab emtansine (Kadcyla®) target the HER2 positive receptor 
that is expressed in certain aggressive forms of breast and colon cancer cells, and it is expressed 100 
times higher in these cells than it is in healthy cells [24], [25]. Other common targets for ADCs 
include CD33, used as a biomarker for the identification of acute myeloid leukemia, and CD22, 
expressed in several B-cell malignancies in different types of leukemia. They are targeted by the 
clinically approved ADCs gemtuzumab ozogamicin (Mylotarg®) and moxetumomab pasudotox 
(Lumoxiti ®) respectively [26], [27]. 
 
2.2.3 Conjugation to linker 
Conjugation of the linker onto the mAb is an important aspect of assembling the ADC. It is 
normally done through either lysine or cysteine residues located on the mAb. The amount of linker 
attached to the mAb is referred to as the drug-antibody ratio (DAR), which has a range of 0–8. 
==PAGE:26==
                   
10 
 
Heterogeneous ADCs can be a problem: heterogenicity can lessen the therapeutic effect, due to the 
competitive binding between unconjugated mAbs and drug-conjugated mAbs. Furthermore, when 
mAbs have a high level of drugs conjugated to them, it can cause aggregation of the ADCs, 
resulting in faster clearance and non-specific toxicity [20]. Research indicates that an optimal DAR 
for most ADCs is about four, which seems to be the best balance between the necessary cytotoxicity 
and good retention [28]. 
 
2.2.3.1 Conjugation via lysine 
An IgG antibody contains about 100 lysine residues, with a potential of 40 that can be modified for 
conjugation. This results in a DAR of zero to eight per antibody, which means that over one million 
different conjugated ADCs can be generated. The lysine contains a side chain with a primary amine 
group that allows for attachment of the linker via an N–hydroxylsuccinimidyl (NHS) ester reagent, as 
shown in Figure 2.3A below. The ADCs Kadcyla® and Mylotarg® use lysine conjugation [18], [25]. 
 
2.2.3.2 Conjugation via cysteine 
Cysteine makes the disulfide bridges in antibodies (see Figure 2.2 above), and an IgG antibody has 
12 intrachain disulfide bridges. Furthermore, IgG1 and IgG4 have four interchain disulfide bridges 
that can be selectively reduced to provide eight free thiol groups. These disulfide bonds do not 
affect the function of the antibody. The thiol groups are available for conjugation, creating a 
thiosuccinimide link through reaction of the thiol groups with a maleimide moiety; see Figure 2.3B 
below. Taking isomers into account, cysteine conjugation generates an assortment of over a 
hundred different drug-loaded ADCs. This still generates a heterogeneous mixture, but is 
significantly less heterogeneous than when using lysine residues for conjugation [28].

2.3 Cytotoxic payload 
The drugs that the ADCs carry are referred to as the warhead or payload. A number of requirements 
must be met when selecting the drug to be used. One requirement is that the payload have an in 
vitro subnanomolar half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) so that it is effective against 
cancer cells even at low doses, which decreases the systemic toxicity [8]. The drug should also have 
sufficient solubility and a long half-life, as well as possess a functional group that can be either 
directly used or modified, allowing it to be conjugated onto the linker. If the drug has to be altered, 
any changes made should not result in a significant loss of potency. Moreover, the conjugated drug 
should not affect the stability, solubility or internalization properties of the ADC [20]. Several types 
of cytotoxic drugs are used in ADCs, including tubulin-acting and DNA-targeting agents.  
 
==PAGE:28==
                   
12 
 
2.3.1 Tubulin inhibitors 
Tubulin inhibitors cause cell death (apoptosis) by inducing cell cycle arrest at the G2/M phase. They 
do this by binding to and either stabilizing or destabilizing microtubulins. Different classes of 
tubulin-acting drugs have different binding sites, but they are generally divided into two categories: 
Stabilizing and destabilizing. Stabilizing agents, e.g. taxanes, prevent the microtubulins from 
depolymerizing at cell division (mitosis), thereby causing apoptosis. Destabilizing agents bind to 
microtubules and prevent their polymerization, inhibiting mitosis and thus causing apoptosis [30]. 
The most common destabilizing agents used in ADCs are auristatins and maytansinoids; only 
auristatins will be discussed in the next section.  
 
2.3.1.1 Auristatins 
Auristatins are derived from dolastatin 10, which has been investigated for anti-cancer properties 
and showed in vitro toxicity at picomolar concentrations; however, development was discontinued 
due to its non-specific toxicities as a free drug [8].  
 
Figure 2.4: Structure of dolastatin 10 and its derivatives MMAE and MMAF. 
Monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) and monomethyl auristatin F (MMAF) are both used in ADCs. 
Their structures are based upon SAR studies of dolastatin 10 [8]. They are conjugated to the linker 
by converting the tertiary amine in dolastatin 10 (marked in blue above) into a secondary amine. 
MMAF is 100 times less potent than MMAE. However, a methyl ester derivative of MMAF 
(MMAF–OMe), is 100 times more toxic than MMAE. This toxicity of MMAF–OMe is attributed to 
its ability to diffuse into cells, and the charged carboxy group on the unaltered MMAF makes this 
==PAGE:29==
                   
13 
 
more difficult [8], [31]. Auristatins are currently used in four ADCs on the market: e.g. MMAE is 
used in Adcetris® and MMAF is used in belantamab mafodotin (Blenrep®) [2].  
 
2.3.2 DNA-targeting drugs 
DNA-damaging drugs are typically more potent than microtubule inhibitors, as they have IC50 
values in the picomolar range. Microtubule inhibitors, however, have sub-nanomolar IC50 values. 
Furthermore, DNA-damaging drugs can target tumor antigens that are not associated with rapid cell 
division [32]. Calicheamicin is a potent antibiotic antitumor agent that targets the minor groove 
sequence in DNA and causes strand cleavage, leading to cell death [15]. Currently, the ADCs 
Mylotarg® and inotuzumab ozogamicin (Besponsa®) have calicheamicin derivatives as their 
payload [26]. Another DNA-targeting drug class that is being researched for use in ADCs is 
pyrrolobenzodiazepines, which are alkylating agents [33],[20], [34].

Zynlonta® [35]. 
 
2.4 Linker technologies 
The linker technology utilized in ADCs is essential in determining the safety and efficacy of the 
ADC. The linker must connect the mAb and the drug without significantly altering either. The 
linker should remain stable in circulation and keep the conjugated drug in an inactive state. 
Moreover, upon internalization, the linker should efficiently release the payload as a free drug with 
sufficient therapeutic effect [36], [37].  
==PAGE:30==
                   
14 
 
Linkers are typically divided into classes depending on their various release mechanisms, although 
they are generally classified as either cleavable or non-cleavable. The next sections will consider 
non-cleavable linkers briefly and thereafter discuss cleavable linkers in depth. 
 
2.4.1 Non-cleavable linkers 
Non-cleavable linkers are not cleaved via environmental factors: rather, they depend on lysosomal 
degradation of the mAb for drug release. The ADC is degraded, and the drug is released as a 
metabolite with an amino acid residue attached. The amino acid residue is dependent on the 
conjugation method used when attaching the linker to the mAb. The drug metabolite should 
therefore maintain sufficient cytotoxicity towards the cell [37]. Nevertheless, this also means that 
non-cleavable linkers are more stable in circulation than cleavable linkers, as the linker is resistant 
to hydrolysis by proteases and does not rely on environmental differences between systemic 
circulation and the intracellular environment [20].  
One example of a drug that is unsuitable for use in non-cleavable linkers is MMAE, because it loses 
its efficacy as a metabolite after degradation of the ADC. MMAF and maytansinoids, however, are 
suited for use in non-cleavable linkers [8], [15]. Blenrep® makes use of MMAF as its payload, 
linked through a non-cleavable maleimidocaproyl linker [2]. Additionally, Kadcyla® uses a non-
cleavable maleimidomethyl cyclohexane-1-carboxylate (MCC) linker with a maytansine derivative 
called DM1 [25]. When Kadcyla® is degraded in the lysosomes, it turns into a metabolite consisting 
of the linker with a lysine residue attached, as illustrated in Figure 2.6 below.

2.4.2 Cleavable linkers 
Cleavable linkers rely upon differences between the environment of systemic circulation and the 
environment inside the cancer cell. Typically, cleavable linkers are divided into three categories: 
acid-labile, reducible and enzymatically cleavable.  
 
2.4.2.1 Acid-labile linkers 
Acid-labile linkers contain a hydrazone moiety that is hydrolyzed in the acidic environment of the 
lysosome/endosome (pH below 6), while being stable in the slightly alkaline environment of 
systemic circulation (pH of about 7.4) [37]. The ADC Mylotarg® contains an acid-cleavable linker, 
although its release mechanism is a two-step process. Initially, the hydrazone group is hydrolyzed, 
releasing the drug with a disulfide spacer still attached; see Figure 2.7 below. The calicheamicin 
derivative binds to the minor groove of DNA, and the disulfide bond is then cleaved when reduced 
by gluthathione, allowing the calicheamicin to break the DNA strand and kill the cell [36]. The 
disulfide reduction reaction is explained in the next section.  
 
Figure 2.7: Hydrolysis of a hydrazone group (blue) cleaving the linker into two derivatives. Cal=calicheamycin. 
Acid-labile linkers have been associated with non-specific release in circulation, and this limits the 
effective dose that can be administered. Mylotarg® was withdrawn from the market in 2010 after a 
clinical trial showed that no benefit from using the ADC in combination chemotherapy. After 
several clinical trials, Mylotarg® was reintroduced in 2017, with an updated dose regimen and a 
lower dosage, among other things [38]. 
 
2.4.2.2 Reducible disulfide linkers 
Disulfide linkers are sensitive to the differences in reduction potential in the cytoplasm and plasma. 
The linker has a disulfide bond that is sensitive to gluthathione, which is highly expressed in cancer 
cells, since gluthathione is involved in controlling cell survival, tumor growth and cell stress [39]. 
The gluthathione in the cells reduces and cleaves the disulfide bond to thiol groups, as illustrated in 
==PAGE:32==
                   
16 
 
Figure 2.8 below [40]. Methyl groups are commonly attached next to the disulfide linkage in order 
to increase stability [41]. 
 
Figure 2.8: Reduction of a disulfide bond in a Mylotarg® derivative by gluthathione, resulting in reduction and thereafter 
protonation to give the free drug as a thiol. Cal=calicheamycin. Figure drawn with inspiration from [40]. 
Maytansinoids can be used in combination with disulfide linkers, as the free drug contains a thiol 
group. The resulting maytansinoid-containing conjugates were effectively cleaved and exhibited 
excellent cytotoxicity [8]. 
 
2.4.2.3 Enzymatically cleavable linkers 
Enzymatically cleavable linkers are peptide-based linkers designed to be cleaved by specific 
enzymes that reside in the lysosomes of the target cell. The most common dipeptide linker is the 
valine-citrulline (V-C) linker, but also valine-alanine and phenylalanine-lysine dipeptide linkers 
have been researched in ADC design [15]. 
 
2.4.2.3.1 Protease-sensitive linkers 
Cancer cells have a high expression of lysosomal proteases, including the protease cathepsin B [42]. 
Different peptide sequences have been investigated for cleavage by cathepsin B and especially the 
V-C dipeptide showed good stability in plasma while effective cleavage in the cell. The ADC 
Adcetris® contains the V-C dipeptide linkage with a self-immolative para-aminobenzylcarbamate 
(PABC) spacer, which upon cleavage undergoes 1,6-elimination and decarboxylation; see Figure 
2.9 below. The PABC moeity ensures that the peptide sequence is effectively recognized by 
cathepsin B, without being sterically hindered by a bulky drug [36], [41]. 
==PAGE:33==
                   
17 
 
 
Figure 2.9: Mode of action for the linker Adcetris®. Figure adapted from [36]. 
The majority of ADCs on the market possess a dipeptide linker [2], [15]. However, dipeptide 
linkers are unstable in mouse models due to cleavage by the Ces1C hydrolase enzyme, which could 
lead to reduced accuracy of preclinical studies [43], [44]. Furthermore, they are unsuited for use 
with hydrophobic payloads, as the linkers are hydrophobic themselves. For these reasons, there is a 
great deal of interest in developing novel linkers that circumvent these challenges. In this project, 
sulfamidase-cleavable linkers were investigated. 
 
2.4.2.3.2 Sulfatase-cleavable linkers 
Sulfatase enzymes are overexpressed in breast cancer [45] and and reside in the lysosome of the 
cells, where they catalyze the hydrolysis of sulfate esters to alcohols [46], [47]. 
Bargh and colleagues showed that arylsulfate linkers had an efficient release of their MMAE 
payload by being selectively hydrolyzed by sulfatase enzymes, as illustrated in Figure 2.10 below. 
Furthermore, the linkers showed high stability in mouse and human plasma over several days, also 
when in the presence of glutathione, and they showed selectivity and toxicity against HER2-positive 
cells when conjugated to the mAb trastuzumab [44].  
 
Figure 2.10: Arylsulfatase linker in ADC 2 of Bargh and coworkers work. The linker is cleaved by sulfatases and undergoes 
spontaneous elimination and decarboxylation. Figure adapted from Bargh and coworkers [44].

2.4.2.3.3 Sulfamidase-cleavable linkers 
Sulfamidases are a subtype of sulfatase enzymes, which are encoded by the SGSH gene. Their 
natural substrate is heparin sulfate glycosaminoglycans. Furthermore, sulfamidase esters are stable 
in plasma and hydrophilic, which makes them suitable for conjugation with hydrophobic drugs [47], 
[48]. Therefore, sulfamidase-cleavable linkers could be of high interest when designing new and 
selective enzyme cleavable linkers. As discussed in section 1.1, the aim of this project was to 
develop two sulfamidase-cleavable linkers.

3.2.2.1 Reductive amination with building block IA and halogenated benzyl aldehydes 
Reductive amination is a reliable method of synthesizing secondary amines. A reductive amination 
goes through an imine intermediate, which is then reduced to the corresponding secondary amine. 
Imines are typically unstable and are easily hydrolyzed, which would reform the starting aldehyde 
and primary amine. To circumvent this, a drying agent is added to the reaction mixture. This will 
trap the water molecule that is produced when the imine is formed, and by removing it, the

equilibrium is shifted towards formation of the imine. The reaction proceeds with a nucleophilic 
attack from the amine on the carbonyl aldehyde to form the hemiaminal intermediate, whereafter 
dehydration occurs to form the imine. Sodium borohydride (NaBH4) reduces the C=N double bond 
to form the secondary amine. The proposed mechanism for the reductive amination can be seen in 
Scheme 3.8 below [51]. 
 
Scheme 3.8: Suggested mechanism for the reductive amination. 
The reductive amination with 4-bromobenzaldehyde and compound IA_1  was performed through a 
modified procedure from the literature [52], involving a three-step reaction; see Scheme 3.9 below. 
In the first step, the primary amine and aldehyde are combined under a nitrogen (N2) atmosphere 
with dry methanol (MeOH) as the solvent and magnesium sulfate (MgSO4) as the drying agent, 
since Mg2+ can complex water molecules and thereby prevent hydrolysis of the imine. The mono-

nucleophilic attack. The lone pair on the hydroxyl oxygen atom attacks the electrophilic sulfur 
atom, and the DMAP regains aromaticity and is dispelled, ready to react again [51]. The positive 
tosylate ion is then deprotonated to afford compound II; as illustrated in Scheme 3.36 below.

4 Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate and develop novel sulfamidase-cleavable linkers. The 
linkers were designed to (i) allow attachment of a maleimide moiety for conjugation onto a mAb, 
(ii) be a site for attachment of a cytotoxic drug and (iii) have a sulfamidase recognition site. The site 
for drug attachment was designed to allow conjugation of hydroxy-functionalized drugs, whereas 
current linkers only allow for amine-functionalized drug attachment. The sulfamidase recognition 
site was installed to allow efficient release of the drug by sulfamidases once the ADC was 
internalized into a cancer cell. Two novel linkers were investigated. 
A synthetic pathway for Linker 1 was established, and the synthetic strategy to afford the desired 
linker was optimized, except for the drug conjugation. However, a successful strategy was 
identified, although the conditions have yet to be optimized. The bottleneck of the experimental 
work on Linker 1 was installment of the azide group. The first strategy to obtain Key Intermediate I 
employed halogen-functionalized benzenes to allow installation of the azide group via a copper-
catalyzed nucleophilic aromatic substitution. This strategy was employed on both a bromo-
functionalized benzene (compound 4a) and 4-iodobenzaldehyde (compound 3a). However, neither 
strategy provided Key Intermediate I. The second strategy involved a diazotization-azidation of 4-
aminobenzalcohol and subsequent oxidation to afford 4-azidobenzaldehyde (compound 3b) to be 
used in a reductive amination, providing Key Intermediate I in an overall yield of 20%. The 
intermediate was then Fmoc-deprotected and sulfated, after which drug attachment was achieved 
through reaction with a model drug that had been activated with p-nitrophenol to ensure carbamate 
formation. The reaction conditions for conjugation of the model drug must be optimized in future 
research.  
For Linker 2, a synthetic strategy was established that followed well established and optimized 
chemistry. Thus, this thesis will provide value for future development of sulfamidase-cleavable 
linkers. 
 
4.1 Future perspectives 
This thesis is part of a larger project in which the development of novel sulfamidase-cleavable 
linkers and linker technologies is ongoing. This research is of paramount importance for continuous 
improvements in the fields of cancer treatment and ADCs. 
==PAGE:62==
                   
46 
 
If the cleavage of the attached model drug shows promise, the maleimide handle could be 
introduced and conjugation upon a mAb could be performed. Thus, the resulting antibody-drug 
conjugate could in future be used to study stability and cleavage in in vitro models. If these studies 
prove promising, ADCs containing the linkers could be used in clinical trials.
