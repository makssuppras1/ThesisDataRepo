Abstract
Building integrated photovoltaics came into spotlight as both building requirements man­
date and financial initiative supports new constructions be become nearly zero­emission
buildings. In this study, energy generation simulating software, SAM, was analysed in
order to determine how accurate and reliable is this tool for BIPV energy yield and tem­
perature modelling. To determine accuracy of simulated data by SAM, BIPV test setup
was placed in Riso campus at Technical University of Denmark. For comparison analysis
weather data was taken from weather station at Riso campus as well as pyranometer’s
data of GPOA measurements was collected at BIPV test setup.
This study aimed to investigate BIPV module performance and temperature as well as
optimizers and inverters power input data and validate simulations done by SAM model.
In this work only south facade of BIPV was investigated. Analysis results concludes that
SAM is able to accurately model optimizers power under stable conditions, error rate is not
higher than the conventional models being under 5%. However SAM did not provide that
kind of accuracy modelling under rapid weather changes nor optimizers/inverter neither
module temperature.
Study finds possible error sources such as optimizers’ losses those were greater than
stated in manufacturer data­sheet, time averaging mismatches and delay in signal com­
munication between inverter and optimizer.

Introduction
On May 2022 European Commission presented an updated REPowerEU plan which is a
part of European Green deal aimed to save energy, diversify and secure Europe’s energy
supply and increase renewable energy deployment. [1] This plan includes the requirement
for all buildings to install solar generation capacity and to support BIPV. The European
union decided that counties can not solely rely on energy import from outside the EU,
therefore renewable energy seems to be one of the few possible solutions.
Solar energy plays a huge role both in Short term action measures (”Rapid roll out of solar
and wind energy projects combined with renewable hydrogen deployment to save around
50 billion cubic meters of gas imports”) and also in long term measures (”Increase the
European renewable target for 2030 from 40% to 45%”), additionally installed capacity of
renewable energy sources target was updated in order to reach the level required by Paris
1.5C agreement.[2]. Development of more efficient renewable technology, studies into
existing technologies to upgrade them is required in order to achieve this target. Because
of this reason this study chose to analyse BIPV technology which is expected to show
rapid growth in upcoming years.
1.1
Solar energy outlook
By the year 2020 there were 357 000 full time jobs in EU solar sector and this figure
is expected to at least double by 2030. 80% of those are working in solar installation,
while the rest works in operation and maintenance accounts and for manufacturing and
recycling of photovoltaics. However with the intention to boost RES to 45% by the year of
2030, full times employees in solar sector might reach 1 100 000 [3]. Increase in workforce
shows that there is demand for new investments and as more people is involved into this
sector it raises awareness of different possibilities of solar technology as both consumers
and investors are looking for new edge.
Globally, solar energy generation capacity which was installed last year, accounts for more
than a half of all RES new capacity that year, surpassing wind as seen in Fig. 1.1. One
of the reason which could explain rapid expansion of solar energy is the low (36 $/MWh)
generation cost compared to other sources, wind ­ 38 $/MWh, gas ­ 60 $/MWh, coal ­ 108
$/MWh, nuclear ­ 167 $/MWh. Future studies in PV technology might even decrease this
generation cost which further make a solar most wanted technology in electricity genera­
tion, especially in Europe, which has little to none fossil fuel.

1.2
BIPV
Building Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV) are solar power generating systems that are in­
tegrated into building and acts as a component of the building itself such as windows,
rooftops, wall’s facades. BIPV serves a dual purpose, first one is to provide building
functions such as: noise or thermal insulation, safety, weather protection or daylight illu­
mination and second one converting solar energy to electricity.
Greatest advantage of BIPV over common non­integrated solar systems is that the initial
cost can be reduced by amount of money and time spent on building materials and labor
that would typically be required to construct the part of the building which BIPV system
replaces for example while constructing a new house, instead of building a roof out of
regular tiles and later install solar PV system on top of it, you can replace your tiles with
PV modules.
There is also few positive outcomes on macro scale such as investment in local BIPV sys­
tem supports local businesses and construction companies. Moreover micro­grid system
increases energy grid safety and reliability [6]. Additionally when using different billing
mechanism BIPV owner can lower their electricity bill or even make profit.
BIPV modules which are currently on the market are based on crystalline silicon (c­Si) [7]
solar cells or thin film such as cadmium telluride (CdTe), Copper Indium Galium Selenide
(CIGS) [8] and amorphous silicon (a­Si). BIPV modules are commonly used to replace fa­
cades or roofs because it can be light transparent. Its still an evolving technology, but has
enormous potential as with better visual performance business might invest into BIPV
constructing new business centers/shops. Nonetheless, efficiency of solar module de­

1.4
Goal and steps of this study
BIPV performance depends on various factors such as installation geographical location,
air­gap configuration between solar module and building insulation, ventilation of BIPV
modules.
These weather factors and installation properties have lower impact while modeling typical
PV system, therefore with this difference in mind, this study raises a hypothesize whether
conventional modeling software are capable of accurately predict energy generated by
BIPV and additionally various parameters of this system.
The BIPV analysis study was done using DTU testing setup in Riso campus and modeling
that specific setup in SAM application. To achieve main goal, project was divided into
following steps:
1 step Raw data from DTU BIPV testing setup at Riso, was analyzed and filtered
out.
2 step Filtered data from DTU testing setup and weather station was applied
into SAM software.
3 step Modelled data from SAM simulation software was used to compare to
DTU setup.
4 step error factors and miss­matches were calculated to provide final results.

2.1
Variables affecting BIPV performance
While popularity of BIPV systems increases, researchers studies various parameters try­
ing to determine how these effect performance on BIPV systems. Power generated by
BIPV system depends on different weather factors such as global irradiance in plane of
array (GPOA), wind speed, air flow temperature under BIPV modules and ambient tem­
perature.
In particular, the work in Ref. [13] tested several temperature models and found that there
is positive correlation of power and GPOA input and negative correlation between power
and ambient temperature. The results of this study is shown in Fig. 2.1. BIPV module
temperature increases with higher ambient temperature as well as with increasing GPOA.
This study shows that external heat transfer coefficient has high negative Spearman’s
correlation with BIPV module temperature and high positive correlation with BIPV power.
Figure 2.1: Power and temperature dependency on GPOA, inlet and ambient tempera­
ture. Source: [13]
The air flow of ambient temperature is being deployed to ventilate and cool­down BIPV
modules having rack mounting configuration with air­gap. Therefore correctly determining
air temperature which ventilates BIPV modules is crucial in order to have low error mar­
gin cell temperature data. Study [14] shows that air which is flowing next to BIPV setup is
heated by surrounding structures, therefore temperature of air from outside which gets be­
tween the building facade and the BIPV module can be higher than ambient temperature
for both mechanically and naturally ventilated facades.
This study suggested that air flow, which ventilates BIPV module, temperature almost
never equals the interior air temperature both for mechanically and naturally ventilated
facade variants. The temperature of air flow is determined by the transmission losses and
increase by surrounding surfaces. air flow temperature of naturally ventilated facade are
somewhat higher which indicates that the air heats up passing through mounting structure

2.2
Temperature modelling studies
Operational temperature of any PV/BIPV module is determined by an energy balance.
The solar energy reached the solar module and was not reflected is absorbed and then
part of this energy is converted to useful electrical energy (up to 25% for industrial PV
modules) and the rest is converted into thermal energy. In order to avoid overheating of
module, thermal energy has to be dissipated to environment by a combination of heat
transfer processes.
Various models has been proposed for solar cell temperature prediction. The research
and development of temperature models in solar energy field has been studied for years
because of its relevance on both regular PV and more importantly BIPV performance
analysis.
Two types are temperature models are most commonly used in PV performance predic­
tion ­ dynamic and steady state. Dynamic model includes solar module thermal response,
while steady states model assumes instant changes in cell temperature when wind, am­
bient air temperature or other weather factors changes.
Although dynamic model is more precise and provide more accurate predictions, steady
state models are still being used widely because of their simplicity and being easier to
adapt to existing solar energy performance models. Transient temperature models are
required to simulate the multi­mechanism heat transfer integrated with chemical reactions
[15]. Most widely used temperature models are summed up and shown in Fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Solar cell and module temperature models: [16]
These models shown in Fig. 2.2 was analysed in work [16] and compared to each other
by modelling BIPV module temperature. Additionally author of the study proposes a com­
bined model derived from few models mentioned above. Comparison of these models
were presented in this study and be seen in Fig. 2.3. It can be seen that during clear sky
day King’s (Sandia) model tends to overestimate cell temperature, while Mani and Faiman
models tend to underestimate cell temperature compared to the values measured in BIPV
field.

Energy prediction
Various weather parameters and material properties are required for modelling BIPV sys­
tem’s energy prediction such as GPOA, heat transfer coefficient, wind speed and direction
et cetera. BIPV is still considered a niche area as most of the studies on solar energy is
done on conventional PV systems. There is only few studies has been conducted which
tries to compare and determine accuracy of existing energy prediction models for BIPV
applications as further studies still required for additional input sensitivity analysis.
2.3.1
Energy prediction studies using traditional models
A set of models for BIPV energy prediction was analysed in particular study [17]. Mathe­
matical and experimental research was done in comparison of traditional models against
NREL SAM model based on Duffie and Beckman proposed formulas. This study con­
cluded that for poli­si BIPV setup annual energy prediction relative error rate ranges from
1% (Skoplaki) to 5% (King et.) while used in SAM software Duffie and Beckman model
showed around 4% annual error.
Particular energy prediction inaccuracy effect is suggested to be because of tendency that
Duffie and Beckman model predicts lower BIPV cell temperature, while Skoplaki model
over­predicted cell temperature. This finding may suggest that temperature inputs are
more important for BIPV applications than it is right now for standard PV models which
are not that sensitive to ambient or cell temperature.

2.3.2
Energy prediction studies using non­traditional models
An alternative approach rather than using conventional mathematical models for tem­
perature prediction is presented in work [18]. Study authors created a model that uses
machine learning algorithms and PV power generation neural network to simulate BIPV
system performance on different sides of the building ­ south, east, west facade and flat
roof. Their model resulted with energy generation forecast with RMSE of 4.42% in NN
(artificial neuron network), 16.86% in QSVM (quadratic support vector machine) and 8.76
% in TREE(decision tree) algorithms. These models results accuracy is promising but the
distribution varies greatly and still performs worse than conventional prediction models.
Similar approach was taken in another study [19] where naturally ventilated BIPV mod­
ule has been studied. Authors developed a multi­physics BIPV model for building facade
applications. This model combines a high­resolution electrical model with thermal and air­
flow models. Created model has following characteristics: modelling external heat trans­
fer coefficient and airflow through the cavity, simulating shading effect, electrical model
and thermal coupling of BIPV module with building indoor environment.
This multi­physics BIPV model provided a relative error estimations of monthly energy
yields between 0.41% in July to 1.2% in August. However for the power model PSTC, the
monthly error rate exceeds 5% for all months which is due to uncertainly in the determi­
nation of the module nominal power. Daily energy yield estimations suggested average
daily relative error from 1.79% in June to 2.56% in August.
It can be seen that energy prediction using machine learning and similar tools provides
results which are close to ones given by traditional energy prediction models such as
Sandia’s, Fiaman’s et cetera. Therefore traditional models could be still used as a point
of reference for SAM validation study.
2.3.3
Partial shading effect on BIPV performance
Partial shading caused by double skin facade overhang has a significant impact on BIPV
yearly energy yield. In particular, in [20] study degradation of BIPV modules was analysed
using on site measured data in addition with their model used to predict BIPV energy
performance. For model creation they used IEC 61724­1:2017 standard [21].
Study found that annual power generation of the BIPV system decreased from 557 kWh/yr
to 521 kWh/yr because of partial shading, which results in 36 kWh/yr (6.5%) output of
electricity lost. Measured annual energy loss varied from 5% to 9%.
Degradation of solar modules caused by partial shading had no correlation on irradiance
level. Degradation of solar modules has to be taken into account while comparing data
from BIPV setup, for example Riso campus BIPV setup to modelled prediction data, be­
cause as setup gets older, because of degradation losses error between two sets of data
might occur. Simulation model used in SAM does not account for module degradation on
individual system parameters, such as voltage, current, temperature, but only on inverter’s
power output.
2.3.4
Wind speed influence on BIPV performance
Obtaining accurate wind speed data might be difficult because of possible geographical
uncertainty. Method of using wind speed measured on the roof might not be suitable for
wall applications because of difficulty to predict movement of air between construction
objects. Sensitivity analysis done by Ref. [17] shows that there is significant energy
prediction difference using incorrect wind speed data.

2.3.5
Air­gap effect on BIPV performance
As mentioned in the previous section, efficiency of BIPV system is proportional to cell tem­
perature and air­gap acts as a tool to ventilate solar cell and by reducing its temperature
increase energy generation efficiency.
Study [22] analysed different BIPV air­gap configurations and concluded that air­gap size
has influence not only on variation in PV temperature but also in temperature variation
rate. Critical air­gap was determined which is required to avoid over­heating of solar
cells. Their studied single module showed 2.7 ◦C (from 69.2 ◦C to 71.9 ◦C) increase in
mean cell temperature when air­gap was reduced from 200 mm to 100 mm and maximum
cell temperature raised by 6.3 ◦C (from 79.2 ◦C to 85.5 ◦C).
Study concluded that 120 mm is a critical air­gap required for 3 row module configuration
and 150 mm is required for 2 module row, additionally for a single module setup, air­gap
has to be between 140mm and 160mm.
BIPV study in Riso uses the setup of 0 mm to 200 mm air­gap configuration, which means
that only few of BIPV modules have required air­gap according to these studies.

2.4
Main takeaways
Short summary of literature review that is worth pointing out:
1. There are a lot of different models for cell temperature prediction created,
but most of them seems to be not perfect for BIPV applications, because cell
temperature is significantly higher, also air flow temperature which suppose to
ventilate BIPV system has a higher temperature then ambient temperature.
2. BIPV model has higher sensitivity on weather factors such as wind speed ,
ambient temperature than traditional PV models. Moreover studies shown that
for each air­gap different air­change per hour is required for optimal performance.
3. all studied models tend to over­estimate annual energy yield by BIPV system,
that might be explained by under­estimating cell­temperature.

5.1.1
BIPV module temperature adjustment
In order to compare SAM modelled cell temperature to temperature measured at Riso
BIPV test setup adjustment had to be made. Because of the reason, that the sensors
are placed on the back of the BIPV modules, we can only access to module temperature
data from Riso setup, while SAM prediction model simulates cell temperature and not the
module temperature that is required.
Simulated cell temperature was calculated Using SAM modelled module temperature data
and applying Eq. 3.5 from Sandia temperature model section. Because of the reason
that in this study wind speed was averaged, this averaged value was used calculating
∆T which ranges from 2.9 (◦C) to 3.7 (◦C) at 0 m/s and 1 m/s wind speed respectively,
therefore by using measured wind speed data, ∆T was linearly extrapolated.
Figure 5.1: Module temperature adjustment from cell temperature

side on test setup and has air­gap of 200 mm. This representation is equivalent for all
remaining BIPV modules. Calculated module temperature is equal or lower than the cell
temperature because of the ∆T which assumes that because of heat losses through the
cell, operational cell temperature is always higher or equal to module temperature and as
GPOA increases, difference in cell and module temperature increases accordingly.

Conclusion
6.1
Main conclusions
The main objective of this study was to analyse SAM software modelling accuracy while
simulating BIPV system with various air­gaps and ventilation setups. In particular mod­
ule temperature, inverter and optimizer input power was analysed. Difference in real life
scenario measurements at Riso BIPV setup and modelled by SAM is expressed in RMSE
and MBE.
It was found that wind speed fluctuations had major impact to model accuracy because
of the reason that SAM uses steady state model as re­evaluates module temperature
without taking into account that it takes time for solar module to heat up or cool down,
therefore wind speed data had to averaged.
The results from temperature modelling shows that SAM tend to over­estimated module
temperature when air­gap ranged from 0 mm to 30 mm and under­estimated while air­
gap was from 50 mm to 200 mm, while RMSE was around 8◦C during both clear sky and
cloudy day period.
Inverter simulation analysis found a that optimizer efficiency which is stated in manufac­
turer’s data­sheet might be not representative of real operating conditions. Since SAM
uses weighted optimizer efficiency, this value was determined using measured data in
Riso comparing optimizers input and output power. It was calculated that optimizer’s
losses vary from 1% to 6%. There was a found a strong correlation between optimizer
losses and its conversion ratio and power output. Average optimizer loss value was used
for further analysis.
By comparing filtered but not averaged inverter’s and optimizers data from DTU Riso BIPV
setup it was found that there is a signal delay/lag between optimizer and inverter and that
this delay is different for each optimizer which causes RMSE to increase, however it had
little effect on MBE.
It was found that two pairs of BIPV modules were shaded for few hours each day, there­
fore while accounting for optimizer and inverter power error analysis, this had to be taken
into consideration. RMSE analysis on predicted power against measured one showed
that there is significant difference in modelling accuracy depending on weather condi­
tions. RMSE during clear sky day ranged from 0.05 kW (3.1%) to 0.22 kW (13.75%) for
shaded modules, while during cloudy day RMSE was around 0.3 kW (18.8%) for all air­
gap configurations. RMSE values for clear sky day was in the range (1% to 5%) of other
power prediction models, however SAM cloudy day model had far greater error rate. On
the other hand MBE showed very low deviation from measured data, values ranging from
0.001 to 0.04 kW if shaded modules were discarded.
Statistical analysis was done on inverter DC power simulated by SAM compared to in­
verter DC data measured at Riso. Similar error results of all BIPV modules suggest no
difference in simulation accuracy on RMSE between different air­gap setups and venti­
lation configurations, however RMSE of clear sky day period is higher than cloudy day,
which suggest that the error might be caused by shaded modules. SAM predicted inverter
data had low MBE for 0 mm air­gap setup, especially for clear sky day simulation, but the
error tend to increase with increasing air­gap.

Relative error data for Riso inverter input and Riso optimizer output ranged from 2.5% to

6.2
Future work
During this study some topics were discovered that generates interesting questions which
would require additional research.
This study was carried out using the setup, where BIPV modules were separated into
pairs with different air­gaps. Low air­gap modules were insulated and high air­gap mod­
ules were ventilated. Deeper analysis of insulation/ventilation would provide interesting
results. Additionally testing the setup to avoid any potential shading might be a step for­
ward.
In this work data was averaged because hardware devices collected data at different time
stamps. For more precise study it would be recommended to use devices which has the
same data logging time period.
Study shown that wind speed has significant role predicting module temperature, therefore
installing wind speed sensor on the facade where solar modules are installed might give
more precise data.
