Abstract
The target in electricity production in many countries plans for a grid powered entirely by renewable
energy sources (wind, solar ...). Given the intermittent character of such sources, storage facilities
will play an important role in the future grid. As RES yield a zero marginal-cost, in cases when they
are insufficient to provide for the whole demand, storage facilities may have a strong impact on the
electricity market price, assuming marginal cost pricing. Such impact however is uncertain today
because of little existing literature to this day. This study will investigate the bidding strategies for
energy storage facilities through a game-theoretical approach under the hypothesis of an oligopolistic
structure of the electricity market. The dominant strategies will then be determined through the
search for Nash equilibria from the supply side. The impact of competition and the social welfare
loss induced by the strategic behaviour will be investigated. The theoretical framework will then
be applied to a scenario inspired by the 2030 electricity strategy of Denmark that plans for a mix
based on renewables. An analysis on the different strategies for sizing the storage actors will also
be led.

1
Introduction
The transition towards a 100% renewable energy system is a key goal for many countries to face the
challenge of climate change and reduce greenhouse gas emissions. However the dominant renewable
energy sources (RES) such as wind and solar power are as well intermittent and variable sources.
This may create a lag between the periods of high demand and those of high production, leading
to a risk of insufficient supply during events of low production or peak demand. Their integration
to the existing energy system thus presents challenges to maintain the grid stability. The technical
assessment of such systems has been widely studied and applied to the specific context of certain
countries, demonstrating its theoretical feasibility ([1], [2], [3]).
Among the solutions mentioned to align demand with production comes the use of energy storage
systems (battery system, pumped-hydro storage, compressed air energy storage . . . ). By storing
excess energy generated during periods of high supply and releasing it during periods of high de-
mand, energy storage can help to balance the grid and increase the reliability of renewable energy
sources. This would require the development of large-scale storage facilities designed for the grid to
handle flexibly important power fluctuation as referenced in [4] and [5].
Beyond the grid considerations of such context, lies as well the impact on the electricity market
of a high penetration of renewables. This development may indeed require to adapt the current
market structure. In the marginal cost structure, as RES tend to bid with a zero-marginal-cost
strategy, a market based a 100% on RES may prove highly unstable [6]. However, its framework
may still prove efficient in an integrated system where the network may adapt to the needs of the
market [7]. This calls again for a development of storage actors on the electricity market. They
would take advantage of the fluctuation of electricity prices, buying electricity in periods of high
production with cheap prices to sell it back at a higher price when the demand is high, a strategy
called arbitrage. In this context, storage operators may be seen as a small group of players who
have a significant impact on market outcomes. This is because they have the ability to control
the supply of electricity during periods of high demand, and as a result, they can highly influence
prices. This oligopolistic structure would then make the storage players dominant price-makers on
the maker. It is then necessary to understand the bidding strategies that energy storage operators
may employ in order to maximize their profits. The analysis of said strategies can help in forecasting
the behaviour of the future electricity markets. This would then help in sizing the needs of RES and
associated storage facilities. Such understanding would help policy-makers and market operators in
designing efficient regulation to tend to a fairer market, optimizing not only the profits from storage
operators but the whole social welfare. This is a mandatory requirement for the green transition
to be considered not only better for the environment but as well for the whole society and thus
sustainable.
The approach in the following work is to model the interactions between the storage operators
and the market as a Cournot game. This study is structured as follow. First, section 2 makes an
extensive literature review of existing work related to optimization of storage bidding strategies on
the electricity market. Then in section 3, the theoretical framework for the optimization problem is
defined. It sets the method used in the article to linearize and solve the decision problem for a P-
players game and for a hypothetical social planner that would control all the players. The strategy
to find the Nash equilibria of the designed game is also presented. Then section 4 explains how the
study case based on the danish 2030 mix is designed. It first describes the chosen mix based on the

welfare, but where the producer surplus dominates the consumer surplus. Competition is shown to
be a good tool to lower the market price, although the oligopolistic structure shows flaws compared
to the social optimum on that aspect.

Literature review
The bidding strategies for storage facilities in the electricity market has been widely studied in the
literature in a variety of context and with different hypothesis. Two main types of optimization
problems seem to emerge: economic bidding (bids containing both a price and quantity component)
and self-scheduling problems (bids containing only a quantity bid, the bidder will then buy/sell
electricity no matter the market price). The former is usually solved via bi-level problems where
the upper problem represents the storage actor revenue optimization and the lower problem the
market clearing process from the market operator ([9], [10], [11]). According to [11] and [12], it
outperforms the self-scheduling approach for storage facilities with high flexibility and in analy-
sis involving stochastic decision-making. The self-scheduling approach is however more common
as storage facilities usually don’t require high flexibility (periods of arbitrage needs are usually
consecutive and not split randomly through the day) and for deterministic models.
The self-scheduling papers can be split in three categories depending on the method chosen for
linking the storage bidders operation with the market price. The first method uses historical prices
and assumes the storage to be pure price-takers. It doesn’t model any other bidders on the market
and thus take the price as a given parameter ([13] uses historic prices in the German market).
This approach could make sense for small capacity storage and [12] shows that it could make sense
when considering only the day-ahead market. However, this approach is limited and may strongly
overestimates the revenue from arbitrage according to the results found in [10] and [14]. The second
method models the price as a function of the total energy submitted on the market based on
historical data. It is often considered as a linear function ([14], [15], [16]) but other models exist as
well ([17] uses a sigmoïd representation). In [14], the relation is extended to take into account how
penetration of renewable energy sources would impact the shape of the curve. The third method, the
most common, assumes inelastic electricity demand, thus giving the market power to the generators.
The aggregated supply curve then defines the market price. This assumption can be made when
day-to-day demand varies slightly independently of price. For example in [12], authors develop a
model of self-scheduling for a storage aggregator applied to the Great Britain market with Nord
Pool, National Grid and Elexon data. The aggregator is shown to have a positive impact over
the electricity bills of households accepting to use their battery less for personal use and instead
offering more supply flexibility. In [18], based on historical data from the Alberta electricity market,
a generation price quota curves (GPQC) and demand price quota curves (DPQC) are modelled to
forecast the impact of the storage when charging or discharging on the market price. This approach
also allows to model the supply curve as a step-wise function, facilitating the formulation of a linear
objective function for the problem. This approach however implies perfect knowledge of the supply
on the market. Similarly, [19] builds a lower and upper residual supply curve to solve a robust
optimization problem with nodal constraints of the storage self-scheduling in the New York market.
Although most papers using self-scheduling bids schedule bids for a single storage, some consider
as well the interactions between multiple bidders to develop game-theory models. In [14], players
compete first on their investment into power capacity and storage level and then on arbitrage
revenue over multiple years in the Californian market. This leads to an analysis of the impact of
competition over the investments into storage development but as well on the expected the market
price. Authors show that increasing the competition on storage allow to overall lower the market
price and they show as well how the efficiency of the storage players can be differentiating in
discriminating shares of market power. Similar results are achieved in [15] where a game between
Page 3 of 40

multiple storage players maximizing their profit and the market operator maximizing society’s social
welfare is performed to clear the market in a multi-nodal environment. It shows as well that although
the social welfare does increase with the addition of storage, the competitive bidding prevents to
reach the social optimum (similar findings were achieved as well in [9]). It then designs a policy
design similar to a feed-in to encourage bidders to aim for the social optimum. In [16], a Cournot
oligopoly game between multiple generators (including a storage player) competing for electricity
demand is modelled. Different strategies are used to find the Nash equilibrium of the problem. The
main method as seen in [14], finds it by reformulating the problem into an equivalent centralized
optimization problem. This can be done as in [15] through the use of the KKT conditions for all
players. In [16], it consists of the solving of a Optimum Generation Plan (OGP) for all players
that maximizes the profit from all players by setting the partial derivatives of that profit to 0. The
resulting set of constraints is then solved using a Newton method.
Another difference in the existing model of the literature is the time horizon chosen for the schedul-
ing, ranging from one hour to multiple years. Models with 1 hour ([16]), 1 day ([9], [19], [15], [17])
or 2 days ([12]) assume perfect information on price (except [19]) and production. Indeed according
to [20], forecast on renewables production and market demand can be accurate on such short-term.
This however impacts as well the operation margin for storage because of boundary effects. This
tends to underestimate the possible revenue from arbitrage ([10], [17]). On the other hand, mod-
els with longer range, from a week ([10]) to a year ([9]) or more ([14]) may reflect better possible
bid strategies but require a stochastic modelling to account for the important uncertainties from
demand and renewables production.
Our work will focus on developing a self-scheduling game between multiple storage players. They
shall optimize their revenue over the time horizon of the next day, assuming perfect information
from the renewables production and the aggregated step-wise demand curve. The impact of strategic
bidding and competition over the social welfare of the system will then be explored as well as the
impact from the size of the total storage available on the market. The main innovation from this
work is the context of the study. Although articles looked into the impact of large wind penetration
([10], [14]), a market composed at 100% by renewable energy and which market power then lies
within the storage player has not been modelled yet. A study case built around the danish strategy
for a 100% RES mix in 2030 [8] and the current Danish electricity demand [21] will then be performed
to analyze the outcomes of such context.

3
Methodology and formulation
3.1
Context of the study
The study will focus on a market where the demand is expected to be met 100% by renewable
energy sources. Given the intermittence of their production, storage facilities are introduced to
flatten the supply through the day. The overall expected outcome of the day-ahead (DA) market
structure is for all generators to bid with their marginal costs. However as renewables such as
windmills or solar panels induce no fuel costs, their bidding price is close to 0. Hence, a market
with high penetration of renewables is sometime considered infeasible, as the generated profit over
time wouldn’t be enough to compensate for the initial investment costs [6]. Other studies however
prove that such market structure can still be more favourable than other such as the pay-as-bid [22].
Moreover, its framework may still prove efficient in an integrated system where the network may
adapt to the needs of the market [7]. In the following, we will assume a market structure similar to
the one described in 3.2. Renewables will be assumed to have no operation costs. They will thus bid
with their full available capacity at every hour. The market power will then be dominated by the
oligopole composed of the different storage players. The players will be bidding by self-scheduling
their operations, thus bidding on the market only with quantity. Their strategy then impact the
market price, making the players price-makers. This forms therefore a Cournot game, which fits
perfectly our intention to study the impact of oligopolistic competition for arbitrage on the player’s
strategy.
As we will only model the operation for one day-ahead, players are considered to be given perfect
information over the renewable production and the demand curve for the day to come [20].
The context can be summed in the following:
• P non-homogeneous players acting as price-makers storage-facilities
• Players bid on quantity only on the DA market
• Demand curve and RES production are given for every time step
3.2
Structure of the electricity market
In European electricity markets such as Nord Pool or Epex Spot, generators and consumers submit
their offers to the market through a pair of quantity and price bid. Those pairs are then aggregated
to generate respectively a supply and demand curve. The intersection of the two then determines
the market clearing price (MCP) and quantity (MCQ). All generators with a supply bid price lower
than the MCP will then sell their electricity at the MCP to all the consumers who bid over the MCP.
The main outcome of the market structure is to encourage generators to bid on the market with
their marginal costs, the MCP is then the marginal cost of the generator of the last unit to enter
the market. This process is applied on different time horizons and at different levels on different
markets. This study will focus only on the day-ahead market (also called spot market). Bids on
the day-ahead market for day d are sent on day d-1 at noon and decomposed in bids for every hour

3.3.3
Constraints on power and price levels
It is assumed that at every hour, the player can either charge or discharge (or stay inactive). The
following constraint ensures that at maximum only one power output level is chosen by the player
(sum of variables equal to 0 corresponding to the idle mode). It also ensures that the power output

3.3.6
Search for the Nash equilibrium
The optimization problem above can be run for all players taken individually where each player
make assumptions on the behaviour of other players.
The search for the Nash equilibrium can be done iteratively. To do so, the game is run a 1st time
with all players assuming that the other players bids with 0-action strategy (no charge neither
discharge for the whole period). The output of a run is entirely represented by the set of charging
and discharging decisions of the player at every time step. The state of the whole system is then
represented by the set of all player states. Then in a second time, players recalculate their strategy,
using for the other players the result of the 1st round. At every iteration the new system state
and the one from the previous iteration are compared. The cycle continues until the result of a
round is the same as one of the previous round. Having power outputs as discrete variables limit
the set of possible actions for both players, therefore requiring less iterations to find the Nash
equilibrium and ensuring the existence of one or many Nash equilibria. This approach is preferred
to the reformulation of the problem through the KKT as doing so would be difficult because of the
MILP structure of our optimization problem. It is however similar to the adjustment process (or
Gauss-Seidel-type method) described in [25] and used to find the Nash Equilibrium of the electricity
market.

4.2.2
Consideration of negative bids
It is possible to notice that there are negative bids on the curve. It means that there are bidders
willing to receive money for consuming electricity. This phenomenon can be explained for a demand
curve from the current electricity market, which contains fossil fuel generators and high penetration
of renewables. In such context, negative bids on the demand side stems from the negative bids
on the production side. This situation usually happens in context of high penetration of RES,
in situation of low-demand and high renewables production [31] [32]. RES are then able to cover
Page 20 of 40
==PAGE:25==
Master Thesis
the whole demand and the clearing price is pushed close to zero or even negative, phasing out all
generators with higher bids. Bidding negatively from production side, in other words generators
willing to pay to produce electricity for the grid, is a strategy used for generators willing to ensure
their presence in the mix. This need can then be explained by multiple factors:
• Low downward reserve on the market [31].
• Conventional generators with high shut-down costs and low flexibility, that the losses incurred
by stopping production on a few hours would overcome that of producing for a negative price
[32]
• Renewable generators receiving support mechanisms following their production level instead
of the market indicators (green certificates, energy credits, tax credit incentives ...) which
income would overcome the losses from curtailment [31], [33], [34]
• CHP facilities answering calls from the heat market that need to evacuate the electricity
generated as a bi-product on the electricity market [35]
Those constraints are widely overcome in the context of this study. Indeed, wind and solar PV
generators are flexible producers, able to curtail easily in case of non-rewarding market prices [36].
Moreover, with a mix composed of 100%-RES, the need for support mechanism would be limited,
if not nonexistent as this would otherwise mean to support the whole market. Support mechanisms
are implemented today to help the development of renewables, but in a context where there are
fully established, to suppress them seems like a reasonable hypothesis. CHP facilities in a 100%
RES grid would probably be replaced either by electrification of the heat demand or the bioenergy
production already included in the mix as a base load (see 4.1.1), thus with limited need to bid
negatively to enter the market. Finally, the presence of storage players, offer both upward and
downward flexibility to mitigate extreme cases of low demand and high production.
We will then assume that in a context with 100%-RES, negative prices would be quite limited, if
not absent. Thus, the demand curve from Nord Pool will be adapted to our context by cutting of
negative bids. The supplement volume lost was seen as an opportunity for revenue in the current
market rather than a real need, hence its curtailment in our study with a different market context.

Impact of storage and capacity levels sizing
To assess the impact of the initial sizing of the total storage and capacity levels, we can compare
the 2 players base case with other scenarios. The sizing choice defined in 4.3 was meant to adapt
the system properties to the typical days defined in winter and summer and for the total demand.
The same approach can be repeated to size the players on the basic demand instead to analyze the
behaviour in a more restricted situation. This "basic scenario" is then an example of a undersized
situation.
Another scenario would be to design the system to be able to withstand extreme events. This
approach is inspired by the regulation set by RTE (French Transmission System Operator - TSO).
The balance capacities need to be designed such that to meet the basic demand for at least 99% of
the time [39]. To size the total capacity, we looked at the residual basic demand (using the same
typical demand defined in 4.2) for all days in winter and summer. We then select the 1-st percentile
value as the necessary capacity to address the capacity need 99% of the time. For the storage level,
we look at the expected RES production every day and decide to keep the 1st percentile value of
the lowest energy generation day. This day is then used with the same methodology as in 4.3.2 to
get the associated required storage level. This "extreme scenario" represents an oversized situation.

6
Conclusion
This study has developed a model to study the strategic behaviour on the day-ahead market of
storage facilities in a grid powered entirely by renewables energy sources. The market structure is
considered to be the same market clearing price as used in most of Europe today. A case study
has been designed based on the danish electricity strategy for the horizon 2030 and the existing
electricity demand in the Nordics scaled to the Danish demand. An analysis of the impacts of
competition and strategic bidding on social welfare and the market price are then performed as well
as a comparison of different sizing for the storage players.
Based on the findings presented in this case study, we can conclude that the presence of storage
players in such electricity market has the potential to increase the overall social welfare of the system.
By strategically bidding with a portion of their capacity, the players can influence the market price
to their advantage and maximize their profits. They allow to flatten the supply of energy through
the day and meet a higher share of the electricity demand. The main strategy followed by players
consists of bidding to follow the necessary demand profile when the supply is low and to charge
using the excess supply regarding the maximum demand when the supply is high.
Furthermore, examining the impact of competition on social welfare and market prices revealed
that increasing the number of players resulted in improved resource utilization, reduced curtailment,
and lower market prices during times of high demand. The competition among players aimed to
maximize the share of total demand met while utilizing renewable energy efficiently.
The results also show the importance of sizing the storage capacity correctly. Undersizing may lead
to insufficient electricity supply at peak demand times while oversizing may risk other operation
constraints.
The developed model could be improved through a deeper analysis on a longer time range which
would offer the storage players a higher operation margin. Limiting to a single day impacts the
strategic behaviour of players as the end of the day constraints have a stronger impact.Considering
the behaviour on an entire week would be a first step in doing so. This would require to consider a
stochastic model to account for the uncertainties on the RES and demand forecasts.
A possible future development for this study could be to analyse the revenue generation for storage
actors and renewable energy sources to ensure that they may cover their initial investment. This
may be coupled to integration of renewables and storage together to optimize synergies and funding,
through exisitng or future policies as mentionned in [40]. This model may also be used to further
imagine possible policy designs to implement on the market to have a better control over the
electricity price. The study has shown that a socially optimum use of the storage players may lead
to a stable electricity price for similar social outcome. A policy design encouraging the players to
naturally behave in such a way may then be necessary to develop. This could include assessing the
impact of feed-in tariffs [15], price caps, market design reforms and demand response programs on
self-scheduling strategies and market performance.
