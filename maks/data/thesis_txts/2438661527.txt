Abstract
Due to the increase of melanoma mortality rates in recent years, it is essential
to contribute to the understanding of the disease, as well as to the research for
elucidating the melanoma tumor evasion mechanisms and the eﬀector functions
of immune cells interacting with melanoma cells.
A melanoma dataset of anti-PD-1 pre-treatment tumors has been analyzed to
identify diﬀerentially expressed genes between responding versus non-responding
tumors, using two pipelines (Pipeline A:Kallisto and Pipeline B:htseq-count) and
two response group comparisons (1. complete and partial response versus pro-
gressive disease and 2. complete response versus progressive disease). Pipeline A
yielded several tumor-favorable genes, such as MMP1, INHBA, MMP13, FOXC2
in the ﬁrst response group comparison, as well as the two cancer-testis genes
MAGEL2 and PAGE2 in the second response group comparison (FDR < 0.05,
|LFC| > 0.58). Pipeline B revealed also FOXC2 and MMP1 as up-expressed in
progressive disease tumors using the ﬁrst group comparison, while in the sec-
ond cancer-testis gene MAGEL2 was identiﬁed as a DEG(FDR < 0.05, |LFC|
> 0.58).
To gain insight in the underlying biology a GO and KEGG over-
representation analysis is perdormed using the diﬀerentially expressed genes de-
termined with FDR < 0.05, |LFC| > 0. This analysis suggests several processes,
and pathways enriched non-responding tumors, such as extracellular matrix or-
ganization, remodelling and disassembly, as well as ECM-receptor interaction
and focal adhesion (p < 0.05). The characterization of immune cell populations
in the tumors of the dataset, did not yield consistent results between the two
pipelines investigated, nor with the results produced by other studies, though
some diﬀerences in abundances of macrophages, CD4+ T-cells, eosinophils and
mast cells were statistically signiﬁcant (p < 0.05).
==PAGE:4==
ii
In addition, gene expression data of four melanoma datasets were acquired, in
order to unravel the immune-mediated tumor killing mechanisms employed by
CD8+ T-cells and the eﬀector functions of CD4+ T-cells. The analysis applied
included immune cell proﬁling in order to identify samples with high inﬁltration
of the T-cell in question. Then gene expression correlations were determined to
identify genes, whose expression is highly correlated with known surface mark-
ers of those cells (Pearson correlation > 0.6). The resulting genes were ﬁltered
with UniProt- and HPA-derived databases to select only for those genes that en-
code cytokines/chemokines, other secreted, membrane and unknown subcellular
location proteins.
27 genes encoding cytotoxic and inhibitory molecules, as well as other immune
checkpoint ligands or signaling molecules important for CTLs exerting their
tumor-killing properties were identiﬁed across all datasets, such as IL32, IL2RB,
GZMA, GZMK, CD2, APOL3, CD38, CD74, CD27, LCK (Pearson correla-
tion > 0.6). 57 common genes across the datasets with aggregated metastatic
and primary tumor samples were determined, including CCL5, GZMK, CD2,
APOL3, CD80, CD74, CCL4, CCR5, GZMH, CD86, GZMA, IL18, CD3D,
CD27 IL21R, IL IGNG, IL-32, PRF1, ICOS, FASLG, LCK. Genes encoding
proteins of unknown subcellular location were GIMAP4, SAMD3, ZBED2 and
C5orf56 (Pearson correlation > 0.6). In primary melanoma samples 35 shared
genes were found, some of which were the GZMK, GZMA, CCR5, FASLG,
PRF1, IL2RB, IL2RG, CD2, CD3E, CD247 genes. In metastatic tumor sam-
ples 33 genes were determined, including CCL5,IL32, GZMK, APOL3, CD2,
CD38, NOD2, TNFAIP2, CD3D, GZMA, IL2RB and CD27 (Pearson corre-
lation > 0.6). The underlying biology of those genes and the pathways they
participate were investigated, resulting in enriched cell-cell adhesion and anti-
gen processing and antigen receptor-mediated signaling processes, as well as
cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction and chemokine signaling pathways, in-
dicative of the direct and indirect mechanisms of cancer cells elimination by
those cells(p-value <0.05). There were no cytokines found to be associated with
CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions in samples with high CD4+ and low CD8+ T-
cell inﬁltration. Yet, there were some genes found to be associated with T-cell
activation and diﬀerentiation, as well as some genes that should be investigated
for their immunosuppressive role, such as yet among the genes identiﬁed are
CDKL1, TRGV2, CDC40, VAMP8, CDK5R2, ADAM15, PDCD2 and PLIN2.
Functional and pathway enrichment analysis of the genes revealed diﬀerent bio-
logical processes between primary and metastatic tumor samples, but enriched
ECM-receptor, Gap junction, PRAR signaling and AMPK signaling pathways
(p < 0.1).

Chapter 1
Introduction
Human melanoma is a malignant neoplastic disease formed by melanocytes,
which are cells stemming from the neural crest that produce the melanin pigment.[3]
Melanoma is considered one of the deadliest types of skin cancer, accounting for
up to two thirds of deaths due to skin cancer, worldwide.[4] Due to the world-
wide increase in morbidity, melanoma has been investigated by multiple studies
in recent years, contributing to the understanding of the disease as well as the
development of novel, more eﬀective therapies, particularly for patients not di-
agnosed in the early stages.[5]
Melanoma tumors are characterized by genetic heterogeneity, high metastatic
rates and tumor cell dissemination. Patients with extensive metastases have
poor prognosis and low median survival.[6] Due to its immunogenic character-
istics (i.e. high number of mutations) melanoma is considered to be a good
model for cancer immuno-oncology. Impromptu remissions, disappearance of
primary lesions in 5% of the patients, abundance of active tumor inﬁltrating
lymphocytes (TILs) and recognition of tumor-associated antigens by TILs are
observations suggestive of the immune system’s ability to mount a consider-
ably eﬀective response against the malignancy.[7] More than three decades of
research have led to the development of several immunotherapeutic approaches,
with little or more success. These therapies act on the host’s immune system
and by using suitable targets, try to boost the immune response against the
tumor. They can be classiﬁed as adoptive or active and speciﬁc or non-speciﬁc.
==PAGE:30==
2
Introduction
Treatments with interleukin-2 (IL-2) and interferon-a (IFN-h) are non-speciﬁc,
requiring the host to identify the antigens and harness the ability of cytokines
to promote T-cell activation. Cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4) and
programmed death-1 receptor (PD-1) are target agents used to inhibit T-cell
suppression; these therapies are considered speciﬁc and called monoclonal an-
tibodies. Adoptive cell therapy (ACT) with tumour-inﬁltrating lymphocytes
(TIL) has additionally been developed as a speciﬁc, adoptive therapy for ad-
vanced stage melanoma patients.[2][8]
All these major advances in melanoma immunotherapy treatments have resulted
in diverse clinical results, with some patient subgroups responding completely to
the molecular inhibitors, while others still progressing.[9]. Regarding anti-PD-1
therapy, it does not provide long-term beneﬁts and around 60-70% of patients
with metastatic melanoma appear to have primary (innate) resistant tumors.
Identifying those patients who are not likely to beneﬁt from immune-therapy
and understanding the mechanisms underlying primary resistance, are clinically
very important questions. There are additional patients that have developed
acquired resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors.[10] Metastatic melanoma
tumors are very genetically heterogeneous, and are associated with extreme im-
mune tolerance, thus producing poorly immunogenic antigens.[9] Even though
there has been progress in understanding the tumor microenvironment and how
the host’s immune cells associate with tumor cells, studies on the role of im-
mune cells, notably T cells, as prognostic markers of the disease have given
contradicting results.[11]
Given the limitations of current therapeutic approaches, alongside the little
knowledge we have of the mechanisms responsible for the response and durabil-
ity variations, exploring other immunologic factors may provide signiﬁcant ﬁnd-
ings towards the treatment of melanoma.[10] Knowledge of the role of known
and unknown eﬀector molecules participating in immune-mediated tumor killing
by cytotoxic T cells will help elucidate tumor killing mechanisms and enhance
current therapies. Furthermore, identifying the positive as well as negative ef-
fects of CD4+ T cells will give us an insight on possible immunosuppressive
cytokines.
To this end, this project was designed in collaboration with the Center for Can-
cer Immune Therapy (CCIT) group at Herlev Hospital, in order to investigate
multiple aspects of CD8+ and CD4+ T cells eﬀector functions along with pos-
sible immunosuppressive factors facilitating tumor cells to elude immune-cell
mediated killing. Three major questions were investigated in my study. The
ﬁrst part focuses in identifying genes that can be responsible for the tumor cells
escaping immune response and immune resistant genes in anti-PD-1 therapy
resistance tumors.
To achieve this, diﬀerential expression analysis (DEA) is
performed on an RNA-Seq dataset, comparing gene expression levels between
==PAGE:31==
3
responsive and non-responsive patients. An extra deconvolution analysis is con-
ducted, to get an overview of the immune cell abundances of the two response
groups. The second part investigates eﬀector pathways utilized by cytotoxic T
cells to induce apoptosis in cancer cells. The third and ﬁnal part deals with
CD4+ T cells eﬀector functions and the analysis followed looks into possible
immunosuppressive molecules secreted by or adhered to the plasma membrane
of CD4+T cells and other eﬀector molecules associated with CD4+ T-cells func-

the resulting genes are ﬁltered to identify genes that encode secreted, plasma
membrane and unknown subcellular location proteins. As a note, throughout
the analysis conducted, implementation of diﬀerent analytical pipelines is ex-
plored, with an aim to identify the best methods serving the purpose of the
project.

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
2.1
Cancer
Cancer is a collective name for a range of diseases originating from various
organs of the body that show two major common features: 1) overproliferation
of abnormal cells and 2) invading the surrounding tissue, and eventually, to
distant tissues.[12] Skin cancer is one of the most common cancer types, along
with breast, prostate, bowel and lung cancer. All of these ﬁve types account
for around 60% of malignancies’ cases.[13] There are three types of skin cancer:
basal cell cancer, squamous cell cancer and melanoma.[13] According to WHO
(World Health Organization), there are between 2-3 million people diagnosed
with skin cancer every year.[14] Although melanoma is the rarest form of skin
cancer, it is the most life-threatening due to its high spreading/growth rates as
well as its ability to develop resistance against current therapies.[15]
2.1.1
Melanoma skin cancer
The cause of melanoma cancer is the uncontrolled division of melanocytes.
Melanocytes originate from neural crest cells. Neural crest cells are cells that
emigrate from the neural tube during the early stages of embryogenesis and are
==PAGE:34==
6
Theoretical background
multipotent, giving rise to many diﬀerent cell-types. After their diﬀerentiation
into melanoblasts, they undergo ﬁnal migration into the dermis, epidermis and
hair follicles, and develop into melanocyte cells. These cells produce the melanin
pigment, which deﬁnes the skin color, but can also be found in the bowel, brain
cortex, the eyes, the nervous and respiratory system.[16][13] Melanoma can arise
in any tissue containing melanocytes.
Both environmental and genetic factors play a crucial role in the development of
the disease. The DNA mutations responsible for the defect pathways leading to
the malignancy can be either inherited (germline), which are responsible for a
fewer percentage of the cases, or acquired and gradually accumulated (somatic)
during someone’s life. The latter ones can be induced by environmental factors,
with extensive UV light sun exposure being the main cause. UV radiation used
in artiﬁcial tanning is also known to promote carcinogenesis and contribute to
the disease[17][13] by regulating the function and division of melanocytes.[16]
Mutational signatures due to UV light have been determined and thoroughly
described. However there are othercontributing factors.[18][19] Caucasian pop-
ulations have the highest risk for this cancer type. Individual factors such as the
color of the eyes (blue, green), hair color and tone, tendency to get and severity
of sunburns or number of moles in the body are reported to be a prognosticator
of melanoma.[13] [15]
Skin pigmentation can be classiﬁed as constitutive or adaptive. The constitutive
refers to the production of a speciﬁc melanin pigment type and the maturation
of melanosomes, which are the vesicles carrying the melanin pigment.
The
adaptive pigmentation is associated with the response of the organism to UV
light, by means of pigment synthesis. DNA damage caused by UV induces the
production of this pigment.[18]
The skin or epidermis consists of 4 or 5 layers, with the lower layer containing
melanocytes and keratinocyte stem cells along with other highly proliferative
cells.
It has been observed that UV-induced mutations have greater impact
in this layer compared to the upper layer.
After any exposure to UV light,
melanocytes respond by secreting melanosomes, which in turn are digested by
keratinocytes, regulating the transfer of pigment to the skin layers.[16]
There are at least three independent genetic changes acting as initiation events
for the transformation of melanocytes into malignant melanoma cells.[16] Below
the main molecular factors implicated in melanoma are summarized.
MITF (microphthalmia-associated transcription factor) is a key regulator of the
development, proliferation and survival of melanocytes. In melanoma, MITF
deﬁciency has been proved to activate melanoma oncogenes and up-regulate
anti-apoptotic genes. Additionally, several oncogenic mutations activate signal
==PAGE:35==

7
transduction pathways promoting angiogenesis and cell growth, aiding to the
survival and immune escape of melanoma cells.
Among them genetic aber-
rations are the primary and mutually exclusive BRAF, NRAS/NF1, CKIT,
GNAQ/GNA11 mutations[18]; mutations in tumor-suppressor genes CDKN2A
and PTEN; in proto-oncogenes CDK4 and MYC and in genes CTNNB1 and
APC, which participate in the Wnt-signaling cascade.[16].
One of the signaling pathways participating in the development of the disease
is the mitogen activated protein kinase signaling (MAPK) pathway, which has
been shown to be important in many cancers.
Hyperactivation of the ERK
kinase, genetic mutations and DNA copy number gains/ampliﬁcations (BRAF
or NRAS) regulate the activation and expression of cyclin D1 enhancing the
uncontrolled growth of melanoma cells. MAPK also contributes to the agres-
sivness of the disease via regulation of pro- and antiapoptotic B-cell lymphoma
2 (BCL-2) genes, such as BIM, BAD, BMF, MCL-1, BAX, NOXA and PUMA.
The second important signaling pathway involved in melanoma is PI3K (pho-
sphoinositol-3-kinase), which promotes cell proliferation, survival, angiogenesis
and induces apoptotic resistance via activation of AKT (Protein kinase B).
Activation of NRAS, loss of function (LOF) or mutation of NF1 , loss of function
of PTEN are some of the mechanisms that activate this pathway.[18]
Additionally, considering some of the aforementioned genetic mutations, dereg-

environmental exposure, as well as the anatomical site where the malignancy
develops.[16]

other tissues. The risk of progression from primary to metastatic melanoma is

2.1.2
Melanoma tumor microenvironment
The aforementioned genetic alterations and environmental factors are necessary
for the transformation of melanocytes into melanoma cells, however, the tumor
microenvironment plays an important role and and is pivotal for the tumorige-
nesis.
The tumor microenvironment (TME) can be described as the cellular environ-
ment surrounding the tumor, and has an active participation in every stage of
cancer progression. TME consists of the extracellular matrix (ECM), stroma
cells, immune and inﬂammatory cells, blood vessels, ﬁbroblasts and signaling
molecules.[22] The malignant tumor and the TME interact constantly in a bidi-

shows the most important cell components of the TME.

Cadherins are important cell adhesion molecules for the keratinocyte control of
melanocytes. Downregulation of E-cadherin and upregulation of N-cadherin has
been linked to melanoma tumorigenesis, suppression of proapoptotic genes and
promotion of tumor survival. The downregulation of E-cadhering contributes to
the previously mentioned phenotype switching of melanoma cells from prolifer-
ative to invasive.[23][22]

2.2
Cancer Immunology
The immune system is divided into two kinds of responses, the innate and adap-
tive, which consist of diﬀerent cell types. The innate immune system acts a ﬁrst
line of defense against cancer, while the adaptive immune system uses its cells to
identify tumor antigens and develop memory in order to respond more quickly
and eﬃciently in case of future encounter with the same antigens.
There is
deﬁnitely a crosstalk between adaptive and innate immunity, translated into
secretion of cytokines as well as activation of membrane receptors. Innate im-
mune cells such as macrophages, NK cells and dendritic cells can diﬀerentiate
between healthy and cancer cells and then either directly kill them or stimulate
adaptive immune cells.
The recruitment and activation of adaptive immune
cells occurs via antigen presenting cells (DCs, macrophaces, Bcells), which rec-
ognize and process tumor antigens and then present them to T lymphocytes (T
helper cells). T helper cells can regulate the function CD8+ T-cells (CTLs),
macrophages and B cells. [28]
The development of melanoma is a rather dynamic process, and melanoma
tumors are considered model tumors in the ﬁeld of immunooncology, due to
their interesting immunogenic characteristics.[29] Metastatic melanoma cancer
though has been closely linked to extreme immune tolerance and poorly im-
munogenic tumor antigens.[9] The role of the immune system exceeds the pro-
tective mechanisms employed against the cancer.
It is now known that the
immune system constantly sculpts the emerging tumors, a process called cancer-
immunoediting.[9] The framework of cancer immunosurveillance consists of three
inter-connected phases:
1. Elimination phase: tumor cells are eliminated by innate and adaptive
immunity via NK , dendritic , T and B-cells, either in the tumor microen-
vironment or peritumorally.
2. Equilibrium phase: can be considered as a balanced, prolonged period,
==PAGE:41==
2.2 Cancer Immunology
13
where melanoma cells are not completely destroyed, but suppressed. Dur-
ing this phase signiﬁcant changes for both immune and tumor cells occur.
These changes deﬁne the immunogenicity of tumor cells and its ability to
evade the immune antitumor responses. In essence, the immune microen-
vironment itself contributes to this evolutionary selective process, so that
tumor cells can grow and survive in this environment.
3. Escape phase: tumor cells acquire survival advantage by losing their im-
munogenic characteristics. Thus they can escape immune response mech-
anisms and suppress the immune system. It has been observed that in this
phase, immune cells facilitate the tumor’s escape and invasion pathways
by reducing the secretion of anti-tumor molecules.
Tumor cells are constantly edited by immune system cells, and in the worst case
scenario eﬀectively select for those dominant (tumor) cells that have the ability
to escape the immune system’s antitumor responses.[22][29][30]
2.2.1
Antitumoral response via CD8+ and CD4+ T-cells
An eﬀective immune response against a tumor starts after T (or B) lymphocytes
recognize the tumor, get activated, proliferate and diﬀerentiate into eﬀector
cells.[5] In this chapter, focus will be given to the eﬀector functions of CD4+
and CD8+ T-cells and their respective subtypes.
2.2.1.1
CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions
After the antigen presenting cells present the tumor antigens to CD4+ T-cells
via MHC class II molecules, these naive CD4+ T-cells can diﬀerentiate into
many types of eﬀector cells.[31] The activation of CD4+ T-cells requires bind-
ing of the MHC class II molecules to the T cell receptor (TCR), as well as
costimulatory signals (binding of B7 to CD28 receptor of CD4+ T cell) and co-
inhibitory signals (binding of B7 to CTLA-4 receptor or binding between PD-1
[PD-L1/PD-L2] and PD-1 in CD4+ T-cells). These signals that either induce
or inhibit activation of T-cells are referred to as immune checkpoints.[28]
The diﬀerentiation process that follows is dependent on developmental and en-
vironmental signals, such as cytokines.[32] The subtypes of CD4+ T-cells have
a broad range of eﬀector functions during all stages of immune response (initia-
tion, expansion and memory phase) and can exert opposing responses toward the
tumor.[33][32] In general, CD4+ T-cells display signiﬁcant plasticity and have

the expansion of CD8+ T-cells and aids in the recruitment of innate immune

which have a dual role toward tumor cells. Th17 cells are deﬁned by IL-17A
secretion, which in turn induces the expression of metalloproteinases stimulating
inﬂammation and CCL2, CCL7, CXCL1 and CCL20 chemokines. On the pro-
tumor side, they exert immunosupressive functions and promote angiogenesis.
Yet, Th17 cells have the ability to switch to Th1 phenotype and produce IFN-

stimulate CD8+ T-cells.[32][37]

2.2.1.2
CD8+ T-cells eﬀector functions
CD8+ T-cells are a subset of T lymphocytes expressing the CD8 marker.[22]
These cells exert functions able to destroy cancer cells upon recognition. CD8+
T-cells are known as cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs).[38] All nucleated cells
and antigen presenting cells (APCs) express antigen-MHC class I molecules
complexes on their surface. Activation of naive CD8+ T-cells occurs when the
T-cell receptor (TCR), associated with a CD3 complex, binds to these com-
plexes. In order for the activation to be successful, interaction of co-stimulatory
molecules such as CD28 and CD2 with their respective ligands B7-1/B7-2 and

tor family member 4 (TNFRSF4 or OX40 or CD134) and member 9 (TNFRSF
or 4-1BB or CD137).
They are of great signiﬁcance for the T cell priming
process and eﬀective antitumor responses.[38] As mentioned in chapter 2.2.1.1
“CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions”, CD4+ helper cells contribute to the activa-
tion of CTLs.[5] Cancer cells that still express MHC class I molecules on their
surface can present foreign tumor-associated antigens originating from genetic
aberrations, consequently triggering the CD8+ T-cells to exert the cytotoxic
destruction mechanism against the tumor.[28]
Recognition of the tumor antigens is followed by the formation of immune
synapse and activation of a transduction cascade, ultimately leading to the path-
ways of cytotoxic elimination of tumor cells. This immune synapse is a result
of the clustering of TCR and respective signaling molecules in the binding area
of the T cell and tumor cell.[38] The identiﬁcation of tumor antigens presented

2.2.2
Tumor immune escape
As brieﬂy described in the introduction of chapter 2.2 “Cancer Immunology”,
the progress of melanoma tumors is very complex, dynamic and related to im-
mune editing, or immunosurveillance. Melanoma cells acquire their migration
abilities during the escape phase of immunosurveillance.[29] A wide spectrum of
mechanisms are employed by tumor cells in order to evade the immune attack.
These mechanisms are closely associated with cellular and molecular character-
istics of the melanoma tumor microenvironment and the chronic stimulation of
the immune systems itself.[43][29]

One of the most important and initial events that melanoma cells utilize to
escape immune anti-tumor response is the deﬁciency in the antigen processing
machinery, especially defects in the transporter associated with antigen pro-
cessing (TAP) subunits.[38]This eventually impedes the tumor-antigen recog-
nition progress by CD8+ T-cells.[29] Nevertheless, there has been some indi-
cation of CD8+ T-cells capacity to use an alternative pathway for processing
target antigens.[38] Additionally, DC maturation is aﬀected by antigen pre-

Melanoma cells create a hostile tumor microenvironment for the
immune cells, and the secretion of VEGF, IL-8 and IL-10 molecules induces
an immature DC phenotype, which cannot act as an eﬃcient APC to activate
T lymphocytes.[29] Another important factor in the defective recognition of
melanoma cells is the progressive loss or mutation or downregulation of MHC
class I and II molecules from the surface of tumor cells, which are necessary for
recognition by T lymphocytes.[44]
A second immune escape mechanism of melanoma cells is the recruitment of
suppressive immune cells, such as Tregs and MSDCs. Their immunosuppressive
functions are described in chapter 2.2.1.1 “CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions”.
These cells inherently function to inhibit T lymphocytes and secrete VEGF
and TGF-β, promoting tumor growth. Previous studies have also shown that
when CD4+ T-cells mainly produce TNF in melanoma cells expressing MHC
class II molecules, that damages the eﬃciency of CD8+ T-cells in eliminating
tumor cells.[38] In melanoma the deregulated Tregs in melanoma characteristi-
cally produce TGF-β, IL-10 and indoleamine 2,3-doxigenase (IDO) blocking the
activity of CD4+ , CD8+ T-cells, as well as NK cells. Another tumor escape
strategy is the release of pro-tumorigenic and pro-angiogenic molecules by the tu-
mor microenvironment, such as nitrix oxide(NO), reactive oxygen species(ROS),
TGF-β and arginase (ARG)-1 that inhibit T-cells and NK cells and promote
angiogenesis.[29]
A fourth mechanism melanoma cells escape immune response is by exploiting
the immune checkpoints provided by the immune system. These checkpoints
are naturally enforced by the immune system to regulate any immune response.
Melanoma cells use these checkpoints to escape recognition and elimination by
the immune cells.[2] Manipulation of the checkpoints usually takes a form of
either impaired expression of immune checkpoint receptors or up-regulation of
immune checkpoint ligands. This leads to T-cell exhaustion, which is a common
phenomenon in cancer, describing the dysfunctional state of T-cells. Inhibitory
surface receptors such as CTLA-4, PD-1, BTLA-4, CD160, LAG-3, Tim-3 and
VISTA are expressed in exhausted T-cells and impede anti-tumor responses.
[29] As described in chapter 2.2.1.1 “CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions”, tumor-
inﬁltrating T-cells are regulated also by co-inhibitory signals, which inhibit their
function. These signals are induced by the binding of PD-1 or CTLA-4 receptors
on the T-cell with their ligands PD-L1/PD-L2 or B7 on the tumor cells. [2]

2.3
Immunotherapy
In recent years the research interest in melanoma’s therapeutic strategies has
shifted towards stimulating the immune system instead of directly targeting the
tumor. Findings supporting the highly immunogenic proﬁle of melanoma cells
and unraveling how immune checkpoints are manipulated by the tumor have
paved the way for scientists to explore new melanoma immunotherapies.[2] To
date, the main study subject of immunotherapy has been advanced, metastatic
melanoma.[7]
2.3.1
Approaches in melanoma cancer immunotherapies
Melanoma immunotherapies can be broadly classiﬁed in two ways: speciﬁc or
non speciﬁc and adoptive or active. The non-speciﬁc include those where the
antigen should be determined by the host, while the speciﬁc are either targeting
T lymphocytes or use well deﬁned antigen sources.
In active therapies it is
necessary that the host develops an immune response, while in the adoptive this
response are produced ex vivo.[8]
More speciﬁcally, the old and the new approaches in melanoma immunotherapy
can be categorized into:
1. Those using biological agents, such as cytokines (nonspeciﬁc, active). The-
se include interleukin-2(IL-2), interferons and granulocyte-monocyte colony
stimulating factor(GM-CSF).[2][8]
==PAGE:47==
2.3 Immunotherapy
19
2. Adoptive cell therapy (ACT), which uses the tumor inﬂitrating lympho-
cytes taken from the tumor of a patient which are processed and expanded
into lymphokine-activated killer TILs, T-lymphocytes with genetic alter-
ations and peripheral-blood-derived melanoma-speciﬁc T-cells. These are
later infused back into the patient. ACT is a speciﬁc and adoptive im-
munotherapy approach. [45][2][8]
3. Vaccines, which can be peptide, whole-protein, virus-based, DNA or den-
dritic-cell-based.[7]
4. Immune checkpoint inhibitors which target T-cells and are considered a
speciﬁc approach.[8] Among them are those inhibiting CTLA-4, PD1 and
PDL1. Additionally, there are immunotherapies being developed targeting
immune co-stimulatory molecules such as anti-CD137.[7]
Recently developed melanoma immunotherapies include those targeting the tu-
mor escape mechanisms using agents that block the inhibitory checkpoints reg-
ulating immune response, namely programmed death 1 (PD-1) and CTLA-4.[9]
As mentioned before, T-cell activation or suppression depend on co-stimulatory
signals. The initial event is the binding of T-cell receptor of T-cells to the tumor-
associated antigen. Successful activation of the T-cell occurs when the CD28
receptor of the T-cell binds to its ligand B7-1/B7-2 on the tumor cell. Though
if B7-1/B7-2 binds to CTLA-4 or if the PD-L1/PD-L2 ligand of the tumor cell
binds to the PD-1 receptor of T-cells, then an inhibitory signal is transmit-
ted that reduces the proliferation of antigen-speciﬁc T-cells and concurrently
reduced Tregs apoptosis (see Figure 2.2).[2] Full T-cell activation leads to up-
regulation of IL2 and IL-2R which promote T-cell proliferation, which conversely
will stop expressing if CTLA-4 receptor is bound instead.[7]
Ipilimumab is an anti-CTLA-4 therapy using a IgG-κ monoclonal antibody. This
monoclonal antibody inhibits CTLA-4’s suppressive eﬀect on T -lymphocytes by

Anti-PD-1 therapies involve antibodies aiming to block the PD-1 receptors of T-
cells, so that they can no longer bind to their ligands PD-L1/PD-L2 on tumor

Consequently, they promote T-cell activation and antitumor response.
Pembrolizumab (IgG4 isotype antibody), nivolumab (IgG4-blocking monoclonal

2.3.2
Challenges of current immunotherapies
During the last ten years there have been several major breathroughs in melanoma
immunotherapy reasearch. As a result, novel therapeutic agents have been de-
veloped that have brought signiﬁcant clinical beneﬁts for melanoma patients.[2]
Nonetheless, there is still progress to be done and many questions to be an-
swered. In this chapter an overview of the challenges associated with current
immunotherapies, focusing on immune checkpoint inhibitors will be presented.
Although several trials have shown that anti-CTLA-4 therapy has long-lasting
clinical outcome, it comes with adverse, immune-related eﬀects and autoimmune
toxicity. In addition, variable response patterns make it diﬃcult to evaluate the
eﬃcacy of the agent towards a speciﬁc patient.[2]
In general, immune criteria have not been clearly established in order to evaluate
response of patients to immunotherapies. The phenomenon of pseudoprogres-
sion describes the initial rapid progression of the disease which is then followed
by stabilization of tumor growth. Immune response criteria are not set in a way
to recognize patients with pseudoprogression, in order to avoid interruption of
therapy and there is great diﬃculty in discriminating between these patients
and non-responders.[28][2]
Even when some of the immune checkpoint inhibitors have proven to be more
eﬀective than the others (e.g. anti-PD-1), the overall conclusion is that tumor
regression is possible but not lasting.[5] When patients receive a single-agent
immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy and not a combination, most of those
not responding progresss at a rate as if they have not received any therapy, a
process known as innate resistance. Another subset of patients may be initially
responsive but display progression of the disease at a later stage, this mechanism
is known as acquired resistance.[46]
Regarding anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy, almost 60% of patients present innate
resistance and around 25% acquired resistance. For these patients, there are
very few, eﬃcient treatment options left.[47] Combination of anti-CTLA-4 and
anti-PD-1 agents results in 28% response rates in melanoma patients.[46]

3.2.2
Pipeline A: Transcript-level quantiﬁcation
Genes in eukaryotes are transcribed and spliced in order to produce mature
mRNA transcripts, which are then translated into proteins. The mRNA-tran-
scripts consist of less unique sequences, since diﬀerent transcripts originating
from the same gene may contain some of the same exons and untranslated re-
gions. The ﬁrst method used in the thesis to quantify the expression of genes
in the samples is measuring the transcripts abundances and then aggregating
to gene-level. The tool used is Kallisto, suitable for measuring the counts of
mRNA transcripts from RNA-Seq data. Kallisto is additionally an alignment-
independent RNA-Seq quantiﬁcation method, using pseudoalignment instead,
working directly on target cDNA or transcript sequences. Transcript-level quan-
tiﬁcation methods can be alignment-dependent or alignment-free. For the pur-
pose of the thesis, an alignment-independent method was chosen. In order to
run Kallisto, no reference genome is required. To determine the compatibil-
ity of the reads with target transcript sequences a cDNA fasta ﬁle is obtained
from Ensembl, for Homo Sapiens, release 91. This fasta ﬁle contains all the
corresponding cDNA/transcript sequences of Ensembl gene predictions.[54][55]
Before running the program, it is essential to build a transcriptome index, which
facilitates the eﬃcient and quick determination of transcript counts.
Then,
proceeding to generate abundance estimates for all samples, the program is run
with the mode –plaintext, which creates for each sample an abundance.tsv ﬁle
with the following columns:
• target_id: Ensembl transcript id
• length: transcript length
• eﬀ_length: a scaling of transcript length by the fragment length distribu-
tion
• est_counts: simple counts, i.e. not normalized by length and library size
• TPM : transcripts per kilobase million, refers to the TPM abundance
estimates and is a gene/transcript expression metric unit. It is a metric
for normalizing for sequence depth and gene length and the sum of all

TPMs in each sample are the same. Thus, it is easier to compare the
number of reads mapped to a gene between samples.

an estimate of the number of reads from a gene, the suggested next step is to
sum the estimated counts from the constituent transcripts of that gene.[55] That
can be done by post-processing the output of Kallisto.

Pipeline B: Gene-level quantiﬁcation
The second method used to quantify the gene expression levels of the Hugo
dataset is gene-level quantiﬁcation, i.e. counting the numbers of reads overlap-
ping with the deﬁned loci, in this case, genes.
After the quality and adaptor trimming and before the actual implementation of
the quantiﬁcation tool, is the important read alignment step. The alignment is
performed using HISAT2, an eﬃcient and fast program that can perform align-
ments to either genomes or transcriptomes. This tool produces a SAM/BAM
ﬁle for each sample.[56]
The reference genome fasta ﬁle for Human genome version 38 (91) was down-
loaded from Ensembl, to be used for the alignment. The next necessary step
is to build the index of the reference genome using the hisat2-build command.
The alignment program was run with the –dta option, which is recommended
for downstream transcript assembly.[56] At the same time, the output SAM ﬁles
from HISAT2 are piped to a SAMtools command to output ﬁnal BAM ﬁle for
each sample.[57]

quantiﬁcation, htseq-count. HTSeq is a Python package oﬀering several utilities
for processing data from high-throughput sequencing assays. htseq-count is an
HTSeq program that is used in RNA-Seq data to count how many reads map
to each gene. According to the documentation, it is important to sort the align-
ment before running htseq-count, either by position or by name. To improve
the eﬃciency of the program, the alignments are sorted by name, which will
ensure that alignment mates are close to each other in the data, hence, reducing
memory buﬀer.[58] htseq-count assumes the RNA-Seq data to be strand-speciﬁc.
This parameter can be deﬁned in the —straded option. Since no information on
strandedness was given with the raw data, visual inspection of the alignments
expressed transcripts in Integrative Genomics Viewer (IGV) browser can assist

3.2.4
Diﬀerential expression Analysis
Diﬀerential expression analysis is important to comprehend and investigate the
biological diﬀerences and pathology between two conditions, such as healthy and
diseased patients. By comparing the gene expression in the two conditions, we
can identify diﬀerentially expressed genes, i.e. genes up- or down-regulated in
each condition.[61] The diﬀerential expression analysis performed in the context
of this thesis focuses in identifying diﬀerentially expressed genes between the
response groups of patients that have received anti-PD-1 therapy.
For the detection of diﬀerentially expressed genes from RNA-seq quantiﬁcation
data, the package DESeq2 is used. The DESeq2 package uses several methods,
which are based on the negative binomial distribution of the counts, to test for
diﬀerential expression. The input of count-based statistical methods such as
DESeq2 should be a matrix of integers. The columns of this matrix refer to
samples and the rows to genes. The integer values in the matrix correspond to
counts of sequencing reads or fragments that mapped to genes in each speciﬁc
sample. An important requirement of DESeq2 is to provide raw counts, i.e.
counts that have not been normalized for library size or sequencing depth. The
statistical model implemented by the method accounts for sequencing depth
diﬀerences internally and is more eﬃcient with raw counts.[62][63]
Read counts and other intermediate quantities during statistical analysis with
DESeq2 are stored in an object class named DESeqDataSet.
This object is
coupled with a design formula. The design formula is an expression that informs
the statistical model for which known sources of variation to control, and for
which variable of interest to test during diﬀerential expression. It is inserted
with a tilde, followed by the variation factors of interest.
It is suggested to
enter the condition of interest as the last variable in the formula.[62][63] For
this project and the Hugo dataset, response to treatment is set as the source of
variation in the formula, since measuring the eﬀect of response on the expression
of genes is the main purpose of the analysis.
There are several ways to construct the DESeqDataSet object, all of which
depend on the methods used to quantify gene counts upstream of DESeq2. In
the next two Chapters, there is a description of how the quantiﬁcation data of
the two upstream pipelines were imported into DESeq2.
After the initial DESeqDataSet object is built, it is important to derive DESeq-
DataSet objects that correspond to the conditions comparison investigated, e.g.
complete response against progressive disease. As mentioned in the introduction
of Chapter 3.2 “Exploring immunosuppressive factors in anti-PD-1 therapy re-
sistant tumors”, diﬀerentially expressed (DEGs) are explored using two response

3.2.4.3
Data quality check
The purpose of this part of the diﬀerential expression analysis workﬂow is to
perform sample- and gene-level quality check on the count data.
Pre-ﬁltering the dataset
The count matrix within each DESeqDataSet object contains multiple rows with
only zeros or multiple rows with fragments counts in very few samples. In order
to make the downstream analysis faster, it is possible to reduce the size of
the object by removing these rows with little or no information on the gene
expression levels. The most simple ﬁltering applied is removing the rows of the
DESeqDataSet object that have only zero counts, or only one count across all
samples.
Samples quality control
Quality control on the sample-level is a valuable step upstream the diﬀerential
expression analysis. This step is used to evaluate the overall similarity between
the samples in the dataset. Furthermore, it can help in identifying the major
sources of variation in the dataset. Since in this project response is already
set as the major variability source, samples quality control is used to assess if
response to anti-PD-1 therapy is enough to separate the samples, i.e. how good
the samples of the same response cluster together.
Finally, through quality
check, sample outliers can be identiﬁed.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and hierarchical clustering are the two
methods used for the sample-level quality control. Generally, these two methods
are more eﬃcient for data that are homoskedastic, i.e.
data that have the
same range of variance across diﬀerent mean values. However, the expected
variance of RNA-Seq counts increases with the mean. Genes with the highest
expression levels dominate the plots produced by PCA, since these genes have
the largest absolute diﬀerences among samples. A commonly used strategy to
overcome this issue is by using the counts normalized with log2-transformation
plus a pseudocount of 1. Depending on the value of the pseudocount, very low
==PAGE:65==

tumors

normalized counts can have increased variance, adding up to the noise in the
results of PCA or hierarchical clustering.
If a small value is chosen for the
pseudocount, low count genes with values close to 0 will have a large eﬀect on
the distances between samples and PCA plots.
The DESeq2 package includes instead two transformations that are successful
in moderating these variance diﬀerences:
• the regularized log transform (rlog), which balances the variance across
the mean, hence enhancing the distances or the clustering of the samples.
This transformation is implemented through the rlog function.[62]
• the variance stabilizing transformation (VST). The vst function is used
to apply this transformation, which is for negative binomial data with a
dispersion-mean trend.[65]
These two transformations generate results similar results to the log2 transfor-
mation, for high count genes. For low count genes, the values of the normalized
counts tend to be decreased close to the genes’ averages across all samples,
which makes the count data almost homoskedastic. For this project, the rlog
transformation is more suitable, since it is considered to be more eﬃcient on
small datasets (n < 30). A comparison between the logarithmic and the rlog
transformation of the counts of the ﬁrst two samples of the Hugo dataset can
be seen in Figure 3.5. Low count genes are on the bottom left-hand corner of
the scatterplots. These genes seem to be overly variable when the logarithmic
transformation is applied. The rlog transformation shrinks the diﬀerences for
these low count genes, i.e. the genes where the data provide little information
on their diﬀerential expression.
In order to assess the overall similarity of the samples of the Hugo dataset,
hierarchical clustering based on three similarity measures is performed:
• Euclidean distance: the R function dist() is used on the rlog-transformed
counts.
• Poisson distance:
the R package PoiClaClu provides the PoissonDis-
tance() function, which is used on the non-normalized counts.
It is a
measure of dissimilarity, and takes into account the fundamental variance
structure of the raw counts when calculating the samples distances.
• Correlations: the base R function cor() calculates all pairwise combina-
tions of correlations between the samples of the dataset.

3.2.4.4
Extracting diﬀerentially expressed genes
In order to identify the diﬀerentially expressed genes, there is a ﬁnal step of the
diﬀerential expression analysis, which ﬁts the raw counts generated before into a
negative binomial model. During this process, a statistical test for diﬀerentially
expressed genes is performed, in order to determine the statistical signiﬁcance
of the diﬀerences of the mean gene expression levels of the diﬀerent response
groups. This is implemented through the DESeq() function, which performs the
following steps:
• size factor estimation, in order to control for sequencing depth diﬀerences
between the samples
• dispersion values estimation, for each gene
• ﬁnal ﬁtting of a generalized linear model
The negative binomial model predicts (estimates of) the log2 fold changes (LFC)
and the standard error values, i.e. diﬀerences between the model’s predictions
and the actual data input. The function results() of DESeq2 is used to give a
ﬁnal results table. In order to deﬁne the comparison to build a results table, the
name argument is used, for example for complete response against progressive
disease. As long as the comparison is speciﬁed with this option, there is no
need to set the order of the factor levels of the response variable in the design
formula. The results table created has the following columns:
• baseMean: average of the normalized counts divided by size factors, for
each gene across all samples
• log2FoldChange: estimation of the eﬀect size, i.e. the change in the gene’s
expression when comparing for example complete response to progressive
disease. It is in logarithmic scale to base 2.
• lfcSE: the standard error estimation for the lo2FoldChange column
• stat: the Wald statistic, i.e. the log2FoldChange divided by lfcSE, com-
pared to a standard Normal distribution to generate a two-tailed p-value
• p-value: the model performs a hypothesis test for each gene to determine
whether the observed fold change would be seen under the null hypothe-
sis. The null hypothesis postulates that there is no eﬀect of the anti-PD-1
treatment on the gene expression and that the oserved diﬀerence between
control and treatment is expected. The result of the statistical test per-
formed is the p-value.
==PAGE:68==
40
Materials and Methods
• padj : the upper bound of the expected false discovery rate (FDR), or
adjusted p-value, calculated with the Benjamini-Hochberg method.
However, the LFC estimates generated are not accurate, since they do not con-
sider the large dispersion caused by low read counts. This can result to increased
false positives, since genes with very low expression levels could be identiﬁed as
diﬀerentially expressed.
The MA plots (plotted via the plotMA() function) can be used to give an
overview of the distribution of the estimated coeﬃcients in the model. These
plots show the mean of the normalized counts versus the log2 fold changes for
all genes tested. When plotting the LFCs with MA plots, the ranges of LFCs
can be observed. If the LFCs have a very small range, for example between -1
and 1, except for the LFC values of very few genes that might be high, that
is an indication that no LFC shrinkage is necessery. That is usually expected
when using technical replicates.
To correct for this, the function lfcShrink() is used, in order to adjust the LFCs.
This function looks at the largest fold changes not generated by low counts,
which are then used to inform a prior distribution. Consequently, large fold
changes from genes with a lot of statistical information are not shrunken. The
shrinkage of fold changes provides more reliable LFCs. From the three available
shrinkage estimators for LFC, in this analysis, the apeglm estimator is used.
According to Zhu et al. (2018), "apeglm provides Bayesian shrinkage estima-
tors for eﬀect sizes in GLM models, using approximation of the posterior for
individual coeﬃcients."[66] When the counts of reads are either lowly or highly
variable in each of the conditions, or in both, the maximum likelihood estimates
for the LFCs have high variance, which leads to large estimates that are not rep-
resentative of the true diﬀerences. Using the lfcShrink() function only updates
the LFCs, but the p-values and padj values are kept the same.

genes are considered signiﬁcant:
• decreasing the false discovery rate threshold (the threshold on padj in the
results table)
• increasing the log2 fold change threshold from 0 using the lfcThreshold
argument of results() function

testing (inside the results()) and it is not correct to ﬁlter the results table post-

3.2.5
Immune Cell Proﬁling
Immune cell proﬁling is the characterization of the immune cell populations from
tissues.[67] In the context of this project, the deconvolution of immune cells is
used to evaluate if tumor inﬁltrating lymphocytes can be reliable predictors of
the response to anti-PD-1 therapy.
The method used to determine the immune cell proﬁles of the Hugo dataset
samples is CIBERSORT, which uses a machine learning algorithm called linear
support vector regression (SVR), and is applied to several cell types in the
immune cell lineage. CIBERSORT is able to deconvolute complex tissues based
on their gene expression proﬁles.[68]
The R source program can be downloaded from the CIBERSORT site after
registration.
CIBERSORT expects two input ﬁles:
• A leukocyte gene signature matrix. The developers of the method have
constructed and validated such a matrix, named LM22, which is available.
This signature matrix contains groups of genes whose expression levels
together can successfully deﬁne a signature for each pure cell population
used in the deconvolution process.
• A mixture table. This is the input ﬁle with the gene expression proﬁles of
the samples of the dataset to be analyzed. Rows correspond to genes and
columns to samples.
Preparation of the input mixture ﬁle includes:
• The mixture table should have column names the samples names. The
ﬁrst column contains the gene symbols.
• The gene expression data included in the mixture ﬁle should ideally not
be in log-linear space, and should be positive values. Thus, for the Hugo
count data of Pipeline A, the raw counts from the txi object of tximport()
functions are used. For Pipeline B, the count data directly from the htseq-
count output ﬁles are used.
Furthermore, the LM22 signature matrix has been pre-processed to update the
annotations of some of the included genes. The mixture ﬁles are constructed to
include only the genes present in the LM22 matrix.
==PAGE:72==
44
Materials and Methods
CIBERSORT runs by default in relative mode, estimating the relative fraction
of each cell type in the signature matrix, so that the sum of all fractions for the
given mixture equal to 1. In addition, an absolute mode is also provided, which is
currently in beta development, and this is chosen to run the ﬁnal deconvolution
analysis. The absolute mode measures the overall abundance of each cell type.
The absolute immune fraction score is estimated by the median expression of
all genes in the signature matrix divided by the median expression of all genes
in the mixture.[68] The reason for choosing the absolute mode is because the
relative fractions of the immune cells are not suitable for comparing between
the samples. CIBERSORT is able to deconvolute mixtures for 22 types (and
subtypes) of immune cells.
CIBERSORT is run with the following inputs and parameters:
• updated LM22 signature matrix
• mixture ﬁle
• 500 permutations. It is suggested to use > 100
• Quantile normalization of input mixture set to false, since input data are
RNA-Seq data
• absolute mode

• absolute immune score for each immune cell type in the mixture
• p-value estimations. The null hypothesis is that no cell type of the LM22
matrix is present in the mixture. The null hypothesis is tested using Monte
Carlo sampling and the produced p-value is the Pearson product-moment
correlation.
• statistical signiﬁcance of the results of the deconvolution across all cell
types.
• the RMS error, which is the root mean squared error between the original
mixture and the estimated mixture.
• absolute score

3.2.6
Functional Pathway Analysis
The functional pathway analysis is used to uncover the functions and pathways
that might be enriched or over-represented in a set of genes. The diﬀerentially
expressed genes determined from the DEA or all the genes of the transcrip-
tomes with their expression changes are used to yield statistically enriched /
over-represented biological processes and pathways.
The functional pathway
analysis applied can be classiﬁed into two types: 1. over-representation analy-
sis (ORA) and 2.gene-set enrichment analysis (GSEA). Both of these analyses
are implemented using Gene Ontology (GO) terms and Kyoto Encyclopedia of
Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways.
The over-representation analysis requires an input list of genes and a background
set of genes, while the gene-set enrichment analysis requires the fold changes of
all the genes as well. In ORA, in order to determine over- or under-represented
pathways or GO terms, the frequencies between the input gene list and the
background set (for example all the genes of human genome) are compared.
This process is called hypergeometric testing. To perform the hypergeometric
test, we calculate the probability of having the observed proportion of genes
associated with a speciﬁc GO term in gene list, based on the proportion of genes
associated with the same GO term in the background set. It is the probability
distribution that describes the probability of e.g. 50 genes(k) being associated
with a speciﬁc GO term for all genes in the gene list (n=500) from a population
of all the genes in the entire background set (N=18000), which contains 94
genes(K) that have GO biological process annotation of the speciﬁc GO term.
All the genes detected from RNA-Seq are used as input background gene set list,
which eﬃciently eliminates biases introduced if the genes of the whole genome
are used.[69]
Gene-set enrichment analysis on the other side can identify modest but coordi-
nated changes in the gene expression levels of a gene list that otherwise could
be ignored. Thus, all the genes with their respective log fold changes can be
used, and not only the signiﬁcant DEGs. The GSEA algorithm contains several
steps, such as the calculation of an enrichment score (whether a group of genes
is over-represented at the bottom or top of a ranked by expression gene list),
permutations to assess the signiﬁcance of the relevant phenotype and adjust-
ment for multiple hypothesis testing. The tool used to perform these analyses
is clusterProﬁler.[69]
In both functional pathway analyses implemented the Benjamini–Hochberg false
discovery rate (FDR) method was used to calculate the p-adjusted values. The
q-values used in clusterProﬁler are the p-adjusted values calculated to correct
for multiple testing and the q-value cutoﬀis set to reduce false positives in

Pre-processing
The gene expression data for each dataset need to be normalized and ﬁltered as

TCGA-SKCM dataset: RNA-Seq
For the TCGA-SKCM dataset, the gene expression data are given in FPKM
values, a metric of RNA-Seq expression values. FPKM stands for Fragments
Per Kilobase Million and is a metric suitable for paired-end RNA-Seq data.
When using FPKM normalized data, the sum of the reads in each sample may
be diﬀerent, hence it is hard to compare the expression values of genes between
samples. A more appropriate metric is Transcripts Per Kilobase Million (TPM).
The order of operations in calculating TPM values are:
1. First, division of the read counts by the length of the gene in kilobases,
which produces the reads per kilobase (RPK).

2. Calculation of the per million scaling factor by counting up all RPK values
in each sample and dividing it by 106.
3. Division of RPK values by the per million scaling factor.
The sum of all gene TPM values in a sample equals to 1, and this sum is the same
for all samples in a dataset. Thus, it is easier to compare the the proportion of
reads that mapped to a gene between samples.[74][75]

to TPM values with the following formula[75]:
TPMi = (FPKMi/sum(FPKMj) ∗106

3.3.4
Filtering for genes encoding secreted and membrane

The resulting outputs of the previous step of the analysis are tables of genes
highly correlated to a gene or a gene ratio of interest in samples with a speciﬁc
T-cell inﬁltration. The purpose of this step is to ﬁlter these genes, for each
of the settings explored, in order to extract those that encode cytokines or
other secreted and plasma membrane proteins. Furthermore, in this thesis it
is attempted to ﬁlter for genes that encode proteins of unknown annotation in
terms of subcellular location.

Databases collection and processing
Initially. two databases that include information on secreted and membrane
proteins are downloaded: (a) UniProt (b) Human protein Atlas.
These two
databases complement each other and are used to create derived databases of
interest that are required for the ﬁltering.
All available entries for Homo Sapiens, reviewed and unreviewed were down-

From the UniProt database three main databases are derived:[77][78]
• Cytokines database constructed by extracting entries that have the fol-
lowing GO terms:
– GO:0005125: cytokine activity
– GO:0008009: chemokine activity
• Secreted proteins database constructed by extracting entries that have the

– GO:0005576: extracellular region
– GO:0070062: extracellular exosome
– GO:0005615: extracellular space
– GO:0030141: secretory granule
– GO:1990182: exosomal secretion
• Plasma membrane proteins database constructed by extracting entries

– GO:0005886: plasma membrane
– GO:0009897: external side of plasma membrane
In addition, a database of "unknown" proteins is built. The ambiguous term
"unknown" is chosen in this study to describe entries in the UniProt database
that have no annotation for subcellular location or GO term for cellular com-
ponent. However, in UniProt, there are diﬀerent entry IDs associated with the
same primary gene name. The ﬁltering performed later is based on the primary
gene names (HGNC symbol), thus, the fact that multiple entries pointing to the
same gene creates an important issue. As mentioned in the UniProt site, when-
ever an entry is reviewed, it receives a unique entry name when it is integrated
into Uniprot/Swiss-Prot. Due to consistency reasons, some entries might get
a new entry ID, so that for example similar entries have similar names. An-
other reason for changing the entry ID is when an entry has ﬁnally fully curated
records.[79] As a result, for one speciﬁc gene symbol, there can be several en-
tries, some of them fully annotated and some not. To deal with this issue, a
database of "known" proteins is constructed from the initial UniProt database.
This database, includes all entries that have subcellular location annotation or
GO annotation for cellular component. Then, the "unknown" proteins database
is compared against the "known" to exclude entries that would be false posi-
tives. Finally the "unknown" proteins database is also compared against the

cytokines, membrane and secreted databases, to ensure that no entry included
there will be present in the "unknown" proteins database.
It is important to note that all entries in the diﬀerent UniProt-derived databases
are ordered by annotation score, from highest to lowest. The annotation score
is a measure of the annotation content of the entry. Although this score should
not be used as a measure of accuracy of the annotated entry, it can be used
to obtain the best literature-based curated entries. The entries in UniProt are
annotated based on a 5-point-system.[79]
The Human Protein Atlas (HPA) is a knowledge resource that consists of three
parts: 1) the Tissue Atlas dealing with how proteins are distributed in all tissues
and organs in humans 2) the Cell Atlas focusing on the subcellular localization
of proteins in single cells and 3) Pathology Atlas focusing on protein levels’
eﬀect on cancer patients’ survival. For this thesis, Cell Atlas is used, since it
contains the human secretome and membrane proteome. Both the secretome
and proteome were constructed using several prediction methods for membrane
protein topology or signal peptides. HPA oﬀers the databases for membrane and
secreted proteins, which include annotations for predicted subcellular location.
From the HPA membrane proteins database, a derived database is built with
only the proteins predicted to be plasma membrane proteins. To enhance the
ﬁltering of the tables of genes with the HPA-derived data there are some extra
pre-processing steps applied:
• Gene data information such as Ensembl ID, HGNC symbol are retrieved
using the biomaRt package for the genes included in the gene expression
matrix of the dataset.
• Ensembl IDs for the genes included in the HPA-derived database are ex-
tracted based on the gene data information from biomaRt.
The HPA-
derived databases already include a column with Ensembl IDs, though
several are from a diﬀerent Ensembl version than the ones included in the
datasets.
• Comparison of the Ensembl IDs from the gene expression matrix to the
Ensembl IDs extracted for the HPA-derived databases.
• For the matching Ensembl IDs, the HGNC gene symbols of the gene
expression matrix are inserted into a new column in the HPA-derived
databases.
• Comparison of the original Ensembl IDs included in the HPA databases
with the Ensembl IDs from the gene expression matrix. For those that
are common, the HGNC gene symbol from the gene expression matrix is
added to the new column (created before) in the HPA-derived databases.

3.3.4.2
Extracting genes encoding secreted, membrane and unknown
proteins
The algorithm written to ﬁlter the tables of genes with any of the Uniprot-
derived databases looks for the presence of each gene from the table provided
in the derived databases. Since there can be several entries (annotated) in the
UniProt-derived entries for that gene, the search stops when the top hit is found.
This corresponds to the best-annotated UniProt entry.
Each of the gene tables is ﬁltered against all the UniProt- or HPA-derived
databases, and a ﬁnal table showing for each gene if it encodes a cytokine/chemo-
kine, secreted or plasma membrane protein, is produced. This ﬁnal table is also
ﬁltered to exclude genes that have zero expression.

4.2
Cytotoxic T lymphocytes and CD4+ T-cells
eﬀector mechanisms in melanoma
During this study, four melanoma datasets of primary and metastatic tumor
gene expression data are used to investigate the eﬀector functions of CTLs and
CD4+ T-cells in melanoma cancer immunity. By applying CIBERSORT to the
gene expression mixtures of these datasets, I extract samples with high CD8+
T-cell (CTLs) or high CD4+ and low CD8+ T-cell inﬁltration. This results in
acquiring the gene expression proﬁles of the datasets, limited to tumor samples
with high abundances of the immune cell in question. The next step involves
the calculation of gene expression correlations of all genes with the gene/gene
ratios chosen to be indicative of the respective T-cell:
• For CTLs: CD8A, CD8B genes and CD8A/CD4, CD8B/CD4 gene ratios.
• For CD4+ T-cells: CD4 gene, CD4/CD8A, CD4/CYT, CD4/CYT/CD8A
gene ratios.
Finally, the lists of genes generated by setting a Pearson correlationthreshold
above 0.6 are ﬁltered against the UniProt and Human Protein Atlas derived
databases. This ﬁltering step serves the purpose of selecting for those genes
encoding cytokines/chemokines, other secreted or membrane proteins, as well
as unknown subcellular location proteins.
Heatmaps displaying the correlations of gene expression for all pairwise combi-
nations of samples in all datasets are available in Figure B.21-Figure B.24 in
Appendix B.2 “CTLs and CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions”.

Chapter 5
Discussion
To learn more about the innate resistance mechanisms of melanoma tumors
in anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, I analyzed the transcriptomes of 28 melanoma
samples (Hugo et al, 2016), in order to identify the diﬀerentially expressed genes
between responding and non-responding tumors. The transcript- and gene-level
quantiﬁcation of the RNA-Seq data was performed by employing two diﬀerent
methods, Kallisto (Pipeline A) and htseq-count (Pipeline B).
The analysis revealed that the transcriptomes of the progressive disease tu-
mors seem to be dominated by relative gene up-expression events, when com-
pared to those of responsive tumors. Among the gene revealed using Pipeline
A and aggregating complete and partial response samples, STC1 overexpression
has been shown to have an oncogenic role in breast and hepatocellular carci-
noma and promote tumor growth, thus investigated as a potential therapeutic
target.[80][81] Inhibin INHBA gene has been reportedly involved in tumor inva-
sion and metastasis, as well as mesenchymal transition in lung adenocarcinoma,
gastric and breast cancer, when upregulated.[82][83] Furthermore, a recent study
summarized the revealed mechanisms of FOXC2 in cancer, associating FOXC2
expression with cancer cells overproliferation and tumor angiogenesis. FOXC2
promotes Epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) by regulating cadherings
and kinases.[84] The matrix metallopeptidases found (MMP1, MMP13) have
been shown to participate in several tumor-favorable processes, such as mi-
gration, proliferation and angiogenesis. MMP-1 promotes local tumor growth
==PAGE:116==
88
Discussion
and metastasis in breast cancer and up-regulated MMP-13 promotes tumor
invasion and angiogenesis in several cancers.[85][86] The DEA performed us-
ing Pipeline A, between complete response versus progressive disease samples
revealed two cancer-testis genes, MAGEL2 and PAGE2, which are both up-
regulated and could potentially be interesting for further investigation, since
cancer-testis genes are considered very good immunotherappy candidates.[87]
Cancer-testis gene MAGEL2 was found up-regulated in non-responding tumors
as well, when implementing Pipeline B and comparing complete response ver-
sus progressive disease tumors samples. Moreover, several pseudogenes and long
intergenic noncoding RNAs (lincRNAs) genes were identiﬁed as diﬀerentially ex-
pressed, by using both Pipelines. However, their role was not explored further
during this study.
The implementation of the two pipelines yielded diﬀerent results, and only 5
common DEGs were found when complete and partial response samples were
aggregated.
Comparing the results of the pipelines when complete response
samples were analyzed against non-responding tumors, showed 20 shared genes.
Overall, and stated with caution, since feedback from the CCIT group is still
pending, the results produced using Pipeline A seem to be of more value in
addressing the question of the study. Although transcript-level quantiﬁcation
methods, such as Kallisto are considered much less accurate than gene-level
quantiﬁcation methods, such as htseq-count, it appears that using alingment-
independent counting methods (Kallisto) and then aggregating to gene-level is
much better. It has been argued before that taking advantage of transcript-level
abundance estimates when determining gene-level expression levels enhances the
diﬀerential gene expression results.[64]
I additionally compared the results of both pipelines with the corresponding
results of the Hugo et al. study. In the Hugo et. al study, Tophat2 was used
to perform the alignment of the trasncriptomes, cuﬀquant and cuﬀnorm were
used for quantiﬁcation and normalization of the counts. Diﬀerentially expressed
genes (in total 3) were deﬁned using the two-sided Mann-Whitney test (p <
0.1) and setting the FDR as p < 0.25 and fold change at least two-fold between
the response groups. Only one gene (CDH1: E-cadherin) was shared among
the study results and my two pipelines, in the case of aggregated complete and
partial response samples.
It is expected when using diﬀerent alignment and
quantiﬁcation methods to get diﬀerent results. Additionally, the DEGs revealed
in this thesis were deﬁned using very strict FDR and LFC thresholds.
Additionally, the characterization of the immune cell proﬁles in the response
groups of the Hugo dataset produced contradicting results. The diﬀerences in
abundances of CD4+ memory resting (higher in complete response), macrophages
M0 (higher in partial response), macrophages M1 (higher in complete response),
macrophages M2 (higher in complete response) are identiﬁed to be statistically

signiﬁcant when complete response samples are compared against partial re-
sponse tumors, using Pipeline A (p < 0.05). The result for M1 macrophages is in
accordance with clinical studies that conclude that M1 macrophages contribute
to immune defense against tumors, have high antitumor cytotoxic activity and
are associated with favorable. Higher inﬁltration of M2 in complete response
is opposite to what has been found in previous studies, where M2 is associated

Eosinophils were also found to have increased inﬁltration in responding tumors

The diﬀerences in abundances of tumor inﬁltrating lymphocytes between the
response groups, across the two implemented pipelines are inconsistent, creating
the need to employ other deconvolution methods as well. Thus, the results as
they are cannot be fully trusted to infer the prognostic role of the immune cell
type in the response of patients to anti-PD-1 therapy.
Through functional analysis of DEGs generated by both pipelines, the DDR1
gene was found to be enriched in multiple extracellular structure matrix organi-
zation/disassembly processes, as well as protein remodelling complexes, in non-
responding tumors. A previous study has shown that DDR1, which is a Receptor
Tyrosine Kinase, is overexpressed in many cancers and promotes tumor invasion
and metastasis.[88] Moreover, the pathway enrichment analysis revealed several
up-expressed genes and two down-expressed (VTN, BUB1B-PAK6) involved in
the ECM-receptor interaction pathway and Focal adhesion pathway, suggesting
the critical role of these DEGs in the metastasis of melanoma. All ECM- and
focal adhesion-associated proteins have an important role in tumor progression
and cell motillity and tumor survival and diﬀerentiation.[89][90][91][92] Genes
such as LAMA2, LAMC3 and genes encoding chains of the collagen protein
have been revealed by other studies to be diﬀerentially expressed and involved
in ECM-receptor and cell-adhesion pathways in melanoma cancer.[93]
By exploring the KEGG pathways represented by the DEGs, ECM-receptor in-
teraction, neuroactive ligand-receptor interaction, IL-17 signaling and GABAer-
gic synapse pathways were found enriched using Pipeline B. The neuroactive
ligand-receptor interaction signaling pathway has been linked with progression
of prostate, bladder cancer and renal cell carcinoma and its blockade is consid-
ered a promising therapeutic approach. This pathway has also been associated
with metastatic melanoma.[94][95] My results are in some part in accordance
with the functional enrichment analysis conducted in the Hugo et al. study,
where the identiﬁed DEGs were also enriched for cell-adhesion, ECM organiza-

Molecules, functions and pathways associated with CTLs
Based on the knowledge that CTLs are a major component of the immune me-
diated cancer cell elimination, I explored the mechanisms by which these cells
induce the killing of cancer cells. Four melanoma datasets were used and the
analysis applied was designed in collaboration with the CCIT group at Herlev
Hospital. By subsetting the gene expression data of the datasets to samples
with high CD8+ T-cell inﬁltration, correlating the gene expressions with the
expression of CD8+ T-cell markers CD8A and CD8B and ﬁltering for secreted,
membrane and unknown subcellular location proteins, several molecules, poten-
tially produced by CTLs were determined and were shared among all datasets.
As expected, cytotoxic molecules such as GZMA, GZMK, GZMH and PRF1
were identiﬁed and present across the datasets, though PRF1 was only identiﬁed
when primary tumor samples were included in the analysis.ANDERSEN200632
In contrast with other granzymes, GZMA has been characterized to participate
in proinﬂammatory processes and promote cancer progression, yet its expres-
sion has been associated with favorable clinical outcome in melanoma.[96][97][98]
Across all datasets and cohorts, CD27 molecule was found, which is a member of
the Tumor necrosis factor (TNF) receptor superfamily, important in the stimula-
tion of CD8+ T-cells and is being explored as a target in immunotherapy.[99][100]
In addition, the analysis identiﬁed the gene encoding the Lag-3 inhibitory molecule
when primary tumor samples were considered.
This molecule acts as an in-
hibitor of CD8+ eﬀector T cells and improves the immunosuppressive activity of
Tregs.Grosso2007[101] FASLG, a member of the TNF ligand superfamily mem-
ber, was among the resulting genes when primary tumor cohorts were included
in the analysis, and it is known to be innvolved in the cytotoxic T-cell-mediated
apoptosis.[102]. Furthermore, both immune checkpoint ligands CD80 and CD86
were identiﬁed to be associated with CD8+ T-cells, which are known for their
role in the priming of T-cells.[102].
Through the functional enrichment analysis, genes enriched in biological pro-
cesses, such as leukocyte cell-cell adhesion, positive regulation of cell-cell ad-
hesion, antigen receptor-mediated signaling and antigen processing and presen-
==PAGE:119==
91
tation of exogenous peptide antigens, were suggestive of the direct or indirect
mechanismss CTLs employ to kill cancer cells. Over-represented cellular com-
ponents of the identiﬁed genes associated with CD8+ T-cell eﬀector functions
include the external side of plasma membrane and MHC protein, or plasma
membrane receptor complexes, as well as immunological synapse, which are crit-
ical in the recognition and interplay between cancer cells and CTLs.[102] These
results, and the genes involved in these processes or cellular components, can
reveal new eﬀector molecules that play an important role in immune-mediated
tumor killing. Most of the identiﬁed over-represented KEGG pathways, across
all datasets and cohorts included cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, Th1
and Th2 cell diﬀerentiation, chemokine signaling pathway, antigen processing
and presentation pathways; which are fundamental in eliminating cancer cells
by CD8+ T-cells.
Molecules, functions and pathways associated with CD4+ Tcells
The ﬁnal major question addressed in this thesis was the eﬀector functions, and
potential immunosuppressive activity of CD4+ T-cells in melanoma. The same
datasets and methodology steps as for CTLs were applied to discover eﬀector
molecules associated with CD4+ T-cells, though the design of the analysis fo-
cused on these immune cells. By subsetting the gene expression data of the
datasets to samples with high CD4+ and low CD8+ T-cell inﬁltration, correlat-
ing the gene expressions with the expression of CD4+ T-cell markers CD4, as
well as with gene ratios of CD4 with CYT and CD8A, in order to ﬁlter out other
immune cells whose activity has been linked with CD8A and CYT. Then ﬁlter-
ing for secreted, membrane and unknown subcellular location proteins, several
molecules, potentially related to CD4+ T-cells were determined.
No shared genes were found across all the datasets, however, functional and
enrichment analysis was applied to investigate the genes in metastatic and pri-
mary tumor samples, separately.
In neither of the cases genes encoding cy-
tokines we found. The lipid droplet protein perilipin 2 (PLIN2) identiﬁed in
primary tumors has been found to be a novel prognostic marker in clear cell
renal cell carcinoma patients, associated with favorable clinical outcome. Yet,
it should be noted that is was shown to be expressed in cancer tissues and not
be a product of immune response.[103] The CSK gene identiﬁed encodes a non-
receptor tyrosine kinase, important in T-cell activation, as well as cell growth,
diﬀerentiation, migration regulation, however its role has been implicated in
tumor-induced T-cell suppression.[104] Interestingly, the CD36 gene found in
primary tumor samples has been reportedly involved in mediating Tregs thymic
development.[105] Based on the analysis, this gene is associated with CD4+
T-cell eﬀector functions, and this could suggest a possible immunosuppressive
activity of CD4+ T-cells through this gene. Another gene identiﬁed in primary
tumor cohorts was programmed cell death 2 (PDCD2). A 2015 study has sug-
==PAGE:120==

gested that this gene might be involved in in tumor suppression in osteosarcoma,
and the corresponding mechanism can be associated with CD4+ Tcells.[106]
In metastatic tumor samples, IGFB5 was identiﬁed, which previous studies have
shown it participates in the migration of immune cells and can activate the
transformation of monocytes into mesenchymal cells.[107] Mesenchymal cells
are considered T-cell suppressive, so this could be suggestive of an immuno-
suppressive function of CD4+ T-cells.[108]. Furthermore the NPNT gene was
determined to be associated with CD4+ T-cells, which is member of the epider-

Functional GO BP analysis, yielded no common biological processes enriched
among primary and metastatic tumors. In primary tumor samples aggrephagy,
protein localization to cell periphery and plasma membrane were enriched. Ag-

unfolded or aggregated proteins. This process can be favorable to tumor survival
in advanced cancer, however the crosslink between autophagy and immune cell
response has not been elucidated.[110] Over-represented cellular components in
genes generated from the analysis of metastatic tumor samples, revealed genes
associated with CD4+ T-cell eﬀector functions include the extracellular matrix,
endoplasmic reticulum lumen, plasma membrane and several insulin-like growth

over-represented pathways in metastatic tumors, and the genes involved could
be explored to investigate the CD4+ T-cell interaction with target cancer cells
or other immune cells.
Future perspectives
The results from the analysis conducted in this study have already been passed
on to the Center for Cancer Immune Therapy (CCIT) group at Herlev Hospital,
for manual curation of each of the genes provided. Ideally, some of the genes
will be chosen to be further validated. Additional analysis strategies consid-
ered for this project are diﬀerential co-expression analysis to identify hubs of
genes what are closely co-expressed and comparison of the biological themes
suggested by the interesting gene clusters. Furthermore the construction and
analysis of the protein–protein interaction (PPI) network and the sub-network
of the diﬀerentially expressed genes among responding and non responding tu-
mors could contribute to the identiﬁcation of innate resistance mechanisms in
==PAGE:121==
93
anti-PD-1 therapy. Future plans related to anti-PD-1 resistance mechanisms
include applying the current developed pipelines to other melanoma datasets.
In addition, implementing the same pipeline to several subsets of the datasets
explored, such as for samples with high cytolytic activity. This analysis has
already been completed for the Hugo et al. dataset, but due to time and length
limitations was not included in this thesis.
The investigation of CTLs and CD4+ T-cells therapy proved to be more diﬃcult
than expected, particularly when trying to determine genes related to CD4+ T-
cell eﬀector functions. Diﬀerent designs for the bioinformatics analysis workﬂow
have been suggested for this part of the project, such as exploring genes encod-
ing secreted and membrane proteins that are highly correlated with CD8A and
CD8B genes in samples with high expression of those genes or including the
inﬁltration of NK cells as a factor to identify samples with high cytolytic ac-
tivity. The ﬁrst analysis has already been completed, though it is not included
in this thesis. Moreover, incorporation of additional methods for immune cell
proﬁling was suggested, such as the Danaher or TIMER method, with TIMER
been reported to perform better than CIBERSORT.[112] Both have been im-
plemented to acquire samples with high CD8+ T-cell inﬁltration upstream the
ﬁltering for secreted and membrane proteins step. Yet again, the results when
these two methods were used are not part of this thesis. Exploring the eﬀector
functions of CD4+ T-cells necessitates the formulation of a diﬀerent analysis
strategy, in order to ﬁlter out random genes showing up or to ﬁlter out genes
associated with other immune cells. Moreover, it is essential to improve the
UniProt- and HPA-derived databases, which, although suﬃced in the context
of this study, need to be reﬁned in order for the ﬁltering step of the analysis to
be more accurate.
One of the limitations of this study was in some cases, the inability to produce
similar results across the diﬀerent datasets, when the same analysis steps were
applied. Thus, it is important to assess the quality of the datasets acquired
for the analysis. An approach to this has been suggested by Parmigiani et al
(2004), and is the correlation of correlations, i.e. datasets cross-correlations of
the pairwise correlation of the genes expression.[113]

Chapter 6
Conclusions
In this study, ﬁrst the failure pathways of PD-1 blockade in melanoma patients
are investigated. As more than half of patients do not respond at all in anti-
PD-1 therapy, elucidating the mechanisms of response that potential predic-
tive response biomarkers is necessary. Diﬀerential expression analysis combined
with functional and pathway enrichment analysis was applied in a dataset of
pre-treatment tumor samples. My analysis has revealed genes involved in tu-
mor invasion, angiogenesis and proliferation as well as two cancer-testis genes.
Exploring the biological processes enriched, extracellular matrix organization,
remodelling and disassembly were over-represented in non-responding tumors,
while several genes were enriched in ECM-receptor interaction and focal adhe-
sion pathways, which play a key role in melanoma metastasis and progression.
The characterization of immune cell populations in the tumors, did not yield
consistent results between the two pipelines investigated, nor with the results
produced by other studies, thus creating the need for implementing the devel-
oped pipelines in other datasets and using other deconvolution methods.
Furthermore, this study includes the investigation of immune-mediated tumor
killing by CD8+ T-cells and the eﬀector functions of CD4+ T-cells. The ana-
lytical steps performed, aimed to identify samples with high inﬁltration of the
T-cell in question and to determine genes, whose expression is highly correlated
with known surface markers of those cells ( CD8A, CD8B and CD4) and that
encode cytokines/chemokines, other secreted, membrane and unknown subcel-

lular location proteins. Four melanoma datasets were acquired for this analysis,
that consist of either primary or metastatic tumor samples, or both. Several
genes encoding cytotoxic and inhibitory molecules, as well as other immune
checkpoint ligands or signaling molecules important for CTLs exerting their
tumor-killing properties were identiﬁed. The underlying biology of those genes
and the pathways they participate were investigated, resulting in enriched cell-
cell adhesion and antigen processing and antigen receptor-mediated signaling
processes, as well as cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction and chemokine sig-
naling pathways, indicative of the direct and indirect mechanisms of cancer cells
elimination by those cells. There were no cytokines found to be associated with
CD4+ T-cells eﬀector functions in samples with high CD4+ and low CD8+ T-
cell inﬁltration. Yet, there were some genes found to be associated with T-cell
activation and diﬀerentiation, as well as some genes that should be investigated
for their immunosuppressive role. The results of the analysis for CD4+ T-cells
demonstrate the need to design the bioinformatics analysis in a more CD4+
T-cell speciﬁc manner, in order to be able to generate more reasonable results.
