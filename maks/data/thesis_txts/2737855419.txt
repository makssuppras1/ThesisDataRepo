1. Introduction  
Lobster aquaculture first emerged as a release aquaculture of larvae or juvenile for fisheries rather 
than a commercial production. Larviculture experiments of clawed lobsters, Homarus gammarus 
and Homarus americanus, started in the middle of the 19th century (Nicosia & Lavalli, 1999). 
Hatcheries of H. gammarus started to be implemented especially in Norway and the United 
Kingdom where eggs were hatch from wild broodstock females (Wickins & Lee, 2008). The main 
goal of these hatcheries was for stock enhancement or restocking purposes by releasing hatchery 
produced juveniles as a supplement to existing fisheries or to depleted fisheries due to the major 
decline in productivity experienced by wild fisheries (Burton, 2001; Ellis et al., 2015). In the 1970s 
and 1980s, longer term culture of H. americanus juveniles remained unsuccessful at large scale 
due to slow growth, high operating costs and technical difficulties (Hinchcliffe et al., 2022). Years 
later, aquaculture of clawed lobster received more interest and a number of research, technical 
development and innovations made major advances on the techniques and adequate methods to 
farm lobster. However, farms closing the production cycle of European lobster and American 
lobster, from egg to adult commercial size, are scarce. Only one company (Norwegian lobster 
farm) managed to produce H. gammarus using an advanced recirculating system with robotic and 
automated system for feeding, sorting or cleaning (Drengstig & Bergheim, 2013). Thus, even 
though a lot of technical advances have improved quality and production of lobsters, gaps in 
knowledge remain to efficiently produce a lobster and become viable economically. Low survival 
rates, slow-growing individuals and high operation costs make lobster farming difficult to develop 
(Hinchcliffe et al., 2022). Cannibalism, water quality and diet represent the most important 
parameters contributing to mortality in lobster aquaculture. While cannibalism and water quality 
have been controlled, diets and nutrition in this sector are still not fully comprehended. No 
commercial diet has yet been produced for clawed lobsters as many gaps in the literature of lobster 
nutrition exist.  
Therefore, this review aims to summarize current diet used in clawed lobster aquaculture (H. 
gammarus and H. americanus only) as well as explaining the challenges in formulating a feed, 
identifying nutritional requirements and selecting relevant additives or attractants. This review is 
not an extensive review and will help to get an overall picture of formulated feed and diets in 
lobster farming rather than a complete list of all possible formulated diets or supplements tested 
in the lobster aquaculture industry.

2. Current diets used in lobster aquaculture and future 
perspective 
 
2.1. 
Natural diets in wild lobsters 
In the wild, clawed lobsters have a mixed diet. Adult H. americanus natural diet consists of 
crustaceans (small crabs), molluscs (limpets and bivalves like mussels, scallops or clams), 
echinoderms (sea stars and sea urchins), fish, macroalgae and polychaetes in various amount 
depending on seasons and areas (Hanson, 2009; Lawton & Lavalli, 1995; Sainte-Marie, 2001). 
Indeed, stomach analysis of American lobsters in mixed habitats showed different diets between 
soft and hard substrate. In hard substrate like rocky reefs or boulder areas, lobsters tend to forage 
on flesh, rock crabs and molluscs (especially mussels) while lobsters from sandy environment will 
eat more crustaceans, polychaetes and fish remains (Hanson, 2009). This mixed diet is mainly due 
to the availability of certain preys in different areas rather than a selective selection of more or 
less crustaceans in one place than another. In addition, adult American lobsters also appear to eat 
other conspecifics, pagurids (hermit crabs) or coralline algae in small proportions (Sainte-Marie, 
2001). In fact, food items that adult lobsters eat is relative to their size and availability where adult 
individuals would be able to eat larger preys than larvae and juveniles due to their higher mobility 
and size, they will also be able to crush or hunt on different preys than smaller individuals (Elner 
& Campbell, 1987; Juinio & Cobb, 1992; Sainte-Marie, 2001). Besides, depending on their needs 
they will actively select food items to meet their requirements. For instance, moulting individuals 
will be more prone to prey on food items high in calcium to help remineralization of their 
exoskeleton (Lawton & Lavalli, 1995). Likewise, ovigerous females will select more lipid-rich 
and nutritious preys like invertebrate species such as polychaete worms, echinoderms, crabs, 
bryozoans and beetles (Goldstein & Shields, 2018). This is called maternal nutrition as the 
selection of these particular species have an important impact on egg quality in ovigerous lobsters. 
Therefore, lobsters have an extensive diet depending on their needs, locations, age or sex.  
 
2.2. 
Food in current lobster aquaculture and the potential for 
formulated feed 
Food used in aquaculture tries to mimic the natural diet of lobsters for optimal growth and foraging 
success. However, some food items are difficult to access, labor intensive or expensive. Wet, dry 
and live feeds using krill, mussels, Artemia or mysids are typical examples in clawed lobster 
==PAGE:13==
Page | 13  
 
aquaculture (Browne, 2009). Wet feed has been reduced and replaced by live feed because of 
problems with water quality and convenience (Hinchcliffe et al., 2022). Indeed, wet feeds are diets 
made from processed ingredients such as fish flesh, bait, waste or crustaceans which leach 
completely into the water when delivered, greatly altering water quality. Thus, live feeds 
(especially brine shrimp, Artemia salina) were gradually preferred to replace wet feed as it shows 
a higher survival of lobsters and easier storage and delivery (Hinchcliffe et al., 2022). Live feeds 
also provide a visual stimulus and promote natural instinct of lobster larvae, improving activity by 
establishing a predator-prey interaction. Live or frozen Artemia are mainly used for larval rearing 
in lobster hatcheries. Many studies found similar results of live Artemia being a good and 
nutritious food source for rearing lobster with higher growth rates and satisfying survival rates 
(Bordner et al., 1986; Fiore & Tlusty, 2005; Thériault & Pernet, 2007). However, they are not 
100% efficient since some dietary efficiencies exist in Artemia with too low proportions of 
essential fatty acids (Gendron et al., 2013). Problems with live feeds are also a substantial cost and 
a labor-intensive management for daily and fresh Artemia. Other issues like a limited shelf-life or 
a risk for pathogens and diseases with bacterial development can limit the use of live feeds 
(Hinchcliffe et al., 2022; Radhakrishnan et al., 2019). Because of that, commercial lobster farms 
struggled to be cost-effective and the development of cheaper and easier alternatives for feeding 
started to emerge with the creation of formulated feeds.  
 
2.3. 
The development of formulated feeds to provide a balance and 
mixed diet 
Formulated feeds can provide a more balanced diet that is easily accessible compared to live feed. 
Hatching Artemia, krill and other species to create a mixed “wild” diet represents more work than 
just giving a pellet formulated with each of these ingredients. Besides, the diet of lobsters can 
significantly affect their overall health and susceptibility to pathogens (Cawthorn, 2011). Thus, 
understanding benefits or detrimental effects of certain diets is crucial for a viable and cost-
effective farm. An unbalanced diet in lobsters can impact their wealth by not supplementing them 
with nutritional requirements making them more sensitive to some diseases. For American 
lobsters, shell disease is a recurrent problem in lobsters’ fisheries. In fact, a diet made completely 
with herring (Clupea harengus) has been confirmed to induce shell disease in American lobsters 
(Quinn et al., 2012; Tlusty et al., 2008). In Canada and USA, herring used as bait for lobster cages 
became the main food source of lobsters, jeopardizing lobster’s health (Tlusty et al., 2008). Thus, 
lobsters only feeding on one single item like fish, especially with a low-quality food such as bait, 
==PAGE:14==
Page | 14  
 
have more chance to develop a disease and low survival rates. A study from Tlusty et al. (2008) 
confirmed the benefits of a mixed diet compared to a single ingredient diet where lobsters fed a 
100% fish diet died by contracting shell disease, while lobsters fed a mixed diet had higher survival 
rates and lower probability of dying. Another study also recorded issues due to a one ingredient 
diet with frozen Artemia (Fiore & Tlusty, 2005). In this study, larvae of lobsters only fed frozen 
Artemia did not survive metamorphosis and experienced a number of molting issues. This was 
mainly due to the low quality of frozen Artemia leading to phospholipid degradation and  to a 
nutritional insufficiency affecting molting larvae (Fiore & Tlusty, 2005). Thus, formulated feeds 
are convenient to use, can offer a balance and mixed diet and represent an alternative to poor 
quality food types. Therefore, commercial Artemia replacement (CAR) started to emerge for 
cheaper and easier alternatives to live feeds. CAR first started as supplements rather than a 
complete diet replacement. The first successful rearing of American lobster with artificial feeds 
was possible with CAR (called Artemac) where they achieved a similar survival rate for larvae to 
metamorphosis compared to frozen Artemia (Fiore & Tlusty, 2005). However, this CAR alone did 
not overcome live Artemia results due to phospholipids deficiencies and has better results when 
combined with live feeds (Fiore & Tlusty, 2005). Thus, to effectively create a formulated feed, 
nutritional requirements of lobster need to be identified for healthy lobster production and prevent 
detrimental health issues from a poor and unbalanced diet.  
 
2.3.1. Nutritional requirements for different life stages 
Identification of nutritional requirements and suitable feeds for all of the different life stages of 
lobster remains complicated as lobsters will need a more or less different diet to meet nutritional 
demands to properly develop during each phase they will undergo during their life. Lobsters 
develop from eggs to a larval phase which contains four different stages (Stage I to IV) to a 
juvenile phase, going through four levels before becoming an adult (Lawton & Lavalli, 1995; 
Radhakrishnan et al., 2019). Thus, for each of these stages they will need an adequate diet to allow 
good development of structures during metamorphosis, or simply for energy purposes. Differences 
in feeding strategy between juvenile, larvae and adult lobsters illustrate those mixed dietary 
requirements as they will forage and have access to different food items. In fact, larvae and sessile 
juvenile phases are reliant on planktonic animals due to their size and mobility, hence favoring 
filter feeding (Lawton & Lavalli, 1995). Phytoplankton and zooplankton are good sources of 
polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) and triglycerides (TAG) essential for optimal growth during 
this stage (Gianasi et al., 2023). Another study comparing larvae and juveniles’ acceptability of 
==PAGE:15==
Page | 15  
 
artificial diets confirmed a different digestive efficiency as larvae had limited enzymatic abilities 
which have had influenced their assimilation efficiency and digestibility compared to juvenile 
lobsters (Kurmaly et al., 1990). In fact, tryptic and amylase enzyme activity are not fully developed 
in early stages of larvae resulting in low digestive efficiencies (Kurmaly et al., 1990). Growing to 
a larger size and entering the benthic phase allow juvenile lobsters to ambush and attack bigger 
and mobile preys like small crustaceans (Lawton & Lavalli, 1995). Thus, because larvae, juveniles 
and adults do not forage on the same prey item, have different needs and digestive abilities, they 
have diverse dietary preferences.  
 
2.3.2. Macronutrients: protein, lipids and carbohydrates in aquafeeds  
Macronutrients which are proteins, lipids and carbohydrates are the main components of a diet 
coupled with micronutrients (additives) later explained in this review. In aquafeeds, 
macronutrients and micronutrients are wisely chosen to mimic wild diets and meet nutritional 
requirements of a species with lower cost and easier storage, access or delivery of the feed. In 
lobster aquaculture, no formulated feed has yet been commercialized due to the lack of information 
in nutritional requirements of each phase of lobster life stages and the difficulty to create a cost-
effective diet. Nevertheless, previous studies showed the total optimal nutrients requirement for 
early benthic juveniles being 40% protein, 15% lipids, with the addition of cholesterol and 
phospholipids to prevent molt death syndrome and maximize survival and growth rates (Conklin, 
1995; D’Abramo et al., 1981 cited by Tlusty et al., 2008). A more recent study revealed that an 
adequate diet for European juvenile lobsters should be formulated with approximately 50% 
protein, 25% carbohydrate and 12% lipids to support similar growth rates as a krill diet 
(Hinchcliffe et al., 2022). Total protein, lipids and carbohydrates differ between studies as each of 
these macronutrients have important components to assess in order to create an effective, mixed 
and balanced diet.   
 
a. Proteins  
Proteins are made of amino acids which are organic compounds acting as building blocks for 
proteins. When formulating a feed, looking at amino acids profile of an animal allows to select 
essential amino acids (EAAs) to create an effective diet. 10 EAAs have been identified to ensure 
good growth performance of lobster, which are arginine, histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, 
methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine (Mente et al., 2001). Thus, amino 
==PAGE:16==
Page | 16  
 
acids profile of a formulated feed should resemble the one of a lobster, considering the possible 
changes between the different life stages. Moreover, proteins should be in good proportion to 
avoid effects due to protein deficiency or excess. In lobster, low protein levels decreased survival 
rates by prolonging intermolt periods (Mente, 2006) while excess protein resulted in higher waste 
from farmed individuals leading to poor water quality parameters (Aaqillah-Amr et al., 2021).  
 
b. Carbohydrates  
Carbohydrates are an important energy source for lobster. In fact, clawed lobsters use 
carbohydrates first for energy, followed by lipids and protein when food supplies are limited 
(Wang et al., 2016). Carbohydrates can also influence intermolt period and eventually, growth 
performance. Indeed, glycogen, which is a form of excess carbohydrates stored in the 
hepatopancreas, is used to prepare chitin synthesis, crucial for the formation of exoskeleton during 
molting (Cuzon et al., 1994). Furthermore, glycogen is also important during stress events, as it 
represents a precursor for an additional energy source that can be hydrolyzed by a specific 
hormone to increase glucose concentration in the hemolymph (Wang et al., 2016). Adequate 
carbohydrates requirement for juvenile H. gammarus ranged from 24% to 35% due to the need of 
glycogen for chitin synthesis as explained by (Goncalves et al., 2020). Moreover, just like proteins, 
total carbohydrates should not exceed a certain level to prevent undesirable effects. For example, 
excessive amounts of carbohydrates showed limited growth and survival rates as lobster were not 
able to efficiently utilize high levels of carbohydrates for energy (Rodríguez-Viera et al., 2017).  
 
c. Lipids  
Finally, lipids also represent a potential energy source for lobster. However, lipids are not the 
preferred energy source compared to carbohydrates as feeds containing more than 10% lipids did 
not perform well (Goncalves et al., 2020; Nankervis & Jones, 2022). Indeed, similarly with 
carbohydrates but to a lower extent, lobsters are not able to efficiently utilize high lipids levels, 
leading to reduced growth rates. Goncalves et al. (2020, 2021) observed poor growth performance 
of juvenile H. gammarus with a high lipid formulated diet (24%) as feed intake was significantly 
reduced. Even though lipids are not present in high amount in formulated diet for lobster, this 
macronutrient is crucial for good development in early stages of lobster (Goncalves et al., 2020). 
In larvae and post larvae stages of H. gammarus, the most dominant class of lipid found in body 
tissues was phosphatidylcholine and the second most important was cholesterol (Goncalves et al., 
==PAGE:17==
Page | 17  
 
2022). Phosphatidylcholine is a class of phospholipid essential to solubilize cholesterol from the 
hepatopancreas to the hemolymph (D’Abramo et al., 1982). Cholesterol is another type of lipid, 
part of the sterol group. Indeed, lipids can be distinguished between a broad group of organic 
compounds with fats, sterols, waxes, fat-soluble vitamins, monoglycerides, diglycerides, 
phospholipids and so on (Molina-Poveda, 2016). Just like amino acids in proteins, fatty acids are 
building blocks of lipids and can be structurally different, with saturated, monounsaturated or 
polyunsaturated fatty acids (Molina-Poveda, 2016). Essential fatty acids (EFA) cannot be 
biosynthesized by lobster, as a consequence, they need to be supplied by diets (Oliva-Teles et al., 
2015). Linolenic acid, eicosapentanoic acid, linoleic acid, docosahexanoic acid and arachidonic 
acid are the five essential unsaturated fatty acids for lobster (Nelson et al., 2006).

No commercial diet has yet been created because of the difficulty to define nutritional 
requirements for a specific stage and create an adequate mix of ingredients to contribute to the 
overall health of lobsters without detrimental effects, be cost-effective and enhance growth at the 
same time. Besides, identifying nutritional requirements is vital to develop effective diet in lobster 
aquaculture but other parameters can also influence the performance of a formulated diet. 
 
2.3.3. Physical characteristics of a formulated diet 
Physical characteristics like the size, shape, form, buoyancy, palatability, water stability or 
attractability represent essential parameters to select when creating a pelleted feed in aquaculture 
(Aaqillah-Amr et al., 2021; Li & Robinson, 2015). The adequate size and shape depend on the 
size and age of lobsters. Small juvenile lobsters might take more time foraging on pellets than 
adult lobsters if the pellet size is not adapted to their body size and digestive physiology, which 
can lead to limited feeding success ultimately, impacting growth rates negatively. Formulated feed 
can be formed following a grinding process before combining all ingredients with water to create 
a dough that will be shaped and cut afterwards. Different forms of pellet can be created depending 
on its moisture content; dry, wet (also called moist) and semi-moist pellet form are the main types 
used in aquaculture (Aaqillah-Amr et al., 2021). Dry pellets are usually produced for floatability 
purposes where they can sink or float depending on the species in question. For lobsters, dry-
sinking pellets are favored as they are principally bottom feeders. Wet pellets have been replaced 
==PAGE:19==
Page | 19  
 
by semi-moist form because fungal and mold growth was a recurrent problem when inadequately 
stored (Aaqillah-Amr et al., 2021). In a study by Goncalves (2021), semi-moist pellets were 
hypothesized to increase feed intake in juvenile H. gammarus as ingestion is easier with softer 
extruded feeds. However, the opposite was observed with reduced feed intake, probably due to 
the faster satiation because of the mechanical expansion of semi-moist pellets in the gut of juvenile 
European lobsters (Goncalves, 2021). Besides, other parameters like pellet water stability and 
durability are important to describe. Pellet water stability can be explained as the ability to retain 
its form and nutrients in water until ingestion of the animal in question while durability relates to 
the ability to stay in shape during handling, storage and transportation before feeding the animal 
(Aaqillah-Amr et al., 2021). For lobsters, pellet water stability is of great interest since leaching 
of nutrients can influence attractability and palatability of a pellet. If nutrients leach instantly or 
completely into the water when food is delivered, orientation towards this pellet (attractability) 
might be greatly reduced as well as the acceptance of the feed (palatability). This is especially 
impactful in lobster species due to their chemoreception ability to smell and taste food cues in the 
water. Additionally, pellets should remain attractive to lobsters even after a long immersion time 
considering their slow eating process. A study from Williams et al. (2005) demonstrated the 
impact on feeding preferences of spiny lobsters with pellets immerged in water for a prolonged 
period. Palatability of pellets gradually diminishes with increasing time from 0 to 7.5h. After 
immersion in water, pellets water stability was reduced and nutrients leaching into the water, 
reducing acceptance and attractability compared to a mussel which was still attractive after 7.5h 
(Williams et al., 2005). Thus, physical properties of pellets are as important as nutritional ones as 
they can alter the delivery or acceptance of a perfectly formulated diet.  
 
2.3.4. Creating a sustainable diet with alternative proteins  
Moreover, aquaculture is becoming more sustainable by incorporating alternative ingredients like 
plant protein, insect meals, shrimp waste meal and other potential replacements for the traditional 
fish meal for protein and fish oil for lipids. Fish meal is an adequate protein source with a high 
protein profile, high protein digestibility, palatability, a good source of minerals, taurine and 
vitamins, easily accessible and cost-effective, making it highly demanded for marine species 
aquaculture (Oliva-Teles et al., 2015). Besides, marine species compared to freshwater ones have 
limited ability to create long chain PUFA which are essential fatty acids for an overall good 
development or energy use (Sargent et al., 2002). Thus, fish oil represents the easiest lipid source 
to meet this requirement and guarantees an appropriate lipid profile for essential fatty acids. With 
==PAGE:20==
Page | 20  
 
increasing demand for fish and farming of fish, the need for fish oil and fish meal is augmented as 
well as its price, risking the overfishing of wild stocks for this protein and lipid source (Oliva-
Teles et al., 2015). In some countries, fish export for fish meal is more attractive than for 
consumption, increasing fishing pressure and impacting food security for an ingredient (FAO, 
2022). Thus, reduction of this protein and lipid source remains the first step to a more sustainable 
aquaculture. Replacing a part of the fish meal with plant-based protein is easy, however only using 
plant protein in a diet without comprising health and survival of marine animals is harder. For 
lobsters, previous studies have tested the potential of soya-based protein instead of fish meal 
(Floreto et al., 2000, 2001). Survival of juveniles was significantly lowered with a diet only made 
with soybean meal (Floreto et al., 2000) and growth rate limited with the lowest Somatic Growth 
Rate (SGR) recorded and failure to moult (Floreto et al., 2001). Nevertheless, plant protein 
supplemented with another protein can have a beneficial effect on survival and growth rates of 
juvenile lobsters. As Floreto et al. (2001) explained, soya-based protein supplemented with freeze-
dried krill hydrolysate showed good growth performance and weight gain in juvenile American 
lobsters. This illustrates the positive effect of adjusting nutritional deficiencies with krill 
supplements. Indeed, krill supplements are a good source of nutrients with high levels of 
carotenoid pigments and amino acid profiles resembling to lobsters’ profile (Floreto et al., 2001). 
Thus, plant-based protein could represent a positive alternative to the traditional fish meal for 
sustainable purposes but should be supplemented with other ingredients to ensure meeting dietary 
requirements for optimal growth and production of healthy lobsters. Supplements like additives 
and attractants represent a good solution to improve palatability and attractivity of plant-based 
proteins.  
 
3. The use of supplements in lobster aquaculture 
Attractants and additives are supplemented to a diet to increase attractiveness and palatability or 
make sure to meet dietary deficiencies due to the choice of certain ingredients but can also be used 
for better digestibility or growth, reducing waste of uneaten feed (Hancz, 2020). In this last section, 
attractants and additives used in clawed aquaculture will be presented and challenges to find the 
adequate supplement explained.  
 
==PAGE:21==
Page | 21  
 
3.1. 
Additives for good growth, health or nutritional requirements  
As the aquaculture industry tries to become more and more sustainable, the use of additives and 
attractants are required to ensure a balanced diet for healthy fish when using plant-based 
ingredients or other alternatives lacking essential nutrients. Additives can be nutritive or 
nonnutritive ingredients included in a diet in small amounts to serve one specific purpose. For 
example, they can be used to prevent contracting a disease with probiotics, for promoting growth 
with growth promoters and hormones, to meet dietary deficiencies with vitamins, minerals, amino 
acids, cholesterol, phospholipids, to enhance feed ingestion and palatability using enzymes, feed 
stimulants or attractants, but also to ensure that physical and nutritional characteristics of a pellet 
stay stable utilizing emulsifiers, binders, stabilizers and antioxidant or mold inhibitors (Bai et al., 
2015). Attractant is a specific type of additive aiming to improve ingestion of a diet and eventually 
growth by increasing palatability.  
 
3.2. 
Food attractants to increase palatability of a diet  
In lobster aquaculture, the best chemical composition of an attractant for pelleted feed has been 
identified to be soluble in water, basic in charge, non-lipid and with a molecular weight of <1000 
Da (Kurmaly et al., 1990; Williams et al., 2005). Examples of chemical compounds characterized 
as best attractants for formulated feed in lobsters are nucleotides, nucleosides, tertiary amine 
compounds, taurine, glycine, total free amino acids (FAAs) and so forth, or a mix of these 
compounds (Williams et al., 2005). Besides, attractants can be synthesized from natural ingredient 
sources or synthetic substances. As an example, mussels represent a potential source for natural 
attractant in formulated feed as they are highly attractive food items to lobsters because of the high 
abundance of glycine and taurine. Indeed, Williams et al. (2005) demonstrated the preference for 
fresh mussels over pelleted feed either made with squid, mussels, prawns or polychaete 
homogenates because of a high content of glycine in leachate of mussels. Natural attractants can 
also consist of crustacean products like shrimp waste as it contains a well-balanced amino acid 
profile and high protein content. High levels of arginine and lysine has been found in the northern 
shrimp (Pandalus borealis) waste (Heu et al., 2003) and represent two of the main amino acid 
compounds found in whole-body of European lobsters implying the significance of these 
compounds for a balanced diet. Other compounds such as L-glutamate, L-arginine, L-aspartate, 
betaine, glutathione, hydroxyproline can also stimulate lobsters feeding behaviour and be added 
as a feeding attractant in a formulated diet (Nankervis & Jones, 2022). These compounds can be 
==PAGE:22==
Page | 22  
 
found naturally but also created synthetically by isolating or purifying specific substances. The 
advantage of synthetic attractant is the control and choice of supplements to add in formulated 
feed. However, because lobster rely on a mixture of chemical compounds rather than one particular 
attractant for growth and another for palatability, it remains difficult to select the adequate 
proportion, type and mixture of compounds to improve feed intake.  
 
3.3. 
Chemoreception relevance in feeding attractants 
In fact, for an optimal feed ingestion and attractiveness, attractants need to stimulate special 
receptors of lobsters. These receptors are specifically used for chemoreception.  Chemoreception 
allows lobsters to identify food from other particles in the water column (Derby & Atema, 1982). 
They can smell and locate food items with the help of specialized structures and appendages 
(Derby & Atema, 1982; Mackie, 1973). Chemoreceptors on these structures can recognize food 
items because of the various binding sites identifying different chemical compounds. Thus, 
chemoreceptors are not the same on all olfactory structures and differ depending on the function 
and use of the appendage in question (Corotto et al., 1992). A normal feeding behaviour in lobsters 
is divided in three parts, (1) the detection, (2) the search and (3) the ingestion (Atema, 2018). 
During each of these steps, different appendages and chemoreceptors are used. At first, 
chemoreceptors located on antennules for long-distance cues (Mackie, 1973; Moore et al., 1991; 
Steullet et al., 2001) and walking legs for short-distance cues (Moore et al., 1991) are utilized to 
smell and detect any food odours. Location of food items is performed with aesthetasc and non-
aesthetasc sensilla of antennules (Steullet et al., 2001). Sensilla are specialized hairs located on 
antennules, legs, mouthparts, telson, cephalotorax, antennae and abdomen (Derby & Atema, 1982; 
Devine & Atema, 1982; Steullet et al., 2001), acting as the first entrance for chemicals as they are 
the first chemoreceptors used during chemoreception (Shelton & Laverack, 1970). Aesthetasc and 
non-aesthetasc sensilla are chemosensory neurons innervated with chemoreceptor neurons and the 
main difference between the two are the number of chemoreceptor neurons they innervate and 
location of these sensilla (Steullet et al., 2001). Aesthetasc sensilla are located on the lateral 
flagella where aesthetasc tuft is present and innervate a high number of chemoreceptor neurons 
while non-aesthetasc sensilla are present on the lateral and medial flagella in parts with no 
aesthetasc tuft hence, innervated with less chemoreceptor neurons (Steullet et al., 2001). These 
two sensilla also exhibit the same function to locate a food source allowing lobsters to have an 
alternative system in case part of its antennules is damaged. After smelling and locating the odour, 
==PAGE:23==
Page | 23  
 
lobsters can perform their first food check by dynamically flicking antennules, grooming, wiping 
or waving of maxillipeds (Derby & Atema, 1982). The search can then begin by walking towards 
the food source. During that exploration phase, antennae and walking legs are used to examine the 
surroundings and stopped once reaching and touching the item (Derby & Atema, 1981). Ingestion 
is then accepted or not after checking of the food initially by walking legs and lastly by mouthparts 
(Derby & Atema, 1982). If enough chemicals are detected and accepted, ingestion can begin. Thus, 
normal feeding behaviour in lobsters follows a number of specific steps. When one of these steps 
is disturbed, ingestion cannot be completed.  
Because of this highly complex mechanism, food acceptance in lobsters can be disturbed by many 
factors. Attractants needs to be chosen wisely in order to be detectable by chemoreceptors of 
lobsters, in sufficient concentration to pass food checks during normal feeding behaviour and be 
attractive for a considerable time considering lobster eating process. Attractans should also be 
formulated in good proportions to avoid undesirable effects while boosting growth rates. For 
example, Floreto et al. (2001) tested different levels of krill hydrolysates in a soy-bean meal. They 
concluded that high arginine profile in higher proportion of krill hydrolysates (75% and 100%) 
resulted in better growth of juvenile lobster with significantly higher body gains than diets with 
lower proportion of krill hydrolysates (Floreto et al., 2001). A similar study by Floreto et al. (2000) 
succeeded to replace fish meal with a soya-bean based diet without any reduction in weight gain 
by using amino acids supplementation of arginine, leucine, methionine and tryptophan in levels 
similar to the essential amino acid profile of juvenile American lobsters considering a maximum 
of 50% soybean protein proportion.    
 
3.4. 
Micronutrients: vitamins, minerals, carotenoids and probiotics  
3.4.1. Vitamins 
On the other hand, the use of other additives like vitamins and minerals have already been tested 
for lobster aquaculture but specific requirements have not been extensively quantified. Vitamin 
supplementation in aquafeeds helps in optimizing growth, survival and metabolic function of 
farmed animals (Nankervis & Jones, 2022). Examples of fat-soluble vitamins used in crustacean 
aquaculture are vitamins A, D, E and K and water-soluble vitamins are vitamin C, choline, inositol, 
thiamin, B12, Niacin, riboflavin, and so on (Nankervis & Jones, 2022). In lobster aquaculture, a 
study from Conklin et al. (1980) reported the inclusion of vitamins for a balanced diet suggesting 
that a positive effect on growth was observed in juvenile lobsters fed a purified diet with a 4% 
==PAGE:24==
Page | 24  
 
vitamin mixture. However, specific proportion has not been identified yet hence, available sources 
of shrimps vitamin requirements functioned as a standard for lobster aquaculture (Kanazawa, 
2000).   
 
3.4.2. Minerals  
Besides, minerals are important for structural and functional conditions in lobster. Calcium, 
copper, phosphorous, potassium, magnesium, selenium and zinc are recommended minerals in 
lobster aquaculture (Davis & Gatlin III, 1996). In the wild, minerals can be absorbed from the 
water but are lost after each ecdysis forcing lobsters to forage on high-calcium preys before 
molting to prevent mineral deficiency (Kanazawa, 2000). In aquaculture, lobsters only forage on 
one uniform pelleted feed which is not changed during molting periods. Hence, to maintain good 
health and structure of lobster, supplementation of minerals is necessary. Phosphorous and 
calcium are of interest as they are interconnected, meaning that the concentration of one influences 
the availability of the other. In fact, higher levels of calcium inhibit the effects of phosphorous 
resulting into poor growth and survival (Davis et al., 1993). This was observed in juvenile 
American lobsters where excessive ratio of calcium/phosphorous (above 1.55) lead to 
abnormalities in the endocuticle of juvenile lobsters (Lee Gallagher et al., 1978). Besides, the 
authors from this study demonstrated that a calcium/phosphorous ratio of 1.01 or below showed 
good growth rates while a diet supplemented with a calcium/phosphorous ratio of 0.51 had the 
best results in growth and survival, increasing up to 85% in length and 50% in weight compared 
to control individuals fed live Artemia (Lee Gallagher et al., 1978). Thus, calcium should be 
supplemented in low concentrations only when needed to prevent the inhibitory effect on 
phosphorous.  
 
3.4.3. Carotenoids  
Carotenoids are also another important micronutrient for formulated feed as it cannot be 
synthesized naturally but only obtained from feeds (Galasso et al., 2017). Carotenoids are essential 
natural pigments and are crucial for the protection of cells. In lobster, the main carotenoid is 
astaxanthin giving its pigmentation to lobsters and acting as an antioxidant which contributes to 
the proper functioning of the immune system and promotes oxidative stress resistance of lobsters 
(Galasso et al., 2017).  Hinchcliffe et al. (2020) confirmed the positive effect of astaxanthin in a 
==PAGE:25==
Page | 25  
 
diet as survival and growth of H. gammarus post-larvae was improved in a herring meal feed 
supplemented with this carotenoid.   
3.4.4. Probiotics  
Another example is probiotics. Aquatic probiotics are live microbial supplements providing 
beneficial effects on gut microbiota by regulating microbial communities and correcting microbial 
balance (Daniels et al., 2010). H. gammarus larvae fed a diet with a mixture of probiotic (Bacillus 
spp.) and mannan oligosaccharides (MOS) showed better growth rates, survival and post-larval 
condition than other groups in this study fed a diet with un-supplemented Artemia, Artemia and 
probiotics only, or Artemia with MOS only. As explained by Daniels et al. (2010), this improved 
growth and weight gain was probably due to a balancing and suppressing effect of probiotics and 
MOS, stabilizing intestinal bacterial communities by preventing variations and development of 
new microbial species.  
 
4. Conclusion  
 
The development of clawed lobster aquaculture and advancements in this field have increased the 
need for a formulated feed. Indeed, formulated feed created to mimic a wild diet can prevent 
negative effects associated with wet or live feed usually used in lobster farming. These effects 
include reduced growth and survival rate due to nutritional deficiencies, a labor-intensive 
management to have fresh live diet on a daily basis, possible storage and delivery issues but also 
the prevalence of potential diseases. Thus, more research is being dedicated to finding an optimal 
feed formulation for lobster aquaculture. To create an efficient and balanced aquafeed, it is 
essential to identify nutritional requirements of lobsters. Macronutrients requirements differ 
between life stages and have not yet been precisely quantified. However, important compounds 
for protein, lipids and carbohydrates are known. Micronutrients are as important as 
macronutrients, as they can ensure a balanced diet through the use of supplements to increase 
palatability like attractants, provide missing dietary compounds with nutritional supplements or 
improve the overall health of lobster using health supplements. However, these supplements 
should be carefully selected and proportioned as lobster’s acceptability and attractivity of a food 
item mainly depends on chemoreception. Besides, supplements can facilitate the acceptance of 
alternative proteins to replace fish meal and oil, contributing to a more sustainable aquaculture 
industry.

Abstract 
Feeding behaviours of lobster are driven by chemoreception, which make feed acceptance and 
attractiveness of a diet dependent on the ability of chemoreceptors to smell chemical cues from a 
prey or ingredients in a formulated diet. With sustainability becoming highly important in 
aquaculture, replacement of fish meal and oil by other alternatives like plant-based proteins and 
supplements have been explored. Hence, plant-based diets need to be supplemented with 
attractants to make them detectable and appealing to lobsters as well as balancing nutritional 
deficiencies.  
This study tested blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) from mitigation farms as potential feed attractants, 
by coating blue mussel products on a plant-based diet for juvenile H. gammarus. A Y-maze was 
designed to assess feeding preferences of juvenile lobster between a negative control (no mussels 
coating), raw mussels surpernatant, solid and steamed mussels supernatant and solid by recording 
the mean time spent in different zones of the maze with the five different diets. Behavioural 
analysis, including the average time spent walking and feeding, was also conducted.  
Results showed no statistical differences in average time spent in the different zones of the maze 
when conducting dual comparisons between the five feeds. Average time spent feeding was higher 
than walking only in three trials with a preference for the negative control, but no statistical 
evidence could confirm that tendency. Average walking time was statistically higher than feeding 
in most of the trials and for most of the diets tested. High walking times might indicate an 
incomplete feeding behaviour due to juvenile lobsters underdeveloped chemoreception ability 
considering their early age and physical development. A preference for the negative control could 
emphasize the need for plant protein at this early stage making them more attractive to juveniles 
than adults. The low concentration (3%) of mussel coatings could also be an obstacle to the 
completion of a normal feeding behaviour, as chemical cues were possibly insufficient for 
detection by relevant chemoreceptors. Other factors like the maze design, setup and issues 
involved during feeding trials, which might have influenced the results, were also discussed.

6. Introduction 
The aquaculture of lobsters is an emerging sector due to its high demand for seafood consumption 
and limited global supply (Jeffs et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2019; Ton Nu Hai & Speelman, 2020). 
In 2020, around 200 000 tonnes of lobsters were fished worldwide with 2% being European 
lobsters, Homarus gammarus (FAO, 2024; WRL, 2022). This highlights the economic importance 
of lobsters which is expected to rise further in the coming years. Therefore, lobster aquaculture is 
developing not only to meet the global demand, but also to lower fishing pressures before total 
collapse of natural stocks. However, lobster aquaculture, for most species, remains a complicated 
economically viable farming process due to the general low survival rates of larvae and slow 
growing of individuals. Cannibalism in larvae represents an important parameter for mortality, 
likely 90% of the mortality in European lobster larvae is due to cannibalism (Lund & Goncalves, 
2023). If cannibalism is prevented and controlled, diet also plays an important role in the overall 
survival of lobster in aquaculture. Formulating attractive and effective diets for lobster represents 
a challenge since lobsters are extremely picky eaters and slow-growing animals. In fact, 
attractiveness and feeding success of a diet by lobsters is completely driven by chemoreception.  
Chemoreception is a complex mechanism in crustaceans involving smelling and tasting chemical 
cues present in the water (Atema, 2018) and is important for social interactions and feeding 
processes. In homarid lobsters, specialized appendages and structures are identified for an 
effective and complete chemoreception process (Figure 1). Chemoreceptors are located on 
antennules of lobsters (Derby & Atema, 1982; Steullet et al., 2001) as well as walking legs, 
mouthparts and any other parts with specialized hairs called chemosensillar (Derby & Atema, 
1982; Moore et al., 1991; Zimmer-Faust, 1989). When a chemical cue is smelled by these 
chemoreceptors, chemosensory neurons transform this chemical message into information to 
initiate a feeding behaviour. At first, the detection phase starts by recognizing a food or prey item 
with the help of antennules for long distance odours or walking legs for short distance ones (Moore 
et al., 1991). Besides, chemosensillar present in the telson, cephalothorax, mouthparts, antennae 
and abdomen are innervated by chemosensory neurons which also receive and translate the 
chemical message when locating and recognizing a food source (Derby & Atema, 1982; Devine 
& Atema, 1982; Steullet et al., 2001). Secondly, the searching phase can start by walking towards 
the food source.Walking ends with a first contact to the food item made by walking legs or 
mouthparts as a certain threshold of chemical cues should be detected by chemoreceptors on these 
appendages to validate and perform the first food check (Mackie, 1973). This is done to confirm 
or not the successful exploration and to proceed with the feeding phase. Finally, chemoreceptors 
==PAGE:35==
Page | 35  
 
in maxillipeds need to check the food quality before completing the feeding process with ingestion 
(Corotto et al., 1992; Derby & Atema, 1982). During checking of the food by maxillipeds, multiple 
rounds might be needed with the help of walking legs picking up and turning the food item around 
to perform a thorough analysis. If any of these steps is stopped, ingestion of the food item might 
be uncompleted. Thus, creating a formulated feed which is attractive enough to go through every 
step of a normal feeding behaviour, being detectable by specific chemoreceptors without having 
any detrimental effects on lobster health or development is a difficult task.  
 
Figure 1. Specialised appendages used for chemoreception of H. gammarus from the side (A), 
front (B) and top view (C). Photo: P.Chanzy 
 
In this study, the main goal was to evaluate the potential of mitigation mussels as a feeding 
attractant with a diet entirely formulated without any fish protein but only plant protein. Fish meal 
and oil are ubiquitous in the aquaculture sector. They are good sources of protein, lipids, vitamins 
==PAGE:36==
Page | 36  
 
and minerals, have good palatability and digestibility and are easily accessible (Oliva-Teles et al., 
2015). In 2020, 11% of the global proportion of fisheries and aquaculture production of aquatic 
animals was used for fish oil and fish meal production, accounting for over 20 million tonnes 
(FAO, 2022). In some countries, whole fish are used for fishmeal rather than human consumption 
due to its higher export value (FAO, 2022). This practice increases fishing pressures on certain 
areas and impacts food security. Replacing fish meal in aquaculture diets, can help reduce these 
pressures and promote the use or development of alternative ingredients that might otherwise go 
to waste, favoring the use of a circular economy and a more sustainable aquaculture industry. 
Using plant protein over the traditional fish meal is an alternative. However, the use of plant 
protein can lead to nutritional deficiencies and reduced palatability, eventually impacting growth 
rates and productivity of farmed individuals (Floreto et al., 2000, 2001; Oliva-Teles et al., 2015). 
Thus, diets formulated with plant protein need additives or supplements to balance dietary 
requirements and improve feed intake. In lobster aquaculture, the inclusion of plant protein 
supplemented with feeding attractant or additives is possible, but no commercial diet has yet been 
produced due to the difficulty to create an effective and balanced diet optimizing growth and 
survival rates with sustainable and cost-effective ingredients. Using by-products of fisheries, 
fisheries waste or resources not intended for consumption as attractants is a good alternative for 
low-cost ingredients promoting circular economy (Filippelli et al., 2020; Rodriguez et al., 2019). 
For lobster aquaculture, mussels are good candidates as feeding attractants because they can stay 
highly attractive to lobster even after a long immersion time (7.5 h) as explained by (Williams et 
al., 2005). Besides, mitigation mussel farms used to filter and improve water quality in Danish 
fjords from nutrient pollution by agricultural or aquaculture run-offs are an accessible and cheap 
resource (Filippelli et al., 2020; Holbach et al., 2020). These mussels being too small for 
consumption, are not utilized besides for water purification purposes but are still harvested to 
control mussel beds and remove nutrients. Reusing these mussels in formulated diets of lobster 
could facilitate the replacement of fish meal and fish oil with plant protein and contribute to a 
circular economy.  
Thus, this study aims to assess any feeding preferences in juvenile H. gammarus between plant-
based diets coated with different mussel products from mitigation farms. Five diets were 
compared, each coated with 3% of different mussel preparation and products except for the control 
diet: negative control (no mussel coating), 3% steamed mussels supernatant, 3% steamed mussels 
solid, 3% raw mussels supernatant and 3% raw mussels solid. The supernatant is the liquid part 
after mussel processing while the solid part is the sediment left after liquid separation. Dual 
==PAGE:37==
Page | 37  
 
comparisons of these five diets were tested during feeding trials in a Y-maze design, by measuring 
time spent between each side of a Y-maze. Behavioural analysis was then conducted to assess 
feeding behaviour and the influence of these diets on juvenile H. gammarus.  
 
7. Methods 
Due to the complex chemoreception mechanism of lobsters, assessing feeding preferences in 
juveniles was difficult hence, the need to perform two experiments in this study. Experiment 1 
was conducted to record how juveniles reacted to different types of feed and if some coating 
ingredients could potentially increase attractiveness of a diet made without fish protein as 
explained in 7.2 Experimental feeds, while experiment 2 was only conducted to confirm or not the 
choice of 3 days for the starvation period used in the first experiment. Indeed, experiment 2 helped 
to evaluate if starvation could have been a possible factor influencing lobsters feeding preferences 
in experiment 1. Besides, to remove any additional factors disturbing lobsters’ behaviour which 
could have been affected by the maze design, a different setup and testing was performed for 
experiment 2, removing the water flow and other components (more details in 7.3 Experimental 
design, setup). 
 
7.1. Individuals and test systems for experiment 1 and 2 
 
European juvenile lobsters (Hommarus gammarus) used in this study were reared from eggs at the 
Nordsøen Oceanarium until reaching the juvenile phase (Hirtshals, Denmark). Afterwards, they 
were placed in raceways and single compartments to prevent them from fighting with each other 
in facilities at DTU Aqua, Hirtshals. Juvenile lobsters were fed thawed krill once daily and kept 
in water temperature around 12 °C with oxygen levels above 70%. For the first experiment, 28 
individuals weighing 1.2 ± 0.2 g and measuring 17 ± 1.0 mm (carapace length, CL) were selected. 
The second experiment only used 20 individuals with the same weighing and measurements as 
mentioned before. Prior to experiment 1, lobsters were starved for 3 days to make sure they were 
hungry enough to search for food during trials. For experiment 2, two groups of 10 lobsters (A 
and B) were chosen with different starvation period. Group A consisted of 10 lobsters starved for 
four days while group B involved 10 other lobsters that has not been starved.  
==PAGE:38==
Page | 38  
 
During trials, all juveniles were kept in an additional tank and maintained at an average 
temperature of 12 ± 1.0 °C and oxygen levels above 70%. Water was changed and tank siphoned 
once daily to renew the water and remove any possible waste.  
 
7.2. Experimental feeds 
 
Five extruded feeds were tested in this study. They were made without any marine protein but 
only plant protein and produced as 3 mm pellets at DTU Aqua, Hirtshals. The formulation of the 
feeds was similar for the five feeds and only coatings of pellets varied between those (Table 2). 
Coatings were made from blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) grown in a farm at the Limfjord, intended 
to mitigate agricultural and aquaculture run-offs in fjords in Denmark (Filippelli et al., 2020). 
Coatings consisted of raw or steamed mussels that were separated into solid or supernatant form. 
To produce these coating ingredients, raw mussels were first deshelled, mussels meat and juice 
were then homogenized and centrifuged for 10 minutes at 4500 rpm to separate the supernatant 
from the solid part. Steamed mussels followed the same process as raw mussels but were first 
cooked using a mild heating until the opening of shells. Afterwards, steamed mussels and raw 
mussels supernatant and solid were freeze dried and used to coat and create the five different feeds.

7.3. Experimental design and setup 
 
The maze was designed and setup following Jutfelt et al. (2017) recommendations when possible. 
Indeed, the choice arena in the maze was planned to measure 17 x 30 cm but got reduced to 10.5 
x 30 cm when assembling all pieces together as the drains were initially smaller in the maze. The 
Y-maze was constructed with plexiglass pannels (thickness of 5 mm) measuring 50 x 30 cm for 
the base, two 50 x 12 cm panels for lateral sides, two other panels of 30 x 12 cm for the back and 
the front of the maze, with a divider of 33 x 12 cm separating the middle to create the two choices 
zones (Figure 2). A layout of all panels used in the maze is presented in Figure 3. A removable 
gate (30 x 5 cm) was used to isolate lobsters in the choice arena during the acclimation period and 
elongated with hard mesh to prevent lobsters from climbing over the gate. Another divider with 
the same dimensions and mesh was used to prevent lobsters from hiding under the drains, 
considerably reducing the choice arena. Drains were placed on the bottom of the front panel on 
each side of the maze and assembled with two pvc unions, two 90° pvc elbows and two pvc pipes 
measuring 7.2 cm with a 12 mm diameter which were bent to the side to allow the water level to 
be at 6.4 cm. This water level allowed lobsters to be comfortable enough and favoured searching 
for food cues in the whole water column. Inside the maze, two sponges of 5 cm thickness with a 
dimension of 12 x 15 cm as well as three layers of mesh (50 µm) were placed after the inlet water 
of each side of the maze to ensure a laminar flow as recommended by Jutfelt et al. (2017). Besides, 
a thin textured mat was placed on the bottom of the base panel to ensure good movements of the 
lobsters inside the maze as it provides a better grip to walk on. A second replica of the Y-maze 
design was created to test a higher number of individuals at the same time (Figure 4). Thus, two 
mazes were used to independently record two individuals at a time. For experiment 2, sponges, 
meshes and flow rate were removed. One maze was divided in two to independently test two 
individuals at the same time with a choice arena of 9.4 x 14.5 cm (Figure 5). Similarly to 
experiment 1, two mazes were used to track a higher number of individuals at the same time thus, 
four trials were run simultaneously (Figure 6).

7.4. Testing 
To select adequate flowrate and the number of sponges or mesh, multiple tests were run before 
the start of experiments. Dye tests were performed in order to confirm the amount of mesh layers 
needed with sponges for a laminar flow (Figure 7). In fact, adding multiple fine mesh obliged the 
current from the water inlet to break and be distributed equally into the water column. Besides, 
these dye tests also helped in determining the flowrate of the peristaltic pump with a current that 
was not too strong to allow the lobsters to move freely but strongly enough to spread food cues in 
the maze. Moreover, exploration tests were also performed to see how lobsters were moving in 
the maze, if any parts of the maze were disturbing lobsters and to configure the camera and light 
properly.

7.5. Behavioural observations and video tracking 
 
All trials were recorded with an IR camera (Basler acA1300-60gm GigE camera) positioned above 
both mazes. To record videos, the camera was coupled to a computer and operated with the 
software Pylon viewer (user interface version 7.4.0). For both experiments, trials were run in the 
dark and mazes placed on an infrared backlight (Noldus Information Technology, Netherlands) to 
be able to track lobster activity without any external disturbance. Besides, it focused lobsters to 
use chemoreception rather than vision to locate and find food sources. A video tracking tool 
(Ethovision XT 17, Noldus Information Technology, Netherlands) was selected to analyze all

automatically recorded by the tracking software after setting up feeding zones and selected 
variables while behaviours were manually scored for each individual. In experiment 2, walking 
and feeding were tracked in the whole maze without any distinct zones as feeding zones were too 
small and feeding of lobsters highly mixing between zones.

8. Results 
8.1. Time spent in each feeding zone 
To illustrate feeding preferences of juvenile lobsters, time spent in zones containing different feeds 
were recorded and compared.  For trial 1 to 4, time spent in zones with pellet feed was not 
considerably different from negative control (NC) zones (Wilcoxon signed-rank test for trial 4, P 
= 0.28, V = 251, Figure 10), as well as time spent across pellet groups (Kruskal-Wallis test, H(3) 
=0.89, P = 0.83) and between the negative control group. (Kruskal-Wallis test, H(3) = 1.69, P = 
0.64). The most time spent was seen in NC side in trial 4 with 288s spent in that zone (48% of the 
time) while all other zones including the negative control zones in other trials recorded around 
200s (Figure 10).  
More variation was seen between trials comparing two types of pellet (Figure 10) but still no 
statistical differences were found (paired t-test for trial 10, t(27) = -2.0, P = 0.055). Besides, no 
difference was observed between groups comparing the same feed. For example, trial 5, 8, 10 
tested RM solid with three other feeds, RM super, SM super and SM solid correspondingly, but 
lobsters staying in RM solid zone was not different between trial 5, 8 and 10 (One-way ANOVA, 
F(2,81) = 1.30, P = 0.28). Likewise, all other pellets did not show statistical differences between 
trials with the same feed,  RM super in trial 5, 7 and 9 (One-way ANOVA, F(2,81) = 0.65, P = 0.52), 
SM super in trial 6,8 and 9 (One-way ANOVA, F(2,81) = 0.16, P = 0.85) and SM solid in trial 6,7 
and 10 (One-way ANOVA, F(2,81) = 0.74, P = 0.48).

9. Discussion 
The goal of this study was to determine if juvenile H. gammarus had feeding preferences between 
feed coated with different mussel products, thus assessing the potential of mitigation mussels as a 
food attractant in a diet formulated with plant protein. Lobsters are extremely picky eaters and 
slow growing. Thus, formulating an attractive diet for fast growth is difficult and challenging 
mainly because of chemoreception. Feeding behaviour of lobsters is driven by chemoreception 
and feeding occurs only after completion of several steps all of which involve chemoreceptors 
located on specialized appendages of lobsters. Functions of these appendages are well-known in 
adult lobsters (Derby & Atema, 1982; Devine & Atema, 1982; Fedotov, 2009; Garm et al., 2005; 
Moore et al., 1991; Shelton & Laverack, 1970; Steullet et al., 2001; Zimmer-Faust, 1989). 
However, knowledge on juveniles’ chemoreception is limited due to the difficulty of assessing 
behaviour on small individuals (4-25 mm CL). In this study, an attempt to record feeding 
preferences of juvenile H. gammarus was performed, where a small tendency for preference has 
been discovered but without a strong statistical evidence for this food choice.  
 
9.1.  Feeding preference of juvenile H. gammarus  
At first, time spent in each feeding zone was recorded to illustrate possible choices made during 
searching and eating of the different diets. However, no preferences could be confirmed as no 
statistical outcomes were recorded. Time spent between the two zones tested was moderately 
similar in all trials (1 to 10). Thus, behavioural analyses of these times helped to further highlight 
possible preferences of juvenile lobsters.   
From our results, it can be seen that SM super and NC were the two feeds eaten the most. However, 
possible explanations for this tendency are difficult to identify. In fact, the average time that 
lobsters spent eating these two diets was higher in three trials, trial 2, 3 and 10. In trial 2, the 
average time eating NC was 153s, trial 3 recorded an average time eating SM super of 164s while 
trial 10 observed an average time of 95s eating SM super but, the mean difference of time spent 
eating compared to walking in each of these trials could not be proven statistically.  
The NC was not covered by any mussel product making that feed hypothetically less attractive 
than the other diets, yet it was one of the most eaten feeds between the five diets tested. This could 
illustrate the possible attraction and need for plant protein in lobsters’ diet during that stage. 
Indeed, a study from Lawton & Lavalli (1995) demonstrated the importance of plant material in 
==PAGE:56==
Page | 56  
 
lobsters’ diet as it was constantly found in their stomachs even in habitats with low abundance of 
macroalgae, emphasizing the active selection of plants in lobster diet rather than by incidence 
when ingesting other prey items. Another study also found a high occurrence of macroalgae in 
natural diets of juvenile lobsters. Stomach content analysis showed that 70% of H. americanus 
juveniles measuring between 10-20 mm (CL) were feeding on macroalgae compared to only 40% 
for larger individuals (Sainte-Marie, 2001). Besides, macroalgae was the third most eaten item for 
juveniles of that size after flesh and bivalves highlighting the selective feeding strategy that 
juvenile lobsters may use in the wild (Sainte-Marie, 2001). Thus, because juvenile lobsters (10-20 
mm CL) are attracted to both mussels and algae in the wild, seeing a clear preference for a specific 
diet in this study was difficult since all diets were formulated with one or both ingredients. 
However, many other reasons could also justify this lack of feeding preference (section 4.2-4.3). 
 
9.2. Chemoreception ability of juvenile versus adult lobsters 
 
Juvenile lobsters have different abilities than adult lobsters, physically and in terms of 
chemoreception. Juveniles do not have fully formed physical parts or specialized appendages to 
perform chemoreception like adults and need to rely on other mechanisms for feeding. For 
instance, it can be seen that juveniles use different feeding strategies than adults favouring 
suspension feeding, ambushing and browsing as explained by Lawton & Lavalli (1995). Their 
seizer and crusher claw not being fully developed, they do not have the dexterity and coordination 
to efficiently crush open large prey items (Lawton & Lavalli, 1995). Likewise, specialized 
structures for chemoreception might not be completely grown in juveniles. In fact, some specific 
setae acting as chemoreceptors in mouthparts are essential to provide the final check of a food 
item before allowing its ingestion, growing from a single seta in the juvenile phase to a more 
complex structure in the adult stage (Lavalli & Factor, 1995). Similarly, walking legs also have 
specialized setae important for chemoreception since stimulation of this structure is required first 
to pick up or grasp the food item (Lavalli & Factor, 1995). Therefore, juveniles are not able to 
smell similar compounds as adults and probably have a smaller range of chemical compounds to 
detect because of the under-developed appendages needed for chemoreception. Another study 
showed that larval stages of European lobster are not attracted towards a food source but rely on 
chance encounters for feeding (Kurmaly et al., 1990). Although the capture of food items appeared 
unselective, ingestion still relied on chemoreception, as Kurmaly et al. (1990) explained, chemical 
cues from a food item only allow H. gammarus larvae to recognize edible from inedible aliment.  
==PAGE:57==
Page | 57  
 
 
9.3. A threshold of chemical cues to recognize a food item  
In addition, the concentration of ingredients tested in the five feeds of this study might not have 
been “strong” enough to record a clear preference. Pelleted diets were coated with mussels 
products with only 3% of material. Having a higher concentration of mussels could have 
potentially triggered juvenile lobsters more and recorded a stronger choice between the five feeds. 
In fact, looking at our results, it can be confirmed that the difference between the NC pellets and 
other feeds was high enough to record a small preference for NC (trial 2) but also for SM super 
(trial 3) as eating time was higher than walking for these two diets. Whereas no preference could 
be strongly confirmed between pellets coated with mussel ingredients (trial 5 to 10) with walking 
time always higher than eating in these trials. Besides, from trial 5 to 10, juvenile lobsters possibly 
did not recognize pellets to further choose and have a preference. Consequently, the searching 
phase was confirmed but not the foraging phase, implying that chemical compounds in mussels 
coated diets were not detectable or strong enough for juveniles to initiate the feeding phase when 
compared to each other. Feeding behaviour in lobsters is related to chemoreception and follow 
several steps before the actual feeding (Atema, 2018; Devine & Atema, 1982). If one of those 
steps is not performed in a specific order, feeding will most likely be unaccomplished. At first, 
lobsters need to smell a certain threshold of chemicals from the food item with chemoreceptors 
located in antennules to be able to continue with the searching phase, using their walking legs and 
mouthparts and finish with a complete feeding behaviour (Mackie, 1973). In our study, trials 
where walking time was significantly higher than eating illustrate the uncomplete feeding 
behaviour of juvenile lobsters being able to detect food cues but stopping at the searching phase. 
Indeed, multiple ablation studies on specialized appendages used for chemoreception have 
demonstrated this complex mechanism during feeding in lobsters (Derby & Atema, 1982; Devine 
& Atema, 1982; Steullet et al., 2001). The addition of glue or distilled water on maxillipeds and 
walking legs of H. americanus had a major impact on feeding success (Derby & Atema, 1982). 
Altered maxillipeds impacted the actual feeding by releasing any food item that was picked up 
whereas treatment on walking legs influenced the recognition of food by increasing the number of 
time lobsters were choosing a fake food item (Derby & Atema, 1982). Thus, after detection, 
walking legs need to first recognize a food item to allow the following food check by the 
maxillipeds and finally the ingestion. In our case, walking legs might not have reach the specific 
threshold of chemicals needed to activate those chemoreceptors and confirm that pellets were 
nearby or maxillipeds of juvenile lobsters might have rejected the pellet if insufficient chemical 
==PAGE:58==
Page | 58  
 
cues were found, resulting in an uncomplete feeding behaviour. Another study by Borroni et al. 
(1986) also confirmed that requirement of a specific amount of chemical cues to allow normal 
feeding behaviour. They discovered that for an adult lobster leg to be able to recognize a food 
item, it needs the 6 best amino acid compounds or the 16 weak amino acid compounds. Given that 
an adult leg needs to detect this specific amount of amino acid to be able to identify a food item, 
juveniles might require a higher concentration due to their lower chemoreception ability compared 
to adults. Thus, to assess a complete feeding behaviour of juveniles H. gammarus and record 
accurate food preferences, the inclusion level of mussel products for coatings should have been 
higher than 3%. Nevertheless, this low inclusion level was not the only parameter that may have 
impacted our results.  
 
9.4. Maze setup and malfunctions 
The starvation period was a possible factor explaining the lack of selection of pellets by juvenile 
lobsters. A starvation period of 3 days was selected for this study to make sure lobsters were 
starved enough to look for food during trials. However, this period might have been too long and 
instead of favouring food selection, it could have influenced lobsters feeding behaviour by eating 
the first food item they encountered without making a choice between other possible feed. Thus, 
experiment 2 was performed to confirm or not if the starvation period of 3 days was adequately 
chosen or too long for juvenile lobsters. However, no significant differences were recorded 
between starved and non-starved individuals for eating and walking time, implying that the 
starvation period of 3 days may not have influenced lobsters feeding choices in experiment 1. 
Hence, lack of feeding choices of juvenile lobsters were most likely influenced by other 
parameters than the starvation period.    
Other parameters related to the maze design may have contributed to this observed outcome of no 
clear feeding preferences between the five feeds. In fact, the initial maze set-up was modified 
throughout the testing of the experiment as a number of issues appeared. The size of the choice 
arena was significantly reduced because of the position of drains inside the maze, taking almost 
half of the initial size of the choice arena. In addition, this choice arena did not allow all lobsters 
to start exactly at the same position as depending on which side they were at the end of the 
acclimation period, they could start on the left or the right. This biased start position might have 
influenced our results since not all lobsters were at the same start position. Some juveniles might 
have just followed the side they were already on without making a choice beforehand for a side to 
==PAGE:59==
Page | 59  
 
go. Added to that, the time that they had to smell chemical cues before being released was not 
high enough. During the experiment, lobsters were released after an acclimation period of 5 
minutes where they were maintained in the choice arena. However, food was administered only 
after the acclimation period and just seconds before releasing the lobsters. This short time interval 
was probably not long enough to allow lobsters to smell food cues from pellets before walking 
and picking a side. Another issue was to manage to stay consistent and have the same exact set-
up throughout the entire experiment. Adding a current into the set-up increased the ”noise” it could 
create from tubing, drains, pump speed or position of inlet water to our observed results. In fact, 
with cleaning and water replacement after every replicate, the set-up may have moved a little, 
favouring one or the other side and influencing lobsters’ choice or movement. To prevent this, dye 
tests should have been performed between a set of trials to verify and make sure a laminar flow 
was still present and that flowrates of both sides were still the same. Therefore, further analyses 
on how the flow was impacted by the introduction of pellets, animals and removal of the gate after 
the acclimation period using dye tests should have been done before starting the experiment to 
make sure every parameter of the set-up was controlled and consistent. Furthermore, for more 
accurate behaviour analysis, a camera recording the bottom of the maze could have helped to 
follow the different steps of feeding behaviour. Indeed, it was difficult to clearly see the actual 
feeding of a lobster with only the top camera as they used their mouthparts and walking legs to 
first check the pellet and ultimately ingesting it or not. Having a bottom camera could have 
confirmed if pellets passed the first check of the walking legs and were stopped by the mouthparts 
or the other way around if pellets were not validated by chemoreceptors of walking legs and 
stopped or changed to another pellet. This type of setup has never been used before but could help 
to illustrate a step-by step feeding process in juvenile lobsters.  
Cage confinement of juvenile lobsters might also have influenced our results. Before the start of 
experiments, juveniles were kept in individual boxes in raceways where walking and exploration 
was limited. During trials, lobsters had much more space and it might have triggered their 
exploration behaviour increasing their walking time. To prevent this exploration time, 
conditioning of the maze with the individuals could have been performed for them to be more 
familiar to the maze set up and possibly prevent excessive walking time.  
 
==PAGE:60==
Page | 60  
 
9.5. Further studies 
Thus, further studies on feeding preferences of juvenile H. gammarus could explore leakage time 
of attractants from pellets and their chemical composition, including analyses of solubilized 
substances to simulate the use of pellets when delivered to lobsters in the water. Various maze 
types and designs should be thoroughly tested considering factors like the presence or absence of 
currents and ensuring adequate sizes. Tests could also conduct different comparisons, for example, 
testing a single feed against no feed, to emphasize the attraction towards a food source. Tests using 
their regular diet as a control could also help to understand their preference. Finally, complete 
steps of a normal feeding behaviour of juveniles lobster should be analyzed and detailed to 
comprehend certain mechanism behind feeding of lobster.  
 
10. Conclusion  
 
The goal of this study was to identify feeding preferences of juvenile H. gammarus between diets 
coated with different mussel products from mitigation farm. Results observed in this study indicate 
a slight preference for the negative control highlighting the potential requirement of plant material 
in diets of juvenile lobsters. Strong statistical evidence showing a higher proportion of the average 
time spent walking compared to eating in most trials could reflect the importance of 
chemoreception when formulating a diet for lobster. Indeed, a high walking time could be 
explained by the underdeveloped physical structures of juvenile lobsters reducing their 
chemoreception ability to complete a normal feeding behaviour. The low concentration of mussels 
products (3%) coating pellets of the five diets could also be a reason for the recorded high walking 
time in trials. However, other factors like the maze design, choice arena size, biased start and 
operation of the trials have possibly influenced our results.
