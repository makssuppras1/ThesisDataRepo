Executive Summary
Supply chain in its complex character with many diﬀerent stakehold-
ers, processes and inﬂuences is one of the main compensation factors when
it comes to new trends in consuming or production. Therefore, currently
consuming trends to increase the personalizing and servitization of prod-
ucts challenge the eﬃciency and established processes of supply chain
management. In general, supply chain management has the possibility to
react to those trends as well as to other modiﬁcations, such as demand
uncertainties, with another recent appearance: Industry 4.0.
Industry 4.0 is deﬁned as the process of linking the physical and the
virtual world to create a digital and connected working environment,
through increasing automation and introducing artiﬁcial intelligence.
The present paper elaborates on How is Industry 4.0 able to improve the
supply chain of manufacturing ﬁrms? in an overall context. Therefore,
new technologies, processes, IT systems and their inter-connectivity are
summarized to a new concept of Supply Chain 4.0. This concept reﬂects
developments of Industry 4.0 transferred into a supply chain management
environment.
A structured literature review in combination with three case studies,
which were performed with three manufacturing companies of diﬀerent
characteristics, reveal the following ﬁndings.
Industry 4.0 aﬀects every single part of the supply chain, not only in a
focused and diﬀerentiated way but as well in a way that every process
needs to be connected with its stakeholders. Such a connection is meant
in a way where the gaps between virtual and physical worlds are bridged
through IT systems, based on real time data. The overall connectivity
merges established IT system to a big conclomerate of Supply Chain 4.0,
which enables an interconnected virtual world to plan, improve and steer
the physical processes of a supply chain. This approach is not possible
without deepening cooperations, extending the technological infrastruc-
ture and investing in work proﬁle adaption. Thus, Supply Chain 4.0 can
be seen as one of the main competitiveness factors of the future.
The case studies show that speciﬁcations of such a Supply Chain 4.0 are
highly depending on the environment, the segment and products of a
single company. Embedded in a Supply Chain 4.0 approach, these com-
pany speciﬁcations can be included into the decentralized decision makers,
which are steered by constraints and objectives embedded in computer
code.
Currently, companies mainly struggle with how to use the existing data
==PAGE:3==
and how to implement crossfunctional IT systems. It helps to include
the Supply Chain 4.0 concept in small steps but ensure during every
implementation stage that add-ons are possible. Add-ons will be an im-
portant aspect of Industry 4.0 since the development is far away from its
culmination.

Introduction
"Industry 4.0 is able to boost the competitiveness of companies", is what Sarvari
mentions in 2018 [42]. To get an understanding of what he means with this state-
ment, it helps to get a grasp of what Industry 4.0 is about.
While the ﬁrst revolution was mainly focused on the discovery and commercial usage
of steam power, the second about mass production and the third about including
IT into manufacturing operations, this fourth digital revolutions goes further [4].
Through the arrival of the internet within the industrial area, named the Internet
of Things (IoT), the introduction of cloud data bases and cyber physical systems,
the possibilities for the industry are more diverse and complex as in the industrial
revolutions before.
One can see the immediacy of the topic by realizing how states are trying to get
a hold on the developments of Industry 4.0. After a ﬁrst national initiative was
launched in Germany (named ’Industrie 4.0’), the United States launched the ’Ad-
vanced Manufacturing Partnership’ (AMP) and China revealed ’Made in China
2025’. The common goal of the speciﬁc programs is to create surroundings to im-
plement digitization solutions and support the national fourth industrial revolution
[32].
One of the reasons national states are trying to improve the adaption of Industry
4.0, is that there are currently arising international consuming trends which can be
directly approached through the latest industrial revolution. The trend of a high
servitization as well as the trend of mass customization and therefore small batch
sizes, reﬂect major current market trends [36]. These trends, as well as other supply
chain provoking challenges can be faced on a diﬀerent level with Industry 4.0.
As scientists still argue about the speciﬁc deﬁnition of Industry 4.0, Wagner summa-
rizes it as an environment of connectability in the Internet of Things and Services
with the vision of a smart factory [51]. Other papers add the increasing impor-
tance of linking the virtual and physical world [40], the autonomously exchange of
information and control of each other [47] and the steps towards an entirely digital
enterprise [42] as main aspects of Industry 4.0.
This paper will focus on the aspects of the Industry 4.0 regarding the supply chain
management. Especially in the supply chain management, the earlier described mar-
ket trends aﬀect manufacturing companies and Industry 4.0 can therefore release its
eﬀects. In addition to the small batch sizes, especially demand uncertainties, higher
supply risks and increasing competitive intensity are the main challenges which sup-
ply chain management is facing in the 21st century [23].
As the literature review demonstrates, many papers do already elaborate on the
possibilities of Industry 4.0 for a speciﬁc part of an Industry and its supply chain
management: Logistics 4.0 focus on the possibilities digitization brings to hardware

and software solution of shipping goods. Procurement 4.0, on the other hand, al-
ready proclaims a complete transparency between suppliers and OEMs.
What is missing so far in the diverse literature landscape, is a conglomeration of
eﬀects, which Industry 4.0 has on the overall area of supply chain management.
Therefore, the present paper focuses on the research question: How is Industry 4.0
able to improve the supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms?. This re-
search question builds up on the hypothesis that Industry 4.0 is able to improve the
supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms. Moreover, additional hypotheses
are deﬁned to focus the outcome of this paper. One of them states that an overall
roadmap design for implementing Industry 4.0 in manufacturing ﬁrms, regarding
their supply chain, can be developed. The other one claims that even though diﬀer-
ent industries have diﬀerent focuses, speciﬁc concepts can be applied to all of them.
Both, the research question as well as the hypotheses will be elaborated on in the
paper.

an important connectivity hub (dark grey background). Furthermore, in light grey

companies from diﬀerent segments [32]. Therefore, the literature analysis represents
the basis not only for the analysis but as well for the case studies included in the
research paper. In addition, the two additional stated hypothesis can be proven
towards the case companies, operating in the observed environment.
To highlight potential outcomes, one can expect, as the hypothesis mentions, that
there are speciﬁc Industry 4.0 aspects which enable companies of diﬀerent character-

4.0 regarding the supply chain can be expected, elaborating on important actions

towards the speciﬁc processes. Regarding the combination of literature and case

To elaborate on the above described research question and hypotheses as well as
to compare the potential outcome with the ﬁndings of the literature review and case
studies, this research paper is structured in the following way.
First of all, the methodologies applied for the research and information gathering
are described in the methodology section. This includes as well the justiﬁcation,
why those methodologies are used to reach the goals of the paper.
In a next step, the literature review gives insights about all the current developments
regarding Industry 4.0 and the speciﬁc aspects of the supply chain. In this context,
not only the classical supply chain aspects like Supplier, Logistics, Warehousing and
Production are highlighted. The literature review chapter focuses as well on speciﬁc
Industry 4.0 aspects and their inﬂuence on the supply chain such as Cyber Physi-
cal Systems, Decision Making, Smart Products, Big Data, Horizontal and vertical
integration, Internet of Things and Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI). On top of that, the
limitations described in the literature are mentioned.
During the literature review, the aspects are described in a speciﬁc way without
being connected to each other, which is content of the analysis part.
Subsequently, the given information is analyzed. First from a product perspective,
explaining a product journey in an Industry 4.0 environment. Secondly, the gath-
ered information is associated in the four diﬀerent main parts of the supply chain:
Supplier, Logistics, Warehousing and Production. As a synopsis, for each one of
these parts, a data stream network is developed.
Thereafter, these data stream
networks are interconnected with each other to represent an overall data stream
network. This can be seen as a roadmap for including Industry 4.0 into the supply
chain management. For this new approach the term Supply Chain 4.0 is introduced.
Afterwards, the three case companies are initially introduced, before the results of
the investigation are explained. Subsequently, these results are contrasted with the
data stream network analyzed before. Inconsistencies are analyzed and depending
on the speciﬁcation of each case company, recommendations are given.
The discussion wraps up on the analysis in stating seven diﬀerent ﬁndings, which
conclude the analysis of the literature research and the case studies. Moreover, a
detailed explanation about the limitation of the paper is given followed by a listing
of potential relating research topics.
Consequently, the conclusion summarizes the outcomes of the research and how the
investigation was able to fulﬁll the literature gap, to conclude with an explanation
on what could occur if ﬁndings of this paper are not taken into consideration from
manufacturing companies.

Methodology
To elaborate on the research question of How is Industry 4.0 able to improve the
supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms?, a structured literature review
was conducted. This literature review represents moreover the origin of the research
question, since the literature gap was conducted through it.
In addition to that, the literature review is compared and analyzed with regard
to case studies of directly aﬀected companies. The detailed approach for both of
the methods is described below. Both of the chapters are divided into the sections
of describing the information collection, the information analysis and the methods
limitations.
2.1
Literature Review
The literature review was based on the four steps to conduct a literature review
[41]: scanning, making notes, structuring, writing the literature review and building
a bibliography. The overall literature review follows a transparent process to reduce
bias and enable a systematic review [48].
In the scanning part, which is the ﬁrst step of the literature collection, diﬀerent
building blocks ensured the right search results [41]. As shown in ﬁgure 2.1, a top
down approach was deﬁned to narrow down the outcome. After starting by exploring
articles about Industry 4.0 in general, the research was declined towards Industry
4.0 in a context of supply chain management and preconditions needed.
In the structuring and deﬁning process of those materials, citation peal growing [41]
has been adapted to look deeper into speciﬁc aspects. Through the hermeneutic cir-
cle approach [5], it was possible to gain a deeper understanding of special topics and
through that, a better overall knowledge. This resulted in specifying the research
towards speciﬁc terminologies like production, warehousing, procurement, logistics
and decision making.
Subsequently to the above mentioned literature collection, the data was analyzed,
compared to related articles and embedded in an overall framework. In this context,
the revealed literature gap got translated into the research question (see chapter 1).
In conclusion, the literature research is based on eleven papers, mainly journal arti-
cles and research papers.
The limitations of this research method can be deﬁned by the amount of papers
which were included into the literature research according to the resource restric-
tions. Moreover, the research was limited by literature written in English and Ger-
man language.
The used database was DTU FindIt by the Technical University of Denmark.

2.2
Case Studies
On top of the literature review which is described in chapter 2.1, three case studies
were conducted. The needed information was extracted through online research and
individually performed interviews. These interviews enabled the author to receive
additional needed information and independent thoughts from experts in a speciﬁc
ﬁeld from a speciﬁc case company [1]. All of the executed interviews were performed
as semi structured interviews.
Semi structured interviews combine advantages of standardized and open session
interviews. They enable hypothesis testing, while receiving additional insight in-
formation [30]. To be able to maximize those professional expertise insights, the
interviews were conducted conversationally for each of the interviewed experts [1].
To get a better understanding of the participants of the case studies, the speciﬁc
backgrounds of the participants of each company are described in the upcoming
part.
Regarding the Bosch Case Study, a lot of information and interviews, dealing with
the subject of Industry 4.0, were already available online. Therefore, general infor-
mation about the Industry 4.0 orientation of Bosch was conducted through online
found interviews. Nevertheless, regarding speciﬁc information about the plant in
Blaichach, Germany, which has been used as a prototype plant, two interviews have
been performed with the assistant of the plant management. Being in contact with
all KPIs on a daily basis and having a great overview about the present situation
and the orientation of the plant, the management assistance was able to respond in
a detailed and profound way.
Regarding Phase One, which represents a way smaller company, a higher amount of
interviews have been conducted to get an understanding about their supply chain.
Three of those interviews have been performed with the director of production and
procurement. Moreover, two data analyst experts participated in an interview to
give further insights.
Additional interviews have been conducted with sales and
forecasting specialists and a senior product manager. This ensured to get additional
insights on related subjects, which determine the supply chain of Phase One.
The responsible supply chain project manager of Seat provided most of the insights
to reveal the current status of the supply chain management. This was realized
through multiple communication rounds and interviews. In addition to that, the
project manager introduced diﬀerent other experts to elaborate on their area. There-
fore, interviews with participants from the production planning, the innovation team
and the smart logistics department were performed.

Literature Review
Looking into the current literature elaborating on Industry 4.0 and its eﬀects on the
supply chain, one of the ﬁrst steps is to separate the supply chain into its smaller
subparts [47]. According to Tjahjono, a reasonable separation is to structure the
supply chain into the areas of Procurement, Warehousing, Logistic and Fulﬁllment
[47]. Since the fulﬁllment is not part of the underlying research (see chapter 1 and
6.9) and Industry 4.0 within the supply chain does always aﬀect elementary parts
of the production, the production is added as another elementary part of the supply
chain [51]. Therefore, the upcoming chapter is divided into the parts: Procurement,
Warehousing, Logistics and Production.
On top of that, there are diﬀerent areas which are aﬀected by the occurrence of
Industry 4.0, such as Decision Making, Smart Products, Big Data, Horizontal and
Vertical Integration, Internet of Things, Artiﬁcial Intelligence and Barriers of Indus-
try 4.0 [40].
All of those aspects will be described in detail in the following chapters. The content
is based on a a structured literature review, which has been conducted according
to Rowley [41]. Through applying the hermeneutic circle approach, more detailed
literature could be identiﬁed regarding speciﬁc topics [5].
3.1
Procurement
According to Nicoletti, one has to diﬀerentiate Procurement 4.0, which is a neolo-
gism following the wording of Industry 4.0 mainly from the e-commerce, which was
the last main development in procurement [36]. Therefore, a procurement depart-
ment, in a 4.0 sense, is responsible to create a value network along all the suppliers
until the own production line. This includes coherent data ﬂow among the diﬀerent
stakeholders, processes and materials [36]. Another important aspect is the ﬂexibil-
ity of suppliers to be able to adjust according to live data updates and small batch
sizes [36].
To handle this ﬂexibility, a modular strategy can be introduced, which will be de-
scribed in a more detailed way in chapter 3.3. Live data and connectivity result
moreover in a similarity between the orders of ﬁnal customers and orders to ven-
dors. This brings a higher accuracy, an increase of speed and a lower human cost
factor [36].
Regarding data, Procurement 4.0 does as well include transparency
for all stakeholders. A shared knowledge with supply chain members about vendor
numbers, logistical processes and other statuses will positively aﬀect productivity as
well as ﬂexibility regarding seasonal ﬂuctuation and other inﬂuencing factors. For
this approach it is furthermore useful to include smart products (see chapter 3.6),
which are able to communicate their location and characterization [36]. All of those
changes involve new requirements for human resources. Working proﬁles need to be

3.2
Warehousing
One of the elementary changes for warehousing displays the straight through data
processing between IT systems, machines and products, which decreases the amount
of needed storage space [51].
Through machine to machine (M2M) communication (see chapter 3.4) and smart
products (see chapter 3.6), warehousing systems can be better aligned with produc-
tion schedules and incoming supplier deliveries. The focus is to schedule the supply
chain and the internal production in a way that stock is where it is needed in a short
period of time, based on live data acquisition. To receive this data, it is ﬁrst of all
important to include data acquisition at every single input and output station [51].
This can be put into practise through smart products (see chapter 3.6), which are
self communicating, or ﬁxed located sensor machines, able to track incoming and
outgoing parts [4].
On top of that, the picking logic warehouse internally depends on data manage-
ment as well and follows a fuzzy logic [29]. As stated by Lee, the fuzzy logic tech-
nique describes the most eﬀective picking logic, which does not select according to
one speciﬁc manner of the available picking approaches like strict order-, batch- or
sequential-picking, but combines them in an optimized way. Moreover, such a fuzzy
logic technique is able to improve its abilities through Machine Learning (see chapter
3.10).
Through live communication between autonomous working pick and place robots,
the Enterprise Resource Planing (ERP) system and the cloud based production
schedule, changes are immediately implemented and products are only transported
from the warehouse to a machine once they are actually needed [29]. On top of that,
an ERP system as an overall resource planning tool, includes information about the
production processes of the speciﬁc products. Therefore, it is able to determine an
auto adapted production plan [51].
Through digital twins in the big data cloud, the structure of warehouses can be
planned [29].
To summarize, the described contemporary Warehouse Management System (WMS)
is able to work with ambient intelligence and thus provide heterogeneity and com-
posability since it is able to handle multiple synchronized decisions [29].
3.3
Logistics
When it comes to logistics the literature represent a variety of approaches. Hachmann
focuses his research on the improvement of hardware and how to enable a modu-
lar logistic system to increase eﬀectiveness of logistic processes [22]. These logistic
modules are able to interact with each other, machines and warehousing systems
10
==PAGE:18==
according to the plug-and-produce principle, which implies an easy possibility of
physical attachment of all kinds of stored products [22]. Especially regarding small
batch sizes, which are increasing through the trend of mass customization, this can
improve ﬂexibility [22].
On the other side, Timm concentrates his keynotes on the overall changing process
of the industry from a hardware oriented logistic to a software oriented logistic [46].
He underlines the necessity of communication between execution and decision sys-
tems, as seen in Cyber Physical Systems (CPS) (see chapter 3.4), to improve the
eﬀectivity of parts going from location A to location B. To give a concrete example,
Timm speaks about products being able to communicate with their environment
in a way that they are able to include their surrounding sub-systems-priorities and
neighbours-priorities. By including those priorities into their own decision making
it is possible to realize the overall optimum output (see chapter 3.5). To implement
this, it is necessary to create new processes in modelling, engineering and implemen-
tation [46].
Logistic 4.0, which is used in analogy to Industry 4.0 for the latest developments in
the logistic industry, needs those modelling methods. The focus of those modelling
methods lies on multi-agent based simulations (MABS) to monitor the results of dif-
ferent logistic scenarios [46]. MABS in speciﬁc are able to simulate how interactions
between diﬀerent sub-systems, including the consideration of the diﬀerent priorities
of sub-systems, evolve.
An additional interesting aspect according to Timm is, that machines or products
are able to negotiate with each other about which process is best to do next (see
chapter 3.6) [46]. Therefore, negotiation outputs can be steered according to an
optimal allocation, machine using costs, machine failure rates or other priorities of
the company in particular [46]. This increases the ﬂexibility and eﬀectiveness in
logistics through having the possibility to change priorities in real time. Moreover,
products create logistical processes on their own, based on the optimum for them
and their surroundings.
A similar approach is proposed by Baretto, who sees as well technological improve-
ments and their inﬂuences towards the diﬀerent sectors of the logistic as the main
attributes of Logistic 4.0 [28]. The diﬀerent sectors he describes are next to the
ERP system, the WMS, the transportation management system (TMS) the intelli-
gent transportation system (ITS) and information security.
To get a deeper insight, the ERP system is in need to increase the ﬂexibility as
well as the agility according to a real time observation of changes. A higher re-
sponsiveness is able to improve logistic processes [28]. The focus on the WMS is
on coordinating and aligning the diﬀerent value chain phases and their priorities to
send and store goods and products in the most eﬀective way and prepare them for
further steps once they are needed.

The cloud based storage of data, including the recording of data through RFID
or similar sensors, is part of the improved TMS. This enables not only tracking the
current location and current movements, but as well predicting future locations [28].
Connecting those TMS data with the IoT, which Barreto deﬁnes as the Smart TMS,
gives moreover a great opportunity for improved decision making (see chapter 3.5).
On a more external viewpoint, the ITS includes external data like the electronic

hardware solution like a modular system to enable a plug-and-produce approach is

3.4
Production
To understand the developments regarding production, it helps to see the main
trends for manufacturing companies which currently inﬂuence production: small
batches, a higher degree of servitization and decentralized production [36] [22].
Especially the overall movement towards mass customization results in the trend for
smaller batch sizes. This includes an individualization of products along the whole
supply chain [35]. Because of this, it is necessary to establish a ﬂexible production
line.
Moreover, the higher servitization implies to add services to the hardware product,
which entails often co-value creation and alliances [35].
On a more detailed level, machines experience a tremendous additional area of re-
sponsibilities through diﬀerent technological aspects: M2M communication, human-
machine interaction (HMI), CPS and cyber physical production systems (CPPS) [51]
[35]. All those aspects will be described in a more detailed way subsequently.
M2M communication explains an interaction of machines without any human inter-
action through the implementation of intelligent applications. One example are auto
adaptive controlling mechanisms in machines which regulate each other through un-
derlying objectives and constraints [51]. The results of M2M communication can be
seen vertically integrated into the internal networking and horizontally integrated
12
==PAGE:20==
along the supply chain [47]. The vertical integration ensures a data communication
at diﬀerent internal level. Therefore, a gapless communication between diﬀerent
systems on the diﬀerent levels is possible. As an example, data between the man-
ufacturing executive system (MES) and the ERP can be transferred in real time.
Machines can act immediately according to those feedback loops [51]. Regarding
the horizontal aspect, diﬀerent machines on the same level are able to communicate
about occupancies, maintenance and additional useful data to create an optimized
auto adapted production plan [35] [51].
HMI, on the other side, focuses on the collaboration between machines and humans.
New technologies like virtual reality or augmented reality improve the eﬃciency of
the interaction between humans and machines through visualization. Moreover, live
data processing is able to improve the safety and the accuracy level for HMI [51].
Decentralized decision making implies to give more autonomy to sub-systems and
machines like CPS (see chapter 3.5). All of those major trends aﬀect the production
and CPS and are an important basis for the elaborations in this chapter [46].
Another optimizing factor of the production in terms of Industry 4.0 represents the
ability of machines to negotiate with each other. Depending on the diﬀerent objec-
tives and requirements of products, the machine allocation can be deﬁned through
a digital negotiation process between products and machines [46]. Therefore, the
objective of the negotiations can be adjusted, according to the production strategy
and situation, towards the most cost-time or resource eﬀective way [46].
All of those aspects can be summarized in the deﬁnition of CPS. CPS are com-
munication systems which are connected through the whole factory [47] [40]. The
system includes besides the physical system such as the hardware, machine sensors,
actuators, and distribution controllers, a network through which it is connected and
therefore able to transfer data [28]. As ﬁgure 3.1 shows, the hardware represent the
physical system. Next to it, the attached sensors are collecting the required data.
This data, as the arrows in the ﬁgure show, is transferred to the network, where
distribution controllers are deducting decisions according to the received data. The
network communicates those decisions to the actuators which interact with the hard-
ware to execute the desired changes.
To summarize this concept, data of the systems and sensors are transferred, com-
bined and executed through feedback loops in real time velocity by an IT system
[51]. Through this analysis of data, the CPS is able to make decisions according to
speciﬁc incoming data.
As a consequence, the CPS connects execution levels with decision levels including
big data through feedback loops [46] [51]. If those CPS are connected with each
other, they are called Cyber Physical Production Systems (CPPS) [40].
In this context, other theories come into place, such as AI and Machine Learning
to ensure continuous improvement [51]. These aspects are described in detail in

3.5
Decision Making
As already mentioned in chapter 3.3, the decision making of manufacturing compa-
nies, which are adapting Industry 4.0 concepts, is aﬀected in many diﬀerent ways. As
mentioned by Marques, it is important to ﬁrst of all diﬀerentiate two main concepts:
centralized-decision making (CDM) and decentralized-decision making (DDM). In
CDM, one single decision center (DC) is responsible to manage the decisions and
operations as well as all the system information. On top of that, this DC is in charge
of optimizing the entire network. In contrast to that, a DDM system enforces every
individual sub system, nod or entity to conduct own decisions according to own
objectives and constraints [35]. In addition to the internal speciﬁcations of each
sub systems, the entities in a DDM system are able to include the objectives and
restrictions of other aﬀected entities. Depending on the correlation and importance,
the inﬂuencing factor of other entities can be adjusted. To build these complex
decisional structures, which are as well able to handle unforeseen events, a commu-
nication through data bases and live-data is indispensable [35].
Both Marques and Lee agree that a DDM is necessary to keep up with the necessary
ﬂexibility, eﬀectiveness and velocity regarding decision making in Industry 4.0 [29]
[35]. Reddy adds that the decision makers of tomorrow are not people, but big data
programs. The following section does therefore focus on how decision making could
work regarding big data solutions [40].
14
==PAGE:22==
According to Marques, there are two diﬀerent approaches. First of all, an horizontal
integration approach for DDM, with the main focus on ensuring leadership of the
single cooperation. This would implicate, that every single entity is getting enabled
to receive the information needed to perform a decision on itself based on speciﬁc
constraints and input data.
Secondly, an approach of virtualizing the automation pyramid for vertical integra-
tion and decision making [35]. Regarding this approach, there are diﬀerent aspects
to consider. The main aspect is about deﬁning the decision level of the problem.
According to the gravity and timeframe, the problem rises up to a speciﬁc deci-
sion level. Depending on the decision level, more comprehensive information and
constraints are included. Another aspect represents the information level, which
deﬁnes the level of management which is able to access and inﬂuence the process of
the decision. In a more detailed way, Marques [35] diﬀerentiates two ways to handle
the DDM in vertical integration: bottom-up or top-down.
If problems and decisions need to be solved and made on a machine-, device- or
component-level, this will be solved on a operational and real time decision level.
Nevertheless, the information will be spread through big data solutions to higher
level so that managers (managerial level) are as well able to oversee the production
schedule and similar decisions. This approach is called bottom-up perspective. On
the other side, the top-down perspective deﬁnes a global structure, where mech-
anisms and modularization are broken down towards smaller sub-systems to still
follow the bigger goals and objectives. The two approaches are not conﬂicting and
can be combined in order to use the advantages of both [35].
3.6
Smart Products
The main idea of smart products or objects is best described by their characteris-
tics, technological features and the possibilities gained through them. As described
by Wagner, smart products diﬀerentiate from conventional products through the
ability of containing, observing and communicating data [51]. To state an example,
Reddy explains that smart products include data about the processes they need to
pass through and how those processes are assessed [40]. Therefore, they are able to
oversee their own production process [36].
The technological features of those products are, according to Wagner, microelec-
tronics, sensors and communication and processing modules. These add-ons enable
the product to have the above named characteristics.
The most popular and most important add-ons are radio-frequency identiﬁcation
tags (RFID) [4]. According to Ben-Daya, there are ﬁve diﬀerent types of RFID tags
which are important. The most simple RFID tag is a passive tag equipped with a
read and write capability. The second type is as well a passive device but comes in
addition with a security function to prevent external access. The diﬀerence of semi
15
==PAGE:23==
passive tags, which represent the third category, is that they are equipped with a
battery and include sensors to receive and store additional data. Category 4 and
5 tags are active tags which means that they are as well capable of communicat-
ing with similar tags (Category 4), activate other tags and interact directly with
back-end networks (Category 5) [4]. Depending on the production and the speciﬁc
requirements additional sensors to measure all kinds of factors are attached to the
RFID codes to fulﬁll additional tasks.
Through those characteristics, which are enabled through the named technology,
diﬀerent advantages can be gained. One of the advantages is that small lots can
be produced in an easier way since products know what they need during the pro-
duction [40]. Another great opportunity is that all the information collected and
analyzed can create a high visibility along the supply chain. This can help to realize
occurring problems in an early stage [4]. Finally, the ﬂow of production as well as
the cycle time of the products can be optimized through a more detailed production
plan (see chapter 3.4) [40].
3.7
Big Data
In general, big data can be seen as one of the main aspects of Industry 4.0. Accord-
ing to Leveling, nowadays big data is one of the key points for creating new business
models and an improved level of service [31].
Essentially, big data can be seen as a new part of the IT system, which companies
are already using [31]. The main idea is to collect, process and store a tremendous
amount of data [40]. The sources of this data can be diﬀerentiated into two kind of
sources: external and internal data sources [31].
External data sources are mainly based on social media-, search engines- or other
data-portals. Moreover, external data can be collected from other companies like
suppliers or competitors. An overall integration of such data is described as a big
data ecosystem [31]. Two main aspects need to be considered regarding external
data collection. Firstly, there may be costs included in accessing the data and sec-
ondly, depending on the source, the data may not be structured.
On the other side, internal data is available through the diﬀerent accesses (cloud-
plugs) at CPS, sensors and connected things (see chapter 3.4) [4].
When it comes to the amount of data, one diﬀerentiates between Nucleus Data,
which are usually gigabytes of data from customers or products, Community Data
in terabyte areas, which are diﬀerent kinds of codes and addresses, and Open Big
Data, petabytes of data from social media streams, sensors and other data creating
parts [31].
On top of this diﬀerentiation, data can be separated by the velocity and variety
[31]. The velocity is a factor that implicates how much data arrives irregularly on
16
==PAGE:24==
top of the regular data stream. The variety implies the diﬀerent data structure
types included into a data stream. In addition to those three Vs (volume, velocity
and variety), value is often used as a fourth describing factor. The value implies
the possibilities to process and analyze the data for further use [31]. To be able
to receive this data, the Internet of Things (IoT) provides a platform and creates
a network of all the products and parts, which are connected [4] (compare chapter
3.9). According to Ben-Daya such a big data set results out of IoT networks, which
can be structured into 4 layers:
• Sensing Layer
Data is emitted from sensors and translated into an electronical signal.
• Networking Layer
The electronical signal is transferred through the network.
• Service Layer
Diﬀerent applications are combined with the electronical signal for speciﬁc
usages.
• Interface Layer
The results are displayed for users in an interactive way.
Another important technical factor represents the cloudplug, which can be located
in the networking layer. A cloudplug can be seen as an access into the cloud and
therefore the big data environment [40]. It connects machines and CPS to the big
data environment and decides as well which data in which format is needed. In
general, incoming data can be structured in many diﬀerent ways. To ensure a good
working service layer, it is important to be able to receive all diﬀerent kinds of data
[31]. Therefore, Leveling recommends NoSQL databases to combine the external
data in an easier way with the internal data [31].

3.9
Internet of Things
The Internet of Things (IoT) can be seen as a continuation to the inﬂuence factors
started by the internet [39]. It diﬀerentiates to what was available before by the fact
that information about a current location or a current state can be available in real
time for decision making and other processes worldwide [45].
Since the internet started by enriching the communication ﬂow and providing ex-
traordinary eﬃciency potentials, the IoT increases those possibilities through bridg-
ing the material- and information-ﬂow. This advantage results from live product-
and object-tracking and a simultaneous representation in the information systems
[39]. On top of that, the technological possibilities are able to increase not only the
amount of data but also the accuracy (see chapter 3.7). These main technologies,
included in the IoT will be described subsequently [39].
• Information Perception Technologies
To eject data from diﬀerent environments and including diverse speciﬁcations,
two main technologies and their inter-connectivity with the internet are im-
portant: on the one side, RFID and on the other side, wireless sensor networks
(WSN). While RFID tags have their main purpose in representing a unique
identiﬁer, which can communicate without direct contact (see chapter 3.6),
WSN are a network of sensors ejecting data from the environment. Especially
the combination of the two technologies results in an important combination
for receiving live data from unique locations.
• Multi Network Fusion Technology
The Multi Network Fusion Technology represents the connector between the
machine data itself and the data generated from the Information Perception
Technologies. It connects these information through the speciﬁc objectives and
constraints of the diﬀerent stakeholders and layers. Because of these diﬀerent
layers it is called a multi network. Therefore, the network is further responsible
for executing decisions according to the earlier mentioned aspects.
• Pervasive Computing Technology
To give speciﬁc objects the possibility to react to certain changes and make
them ’smart’ is performed by the Pervasive Computing Technology. This tech-
nology, based on computer code, is able to enable a widespread amount of
things to process data, get in contact with other objects to exchange informa-
tion and perform speciﬁc actions.
All of those technological processes enable the IoT to make environments like a
supply chain more agile and ﬂexible [39]. On top that, it helps to realize, organize
and structure highly decentralized organizations (see chapter 3.5) [45].

3.10
Artiﬁcial Intelligence
In the context of Industry 4.0 the terms Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning
and Deep Learning are used frequently and thereby often in similar ways. Therefore,
all three of them will be elaborated in this chapter. First, the general idea of AI is
explained, before the sub theme Machine Learning is described. Deep Learning as
a sub theme of Machine Learning is spotlighted subsequently. Finally, the overall
impacts of AI on supply chain management are mentioned.
According to Hokey, AI is introduced to develop thinking machines which can, in
some ways, replace human intelligence [23]. Ongsulee deﬁnes AI a bit more detailed,
describing devices that are able to perceive its environment and take actions to max-
imize the output according to a given goal [38]. Further, he states that the most
important basis for such processes is the ability to distinguish patterns and make
predictions according to data.
Machine Learning, representing a subset of AI, focuses on deﬁning and using patters.
In detail, one diﬀerentiate between four main Machine Learning approaches, which
will be described subsequently [38]:
• Supervised Machine Learning
The idea of Supervised Machine Learning is based on providing the IT sys-
tem with a big amount of labeled historical data. Labeled means that the
information concludes input and output data as well as a related true or false
statement. Therefore, the IT system is able to compare those results with the
present data. This is a common way of using Machine Learning even though,
the approach depends on the availability of a high amount of historical data.
• Unsupervised Machine Learning
In contrast to the approach of Supervised Machine Learning, Unsupervised
Machine Learning does not base its pattern and results on historical data. The
IT system itself is responsible for ﬁnding segments with similar attributes in
the given data.
The system therefore screens the available data to extract
similar patterns, which then can be used to execute the requested output.
• Semi Supervised Machine Learning
Semi Supervised Machine Learning is a combination of the supervised and
unsupervised learning approach. As graph 3.4 shows, input data is not only
historical data, but as well reinforcement data and the given data. In diﬀerent
steps, patterns are identiﬁed and compared with the given data. This process
continues until a match is realized and the outcome determined (yellow dots).
As seen in the graphic, the amount and diversity of considered data decreases
(red and grey dots) according to the progress of the Machine Learning process.
20
==PAGE:28==
• Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement Learning is another method which is used if outcomes are tested
in a trial and error scheme to realize and learn from trying. It results in multiple
feedback loops until the best policy is determined. These results can be used
as well as incoming data for the pattern search.
Figure 3.4: Machine Learning network for Semi Supervised Learning (refer to [38])
If these approaches of Machine Learning conclude of more than one hidden layer,
one is talking about Deep Learning [38]. These layers are IT-constraints, which
depend on sensor based inputs. The layers are connected through diﬀerent inputs
and can therefore handle more complex problems.
One of the main advantages of Machine Learning represents the predictive anal-
ysis, which can be generated. Therefore, the found patterns are used to predict
risks and opportunities.
While AI in general and Machine and Deep Learning in speciﬁc have been in exis-
tence for many years, supply chain management is just about to realize the tremen-
dous potentials. Especially the described predictive analysis potentials have been
recently evaluated regarding the supply chain management [23]: according to Hokey,
AI brings potentials to complex and interrelated decision making processes, which
can be executed and steered by reliable machines or CPS processes [23]. Moreover,
those processes are able to happen in real time. These advantages can be widely used
in diﬀerent supply chain management processes. To state some examples, one can
name the warehouse structuring approach and its picking processes or forecasting
improvement through patterns from historical data [23].

3.11
Barriers of Industry 4.0
New developments always include new challenges and barriers [35]. These barriers
are important to consider for all companies which join this new movement, even
though they do it through diﬀerent strategies [42]. One of these barriers are stan-
dardizations, which are mainly important for the horizontal integration [32]. To be
able to transfer data without a huge eﬀort along the supply chain, it is important to
create worldwide standards for technologies like RFID tags, M2M communication,
Machine Learning or data formatting [32].
Another important barrier represents the security issue. More than two thirds of the
companies, which are about to implement Industry 4.0 into their production pro-
cesses are unsure about the related security [35]. One of the key factors to ensure
security in data communication and storing is the blockchain technology [36]. Some
of the positive attributes are described by Marques: high availability, since the net-
work of participants can be maintained even though some nods fail, transparency, by
ensuring that all transactions are visible for the participants, immutability, through
a high traceability of changes and digitization, since every document is translated
into code (see chapter 5.1.1). Nevertheless, the costs for implementing blockchain
technology are still high and the dissemination among companies is relatively low
[27].
One way to tackle the two barriers of standardizations and safety are cooperations,
in which companies, countries and additional institutions can work together to cre-
ate a common understanding, norms and guidelines [35].
Finally, all these developments create another challenge for organizations and com-
panies. The new arising opportunities, imply a request for new job proﬁles. Often
these proﬁles diﬀer from the current workforce [36]. Especially regarding the sector
that combines IT and manufacturing, a skills gap can lead to missing opportunities
[35].

Analysis: A data stream network description
The following analysis will describe the found theoretical aspects in a more visual-
ized way, and summarize the individual aspects into a combined system.
To start with, the inﬂuences of Industry 4.0 will be described from a product per-
spective, by describing a product journey through the whole supply chain. The
journey begins with the raw material supplier and ends with the ﬁnal manufactur-
ing, including main aspects of Industry 4.0. Therefore, it can be seen as a best
practice product journey.
Subsequently, the ﬁndings of the literature review are combined into data streams
for the supply chain segments Supplier, Warehouse, Logistic and Production. In this
context, speciﬁc data systems and data networks are described in detail. This analy-
sis contains close insights on the speciﬁc data usages and states the interconnections
between the diﬀerent data working systems. Finally, all of the interconnections and
data streams are combined to an overall data stream network.
4.1
Best practice product journey
The below explained product journey is not directly applicable for a particular prod-
uct and needs adjustments to the speciﬁc environment. Nevertheless, the general
processes in the main sections Supplier, Warehouse, Logistics and Production can
be seen as general patters which can be transformed along any supply chain.
To begin with, most product journeys start at an n-tier raw material supplier. Al-
ready at this stage, one of the main attributes of Industry 4.0 comes into play:
the linking between the raw material, which later will be transformed into the ﬁnal
product and an object which is able to receive, store and communicate data. As
mentioned in chapter 3.6, these tasks can be fulﬁlled among others by RFID tags.
Next to a physical attachment of the tag to the material, a new entry into the IT
system needs to store the combination in the digital system. Through this "mar-
riage" between the product and the RFID tag, the product is enabled to receive,
store and send data. Therefore, the product can be considered a smart product [51].
In this early stage the smart product will receive ﬁrst information: the process steps,
the smart product needs to go through during the production and which deadlines
it has to adhere.
In particularly, the smart product communicates with the IT system and therefore
the big data cloud, its current location and receives information about the needed
working procedures (see ﬁgure 4.1). According to that exchange of information,
transportation modules, which are connected to the IT system as well, are picking
up the smart product from its location to transport it to a CPS system, which is
able to perform the needed machine processes. After the smart product determines
its characteristics and presence, the machine embedded in the CPS performs the

==PAGE:31==
required processes.
If the smart product is not in need of any additional processes and is not yet needed
on a further logistical step, smart products get stored in a warehouse. The logistical
process between the n-tier supplier and warehouses is comparable to the logistical
process between warehouses and the ﬁnal production site. These logistical processes
and the general warehouse interaction with the smart product will be explained
subsequently in detail.
Once the smart product arrives at the warehouse, it communicates its arrival and
receives information about where to get stored. In the background, the IT system
is providing the ﬁnal production plan, including the speciﬁc time schedule for the
smart product. According to that and the transportation resources available, the
fuzzy logical system is picking up the smart product in an optimized way. Therefore,
it can happen that the smart product joins another product in an eﬃcient plug-and-
play solution to get stored in a bigger box since they share the same interim or ﬁnal
destination.
The overall logistic scheduling comes into play, once the product is needed at a dif-
ferent plant or production line. Regarding these logistical processes, the IT system
does not only analyze, where the smart product currently is and at which location it
is needed. The IT system analyzes as well the present traﬃc and weather forecasts,
to include this information into the forecasting and therefore ensure, that the smart
product arrives to its destination in the demanded time frame.
After the smart product received a spot in one of the upcoming containers to get
transported to the ﬁnal manufacturing site, it is able to constantly send a live loca-
tion during the travel to keep the IT systems updated.
Once the smart product arrives at the manufacturing plant, it communicates its
location, status and, if necessary, with which other products it is stored through
plug-and-play solutions, with the IT system. Moreover, it receives modiﬁcations of
its speciﬁcation and characteristics if there happened to be any updates or changes.
According to those speciﬁcations of the speciﬁc product, the IT system adapts dead-
lines and machine working times to create an adapted, optimized production sched-
ule according to the objectives and constraints of the smart product.
Depending on the production schedule, the smart product is stored in another ware-
house or directly brought to a speciﬁc CPS. There, the processes are executed in the
same way as described for the supplier production beforehand. Finally, the smart
product receives a quality control and communicates the results to the IT system.
If the results are not satisfying, the smart product is directly send to a rework sta-
tion. Once equipped with the needed characteristics, it gets transported to the ﬁnal
customer. If there are slight deviations revealed by the quality control, the responsi-
ble machines for those characteristics are adjusted accordingly. Even though, those
actions are performed automatically, the information is presented to production

A third external stream is the input data streams for the ERP system. On the one

forecasting is another important data input into the demand planning at the ERP
system.

• Blockchain technology for supply chain applications
To understand the speciﬁc applications of the blockchain technology, it is
important to get a basic understanding about the idea and the technology of
the blockchain concept. Therefore, this chapter is structured into a general
description of the blockchain technology ﬁrst, before speciﬁc application ex-
amples in general and oﬀered by Bosch are explained.
In general, the blockchain technology is an open source technology with the
main idea to store transaction processes in a decentralized structure [27]. The
basis of the technology is that a network of computers work together to store
data [2]. These computers are called nods. Every single nod has to approve
that a transaction is done before it stores an element as a chain of computer
code. This code can not be altered. This results in a linked sequence of blocks
and creates therefore a immutable record of realized activities [27]. As ﬁgure
5.1 summarizes, a request of transaction ﬁrst needs to be validated by the com-
plete computer network, before a new block, which is added to the blockchain,
is created. As a new part of the blockchain, a new block will be added to the
overall blockchain including the speciﬁc computer code.
These characteristics lead to two main advantages: ﬁrst, it provides a secure
way to store transactions without the risk of manipulation and second, since
every nod stores every element, a complete transparency among the partici-
pants is ensured.
Next to an increased level of data security, the high transparency brings possi-
bilities to the supply chain as well. Therefore, the blockchain concept includes
a possibility to increase not only the transparency but as well the traceability,
since these two aspects are directly linked in terms of supply chain manage-
ment [27].

==PAGE:44==
As one example, the blockchain technology could be used to store all processes
and events along the supply chain for a speciﬁc product. This would enable
stakeholders to identify speciﬁc processes even long after the product passed
those stages. Since this information can be stored for every single product
and process step, it is able to provide a speciﬁc feedback for the user about
the product even though the supply chain contains a tremendous amount of
processes and participants. This technology can be combined with other tech-
nologies, like the tracking technology through RFID tags and can therefore
provide a digital map about the locations the product went through [27]. An-
other application example regarding the blockchain technology comes into play,
if algorithms and programs are included to execute processes automatically,
once speciﬁc conditions appear [2]. This direct link between conditions and
executing processes on blockchain basis is called smart contracts.
In other
words, smart contracts are automatically fulﬁlled based on conditions which
are beforehand coded in a speciﬁc application.

the data stream into the control unit is needed.

are active tags, they are able to not only send information like their location but
are as well able to store information and activate other tags (see chapter 3.6). In
the Bosch plant, this would mean an investment into active RFID tags for each
transportation module, since not every part is equipped with a tag but only mul-
tiple usage logistic transportation modules. Thanks to the multiple usage, costs
are manageable and are paying themselves oﬀthrough multiple use. The active
RFID tags are moreover able to store the production data and communicate with
and activate involved machines. Moreover, the location of the logistic module is not
only determined at some speciﬁc gates, but the location and the speciﬁcations of
the products is updated and available constantly for linked IT systems.
Therefore, necessary data results from the active RFID tags. The information about
the process steps and machine requirements are provided from the MES and com-
municated to the RFID tags. This data is used by the underlying IT network which
reacts to the information received through the RFID tags and performs executions
according to the underlying constraints and objectives. A possible extension of this
data usage is an inclusion into the earlier introduced blockchain concept. Therefore,
the diﬀerent milestone data steps can be stored directly in the blockchain. Milestone
data could be the information where materials are stored, which machines were in-
volved in the production and when were production steps performed. This would
result in a transparent and secure storage of the production data for the rest of the
products life-cycle. Besides, this would help to prevent product-piracy, since ﬁnal
retailers and consumers are able to see where the product was manufactured [43].
To summarize, this leads to a complete transparency and traceability for the ﬁnal
customer.
The infrastructure at the Bosch plant regarding quality already includes many In-
dustry 4.0 characteristics. Nevertheless, an important part is missing and can be
recommended: To share quality information and potentials along the whole supply
chain.
This means, that the current quality controls, should be extended towards incoming
parts. Therefore, incoming parts, which are currently already registered through an
41
==PAGE:49==
automatic registration, would initially run through a quality control. This would en-
sure the quality of the parts and brings two additional important advantages which
have to be considered. One of them is, that trends of quality failures and errors
can be directly communicated with the supplier and this supplier can directly react
autonomously or through the responsible quality manager. The second advantage
that such an incoming material control brings along, is the possibility to include the
results of the quality check into the blockchain and make the payment to the sup-
plier depending on the delivery. Therefore, the smart contract, which was described
earlier (see chapter 5.1.1), includes a condition that parts, which are not fulﬁlling
the agreed norms and speciﬁcations, are not paid. This resolves many problems
regarding reverse payment transfers and quality discussions.
Regarding the data structure of this recommendation, the data is generated from
the quality gate. The underlying software compares the present measurements with
the agreed conditions, and removes the inadequate parts. On top of that, the data is
analyzed to screen for trends and increased deviation to communicate those trends
to the supplier. Finally the amount of products, which is within the requirements, is
communicated to the MES and the blockchain methodology to realize the payment.

5.2.3
Internal identiﬁed potentials
Being aware of their current situation and the possibilities Industry 4.0 and the
newest technological developments are oﬀering, Phase One focuses on the following
potentials to work on next.
A better sales forecasting for an improved inventory level is the main objective which
should be managed through a better data analysis of the already ejected data [8].
Accordingly, additional data analysts are already hired to determine the relation
between data, ejected out of the two data sources, and sales, as described in chapter
5.2.2.
The software product Capture One can give an overview about which pictures the
user of the software is taking and how the pictures are edited. The movements and
activities on the webpages are able to represent current trends and interests in new
products. According to this information, anonymous proﬁles can be developed and
clustered. For each one of them probabilities of a purchase of a new product can be
developed. Therefore, the goal is to be able to predict the demand and incoming
sales order of about 80% so that the availability can be improved while stabilizing
the overall inventory [8].
In addition to that, Phase One is elaborating on transforming the inventory system
from a BMI to a Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI). This would imply a complete
responsibility shift, from Phase One towards the suppliers.
According to the literature, VMI is a supply chain system where the supplier is
47
==PAGE:55==
responsible to maintain a speciﬁc inventory level through adjusting lot sizes and
delivery cycles [52]. The advantages are that under speciﬁc conditions the inventory
costs are lower, since the supplier is in full charge of both, the level of inventory and
the lot sizes. Therefore, the supplier can adjust the deliveries better to his product
availability. Often the producing factory tries to lower the overall responsibility of
having suﬃcient inventory through the VMI system, since managing the seasonal
peaks and lows are now responsibilities of the supplier [52].
For these named advantages, the VMI is already based in many diﬀerent industries.
Recently, this VMI experience additional importance, since Industry 4.0 brings ad-
ditional simpliﬁcations. Former, communication about accurate inventory levels and
demand trends were diﬃcult to communicate and to foresee. Through Industry 4.0,
a direct communication and measurement based on live data is possible.
Phase One aims to use exactly these advantages. Giving the supplier the possibility
to look personally into the inventory and let them manage deliveries and therefore
inventory. All the data needs to be live transferred to the supplier including the
proximate manufacturing schedule. As a result, Phase One hopes for a lower level
of inventory and in addition a lower responsibility for inventory overstock. On top of
that, Phase One would eliminate manually processed fulﬁlling orders, which would
mean saving manpower in the administrative sector [8].

Similarly to chapter 5.1.4, the recommendations are structured into the four parts:

By transferring Supply Chain 4.0 towards the concepts and processes of the Phase

A VMI 4.0 system would therefore combine the VMI approach of giving the supplier
the responsibility of maintaining the service level with the advantages of demand
prediction through data analysis.
Therefore, the supplier has access to the predicted demand data and the detailed
production schedule. According to this data, the supplier is able to plan and deliver
the inventory. This helps not only to decrease the amount of inventory dramatically
but helps as well to give the supplier the complete responsibility to have the prod-
ucts on time at the production site to be able to produce constantly according to
the optimized production plan.
Analyzing the data, which is needed to do such a direct commissioning, leads to
overall demand data as well as the resulting production schedule plan. This data

Subsequently, this information and control system of the supplier combines the in-

A second recommendation refers to the main focus of Phase One in general: Qual-

To increase the quality, a predictive control system with automatic quality

change the settings of the machine.

quality provided by Phase One but as well a high decrease of the needed time for
rework and problem ﬁndings.
Finally, the blockchain technology could be included, to store the results of the qual-
ity controls and ensure a long term reliability for Phase One in case of complaints
regarding quality issues later on.

the Seat plant in Martorell, Barcelona [44].

Speciﬁcation of the supply chain
Regarding the supply chain, the above named production volume leads to an enor-
mous amount of complexity. For the production site in Martorell, Seat is working
together with more than 600 suppliers [10]. In addition, these supplier often do not
only deliver Martorell with speciﬁc parts but, according to the blocking strategy of
Seat (see chapter 5.3), more production plants of the Volkswagen Group. Therefore,

5.3.2
Current status of the supply chain
The above described separation of the speciﬁc departments regarding supply chain

This shows that all the diﬀerent departments were successful in introducing speciﬁc

lack of transparency, describing the little visibility for the areas: stock of suppliers
and sub-suppliers, situation of trucks in transit, shipping times and lung coverings

without foreseeing any events.
On top of the above listed characteristics of the current supply chain, Seat imple-

Still, the TMS system only connects with the logistic department and does not in-

Looking at the topic of connectivity, Seat just launched an internal pilot project

To get a better idea about the history and the present development of supply chain

diﬀerentiated between supply chain control towers, logistic services control towers
and research supply chain towers [49]. Even though each of them can be considered
an individual IT entity, all of them are connected and integrated to ensure an over-

event management, the general aim is to give a panoramic view about all supply

of alert to action, task and case management as well as global visibility of demand
and supply as ﬁgure 5.5 illustrates. On top of that, suggested problem resolution
scenarios help to react in the right way [49].

To do all of this, it is necessary to install ﬁve diﬀerent layers in the IT system of a
supply chain control tower[49]:
• Supply Chain Perception Layer
Data needs to be collected and stored. The best way to collect data in the
supply chain processes is to use real time sensing and transmitting devices.
Therefore, data is constantly updated and the proceeding processes are able
to work with the newest data.
• Service Layer
The Service Layer combines the live data with the process ﬂow of the speciﬁc
systems. Therefore, the data is linked for example with the packaging, trans-
porting and unpacking processes of the logistic department. Thus, the data
becomes structured.
• Information Operation Control Layer
This layer represent the information storage and the creation of feedback loops
within the IT system. The data is analyzed and compared with the speciﬁc
stored data, according to the process identiﬁed in the Service Layer. Related
to this analysis, feedback loops are generated: depending on the type of appli-
cation, react to the outcomes or inform managers through the following layers.
53
==PAGE:61==
• Information Service Platform
The information service platform realizes the transparency through visualiza-
tion. All the information, which is gathered, combined and analyzed is provided
in a good visual way in this stage to the user. Therefore, it is easier to see the
important information and to get a general overview over the processes and
their current status.
• Information manpower
The ﬁnal stage represent the information manpower and includes an early
warning system, including possibilities to react to those early warnings, into
the service platforms.
This is where the feedback loops from the Informa-
tion Operation Control Layer are provided to mangers including the potential
reactions to improve the situation and react at an early stage.
Another key point to the supply chain control tower approach is the connectivity
with outside stakeholders. Mainly the connection with supplier is key since their
data input is necessary to get a panoramic view. This starts with their production
data, continues with the quality checks and ends with the whole logistic supply.
Moreover, the supply chain control tower approach gives the possibility to let sup-
plier receive information about the quality and further steps of their products to let
them improve their eﬃciency. The handy possibility of the supply chain tower is to
have all the information in one system and to share or not share speciﬁc information
with speciﬁc stakeholders according to their needs.
Therefore, the idea of Seat is to include one supply chain tower, which combines in
the background all the existing systems to one major IT platform and therefore gain
a panoramic view about the supply chain [10]. The idea is to include the subpart-
suppliers and logistical service providers up until tier 3 supplier into the IT system.
Thus, a complete live tracking of what happened, what is happening and what will
happen is possible.

trol tower represent the inclusion of the overall demand of the Seat plant. Being
able to foresee the demand for the upcoming time period enables the suppliers and
the logistic providers, once the information is shared with them, to improve their
utilization and schedule making. Here it is particularly important that data streams
are constantly updated and the demand planning is as accurate as possible. One
option to increase demand planning furthermore is the customer segmentation as
described in chapter 5.2.4. At Seat, this sales data is already available and can
be accessed through the sales planning department [10]. Once the data is shared
with the IT system, the supply chain control tower managers are able to share the
expected sales data to speciﬁc suppliers.
Finally, a recommendation how to use the blockchain technology can be given. Next
to the already described potentials of the blockchain concept, such as quality and
process traceability, Seat could be increasing their autonomous supplier mentoring
through smart contracting their deliveries.
With the tremendous amount of more than 600 suppliers, the eﬃciency of sup-
ply chain managers could be increased signiﬁcantly by introducing smart contracts.
Such a smart contract could contain information about the amount of products,
which need to be delivered when and where with which speciﬁcations. The contract
states as well the costs the delivery implies, but these costs are only paid, once the
sensors in the product delivery zone approve the right amount of parts with the
right quality within the agreed time frame. If not, a manager is getting involved
with diﬀerent possibilities. This possibilities include receiving the delivery anyway
but paying less and not accepting the delivery at all.
The data systems, which are necessary, are a cloud based blockchain and sensors for
detecting RFID codes and quality measurements in the incoming delivery zone.

Summary
To summarize the third case study, one can see, that it diﬀers through it complexity
resulting of the quantity of more than 600 suppliers. Furthermore, it shows that the
automotive industry, in this case study Seat, is already approaching the advantages
which Industry 4.0 is bringing with it. As seen in the chapter 5.3.3, the speciﬁc
departments already implemented IT systems, which are nevertheless not connected
throughout the whole supply chain. To combine those system and get a panoramic

control tower, included with a VANet and demand data incoming ﬂow can represent
an overall view of the supply chain.
To implement this overall system, it needs to be planned and performed according
to change management concepts step by step. To ensure that the implementation

6
Discussion
Both, the analysis of the literature review as well as the analysis of the case stud-
ies, brought important insights about how the research question of How is Industry
4.0 able to improve the supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms? can be
answered. The following chapter will elaborate the speciﬁc possibilities and discuss
as to which circumstances the hypothesis, that Industry 4.0 is able to improve the
supply chain in general, applies.
The chapter is structured in seven key ﬁndings, which are primarily resulting from
the literature review (ﬁnding 1), the case studies (ﬁnding 2, 4 and 5) or the combi-
nation of both (ﬁnding 3, 6 and 7). Each of the ﬁndings is structured according to
the following subparts:
• Relation to research
The roots of the ﬁnding will be revealed according to the sources in the liter-
ature or case studies.
• Expectation of ﬁnding
This subpart gives an overview about whether the ﬁnding was an expected
development according to the manufacturing environment or can be seen as a
disruptive renewal of speciﬁc concepts and mindsets.
• Comparison to literature ﬁndings
If the ﬁnding is similar to other ﬁndings in the literature or not, which cover
partly similar research areas, will be described in this subchapter.
• Importance towards research question
Finally the ﬁnding will be linked toward the overall research question, its hy-
pothesis and additional research questions. It focuses on whether the ﬁnding
answers the research question or if it helps to understand backgrounds or ar-
gumentative chains of a hypothesis.
The chapter will be concluded by an overview which combines the overall ﬁnd-
ings with the Supply Chain 4.0 approach stated in chapter 4.3. Furthermore, this
chapter includes an overview of the studies limitations and further research areas,
to integrate the research project into a general environment and give impulses for
additional related research areas.
6.1
Finding 1: Industry 4.0 aﬀects all parts of the supply chain
The ﬁrst ﬁnding states, that Industry 4.0 is not only able to aﬀect the supply chain
in general, but is able to aﬀect every single part of it. This arises among others
from the research of Tjahjono, separating the eﬀects of supply chain into four parts:
Procurement, Warehouse, Logistic and Fulﬁllment [47]. Other indications are the
58
==PAGE:66==
neologisms Procurement 4.0 [36] and Logistic 4.0 [46], which imply a direct relation
of the topics towards Industry 4.0. On top of that, Marques states the inﬂuence in
the decision making from Industry 4.0 into all kind of areas too [35].
The three preceding industrial revolutions were mostly based on the manufacturing
part of the industries (see chapter 1). Thus, this ﬁnding that the fourth industrial
revolution does aﬀect way more parts of the manufacturing companies, is quite unex-
pected. Nevertheless, considering the main idea of Industry 4.0, which is connecting
diﬀerent areas and increasing eﬃciency through using big data, the ﬁnding can be
expected. Moreover, diﬀerent departments within a manufacturing company grew
together during the last decades, which makes it more diﬃcult anyway to separate
speciﬁc areas.
In comparison to the ﬁnding, most of the overall research ﬁndings in the area, are
focused on one speciﬁc area. Therefore, Tjahjano [47] realizes that order fulﬁllment
and transport logistic will realize a tremendous impact of Industry 4.0, while Nico-
letti sees a big impact in purchasing [36] and Timm in logistics [46]. Similar, overall
supply chain approaches can be seen in the ﬁndings of Barreto, who states that the
overall supply chain "will become smarter, more transparent and more ﬂexible in
every stage" [28]. On a bit more detailed level, Lojka states the overall eﬀects by
the implementation of "working together through gateways to collect and integrate
data along all processes" [33].
To state the importance of this ﬁnding, it ﬁrst of all shows that Industry 4.0 is
aﬀecting the supply chain and therefore it is able to improve it. Furthermore, it
shows that the topic is of a general importance since all parts of the supply chain
are directly aﬀected. On the other side, this ﬁnding supports the overall data stream
roadmap (see chapter 4.3) since it includes all parts of the supply chain and solves
the problem of improving all parts of the supply chain respectively. This ﬁnding
represents clearly that only if all areas are taken into account regarding the imple-
mentation of Industry 4.0, an overall successful execution is possible.
6.2
Finding 2: Depending on size, structure and organization, Industry
4.0 aﬀects manufacturing ﬁrms diﬀerently
The second ﬁnding represents a diﬀerentiation of the eﬀects Industry 4.0 has towards
the supply chain regarding size and structure of an organization.
Regarding the literature research, only Sarvari implies those diﬀerences in form of
diﬀerent strategies for diﬀerent companies [42]. A deep analysis about speciﬁcations
or detailed diﬀerences is not revealed. These diﬀerences occur mainly in the case
studies, where diﬀerent companies (regarding size, structure and organization) have
been analyzed (see chapter 5).
To state one speciﬁc diﬀerence between the three case studies, one can name the
ordering process. While Bosch, as a producer of standardized products is trying to
59
==PAGE:67==
implement an automatic ordering system based on their production numbers, Phase
One and Seat are following a completely diﬀerent strategy based on Industry 4.0
[6] [8] [10]. As they are depending on customizing products and keeping inventory
levels low, ordering raw materials and subparts and deﬁning production schedules
need to be speciﬁcally matched with future sales data.
Another example shows the cost focus regarding the supply chain. While Phase One
sees the inventory as their main cost factor and neglects the manufacturing costs,
Bosch and Seat focus on the manufacturing costs since it can be seen as their biggest
cost driver.
The general diﬀerences of industries and therefore their cost factors are widely
known. That is why this ﬁnding is not unexpected. Nevertheless, the gravity of
the diﬀerences and therefore as well the importance of following the right objectives
and constraints when implementing Industry 4.0 can be seen as unexpected.
This can be seen as well looking at the conclusions of other literature. Other papers
are calling the attention to the positive and negative impacts Industry 4.0 can bring
to manufacturing companies. Therefore, Tjahjono describes that new technologies
and methods can result in both, opportunities and threads [47], while Marques states
that Industry 4.0 represents not only opportunities but as well a very demanding
strategy [35]. This indicates that it is known that there are as well challenges and
threats regarding Industry 4.0, but the speciﬁc diﬀerences of industries, which need
to be considered are not stated yet and can be stated as a literature gap.
Since this is a new ﬁnding regarding the topic of Industry 4.0, it shows once more
the importance of the research topic. Moreover, it implies that the research question
can not be answered completely in detail for the general manufacturing industry.
The important recognition is, that before introducing new systems and technologies,
the speciﬁc parameters of the company need to be analyzed and adapted. To answer
the second hypothesis, that even though diﬀerent industries have diﬀerent focus ar-
eas, general industry 4.0 applications can be transferred to all of them, one can state
that an overall roadmap for connectivity among the supply chain, which includes an
improvement of eﬃciency, transparency and ﬂexibility, is generally an opportunity
for all companies. Nevertheless, speciﬁcations need to be done regarding the par-
ticular environment and the focus areas of the company. To deﬁne this on a more
detailed level, one can look at the overall IT connectivity system which is described
in the analysis part (see chapter 4). This approach is generally and can be adapted
to all kind of industries. The adaption can take place through the implementation
of the speciﬁc constraints and objectives in the IT structure to achieve the optimal
solution for the speciﬁc company.

6.3
Finding 3: Cooperations are a key part of enabling the Industry 4.0
solutions for manufacturing ﬁrms
The third ﬁnding consists of diﬀerent factors, which are described in the literature.
First of all, Marques describes that companies focus on their core business and there-
fore enhance the amount of cooperations [35]. Secondly, standardization represent a
more and more important part of digitization [32] and thirdly, the trend of serviti-
zation makes cooperations with service providers essential [36]. All this factors lead
to a strongly increased reliability on cooperations regarding the implementation for
Industry 4.0. In addition, it can be seen at the amount of diﬀerent cooperations
Bosch already launched to face the challenges of Industry 4.0 (see chapter 5.1.2)
[15].
This ﬁnding is expected since Industry 4.0 is connecting diﬀerent areas beyond the
internal boundaries. Moreover, Industry 4.0 is based on the IoT and therefore on
software systems which need standardized plugs and data transfer systems to be
able to interact. Therefore, this ﬁnding can be seen as expected.
A similar ﬁnding can be seen in related literature, stating that IoT gateways are
necessary to collect and integrate data and cooperations to conﬁrm on standardiza-
tion [33]. Nonetheless, no literature is focusing on the importance and the approach
of cooperations.
This ﬁnding is important towards diﬀerent levels. First of all, it shows that a com-
plete implementation of the Supply Chain 4.0 data network is impossible without
network thinking and thus cooperations. Secondly, suppliers and logistic partners
need to be included in the underlying IT network. They need access to the (live)
data from the manufacturing ﬁrm as well as the manufacturing ﬁrm needs access
to the data from their partners. Thirdly, cooperations ensure standards and norms,
which simplify the inclusion of data into the system and the communication.
This topic reveals furthermore, that it is impossible for manufacturing companies to
refuse themselves completely from the progress of Industry 4.0. Through suppliers,
customers or cooperation partners, companies will get in touch with digitization and
the IoT. Therefore, it can be aﬃrmed once more that Industry 4.0 is changing the
supply chain management of every ﬁrm and it can be seen that it aﬀects all partici-
pants of it. Likewise, one can see that companies should deﬁnitely try to become an
active part in it to be able to shape their own future in the Industry 4.0 area. Thus,
a roadmap to include Industry 4.0 solutions and dataplugs to cooperate internally
but as well externally is from a high importance.
6.4
Finding 4: Literature review approaches are only partly reﬂecting
the reality
One of the main advantages of doing case studies, next to getting feedback on the
literature review and analysis, represents the possibility of comparing the status of
61
==PAGE:69==
the literature with the current status in supply chains of the industry. The results
are stated in this ﬁnding and ﬁnding number 5 (see chapter 6.5).
In general, one can state that there is still a huge gap and diﬀerence between the
ways, Industry 4.0 is able to improve supply chain according to the literature and
the way it is already implemented and about to be implemented in the industry.
To state some of the main diﬀerences one can cite Nicoletti who is referring to the
blockchain technology and its implementation in the Industry [36]. The manufac-
turing industry on the other side is at a point where it gets an idea of what the
blockchain technology is about. The industry is about to start to think about ways
to implement and use the blockchain technology. Seat as an example does not see
any advantages in their current status at all [10], while Phase One and Bosch are
not providing any information yet of how they may want to include the blockchain
technology in their supply chain [6] [9].
Another example can be seen regarding the cloud manufacturing of integrated ma-
chines to structure work processes in an optimized way [4]. Speaking with members
of all three case studies, none of them has implemented or is about to include a
cloud based manufacturing system yet [10] [6] [9].
Finally, the horizontal integration and transparency along the supply chain as well
as the digitization through CPS and IoT are one key part of the literature analysis
[47] [40]. Simultaneously, the case studies reﬂect a picture, which does not include
any CPS systems and IoT systems to improve transparency or horizontal integration
in neither one of them [10] [6] [9].
To assault this ﬁnding, one could state that it is a regular occurrence that new
technological progresses and concepts need time to get adapted in the industry after
being proposed in the literature. Nevertheless, it is unexpected that the gap is that
immense and that none of the case companies is about to implement some of the
mayor ﬁndings. To explain this, one can state two main concerns: First of all, com-
panies still do not trust completely into the new technologies and their underlying
security and secondly the companies do not want to take disproportionate risks and
prefer to await, which opportunities are in speciﬁc the right ones for them [10].
Next to this research paper, Liao states as well that there is still a huge diﬀerence
regarding the laboratory experiments and the industrial application when it comes
to Industry 4.0 [32]. The other research papers, included into the overall literature
research, do not state any ﬁndings regarding a comparison between literature and
industry.
The importance of this ﬁnding is, that companies need a guideline and a roadmap
to ﬁgure out, what Industry 4.0 is able to provide for them. Furthermore, it can be
seen that the actual solutions may not bring positive cost beneﬁt calculations yet
to the companies and are therefore not yet implemented. Regarding the research
question, this ﬁnding shows that Industry 4.0 may be able to improve the supply
62
==PAGE:70==
chain of manufacturing ﬁrms but currently is not doing it yet, at least not according
to some of the major ﬁndings in the literature.
6.5
Finding 5: Not all potentials regarding Industry 4.0 are revealed in
the literature
Finding number ﬁve bases like ﬁnding number four (see chapter 6.4) on the com-
parison between the analysis of the literature review and the feedback of the case
studies. However, it represents another point of view: the case studies revealed
potentials of Industry 4.0 which have not been revealed in the literature analysis.
One of the main feedback points of the three case studies is the approach of having
one connected system which includes all the data network of the supply chain [10]
[6] [9]. Regarding Seat, to give an example, this is represented in the supply chain
control tower approach [26]. Such a general central system for all approach is not
seen in the literature. Another aspect is the direct through communication from
sales to the supplier, which is enforced from Phase One [8]. This approach is as well
not stated in any of the literature.
This ﬁnding can be seen as expected since most literature focuses on one speciﬁc
area of the supply chain. The aspects, which are described above from the case
companies are cross-functional and therefore not in the focus of given literature.
Such a ﬁnding is as well not part of any other literature related to the topic.
This ﬁnding gives an example that companies in the manufacturing industry are
already thinking about how Industry 4.0 can bring potentials to them and how they
are able to include those potentials into their supply chain. It gives as well an idea of
how companies are looking for a guideline to help them getting a combined solution,
which is cross-functional and developed closely together with the industry.
6.6
Finding 6:
Comprehensive Industry 4.0 solutions are inﬂuencing
each other
The present ﬁnding combines many aspects of both, the case studies and the studied
literature. These aspects are summarized in the ﬁnding, that Industry 4.0 meth-
ods and technologies are inter-connective, comprehensive and need to be seen as an
overall concept. This ﬁnding includes as well that even if this overall concept is not
implemented at once, it needs to permit add-ons through plug-and-play solutions.
One of the aspects which is described in the roadmap approach [42], but as well by
the explanation of decision making [29], is that adaptions of Industry 4.0 concepts
in speciﬁc areas, inﬂuence other areas.
Moreover, some of the literature papers
are introducing big data platforms and cloudplug entrances to connect certain data
streams [40] and ensure connectivity [4]. In addition, chapter 6.5 explains, that the
concepts of the case companies are requesting an overall approach since they realized
the inter-connectivity of Industry 4.0 concepts. Nevertheless, the previous ﬁndings
63
==PAGE:71==
show that often, ﬁrst approaches to implement Industry 4.0 solutions are realized,
but that an overall, complete implementation is far from realization. Therefore,
the mentioned possibility of subsequently adding features into the system shows an
indispensable element of an Industry 4.0 solution.
To rank the ﬁnding into an expected or unexpected category, the necessity of subse-
quent modiﬁcation for Industry 4.0 solution can be described as unexpected, based
on the assumption that new tools are implemented at once. On the other side, it
is expected that the solutions should be gathered in one system since the inter-
connectivity creates comprehensiveness.
The ﬁnding of inter-connectivity of the solutions for the supply chain are as well a
ﬁnding in other related research projects. Reddy sees Industry 4.0 as an environ-
ment, which is interconnected and needs to share constantly information with its
surroundings [40]. Furthermore, Reddy implies that a step wise approach can be
useful to implement the results of Industry 4.0 [40]. Such a strategy with diﬀerent
phases is supported by Sarvari, who consults a roadmap including multiple imple-
mentation phases [42].
The most important aspect of this ﬁnding is that Industry 4.0 is able to improve
the supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms through an overall united and
connected system. Only if there is one central system, which is based on an IT
software and includes data transfers within all stakeholder areas, the potentials of
Industry 4.0 can be realized. The addition that it does not have to be implemented
at once is a positive aspect for all manufacturing companies, which can not aﬀord
the resources to implement a completely new and integrated system while running
the daily processes. Important is, that there are interfaces for further add-ons, so
that additional implementations can be added without changing the overall system.
This ﬁnding can be transferred to all kinds of manufacturing ﬁrms and answers
therefore the second hypothesis, that general advises for the overall manufacturing
industry can be given.
In general, the ﬂexibility and adjustability of a Supply Chain 4.0 concept is another
key part, which needs to be considered, answering the question about How is In-
dustry 4.0 able to improve the supply chains of manufacturing ﬁrms?. The actuality
of the topic necessitates the possibility for potential adaptions, since there will still
be further changes and improvements in the area of Industry 4.0. Therefore, this
ﬁnding implies that the research question can not be ﬁnally answered, since the
development of Industry 4.0 is not yet completed.
6.7
Finding 7: Implementation of Industry 4.0 concepts deﬁnes com-
petitiveness
As mentioned earlier, supply chain management in general faces diﬀerent kinds of
trends. Facing these trends is from a high importance (see chapter 3.4). The ﬁnd-
64
==PAGE:72==
ing states, that not following those trends make it diﬃcult for companies to stay
competitive.
The studied literature support the existence of trends which are inﬂuencing the sup-
ply chain of manufacturing ﬁrms. Therefore, Nicoletti [36] refers to a trend for small
batches and a trend of servitization which result from a high customization. This,
on the other hand, lead to a higher complexity and variety from customer orders
[29]. To summarize the impacts, Sarvari [42] assigns Industry 4.0 the possibility to
deﬁne the competitiveness of manufacturing ﬁrms.
Each trend can be linked towards aspects of Industry 4.0. The trend of mass cus-
tomization and the therefore arising small batch sizes, Industry 4.0 answers through
smart products and CPS, which enable an auto adapted production plan [51]. This
results in an overall higher ﬂexibility and agility for the supply chain.
As a second challenge the increasing demand uncertainty is described. Thus, one
can argue that introducing a direct through communication between customer and
supplier increase the transparency and the demand visibility over proportionately.
This is possible through gaping the physical and digital world by introducing one
centralized IT system [9].
This ﬁnding can be seen as expected, since Industry 4.0 inﬂuences all parts of the
supply chain and therefore has a signiﬁcant impact on the overall performance of
a company. In a period of time, where trends are moving fast and industries are
market oriented, companies need to provide competitive products. Only if customer
needs are satisﬁed, while achieving a positive impact on the business performance,
a possible long time existence of a company can be ensured [50].
The ﬁnding is as well broadly accepted and conﬁrmed in other, related literature.
Tjahojono [47] underlines the possible impact of increasing ﬂexibility, quality stan-
dards, eﬃciency and productivity for the trend of mass customization. Above that,
Barreto [28] ﬁnds that new business models for individual customer needs are im-
proved by Industry 4.0, while Leveling [31] and Sarvari [42] even see it as necessary
to include new supply chain methods to keep up with the challenges and stay com-
petitive.
Concluding, Marques argues that companies, which will not engage in
Industry 4.0, will struggle to stay alive [35].
This ultimate ﬁnding has a high relevance since it reﬂects how important it is for
companies to face current challenges and trends through Industry 4.0. The trends
of mass customization, higher ﬂexibility and transparency need an inclusion of In-
dustry 4.0 solutions. Therefore, one can even enlarge the hypotheses that Industry
4.0 is able to change the supply chain management of manufacturing ﬁrms to the
statement that Industry 4.0 will change and inﬂuence every supply chain of manu-
facturing ﬁrms and will co-determine the competitiveness of a company.

Supply chain fulﬁllment
As mentioned by Tjahjono and many other papers, the deﬁnition of a supply chain
includes not only all parts from tier n towards the ﬁnal manufacturer, but as well the

6.9.2
Technological depth
Another limitation represents the technological depth, meaning that IT solutions
and other technological methods and systems, which are included in the litera-
ture research, analysis, case studies and discussion are not of a profound technical
explanation. To focus on analyzing the overall inﬂuence factors, correlations and
consequences instead of pursuing technical deep dives has two reasons. First of all,

dives are not covered by the ﬁeld of the authors study.
6.9.3
Focus area: Manufacturing ﬁrms
As mentioned in chapter 1, Industry 4.0 is able to not only inﬂuence and improve the
supply chain of manufacturing ﬁrms, but of all kind of industries and sectors. Mainly
in the food industry, huge changes are already taking place, based on Industry 4.0
concepts [10]. Nevertheless, the aspects of diﬀerent industries are excluded, since
the resources of this research paper were centralized on manufacturing companies.
6.9.4
Amount of three case studies
Since the amount of case studies was restricted according to the resources available to
three, one can not state the results as fully representative. The three case companies
of diﬀerent industries and from diﬀerent countries (Bosch (Germany), Phase One
(Denmark) and Seat (Spain)) are still able to give an insight into manufacturing
ﬁrms. Yet, a representative comparison between company sizes, locations or areas
was not conducted as the amount of case studies can not be seen as representative.
6.9.5
No cost beneﬁt analysis
Another limitation is the exclusion of a cost beneﬁt analysis for the implementation
of Industry 4.0 solutions for the supply chain. Such an analysis would represent the
beneﬁts and expenses for the diﬀerent stakeholders to implement a Supply Chain
4.0 concept. One example is stated by Wang, arguing that regarding the VMI the
supplier carries all the costs, while the buyer enjoys all the beneﬁts [52]. Conse-
quently Wang asks, who is taking the costs of the implementation and the carry out
[52].
Such an analysis is not included, since there are no data available, neither in the
literature, nor from the case companies cooperated with.

==PAGE:76==
6.10
Suggestions for further research
Some of the limitations of the study can be seen as well as indicators, where further
research can start from. Moreover, additional areas, which are in need of further
research were found while analyzing the research question. The upcoming chapter
will describe these suggested additional research areas.
6.10.1
Sustainability at Industry 4.0 regarding the supply chain
A topic, which receives a growing attention from decade to decade is the sustain-
ability of products and the corresponding processes. Industry 4.0 brings new oppor-
tunities to decrease the environmental impact through environmental free products
[40]. In what form and to what extend Industry 4.0 is able to improve sustainability
in a supply chain context could represent the research question of another project.
6.10.2
Cooperation roadmap
To elaborate further on the ﬁnding, that cooperations are key for the implementation
of Industry 4.0 could be another additional research area. To include aspects about
how cooperations should be organized and structured could be one aspect next to
a speciﬁc explanation of how expenses and beneﬁts of Industry 4.0 are shared in a
matter that all participants beneﬁt [6].
6.10.3
Order Fulﬁllment 4.0
As mentioned in chapter 6.9.1, the present study does not include the order ful-
ﬁllment. Nevertheless, this part of the supply chain provides an important aspect
to the overall performance of the supply chain. Depending on the retail system,
agreements with potential vendors and other customers, Industry 4.0 could aﬀect
this last step of the supply chain enormously.
6.10.4
Best Practice Supply Chain 4.0
To realize the idea from Phase One Manager Claus Nymark to include a best practice
of supply chain concepts, cross-industrial case studies could be performed [7]. The
food industry, for instance, uses diﬀerent methods since other constraints, such as
durability of products, have a bigger impact. Therefore, an overall comparison of
diﬀerent industries and companies could reveal diﬀerences and potentials for some
of the industries. Nevertheless, this aﬀords a high quantity of case studies to receive
a general overview of many diﬀerent industries.

Conclusions
As seen throughout the ﬁndings (see chapter 6), there are deﬁnitely possibilities for
Industry 4.0 to improve the supply chain of manufacturing ﬁrms. Thus, the research
paper is able to answer the research question.
Moreover, the analysis shows, that in order to realize the opportunities, it is im-
portant to include an IT system which increases the transparency as well as the
connectivity throughout the whole supply chain. As mentioned, every single part
of the supply chain can contribute to improve the performance regarding Industry
4.0. The importance lies in the realization of inter-connecting the speciﬁc processes
according to the objectives and constraints of a speciﬁc company.
Nevertheless, it is important to mention that the research question can not be deﬁ-
nitely resolved, since Industry 4.0 is still an ongoing revolution and will be further
developed in the next years. This includes the important message, that a roadmap
for Industry 4.0 always needs to be structured in a way, that new stakeholders, new
processes and new technologies can be included.
Regarding the additional hypothesis and the roadmap approach in general, the Sup-
ply Chain 4.0 data stream network gives an overview about which processes need to
be adapted and which systems and stakeholders need to be connected. On top of
that, it leaves scope for far reaching detail adjustments since the paper shows, that
even though overall concepts can be applied towards every company, it is important
to specify details according to the particular environment.
Concerning the general analysis, it is interesting to see, that the challenge for com-
panies does not lay in the data ejection but in the use of the data. This shows the
extraordinary importance of the paper. Another aspect that can be seen throughout
many chapters in the paper is, that during an implementation of Supply Chain 4.0
one often faces contrary goals which need to be recognized to be able to improve the
overall outcome. In some ways, Industry 4.0 even brings the possibility to improve
both of the contrary goals like seen in the Phase One case study about inventory
and availability.
Another aspect, which was deﬁnitely not expected by the author of the article, will
be explained below. All of the three case companies are already about to imple-
ment major key technologies in terms of Industry 4.0: Bosch oﬀering the blockchain
technology for diﬀerent stages of the supply chain, Seat piloting an overall Supply
Chain Tower approach and Phase One restructuring their inventory towards a VMI.
The unexpected in that context is that the literature does not state those aspects
regarding Industry 4.0. As the analysis and discussion revealed, this can be traced
back to the fact that all of the new concepts are interconnected between two or more
parts of the supply chain. As mentioned in chapter 1, this interaction has been re-
70
==PAGE:78==
vealed as a literature gap in the beginning of the research of this paper. Therefore,
the paper succeeds in ﬁlling this literature gap of transactional possibilities for an
overall supply chain approach. On top of that, additional literature gaps have been
revealed and show, that there are many other research ﬁelds which need to be dealt
with in the future.
To realize the value of a paper and research outcomes, it helps to reﬂect them
in the related industry. Since, according to the ﬁndings in this paper, recommenda-
tions have been already given to the case companies, evaluation of the research can
be implemented.
Regarding Bosch, the overall proposal of an integration of an automatized ordering
and machine eﬃciency program was accepted with a high interest. Especially the
combination with a blockchain technology to simplify paying transactions will be
assessed internally for one supplier in the Blaichach plant.
Regarding Phase One and the recommendations for an overall VMI 4.0 approach
to include all processes between sales and suppliers, ﬁrst implementation steps are
already in planning. The newly hired IT engineers are currently trying to analyze
customer data in a way so that it can be used directly for improving the inventory
management.
Seat as the third case company will focus in the meanwhile on implementing the
pilot into more areas before extending it for an overall transparency approach as
recommended. Furthermore, they already included additional add-on possibilities
to be able to include additional systems like the VANet concept in an easier way
later on.
These examples show, that the concept is accepted in the industry and on its way
to get included into the diﬀerent case companies.
To state a general outlook, diﬀerent obstacles deﬁne if the new approach is about
to be implemented, which will be described subsequently.
One of the problems, especially for smaller companies, may be realizing an invest-
ment to such a major change since the ﬁnancial beneﬁts are not yet measurable.
For companies which do not include a speciﬁc innovation department in their orga-
nization, already to create a team to work for such an implementation can result
in problems for the daily work. To comply with those challenges one can restate
some of the mentioned aspects. First of all, there is the chance of a step by step
inclusion of Supply Chain 4.0 solutions. Secondly, external consulting ﬁrms are able
to support in terms of temporary support for Supply Chain 4.0 implementations.
Another challenge that can occur is mainly relevant for pioneers regarding Supply
Chain 4.0. Since the whole concept is based on connecting diﬀerent parts, it is nec-
essary that suppliers as well as logistic service providers cooperate in this context.
If they do not have the resources or the will to implement new systems, some po-
71
==PAGE:79==
tentials of Supply Chain 4.0 can not be realized. To face these obstacles it helps to
make it clear to the participants that Industry 4.0 will be necessary sooner or later
for all kind of stakeholders of the manufacturing industry as mentioned earlier (see
chapter 6.7).
Finally, new approaches always represent a change and a need to implement a new
way of thinking from aﬀected people. In many cases such changes create fears for
employees and managers which lead to restraints towards innovation. This may oc-
cur as well for the developments in Supply Chain 4.0 since some speciﬁc work spaces
may become obsolete. Nevertheless, it is of a high importance to not underestimate
the potentials which have been explained in this paper. In the long term it is possi-
ble not only to maintain the amount of employees but to increase the eﬃciency and
the overall performance of the company through Supply Chain 4.0 and an early on
working proﬁle adaption.
In this context one can summarize the message of this paper with the statement
mentioned in the beginning, that "Industry 4.0 is able to boost the competition of
companies" [42]. One can add, that at least for the supply chain management of
manufacturing ﬁrms, Industry 4.0 will be one of the main success drivers for the
future development.
