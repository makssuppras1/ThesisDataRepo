Abstract
All the living organisms on Earth are relying on the capability of their body to function as
a whole. The purpose of this project is to highlight a prototype that provide a large amount
of degrees of freedom, all the while having an affordable cost of manufacturing and designing.
One of the major issues for having a prosthetic limb is the correlation between the complex-
ity of the device (materials, sensors, actuators, control etc) and the affordability, since many of
the components have a high price of acquisition in order to achieve desirable results, such as
good control of the finger movement, structural integrity of the hand, quality materials and so
on.
The metrics that this project aims at is to test the prototype hand is more oriented to-
wards the capabilities of the hand, such as the speed at which the finger closes, the possibility
of grabbing oddly shaped objects(includes the sideways movement of the finger) and the dif-
ference of using âsoft roboticsâ such as foam for the coating of the hand or the pneumatic
actuators instead of servos for the closing of the fingers.
After many tests that have been carried on the prosthetic prototype shows that there is
potential in this type of actuation with 4 synthetic muscles per finger. Even with the current
shortcomings (material quality, faulty connection, improper fastening system) and time limita-
tions, this type of prosthetic could be used as a base for further developments .

1
Introduction
The Prosthetic hand prototype present in this project serves as a starting point to develop a
fully functioning prosthetic, using off the self components, affordable materials used in building
the mechanical part and easy to command electronics, while trying to achieve high complexity
movements of the finger joints. The are plenty of existing models that vary from a very basic
wooden hand with servos and trains of gears up to fully integrated bionic prosthetic.
This project was created out of the desire to further research the capabilities of prosthetic
prototypes, using affordable components and testing the complexity of their movement that
the prototype is capable of. It combines passion from previous projects, research on existing
designs and testing done on this current model.
The project is divided into multiple parts: understanding the theoretical notions of mechan-
ical movement, using the information from the anatomical model of a human hand, design of
the mechanical part, simulation of the electrical and control parts with sensors, manufacturing
the prototype and testing using different metrics (freedom of movement, speed, grip, force ap-
plied).
It also combines elements from ordinary upper limbs prosthetics, such as rigid internal
structure to mimic the anatomical bones, tendon driven and servo based actuators, with the
soft robotics side, which are the foam coating, synthetic muscles as actuators and sensors on
the finger for a feedback loop.

Basic notions
The very first basic notion to use is the length of the main axis of the finger, which are the
distances between the pivot joint. In order to understand these, I have done research about
mechanical movement of the human hand. Using various sources for the anatomy of the upper
limb, I have extracted valuable information.

From the sources I have studied about the human hand, with the skeleton and the muscles

The bones are connected

In terms of robotics, the human finger acts more akin to a miniature robotic arm, with 4
DOF(degrees of freedom). If the we take the palm of the hand as the XY space and the plane
perpendicular to the hand as the Z, then the similarities are very clear. This notion will be very
useful later in section about Development of teh project. In mechanical terms there are âclasses
of jointsâ which represents the number of DOF a joint has (a class V has only 1 DOF while a
class I has 5 DOF). In reality, especially in industrial sectors, most of the robots have class V
joints. Class IV or III are very hard and expensive to replicate from biological examples, due
to the fact that there are ligaments that allow the bones to move a certain direction. With this
notions in mind, I have made the decision to use a rigid inner structure made of plastic and a
softer exterior structure made out of polyurethane.
2.2
Movement of the fingers
In the previous part the degrees of freedom and the types of joints were explained, now it
is the time to present the basic movement of the fingers. The base of the finger has 2 joints, in
order to replicate the human hand complex movement. One joint links the finger to the palm of
the hand and the other links the 3 phalanges to the base piece. The three phalanges execute a
rotation at a given angle in order to achieve a grip over different objects and also leave enough
room for the âtendonsâ and the âskinâ. The calculation was made in such a way that the tip
of the finger must be perpendicular on the palm of the hand.

2.3
Pneumatic synthetic muscle
One of the main goals for prosthetic development is to mimic the human hand as much
as possible to the biological model. To achieve this type of result, many options are available
from metal springs to servo motors with different amount of gears. In more recent days there
is also the push towards pneumatic or hydraulic actuators, such as in this project. The reason
for choosing pneumatic actuators instead of a servos is that it eliminates the need of geared
transmission to control the force and speed of the finger movement, although another issues
appears, such as the speed at which the air flows into the actuator and the pressure of the gas.

2.4
Sensors and control
The sensors serve a role to announce the user the presence of an obstacle or object, or to
limit the movement of the fingers, much alike the real biological model. Of course, the project
is far from achieving real human hand capabilities, but with the given conditions and imple-
mentations it does produce some satisfactory results towards that direction. The sensors are
directly attached to the fingers, in order to mimic (to a certain level) the capabilities of the
human skin and the reaction a human has to the touch sense [4]. Their main task is to transmit
the signal to the either the pneumatic or the servos that the finger has reached a collision point,
to which the answer will be the full stop of the movement in that direction.
In theory, the way this prosthetic prototype is controlled goes as such: the amount of gas
(air in this case) is pumped inside of the pneumatic actuator, which in turn pulls the tendons
that closes/releases the finger and once the sensor detects an object, the command is sent to
stop the flow of gas.

The mechanism that connects the finger and the moving head of the servos and allows the
sideway movement between the plates. There is one bolt that connects the whole finger with
the hand and also it offers a decent amount of freedom of movement. The base joint has a
smaller plastic tube that bridges the gap between the piece and the bolt. This small plastic
tube prevents wear on the base of the finger and helps center the finger on the specific hole.
The finger has 4 major components/phalanges and several smaller ones. The major parts
create the moving structure of the finger while the smaller parts create the tensioning system
for the cable that acts as the tendon (pulling/releasing system). The tensioning system is com-
prised of 2 small plates that are connected to the moving parts or phalanges and 2 rollers that
keep the cable close to the âboneâ in order to reduce the amount of contact with other moving
parts. Each of the major moving part of the finger has this type of keep the cable in tension,
which also allows the finger to be covered later with the foam coating.
Every finger has around the joints certain channels and groves that direct the cable unto a
certain path, such as those in Figure 24. This option was chosen to avoid the cables overlapping
that might cause issues when the actuation begins.

3.3
Electric and pneumatic actuators
The actuators are split into 2 categories: the electric ones, represented by the 5 servos, and
the pneumatic ones, represented by the 20 synthetic muscles.
Each of the servos is a Micro Servo SG90 for 9g, capable of a 180 âŚrotation. In this case,
4 servos are executing a 20 degree rotation and one does a 60 degree, which represent the base
movement of the opposable thumb. Each of the servos is connected with a rod to the base part
of the fingers
The pneumatic actuators are comprised of 20 McKibben style synthetic muscle. These mus-
cle are built by myself, using various information gathered online. They are built using medical
devices, such as IVs, tubes and 20ml syringes, combined with a balloon and a plastic mesh used
for cable management. The basic structure is a balloon that is attached to one end of the IV
probe and then checked to be airtight. Over the balloon is put the mesh, that is glued at the
base of the IV probe and later a plastic cone is put in order to contain irregular expansion of
the balloon once is inflated. At the other end of the probe, a syringe pumps air into the tube
that goes into the balloon.

Figure 29: Example of a manufactured McKibben synthetic muscle, inflated

The muscles are attached to the tube that serves as structural housing and each of them
actuate one part of the finger. The last 2 phalanges of the finger are actuated by one finger
and the first phalange by another muscle. The movement is bidirectional, which means that 2
muscles are contracting while the other 2 are releasing tension. This model of actuation was
chosen on the basis that the human hand also behaves the same. Without the need of âpassiveâ
tendons, such as metal springs, it creates the possibility of smaller finger components and more
room for the coating over the âskeletonâ, but at the same time it implies a more fine control
for the muscles, which requires careful analysis.
All of the muscles are inflated using a 20ml syringe due to 2 main reasons: firstly being the
fact that it uses off the market components which are not suited for high pressure (such as 2-6
bars) and secondly it gives the possibility to finely control the muscles, in order to troubleshoot
any issues with the components of the muscles (the mesh, the balloon or the tube).
3.4
Control of the prosthetic
3.4.1
Feedback and sensors
One of the main differences from other prototypes is the inclusion of sensors on the finger
itself. The purpose of the sensors is to create a feedback loop, that is once the finger detects
an obstacle, it give out the signal to cease itâs movement [8]-[2].
The sensors that are used in this project are resistor based one for detecting force, which
is the pressure applied on the surface of the resistor. The sensor stretches if there is a force on
the resistor. On the finger there are 3 sensors on the inner side and 2 on the sides. The sensors
mounted on the side have the purpose to detect collision with other fingers, to replicate in a
more crude way how the human hand respond when we move the fingers sideways and stop
when we hit our fingers.
3.4.2
Programming using Arduino environment
The purpose of using very few electronic components was to try and make a robust and
simple prototype, while managing the scarce resources available at the time. Nonetheless, it is
useful to have a feedback system that brings this prototype closer to more advance options[11].
All of the main electronic devices rely on the Arduino architecture. In this case, I have used

an Arduino Mega, with the ATmega2560 Processor, that has 54 Digital and 16 Analog pins, of
which 15 are PWM outputs. This would allow me to use multiple inputs and outputs. Of course
there are some disadvantages, such as the need for power adaptor and the time response due to
the fact that there are multiple sensors and actuators linked. But the key advantages for which

In order to reduce the amount of time put into connecting and programming the function-

3.5
Testing and metrics
One of the metrics for the prosthetic to test was the capability to grab certain objects
with different shapes and sizes. Due to the fact that the pneumatic actuators were activated
manually instead of automatically, it meant that the time to reach and grab that said object
was long, not to mention the fact that each individual âvalveâ had to be operated one by one.
The sensors, in this stage, would only give a written message on the Arduino monitor instead
of the signal to stop the valve. This method is to ensure that the feedback part is still present,
even if the direct connector is a human operator.
Thus, the other objectives beside moving the finger is making sure the sensors give proper
signals that could be used and analysed what is working and what is causing more troubles to
the prototype.
Figure 33: Example of the base design for grabbing a soda can
Another metric was the time it took for the finger to close, from the moment gas starts to
flow intro the muscle until it reaches the closed position. One of the key factors to ensure a
normal response time is to make sure that the tendons that connect the finger to the actuators
is fasten enough. One of the more empirical methods to check that is to press the cable and
feel if it is loose or not. There is also the visual inspection, to compare the theoretical close
position to the real one. This method also gives us the results on how much of the cable has
been pulled or if there is a problem with the pneumatic actuators.

3.6
Further research and potential improvements
One such area to improve is using dedicated pneumatic equipment, such has either auto-
mated solenoid powered valves (see Appendix for the pneumatic diagram), or synthetic muscles
that uses more resistant material. For the muscles in particular there must be a more stiff elas-
tic material used, with a larger thickness compared to the simple balloons used in this example
[7].
In terms of the mechanical part, the fastening system could be improved or completely
redone. Another problem is that the tendons must not come into contact with the servos (if
the same dual actuating system is kept) or to add different paths if there will be a version that
uses only pneumatic actuators (only active actuation and no need for passive actuation, such
as metal springs). The main issue with the fastening system was the size and material used.
Due to the precision margin of error for the 3D printer used, many parts were in a very tight
fit which leads to problems for the ease of pulling and releasing the tendons.
There is also potential in making the whole finger out of a more stiff material that the one
used in this prototype that could ensure more desired results [1]-[6].
With the results form the testing and from research that has been done, there are clear
sign that this prototype is far from the end goal. It does show significant potential for further
development within this field [5].

Conclusion
All things considered, it can be assumed that prosthetics evolved organically as a technology
alongside the needs of the human society. From the socio-cultural aspects to the biomedical
[9] or military usage [13] there plenty of examples. Many lives had benefited from this type
of technology, since itâs main goal was to replicate the functionality ans aesthetics of a human
limb. .
At the beginning of this project, I decide to test the possibility of using soft robotics com-
ponents instead of the usual servos or motor driven actuators. The synthetic muscles came up
as an idea, since it moves close towards the anatomical model. Yet it comes with a series of
challenges, one of them being having proper equipment in order to replicate human muscles.
That is why I have chosen to manufacture the muscles and in order to better control the muscle,
a couple of servos will be used to mimic the functionality of a solenoid driven valve.
Many ideas could be extracted from across the project. During the initial phase, were the
gathering of the data was made, it could be argued that the amount of time was not enough,
even if there are already plenty of information available either in digital or physical format.
And yet 2 months might seem as very short amount of time to obtain certain notion and val-
ues. Fortunately, for the research that I have conducted I have previous information from past
project, the 3 finger robotic gripper, that really helped in further developing this prototype.
For the outcome of the project it can be said that it lacks certain elements that could
be found in other existing projects that had been already tested, such as the integrity of the
mechanical part, the materials of which the fingers and the hand are built, the quality of the
servos. The structure needs to be made from a stronger material, but also kept in mind the
overall weight of the whole prototype. That was the main reason to choose plastic over metal,
although the plastic used was not of the highest quality.
This project showcased both the challenges and the opportunities of such device, from the
research phase to the design and simulation, from the manufacturing of the skeletal part all the
way to the implementation of the code and analysing the data from the metrics. This prototype
must be considered as a first step into developing a fully fledged prosthetic hand with certain
levels of autonomy and feedback control.
