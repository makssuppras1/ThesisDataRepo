Abstract
This study explores the design and implementation of a virtual incubator platform tailored for
early-stage female entrepreneurs, based on the case of HerStartup. By combining data modeling,
expert profiling, and qualitative user interviews, the project identifies core entrepreneurial personas
and maps their needs to functional and UX requirements. Through a structured three-phase design
iteration—including low-fidelity sketches, clickable prototypes, and user testing—the study delivers
a web-based prototype that enhances mentor matching, resource visibility, and operational
transparency. The research contributes to the practical development of inclusive incubation tools
and offers a methodological framework for integrating data-driven modeling with user-centered
design in entrepreneurship support systems.

1 Introduction
1.1 Background
There is no single answer regarding the definition and classification of incubators [1].
Džemai and Suad (2011) define Virtual business incubators as services provided in
cyberspace, that bring together businesses, customers, suppliers, and partners, and
operate and manage the virtual incubator through the Internet, electronic data
interchange, videoconferencing facilities, etc. [2], which aims to accelerate the growth
and success of start-ups by providing a range of business resources and support services
[1]. Robertson (2015) describes incubators as providers of rental space, business
consulting services, shared office space, services, office equipment, and a range of
administrative services at very low or no cost to small businesses [3]. Some of the
current literature categorizes incubators into several types, namely business incubators,
virtual incubators, cyber incubators, academic incubators, student-run incubators,
business incubators, and social incubators [4]. One of the categories of incubators is the
virtual incubator, which fits the definition of a digital entrepreneurship platform, and the
virtual incubator matches the positioning of this case study, HerStartup.
Virtual incubators support the creation of digital businesses or startups [5][6][7], which
play a vital role in promoting sustainable economic growth and social development [8][9],
helping to boost entrepreneurship across multiple business sectors and domains,
technology and innovation capabilities across multiple business sectors and areas, as
well as contributing to the creation of new jobs and wealth [4][10][11]. They undertake
multiple functions and provide a wide range of support services and assistance to
strengthen new start-ups, existing businesses, or entrepreneurial teams, and to facilitate
their survival and growth, even in the most vulnerable times [1]. Some practical
examples of the support provided by virtual incubators include the provision of
consulting services, training courses, networking, and venture capital [12], assistance in
the development of new products and services [13] and helping entrepreneurs to enter
the marketplace and scale up the commercialization of their products and services, etc
[14][15]. Virtual incubation centers provide startups with the same resources as
traditional incubators through digital platforms, and they are particularly attractive to
startups in their infancy or located in remote areas where traditional incubation facilities
may not be available [16].
Virtual incubators play an important role in the business incubation process [3]. At the
same time, the rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI) technology in the 21st
century has likewise attracted the attention of relevant researchers. Related literature
has begun to examine the role of AI technology for entrepreneurial empowerment [17],
tapping into the importance of AI for online consulting [18], entrepreneurial training

startups [20][21].
HerStartup, as a virtual incubator positioned for global female entrepreneurs, also

（1）Financing gaps
Women miss out on financing opportunities. This is probably the biggest obstacle
women entrepreneurs face at every stage of their business journey [62]. The gender pay
gap is well known, but the funding gap between female-led and male-led companies is
less well known [62][38].In 2021, women will account for less than 12% of the 1.3 million
investments received by UK companies - equivalent to just 0.1% of UK private equity
investment (by value) and 0.5% of UK venture capital investment (by value), compared to
61% of active companies led by men and attracting two-thirds (66%) of all investment, all
of which are taken from the Gender Index [63], published in March 2022, which aims to
provide a clear picture of the profile of women-run companies. A similar picture is
presented by a study conducted by startups.co.uk in 2022, which shows that [62],
businesses with male founders or co-founders receive on average almost seven times
more funding than those owned by female entrepreneurs. This is then contrasted by the
fact that sales of female-led firms in England grew faster than male-led and mixed-led
firms between 2022 and 2023 [43].
1.1.1 Challenges facing digital enterprise platforms
（1）The effectiveness of incubators for startups
For first-time entrepreneurs, most have the dilemma of limited experience, and one
solution to remedy this is to combine with a savvy incubator [6]. However there is
limited understanding of the effectiveness of business incubators in strengthening
startups, and some business incubators tend to provide educational programs based on
what they offer rather than what the entrepreneurs need, so they lack the skills to adapt
to the needs of the entrepreneurs [3]. Most failed incubators suffer from insufficient
empowerment effects, especially in developing countries [22][23]. They don't provide
real mentorship because funders don't care about return on investment [24] Incubators

and education they need to get off the ground, allowing tech startups to focus on the
task at hand, which has led to a 90% failure rate for the incubators themselves out of the

Incubators were envisioned as an extension of what venture capital firms have been
doing for Silicon Valley startups for the past 40 years [24]. However, the big difference
between most modern incubators and VC firms is that VC firms are actually in it for the
money - they invest their company's money in startups rather than operating on free

people at the venture capital firm are in charge of the startup's business, making
important decisions, and if they feel they can't get a return on their investment, they'll
sell the remaining work to another company on a whim in order to recoup their
investment or cut their losses. From a business point of view, this is brutally effective
because venture capital firms have no intention of losing money [24][25][26].
On the other hand, incubators are friendly, inclusive environments that give pride of
place to people with big ideas, leading them to spend most of their time on initiatives
that don't actually contribute to getting a business off the ground, such as logos, website
design, and productivity apps [24]. This is because, according to research [27][28][29],
negative emotions such as anxiety and anger instead promote entrepreneurs to take
action and accelerate the process of market expansion, which contributes to profit
accumulation.
（2）Incubator's own financial difficulties
Startups are always faced with the challenge of obtaining funding, and business
incubators serve to solve this problem [30]. However, many incubators struggle to secure
sufficient funding for their programs, limiting the financial resources available to the
startups they support [3], which often occurs when the incubator has not yet established
a reputation in the marketplace or has not yet partnered with a number of clients, and
can impede the growth and sustainability of the startups, undermining the incubator's
original intent [22].

HerStartup hosts a series of startup camp sessions each year, each lasting several weeks.
These programs provide intensive online training that covers the complete startup
process, including idea generation, market research, team building, solution design,
product prototyping, and access to capital. Participants are provided with step-by-step
guidance to apply for financial support from the Women's Entrepreneurship Fund, VCs,
gas pedals, and incubators.

backgrounds, many of whom have experience in university entrepreneurship programs
or have worked with startups. These mentors provide expertise through guest lectures,
online consulting, and feedback sessions. Their responsibilities include helping teams
assess the feasibility of ideas, evaluate market demand, refine business plans, and
improve product development processes.
To manage resources and provide equal access, HerStartup has created a consulting
department that offers a fixed number of free weekly consultations. Teams in need of
project data or insights work closely with the consulting team and mentors to scope their
needs and receive tailored guidance.
As an open platform for digital entrepreneurship, HerStartup serves women from
different regions and at the startup stage, whether they already have a project idea or

1.2 Motivation
1.2.1 The importance of entrepreneurial empowerment with female
Female entrepreneurs are one of the fastest-growing entrepreneurial groups in the
world [35]. As female empowerment continues to grow, women are taking control of
their professional and personal lives [36]. According to data from the 2023 Ross Review
of Women's Entrepreneurship Progress Report [37], more women are starting businesses
than ever before [37]. In 2022, women in the UK set up more than 150,000 new firms
[37], more than twice as many as in 2018. This means that in total, more than 20% of
new start-ups are now led by women (up from 16% in 2018) [38], a record high [38]. The
first Alison Ross Review of Women's Entrepreneurship, published in March 2019,
highlights [39] that if women started and expanded new businesses at the same rate as
men, the UK economy would be able to add up to £250 billion of new value.
Meanwhile, female entrepreneurship is recognized as a pathway to sustainable
economic growth in developing countries [40][41][42], which has made female
entrepreneurship a focus of attention in many countries. A study by Kaviarasu et al.
(2022) suggests that [43], female entrepreneurs not only contribute to a country's
economic growth but also play an important role in social development. Guillén
mentioned [42] that female entrepreneurs can indeed make a difference in developing
countries and contribute greatly to the economic development and poverty eradication
of the countries in which they work. Dewi et al.'s (2022) study in Indonesia also showed
[44] that female entrepreneurs have a positive and significant impact on social and
economic well-being in a positive and significant way. Similar results were found in the
study by Sajjad et al. (2020) which showed that [45], female entrepreneurship not only
improves the status of female entrepreneurial families but also has a significant impact
on the economic and social well-being of the society. Female entrepreneurs help to
create jobs, which in turn increase income and provide more new products and services
to society, thus bringing greater benefits to society [46]. At the same time, investing in
female entrepreneurship can help to close the gender gap as studies have shown that
[47], female business owners are more likely to hire female employees. Aguiar (2021)
also stated that [46], female entrepreneurs are more likely to reinvest the money that
they earn, thus contributing to the growth of the local economy. In recent years, women
have become increasingly interested and involved in entrepreneurship and have proven
that they can be successful in starting their own businesses, but there are still many
opportunities to be pursued and more progress to be made [40].

1.2.2 Problems faced by female entrepreneurs
Despite the fact that women, as one of the fastest growing groups of entrepreneurs in
the world [48][49], and according to the World Bank, women entrepreneurs make up
38.8% of all entrepreneurs globally, and that they contribute significantly to economic
growth, innovation and employment [50], they still face many challenges depriving them
of their right to get close to the top, and limitations Business World [51]. Over the years
female entrepreneurs have faced persistent dilemmas including gender discrimination,
venture capital bias, and gender-based social issues that reduce the likelihood of female
entrepreneurs realizing their business dreams [43][52], and according to a study
conducted by Strawser et al. (2021) [53], females are underrepresented in many fields,
including the entrepreneurship, are underrepresented. Elam et al. (2019) state that [54],
women are still a minority of entrepreneurs almost everywhere and the proportion of
female entrepreneurs is still lower than that of men to date. Maheswari et al. (2023) also
state that [55], the socio-economic contribution and entrepreneurial potential of female
entrepreneurs is still not widely appreciated. According to Adiza et al. (2020) [56], female
entrepreneurs face more difficulties than male entrepreneurs in running their
businesses.
Although a global movement for gender equality has been occurring for the past 30
years, female entrepreneurs still face too many limitations compared to their male
counterparts [57]

Bias and discrimination against female founders are common, with a recent poll by The
Daily Telegraph finding that [38] two-thirds of 750 female founders felt they were not

male counterparts. In a survey conducted by Simply Business in 2022 [64], 91% of female
business owners said that gender discrimination or inequality was prevalent in business,
with a third describing it as “widespread” or “severe”.
New research from Copenhagen Business School suggests [65][66] that female founders
are not only at risk of discrimination from external stakeholders such as banks and
investors but also from their own employees. This study proves that gender bias is not
just a self-reported claim by female entrepreneurs; gender bias is real, and it has an

（3）Stereotypes
Entrepreneurship is prone to triggering stereotypes, from labels such as “mompreneur”

（4）Limited networking and mentorship resources
Due to the smaller number of female founders, women are less likely to know other

concerns about the quality of the entrepreneurial ecosystem [72].

1.3 Research objectives and research questions
This study aims to build a virtual incubator platform designed for female entrepreneurs,
with the ultimate goal of providing a one-stop resource integration platform that
supports women in their entrepreneurial journey. Specific goals include:
Virtual incubator website: Using Figma to create UI prototypes and refine them through
iterative testing, this study seeks to develop a functional and accessible virtual incubator
click-through prototype.
User-centered design process: Based on the challenges and needs identified from both
qualitative interviews and quantitative application data, this study adopts a structured
user analysis methodology. A combination of manual data cleaning, keyword extraction,
and clustering techniques is applied to classify participants into representative user
segments. These findings, together with insights from interviews, inform the iterative
prototyping process in Figma, enabling the design and optimization of the website
interface to specifically address the barriers faced by female entrepreneurs in different
regions and stages.
The thesis research question is: How can a virtual incubator prototype be designed to
support women entrepreneurs?

2 Literature review
This section discusses key elements such as the characteristics and types of incubators
and their impact on entrepreneurial teams, focusing on how virtual incubators can help

2.1 Introduction to Virtual Incubators
With the ongoing development of innovation and entrepreneurship, incubators—serving
as vital platforms for supporting startups—have evolved into diverse types and models.
Driven by advances in information and communication technology (ICT), virtual business
incubators are emerging as a powerful force within the entrepreneurial support system,
reshaping the way services are delivered and resources are integrated. By leveraging the
Internet, electronic data interchange, video conferencing, and other modern tools,
virtual incubators efficiently connect enterprises, customers, suppliers, partners, and
operators in cyberspace [2]. This capability to transcend physical boundaries and

industry, and geographic focus, but also need to be adapted to specific entrepreneurial
ecosystem contexts [86].
2.1.3 Roles, Impacts and Efficacy Mechanisms of Incubators
Literature reviews and empirical studies widely recognize the multiple roles of incubators
in driving industrial innovation, economic growth, and social entrepreneurship [87][88].
Incubators not only enhance startup success rates but also promote the development
and network expansion of incubated firms through network mediation, resource linkage,
and assertiveness strategies [89][90]. Moreover, incubators of different scales and
specialization paths exhibit varied performance in resource allocation and network
benefits; large-scale incubators tend to have stronger integration and external
connectivity capabilities [91]. The application of virtual incubators in specialized sectors
such as agri-food highlights both the potential and challenges of developing
industry-specific online platforms [92].
2.1.4 Incubator Innovation and Digital Transformation Trends
Innovation in digital and virtual incubator business models is driving new requirements
for traditional incubation concepts and management. Scholars advocate for the
development of unified virtual incubator models that incorporate diverse stakeholders to
enhance service specialization and user experience [93]. Digital management and the
rise of accelerators not only diversify the incubator landscape but also promote the
professional division and collaboration between incubators and accelerators [94][95].
Research exploring the employment consequences of entrepreneurial experience among
STEM founders further enriches the theoretical understanding of the incubator’s impact
[96].

2.3 The Impact of Virtual Incubators on Startups
Virtual business incubators, with their efficient and flexible online service models, are
increasingly becoming vital platforms supporting the growth and innovation of startups.
Compared with traditional physical incubators, virtual incubators can overcome
geographical and resource limitations by offering a variety of services—such as outreach
personnel, online tools, and off-site consulting—to help startups obtain the resources
and support they need in the early stages [97]. Particularly in environments
characterized by high capital intensity and limited physical service coverage, virtual
incubation services demonstrate unique advantages and practical necessity.
However, existing studies indicate that no single operational model, policy, or service can
guarantee the success of virtual incubation projects; rather, it is the integration of
diverse operational approaches, policies, and services that produces optimal results.
Thus, the synergy between effective policies and services becomes a decisive factor in
determining whether startups can benefit from incubators [97].
In the global entrepreneurial landscape, virtual incubators are seen as crucial network
nodes for diversifying investment risks and facilitating entrepreneurial support. On the
one hand, venture capitalists leverage incubators to diversify high-risk investment
portfolios; on the other hand, aspiring entrepreneurs rely on virtual incubators for
multi-level support and resources needed to launch their businesses [98]. This "GloCal"
(global–local) network structure helps accelerate the circulation of entrepreneurial
resources and enhance innovation capabilities, which is particularly important for
startups in transitioning and developing economies.
It is worth noting that, when faced with highly uncertain and forward-looking innovation
demands, traditional market research tools often prove inadequate. Innovative
methodologies, such as “consumer flash fiction,” have been introduced to capture the
foresight and imagination of non-experts (i.e., potential users) regarding the distant
future. This approach enables enterprises to gain a better understanding of future
markets and technological opportunities during the early sensing phase of innovation,
thereby providing valuable insights for strategic decision-making in startups [99].
Although incubators provide a growth platform for startups, numerous challenges persist
in actual operations. For example, research on South African SMEs has found that
incubators often lack genuine cooperative relationships with client firms, and support
policies frequently fail to address the needs of a diverse range of entrepreneurial actors.
Furthermore, the unique value proposition and operational model of incubators are
often not clearly articulated [100]. Therefore, incubators must clarify their own
operational
models,
optimize
service

and
enhance
targeting
and
sustainability in order to better support the long-term growth of startups and the
healthy development of the entrepreneurial ecosystem.

2.4 The Role of Virtual Incubators in Entrepreneurship
Virtual incubators are playing an increasingly vital role in promoting innovation and
entrepreneurship. Studies show that incubators guide startups through the entire
process from ideation to maturity, making them key platforms for fostering innovation
and entrepreneurial activity. In Europe and the United States, the selection criteria for
incubators have evolved, with more incubators adopting balanced, multidimensional
screening methods that consider both market characteristics and management team
quality—practices that help increase startup survival rates and enhance incubation
performance [100].
Against the backdrop of digitalization and globalization, virtual incubators and related
services provide entrepreneurs with a more flexible and open support environment.
Research reveals that traditional public incubators, private incubators, and digital service
providers are all leveraging digital tools to optimize service processes and expand
external collaboration, thereby lowering the barriers to entrepreneurship and making
entrepreneurial support less dependent on local ecosystems or geographic constraints
[101][102] Incubators not only reach potential entrepreneurs through channels such as
social media, but also serve as intermediaries for networking, funding, mentoring, and
talent acquisition, helping startups navigate critical stages of development. During the
pandemic, many incubation activities have shifted online, further highlighting the
significance of virtual incubators.
The rise of digital entrepreneurship platforms has also led to new entrepreneurial
ecosystems. Recent literature identifies five key themes of digital entrepreneurship
platforms: success factors, innovation ecosystems, entrepreneurship models, sharing
platforms, and co-creation mechanisms, providing a theoretical foundation for
understanding the diversity and developmental trends of digital entrepreneurship [103].
Innovation is the core competitiveness of technology-based startups, both during and
after incubation. Case studies show that these enterprises mainly focus on incremental
innovation,
continually
improving
through
interactions
and
collaborations
with
universities, research centers, and other incubated companies. As startups transition
from incubation to graduation, their innovation processes become more formalized,
resource investment increases, and there is a stronger emphasis on customer needs
[104].
Global incubation practices also demonstrate that building an innovation-driven
economy relies heavily on incubator support. Incubators in countries such as the United
States, China, Brazil, and India have developed diverse operational models tailored to
their respective economic and cultural contexts. The establishment of global incubation
networks and advances in digital technology have created broader opportunities for
international collaboration in innovation and entrepreneurship [105].

2.5 The Impact of Virtual Incubators on
Technology-Based Enterprises
Technology business incubators (TBIs) play a crucial role in fostering technological

Studies have shown that during special periods such as the COVID-19 pandemic,
traditional incubator services faced significant challenges and struggled to meet
enterprises’ needs for remote support and resource access. Virtual incubators,
characterized by their online and flexible services, have effectively filled these gaps.
Evidence suggests that neither traditional nor purely virtual models are sufficient on
their own; instead, a hybrid incubation model that integrates both online and offline
resources, services, and management proves to be more effective. This approach helps
technology-based enterprises respond to uncertainty and achieve sustained innovation
and development [107].
Innovation capability remains the core competitive advantage for technology-based
firms throughout the incubation and post-incubation phases. Multiple case studies
reveal that these enterprises predominantly engage in incremental innovation in

From a broader perspective, technology business incubation has become a key tool in
innovation policy and entrepreneurial support systems. Although different incubation
mechanisms and policies have unique characteristics and their effectiveness often
depends on target groups, positioning, and practical contexts, the prospects for
technology business incubation remain promising. Ongoing research and continuous
innovation in practice will further enrich and enhance the theoretical and practical
foundations of technology business incubation [109].

3.1.4 Clustering results and user portrait construction method
Based on the above pre-processed entrepreneurial applicant data set, combined with
natural language extraction of keywords, standardized background feature vectors and
principal component dimensionality reduction (PCA), this study uses the K-means
clustering algorithm to divide more than 550 entrepreneurs into groups, with the goal of
identifying the potential entrepreneurial motivation patterns and ability characteristics
differences among them, and providing a scientific basis for subsequent function design
and interface iteration.
After determining k = 3 as the optimal clustering number, a multi-dimensional analysis of
each cluster sample is performed to extract representative features to construct a typical
user portrait. The weight distribution of each cluster keyword vector, regional and
industry focus constitute the "group label" of each type of entrepreneur.

3.3.1 Design Methodology and Tool Selection
In the Develop stage of the platform interface and functions, this study continued the
user-centered core methodology, combined with the agile development concept, and
adopted an iterative UI prototype construction and evaluation process to ensure a high
degree of fit between user needs and actual functions. Through multiple rounds of
prototype design, feedback collection and optimization and correction, the core
functional structure of the platform was gradually formed.
（1）Methodological framework: Combining UCD with agile iteration
To ensure that prototype design is centered on real user needs, this study adopts the
User-Centered Design (UCD) method, which emphasizes that design activities are carried
out around user portraits, demand characteristics, and usage behaviors [130]. The UCD
framework provides a structured path for this study, and all aspects from function
definition, information architecture to interaction layout are verified by users and
iteratively improved.
At the same time, the design process combines agile development methods to achieve
rapid response to user feedback through short-cycle prototype testing. This method
helps to discover problems with interface usability and interaction logic in the early
stages of design, effectively reducing later development costs [131].
（2）Toolchain selection and coordination mechanism
Figma is used as the main design platform for UI prototype design and interactive
flowcharts. The platform has good cloud writing capabilities and fast building functions,
which is suitable for building clickable prototypes. In addition, Tencent Meeting is used
to record interviews, and Notion is used to record design decisions, user testing feedback,
and demand iterations to achieve decision-driven UI design.

3.4.2 Click-Through Prototype
In order to further verify the rationality of the functional path and the usability of the
interactive process, this study uses the click-through prototype method to construct the
system operation path. Click-through prototype is an interactive low-fidelity prototype
(or medium/high-fidelity prototype) commonly used in user experience design. It refers
to simulating the actual usage path of the product by clicking and jumping between
pages, allowing users to "click" the page to experience the product's process and
structure without the need for real background function support [133]. This method has
the following characteristics and implementation strategies:
(1) Tool selection: Use visual prototyping tools such as Figma to build click-to-jump logic
to ensure that users can complete the real-perception "operation flow" in the preset
path;
(2) Module construction principles: Based on the user function theme, the core needs
and pain points in the interview are mapped into clickable page paths; each page
contains the core entrance, guidance information and jump buttons to support
task-oriented independent exploration;
(3) Interaction strategy: The interface layout structure is clear and the information
hierarchy is clear; the homepage contains the core introduction of the platform and
module navigation, and
the
remaining
pages are introduced in
turn
to the
entrepreneurial school, mentor docking, investment docking, community interaction and
support resources;
(4) User testing: Use the task list method [134] to invite target users to conduct actual
click tests and record their completion path, click feedback, jump judgment and
operation deviation; the test content covers key task nodes such as "course viewing and
registration", "investor screening and matching", and "mentor information reading and
appointment".

（1）Most platforms focus on general entrepreneurial support and mentoring services,

basic services

personalized designs for specific entrepreneurial groups (such as women and

the content level, making it difficult to form differentiated competitive barriers. Although
some incubators for women have gender-friendly concepts, their functional composition
is still mainly based on traditional incubation structures, lacking the psychological
support and emotional encouragement design unique to female entrepreneurs.
（2）The interaction between tutors and the community is the mainstream feature, but
the platform communication mechanism is mostly static content presentation, and the
interactivity and instant feedback capabilities need to be enhanced:
Most platforms adopt the consultation mechanism of "mentor's personal homepage +

community functions are mostly information display type, lacking user-led interactive
blocks and communication mechanisms, making it difficult to promote an active
entrepreneurial ecological community atmosphere. Only a few platforms such as
Fe/Male Switch have introduced storytelling and interactive feedback mechanisms.
（3）The investment matching mechanism is not transparent, the display of investment
and financing resources is weak, and there is a lack of sustainable growth path design:
Many platforms do not clearly display information about investors or institutions, and
only provide project display functions. Although some platforms claim to provide funding
links, they lack systematic paths or transparent matching mechanisms. Most platforms
remain in the early guidance stage and are unable to provide the resource docking and
continuous support required for the later development of entrepreneurship, affecting
the closed-loop efficiency of entrepreneurs from "learning" to "implementation".
（4）The platform interface design is relatively simple, lacks guidance for task paths,
and the page interaction experience needs to be improved:

5 Discussion and Conclusion
5.1 Summary of Key Findings
This study aims to explore how virtual incubator platforms can better support the unique
needs of early-stage female entrepreneurs. The study follows a structured path:
identifying the functions of mainstream virtual incubator websites, conducting
user-centered needs analysis, and iteratively improving the interface through three
rounds of Figma Click-Through Prototype design.
The key conclusions are as follows:
(1) Female entrepreneurs generally have a need for mentorship
User interview data show that female entrepreneurs generally want mentorship and
have missed business opportunities due to lack of mentorship. These insights were
collected through in-depth interviews and multiple UI iterations were performed
through user-centered design (UCD) methods [130], [131], ultimately achieving a mentor
consultation interface that meets customer needs and has a zero error rate.
(2) The core functions of the platform are established as investor search and mentor
consultation interfaces
Through thematic coding [128], the study identified and prioritized the core functions of
the platform, with particular emphasis on mentorship, community liaison, and investor
search. The coding process combined deductive and inductive strategies to ensure
consistency with the research objectives while capturing emerging user needs [129].
(3) Users generally pursue information simplicity rather than information richness
During the interview process, many users mentioned the simplicity of the page, the
pursuit of information foldability and key highlights. Therefore, when designing, we
focused on information communication, changing the original high failure rate to 0
failure rate.
5.2 Limitations of the Study
Although this study has gained valuable insights, it still faces several limitations.
First, the sample size and interview scope of mentors and investors are relatively limited
compared to entrepreneurs, focusing mainly on participants of First HerStartupCamp.
Although this ensures the depth and contextual relevance of the research, the lack of
==PAGE:84==
Enhancing The Effectiveness Of Virtual Incubator
76
generalization may limit the applicability of the research results to a wider range of
entrepreneurial experiences.
Second, the user interface prototype developed in this study has not been deployed in a
real online environment, and only click-through prototypes were developed based on
Figma rather than actual deployment [133]. Therefore, this study cannot assess
long-term user engagement, system adoption, or changes in behavior over time—factors
that are critical to verifying the effectiveness of the platform in practice.
Third, this study relies primarily on qualitative methods, such as semi-structured
interviews and thematic analysis [127][128]. While these methods are well suited to
discovering subtle user needs, the lack of large-scale quantitative data may limit the
generalizability and statistical robustness of the conclusions.
5.3 Conclusion
This study aimed to explore how to design a virtual incubator platform to better support
early-stage female entrepreneurs through an iterative, human-centered design approach.
The project followed a structured research process—starting with problem identification,
moving through user interviews and thematic analysis, and culminating in three rounds
of user interface prototype iterations—and ultimately yielded actionable insights and a
user-validated click-through prototype [133].
The results show that combining user-centered design (UCD) with agile prototyping can
help designers align platform functionality with users’ real needs and emotional context.
Key features such as mentorship, investor connections, and community support were
critical to the target audience and were reflected in the interface priorities and
interaction flows developed during the design iteration process [130].
This study demonstrates how to make virtual incubators more inclusive and more in line
with user pain points through an iterative UI design framework and human-centered
design, providing a template for UI design in virtual incubators that better meets the
needs of female entrepreneurs.

6 Recommendations for future work
There are still many aspects that can be optimized for future application work. The
following are some suggestions for future practical applications:
(1) Technical Development Suggestions
Convert the click-through prototype into an interactive platform (Figma →Web App),
add a database and user account system, support course registration and mentor
matching, and introduce AI/algorithm mechanism for intelligent recommendation of
mentors or investors.
(2) Longitudinal User Testing
Deploy the system in real incubation projects, conduct long-term user tracking, and
introduce quantitative data (such as registration rate, conversion rate, mentor response
time, etc.) to monitor the effectiveness of use, and compare user satisfaction and
retention rate before and after the revision.
(3) Content and Community Expansion
The platform content can be enriched, such as real entrepreneurial case libraries, mentor
video introductions, and online entrepreneurial training camps, and interactive sections
or community mechanisms can be added to enhance connectivity and engagement
among users.
(4) Inclusion and Localization
Consider the needs of female entrepreneurs from different cultural and language
backgrounds, make multi-language adaptation, and especially study the limiting factors
for users in developing countries and remote areas when using virtual incubators.
(5) Methodological Enhancements
In the user research phase, a mixed method (quantitative questionnaire + interview +
behavior record) is used, and the sample of respondents is expanded to include mentors
in multiple fields, investors, and government support agencies, to improve the platform
structure from multiple perspectives.

A.1.1 Participant 1: Entrepreneurs and volunteers of
the first HerStartupCamp
Question: Please explain your relationship with HerStartup platform.
Answer: I was a volunteer for the first phase of the platform, and during the event, I
had a strong desire to contribute to the platform. Since the platform was short of
staff at the time, I took on some logistical support work, mainly responsible for
meeting minutes, recruiting volunteers, and editing tutors' lecture videos, etc., doing
what I could when everyone was busy. This also prompted me to establish a deeper
connection with the platform.
A.1.1.1 Needs and Challenges
Question: As a female entrepreneur, do you feel any inconvenience in the process of
starting a business?
Answer: First of all, as an inexperienced entrepreneur, I feel very confused and don’t
know where to start. I really hope to have professional support. Secondly, having ideas
but not being able to execute them well can easily lead to a stagnation in the entire
entrepreneurial process, so I think someone needs to supervise me in a timely manner.
Thirdly, I am not familiar with many rules, regulations, laws and regulations in the
entrepreneurial process, which may lead to mistakes and negative impacts. Therefore, I
also hope to have professional teachers to give guidance. Finally, I am worried that even
if I make a lot of efforts, if the project is not well-known and popular, people will feel that
the influence of entrepreneurship is not enough, or even equal to no entrepreneurship.
Question: In the early days of your business, have you ever missed out on business
opportunities due to lack of guidance or resources?
Answer: Yes. In the early days of my business, I had the idea of trying a project with my
friends. We invested a lot of time and energy in communication, but because we had no
idea how to start and what to do first and what to do later, the project did not land.
Although strictly speaking, there was no clear business opportunity in front of us,
==PAGE:98==
Enhancing The Effectiveness Of Virtual Incubator

because there was no guidance, we took many detours like headless flies and eventually
missed the results that could have been achieved.
Question: As a female entrepreneur, have you ever been treated differently by investors
because of your gender? Please give an example.
Answer: Because the project has not yet been implemented and I have not yet come
into contact with investors, I have not yet encountered any gender-based discrimination.
A.1.1.2 Functional Requirements
Question: As an entrepreneur, if there is a virtual incubator to help entrepreneurs
start their businesses, what functions do you most hope it will achieve?
Answer: I hope that this virtual incubator can provide a clear, logical, and
well-structured program guidance. It would be best if it could first tell me an overall
entrepreneurial framework, let me know what the whole process is like, and then
guide me step by step what to do and what to prepare for each step. In this way, I
will not be stuck at the beginning due to lack of direction. I also hope that this
platform is not the kind of marketing content that just "listen to video lessons" or
"watch some cases", but can tell me that there are theoretical basis and practical
cases behind these contents. I need to know that this method is really effective for
others, or what kind of entrepreneurial scenario it is suitable for, only in this way will
I be convinced. In addition, I hope that the staff or operation team of the platform
are gentle and patient, especially for beginners like us who know nothing at the
beginning. If they have a bad attitude and are impatient with our questions, then I
definitely don't want to continue using this platform. I also value the efficiency and
convenience of the platform. I don't want to spend a lot of time dealing with
inefficient steps, such as repeatedly uploading materials, filling out complex forms,
and repeatedly looking for information. I hope that this incubator itself is a simple
and efficient system that allows me to focus on entrepreneurship itself.
Question: Have you looked at the existing business courses on the market? What do
you think are their biggest shortcomings and advantages?
Answer: I have not systematically participated in business courses in society, but I
have discussed such content with my friends. My friend once wanted to participate
in the entrepreneurship course of the Shenzhen campus of the Chinese University of
Hong Kong. The advantages of that course are strong systematization and rich
resources, but there are several problems: first, the registration threshold is high,
and you must join their project to be eligible to attend the class; second, the course
requires you to implement the entrepreneurial project, that is, there must be actual
output; third, the course time conflicts greatly with normal classes, which puts a lot
of pressure on students to make choices. Because of these restrictions, my friend
==PAGE:99==

was unable to take this course in the end. And I personally lost the opportunity to
receive formal business guidance because I had no other choice at the time. As for
courses in society, I don’t know much, but I feel that many courses currently have a
high entry threshold for beginners, and it is difficult to get support that is truly
suitable for them.
A.1.1.3 UX Design Preferences
Question: When using similar entrepreneurial platforms, what interfaces or
interaction designs impressed or troubled you?
Answer: I have not systematically used a complete entrepreneurial platform, but I
am quite impressed that some websites will directly display buttons such as "Apply
Now" and "Learn More" on the page, allowing users to quickly participate or submit
their ideas. I think this direct interactive method is very convenient and user-friendly.
In addition, some platforms will have 24-hour online customer service robots.
Although I am not sure whether this design is applicable to entrepreneurial
platforms, it does provide a possibility of timely response, which is helpful for
beginners. In contrast, although using forms to collect information is sufficient on a
small scale, it is relatively weak in improving user experience.
Question: When browsing similar web pages, what kind of information display
method do you prefer?
Answer: I prefer the combination of pictures and text. Pure text is resistant,
especially many young people will skip it when they see a long paragraph of text. So
it is best to use images, keywords, or short videos to convey key information. I also
like to distinguish different sections of the web page with colors or design styles,
such as the structure of a school website with clear classifications, different content
corresponds to different styles and pictures, so I can know which page I am on
without reading the text. I hope that the content is progressive, rather than all laid
out in front of me as soon as it is opened. For example, the page can be divided into
several guide blocks, allowing me to unfold the information step by step, rather than
letting me keep scrolling down. In addition, the key information must be highlighted,
whether through fonts, colors or animations, putting the core content in the visual
center, and the non-key content should also be retained, but it can be weakened. In
this way, I can quickly grasp the structure without being overwhelmed by the
information.
A.1.1.4 Improving Accessibility and Usability
Question: How do you hope the platform will guide beginners or those with a
non-technical background?

Answer: I hope that the platform can make the registration process clear and

and the operation is very troublesome, users will easily back off, especially office
workers or students who have limited time and are unwilling to spend a lot of energy
on trivial processes. After the registration is completed, the platform should provide
timely feedback, such as telling me "We will contact you within three days" to let me

relying on cold standard answers. For beginners, the platform's guidance should not

confidence to continue using it and explore it in depth.

should minimize nonsense so that I can quickly grasp the key points. Visually, it is
best to be beautiful and simple, not too complicated, but not sloppy or
unprofessional. If the overall style is comfortable to look at and easy to use, it will be
a big plus. In addition, I really hope that the platform can build a multi-language
interface, so that entrepreneurs from different countries can freely choose to switch
the web page to the language they feel comfortable with, rather than just English. Of
course, the difficulty of implementing a multi-language interface should be relatively

A.1.5.1 Needs and Challenges

Answer: If we talk about the entrepreneurial process, I personally feel that in addition to
the common problem of funding, the bigger problem is resource coordination, especially
the scheduling of human resources. For example, when you are the main entrepreneur
or initiator, your vision and ideas can easily affect your partners or team members. But
the problem is that when you start a business for the first time, there is often a big
deviation between your ideas and implementation, which requires strong cooperation
from team members. The reality is that many teams have too many people at the
beginning, and some are not full-time participants, and they do not have a strong
willingness, but only participate at the "idea level". This leads to the project remaining at
the conceptual interface even after a few months, and it cannot be truly implemented,
and it is impossible to verify the market value. Everyone tends to focus on "funding
difficulties", but in fact, resource coordination and human resources promotion are the
reasons why many projects fail when they "seem to have not started yet". This process is
particularly obvious for female entrepreneurs, because once the vision you put forward
is not well absorbed or executed by the team, you are likely to passively bear the
consequences of the project being out of control. As a mentor, I have not actually felt
any particularly obvious inconvenience yet. But I do pay more attention to female
entrepreneurs on a psychological level. For example, they are more likely to be
unconfident and afraid to express themselves. I will pay special attention to these details
and give them encouragement and support. Because I know that many female
entrepreneurs are actually very capable, but they are more conservative in expressing
themselves, so they need a mentor who understands their rhythm to guide them.

A.1.5.2 Functional Requirements
Question: As a mentor, if there is a virtual incubator to help mentors help
entrepreneurial teams realize their own value and earn extra income, what functions
do you most want it to achieve?
Answer: If it is an online virtual incubator that does not provide physical space and
resources, but helps mentors connect with entrepreneurial teams, realize their own
value and earn income, I think the most important functions are the following: First, I
would especially hope that it can let me clearly see what the entrepreneurial team
has achieved so far, such as whether the product has been initially launched,
whether there is data, and whether there is market feedback from the trial. In this
way, as a mentor, I know what kind of support I can provide. Many mentors do not
want to talk in general terms, but provide suggestions for a real "stage confusion".
Second, the platform should preferably allow mentors to directly point out the
existing promotional materials and product copywriting of the entrepreneurial team,
rather than just giving ideas. For example, if the other party already has a version of
the promotional strategy, then I can directly point out where they need to optimize
and what communication tips they can use. This kind of "precise feedback" based on
existing content is more valuable and more efficient than empty talk. Third, from the
perspective of mentors, we hope that this platform can provide transparency in
project docking, so that we can choose the mentoring object ourselves, rather than
passively accept the assignment. At the same time, we also hope that there are
some mechanisms to record the extent to which our feedback is adopted, even if it is
not mainly based on benefits, at least it is a manifestation of recognition or influence.
Fourth, if the platform can clearly classify the professional directions of mentors,
then the entrepreneurial team will be more accurate in choosing mentors, reducing
the time cost of both parties. The last point is that no matter how many functions
are designed, it is best to be simple and direct. Because entrepreneurs already have
a lot of information, if the platform is too complicated, it will affect their use. So
what I value most is practicality and clarity, not showmanship.

A.1.5.3 UX Design Preferences

detailed content is hidden in the application interface.

directly, and it will not make people feel redundant or complicated. In contrast,
some domestic web pages are too crowded, and the information is arranged in a
mess, which makes people dizzy.

A.1.5.4 Improving Accessibility and Usability
Question: How do you hope the platform will guide beginners or those with a

needs of users, rather than relying solely on guidance design to attract people. For
entrepreneurs with non-technical backgrounds or those who are just starting out,
they may not care about what technology you use or what cool guidance methods
you design. What they care about is: "Can I get the help I want on this platform?" I
suggest that some truly valuable information - such as what mentors can provide,
project promotion support, and solutions to common problems - can be
concentrated on the mentor page. As for the so-called "guidance", it doesn't need to
be too complicated. If the design is too complicated, it will easily make users lose
their way. My point of view is: provide clear classification and clear purpose
navigation, so that users can quickly locate resources according to their goals, such
as "I need market strategy advice" and "I want to find someone to optimize the
product", so that they can jump to the corresponding content immediately. In
addition, it is best for the platform to avoid piling up irrelevant information,
especially for users in the early stages, who are most afraid of information overload.
