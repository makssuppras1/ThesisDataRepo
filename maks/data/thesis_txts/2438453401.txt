A Design Proposal for a 24/365 Public Space
by Ioannis Antoniou
Public Spaces are essential parts of livable cities, providing ground for social in-
teractions and ensuring a sense of citizenship. This thesis aims to investigate the
unrealized potential of public spaces in the winter time and night time, two time
periods where urban life is believed either to decline or to be mostly experienced
in commercialized urban zones. Based on this analysis, this thesis will propose
public space concepts for 24 hour and all season conditions.
A literature review on the current state of public spaces was conducted to deter-
mine the role they play in social sustainability and the urban experience during
the night time and the winter time. The ﬁndings showed that over recent decades
the public realm has been signiﬁcantly commodiﬁed, resulting in a number of
problematic urban spaces, while globalization has created a globally homogenous
urban landscape. However, robust and accessible public spaces are a high priority
of the current global strategies for creating sustainable cities. Even though it is
met with many negative preconceptions in the public sphere, urban night life is
valuable in strengthening a city’s social capital, while the concept of the 24 hour
city, which emerged from the evolution of lighting and other technologies, is cen-
tral in the agenda of many global cities. Winter is problematic mostly in cold
countries, where it is limiting social interaction and mobility, resulting in inactive
public spaces. Solutions to these revolve mainly around creating incentives for
people to go out in the winter, while projects that aim to create a ”third space”
of appropriate climate conditions are on an experimental level.
Key informant interviews and a questionnaire were used as further research tools.
Interviews were conducted with Copenhagen-based organizations and companies
whose work is related to activating public spaces in the city during the winter and
night time, while the questionnaires mapped the behavioral patterns of the people
of Copenhagen during those time periods. Regarding winter time, it was found
==PAGE:4==

that the city oﬀers a variety of indoor and outdoor spaces for diﬀerent activities
but issues remain such as ﬁnding refuge from the cold in a highly commercialized
public realm and enhancing the visibility of the existing spaces. For the night time
aspect, the most prevalent issues that could be addressed by public spaces were
the limited options for non commercial spaces as well as the exclusion of certain
social groups from the urban night life. Copenhagen municipality’s strategies were
investigated in order to understand the relevant framework for the implementa-
tion of the proposed spaces. Enhancing bike mobility, investing in experimental
and temporary projects that will give an edge to the city and providing intensiﬁed
support to its vulnerable areas in order to achieve social cohesion were the main
points of the vision for the city.
Two separate concepts were developed for each time period. For the winter time,
a network of spaces called Winter City Hubs within the city is proposed. These
spaces provide shelter from the harsh winter conditions, while they also accom-
modate public participation processes for new projects in the city, in a symbiotic
operation. An environmentally sustainable approach to the design of the spaces
was adopted. Regarding the night time, Døgn Space is a temporary project that
will serve as an experiment of creating a night life node within the urban fabric.
In order to assess the eﬀects and risks of such a project and develop management
strategies, a Social Impact Analysis was followed.
Even though an indicator set that would quantify the contributions of the design
proposals to Social Sustainability was not constructed, the design principles that
were fulﬁlled respond to its deﬁnition and content. It was concluded that there is
potential in the winter and night time public spaces when combined with Social
Sustainability values.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the course of my masters program, I have worked on a number of projects re-
lated to sustainable urban development and smart cities. During these projects, I
was highly interested in the central role public spaces play in the aforementioned
concepts. It is clear that public spaces can bring valuable social, economic and en-
vironmental beneﬁts in cities and greatly enhance sustainability. Moreover, living
in Copenhagen, which is considered an exemplar of eﬃcient urban planning and
urban design on a global level, one can experience how an urban landscape with
diverse and well thought out public spaces contributes to a livable and appealing
city.
The concept of sustainability has been an area of focus during my studies, rec-
ognizing its signiﬁcance for the future of cities and the planet at this moment in
time. However, even though the economic and environmental aspects involved
more tangible and easily quantiﬁable indicators, which made them easier concepts
to grasp, the social aspect of sustainability appeared to be more elusive, and thus
somewhat overlooked in the process of measuring sustainability. Looking at pub-
lic spaces through the lens of social sustainability seemed like an interesting and
challenging approach to exploring the potential and limitations of both concepts.
In order to delimit the scope of the project, the question that was posed was
where unlocked potential of public spaces lies in terms of spacial distribution or
time frame of use. From my lived experiences, as well as with discussions with
fellow students and my supervisor, the winter and night time seemed to respond to

in general. The urban night time, which for most people in the city entails enter-
tainment and socialization activities, manifests itself mainly in commercial zones.
Thus, the intersection of the concepts of public space and urban winter and night
time oﬀered promising ground for research. Furthermore, the variation in the two
time frames (24 hour and all year round) appeared to provide the opportunity for
a fully realized investigation of the above issues.
1.2
Goal
The goal of the project is to research the current state of public spaces worldwide,
their contribution to social sustainability and the ways urban life unfolds in the
examined time frames. The research aims to lead to a design proposal for a 24 hour
and all season public space that will address cities’ future needs. The project’s
goal is best expressed through the following problem statement:
Public spaces are central to achieving livable and socially sustainable
cities.
It has been observed that urban life can decline during the
winter time, while human activity in the night time mainly takes place
in the city’s commercial spaces.
How can public space, speciﬁcally
designed to accommodate needs of these time frames, improve quality
of life in cities and amplify social sustainability?

2.1
Public Space
2.1.1
Deﬁnition
Public space can be viewed through diﬀerent contexts and is the subject of study
in various disciples.
It can be understood as the space which all people have
access to and can use. In Public and Private in Social life, Benn and Gaus Mada-
nipour [2003] identify three dimensions of social organization that delineate the
distinction between publicness and privateness: access, agency and interest; ac-
cess is further divided into access to spaces, activities, information and resources.
This subdivision leads to more complex space classiﬁcations: a public space can
provide access to the people but not necessarily to the activities within (for ex-
ample a group of friends meeting in a square). It is therefore apparent that a
nuanced approach is needed when trying to classify a space as public or private.
However, using Benn and Gaus’ three dimensional theory, Madanipour considers
public space when ”it is controlled by the public authorities, concerns the people
5
==PAGE:17==
6
as a whole, is open or available to them, and is used or shared by all the members
of a community”Madanipour [2003].
2.1.2
A critique of modern public spaces
When reviewing the broad literature regarding the form of public space in the past
three decades, a general consensus appears to be that there is a decline in its qual-
ity, with proponents of this theory providing diﬀerent explanations. In ”Contem-
porary Public Space: Critique” Carmona [2010], Matthew Carmona supports that
the various critiques can be organized into two categories: these which identify the
under-management of public space as the reason for its decline, and those which
blame over-management practices. According to Carmona, under-management in-
volves factors such as the dominance of the private car, the privatization of public
space, the functional separation of uses. These have lead to the production of sev-
eral typologies of problematic public space. Over-management critiques mainly
revolve around the commodiﬁcation of public space and the homogenized experi-
ences that have resulted from globalization, as well as the increased surveillance
within. Loukaitou-Sideris Loukaitou-Sideris [1996] argues that postmodern design
”eliminates unwanted and feared political, social and cultural intrusions”, while
Sorkin states that today’s world is ”producing a standard urbanism where public
space is for consumption...with public space being heavily managed with an ob-
session on security” Sorkin [1992].
Despite these valid critiques, public spaces continue to be a vital ingredient of
urban prosperity and investing in them is extremely important for the future of
cities on a global level. High-quality public spaces increase the value of an area and
boost economic activity, while they provide signiﬁcant mental and physical health
beneﬁts to the citizens who use them for sport, leisure and socialization Woolle
and Rose [2004].The Global Public Space Kit , published by UN-habitat in 2015,
underlines the importance of public spaces for the quality of life in modern cities.
According to the report, which serves as a guide to civic leaders worldwide for suc-
cessful urban development, lively and continuously used public spaces strengthen
the social capital and empower civic engagement, and greatly contribute to an
appealing and safe urban environment Garau [2015].

2.1.3
Placemaking
Placemaking refers to the process, or philosophy, of creating inviting public spaces
that are human-centric and address the needs of the local community, making the
best use of the area’s characteristics. According to the Project for Public Spaces,
which has been dedicated to promoting the importance of public spaces since 1975
Project for Public Spaces [a], ”more than just promoting better urban design,
Placemaking facilitates creative patterns of use, paying particular attention to the
physical, cultural, and social identities that deﬁne a place and support its ongoing
evolution” Project for Public Spaces [b]. The origins of Placemaking can be traced
back to the work of Jane Jacobs and William H. Whyte, whose work in the 60’s
has focused on placing human needs and activity on the center of urban design,
contrary to the modernist car-centric approach of the era.
PPS’s long experience in engaging the public in the placemaking process has re-
sulted in the development of many useful guidelines for public participation. The
Place Diagram 2.1, is a tool developed for the evaluation of places by a community.

As it was mentioned above, not only the distinction between public and private
spaces is complex, but there is also a wide variety of public spaces in terms of
size, form and function. An important part of successful Placemaking is studying
the public space typology of the examined area. Typology, in an urban planning
context, refers to the study and classiﬁcation of the patterns and forms of space
that appear in the urban landscape. Typology can be very useful in an urban

CPTED is a set of measures and design principles applied to the urban space that
aim to mitigate crime and enhance the feeling of safety; the term was ﬁrst coined

• Natural Surveillance refers to the design elements that provide visibility
to the space: the presence of windows, balconies and doors of residences in

pedestrian traﬃc and minimized opportunities of concealment also belong

• Image Management is the positive feelings the built environment can

• Legitimate Activity Support is strongly connected to Jacobs’ theory of

• Territoriality is the main concept of CPTED and is made up from several

Urban Winter and Night
2.3.1
The 24 hour city
The urban landscape during night time has always been a space of contradiction
in terms of human experiences and expectations. Sharpe claims that it is a second
city with its own geography and its own set of citizens that emerges when daylight
fades William Chapman Sharpe [2008]. According to M. Roberts and A. Eldridge,
”the night is commonly represented and understood according to the opposing
narratives of fear and pleasure.” Roberts and Eldridge [2012].
Life in the city after the sun sets has traditionally been associated with crime,
disorder and anti-social behaviour. The halt of commercial activities and the end
of business hours means that many of the busy areas of the day are deserted,
while the darkness of the nocturnal city creates feelings of fear and discomfort
when walking alone on a dark alley. Whether these fears surrounding urban life
during the night are justiﬁable or not, they have been the basis of a long history
of restrictions and regulations of the human activity in urban areas; from more
common ones, such as the closing down of parks and other public spaces during
the night, to more extreme ones, such as youth curfews Tony Favro. Even though
policing, regulation and surveillance have been the most common street safety
practises, other urbanists argue that the most eﬀective way of evoking a feeling
of safety on the streets is simply when achieving to keep people out in the streets
for as long as possible.
The most famous advocate of this theory is urbanist
Jane Jacobs, who in the 60’s developed the theory of ”eyes on the street” Jacobs
[1993]. According to Jacobs, when streets are full of people and human movement
is continuous, then there is a sense of mutual protection that can possibly lead to
less crime.
Despite the negative preconceptions about the urban night, the value of the night
time as a ground for conviviality and socialization in cities is indisputable. A city
which provides a variety of bars, clubs and other nocturnal cultural activities to
its citizens is creating more opportunities for the establishment of interpersonal
==PAGE:29==
18
relationships between them and the overall strengthening of its social capital.
Chatterton and Hollands [2003]
In addition, signiﬁcant cultural activity can transpire in a lively urban night; urban
nightlife scenes bring new forms of creativity and give birth to innovations in music,
art, politics and performance Grazian [2009], and their recognition as spaces of
cultural value from local authorities and governments has been increasing.
A
recent example is Berlin nightclub Berghain, which, in 2016, appealing a tax raise,
was granted a ”cultural centre” status from regional ﬁnancial courts Garcia [2017].
Municipal and national governments as well as private actors have become aware
that the investment in the night time economy and maximizing the time frame of
the use of urban space and infrastructure can reap a number of economic and social
beneﬁts and this is reﬂected in the emergence of the 24 hour city concept. Apart
from the common nocturnal activities related to alcohol consumption, spaces such
as supermarkets, cinemas and gyms that operate 24 hours a day can now be found
in many cities worldwide. Furthermore, more and more public spaces are hosting
nocturnal events that involve cultural or other activities. These activities can last
until the morning hours and often involve local businesses that stay open all night.
Another indicator of the 24 hour city concept’s signiﬁcance is the institution of
the Night Mayor, which ﬁrst appeared in Amsterdam and has been followed by
many cities such as London, Paris and New York Feargus O’Sullivan. The role of
the night mayor is to enhance the night-time economy of the city and bridge the
communication gap between the various stakeholders involved in it.
The ”Night Time is a Right Time” competition in London is one of the many
initiatives that show that the adaptation of cities to the 24 hour city principles
is highly prioritized by municipal governments around the globe. In cooperation
with the Built Environment Trust, the competition aims to gather ideas from
”architects, landscape architects, planners, environmentalists, material scientists,
economists, product designers, engineers, acoustic experts and other interested
parties are invited to submit ideas for better 24 hour cities.” The Building Centre.

2.3.3
Nightscapes
In an attempt to categorize the urban spaces in terms of the diﬀerent types of night
life entertainment that appear in them, Chatterton and Hollands have identiﬁed
three types of ”nightscapes” in their book ”Urban Nightscapes: Youth Cultures,
Pleasure Spaces and Corporate Power” Chatterton and Hollands [2003]:
• Mainstream spaces :the bars, pubs or nightclubs, that can be found in the
commercial centers of most large cities worldwide. The commmon denomi-
nator in this category is that these spaces are proﬁt driven, and their target
group is professionals, service sector workers and higher education students.
• Residual spaces : traditional pubs and bars that cater for the local com-
munity. These tend to dissappear or decline because of the shift of consumer
tastes towards the mainstream spaces, though they can still be found in the
underdeveloped urban centers.
• Alternative spaces : these spaces are independently run by and cater for
youth cultures and speciﬁc tastes, and such as unoﬃcial raves in abandoned
warehouses and squatted buidings. Alternative spaces are more creative-
oriented and prioritize inclusion and equal access. They are usually located
in the margins of urban areas.
The distinctions between these types can often become blurry, since what is alter-
native at one time period can turn into mainstream in the future.

Sustainability
Sustainability is a concept that has come to be omnipresent and the main frame-
work in various scientiﬁc ﬁelds, including engineering and urban planning. The
rapid global urbanization, the growing concern over climate change and the strain
==PAGE:32==
21
of resources have resulted to a focus on the ways societies can reproduce them-
selves which will provide a livable environment for future generations. Reviewing
the relevant literature, one can see that various deﬁnitions on the term can be
found by diﬀerent scientists in diﬀerent contexts. Most of the deﬁnitions however
recognize that a useful framework for looking at sustainability is the analysis in
three parameters, also referred to as ”pillars” of sustainability : economic, social
and environmental. The most prevalent deﬁnition of sustainability is the one that
can be found in the United Nation’s report ”Our Common Future: Report of the
World Commission on Environment and Development”, also known as the Brundt-

the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their

3.1
Urban Design in Copenhagen
In the 21st century, Copenhagen has been experiencing signiﬁcant urban develop-
ment and is considered to be a paragon of urban design on a global level. The
city’s urban landscape consists of appealing open and green spaces, iconic architec-
ture and eﬃcient public transport backed by a strong biking infrastructure . The
unique approach to urban planning which is now adopted by various cities around
the world has led to the creation of the term ”Copenhagenize” Atlyn Cathcart-
Keays.
Figure 3.1: The Bicycle Snake, Islands Brygge
However, the high quality of the urban fabric in Copenhagen does not mean that
there are not challenges in the future development of the city. In a conversation
for Gehl Architects about working in a Scandinavian context, entitled ”Evolving
Scandinavia”, architect Ewa Westermark argues that in a city where all the basic
needs are met it can be hard to identify the areas for improvement Janne Bjørsted.
However, she considers the focus to be on human life; elevating unsafe neighbour-
hoods and integrating them with the other areas of the city. Project Manager Ola
Gustafsson agrees with the lack of urgency in urban issues of Scandinavian cities.
She mentions:
In many other cities that we work in, you can go out, take a photo of a
very poor, inaccessible or even dangerous pedestrian environment and
try to change things quickly. Sometimes you lack that possibility in
Swedish cities because, at least in the central locations of the city, all
the basic needs are covered there. However what is often lacking or is
==PAGE:44==
33
less obvious at least, is the second layering, invitations to use and enjoy
the city. It is in many cases a series of smaller changes that will uplift
the situation, which is hard to present when talking about a sense of
urgency.
The issues that result from the strong trust in government and the municipalities
in Scandinavian countries are also brought up. Westermark stresses the impor-
tance of strengthening collaborations between the public and private sector and
overcoming the ”relying solely on the government to ﬁx it for you” mentality. In
addition, the high trust level is responsible for a low public engagement in ur-
ban transformation; the public is relying on the public sector for change rather
than actively participating in the process. It is underlined that identifying non-
traditional tools that will eﬃciently engage local people in the urban development
and possibly learning from other cities with more relevant experience is key for
the future.
3.2
Copenhagen Municipality’s Strategy
Copenhagen is a city that is in the midst of massive urban development and a
series of policies have been constructed to give the right direction to it, as well
as address problems that persist within the city. In this chapter, two of the most
relevant policies are presented, ”Co-Create Copenhagen- A vision for 2025”, and
the ”Policy for Vulnerable Areas” (”Politik For Udsatte Byomr˚ader”). These were
deemed the most relevant because the role of public spaces is stressed in both
of them, and they provide useful guidelines in developing design principles that
answer to the city’s needs.
3.2.1
Co-Create Copenhagen- A vision for 2025
”Co-Create Copenhagen-A vision for 2025” is a guideline outlining the vision for
Copenhagen, prepared by the Technical and Environmental Administration of the
City of Copenhagen in 2015 (Kommune [2015a]). It is constructed around three
main goals, ensuring that Copenhagen will be ”A Livable City”, ”A City with an
Edge” and ”A Responsible City”.
==PAGE:45==
34
Starting with an evaluation of the current situation, Copenhagen is praised for
its high quality of life and its international recognition as an innovative city in
terms of environmental and transport policies. However, future challenges such
as the climate change and the 100.000 inhabitants that are expected to be added
to the population of the city by 2025 require even more focused planning and
development than before. Several areas of improvement are underlined: Copen-
hagen is mentioned to be ”a nice, well-planned city - perhaps a little bit too nice
and predictable”, which requires non traditional urban planning practices. These
practices will have to involve all citizens and the decision making has to transpire
in constant dialogue with other cities on a global level, in order to create a ”more
dynamic city with an edge that better reﬂects the diversity of its population and
allows for individual diﬀerences.” The direction of each of the three goals is sum-
marized below:
A livable city: The rapid increase of Copenhagen’s population means that more
and more people will have to use the city’s public spaces. These spaces will have
to host a wider range of activities and provide citizens with more choices. The
quality of everyday life must be enhanced for all social groups, biking culture must
be supported with the necessary infrastructure and nature must thrive in various
forms and sizes across the urban landscape.
A city with an Edge: In order to compete with other global cities, Copenhagen
has to shape a bolder and edgier urban environment. The diversity and contrasts
of its population should be celebrated and ampliﬁed while the current social co-
hesion is still maintained. Flexibility and experimenting with the urban fabric,
innovation and creativity are important values of the future city.
A Responsible City: Climate change and the strain of resources on a global level
mean that Copenhagen has to invest even further in its sustainable and climate-
friendly approach. The goals for 2025 in this aspect include Copenhagen being
the ﬁrst carbon neutral capital city, intensifying the circular economy and more
green urban spaces.

Questionnaire
A questionnaire of 16 questions was posted and shared on Facebook, as well as in
various groups about sharing events in Copenhagen. It was also posted on LinkedIn
and sent to various acquaintances residing in Copenhagen by e-mail, prompting
them to forward it as well. The time frame of the survey was 2 weeks, with a total
==PAGE:53==
42
number of 126 respondents. The questionnaire was made up of three segments: an
introductory one with demographic questions, while the other two aimed at inves-
tigating people’s perception of life in Copenhagen during the winter and the night
time. Getting feedback on the questionnaire from other students before oﬃcially
publishing it as a test showed that there were diﬀerent understandings of the term
public space among them. Thus, the term social space was used and deﬁned in
the description of both segments as : ”the space where one does not have to spend
money to gain access to and stay. It can be a park, a square, a museum or sports
facilities. In these spaces, social activities occur : sports, conversations, meeting
new people.” It is evident that the way it was circulated set certain limitations in
acquiring a varied population size, in terms of age and nationality. The majority
of the respondents belonged to the 21-29 age group, since it was mostly circulated
between students. Another weakness of the questionnaire was the use of the term
”ethnicity” instead of ”nationality” in the introductory section (the mistake was
due to being no distinction between the term on the author’s native language); this
resulted in many people responding with their race or ethnic background. This
made it diﬃcult to assess the sample: the respondents were divided into Danish,
European and non-European. The grouping is not concrete, but it can be assumed
that the majority was of European nationality. Almost half of the respondents re-
side within the municipality’s boundaries, with Nørrebro and Østerbro on the top
two responses.
Regarding the winter time, opinions on whether options for social spaces in Copen-
hagen are limited were quite divided. While the majority agreed, a considerable
amount responded that they neither agree nor disagree or that they disagree. The
majority of responses to the weather being a considerable factor when deciding to
leave the house were aﬃrmative; most of the respondents believe that it is the rea-
son why socializing occurs less often during the winter. A strong majority agreed
that there is often a need to ﬁnd shelter from the cold and the rain in the city but
there are limited options when it comes to non-commercial spaces.
In the night time segment, the three top preferences of areas where one enjoys
night life were Nørrebro, Indre By and Vesterbro, as expected.
The majority
agreed that options for social spaces in Copenhagen during the night are limited.
Most of the respondents are satisﬁed with the options for night life in the city,
which combined with the previous question, mainly involves commercial spaces.
Two questions whose results are ambiguous are the ones regarding embracing in-
dividuality and exclusion from the urban night. People in general felt that a wide
==PAGE:54==
43
variety of cultures and types of entertainment are being represented and that they
do not feel excluded by the prevailing forms of entertainment in Copenhagen.
However, after publishing the questionnaire, it was realized that these questions
are inherently problematic since people who may be excluded or not represented
by the night life options in the city will inevitably be a minority within society
and consequently in the questionnaire results as well. The most popular reasons
for not enjoying the night life in Copenhagen were the ﬁnancial factor, dissatisfac-

Evaluation
In this chapter, the ﬁndings of the literature review are discussed, along with the
interviews, questionnaires and the municipal documents, in order to formulate a
set of relevant design principles for public spaces that promote urban life.
3.5.1
General
The research showed that the quality of the physical space in Copenhagen is high,
and future projects should prioritize civic engagement in their developing pro-
cesses. Especially in the vulnerable areas, public participation in the development
of the city and utilizing its public spaces will strengthen neighbourhoods and cit-
izenship among its residents. Feedback from existing projects conﬁrm that there
is a signiﬁcant willingness from the public to occupy the city spaces with events
and temporary projects that will contribute to a more lively urban environment.
Providing physical space free of charge to artists and people who want to produce
and experience culture, in an expensive city as Copenhagen, is another common
thread between the interviews and the questionnaire.
3.5.2
Winter Time
From the questionnaire and the public space typology analysis results there is not
a strong case for more public spaces speciﬁcally focusing on winter time activities.
==PAGE:55==
44
Most of the activities seem to be already represented in Copenhagen’s various
indoors cultural spaces and sports facilities, while attempts to capture outdoors
experiences of nature, such as the Sankt Eriks indoors park can prove to be too
costly.
More emphasis appears to be on the visibility of the already existing
spaces, as well as the issue of mobility in the city during harsh weather conditions.
A space that provides shelter from the cold and the rain of the winter for free in
Copenhagen was met with positive feedback in the questionnaires.
3.5.3
Night Time
The main problematic area of urban night life in Copenhagen that was identiﬁed
in the questionnaire results and corresponds with the literature review ﬁndings for
night life in modern cities is its highly commercialized nature, as the opportunities
to enjoy the city for free by occupying its public spaces are far less than the day
time. Another, though less strong, correlation between the questionnaire and the
literature review is the potentially exclusionary nature of urban night settings for
some parts of the population. In terms of spatial distribution, night life is mainly
concentrated in the Nørrebro, Indre By and Vesterbro districts, with other districts
scoring very low in the questionnaire results.

3.6.2
Døgn Space
The night time concept is named Døgn Space (Døgn means a 24 hour period in
Danish), a culture and urban night life park. Døgn Space’s main focus is creating
a public space where one can experience urban night life, which was shown to be
exclusionary for some and strictly commercial, free of charge.
Designing and implementing an urban space that is destined for 24 hour use can
be a complex task, since hubs of nocturnal activity can be diﬃcult to integrate
within an urban area. Issues such as noise and safety have to be addressed to en-
sure that the surrounding neighbourhood will accept and beneﬁt from the project.
In addition, since the concept aims to fulﬁll goals of the municipality, its impact
has to be investigated on diﬀerent geographical levels.
In order to achieve the above, a Social Impact Assessment (SIA) of Døgn Space will
be developed along the design process. According to the International Association
for Impact Assessment,” (SIA) includes the processes of analysing, monitoring
and managing the intended and unintended social consequences, both positive
==PAGE:57==
46
and negative, of planned interventions (policies, programs, plans, projects) and
any social change processes invoked by those interventions. Its primary purpose
is to bring about a more sustainable and equitable biophysical and human envi-
ronment.” (International Association for Impact Assessment). An SIA is essential
in maximizing the positive impacts of an intervention and developing mitigation
strategies for the negative impacts.
It is understood that a successful SIA for an urban development project requires
the collection of a wide variety of data types and their processing should be carried
out by skilled professionals with a clear grasp of the socioeconomic context of the
area and its demographics. Thus, not only a group of engineers, architects and
sociologists have to work over an extensive period of time, but the local commu-
nity also has to be involved actively to provide the aforementioned local context.
The SIA for this project will attempt to identify all the important areas of focus,
evaluate the performance of the investigated area in quantitative and qualitative
measures, address the problematic aspects through the design and develop miti-
gation and adaptation strategies. There is not a single SIA method; it can appear
in diﬀerent contexts and should adapt speciﬁcally in each case. Here, the Social
Impact Analysis (Social Konsekvensanalys) developed by the City of Gothenburg
City of Goteborg is used as a basis and is adapted to the needs of a project of this
scale. The method was chosen because of its simple structure allows for adapt-
ability and the social aspects explored are relevant to the project.
A schematic representation of the Gothenburg model can be seen in 3.8. Even
though the model can be edited to ﬁt diﬀerent cases, reasoning should be made
explicit when omitting or modifying elements of it, in order to ensure that there is
a clear understanding of the entire process and the context of the case. The assess-
ment comprises of three distinct phases that should evolve organically as the urban
development process moves forward. The Inventory phase involves the evaluation
of the current situation in the area: gaining an understanding of the local context
and functions, identifying its strengths and weaknesses and which information are
available to the developers. In the Solutions phase, all the relevant knowledge
acquired in the previous phase is translated into design proposals and policies
that enhance the strengths and minimize the undesired elements. The Eﬀects
phase investigates the ways the planned measures might aﬀect the life in the area
and their contribution to a more socially sustainable city. The last phase should
provide a monitoring and mitigation strategy for the entire life cycle of the project.
==PAGE:58==
47
Figure 3.8: Ghotenburg model of SIACity of Goteborg
The content of each phase is looked at through the prism of four social aspects,
which constitute the ﬁrst axis of the SIA matrix:
• Cohesive City: what are the physical and social barriers that characterize
the area? how can they be overcome?
• Interactions: Does the physical space allow for lively urban life and inter-
actions that will strengthen the local social capital?
• Everyday Life: How can urban space make everyday life easier for all social
groups?
• Identity: Which spaces and the functions that take place there create a
sense of place and belonging among citizens? Can they be increased?
The second axis that completes the Ghotenburg SIA matrix is the geographical
scale of focus. In all the social aspects, information is being gathered and pro-
cessed and strategies are formed always by ”zooming in” on ﬁve diﬀerent levels:
the Building, the Neighbourhood, the District, the City and the Region. Paying
attention to all the levels is important since an intervention in the urban land-
scape of a neighbourhood can aﬀect another one in the city and the Social Impact
Analysis should take this into consideration.
A slightly modiﬁed structure of the Ghotenburg SIA matrix is adopted for the
assessment of Døgn Space. The phases are maintained since all three of them
make up for a relevant framework. In terms of social aspects, a preliminary out-
line of what each aspect entails in each geographical level, for such a project,
==PAGE:59==
48
identiﬁed several overlaps, especially between the Everyday Life and Interac-
tions aspects. Prerequisites for interactions within the city are the existence of
meeting spaces, having physical access to these spaces and opportunities to visit
them (such as festivities and other cultural and social activities). These are all
also important elements of a satisfactory urban everyday life. Thus, their elements
will be evaluated in a uniﬁed aspect called Everyday Life. Regarding the geo-
graphic scales, the Region geographic level is not taken into account because it
is deemed that an intervention of this size will not have any signiﬁcant impact
outside of Copenhagen; any impacts that may be identiﬁed in this category will
be mentioned separately in the discussion segment. Moreover, the Building scale
is referred to as the Area scale, since Døgn Space will be a park and not a single
building. Speciﬁcally for this scale, it is useful for the analysis to involve not only
the functions and value within (which in the Inventory phase are none since it con-
stitutes an unused area), but also its interaction with its immediate surroundings.

From the above, it is evident that the area and neighbourhood scales present ad-
vantages as well as challenges for the desired purpose. Few residences are found
on the immediate surroundings of the area, which means that friction with res-
idents about noise during the night time may be avoided. The location is well
connected with public transport on both scales and will be more easily accessible
from other central neighbourhoods in the city with a new metro station and Super-

bourhood, with planned redevelopment on the periphery of the location. Both
plans of Beauvaisgrunden and Mjølnerparken prioritize the need for pedestrian
and bike ﬂow in the proposed public spaces and Døgn Space is located between
them, which means that new infrastructure could connect the two plans. In addi-
tion to improving physical connectivity, Døgn Space has the potential to enhance
the Mjølnerparken local plan goals with a unique urban space that will attract
people from all over Copenhagen and create an aesthetically appealing and safer
environment where local business can thrive. Moreover, a new meeting space for
leisure in the neighbourhood, which has a high percentage of small apartments,
will contribute to a higher quality of life for its residents. Functions that involve
civic engagement can also be beneﬁcial to the social cohesion of this highly di-
verse neighbourhood; the Lersøgrøften urban farming project shows that there is
already an eﬀort for social mixing, which can be ampliﬁed by a park where locals
will have opportunity to meet and use urban space to create content.
The chosen location being on the border of two quite diﬀerent districts in terms
of urban life and demographics can also be an advantage. Nørrebro is the district
with the youngest population, which is a signiﬁcant target group of the project,
while the location’s proximity to Inner Nørrebro, which concentrates the majority
of night life within the district, raises its chances of being successful, since it can
attract some of the night life concentrated in that area. At the same time, Bispe-
bjerg, which lacks signiﬁcantly in oﬀering varied and lively urban life both for day
and night time, can beneﬁt from Døgn Space. The need for a new green space is
also urgent for Nørrebro, the least green district in the city.
In terms of challenges, the biggest considerations are the acceptance of the space
by the local community on the neighbourhood scale. Noise pollution and litter are
common in urban night life settings. In addition, while the design can positively
aﬀect the safety in the area with improved Image Management, other safety related

4.2.1
Built Environment
The proposal for Døgn Space involves two types of structures for a 24 hour urban
park: the Domes and the Warehouse.
The Domes
The Dome is a proposition for a new public space typology. The aim of the Dome
is to provide the space and tools for a public space of conviviality, free of charge.
Drawing inspiration from the OurHub case, the Domes are equipped with a box
containing night time ”essentials”: a built in audio player with a USB port and a
mini freezer. Their use is not only restricted to night time though, as they can be
used for day time activities such as communal eating or any social gathering.
A simple dome design was created on Rhinoceros 3D, with the graphical algorithm
editor Grasshopper. The dome’s form is presented in 4.31.
Figure 4.31: the Dome
With a gross area of 28 m., each Dome has the capacity to host 6 to 8 people.
==PAGE:88==
77
Figure 4.32: Domes’ dimensions
A number of factors come into play regarding the Domes’ form and materials.
There are two possibilities in terms of the Dome’s communication with the outside
environment: an open space with a permanent opening, or an airtight structure.
The performance of each scenario in optimizing the functionality and minimizing
the cost of the Domes is a multi-dimensional issue which is not thoroughly ex-
plored in this project. Even though Døgn Space addresses the 24 hour aspect of
public spaces, design measures which will maximize the Domes’ use during the
winter should not be ignored. The aesthetic quality of the Dome, as well as the
re purposing measures that are involved in the strategies discussed in 4.3.5 also
inﬂuence the structure’s design. Last but not least, the resistance of the materials,
considering this is a public space exposed to vandalism, has to be considered.
On one hand, even though constructing the Dome as a closed space with a door
can achieve better thermal comfort, it will most likely not reach an acceptable
temperature range during the winter without a heating system, and the indoor air
quality will be poor. In addition, achieving the airtightness of the construction
will increase the cost. On the other hand, a space which is communicating di-
rectly with the outdoor environment will not make the Domes popular during the
winter. Taking the aforementioned issues into account, it is proposed to construct
the Domes as an open space with a permanent opening. Given the experimental
nature of the project, it is deemed wise to keep initial costs low.
The Dome’s frame is constructed from an aluminum frame and transparent panels.
Aluminum was chosen for the frame since it possesses many advantages for this
type of construction; it is lightweight and has a high corrosion resistance, which
makes it resilient to weather conditions. Moreover, it is durable and easy to fab-
ricate and assemble. For the transparent panels, two materials were considered:
ETFE and glass. Both present advantages as well as disadvantages for the desired
==PAGE:89==
78
use.
ETFE is a relatively new material which is gaining growing popularity due to its
light weight (0.35 kg/ m2) and ﬂexibility which makes it ideal for curved glazing
coverings. ETFE panels are produced with inﬂating ETFE ﬁlm into cushions and
fasting them together into two or three layers Knudsen [2016]. ETFE has a life-
time of about 30 years Vector Foiltec, is recyclable and its smooth surface allows
dirt to be washed oﬀfrom the rain, which can reduce maintenance costs. More-
over, ETFE allows UV transmittance, which is important for vegetation to grow
(strategy-related measure). A considerable drawback is its low scratch resistance
to sharp objects which makes it vulnerable to vandalism Knudsen [2016]. Two of
glass’s biggest advantages are its lower U value, which means higher solar gains
during the winter, and its durability. However, since the Dome is designed to be an
open space, the choice of glass will not contribute signiﬁcantly to higher tempera-
tures during winter. Its weight ( 20 kg/ m2) is another disadvantage, compared to
ETFE, as well as its higher cost. Based on the above, and the strategies developed
in the Eﬀects phase, ETFE is chosen as the panels material.
Another design challenge for the Domes is the compromise between creating a
comfortable and attractive space and ensuring that the design elements such as
the furniture are durable and resistant to theft and vandalism. An example of
outdoor furniture that fulﬁll these requirements is presented in z 4.33.
Figure 4.33: Superkilen, Copenhagen
The Warehouse
The Warehouse is a building which will host social activities of a diﬀerent nature
==PAGE:90==
79
than the Domes, for both day and night time. It will address the following design
principles:
• Cultural Incubator: The Warehouse will provide space to artists from
all over the city to exhibit and promote their work, whether it is music,
performances or any type of art.
• Night Time Festivities: The space will be available to be booked by people
that want to organize night parties. These will be limited to the Friday and
Saturday function of the Warehouse.
• Social Integration: In an eﬀort to bridge cultural diﬀerences of the local
community and contribute to social integration, the Warehouse will provide
a space for celebration of various holidays for the ethnically diverse residents
of the area, showcasing the diﬀerent cultures present in the neighbourhood
and foster cultural awareness.
For the Warehouse to act as a truly public space, it is important that all the
events are physically accessible to all. This may be contradictory to the case of
night time festivities, which are traditionally private social occasions not open to
all; this falls within the experimental scope of the project. In addition, entrance
to space as well as booking the space will be free of charge.
In order to cover part of the maintenance costs, a DIY operating scheme has to
be adopted. Groups that use the Warehouse will have to be responsible for the
facilities from beginning to end and carry out tasks such as cleaning the space. A
caf´e/bar run by the organizers of the event during the various events or parties is
another source of revenue for the ﬁnancial sustainability of Døgn Space. Additional
ﬁnancial support could be acquired by funds from public and private entities; all
of the interview cases, which share several goals with this project, are partly
supported by such.
It is decided to repurpose the already existing warehouse of the lot,which will
signiﬁcantly reduce the project cost compared to a new construction. The current
building is 100 m2, with a ceiling height ranging from 3,2 to 3,8 m, according to the
3D CAD building models of the municipality. In order to increase the ﬂoor plan,
a glass extension is proposed which can serve as a foyer or additional functional
space for the Warehouse. The approach of the Warehouse, in terms of aesthetics,
as an alternative space,similar to KPH Volume (presented in the literature review),
will also reduce costs as the needed interventions will be minimized.

Landscape Design
The landscape design began with identifying the human movement in terms of
how and why people navigate the park. Døgn Space’s surroundings point to a
homogeneous proﬁle of travelers: vertical ﬂow is not possible due to the S-Tog
tracks, while horizontal ﬂow consists of people arriving from Mimersparken, i.e.
people enjoying leisure, whose travel is not characterized by urgency. Commuters,
people in transit and residents of the wider area are more likely to be serviced by
the adjacent street network (Rovsingsgade and Tagensvej in regards to horizontal
and vertical ﬂow, respectively), rather than taking a detour through the park,
which means that human movement occurs mostly from people visiting the space
to use it. This gave relative freedom to the design by not having to separate the
ﬂows within the park to avoid the clash of diﬀerent categories.
Similar patterns to Mimersparken were chosen, for a natural transition between the
two urban spaces in terms of aesthetics. A curved path of varying widths, including
a bi-directional bicycle path, extends throughout the park; in its centre, it forms
the axis where ﬁve Domes are placed on each side. Their spatial arrangement aims
to reproduce the atmosphere and movement pace of a lively pedestrian street with
bars and cafes 4.35.

For the successful operation of the Warehouse, an organization similar to the case
of Kulturhavn365 is needed, in order to:
• Curate the content: Even though the Warehouse aims to be available to all
citizens, it goes without saying that not all events can be endorsed. Through
Døgn Space’s online platform, citizens can apply to use the Warehouse with

responsible for reviewing them will ensure that no hateful content or political
agendas are promoted in these projects.
• Ensure the engagement of the community: While the events of the
Warehouse will be free of charge and accessible to all, this does not guar-
antee the active involvement of a diverse group of people. Similarly to HF
Lersøgrøftens, the weekly schedule of the Warehouse will be equally divided
into use by native Danish citizens and non Danish citizens. The organization
will also promote the cultural events through Social Media and Døgn Space’s
website, as well as amplifying the visibility of the space to the neighbourhood
in its initial stages.
• Facilitate the events’ realization: The organization will help the cul-
tural events’ organizers with logistics and the communication with diﬀerent
municipality departments, if needed.

4.3
Eﬀects
In the Eﬀects phase, the anticipated positive and negative impacts of the imple-
mented project are analyzed for each geographic scale as well as weaknesses of
the design that may lead to the failure of the park. Adaptation and monitoring
strategies that address the above are proposed.
4.3.1
Area
The Area scale presents the biggest challenges in terms of negative impacts. Døgn
Space is a space of night time activity which may result to noise pollution of
the immediate surroundings. Even though an architectural acoustics design of
the Warehouse and an environmental acoustics design of the Domes’ placement
can eliminate noise pollution due to music or other social activities, the problems
caused by increased human movement on a 24 hour basis might be detrimental to
the acceptance from the residents of the houses in close proximity. For example,
Rovsingsgade, where a lot of residential buildings can be found, is part of a night
bus route, with a bus stop close to Tagensvej, which can concentrate noise in the
evening. In addition, other social and environmental problems related to night
life and alcohol consumption such as litter and altercations have to be considered.
These issues may cause friction not only with the residents but also with the mixed
industry area on the south. It is common for businesses such as warehouses to
operate on early morning hours, which may be disrupted by people departing from
the space.
That being said, the functions of the space and the issues that stem from them are
not diﬀerent than the ones that can be found on the neighbourhoods of Nørrebro
featuring commercial night spaces. Even though the district lacks in places of
quietness, the Inventory phase attests that it is still one of the most attractive in
==PAGE:99==
88
the city for residents and the night life transpires harmoniously with the day life.
In terms of safety, the park’s popularity will deﬁne the outcome in this aspect.
For example, an empty park with just one Dome being used during the night can
be attractive to thieves. Whether the park’s function constitutes a Legitimate
Activity Support remains to be seen after implementation.Vandalism of the box
equipment is also a consideration; however, equipment of equal value exists in
public spaces throughout the city (for example screens at bus stops), so this is not
a major negative impact to be expected.
A positive impact is the shared vision of Døgn Space and HF Lersøfrøften, which
will provide visibility to the latter and together constitute a joint eﬀort of social
cohesion.
4.3.2
Neighbourhood
On the neighbourhood scale, the strongest positive impacts can be identiﬁed when
viewing Døgn Space incorporated into the general redevelopment goals of the
neighbourhood. Improving the quality of the urban fabric will generate human
movement in the neighbourhood that will have a positive impact for its safety, in
addition to creating fertile ground for local businesses to develop. Another positive
impact is the opportunities for social integration in the highly diverse neighbour-
hood with the functions of the Warehouse.
Again, these positive impacts depend on the popularity of the park. The Domes
being barely used during the night may expand the zone of high crime to Døgn
Space and exacerbate the safety issues in the neighbourhood, which consequently
will cause the threshold capacity for policing of the neighbourhood to be exceeded.
4.3.3
District
On the district scale, only positive impacts are identiﬁed. Nørrebro gains a new
green space, and a new meeting place and night life option is oﬀered to the Bis-
pebjerg residents.
==PAGE:100==

4.3.4
City
Regarding positive impacts, Døgn Space addresses several of the goals stated in
the municipal strategies for the city of Copenhagen, as well as needs of its citizens
that were identiﬁed in the interviews and questionnaire. It is an experimental
project that will add an edge to the urban landscape and its success may enhance
the city’s branding as one of the most livable cities in terms of urban life. Døgn
Space’s unique concept has the potential to attract citizens from all over Copen-
hagen who will interact with the local community, which is a necessity for the
Vulnerable Areas and the social cohesion of the city. Furthermore, it provides the
citizens of Copenhagen with new open spaces and increases the green spaces of
the city. The Warehouse oﬀers new ground for the public to occupy city spaces
and a platform for emerging artists that will strengthen the cultural capital of the
city. The Domes can accommodate night life entertainment free of charge, which
is the prime restriction in enjoying urban night life.
However, it is possible that the Dome proposition will not be popular among
Copenhageners. Cultural habits regarding night life are quite limited to traditional
urban spaces of entertainment (bars, nightclubs) and it is uncertain whether the
Domes will manage to capture the appeal and social environment of those spaces.
Another issue on a municipal level will be the ﬁnancial viability of the project.
With few revenue from the park’s activities, Døgn Space may prove to be ﬁ-
nancially unsustainable. That is not to say that public spaces are proﬁt-driven;
however, it may be decided that providing a public space for night life may not be
in the municipality’s priorities.

4.3.5
Strategies
Reviewing the estimated impacts, it is decided to develop three strategies in re-
lation to implementation time: Before, During and After implementation. It is
essential to carefully plan all three strategies beforehand, since measures involved
in each stage are interdependent.
Before Implementation
Before implementing the project, it is important to investigate its reception by the
citizens, especially on a neighbourhood scale, which will be the most aﬀected by
the project. This can be achieved with a Land Use Conﬂict survey. The survey
can be carried out with community appraisals, making sure that all the diﬀerent
stakeholders (varied sample of local residents, businesses) of the area are involved.
Moreover, in case multiple locations throughout the city are considered, a Land
Use Conﬂict survey through a participatory mapping process that would be per-
formed on a city level could point to the location with the least potential for
conﬂict.
During Implementation
The main strategy to be followed during implementation that will maximize the
potential of Døgn Space is actively engaging the citizens in the design. This en-
gagement involves processes like the ones presented in 2.2.1, such as workshops,
or ICT tools for visualizing the landscape design of the park or the interior design
of the Domes.
After Implementation
With the completion of the test period for Døgn Space, an evaluation of its success
will determine the strategy to be followed.
In case the Domes prove to be unused or their use will result in the aforemen-
tioned safety and community friction issues, their repurposing is chosen as an
adaptation strategy. In this case, the interior design elements and the box are
removed, in order to discontinue their night use and two new functions that can
be accommodated in the Domes are proposed:
• Greenhouses: The choice of ETFE as a covering allows the space to be
easily modiﬁed into a greenhouse. The Domes zone can then be granted to
Lersøgrøftens for more land of urban farming, or turned into small gardens
of various ﬂowers and plants. With the latter solution, and combined with
the biodeversity elements of the lake and the green facades, Døgn Space can
turn into a nature park.
==PAGE:103==
92
• Kiosks: the Domes’ arrangement allows them to be used for festivals as a
place for stands or citizen organized ﬂea markets. These social events, which
are popular in Copenhagen during the summer time, could be extended into
the winter time, being hosted under the Domes.
In the case of vandalism of the boxes, an adaptation strategy is modifying the open
box into a safety one, drawing inspiration from the OurHub case. 24 hour access
to the box could be gained with the development of a Døgn Space app, where one
can register with their CPR number and credit card to the system. Even though
this would potentially limit their use,it would remain free, ensuring the protection
of the equipment. Lastly, in case the project proves to be ﬁnancially unsustain-
able, the Domes’ lightweight materials allow them to be easily transported away,
so that the area can be redeveloped.
Regarding the Warehouse, in the case of the project not succeeding in its social
mixing goal, no realistic strategy has been formulated. Instilling agency to people
of various backgrounds to occupy urban spaces and socialize with native citizens
is a complex issue that is hard to address with a concrete strategy. The Ware-
house can continue to be used from the users that have beneﬁted from it with the
completion of the test period.

5.3.2
Fire Safety
It goes without saying that the Hubs have to be adequately equipped in case of a
ﬁre, so as to ensure that lives will not be endangered and the ﬁre will not spread to
adjacent buildings or vegetation. The Hubs could potentially be crowded in cases
of harsh weather conditions and a clear evacuation plan is necessary. However,
given that they are a single storey construction with a simple layout and clear
entrance and exit points which lead directly outside, this is not a complex task.
According to the Danish Building Regulations The Danish Ministry of Economic
and Business Aﬀairs [2010], the building belongs to Usage Category 3: ”building
sections for day time occupancy by large numbers of people, in which the people
who occupy the building section are not necessarily familiar with the escape routes
from the building section but are capable of taking themselves unaided to a place
of safety”. Throughout the regulations, a threshold of 150 people is used to deﬁne
the necessary provisions. In the day time period, when the City Lab will be open,
it is deemed possible that the total number of occupants will be greater than 150
people. Thus, the ﬁre safety considerations are chosen accordingly and are listed
below:
• Automatic Fire Alarm System
• Automatic Warning System
• Smoke alarm system
• Hose Reels
• Panic Lighting
An Automatic Fire Ventilation System or Automatic Sprinkler System is not in-
stalled, since for buildings of Usage Category 3 it is required only for ﬂoor areas
greater than 1000 m2.

Chapter 6
Future Work-Discussion
The proposals for public spaces that address needs of urban life in the winter and
night time presented on this master’s thesis are developed on a conceptual level.
The fulﬁllment of the design principles, the future work needed in each case, as
well as the limitations and conclusion of the project are discussed below.
Regarding the winter space, the Winter City Hubs covered most of the design
principles set in the Framework, some more obviously than others. The Comfort
Zone’s function as a temporary shelter during harsh weather conditions addresses
the mobility issues during the winter, and their function as an information centre
enhances the visibility of the already existing indoors spaces that can host ur-
ban life and cultural events during the winter. A design element which was not
directly addressed by the Hubs with a concrete proposal is a social interaction trig-
ger; settings that lead to social interactions in the city are elusive and attempting
to reproduce them with a design element of a space was an important limitation
of the project. However, it could be assumed that the communal experience of
seeking refuge from the cold in the Comfort Zone could be that trigger.
The City Lab aims to serve as a new institution, a physical space where the dif-
ferent stakeholders involved in the development of the urban spaces can meet,
which will facilitate the collaboration between the public and the private sector
in these processes. Enhancing public participation in urban planning processes is
addressed by the symbiosis of the two zones of the Winter City Hubs. By using the
everyday function of the Comfort Zones, people will be exposed to the activities of
the City Lab, which will raise awareness of these processes and has the potential
to get citizens to participate. The environmentally sustainable design is the design
principle least explored in the project. Deﬁning the Comfort Zones as a new space
106
==PAGE:118==
107
typology that is allowed to operate on less strict indoor climate conditions and
possibly only with a passive design contributes to the environmental sustainability
of the Hubs; the complexity of the task did not make it possible to examine its
feasibility within the thesis’ time frame. The HVAC design of the Hubs, and the
cost estimation of the proposal, are the two main areas of future work for the
winter spaces.
An important weakness in the proposal,identiﬁed late in the process, is the func-
tionality of the chosen locations in relation to the goals of the aforementioned
space symbiosis. Hubs were placed in several Vulnerable Areas where urban de-
velopment will occur in the following years. As it was explained before, it is desired
for local residents to use the Comfort Zones in order to come into contact with
the functions of the City Labs, but since their residence is in a close radius to
the Hubs it is unlikely that they will do so. Commuters, people in transit and
those who are away from their residences will beneﬁt from the Comfort Zones of
a location, but are not the target group of the speciﬁc City Lab. It is essential
to focus on the promotion of the current activities in each Hub between the Hubs
network, so that people may be informed about an activity from a diﬀerent Hub
than their local one.
Regarding night public spaces, all of the design principles were addressed in the
design proposal. Given the experimental nature of the project and the fact that
many of the design principles are dependent on its positive reception by the citi-
zens, the only principles surely fulﬁlled are the ﬂexibility of the proposal expressed
in the strategies, and the provision of more green spaces. The speciﬁcity of the
urban night life explained in the literature review, with the diﬀerent ”rules” and
the element of the unexpected that characterize it, ran through the entire process
of the project and is the most prevalent limitation of attempting to design such
a space. Furthermore, the urban night life is not the only unpredictable concept
involved in the proposal: achieving social integration and the mixing of diﬀerent
social groups is another multidimensional issue whose outcome is hard to predict.
The low participation of Non Danish citizens in the urban farming project of the
area is not a positive sign. However, as it was mentioned in the literature review,
the conviviality of urban night spaces has the power to strengthen the social cap-
ital of a city and has the potential to bring positive results in this aspect.
Another limitation of the night space proposal is the fact that it focused only on
the aspect of night life entertainment and socialization, even though the literature
review showed that the 24 hour city involves other users of urban spaces as well,
==PAGE:119==
108
such as night shift workers and commuters. The entertainment is the most preva-
lent human activity during night time though, and an initial eﬀort to conceive
public space interventions that would accommodate the aforementioned users was
not fruitful. It is also evident that the night time proposal is not so separate to
the winter time in terms of design considerations, as the future work of the project
includes an optimal design of the Domes that will maximize their seasonal use.
Achieving an aesthetically pleasing interior design for the Domes is also the biggest
future challenge for the project.
Returning to the problem statement of the thesis, it has not been possible to quan-
tify the contribution of night and winter public spaces to Social Sustainability. Es-
pecially for the night public space, the complex nature of both urban night time
and social integration did not lead to the development of eﬃcient indicators that
would quantify the value of the design proposal, as the same design elements that
respond to the design principles could have either positive and negative impacts;
the same could be said about the uniqueness of the new public space typology that
resulted from the winter space design proposals. These design elements, though,
align with the deﬁnition of Social Sustainability and the goals of the New Urban
Agenda regarding public spaces, stated in the Literature Review. Døgn Space is
designed to be a safe, inclusive place of cultural expression that encourages social
integration, while the Winter City Hubs aim to enhance the quality of life by fa-
cilitating mobility and lead to a more inclusive and socially participatory society.
Concluding, the unlocked potential of winter and night public spaces regarding
Social Sustainability was located in utilizing the elements that make these time
frames unique: taking advantage of the need to protect oneself from the cold to
enhance visibility and awareness to a participatory process, and combining the
basic human need for night festivities with opportunities for social integration.
