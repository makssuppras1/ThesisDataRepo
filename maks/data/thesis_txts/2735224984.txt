Abstract
A collaboration with the National Gallery of Denmark (SMK), this thesis delved into the is-
sue of lead white darkening in old master drawings. Despite being a well-known phenomenon
in conservation, the understanding of the underlying mechanisms of this degradation is still
limited. The goal of the current research was to explore which combinations of factors most
affected the stability of lead white paint.
The primary objective was to investigate the
influence of temperature and relative humidity in environments with or without hydrogen
sulfide. Additionally, the study addressed the effectiveness of a commercially available coat-
ing (Klucel G) for the protection of the drawings. The research uncovered that the darkening
was triggered by the combined action between high relative humidity and hydrogen sulfide.
Furthermore, contrary to expectations, Klucel G failed to provide adequate protection in
this context, contributing instead to an even heavier darkening effect. The current research
involved accelerated aging and a number of characterization methods, including: camera
imaging, microscopy, color analysis, and SEM imaging; moreover, attempted FTIR and pH
measurements were employed. The outcomes of the current project highlight the complexity
of the issue, and there are some challenges yet to be investigated.

Introduction
1.1
Problem statement
Old masters drawings are works of immeasurable value. The National Gallery of Denmark
(SMK) archive hosts a collection of 245.000 drawings on paper, tracing back all the way
to the 16th century, and with their provenance spanning many European countries, such as
the Netherlands, Germany, and Italy, together with Danish art. This tremendous heritage is
currently exposed to lead white darkening, a degradation process that is not fully understood
yet. Namely, highlights made of lead white paint have begun to darken rapidly since the
early 2000s. This phenomenon affected hundreds of drawings in the collection and is still
ongoing.
Degradation of lead white paint is a well-known phenomenon in conservation [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6,
7, 8], but the short period of change observed in the archive is anomalous, hence, the need of
research on its causes is especially compelling. Also, only some of the drawings containing
lead white details report this type of damage (approximately half of them), making the
understanding of what triggers the darkening particularly challenging.
In this framework, the current project has two main scopes.
• The first is to investigate the causes that led to such a relatively fast degradation. In
particular, this project focuses on investigating the effect of temperature and relative
humidity on the stability of lead white, both independently and when combined, in
the presence of hydrogen sulfide.
• The second is to determine whether it is possible to preserve the undamaged drawings
using commercially available protective coatings
Finally, it should be noted that this project is not limited to the National Gallery of Denmark,
but uses the museum’s experience as a case study.

2.1
Lead white paint and its darkening
The current project revolves around the degradation of lead white paint. Thus, a description
of lead white paint must be the starting point of this review.
This section contains an
introduction to the material, its basic properties, the safety concerns, and the composition
of the paint that is going to be used in this project.
Then, some notable examples of
lead white darkening in literature are displayed. Finally, this chapter presents five chemical
reactions which theorize the degradation of lead white.
2.1.1
Lead white paint composition
Lead white paint is a paint that has lead white as its main pigment. Lead white is a thick,
opaque, and heavy white pigment [9] that was particularly popular in art production before
the 19th century. It is usually made of basic lead carbonate 2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2, typically
mixed with a smaller part of normal lead carbonate PbCO3.
As with every paint, the pigment is held together by the binder, and mixed with fillers.
The binder is a medium that holds the particles together and guarantees adhesion to the
substrate. Different kinds of binders were used historically in the production of lead white
paint: gum arabic, animal glues, or oil (linseed or other drying oils). Finally, the fillers
are materials that are cheaper than lead white, often mixed in the paint for convenience,
to provide opacity, adjust the rheology, and guarantee a less expensive end product. The
specific recipes of lead white paint depend on when, where, and how it was manufactured.
Given its affordability and distinctive visual qualities, lead white was particularly favored and
generously used by artists in their paintings [10]. However, these days, lead white has been
replaced by other white pigments, due to safety regulations. Systems such as the National
Fire Protection Association (NFPA) and Hazardous Materials Identification System (HMIS)
assigned lead white a health rating of 2: moderate hazard if ingested, inhaled, in contact
with skin, or with intense or continued exposure.

2.1.3
Degradation pathways of lead white
Lead white darkening is a compelling issue in art conservation, and its mechanisms are yet to
be fully discovered. The current project is aimed at unveiling some parts of this conundrum.
However, before any hypothesis is developed, the degradation pathways of lead white must be
taken into account. This section reports relevant chemical reactions, dividing the pathways
for normal (PbCO3) and basic (2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2) lead white - since lead white paint
contains both. Some of the presented reactions match the findings of the literature studies
presented in Section 2.1.2. Also, Sections 2.2 and 2.6 will confirm their relevance.
Note that this section is purely theoretical, while Sections 2.3, 2.5 and 2.4, further in the
review, analyze the practical aspects and evidence of degradation.
Degradation to galena
Two fundamental reactions are those transforming lead white to galena, or lead(II) sulfide,
a black compound with chemical formula PbS.
Lead(II) carbonate reacts with hydrogen sulfide to produce lead(II) sulfide, carbon dioxide,
and water. According to [15], this reaction takes place at a temperature near 60◦C.
PbCO3 + H2S →PbS + H2O + CO2
(1)
The reaction for the degradation of basic lead carbonate PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2 to galena PbS
(black) is reported in [1].
2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2 + 2H2S →2PbS + 2CO2 + 6H2O
(2)
Degradation to anglesite
Degradation of basic lead carbonate (PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2) to anglesite, or, lead(II) sulfate
(PbSO4) is an extension of the degradation phenomenon in equations 1 and 2. Here, lead
sulfide (PbS) converts into lead sulfate (PbSO4) through an oxidation reaction. This reaction
needs oxygen (O2) and an oxidizing agent to happen. It is a redox, meaning that sulfur in
PbS oxidizes from sulfide to sulfate (S2−to SO2−
4 ). Lead(II) sulfate is a white compound.
PbS + 4O2 →PbSO4
(3)
Degradation to lead(II) hydroxide
Degradation of basic lead carbonate (PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2) to lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2) is a
result of the exposition of lead white to humidity. Lead hydroxide is white.
2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2 + H2O →3Pb(OH)2 + CO2
(4)
Degradation to lead oxide
Another reaction that was considered was the thermal decomposition of lead(II) carbonate
to produce oxide lead(II) and carbon dioxide. Nevertheless, this reaction takes place at a

2.2
Case study: lead white darkening at SMK
The first half of this section describes the "LeadMad project", the ongoing work led by the
SMK research group, in an effort to delve into the quick darkening of lead white paint in
old masters drawings. The findings of the research are listed. Also, the complexity that is
embedded with a comprehensive study is presented.
The second half of this section is a brief description of the SMK archives, their regulations,
and the issues linked to an in-depth screening of the environmental conditions. Finally, the
section distinguishes and lists the available and unavailable data from SMK.
2.2.1
The LeadMad project
Project description
In 2013, quick lead white darkening was discovered in drawings in the SMK collection (e.g.
Figure 6, more examples in [17]), and an investigation on its causes was launched. The
project, called "the LeadMad project", it is still in progress at the time of writing, and
carried out by SMK in collaboration with other EU research institutions.
Figure 6: Detail from Niclas Lafrensen II. (1737-1807). "To galante par i en have, det ene
liggende, det andet stående." Gouache drawing. SMK Open online collection. The lead
white highlights on the face of the seated woman have blackened, as indicated by the arrow.
The aim of the LeadMad project was to classify the darkened paint according to several
parameters, such as compositional change, provenance, and production methods of lead

white pigments, as well as the environmental factors that contribute to their discoloration
on drawings [18]. In its first phase, around 50 drawings were analyzed, using a variety of an-

Key findings
The outcomes of the LeadMad project that are relevant to the current thesis can be divided
into the following categories:
• Sulfur detection and its plausible role in the degradation of lead white
→Crystallographic analyses lead to the identification of black galena (PbS) in most of
the darkened parts, suggesting that the discoloration might be due to the interaction
with atmospheric sulfur compounds. Anglesite (PbSO4) was also found. Darkening
of lead white due to sulfur is an extensively reported phenomenon, as mentioned in
previous Section 2.1.2, and as will be explored in Section 2.3. To further reinforce this
hypothesis, traces of sulfur were detected in the SMK archives, as will be reported
later in this section.
• Appearance of the degradation
→It was observed that the damage in drawings is more prominent along the edges of
the artworks, rather than at the center. There are no hypotheses currently explaining
this phenomenon.
• Characterization of lead white pigments coming from different times and places
→Paints produced after the 19th century are different in composition and particle
morphology to those produced before. First, they have a higher percentage of basic lead
carbonate. Second, the pigment is dispersed as small, uniform particles - whereas older
paints host a distribution of both large and small particles together. Such "modern",
or "industrially-produced" paints may be more prone to darkening.
• Monitoring of storage conditions and cooperating effects
→Monitoring of storage conditions, which play a crucial role in the darkening process,
suggest relative humidity and concentrations of airborne pollutants as key factors [18].
However, a thorough screening of storage conditions is not a trivial task, as will be
explained in Section 2.2.2.

Description of SMK archives and linked issues
Due to non-disclosure agreements, this section is purposefully left generic for what concerns
the rooms’ positioning. However, a description of the SMK archive system is needed as
background knowledge for the current thesis work.
The SMK archives are divided into three main areas, with varying ventilation systems. Two
of them are connected with the rooms above, which host SMK’s exhibitions.
⇒This is thought to be a source of pollution, as it could result in pollutants (VOCs
from makeup and clothing, odors, dust, particulate such as shedding skin cells, etc.)
blowing into the archives when the museum is very crowded. Also, it is known that
people produce reduced volatile sulfur compounds by a variety of mechanisms, such as
bowel activity and microbial microorganisms in the mouth, which could then also be
blown in the archives [19]. However, this hypothesis has not been tested.
All these ventilation systems have changed over time, in the gallery’s attempt to promote
consistent ventilation and stable values of temperature and relative humidity. Nonetheless,
some damages caused by the past inadequate environmental conditions are visible, for in-
stance, in rust marks on the old boxes, or on the deterioration of the paint on walls. For
instance, one painted wall shows flaking, which might be linked to low humidity - if venti-
lation is excessive, it might dry out a room too much, hence humidity may be drawn from
humid walls.
⇒Rusting and wall deterioration are not the only problems associated with a poor venti-
lation system. It is reported that a restricted airflow can create uneven micro-climates
within a room, with thermal stratification as well as accumulation of indoor generated
pollutants, which may accelerate the deterioration of the collections [20]. Were that the
case before the HVAC was renewed, then there might have been a higher concentration
of pollutants in some points of the archive.
The storage architecture consists of custom-made shelves made of wood (painted chipboards)
and metal bars. The drawings are kept in the so-called "solander boxes", in various quali-
ties. The oldest boxes are made of grey bookbinding cardboard, and covered with various
bookbinding canvas; the newest boxes are acid-free. A variety of mats/cardboards are found
for mounting the drawings, as it is a large old collection.
⇒All these materials, together with wooden furniture, wooden frames, other types of
works of art, the boxes themselves, pigments from other drawings, etc., can outgas
substances (as will be explained in Section 2.5). If this is associated with an uneven
flow (see: previous point), accumulations of pollutants could form.
The environmental conditions are currently regulated according to two guidelines.
The
"incoming art" exhibition space’s parameters are RH = 50% ± 5% and T = 20◦C ± 2◦C.

Hydrogen sulfide is a chemical compound with the formula H2S. It is a colorless gas, and
is poisonous, corrosive, and flammable, with trace amounts in ambient atmosphere having a

Carlyle and Townsend [8] reported how the degradation of lead white pigment due to atmo-
spheric elements, such as sulfur, was perceived already in the 18th century. The article also
mentioned later studies from the 19th century, which linked the amount of lead hydroxide in
the paint and reactivity towards sulfur vapors. Furthermore, Carlyle and Townsend propose
that degraded color in Turner paintings (19th century) could be due to lead sulfide darkening,
as the painter employed lead white as a primer [22].
Clark and Gibbs [6] employed Raman microscopy on degraded areas of 13th century illustra-
tions. Surfaces that were originally colored with a mix of white and other pigments turned
black and is more intense around the edges. An example is reported in Figure 1 The analysis
revealed that such darkening was due to the degradation of lead white to PbS. The sources
of sulfur that were identified are pollution of the city and sulfur-containing pigments.
It should be underlined that the behavior of the two components of lead white (basic and
normal lead white) is reported to be different in the presence of sulfur. For instance, in the
aforementioned study by Smith and Clark [4], PbCO3 showed only a partial transformation
to galena after 5 days of exposure to H2S, while basic lead white blackened rapidly and com-
pletely. The article justified this phenomenon citing [8], which suggests that the reactivity
of lead white is influenced by the basic component of the crystal. In other words, the more
Pb(OH)2 that is incorporated into the crystal, the more likely the pigment is to darken.
This claim is justifiable chemically since, as an acid, H2S should react more vigorously with
the lead hydroxide moiety than with the less basic carbonate species [4]. Finally, Equation 4
explored how the degradation pathway leading to Pb(OH)2 is encouraged by high humidity.
For convenience, the equation is:
2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2 + H2O →3Pb(OH)2 + CO2

2.4.1
T and RH definition and interconnection
Temperature (T) and relative humidity (RH) are environmental parameters of crucial im-
portance, as well as deeply interconnected. In fact, a change in temperature will also result
in a change in RH [20], as warmer air can hold more moisture than colder air. To understand
why, at equal levels of absolute humidity, an increase in temperature leads to a decrease in
relative humidity, a short explanation is needed. Absolute humidity (AH) measures the mass
of H2O vapor per volume (g/m3).

Although intuitive, this parameter is limited, as it does not take
temperature changes into account. If different temperatures need to be considered, relative
humidity (RH) is used, which is defined as the ratio between the absolute humidity of the

2.4.2
Cooperating effects of parameters on the artworks
Both temperature and humidity can heavily affect materials, from several points of view:
• Dimensional changes. Varying temperatures could result in residual stresses linked to
the expansion and contraction of materials, which are often associated with cracking
or delamination. In the same way, moisture can be absorbed and released, leading to
swelling and dimensional alterations.
• Biological activity. Low temperatures and high humidity can promote biological ac-
tivity, such as mold or fungi growth.
• Chemical reactions. Higher temperatures promote chemical reactions and degradation,
as well as higher humidity promotes corrosion.
All these considerations are valid in general. Focusing on the world of art conservation, the
impact of T and RH is twofold:
• Fluctuations in such parameters can affect the paint, both in terms of binder and pig-

2.5
Degradation of lead white due to VOCs and paper acidity
This section concerns the degradation of paper, an issue of great complication. Strlič et
al. [27] state how "the complexity of heritage objects is to an extent comparable to the
complexity of living organisms". Such intricate nature is due to multiple factors, to list
some: the limited understanding of aging processes, the numerous materials involved, and
the many physical and chemical causes that concur to cause the degradation.
A major
difficulty in its understanding lies in the vicious circles of reactions that will be explored
in this section. Paper degradation triggers further paper degradation, compromising the
distinction between causes and effects.
The notion of paper decay is tightly linked to acids and acidity. First, there is acidity as an
intrinsic property of many paper media, and how this is related to their degradation. Then,
there are the organic acids (VOCs) released when paper degrades, that then cause further
degradation. This article presents both, discusses how they are linked, and introduces a
hypothesis connecting acidity to the degradation of lead white.
2.5.1
Paper pH
The concept of paper acidity will be the core of the current section.
However, a short
technical explanation is needed, to extend the definitions of "acidity" and "pH", usually
referred to liquid solutions, to the field of paper conservation.
Baty et al. [28] defined paper pH in a way that is similar to traditional pH. In fact, ac-
cording to Arrhenius, acids and bases are substances introducing protons or hydroxide ions
into aqueous solutions. Paper does not fit the criteria of an aqueous solution, being instead
a network of hydrophilic, insoluble fibers with absorbed water molecules. Despite this dif-
ference, a useful concept of acidic or alkaline papers has emerged, based on the acidity or
basicity of water extracts [28]. There are two methods to measure such extracts: paper pH
and surface pH. The first macerates paper in excess water, thus it is destructive, and the
second is a surface measurement.
Surface pH measurements will be taken in the current thesis following the directions in
Section 5.1.7.

2.5.2
Paper embrittlement, acidity and moisture
In their review article, Baty et al. [28] underlined the link between the paper’s tendency
to embrittlement and its acidity.
The main cause of paper acidity was identified in the
acidic conditions under which it was manufactured. The majority of paper produced be-
tween approx. 1820 and 1990 would register as acidic due to the additives involved in the
production. Such paper has been shown to degrade more rapidly if stored at higher humidity
and temperature, leading to more rapid chemical breakdown and an increased likelihood of
breakage [28]. Hence, the extent of acidity within a given volume will also be affected by the
conditions of storage (temperature, relative humidity (RH), atmospheric pollutants, quality
of shelving and boxing materials, etc.) [28]. Furthermore, it can be stated that the acidity
of paper is both a measure of the accumulation of acids, because of degradation, and the
result of acids introduced into the material during sizing [27].
Other articles delved into the topic of paper decay and acidity. Zou et al. [29] conducted
accelerated aging of papers of pure cellulose (cotton) under different conditions. They con-
cluded that the predominant mechanism of degradation was acid hydrolysis, in agreement
with [28]. Also, their paper did not degrade under dry air at 90◦C, and it was hypothesized
that this temperature was too low, as thermal degradation of pure cellulose occurs above
140◦C.
Also, Karlovitz and Gregor-Svetec [30] studied the response of different kinds of paper under
accelerated aging (moist-heat at 80◦C and 65% RH, dry-heat 105◦C and light exposure).
The study reported brightness loss and yellowing that was especially pronounced for cellulose
paper and dry-heat treatment. Paper yellowing is a natural process of paper aging, which
is caused by sunlight, moisture, and air [30]. The loss of brightness and paper yellowing
during the aging procedures is attributed to the presence of chromophores formed by the
degradation of paper components (cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin) [31].
In summary, there is a close link between paper acidity, its embrittlement, and the environ-
mental conditions, especially in terms of moisture.
2.5.3
Volatile Organic Compounds
Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) are carbon-based molecules often present as indoor
contaminants. They are usually found in museum archives due to the off-gassing of many
elements: wood- or cellulose-based materials, adhesives, paints. The prevalence of VOCs in
literature consists of acetic acid, formaldehyde, formic acid, and furfural; nevertheless, there
are more, as listed in [32, 33].
Organic acids are among the most critical indoor air pollutants causing damage to heritage
collections [20], or accelerating existing processes. An important concept is that VOCs have
a twofold role: they are both the result of the degradation of the materials in the archive,
and a factor that encourages further degradation. Thus, cellulose-based materials exposed
to VOCs will go through a chain reaction of degradation, more VOCs emitted, additional
damage, and so on. This is explained and quantified in [32, 33]. This information is relevant,
Page 18 of 56
==PAGE:31==
2.5
Degradation of lead white due to VOCs and paper acidity
as the current project is concerned with paint-on-paper specimens, hence the role of paper
cannot be excluded.
Section 2.5.2 defined the acidity of paper. Now, one very important concept is that papers
that are more acidic generally emit more VOCs. Some of those VOCs are the acids them-
selves and some of those VOCs are those that are produced in paper during the process of
degradation, which is accelerated by acidity.[34] This is stated in [27], which found a cor-
relation between low pH and VOCs emission, such as acetic acid, furfural, and compounds
associated with rosin content.
Just as paper decay, VOC emissions are highly interlinked to environmental parameters.
For instance, Smedemark et al.
[24] showed that the temperature-dependent changes of
formic acid and acetic acid levels during the seasons correspond to the changes in off-gassing
from the six paper samples determined at laboratory conditions. The study indicated that
reducing the temperature in storage facilities of libraries and archival collections will reduce
the off-gassing of organic acids from paper and by this their concentration in air.
The
same article mentioned how, generally, the rate of chemical degradation of organic materials
follows an Arrhenius style function, in which a 5◦C temperature increase roughly halves the
lifespan of a stored object [25]. Also, it stated that decreasing the relative humidity will
reduce the amount of water available for the hydrolytic degradation of paper and thereby
the production of degradation products such as formic acid and acetic acid.
The hypothesis of VOC-borne degradation on the SMK collection is corroborated by mea-
surements taken in the 2010s in different SMK storage rooms. The sensors recorded notable
amounts of acetic acid, formaldehyde, and formic acid, reported in the tables in Appendix
B.
The experimental part of the current thesis did not delve into the quantification of released
VOCs due to the absence of sensors for their detection. However, as mentioned in Sec-
tion 2.5.3, paper acidity and release of VOC were correlated before in literature. Hence, a
measurement of the paper pH might be able to give a qualitative idea of VOC release.

2.6
Additional hypotheses for degradation of lead white
This section presents hypotheses that could take part in the degradation process. Nonethe-

2.6.1
Light exposure
Light can be a contributing factor for chemical reactions, and degradation of artworks due
to exposure to light is extensively reported. However, this hypothesis was disregarded in the
framework of the current project for a series of reasons. First of all, the drawings’ exposure to
light is minimal: they are kept in solander boxes (opaque), which are in turn kept in storage
environments, where the light is off most of the time. Also, during exhibitions, drawings are
illuminated by halogen spots and no natural light.
Halogen bulbs mostly emit light in the range of visible light (400-700 nm). Compared to UV
light (100-400 nm), often associated with polymer chain scission and material degradation,
visible light has lower energy.
This is due to Planck’s law, stating that the wavelength
of radiation is inversely proportional to its energy (E = hν = hc/λ). Halogen lights are
reported to emit in the UV range [36], nevertheless, such bulbs are placed meters away
from the drawings. The received irradiance varies with distance r as 1/r2, hence it will be
considered irrelevant for the scope of this project.
In addition, multiple sources [9, 23, 37] claim that lead white is not affected by light.
2.6.2

Radon is a radioactive gas.
It occurs naturally in minute quantities as an intermediate
step in the normal radioactive decay chains through which thorium and uranium slowly

is chemically inert to most common reactions, due to its full outer electron shell. Radon is

2.6.3
Biological activity
Microbial diagenesis is a known phenomenon in conservation. For instance, it can convert
lead white to galena on murals [41], but also, binding media in artwork are reported to
provide the requisite nutrients for bacterial growth [4]. The transformation [to galena] is
linked to bacteria that digest sulfur-containing amino acids, thereby generating H2S as a
metabolic byproduct.
While biological activity holds relevance in the broader context of lead white darkening, it
has been excluded from the scope of this thesis as it would introduce a too high level of
complexity for the thesis’ scope.
2.6.4
Degradation to lead dioxide
In mural painting, the blackening has been often related to the transformation of lead car-
bonate into black lead dioxide PbO2 (plattnerite), as stated by Vagnini et al. [42]. They also
affirmed that, due to its high value of redox potential, the oxidation of lead(II) to lead(IV)
can occur in normal conditions only in the presence of very strong oxidizing agents, and can
be facilitated in an alkaline environment.
This is an example of lead darkening which is not linked to sulfur. Nevertheless, from the
literature, this seems to be unlikely and mostly linked to frescos. For this reason, it was
disregarded.
2.7
Conservation treatments and existing solutions against darken-
ing
Conservators face a dilemma in balancing the preservation of an object’s historical look and
ensuring its long-lasting durability.
Interventions such as restoration (for instance, bleaching) have the advantage of bringing
back the works’ original looks, which has both an aesthetic meaning and the scope of recre-
ating the artist’s intent. But such tasks are invasive and can damage the works irreversibly.

Furthermore, it is a common standpoint in conservation to consider aging as a part of the
artwork, and as such, it should not be modified.
On the other hand, applying coatings or consolidation media can defend the artworks from
further damage, but could alter the surface appearance and result in an irreversible pro-
cess as well. In particular, the aging properties of the applied system must be taken into
consideration, to avoid additional damage to the work.
This section delves into all the aforementioned techniques, with a particular focus to the
chosen consolidation medium for the current project, Klucel G.
2.7.1
Conversion treatment via hydrogen peroxide
Lussier et al. [3] covered the topic of the conversion (chemical change that takes place as a
result of treatment) of darkened lead white, to bleach it back to a white appearance. They
explained how the treatment of discolored lead white usually involves the oxidation of black
lead sulfide to a new white material, lead sulfate, typically through the use of hydrogen
peroxide (H2O2) oxidant. The article made claims on the long-term success of treatments,
as lead sulfate is supposed to be less reactive than lead carbonate.
One aspect to be taken into account during conversion treatments, though, is that a prior,
correct, identification of the darkened material is fundamental. In fact, this process cannot
suit all types of darkened paint. And, as explored earlier in the report, darkened parts can be
PbS, but also PbO2 (more rarely). Moreover, the same article pointed out the risks associated
with every chemical transformation (including both discoloring and conversion). It stated
that, during such processes, materials endure: thermal changes, modification of crystalline
phases, wetting and swelling, the production of disruptive gas bubbles, and unintended
reactions with binder, substrate, and ancillary pigments. The long-term effect of all these
changes had not been rigorously established, except through anecdotal evidence.
Acknowledging all these drawbacks led SMK conservators to conclude that bleaching the
deteriorated works posed too big a risk, resulting in their decision not to pursue this approach.
Another article in which a "reversing" treatment was not carried out due to concerns for the
artwork, is [6]. It stated that, although such a procedure leads to an aesthetic improvement,
the white pigment so generated is lead(II) sulfate, PbSO4. The worry about this procedure
is that, since two chemical transformations have been carried out in situ on the paper (basic
lead(II) carbonate to lead(II) sulfide to lead(II) sulfate), the binding of the pigment (through
volume expansion/contraction) could deteriorate, and the lead(II) sulfate become friable and
crumble from the paper [6].
On the other hand, [7] reported many successful examples of peroxide bleaching on Italian
drawings before 1600. Similarly, [13] and [14] applied hydrogen peroxides and showed the
successful short-term results of the bleaching.

2.7.2
Coatings, consolidation media and Klucel G
This section delves into two strategies in conservation: the application of coatings and con-
solidation. They are defined as follows:
• A coating is a layer that is added over the surface of the artwork, to protect it from
damage linked to environmental or physical factors. It can be reversible or fixed.
• Consolidation consists of the application of a consolidating agent, which reinforces the
structure of a deteriorated artwork. It implies the penetration of the agent inside the
pores of the material, hence, it acts as a binder, and it is permanent.
These two strategies serve different purposes, nevertheless, there might be some overlap -
for instance, a consolidating agent might have protective features. This section explores this
kind of media. Both approaches are considered less invasive than restoration, as they should
not modify the appearance of the pieces.
Appendix C provides a summary of various types of consolidation media. The adhesive
that was selected for the current project is Klucel G. It is a semi-synthetic, cellulose-based,
water-soluble polymer, also known as hydroxypropylcellulose (HPC). Its popularity in the
industry is due to its film-forming ability, and its capacity to change the viscosity of aqueous
solutions. In art conservation, it is employed, for instance, for treating and strengthening
leather, book bindings, paper, and furniture.
The choice of Klucel G in this thesis stems from its widespread use in the field. For instance,
[43] used Klucel G for consolidation, and stated that the product performs as well as other
popular consolidants at ambient temperature and humidity. Also, Gill and Boersma [44]
reported three case studies in which Klucel G was applied on severely degraded textiles, with
satisfactory results. Furthermore, [45] stated how Klucel G has been thoroughly investigated
and it performed well in both artificial and natural aging studies. Finally, SMK laboratories
have worked with Klucel G, for example, in restoring a drawing from Eugene Delacroix, as
explored in [46].
On the other hand, in [47], Ibsen summarized a study by Feller and Wilt [48] indicating some
drawbacks of Klucel. The volume underlined that thermal aging of various grades of Klucel
at 90◦C led to considerable discoloration and a strong loss in weight.
Hence, although
the compound was reported to have excellent photochemical stability and little tendency
to form peroxides during aging, Klucel G was labeled with intermediate thermal stability.
Furthermore, this blog post [49] mentioned unsatisfactory long-term aging characteristics of
Klucel G, particularly in an acidic environment, e.g. in the presence of sulfuric acid from
absorption of sulfur dioxide in the atmosphere.
Also, Poulis et al. [50] studied the mechanical and physical properties of Klucel G after
exposure to medium (55%) and high (85%) moisture for 7 days at ambient temperature.
They report how it became very weak at the end of the exposure, both in terms of increased
plasticity and in the tendency to failure in cohesion.

Hypothesis statements
This thesis takes into account drawings that underwent darkening in their lead white high-
lights. The detection of sulfur in the darkened areas, together with the recorded presence
of H2S in the SMK storage rooms, indicates that the substance has a central role in the
degradation of lead white. However, the rate at which the discoloration has occurred is a
question that remains unanswered.
The very fast darkening is likely due to a combination of many factors. First, the stor-
age conditions (e.g., old sulfur-containing cardboards, improper boxes and insufficient air
filtration) might have contributed to the accumulation of hydrogen sulfide, until it reached
concentration levels that sped up the darkening of the lead white highlights significantly.
Additionally, improper climate control (e.g., high temperature and relative humidity during
most summers between 2000 and 2020 due to climate change) may also have exacerbated the
problem. It is possible that the high relative humidity and temperature levels have caused
the H2S to react more quickly, which could have accelerated the degradation of the objects.
Thus, parameters such as temperature and relative humidity were hypothesized to influence
the stability of the lead white paint, with this instability being the starting point for the
discoloration. The exposure of lead white paint to the above parameters individually, as
well as in combination, was planned. The goal was to pinpoint if such factors, in any way,
catalyzed the reaction with sulfur. Or if, on the other hand, it was only the increased amount
of sulfur that directly reacted with lead carbonate, irrespective of T and RH parameters.
The following are hypotheses statements that could not be denied theoretically but needed
experimental evidence. The core of the current project, then, was to dispute (or confirm)
them.
1. High temperature alone and high relative humidity alone, as individual parameters, in
a controlled environment, in the absence of H2S, do not affect the phenomenon of lead
white darkening.
→Reactions that transform lead white into black lead compounds are not favored by
T or RH in the range considered in this experiment (∼20 −50◦C, 11 −75%).
2. High relative humidity, in a controlled environment, in the presence of H2S, enhances
the darkening effect of H2S.
→High relative humidity encourages the reaction of lead white to white lead hydroxide
Pb(OH)2, which is more reactive towards H2S. Also, lead hydroxide Pb(OH)2 is white,
thus, high relative humidity, as an individual parameter, in the absence of H2S, will
not lead to darkening.
3. Klucel G is a valid method for preventing lead white darkening in moist environments
and H2S atmosphere.
→Consolidation medium Klucel G is widely employed in conservation, and will not
be impacted by the exposure to H2S, high temperature or high relative humidity in
the ranges presented in this thesis.

4.2.1
Control of parameters for accelerated aging
The accelerated aging setup in the current thesis relied on three parameters: temperature
(T), relative humidity (RH) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S). Before the DOE of the project is
introduced, a short explanation of the parameters control and generation is necessary.
Temperature control using heating plates
The high temperatures were achieved by placing the exposure chambers on top of heat-
ing plates. Measurements with a probe were conducted inside the chambers, recording no
gradients of temperature inside the volume.
Humidity control using salt solutions
The method chosen for the current project is based on saturated salt solutions. Their working
principle is explained in depth in Appendix E.
The selected salt solutions were:
• LiCl - with a target RH = 11%.
53.33 grams of salt were mixed with 43.64 grams of water. The reaction released heat
for around 10 minutes.
• NaCl - with a target RH = 75%.
53.33 grams of salt were mixed with 27.28 grams of water.
Both were prepared following the instructions in [52] (whose containers have similar dimen-
sions as the ones used in this thesis) and placed in food containers, as conducted by [23] and
[53]. GORE-TEX® sheets were employed to prevent salt creep.
The low-RH chambers reached the desired level of RH after 24 hours, once the salt solutions
stabilized (probably also facilitated by the dry air in the fume hood). The high-RH chambers
took longer to reach the target value, around 48 hours. Humidity was checked with a sensor
every 2 weeks and gradients were not recorded. At the same time, humidity strips were
left in the boxes during exposure, and changed every 2 weeks, ensuring the RH values to
be within ranges throughout the whole 9 weeks of exposure. Maintenance was needed only
in chamber E, where the water in the solution tended to evaporate and thus needed to be
refilled every 2 weeks. This was possible as all environments (except E) were kept air-tight
for most of the time, and when they were opened to collect the samples, operations were
carried out very quickly.

highly soluble in water.
Solid Na2S was placed in the system to gradually react with
the surrounding humidity (H2O) and release hydrogen sulfide according to the reaction:
Na2S + H2O →H2S + Na2O.
In the absence of a H2S sensor, lead acetate paper strips were left in the setup as indicators.
During the first 5 weeks of exposure, the lead acetate strips did not change color, indicating
that, if H2S was being generated, it was below 5 ppm. Thus, during Week 5, one drop
of water was added to each vial, significantly increasing the H2S level. Within minutes,
the indicators turned brown, indicating that at least 5 ppm of acid were being generated.
Another drop was added in Week 7. Appendix F reports calculations for the theoretical H2S
concentration.
This design change in week 5, and the absence of sensors to constantly monitor the H2S
levels, could lead only to qualitative considerations of this thesis’ results. But the current
project is aimed at understanding if the parameters, alone or in combination, have an effect
at all on the darkening, in a 9-week span. Thus, being sure of having considerable amounts
of H2S, enough to generate visible change, was favored over an excessively low amount,
although consistent.
4.2.2
Design of experiment
One of the purposes of accelerated aging is to speed up the processes of deterioration and
disclose the underlying degradation mechanisms, as explained in [54].
The accelerated aging setup in the current thesis relied on three parameters:
1. Temperature (T): can be low (20◦C) or high (50◦C - target 70◦C)
2. Relative humidity (RH): can be low (11%) or high (75%)
3. Gaseous hydrogen sulfide (H2S): can be absent (−) or present (+)
A complete, 2k factorial DOE would then need 23 = 8 environments (Table 5). However, the
two combinations including both high temperature and H2S (no. 7 and 8 in Table 5) were
excluded, due to limitations in the design of the setup. In fact, the current project did not
employ environmental chambers, but enclosed volumes controlled with salt solutions. When
such chambers are subjected to high temperatures, humidity control is far from ideal, with
possible phenomena of condensation. The risk of having liquid water is incompatible with
the suggested procedure for H2S generation (explored later in this section), hence, it was
considered to be avoided, and such setup combinations were ignored.

Measurements and results
5.1
Characterization techniques
This section begins with an explanation of the protocol for exposure and characterization of
the current thesis, and its limitations. Subsequently, it summarizes the working principles
of the characterization techniques employed in the current project.
More thorough explanations of the employed characterization techniques are provided in
Appendix H.
5.1.1
Timeline of characterization procedures
The samples were kept in their aging chambers (described in Section 4.2.3) for a total of 9
weeks. The week in which the exposure began was marked as "Week 0".
Non-destructive characterization measurements (pictures, microscope, color analysis, FTIR
spectroscopy) were performed at Weeks 0 (reference samples), 2, 5, 7 and 9.
Destructive characterization techniques (SEM and surface pH measurements) were carried
out after the exposure was concluded.
5.1.2
Visual analysis
Visual analysis consisted of simple observation of the samples by the naked eye and smart-
phone camera pictures. To ensure the same light conditions for all pictures, a light box
was used, and consistent camera parameters. Also, before analyses, samples were blown
delicately with compressed air, to remove dust and debris linked to handling.
Visual analysis provided an initial assessment of the samples’ conditions and anomalies.
5.1.3
Light microscopy
The instrument used in the project was a Keyence VHX-6000 digital microscope. The lens
used was VH-Z20T, with a magnification range going from 20 times (minimum) to 200
(maximum). The samples were examined under reflected visible illumination.
In this thesis, microscopic examination was aimed at revealing fine details about the samples’
structure and composition that were not visible to the naked eye.
5.1.4
Color analysis
The instrument used in the project was an X-Rite Ci64 portable handheld spectropho-
tometer. The conditions for color measurements were the following: UV component 100%;
Illumination D65; Observer 10◦; Specular component Included; measuring area (8-mm win-
dow) and 1 measurement per sample. Colour space CIE L*a*b* was chosen, which means,
the results are expressed in terms of:
Page 34 of 56
==PAGE:47==
5.1
Characterization techniques
• L*: Lightness, ranging from 0 (black) to 100 (white)
• a*: Red-green axis, with +a indicating red and -a indicating green.
• b*: Yellow-blue axis, with +b indicating yellow and -b indicating blue.
• dE00*: total color change parameter, if above 1, it reflects a color change detectable

These values can be expressed as differences between the sample and a reference (i.e. ∆L∗).
Appendix H provides a more thorough explanation.
Color analysis was chosen as it gives a quantitative and consistent meaning to color. It
provided objective data on the degree of darkening and made it possible to follow the color
changes over time.
5.1.5
Scanning Electron Microscope
The employed instrument was a Prisma E-SEM, Thermofisher Scientific, Denmark, operated
at an accelerated voltage of 10 kV. Other parameters: spot 4.0, magnification range used:
1000x to 5000x.
In the framework of the current project, SEM was employed for the level of detail it is
capable of. Images at such big magnifications can reveal surface changes that are otherwise
invisible, and possibly unveil degradation mechanisms that contribute to darkening.
5.1.6
FTIR spectroscopy
The instrument used was an ATR-FTIR - Nicolet iS5 Spectrometer. Multiple recordings (3
to 7 spectra) were recorded for every sample, the outliers were removed, and the remaining
spectra were averaged. Baseline correction was performed for every measurement, and results
were eventually normalized via min-max scaling. The instrument settings were the following.
Spectral range: 400-4000 cm-1. Number of sample scans: 8. Numer of background scans:
16. Resolution: 4.000. Simple gain: 1.0. Optical velocity: 0.4747. Aperture: 100.00.
ATR-FTIR was selected as a characterization method because it can identify the chemical
compounds on the samples’ surfaces in a fast and non-destructive way.
5.1.7
pH measurements
The pH measurements of the current project were taken at the Institute of Conservation,
the Royal Danish Academy in Copenhagen. The instrument employed was a Hach HQ411D
Laboratory pH Meter with an IntelliCAL PHC729 flat sensor electrode.
Surface pH measurements were conducted to understand if paper acidity played a role in the
darkening.

Discussion
6.1
Summary: theorized degradation pathway vs observations
Lead-containing samples showed three types of behaviors:
1. no darkening in environments where H2S was not involved (chambers A,B,E,F);
2. no darkening but slight changes (appearance of red spots visible with microscope) in
the presence of H2S and low RH (chamber C) - with this concentration being lower if
Klucel G was applied;
3. darkening in the presence of H2S and high RH (chamber D), in the form of black and
red spots and brown areas by the defects - with a heavier deterioration if the sample
contained Klucel G. Detailed descriptions can be found in Section 6.2.
Therefore, the factors responsible for darkening are to be researched in the high-moisture
exposure in combination with H2S.
One possible explanation could be an increase in the amount of cracked areas in environments
with high relative humidity. In fact, an increase in cracks leads to greater exposure of the
underlying pigment. However, this cannot be the only reason behind the darkening, as will
be explained in Section 6.3
Another argument, together with the cracks, could be of chemical nature. The theorized
degradation pathway in this thesis, stemming from its literature search, stated that an
increase in humidity (H2O) may bring an increase in lead hydroxide Pb(OH)2, which is
more sensitive to H2S. Summarizing:
2PbCO3 · Pb(OH)2 (white)

−−−→⇑Pb(OH)2 (white)
+H2S

not able to confirm nor dispute the statement (Section 6.4).
The reason behind the failure of Klucel G in preventing the darkening is investigated in

Finally, the literature search hinted at the possibility that paper acidity and release of VOCs

Samples were in contact with high levels of H2S only for 4 weeks, and the darkening, although

6.2.2
Red spots
The appearance of red spots on the paint surface was initially ascribed to the presence of
metal impurities in the pigment. Ciofini et al. [56] reported the observation of iron oxide
impurities when a lead white surface was irradiated with laser for Raman spectroscopy.
However, SMK had also analyzed the raw pigments employed in the current thesis, which
only showed the presence of lead carbonates. Iron oxides were identified on some drawings,
but that was due to the presence of other pigments such as ochre.
The second hypothesis for the red spots, then, is linked to lead oxides. However, the reac-
tion leading to the thermal decomposition of lead white to lead oxide usually takes place
at temperatures that are outside the range considered here (∼300 −400◦C). Also, the
aforementioned analysis by X-ray powder diffraction run by SMK did not detect any lead
oxide on the analyzed drawings. However, such study focused on samples with significant
discoloration (dark brown, dark grey, and black), and not red.
A third hypothesis, put forward by SMK researchers, is foxing of the paper support (stains
linked to mold and contamination). However, it is unlikely that the reddish-brown color of
the stains would be visible through the layer of paint. Also, the paper samples do not show
signs of foxing on the back of the samples, without paint.
Thus, the nature of the red spots remains an open question.
6.2.3
Brown areas
Brown areas were observed on lead white samples both with and without Klucel G. Their
shape and extent are different from sample to sample, but it essentially differs from PbS as
it does not look punctiform, but rather, a uniform field.
One suggestion could be anglesite, PbSO4, defined as "light cream" after exposure to H2S
by Smith and Clark [4]. Nevertheless, it could be argued that such areas look "beige" or
"brown" rather than "light cream". In such case, they could be associated with galena, PbS.
This distinct darkening - uniform and consistent across an entire area, rather than localized
dark points - might be attributed to the binder or coating having a different behavior in
those particular regions.

6.3
Paint integrity and extent of darkening
6.2.4
White areas (SEM)
SEM pictures of lead white paint reveal the appearance of white areas with a different texture
and color around defects. One hypothesis could be white PbSO4, corroborated by the fact
that such areas appear in samples exposed to a sulfur-containing atmosphere.
6.3
Paint integrity and extent of darkening
The results of the current thesis showed that lead white darkening is a localized phenomenon,
generating on cracks, defects and edges. This finding aligns with existing literature (e.g.
Figure 4).
Defects and cracks are generally vulnerable sites for localized darkening. The occurrence of
cracks on painted samples is an ordinary phenomenon in conservation, due to the unstable
nature of many painting materials. Also, samples were handled often, they were physically
constrained by the sample holders, and the paper substrates underwent heavy bending. All
these reasons are grounds for cracks.
It would be natural to suppose that samples exposed to high RH were subjected to heavier
cracking, due to water absorption from the binder and its subsequent deformation. However,
it was not possible to quantitatively assess this statement with the employed methods in
environments without H2S. Because the binder was transparent, and both the paint and
the underlying paper were white, it was difficult to compare the extent of cracked areas on
samples exposed to low- and high-RH environments.
Cracks are preferential sites for the appearance of black spots, but they are not the only
necessary condition leading to that. This is demonstrated by the observation that all samples
from chamber C (low RH and H2S) had cracks, but merely reported red spots on the defects
and no visible color change.
6.4
White patches (SEM), lead hydroxide, and need for further
chemical analysis
SEM pictures of lead white paint reveal a patchy pattern at a 1000x magnification. The au-
thor hypothesizes this is due to the two-component composition of lead white paint (normal
and basic lead white). The color of such patches shifts towards white when exposed to high
RH only, and to black when H2S is also involved.
This behavior could be linked to the pathway summarized in Equation 6: the white patches
could be Pb(OH)2, increasing with high relative humidity, and turning black when exposed
to hydrogen sulfide. However, this is only a hypothesis, as it was not possible to corroborate
this assumption with the conducted chemical analysis (FTIR).

6.5
Behavior of Klucel G
6.5
Behavior of Klucel G
The application of Klucel G reduced the appearance of red spots in conditions of low RH
(chamber C), but intensified the darkening in the high-moisture chamber (D).
This behavior could be linked to moisture absorption in the polymer structure, as observed
by Poulis et al. [50]. Water uptake from Klucel G might have caused plasticizing of the
polymer and a decrease in its Tg, leading to softening. In other words, moisture led to a
reduction in the mechanical performance and cohesion of Klucel G.
It is difficult to draw conclusions on the SEM pictures of the samples with Klucel G exposed
to high moisture only (chamber B), because of the uniform dark color of the surface, which
makes cracks very hard to detect. As for the high-RH and H2S exposure (chamber D), in
which Klucel G does not protect the underlying layer of lead white, it is easy to see the
darkening in SEM pictures, completely localized inside the cracks.
Consequently, the softening effect of Klucel G due to water absorption resulted in more
cracks forming within the substrate. As a result, when the coating was applied, the overall
change in color was more noticeable compared to when the coating was absent
Thus, the softening of Klucel G due to water absorption led to an increase of cracks in the
substrate. As a result, the overall color change was more noticeable when the coating was
applied, compared to when it was absent.
6.6
Discarded hypothesis of paper acidity
Measurements of surface pH were not able to provide any valuable information or trends on
the changes in acidity. It is also possible that such changes were too small to be detected.
This allows the author to conclude that surface pH did not play a role in the darkening of
the samples in the current thesis.

7
Conclusions
7.1
Hypothesis statements examination
1. Statement: High temperature alone and high relative humidity alone, as individual
parameters, in a controlled environment, in the absence of H2S, do not affect the
phenomenon of lead white darkening.
Answer: This statement is confirmed by the experimental work carried out in the
current thesis.
Samples exposed to high temperature only (chamber A) and high
relative humidity only (chamber B) do not exhibit a visible change in color after 9
weeks of exposure.
2. Statement: High relative humidity, in a controlled environment, in the presence of
H2S, enhances the darkening effect of H2S.
Answer: Also this statement was validated and quantified by the current experiment.
It is believed that this enhancement happened for two reasons. The first is that high
relative humidity probably impacted the gum arabic binder of the paint, which cracked
as a result of water absorption, and thus exposed more lead white pigment to the H2S-
rich atmosphere. The second might be linked to a degradation pathway that sees an
increase in lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2), which is more prone to react to galena (PbS)
in a high-RH environment. However, it was not possible to demonstrate (nor dispute)
the latter assumption with chemical analysis.
3. Statement: Klucel G is a valid method for preventing lead white darkening in moist
environments and H2S atmosphere.
Answer: This statement is denied by the results of the current work. Samples with
Klucel G are even more prone to darkening in a moist environment with H2S. This is
considered to be linked to water absorption from Klucel G and subsequent softening.
As a consequence, larger areas of "vulnerable" lead white paint were exposed to the
H2S atmosphere which increased the darkening phenomenon.
7.2
Summary of findings
This study investigated the behavior of lead white paint under various environmental con-
ditions. Three distinct behaviors were identified: no darkening without hydrogen sulfide
(H2S), no darkening but slight changes with H2S and low relative humidity (RH), and dark-
ening with H2S and high RH, the latter being particularly pronounced when Klucel G was
applied.
A suggested degradation pathway involving lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2) and lead sulfide (PbS)
aligned with observations but lacked chemical confirmation. Visual examinations revealed
black spots (galena), red spots (whose nature remains unresolved), and brown areas (possibly
linked to PbSO4 or PbS).
SEM images clearly showed the localized nature of the darkening, which prospered around
cracks. The integrity of the paint layers was then explored, with water absorption from gum

7.3
Implications for art conservation
The findings of this study carry significant implications for art conservation:
• The risk of high-moisture areas in storage conditions should be mitigated.
• Conservators can now make informed decisions regarding the use of Klucel G as a
consolidation medium. The results of the current thesis discourage such practice.
• Minimizing the physical stress on artworks is crucial to reduce the possibilities for the
formation of cracks, which are vulnerable sites for darkening.
• The study laid the foundation for future investigations on the degradation pathways
of lead white. Further chemical analysis could lead to the discovery of which white
lead compounds are more or less susceptible towards H2S, and how to encourage (or
discourage) their formation.
7.4
Future work
7.4.1
Changes for setup
This thesis used plastic and glass boxes as aging chambers. Laboratory desiccators with
saturated salt solutions were considered as an alternative. A better solution would have been
to employ environmental chambers, which can simulate T and RH conditions, and offer a
sophisticated and controlled way to conduct tests and detect polluting gases. However, the
main downside of environmental chambers is their high price.
A cost-effective "home-made" environmental chamber could address issues like condensation
while providing better parameter control. The setup could be housed in an insulated box,
with a heating element, temperature and humidity sensors, fans for air circulation, and
an external water reservoir for humidity control. This setup would also allow monitoring
of exposure conditions without light exposure. Additionally, an external unit for hydrogen
sulfide generation could be added to the setup, using Na2S and H2O as in the current thesis,
and gradually introducing this into the setup with fans to improve the irregular evolution.
7.4.2
Further analyses
This thesis suggests further analyses for a more comprehensive understanding of the sub-
ject. Studying individual components, such as pure lead white pigment and free films of
binder and paint, could help pinpoint the effects of external factors. The role of volatile
organic compounds (VOCs), currently not quantified due to lack of sensors, could be better
understood with appropriate equipment.
As for the characterization, hyperspectral imaging could be a valuable non-destructive tech-
nique for distinguishing different white compounds on a drawing. Lastly, other methods

(MC), carboxyl-methylcellulose (CMC), and hydroxy-propylcellulose (HPC, Klucel G). The

Appendix E
This appendix is related to Sections 4.2.1 ("Control of parameters for accelerated aging") and
4.2.3 ("Design of chambers for accelerated aging"). They mention the use of salt solutions
for humidity control in this thesis. This appendix delves more into the topic, explaining
their working principle, their advantages and disadvantages.
Many methods are available for humidity control - to name some: environmental chambers,
desiccator beads, salt, or glycerol solutions. The method which was chosen for the current
project is based on saturated salt solutions. Their working principle is the following. Sum-
marizing the explanation in [53], if a container of water is placed in a closed chamber, this
will evaporate until RH = 100%. If the water is salty, the RH that can be achieved is
lower, and this value depends on the kind of salt and the temperature. In other words, every
salt has its characteristic RH(T) value. If dry air is introduced into the chamber, water
molecules evaporate from the solution; analogously, if moist air is present, the solution will
absorb atmospheric water molecules. In both cases, the system will try to adjust so that the
characteristic RH is reached.
The main advantage of salt solutions is the simplicity of the servicing, which consists of
adding salt or water, if necessary. On the other hand, one of the most common drawbacks of
using saturated salt solutions is salt creep. This is defined in [23] as the recrystallization of the
salts further up the sides of the vessel containing the solution and, ultimately, elsewhere in the
chamber, at worst on the samples themselves. This was experienced first-hand, as depicted
in Figure 35a. The solution to this issue was found in using a GORE-TEX® membrane,
as explored in [23, 53, 62]. GORE-TEX® allows the water vapor to pass through, but not
liquids or solid salt. The method used in this thesis was analogous to those in the cited
articles. The solutions were placed in cylindrical glass containers, and the membrane was
kept in place using cable ties (Figure 35b).

Appendix G
Summary of the SMK project
The current appendix extends the explanation in 4.2, "Experimental setup for accelerated
aging tests", by delving into the ongoing SMK work on lead white darkening.
The project at SMK facilities was also aimed at understanding which environmental condi-
tions had an impact on lead white darkening. Differently from the current thesis’ experimen-
tal project, though, the SMK experiment relied on real-life conditions of temperature and
humidity. In other words, samples were not kept in a laboratory setup, but in four, actual,
storage rooms.
The variables that were considered are:
• Temperature. It was controlled via climate systems and could be low (3-16oC) or high
(≥20oC).
• Relative humidity. It was also controlled via climate systems and could be low (30%)
or high (45-50%).
• Presence of hygroscopic salts, such as calcium sulfate anhydrous CaSO4 from salt
efflorescence. This could facilitate acid ionization and dissociation, potentially leading
to a reaction with the normal water content of lead white. This was present or absent.
• Lead white paint recipe. 8 paint recipes were explored. The binder was gum ara-
bic or animal glue. The pigment was historically-reproduced lead white (potentially
more stable) or modern. Also, the pigment-to-binder ratio (P/B) was underbound or
overbound.
• Support. This meant that the paper support could be sized or non-sized.
This list of factors motivated 16 setup combinations in four different archival facilities. All
samples were left inside boxes in dark and moisture-free areas of the storage rooms, together
with one vial per setup, containing 0.3 g of Na2S. The reason behind Na2S is to accelerate
hydrogen sulfide generation, as will be explained in the dedicated paragraph in Section 4.2.2.
Color measurements were taken by SMK employees every 2 weeks. The exposure lasted for a
total of 7 weeks. H2S generation was sped up in half the setups after 4 weeks. However, this
alteration of the protocol was considered to be too pervasive, thus, a reset of the experiment
was needed.
However, the data for the 7 weeks is still considered relevant and will be
presented in Section 5.2.6. Due to the limited amount of available data from the test, these
results will only be included as a qualitative indication in Section 5.2.6.
It was observed that the most impacted samples were those exposed to high H2S, at very low
temperatures (3-16oC) and a relative humidity of 30-40%. However, it should be repeated
that this is only qualitative information and does not carry statistical relevance.

Appendix H
Characterization techniques
The current appendix supplements 5.1, "Characterization techniques" with theoretical ex-
planations on how the employed characterization techniques work.
Light microscopy
In a microscope, a light beam is converged and focused with a system of lenses to form a
magnified image. The main characteristic of a "digital" microscope is that it has a camera
instead of an eyepiece. [63]
Color analysis
Color measurement is the quantitative expression of color. [64] It is measured through a
spectrocolorimeter, or color spectrophotometer. Spectrophotometers measure reflected or
transmitted light across the spectrum and create a visual curve that describes the color on
that substrate, under that lighting condition. [65] In a spectrocolorimeter, the light source
is positioned upstream of the sample, while the monochromator and the detector are located
downstream of the sample. This means that the light source travels towards the sample, it
interacts with the sample, then it is passed through a monochromator (that splits the light
into its monochromatic wavelengths, acting as a prism), and is recorded by the detector.
Such measurements are more accurate than the human eye, consistent because the observer
does not change, and can perceive differences also in ranges that are not accessible to human
sight. This data can be pictured and correlated to human vision using the Lab, or L*a*b*,
color space. The Lab is a model that relies on three base parameters:
• L*, Lightness, going from 0 (black) to 100 (white).
==PAGE:84==
• a*, whose positive values are red, and negative values are green.
• b*, whose positive values are yellow, and negative values are blue.
These a* and b* values are plotted on an XY axis with the L* value piercing the origin of
the plot as a three-dimensional space. [64] These values are often expressed as a difference.
For instance, using the unexposed sample as a reference, the difference in lightness and color
will be expressed by ∆L∗, ∆a∗, ∆b∗(where ∆L∗= L ∗(sample) −L∗(reference) and so on).
Another parameter of importance is ∆E∗, or, the total color difference value. It measures
how the visual perception between two colors (one of them being the "reference") differs.
There are three subsequent definitions of ∆E∗, named dE76∗, dE94∗, and dE00∗. This
blog post [66] provides an explanation of the three of them. In short, dE76∗and dE94∗
are simpler, but present heavy shortcomings. On the other hand, dE00∗is complicated but
more accurate. The ∆E∗calculations in the current project employed a dE00∗-based script,
developed specifically for the project. It is believed that the "just noticeable difference",
defined as the color change detectable by the human eye, lies above a dE00∗value equal to
1, as assessed in [67].
Scanning Electron Microscope
A scanning electron microscope (SEM) is a type of electron microscope that produces images
of a sample by scanning it with a focused beam of electrons. [68] This technology allows
the user to reach much higher resolution and magnification than light microscopy, achieving
structural information of a specimen at atomic-scale resolution. [68] Another major advan-
tage of SEM is that it generates an image of the sample that contains information about
the surface topography. However, conventional SEM has some important downsides: it is
performed under high vacuum and requires complex and extensive sample processing, includ-
ing dehydration, fixation, and metallization. [69] In short: in SEM, a metallic ion coating
is applied to the specimen through vaporization. When the electrons of the beam ("pri-
mary electrons") interact with this coating, they induce the emission of other electrons from
the specimen’s surface ("secondary electrons"). Secondary electrons are then detected and
converted into a light signal, that is amplified and displayed onto a computer monitor. The
name of this method comes from the fact that the electron beam "scans" over the specimen’s
surface, giving back a comprehensive view. Secondary electrons are not the only signal that
derives from the interaction between the primary electrons beam and the sample. However,
they will be the main focus of this thesis, since they allow users to visualize the topography
of the sample. In other words, with this method, it is possible to see the distribution of the
sample’s surface features.
FTIR spectroscopy
The following paragraph is inspired by the analogous paragraph in [63], by the same author
of the current report.
FTIR stands for "Fourier transform infrared" and it is the most widely used vibrational
==PAGE:85==
spectroscopic technique. Vibrational spectroscopy is a method to analyze the structure of
molecules by examining the interaction between electromagnetic radiation and vibrations in
molecules. For clarity, a short theoretical explanation is provided.
Molecules in solids are always in vibration at their equilibrium positions, except at the
temperature of absolute zero (−273.15oC), and molecular vibrations can be simply modeled
as massless springs connecting nuclei in a molecule.
In FTIR, vibrational spectroscopy
detects such molecular vibrations by the absorption of infrared light, exploiting the Fourier
transform method to obtain an infrared spectrum in a whole range of wavelengths. [70]
When a molecule is irradiated by electromagnetic waves within the infrared frequency range,
one particular wave frequency (νph) may match the vibrational frequency of the molecule
(νvib= νph). [70] The type of vibration (stretching or bending) induced by infrared radiation
depends on the atoms in the bond. Because different bonds and functional groups absorb
different frequencies, the transmittance pattern is different for different molecules. [71] Sum-
ming up, when IR radiation is passed through a sample, some radiation will be absorbed by
the sample, and some will pass through (will be transmitted). The resulting, transmitted,
signal at the detector is a spectrum representing a molecular fingerprint of the sample. [72]
Reading the spectrum is a matter of determining which groups and bonds correspond to
which peaks; for this, simple reference tables are used.
[71] The resulting spectrum is
absorbance vs wavenumber (1/λ, expressed in cm−1), where the relationship between ab-
sorbance A and transmittance T is A = −logT. Transmittance is T = I/I0, where I is
the intensity measured in the sample and I0 isethe intensity measured in the background
spectrum.
The instrument used in the current report is an ATR-FTIR. ATR stands for "Attenuated
Total Reflection".
In this technique, the penetration depth of IR light in the sample is
constant (0.5 to 2 microns), hence independent of sample thickness. [73] ATR is achieved
using a material of high refractive index, which, in this thesis, is a diamond. A beam of IR
light passes through the diamond and it reflects internally. This reflection is "attenuated"
and not "total", thus it produces what is known as an "evanescent wave" that propagates
along the interface and penetrates in the sample at low depths.
pH measurements
The following description comes from [28]. In surface pH measurements, a drop of deionized
water or neutral salt solution is placed on the paper surface, and after a suitable time of
equilibration, the pH is measured with a specially designed "flat" electrode. One should
recall that a "paper pH" criterion is a snapshot of the concentration of protons at any
particular time and will not indicate how much of a strong acid or base is needed in order
to change the pH to a desired value. That is, pH measurements cannot predict the pKas of
weak acids or bases present within the sheet, nor their concentration.

Appendix K
Considerations on color analysis
This appendix supplements Section 5.2, "Results" with statistical considerations on the color
analysis. The data is extrapolated from Tables 10, 11 and 12.
Due to time constraints during the experimental process, exhaustive measurements of all
samples were not feasible. Instead, the focus was placed on the characterization of a single
representative test sample each time in Weeks 0-7.
This decision was influenced by the
absence of observable changes in the initial weeks, and it allowed for more efficient time
management.
However, after the experiment was completed (Week 9), all samples were characterized,
enabling the execution of statistical analyses. Figure 50 displays the values for lightness, color
==PAGE:95==
and total color change after 9 weeks of exposure for sample types 2, 4 and 5. In the samples
where observable changes occurred (mainly those exposed to chamber D), the standard
deviation bars are notably larger, indicating a higher degree of intra-class variability. This
is not surprising, as the samples were not uniformly darkened, and there was variability also
on the surface of single samples. On the other hand, in samples that did not exhibit visible
changes, the standard deviation bars are smaller or even negligible, suggesting consistency
between samples.
These column bars also suggest that the parameters really driving the change in dE00*
(which, is reminded, is a function of L*, a* and b*) are L* (ligthness) and a* (with a sharp
shift towards red for samples in chamber D).
(a) L* (lightness) on the y-axis. L* goes from 0
(black) to 100 (white).
(b) a* on the y-axis. Positive values of a* mean
red, negative values of a* mean green.
(c) b* on the y-axis. Positive values of b* mean
yellow, negative values of b* mean blue.
(d) dE00* (total color change) on the y-axis.
When dE00*>1, the color change is visible.
Figure 50: Measurements on various color parameters (L*,a*,b*,dE00*) after 9 weeks of
exposure. Each column represents the average of 3 samples of the same type exposed to the
same conditions. Standard deviation is represented for each column. x-axis: Letters A-F
refer to the exposure conditions (chamber ID).

Preparation 
 
Paper 
A sheet of 100% cotton paper was sized with gelatin (3%) and left to dry. Afterwards the paper was  
treated in an alum bath (0,81 g aluminium sulfate to 100 mL water) and then dried. 
On the back of the treated paper, samples were marked out using a pencil and ruler (fig. 1). An extra area 
was added around the samples, making it possible to tape the paper on cardboard during the application 
paint, binder and coating. 
 
 
Fig. 1. Samples marked out on the back of the paper sheet. 
 
Paint 
The paint was prepared by conservator Kerstin Lyckman Gevert at Mandelgren AB in Sweden 
(https://www.mandelgren.se/).  
 
7.2 g gum arabic powder was mixed with 52.8 g demineralized water and left for 8 hours. 45.6 g lead white 
pigment was then added little by little during constant stirring.  
The paint was prepared in week 35 2023, and has been kept refrigerated in a sealed container since then.
Before the paint was used for samples, the container was shaken and the paint stirred for a couple of 
minutes to obtain a uniform distribution of the pigment, which had settled at the bottom. 
 
Binder 
7.2 g gum arabic powder was mixed with 52.8 g demineralized water. The mixture was stirred for a couple 
of minutes, and then left in the refrigerator overnight. Before use, the binder was stirred again, and foam at 
the top was removed with a spoon. The binder was used for samples the day after it was prepared. 
 
Coating 
1 g Klucel G was mixed with 50 mL ethanol, and left in the refrigerator overnight. The mixture was stirred 
again before use. It was applied on the samples the day after it was prepared.

Observations  
 
As seen in fig. 3 and fig. 5, the paper curled a bit when binder and paint were applied. This might have 
caused an uneven distribution of the binder/paint. All paper samples are covered with a layer of binder 
(sample type 2) or paint (sample type 4 and 5), but it might be a little thicker on some samples. Fig. 7 shows 
paper with dried paint. The shiny areas could have a thicker paint layer. 
The curling of the paper might have been less, if the amount of paint applied was smaller.   
 
 
Fig. 7. Paper with dried paint.
