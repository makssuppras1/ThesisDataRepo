Abstract
Scientific research is shifting towards the extensive use of computational methods. However,
these methods are limited by the lack of experimental data, particularly for less-explored com-
pounds like viscous polymer electrolytes, which could be generated in automated experiments.
This thesis addresses the critical gap in automating the handling of highly viscous matter within
self-driving laboratories (SDLs). A novel, cost-effective, and modular autonomous system, named
Viscotron, has been developed to manage fluid mixtures up to a simplified zero-shear viscosity
of 𝜂0 = 482, 902𝑐𝑃. Unlike established SDLs that struggle with fluid transport at high viscosities,
Viscotron employs an in-situ methodology where the sample container itself moves between
workstations for dispensing, mixing, viscosity assessment, and cleaning. A full iteration took 69
minutes and 6 seconds.
The system is a ’proof-of-concept’ and integrates five key in-line functionalities: 1) Dispensing
of both solid and liquid components with a lower limit precision of 0.1 g and 2.22 ml respectively.
2) The effective in-situ transport of viscous mixtures within a container to respective worksta-
tions. 3) A mixing procedure which can mix highly viscous matter homogeneously, reducing
the stirring time from multiple days to a few hours. Here, an optimised helical impeller is em-
ployed to minimise residue adherence and increase elongated flow regimes. 4) The continuous
assessment of the mixture’s viscosity using motor current measurements, establishing a reliable
decision criterion. 5) For closed-loop operation, an automated, four-step cleaning procedure, in-
cluding high-pressure cleaning, rinsing with stirring, and ultrasonication, has been integrated to
ensure reusability and enhance laboratory sustainability.
This work explains the design process of the Viscotron system. It highlights how Viscotron
differs from the established SDL system and examines the options for handling different viscosity
tasks. Finally, it tests the system to see what its limits are and how well it performs.
Viscotron offers a robust, affordable solution (≈€ 1,050 excluding an ultrasonic cleaner) for the
challenging handling of viscous matter, previously requiring laborious manual processes. Its
modular design and open-source nature facilitate the wide use of SDL’s to accelerate research in
various research domains, like battery materials.

Introduction
In the last decades, the manner in which research is being conducted is altering from a purely
human based reasoning approach to the utilization and reliance on computer guided algorithms
and their predictions [1]. The predictive work done by computational approaches such as opti-
mizers, neural networks, evolutionary generation and other models is employed in the chemical
and material research space to identify new compounds [2]–[4]. The search for materials with
the desired properties is supported by physical experimentation of compounds to understand
further complex relationship and the vast dimensionality, which cannot be depicted fully by
computational methods yet, as a priori data is missing [4], [5]. While computational methods
enable the creation of new compounds, their predictive capabilities often rely on proprietary
data for their prediction and especially struggle with assessing synthesizability [6]. Less re-
searched compounds, such as deep-eutectic solvents are often lacking molecular simulation and
chemically diverse experimentation results to apply advanced computational methods [7]. Fur-
thermore, the results of ’non-successful’ experiments are often not published. However, making
successful and novel prediction is difficult, if this valuable data from the experiments is lacking
[2]. Automating the experimental procedure in self-driving-laboratories (SDL) could overcome
these 2 shortcomings and accelerate research efforts.
SDL´s can have the following benefits over manual preparation 1) Increased safety, through a
sealed system that handles hazardous compounds, 2) Higher throughput, 3) Precise operation, 4)
Repeatability and 5) freeing up time for the operating researcher [8]. Pairing them in a closed
loop system with the computational methods creates a powerful tool for further increasing the
research output and consistency. [9].

Scientific research also annually produces between 2-3% of all worldwide plastic waste with pre-
dominantly disposable lab materials, such as Petri-dishes, pipettes and gloves needed in manual
operations [11] While the SDL domain will not entirely eliminate the production of waste, an SDL
which reuses its equipment, eases the cleaning procedure and keeps material use at a minimum,
offers both sustainability and economic advantages [6].
Different applications of SDL´s are designed in numerous concepts, from collaborative robotic
arms provided by e.g. universal robots [12], gantry systems as in the systems from unchained
labs [5] or Opentrons [13], rotary pipette systems found in the Chemputer [8] or a tube pipeline
as in the AutoProBE. The different systems usually combine the preparation, mixing, reaction,
the measurement of various parameters with different techniques, such nuclear magnetic reso-
nance, cryogenic electron microscopy or spectroscopy [14], [15]. Usually these systems are run

in a closed loop to enable high-throughput experimentation (HTE). This closed loop of evolved
SDL versions usually combines the dispensing of components, mixing, reacting, measuring and
analysing of liquid samples over multiple iterations without human intervention [15]. The final
design concept is based on the problems the SDL is trying to solve and the requirements regard-
ing dimensions, speed, measurement capabilities, and cost. Salley et al. raise the concern that the
effort to build custom applications can incur significant cost and time expenditure which might
outweigh the benefits an SDL can bring [16]. Thus, it is important that the systems are modular,
offer a wide range of applications and are cheap to build and setup. This can be balanced by
sharing the individual research and design effort to a the research community and democratis-
ing the use of SDL’s, by using off-the shelf products, 3D printed structures, open-source software
and accessible instructions [9]. Other research groups can then quickly apply and implement the
solution for their purposes. This work also follows this philosophy and applies the mentioned
techniques in its development.
The application of SDL´s is huge and can theoretically be applied to any research domain in need
of experiments. The domain of battery research has huge exploitative potential to use SDL’s to
drive further advances in cathode, catalyst and electrolyte materials [2]. The conduct of ex-
perimental synthesis, characterization and testing is the limiting factor in accelerating material
research further when using AI and other data driven approaches, thus making advances partic-
ularly desirable [2]. The use of SDL’s for the experimentation on electrolytes is mostly employed
for aqueous electrolytes [17], [18]. For the discovery of liquid compounds multiple concepts of
modular systems are used [16]. Conductive polymers which are solvent-casted can be automated
but require an aqueous formulation during the process phase [19]. Testing on higher viscosity
levels, exceeding 10,000 cP, and on solid electrolytes has not yet been fully incorporated into
fully automated laboratory setups. Despite the claim of Lo et al. that SDL systems designed for
viscous fluid testing have already been established and built, the subsequent research could not
confirm this statement, as most established systems struggle with highly viscous liquids [1] or
only implement solutions to measure the viscosity, but are not integrated into a closed loop SDL
[1], [10], [13], [20]. However, the handling of viscous materials is highly relevant, as a num-
ber of battery electrolytes reach gel and solid-like properties. Polymer supported electrolytes
are a promising technology to enable the use of full metal electrodes, as the polymer chains can
suppress the inherent dendrite growth [7].
Thus, a solution to handle mixtures with a zero-shear rate viscosity up to 100,000 cP in an SDL
environment is proposed. It is to be demonstrated that viscous matter can be handled in an au-
tonomous system, which can incorporate the five discrete functionalities below, where previous
systems have not yet provided an applicable solution. This system, called Viscotron, is a ’prove
of concept’ system for the mentioned functionalities and should be enhanced in the future to
conduct the electrolyte tests.
1. Dispensing of liquid and solid components: Develop an accurate and integrated plat-
form for the provision of raw components.
2. Transporting of the electrolyte: Design a system capable of deploying viscous matter
to various workstations.
3. Mixing of subcomponents to a homogeneous mixture: Provide a mixing solution for
solids and liquids that ensures operability across the viscosity range.
4. Assessment of the mixture’s viscosity: Establish a decision criterion that can accurately
evaluate the range of the mixture’s viscosity.
5. Cleaning of contaminated parts to ensure a pure environment for the next itera-
tion: Implement a visual inspection system and best practice procedures to ensure a clean

Rheology
Rheology describes the properties of a compound regarding their solid or fluid behaviour. Solids
experience elasticity; when the stress on the material is resolved, the energy to deform the mate-
rial is recovered to varying degrees [21]. Fluids (gases and liquids) deform irreversibly, causing
them to flow. Most fluids, except those which exhibit Bingham behaviour, will flow naturally
when any force, like gravity, is applied; however, the speed of the flow is vastly different in fluids
with different viscosities. Viscosity is the intrinsic resistance of a fluid upon applied positional
change. The basic law of viscosity is denoted as 𝜂with the units [Pa⋅s] or centipoise (cP), where
1 cP = 0.001 Pa⋅s. For reference, water has the (dynamic) viscosity of 1 cP.

Viscometers have a starting price of several € 1,000 and quickly reach more than € 50,000 for
intricate rheometers [28]. Such precision is not needed to characterize the viscosity of the elec-
trolyte in the proposed system. Furthermore, the testing procedure for specialized measuring
is lengthy, as it involves loading, trimming, and cleaning and is optimized for human use. The
proposed alternative is the integration of the viscosity measurement into the stirring mecha-
nism, which allows the reuse of existing components and the application across a wide range of
viscosity. There are several low-cost approaches for automated systems which can determine a
material’s viscosity. Pipette-based viscosity measurements estimate the viscosity with 6% toler-
ance, but are only applicable up to 10,000 cP [29]. Other previous works have investigated low
cost alternatives for viscosity measurements based on rotational viscometers [20], [30]–[32].

2.2
Mixing
Mixing aims to transform a heterogeneous set of compounds into a more homogeneous unit.
Mixing can be categorized into passive and active. Passive mixing involves manipulating the
geometry of the mixing vessel and inlet to allow mixing via natural diffusion [38]. Active mixing
involves applying an external energy source to create an electric, magnetic, pressure, acoustic,
or thermal field to mix a compound.
Mixing techniques usually employ more than one method to enhance the degree of mixing.
Blending miscible or soluble liquid-liquid solutions is a fairly simple process. Molecular dif-
fusion and natural convection will eventually lead to homogeneity, and forced convection can
speed up this process [39], [40].
However, systems with non-soluble liquid-liquid or liquid-solid solutions require more advanced
procedures to achieve homogeneity. Here, several phenomena complicate the mixing procedure:
• Insolubility, requiring emulsification for permanent mixing.
• Agglomeration, an increase in particle size of solid components. To decrease the total
surface energy in a solution, particles agglomerate, which in turn inhibits the formation
of a homogeneous solution [41]. The underlying principle also causes complications in
wetting particles [42].
• Stratification: different densities of solids and liquids can cause them to settle at the bottom
or float on the top.
• Attrition: the time needed to reach a desired suspension may already induce particle attri-
tion.
• Cavern build-up: along the stirrer path, zones similar to voids are created with little re-
maining mixture solution. The mixing quality rapidly decreases with further distance from
the cavern. This is notable in high viscosity fluids with Reynolds numbers < 10.
• Weissenberg effect: a stem of the mixture is created around the central axis of the stirrer,
which experiences little agitation and shear forces. Materials with viscoelastic behaviour
exhibit this trait.
Some of these issues can be solved by running the stirrer at high agitation speed. The utilization
of certain mixer designs (e.g., impellers or anchors) and vessel geometries (such as satellite heads
instead of flat-bottomed vessels) can further improve these characteristics. Latter can increase
the average medium’s velocity by 10-20% [43, 342ff.].
Several of the described phenomena arise from multiple causes, one being the presence of laminar
flow at low Reynolds numbers. In such circumstances, disrupting the laminar flow to a more
turbulent or elongation flow is needed. This can be achieved via chopping, cutting, dicing, etc.

2.3
Cleaning
High-throughput autonomous labs require adequate cleaning between each new iteration. In a
chemical context, ’clean’ means being free of contaminants so that new experiment rounds can
be conducted in pure conditions. Providing a 100% clean environment is achievable but requires
diligent methods, where the time and procedural steps might not be suited for high-throughput
experimentation (HTE / HTP). Thus, it is important to strike a balance between purity and speed
[56].
Cleaning in manual experimentation is done via good practice procedures and local cleaning
etiquette, which are only partly documented and rely on the respective researcher’s intuition to
assess if the part (e.g., beaker or spoon) has been sufficiently cleaned.
Autonomous labs need their own cleaning procedures when parts are reused for the next ex-
periment iteration [57]. The outcome should ideally be verified autonomously to guarantee a
desirable cleanliness level. The automation also brings common advantages of precision and re-
peatability, as well as the use of wider temperature ranges, mechanical movements, and cleaners
[58]. A sensible cleaning routine can further prolong the system’s lifespan and reliability [58].
As the wider adoption of autonomous labs is still picking up, new cleaning processes have to be
created and documented.

to the stirring magnet, a fixed stirrer also offers more precise motion control and can easily be
adjusted to the container’s geometry. Taking inspiration from the polymer industry, a helical
impeller is used to achieve an elongation flow regime [35].
This type of impeller, as can be seen in Figure 3.13 enables the scraping along the container’s wall.
This causes high velocity regions along the wall and lower velocity regions close to the centre
of the axial rotation [43, 332f]. In these low velocity regions a stem caused by the Weissenberg
effect could develop in the mixture. Thus, the geometry of the impeller is optimised to reduce
the Weissenberg effect and enhance mixing throughout all regions of the mixture.

Gain parameter search
While a ’free speed’ motor produces the highest shear rates, fixing
the speed with a PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) control loop allows for clearer compar-
isons across samples. This makes the interpretation of a change in current more meaningful, as
predictions about the motor’s underlying load torque (𝑇𝐿) can be handled more easily. Thus, a
PID control loop has been integrated into the microcontroller’s logic. PID control is an effective
and simple tool for controlling a motor’s speed by altering the PWM percentage (𝑃𝑊𝑀%), and
is used extensively in industrial applications. A schematic of a PID control loop can be seen in
Figure 3.16.
A PID control loop in essence repeatedly sums three terms and compute steering outputs. The
proportional term computes the immediate error (𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑖), that is the error between the set point
(here, the reference speed 𝜔𝑟𝑒𝑓) and the process variable (here, the actual speed 𝜔𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑢𝑎𝑙). The inte-
gral term sums the error (𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑖) in each time interval (Δ𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒). And the derivative term computes
the difference between the error (𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑖) in the current measurement and the error (𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑖−1) in the
last measurement, with respect to the elapsed Δ𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒. Each of the terms is weighted with a gain
parameter: 𝑘𝑝, 𝑘𝑖, and 𝑘𝑑respectively. The weighting is unique to most systems. In this case, the
response of the motor mostly depends on the motor characteristics, the stirrer, and the medium
being stirred, and thus the viscosity. Finding the parameters that best suit the underlying system
is crucial and can be optimised by reducing the mean squares of errors (MSE) (∑𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟2
𝑖) for the
set gain parameters [67].
As the space of potential parameters is vast they have been tuned in a two-step mapping process.
First, only combinations of 𝑘𝑝in the range of 0.4 to 3 and 𝑘𝑑in the range 0 to 3 have been assessed
by gradually increasing them and noting the MSE of each. For the combinations of 𝑘𝑝and 𝑘𝑑with
the 10 lowest MSE values values, a range of 𝑘𝑖values between 0 and 3 has been investigated. For
both loops, the stirrer was spun in a 20 g, 5 wt% Xanthan mixture for 15 seconds per iteration
with a 2-second resting time in between the respective iterations. The reference speed was set
to 𝜔𝑟𝑒𝑓= 330𝑟𝑝𝑚. The 𝑒𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟𝑖represents the errors of the later 70% of speed measurements

Viscosity limit
The system needs to evaluate when the viscosity is too high to process the mixture further. The
explored limit in viscosity ensures that all functionalities discussed in this work will remain
usable under that condition. Thus, the workstations ’mixing’, ’electrode testing’, and ’cleaning’
must be able to perform their functions at a given viscosity value. The following criteria have
been defined: 1) The mixer must not stall during its operation; 2) no other mechanical issues must
occur during operation; 3) the mixture must be adequately stirred to allow for the detection of
changes in homogeneity; 4) during the retrieval of the mixer, a minimum of 90% of the mixture’s
mass must remain in the container to ensure sufficient volume for electrode submersion; 5) the
mixture must not contain an excessive amount of bubbles; and 6) the mixture must be cleanable
with the high-pressure cleaner. During the tests, initially unmixed 20 g mixtures of 10, 12.5, 15,

3.8
Cleaning
3.8.1
Proposed system
The viscous behaviour of the electrolyte limits the applicability of the cleaning procedures in the
established system. Decreasing the viscosity of the mixture using only a water and acid based
cleaning procedure proved ineffective in initial prototyping, as the mixture could not be liquefied
enough to pour the waste away. Thus, the proposed mechanisms have been developed:
High-pressure cleaning
Should the mixture be assessed as viscous enough, the container is moved towards the high-
pressure cleaning area. Here, most of the mass of the electrolyte is removed with three, 5 sec-
onds long bursts of water. The water is pressurised to 10.5 MPa from a ’Scheppach High-pressure
cleaner HCE1400’ and then ejected through a thin nozzle, pictured on the right in Figure 3.17.
Spray and vapour are sealed by docking the container onto the spray protection as seen in Fig-
ure 3.17 on the right; the docking point is sealed via an O-ring and the waste flows out of an outlet
at the bottom of the spray protection. High-pressure cleaning might be considered forceful, but
it provides redundancy to adequately abrade even highly viscous or already solidified samples,
as solvent might have evaporated too soon. The target of the high-pressure cleaner is to reduce
visual stains to 0.1% of the mass which was present in the pre-cleaning process. Another tech-
nique to clean the container from viscous matter was also investigated. Here, water was added
and stirred with the already homogenous and viscous matter, to try to lower the overall viscosity
and enable the pouring of the mixture into the waste container. A 5 wt% Xanthan gum mixture
was investigated with an initial mass of 20 g and an additional 15 g of water added. After stirring
the mixture for 15 minutes, it was however not possible to pour the mixture away; thus, the
technique was rejected.

Rinsing of stirrer and container
Afterwards, the container is moved to the mixing location, is filled with ethanol and the stirrer
turns for a certain duration. The procedure was tested for removing most visible stains from
the container and stirrer. As the stirrer cannot be cleaned with the HPC, visible residues must
be removed from the tip and blade during this step. For a test, the stirrer was stirred in 99.9%
ethanol for 1 minute and 10 minutes respectively and evaluated visually for the stain removal
effectiveness.

After emptying the container, the stirrer and container are submerged in an ultrasonic bath for
60 seconds in half-wave mode to remove electrolyte from hard-to-reach areas and provided Fig-
ure 3.18. Full-wave mode is not chosen to prevent the other electrical components from damage.
The container has a 4 cm offset to the rotational axis of the ’rotational stepper’ to allow full
submersion into the ultrasonic bath.

5
Conclusion
This research addressed several research problems in the development of democratised, self-
driving laboratories for handling viscous materials, which is particularly important for polymer
electrolytes. The main goal was to create a system that can handle viscous matter. This goal was
achieved by developing a system, called Viscotron, which can handle Non-Newtonian substances
with a zero-shear viscosity 𝜂0 of up to 482,902 cP with an infinite shear viscosity 𝜂∞of up to
3,032 cP. This exceeds the initial goal of 100,000 cP and provides a foundation for future work on
handling substances with other rheological properties.
The primary goal to design and engineer a solution which can handle viscosity has been achieved
by offering a system, Viscotron, which can handle viscosity up to a simplified zero shear viscosity
of 𝜂0 = 482, 902𝑐𝑃, surpassing the set goal of 100,000 cP.
The system’s integration includes the control and communication between the hardware and
software, and it was designed for easy reproducibility with its democratised approach. The cost
of the overall system, excluding the Ultrasonicator, is 7,830.55 DKK (€ 1,049.67).
The Viscotron is a portable device which can easily be fitted within a glovebox, simplifying the
operation for research and striving to offload manual tasks. The system offers five unique mod-
ules for the specific use case of high viscosity handling, which are integrated in one closed system,
but could also be used as stand-alone solutions. Each of the modules has been investigated for
its application window and the limits of the respective modules were highlighted.
Dispensing and weighing of powder has been integrated into the workflow, expanding the
chemical component space with solid powders, which can be weighed and precisely controlled.
A major novel approach of the Viscotron is the use of an in-situ transport method for viscous
matter. The matter is not pumped throughout the system as in previous systems, but remains
in one container, which itself moves to the respective modules, simplifying the complex flow of
viscous mixtures. The mixing procedure of viscous matter has been optimised for that concept
by using a helical impeller with a small tip. This geometry produced the best mixing results by
increasing the shear rate in the mixture and lowering the adherence of the mixture on the stirrer
after removal from the container. The degree of homogeneity was approximated by monitoring
the motor’s current draw. While it was not possible to easily extract the state of mixing from
the current data, mixing with the stirrer greatly increased the speed of homogenisation of the
mixture. Further optimisation of the mixing procedure to reduce agglomeration, increase the
speed of mixing and improve the stirrer’s retrieval from the container will improve the system
further. Verifying the homogeneity of a mixture more exactly could be a useful module for au-
tomation procedures, as time-based verification is impractical for in-line processes. The overall
viscosity of the mixture was assessed with current measurements during the mixing procedure.
While initial tests using a free-running motor yielded inconclusive data, the implementation of a
PID control loop to maintain a constant motor speed showed a more reliable correlation between
motor current and mixture viscosity. At high viscosities (between 2.5 wt% to 10 wt% Xanthan
gum), the method proved sufficiently selective to differentiate between high-viscosity samples,
establishing a functional decision criterion. For a 20 ml sample, an average current above 140
mA at 330 rpm indicates samples should be kept in the high viscosity testing in the Viscotron.
At a lower current, the in-line use of AutoProBE could be established, integrating different SDL
workflows. A novel automated cleaning procedure was established to reuse the equipment with
in-situ operation, reducing chemical waste and improving the sustainability of laboratories. The
four-step cleaning process includes an effective removal of highly viscous and solid matter from

the container by high-pressure cleaning. Cleaning the stirrer in Ethanol is effective, and can be
optimised in future iterations of the system to reduce the time spent on cleaning. The initial
research brought up the lack of cleaning verification methods. The current system follows prac-

Viscotron demonstrates the application of viscous matter in autonomous laboratories and show-
cases the potential of automating previously manual tasks. The new applicability of viscosity
in autonomous laboratory workflows could drive forward multiple research applications which
previously had no access to affordable, automated systems. The next steps would be the in-
tegration of electrodes into the system and the introduction of chemicals for actual electrolyte
testing. While the use for polymer electrolytes, to which the system was tailored, is the obvious
domain, other domains might also benefit from its functionality. The deployment of the system
into an experimental setup enables more precise, repeatable, and quicker experimentation, while
providing an easy integration of proprietary (un)successful experimental data for computational
research methods. This could accelerate various research topics and free up valuable time for
the researcher.
