ABSTRACT (ENGLISH) 
The purpose of this thesis is to research current methods in procedural map generation, with a focus on 2D 
environments, as well as decide on the best way to create a game map using selected methods. The thesis will 
describe several different methods and techniques required for procedurally generating a map, and cover methods 
used by selected games, as well as the ones chosen for the final prototype created throughout the writing of this 
thesis. 
This thesis will cover some of the most used methods in procedural 2D map generation, as well as methods with 
potential to improve upon the field. The aim of the project is to find suitable methods to create a Pokémon style 
world entirely through procedural generation. 
To generate 2D worlds of various terrains, different noise functions were used and examined.  
A prototype containing different techniques was created and tested by several people. The testers showed great 
interest in the project, and suggested many uses for the final demo, or parts of its functionality, so my goals have 
been reached for this project.

INTRODUCTION 
In using Procedural Content Generation (PCG), methods and rules are used to generate various things. It is a 
process where a computer – rather than a person – creates information or data. In games it is mostly used for 
adding variety to item placements, textures, map generation, and even NPC personalities and AI [1]. In its most 
basic form, it manages this through a controlled randomization of predefined content. There are many benefits to 
PCG, first of all that it decreases the amount of manual labour required by game- and graphics designers, by 
pseudo-randomizing any part of the game, to create more variation from limited tools. 
Examples of games that utilize this method to a great effect includes the award-winning Minecraft [2] and 
Spelunky [3], as well as games such as Super Meat Boy [4], Spore [5], Diablo [6], Path of Exile [7], and TinyKeep [8]. 
Minecraft creates the world as you progress throughout, whereas other games, such as Animal Crossing, creates a 
world upon starting the game, allowing for the player to feel attached to a game-world that was created personally 
for them. 
Procedurally generating or changing up content would also be very useful for games such as Stardew Valley [9], 
which is a Farm-life RPG, in which a large part of the story revolves around taking care of your now deceased 
grandfather’s farm. It applies some randomization to dungeons, but applying it to the overworld as well would add 
many more gameplay hours.  
This project will focus on map-generation, specifically in a 2D environment, and uses Pokémon as a reference for 
the layout and feel of the map. Different techniques will be employed, and the final product will be a ‘game’ which 
has a certain degree of customizability and flexibility by allowing the ‘player’ to set the input parameters for the 
generated maps. 
1.1 
CHALLENGES OF PROCEDURAL GENERATION 
A lot of progress has been made in the field of procedural generation in general, but there are still a lot of 
challenges to overcome. 
One of the biggest ones is the lack of control over the results: as shown in section Tests [8], and in Implementation 
[7], the resulting appearance of the map is difficult to anticipate, especially when several different techniques and 
functions are working together. In this project, for example, I had one function for mountains (floodfilling areas 
with appropriate height values), another for rivers, another for placing trees or cities – all of these have to come 
together in the end to create a sensible map, but one technique may change the results of another completely. I 
could either backtrack and change the shapes recursively, which is a slippery slope, or do some minor changes to 
the shapes of biomes, mountains, rivers, etc., as I was placing the correct transition tiles. 
This lack of control is a general issue in procedural generation, and is one of the main reasons that it is not more 
widely used: in some ways, the developer has to spend just as much time, if not more, in creating the procedural 
generation techniques as they would in creating the game manually with predefined assets. The benefits, of 
course, is replayability, as well as individually-tailored and unique gameplay. 
1.2 
WHAT IS A MAP? 
Maps come in many different forms. In 2D games, the map is 2-dimensional, flat, and the players tends to only be 
able to move along this surface. For 2D games, most studies in procedural map generation focuses on dungeons, 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
2 
 
which are often simpler, compared to the focus of this thesis, which will be on procedural generation of overworld 
tile-maps in a 2-dimensional setting; this means that generation of the map will have to take several different tile-
types as well as their relationship to each other into consideration. 
An overworld map is an outdoors area, whereas dungeon maps are indoors, often underground. In their simplest 
forms, dungeon map are tunnels with only ground and walls, and overworld maps are wide open areas, usually 
with varying types of terrain. 
Maps in the Pokémon style are tile-based, with each tile being of equal size. The effect is much like a grid. The 
player-character’s movement is also tile based. 
1.3 
CONTRIBUTION 
This thesis examines a novel approach to generating overworld maps. The novelty of this project lies in the fact 
that though many procedural generation techniques, such as noise functions [see Noise 4.1.2], have been used to 
create various things, the number of attempts at creating a procedurally generated world is still limited, as well as 
worth additional research. 
My inspiration for this project comes partly from the surge in popularity for 2D rogue-like games, such as 
Undertale [10] and Pokémon [11], as well as the ease of which such games can be created, and the reusability of 
any code made for such a game. The increasing amount of Indie-developers often focus on 2D environments, and 
many casual game-creators do so as well: one such example can be found at Pokecommunity.com, which plays 
host to a large amount of Pokémon fan-games, ranging from mere story ideas, games under development, and 
finished products. 2D games are a good place for any developer to start, and many stories can with success be told 
with such a medium. 
The results of this thesis could possible benefit interactive computer games, and people with little programming 
knowledge, as well as show the effects of several different techniques useful for procedural world generation that 
would benefit any game. 
1.4 
OBJECTIVES 
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate current procedural techniques, and develop an approach 
therefrom, which can be used to generate 2D maps of any size.  
Secondly, the resulting approach should allow for user interaction and input to change the visuals of the resulting 
maps.  
Thirdly, this project aims to develop a product that can be used by level designers and people with little-or-no 
programming knowledge. 
Fourth, can the project, of parts of it, be of use to designers and programmers? 
Finally, if the tool is not useful to users directly, I hope that the product can be used as a way to gain inspiration for 
creating their own maps.

RELATED WORK 
This section describes related games that rely on procedural generation to design their game levels. Their 
generation methods were researched to gain an overview of current methods and trends. Games that are 
primarily 3D were also considered, so as to evaluate if methods most commonly used in 3D map generation could 
also be used in 2D generation with benefit. 
2.1.1 
TINYKEEP 
The game, TinyKeep [8], is a 3D top-down dungeon crawler that stands out not only with its procedurally 
generated dungeons, but with its versatile gameplay. It was developed by PhiGames, and applies a unique method 
for generating the dungeons, which the developer has talked about at length and explained in reddit posts [13]. His 
goal was to create a generator which creates a dungeon which keeps the rooms close enough to easily make 
corridors between them. 
 
Figure 2 - TinyKeep Gameplay 
Tiny Keep’s creator explains that the game takes a number of cells to be generated and spawns a rectangle of 
random width and length for each. To have some amount of variation in room sizes, games often use random 
numbers to decide the size, but the developer instead used Park-Miller Normal Distribution to ensure that the 
cells/rectangles were also more likely to be smaller, resulting in fewer large rooms compared to the amount of 
cells; this becomes useful in the final stages of the generation, as it enables the smaller cells to become L-shaped 
corridors to connect the rooms. 
In order to create the pathways between the rooms, the game uses a separation steering behaviour to keep the 
cells close without overlapping, and then calculates the center points of each rectangle using Delaunay 
Triangulation[4.3], which results in a graph in which every single room is connected to each other. This process is 
likely to be easier with a physics engine such as Unity, which can add solid bodies to each room. 
A Minimal Spanning Tree creates a graph in which every room is reachable, but not connected to every other 
room, but this leaves the player with only one set path to take. To ensure a more interesting design, around 15% of 
the connections from the Delaunay graph are added to the new graph. Finally, the nodes are connected with 
vertical or horizontal lines, or L-shapes, depending on their positions. The smaller cells that are not large enough to 
be considered rooms are added to the corridors that these lines form. See second figure below; blue rooms are 
cells which coincided with corridor lines. 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
6 
 
 
Figure 3 - TinyKeep Dungeon Creating Method: Main connections are established for all rooms, as well as secondary connections 
 
Figure 4 - TinyKeep Dungeon Creating Method: Final dungeon, rooms that do not overlap with lines connecting the rooms are deleted 
By using Delaunay Triangulation, the game has greater control of the number of rooms in the dungeon than other 
techniques, such as Cellular Automata[4.1.1], would allow. In addition, the result is much more organic and 
natural-looking than the simple grid-based solutions. 
This method is very useful for dungeon creation, but it might not hold the same value for an open overworld map; 
however, if the map has to contain certain areas, and these have to be interconnected to a certain degree while 
still preserving a level of maze-like qualities, then this method could be very useful. 
2.1.2 
PATH OF EXILE 
Path of Exile is an online RPG developed by Grind Gear Games. It is similar to the Diablo series, in that the player 
controls a chosen character through dungeons. The game contains both overworld maps and dungeons, but this 
analysis will focus on how the game handles its overworld. 
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
7 
 
 
Figure 5 - Path of Exile Gameplay 
The development team uses procedural generation to, amongst other things, allow level designers to define 
certain expectations for a level – a river here, a wall there, etc. – without having to place trees and terrain-tiles 
individually.  The designer only adds a line [see Figure 6 a below] for the location of the wall, and then lets the 
procedural environment generator randomize the level design, while still maintaining the overall layout. 
In the case of a wall, the generator uses macro-node permutation to pick semi-random places for the nodes of the 
wall line to go (figure b below), and then follows up with macro-edge permutation (figure c), in which the edge is 
divided up into segments, and each is given a random weighting.  
 
Figure 6 - Path of Exile: creating a semi-random bends in walls and rivers 
This method results in a very fluid line, which is translated into a Bezier curve [4.5], and the game matches the 
closest point between the curve and the connection points, and uses this to look up which of their tile-prefabs to 
use for this segment of the wall [see Figure 6 d above]. 
When creating rivers, the game splits the initial line immediately following macro-edge permutation and rounds 
the resulting edges to create slightly randomized river-width (figure d above). 
The game uses a Voronoi diagram[4.4], which divides the map into polygon-shaped cells corresponding to the 
closest paths to each macro-node. Each cell consists of the nodes in the graph that are closer to the macro-node 
than the other nodes, so the developers know that they can move the node within this cell/space all they want 
without risking an edge of a river crossing a wall, when it is not supposed to. The Voronoi diagram is also important 
for the road-generation, since it relies on a pathfinding algorithm that benefits from the Voronoi structure. 
One of the reasons that 3D games often use polygon-based meshes, such as voronoi diagrams, to compute them, 
is because it allows them to avoid having to make many predefined tiles; the downside is that the transitions 
between polygons can often seem jagged. 
==PAGE:18==
8 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
8 
 
The use of Bézier curves for what is essentially similar to auto-tiling[4.2] is very interesting, and might be useful in 
a 2D map as well, especially since it allows for making an overall structure for a level, and leaving the rest to be 
generated. 
2.1.3 
SPELUNKY 
Spelunky is an award-winning roguelike platform game where the player has to navigate levels of sixteen rooms 
and collect treasures, until they reach the final room. It uses procedural generation for virtually every aspect of its 
level generation, including but not limited to room structure, item placements, and enemy placements. It was 
created by Derek Yu, as freeware for Microsoft Windows. 
 
Figure 7 - Spelunky Level Design 
Initially, each level is divided into a 4x4 grid of 16 rooms and a path is drawn from the entrance of the level to the 
exit.  
The four different kinds of rooms are: 
0 
A side room 
1 
A room with exits left and right 
2 
A room with exits left, right, and bottom, and up, if the 
above room is also a ‘2’ 
3 
A room with exits left, right, and top 
Each ‘room’ is selected from a set of 4 different types, depending on where on the path it is located, and parts of 
the room is randomized to add variation to the gameplay. Finally, items (traps, enemies, etc.) are added to the 
rooms. 
Initially, every room is a 1, but as the solution path is calculated, the room type changes accordingly. The solution 
path is found by picking a random number, 1 – 5, from a normal distribution; for 1 or 2, the path goes left, for 3 or 
4, the path goes right, and on a 5, the path goes down. If it hits the edge of the grid, it goes down and switches its 
direction. Every remaining room/grid space that is not part of the solution path are designated as ‘0’, which does 
not have any entrances or exits. 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
9 
 
From here, each room type has 8 to 16 templates to choose from, which consist of basic room layouts with a 
number of static tiles -which will always be of a certain type- and probabilistically determined tiles. Each template 
room is a 10x8 grid of tiles, and each level contains 16 rooms.  
The resulting solution path also affects the choice enemies and dangers in the level; for example, a completely 
vertical line across the grid heightens the chances that one of those rooms will lead to a snake pit [Figure 8 
below],the room generation will place a snake pit-room somewhere underneath the vertical line of rooms, and 
have rooms above it designed to let the player fall in. 
 
Figure 8 - Final Level design's effect on the map 
The game also utilizes different procedural generation algorithms to add more variation to the level style and 
platforming design, so each play-through feels unique. 
This simplified version of procedural generation and pathfinding is possible because the game is 2-dimensional, 
every level only consists of sixteen rooms of set types, and the pathfinding algorithm does not need to be very 
complex. Pathfinding in a more expanded 2D game would require a more involved solution than in Spelunky, 
where a simple algorithm that only checks the nearest neighbour-rooms, and does not need to ensure that the 
path is of a certain length, is more than enough to fulfil the requirements for the game. 
2.1.4 
SHATTERED PLANET 
Shattered Planet is developed by Kitfox Games, and resembles a roguelike survival RPG. The player must navigate a 
‘shattered’ planet Earth to find a cure to virus. 
 
Figure 9 - Shattered Planet Gameplay 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
10 
 
The game has a ‘shopping list’ of possible rooms, which decide the overall size-range of the room. These prefab-
rooms also decide the main ‘critical’ paths, as well as the ‘non-critical’ side-paths. The development team settled 
on these standards by creating handmade levels and evaluating the most natural-looking ones based on player 
feedback. 
The map layout is partly decided by Cellular Automata, but utilizes geometric decay afterwards on tiles along the 
edges. The probability of geometric decay changes based on the tiles’ neighbours, and exceptions are made for 
significant tiles, such as doors. 
 
Figure 10 - Shattered Planet: decaying geometry to create final map design 
A modified version of Perlin noise [4.1.2.1] is used when applying texture to make the transition of texture feel 
more natural. 
To make the levels feel more realistic, the game makes use of Cellular Automata yet again to decide the probability 
of each edge-tile having a ‘crumbly’ tile, which is purely for decoration and inherits the texture of the tile. 
The team used a depth-first maze search algorithm (which explores in every direction, as far as it can: it is ensured 
to find the shortest path, but at a high cost) in conjunction with a pathfinding algorithm [see section 4.7.1 on A* 
pathfinding] to figure out where to place obstacles. The first algorithm is used for traversing graph structures, and

4.1.1 
CELLULAR AUTOMATA 
Cellular automaton [14] is a discrete model used in several fields, such as computability theory and theoretical 
biology. In short, it is a grid of cells that are governed by certain rules. In gaming, it is often used to create maps, 
especially for dungeon crawlers. It was first described by John Von Neumann in 1940 and was used by John 
Conway in 1970 in his simulation ‘Game Of Life’, which takes an initial input (a so-called seed) from the player and 
creates an evolving system based upon the seed and four rules for behaviour set in the code. Cellular automata is 
also referred to as ‘cellular spaces’, ‘iterative arrays’, and ‘tessellation structures’. 
The model consists of a regular grid of cells, of which each is in a finite number of states; this could be something 
as simple as ‘dead’ or ‘alive’. Each cell is part of a neighbourhood, which includes its neighbouring cells up to a set 
distance (such as 2 or cells from the center cell). 
There is a wide variety of ways to implement a cellular automaton, as it depends on the rules that the developer 
sets, but in a short example from gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com, the writer randomly sets each cell to dead or 
alive, and then sets a deathlimit that kills cells if they are surrounded by less than a certain amount of dead (wall) 
cells, and sets them to alive (ground) if they have enough alive neighbours. Finally, they tweak the code by adding 
input-variables that change the chance of alive cells, the death- and alivelimit, as well as the number of times the 
simulation is run, which makes the map less rough at higher values (see figure below: a) cellular automata, b): a) 
after 10 steps). 
 
Figure 18 - Cellular Automata. a: 1 step, b: 10 steps [14] 
Since this method leaves us with code that counts how many neighbours a cell has, the finished map can be easily 
filled with objects in whatever way is suitable by checking what kinds of tiles the square is surrounded by. If a 
ground tile is surrounded by walls, it may be a good location to place a hidden treasure. 
Cellular Automata is a tested and tried method which is often used in cave generation; however, it is based on a 
Boolean principle, and as such generally works best with caves, where the choice of tiles often falls between the 
simple choice of ‘wall or ground?’. Utilizing several layers of cellular automata could rectify this, but ultimately the 
result would be considerably inorganic. 
4.1.2 
NOISE 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
16 
 
Procedural noise is very important to PCG; partly because PCG is often reliant on semi-randomness to achieve 
natural looking results.  
Often, noise simply refers to ‘white noise’, which consists of random number within a certain range, with no 
regular distribution pattern. This sort of noise is not very useful in procedural generation, as it is in-consistent, 
which means that it is difficult to use it to generate content that fulfils certain requirements. 
A procedural noise function is a type of gradient noise that aims to recreate a natural pattern of ‘noise’ with 
consistent results, which seems random, but is not, so it is usually referred to as pseudo-random. A noise function 
is essentially a seeded pseudo-random number generator, which produces a certain number if given a certain 
seed. This is different from simply using any random number, as it adds some meaning to the madness. The noise 
function is generally made by adding several functions with different frequencies and amplitudes together. 
There are three categories of procedural noise: lattice gradient noise, explicit noise, and sparse convolution noise 
[15]. The first is the most used, and includes successful noise generation functions such as Perlin noise, described 
in the following section. The second, explicit noise, tend to cost more memory to store, so they are not as popular. 
Sparse convolution noise does not work with pseudo random numbers, and only sums the values of several 
random values. It costs less memory, but it is not as fast to create. 
Ideally, a procedural noise function should result in repeatable pseudorandom values, with a range from -1.0 to 
1.0. No obvious patterns should be visible. 
4.1.2.1 
PERLIN NOISE 
In games, Perlin noise [16] is the go-to algorithm, and it is typically used to generate textures, or blend together 
existing textures in natural-looking transitions, as well as generating height maps. It is also used for tiling grids to 
simulate organic regions. 
 
Figure 19 - Perlin Noise used to blend textures [16] 
Perlin noise was invented by Ken Perlin in 1985, and is a ‘lattice noise’, which means that it consists of using noises 
with different values – amplitude and frequency – and sum all of them up to a single noise. 256 pseudorandom 
gradients are generated and used at specific coordinates. Unity offers an implementation of Perlin’s noise. 
The widely popular sandbox-game, Minecraft, uses Perlin noise to create worlds, which allows other players to use 
the same parameters to create a replica for their own games. 
An alternative to Perlin noise is Simplex noise, which was created by Ken Perlin as a direct alternative to his Perlin 
noise, and is also a lattice gradient noise, but has a lower computational complexity than Perlin noise. It uses fewer 
vertices to do the interpolation. However, it is not as often used or implemented, so I will be using the more tried-
and-tested Perlin noise as a base for my noise maps.

4.2 
AUTOMATED TILE PLACEMENT 
A tile is small image of a certain size, which can appear in various shapes, although they are usually rectangular or 
isometric, and together they form the larger map, like puzzle pieces. The word ‘tile-based’ refers to the method of 
building levels in a game, in which the code itself lays out the locations of the tiles on the map. Another benefit to 
this method is that it becomes easier to locate objects on the map. 
Procedural generation has been used often and to great effect in 2D dungeons, and in simple overworld maps, but 
since the aim of this project is to be able to make a map of the same complexity as those from the Pokémon 
franchise, I face certain issues that most dungeon creators do not. One of these issues is that placing transition 
tiles of multiple different terrain-types becomes somewhat difficult and prone to error. 
Whenever two different terrain-type tiles are placed next to one another, the game has to figure out which tile 
matches the specific transition. On an 8-bit square grid, there are 8 possible points of transition, and even for the 
simplest case, this requires 28 = 256 different transition tiles, which is extremely excessive. When using more than 
two different terrains, the computations become more complex. Below are described a couple of methods that 
were considered for the automated placement of transition tiles. 
4.2.1 
TRANSPARANCY 
One of the best methods to simplify auto-tiling and lessen the work-load in designing tiles, is to overlay tiles with 
transparent half-tiles, or gliding transition using noise (see Figure 19). This project will be using the former.  
This method is particularly useful, as it not only limits the amount of transition tiles to one set, which can be 
overlaid on any other tile, but also makes it easy to add new ground-types to the tiles selection, since the designers 
will not need to take the old transition tiles into consideration, since the new transition tiles will simply be overlaid 
on the base tile. 
The order of priority given to each tile-type is further explained in section Implementation - Autotiling. It is used in 
combination with Tile Bitmasking, which is described in the following section. 
4.2.2

Tile Bitmasking (or Neighbour Aware Tile Selection) can be used to auto-tile a level. Using this method allows the 
program to only draw the shape of the terrain in a primary algorithm by placing generic placeholders, and then 
have the appropriate sprites placed, by checking which tiles surround it.

4.3 
DELAUNAY TRIANGULATION 
The Delaunay triangulation of a discrete point set corresponds to the inverse of the Voronoi diagram; it is a dual 
graph, meaning that the Delaunay graph has a vertex for each face of the Voronoi graph, and shares it points with 
the Voronoi diagram. The diagram consists of lines from each point to its nearest neighbours, and each line crosses 
only one Voronoi edge, and is perpendicular to it. 
 
Figure 25 - Delaunay Triangulation: connecting the vertex of the Delaunay produces the Voronoi diagram [19] 
In pathfinding, the game generally applies a weight to each edge based on its distance to certain points on the 
map. 
Unlike Cellular automata, generating areas with Delaunay triangulation (and Voronoi Diagrams) allows the game 
have more control over the number of areas and their sizes. 
4.4 
VORONOI DIAGRAMS 
A Voronoi diagram is named after Georgy Voronoy, and is a partitioning of a plane into regions based on 
predefined points (seeds) on the plane. Each point (seed) has a corresponding region of points whose distance to 
the seed is less than or equal to any other seed. These regions are called Voronoi cells, and are obtained from the 
intersection of half-spaces. The Voronoi diagram is a graph, and is the inverse of/dual graph of the Delaunay 
triangulation of the same set of points.  
Figure below: Each line in a Voronoi region is halfway between two points. Together, all of the lines form the 
Voronoi Diagram. Each cell consists of points closest to the center point/seed. 
 
Figure 26 - Voronoi Diagram. Each line is exactly halfway between two points. [19] 
==PAGE:31==
21 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
21 
 
Voronoi Diagrams are useful for finding out a certain point’s distance to another – in games, it is often used in 
conjunction with pathfinding algorithms. Without a Voronoi diagram, locating the closest seed to another point 
would take O(n) time, but with the diagram it can be done in only O(log n) time.  
The complexity of the diagram is linear; for n sites, there are n faces, up to 2n-5 vertices and at most 3n-6 edges. It 
takes O(n2 log n) time to finish an entire voronoi diagram, unless Fortune’s Line-Sweep Algorithm is used. This 
algorithm was made by Steven Fortune in 1986, and uses a Sweep line algorithm to cut the runtime to O(n log n) 
[19]. A downside to this algorithm’s speed, is that it requires that all seeds are present before the generation of 
the graph, meaning that by adding or removing points later, the graph has to be re-generated entirely.  
The algorithm consists of a line sweeping across an area of points, and every time it encounters one, it draws a 
parabola along the sweeping line. The parabola continues to grow until it starts to overlap with a new one. 
 
Figure 27 - Fortunes Sweep Algorithm [20] 
Using graph theory with pathfinding algorithms such as A* makes traversing the nodes cheaper, since there are 
typically less nodes than in a grid-based game. In addition, the neighbouring nodes can be determined ahead of 
time, and by using an algorithm like Voronoi Diagrams, cells can be removed and inserted into the map 
dynamically. 
4.5 
BEZIER CURVE 
In procedural generation, the Bezier Curve method is commonly used for curve drawing, such as in crafting roads. 
Path of Exile uses Bezier curves on the procedurally generated shapes for walls and rivers, in order to identify 
which shapes the wall or riverbank should have at a particular point. Bézier is particularly popular in 3D games, 
since they are easy to use and implement for any kind of shape, and the product appears reasonably smooth, as 
opposed to polygonal lines. 
A Bézier curve is a parametric curve consisting of a set of control points, which is uses to align the curvature to a 
more smooth curve.

GRAPH GRAMMAR 
Graph grammar is a type of graph that manipulates graphs instead of strings. A graph, in turn, is a collection of 
nodes/vertices connected by edges. These edges can be directed or undirected.

4.7.1 
A* 
Pathfinding is about finding a path through a plane from point A to point B, which can be complicated if the path is 
littered with obstacles. 
Graph search algorithms like Dijkstra and A* work on weighted, directed graphs. In this case, it means that they 
work with sets of nodes connected by edges that have numeric weights attached to them, which works as 
information on cost of movement. Graph-based algorithms need to know locations – nodes – as well as which 
locations are connected to other locations – these would be the edges. The layout of the graph is not part of the 
graph itself, since it only deals with connections and locations. See figure below; these two graph are the same. 
Dijkstra’s algorithm work by visiting every node in the graph from the starting point or current point, until it 
reaches the goal. As long as none of the edges have a negative cost, the algorithm always finds the shortest path. 
A*, developed in 1968, is a simple but useful algorithm, because it works in a lot of different situations, and can be 
applied both to graph-structures and grid-structures. It generally works as a preconditioner that adjusts the 
weights of a graph to allow another algorithm (such as Dijkstra) to work more efficiently. It is commonly used for 
traversing graphs in video games. A* is almost always the search method of choice. This is because A* is 
guaranteed to find a shortest path on the graph. The problem with A* is that a shortest path on the graph is not 
equivalent to a shortest path in the continuous environment. 
 
Figure 28 - A* example of the same graph, but with different node placements 
A* is a best-first graph-searching algorithm, which is typically represented with the function: 
f(n) = g(n) + h(n) 
A* uses heuristic values h(n) to give an estimate of the minimum cost from any vertex n to the nearest goal vertex, 
or node. g(n) describes the true cost from the start node to the current node. In other words; the cost so far, and 
the ‘expected’ cost to the next node. Typically, whatever goal node gives the lowest f(n) value is chosen. One of 
the reason that A* is such an often implemented tool, is that many pathfinding algorithms either evaluate either 
the distance from the goal, or from the start to the current node, but A* does both. 
==PAGE:33==
23 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
23 
 
 
Figure 29 - A*: The difference between heuristic value and true cost 
If h(n) ‘admissible’ - less than or equal to the cost of moving from n to the goal vertex - it means that it does not 
overestimate the true cost of moving, and it ensures that A* will find a shortest path. If h(n) equals 0, then the A* 
algorithm functions as a Dijkstra’s algorithm. A* runs faster the higher h(n) is, but if it is high compared to g(n), 
then A* functions as a Greedy Best-First-Search, which is also similar to Dijkstra, but employs heuristics; this 
algorithm finds the node closest to the goal, and not the node closest to the starting point, and is as such not 
guaranteed to find the shortest path – if met with obstacles, GBFS will move towards the goal regardless, often 
getting caught inside the obstacles, which results in a longer path. 
This dynamic between h(n) and g(n) is very useful in games, as it allows the developer to modify either one, so 
they do not necessarily end up with the shortest path. As one article by Christer Ericson states [21], instead of 
finding the shortest, ‘admissible’ paths, focusing on finding an optimal shortest-path using ‘non-admissible’ 
heuristics would be more useful for most games, especially since the ‘admissible’ path forces A* to spend too 
much time evaluating roughly equal candidates.  
There are different ways to achieve a more dynamic trade-off between the shortest path and a more organic path, 
could be to have g(n) depend on a scaling variable, which would make the algorithm ignore terrain costs at certain 
times. 
In grid maps there are many paths of the same length, which can lead to A* checking each and every one of them – 
this is computationally heavy, and is one of the reasons A* is typically used with graphs. The best way to avoid this, 
is to employ a more dynamic solution, as mentioned in the paragraph above, by changing or adding conditions to 
h(s) or g(s). Grid-based A* can also be very advantageous for games, however, as it simplifies such things as 
directions (east, west, etc.). While graphs only have connections, but no real placement on the map, a grid is often 
better suited for game maps, since the symmetry makes direction more predictable. 
Waypoint-based: h(n) = h’(n, w1) + distance(w1, w2) + h’(w2, goal) 
For this project, the roads will only be allowed to stretch in four directions, so I will be using heuristic function 
known as Manhattan distance (L1): D * ( abs(tile.x – goal.x) + abs(tile.y – goal.y) ), where D is the minimum cost for 
moving one space; on a terrain with a minimum movement cost, this means that with each move, g(n) decreases 
by D, and h(n) increases by D.  
For this project, two alternatives to A* were considered; Jump Point Search and Hierachical Annotated A*, both of 
which I describe in the following subsections. 
4.7.1.1 
JUMP POINT SEARCH 
==PAGE:34==

Before I settled on A*, I considered some alternatives to the popular algorithm: one such alternative was Jump 
Point Search, which is an optimality-preserving technique that identifies and eliminates symmetry to make 
pathfinding faster by an order of magnitude. It was developed in conjunction with Alban Grastien and first 
appeared in Advancement of Artificial Intelligence in 2011. [22] 
Often there are several ways to reach the same target on a grid map, and many of these are symmetric, meaning 
that the only difference is the order in which the moves are made. The Jump Point Search technique prunes 
symmetric nodes. It has two rules, which check the direction of travel from a parent node to a given node, and 
prunes local neighbours. The first rule is specific for straight steps, the other for diagonal steps. (figure below: gray 
neighbours are prunes, as they can be reached optimally from the parent of x, without going through x. The black 
tiles represent obstacles, which hinders the pruning). This means that, when considering node x, all grey tiles can 
be ignored, as they can be reached by x’s parent tile without going through the x node itself (so one would assume 
that they do not represent a correct path). 
 
Figure 30 - Jump point search: a: deciding direction of travel, b: pruning neighbours that would otherwise need to be considered. c and d are 
the same situations, but with obstacles (black). [22] 
The recursive ‘jumping over’ nodes stops either when all nodes are reached, when an obstacle blocks the path, or 
when a jump point successor is found, meaning that it has neighbours that cannot be reached through an 
alternative symmetric path optimally. in the figure below, y is a jump point successor. In figure b) rule 1, straight 
steps, are considered first. 
 
Figure 31 - x is being expanded. a: there are no obstacles between x and y, so the nodes between them are not evaluated because of JPS' 
first rule. b: Applying JPS' second rule, straight steps are considered first. [22] 
One of the drawbacks of this technique is that game binds itself to certain nodes when choosing a path. However, 
the increased speed is often worth it. It also involves no pre-processing, unlike Rectangular Symmetry Reduction, 
nor does it require extra-memory overheads. 
4.7.1.2 
HIERARCHICAL ANNOTATED A* 
Another alternative to A* I considered was Hierachical Annotated A* [23], created with a mind on tactical games 
with moving units or NPC’s of varying size. HAA* is a pathfinder that first analyzes the terrain of a game map and 
==PAGE:35==
25 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
25 
 
then builds a much smaller approximate representation that captures the essential topographical features of the 
original. The point is that the algorithm can answer how much space is available for the moving unit, which is why 
the creator recommends it for games like Red Alert. It is also made with a grid map in mind, and since this project 
worked on a similar basis, it made for a tempting choice, especially since it had shown to be faster than low-level 
A*.  
However, there was no need for an advanced tactical pathfinding algorithm, and jumping over parts of the map in 
this way might have interfered with the logic dividing the level, as well as some of the minor map-corrections the 
program does while generating the map. 
4.7.2 
L-SYSTEMS 
Lindenmayer systems are sets of grammar-like substitution rules used recursively. It is mostly used for generating 
plants, but has in the past been used for generating city-roads as well; one such example is CityEngine, by Esri R&D 
Center Zurich [24].

CityEngine is a system which can model a complete city using a comparatively small set of statistical and 
geographical input, and has a high amount of flexibility which lets the user control the initial input. Figure 24 and 
Figure 25 show some of the possible results [24]. 
 
Figure 33 - CityEngine example of a roadsystem and city [24] 
I was initially very enthusiastic about L-systems, but as I described briefly in the beginning of this chapter, I 
ultimately chose not to use L-systems, since the resulting road system was not necessary for this project, and 
because I preferred to have more direct control over from where the roads began, and where they ended. I also 
==PAGE:36==

MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524

counted against L-systems after reading a rather scathing article on them [25]. As the article describes, small 
changes to the system can cause major unpredictable changes to the output: since this project required some 
amount of predictability, and since I have no previous experience working with L-systems, I judged that 
implementation would be more work than it was worth, and might not produce anything useful in the end, unless I 
tailored the entire map around the road-system, which could have had strange looking results. Additionally, L-
systems are tree-based, and games such as roguelikes and 2D tile-based RPGS are dependent on inter-connectivity, 
which trees are not suitable for. 
4.7.3 
CONCLUSION 
These two alternatives to A* (Jump Point Search and Hierarchical Annotated A*) have their benefits (being speed, 
and taking terrains into consideration) which made them very tempting, but for this project I chose the vanilla A* 
pathfinding, as it fulfils the needs of the project, and because so many people have worked with it before, that 
implementing it is a relatively simple process. 
I also considered L-systems, as they have shown promising and natural-looking results in programs like CityEngine 
and the like, but it is not as well documented, and it would leave the paths more up to chance, and give me less 
freedom to work them into the map to create ‘level’ like structures for the player. If the cities I create for the map 
had been large enough to merit it, I would have considered using L-systems for those, if not the rest of the road 
system.

7.1 
DATA HOLDER 
The data holder is a class of Tile[][], but perhaps using a different data holder would be benefactor, such as a 
binary dataholder (in which grassland would be case 1, desert case 2, etc.), however, it was more convenient to 
save all of the necessary information in the Tile class. 
The Tile class primarily exists to hold the height/heat/rain/shrubbery/population values, which are used to create 
the overall biomes, as well as the tilenumber, which is determined by the biome, and the transitiontiles, which 
overlay the tilenumber when assigned to the mesh in the TileMap class. 
The Tile class also includes bools for determining if the tile has been floodfilled for mountains, lakes, and the 
various terrain types, as well as for determining if the tile has a tree, or other kinds of vegetation, if it is a tile with 
a house, road, etc. 
Additionally, it contains the bools assignedToLevel and levelroad to determine if the tile was assigned to a level 
and/or a road after the voronoi diagram was generated. 
Finally, it holds g, f and h values for use in the pathfinding algorithm. 
7.2

7.2.1 
CONSIDERATIONS 
For the creation of the map, the simplest and most computationally-cheap method would be to only use a grid 
approach, and a more advanced method would be to involve an actively used graph-structure in the level 
generation. This decision is important since pathfinding can be used on grid-structures, but often it is 
computationally cheaper for graph-structures. 
For this project, I decided to base my work on a grid structure, and adapt it for use by path-finding algorithms, 
which I would need later in the programming phase. 
There are several grid-based solutions for creating maps in a 2D space, and many of these are more than 
acceptable for creating dungeons, which are mostly made up of walls and ground. However, when creating an 
overland map, the developer will have to take different kinds of terrain into consideration; this makes the 
generation more complex, as the algorithm has to take all of them into consideration, and not all of the terrains 
might behave in the same way. 
==PAGE:43==
33 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524

For the overall shape, I considered either assuming it was an island, and place water tiles around it, or attach non-
functional mainland part to it – however, I did not want to use up space for the map with a lot of water, so I 
decided to assume that the region was part of a larger landmass. 
The basic steps for the actual generation will be: 
- 
Using adapted Perlin Noise (Heightmaps, moisturemaps, etc.) for generating the biomes from a 
biometable. 
- 
Based on area size, location, population value, etc. add locations for cities to lists. 
- 
Simulate rainfall and change the rain values. 
- 
Use floodfilling to identify areas and their sizes, so small areas can be ‘swallowed’ by larger ones, and to 
identify mountains and mountain edges. 
- 
Start/end points for roads are generated to resemble real roads from each building in the cities. 
- 
Using A* pathfinding to create the roads, which avoids trees and such, unless they are blocking the path 
to the next midpoint. 
- 
Auto-tiling with bitmasking and transparent transition tiles. 
Although the use of noise gives an interesting landscape, one of the drawbacks is that mountains in the real world 
tend to follow a line – the places where tectonic plates meet – while the noise algorithm generates round-ish 
areas. An alternative could be to have two different functions work together and deliver half of the height values 
each, so that the result is more like a realistic world region. However, mountains in Pokémon often do the exact 
opposite, so for the purposes of this project, the noise function will suffice. 
The BiomeTable that I use to find the correct biomes based on heat and moisture is mostly arranged towards what 
looked best when I tried it out on the prototype with certain values. 
Other world generators such as dwarf fortress (and many polygon-based maps-generators) are not as specific 
about realism, the space each biome has, small details like the exact distribution of tall grass, or the width of roads 
(if they have any), which simplifies the generation. Since Pokémon maps are made to herd the player through the 
levels or zones in a specific order, and the tile sprites are also designed to come together in specific ways, the 
overall space, tile-types, and map navigability has to be taken into consideration, whereas other world maps tend 
to give the player options to remove obstacles in various ways. The Pokémon map is also, in effect, a mix between 
an open world and a dungeon. 
I intended to divide the map into areas which the player could be herded through in a manner similar to the 
games, but I did not find the time to add the logic for separating the map or creating levels in the first prototype, 
because I prioritized the generation of an open world with different biomes instead, and because the separation of 
the areas in this way does not necessarily add to the aim of the project. The function was added in the second 
prototype.

7.2.5 
NOISE 
The natural first step to generate the map is to create the biomes, which are different terrain types based on 
environmental factors. I had to find a way to get some semi-random height, moisture, temperature, population 
density, and shrubbery values that still had some sort of pattern to them, and hopefully a pattern that could be 
manipulated. 
I checked out a lot of different noise functions, such as simplex noise and blue noise, but eventually settled for a 
version of perlin noise. 
The noise function I ended up creating is based on a Unity tutorial by Sebastian Lague [32]. The function is based 
on Unity’s own function for creating noise: Mathf.PerlinNoise(); which takes two floats x, y as coordinates of a 
sample point and returns a float value between 0.0 and 1.0. The function allows me to change input values and 
receive wildly different noise maps. 
Unity’s perlin noise function is good, but the form of the noise it generates is too uniform, so this project’s noise 
function add several layers of noise to give more detail and variance. 
For a predefined range, the sample coordinates for the perlin noise function are set to ((x- or y- range) /detailScale 
* frequency). The actual noise value is set to the perlin value times the amplitude, which is multiplied by the 
persistence value. 
In the end, the noisemap is normalized using inverse lerp which returns a value between 0 and 1 (meaning that the 
values are compared to the largest and lowest values found during the procedure, and changed accordingly: a 
point halfway between the two, for example, would return 0.5).

MOUNTAINS

7.2.10 RAIN CLOUDS AND MOISTURE 
The raincloud’s rain value is added to the existing moisture value: this effects the biomes, of course, but this also 
changes the shape of the rivers, since these are designed to choose the neighbouring tile with the highest moisture 
value when picking a direction for the river. 
The raincloud logic also ensures that deserts will be more likely to appear in between mountainous regions, which 
is also common within the Pokémon genre maps. 
The RainClouds function in the MapGenerator script picks out coordinates equaling 1/10 of the map length and 
creates a map of RainClouds. The RainCloud class contains a heat factor and a rain factor: these are the values that 
they bring the tiles they pass over.  
The RainClouds function then goes through the length of the map, and checks every tile in the width of the 
raincloud, which is assumed to be maplength/10*2 tiles long.  
If the height value of the tile the raincloud is hovering over is more than 0.8, as in a mountain, and the next one is 
less, the function assumes that we are past the tip of a mountain, and decreases the rain factor and increases heat 
==PAGE:55==
45 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
45 
 
factor, since mountains generally act as barriers against rain and moisture (see section on Constructing the Map 
Foundation 7.2.6). In the opposite situation, the heat is decreased, and the moisture increased. 
If both the current tile and the next tile is a mountain, the raincloud’s heat factor is slightly increased. 
In the end, the raincloud changes the tile moisture and heat values accordingly, adjusts its position to one tile 
forward, and continues in the same manner until the end of the map. 
7.2.11 CITIES  
Cities are created from a population map, created from Unity’s Perlin noise algorithm. The areas with a population 
density value exceeding the required value are divided into tilegroups using floodfilling, and added to a list of 
population areas, which is finally sorted by order of size descending. 
My initial intention was to use the largest area is used for the ‘capital’, and the remaining areas are used for ‘small 
towns’, but due to time constraints I decided to go with the same town for each area.  
All areas are equipped with a Gym, as well as a Pokémon Center and a PokéMart, and several houses. From here 
on, based on the overall map size, a number of points are used as villages, that may or may not contain a 
PokéCenter or PokéMart (this is up to chance, to maintain the feeling of being far from the big city, and is 
dependent on whether the area is big enough for the building). 
The buildings are saved in a list of houses, which contain the house-length and -height, as well as the position of 
the door. In order to allow player to walk behind the ‘roof’-part of the building – the very top – the height of the 
house only counts from two tiles down from the top of the actual sprite, and as such, the code takes this into 
consideration when placing buildings. The ability to place buildings is dependent on the affected tiles being non-
collidable, so the main part of the building cannot be placed on mountain sides. The type of building placed is also 
dependent on the biome type.  
The building type and placement is semi-random, but within the chosen city-area. 
Finally, each city is saved in a list of cities, where the City class denotes the CityType, and a list of houses in the city. 
In order to keep cities a certain distance from each other, city areas within a certain radius of each other will be 
merged together and treated as one area. I considered a few different ways to do this; the first, and most accurate 
way to solve this problem would be to go through each tile in each city area, and compare their distance to every 
tile in every city area, and merge the cities if they are within a certain distance. However, this would require 
checking a whole lot of tiles, in eight directions, and more, to ensure that the tiles are found, even if the code only 
checks the tiles that are bordering non-city tiles. 
I settled on using a simplified version of this, where I save the maximum and minimum values of each direction, x 
and y, when first creating the list of city areas. I compare each tile in the city area that is bordering a non-city tile 
with these x and y values for the other cities, and merge the areas if the requirements are met. One of the 
downsides to this method is that certain city areas might be relatively small, but span a wider area, which gives it 
an edge in being merged with the other city areas.

7.2.13 CREATING THE MESH 
There are a variety of ways to create a tilemap in Unity, such instantiating each tile as an individual GameObject, 
but after searching Unity forums, I reached the conclusion that instantiating the map as one mesh was the best 
solution. It might be computationally easier to only create smaller meshes behind the player character, and 
instantiating and deleting them as the player moves around, but this would make it impossible for users to see the 
full results of the generation, which was part of the aim of this project. 
Using smaller meshes does not improve generation times. They remain largely unchanged, until I tried larger maps 
(with smaller meshes), at which points the generation increased. 
The mesh creation is based upon the tutorials on meshes and tilemaps by quill18 [34]. 
The BuildMesh function takes a set of vertices, UVs, normal and triangles and turns them into a mesh in Unity. 
They have to be assigned in a specific order for Unity to understand the connection to each other. The script 
TileMap calls the BuildMesh function last, after the map information has been generated and the tile graphics 
assigned to the textures. 
Unity uses 2-3 components to create a mesh: 
 
Mesh Filter: this is used to create the core mesh consisting of vertices, faces and normal. 
 
Mesh Renderer: this renders the Mesh Filter and makes it visible. 
 
Mesh Collider: This add a collider to the mesh, which makes it able to interact with other objects in the 
game environment.  
Before generating the map, the TileMap script calculates the number of necessary meshes in the createTileMap 
function, and the BuildMesh function uses this information when instantiating the mesh in the correct position in 
the world, and when it assigns the correct textures.

For the player movement, I simply used the GetTile(Tile t) functions from the MapGenerator script whenever the 
player presses a movement key; using the information stored in the Tile class, the game decides if the player can 
be moved to this location or not. For appearances sake, the movement is done over time, with some general 
animation, and movement is only allowed in 4 directions, and only in one direction at a time. The 
CharacterController script does not accept new input until the current movement has been finished. 
Instead of manually getting a tile and checking it at each movement, I considered using Unity’s box colliders, and 
place them in the correct locations through the same script that applies the final map texture: TileMap.cs. 
However, I wanted the code to be easy to adapt to another game engine, so I wanted to avoid using something so 
Unity specific; I used Unity’s Perlin noise for my noisemaps, but this function is fairly easy to recreate in other 
engines, unlike box colliders, which are not so easily replicated. Additionally, I wanted to rely on the generated 
tilemap information for moving the character, so that it would not only be easier to judge what terrain the player 
is walking on (which decides what the player animations should be), and so that the code would be more versatile 
and easier to adapt if there was ever a need to use pathfinding with the player character (or any NPC’s for that 
matter) in the future. 
The player character, or PlayerPrefab, contains only basic walking animations in this prototype, and they are 
assigned and initiated through Unity’s animation mechanic, which allows the developer to easily assign certain 
animations to initiate if certain conditions are met (usually a bool or a value is changed from a script). Due to how 
Unity’s animation system works, and my limited experience with it, the animation sometimes lag behind or change 
unexpectedly. 
One of the drawbacks to this ‘realistic’ movement, is that moving around the entire map takes a long time.

7.3.4 
LEVEL CREATION 
This section describes how I divided the larger map into smaller sections for the purpose of treating each as an 
individual level or area. This feature is also implemented as a way to show how procedural generation could be 
used to create a fully functional game from the ground up. 
7.3.4.1 
BRAINSTORMING 
One of the more challenging tasks of this project was to find a way to divide the map into ‘level’ areas. As 
mentioned in a previously, a Pokémon map has the appearance of an open world map, but each sub region tends 
to have its own particular climate, so any algorithm dividing the map into levels would ideally have to take the 
overall climate into consideration, but this might be difficult. Below follows some of the solutions I considered: 
My first idea included using a Greedy search, which would expand from a given seed until it fulfilled certain 
requirements, such as the number of tiles required in the level. In a thesis by Nathaniel Buck [35], the writer uses a 
method similar to this one, in his thesis “Procedural Content Generation in Strategy/Role-Playing Games”, in which 
he faces a similar issue to mine when he tries to generate a large open world 2D map, which has to be divided into 
levels, with each having different biomes and environments, and in the finished game will have to play different 
roles in the game. He originally made all of these areas in one go, using the greedy search, and picked the best 
areas for his cities: however, he then ran into the issue of connecting them, which eventually lead him to create 
each area one at a time, to ensure connectivity. 
I considered drawing inspiration from the method described in the previous section on TinyKeep[2.1.1], in which 
the developer generated a large number of rectangular areas, chose the largest as rooms, and then used the 
smaller ones as connecting corridors; however, with this method the generated rooms would not necessarily fit 
into the overall map that the project creates. However, dividing the map into individual parts in this way from the 
beginning would also make it easier to repurpose the code to create individual areas or levels, rather than one 
open world map: however, in this project I focused on creating one large, open map. Otherwise, the user could 
have taken advantage of the code to decide the order of each individual area more easily. The empty space 
leftover between the ‘rooms’ could have been filled out with a few simple noise functions. 
The most obvious way to divide the map would be to simply use the biomes generated by the noise algorithms – 
however, these come in many numbers, shapes, and sizes, depending on user input, so I could not rely on the 
biomes to divide the level alone. 
==PAGE:60==
50 
 
MIA LOUISE SØGAARD S100524 
50 
 
I read up on several different solutions, and found a great paper by Nathan Williams [36] which studied three 
different methods for creating these areas: Binary Space Partitioning (BSP), Delaunay Triangulation, and Cellular 
Automata. 
Cellular Automata produced areas of wildly different sizes, and left me with little control over the amount of areas 
I would end up with.  
BSP seemed promising, and had some good documentation, but, like the TinyKeep method, it didn’t give me much 
control over the areas I ended up with, and additionally didn’t let me decide the location of these areas. 
At first, I did not want to use the Delaunay approach, since the result at first glance did not seem like it would work 
well with the more rectangular structure I was looking for. The Delaunay approach generates Voronoi polygons - 
which is a technique often used in procedural map generation that divides a map into irregular polygons. 
In the end, I decided to use the Voronoi approach anyway, partly because I found a project directly designed for 
use in Unity projects [26], which made it easier to implement. I could have used a more elegant method, but due 
to time constrains, and because a more sophisticated method would have involved changing all of the previously 
made code drastically, I chose a simpler approach.

9 
ANALYSIS 
Throughout the course of this project, I learned that procedural generation is a powerful tool that can be used on 
virtually anything, and that the basics can be very simple to introduce; finding some meaning in the madness of 
noise (such as perlin noise) proved to be one of the biggest challenges in the beginning of the project, as the 
settings that looked amazing for a large map looked strange on a smaller one: Søren Andersen, one of my 
interviewees, comments shortly on the effects of this, where his input choices in the first prototype resulted in a 
large desert area with little to no vegetation.  
9.1 
GOALS 
The first three objectives of this project was reached: I investigated current procedural generation techniques and 
applied them to a game-demo with user-defined parameters to influence the generated map. The ‘game’ was easy 
to use, and most of the techniques are relatively easy to implement in other programs.  
The fourth objective, whether the product can be useful for designers and programmers, varies: many of my 
interviewees showed interest in the code, and asked for access and permission to edit the code, but the program 
as a whole was not entirely useful to the programmers; perhaps it would have been, if its functionalities was 
divided into individual programs.  
Designers and more inexperienced programmers showed more interest in the program as a whole, but this was 
often for the inspiration it could give them. However, this fulfilled my final objective, and was not unexpected. 
9.2 
WORK SCHEDULE 
Creating a work schedule may not be of vital importance for a one-person production, but it helped me to better 
visualize how much time I should spend on each subject, and when I should wrap it up, since I had a habit of 
focusing too much on smaller issues during this project. It also allowed me to plan in advance, so that I had time to 
finish the prototype and initial research before I moved on to the interviews. 
If I had known what I do know, I would have spent more time in the beginning of programming the prototype on 
the ability for the user to define the values used by the generator. I would also have begun the interviews sooner, 
even if I didn’t have a prototype to showcase. 
I learned a lot about managing my own time when completing a project, as well as finding useful references and 
research material, and similar work to learn from. 
9.3 
OPTIMIZATION & FUTURE WORK 
9.3.1 
 ‘COSMETIC’ CHANGES 
Adapt the code to better allow for smaller maps to be created, so that the program can be used for generating 
individual areas or levels. 
Much of the map is decided by the Biometable, which uses moisture- and temperature levels to decide the 
biomes. An option to directly change this through the generation menu would allow players to easily eliminate

areas they do not want in the game, tweak them to only appear in certain cases, or even substitute everything in 
the table with, say, swampland. This might be especially useful if the user wants to generate small, single-areas 
through this code. 
I would have liked to implement different meshes to hold objects and tiles that were supposed shadow over the 
player, instead of creating gameobjects – it does not affect the game negatively at this level, but in a different 
situation it could have, so assigning these tiles to a mesh with different sorting layer would have been the most 
useful actions. However, I had some difficulty assigning sorting layers to meshes, and although I looked up some 
possible solutions, none of them seemed too useful or easily-implemented, so I decided to continue making things 
such as houses instantiate as GameObjects in the scene. 
Additionally, the current map doesn’t allow for the player to walk behind a tree top, since it is part of the map 
behind it: I decided to instantiate the houses as separate GameObjects, because it was easier, but instantiating 
every single treetop might be difficult for the engine to handle. A solution to this would be to place things like 
treetops or shadows on the second mesh working ‘on top of’ the map and the player. However, I deemed that 
would take too much time to implement, especially since Unity’s meshes are a tad difficult to figure out when it 
comes to the sorting order. I also did not want to add another large mesh to the mix, since generating, and going 
through each ‘tile’ in the mesh already took up a lot of processing power and time. 
9.3.2 
EASE OF USE 
In extension of the current game, I would like to add the ability for the developer to more easily exchange the 
tilesheet with their own work, and have the program be able to shift between considering the tiles in 8-bit and 4-
bit for the auto-tiling, as different tilesets work differently. Additonally, the users should be able to add new 
terrain types, and change the Biome table accordingly: since the method I use to add transition tiles is very 
versatile, it is easy to simply add another terrain type. 
The kinds of vegetation in the game is also very limited, as I didn’t want to spend too much of my limited time on 
acquiring assets for individual kinds of vegetation or programming the individual codes for their varying sizes. If I 
were to redo this project, I would make it possible to dynamically add new kinds of vegetation using tilesheets, and 
assign which areas they were allowed to appear in (such as cacti in deserts, and large pine trees in more lush 
areas). 
One person who answered my questionnaire, Søren Andersen, expressed a desire for an option to show a preview 
of the map’s ‘negative spaces’; this would allow the user to more quickly determine if their input parameters 
would give the output they wanted, without waiting for the full generation time. 
Finally, I believe the project and potential users would benefit from being allowed to customize parts of the map in 
real-time, or define things that the program has to take into consideration when generating the map, such as cities 
or even forests. Alternatively, the product could be adapted, so the map could be used with another tilemap 
editor, such as Tiled [40].

10 CONCLUSION 
I have created a Unity project which enables the user to easily generate a 2D map according to user specifications 
and input values. The result is based on the adapted output of Unity’s Perlin noise function, which means that each 
map can be easily recreated by inputting the same values again. The project uses an auto-tiling algorithm to 
choose the correct tile shapes and transitions. The project is easily expanded upon with new features, or adapted 
to other needs for different projects. 
I provide a solution for designers or people with little-to-no programming knowledge who want to create large 
scale overworld maps for 2D games. 
10.1 PRODUCT EVALUATION 
The product evaluation terms are described in section: Product Evaluation. 
 
Realism   
The generated maps are for the most part realistic, but are largely dependent on user input. Certain input value 
can result in large parts of the map simply consisting of rocky ground. 
 
Structure  
The biomes are clearly defined, with more environmentally important regions being limited to only having one 
large area (and the rest are absorbed by close by areas). Entering an amount of levels ensures that most of map is 
traversable by the player character. The city generation is acceptable, but not incredibly interesting to test users. 
 
Variation  
Due to this project allowing the user to manipulate various input values, the maps are very varied. The roads are 
also made to take the environment into account, making their shapes more interesting, but still within a realistic 
form. 
 
Efficiency  
The map generation can take a long time for large maps in particular, and can even cause crashes due to low 
memory. This is possibly an issue that can be remedied, but I ran out of time. 
 
Visually  
The resulting maps are very visually appealing, and test users and reviewers all stated compliments to the variation 
and interesting shapes. 
 
 
Playability  
The game is playable as a Pokémon game, as far as movement is concerned, and even incorporates some of the 
typical progression blockers, such as small trees or bodies of water. The map is d

10.2 PROJECT EVALUATION 
I didn’t quite know what my goal would be in the beginning of this project, or if those goals were even possible to 
achieve in a reasonable span of time, partly since I couldn’t justify devoting all of my allotted time to implementing 
algorithms that would not benefit the final product. I came to focus on doing overall research into procedural 
techniques that seemed useful for creating 2D Pokémon style map. 
There are plenty of things to consider when creating a playable prototype, such as ensuring graphical quality and 
player movements, as well as testing thoroughly to catch game-breaking bugs. This took quite a bit of time away 
from the implementation of actual procedural techniques.

The overall results of this project is satisfactory, as I reached my goals described in section 1.4 Objectives. If I had

10.3 FURTHER RESEARCH 
Several areas of research lead to topics that were not researchable within the time frame of the project, or were 
considered either overly complex for 2D map generation, or showed little documented usability in such games. 
In the future I would love to have the opportunity to look at the potential of L-systems again, but in this case there

biomes before the towns, and the fact that Pokémon towns were often small, with only a few houses. 
10.4 PROJECT CONCLUSION 
The project overall was a great learning experience. Some areas and goals I set were unsuccessful to some 
degrees, and some areas should be explored further (see section Optimization and Future Work [9.3] and Further 
Research [10.3]), but I met my basic objectives, and all of the techniques I researched and used can with benefit be 
used in other projects. 
As Henrik Laust Schou-Pedersen mentions in his interview, in appendix B, the project also functions as a collection 
of useful techniques for procedural generation, with a working Unity project for testing and showcasing procedural 
generation techniques. 
Noise is absolutely great for placing vegetation and appealing graphical variances or obstacles, but for the creation 
of actual regions, it often left me with ill-fitting areas, too small or too large, and it was difficult to manipulate the 
output in such a way that I could anticipate the results, and choose the ‘best’ settings – allowing the user to 
change the input parameters is just as much a necessity as it is a design choice; without it, certain maps can get 
very boring and useless.  
Noise is often the go-to method for procedural generation, and it is often sufficient, but when several factors have 
to go together, and especially when the final map has to be divided into areas of roughly the same size, this 
method becomes very restrictive without further enhancements to the method. Even ‘raw’ noise is still very useful 
for such things as vegetation, however, and is very useful for adding variation to a landscape. One solution to this, 
is to decide a general biome for each ‘level’, which affects the noise values: choosing ‘ice’ would halve the noise 
values, for example. 
One of the biggest negatives of the final prototype is the generation time: large maps take an equally long time to 
generate, and it is difficult to cut down on the time, since the nature of a Pokémon map has specific requirements 
that force the code to backtrack and change minute details, or to floodfill large areas multiple times and change 
their biome in order to make the general map look realistic and appealing. This is suboptimal and takes up a lot of 
time and risks freezing Unity entirely.

master’s project: I could have created many useful tools for tile placement, or heightmaps, and so on, but all 
together it is difficult to make such a large working tool without any bugs. However, the result is a useful 
compilation of algorithms and techniques for procedural generation, which can be used in any project.

 
What does Procedural Generation mean to you, in terms of creating game maps? 
Procedural generation is a very interesting topic to me. It is a fantastic way to reduce project time spent creating

Which features/aspects are missing? 
Aside from GUI explanations mentioned by the other guy on PokeCommunity, there are a few things that

2. There are no checks for inaccessible regions of the map. 
3. There isn't much along the lines of level flow (big open spaces around the critical path instead of things to guide
