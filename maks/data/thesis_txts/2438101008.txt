Abstract
The detailed development of three beach design proposals was performed. The develop-
ment required the establishment of an offshore hydrodynamic climate based on wind data
that was used in the development of cross-shore and longshore proﬁles, and the subse-
quent analyses of three beach design proposals.
The study successfully modelled wind generated waves based on provided wind and
wave data using numerical models and a self-developed parametric model.
Subsequently, the wave climate was utilized in the development of a cross-shore pro-
ﬁle following Dean’s equilibrium proﬁle formulation and Hallemeyer’s depth of closure
equation.
Conclusively, numerical models were used to develop a coastline evolution model,
which was used in the coast morphological analyses of the beach design proposals.
The ﬁndings of the study exhibits that the development of a beach and coast using an
offshore hydrodynamic climate was possible and resulted in well-founded beach design
proposals.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction and hypothesis
As the population of Copenhagen is growing at a steady rate the city sees an opportu-
nity in developing the coastal areas of the city. In recent years, the largely municipality
owned company, CPH City and Port Development, has found a solution to the growing
population, whilst creating further prosperity within the city.
1.1
Nordhavn
The area of Nordhavn has been the subject of development worth more than a billion
kroners within a short time frame. The development carried several beneﬁcial aspects
and consisted of many projects within the area.
By utilizing the process of land reclamation the company has and continues to create
more space within Copenhagen, such that attractive new communities for future residents
have become available.
Land reclamation can be used in coastal protection schemes, where smart solutions
to infrastructural problems can be implemented during the development of the area. This
method holds relevance for Denmark as climate change is causing an increase in water
levels. Especially as phenomena such as cloud bursts are becoming increasingly frequent
in Denmark and poses a threat to the city‘s old drainage and stormwater systems. Threats
such as these can, partly, be avoided by creating solutions within reclaimed land areas.
Additional projects within the area includes measures to combat nearby high traf-
ﬁc roads, creating new public facilities, attracting more tourism, connecting new public
transport routes.

Beach and Coast Development
As new communities have been formed in Nordhavn and major projects such as the land
reclamation in Nordhavn and the city ring project are nearing their completion, the general
public is becoming more interested in the area. The growing interest and the establish-
ment of new cruise ship terminals are creating an opportunity to develop new attractions
for both the general public and tourists.
This has lead CPH City and Port Development to consider the development of a beach
and coast in the area adjacent to the reclaimed land in Nordhavn facing the Oresund strait,
as highlighted loosely in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Satellite image of Nordhavn and possible beach location.
The Establishment a beach in Nordhavn will further strengthen the coastal protection of
the area, which is a very desirable feature. Moreover, a beach has great amenity value
that contributes positively to the commodities of the entire area.
This coincides well with the expected use of the reclaimed land, which boasts of
several recreational areas such as football ﬁelds and a park, in addition to the newly
developed housing areas of Nordhavn. Furthermore, the development of a beach can be
combined with land reclamation and create more land for additional residency or business
opportunities.
In regard to pre-existing beaches in Copenhagen, the municipality has two existing
beaches, namely Svanemølle beach and Amager beach park. Location wise, the former is

quite close to Nordhavn, whereas the latter is far. Although Svanemølle beach is close to
this area and it has been one of the most visited places in Copenhagen during summertime,
it is easily packed to full capacity due to the size. This only further strengthens the
argument for the development of a beach in Nordhavn, which can be combined in land
reclamation, business opportunities and climate change protection.
Study Purpose
In a collaboration among CPH City and Port Development, DHI and the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark, this study intended to investigate the possibility of a beach and coast
development in the desired area. This study was founded on the premise that a beach and
coast design may be based on the offshore hydrological environment at the investigated
site. Especially wind, wave, current and seabed interactions (metocean interactions) may
have strong impacts on the possibility of a beach and coastal development and designs.
1.2
Methodology
In order to get a better understanding of the hydrological environment prior to the beach
development, the study utilized various computational models to simulate the wind gen-
erated surface waves.
The waves were subsequently used in the development of a cross-shore coastal pro-
ﬁle following a theoretical approach. Subsequently, the simulated wave climate and the
developed cross-shore proﬁle were employed in the development of three beach design
proposals and the coast morphological investigations that ensued. These investigations re-
lied on additional numerical models capable of simulating the occurring littoral processes
to obtain the desired conclusions.
Research tools
Initially, the study intended to use the MIKE 21 Spectral Wave module, to create a nu-
merical model solely using the wind data provided by DHI. Subsequently, the model was
to be calibrated using hourly in situ buoy measurements of signiﬁcant wave height, Hs,
peak wave period, Tp and mean wave direction, which were measured at the tip of the
reclaimed land in Nordhavn.
However, due to this study being completed on a single computer, it was found that
running the numerical simulations of MIKE 21 SW for the entire wind data time span
required a simulation time of up to one month in physical time, restraining the computer
from being closed, put on standby or lose connection to the internet. Such a long simu-
==PAGE:16==
4

lation time, with such demanding restrictions were undesirable for a study as this, with
limited resources.
The solution to this issue was found in the development of a new model created in
MATLAB which used the available long term wind data to develop a hindcast model
capable of modelling wind generated surface waves. This hindcast model required signif-
icantly less computer power to solve the parametric wave growth estimation approach by
Breugem and Holthuijsen [1] when compared to the wave action density approach used
in MIKE 21 SW, making it more suitable for the purpose of this study.
By validating the newly created parametric model with the MIKE 21 SW results for
short simulation periods, it became possible to optimize the model such that it yielded
applicable results, as this method was capable of acquiring the necessary wave parameters
Hs and Tp for a fraction of the time required to obtain similar results using MIKE 21 SW.
Subsequently, the acquired wave parameters were utilized to develop a coastal proﬁle
using a MATLAB-model, following the equilibrium coastal proﬁle formula proposed by
Dean [5]. Based on long term wave parameter statistics Dean’s formula determines a pro-
ﬁle in dynamic equilibrium, such that it maintains its form, with slight variations affected
by seasonal and annual changes.
Once a cross-shore proﬁle was established, The MIKE Zero LITPACK module was
used to investigate the three proposed beach designs. The LITPACK module consists of
several models, of which the majority was used to investigate the coast morphological
processes that occurred in this study. This was done numerically, using the wave cli-
mate, deﬁned sediment and coastal properties, enabling analyses such as a Q-alpha to be
performed.
Expected Results
Following the extensive scope of this study, several results were expected to be obtained
as deﬁned below.
• Establish a valid wind generated surface waves model
• Generate a modelled wave climate based on the provided wind data.
• Deﬁne a cross-shore proﬁle based on the modelled offshore hydrodynamic condi-
tions.
• Establish a coastline model and investigate the coast morphology of the developed
beach design proposals.

Current
This study was founded on the results of an environmental study performed by CPH City
and Port Development, DHI and the municipality of Copenhagen [4]. Particularly, the hy-
drographical and coast morphological ﬁndings of the study were vital in the formulation
of both the parametric and numerical models.
Presented in Figure 2.1 is the result of the study in regard to a northbound current in
the vicinity of Nordhavn and Figure 2.2 presents a southbound current. The results are
illustrated in contour plots over the current velocities of the area, with vectors illustrating
the direction of the local velocities.
Figure 2.1 shows that a northbound current will have a velocity of up to 0.150 m/s at

beach and was therefore neglected in this study. Furthermore, it should be noted that the
results are shown for Nordhavn prior to the expansions, and as the effects of the expansion
only further decrease the current (sheltering effects) at the investigated site, the case for
neglecting the current was further justiﬁed.

Waves
The wave climate investigated in this study may have consisted of wind waves and swell.
Wind generated surface waves (wind waves) are generated by local wind exerting a sheer
stress on the water surface, whereas swell waves originates from remote storms. Due to
the speciﬁc location of the investigated site it could be expected that the waves approach-
ing the coast will almost exclusively be wind waves.
A lack of swell waves was assumed and attributed to the Oresund strait having a
narrow sheltered passage to the north between Helsingør and Helsinborg, and due to the
beach being sheltered from waves approaching from the south by the reclaimed land in
Nordhavn as seen in Figure 1.1.
This sheltering was expected to signiﬁcantly reduce any contribution from swell waves,
leaving the majority of the waves approaching the coast to be wind generated surface
waves. But given the bathymetry of the domain wave refraction may have resulted in
some swell wave contribution from waves approaching from the south.
Wind Data
As the wave climate in this study was mainly generated by a wind driven sheer stress on
the water surface, it became necessary to have a sufﬁcient representation of the meteoro-
logical climate in the area. In this case, it was sufﬁcient to only focus on wind forcing.
The wind forcing used to determine the wave generation was based on wind data
provided by DHI, which consisted of hourly wind measurements spanning from the 1st
of October 2004 at 00:00 to the 1st of January 2012 at 00:00.
The provided wind data was made up of wind pressure at MSL [hPa], wind direction
[◦] and wind velocity at 10 meter above MSL [m/s] measurements. This was all presented

MIKE 21 Spectral Wave Module
The MIKE 21 Spectral wave FM module simulates the wave growth, decay and transfor-
mation of wind generated surface waves and swell on a ﬂexible mesh (FM). The ﬂexible
mesh refers to a "spatial domain that is discretized into non-overlapping cells", such that
calculations are performed for each individual inter-connected cell.
The spectral wave simulations of this study were based on a domain, mesh resolu-
tion and forcings provided by DHI, such that the creation of a hindcast model could be
achieved by varying the spectral discretization, wave-seabed interaction and wave proper-
ties such as the wave energy dissipation. A full list of the physical phenomena The MIKE
21 SW module is capable of modelling is presented below as copied from [11];
• Wind generated wave growth
• Non-linear wave-wave interaction (quadruplet and triad-wave interactions)
• Dissipation due to white-capping
• Dissipation due to depth induced wave breaking
• Refraction and shoaling due to depth variations and currents
• Wave-current interactions (not included in this study)
• Effect of time-varying water depth and currents
• Effect of ice coverage on the wave ﬁeld (not included in this study)
• Wave diffraction (not included in this study)

rectional decoupled parametric formulation and the fully spectral formulation of the wave

Model Results
In order to validate the model, short period simulations were run and compared with buoy
data. The duration of the short period simulation was from November 11th. 2011 at

Discussion/Model Uncertainties
While Figure 2.22 shows that the parametric model was applicable, it also shows that
some disadvantages can be found when compared to the Numerical MIKE 21 SW model.
A big disadvantage was the exclusion of several physical processes of wave mod-
elling. Most notable is the inability to model wave shoaling and refraction, that has a
signiﬁcant inﬂuence on waves approaching coastal environments.
As shoaling regards the constant energy ﬂux of a non-dissipative wave, this process
will undoubtedly have an effect on the wave properties in coastal regions, as they are
characterized by morphological changes.
Wave refraction is caused by the water depth becoming more shallow as the waves
approach the shoreline, such that the waves "bend" and ultimately lead to the waves strik-
ing the shoreline perpendicularly. During this bending of the waves, the wave height
will experience change as conservation of energy ﬂux must be satisﬁed, even during the
"bending" of waves.
The parametric model was unable to take processes such as these into account, as
it was only given one ﬁxed fetch distance and water depth for each directional interval.
Additionally, the effects of physical processes such as refraction and shoaling on the wave
height is determined following the conservation of energy ﬂux, which is not regarded in
the derivation of the parametric model.
While the model was deemed accurate and produced wave height results with a 1-10
% error when compared to the MIKE 21 SW model, two directional bins produced errors
of up to 38 %. This may be due to the lack of wave refraction, which is most likely a
process that is occuring in the nearshore regions of the Oresund Strait.
Other signiﬁcant energy ﬂux related interactions including wave-seabed and wave-
wave interactions are thus also neglected and may contribute to further inaccurate wave
developments.
Another simpliﬁcation is that due to the location of the beach, wind and thereby wave
contributions from directions of angles between ]90◦270◦[ are disregarded. This simpliﬁ-

cation was applied as the expansion of Nordhavn would provide the beach with sheltering
effects from waves approaching from speciﬁc directions, while leaving the formation of

However, taking all these disadvantages into account, the parametric model was ca-
pable of modelling surprisingly accurate wind generated surface waves. The accuracy of
the model may be attributed to the simple wave climate of the area, as swell waves were

4.6
Analysis/Discussion
The concerns and uncertainties raised in regard to the cross-shore and longshore coastal
proﬁles are addressed in this section with their possible explanations
Cross-shore Proﬁle
Following the simulated littoral drift presented in Figure 4.5 it was observed that a small
littoral drift was occurring outside the deﬁned cross-shore littoral zone. This raised the
concern that it was unknown whether the drift did not begin further offshore due to the
wave climate or simply due to the ﬁxed proﬁle having such a steep slope outside the surf
zone.
The probable explanation to this inaccuracy in the cross-shore proﬁle lies with the
chosen value of the exponent m, that was utilized in Dean’s equilibrium proﬁle in equa-
tion (3.1). The cross-shore proﬁle sensitivity study performed in section 3 showed that
the proﬁle was very sensitive to the value of the exponent as m = 0.4 resulted in a surf
zone of more than 1 kilometer long and m = 0.8 resulted in a length of 36 meter. The
shoreline management guidelines by Mangor et al. (2017) [7] recommends a value of
m = 0.67 but this resulted in a littoral zone of 70 meter, which was deemed to short.
However, while this uncertainty in the proﬁle has been presented, it should be noted
that this cross-shore proﬁle, that was developed from waves obtained from a parametric
model was well deﬁned, and yielded accurate results, it simply required further optimiza-
tion.
Beach Designs Economics
Basic cost of sand estimates of the beach design proposals showed costs ranging from 22
million dkk. to 39 million dkk. But as the author has no prior experience in the bidding
and tender of a beach design it was assumed that based on these estimates, total beach
costs could amount up to 120 million dkk., which is within the realm of a realistic bid,
taking into account that Amager Beach Park was estimated to have a cost around 200
million dkk.
Coastal Evolution
The results obtained for each beach design showed that the established beach designs
had not reached dynamic equilibrium after almost 30 years. This was rather perplexing,
as the littoral drift showed a fairly manageable annual net drift, and the coastal evolu-
tion envelopes illustrated rather mild retreats and advances of the coastlines. Although
==PAGE:96==
84
CHAPTER 4. LITPACK
equilibrium proﬁles were not reached, the model simulations still resulted in valuable in-
formation and great solutions which can be used in further studies. They simply raised
the question; why does it take so long to reach equilibrium stability?
An explanation could be related to the annual variation in the approaching waves. As
the wave roses in Appendix G.2 show, the dominating waves consisted of either small
but frequent waves from westward direction or larger waves approaching from a northern
and eastern direction. As this was repeated in the time series, it could explain the rather
stable proﬁle, as the waves are not larger than a maximum of Hrms < 1.2, but still not
consistent in their approach to the coastline.
This was having a big effect on the beach designs as these were all established with an
orientation of α = 34circ to North. So for years when waves approached the beach with
small waves, the integrated drift approached zero, whereas years containing larger waves,
showed signiﬁcantly smaller variations to the integrated drift across the entire coastline,
for all the beache proposals.
4.7
Conclusion to Coastal Morphology Investigation
The ﬁndings related to the metocean scope of this study were concluded at the end of
chapter 2, but a brief reiteration is as follows
• A MIKE 21 SW FM model was successfully developed and validated against in
situ buoy measurements.
• A Parametric model created by the author have been speciﬁcally developed and
optimized for this study, and when validated against the MIKE 21 SW model, it
resulted in quite accurate results, using signiﬁcantly less computer power than re-
quired by the numerical MIKE 21 SW model. Subsequently, the model was used
to obtain a modelled waves, using a 7.4 year long wind time series.
• Statistics were performed on the modelled wave climate, from which design param-
eters necessary for the formulation of several coastal components were acquired.
This lead to the conclusions of the coastal morphology part of the study, which will be
presented here. The conclusions are related to the various proﬁles, designs and models
developed, and the overall results of said components.
Cross-shore Proﬁle
Using the modelled wave climate obtained from the parametric model, a cross-shore pro-
ﬁle was developed by utilizing the theoretical equilibrium proﬁle proposed by Dean [5]
==PAGE:97==
4.7. CONCLUSION TO COASTAL MORPHOLOGY INVESTIGATION
85
The proﬁle followed Dean’s formulation until a depth of closure, determined using Halle-
meyer’s equation was reached. Using a d50 = 0.2 mm. the resulting cross-shore proﬁle
stretched out to a distance of 177 meter offshore and a depth of closure of dl = 1.829
meter was found using the wave parameters Hs,12h/y = 1.036 meter and Ts = 4.076
seconds.
Littoral Drift
Using the littoral drift module of LITPACK, and by use of the cross-shore proﬁle and the
modelled wave climate, the annual sediment transport was investigated for 11 orienta-
tions from 0-50 degrees from North. By transforming the modelled Hs to Hrms and then
performing a Q-alpha analysis, a zero-net orientation was found at α = 34circ.
In order to reduce computation time signiﬁcantly a littoral drift table was generated
and successfully validated againt determined littoral drift. The error in the annual littoral
drift of the table generation model was found to be less than 4 %, which was deemed
acceptable.
Groyne Analysis
The distance at which the longshore sediment transport is reduced, in LITPACK, due to
the presence of a groyne was investigated. The manual states that this length is 10 times
the groyne lengths, whereas it was suspected that the correct length is equal to 10 times
the length of the shadow area along the coastline.
By calculating the wave refraction of an approaching wave using Snell’s law, and
comparing it to the simulated longshore sediment transport, it was indeed determined that
the length at which the transport was reduced is related to the length of the shadow area.
Beach Design Proposals
Three beach designs were proposed in this study. These were developed in accordance
to numerical analyses, urban planning and prized architectural designs. As such, three
alternative designs were successfully designed and estimations to the cost of constructing
these were given.
Coastal Evolution
Lastly, The numerical coastal evolution module of LITPACK was used to investigate the
beach design proposals with the implementation of the pre-generated littoral drift table.

The simulation were based on a 7.4 year long wave climate, that was repeated 4 times,
such that the beaches were exposed to a 29.6 year long wave time series.
The obtained results showed that none of the beaches had reached a dynamic equi-
librium beach proﬁle within the simulated period. However, the results proved that all
3 beach design proposals were quite stable and were steadily approaching a stabilized
equilibrium.
Concluding Remark
Having thus established a wind to wave generation model, created a cross-shore proﬁle,
and established a coastline evolution model, the expected results of this study have been
obtained. By using numerical models and developed proﬁles, 3 beach design proposals
were developed and investigated. The investigation lead to the conclusion that all 3 beach
design proposals provided possible solutions to the development of a beach in Nordhavn.
4.8
Future Work
The scope of this study was quite broad and time-constraints were the deciding factor for
when a certain aspect of this study was to be concluded. As such, there are several aspects
of this study that could be optimized in future work.
MIKE 21 SW
While the MIKE 21 SW FM model played a big role in this study, the model was founded
on roughly 1.5 months worth of hourly data. A model validated by a short time series
as this can only be regarded representative to a certain degree. Therefore, future work
should work on obtaining wind data within the range of the buoy measured data, such
that a more extensive calibration and validation process can be performed.
Cross-shore proﬁle
The LITPACK simulations illustrated that littoral drift was occurring outside the deﬁned
littoral zone. This indicate that the established cross-shore proﬁle could be optimized,
and as the sensitivity analysis of the proﬁle indicated, the optimization could be related
to the exponent m. As such, future work should investigate the exponent further.
Alternatively, future work could consider determining the end width of the littoral
zone based on a littoral drift analysis performed in LITPACK. This would simply require
the creation of a proﬁle following dean’s formulation spanning sufﬁciently far offshore.
