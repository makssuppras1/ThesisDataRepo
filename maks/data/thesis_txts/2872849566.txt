Abstract
Accurate and coherent passenger demand forecasts are essential for the planning and operation
of urban rail rail transit systems. This thesis investigates forecast reconciliation as a means
to improve both the accuracy and structural consistency of hierarchical forecasts, particularly
between station-level and origin-destination predictions. We evaluate a range of reconciliation
techniques, including traditional statistical approaches and deep learning-based models, applied
to forecasts generated by two dedicated models: mGraphSAGE for origin-destination demand
and SDT-GRU for station ﬂows.
Experiments are conducted using real-world data from the Copenhagen urban rail network,
covering both one-step and multi-step settings with a focus on periods of demand disruption.
Results show that reconciliation consistently enhances coherence and often improves point
accuracy, particularly when predictions are made over multiple horizons. Traditional methods
such as Minimum Trace Optimisation, perform well in short-term settings, while deep learned
reconcilers oﬀer greater robustness and adaptability for long-range planning.
Overall, the
study demonstrates that forecast reconciliation is a valuable addition to modern transit
forecasting pipelines, especially in complex, data-sparse environments like urban rail networks.

Introduction
Urban Rail Transit (URT) systems are fundamental for the mobility infrastructure of modern
cities, supporting millions of daily journeys. As population densities rise and transit networks
become more interconnected, the ability to accurately forecast passenger demand becomes
increasingly critical.
These forecasts guide a wide range of operational decisions from
scheduling and capacity planning to sta"ng and disruption management, and ultimately
contribute to more reliable and resilient transportation systems [LDH24].
However, the
rapidly growing demand for passenger travel has led to frequent situations where the number
of boarding passengers exceeds train capacity, resulting in overcrowded trains and increased
passenger waiting times [Lv+24]. These challenges directly undermine the perceived reliability
and comfort of urban rail systems and emphasise the urgent need for forecasting solutions
that can anticipate demand ﬂuctuations. Given how deeply transportation is integrated into
daily life, advancements in this sector can have broad societal impact [HMH22]. This has led
to growing interest in data-driven methods to improve the performance and resilience of URT
systems.
A critical aspect of building such solutions lies in understanding the hierarchical structure
of transit data. Passenger demand can be measured at diﬀerent levels of granularity: coarse
station-level totals (inﬂows and outﬂows) and ﬁne-grained origin-destination (OD) ﬂows between
pairs of stations. These two views are tightly coupled as the sum of all trips departing from
a station should equal its total outﬂow, yet models trained independently at each level often
produce forecasts that violate these structural relationships. This incoherence can degrade the
reliability of the forecasts and complicate downstream tasks such as network optimisation and
load balancing.
Traditional methods in the forecasting literature have addressed this issue through forecast
reconciliation:
the process of adjusting forecasts to satisfy known aggregation constraints.
These techniques are well-studied in domains such as economics and energy, where hierarchical
time series are common. However, their application to urban mobility data, particularly for
reconciling OD forecasts with aggregate station-level predictions, remains largely unexplored.
This thesis investigates the use of forecast reconciliation as a tool for improving both accuracy
and structural consistency in transit demand forecasting. Speciﬁcally, we consider a setting
where two distinct deep learning models are used to predict station-level and OD-level demand
in the Copenhagen rail network, respectively. We evaluate multiple reconciliation strategies
including deep learning-based and statistical methods for aligning these forecasts in a way that
preserves both their individual accuracy and their mutual coherence. A key goal is to assess
whether deep learned reconciliation methods can generalise across time horizons and levels
of the hierarchy, and how their performance compares to traditional approaches. To better

performance. Multi-step prediction is especially relevant for operational planning and control,
enabling transit agencies to anticipate demand over longer time periods and mitigate error

These include times of atypical demand caused by events like holidays or service changes which
often challenge model robustness.
1.1
Problem Deﬁnition
Using Rejsekort data from Copenhagen’s S-trains, the study aims to:
1. Adapt a global model to predict station inﬂow and outﬂow across multiple time horizons
(both one-step and multi-step), exploring sequence-to-sequence and graph-based architectures
to capture both immediate and extended forecasting needs.
2. Employ and expand an established GNN-based OD model to estimate sparse demand between
station pairs, leveraging spatial and temporal graph structures to maintain accuracy for both
short-term predictions and extended forecasting windows.
3. Integrate the base forecasts through a deep reconciler to ensure OD predictions properly
aggregate to station-level ﬂows, evaluating performance across all time horizons with particular
attention to disruption periods.
To guide this investigation and structure our analysis, we address the following research
questions:
• RQ1: How accurately can a global model forecast station-level ﬂow for both one-step and
multi-step, and to what extent do graph-based spatial encodings improve both immediate
and extended predictions?
• RQ2: How eﬀectively can an expanded GNN-based OD model handle conditions of high
sparsity and irregular demand patterns while maintaining accuracy for both one-step and
multi-step predictions?
• RQ3: How does deep reconciliation impact forecasting accuracy and coherency across one-
step and extended multi-step forecasts, compared to traditional reconciliation methods?
• RQ4: Do reconciliation gains diﬀer during real-world disruption scenarios compared to
routine operations for both immediate and extended forecast horizons?
Together, these questions frame our investigation into whether a uniﬁed deep-learning
reconciliation framework can deliver reliable, coherent demand forecasts that support both
real-time operational decisions (through one-step predictions) and strategic planning (through
multi-step forecasts) in URT systems.

2
Theory
This chapter lays out the theoretical foundations of our integrated forecasting framework for
urban rail transit. We begin by formalising the two key components in short-term passenger
prediction (STPP) in URT systems: passenger ﬂow prediction and origin-destination (OD)
demand forecasting. While these tasks diﬀer in scope and granularity, they describe related
aspects of passenger movement within the network.
We then introduce the foundation behind the deep learning models used to capture the
temporal and spatial dependencies inherent in passenger ﬂows. This is followed by a overview
of hierarchical time series, which formalises the structural relationships between station-level
and OD ﬂows, and forecast reconciliation methods that enforce coherence across the hierarchy.
2.1
Passenger Flow Prediction
Researchers ([SMM24], [Lv+24], [Liu+20]) highlight two key prediction tasks that emerge in
URT systems. The ﬁrst, station passenger ﬂow prediction, involves forecasting the number
of passengers entering (inﬂow) and exiting (outﬂow) a transit station during a future time
interval. Typically, the goal is to estimate passenger demand in the next 15, 20 or 30 minutes
using historical ridership data along with external inﬂuencing factors such as weather conditions,
holidays, and special events [Yan+24].
Several researchers ([Yan+24], [JMK22], [SMM24])
highlight that the increasing availability of Automated Fare Collection data has enabled this
task to be explored in greater depth, supporting the development of accurate STPP models.

2.2
Origin-Destination Demand Prediction
The second task, origin-destination (OD) demand prediction, focuses on estimating how
many passengers will travel between speciﬁc station pairs, i.e.
from an origin station to a
destination station during the next time interval. The motivation behind this task lies in its
ability to support real-time operational decisions and passenger information systems.

Challenges in Origin-Destination Prediction
Despite its advantages, OD demand prediction in URT systems presents several challenges
that make it more complex than station-level prediction.
One key issue is the greater
variability in OD ﬂows.
As noted by Lv et al.
[Lv+24], OD passenger volumes tend to
ﬂuctuate more signiﬁcantly than station-level ﬂows, making them harder to predict with
accuracy. This complexity is further increased by the discrete and diverse movement patterns
of passengers across diﬀerent origin-destination pairs. Shanthappa et al. [SMM24] highlight
that the inherent non-linearity in time series passenger ﬂow data combined with the
randomness in spatial movement makes OD prediction a more di"cult task. Furthermore, OD

many OD pairs exhibit little or no observed demand during most time intervals.
As

prediction, requiring models to be not only sensitive to temporal dynamics and spatial
dependencies but also resilient to missing or highly imbalanced data.

Another major challenge in OD demand prediction is the issue of partial observability.
In contrast to ride-hailing services and station-level demand prediction, where data is fully

2.3.1
Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)
A Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) is a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) designed to model
sequential data while addressing the vanishing gradient problem in traditional RNNs.
Like
LSTMs, GRUs use gating mechanisms to control information ﬂow, enabling the network to
retain relevant data and discard the rest. However, GRUs are more e"cient, with a simpler
architecture and fewer parameters, achieved by combining LSTM components into two gates:
the update gate and the reset gate [Cho+][CGC][Gup25].
The internal structure of a GRU can be described using the following set of equations. The
update gate Zt determines how much of the newly computed information from the input It
should be used to update the hidden state. It is computed as:
Zt = σ(W zIt + U zht−1 + bz)
(2.5)
where σ(·) denotes the sigmoid activation function, which maps its input to a value between 0
and 1. Unlike LSTMs, GRUs do not have an explicit output gate. Instead, the entire hidden
state is exposed at each time step without any gating mechanism controlling its output.
The reset gate Rt computed similarly to the update gate, determines how much of the previous
hidden state ht−1 should be forgotten.
Rt = σ(W rIt + U rht−1 + br)
(2.6)
The candidate activation ˜ht represents the potential new hidden state, based on the current

˜ht = tanh(W nIt + Rt ⊙U n(ht−1 + bn)),

U z, W r, U r, W n and U n are all weight matrices to be learned and bz, br and bn are all biases
to be learned.
Finally, the new hidden state ht, is obtained by linearly interpolating between the previous
hidden state ht−1 and the candidate activation ˜ht, using the update gate Zt:

2.4
Hierarchical Time Series
Building on the previous sections, which introduced the station-level and origin-destination
prediction tasks along with the deep learning models used to address them, we now consider
the structural relationships between these series.
Station-level and origin-destination ﬂows
are structured multivariate time series system in which multiple series are related through
aggregation constraints. As Athanasopoulos et al. [Ath+24] explain, collections of time series
formed via aggregation are common across many domains. For instance, total sales of a product
in a country can be disaggregated into sales by state, region, or city. Similarly, time series
measured at a quarterly frequency can be aggregated to produce annual summaries. These are
all examples of hierarchical time series, which may be constructed either cross-sectionally
across geographic or categorical subdivisions or temporally, by aggregating observations across
time at diﬀerent frequencies.
In hierarchical time series, the structure is organised such that data at lower levels sum to form
the series at higher levels. This is visually represented as a tree-like structure, where nodes
represent individual time series, and edges reﬂect aggregation relationships.
In the context of public transit, station-level and OD ﬂows can be interpreted as a hierarchical
time series. However, unlike conventional hierarchies, OD ﬂows introduce a unique structure
because each ﬂow is directional.
Speciﬁcally, the ﬂow from station A to station B (AB) is
distinct from the ﬂow from station B to station A (BA). This introduces two separate series for
each OD pair, reﬂecting inﬂow and outﬂow dynamics.
Figure 2.4 illustrates a two-level hierarchy where the top level aggregates represent total ﬂows
across the entire network, while the second level disaggregates into speciﬁc station groups (A,
B, and C), and further into individual OD pairs (AA, AB, AC, etc.). This structure enforces
the property that:
Total = A + B + C
and each group (e.g. A) further disaggregates to:
A = AA + AB + AC

2.5
Forecast Reconciliation
In hierarchical time series, the individual time series are said to be coherent if the forecasts
at diﬀerent levels of the hierarchy satisfy the aggregation constraints deﬁned by the structural
relationships. Speciﬁcally, the sum of the disaggregated bottom-level time series should always
match the forecasts of the corresponding upper-level series. However, this is not always the
case when forecasting is performed independently at diﬀerent levels.

14
==PAGE:24==
Athanasopoulos et al. [Ath+24] illustrate this concept with a simple example: consider three
time series, X, Y , and Z, where Z = X + Y . If one model is used to predict the bottom-level
series X and another model predicts the upper-level series Y , then in practice, the forecasts
for X and Y are unlikely to sum exactly to the forecast for Z. This discrepancy creates an
incoherent forecast, as the natural aggregation constraint is violated:
ˆZ ̸= ˆX + ˆY
This lack of coherence can lead to interpretability challenges and suboptimal decision-making,
especially in applications where consistency across hierarchical levels is crucial.
Forecast
reconciliation is the process of adjusting the forecasts across the hierarchy to restore
coherence. Hyndman et al. [Hyn+11] were among the ﬁrst to formalise forecast reconciliation
in the form widely applied today. In this setting, letting ˆyt denote our base forecasts for all
time series in the hierarchy stacked in the same order as described in Equation 2.25, the
reconciled forecasts, ˜yt, can then be expressed as:
˜yt = SP ˆyt
(2.26)
where P ∈Rnb×n is a projection matrix that maps the (potentially incoherent) base forecasts
ˆyt ∈Rn into the bottom-level forecasts of dimension nb. The role of P is to enforce coherence by
projecting the original forecasts onto a subspace where the hierarchical summation relationships
are satisﬁed.
The aggregation matrix, S, sums the bottom-level forecasts according to the
hierarchical structure to produce the fullly reconciled forecast ˜yt [Wan24].
2.5.1
Traditional Methods
Athanasopoulos et al. [Ath+24] describe that traditionally, hierarchical time series forecasting
involved predicting values at only one speciﬁc level of the hierarchy, followed by linearly
combining these forecasts to produce a coherent set for the entire structure. This ensures that
aggregation constraints are satisﬁed, but the choice of which level to forecast can impact both
accuracy and interpretability.

which are then summed to generate predictions for all levels in the hierarchical structure [HA18].

Literature Review
This chapter presents a review of the relevant research and methodologies used across the
three main prediction tasks discussed in this thesis: station passenger ﬂow prediction, origin-
destination demand forecasting, and forecast reconciliation. For each task, we highlight key
studies, outline the modelling techniques employed, and discuss how these approaches address
the speciﬁc challenges associated with short-term demand prediction in URT systems. The goal
is to provide a foundation for understanding the strengths and limitations of current methods
and to motivate the need for more integrated forecasting solutions.
3.1
Passenger Flow Prediction
In recent years, extensive research has been conducted on predicting the ﬂow of people across
various public transit systems. This includes buses, metro networks, rail systems, and shared
mobility options such as taxis, electric bikes, and scooters. Advances in data analytics, artiﬁcial
intelligence, and real-time tracking have enabled more accurate forecasting of passenger ﬂow,
helping optimise transit operations, reduce congestion, and improve overall urban mobility.
These studies primarily utilise data from ticketing systems and Automatic Passenger Counters
(APCs) to analyse commuting patterns and improve the e"ciency of transportation networks.
Various methods have been employed to predict passenger ﬂow. Lv et al. [Lv+24] categorise
passenger ﬂow prediction methods into three classes: statistical methods, machine learning
methods, and deep learning methods.
Statistical methods, although now largely outdated,
serve as the foundation for the ﬁeld and will be brieﬂy discussed. Machine learning methods,
which were soon surpassed by deep learning techniques, will not be covered in detail here.
The traditional, statistical approaches, such as ARIMA, have been widely used due to the time-
series nature of the data. Given that most passenger ﬂow data exhibit highly seasonal trends,
the Seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA) model has been applied to generate adequate predictions
[Mil+18]. Halyal et al. [HMH22] found that SARIMA, as a traditional linear model, struggles
to capture the stochastic and non-linear characteristics of passenger demand. They suggest
that more advanced techniques, such as LSTM networks, should be utilised. LSTM networks
can eﬀectively learn long-term dependencies and model non-linear relationships, making them
a more suitable alternative for passenger ﬂow prediction.
The results of the study clearly
demonstrate that LSTMs can be eﬀectively utilised for forecasting passenger demand using APC
data from a bus line in India, giving better results compared to conventional methodologies.
Additionally, Gated Recurrent Units, which has a similar structure as LSTM, has also been
utilised to predict short-term tra"c ﬂow and shows promising results as well [FZL17].
Graph Convolutional Networks have become a cornerstone for passenger ﬂow forecasting by
leveraging the transit networks graph structure to capture spatial dependencies alongside

3.3
Forecast Reconciliation
The topic of forecast reconciliation has been widely studied, primarily in the context of
traditional time series reconciliation methods.
Various approaches have been proposed to
adjust and align forecasts across diﬀerent hierarchical levels to improve accuracy. However,
research on applying forecast reconciliation speciﬁcally to urban rail transit, particularly in
the context of reconciling passenger ﬂow forecasts with origin-destination demand, is relatively
sparse. Only a handful of studies have explored this subject using other datasets, and their
ﬁndings and methods will be discussed in the following section. To the best of our knowledge,
no prior work has applied a similar reconciliation approach within the context of urban rail
transit using OD demand and station-level passenger ﬂow data.
Forecast reconciliation methods can broadly be divided into classical statistical techniques and
more recent machine-learning approaches.
As detailed in Section 2.5, classical methods
include

benchmarks due to their theoretical optimality and ease of implementation.
3.3.1
Machine- and Deep Learning Approaches
In recent years, more advanced methods have been increasingly employed to enhance
traditional forecast reconciliation frameworks.
Many contributions build on the optimal
combination approach of Hyndman et al.
[Hyn+11] by replacing its linear regression
formulation with more ﬂexible, non-parametric learners, such as tree-based ensembles or
neural networks, to combine information across hierarchical levels.
Coherency is enforced
either through bottom-up aggregation of ML-generated base forecasts or by embedding
aggregate-consistency constraints directly into the ML training process [Ath+24].
Abolghasemi et al. [Abo+19] evaluate their approach on 120 weeks of Australian weekly sales
data structured in a three-level hierarchy (total →2 retailers →12 distribution centres (DCs)).
They introduce dynamic proration as a MO strategy for hierarchical forecasting, where an
ARIMAX model ﬁrst generates forecasts at the retailer level (middle tier), and ML models
(XGBoost, neural networks and SVR) then predict time-varying disaggregation weights for each
DC. These weights are normalised to sum to one, ensuring bottom-level coherency without a
separate reconciliation step. While dynamic proration yields more accurate DC-level forecasts
under shifting demand patterns, it does not outperform traditional top-down approaches at the
total or retailer levels.
Mancuso et al.
[MPS21] introduce a neural disaggregation framework that replaces the
traditional two-step forecast-then-reconcile process with a single deep learning model. Their
approach learns to produce both accurate and coherent forecasts in one step. The model takes
historical aggregate data, processes it with a 1D convolutional network, combines it with
external features processed through a small MLP, and feeds everything into a fully connected
layer that outputs bottom-level forecasts. Crucially, coherence is enforced during training by

22
==PAGE:32==
adding a penalty to the MSE loss that measures how much the aggregated bottom-level
outputs diﬀer from the given aggregate forecast.
At inference time, the model takes in an
h-step-ahead aggregate forecast (e.g., from ARIMAX) and outputs consistent bottom-level
predictions.
Anderer et al. [AL22] propose a hierarchical forecasting method that focuses on improving
top-level accuracy by adjusting bottom-level forecasts. Their approach combines N-BEATS for
forecasting continuous top-level series with LightGBM for handling more irregular bottom-level
data. This creates a practical version of bottom-up reconciliation. By correcting hidden biases in
the lower-level forecasts, the method helps achieve better coherence across the whole hierarchy.
A key component is hierarchical alignment where a loss multiplier, λ, is tuned to minimise
the RMSE between aggregated bottom-level forecasts and independently generated top-level
forecasts, ensuring alignment throughout the hierarchy. Similar to the coherence penalty in
Mancuso et al. [MPS21], this alignment mechanism acts as a soft constraint that encourages
structural consistency during training.
Leprince et al. [Lep+23] introduce a hierarchical forecasting framework that jointly models
spatial and temporal hierarchies using structurally-informed machine learning regressors. At
the core of their approach is a coherency-aware learning strategy, where a custom loss function
incorporates

forecasting and reconciliation into a single, ﬂexible framework.
Spiliotis et al. [Spi+20] introduce a machine learning approach to reconciliation that moves
beyond ﬁxed structures by learning non-linear, data-driven combinations of base forecasts.
Unlike other methods that apply soft penalties or enforce consistency across all series, their
model selectively reconciles only where meaningful cross-level correlations exist. This makes
the reconciliation more e"cient and targeted - especially useful in hierarchies where diﬀerent
series behave very diﬀerently. Tested on tourism and retail data, the method shows better point
accuracy and lower bias, highlighting the beneﬁts of ﬂexible reconciliation that adapts to the
structure of the hierarchy.
Burba et al. [BC21] propose a general and ﬂexible reconciliation method for hierarchical time
series forecasting based on an encoder-decoder neural network architecture. The encoder is
a trainable neural network that transforms independent forecasts into reconciled bottom-level
forecasts, while the decoder is a ﬁxed matrix that reconstructs forecasts at all levels, ensuring
exact hierarchical consistency. This approach generalises the representational space of existing
reconciliation methods and oﬀers a straightforward implementation. Evaluated on four real-
world datasets, the method consistently matches or outperforms state-of-the-art techniques,
demonstrating robustness across diverse settings.
Its ﬂexibility, ease of use, and empirical

Holiday Features
An important feature when working with time series data spanning an entire year is identifying
special occasions that may impact travel behaviour and demand patterns. To account for this,

free from school, such as during winter, summer, and autumn breaks, which could also inﬂuence
demand patterns. In 2017, there were 15 bank holidays [Tit17] and 93 school holidays [Kal17],
which were included as binary variables in the feature data for both models.

Lagged Features
For the OD model, two sets of lagged features are used.

Supply Features
To investigate model performance under both routine and disrupted operational conditions,
we account for the reliability of the URT system and its inﬂuence on passenger demand by
incorporating a set of supply-side reliability indicators, inspired by Nguyen et al. [Vie+24].
Popular reliability measures in public transportation systems include waiting time, regularity,
and travel time. In the context of this study, regularity refers to the frequency of train arrivals
and the rate of cancellations within a one-hour period, while waiting time and travel time
account for delays in train arrivals at speciﬁc stations, relative to the corresponding OD pairs.
For each origin-destination pair, we compute three reliability dimensions at both the origin and
destination stations over the preceding hour:
• Number of trains at the station during the past hour for lines and directions corresponding
to the OD pair.
• Mean delay of trains at the station during the past hour for lines and directions
corresponding to the OD pair.
• Proportion of cancelled trains at the station during the past hour for lines and directions
corresponding to the OD pair.
Since stations may be served by multiple lines, we derive both the mean and maximum values
for each of these three dimensions at the origin and destination. This yields a total of 3·2·2 = 12
supply features, which enable the model to account for variations in operational reliability.
Time-speciﬁc Features
To capture periodic and scheduling patterns in OD ﬂows, time-speciﬁc categorical variables
are included as part of the input features. Speciﬁcally, two types of temporal information are
encoded: the day of the week and the time interval of the day.
The day of the week is represented using one-hot encoding across ﬁve categories, as the data
only spans weekdays (Monday to Friday).
This encoding allows the model to distinguish
between typical weekday variations in passenger behaviour, such as diﬀerences between
commuting patterns on Mondays and Fridays.
The time interval of day is discretised into 20-minute intervals, resulting in 3 intervals per hour.
Given the operational window from 5:00 to 23:00, this provides 18 · 3 = 54 distinct intervals.
An additional category is included to represent the time interval at 23:00, resulting in a total
of 55 categories for the time-of-day representation. These categories are one-hot encoded to
enable the model to distinguish between diﬀerent time periods throughout the day, such as
early morning, midday, and evening travel patterns.
Together, these encodings contribute 5 + 55 = 60 binary features to the model input, providing
a structured way for the model to learn time-dependent travel patterns.

Methodology
This chapter outlines the methodology used in the thesis, covering the design and training
of the neural network models, as well as how their performance is evaluated. The chapter is
structured around three main components: station-level forecasting, OD-level forecasting, and
the reconciliation of these forecasts into a coherent and uniﬁed prediction. It also describes how

5.1
Global Model for Passenger Flow Prediction
The primary model investigated in this thesis is SDT-GRU, introduced by Yang et al. in
their work "Are Graphs and GCNs Necessary for Short-Term Metro Ridership Forecasting?"
[Yan+24]. They propose a solution for the multi-step short-term metro ridership prediction
problem i.e. passenger ﬂow prediction.
5.1.1
DT-GRU
To eﬀectively model spatio-temporal patterns in metro ridership data, Yang et al. propose a
novel architecture called DT-GRU, which integrates Dual-branch Transformer encoder layers
within a GRU framework. As seen in Figure 5.1, one branch processes the current ridership data
to capture synchronous spatial correlations, while the other processes the previous historical
ﬂow data to capture asynchronous spatial dependencies. By replacing GNNs with Transformer
branches, this approach removes the need for manually constructed graphs, captures both local
and global spatial patterns via self-attention, and avoids the over-squashing problem common
in GNNs.
In this context, over-squashing refers to the phenomenon where important long-
range spatial dependencies are compressed into ﬁxed-size node representations, which leads to
information loss and degraded performance. Overall, DT-GRU simpliﬁes the modelling pipeline
and improves prediction accuracy [Yan+24].

outperformed by SDT-GRU and PVCGN in previous studies. As a result, a direct comparison
was conducted between SDT-GRU and PVCGN, as these represent the best-performing
architectures in the ﬁeld. PVCGN will be brieﬂy described in the following section, providing
a concise overview shorter than the original paper.
PVCGN forecasts station-level metro ridership using a seq2seq model that combines local and
global spatio-temporal patterns.
First, it represents station interactions via three distinct
graphs: a physical graph encodes the actual track layout, a similarity graph links stations
with comparable historical usage patterns using Dynamic Time Warping, and a correlation
graph reﬂects observed origin-destination ﬂows. Each adjacency matrix is row-normalised to
highlight relative connections.
These graphs feed into a Graph Convolutional GRU
(GC-GRU), which replaces the GRUs usual linear transformations with graph convolutions to
learn how spatial relationships evolve over time. In parallel, a standard fully connected GRU
captures broader temporal trends across all stations.
At every time step, the GC-GRUs
spatially informed state and the FC-GRUs global state are fused to form a uniﬁed
representation using a Collaborative Gated Recurrent Module (CGRM), that feeds into the
seq2seq encoder and decoder.
This enables PVCGN to leverage both ﬁne-grained station
topology and system-wide dynamics, making it a powerful benchmark for the SDT-GRU
model. The CGRM module and PVCGN can be seen in Figure 5.3.

5.1.4
Model Comparisons
To ensure a fair comparison, a controlled experiment is conducted using the ﬁnal conﬁgurations
reported in the respective papers. Both models are evaluated using the hyperparameter settings
shown to yield the best results in their original papers, without incorporating any additional
features or architectural modiﬁcations. For the SDT-GRU model, the number of RNN layers
is set to 2, the number of RNN units to 128, the number of attention heads to 8, and the feed-
forward network dimension to 128. The only exception is the learning rate, which is uniformly
set to 0.00001 to enable longer training and prevent early stagnation in the learning process.
The goal of this evaluation is to assess the base performance of both models on the current
dataset and identify the most suitable candidate for the forecast reconciliation task.
To benchmark the global model, two machine learning models are also evaluated: Random
Forest [Bre01] and XGBoost [CG16], as well as a statistical baseline, the Historical Average
(HA), which simply predicts the next time step as the mean of past observations for the same
time period. The ML models are well-known tree-based methods that serve as powerful baselines
for predictive tasks. Although not speciﬁcally designed for time series forecasting, they can still
perform eﬀectively when temporal patterns are captured using lagged features. Both models are
trained with their default settings, using 100 trees. XGBoost, in particular, is often competitive
with deep learning models. Their performance provides a reference point for evaluating the
added value of the deep global model.
Although all models share the same core structure - taking 8 lagged inﬂow and outﬂow values as
input to predict the next time step - they incorporate diﬀerent auxiliary components. SDT-GRU
includes an embedding of the number of 20-minute time steps after 05:00 to capture temporal
progression throughout the day (as seen in Equation 4.1), while PVCGN integrates the three
separate graph structures to model spatial relationships. Despite these diﬀerences, their core
forecasting objective and input format remain aligned, making them directly comparable under
the same evaluation setup.
For evaluation and testing, the Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error (MSE), and
Mean Error (ME) are reported to provide a comprehensive view of model performance across
diﬀerent error perspectives. Since all models are evaluated on the same test samples, paired
t-tests are used to compare performance on the two best-performing models. This approach
accounts for the dependency between predictions by diﬀerent models on the same data points,
reducing variance and improving the sensitivity of the test. Signiﬁcance is assessed at both the
1% and 5% levels.

5.3.1
Implementation
We build on the implementation of the paper [Zhu+22] and modify both the SDT-GRU and
mGraphSAGE networks so that each model outputs the three ZINB parameters π, n, and p
instead of a single scalar count.
Speciﬁcally, in the SDT-GRU architecture, the original ﬁnal fully-connected layer (which
produced one inﬂow and one outﬂow prediction per station) is replaced by three parallel linear
heads.
After the stacked encoder and decoder produce a ﬁnal hidden representation, this
vector is passed through three separate linear layers: one to predict n, one to predict p, and
one to predict π for inﬂow and outﬂow, respectively. The raw outputs of these layers are then
transformed via softplus(·) for n (to ensure n > 0) and sigmoid(·) for p and π (to constrain
them to [0, 1]).
Similarly, for mGraphSAGE, the original linear projection that produced a single scalar per
OD pair is replaced by a linear layer with three outputs. After concatenating the four graph-
convolutional hidden representations and applying a ReLU activation, the resulting vector is
passed through a fully connected layer that maps it to three outputs. The three outputs are
then converted to valid ZINB parameters by applying the same transformations as above.
In summary, by extending each models ﬁnal projection to three heads and enforcing the
appropriate activation functions, we convert the OD and station-level predictors into fully
probabilistic ZINB estimators.

5.4
Forecast Reconciliation: One-step Forecasting
This section describes the forecast reconciliation methodology applied speciﬁcally to one-step-
ahead forecasting. It is largely inspired by the approach presented in [BC21], due to its strong
theoretical and practical advantages. In the framework, Burba et al. extend the traditional
(M × N) reconciliation matrix P , described in Section 2.5 and speciﬁcally in Equation 2.26, by
replacing it with a more ﬂexible, learnable function P : RN →RM, implemented as a neural
network. This function serves as a trainable mapping from the base forecasts to reconciled
forecasts at the bottom level of the hierarchy.
The proposed method can be interpreted as a simple encoder-decoder architecture: the neural
network P acts as the encoder, while the hierarchy-speciﬁc matrix S ∈RN×M serves as a ﬁxed
decoder. The encoder transforms base forecasts into bottom-level reconciled forecasts and the
decoder reconstructs forecasts at all hierarchical levels by aggregating the bottom-level outputs.
A schematic of this trainable reconciliation structure is shown in Figure 5.5.
Figure 5.5: Trainable reconciler structure [BC21]
This method is advantageous because it extends traditional linear reconciliation approaches to
a broader class of non-linear models by leveraging neural networks,
enabling greater
generalisation.
This allows our method to capture more complex relationships within the
hierarchy. Second, it provides ﬂexibility by enabling the use of custom loss functions tailored
to speciﬁc forecasting objectives, whether prioritising particular error metrics or focusing on
performance at speciﬁc hierarchical levels.
In contrast to traditional reconciliation methods, such as bottom-up, top-down, or optimal
combination approaches that rely on ﬁxed heuristics or assumptions, this learning-based
approach allows us to directly optimise for the forecasting goals most relevant to our
application. Additionally, the neural network framework is easy to implement and integrates
seamlessly with modern deep learning pipelines.

Trainable Base Forecast Models

of all components, leading to suboptimal performance.
Given these observations, we adopted a sequential training strategy.

global and OD models independently and selected the best-performing versions based on
validation performance. These selected models were then used to generate input forecasts for
the reconciliation network, which was trained separately.

5.6
Disruptions Periods
To further investigate and validate our approach, we conduct a dedicated disruption analysis
for both one-step and multi-step forecasts. It serves two primary goals: (1) to evaluate whether
the model can maintain predictive accuracy under disrupted conditions, and (2) to determine
whether the reconciliation method improves forecasting performance in the presence of such
disruptions.
We examine three key types of disruptions for one-step forecasting: operational disruption (train
cancellations and delays), weather events, and holiday periods where the demand patterns might
ﬂuctuate. For multi-step forecasting only operational disruptions will be investigated.
Operational disruption conditions are deﬁned based on elevated train cancellation rates and
mean delays at either origin or destination stations. These conditions, adapted from Nguyen
et al. [Vie+24], help us identify periods of degraded service and consistently evaluate model
robustness under diﬀerent levels of operational stress. Note, that these periods are deducted
from the features also integrated in the OD model, as seen in Section 4.3.4.
These disruptions are identiﬁed using threshold-based criteria, such as a non-zero cancellation
rate or mean delay exceeding 60, 180, or 300 seconds. These thresholds span a spectrum from
moderate to severe disruptions, allowing us to examine model performance under both typical
and challenging conditions. Periods with no cancellations or delays are also included to serve
as a baseline for comparison. The speciﬁc disruption conditions used in the evaluation are as
follows:

7.1
Global Model Performance (Station-Level Predictions)
RQ1: How accurately can a global model forecast station-level ﬂow for both one-step and multi-
step, and to what extent do graph-based spatial encodings improve both immediate and extended
predictions?
7.1.1
One-Step Forecasting Performance
The results demonstrate that a global forecasting approach, particularly the SDT-GRU
model, achieves strong accuracy at the station-level for both one-step and multi-step. Across
key evaluation metrics such as MSE and MAE, SDT-GRU consistently outperforms both
traditional machine learning models (e.g., XGBoost, RF) and other deep learning approaches
(e.g., PVCGN). Its superiority in one-step forecasting is statistically signiﬁcant and likely
attributable to its transformer-based architecture, which eﬀectively captures both spatial and
temporal dependencies.
When examining predictions across the full range of demand values, limitations emerge.
While SDT-GRU performs reliably within the common low-demand range (0-12),
it
systematically underestimates higher demand values.
This indicates a tendency toward
conservative predictions centred around the most frequent values - a common outcome when
models are trained on imbalanced datasets where high-demand instances are rare.
These
results highlight the ongoing challenge of accurately modelling rare but impactful demand
spikes in global forecasting settings.
7.1.2
Impact of Graph-Based Spatial Encodings
Interestingly, while graph-based spatial encodings were initially assumed to oﬀer a signiﬁcant
performance advantage, this assumption is not strongly supported by the results. PVCGN, a
model that explicitly incorporates graph convolution mechanisms to represent spatial
structure, performs worse than SDT-GRU despite its graph-based design. This suggests that
the spatial dependencies learned implicitly through SDT-GRU’s attention mechanisms may be
su"cient - or even more eﬀective - than the explicitly deﬁned graph topology used in PVCGN.
Consequently, the added architectural complexity of integrating graph convolutions does not
necessarily translate to better predictive accuracy, and may in fact introduce additional noise

if the graph structure does not align perfectly with the dynamics of the data. This highlights
the need to carefully evaluate the actual utility of spatial encoding strategies rather than
assuming their beneﬁt based on model design alone.
7.1.3
Feature Importance and Loss Function Eﬀects
Feature studies further reveal that incorporating temporal features such as holiday and cyclic
time substantially improves model performance, whereas weather-related features contribute
less.
This suggests that predictable calendar-based patterns, such as weekday cycles and
holidays, are particularly informative for public transportation systems, where passenger
behaviour tends to follow regular weekly rhythms.
In addition to feature engineering, experiments with diﬀerent loss functions demonstrated that
both MSE and ZINB losses yielded competitive results, with ZINB achieving the lowest MSE
among all global models. This improvement is likely attributable more to the negative binomial
distribution component than to zero-inﬂation, as the global model’s data contains only about
10% zeros. These ﬁndings suggest that further exploration and reﬁnement of distribution-based
loss functions could be a promising direction for improving model performance in future work.
7.1.4
Optimisation and Model Complexity
Although the performance gains from hyperparameter tuning and loss function selection were
relatively modest, they highlight the value of ﬁne-tuned optimisation. At the same time, these
improvements reﬂect diminishing returns: increasing model complexity beyond a certain point
yields limited additional beneﬁt compared to feature selection.
Therefore, in practical
applications, it is important to weigh the beneﬁts of improved accuracy against the increased
complexity and computational cost of more sophisticated models.
7.1.5
Multi-Step Forecasting Performance
In this study, it was assumed that the model achieving the best one-step forecasting
performance, SDT-GRU, would similarly excel in the multi-step setting. SDT-GRU maintains
competitive accuracy from horizon T+1 through T+6.
As expected in autoregressive
multi-step forecasting, the model’s MSE increases gradually over time, reﬂecting the growing
uncertainty associated with predicting further into the future.
This pattern is a natural result of the model’s sequential design, where each forecast step is
based on the previous one. As predictions are made further ahead, small inaccuracies introduced
early on can inﬂuence later outputs, leading to a gradual rise in error. This behaviour aligns
with the inductive bias of autoregressive models and indicates that SDT-GRU is eﬀectively
capturing the temporal structure of the task.

7.2
OD Model Performance (Origin-Destination Predictions)
RQ2: How eﬀectively can an expanded GNN-based OD model handle conditions of high sparsity
and irregular demand patterns while maintaining accuracy for both one-step and multi-step
predictions?
The origin-destination demand prediction task posed substantial challenges due to the
high-dimensional, sparse, and irregular nature of the data. In this thesis, we addressed these
challenges by applying and extending the mGraphSAGE model, a graph neural network
architecture designed to learn spatio-temporal dependencies across multiple graph types. The
results demonstrate that the model is capable of capturing meaningful patterns in OD ﬂows.
7.2.1
One-Step Forecasting Performance
Beginning at the one-step forecasting task, the mGraphSAGE model consistently outperformed
all baseline models, including Random Forest, XGBoost, and the historical average. This result
reinforces the strength of GNN-based approaches in capturing complex spatial dependencies and
temporal dynamics across the OD matrix, even under sparsity. The multi-graph architecture
enabled the model to leverage various relational signals, which likely improved its ability to
generalise across OD pairs with limited demand variation.
Notably, the beneﬁt of using a GNN is more evident for OD-level forecasting than for station-
level predictions. This likely stems from the structural diﬀerences between the two tasks. The
OD matrix includes 132 pairs, which are more interconnected than the 12 stations used in the
global model. This gives the GNN more opportunity to learn from spatial relationships across
the network. In contrast, the station-level setup has fewer locations and less spatial complexity,
so a GNN may add little value. This shows that the usefulness of graph-based models depends
on the task and is especially strong in settings with more complex or detailed spatial structure.
Finally, as the mGraphSAGE model was predeﬁned for use in this thesis, we did not
benchmark it against other GNN-based or non-GNN deep learning approaches on our speciﬁc
dataset or within the context of forecast reconciliation. Although the original paper includes
benchmarking, future work should revisit this comparison to ensure that mGraphSAGE
remains the most eﬀective and scalable architecture for short-term OD demand prediction.
7.2.2
Alternative Loss Formulations: ZINB
An alternative formulation explored in this thesis involved training mGraphSAGE using a ZINB
loss, motivated by the distribution’s suitability for sparse count data. However, this variant did

not outperform the MSE-trained model, despite expectations that ZINB would better capture

introduces added complexity due to its parameterisation (mean, dispersion, zero-inﬂation),
which can lead to overﬁtting or unstable training in sparse or imbalanced settings. Second,
while ZINB aligns more closely with the data-generating process, maximum likelihood training
does not always yield lower point forecast errors like MSE or MAE. Finally, the MSE loss may
have implicitly encouraged smoother and more generalisable predictions - an advantage in sparse
and noisy environments. This suggests that while distributional modelling holds promise, it may
require additional regularisation or tailored training strategies to outperform simpler objectives
in practice.
7.2.3
Error Distribution and Predictive Behaviour
To gain a more granular understanding of the mGraphSAGE model’s predictive behaviour, we
examined the distribution of its predictions conditioned on true OD demand levels.
The
predictive patterns reﬂect the data imbalance inherent in OD forecasting: the model is heavily
exposed to zero or low-demand intervals, while high-demand cases are rare.
As a result,
mGraphSAGE becomes increasingly conservative for larger true values, underpredicting high
ﬂows and defaulting toward the mean. While this behaviour is expected, it limits the model’s

In the multi-step setting,

lacks a strong sequential inductive bias - resulting in stable predictions that do not meaningfully
diﬀerentiate between near and distant horizons. While this stabilises performance, it also limits
the model’s ability to reﬂect growing uncertainty in longer horizons, which is a trade-oﬀworth

7.2.5
Scalability and Adaptability
Overall, the mGraphSAGE model demonstrates strong scalability and adaptability within the
OD forecasting task. Its inductive graph-based design allows it to operate eﬀectively across a
large number of OD pairs. The model scales well to multi-step prediction, leveraging shared
representations to maintain stable performance across horizons. However, its generalisation is
inﬂuenced by the underlying data distribution, particularly the prevalence of zero demand and
scarcity of high-ﬂow examples, which can lead to underprediction and reduced precision for
low-to-moderate demand levels. Addressing these limitations through class-balanced sampling

varying demand structures and transit network conﬁgurations.
7.3
Impact of Forecast Reconciliation
RQ3: How does deep reconciliation impact forecasting accuracy and coherency across one-step
and extended multi-step forecasts, compared to traditional reconciliation methods?
To evaluate the impact of reconciliation methods, we assess them across three dimensions: (1)
their ability to improve OD-level forecasts, (2) their eﬀect on station-level predictions, and (3)
their capacity to enforce aggregate coherency across the OD-to-station hierarchy. This framing
allows us to compare deep learning-based and traditional reconciliation techniques not only in
terms of predictive accuracy, but also in how well they preserve the structural integrity of the
forecast system.
7.3.1
Performance of Deep Learning-Based Reconcilers
Beginning with the one-step setting, we see that among the deep learning-based methods, the
Fully Connected Reconciler (FCR) trained with MSE loss emerged as a strong performer. It
improved overall forecasting accuracy relative to the base model, while achieving exact coherence
by construction (reﬂected in a mean error of zero across all levels). The architecture, though
simple, was able to learn a meaningful mapping from base forecasts to reconciled outputs
without requiring handcrafted rules or explicit structural encoding - structural information was
instead enforced indirectly through the loss function via the summing matrix.
What is particularly noteworthy is the reconciler’s eﬀect on the OD model’s hierarchical
behaviour.
Not only did the point forecast accuracy at the OD level improve, but the
aggregated OD forecasts also became more coherent with the station-level observations. This
demonstrates a dual beneﬁt of forecast reconciliation:
it enhances local accuracy while
simultaneously enforcing consistency with the system’s aggregation logic.
Although the
absolute improvements in metrics are modest, the reconciled model succeeds in reducing both
dispersion and bias, oﬀering better OD forecasts that respect the aggregation hierarchy to a
greater extent.

just as a consistency tool, but as a strategic asset for handling disruptions.
7.5
Limitations
As prefaced, one of the primary limitations of the project lies in the dataset and its extreme
sparsity. A signiﬁcant proportion of the entries are zeros, corresponding to time periods or
regions with no recorded activity. This sparsity poses a substantial challenge for modelling, as

==PAGE:96==
it reduces the signal-to-noise ratio and hinders the models’ ability to learn meaningful temporal
or spatial dependencies. In practice, models may either overﬁt to the most common demand
or converge to predicting all zeros in an attempt to minimise loss - particularly when using
standard loss functions such as mean squared error.
In addition to data sparsity, the limited observability of OD ﬂows presents a signiﬁcant obstacle
to real-time prediction.
Since OD movements are only partially recorded, the model must
operate with an incomplete view of the system’s true state.
There are also notable computational trade-oﬀs.
Training a multi-component system that
integrates hierarchical reconciliation, graph-based encoders, and sequence models requires
signiﬁcant resources and engineering complexity.
These systems often involve nested
optimisation or sequential training stages, which increases training time and hyperparameter
sensitivity. This complexity can make the model hard to use in real-time or in environments
with limited resources.
Furthermore, the generalisability of the proposed approach to other cities or transportation
networks is not guaranteed. Network topology, travel behaviour, and data quality can vary
substantially across locations. Without adaptation or re-training, the model may underperform
when transferred to a diﬀerent context.
7.6
Implications
7.6.1
Theoretical
This work advances hierarchical time series forecasting by jointly modelling OD- and
station-level ﬂows: an integration that, to our knowledge, has not been previously explored.
By combining two distinct forecasting models within a uniﬁed reconciliation framework, it
enables improved predictive performance for both one-step and multi-step horizons under
varying operational conditions, including disruptions, weather events, and holidays.
It also
contributes to the broader goal of coherence in hierarchical forecasting, ensuring that
predictions at diﬀerent aggregation levels are structurally aligned. This alignment is especially
important in the context of urban rail networks, where few existing approaches address
consistency across spatial and functional hierarchies. The proposed framework oﬀers a novel
direction for incorporating coherency into deep learning-based forecasting pipelines in complex
transportation systems.

7.6.2
Practical
In real-world transit operations, having coherent forecasts between OD-level ﬂows and
station-level counts is essential for consistent and eﬀective planning. Train operators rely on
OD-level forecasts to manage passenger movements between speciﬁc origin-destination pairs,
while station-level forecasts guide decisions related to sta"ng, platform use, gate operations,
and train assignments.
When these forecasts are not aligned, for example, when OD ﬂows
suggest high arrivals at a station but the station-level forecast does not, planners face
conﬂicting signals that can undermine operational decisions and reduce trust in the predictive
system. Coherency ensures that resource allocation is both accurate and e"cient: staﬀand
vehicles can be directed where demand is actually expected, and crowd control measures can
be activated proactively.
This alignment becomes even more critical during unexpected
events, such as service disruptions or emergencies, where operators must react quickly to
shifting demand patterns and need reliable, uniﬁed forecasts to do so. Additionally, coherent
forecasting supports accurate reporting to stakeholders and contributes to safer and smoother
passenger travel by supporting better coordination across the network.
The ability to generate accurate multi-step forecasts is particularly valuable for transit
operators, as it provides a longer planning horizon and more lead time to implement
operational changes before demand shifts occur.
For example, if the model predicts an
unexpected surge in demand during what is typically an oﬀ-peak period, operators can
proactively adjust service levels, reallocate staﬀ, or prepare for increased crowding.
In
contrast, one-step-ahead forecasts, especially those only 20 minutes ahead, often dont allow
enough time to respond, especially when actions require coordination across teams or moving
resources.
Multi-step forecasts are therefore essential for enabling proactive, eﬀective
responses to changing demand.

8
Conclusion
This thesis examined how forecast reconciliation can enhance the accuracy and coherence of
hierarchical demand forecasts in urban rail transit systems. The core objective was to evaluate
whether
combining
origin-destination
and
station-level
predictions
using
reconciliation
strategies could yield better forecasting performance, particularly in settings where data is
sparse and long-range operational planning is critical.
We found that while deep learning models such as mGraphSAGE and SDT-GRU are highly
eﬀective on their own, their independent predictions often violate structural relationships
inherent in the OD-to-station hierarchy. This motivated the use of forecast reconciliation as a
post-processing step to enforce consistency and potentially improve overall accuracy. Across
both one-step and multi-step settings, reconciliation consistently reduced error and eliminated
bias.
These gains,
although modest in absolute terms,
reﬂect meaningful structural
improvements.
The already high performance of the base models meant that the room for
further improvement through reconciliation was limited. This suggests that in the context of
URT, the primary value of reconciliation may lie not in boosting point accuracy, but in
enforcing structural integrity.
A key ﬁnding of this work is that traditional statistical methods, like Minimum Trace
Optimisation, tend to perform best in the one-step setting, where assumptions about unbiased
residuals and error structure are more likely to hold.
In contrast, deep learning-based
reconcilers excelled in the multi-step setting by learning stable mappings that generalised
across all horizons. This robustness enabled reconciled multi-step forecasts to match or even
exceed the accuracy of one-step predictions, despite being trained under greater uncertainty.
Performance during disruption periods showed that structurally aware forecasting with a deep
learning-based reconciler oﬀers greater robustness under atypical conditions. Reconciliation is
particularly valuable in these scenarios, where the traditional methods struggle to generalise.
This thesis shows that forecast reconciliation works well with modern deep learning models and
brings real beneﬁts to structured prediction tasks in public transportation. Such results are
promising for urban rail systems where accurate and coherent forecasts are crucial for real-time
decision-making, disruption handling, and resource allocation over multiple future time steps.
Future work could extend this to probabilistic forecasting, multi-modal transport systems, or
more adaptive models that respond better to disruptions. As urban mobility systems grow in
complexity, using reconciliation to ensure coherent and reliable forecasts will be increasingly
important.
