Abstract
It is suspected that PD patients have an abnormal pattern of nocturnal EMs since the areas of the
brain responsible for EMs are aﬀected in PD, but investigation of EMs in PD patients is not a part
of the routine for diagnosis. Manual EM scoring is a time consuming task, greatly aﬀected by the
individual scorer and today no systematic approach for EM scoring is deﬁned. PD is one of the
most prevalent neurodegenerative diseases world-wide and it is of high interest to investigate early
biomarkers in PD.
Two detectors are developed for REMs and SEMs, trained on a GS obtained from a consensus
between two individual experts. The experts have scored EMs in 12 subjects of which nine are
used for training and three are used for testing and validation. The validated detectors are applied
on data from 128 subject including controls, PLMD, iRBD, PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients.
The detectors perform with F1-scores of 0.410 for REMs and 0.267 for SEMs when compared
to GS. When applied to new data duration, density and coverage (percentage of time covered by
EMs) of EMs across the sleep stages wake, REM, N1, N2 and N3 are calculated. In a comparison
of the groups, the signiﬁcant ﬁndings are that controls and PLMD patients have a higher density
and coverage of REMs in REM sleep, lower coverage of SEMs in N2 sleep and higher density of
SEMs in wake than PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients. Additionally controls have more REMs in
N3 sleep, which might be due to slow-wave artefacts.

Flow diagram showing the steps of the EM detector implemented by Christensen

Introduction
The most pronounced change in brain and body activity occurs when a person changes between
the stages of awake, REM sleep and non-REM sleep. A classiﬁcation of the stages is based on
complex electrophysiological signals including brain activity measured by electroencephalography,
eye movements measured by electrooculography, motor activity by electromyography and several
other physiological modalities. Eye movements (EM) are extensively investigated when classifying
which of the above stages a person is in since they vary greatly over the stages. The interest in
nocturnal EMs started years ago and they have been used as a marker for stage classiﬁcation since
the sleep stage deﬁnition stated by Rechtshaﬀen and Kales in 1968 [7]. The current deﬁnition of
EMs relies on the newest standard for sleep stage classiﬁcation, the American Association of Sleep
Medicine (AASM) standard [8], which is based on a consensus between experts. Nevertheless, a
systematic study of the distribution and morphology of nocturnal EMs still lacks to this day. There
is no clear guideline for examining EMs [9], i.e. no deﬁnition for amplitude, duration, frequency,
distribution or start/stop characteristics for EMs.
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common age-related neurodegenerative disease today
and the disease not only aﬀects patients but also their relatives. It is of high interest to ﬁnd a
method to detect PD as early as possible in order to give the patient a chance to make a lifestyle
change which may slow down the progression of the disease [10]. It may even be possible to make a
cure or discover a medication that will slow down the progression of PD if taken early in the course of
disease. The diagnosis of PD today is primarily based on a clinical examination, which means that
motor symptoms must be present before the disease can be diagnosed and treatment can begin [10].
It may be that analysis of nocturnal EMs could reveal new information about brain activity within
health and disease. Speciﬁcally, a deeper and more systematic characterization of nocturnal EMs
could potential be indicative of certain diseases. In current project, it is hypothesised that abnormal
nocturnal EMs within the diﬀerent sleep stages can be seen for patients with Parkinson’s disease.
In this thesis, two EM detectors will be implemented to automatically detect the two most
common form of nocturnal EMs; slow EMs and rapid EMs.
The detectors will be applied on
clinical data to evaluate if any abnormalities in EM duration, density or coverage are seen for PD
patients and patients at high risk of developing PD compared to healthy control subjects. This
thesis will get closer to answer whether analysis of nocturnal EMs hold potential to be a biomarker
for PD.

1.1
Problem Statements
The main objective of this MSc project is to implement two eye movement detectors, for automatic
detection of slow and rapid nocturnal EMs. The automatic detectors are trained, validated and
tested using a gold standard found from a consensus between two experts scoring diﬀerent types
of EMs in data from control subjects. The detectors are furthermore applied on a clinical data
set containing PD patients, patients at high risk of developing PD (patients with a disorder called
idiopathic REM sleep behavior disorder (iRBD)), patients with nocturnal motor abnormalities but
not linked to PD (patients with a disorder called periodic leg movement disorder (PLMD)) and
a group of control subjects. It is hypothesized that abnormal EMs are seen for PD patients, and
to a lesser degree for iRBD patients compared to healthy subjects and that EMs, therefore, have
potential to be a biomarker for early identiﬁcation of PD.
The speciﬁc problems to be solved are:
1. Investigation of the inter-rater variability of identifying diﬀerent types of nocturnal EMs, in
order to get an idea of what an acceptable detector performance would be.
2. Development of two detectors for the following types of nocturnal EMs:
(a) Rapid eye movements
(b) Slow eye movements
3. Investigation of the potential for nocturnal EMs to be a biomarker for PD and to look into
the following measures of the EMs across sleep stages:
(a) Duration
(b) Density
(c) Coverage

1.2
Literature Study
For this section a literature study has been carried out to give an overview of the state of the art for
detection of nocturnal EMs. The litterature covered has been identiﬁed using PubMed [11] with the
following keywords; "sleep", "eye movements", "nocturnal eye movements", "EOG", "automatic
detection", "Parkinson’s disease" and "REM behavioural disorder", and the criteria: written within
the last 20 years (except a few earlier important studies found via citations in the identiﬁed articles).
1.2.1
Sleep Disturbances in PD Patients
In 1988 Lees et al. studied the relation between disturbed nocturnal sleep and PD [12]. They
found in a survey among 220 PD patients that 215 patients reported some kind of sleep disabilities
at night or upon waking. Most answers referenced less severe problems such as inability to turn
over or a frequent urge to urinate and two third of the patients reported an acceptable or good
sleep quality despite the sleep disabilities. Still, this survey indicates that sleep disturbances are a
frequent challenge for PD patients.
In 2002 Chaudhuri et al. [13] developed a PD sleep scale (PDSS) for quantiﬁcation of nocturnal
sleep problems in PD. The sleep scale is based on 15 questions about sleep quality and commonly
reported sleep disturbances. Answers are given on a visual analogue scale ranging from 1 - 10,
where high values represent good sleep quality and no sleep disturbances. The sleep scale was
tested on both PD patients and healthy controls, and it was seen that PD patients had a lower
score, indicating more problems during nocturnal sleep. This study found that decreasing scores
followed an advance in the progression of PD. Svensson et al. [14] performed a study in Norway in
2012 using the PDSS on 176 PD patients and found that 17 % had a general score below 82 (out
of a maximum 150) and 70 % had a score of maximum ﬁve in minimum one question, indicating
sleep disturbances and poor sleep quality among PD patients.
In a review by dos Santos et al. from 2014 [15] it is stated that a disturbed sleep pattern is not
considered part of the diagnosis of PD, even though many studies suggest that sleep disturbances
and changes in the sleep cycles may occur years before PD motor symptoms are observed [15–21].
1.2.2
Link Between iRBD and PD
Schenk et al. [22] were the ﬁrst to report the relation between PD and idiopathic REM sleep
behaviour disorder (iRBD) in a study conducted in 1996, which has led to much research within
the ﬁeld subsequently [16–20, 23, 24]. Schenk et al. found in their ﬁrst study that 38 % of the
subjects developed PD or dementia after a mean of 13 years from the onset of iRBD. In a follow-up
study 16 years later they found that 81 % of the subjects developed PD or dementia 29 years after
the onset of iRBD. This study was conducted on males at age > 50 years. Accordingly, a study by
Iranzo et al. [17] involving 44 iRBD patients showed that 45 % developed some kind of neurological
disorder including PD at an average of 11.5 years after iRBD onset. All these studies have clearly
demonstrated that a diagnosis of iRBD is the most certain PD biomarker to date.

==PAGE:26==
1.2.3
Nocturnal EM, iRBD and PD
Since iRBD is a disease aﬀecting REM sleep, it is potentially interesting to investigate nocturnal
EMs in iRBD and PD patients. Also, neurons controlling nocturnal EMs are found in the brain
stem in the same locations as neurons involved in early PD or even pre-PD [25]. Therefore analysis
of nocturnal EMs hold huge potential to be an early marker of PD.
EMs during wakefulness in PD patients have been studied before, and in a review from 2017 by
Pretegiani et al. [26] it is stated that there is a relation between changes in saccades (rapid voluntary
movements of the eyes) and PD. In a study from 2013 by Christensen et al. [24] analysis of EOG
is used for automatic classiﬁcation of controls, iRBD and PD patients based on features reﬂecting
nocturnal EMs.
They use a Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic model based on feature
extraction from two EOG signals from both control subjects, PD and iRBD patients to distinguish
between controls and patients. The automatic detection algorithm in this study performs with an
accuracy of 0.900 in separating subject groups with a limited number of subjects.
1.2.4
Automatic EM Detection Procedures
The use of automatic methods has been investigated widely in the ﬁeld of sleep research, e.g. for
sleep stage classiﬁcation [27–30] and sleep spindles detection [31–34]. For the detection of nocturnal
EMs, only few studies have been made [3,9,35–38], shortly went through here.
Investigation of EOG is included in a PSG analysis, but scoring of single EMs is not a part
of the clinical routine. Manual scoring of EMs is a highly subjective and time-consuming task
which is why it is of great value to design an automated algorithm for nocturnal EM detection
[9,38–40]. Detection of EMs in wakefulness might also be useful in other contexts, for example in
a human/computer communication system for amyotrophic lateral sclerosis patients, as suggested
by J. Heo et al. [41] or for detection of driver drowsiness (also using EEG) to avoid road accidents
as done by Firke et al. [42].
In an article from 2000 by Tsuji et al. [36] discrete wavelet transform (DWT) was used to detect
REMs as an indication of REM sleep. The Haar wavelet (which resembles the REM waveform)
and thresholding were used. A total of 30 minutes out of 8 hours of sleep was examined, and the
wavelet transform was applied to 8 s at a time. The algorithm was compared to one visual scorer,
who scored REM sleep and tested on one subject. By shifting the wavelet phase by π/4, 96 % of
the REMs were detected, deﬁned as true positive. In addition, the algorithm found 22 % REMs
not detected by the visual scorer, deﬁned as false positive.
Another application of DWT is described by Magosso et al. [37] in a pilot study from 2006
concerning detection of slow EMs (SEM) in patients with sleep apnea using multi resolution DWT
with the Daubechies wavelet at 10 levels. SEMs are detected in EOG signals from 10 males in the
period from pre-sleep wakefulness to ﬁrst N2 sleep. The results are compared to a gold standard
obtained from a consensus between two experts. The algorithm performs with an accuracy of 0.804
± 0.040, sensitivity of 0.672 ± 0.074 and speciﬁcity of 0.839 ± 0.087 when compared to the gold
standard. Disagreement mainly occurred due to diﬀerent detection of beginnings and endings of
4
==PAGE:27==
the SEMs. One year later Magosso et al. tested the algorithm on 10 overnight and ﬁve 24 hour
EOG recordings from healthy subjects [9] and found a concordance index of 0.620, sensitivity of
0.807 and speciﬁcity of 0.630 on detection of 30 seconds epochs containing SEMs when compared
to a gold standard obtained from three experts.
In 2011 Boulus et al. [43] developed an automatic SEMs detector, by ﬁltering an EOG signal in the
range 1-5 Hz and using the fact that left and right EOG signal appear anti-correlated. The algorithm
was applied on the ﬁrst and last ﬁve minutes of each sleep stage (excluding slow wave sleep) and had
an accuracy of 0.830. Yetton et al. [38] performed a study in 2016, where they detected REMs using
feature thresholding and machine learning. The features were amplitude, slope, cross-correlation
and components from DWT, and the machine learning approach was using an adaptive boosting
classiﬁcation algorithm. The algorithm automatically regulated the thresholds and combinations
of multiple features using a training data set. When combining the two approaches and comparing
the performance to a consensus between three experts F1 = 0.807.
The use of wavelet transform in a complex form (dual-tree complex wavelet transform (DTCWT))
for automatic EMs detection is described in a study by Christensen et al. from 2017 [3]. This
detector utilises that the left and right EOG signal appears anti-correlated during an EM and
calculates the phase of the signals. The accuracy of this detector is 0.870 when no overlapping
method is used, but increases to 0.910 when periods with EMs are counted as true positives if at
least 50 % of the period is identiﬁed as EMs by both the gold standard and the detector. The gold
standard in this study is a consensus between two experts. This EM detector will be described
thoroughly in a later section since the development of new detectors in the project will be based
on this detector.

1.3
Clinical Background
1.3.1
How to Analyse Sleep
A polysomnography (PSG) is the standard test performed to analyse sleep and diagnose a range
of sleep disorders. In a PSG several physiological modalities are measured while the subject is
sleeping, including; brain activity (electroencephalography, EEG), heart rate (electrocardiography,
ECG), snoring, muscle activity from various muscles (electromyography, EMG) and eye movements
(electrooculography, EOG), among others [44]. Sleep data is classiﬁed into ﬁve stages, and the
classiﬁcation relies on visual inspection of the PSG data and identiﬁcation of speciﬁc patterns. The
basic understanding and classiﬁcation of sleep staging is based on the deﬁnition by Rechtshaﬀen
and Kales back in 1968 [7]. A newer guideline which is deﬁned by the American Academy of Sleep
Medicine in 2007 is used to score sleep data today [8,45,46].
From visual inspection of the PSG data (mainly EEG, EOG and EMG), a hypnogram can be
conducted by sleep technicians. The hypnogram is a summary of the night’s sleep giving an overview
of the overall sleep architecture of the night. Each 30 seconds period is called an epoch, and every
epoch is labelled with one of the ﬁve stages of sleep; wakefulness (W), rapid eye movement sleep
(REM) and non-rapid eye movement sleep (N1, N2 and N3). An elaboration of the sleep stages and
its characteristics will follow. The hypnogram does not take into account important but unexploited
macro and micro sleep events e.g. individual sleep spindles, K-complexes, slow waves or EMs. This
results in limitations for both diagnosis and a deeper understanding of the pathophysiology of
diﬀerent diseases.
A hypnogram typically comes with a PSG report, containing information about total sleep time,
sleep eﬃciency, sleep latencies, distribution of sleep stages and sleep events, i.e. sleep apneas and
arousals. Total sleep time is the sum of time spent in the stages N1, N2, N3 and REM, and time
in bed is the time between lights oﬀand lights on, i.e. including time laying in bed being awake.
Sleep eﬃciency (SE) is calculated as total sleep time divided by time in bed and normal values
for SE is in the range 85-95 %, where an SE of 85-90 % is classiﬁed as "normal", SE > 90 % is
"very good" and SE <85 % is "poor". Sleep latencies include the sleep latency from lights oﬀuntil
ﬁrst non-wake epoch is scored (sleep onset) which is typically longer than eight minutes, and the
REM latency is the period from sleep onset until ﬁrst REM epoch is scored and is typically 90-100
minutes.
1.3.2
Sleep Scoring and Stages
Sleep scoring is done by looking at EEG, EOG and EMG, primarily. From the EMG signals it can
be seen how the patient is moving, and from the EOG the EMs can be investigated. Some EM types
are characteristic for speciﬁc sleep stages, i.e. rapid EMs (REMs) in REM sleep, and slow EMs
(SEMs) in N1 sleep. EEG shows brain activity and can be measured in multiple channels. Typically
six EEG electrodes are used in a PSG; two central, two occipital and two frontal electrodes. Four
deﬁnitions of frequency bands of the EEG are used to decide the stage of sleep [47]:
6
==PAGE:29==
• Slow wave sleep (SWS): 0.5-2.0 Hz and amplitude >75 µV peak to peak frontally
• Delta waves: 0-4 Hz
• Theta waves: 4-8 Hz
• Alpha waves: 8-12Hz
• Sigma waves: 12-15Hz
• Beta waves: >15 Hz
Each epoch is labelled with one sleep stage. When two sleep stages are present in one epoch, the
stage that takes most of the epoch time (more than 15 s) is scored. When characteristics of three
or more sleep stages appear in one epoch, the dominant stage is scored. A period of NREM sleep
followed by a period of REM sleep is called a sleep cycle. An average sleep cycle has a duration
of 90-100 minutes, and during a night a person will have 4-6 sleep cycles. The typical order of
a sleep cycle is W, N1, N2, N3 and then REM sleep, where only the ﬁrst cycle starts with W.
Characteristics of the sleep stages are elaborated below based on [8,45,46].
W: In this stage the person is awake in the range from full alertness to the beginning of drowsiness.
This stage take place as the ﬁrst stage of the night and potentially multiple times during the night.
The EOG in this stage contains rapid blinks in frequency range 0.5-2 Hz, and if the eyes are open it
may contain voluntary rapid EMs or EMs representing reading. When drowsiness begins, the EOG
is characterised by an absence of blinks and the presence of slow EMs. Rapid EMs may appear in
W, but W is only scored if they appear together with normal or high muscle tone in the EMG. In
the EEG alpha activity is seen for most people (a minority of people generate limited or no alpha
activity) when eyes are closed. Typically 5-10 % of the period between light oﬀand light on is
marked as W.
N1 sleep: This stage typically appears after W in the ﬁrst sleep cycle and is the lightest stage of
sleep. In this stage, slow EMs are seen in the EOG. In the EEG, low amplitude mixed frequencies
(LAMF) or theta activity is seen together with vertex sharp waves which are sharp waves of
minimum 2 Hz arising from the central part of the brain. In N1 sleep hypnic jerks can be seen,
which are small sudden muscle contractions that might feel like falling. Typically 5-10 % of the
night is spent in N1 sleep.
N2 sleep: N2 sleep is deeper than N1 sleep but still lighter than N3 sleep. In N2 sleep K-
complexes and sleep spindles occur. K-complexes are sharp waves of maximum 2 Hz, arising from
the frontal part of the brain, thought to be a sleep protecting mechanism, preventing waking from
non-dangerous stimuli. Sleep spindles are sinusoidal waves of 11-16 Hz, lasting for 0.5-3 s, thought
to be related to memory consolidation. N2 sleep is scored when a K-complex or sleep spindle is
found in the ﬁrst half of current epoch or the last half of the previous epoch. N2 scoring is continued
if no arousals (short awakenings) occur, if LAMF is present and if no criteria from the other sleep
stages are fulﬁlled. In the EOG signal in N2 sleep, no or very few EMs are present according to
the AASM criteria. N2 sleep typically accounts for 50-55 % of the sleep.
7
==PAGE:30==
N3 sleep: This is the deepest stage of sleep and the most restorative stage. The EEG in N3
sleep is all measured in the delta range but is characterised by SWS. The slow waves are seen as
a slow synchronized pattern in the brain activity. N3 is scored if a minimum 20% of an epoch
contains SWS. Typically 10-20 % of the period between light oﬀand light on is marked as W.
REM sleep: REM is a light sleep stage characterised by rapid EMs. It is the end stage of a
sleep cycle and it is the stage where most dreaming takes place. The duration of the REM sleep
periods lengthen during the night and the last REM sleep period is typically the longest. REM
sleep is scored if LAMF activity is present without the presence of K-complexes or sleep spindles,
if the muscle tone is low (called muscle atonia) and if rapid EMs are present. REM scoring is
continued if LAMF continues, no criteria for other stages are present and no arousals occur. The
EEG is characterised by desynchronisation and sawtooth waves, which are a serrated shaped type
of theta wave in the frequency range 2-6 Hz that typically appear in the phasic part of REM sleep
(i.e. the part where no rapid EMs appear). The EEG resembles W but a clear diﬀerence is seen
in the EMG, where the muscle activity is limited to small twitches. This is due to muscle atonia,
i.e. paralysis of the muscles to avoid dream enactment. REM sleep typically accounts for 20 % of
sleep.
1.3.3
Nocturnal eye Movements
During the daytime, EMs are related to the sense of sight via both voluntary and involuntary EMs,
as smooth pursuit and saccadic EMs [48]. When investigating nocturnal EMs, the characteristics
are diﬀerent than during W and the sleep stages are characterised by diﬀerent types of nocturnal
EMs or absence of EMs.
The description of REMs and SEMs is based on the deﬁnition from
the AASM. REMs are conjugate (i.e. both eyes move with the same speed and direction at the
same time), irregular, sharp peaks with an initial deﬂection usually lasting less than 500 ms [8].
The deﬂection is the period from the start of the EM to the peak. REMs are mainly found in
REM sleep. SEMs are conjugate, regular, slow, sinusoidal peaks having a longer deﬂection times,
typically lasting more than 500 ms [8]. SEMs are mainly horizontal and diﬀer in amplitude. They
typically appear in the sleep onset period, i.e. the transition period from W to sleep or in N1 sleep.
1.3.4
Electrooculography
Eye movements are measured by the modality electrooculography (EOG) as part of a PSG. EOG
is measured by two electrodes placed beside the outer canthus of each eye, one electrode a bit
below the outer canthus of one of the eyes and the other one a bit above the outer canthus of the
other eye. The signal is generated due to a measurable diﬀerence in potential between the posterior
and anterior part of the eyeball, i.e. retina and cornea respectively, which makes the eyeball act
like a dipole. When the eyeballs are moving, positive and negative potentials will be recorded by
the electrodes. When the eyes move simultaneously in the same direction the signal will appear
negative in the electrode from which the eyes move away and positive in the electrode located in the
direction that the eyes move towards. This makes left and right EOG signals appear anti-correlated
when EMs are present. Examples of EOG signal in ﬁve diﬀerent epochs representing the ﬁve sleep

stages; W, N1-N3 and REM sleep are seen in Figure 1.1. Typical REMs are seen in the REM sleep
panel and typical SEMs are seen in the N1 sleep panel of the ﬁgure.
When EEG artefacts are present in the EOG signal, the left and right EOG signal will typically
appear correlated with the EEG signal. Artefacts could be EEG delta waves, EEG micro-sleep
events (such as sleep spindles or K-complexes). Other typical artefacts are baseline drift, EMG
activity or inﬂuences from ECG on the EOG signal.

1.3.5
Types of Eye Movements
Eye movements are controlled in the brain stem, where the horizontal gaze centre is found in the
pons and the vertical gaze centre in the midbrain [49]. Gaze is the sum of EM and movement of
the head. EMs are controlled by the extraocular muscles; lateral and medial rectus (responsible
for horizontal movement), superior and inferior rectus (responsible for vertical movement, pulling
the eyeballs a little inwards), and superior and inferior obliques (responsible for more complex
movements). The muscles are innervated by three nerves; the third, fourth and sixth cranial nerve
and are the most precisely controlled muscles in the body because of the small number of muscles
ﬁbres per neuron [50].
When the eyes are in neutral position (at rest), there is a constant excitation in the lateral and
medial rectus, which is diﬀerent from all other skeletal muscles [49]. This constant excitation is
9
==PAGE:32==
Figure 1.2: Excitation in left and right medial and lateral rectus when eyes are in neutral position and

caused by a continuous inhibition of superior colliculus (SC), which is found at the top of the brain
stem and responsible for controlling EMs [51]. It is suggested that when the eyes are in neutral
position movement of the eyes is inhibited by inhibition of SC and when it is decided to move the
eyes the inhibition of SC stops brieﬂy, allowing the eyes to move. When the eyes look completely
in one direction, the lateral rectus is excited on the eye in the direction of gaze and the medial
rectus is relaxed, and vice versa on the other eye (which the gaze is away from). When the eyes’
position is between other position and neutral, there is no relaxation in the muscles, but increased
excitation in lateral/medial muscles in the direction of gaze, as seen in Figure 1.2.
It is assumed that nocturnal EMs are similar to smooth pursuit i.e. following something with
the gaze and saccades. A saccade is a rapid, ballistic (i.e. the movement cannot be stopped once
started) change of vision from one ﬁxation point to another [50]. The EM has to be extremely fast
since the eye is eﬀectively blind while moving. Moving the eyes can be divided into three levels. The
decision-making, i.e. planing of the saccade is the longest part of the EM, lasting approximately
200-250 ms before the actual movement is executed.
The initiation starts in the horizontal gaze centre, speciﬁcally in the paramedian pontine reticular
formation in the omnipause neurons (OPN) [2]. OPNs are gamma aminobutyric acid (GABA)
containing neurons, constantly and rapidly ﬁring in rest. When a stimulus causes a saccade, OPNs
stop ﬁring leading to excitation in the excitatory burst neurons (EBN) which are inhibited by OPNs
in rest. EBNs send a signal to motor neurons (MN) in abducens nucleus (Abd N), responsible for
contraction of the lateral rectus. The same signal is sent to internuclear interneurons (II), and from
II to oculomotor nucleus (OMN) crossing the mid-line of the brain via the called medial longitudinal
fasciculus (MLF), causing a contraction of medial rectus in the opposite eye.
Simultaneously
inhibitory burst neurons (IBN) inhibit MNs and IIs in the other side of the brain. I.e. a saccade to
the right is processed in the right side of the brain, right lateral rectus and left medial rectus are
contracted and neurons in the left side of the brain are inhibited.

1.3.6
Age Related Changes in Sleep
Age is a factor that has a considerable aﬀect on sleep and it is reported that over 50 % of older adults
experience a deterioration in sleep quality including the ability to initiate or maintain sleep [52].
Reduced sleep quality can be reﬂected in diﬀerent cognitive and restorative functions. Older adults
wake up more often after sleep onset and the time awake increases [53]. Changes in the circadian
rhythm are observed for older adults but the reason for this is unclear [52]. Furthermore, it is seen
that older adults spend more time in sleep stage N1 and N3, less time in REM sleep, have shorter
REM latency and more N2 sleep [53]. This may be due to N3 sleep being scored as N2 because
the amplitude criterion of SWS is not met, and EEG amplitudes decay with age. To the best of
knowledge, no speciﬁc age-related changes of nocturnal EMs have been investigated or reported.
1.3.7
Parkinson’s Disease
PD is the second most common age-related neurodegenerative disease today, following Alzheimer’s
disease, and it is estimated that seven to ten million people worldwide have the disease [54]. In
Denmark, the prevalence is approximately 1% in the age group 50-70 years and the average age
at diagnosis is 60-65 [55]. The disease was medically described by James Parkinson in the year
1817, but PD like symptoms had previously been described in Indian, Chinese and Greek texts
from around 1000 BC. The development of PD involves diﬀerent parts of the brain, including the
11
==PAGE:34==
brain stem and midbrain [25]. PD is a progressive, neurodegenerative movement disorder, aﬀecting
the dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra (SN). The SN is darker than other regions of the
brain and is located in the basal ganglia in the midbrain. The basal ganglia controls movements
and connects to the motor cortex. It also deals with behaviour, cognition, reward and emotions.
SN can be divided into two parts; SN pars reticulata and SN pars compacta. It is SN pars compacta
which is aﬀected in PD due to degeneration and loss of striatal dopamine [56]. The Striatum is
a part of the basal ganglia as well. It is a group of neurons dealing with the motor and reward
system which receives messages from SN pars compacta by the neurotransmitter dopamine via the
nigrostriatal pathway. If the neurons in the pathway die, as happens for PD patients, a variety
of motor symptoms are experienced, including the four that are diagnostic for PD; bradykinesia,
tremor, rigidity and postural instability.
Biomarkers for PD
A biomarker is a biological marker indicating a biological change or state which can be measured
or observed [57]. Often biomarkers are used clinically as a diagnostic tool either alone or combined.
The main biomarker for PD is motor symptoms, such as hypokinesia (less movement), bradykinesia
(slow movement), rigidity, tremors, short shuﬄing steps, slightly ﬂexed elbows, hips and knees,
balance problems and a loss of facial expressions [56,58,59]. The diagnosis of PD today is primarily
based on a clinical examination, which means that there must be motor symptoms present before the
disease can be diagnosed [10]. This is required despite that other symptoms may occur years before
motor symptoms do [15]. Other symptoms/biomarkers include memory loss, olfactory dysfunction,
constipation, bladder disturbances, neuropsychiatric symptoms (e.g. depression, anxiety, apathy,
confusion) and sleep disturbances.
The sleep disturbances include excessive daytime sleepiness
(which aﬀects up to 50 % and can be a preclinical biomarker), restless legs, periodic limb movements
and/or REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) [60].
RBD is a sleep parasomnia manifested by
dream enactment and loss of muscle atonia during REM sleep. Examples of talking, shouting and
abnormal movements are commonly reported by bed partners of RBD patients [60]. When the RBD
is not associated with another underlying neurological condition such as narcolepsy or brain stem
lesion, it is deﬁned as idiopathic (or in some cases isolated) RBD (iRBD) [61]. RBD is especially
interesting in relation to PD since approximately one third of all PD patients experience RBD [22].
Many studies have investigated the relationship between iRBD patients and PD, and have found a
particularly high risk of a patient suﬀering from iRBD later developing PD [15–20].
As the symptoms above precede the motor symptoms required to diagnose PD, the investigation
of them as biomarkers may help to diagnose PD earlier than is possible today. Early diagnosis is
of high interest since it gives the patient a chance to start a lifestyle change which may slow down
the progression of the disease [10]. Early diagnosis may also make it possible for a medication to
be developed in the future that can slow down or even stop the progression of PD if taken before
motor symptoms occur indicating that too much of the brain is degenerated. [10].

The method section for this thesis consists of three parts.
1. Investigation of the scoring data used to train the EM detectors including a comparison of

2. Developing two detectors, one for REMs and one for SEMs and evaluation of the detectors

When p-values are stated, they are calculated by Wilcoxon Ranksum (WR) test using ranksum
in Matlab unless anything else is mentioned. The WR test, tests the null hypothesis that mean
and median from two populations are equal against the alternative that they are diﬀerent. The
WR test accounts for non-parametric data and is applied when it is unknown if the data comes
from a normal distribution.

2.1.1
Data
The data used in this project is provided by the Danish Center for Sleep Medicine (DCSM),
Department of Clinical Neurophysiology, Rigshospitalet Glostrup. For current study, EOG recorded
as part of a standard PSG, from 12 healthy subjects (age 50±11.15, six females) is used. The data
is sampled with a sampling frequency of 256 Hz and raw data is used when training and testing
the detector.
2.1.2
Manual Scoring in EEG Viewer
EMs in the data are scored, i.e. marked, by two independent experts; expert 1 (E1) and expert 2
(E2) between lights oﬀand lights on for all subjects (except one subject for one expert). EMs are
scored in the Matlab-based software program “EEG viewer”, mimicking the standard sleep scoring
program used in a clinical setting, developed at DCSM. In EEG viewer the data is pre-ﬁltered with
a notch ﬁlter at 50 Hz and a bandpass ﬁlter with cut-oﬀfrequencies at 0.3-35 Hz for visualisation.
An instruction on how to do the scoring in EEG viewer is found in Appendix A.1. Two labels;
"startEMscoring" and "stopEMscoring" are pre-marked and all EMs in the epochs between those
labels must be scored. According to the instruction, an EM starts when left and right EOG signals
are still parallel, just before the signals get anti-correlated, i.e. the deﬂection and an EM ends when
the signals get parallel again. Each EM is labelled with one of the labels seen in Table 2.1. The
experts are allowed to set more markings on top of each other and if it is impossible to separate
starts and endings between more EMs the experts are allowed to mark a "train" of EMs as one
long EM. Furthermore, movement not related to EMs can be marked. This may be movement of
the head which is seen as strong noise in the signal and is marked to exclude it from the data
when training the detector. The author of this thesis has scored two nights of sleep as well. These
scorings were supervised by E1 and therefore the author’s second scoring is used where E1 is missing
one scoring (subject 4). The author will henceforth be referred to as non-expert (NE) in relation
to the manual scoring.
2.1.3
Processing of Manual Scorings
A manually scored EM is deﬁned by; start sample, duration and a number deﬁning the label of the
EM. The manual scorings are transformed into binary vectors where ones indicate the presence of
EMs and zeros indicate absence. Seven binary vectors, one for each type of EM, are created; REMs,
SEMs, REMSEMs, NREMREMs, MEMs, blinks and WEMs. The binary vectors are shortened to
focus only on the data between the "startEMscoring" and "stopEMscoring" labels since EMs are
only scored within this part of the data. The manual scorings from the two experts are shown in
one plot along with the hypnogram for each subject, to get an overview of the scorings. A plot for
subject 7 is seen in Figure 2.2. The 11 other plots are found in Appendix A.2.

The two experts score the diﬀerent types of EMs quite diﬀerently and therefore it is interesting
to evaluate how much the two experts agree. The F1-score is a statistic measure for inter-rater
variability and is calculated by as F1 = 2· Precision·Recall
Precision+Recall, where Precision =
TP
TP+FP and Recall =
TP
TP+FN .
20
==PAGE:43==
The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, and deﬁned in the range from zero
to one, where zero corresponds to no agreement and one corresponds to perfect agreement. TP
denotes true positives (hit), FP denotes false positives (false alarm) and FN denotes false negatives
(miss). In addition, TN denoting true negatives (correct rejection) is considered in some cases,
but not when calculating F1-score. Inspired by Warby et al. [32] two diﬀerent methods are used
to calculate the agreement between the experts; the by-sample method and the by-event method.
The two methods are elaborated below.
By-sample: In the by-sample method each sample is indexed as TP, FP or FN by comparing
corresponding samples in two manual scorings (from E1 and E2). The majority of samples in the
data will be TN as most sleep has no presence of EMs and therefore a comparison might be biased
by the high number of TNs. TN is not used to calculate F1-score which is why the F1-score is
a good measure when comparing EMs. The F1-score does not depend on which way you do the
comparison for this method, i.e. if you use E1 as the true value and E2 as the predicted value
or opposite. In Figure 2.3 the by-sample method is elaborated. It is seen that indexation of the
samples depends on which expert is used as "true", but when the F1-score is calculated, the result is
the same regardless of which expert is considered giving the "truth" and which expert is considered
to "predict".
By-event: In the by-event method the separate EMs are compared as events and the exact
start and end of each EM is not taken into consideration. Each true event counts as a TP, and
duration of the events are unimportant. Using this method, the F1-score depends on which way
you do the comparison and therefore the test is performed twice, ﬁrst with E1 being the scorer
with the "truth" and E2 with the prediction and then the opposite way. If any overlap between a
"predicted" EM and a "true" EM is found, the "predicted" EM counts as a TP. If a predicted EM
is not overlapping with a true EM it counts as an FP and when no EM is predicted, but a true
EM is found it counts as an FN. In current project with two very diﬀerent ways of scoring EMs, an
overlap of only one sample was decided to be required. With a predeﬁned overlap, the duration of
the EMs will inﬂuence the F1-score. In Figure 2.3 the by-event methods is seen for varying expert
used as "true" value.
The F1-score can only be calculated for the types of EMs that both experts have scored, i.e.
REMs, SEMs, MEMs and blinks. Regarding MEMs and blinks, both experts have used that label
in some subjects but they do not agree on the location and therefore no F1-scores are found for
these events. Furthermore, the focus of this project is the REMs and SEMs, and the F1-scores are
therefore only calculated for REMs and SEMs. The F1-scores for each subject are seen in Tables
2.4 and 2.5.

2.2.1
Wavelet Transform
The original detector from [3], described in the next section, is based on wavelet transform (WT).
A WT is an approximation of a signal using short waves, so-called mother wavelets, with compact
support (waves that are oscillating ﬁnitely) and the sum of the area under the wave curve must
equal to zero [6]. A wavelet can be scaled and translated over a signal, giving one vector of wavelet
coeﬃcients per scale (or decomposition level). A low scale results in the resolution being low in
frequency and high in time and vice versa for a high scale. The scaling properties are shown in
Figure 2.6, where it is seen that the time resolution increases and frequency resolution decreases with
higher frequencies. Wavelet transform is a relatively new method, giving more precise information
about both time and frequency than Fourier-transform [6].
A list of decomposition levels and
corresponding frequencies, when a sampling frequency of 256 Hz is used, is present in Appendix
A.3.
Figure 2.6: Resolution in time and frequency for varying scale [4]
In discrete WT a ﬁlter bank is implemented with a high- and a low-pass ﬁlter used several times.
To avoid changing the ﬁlters, the signal is down-sampled by two.
This is due to the Nyquist
sampling theorem saying that the maximum frequency represented in a signal is determined by
half the sampling frequency. By down-sampling the signal the maximum frequency in the signal
is lowered before the ﬁlters are applied again. In Figure 2.7 DWT is shown, where h[n] is a real
low-pass ﬁlter and g[n] is a real high-pass ﬁlter. The circle with an arrow down and the number "2"
indicates down-sampling of the signal by a factor two. The coeﬃcients include the low frequencies,
and one vector of coeﬃcients is given per decomposition level.

two. This ﬁgure is taken from [5].
The complex WT is inspired by Fourier transform as the Fourier transform is based on complex
oscillating sinusoids. Sine and cosine are 90° out of phase, i.e. they form a Hilbert pair. When
this idea is applied to WT a complex wavelet is deﬁned by two wavelets; a real and an imaginary
forming a Hilbert pair as in
Ψc(t) = Ψr(t) + jΨi(t),
(2.1)
where j = √−1, c denotes the complex wavelet and r and i denote the real and imaginary
wavelets, respectively. In the time domain, the complex wavelet is a 2/π shifted version of the real
wavelet, i.e. both wavelets are real.
The discrete version of the complex WT can be applied in two ways, either the two ﬁlters can be
rotated 90° in the z-plane to get two additional ﬁlters, i.e. two pairs of ﬁlters as shown in Figure
2.8 or the signal can be Hilbert transformed to form an additional signal and real WT is applied on
both signals [6]. The ﬁrst method is called dual-tree complex wavelet transform (DTCWT), and is
the one used in current project. It uses a low-pass and a high-pass ﬁlter and a Hilbert transform
of both ﬁlters. In Figure 2.8 h0(n) and g0(n) are two real low-pass ﬁlters and h1(n) and g1(n) are

version of h(n). The circle with an arrow down and the number "2" means down-sampling of the
signal by a factor two.
DTCWT can be applied in Matlab using the function dddtree. This function takes as input the

2.2.2
Description of the Detector
In current study the EM detector by Christensen et al. [3] is tuned to search for individual REMs
and SEMs.
The original detector aims to detect all types of EMs and not REMs and SEMs
separately. The optimal parameters for the original detector were chosen as the ones where the
maximal mean value for the fraction %REM+%N1+%W
N3
was found, since it is known that most EM
activity is found in REM, N1 and W and less/no EM activity is seen in N3 sleep. In the fraction,
"%" represents the percentage of samples detected as EMs in the respective sleep stage between
lights oﬀand on. A schematic overview of the detector procedures is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.9.
The detector takes two EOG signals as input; left EOG (EOGL) and right EOG (EOGR). The
signals are high-pass ﬁltered with a zero-phase forward and reverse digital IIR ﬁlter, using a fourth-
order Butterworth ﬁlter. The ﬁlter has a cut-oﬀfrequency (-3dB) at 0.1 Hz and is applied to remove
the lowest frequencies, e.g. baseline drift.
Subsequently, a cleaning procedure is applied to keep the components reﬂecting EMs. EOGL
and EOGR typically appear anti-correlated when EMs are present, and components reﬂecting EMs
would therefor reﬂect an angle (or phase) between the two signals of a certain degree. Also the com-
ponents would be in a certain frequency range reﬂecting EMs. The eliminated components reﬂect
noise coming from e.g. EEG, ECG or EMG measured in the EOG. The signal is decomposed using
dual-tree complex WT and reconstructed after eliminating the components not reﬂecting EMs.
The coeﬃcients related to the frequencies in which EMs appear were used for phase calculation
whereas coeﬃcients not related to EMs are set to zero. This applies to coeﬃcients levels 1 - 5 and
11 - 14, i.e. frequencies outside the range 0.125 - 4 Hz are eliminated. The retained coeﬃcients for
levels Jinc = (6 −10) are used to calculate the phases of EOGL and EOGR.
The phase is calculated using the Matlab-function angle that takes a vector of complex elements
as input and outputs the phase angles in radians. The phase is calculated for EOGL and EOGR
and the vectors containing of the signals are subtracted. Phase diﬀerences above the threshold

Parameter Study
In order to tune the original detector, so it can detect individual REMs and SEMs, the optimal
parameters must be found for REMs and SEMs. The detector will be trained based on a grid

three subjects are left out for testing. The choice of parameters to be used in the training of the
two detectors relies on an investigation of the manual detected EMs as well as parameters tested
by Christensen et al. [3]. The investigated parameters are the same as in [3]; frequency content
(Jinc), phase content (Tangle), duration between EMs (durhole) and duration of EMs (durhole).

2.2.4
Training of Detectors
The detectors are trained in two rounds; a preliminary round and a ﬁnal round. The training is
based on a grid search, to tune the detectors by ﬁnding the highest possible mean by-event F1-score
when comparing to GS. The term training might be misleading since the detector is not using the
data to ﬁnd new parameters. It follows the same procedure every time it is trained and only the
parameters are changed. Therefore no model is saved in the process, and no cross-validation is
done. In the ﬁrst round, the detector is trained on the set of parameters found in Section 2.2.3 to
get an overview of which parameters to use in the ﬁnal training round. An initial study is made to
focus on relevant parameters before the ﬁnal training round. In the by-event method, a detected
EM must have an overlap of minimum 20 % with an EM in the gold standard to count as a TP.
Figure 2.12 shows how TPs, FPs and FNs are counted.

Detection of REMs
The detector is trained on nine subjects using 960 combinations of parameters.
The best by-

0.35 and in 25 cases (2.6 %) the F1-score exceeds 0.3.
The parameters are evaluated in the ﬁve cases where the by-event F1-scores are above 0.35 to

therefore it is decided that there is no reason to investigate the other level-ranges again in the ﬁnal
training round. In addition it can be seen that Tangle has less inﬂuence, but in all cases where
F1 ≥0.35 P th = 95, durhole = 0.25 and durEM = 1.

Duration, density and coverage of REMs and SEMs are calculated for both training and testing

of all EMs scored by the experts. Density in each stage is calculated as the number of EMs in a
given stage divided by minutes spent in the stage and is thus given in EMs per minute. Coverage
is calculated as the percentage of time in each stage that is covered by EMs.

2.3.1
Applying the Detector to New Data
The detectors will now be applied on data which has not been scored by any experts (except
for sleep stage scoring). The data used for application is provided by Danish Center for Sleep
Medicine (DCSM), Department of Clinical Neurophysiology, Rigshospitalet, Glostrup, from sub-
jects recruited in the period 2009-2015 [63]. The included subjects are 24 healthy controls, 28
subjects with periodic limb movement disorder (PLMD), 24 iRBD patients, 23 PD patients with-
out RBD (PD-RBD) and 29 PD patients with RBD (PD+RBD). The PLMD subjects are included
as an extra control group to investigate limb movement activity in PLMD in relation to the motor
activity in iRBD. The controls and PLMD patients had at the time of recording not previously
suﬀered from sleep disorders and medications aﬀecting sleep were discontinued at least two weeks
before the PSG recordings. For PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients dopaminergic medication was
not discontinued and medication was assessed to be stable for at least a month before the record-
ings [63]. Recording from controls and some of the PLMD patients were performed outside of the
hospital while the rest of the recordings were performed at the hospital. For some patients at the
hospital video recording was carried out to diagnose the disorder. For iRBD patients the diagnosis
is based on REM sleep without atonia seen as dream enactment on a video, and clinical history [63].
Using the validated detectors; RD and SD it will be investigated whether there is any diﬀerences
in duration, density or coverage of EMs across sleep stages between the groups. Demographics of
the patient groups are seen in Table 2.15.
No multiple correction is applied since a maximum of 20 tests are completed within each ﬁeld of
testing, but with the inclusion of Bonferroni correction in cases with 20 tests a p-value of 0.0025
applies and with 10 tests a p-value of 0.005 applies. Values that diﬀer signiﬁcantly when Bonferroni
correction is applied are marked in bold, but the test results will be described and discussed without
the inclusion of multiple correction.
2.3.2
EM Measures
The measures; duration, density and coverage are calculated as described in Section 2.2.8 for all
groups across all sleep stages. All groups are compared at each stage within each of the three
measures using the Kruskal-Wallis (KW) test with a signiﬁcance level at p < 0.05. If a KW test
is signiﬁcant all groups are compared pairwise using the Wilcoxon Ranksum (WR) test. The KW
test is non-parametric, testing if samples come from the same distribution and is an extension of
the WR test since it compares more than two groups.
In many cases EMs are detected as both REMs and SEMs, and by visual inspection those EMs
look like REMs, with a component of SEM (a REM appearing on top of a SEM). To investigate
clean SEMs, all SEMs appearing on top of REMs are removed, giving an EM vector containing
isolated SEMs (iSEMs). The measures are calculated and compared for REMs, SEMs and iSEMs
which are SEMs found with SD with no overlapping REMs.

Discussion
In this chapter the EM scoring method and it’s associated complications and challenges will be
discussed along with the inter-rater variability between the experts and the approaches used to
calculate the F1-score. Also, a discussion of GS, training and testing of the detectors and the results
obtained from testing and training will be included in current chapter. Finally, the measures from
the comparison of the ﬁve groups; controls, PLMD, iRBD, PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients will
be discussed.
4.1
Part 1
4.1.1
Manual EM Scoring by the Experts
The two experts have a very diﬀerent approach to the manual scoring of EMs.
E2 marks the
deﬂection of the rapid EMs (according to E2’s own statement, but supported by visual inspection
of the manual scorings), whereas E1 marks larger areas in the data, i.e. more samples per EM
and sometimes more EMs as one event (in an EM train). This trend can be seen clearly when
comparing the duration plots in Figures 2.4 and 2.5. Before the removal of short EMs the mean
duration of E1’s REMs and SEMs are 5.2 s and 7.4 s, respectively, whereas the mean duration of
E2’s REMs and SEMs are 0.25 s and 1.71 s, respectively. For E2 there is a clear diﬀerence in the
duration between REMs and SEMs (p < 0.001), which is not the case for E1 (p = 0.970). When
comparing scored REMs between the experts they do not agree signiﬁcantly on the duration (p <
0.001), and the same goes for SEMs (p < 0.001). The scoring instruction found in Appendix A.1
states that an EM starts when the signals are still parallel, just before the deﬂection where the
signals begin to appear anti-correlated, and the EMs end when the signals get back to parallel.
This means that the period where the signal is zero, i.e. neutral position of the eyes until it gets
back to zero, should all be marked as an EM. By visual inspection of the manually scored REMs
it is apparent that E2 has only marked the movement away from zero, i.e. the deﬂection and not
the movement back to neutral.
In Table 2.3 it is visible that E2 has scored a higher number of EMs than E1, whereas E1 has
used the variety of labels more widely. The instruction for scoring (in Appendix A.1) states that
the experts are allowed to score a train of EMs if they are not able to separate the EMs. This
opportunity has been used more by E1 than E2 (according to E1’s own statement, but supported
by visual inspection of the manual scorings). The fact that E1 has used the selection of labels to a
63
==PAGE:86==
greater extent than E2 challenges the comparison of the scorings from the two experts. Making E1
look through the EMs labelled as REMSEMs again was considered which would require a review
of 628 EMs distributed over eight subjects and there was unfortunately insuﬃcient time for E1 to
take on this big workload. E2 has used four out of the possible selection of labels; REMs, SEMs,
EMs and blinks. E2 has scored 4.9 times as many REMs and 7.7 times as many SEMs as E1 and
in total E2 has scored 2.1 times as many EMs as E1 (see Table 2.3). E1 has generously used the
label MEM and scored 1100 more MEMs than REMs and SEMs together.
A great variation is also seen between the diﬀerent subjects. E1 ranges from 298 to 2254 scored
EMs per subject and E2 from 431 to 4219 scored EMs per subject. This is certainly in part due
to diversity among subjects, but a variation in the expert performance may also have an impact
since there is no trend for which subjects have the most EMs when comparing scorings from the
experts. All in all, these investigations reveal that there exist a huge uncertainty in the labelled
data - both due to the variation between subjects, but also due to how raters interpret the data.
Inter-Rater Variability Between Experts
The F1-scores representing the agreement between E1 and E2 after adjustments of the scoring data
(from Section 2.1.7) are seen in Table 2.9 and 2.10. The two diﬀerent methods for comparing the
manual scorings from E1 and E2 are discussed below.
By-sample: When using the by-sample method, the mean F1-score is 0.223 ± 0.159 for REMs
and 0.228 ± 0.138 for SEMs. The low F1-scores indicate that the experts score EMs diﬀerently and
do not agree on the exact positioning of EMs. Using the by-sample method, the experts have to
agree completely on location, starting and ending of each EM to get a high F1-score and therefore
it is expected that the by-sample F1-scores are lower than the F1-scores found using the by-event
method. It can be seen that the F1-scores vary over the subjects and that the highest agreement
between the experts is obtained for subject 4, regarding both REMs and SEMs. Although this is
just one subject, it may indicate that NE and E2 agree to a higher degree compared to E1 and E2.
By-event: Using the by-event method there is no requirement of similar starting and ending
points for the scored EMs to count as a TP. EMs in the two scorings must overlap with just one
sample for a TP to be counted (when comparing the experts). The decision not to use a strict
overlap of e.g. 20 % was made because it was known in advance that the experts scored diﬀerently
and the agreement was supposed to be examined independently of the duration of EMs. If a strict
overlap was used, the duration of the EMs would matter and some EMs with a small overlap would
not be counted as TPs because they would have a too long duration to obtain a 20% overlap. An
example is seen in Figure 2.12, where the ﬁrst detected EM has too long a duration to count as
a TP since the overlap of 20 % is not obtained. Using the chosen method where only one sample
should overlap in order to count a TP, the F1-score depends on which expert’s scoring is used as
the truth and which is used as a prediction. It can be seen for the REMs that the F1-scores are
higher when using the by-event method compared to the by-sample method, regardless of who is
used as the truth (see Table 2.9). E1 has scored EMs of longer duration than E2 therefore one long
EM scored by E1 will count as one TP, whereas E2 has scored many short EMs in the same period
64
==PAGE:87==
so E2’s scorings will count as more TPs. Along with more TPs counted for E2 comes more FPs,
and this is one reason for a lower F1-score when E1 is used as the true value. Figure 2.3 elaborates
how E2 gets more TPs than E1.
Based on the F1-scores for REMs it can be seen that the agreement is high when both experts
are used as the "truth", although highest when E2 counts as the "truth" (see Table 2.9). Looking
at SEMs both mean F1-scores are lower than for REMs, meaning that the experts agree less in
SEMs detection (see Table 2.10). The lowest F1-scores are found for subjects 2 and 3, which are
the subjects where E1 has scored only ﬁve and two SEMs, respectively. By visual inspection, it
can be seen that E1 in several cases has marked REMSEMs where E2 has scored SEMs. This is
accounted for in GS where these counts for both REMs and SEMs, but REMSEMs are not included
as SEMs in the inter-rater variability study since it is unknown whether it accounts for a REM or
a SEM Again the best F1-score is obtained when E2 is used as "true". As when using by-sample,
the highest agreement is found between E2 and NE, i.e. in subject 4.
In a study by Warby et al. [32] an inter-rater variability study was made between experts scoring
SS and the mean by-event F1-score for agreement between the experts was 0.750 ± 0.06 (requiring
an overlap of 25 %), which is a higher agreement than obtained in current study. However SS
have been studied more than EMs, and the characteristics of SS are well deﬁned compared to the
characteristics of nocturnal EMs. Furthermore, more experts were recruited in the study by Warby
et al. [32], which also can have an impact on the inter-rater variability. Danker-Hopfe et al. [64]
did a study on inter-rater reliability between sleep stage scorers in diﬀerent sleep laboratories in
diﬀerent European countries, showing that the scorers had an agreement on κ = 0.619 in PD
patients. This emphasizes the fact that sleep scoring, both sleep staging and identiﬁcation of sleep
events, is highly subjective and can aﬀect the results.
4.1.2
Gold Standard
It was decided to include REMSEMs, MEMs and WEMs from E1’s scorings as REMs or SEMs
in GS where E2 has marked either REMs or SEMs. This decision is amongst others based on an
investigation of Table 2.3 where it can be seen that E1 in some cases barely has used the labels
REMs and SEMs compared to the great use of the other labels. As an example, E1 has only scored
ﬁve SEMs for subject 2 and two SEMs for subject 3, whereas E2 has scored 428 and 124 SEMs
for the same two subjects, respectively. In the ﬁgures showing hypnograms and scorings for each
subject in Appendix A.2 it can be seen that E1 has scored REMSEMs, MEMs and WEMs in same
locations as E2 has scored REMs and SEMs and it was therefore decided to make this inclusion in
GS.
In the training of the detectors, two diﬀerent approaches for the by-event method were considered
with diﬀerent requirements on the overlap (for comparison between events in GS and detected EMs).
In the ﬁrst method, the detected EMs must overlap a minimum of 20 % of GS, i.e. the detected
EM must be overlapping with at least 20 % of an event in the GS. This allows EMs with large
durations to be counted as TPs. Secondly, it was considered that GS has to overlap a minimum
of 20 % of the detected EM. Here EMs with short durations will count as TPs, which is consistent
65
==PAGE:88==
with the instruction on how the experts were supposed to score. The ﬁrst approach would allow
trains of EMs to be detected as one EM and therefore the second approach was chosen.
When the measures of the EMs in GS are investigated it can be seen in Table 3.1 and 3.2 that there
is a diﬀerence in duration between REMs and SEMs, which was expected based on the deﬁnition
in AASM saying that REMs must have a deﬂection of < 500 ms and SEMs must have a deﬂection
of > 500 ms. The duration of EMs is GS is similar to the duration of EMs scored by E2, indicating
that GS is dominated by E2’s scorings. The claim that E2 dominates GS is substantiated in Figure
3.5, where it is apparent that E2’s short EMs are represented in GS. In Table 3.1 showing the
density of EMs in GS it can be seen that most REMs are found in REM sleep. There are no known
normal values for the amount of EMs in each sleep stage, but according to the deﬁnitions of sleep
stages saying that REMs are a characteristic for REM sleep it is a realistic proportion. The lowest
mean density of REMs is found in N3 sleep and according to the AASM deﬁnition of sleep stages
no EMs should be found in N3 sleep, so this is realistic as well. Sometimes more sleep stages are
represented in one epoch and therefore it is realistic that EMs can be present in epochs scored
as N3 sleep, although limited. In Table 3.3 the density of SEMs in GS are shown and it can be
seen that the mean density of SEMs is highest in W followed by REM sleep and N1 sleep. A high
density of SEMs in W and N1 are seen since SEMs are indicative of drowsiness and the transition
from W to N1 sleep. Often REMs are seen on top of SEMs and therefore many SEMs are seen in
W and REM sleep as well. All in all, the distribution of REMs and SEMs in GS across sleep stages
goes in line with the overall expectations of where EMs should be present and absent.

4.2.1
Parameters for RD and SD
When looking at the parameters giving the best F1-scores (in Table 2.13), diﬀerences between RD
and SD are seen in all parameters. It is not surprising that REMs are detected in a higher frequency
range than SEMs. REMs are detected in frequencies 2-8 Hz and SEMs in the frequencies 0.125-2
Hz. Also, it was expected to get a higher duration limit, EMdur, for SEMs than for REMs, as
these events are normally longer compared to REMs [8]. The threshold Pth deﬁnes the start and
stop samples of an EM, i.e. a higher threshold is more strict regarding the duration and only the
peak of Adiff will be detected as an EM. A lower threshold "accepts" a bigger area of the peak and
samples around it, and thereby events with longer durations. For SEMs the threshold is lower than
for REMs, indicating that REMs have a shorter duration than SEMs, which is consistent with the
AASM deﬁnition of REMs and SEMs.
It can be seen that the performance of RD depends least on Tangle as only small improvements
are seen across the investigated values for this parameter. There is little diﬀerence in the F1-scores
using the best parameters deﬁned in Table 2.13 but varying Tangle, i.e. F1=0.504 for Tangle=0.6
and F1=0.503 for Tangle=0.7. It is suspected that the high number of REMs detected in N3 sleep
may be artefacts from delta waves in the EEG signal or baseline drifts appearing somewhat anti-
correlated in the EOG signals. Investigating the sum of F1-score in REM sleep and F1-score in N3
sleep for Tangle=[0.6, 0.7] to ﬁgure out which angle to use was considered, but since there are almost
no REMs found in N3 sleep in GS (see Table 3.1) the F1-scores become NaN for most subjects,
which makes it a poor measure. Therefore it was decided to keep the angle found in the training.

4.2.3
Kappa, Accuracy, Sensitivity and Speciﬁcity of RD and SD
Sensitivity is a measure of how often the detector correctly identiﬁes a TP, i.e. the probability of
detecting a positive event where there truly is a positive event. A detector with high sensitivity
will catch most EMs and avoid a high number of FNs, which typically comes with a high number
of FPs. The advantage of a detector with high sensitivity is that almost all EMs are detected, but
this is at the expense of a lot of FPs. Mathematically the sensitivity is high if the number of FNs
is low, and the sensitivity does not say anything about the number of FPs. Speciﬁcity is a measure
of how often the detector correctly detects "no EMs" in cases were there truly are no EMs to be
detected. A high speciﬁcity means that few FPs are generated, but it typically comes with a high
number of FNs, i.e. a high number of undetected EMs.
When developing a detector and deciding whether to prioritize sensitivity or speciﬁcity, the con-
text of the detector use is of course important.
As an example, a high sensitivity is typically
desired when screening for a disease, since missing any correct positive cases can be fatal, while
false negative detection may be inconvenient and expensive but otherwise not harmful. In the case
of the EM detectors, a high sensitivity and speciﬁcity are equally prioritized.
The performance in terms of sensitivity and speciﬁcity of RD is shown in Table 3.8. RD has a
high speciﬁcity which is greatly inﬂuenced by the majority of samples being TNs. The sensitivity
of the RD is lower than the speciﬁcity, meaning that the detector does not detect all EMs, but
the detected EMs are most often correct. The accuracy of the RD is 0.985 ± 0.002 and kappa
is 0.281 ± 0.238, which is considered a fair performance according to Table 2.14 [1]. There is no
diﬀerence in the accuracy, sensitivity and speciﬁcity between training and testing subjects, but
kappa is decreased in the test.
The performance in terms of sensitivity and speciﬁcity of SD is shown in Table 3.12. SD has
a high speciﬁcity, also due to many TNs and a lower sensitivity. The sensitivity is lower for the
68
==PAGE:91==
test subject than for the training subjects, and it can be seen that subject 11 and 12 have very
low sensitivity compared to the training mean. This indicates that the number of FNs is high,
i.e. many SEMs are not detected by the SD. There is no notable diﬀerence in kappa, accuracy
and speciﬁcity between training and testing subjects. The accuracy is 0.921 ± 0.017 and kappa is
0.199 ± 0.102 which corresponds to a "slight" performance when using the deﬁnitions from Table
2.14 [1].
4.2.4
Steps in Detection of REMs and SEMs
In Figure 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7 and 3.8 the detection steps are seen in examples from diﬀerent sleep
stages. In the example of W, no REMs are found by either the detector or any of the experts,
but some REMs candidates are detected before elimination of REMs with duration less than the
threshold EMdur. Both the detector and the experts ﬁnd SEMs, though the detected SEMs have a
longer duration than the manually scored SEMs. It is very common to have REMs in W when the
eyes are open, but the presence of SEMs and absence of REMs in this example indicated that this
epoch may be in the transition from W to N1 sleep. Two examples from REM sleep are included;
with and without EMs in GS. In the ﬁrst example (in Figure 3.4), the detectors ﬁnd REMs and
SEMs at the same locations, but no EMs are found in GS. E1 has marked a REM at the place
where E2 has marked a SEM, resulting in no EMs in GS. This is a good example of one of the main
challenges in this study - the experts do not agree, resulting in a poor GS. In the second REM
sleep example (in Figure 3.5) it can be seen how an EM is included in GS when E1 has used one of
the labels; MEM, WEM or REMSEM (dashed red line). The fact that E2 scores REMs of shorter
duration is supported by this ﬁgure as well. Since a minimum of 20 % of an EM must overlap with
an event in GS to count as a TP none of the detected SEMs in this example are counted as a TP,
which may be misleading. This indicates that another by-event method could have been used but
since the aim was to detect individual EMs, it was decided to avoid detection of too large EMs
when counting TPs. In Figure 3.6 an example of EM detection in N1 sleep is shown and it can
be seen that only SEMs are found by the detector and scored by the experts, which is consistent
with the deﬁnition of N1 sleep where SEMs indicate drowsiness. In N2 sleep (in Figure 3.7) no
EMs are detected, but E1 has scored either MEM, WEM or REMSEM which are not represented
in GS since E2 has not scored any type of EMs. In N3 sleep (in Figure 3.8) no EMs are scored by
the experts, but the detector ﬁnds two REMs. Those REMs are detected when slow-wave artefacts
from the EEG appear slightly anti-correlated in the two EOG channels. It is unknown to which
degree the detector is challenged by this artefact, but is is assessed that most REMs in N3 are
detected due to this challenge.
4.2.5
Challenges With Automatic Detectors in Sleep Research
In the ﬁeld of sleep research, the performance of automatic detectors are somehow low compared
to other areas of automatic detection, e.g. QRS complex detection which is much more precise. As
examples, the sleep spindle detector in [34] performs with kappa values indicating agreements from
slight to moderate and a maximum F1 = 0.450 for SS detection in controls. Magosso et al. [37] had
an accuracy of 0.804 ± 0.040, sensitivity of 0.672 ± 0.074 and a speciﬁcity of 0.839 ± 0.087 when
69
==PAGE:92==
detecting SEMs and Boulus et al. [43] had an accuracy of 0.830 when detecting SEMs. One general
problem with automatic detectors in the ﬁeld of sleep research is that they are often trained on
data from young subjects with an unknown performance in older adults [32]. Generally, scoring
of stages and sleep events in older adults get harder due to changes in brain-activity with age.
This appears as signiﬁcant changes in the amount of time in SWS, N2 and REM sleep [53]. When
detectors are developed using data from young people they will probably perform diﬀerently on
older adults.
When a detector is developed to ﬁt a GS, the detector performance is aimed to be correct when
compared to GS, but if GS is not ideal then it is hard to say if the detector is performing correctly
or just agreeing with the GS. It can be seen in Tables 3.5 and 3.9 that though the performances of
the detectors are imperfect when compared to the experts, the experts do not perform better than
the detectors when compared to each other. This indicates the big disagreement between experts,
making it diﬃcult to obtain a GS on which the detector can be trained. In the abstract by Pizza
et al. [65] the detector developed by Christensen et al. [3] is applied to ﬁnd all types of EMs in all
stages, REMs in REM sleep and SEMs in N1 sleep. The accuracy between the detector and two
experts and the accuracy in between the two experts are compared and there are no signiﬁcant
diﬀerence in the performance of the detector and the performance of the experts, as also found
in current project. This again indicates that though a detector performs only fair, it does not
agree less with the experts than the experts agree with each other. One important thing to note
is that the performance measures may be calculated diﬀerently in diﬀerent studies, e.g the amount
of overlap required when using by-event may vary.
4.2.6
Measures of Detected EMs
Duration of EM Across Sleep Stages
In Figure 3.9 and 3.10 the duration of detected REMs and SEMs in the ﬁve sleep stages is shown.
Generally, the duration of SEMs is longer than for REMs, which is consistent with the AASM
deﬁnition.
In AASM it is stated that a REM is deﬁned as an EM with initial deﬂection usually lasting less
than 500 ms. The REMs detected by RD last an average of 670 ms including the deﬂection and the
part of the EM where the eyeball moves back to neutral. When comparing the durations to GS,
the duration of REMs is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent in stages W, REM and N1 (all p < 0.001) and N2
(p = 0.01), but not for REMs in N3 (p = 0.600). For SEMs, the duration is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
in all stages (p < 0.001). The duration of all detected REMs are approximately twice as long as in
GS and the duration of all detected SEMs are more than three times as long as in GS. This may
also be due to the fact that E2’s very short scorings dominate the GS as seen in Figure 3.5.
Density of EM Across Sleep Stages
When the density of REMs and SEMs in GS is compared to the detected EMs it is found that
the density of REMs and SEMs in all stages is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent (all p < 0.001). In GS most
70
==PAGE:93==
REMs are found in REM sleep and this also applies for the detected REMs. In total, the detector
ﬁnds more REMs than GS. In GS very few REMs are scored in N3, which is consistent with the
deﬁnition of N3 sleep, stating that no EMs must be present in N3 [8]. RD though ﬁnds 0.573 REMs
per minute in N3, which might be delta waves in the EEG signal reﬂected in the EOG signals and
appearing slightly anti-correlated.
Regarding detected SEMs, the density is lower in all sleep stages when compared to what was
found for the SEMs in GS. The density of detected SEMs is high in W which might be the underlying
SEMs combined with REMs or the W epochs just before sleep onset where the subjects start having
isolated SEMs. A high density is also seen in N1 sleep for which a characteristic is SEMs. Few
SEMs are found in N3 sleep both in GS and by SD. The density is also high in REM sleep which
is expected to be due to the SEMs components in the REMs.
Coverage of EMs Across Sleep stages
The coverage of REMs and SEMs in the ﬁve sleep stages diﬀer signiﬁcantly when comparing
detected REMs and SEMs to GS (all p < 0.001). The coverage of detected REMs is highest in
REM sleep which is the same trend as seen for the REMs in GS. The coverage of REMs is higher
in N3 sleep than in N1 and N2 sleep which may be due to the delta wave artefacts. The coverage
of detected SEMs is generally higher than of detected REMs except in N3 sleep. The deﬁnition
of SEMs states that they have a longer duration than REMs which is why it is expected to see
higher coverage of SEMs. The coverage is highest in REM sleep which may be due to the slow
components of REMs. The coverage is also high in W and N1, which may be due to drowsiness
and the transition from being awake to falling asleep.

4.3.1
Application of the detector
It is hypothesised that no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in duration, density and coverage of REMs, SEMs
and iSEMs between controls and PLMD patients would be found because PLMD patients should
have no signs of related neurodegeneration. Assuming that the brain may be most damaged in
PD+RBD patients and least damaged in iRBD patients (except for controls and PLMD patients),
the measures of a progression biomarker would either increase or decrease in relation to the degree
of brain-damage, i.e. having the iRBD patients to show an intermediate level between controls and
PD patients. Furthermore, it is hypothesized that signiﬁcant diﬀerences would occur in SEMs and
iSEMs in NREM sleep between PD patients when compared to controls and PLMD patients, since
the brain stem controlling SEMs is aﬀected in PD. The main ﬁndings for detected REMs, SEMs
and iSEMs in the clinical cohort will be discussed in the following subsections.
The main ﬁndings for REMs:
In N3 sleep iRBD patients showed signiﬁcantly higher REM duration than all other groups, which
needs to be explored further. It may be noise due to body movement from the EMG channels
aﬀecting the EOG signals or movement artefacts on the EOG channels, though heavy movement
among iRBD patients is mainly seen in REM sleep.
During REM sleep both PD patients groups (but mostly PD+RBD patients) have signiﬁcantly
lower REM density than controls and PLMD patients. This indicates an aﬀection of the oculomotor
system in PD patients due to the neurodegenerative process [63].
In REM sleep PD+RBD patients have signiﬁcantly lower REM density and coverage than iRBD
patients. The brain is more damaged in patients with PD+RBD than in patients with iRBD which
may results in fewer REMs in PD+iRBD patients.
During N3 sleep, controls showed signiﬁcantly higher REM density and coverage than all other
groups. The same trend can be seen in part 2 for the test subjects in the validation of the detector.
One suggestion is the clear slow waves in SWS from the EEG represented in the EOG in N3 sleep
appearing slightly anti-correlated being detected as REMs. Slow waves are mainly measured from
the frontal part of the brain in EEG and are likely to aﬀect the EOG channels [23]. In Figure 3.8 an
example of REMs in N3 sleep can be seen and visual inspection of the detected REMs in N3 shows
that this is a general example. Furthermore, the experts have scored REMs in N3 as well, meaning
that the detector is trained to ﬁnd some REMs in N3. The reason why controls have higher density
may be because of less SWS in iRBD and PD patient. However this does not explain why controls
have a higher density of REMs in N3 than PLMD patients.
The main ﬁndings for SEMs and iSEMs:
In REM sleep both PD patient groups showed signiﬁcantly lower SEM duration than controls.
These are likely the slow component of the REMs and indicating that PD patients have shorter
72
==PAGE:95==
periods with REMs.
During W, iRBD, PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients had signiﬁcantly lower density of SEMs and
iSEMs than controls, and PD+RBD patients also had signiﬁcantly lower densities compared to
PLMD patients. This may be due to variations in sleep stage scoring, i.e. epochs with SEMs might
have been scored as W for controls and PLMD patients and as N1 for PD patients. However, no
diﬀerence in density in N1 is seen, which contradicts this statement.
In N3 sleep iRBD and PD-RBD patients showed lower density of SEMs and iSEMs than controls,
which needs further exploration. Maybe the detector does not work well in N3 sleep as described
above.
In N2 sleep PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients showed signiﬁcantly higher coverage of SEMs and
iSEMs than controls and PLMD patients, which is consistent with the ﬁnding in [63]. This ﬁnding
may be due to an aﬀection of the brain stem, including SC, leading to an aﬀection in the inhibition
of EMs resulting in slow involuntary drifting of the eyes [63].
Though not signiﬁcant in all stages, a general trend for SEMs was a decrease in density in W, REM
and N1 sleep and an increase of SEMs in N2 sleep following disease progression. For iSEMs, the
same trend can be seen, though not in REM sleep. It is worth considering if it is more interesting to
investigate iSEMs since many SEMs may just be slow components of REMs. The AASM deﬁnition
states that SEMs are found outside REM sleep and it can be seen that both density and coverage
is lower for iSEMs than SEMs in REM sleep.
Limitations of the Study
The study of EMs in the clinical cohort has some limitations. First, the sleep stage scoring is
done manually for all subjects which has potentially aﬀected the results since sleep stage scoring
in iRBD and PD patients may be diﬃcult due to changes in the events which the scoring is based
on, such as sleep spindles [66] and lack of muscle atonia. Also reduced sleep stability is seen for
PD patients [19], which leads to the suggestion of limiting the study to only look at stable sleep
when investigating the detected EMs [63]. In [63], a sleep stage was deﬁned stable when the epochs
surrounding the current epoch were the same as the current epoch.
Another limitation of the study is that we do not have a true indication of how the detector
performs in data from patients. One type of artefact potentially aﬀecting the results could be
baseline drift on the signals due to high impedance. PD patients sweat more during the night [60],
which may aﬀect the impedance of the electrodes. It is unknown if EMG artefacts from excessive
movement aﬀect the EOG and if any movement artefacts have been detected as REMs, although
unlikely due to the non-overlapping frequency content. A screening of the quality of the recordings
has been performed by personnel of DCSM and subjects with a great number of artefacts were
removed, but no further artefact rejection has been applied on the data prior to EM detection.
For the ﬁndings, it is not possible to say whether it is the detector making questionable detection,
the sleep stage scorings that may be wrong or if there is an actual diﬀerence in the EM. Further
73
==PAGE:96==
studies must be performed to make conclusions on diﬀerences in the patient groups and to verify
EMs as a reliable biomarker for PD.

Conclusion
Two EM detectors were developed for automatic detection of slow and rapid nocturnal EMs. The
detectors were trained, validated and tested based on a GS generated from EM scorings completed
by two independent experts investigating EOG signals from 12 healthy controls. The detectors
were trained on nine subjects and tested and validated on three subjects, and the performance of
the detectors when compared to GS was an F1-score of 0.410 for detection of REMs and 0.267
for detection of SEMs. It was investigated if the two experts agree more with each other than
the detectors agree with the experts, and no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in performance of detectors
and experts when looking at F1-scores were found. This indicates acceptable performances of the
detectors.
The detectors were applied to clinical data from 128 subjects in the groups; controls, PLMD,
iRBD, PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients and the EM measures duration, density and coverage
were investigated across sleep stages.
The main ﬁndings were signiﬁcantly lower REM density
in REM sleep for PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients compared to controls and PLMD patients,
and signiﬁcantly higher coverage of SEMs and iSEMs in N2 sleep for both groups of PD patients
compared to controls and PLMD patients. Patients with iRBD showed signiﬁcantly higher REM
density than all other group. These ﬁndings indicate an aﬀection of the oculomotor system in PD
patients due to the neurodegenerative process but further studies must be made to verify EMs as
a reliable biomarker for PD.
5.1
Future Work
In a future study more experts should be included to obtain a more reliable GS. The detector
is developed to ﬁt a GS, but if the GS is questionable due to disagreement between the experts
on EM location and duration then the same applies for the detector. Training of detectors using
data from patient (e.g. PD patients) is suggested as well, to investigate how the detectors perform
in non-healthy subject. Additionally, morphology of the EMs as e.g. the amplitude, deﬂection
time, oscillation frequency should be investigated to learn more about the morphology diﬀerences
of REMs and SEMs between the groups.
When the measures are investigated in the sleep stages, the results depend highly on the sleep
stage scoring which is done manually for the data used in the current study. In a future study the
sleep stage scoring could be done automatically to remove subjectivity from the scoring. Also the
75
==PAGE:98==
study could be limited to stable sleep stages as in [63]. Scoring is more diﬃcult in PD patients
than in controls which potentially aﬀects the results. In the current study no artefact rejection is
done, and it is unknown how EMG and EEG artefacts aﬀect the results. A suggestion in a further
study is to subtract the EEG signal from the EOG signal to remove EEG artefacts or use adaptive
ﬁltering to remove EEG components from the EOG signal. Another suggestion is to examine the
correlation between the EEG and EOG signals to eliminate EEG artefacts, as EOG data correlated
with EEG data probably not contain EMs.
The iRBD patient group is a highly heterogeneous group including patients who have only subtle
abnormalities through to patients who may soon develop PD. One interesting retrospective study
could be to split the iRBD patients into two groups (using the knowledge of which develop PD and
which do not) and investigate who diﬀers most from the control group. It could then be investigated
if it is possible to classify the iRBD patient into PD risk groups based on the measures of EMs.

I. INTRODUCTION
The most pronounced change in brain and body
activity occurs when a person changes between the
stages of awake, REM sleep and non-REM sleep. A
classiﬁcation of the stages is based on complex elec-
trophysiological signals including brain activity mea-

sured by electroencephalography, eye movements
measured by electrooculography, motor activity by
electromyography and several other physiological
modalities. Eye movements (EM) are extensively
investigated when classifying which of the above
stages a person is in since they vary greatly over the
stages. The interest in nocturnal EMs started years
ago and they have been used as a marker for stage
classiﬁcation since the sleep stage deﬁnition stated
by Rechtshaffen and Kales in 1968 [1]. The current
deﬁnition of EMs relies on the newest standard for
sleep stage classiﬁcation, the American Association
of Sleep Medicine (AASM) standard [2], which is
based on a consensus between experts. Nevertheless,
a systematic study of the distribution and morphol-
ogy of nocturnal EMs still lacks to this day. There
is no clear guideline for examining EMs, i.e. no
deﬁnition for normal distribution or start and stop
characteristics for EMs.
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most com-
mon age-related neurodegenerative disease today and
the disease not only affects patients but also their
relatives. It is of high interest to ﬁnd a method
to detect PD as early as possible in order to give
the patient a chance to make a lifestyle change
which may slow down the progression of the dis-
ease [3]. It may even be possible to make a cure
or discover a medication that will slow down the
progression of PD if taken early in the course of
disease. The diagnosis of PD today is primarily based
on a clinical examination, which means that motor
symptoms must be present before the disease can
be diagnosed and treatment can begin [3]. It may
be that analysis of nocturnal EMs could reveal new
information about brain activity within health and
disease. Speciﬁcally, a deeper and more systematic
characterization of nocturnal EMs could potential be
indicative of certain diseases. In current project, it
is hypothesised that abnormal nocturnal EMs within
the different sleep stages can be seen for patients
106
==PAGE:129==
with Parkinson’s disease.
In this study, two EM detectors will be imple-
mented to automatically detect the two most common
form of nocturnal EMs; slow EMs and rapid EMs.
The detectors will be applied on clinical data to
evaluate if any abnormalities in EM duration, density
or coverage are seen for PD patients and patients
at high risk of developing PD compared to healthy
control subjects. This thesis will get closer to answer
whether analysis of nocturnal EMs hold potential to
be a biomarker for PD.
The main objective of this study is to implement
two eye movement detectors, for automatic detection
of slow and rapid nocturnal EMs. The automatic
detectors are trained, validated and tested using a
gold standard found from a consensus between two
experts scoring different types of EMs in data from
control subjects. The detectors are furthermore ap-
plied on a clinical data set containing PD patients,
patients at high risk of developing PD (patients with
a disorder called idiopathic REM sleep behavior dis-
order (iRBD)), patients with nocturnal motor abnor-
malities but not linked to PD (patients with a disorder
called periodic leg movement disorder (PLMD)) and
a group of control subjects. It is hypothesized that
abnormal EMs are seen for PD patients, and to a
lesser degree for iRBD patients compared to healthy
subjects and that EMs, therefore, have potential to
be a biomarker for early identiﬁcation of PD.
In 1988 Lees et al. [4] study the relation between
disturbed nocturnal sleep and PD and ﬁnd that sleep
disturbances are a frequent challenge for PD patients.
However a disturbed sleep pattern is not considered
as a part of the diagnosis of PD [5], even though
many studies suggest that sleep disturbances and
changes in the sleep cycles may precede years before
observed PD motor symptoms [5]–[11]. Today the
diagnosis is primarily based on a clinical examina-
tion, which means that motor symptoms must be
present before the disease can be diagnosed [3].
Schenk et al. [12] were the ﬁrst to report the
relation between PD and idiopathic REM sleep be-
haviour disorder (iRBD) in a study conducted in
1996, which has led to much research within the ﬁeld
subsequently [6]–[10], [13], [14]. Schenk et al. found
in their ﬁrst study that 38 % of the subjects developed
PD or dementia after a mean of 13 years from the
onset of iRBD. In a follow-up study 16 years later
they found that 81 % of the subjects developed PD
or dementia 29 years after the onset of iRBD. This
study was conducted on males at age > 50 [12].
Accordingly, a study by Iranzo et al. with 44 iRBD
patients shows that 45 % developed some kind of
neurological disorder including PD after an average
of 11.5 years after iRBD onset [7].
The neurons controlling nocturnal EMs are found
in the brain stem in the same locations as neurons in-
volved in PD [15]. Investigation of EOG is included
in a PSG analysis, but scoring of single EMs is not a
part of the clinical routine. Manual scoring of EMs
is a highly subjective and time-consuming task why
it is of great value to design an automated algorithm
for EMs detection [16]–[19]. The use of automatic
methods has been investigated widely in the ﬁeld
of sleep research, e.g. for sleep stage classiﬁcation
[20]–[23], sleep spindles detection [24]–[28] and
detection of nocturnal EMs [16], [19], [29]–[31].
The use of wavelet transform in a complex form
(dual-tree complex wavelet transform (DTCWT)) for
automatic EMs detection is described in a study
by Christensen et al. from 2017 [31]. This detector
utilises that the left and right EOG signal appears
anti-correlated during an EM and calculates the
phase of the signals. The accuracy of this detec-
tor is 0.870 when no overlapping method is used,
but increases to 0.910 when periods with EMs are
counted as true positives if at least 50 % of the period
is identiﬁed as EMs by both the gold standard and
the detector. The gold standard in this study is a
consensus between two experts. This EM detector
will be described thoroughly in a later section since
the development of new detectors in the project will
be based on this detector.
During the daytime, EMs are related to the sense
of sight via both voluntary and involuntary EMs,
as smooth pursuit and saccadic EMs [32]. When
investigating nocturnal EMs, the characteristics are
different than during W and the sleep stages are
characterised by different types of nocturnal EMs
or absence of EMs. The description of REMs and
SEMs is based on the deﬁnition from the [2]. REMs
are conjugate (i.e. both eyes move with the same
speed and direction at the same time), irregular, sharp
peaks with an initial deﬂection usually lasting less
than 500 ms [2]. The deﬂection is the period from
the start of the EM to the peak. REMs are mainly
found in REM sleep. SEMs are conjugate, regular,
slow, sinusoidal peaks having a longer deﬂection
times, typically lasting more than 500 ms [2]. SEMs
are mainly horizontal and differ in amplitude. They
typically appear in the sleep onset period, i.e. the
transition period from W to sleep or in N1 sleep.

==PAGE:130==
A biomarker is a biological marker indicating a
biological change or state which can be measured or
observed [33]. Often biomarkers are used clinically
as a diagnostic tool either alone or combined. The
main biomarker for PD is motor symptoms, such
as hypokinesia (less movement), bradykinesia (slow
movement), rigidity, tremors, short shufﬂing steps,
slightly ﬂexed elbows, hips and knees, balance prob-
lems and a loss of facial expressions [34]–[36]. The
diagnosis of PD today is primarily based on a clinical
examination, which means that there must be motor
symptoms present before the disease can be diag-
nosed [3]. This is required despite that other symp-
toms may occur years before motor symptoms do [5].
Other symptoms/biomarkers include memory loss,
olfactory dysfunction, constipation, bladder distur-
bances, neuropsychiatric symptoms (e.g. depression,
anxiety, apathy, confusion) and sleep disturbances.
The sleep disturbances include excessive daytime
sleepiness (which affects up to 50 % and can be
a preclinical biomarker), restless legs, periodic limb
movements and/or REM sleep behaviour disorder
(RBD) [37]. RBD is a sleep parasomnia manifested
by dream enactment and loss of muscle atonia during
REM sleep. Examples of talking, shouting and ab-
normal movements are commonly reported by bed
partners of RBD patients [37]. When the RBD is
not associated with another underlying neurological
condition such as narcolepsy or brain stem lesion, it
is deﬁned as idiopathic (or in some cases isolated)
RBD (iRBD) [38]. RBD is especially interesting in
relation to PD since approximately one third of all
PD patients experience RBD [12]. Many studies have
investigated the relationship between iRBD patients
and PD, and have found a particularly high risk of
a patient suffering from iRBD later developing PD
[5]–[10].
As the symptoms above precede the motor symp-
toms required to diagnose PD, the investigation
of them as biomarkers may help to diagnose PD
earlier than is possible today. Early diagnosis is
of high interest since it gives the patient a chance
to start a lifestyle change which may slow down
the progression of the disease [3]. Early diagnosis
may also make it possible for a medication to be
developed in the future that can slow down or even
stop the progression of PD if taken before motor
symptoms occur indicating that too much of the brain
is degenerated. [3].
II. METHODS
A. Development of Detector
1) Data: The data used in this project is provided
by the Danish Center for Sleep Medicine (DCSM),
Department of Clinical Neurophysiology, Rigshospi-
talet Glostrup. For the study, EOG recorded as part
of a standard PSG, from 12 healthy subjects (age
50±11.15, six females) is used. The data is sampled
with a sampling frequency of 256 Hz and raw data
is used when training and testing the detector.
2) Manual EM Scorings and Gold Standard:
The data is scored by two independent experts;
expert 1 (E1) and expert 2 (E2), who have labelled
different types of EMs between lights off and lights
on for all subjects (except one subject for one expert,
which is scored by the author but supervised by
E1). According to the instruction for scoring, an
EM starts when left and right EOG signals are still
parallel, just before the signals get anti-correlated,
i.e. the reﬂection and an EM ends when the signals
get parallel again. From the scorings of REMs and
SEMs a gold standard (GS) is found as a consensus
between the two experts, i.e. the samples that both
experts have marked as EMs. A GS is found for
both REMs and SEMs, after the data is adjusted as
described in previous section.
3) Inter-Rater Variability:
To investigate how
much the experts agree with each other and how
much the detectors agree with GS the inter-rater
variability is calculated as the F1-score. The F1-
score is a statistic measure for inter-rater variability
and is calculated by as F1 = 2 ·
Precision·Recall
Precision+Recall,
where Precision =
TP
TP+FP and Recall =
TP
TP+FN .
The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision
and recall, and deﬁned in the range from zero to
one, where zero corresponds to no agreement and
one corresponds to perfect agreement. TP denotes
true positives (hit), FP denotes false positives (false
alarm) and FN denotes false negatives (miss).
Separate EMs are compared as events and the
exact start and end of each EM is not taken into
consideration. Each true event counts as a TP, and
duration of the events are unimportant. The F1-score
depends on which way you do the comparison and
therefore the test is performed twice, ﬁrst with E1
being the scorer with the ”truth” and E2 with the
prediction and then the opposite way. If any overlap
between a ”predicted” EM and a ”true” EM is found,
the ”predicted” EM counts as a TP. If a predicted EM
is not overlapping with a true EM it counts as an FP

==PAGE:131==
and when no EM is predicted, but a true EM is found
it counts as an FN. An overlap of 20 % sample is
required between two EMs for a TP to be counted.
In Figure 1 a visualisation of the overlapping method
is shown for a comparison between the detector and
GS.
4) Description of the Detector: In current study
the EM detector by Christensen et al. [31] is tuned to
search for individual REMs and SEMs. The original
detector aims to detect all types of EMs and not
REMs and SEMs separately. A schematic overview
of the detector procedures is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.
The detector takes two EOG signals as input; left
EOG (EOGL) and right EOG (EOGR). The signals
are high-pass ﬁltered with a zero-phase forward
and reverse digital IIR ﬁlter, using a fourth-order
Butterworth ﬁlter. The ﬁlter has a cut-off frequency
(-3dB) at 0.1 Hz and is applied to remove the lowest
frequencies, e.g. baseline drift.
Subsequently, a cleaning procedure is applied to
keep the components reﬂecting EMs. EOGL and
EOGR typically appear anti-correlated when EMs
are present, and components reﬂecting EMs would
therefor reﬂect an angle (or phase) between the two
signals of a certain degree. Also the components
would be in a certain frequency range reﬂecting
EMs. The eliminated components reﬂect noise com-
ing from e.g. EEG, ECG or EMG measured in
the EOG. The signal is decomposed using dual-
tree complex WT and reconstructed after eliminating
the components not reﬂecting EMs. The coefﬁcients
related to the frequencies in which EMs appear are
used for phase calculation whereas coefﬁcients not
related to EMs are set to zero.
The phase is calculated using the Matlab-
function angle that takes a vector of complex
elements as input and outputs the phase angles in
radians. The phase is calculated for EOGL and
EOGR and the vectors containing of the signals
are subtracted. Phase differences above the threshold
Tangle are maintained and phase differences below
below the threshold are set to zero. After those steps,
the signals are reconstructed using only the relevant
coefﬁcient levels and the anti-correlated parts of the
signal.
After the cleaning procedure, the EOGL signal
is subtracted from EOGR, resulting in a vector
Adiff. Outliers not related to EMs, deﬁned above the
threshold Tamp = 600µV are removed. All samples
above the threshold Pth, i.e. the Pth-percentile, are
deﬁned as EM candidates and by modifying the
EM candidate vector in two steps the ﬁnal EMs
are found. First EMs are combined if the duration
between two EMs is less than durhole, then EMs
with a duration of durEM or shorter are eliminated.
The ﬁnal EM vector is a binary vector with ones
indicating the presence of EMs and zeros indicating
absence of EMs.
5) Training and Testing: The training is based on
a grid search, to tune the detectors to ﬁnd individual
REMs and SEMs by ﬁnding the highest possible
mean by-event F1-score when comparing to GS.
After randomisation of the subjects the ﬁrst nine
subjects are used for training and the last three

V. DISCUSSION
A. Manual EM Scoring by the Experts
The two experts have a very different approach to
the manual scoring of EMs. E2 marks the deﬂection
of the rapid EMs (according to E2’s own statement,
but supported by visual inspection of the manual
scorings), whereas E1 marks larger areas in the
data, i.e. more samples per EM and sometimes more
EMs as one event (in an EM train). The mean
duration of E1’s REMs and SEMs are 5.2 s and 7.4
s respectively, whereas the mean duration of E2’s
REMs and SEMs are 0.25 s and 1.71 s respectively.
The scoring instruction states that an EM starts
when the signals are still parallel, just before the
deﬂection where the signals begin to appear anti-
correlated, and the EMs ends when the signals get
back to parallel. This means that the period where
the signal is zero, i.e. neutral position of the eyes
until it gets back to zero, should all be marked as

In a study by Warby et al. [26] an inter-rater
variability study was made between experts scoring
SS and the mean by-event F1-score for agreement
between the experts was 0.750 ± 0.06 (requiring an
overlap of 25 %), which is a higher agreement than
obtained in current study. However SS have been
studied more than EMs, and the characteristics of
SS are well deﬁned compared to the characteristics
of nocturnal EMs. Furthermore, more experts were
recruited in the study by Warby et al. [26], which
also can have an impact on the inter-rater variability.
Danker-Hopfe et al. [40] did a study on inter-rater
reliability between sleep stage scorers in different
sleep laboratories in different European countries,
showing that the scorers had an agreement on κ =
0.619 in PD patients. This emphasizes the fact that
sleep scoring, both sleep staging and identiﬁcation
of sleep events, is highly subjective and can affect
the results.

C. Accuracy, Sensitivity and Speciﬁcity of RD and
SD
Sensitivity is a measure of how often the detector
correctly identiﬁes a TP, i.e. the probability of detect-
ing a positive event where there truly is a positive
event. A detector with high sensitivity will catch
most EMs and avoid a high number of FNs, which
typically comes with a high number of FPs. The
advantage of a detector with high sensitivity is that
almost all EMs are detected, but this is at the expense
of a lot of FPs. Mathematically the sensitivity is high
if the number of FNs is low, and the sensitivity does
not say anything about the number of FPs. Speciﬁcity
is a measure of how often the detector correctly
detects ”no EMs” in cases were there truly are no
EMs to be detected. A high speciﬁcity means that
few FPs are generated, but it typically comes with a
high number of FNs, i.e. a high number of undetected
EMs.
When developing a detector and deciding whether
to prioritize sensitivity or speciﬁcity, the context
of the detector use is of course important. As an
example, a high sensitivity is typically desired when
screening for a disease, since missing any correct
positive cases can be fatal, while false negative
detection may be inconvenient and expensive but
otherwise not harmful. In the case of the EM de-
tectors, a high sensitivity and speciﬁcity are equally
prioritized.
The performance in terms of sensitivity and speci-
ﬁcity of RD and SD is shown in Table VI. RD has
a high speciﬁcity which is greatly inﬂuenced by the
majority of samples being TNs. The sensitivity of
the RD is lower than the speciﬁcity, meaning that
the detector does not detect all EMs, but the detected
EMs are correct. The accuracy of the RD is 0.985
± 0.002, which is higher than in [31]. SD has a
high speciﬁcity, also due to many TNs and a lower
sensitivity. This indicates that the number of FNs
is high, i.e. many SEMs are not detected by the SD.
The accuracy is 0.921 ± 0.017, which again in higher
than in [31]

E. Challenges With Automatic Detectors in Sleep
Research
In the ﬁeld of sleep research, the performance
of automatic detectors are somehow low compared
to other areas of automatic detection, e.g. QRS
complex detection which is much more precise. As
examples, the sleep spindle detector in [28] performs
with kappa values indicating agreements from slight
to moderate and a maximum F1 = 0.450 for SS
detection in controls. Magosso et al. [30] had an
accuracy of 0.804 ± 0.040, sensitivity of 0.672 ±
0.074 and a speciﬁcity of 0.839 ± 0.087 when
detecting SEMs and Boulus et al. had an accuracy
of 0.830 when detecting SEMs. One general problem
with automatic detectors in the ﬁeld of sleep research
is that they are often trained on data from young
subjects with an unknown performance in older
adults [26]. Generally, scoring of stages and sleep
events in older adults get harder due to changes in
brain-activity with age. This appears as signiﬁcant
changes in the amount of time in SWS, N2 and
REM sleep [41]. When detectors are developed using
data from young people they will probably perform
differently on older adults.
When a detector is developed to ﬁt a GS, the
detector performance is aimed to be correct when
compared to GS, but if GS is not ideal then it is
hard to say if the detector is performing correctly or
just agreeing with the GS. It can be seen in Tables III
and IV that though the performances of the detectors
are imperfect when compared to the experts, the
experts do not perform better than the detectors
when compared to each other. This indicates the big
disagreement between experts, making it difﬁcult to
118
==PAGE:141==
obtain a GS on which the detector can be trained.
In the abstract by Pizza et al. [42] the detector
developed by Christensen et al. [31] is applied to
ﬁnd all types of EMs in all stages, REMs in REM
sleep and SEMs in N1 sleep. The accuracy between
the detector and two experts and the accuracy in
between the two experts are compared and there are
no signiﬁcant difference in the performance of the
detector and the performance of the experts, as also
found in current project. This again indicates that
though a detector performs only fair, it does not
agree less with the experts than the experts agree
with each other. One important thing to note is
that the performance measures may be calculated
differently in different studies, e.g the amount of
overlap required when using by-event may vary.
F. Application of the detector
It is hypothesised that no signiﬁcant differences
in duration, density and coverage of REMs, SEMs
and iSEMs between controls and PLMD patients
would be found because PLMD patients should have
no signs of related neurodegeneration. Assuming
that the brain may be most damaged in PD+RBD
patients and least damaged in iRBD patients (except
for controls and PLMD patients), the measures of
a progression biomarker would either increase or
decrease in relation to the degree of brain-damage,
i.e. having the iRBD patients to show an intermediate
level between controls and PD patients. Furthermore,
it is hypothesized that signiﬁcant differences would
occur in SEMs and iSEMs in NREM sleep between
PD patients when compared to controls and PLMD
patients, since the brain stem controlling SEMs is
affected in PD. The main ﬁndings for detected
REMs, SEMs and iSEMs in the clinical cohort will
be discussed in the following subsections.
1) The main ﬁndings for REMs:: In N3 sleep
iRBD patients showed signiﬁcantly higher REM
duration than all other groups, which needs to be
explored further. It may be noise due to body move-
ment from the EMG channels affecting the EOG
signals or movement artefacts on the EOG channels,
though heavy movement among iRBD patients is
mainly seen in REM sleep.
During REM sleep both PD patients groups (but
mostly PD+RBD patients) have signiﬁcantly lower
REM density than controls and PLMD patients. This
indicates an affection of the oculomotor system in
PD patients due to the neurodegenerative process
[39].
In REM sleep PD+RBD patients have signiﬁcantly
lower REM density and coverage than iRBD pa-
tients. The brain is more damaged in patients with
PD+RBD than in patients with iRBD which may
results in fewer REMs in PD+iRBD patients.
During N3 sleep, controls showed signiﬁcantly
higher REM density and coverage than all other
groups. The same trend can be seen in part 2 for
the test subjects in the validation of the detector.
One suggestion is the clear slow waves in SWS
from the EEG represented in the EOG in N3 sleep
appearing slightly anti-correlated being detected as
REMs. Slow waves are mainly measured from the
frontal part of the brain in EEG and are likely
to affect the EOG channels [13]. Furthermore, the
experts have scored REMs in N3 as well, meaning
that the detector is trained to ﬁnd some REMs in N3.
The reason why controls have higher density may
be because of less SWS in iRBD and PD patient.
However this does not explain why controls have a
higher density of REMs in N3 than PLMD patients.
2) The main ﬁndings for SEMs and iSEMs::
In REM sleep both PD patient groups showed sig-
niﬁcantly lower SEM duration than controls. These
are likely the slow component of the REMs and
indicating that PD patients have shorter periods with
REMs.
During W, iRBD, PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients
had signiﬁcantly lower density of SEMs and iSEMs
than controls, and PD+RBD patients also had signif-
icantly lower densities compared to PLMD patients.
This may be due to variations in sleep stage scoring,
i.e. epochs with SEMs might have been scored as W
for controls and PLMD patients and as N1 for PD
patients. However, no difference in density in N1 is
seen, which contradicts this statement.
In N3 sleep iRBD and PD-RBD patients showed
lower density of SEMs and iSEMs than controls,
which needs further exploration. Maybe the detector
does not work well in N3 sleep as described above.
In N2 sleep PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients
showed signiﬁcantly higher coverage of SEMs and
iSEMs than controls and PLMD patients, which is
consistent with the ﬁnding in [39]. This ﬁnding may
be due to an affection of the brain stem, including
SC, leading to an affection in the inhibition of EMs
resulting in slow involuntary drifting of the eyes [39].
Though not signiﬁcant in all stages, a general
trend for SEMs was a decrease in density in W,
REM and N1 sleep and an increase of SEMs in N2
sleep following disease progression. For iSEMs, the
119
==PAGE:142==
same trend can be seen, though not in REM sleep.
It is worth considering if it is more interesting to
investigate iSEMs since many SEMs may just be
slow components of REMs. The AASM deﬁnition
states that SEMs are found outside REM sleep and
it can be seen that both density and coverage is lower
for iSEMs than SEMs in REM sleep.
3) Limitations of the Study: The study of EMs
in the clinical cohort has some limitations. First, the
sleep stage scoring is done manually for all subjects
which has potentially affected the results since sleep
stage scoring in iRBD and PD patients may be difﬁ-
cult due to changes in the events which the scoring
is based on, such as sleep spindles [43] and lack of
muscle atonia. Also reduced sleep stability is seen
for PD patients [9], which leads to the suggestion
of limiting the study to only look at stable sleep
when investigating the detected EMs [39]. In [39],
a sleep stage was deﬁned stable when the epochs
surrounding the current epoch were the same as the
current epoch.
Another limitation of the study is that we do not
have a true indication of how the detector performs in
data from patients. One type of artefact potentially
affecting the results could be baseline drift on the
signals due to high impedance. PD patients sweat
more during the night [37], which may affect the
impedance of the electrodes. It is unknown if EMG
artefacts from excessive movement affect the EOG
and if any movement artefacts have been detected as
REMs, although unlikely due to the non-overlapping
frequency content. A screening of the quality of
the recordings has been performed by personnel of
DCSM and subjects with a great number of artefacts
were removed, but no further artefact rejection has
been applied on the data prior to EM detection.
For the ﬁndings, it is not possible to say whether
it is the detector making questionable detection, the
sleep stage scorings that may be wrong or if there is
an actual difference in the EM. Further studies must
be performed to make conclusions on differences in
the patient groups and to verify EMs as a reliable
biomarker for PD.
VI. CONCLUSION
Two EM detectors were developed for automatic
detection of slow and rapid nocturnal EMs. The
detectors were trained, validated and tested based
on a GS generated from EM scorings completed by
two independent experts investigating EOG signals
from 12 healthy controls. The detectors were trained
on nine subjects and tested and validated on three
subjects, and the performance of the detectors when
compared to GS was an F1-score of 0.410 for
detection of REMs and 0.267 for detection of SEMs.
It was investigated if the two experts agree more
with each other than the detectors agree with the
experts, and no signiﬁcant difference in performance
of detectors and experts when looking at F1-scores
were found. This indicates acceptable performances
of the detectors.
The detectors were applied to clinical data from
128 subjects in the groups; controls, PLMD, iRBD,
PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients and the EM mea-
sures duration, density and coverage were investi-
gated across sleep stages. The main ﬁndings were
signiﬁcantly lower REM density in REM sleep for
PD-RBD and PD+RBD patients compared to con-
trols and PLMD patients, and signiﬁcantly higher
coverage of SEMs and iSEMs in N2 sleep for both
groups of PD patients compared to controls and
PLMD patients. Patients with iRBD showed signif-
icantly higher REM density than all other group.
These ﬁndings indicate an affection of the oculo-
motor system in PD patients due to the neurodegen-
erative process but further studies must be made to
verify EMs as a reliable biomarker for PD.
VII. FUTURE WORK
In a future study more experts should be included
to obtain a more reliable GS. The detector is devel-
oped to ﬁt a GS, but if the GS is questionable due
to disagreement between the experts on EM location
and duration then the same applies for the detector.
Training of detectors using data from patient (e.g.
PD patients) is suggested as well, to investigate
how the detectors perform in non-healthy subject.
Additionally, morphology of the EMs as e.g. the am-
plitude, deﬂection time, oscillation frequency should
be investigated to learn more about the morphology
differences of REMs and SEMs between the groups.
When the measures are investigated in the sleep
stages, the results depend highly on the sleep stage
scoring which is done manually for the data used
in the current study. In a future study the sleep
stage scoring could be done automatically to remove
subjectivity from the scoring. Also the study could
be limited to stable sleep stages as in [39]. Scoring is
more difﬁcult in PD patients than in controls which
potentially affects the results. In the current study
no artefact rejection is done, and it is unknown how
120
==PAGE:143==
EMG and EEG artefacts affect the results. A sugges-
tion in a further study is to subtract the EEG signal
from the EOG signal to remove EEG artefacts or use
adaptive ﬁltering to remove EEG components from
the EOG signal. Another suggestion is to examine
the correlation between the EEG and EOG signals
to eliminate EEG artefacts, as EOG data correlated
with EEG data probably not contain EMs.
The iRBD patient group is a highly heterogeneous
group including patients who have only subtle abnor-
malities through to patients who may soon develop
PD. One interesting retrospective study could be to
split the iRBD patients into two groups (using the
knowledge of which develop PD and which do not)
and investigate who differs most from the control
group. It could then be investigated if it is possible to
classify the iRBD patient into PD risk groups based
on the measures of EMs.
