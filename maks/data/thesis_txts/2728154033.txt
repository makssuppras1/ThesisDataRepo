Abstract
Introduction. T cell exhaustion is a process driven by chronic antigen stimulation.
This process induces the stable expression of inhibitory surface receptors, impaired response
to cancer antigens and low cell proliferation and persistence in vivo. Therefore, understanding
the molecular determinants of exhaustion is crucial for improving current immunotherapeutic
strategies. Several in vitro assays are available to induce an exhausted phenotype in T cells,
but they fail to reproduce the surface of tumor cells, which is enriched of different co-
inhibitory ligands. One mean for recreating this complex surface is developing co-inhibition
assays using artificial antigen presenting cells (APCs).
Material and methods. To generate artificial APCs that could activate and exhaust T
cells, five parental cancer cell lines (FM3, FM93/2, HEK293T, T2, and K562) were modified
to express a membrane bound (mb) OKT3 single-chain variable fragment (ScFv) derived
from the OKT3 antibody, which is a potent activator of T cells. FM3 and K562 OKT3-ScFv+
and wild-type (WT) were further modified to overexpress selected co-inhibitory ligands.
OKT3-ScFv+ K562, OKT3-ScFv+ FM3, OKT3-ScFv+PD-L1+ FM3 and OKT3-ScFv+PD-
L1+ FM3 were further used in in vitro exhaustion assays to chronically stimulate T cells and
simulate T cell exhaustion.
Results. I successfully generated five cancer cell lines expressing the mbOKT3-
ScFv. All cell lines, besides OKT3-ScFv+ T2, were able to efficiently re-activate T cells.
We also successfully generated FM3 and K562, both OKT3-ScFv+ and WT, separately
overexpressing eight co-inhibitory ligands. Chronic stimulation with OKT3-ScFv+ cancer
cells induced elevated expression of different exhaustion markers in CD8+ T cells. In addition,
OKT3-ScFv+PD-L1+ and OKT3-ScFv+PD-L2+ FM3 induced a dampened T cell phenotype
compared to their parental cell line, but CD8+ T cells showed a higher degree of triple and
quadruple positives for the different exhaustion markers.

Conclusion. The use of the generated artificial APCs for in vitro exhaustion assays
show promise in reproducing the phenotype of exhausted T cell in vivo. However, further
in vitro experiments are required to fully validate this system and assess its suitability for
CRISPR screens.

inhibition

Introduction
Cancer is one of the leading causes of premature death worldwide and a great barrier to
increasing life expectancy (Bray et al., 2021). According to estimates from the International
Agency for Research on Cancer, 7.6 million deaths were reported worldwide in 2018 and
12.7 million new cases are expected yearly (Sung et al., 2021). Different treatment options
are available to treat and manage cancer, including conventional therapies like surgery,
chemotherapy, and radiotherapy. However, novel cancer treatment options are tested in
clinical trials due to side effects, resistance, and lack of efficacy associated with traditional
methods (Abbas et al., 2018).
Immunotherapy is a promising type of therapy that has revolutionized the field of
oncology. Immunotherapies aim to engage the immune system to recognise, attack, and kill
cancer cells. While conventional therapies directly target cancer cells, immunotherapies use
different strategies to either amplify immune activation mechanisms or reshape the tumor
microenvironment to restore lost antitumor immunity (Sanmamed et al., 2018).
The emergence of immunotherapies, particularly immune-checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs)
and adoptive cell transfer (ACT), has sparked significant research and clinical interest in the
T cell exhaustion. T cell exhaustion is a cellular differentiation state linked to the gradual
declines of T cell effector functions following chronic antigen exposure (Chow et al., 2022).
Exhausted T cells in the tumor microenvironment (TME) display several distinct features that
distinguish them from functional effector T cells. These include upregulation of inhibitory
receptors and reduced proliferation, cytokine production and cytotoxic potential against
tumor cells (Chow et al., 2022, Wherry et al., 2015).
Cancer poses a significant challenge to the immune system due to its ability to evade
immune surveillance. A common evasion mechanism is the suppression of antitumor immune
responses by either expressing molecules with direct immunosuppressive properties or
recruiting regulatory T cells that suppress T cell responses (Hanahan et al., 2011). Some of
1
==PAGE:16==
these molecules are directly involved in molecular pathways leading to T cell exhaustion
(Wherry et al., 2015). For these reasons, understanding the features and mechanisms of T
cell exhaustion in cancer is vital for developing effective immunotherapeutic strategies.
One of the most recent advances to interrogate immunity are CRISPR screens, a high-
throughput genetic screening technique that uses the CRISPR technology to study the effects
of gene perturbations on cellular phenotypes (Shi et al., 2022). Due to the low efficiency of
inducing T cells exhaustion in vivo, alternative means of recapitulating the in vivo phenotype
are crucial for CRISPR screens.
Several in vitro assays are available to reproduce the dysfunctional phenotype of ex-
hausted T cells, including chronic stimulation with plate bound anti-CD3 (αCD3) (Vardhana
et al., 2020) or αCD3/αCD28 beads (Dunsford et al., 2020). However, these methods
fail to reproduce the complex surface of tumor cells, which are enriched in co-inhibitory
molecules. Consequently, methods for studying T cell exhaustion signatures and signalling
pathways upon co-inhibition are highly valuable. In this thesis I therefore seek to develop
novel methods for evaluating T cell exhaustion in vitro and a set of assays for studying T
cell coinhibitory pathways using artificial APCs expressing stimulatory and co-inhibitory
molecules.
1.1 T cells as a key components of the immune system
The immune system is a complex and fascinating network of organs, cells, and proteins
that defends the body against pathogens and unwanted cells, such as cancer cells. This
network encompasses three different lines of defence: anatomic and chemical barriers, the
innate immune system, and the adaptive immune system. The first line of defence comprises
the skin and the mucosal surfaces (physical barriers) and the complement and antimicrobial
proteins (chemical barriers). The second and the third lines of defence act in concert to
defend the host from pathogens that manage to escape through the physical and chemical
barriers (Medzhitov et al., 2000).
The innate immune system offers fast, non-specific responses towards a pathogen-
associated molecular patterns in the form of conserved molecules that are present in large

groups of organisms but not in the host itself (Medzhitov et al., 2000). In contrast, the
adaptive immune system includes both humoral and cell-mediated responses toward specific
pathogens. Adaptive immunity depends on the function of two classes of specialized cells, T
and B cells, and provides long-term protection against the encountered antigens (Flajnik et
al., 2018).
T cells develop in the thymus, where progenitor cells originating from the bone marrow
experience a series of maturation steps. These steps, which include a positive and a negative
selection, ensure the generation of T cells with functional T cell receptors (TCRs) capable of
recognizing foreign antigens while avoiding self-reactivity. After successful maturation, T
cells migrate to peripheral lymphoid tissues, where they await their cognate antigens (Ashby
et al., 2023, Sun et al., 2023).
T cells can be broadly categorized into two main subsets based on the presence of
distinct surface markers: CD4+ T cells (helper T cells or TH) and CD8+ T cells (cytotoxic T
lymphocytes or CTL). CD4+ T cells express the CD4 co-receptor and play a crucial role in
coordinating the immune response. They can differentiate into subsets, such as TH1, TH2,
TH17, and regulatory T (Treg) cells, each with specialized functions. TH1 cells promote
cellular immunity and enhance the activity of cytotoxic T cells, while TH2 cells are involved
in humoral immunity and B cell activation. TH17 cells play a role in the defence against
extracellular bacteria and fungi. Treg cells are essential for maintaining immune tolerance
and preventing autoimmunity (Luckheeram et al., 2012). CD8+ T cells express the CD8
co-receptor and are primarily responsible for targeting and eliminating infected or abnormal
cells (Sun et al., 2023). T cells, like other cells of the immune system, release and are
influenced by cytokines, which are soluble proteins that regulate immune responses. The
most important cytokines released by T cells are interferon-gamma (IFNγ), interleukins (IL-2,
IL-4, IL-17, etc.), and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNFα). These cytokines mediate immune
cell communication, promoting inflammation, immune cell recruitment, and differentiation.
Additionally, cytokines influence the polarization of TH cells into distinct subsets with
specialized functions (Altan-Bonnet et al., 2019).
T cells recognize antigens through the TCR in the form of short immunogenic peptides
bound to Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) class I or class II molecules on APCs

conditions are met, the T cell undergoes a series of metabolic changes that lead to activation

These cells exit peripheral lymphoid organs and, through the blood stream, migrate to the site

Following successful clearance of an infection, most of the activated T cells die.
However, a small subset persists and transitions into a pool of memory T cells (Masopust
et al., 2013). Memory T cells downregulate most of the activation programme of effector T
cells but exhibit a more rapid and robust response upon re-encountering the same pathogen,
providing long-lasting immunity. This phenomenon is central to vaccination and the concept
of immunological memory (Wherry et al., 2015, Masopust et al., 2013).
1.2 The T cell Receptor Complex
The TCR complex is a transmembrane multi-subunit protein complex located on the
T cell surface that plays a central role in antigen recognition and T cell activation. This
complex comprises the TCR and several co-receptors, including CD3, CD4, and CD8 [Figure
1.2A].
The TCR consists of two distinct chains: an α-chain and β-chain in the case of αβ T
cells (95% of T cells), and γ-chain and δ-chain in the case of γδ T cells. The α and β chains
of the TCR heterodimer each contain an immunoglobulin (Ig)-like extracellular variable
and constant domain, a membrane-proximal connecting peptide, a single TM region, and
a short cytoplasmic tail (Mariuzza et al., 2020). The variable regions of the TCR chains
are responsible for antigen recognition, as they form the antigen-binding site. The constant
regions, conversely, are involved in signalling and mediate the TCR complex stability.

The ability of the TCRs to recognise a vast repertoire of antigens is obtained by
variations in the amino acid sequence at the antigen binding site. This variation is obtained
by V(D)J recombination of the alpha and beta chains during T cell development in the
thymus. Additional variability is given by the hypervariable complementarity-determining
regions (CDRs) within the TCR and from random pairing of different TCR α-chain and TCR
β-chains. Overall, after thymic selection up to 2×107 TCRs have been predicted to arise per
person (Rossjohn et al., 2015).
The CD3 protein complex is composed of four invariant protein chains. In mammals,
the complex contains a CD3γ chain, a CD3δ chain, two CD3ε, and two D3ζ organised in
three different dimers (CD3εγ, CD3εδ, and CD3ζζ) non-covalently associated with the
TCR [Figure 1.2A]. Each subunit of the CD3εγ and CD3εδ heterodimers comprises a single

to CP, TM, and cytoplasmic regions. The TCR–CD3 complex exists on the T-cell surface

Upon binding to specific antigens presented by MHC molecules, the TCR complex
initiates a signalling cascades, leading to T-cell activation. The cytoplasmic tails of TCR
chains lack intrinsic signalling motifs. Instead, they rely on the associated signalling subunits,
primarily CD3, to transduce signals. The CD3 ε, γ, δ, and ζ chains each contain one
(ε, γ, and δ) or three (ζ) immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs) that
undergo phosphorylation by the Src kinase Lck, thereby initiating a downstream T-cell
signalling cascade (Samelson et al., 2003). Phosphorylated ITAMs on CD3 recruit and
activate SH2 domain-containing protein tyrosine kinases (PTK), such as ZAP70, to further
mediate downstream signalling through tyrosine phosphorylation (Zhang et al., 1998). These
signals lead to the activation of transcription factors under the control of three different
pathways (MAPK, NF-κB, and calcium pathways) to promote T cell activation (Choudhuri
et al., 2005) [Figure 1.2B]. An additional function of the CD3 complex is its contribution
to TCR stability on the cell surface, TCR trafficking, and expression. In particular, the
complex is necessary for TCR clustering, which is required for T cell activation (Goyette et
al., 2019).
The CD4 and CD8 are co-receptors expressed on CD4+ helper T cells and CD8+
cytotoxic T cells, respectively. These co-receptors assist in the interaction with APCs by
enhancing TCR binding to MHC molecules and stabilizing TCR-pMHC interactions. The
constructive binding allows phosphorylation of ITAMs on CD3 to amplify signal generated
by TCR engagement, leading to increased TCR signalling and T cell activation (Goyette
et al., 2019). CD4 and CD8 are downregulated with similar kinetics of the TCR, and their
contribution becomes crucial during low-affinity interactions (Viola et al., 1997).

The cSMAC is enriched in TCRs microclusters, CD28 and its cytosolic mediator

This receptor binds to CD80 (B7-1) and CD86 (B7-2) molecules expressed on APCs. CD28
engagement provides a co-stimulatory signal that enhances TCR signalling. In complex
with TCR-CD3, CD28 initiate the recruitment of downstream signalling effectors, such as
as PKCθ (Kong et al., 2011). These proteins enhance the signalling pathways triggered
by pMHC-TCR engagement. Additionally, CD28 signalling counteracts the suppressive
activity of regulatory T cells, promoting effector T-cell responses (Kong et al., 2011). After
activation, additional co-stimulatory and co-inhibitory receptors are recruited in the cSMAC.
For example, cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA4) colocalize with CD28 to physically
exclude it from the cSMAC and disrupts positive TCR signalling (Chen et al., 2013).
In a mature synapse, the pSMAC is mainly constituted by cell adhesion molecules.
In particular, it is enriched in the adhesion integrin leukocyte function-associated antigen 1
(LFA1) and the associated cytoskeletal linker talin. However, TCR microclusters initially
contact agonist pMHC in the pSMAC and then start to move radially to the cSMAC while
new activated TCR microclusters continually form in the dSMAC and then translocate
through the pSMAC (Goyette et al., 2019). In addition, the presence of a CD2 corolla in the
pSMAC amplifies active phosphorylated Lck, LAT and PLC-γ, thus boosting TCR signalling
(Demetriou et al., 2020).
Finally, large and bulky molecules like CD43 and CD45 are excluded from the cSMAC
and pSMAC in the distal region of the dSMAC (Huppa et al., 2003). CD45 is a highly
abundant, constitutively active phosphatase on T cell surfaces and plays a dual role in T
cell activation. It dephosphorylates Lck, thus maintaining a pool of active enzyme, but also
directly dephosphorylates the TCR (Choudhuri et al., 2005).
Overall, the structured spatial organization of the IS guarantees the optimal arrangement
of co-stimulatory molecules and the exclusion of bulkier molecules that might hinder pMHC-
TCR interactions, thus allowing efficient T cell activation.

1.4 Co-inhibitory pathways in T cells
Co-inhibitory receptors, also called immune checkpoints, are responsible for regulating
and terminating T cell responses, and act in a concerted manner with co-stimulatory receptors
to coordinate T cell functions (Chen et al., 2013). These receptors are therefore are crucial
for hampering excessive T cell activation, immune pathology, and autoimmunity (Choudhuri
et al., 2005). However, the ligands for co-inhibitory receptors are found in tumor lesions
like melanoma metastases and mediate tumor evasion (Baitsch et al., 2012). Therefore, their
study is central in immuno-oncology.
Different co-inhibitory receptors and their ligands have been identified [Figure 1.4].
The most well studied example of co-inhibitory receptor is CTLA4. CTLA4 competes
for the same ligands as CD28. CTLA4 prevents the optimal formation of microclusters at
the immunological synapse (Pentcheva et al., 2004). Also, CTLA4 disrupts positive TCR
signalling by recruiting phosphatases, which dephosphorylate key signalling molecules like
CD3 tails (Chen et al., 2013).
Programmed cell death 1 (PD1) regulates T cell function through two mechanisms.
First, it stimulates apoptosis of potentially pathogenic self-reactive T cells in secondary
lymphoid organs. Second, it promotes Treg cells development and function (Francisco et al.,
2010). Upon ligand interaction, PD1 inhibits TCR signalling by recruiting phosphatases on
its intracellular domain (Chemnitz et al., 2004). PD1 ligands are programmed death ligand
1 (PD-L1) and 2 (PD-L2). These ligands are induced under inflammatory conditions with
PD-L2 being ubiquitously expressed in inflamed tissues, whereas PD-L1 being restricted
to antigen-presenting cells (Liang et al., 2003). PD-L2 binds to PD-1 with 3-fold stronger
affinity compared with PD-L1 (Cheng et al., 2013).
T cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain-containing protein 3 (TIM3) binds to
Galectin 9 (GAL-9) and CEACAM1. In its unbound form, it maintains T cell activation.
Upon binding with its ligands, the intracellular domain of TIM3 is phosphorylated. This
results in the disruption of IS formation and recruitment of effector molecules. Ultimately,
the cell becomes anergic or undergoes apoptosis, which is mediated by intracellular calcium
release (Wolf et al., 2019).

1.5 T cell exhaustion
Cancer poses a significant challenge to the immune system by evading immune surveil-
lance and suppressing antitumor immune responses. It has been shown that chronic exposure
to tumor antigens and to the TME can lead to T cell exhaustion, a state of functional im-
pairment that compromises antitumor responses Wherry et al., 2015. Ineffective tumor cell
clearance allows tumor cells to grow and metastasize, leading to disease progression and poor
patient outcomes. The extent of T cell exhaustion has been correlated with the prognosis of
certain cancer types, highlighting its clinical significance.
T cell exhaustion is not unique to cancer. It was first described in chronic viral infections
in mice, but it has also been observed in humans during chronic infections like HIV and
12
Chapter 1
Introduction
==PAGE:27==
hepatitis C virus (HCV) Wherry et al., 2015. For this reason, exhaustion has been thoroughly
described in chronic infections. Also, exhaustion has predominantly been associated to CD8+
T cells responses, but also exists for CD4+ T cells (Saeidi et al., 2018). However, exhaustion
in cancer does not always recapitulate all the features of chronic infections (Thommen et al.,
2018).
T cell exhaustion in both chronic infections and cancer manifests several characteristic
features of CD8+ T cells, such as progressive and hierarchical loss of effector functions and
proliferative capacity, and persistent upregulation and co-expression of different co-inhibitory
receptors. In addition, exhausted T cells show a distinct transcriptional and epigenetic
program, with altered expression and use of transcription factors and screwed memory T
cell differentiation (Doering et al., 2012, Schietinger et al., 2014) [Figure 1.5]. However,
exhausted T cells are not inert or anergic. Instead, they retain suboptimal functions that
limit their ability to control tumor progression. Significantly, the manipulation of pathways
upregulated during exhaustion can effectively reverse this impaired condition and rejuvenate
immune responses (Wherry et al., 2015).
Different cell-intrinsic and cell-extrinsic pathways are involved in regulating and
inducing T cell exhaustion Wherry et al., 2015:
• Cell to cell signals including prolonged T cell receptor (TCR) engagement and co
stimulatory and/or co inhibitory signals expressed by cancer cells.
• Soluble factors such as excessive levels of inflammatory cytokines (for example, type I
interferons (IFNs) and suppressive cytokines including IL-10 and transforming growth
factor β (TGFβ).
• Direct repression of T cell function via production of metabolites (adenosine, prostaglandins,
and lactate) and of suppressive compounds and enzymes (nitric oxide synthase, reactive
oxygen species and indoleamine-2,3 dioxygenase (IDO)).
• Physiological changes in the TME such as hypoxia, low pH, and the deprivation of
nutrients.

Besides the sustained expression of inhibitory receptors and the activation of inhibitory
signalling pathways, transcriptional and epigenetic changes also shape the exhausted T cell
phenotype. Among all, the transcription factor TOX was identified as a key regulator of
exhaustion (Thommen et al., 2018). TOX is induced by TCR signaling and upregulates of
multiple co-inhibitory receptors genes upon chronic antigen stimulation (Belk et al., 2022).
Overexpression of TOX also induces Eomesodermin (EOMES) overexpression, which
contributes to the upregulation of co-inhibitory receptors. However, EOMES involvement in
cancer-induced exhaustion remains controversial (Thommen et al., 2018).
The identification of these pathways has been challenging. Gene signatures of ex-
hausted T cells show a degree of overlap with recently activated T cells, and co-inhibitory
receptors on exhausted T cells are also transiently expressed during T cell activation (Thom-
men et al., 2018). In addition, the expression of these receptors is not consistent. For example,
it has been found that T cells can differentiate into an exhausted state even in the absence of
PD1, which is considered the hallmark of T cell exhaustion (Odorizzi et al., 2015). Finally,
most of the research assumed a complete overlap in the exhaustion signatures of cancer
and chronic infections. However, these signatures show some clear differences, including
differential upregulation of inhibitory receptors and involvement of transcription factors
(Thommen et al., 2018).
1.6 Implications of T cell exhaustion for cancer and
immunotherapy
Immunotherapy has revolutionized the field of cancer treatment and it is a continuously
evolving field. Early stages immunotherapies focused on amplifying immune activation
mechanisms to improve antitumor immune responses. For example, early targets focused
on modulating general regulatory and/or activating mechanisms (Sanmamed et al., 2018).
However, the recognition of T cell exhaustion as a major immune evasion mechanism in

cancer, and the demonstration that it is reversible rather than a terminal or irreversible state,
has spurred the development of immunotherapeutic strategies to overcome this phenomenon
and restore T cell function Wherry et al., 2015. Some of these strategies include immune
checkpoint blockade (ICBs) therapies and adoptive cell therapies (ACTs).
1.6.1 Immune Checkpoint Blockade
ICB therapies have revolutionized cancer treatment by modulating pathways over-
expressed in exhausted T cells. The main targets of ICBs are co-inhibitory receptors and
molecules expressed by tumors with direct immunosuppressive properties or that recruit
regulatory T cells that can secrete immunosuppressive cytokines (Hanahan et al., 2011,
Wherry et al., 2015). The primary ICBs in clinic target the PD1/PD-L1 and CTLA4/B7 axes.
For example, nivolumab, an anti-PD1 mAbs, have been approved as first line treatment for
different types of cancer, like refractory and unresectable melanoma, renal cell carcinoma,
Hodgkin lymphoma and colorectal cancer with a high degree of microsatellite instability
(Waldman et al., 2020). However, due to resistance and cancer escape mechanisms, novel
immune checkpoints targets are in continuous evaluation (Ziogas et al., 2023). Combin-
ing ICB with chemotherapy or with other immune modulators, such as TIM3 and LAG3
inhibitors, has shown to synergistically enhance T cell responses and overcome resistance
mechanisms Wherry et al., 2015. For this reason, understanding the mechanisms underlying
T cell exhaustion can offer novel targets for future immunotherapies.
1.6.2 Adoptive Cell Transfers
ACTs involve the ex vivo expansion and manipulation of T cells to enhance their
antitumor activities (Perica et al., 2015). Three main types of ACTs are currently used in
the clinic: tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy, engineered T cell receptor (TCR)
therapy, and chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy.
In TIL therapy, tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes from surgically removed tumors, are
expanded ex vivo, and reinfused in the patient. This therapy has shown promising results in
melanoma treatment and is under evaluation for other types of cancer (Rosenberg et al., 2011).
However, challenges are associated with the need of tumor biopsies and the manufacturing

process. Also, cells may show an exhausted phenotype, which dampens antitumor responses
(Ziogas et al., 2023).
TCR therapy involves genetically modifying a patient’s own T cells by inserting genes
containing a TCR that specifically targets any selected antigen in a MHC-restricted manner.
Despite their efficacy in treating several solid cancers, some clinical trials have observed
severe cases of both on-target off-tumor toxicities and off-target toxicities. Thus, the safety of
preclinical TCRs and their cognate antigen targets needs to be thoroughly evaluated (Shafer
et al., 2022).
CAR T cell therapy also involves genetically modifying a patient’s own T cells. In
contrast to TCR therapy, these modified T cells express a CAR that targets specific surface
proteins on cancer cells. CAR T cell therapy has shown great promises in hematological
malignancies, but low efficacy in the treatment of solid tumors (Nielsen et al., 2020). Some
drawbacks of this therapy are the induction of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and the high
costs of production. In addition, exhaustion of CAR T cells impairs their function due to
ligand independent tonic signalling. For example, CD19 CAR T cells infused in patients with
CLL or B-cell lymphomas tended to show exhaustion phenotype in non-responding patients
(Kouro et al., 2022).
Understanding exhaustion mechanisms involved in these therapeutic cell products
would be beneficial to improve their efficacy in cancer treatment. This includes optimizing
the manufacturing process by selecting less exhausted T cell subsets, reinvigorating exhausted
T cells or using gene editing technologies like CRISPR to avoid an exhausted phenotype in
the infusion product.
1.7 Current approaches in T cell exhaustion modelling
Accurate modelling of T cell exhaustion is crucial for studying its mechanisms in cancer
settings and developing efficient immunotherapeutic strategies. Mouse models of chronic
viral infections and cancer have been key in understanding T cell exhaustion (Thommen et al.,
2018). For example, the altered functional state of PD1+ T cells has originally been described
and most extensively studied in murine models of chronic LCMV infection (Wherry et al.,

Current approaches in T cell exhaustion modelling

==PAGE:32==
2007). In vivo models offer a physiological replica of systemic immune responses and tumor
growth dynamics. However, these models are expensive, time-consuming, and limited by
efficiency and cell yield. Therefore, in vitro models are an appealing alternative for screening
and other discovery platforms Wu et al., 2023.
In vitro models of T cell exhaustion imply chronic antigen stimulation of T cells
under controlled experimental conditions. A common approach is to use plate-bound αCD3
antibodies, which enforce antigen-independent TCR clustering and induce chronic TCR
signaling (Vardhana et al., 2020, Belk et al., 2022). A very potent αCD3 antibody is
Muromonab CD3 (also called OKT3), which binds to CD3ε chains. Despite having potent
immunosuppressive properties in vivo, this antibody can strongly activate T cells at very
low concentrations (VanWauwe et al., 1980). T cell exhaustion can also be induced by
αCD3/CD28 beads (Dunsford et al., 2020). These beads are loaded with αCD3 and αCD28
antibodies, which interact with the TCR complex. CD28 provides co-stimulatory signals in
a spatially oriented fashion to efficiently activate T cells. An alternative method based on
dendritic cell (DC) stimulation was recently developed by Wu et al.. In this method, T cells
are chronically stimulated with DCs pulsed with their respective cognate peptide. These DC
are then cocultured with T cells to induce an exhausted phenotype (Wu et al., 2023).
These in vitro models isolate the core determinant of T cell exhaustion (chronic
stimulation through the TCR complex) and have been widely adopted for CRISPR screens on
exhausted T cells (Wu et al., 2023, Belk et al., 2022). A CRISPR screen is a genetic screening
technique that uses the CRISPR technology to study the effects of gene perturbations on
cellular phenotypes in a high-throughput manner (Shi et al., 2022). This offers an ideal
platform for identifying novel genetic regulators of T cell exhaustion. However, the above-
mentioned in vitro models fail to reproduce the complex surface of tumor cells, which are
enriched in co-inhibitory molecules. For this reason, no CRISPR screen has been performed
on exhausted T cells that have received co-inhibitory stimulation. Consequently, methods for
evaluating T cell exhaustion and signalling involved in the suppression of T cells are highly
valuable.

Aim and purpose
This project aimed to develop a set of assays that could recapitulate the phenotypic
and functional changes of in vivo exhausted T cells. This includes the overexpression of
co-inhibitory surface receptors, such as PD1, TIM3, CTLA-4, TIGIT and TIM3, and reduced
proliferative and cytotoxic capacity.
We hypothesized that chronic stimulation with artificial APCs expressing a mbOKT3
ScFv-CD8 hinge could activate T cells and further induce the exhausted phenotype seen in
vivo [Figure 2.1]. Since the interaction between the mbOKT3-ScFv and the TCR complex is
pMHC independent, the activation should occur polyclonally. In addition, the stimulation
mediated by OKT3-ScFv+ artificial APC should offer an additional layer of complexity to
current in vitro models by reproducing the immunological synapse formed between T cells
and cancer cells.
Moreover, we further hypothesized that OKT3-ScFv+ artificial APC expressing co-
inhibitory ligands could be a valuable tool for investigating genes involved in T cell exhaustion
in cancer. The ligands that were tested play a key role in T cell exhaustion and include PD-L1,
PD-L2, GAL-9, HVEM, CEACAM1, CEACAM5, CD155, B7-H3 and B7-H4.
Based on this, the following project aims were defined:
• Generation of 5 artificial APC cancer cell lines expressing OKT-ScFv+ (FM3, FM93/2,
HEK293T, T2 and K562)
– Validation of the obtained artificial APCs
• Generation of artificial APC expressing the selected panel of co-inhibitory ligands
starting from at least one OKT-ScFv+ cancer cell line and one WT.
• Development of exhaustion assays using the generated cancer cell lines

4.1 Cancer cell lines generation
This thesis aims to generate a set of chronic stimulation and co-inhibitory assays that
can recapitulate T cell exhaustion in vitro using artificial APCs. The first step is to generate
a set of cancer cells that overexpress mbOKT3-ScFv, derived from the potent αCD3 clone,
OKT3. Once these generated artificial APCs are validated for their mitogenic properties, they
need to be transduced with the co-inhibitory ligand of interest. This second generation of
cancer cells can then be used in co-inhibitory assays.
4.1.1 Generation of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cell lines
Five cancer cell lines were selected for transformation with mbOKT3-ScFv (plasmid
#96). This plasmid also encodes GFP under the same promoter as the mbOKT3-ScFv. These
cells should polyclonally activate T cells by engaging with the CD3-gamma subunit of the
TCR-CD3 complex. Three adherent cells (FM3, FM93/2, HEK-293T) and two suspension
cells (T2, K562) were chosen for transduction based on the Sine Reker Hadrup lab’s interest
in melanoma or widespread use as models.
The cell lines were transduced with a serial two-fold dilution of lentivirus containing
the mbOKT3-ScFv construct. Successful transduction was evaluated by GFP-expression
using an inverted fluorescence microscope [Figure 4.1A]. The optimal dilution of lentivirus

suspension cells. In addition, transduction levels were evaluated by measuring the levels of

select cells with lower levels of GFP expression (GFPlow) [Figure 4.1B]. Adherent cancer cell
lines were bulk sorted, while both bulk and single sorting was attempted for the suspension
cell lines. The sorted cells were then cultured and expanded in appropriate culture media,
thus obtaining artificial APCs expressing the mbOKT3-ScFv construct. It was possible to

avoiding cell growth. OKT3-ScFv+ PD-L1+, and OKT3-ScFv+PD-L2+ cells were more

5
Discussion and future
perspectives
This thesis aimed to generate a set of chronic stimulation and co-inhibitory assays that
could recapitulate T cell exhaustion in vitro using artificial APCs. The preceding results
provide an initial assessment of the potential of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cell lines in exhaustion
assays. These assays would be relevant for studying metabolic and transcriptional pathways
that mediate T cell dysfunction in a cancer. The key achievements and findings can be
summarized as follows: (1) successful generation of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells, (2) successful
generation of FM3 and K562 cell lines bearing co-stimulatory ligands, (3) OKT3-ScFv+
cancer cells can potently activate T cells, (4) chronic stimulation with OKT3-ScFv+ K562 and
FM3 induce dissimilar phenotypes in T cells, (5) OKT3-ScFv+PD-L1+ and OKT3-ScFv+PD-
L2+ FM3 induce an attenuated T cell phenotype compared to their parental OKT3-ScFv+ cell
line.
These findings will be examined and discussed regarding their significance and poten-
tial implications, which include proposals for additional research based on the limitations of
the current project.
5.1 Successful generation of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells,
with or without co-inhibitory ligands
Artificial APCs are a common mean in research for inducing T cell activation, such as
coating magnetic beads with αCD3/CD28 antibodies (Dunsford et al., 2020) or generating
cancer cell lines co-expressing MHC and ligands for co-stimulatory molecules (Maus et
al., 2022). Butler et al. developed a K562 like the one developed in this thesis. This cell
line was transduced to express the heavy and light chains of OKT3, CD80 and CD83 on
the cell membrane (Butler et al., 2012). On the other side, generation of cancer cell lines
53
==PAGE:68==
expressing co-inhibitory ligands has been a useful tool to study receptor-ligand interactions
(Wang et al., 2018). However, up to date, no one has ever paired the expression of OKT3
with co-inhibitory ligands.
I successfully generated an array of cancer cells expressing mbOKT3-ScFv. This array
includes three adherent cell lines (FM3, FM93/2, HEK-293T) and two suspension cell lines
(T2, K562). Cells were transduced using lentivirus bearing the mbOKT3-ScFv construct and
GFP, under the control of the potent constitutive promoter EF-1α. Transduced cells were
FACS sorted and expanded in culture.
Aware of the high potency of OKT3 in activating T cells, we were worried that
excessive stimulation could overshadow signalling mediated by co-inhibitory receptor/ligands
engagement. For this reason, I aimed to sort cells that have a low number of integrations of
the construct. However, assessing the average number of integrated events in a cell population
can only be performed with deep sequencing (Kim et al., 2021). In addition, the number of
integration sites can vary between cells in the same culture. For this reason, I approximated
low levels of integration events by sorting cells that were GFPlow. Obtaining sustained but
lower levels of expression of the mbOKT3-ScFv construct could also have been done by
using a weaker promoter, such as the CMV promoter, in the lentiviral cassette (Persic et al.,
1997).
Further, we generated FM3 and K562 cells (OKT3-ScFv+ and WT) overexpressing
co-inhibitory ligands PD-L1, PD-L2, CD155, B7-H3, IGSF11, HVEM, CEACAM1 and
B7-H4. The expression efficiency ranged between 68% up to 99% for FM3 cancer cells.
However, it was not possible to determine the degree of expression of PD-L1, PD-L2 and
CD155 in the transduced cell lines due to technical issues. In addition, the coinhibitory
ligand expression efficiency of transduced K562 was not assessed. Further experiments and
analyses are therefore required to determine the expression efficiency in these populations.
Nonetheless, chronic stimulation with PD-L1 and PD-L2 transduced OKT3-ScFv+ FM3 cells
did lead to differences in the activation state of the T cells compared to their parental cell line.
This might be an indication that these cancer cells overexpress their respective co-inhibitory
ligand.

It was not possible to establish GAL-9+ and CEACAM5+ cell lines. This might be
due to poor lentiviral transduction efficiency. Another explanation for the missed sorting of
GAL-9+ cancer cells is that GAL-9 is normally secreted through a non-conventional secretory
pathway (Delacour et al., 2009). To assess whether the transduced cells are overexpressing
GAL-9, it would be useful to perform western blotting on cell lysates. In addition, sorting
could be performed using a surface affinity matrix that binds to the secreted protein (Manz et
al., 1995).
It is worth noticing that during the sorting of cancer cells expressing the co-inhibitory
ligands, a population of GFP- cells was identified in cells that were previously sorted for
mbOKT3-ScFv construct expression. These cells are possibly contaminating cells during
the first round of sorting. However, the fact that some negative populations exist also when
validating the FM3 cell lines suggests that the expression of mbOKT3-ScFv might be affected
by gene silencing (Chavez et al., 2023). To make sure that the cells expressing co-inhibitory
ligands are positive for them, it would be useful to sort the cells again.
Overall, the panel of cell lines that I generated offers a valuable tool for studying T
cell activation and possibly exhaustion. By originating from different tumor tissues, these
cancer cells offer a variety of features, such as differential expression of surface receptors
and ligands. In addition, these cell lines could be used as positive and negative controls in
other experiments.
5.2 OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells can potently re-activate T
cells
We hypothesised that stimulation with artificial APCs expressing the mbOKT3-ScFv
could activate T cells in a pMHC-independent manner. In fact, the interaction between
OKT3 and CD3 has shown in vitro to potently activate T cells by crosslinking TCRs. In
addition, it was further hypothesised that OKT3-ScFv+ artificial APC mediated stimulation
could offer an additional layer of complexity to current in vitro models by reproducing the
immunological synapse formed between T cells and cancer cells.
5.2
OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells can potently re-activate T cells
55
==PAGE:70==
To address the mitogenic potential of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells, an activation assay
was performed. OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells were cocultured overnight with T cells that had
been previously activated. Upon visual inspection, it was clear that T cells cocultured with
OKT3-ScFv+ adherent cells were activated due to the classical resetting. Flow cytometry
analysis demonstrated that all the cancer cells besides T2 were able to polyclonally re-activate
CD8+ T cells, as detected by the expression of different activation markers CD69, CD25,
CD137, PD1, and CD39. In particular, the elevated expression of CD69, which is an early
activation marker of T cells, suggests the recent activation. In contrast, T cells treated with
WT cancer cells or cytokines showed only some residual expression of CD25, which is a
late activation marker, from the first activation. Interestingly, CD8+ T cells stimulated with
cancer cells showed a marked upregulation of CD137 compared to Dynabeads stimulation,
with OKT3-ScFv+ FM93/2 inducing the highest levels of expression. CD137 expression is
quickly induced by TCR stimulation, and CD137 can be detected on the T cell surface a few
hours after priming (Vinay et al., 1998). On the membrane, CD137 physically associates
with Lck through its intracellular domain (Kim et al., 1993). Enhanced expression of CD137
might be induced by a more potent stimulation of the TCR by the mbOKT3-ScFv. This
increased stimulation suggests that OKT3-ScFv+ FM93/2, FM3 and K562 are establishing a
better IS with T cells compared to Dynabeads. An alternative explanation is that the different
cancer cell lines are expressing different levels of mbOKT3-ScFv on their surface, with the
OKT3-ScFv+ FM93/2 expressing the construct the most.
The hypothesis that OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells are generating a better IS than Dyn-
abeads? is supported by evaluations on the size of the mbOKT3-ScFv. It has been shown that
TCR triggering is critically dependent on the dimensions of its pMHC ligand (Choudhuri et
al., 2005). This has been proposed to exclude membrane proteins with a bulky and highly
glycosylated extracellular domain (Goyette et al., 2019). The optimal dimensions of the
intercellular space between T cells and APCs are ~4 Ig-like domains. This distance is natu-
rally generated when pMHC (2 Ig-like domains) interacts with TCRαβ (2 Ig-like domains)
(AlAghbar et al., 2022). The mbOKT3-ScFv has a single extracellular Ig-like domain, and
interacts with CD3, which has an extracellular region corresponding to 3 Ig-like domains.
This supports the theory that OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells offer a better replication of endoge-
nous pMHC-mediated TCR activation in the synapse. Further analyses are required to assess

the effective generation of the synapse, including in vivo imaging using a three-dimensional
collagen matrix (Dustin et al., 2010).
A drawback of OKT3-mediated stimulation is that OKT3 binds to the CD3-TCR
complex with substantially higher affinity compared to pMHC-TCR interactions. However,
it was found that even if some T cells can respond to even a single ligand, two ligands in
the interface produce a more sustained rise in calcium, and ten or more ligands produce a
maximal response and promote the formation of a stable synapse (Huppa et al., 2003). Thus,
if the OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells are expressing low levels of mbOKT3-ScFv, this should
not be an issue. To test this, it would be important to compare the Ca2+ influx typical for
strong pMHC interactions and T cells stimulated with OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells (Huppa et
al., 2003).
By microscope analysis, it was clear that OKT3-ScFv+ adherent cells were efficiently
killed by stimulated T cells. To address the cytotoxic potential of OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells,
it would be important to perform a flow cytometry or Incucyte based killing assay.
Altogether, these results suggest that stimulation of T cells with OKT3-ScFv+ cancer
cells is establishing a superior IS with T cells compared to Dynabeads, thus inducing a more
physiological activation of T cells. This shows the potential of using OKT3-ScFv+ cancer
cell lines for the activation and expansion of T cells in future experiments.
In contrast with the other cell lines, T2 did not activate T cells as efficiently. This might
be explained by a generally lower expression of mbOKT3-ScFv. This was highlighted during
the sorting, where lower levels of GFP expression were found. Lower levels of activation
might also be due to coinhibitory molecules expressed on the T2 cancer cell line, or cytokines
found in the media.

exhausted phenotype in T cells

expressing the mbOKT3-ScFv could induce the exhausted phenotype seen in vivo. Moreover,

In both experiments, T cells stimulated with cancer cells showed a clear increased
expression of TIGIT when compared to αCD3 stimulation. Vice versa, plate-bound αCD3
stimulation induced sustained expression of TIM3 and LAG3. This suggests that chronic
stimulation in the two different settings induces different metabolic changes that lead to
differential expression of co-inhibitory markers. TIGIT upregulation might be explained
by the abundance of its ligand, CD155, on the surface of both K562 and FM3. The high
expression of CD155 was confirmed by the staining of WT cancer cells and is also reported
in literature (Bottino et al., 2003). CD155 binds to both CD226 and TIGIT. CD226 Is a
costimulatory molecule of T cells and it was shown that co-stimulation via CD226 is required
for stimulatory effect of TIGIT blockade on CD8+ T cells (Ge et al., 2021). It is possible that
CD266 engagement with CD155 induces higher induction of TIGIT, which might compete
with CD226 for CD155 in a similar way to CTLA4 competition with CD28. The preferential
induction of TIGIT might have an effect on the expression of other exhaustion markers. RNA
sequencing analysis should be performed on T cells stimulated at the different conditions to
assess different the transcriptomic profile of these cells.
The expression levels of other markers, such as PD1, CD39 and CTLA4, was instead
not consistent between experiments. In addition, OKT3-ScFv+ K562 stimulation in the
second experiment showed significant levels of TIM3 expression, which was not seen
previously. This different pattern of expression could be explained by the different timing
of cell freezing during the experiments. In fact, T cells were frozen down at different time
points in the two experiments, with the first being done the day of the following restimulation
(2 days after the previous restimulation) and the second being done the day after (3 days

after the previous restimulation). This suggests that the different co-inhibitory receptors are
removed from the cell surface after only two days if the cell is not continuously stimulated.
Further experiments and optimization are therefore needed for using these cells in in vitro
co-inhibitory assays, to guarantee the reproducibility of the results.
Stimulation with OKT3-ScFv+ FM3 significantly induced TIM3 and TIGIT expression,
with the former being at a lower degree compared to OKT3-ScFv+ K562 stimulation. OKT3-

decreases TIM3 induction. Nonetheless, the abundance of triple and quadruple positives for
the different exhaustion markers in T cells stimulated with all the OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells
suggests that these cells more effectively induced an exhausted phenotype.
One striking feature of OKT3-ScFv+ K562 stimulation is its ability to induce a potent
CD3 internalization. This was seen in both experiments, in contrast with αCD3 stimulation.
The latter was, in fact, seen in the first, but not in the second. In addition, sustained CD3
internalization was seen also for CD4+ T cells stimulated with OKT3-ScFv+ K562. Since the
freezing times were different between experiments, this suggests that CD3 internalization
mediated by OKT3-ScFv+ K562 stimulation is more potent than all the other means of
stimulation. CD3 internalization is one of the shut-down mechanisms protecting from
excessive stimulation (Valitutti et al., 1997). It might be that OKT3-ScFv+ K562 cells, being
in suspension, are interacting with T cells in a different way compared to plate-bound αCD3
and to adherent OKT3-ScFv+ FM3 cells, maybe creating more synapses with T cells.
There are two clear drawbacks of these two experiments. First, the panel used for
evaluating exhaustion was not optimal. In fact, some of the fluorochromes had a clear overlap
in their emission spectra. Thus, the results might have been screwed for some of the receptors.
In future experiments, I would design a panel using fluorochromes with a more balanced
spread across the light spectrum. In addition, I would use brighter antibodies for markers like
PD1, which expression was found to be not very high. A second drawback is the challenges,
for now, to separate T cells from the cancer cells in culture. This might be a limitation in
future experiments where the features of these T cells need to be assessed.

Despite not fully consistent results, the upregulation of the different exhaustion markers
induced by OKT3-ScFv+ cells and the presence of multiple double, triple and quadruple
positives highlights the potential of using these cancer cell lines to efficiently exhaust T
cells for CRISPR screens. This would possibly allow the identification of novel metabolic
pathways and genetic features of exhausted T cells.
However, prior to CRISPR screens, numerous other assays are needed to fully validate
this method as a means to obtain exhausted T cells with a phenotype similar to the one that is
found in vivo.
First, I did not stain for TOX. Due to the importance of TOX for defining the exhausted
state of T cells in cancer (Belk et al., 2022), it is critical to perform an intracellular stain
for this marker. In addition, I did not perform any functional assay on these exhausted T
cells. Exhausted T cells show decreased ability to produce proinflammatory cytokines such
as IL-2, TNFα and IFNγ. A critical experiment is therefore an intracellular staining for
these cytokines by blocking their release from the Golgi apparatus (Horton et al., 2007).
An alternative assay would be performing an ELISA on the supernatant to measure the
abundance of proinflammatory cytokines. Also, it is key to assess the cytotoxic potential
of the exhausted T cells, it would be useful to perform a flow cytometry or Incucyte based
killing assay and to execute an intracellular staining for GrzB and PFN.
Finally, it is critical to assess the proliferative capacity of T cells chronically stimulated
by OKT3-ScFv+ cells. During the two exhaustion experiments, I did not track the cell count
of T cells. This could be achieved by performing a proliferation assay. In this assay, T
cells are labelled with tracking dyes that are retained long-term within the cell (Tario et al.,
2018).

6
Conclusions
Despite significant progress in the field of cancer immunotherapy, challenges remain
in fully overcoming T cell exhaustion. The identification of novel inhibitory pathways and
the development of strategies to selectively target exhausted T cells while preserving normal
T cell function are areas of active research.
One of the most recent advances to interrogate immunity are CRISPR screens, a high-
throughput genetic screening technique that allows studying the effects of gene perturbations
on cellular phenotypes (Shi et al., 2022). Due to the low efficiency of inducing T cells
exhaustion in vivo, and oversimplification of the current in vitro models, alternative means of
recapitulating the in vivo phenotype are crucial.
This thesis aimed to generate a set of chronic stimulation and co-inhibitory assays that
could recapitulate T cell exhaustion in vitro using artificial APCs. I generated a diverse array
of cancer cells expressing the mbOKT3-ScFv and tested them for their potential to induce an
exhausted phenotype in vitro.
OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells induced a different phenotype compared to the conventional
αCD3 stimulation, and activation experiments suggest that these cancer cells activate T cells
more efficiently by forming a more physiological synapse. However, further experiments are
needed to fully validate this model, including functional and proliferative assays.
This initial assessment suggests that OKT3-ScFv+ cancer cells represent a valuable
tool for CRISPR screens focused on co-inhibitory pathways. This technology would allow
the identification of novel genetic regulators involved in T cell exhaustion.
