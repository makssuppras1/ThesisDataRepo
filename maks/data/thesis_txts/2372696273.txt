Abstract  
This study investigates the effect of solid-phase sulphation on the adhesion strength of model 
fly ash deposits. Experiments were performed in a custom designed oven, where multiple 
artificial deposits were prepared on superheater steel tubes, and sulphated at temperatures 
up to 800 °C, in an SO2 rich atmosphere. The steel tube was cooled using air, incorporating 
temperature gradients across the ash deposit layer and thereby, inducing superior replication 
of boiler conditions. After sintering, it was possible to remove the deposits using an electrically 
controlled arm, thereby measuring the corresponding adhesion strength. In addition, the 
effect of sintering duration, SO2 concentration, and water-vapor and deposit composition was 
investigated. The deposit composition and morphology was studied with the help of SEM/EDS. 
The study revealed that the adhesion strength might increase with increasing sulphation time, 
while the SO2-concentration seemed to have no significant effect on adhesion strength. 
SEM/EDS measurements also showed that sulphation might not be the primary cause for 
increase in adhesion strength. However, corrosion at the deposit-tube interface may lead to 
the formation of partially molten layer, resulting in increased adhesion strength. 
The formation of a molten eutectic mixture at the top of the deposit was observed. It could be 
hypothesized that the molten layer was sulphated, which was later on deformed/reduced due 
to the elevated temperature at the top of the deposit. SEM analysis revealed that the eutectic 
mixture at the top of the deposit formed a dense molten surface that hinders further 
sulphation from taking place. Non-molten layers were also detected in these areas, however 
the rate of sulphation was below a detectable limit. Water-vapor and addition of CaO to the 
model fly ash deposit did not have a significant effect on the adhesion strength. It was also 
seen that the adhesion strength data followed a log-normal distribution.

1 Introduction 
Coal has typically been used as the fuel for boilers in power plants. However, due to environmental 
concerns aiming to reduce net CO2 emissions, combustion of biomass in boilers is gaining increased 
attention over the past few decades. Denmark is one of the pioneers in this direction, with more than 10 
biomass fired power plants currently operational in the country. However, the use of biomass, especially 
straw, constitutes a serious technical challenge due to the presence of large amounts of alkali metals and 
chlorine in the resulting fly ash (Philipp Fritsch 2016)(Mayer 2014).  
Ash deposits formed during fuel thermal conversion and located on furnace walls and on convective pass 
tubes, may seriously inhibit the transfer of heat to the working fluid and hence reduce the overall process 
efficiency. Combustion of biomass causes formation of large quantities of troublesome ash deposits which 
contain significant concentrations of alkali, and earth-alkali metals. Furthermore, ash deposit may cause 
severe corrosion of boiler surfaces (Laxminarayan et al. 2016). 
In some Danish pulverised fuel boilers, wood firing is done together with injection of coal fly ash. This can 
ideally fix the alkali in alumina silicates that have high melting temperature and normally only forms loose 
deposit that are difficult to remove. However, recent studies by the company DONG energy have shown 
that some hard deposits in pulverised fuel (PF) wood boilers that are rich in K, Ca and S are formed. A 
possible reason for the formation of the hard deposits is that KCl, CaO and K2CO3 present in the deposit are 
sulphated by sulphur from the flue gas. 
This study aims to understand the effect of sulphation on ash deposits. Experiments were performed in an 
experimental setup, where multiple artificial deposits will be prepared on superheater steel tubes, and 
allowed to sulphate at temperatures up to 800 °C, in an SO2 rich atmosphere. The steel tube was cooled 
using air, incorporating temperature gradients across the ash deposit layer and thereby, inducing superior 
replication of boiler conditions. After sintering, it was possible to remove the deposits using an electrically 
controlled arm, thereby measuring the corresponding adhesion strength.  
In addition, the effect of gas phase temperature, steel surface temperature, sintering duration and SO2 
concentration was investigated. The deposit composition and morphology was studied with the help of 
SEM/EDS, analysis.

2 Literature review  
 
 Firing Option 
2.1
In Denmark, the most established firing option for biomass combustion are grate firing and suspension 
firing, Figure 1. In grate-fired boilers, the fuel is combusted in the bottom part of the furnace chamber on a 
moving bed. Suspension-fired systems, primary combustion air is mixed with milled biomass powders to 
form a suspension, in which the combustion occurs (Hansen et al. 2012). Originally used for coal 
combustion, have also been considered for biomass combustion. Combustion of straw and/or wood in 
suspension-fired units is an attractive option because of the often high electrical efficiency of these plants 
(46–48%), compared to traditional grate-fired systems (25–30%) (Bashir et al. 2012). However, according to 
(Bashir et al. 2012) when biomass is used in suspension-fired boilers, the deposit flux onto the superheater 
tubes is expected to increase due to the high temperature.

boiler. The locations of the probe measuring positions  (P1, P2) at AMV2 (a) and probe position (P) at AVV2 (b) (Bashir et al. 2012). 
 
 Boiler tower 
2.2
The aim of the boiler is to optimize the thermal efficiency and minimize the economic cost by arranging the 
heat transfer surfaces and the equipment for fuel burning.   
==PAGE:9==

The primary elements of a boiler are the heat transfer surfaces and in modern boilers, these surfaces are 
furnaces, evaporators, superheathers, economizers, and air pre-heathers. The surfaces transfer the heat 
from the flue gases to the water/steam circulation by convection. When flue gases are exiting the boiler it 
will be cooled down to a limit that is close to the dew point temperature. There are many differences in 
position and type of heat exchangers, but Figure 2 shows a simple diagram of a typical boiler system. 
 
 
Figure 2 - Physical location of heat transfer surfaces in a boiler (Teir & Jokivuori 2002). 
 
Modern boilers are divided into a radiation and a convectional area, with respect to the predominant heat 
transfer mechanism in the concerned area. In overall, the major part of heat is transferred through 
radiation. That comes directly from the flame in the radiation area. The individual heat transfer units have 
different assignments to perform (Teir & Jokivuori 2002) . In typical industrial combustion of biomass is the 
process temperature executed at different temperatures e.g. in grate fired boilers is the temperature 
between 1000 – 1200 °C, while suspension fired boilers operates up to 1600°C (Hansen et al. 2012). Once 
the combustion is done, the hot flue gases are cooled down by various heat exchanger configurations. 
 
- 
Evaporator: In this unit, saturated stream is generated by vaporizing water at its saturation 
temperature. This section consumes most of the energy hence the evaporator is placed in the 
radiant section where most of the heat transfer occurs (Teir & Jokivuori 2002). 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
10 
 
- 
Superheater: A superheater section is employed to overheat the saturated steam. The result is a 
steam flow with higher energy production efficiency and no moisture content + no condensate in 
the steam pipelines(Teir & Jokivuori 2002). The flue gas temperature in this region in between 600 - 1050 
°C, while the steam temperature is between 380-600 °C (Zbogar et al. 2009). Normally, the surface 
temperature of the tube is between 450-600°C (Zbogar et al. 2009).     
 
- 
Economizer: In this section is the feedwater heated up to saturation temperature and the flue gas 
is additionally cooled before it leaves the superheater region; hence the boiler efficiency is 
increased (Teir & Jokivuori 2002). The flue gas temperature in this position is between 200-400°C (Zbogar 
et al. 2009).  
 
- 
Air preheater:  In this unit the combustion air is preheated and the flue gas is cooled down to the 
preferred level. This is the last heat transfer surface step before the flue gas exits the boiler tower 
(Teir & Jokivuori 2002)

 Sequence of combustion  
2.4
Combustion of coal and biomass as solid fuels occurs in several stages in a grate fired boiler, which is shown 
in Figure 4(Venturini 2010). Initially the biomass enters the furnace from a feeder, forming the fuel bed, 
while primary combustion air flows from the under the grate. The temperature of the biomass increases as 
a result of heat transfers from the overbed flames and the hot furnace walls and lead to loss of its moisture 
content (Venturini 2010). Next, volatile components and combustible are released from biomass structure 
at higher temperatures (Venturini 2010) (Loo et al. 2002). This process occurs in the absence of oxygen 
which is defined as pyrolysis (Loo et al. 2002). Dried fuel starts devolatilizing and the rate of devolatilization 
is dependent on the bed temperature. Typical volatile compounds that are realised in the fuel bed under 
devolatilization are; i.e. CO, CO2, H2, CH4, etc., that are further transformed into char (Loo et al. 2002).

2.4.2 Formation of fly ash  
During combustion of solid inorganic fuel, incombustible materials in the fuel is transformed into 
intermedia ash species (Wu, H. 2013). Those are observed in the solid, liquid and, gas phases with different 
composition and size which affects the overall property of the ash deposit.  
Residual ash formation during suspension-firing of biomass can be divided into fly ash and bottom ash. The 
bottom ash is the remains found at the bottom of the furnace, while the fly ash enters down through the 
flue gas channel, where it may deposit onto the heat transfer surfaces (Zbogar et al. 2009)(Frandsen 2011).          
The ratio of fly ash and bottom ash are mainly dependent on the design of applied furnace, and moreover 
the size and density of residual ash particles. In grate-firing systems, most of the residual ash (~70 −98%) 
ends up as bottom ash products (Frandsen 2011). However, in PF-fired boilers the majority of residual ash, 
is fly ash (~80 −95%) (Frandsen 2011). When fly ash is directed down through the flue gas channel it may 
deposit onto heat transfer surfaces. The flue gas is exposed to cooling when it passes through the boiler. As 
a result, supersaturation may take place where e.g. K-species may condense either homogeneously, as 
aerosols (submicron particles), or heterogeneously on heat transfer surfaces or fly ash particles. Fly ashes 
formed during biomass can be divided into two main fractions, aerosols and coarse fly ashes. 
As shown in Figure 7, in grate-fired boiler the particle size of fly ash emissions from biomass are generally 
divided into two fractions, fly ashes (Particle size > 1 𝜇𝑚) and aerosols (Particle size < 1 𝜇𝑚) (Obernberger 
et al. 2003a). Fly ashes consist of small ash species entrained from fuel bed with the flue gas. In addition, 
parameters like, size of the fuel particles formation of fly ash depends, load of the combustion unit or 
supply with combustion air firing-option also contribute to the formation if fly ash (Frandsen 2011). While 
aerosols are colloidal suspension of particles that are mainly influenced by the chemical composition of the 
fuel (Shao et al. 2012).

In addition, some volatile particles also tend to condense on residual fly ash. The high amount of K and Na 
(particularly K) through condensation, nucleation, and reactions result in a variety of ash-related problems 
like agglomeration, silicate melt-induced slagging (ash fusion), and alkali-induced slagging (Niu et al. 
2016b). 
 
 Ash Deposit 
2.5
2.5.1  General 
During biomass combustion layers of materials, in terms of ash, are collected on the surface of heat 
transfer units like the furnace walls and on convective pass tubes (Khan et al. 2009). This may inhibit the 
heat transfer to the working fluid and the overall efficiency of the process (Zbogar et al. 2009).  
The deposits can be distinguished roughly into two types, slugging and fouling. The physical characteristics 
of the formed deposit depend on factors like local environment (gas flow velocity, gas flow pattern, and 
temperature) and the temperature of the substrate surface (Zbogar et al. 2009). According to (Miles et al. 
1996) critical slagging and fouling problems are observed in conventional boilers because of the content of 
alkali materials in annual biomass fuels. 
 
2.5.2 Slagging deposits 
This type of deposit is usually observed within the furnace, heat transfer arears that are directly exposed to 
radiation from flames which refers to the hottest part of the boiler (i.e. furnace walls), and in some cases 
also in superheaters (Zbogar et al. 2009). This type of deposit has powdery inner layer that is covered by 
molten ash layer (Frandsen 2011). In a study made by (Miles et al. 1996) they investigated the deposit 
formation of biomass combustion in grate fired boilers. They observed that the present deposits on the 
furnace walls and on the grate were hard, fuse, and glassy. 
During biomass combustion, the formation of alkali particles such as alkali sulphates and chlorides cause 
alkali-induced slagging. The mechanisms for formation of alkali-induced slagging, silicate melt-induced 
slagging, agglomeration, and fly ash are illustrated in Figure 9. Based on studies conducted by (Niu et al. 
2010) potassium chloride (KCl) is considered to be the most stable gas-phase alkali-metal containing 
substance in the entire combustion process (Shao et al. 2012) and the dominant substance that influences 
biomass slagging (Niu et al. 2016b).

2.5.3 Fouling deposits 
The fouling process refers to deposits arears that are not directly exposed to thermal radiation from flames. 
This is observed for more closely spaced tubes in the convection sections of a furnace and other technical 
installations where heat exchange takes place. The contamination from this process (combustion residues 
or biological fouling and chemical precipitations from cooling media such as heat exchangers) leads to 
reduction of plant performance (Skrifvars et al. 2005). 
Fouling may result from the accumulation of ash by condensation with subsequent sintering of the boiler 
heating surfaces. It may also be looser deposits, which is formed by impaction of solid ash particle on heat 
exchange surfaces. However, if these ashes deposits are heated above the melting point of some of the 
constituents, they may become hard and difficult to remove. There are two types of fouling deposit (Zbogar 
et al. 2009): 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18 
 
 
Formation during high temperature, where it occurs in the area of high enough temperature in 
order to destabilize the sulphated phase and consist of fly ashes. In coal fired boilers, high 
temperature fouling occurs above approximately 1050 °C (Benson SA, Jones ML 1993)(Zbogar et al. 
2009).  
 
As mentioned earlier the composition of ash deposits from biomass combustion is strongly dependent 
on the fuel composition and the firing technique. Likewise the structure and composition varies in 
fresh and mature deposits, as well as for upstream-downstream position of the tube deposit (Hansen 
et al. 2012) and the tube position in the flue gas channel (Sengeløv et al. 2013). 
 
 Mechanism and chemistry of ash deposition  
2.6
Major mechanisms of ash deposition are defined as condensation, thermophoresis, inertial impaction, 
diffusion and chemical reaction. 
 
2.6.1 Condensation of gaseous species 
This mechanism is based on liquefying of vapors and collection on the surface which is colder than 
surroundings gas phase(Baxter 1993). As the flus gas is getting colder with increasing distances to the 
combustion zone, k-species in ash particle passing thorough flue gas channel may condense on ash particle 
and deposit on the heat exchanger which are colder than surroundings gas phase (Frandsen 2011). 
Condensed Potassium species has low smelting point and that will lead to make particle and tube surface 
stickier. This is why, there is big chance that fly ash with potassium content to attach to heat transfer

aerosols starting, by condensation of inorganic vapors on exposed surfaces cooler than flue gas in the 
boiler, the deposit are formed. Following chemical reaction within deposit and sintering will lead to further 
impact of building up the deposit. The ash deposit sulphation is an example where deposit gets partially 
sulphated by SO2 present.

2.6.3 Thermophoresis  
Thermophoresis is a transport mechanism observed in mixtures of mobile particles in which the different 
particles show different responses due to the force of a temperature gradient (Zbogar et al. 2009) (Shao et 
al. 2012). In this phenomenon a particle suspended in a fluid with a temperature gradient interacts with the 
fluid molecules. The average kinetic energies of the fluid molecules are higher on the hot area compared to 
the molecules, the cold area as the velocity, is higher, than those on the cold fluid area (Shao et al. 
2012)(Zbogar et al. 2009). The momentum of high energy molecules that are colliding on the hot area of 
the particle is higher than those on the cold area; this contributes to a net force on the particle (Zbogar et 
al. 2009). The net force will act in the opposite direction of the temperature gradient. The formation of 
deposits through thermophoresis and condensation will enclose the entire circumference boundary of the 
tube (Baxter 1993).

2.6.4 Diffusion 
Diffusion is a mechanism based on the transport of molecules from a region of high concentration to a 
region of low concentration. The diffusion phenomenon appears due to the difference in concentration of 
the flue gases which is caused by the difference in temperature between the deposit and tube surface. 
Once the species are transported to the surface through the tube boundary layer, vapors can diffuse onto 
the surface of a previously formed deposit, condense on the heat transfer surface, or inside the porous 
deposit. The condensation results in a decrease of the porosity of the deposit and increase the bonding 
strength of the deposit so the deposit is harder to remove. Condensation can be categorized as; 
homogeneous and heterogeneous. Homogeneous condensation appears when the local vapor pressure is 
high( Zbogar et al. 2009).  
Once there is not enough pressure, vapors heterogeneously condensed onto a surface which is cooler than 
the local glue gas. Heterogeneous condensation is a phase transformation by which vapors condense on a 
surface which is cooler than the local flue gas. Heterogeneous condensation can be present also in the 
boundary layer, where vapors are condensed on the incoming fly ash particles (Zbogar et al. 2009).

 Sintering of ash deposit  
2.7
Sintering occurs during the deposit consolidation, where the physical and chemical characteristics of a 
deposit are changing during the process of deposit initiation, growth and maturation. In general sintering is 
described as a thermal process in which the attachment between two particles increases with elevating 
temperature.  Sintering leads to a build-up of deposit strength (Piroozmand et al. 1998)and difficult in to 
remove (Zbogar et al. 2009). Unsintered deposits contain ash particles that appear isolated from their 
neighbours, or with only weak particle-to-particle interaction. When the deposit is sintered, the solid phase 
is interconnected and forms a large plate-like feature near the outside surface of the deposit. 
 
2.7.1 Liquid State Sintering 
Liquid phase sintering is commonly used for materials that are difficult to sinter. The material in this 
sintering process is brought into the form of melt. The amount of melt that is formed controls the neck 
formation rate between the particles. In liquid state sintering involves a highly viscous liquid the process is 
defined as viscous flow sintering (Zbogar et al. 2009). The neck formation is controlled by time and the 
viscosity of the glassy phase, which is a function of chemical composition and temperature. An illustration 
of the mentioned neck formation is given below.

2.8.4 Thermal Shock 
Thermal stress occurs due to an uneven thermal expansion (thermal mismatch) between the deposit and 
the tube metal, or between different deposit layers. The thermal expansion is directly influenced by the 
thermal expansion coefficient, a [m/mK], which is defined as the relative increase in the length of a sample, 
per unit increase in temperature. The thermal expansion coefficient determines if the materials are 
thermally compatible. Also, sudden heating/cooling may cause thermal shock of the material. The sudden 
shrinking of a deposit caused e.g. by a sudden cooling, decreases the adhesion force between a deposit and 
a tube. Thermal stresses may lead to deposit crack, as shown in Figure 21 (Frandsen 2011)(Zbogar et al. 
2009).

2.8.5 Brittle break-up 
Brittle failure originates and emerges from the deposit surface when pieces of deposit break up. This 
process is more usable for brittle, weak deposit. The break of the deposit occurs when a jet hits a brittle 
deposit, which fragments the deposit into pieces (Frandsen 2011). A sootblower jet impact can cause high 
internal stresses in the deposit. For weak, brittle deposits this leads to a deposit break-up, Figure 22a. 
 
2.8.6  Debonding  
Debonding seen in Figure 22a, occurs when, the connection between tube and deposit breaks. According to 
investigation of Kraft recovery boiler (Kaliazine et al. 1997) due to the flue gases temperature, hard 
deposits in the superheater area are formed and and cannot be broken and removed by the brittle break-
up mechanism as seen in Figure 22b. They are usually dense and may have a melted surface due to the high 
temperatures of the deposit surface and the flue gas (Zbogar et al. 2009). Debonding is the predominant 
shedding process when the deposit cohesive strength is larger than the tube deposit adhesion. When a 
Hard deposit is formed weakly on the tube-surface then shedding can occur with a weak jet.

 Deposit adhesion 
2.9
Fireside deposit accumulation on tube surfaces in boilers can significantly reduce heat transfer efficiency, 
causing high flue gas temperatures and accelerate plugging of flue gas channels. Thus, Effective deposit 
removal is very important for boiler control and optimization. The removal of fireside deposits are 
complicated in boilers due to the interaction between the solid deposit mechanics and the fluid mechanics 
of the soot-blower jet. 
Study shows that deposit removal mechanisms with soot-blowing have some resistance that depends 
primarily on deposit mechanical strength of adhesion to the tubes (Zbogar et al. 2009). The deposit 
strength can be explained by two processes such as sintering of solid ash particles and Solidification of 
molten or partially molten phases.  
 
2.9.1 Deposit strength 
One of the most crucial factors influencing the process of deposit removals is the deposit strength. That is 
dependent on the deposit composition, porosity, temperature distribution inside the deposit and the 
interfaces of deposit and tube surface (Zbogar et al. 2009). In addition, the tensile strength is also an 
important property that needs to be considered to remove of a deposit as the materials regularly break at 
its weakest point. The mechanical tensile strength of the deposit is an important property factor in 
shedding processes and with different deposition this property will vary differently in the boiler depending 
on the location. The removal of the debonding effect at the interface between the deposit and tube surface 
is controlled by the adhesion strength, which is the maximum tensile stress possible at the interface that is 
required to detach the adhesive perpendicular to the substrate (Zbogar et al. 2009). 
According to Ruiz V.(Ruiz Ruiz 2014) the porosity of biomass deposit sample decreases with increase in 
temperature and the deposit bend strength starts to increase until it reach a maximum for a minimum 
porosity. Suddenly the bend strength starts to decrease at temperatures above the first melting point and 
ends approximately at zero for 600°C. The low bend strength is related to liquid formation (i.e. the deposit 
acts like a lubricant between dust particles an allowing the deposit to deform plastically). 
(Piroozmand et al. 1998) studied the adhesion strength of recovery boiler deposits at different 
temperatures by dripping precipitator dust into steel rings with different diameters onto a steel plate. The 
obtained results from the study are given in Figure 24. The Figure shows that the adhesion strength is a 
function of the substrate temperature. The strength is close to zero for T < 300°C, and then it increases to a 
maximum at 415°C before it decreases to a fixed value because of liquid phase formation. Clearly, the 
adhesion strength and tensile strength peaks at different temperatures.

3.3.1 Model fly ash 
Studies have revealed that the innermost layer of deposit is rich in K and Cl (give reference to Peter’s SEM

would also quantitatively determined the amount of K2SO4 formed, and hence the degree of sulphation. In

obtained from biomass fired boilers were used. CaO (Sigma Aldrich) was added to test the effect of

3.3.1.1 Superheater tubes 
It can be concluded from the observations of Ruiz et al. (victor) that the metal surface of the super-heater 
tubes should be pre-oxidised to provide superior replication of boiler tubes. The aim of this treatment is to 
improve the surface adhesion of the tubes by thermal removal of some chemical species (i.e. impurities) 
and obtain a homogenous oxide layer. Pre-oxidization of the steel tubes is performed in an oven, at 600 ℃ 
for 24 hours. Pre-oxidation also ensures that the conditions of in real boilers are well simulated, since super 
heater tubes are oxidised to a certain undetermined degree when exposed to fly ash and flue gases. TGA

Results and discussion  
Studies performed in full scale boilers indicate that the heavily sintered deposits in boilers, which are 
difficult to remove, are rich in sulphur (Sander 1997). For forming sulphur rich deposits, sulphation can 
occur either in gas phase prior to deposition, or in solid-phase after deposition. Other studies have 
indicated that sulphation of ash deposits leads to an increase in adhesion strength (Laxminarayan et al. 
2016). However, the deposits were pre-mixed in specified composition in that study, simulating gas-phase 
sulphation.  
This study focusses on solid-phase sulphation of ash species, and its effect on the adhesion strength of 
deposits. The effect of sulphation time, SO2-concentration and deposit composition on deposit adhesion 
strength has been investigated.

6 
Conclusion  
The aim of this study was to investigate the effect of sulphation on the adhesion strength of biomass ash 
deposits. Moreover, the effect of sulphation time, SO2-concentration and composition on the adhesion 
strength was studied. 
  A literature study was conducted in order to gather the necessary information on biomass combustion, as 
well as different parameters that effect ash deposits formation/shedding, and the influence of solid phase 
sulphation on the adhesion strength of deposits. Previous investigations understanding the influence of 
solid-phase sulphation on adhesion strength could not be identified. Therefore, experiments in this study 
were oriented towards finding any possible evidence of solid-phase sulphation on the deposits. 
   The experimental setup work was used to performed the tensile adhesion strength of solid deposits on 
superheater tubes under the influence of a temperature gradient across the deposit. In addition, the setup 
was also used to understand the effect of online sulphation of biomass ash deposits on deposit sintering 
and adhesion strength. 
Eight artificial deposits was made and placed on a pre-oxidized superheater tube and sintered in an oven at 
temperatures up to 800°C. The superheater tube was cooled by an air-cooled probe, upto temperatures of 
550°C. 
The experimental results from the setup shows that the SO2-concentration has no significant effect on 
adhesion strength in the investigated range of 0 to 600 ppm within 24 hours. However, sulphation time 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
56 
 
exhibited significant effect on adhesion strength up to 24 hours. An increase in the adhesion strength as 
function of sulphation time was observed. 
   Spectrographic evidence obtained from the SEM/EDS analysis revealed that the adhesion mechanism can 
be divided into six stages. Initially, for increasing temperature, corrosion propagates at the interface. Most 
of the corrosion products contribute to a complex eutectic mixture with components present in the 
deposit. This results in a lower eutectic temperature at the interface than the outer layers of the deposit. 
The partial molten layer triggers high surface wetting and adsorption which leads to increased surface 
adhesion as function of time.  
Due to a higher temperature at the outer layer of the deposit, when compared to the inner layer, 
sulphation occurs at a faster rate in the outer region of the deposit. As a result, a eutectic mixture is formed 
at the outer layer of the deposit where the deposit becomes molten and flows off or vaporizes. Non-molten 
layers are also detected in some regions of the deposit, where the rate of sulphation was below detectable 
limits. It was also noticed that the eutectic mixture at the top of the deposit formed a dense molten 
surface, which was seen to hinder the further sulphation of the samples. 
In addition, surface characterization from SEM/EDS also indicated that although sulphation occurs in some 
regions of the deposit, it cannot be concluded that sulphation is the primary cause of an increase in 
adhesion strength. However, it is likely that corrosion occurring at the deposit-tube interface leads to 
formation of partially molten layer, resulting in increased adhesion strength. The effect of water vapor on 
adhesion strength was studied. The results revealed that water-vapor did not seem to have any 
considerable influence on an increase on adhesion strength.  
Experiments with a mixture of CaO (33 wt. %) + KCl (67 wt.%) is used to form deposits showed that a 
combination of the two materials did not have any significantly effect on the adhesion strength. 
Furthermore, the study showed the adhesion strength follows a log-normal distribution.

8.1.1  Oven 
The oven comprises of a heating chamber of inner dimensions 200mmx200mmx400mm (WxHxD), heated 
using 10 Kanthal rods which can be heated upto a temperature of 1500ᵒC. The oven can be heated upto a 
maximum temperature of 1372ᵒC. However, for the current experiments the temperature is varied 
between 600-800ᵒC. The inner chamber is surrounded by SiC plates, which separate the heating elements 
from the oven chamber where the experiments are conducted. This ensures that the heating elements are 
protected from the volatile components of the salt deposits, which could condense on and corrode the 
kanthal rods. 
Table A1 - Name plate reading 
Manufacturer:  Entech A/s 
Power: (32 amps) 
Maximum Temperature:  1372oC 
 
The oven chamber is enclosed in a metallic casing. Wiring and supplementary mechanical components are 
located in a chamber in the right wall of the oven. The oven door is located in the front and contains a 
window of diameter 50m. The oven has a suction port for cooling the outer shell of the oven, and it is 
located on the top right corner of the oven. The suction port also serves towards removing gases (e.g. SO2) 
out of the oven. 
The oven also has one ¼ inch hole in the back wall of the oven that is used for SO2 supply. In addition, it 
consists of a slit of dimensions 10mmx 240mm on the top of the outer wall which will be used for tensile 
strength measurements. There are two holes on the top of the inner chamber housing the heating 
elements-one on each side of the heating chamber-for supply of air. This ensures that a convective heat 
transfer that further facilitates the reduction in the temperature gradient across the chamber and also 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
59 
 
ensures that the volatile components are removed, thus preventing their deposition onto to the heating 
elements. There are also ¼ inch holes on the top of the tube for removing the exhaust gases via the suction 
port. The Exhaust gases are guided using pipes, which pass through the cooling plate as explained in section  
11.1.2. 
Temperature in the in the oven is controlled using a Eurotherm Dual loop controller. It uses the 
temperature data from the thermocouple at the (S type), at the center of the back wall, that protrudes 40 
mm into the oven chamber. It also uses the temperature measured by thermocouples near the heating 
elements. These two temperatures are simultaneously measured and tabulated to ensure that the 
temperature gradient across the oven remains at a minimum and to ensure that the heating elements are 
not overheated.  The oven has a maximum current capacity of 32 amps. However, for safety reasons it is 
run at 70% of the maximum. The Temperature can be set manually, on the load box that houses the 
controller as well. However the controller is connected to the laptop via a USB-COM port and accessed via 
the software itools. 
Besides setting the limit for the current supplied to the oven, there are other features installed to ensure, a 
foolproof safety system. The Temperature of the outer casing, which houses the wiring for the oven and 
the cooling plate, that protects the oven top chamber and the strength measuring device are constantly 
monitored. A maximum temperature of 200˚ and 130˚C are respectively set to prevent damage to these 
components. When the temperature exceeds these limits, a trip switch automatically shuts off the oven 
heating. There is also a trip switch installed that ensures that oven heating is turned off if oven door is not 
properly sealed. This prevents leakage of harmful gases. 
 
8.1.2  Cooling probe 
A double-annular air cooled tube, is used to cool the sample tube.  The primary air flows in through the 
innermost channel and flows out through the outermost channel. The flow rate of the air is controlled 
through a solenoid valve (Burkert 2873) and a Eurotherm controller (3216).  The secondary air flows in 
through the outer annular channel and flows out through the inner annular channel. The flow rate of the 
air is controlled manually through a rotameter.  Temperature of the air-cooled probe is measured through 
4 thermocouples, present in grooves along the air-cooled probe. For monitoring the temperature gradients 
across the sample tube, 2 thermocouples are positioned at 12.5 mm from both the ends of the sample tube 
on the top side of the air-cooled probe. Furthermore, 2 thermocouples are positioned at the center-point 
of the sample tube, one on the top side (Thermocouple-3) and one on the bottom side (Thermocouple-4) of 
the air-cooled probe. The Eurotherm controller for controlling the primary air flow rate is connected to 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
60 
 
Thermocouple-3. The thermocouples are connected to the laptop via a USB-Data Acquisition Device 
(MCDAQ USB-TC). Data reading and logging can be accessed via the software Tracer-DAQ. 
 
8.1.3 Force Guage  
The force required to debond artificially prepared biomass ash deposits is measured using an electrical arm 
controlled by a linear actuator (Linak LA25, 50mm stroke length) and a load cell (S beam type, Nordic 
transducer TCTN-9363-50kg). A frame built holds these on a linear motion system (Hepco HGH30). The 
actuator is controlled via a control unit (EM-236), whereas the load cell is connected to the laptop via LDU 
68.2 amplifier. The load cell can be accessed via the software Haubac, installed on the laptop. 
The actuator can measure forces upto 2500N and pushes the piston at a maximum speed of upto 3.2mm/s 
at no load and 2.5 mm/s at full load conditions. The applied force is measured using the load cell. The signal 
is transferred to a laptop via a USB-sensor interface. The electrical signal of the load cell (mV/V) is 
transformed into applied force (N) using a calibration line. 
The control unit has a control knob which is moves the electrical arm forward, backwards or stops it. An 
interface unit is used to control the behavior of the linear actuator. Since the load cell can measure only 
weights up to 50kg, the overload setting of the interface unit, ensures that the force applied do not exceed 
this value. Since the overload setting limits the maximum current that can be drawn by the actuator, the 
speed of the control arm is also limited. This is again a necessary to ensure accurate and repro ducible 
results. It also prevents damage to the system. When the load exceeds this set value, the motor stops. The 
speed of the electrical arm is set to 23% of the maximum speed of the motor. Hence at zero load the 
electrical arm moves with 23% of the maximum speed of the motor.

 Appendix C: Initial Trouble shooting: 
8.3
The initial trouble shooting with the setup was with regard to the melting of the the rods used for 
measuring the tensile strength and the crystallization of an unknown element on the super heater tube and 
the cooling tube as shown in Figure 12. It was later found that the rods used where made of a molybdenum 
alloy. Molybdenum corrodes into molybdenum trioxide at temperatures around 750C. This oxide is 
vaporized over time and it recrystallizes on the cool surfaces as the oven is shut down. The prolonged and 
repetitive heating and cooling of the rods led to it melting after a few trials. These rods were replaced with 
rods of grade MA253, which were stable in the operating range of the oven. 
 
 
                
 
Figure C1 - The melted rods molybdenum trioxide. Made from an alloy of molybdenum. The crystals and thin white layer seen are 
deposits of molybdenum trioxide. 
 
The setup also had alignment issues with respect to the tensile measurement rods, when the measurement 
was taken, which led to destruction of a few samples. This was solved by using fabricating alignment plates 
and bolts which standardized the placement of the rods before each run of the experiment.

8.6
The two-sample t-tests are handy hypothesis tests typically used in statistics to determine if two samples 
have equal means. 
 
Assumptions 
1) Y1, …, Yn are n independent measures of group 1 
2) X1, …, Xm are m independent measures of group 2 
3) Y1, …, Yn are independent of X1, …, Xm 
4) The measures data of both groups are normal distributed, N(µ,σ2) 
5) Each group have equal but unknown variances of the distributions, σ2
X = σ2
Y = σ2

In case of all assumptions are satisfied and the null hypothesis is true, the test statistic t follows a student’s 
t distribution with (Nn+Nm-2) degrees of freedom. This is a result of the difference of two normal 
distributions with one unknown parameter each under the restriction, that both are equal. 
 
Area of rejection and p-value 
A significant level is chosen before the test is performed. For the test the maximum probability is chosen 
against the null hypothesis, although it is true. In this case the probability of deciding that the upper group 
is not the maximum, even though it is the true maximum.The rejection area is formed by chosen an 𝛼 of 
0.05. This is an interval of all realizations of the test statistic t, that includes 𝛼∙100% of the values, if H0 is 
true. If the calculated t is inside this interval means that the null hypothesis is assumed to be wrong. 
The p-values is very important as it is the probability of observing this data when the null hypothesis is true.  
A small p-value indicates, that it is very unlikely to get the observed data under H0. A p-value smaller than 𝛼 
corresponds to the test statistic being inside the rejection area. The p-value can also be interpreted as the 
probability, that we observe a bigger difference in means 𝑋̅and 𝑌̅ even though μY > μX.
