Summary
3D LiDAR technology gets a lot of attention nowadays, mostly because of the adequacy
of the obtained data for robotic applications and because of the predicted price drop in
the sensor cost. This thesis focuses on developing an outdoor mobile robot navigation
solution, with a focus on sensor fusion using a 3D LiDAR. The outdoor environment is
defined as the buildings at the Lyngby campus of the Technical University of Denmark
and a real robotic platform (iRobot ATRV-JR) is used to evaluate the developed solution.
The thesis argues, why using a 3D LiDAR would be a good choice among other possible
approaches. Then, a framework for achieving an outdoor navigation is explained. First,
it is decided to focus on detecting the planes of the building walls. A choice of the most
suitable method is described. Then, a sensor fusion algorithm, using the obtained wall
plane information, is developed and explained in detail.
After developing the algorithm, an offline testing framework is created in MATLAB. The
algorithm is adjusted until obtaining satisfying results. Then, it is moved to the C++
ROS framework to be used on the real robot. Another algorithm for motion control is
also created.
The final part of the thesis shows the test of the created algorithms on the real robot
in the outdoor environment, assesses the completion of reaching the thesis goals and
discusses the possible improvements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The market of industrial robots is dominated by robotic arms (Figure 1.1), with more
than 400.000 units sold in 2017 [Andrew 2017]. Robotic arms are capable of grabbing
and moving objects in any arbitrary location, therefore they are commonly used to per-
form repetitive tasks on industrial assembly lines. The main research and development
areas within the field of robotic arms include cobots (collaborative robots), which could
safely collaborate with humans and with each other.
However, the second most popular type of robots are mobile robots - robots which are
not fixed to any location (Figure 1.2). These give totally new application opportunities
and pose totally new challenges, as the robot’s range of work is not constrained by its
size. The applications of such robots include e.g. moving objects in a warehouse, clean-
ing floors, or even driving on the streets as autonomous cars.
The research areas within the mobile robots are very diverse. They include for example
localization of the robot in the (known or unknown) environment, path planning, obsta-
cle avoidance.
New opportunities for mobile robots are also offered by new sensor solutions. One
of the types of sensors characteristic for mobile robots and being under intensive devel-
opment nowadays is a LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging). LiDARs are considered

to be the most important sensor type for the autonomous cars [Alex 2018].
The data returned from the LiDAR is a set of distances to the target(s), using pulsed
laser light and measuring the time and wavelength difference between the transmitted
and returned pulse. If many lasers are used or the laser is moving with respect to the
measured target(s), a 2-D or 3-D representation of the measured target(s) is obtained.
LiDARs for mobile robots are rapidly developed nowadays and both their capabilities
and principle of operation are changing. Up to 2005, the most popular type of a Li-
DAR for mobile robot applications was a device using one rotating laser. With a typical
rotating frequency of 5 to 20Hz, the returned data was a 2D array of distances with theo-
retically achievable 360 degree field of view (Figure 1.3). This type of sensor was used in
the 2004 and 2005 DARPA Grand Challenge, the first competition for autonomous cars
at a long distance (212km). In 2005, one of the competition participants and the CEO
of the company Velodyne, realized the shortcomings in the existing LiDAR technology
and created a new type of LiDAR for the 2007 competition [John 2007]. The new type
of LiDAR was using an array of lasers, placed at different angles, to achieve a 3D array
of distances to the measured objects. This is the most common type of LiDAR for au-
tonomous car research programs nowadays and Velodyne collaborates with 25 different
self-driving programs [Marco 2016].
Currently, many LiDAR manufacturers, such as Luminar, LeddarTech, Innoviz or
Quaenergy, focus on developing a solid-state version of the device, which would not
involve mechanical rotating elements and be therefore more robust and well-suited for
automotive applications.
The type of data returned from the 3D LiDARs is especially useful for autonomous cars
and mobile robots, as the distance to the surrounding objects is often a very desirable
information. The range of this type of sensor is typically in an order of 100-200m, which
is enough to capture the characteristic features of the static surroundings (such as road,
buildings) and navigate according to them. On the other hand, a distance to the moving
objects can be used for obstacle avoidance.
3D-LiDAR is a relatively new technology, still available at the price tag of rather a
research, than a commercial, grade. Released just in 2016, Velodyne VLP-16 ’PUCK’
was the first 3D-LiDAR sensor available at the price under 10.000USD (8.000USD com-
pared to the 85.000USD for the model used in 2007 DARPA Challenge, HDL-64E). On
the other hand, manufacturers of the upcoming solid-state LiDARs expect their mass
production price to be in the range of 100-250USD [Alan 2017; Gabrielle 2018].
’Research grade’ LiDARs, produced by Velodyne, can deliver the 3D distance data point
clouds, which represent the capabilities of this type of a sensor. As this technology is
expecting a significant price drop, making it suitable for many mobile robot applications,
it is interesting to investigate the usability of this type of a sensor for a specific mobile
robot application. The thesis focuses on a ’typical’ outdoor robot navigation problem
- navigation over a long distance with inertial sensors - and the usage of a 3D LiDAR
instead of a ’popular’ GPS solution.

2.1
Setup
The thesis focuses on sensor fusion for outdoor robot navigation, using available robot
platform, inertial sensors and a Velodyne VLP-16 ’PUCK’ 3D LiDAR sensor. The robot
platform is iRobot ATRV-JR robot with a Core i7 class computer installed. The mo-
tors are controlled through a MobotWare framework [Beck et al. 2010]. Along with the
MobotWare, the robot runs on Linux Ubuntu 14.04.1 LTS with ROS Indigo installed.
The used inertial sensors are the wheel odometry sensors with quadrature encoders in-
stalled by default on iRobot ATRV-JR and CruizCore XG1010 single-axis gyroscope.
The gyroscope is aligned with the ground. The developed algorithms run on a Core
i5 class laptop, which communicates with the robot’s computer over a serial port. Fig-
ure 2.1 shows the robot with a LiDAR mounted on a tripod.

between the buildings. The favored method is to use the data from Velodyne VLP-16

CHAPTER 3
Analysis of possible
approaches
A number of solutions to the presented localization problem already exists. The following
section summarizes the most popular ones.
3.1
Global Positioning System
One of the sensors capable of providing the localization information with the accuracy
independent from the amount of driven distance is GPS (Global Positioning System).
GPS receivers deliver the absolute orientation of the sensor with respect to the satellites,
which have a fixed position with respect to the Earth’s orbit.
This is achieved by
communicating through the radio waves. Therefore, provided information is independent
from the travelled distance. However, the accuracy of this method is dependent on other
factors:
• Amount of satellites in the line of sight of the receiver; less satellites are available
closer to the Earth’s geographic poles; a minimum of 4 satellites is required to
deliver the position;
• the system does not work indoors;
• Surroundings, e.g. buildings, mountains, reduce the access to the clear sky and
therefore the amount of visible satellites.
In perfect conditions, the civilian GPS gives the accuracy on the level of 5 meters [Defence
2008]. This can be further reduced by using GPS RTK technology, which is capable of
calculating and thus correcting the errors from the GPS readings, therefore achieving
even a centimeter-level accuracy [Pirti 2011] . However, this technique needs at least one
additional receiver-transmitter, called a base station, which would send the corrections
to another receiver, called a rover. The centimeter-level accuracy is also only achieved
relatively between the base station and the rover, as the absolute accuracy is limited
to the accuracy of the positioning of the base station. Another method, similar to the
RTK technology, is called DGPS (Differential-GPS), where the positioning accuracy is

increased by a number of base stations at known locations.
There is a number of published articles showing the successful implementation of a GPS
system to navigate the robot. [Ohno et al. 2004] uses a DGPS-odometry fusion system
and achieves an accuracy of 50-70cm over hundreds of meters long rides.
However,
the authors found it especially problematic to navigate in specific places between the
buildings, as the buildings reduce the availability of the GPS signal.
3.2
Camera - based approaches
The other approach to enrich the odometry data by other sensor data is to use the
camera images and apply computer vision algorithms to obtain the data that can be
used for localization.
Again, a number of different approaches exist. [Lingyan et al. 2017] uses a neural network
on a fisheye camera images to localize the robot. [Schindler, Brown, and Szeliski 2007]
is able to localize the robot within a city-scale database of road images with the average
accuracy of 10m. Both of these methods use a map of images with associated pose
information to match with a camera image taken by the robot.
[Irie, Yoshida, and
Tomono 2010] keeps the average pose error under 1 meter while driving the robot in a
road environment, by matching the stereo camera images to the generated 3D occupancy
map, under various lighting conditions. However, this approach is similar to the approach
mentioned in Section 3.3, as the camera data is transformed to the 3D map of points
and localization is performed on the point cloud, not on the images themselves.
3.3
Range sensor - based approaches
Instead of using the camera data, one can use the range sensor (sonar/ radar/ LiDAR)
data to localize the robot in a map.[Leonard and Durrant-Whyte 1991] defines a concept
of a geometric beacon - a unique observable feature of the environment- and implements
a framework to localize the robot basing on sonar observations of such beacons.
A
similar approach (geometrical matching between the map and the sensor data) is used
in virtually all range sensor localization methods, as well as many methods using the
stereo cameras (like [Irie, Yoshida, and Tomono 2010]).
Figure 3.1 shows the basic
localization framework for such approach.

information (i.e. speed), integrated over time, can be used to deliver the object’s pose

3.5
Conclusions
To conclude, there are many choices for the enhancement of the proprioceptive robot
sensors with an exteroceptive sensor, both in the sensor type and the localization method.
The most suitable localization method is matching with a map of the environment.
As the thesis focuses on navigation between the buildings, the buildings themselves
guarantee that the map would have the features that could be matched to the sensor data.
On another hand, the presence of buildings decreases the accuracy of GPS (buildings
obstruct the signal) and long distances disard laser / visual odometry.
After choosing map matching as a localization method, one needs to further choose
between the sensor type. Both the cameras and the LiDAR are suitable for such task, as
they both provide information about the local surroundings of the robot. However, the
LiDAR data is given in a more convenient form than the camera data. The distances
to surrounding objects are the feature that does not vary with e.g. lighting conditions,
contrary to the colors in the image.
One could also obtain such data (distances to

4.3
Plane detection
To find the planes of the buildings, a wall finding algorithm was designed and tested on
the collected LiDAR data, obtained from the test run illustrated on Figure 2.3. It was
decided to use a PCL library [Rusu and Cousins 2011], as it provides a lot of out-of the
box algorithms, suitable for point cloud manipulations.
4.3.1
Point cloud reduction
At first, it was decided to reduce the point cloud data, discarding the unnecessary points.
As the LiDAR works by casting a laser beam, its resolution is higher at lower distances
to obstacles.
In case of detecting building walls, a resolution on the level of single
centimeters is not necessary, but it is important to preserve the (lowering) resolution
at long ranges. It was decided to downsample the point cloud, using a VoxelGrid filter
from the PCL library.
Voxelgrid filter downsamples the given point cloud into the single voxels (3D cube grid)
of a given length and leaves only one point inside each voxel. Figure 4.2 shows the idea
of downsampling using a grid of voxels on an example of 2D LiDAR scan. Figure 4.3
shows the input point cloud before applying the filter and the output point cloud after
applying the filter.
With the size of the grid set as 0.2 x 0.2 x 0.2 m and the test run data from the path
on Figure 2.3, the average point cloud size before filtering was 26825 data points, after
filtering - 3232 data points and the average CPU time of 5.8ms.
4.3.2
Plane finding
After filtering the point cloud, the actual planes had to be found. There are many meth-
ods to segment the point cloud and fit the planes and many publications connected to
that topic, e.g. [Deschaud and Goulette 2010] and [Grant, Voorhies, and Itti 2013]. Most
of these approaches begin from using a RANSAC algorithm [A. Fischler and C. Bolles
1981] to estimate the planes and then apply various modifications, therefore it was de-
cided to begin the plane estimation by using a RANSAC algorithm available in PCL
library.

plane to the point) for all the other points in the model and stores that information
as a score. Then the procedure is repeated iteratively, e.g. until a certain number of

4.3.2.2
Region growing and RANSAC
It was decided to follow the approach described in [Rabbani 2006] and segment the point
cloud using a smoothness constraint. The smoothness constraint computes the surface
normals for the planes created by neighbouring points and locally clusters them, basing
on the variation in the normals versus the given threshold. The PCL RegionGrowing
library was used. The total time for segmenting the point cloud using that method
was usually below 100ms. Then, the RANSAC algorithm was used for each cluster to
estimate the plane. According to Equation 4.3.2.1, RANSAC algorithm should work
much faster on smaller point clouds with one expected plane. The observation matched
with the expectations, with the average time to find a plane using RANSAC algorithm
being under 0.1ms.
The problem with region growing algorithm is that it can detect the same wall plane
twice, if not all of the plane points are direct neighbours.This is for example the case,
when a tree is standing in front of the wall and therefore splits the wall plane into 2
regions. To overcome that problem, a simple check was created, which only adds the
plane to a vector of found plane coefficients wallCoeffs, if a similar plane (with respect
to the distance and angle to the robot) was not already detected.

4.5
Sensor fusion algorithm
There exist a number of sensor fusion methods, to fuse the map information with the
sensor readings. One has to choose the method depending on the application.
4.5.0.1
Overview of existing methods
The first question one needs to answer while developing a sensor fusion algorithm, is
whether to perform any localization at all. Knowing the pose of the robot with respect
to the given map is not always necessary. An example of navigation strategy without
any localization is a behavioral navigation, where a robot uses a set of behaviors to
navigate. The behaviors are e.g. ”follow the wall” or ”go through the door”. Then,
==PAGE:35==
4.5 Sensor fusion algorithm
23
instead of localizing itself in the world map, the robot only executes a sequence of given
behaviors.
In the given case of navigating between DTU buildings, a behavioral method could not
be utilized without studying a map before. Using a set of behaviors that would work
in e.g.
a maze, like ”always follow a right wall”, would not guarantee reaching the
destination. However, it would be possible to generate a set of behaviors automatically
from a given world map and a given path. For instance, in a considered navigation case
from Building 326 around Building 327/328, the set of behaviors could be: ”follow left
wall for 2m” -> ”turn -90 degrees” -> ”follow left wall for 2m” -> ”turn +90 degrees”
-> follow left wall for 1m” -> ”turn -90 degrees” -> ”follow left wall for 5m” etc. One
could easily imagine developing a program to generate a set of behaviors from a given
track.
Unfortunately, this approach would most probably fail with the map illustrated on Figure
4.7.
This is the case, because the map does not include many obstacles that pose
additional constraints on the possible robot path. A great example is navigating in the
garden between Building 326 and Building 327. The robot cannot take any arbitrary
path between these buildings, as most of the area is covered by trees and low vegetation.
Therefore, to use a feature-based navigation, the world map would need to accurately
include all obstacles other than walls of the buildings.
However, this is not a concern when using a global localization algorithm. To be certain
that a chosen path is collision - free with respect to stationary obstacles, one could just
drive the robot manually through the path and register the position of the robot. If
the localization algorithm and the motion control algorithm are accurate enough, the
realized path could be then reproduced autonomously with a certainty of the robot not
colliding with any stationary obstacle. It was decided to use this approach and achieve
autonomous navigation in the following way:
1. Develop a localization algorithm;
2. Using a localization algorithm, manually drive the robot and register its poses to
obtain a collision-free path;
3. Drive the robot autonomously through the obtained path.
The only disadvantage of the selected method is that the robot cannot go to a new
location autonomously, with a guarantee of avoiding an obstacle not included in the map,
e.g. a tree. With a current map, that would require an additional obstacle avoidance
behavior, which is not a focus of this thesis. However, taking into account the time
needed to develop a map including all stationary obstacles, it was decided to use a
simplified map.
4.5.0.2
Markov versus Kalman localization algorithm
Localization of the robot using a map of its environment can be divided into 2 main
categories: Markov localization and Kalman localization methods. Both of the methods
==PAGE:36==
24
4 Offline implementation
use a probability distribution to describe the current pose of the robot. As any sensor
(e.g. odometry encoders, gyroscope, LiDAR) has its own uncertainty, these uncertainties
can be fused into one probability distribution. The proprioceptive sensors (odometry
encoders, gyroscope ) perform a position prediction step (as they measure internal states
of the robot, their error accumulates), while the exteroceptive sensor (LiDAR) performs
a measurement update step with a bounded absolute uncertainty. These are fused con-
tinuously in a loop.
The fundamental difference between the Kalman and Markov localization categories lays
in the method of tracking the robot’s pose. Markov localization computes the proba-
bility of the robot being in a certain position for all possible robot positions in a map.
Therefore, depending on the map size, the method becomes computationally expensive.
On another hand, it provides a solution to the ’kidnapped robot’ problem - when the
robot appears in an unspecified place in the map with no previous knowledge about its
position.
While considering Markov localization, it is worth mentioning methods that reduce its
computational complexity. One of the most popular realizations of Markov localization
algorithm is AMCL (Advanced Monte Carlo Localization), released and maintained as
open-source in 2013 [Zhang, Liu, and Chen 2013]. Monte Carlo realization of the algo-
rithm, in brief, draws a group of best (e.g. 10 %) samples from all the tracked robot’s
positions and performs position prediction only on them. There are many strategies for
choosing the subset of the samples, depending on whether one wants to achieve a better
accuracy (most probable samples) or a better ability of escaping the ’kidnapped robot’
problem (randomly selected samples). This is one of the reasons why, until 2018, the
open-source algorithm had more than 110 releases.
The complexity of Markov localization method can be however avoided to tracking only
the most possible pose of the robot, with a given distribution. Kalman filter can fuse all
available information from all the sensors, with a varying level of accuracy. Assuming
a linear system and a Gaussian noise, the Kalman solution would be even the optimal
one.
However, Kalman filter requires an initial robot pose to be known. This was not con-
sidered as a big disadvantage and Kalman filter approach was therefore chosen as a
localization method.

A motion

6.1
Motion control algorithm
To achieve autonomous navigation, the robot not only needs to localize itself, but also
to navigate along a selected path. The robot had the hardware abstraction layer to com-
municate with the wheel motors already implemented and it was possible to send the
signals to the wheels by a ROS topic. Achieving autonomous navigation was therefore a
matter of designing an algorithm to convert an information about a current and desired
robot location into corresponding wheel speeds at a given time instant.
6.1.1
Algorithm requirements
As a simple way of achieving motion control, one could develop an algorithm that simply
takes the desired relative position of the robot, turns the robot in the direction of the
goal position, then drives along a straight line until the goal position is reached and turns
until the goal orientation is reached as well. This will be later called the ’turn-forward-
turn’ algorithm. However, the localization algorithm can return ’jumps’ in the robot
position, as seen of Figure 5.4 and Figure 5.5. Then, after receiving a different relative
goal, the ’turn-forward-turn’ algorithm would need to turn the robot into the direction of
the goal again, before driving straight. That would result in the robot constantly turning
in place, if the localization algorithm is not precise enough. Therefore, not every motion
control algorithm could be used to control the robot. This leads to identifying the main
features that the used algorithm would need to demonstrate:
==PAGE:54==
42
6 Online implementation
1. Combination of turning and forward motion at any time instant;
2. Ability to converge to the goal position from any current position and orientation
3. Ability to re-compute the motor velocity at any time instant.
The first requirement guarantees that the robot is executing some motion towards reach-
ing the goal, even if the goal position changes. The ’turn-forward-turn’ algorithm or
any other algorithm not combining the translational and rotational motion could need
to totally stop the robot to continue executing the motion. For example, if the ’turn-
forward-turn’ algorithm was executing a forward motion and the new localization update
would come, it could have to stop the robot and turn it to align with the direction of the
goal again, instead of making a smooth curve to correct the heading for the new goal
direction. The requirement also avoids ’jumps’ in the motion trajectory.
The second requirement ensures that the robot would not end up in a location, where it
cannot compute the correct motor speeds to reach the destination.
The third requirement ensures that the algorithm would work correctly, even if the cur-
rent localization update demands totally changing the required motor speeds.
Analyzing the requirements, it was decided to look for the control algorithm that would
model the motion control problem as a continuous feedback control system, with the
deviations in target position and heading to be controlled simultaneously. Simultaneous
control would satisfy the first requirement, while the feedback control would address the
second and the third requirement.

until the goal orientation is reached.

Path planning
While mentioning the vector of goals, one should mention the structure of the goal array
and the method, by which it was created. The structure of the goal entry in the vector
of goals is as follows:
• pose X
• pose Y
• pose θ
• tolerance
• isTurn
The tolerance is the translational motion tolerance, which has to be met by the X
coordinate and the Y coordinate of the robot to assume that the goal was reached. Two
values were used: tolIndoors of 0.2m and tolOutdoors of 0.4m. The isTurn property
could be turn, if the goal is purely rotational, or noturn, if the goal is translational (or
translation + rotation).
The array of the goals was established by taking the coordinates of the robot poses from
the test runs ’15-03’ and ’06-05’ (illustrated on Figures 5.5 and 5.4). This resulted in a
vector of entries, which, for convenience, was put in a separate file globalGoals.cpp.

data and follow the odometry for some period of time.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
The goal of the thesis was to come up with a method for outdoor mobile robot navi-
gation. A specific path around DTU buildings was selected as a case study. A robot,
which was provided to perform the task, could not accomplish it with a standard set of
proprioceptive sensors, as Chapter 2 concludes.
The focus of the thesis was on a relatively new 3D LiDAR sensor technology and it was
decided to use a Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR together with map-based sensor fusion as a
method to reach the goal, as argued in Chapter 3.
The first problem of the thesis was to come up with a method to actually make use
of the rich LiDAR information. It was decided to focus on detecting the walls of the
buildings and Section 4.2 arguments, why they are a good choice. Section 4.3 describes,
how a wall finding algorithm was later developed.
The information about the building walls surrounding the robot had to be somehow
used to correct its position. For that, a map of the DTU buildings was created (Section
4.4). Section 4.5.1.4 presents the created sensor fusion algorithm, utilizing existing robot
sensors and the information about the building walls.
To adjust and test the algorithm, an offline testing framework was created, described
in Chapter 5. After testing and adjusting the algorithm on offline data, it was moved
to the actual robot framework, based on C++ / ROS. A motion control algorithm was
also added. Chapter 6 presents that step of the thesis.
After all the necessary algorithms for autonomous navigation were developed, a test on
the selected DTU path was performed. The test ended with a success and the robot
achieved the goal with a centimeter-level precision (Table 7.1). Chapter 7 describes the
test and also points out some imperfections of the created navigation algorithms.
8.1
Reaching the goals of the thesis
As formulated in Section 2.3, the purpose of the thesis was to come up with a method to
navigate the robot in the outdoor urban environment. The required navigation accuracy
was defined as allowing to navigate between the DTU buildings.
The proposed navigation method allowed not only outdoor, but also indoor accuracy,
and accomplished a path, where the robot returned to the same place inside a room,
after driving more than 500m outdoors. Therefore, the purpose of the thesis was met
and the proposed method exceeded the expectations.
==PAGE:66==
54
8 Conclusions
However, the method is not perfect and the main disadvantage lays in the type of the
surrounding of the robot. As the localization relies on finding the walls of the buildings,
such walls have to be present in both directions (parallel and perpendicular to the robot),
otherwise the localization accuracy would decrease over the travelled distance. Anyway,
it would be unreasonable to expect the robot to localize itself using information about
the environment, if not such information is available.
Nevertheless, it is possible to adjust the created algorithm further. This points to the
following section, which describes possible future work.
8.2
Future work
A lot of possible adjustments could be done, in order to improve the created navigation
method. The biggest disadvantage of the method is decreasing accuracy with a lack of
building walls in 2 directions, to be able to correct the robot’s position in 2 axes.
On approach to solve this problem would be to extend the matched environment in-
formation by other commonly present objects, e.g. the trees. However, as Section 4.2
argues, it would be cumbersome to create a map of the environment for the trees.
A probably easier solution would be to change the description of the robot’s path to
a behavioral navigation. Then, the information about the environment is necessary in
only one direction, e.g. a robot could drive along the wall until the end of the wall. The
only necessary adjustment is to upgrade the map with the areas that are not reachable
by the robot, as described in Section 4.5.0.1.
Having an accurate map of the environment with all obstacles, one could try to create a
script generating a collision-free robot path autonomously, to achieve a desired destina-
tion. This could allow the robot to reach virtually any destination in an urban setting
(with a presence of the buildings), without a previous ’test run’, as long as the accurate
map of the obstacles is available.
To take the work even further, one could take into account dynamic obstacles and navi-
gating the robot in a vehicle / pedestrian traffic.
