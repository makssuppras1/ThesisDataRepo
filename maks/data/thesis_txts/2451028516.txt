Abstract
Recent studies have shown evidence for liquid water on Mars at the base of the layered
deposits on Mars’ south pole at 193◦E, 81◦S. The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate the
plausibility of liquid water occurring at the base of the layered deposits. Topography data
was analysed to help select the appropriate model for propagation of the electromagnetic
signals. A peak detection procedure was created to extract surface and basal echo data
from MARSIS data. The existence of liquid water at the base of the layered deposits was
found to be plausible at 193◦E, 81◦S at depths of approximately 3 kilometres.

Introduction
Water on Mars, both past and present, is key to all four science themes of NASA’s Mars
Exploration Program: climate, geology, life, and preparation for human exploration [1].
In 2018, Orosei et al. published a paper showing a stable body of liquid water under the
South Polar Layered Deposits (SPLD) [2]. A 200 km wide area centred at 193◦E 81◦S
was investigated. Synthetic signals matching those of the MARSIS radar sounder was
created and compared to the surface and basal echo power measured by MARSIS. This
resulted in a 20 km well deﬁned zone at 193◦E 81◦S, where the dielectric constant at the
base of the SPLD was found to exceed 15. This value of dielectric constant matches that
of water-bearing materials [2].
To investigate the subsurface at a global scale, space borne radar sounders are used.
These send out electromagnetic waves at low frequencies, which penetrates hundreds or
thousands of metres into the subsurface, and is reﬂected at dielectric discontinuities when
a change in permittivity occurs [3]. The radar then measures the received power at dif-
ferent time instants.
To describe the surface and subsurface properties, models for the propagation and scat-
tering of the transmitted signal is needed. The received power is described by the radar
equation, which relates the received power to the transmitted power, range, antenna char-
acteristics and the reﬂection characteristics of the target. Two space borne radar sounders
orbits Mars: Mars Advanced Radar for Subsurface and Ionospheric Soundings (MARSIS)
onboard ESA’s Mars Express (MEX), and Mars Shallow Radar Sounder (SHARAD) on-
board NASA’s Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO).
The purpose of this thesis is to use electromagnetic modelling to study and interpret
radar data from the area centred at 193◦E 81◦S. First the available radar sounder data
within the area will be evaluated in reference to available parameters and basal echo
strength. Then topography data from the area will be analysed to determine the surface
roughness. Based on the selected radar data, the surface roughness and the propagation
theory of electromagnetic signals, a model to calculate the dielectric constant of the basal
echo will be created. Next the surface and basal echo will be detected from the data
set. To do this a peak detection procedure will be created. Lastly a range of parameters
describing the SPLD will be chosen to be included in the model.

Theory
In the following section the SPLD is ﬁrst described in terms of topography, composition
and temperatures.
Then the principles of sounding radar is presented describing the
propagation of an electromagnetic wave and the relation between transmitted and received
power. Then surface scattering and the criterion of smoothness is described, which leads
to the theory of Fresnels reﬂection and transmission laws. Lastly a model for determining
the basal permittivity is created, both in the case of an absolute and a relative analysis
of a received basal echo.
2.1
South Polar Layered Deposit
2.1.1
Geological setting and topography
The following section is based on "Water on Mars and Life", chapter 6 by C. S. Hvidberg
[4]. Mars’ southern pole is covered by a permanent ice cap known as the South Polar
Layered Deposit (SPLD). High resolution topographical data from Mars Orbital Laser
Altimeter (MOLA) has measured that the SPLD has a diameter of 1500 km and rises to
approximately 3 km above the surrounding topography. The SPLD is characterised by its
smooth surface and internal layered structure, the latter which can be seen from scarps.
The deposit is located in the southern highlands, which is a heavily cratered area. It sits
partly in the the Prometheus Basin, which is a large, eroded impact basin. At least 15
impact craters with a diameter of at least 800 m are located in the SPLD.
2.1.2
Composition
The SPLD consists primarily of H2O ice with an unknown amount of dust and CO2 ice
in the layered structure. The SPLD is covered by a seasonal cap are composed of CO2
frost in the winter on the southern hemisphere. Constraints on the density, and hence
composition, has been estimated using gravity and topography data. The results showed
that the SPLD could consist of 55% CO2 ice by volume if no dust are present, and it could
consist of 14-28% dust by volume, if no CO2 ice is present [5]. The Martian soil near the
north pole was investigated by the Phoenix Wet Chemistry Lab (WCL), which discovered
substantial amounts of magnesium, calcium, and sodium perchlorate. Magnesium and
calcium perchlorate salts both have a strong exothermic aﬃnity for water and increases
the freezing point to 204 K and 198 K respectively [5]. If these are present at the base of
the SPLD, this increases the range of temperatures capable of supporting water.

2.2.1

2.2.2
Fresnel zones
When electromagnetic radiation is emitted from the antenna, it will propagate as a cir-
cular wavefront. If the surface is smooth and perpendicular to the emitted radiation, the
electromagnetic rays will be reﬂected back along the same paths. Because the wave front
is circular, the length of the path increases further away from the centre, which can lead
to constructive or destructive interference depending on the length of the path relative to
the wavelength [3].
If the two way travel path equals N · λ + x, where N is an integer number and x < λ/2,
the reﬂected wave will interfere constructively, where if x > λ/2, the reﬂected wave will
interfere destructively. Areas where one of these conditions are met are called Fresnel
zones, which are annular shaped zones, except for the innermost zone, which is circular.
A boundary between the zones are found when the length of the two way travel path
is increased by a half wavelength, i.e. when the range is increased by a quarter of the
wavelength [3].

2.3
Surface scattering
When an electromagnetic wave reaches a surface boundary between two media of diﬀerent
relative permittivities, some of the energy is scattered backward into the original medium,
and the rest is transmitted forward into the new medium. If the new medium can be
considered homogeneous, all the scattering will take place at the surface, i.e this is the case
of surface scattering. The type of scattering that happens at the surface boundary between
two media of diﬀerent relative permittivities depends of the smoothness of the interface.
A surface boundary is considered smooth when the reﬂection is specular, meaning that
the reﬂection can be described by the Fresnel reﬂection and transmission laws [9].

Instrument speciﬁcations
In this section the speciﬁcations of the two radar sounders orbiting Mars, SHARAD and
MARSIS are described. The speciﬁcations along with the available data will determine
which of these instruments will and can be used in the analysis of this thesis.
3.1
SHARAD
Mars SHAllow RADar sounder (SHARAD) is a radar sounder on board of the Mars
Reconnaisance Orbiter (MRO) [18]. The spacecraft ﬂies in a nealy circular polar orbit,
completing a full orbit every 112 minutes. The satellite altitude ranges from 255 kilo-
metres over the South Pole, to 320 kilometres over the North Pole [19]. MRO has been
operating since 2006 as the critical next step in the NASA Mars Exploration Program of
“following the water”, a multi-mission strategy for learning about Mars’ changing climate,
geologic history and potential ability to harbor life [19].
One of the key science objectives of MRO is to probe beneath the surface for evidence
of subsurface layering, water and ice, and proﬁle the internal structure of the polar ice
caps, to which SHARAD is used. SHARAD uses ground penetrating radar capabilities
to investigate the surface and subsurface for information about layers of ice, rock, and

5.2.1
Locating surface and basal echoes
To do a relative analysis of basal echo power compared to the surface echo power, both the
location and echo power of the surface and base must be extracted from each radargram.
Due to the large data sets, a method of automatically locating the surface and subsurface
must be created. To do this a peak detection procedure is created, which uses two steps
to locate the surfaces. Step one consist of computing the average radar echo in the along
track dimension of a radargram. This reveals two prominent peaks, a strong peak at the
surface and a smaller peak at the subsurface. Step two consist of using these peaks as a
reference to locate the individual peaks for each radar echo.

Discussion
7.1
Summary of main ﬁndings
Based on available sounding radar data from Mars, raw MARSIS data processed by Orosei
et al. was selected as the data best suited for analysis. Due to the uncalibrated data,
a method relating the basal power to the surface power was created. The model was
based on electromagnetic propagation and backscatter theory, to which an analysis of
the topography was needed. The result of this analysis showed that the surface causes
specular reﬂection. A peak detection procedure was created to extract the surface and
basal power and location.
The basal dielectric constant was then calculated for each
echo using diﬀerent assumptions of the SPLD. Most of the large values of basal dielectric
constants were found to be grouped together at 193◦E 81◦S, with a few large values seen
elsewhere. Some of the large basal dielectric constants are caused by decreasing surface
power, while some are caused by increasing basal power. No combination of parameters
and surface properties result in all calculated dielectric constants to be in the expected
range. Some data sets with relatively constant surface power values resulted in multiple
combinations that gave calculated basal dielectric constants within the expected range.
7.2
Discussion of results
The basal dielectric constant at locations with very little surface power variance, e.g.
orbit 10737 (4 MHz) and 15198 (3 MHz), both resulted in all calculated basal dielectric
constants below 100 for some combinations of parameters, when assuming a dielectric
constant of 2.25 for the surface. Orbit 15198 (3 MHz) also resulted in all calculated basal
dielectric constants below 100 for multiple combinations of parameters, when modelling
the surface dielectric constant as the average of the SPLD. These combinations of param-
eters resulted in a surface dielectric constant of up to 3.15. The reason why orbit 15198
(3 MHz) can be explained by a larger surface dielectric constant appears to be caused by
the basal echo, which does not show the same large peak in basal power as orbit 10737
(4 MHz) does. Because of this, and assuming fairly constant properties of the SPLD, it
seems likely that the surface dielectric constant will match that of CO2, which causes the
basal dielectric constant of orbit 15198 (3 MHz) to show values peaking at between 14-49.
Since the values does not coincide with any large change in surface power, these could be
caused by the presence of water, but since the model parameters can also explain the base
of the SPLD to have a basal dielectric constant of 14, then orbit 15198 does not show
strong evidence for water.
The basal dielectric constant at the peak in orbit 10737 could however not be modelled
to have a smaller basal dielectric constant value than 33, and since the surface power
at the location of the large basal dielectric constant does not show any large ﬂuctua-
tions, the large value must be caused only by the large increase in basal power. Multiple
combinations of parameters gives a basal dielectric constant of approximately 80 at the
echo with the largest basal to surface power ratio, but the largest basal temperature is
found to be 210 K. Since all areas that can be described by CO2 at the surface show that
temperatures of 210 K, or even above for some orbits, are possible, it appears to be a
probable temperature. To further test the plausibility of the estimated maximum basal
temperature, it would be ideal to calculate the theoretical basal temperature from the
56
==PAGE:58==

s144144
probable SPLD compositions for comparison. However due to large uncertainties of the
thermal conductivity of the SPLD, the theoretical basal temperature cannot be calculated
with great accuracy.
210 K is below the freezing point, and liquid water cannot be explained by assuming
a composition of H2O, CO2 and dust alone. If the soil at the base of the SPLD contains
substantial amounts of magnesium and calcium perchlorate salts, as shown near the north
pole by the Phoenix Wet Chemistry Lab, the freezing point is reduced to 204 K and 198
K respectively. This makes the existence of liquid water at the base of the SPLD plausible
at 210 K.
7.3
Discussion of assumptions
The most critical assumption used in the model is that the surface is smooth and therefore
supports specular reﬂection. For this reason the analysis of the surface roughness was
included, which showed that the assumption holds.
The MARSIS data processed by
Orosei et al. is corrected for geometric fall-oﬀ, which they assume to be the inverse of
the squared distance. This is only the case for specular reﬂection. If the surface is not
specular, either the data would have to be corrected diﬀerently, or the model should take
the specular fall-oﬀcorrection into account. If the surface is not specular, the data from
the base of the SPLD would be expected to be smaller, as the geometric fall-oﬀfor non-
specular reﬂection is the inverse of the distance to the power of four. This means that the
basal dielectric constant would be larger than for specular reﬂection, meaning that the
temperature, dust and CO2 percentages will have to be even smaller to explain the data.
7.4
Earlier work
Orosei et al. published paper in 2018 showing evidence of liquid water. This is based on
synthesised data using a SPLD model with 50 layers, to which the surface and basal echo
power is compared. The results of this thesis adds to the existing results, by including an
analysis of the topography in the area. The results showed strong evidence for specular
reﬂection in the area, supporting the hypothesis of specular reﬂection, which is used to
determine the basal dielectric constant, both by Orosei et al. and in this thesis. A new
peak detection procedure was also created to detect the surface and basal power and
position in each radargram. Based on the results, the plausible temperature range was
reduced to temperatures below 250 K. Lastly the depth of the strong basal reﬂections
were located at a depth of approximately 3 kilometres depending on the composition
of the SPLD. This diﬀers from the results by Orosei et al., who found the depth to be
approximately 1.5 kilometres.
7.5
Limitations
The analysis of the basal dielectric constant was limited by the lack of calibration of the
MARSIS antenna. This resulted in an unknown transmitted power, meaning that the
relative method had to be used. Therefore the basal echo power had to be compared to
the surface echo power, which is aﬀected by the topography. The eﬀect of the topography
will therefore indirectly aﬀect the estimation of the basal dielectric constant.
57

s144144
Many of the parameters used to describe the SPLD are also not well known. There-
fore a range of parameters had to be used. In order not to create a very large number of
combinations of parameters, the step between each value for a parameter has to be fairly
large.
7.6
Future work
An improvement to the analysis could be to create a surface and basal echo detection
procedure that take into account the detected signal from the echo samples originating
from free space. This value could be used to deﬁne a lower limit to which all echo power
values should be above, e.g. a detected power value should be x times larger than the
average echo from free space.
For some orbits the surface power showed many ﬂuctuations in areas with no appar-
ent topography variations. Theis caused large values of calculated basal dielectric con-
stants, that could not be explained by any of the surface dielectric constant assumptions.
More information on the topography or assumptions about these surface power values are
needed. An assumption for future modelling could be a variable dielectric constant along
the track, ranging from various water ice, CO2 and dust mixtures, to CO2 snow.
Lastly other data from the same area could used in the same analysis. The MARSIS
data described in section 4.1.2 show that both the surface and basal echo can be seen in
the radargram. It can therefore be used in the relative analysis.

Conclusion
Based on propagation theory of electromagnetic signals, and the analysis of surface topog-
raphy roughness, a model for calculating the basal dielectric properties was successfully
created. The created peak detection procedures works as intended, and are able to extract
the surface and basal power and location from the radargram. Lastly the properties and
likelihood of water at the base of the SPLD has successfully been analysed and interpreted.
Basal dielectric constants of > 14 are found at 193◦E 81◦S at a depth of approximately 3
kilometres. Multiple combinations of plausible values of the temperature, dust percent-
age and CO2 percentage result in basal dielectric values of approximately 80. The basal
dielectric constants are often only explained by surface dielectric constant below that of
pure water ice indicating amounts of CO2 at the surface. Due to the material found on
Mars and in the SPLD, a dielectric constant of 80 is interpreted as water. These values
of dielectric constants are found for temperature values below 250 K where where liquid
water can be supported under conditions of the soil composition, which are plausible on
Mars. Therefore it is concluded that there is a plausibility of liquid water at the base of
the SPLD at 193◦E 81◦S. These ﬁndings are in agreement with previous studies, which
have found evidence of water in the same area. The depth however are in disagreement
with previous ﬁndings. Previous ﬁndings has shown a depth of 1.5 kilometres, whereas
we ﬁnd the depth to be approximately 3 kilometres.
