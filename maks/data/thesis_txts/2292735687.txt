Summary
This thesis explores the possibilities of visualizing big data in a social media context.
In order to produce and present social media data live, it uses a graph database ap-
proach for storing the data, and it investigates whether it is fast enough and viable to
retrieve social media data from a graph database and present it on a website without
prior data processing. A further point is to examines how to obtain information from
social media data, by looking at how they are related.
Over the course of four iterations, a website for the presentation of large social
data is created, a graph database is implemented and the website and graph database
are connected.
A prototype has been developed in cooperation with the company Falcon Social,
a leader in the presentation of social media and other social data. They have advised
and provided feedback on ideas and solutions throughout the thesis. Furthermore, the
thesis focuses on designing a product that can be used by Carlsberg, one of Falcon
Social’s customers. A team from Falcon Social helped create the framework for what
the needs of Carlsberg and how a social media product like the one presented in this
thesis, can provide value for them.
The data in this thesis has been gathered from the social networking and mi-
croblogging service, Twitter.
The focus has been on collecting information about
users, tweets and tags and their corresponding relationships.
It was found that it is viable to use a graph database as a back-end to view social
big data, however some preprocessing may still be needed.

Preface
This thesis describes the work done in the period from January 2015 to June 2015 at
the department of Informatics and Mathematical Modeling at the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark in fulﬁllment of the requirements for acquiring a master’s degree in
Digital Media Engineering . It is written under guidance of Michael Kai Petersen and
external supervisor Dennis Green-Leiber from Falcon Social. This project accounts
for a total of 35 ECTS points.
The thesis deals with visualizing networks in a social data context with the main
objective of investigating how to understand, explore and display a social network.
The thesis consists of the following chapters:
Chapter 1: Introduction to the context and motivation of the study.
Chapter 2: Research objectives.
Chapter 3: Related studies and research ﬁelds.
Chapter 4: Presentation of the tools which have been used.
Chapter 5-8: Four iterations with details about theory, methods and ﬁndings. (ba-
sic prototype, database and algorithms, feedback from company and proof of ﬂexibil-
ity.)
Chapter 9: Conclusion of the four iterations.
Chapter 10: Discussion of possible points of improvement, future work and poten-
tial challenges with this type of analysis.
To aid the readability of this thesis, a list with brief explanations of technical
terms and social media terminology is provided in Appendix A.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, social media has increased in popularity. Social media like Google+,
Twitter and Facebook focus on giving the user an easy possibility to be social and
connect with other people. It provides companies with the possibility of interacting
directly with customers, and customer feedback of the product based on what is said
on such social media sites. It also makes it possible for companies to ﬁnd potential
customers, by looking at people who have bought the product and ﬁnd similar people
or those that are closely related to them. To seize this opportunity, tools to handle
social media are needed.
People who were interesting yesterday, might not be interesting today. For that
reason, there is a need for tools that can provide current information about people
of interest based on what is happening right now in the network, while being able to
serve the information in real-time.
Recently, graph approaches to handling data have been given more attention.
Many major internet companies, like Google, Facebook, ebay, Walmart and TomTom,
build their products on graph databases to handle the need for information on the ﬂy.
According to Forbes1, Donald Feinberg and Nick Heudecker stats in a Gartner report:
“Graph analysis is possibly the single most eﬀective competitive diﬀerentiator for
organizations pursuing data-driven operations and decisions after the design of data
capture.” [FH14]. Furthermore, Forbes states that according to Forrester Research it
can be estimated that over 25 percent of enterprises will use graph databases by 2017
[Yuh14]
A graph approach gives the possibility to get more information on a person, based
on the persons friends. E.g. if person A is together with person B, and person B is
in Copenhagen, person A must also be in Copenhagen. Looking at relations in their
network, can also be done for more that two persons.
The goal of this thesis, is to examine the possibilities of visualizing social big data
in a way where the data is presented in real-time, giving the user a tool for getting
insight about twitter users, their network , their interests, as well as the interests of
the users in the network. To be able to do this on the ﬂy with live data, it will be
explored if a graph database is fast enough for this purpose.

1 Introduction
To limit the scope of this thesis, the social network Twitter has been chosen as
the source for social media data. The script gathering friends and followers is only
going to look at up to 5.000 friends and 5.000 followers (more connections can occur
as a connection is made, if the person occurs in only one of the lists between two
users.) For each user, only the 200 most recent tweets are considered. Finally, to give
consistent results while prototyping, the database is kept static, but it is possible to
add data later on if wished for.

CHAPTER 2
Research Objectives
The main objective of this study is to explore the possibilities of using real-time
visualization for exploring a social network, to show the overall network interactions
and be able to zoom in on each of the user’s roles in the network. In order to achieve
a useful visualization for customers and to optimize development, the study has been
conducted with a lean approach [Kle13], using User story mapping [PE14] to prioritize
the interactions. In accordance with the lean methodology, the research process has
consisted of an ongoing number of iterations, each aiming at validating or invalidating
hypotheses with the help of various user stories and user tests.
To show the applicability of the data extraction and presentation concepts in this
project, Carlsberg as a company is going to be used as a case for evaluating the value
of a proposed solution.
2.1
Research question
This study aims at answering the following research question:
Can a graph approach in storing and visualization of a social network
data help users valuing and take evaluated decisions in a social network
real-time?
This main research question will be answered with the help of the following sub-
questions:
What information are you able to obtain from a user, by looking at the user’s
network and its meta data?
Is an eigenvector centrality approach good to ﬁnd inﬂuential posts and users in a
network?
Which features are important to visualize in order to understand sub-clusters in a
cluster?
In answering all these questions, diﬀerent types of visualizations are tested, in-
cluding graphs, charts, lists and others.

A lot of companies have explored the possibilities of and are working with visualizing
social media data, i.e information about people and their networks. While most of
them are using some sort of graph database, it is not always possible to inspect the
underlying architecture. The following section will present a number of interesting
cases showing unique ways of mapping and representing data, including the use of
graph databases, as they relate to this thesis. They are divided into two categories,
that partly overlap: Cases related to visualizing big data and cases where a graph
database is the essential part.

3.1.1
Google Analytics
Google Analytics is a tool for tracking and visualizing user behaviour from websites.
It is the most used tool for tracking such data1. The idea behind is that a little piece
of code is placed in the html code of every page on a website, which sends information
to Google about when the visitor was there, if it was a new or a returning visitor, what
browserhe used, where the visitor came from and much more. All of this is visualized
in the Google Analytics dashboard, showing how many active sessions there are on
the website right now, where the visitors are from and as well as an abundance of
other information, such as demographic data about the visitors. Rather than being
visit-based, Google collects this data from the user’s behaviour around the internet.
Figure 3.1 shows the dashboard of Google Analytics. It mostly consists of a basic
map and a number of line charts, but provides additional information in potentially
interesting areas, in order to get a deeper insight into the behaviour of the users
visiting the website. More information can be found on www.analytics.google.com.

CHAPTER 5
Iteration One
The ﬁrst iteration of this thesis focuses on the design part of the basic framework
for the front-end of the prototype. The prototype is developed with a Lean Startup
approach in mind, ensuring fast and focused feedback on proposed prototypes.
In order to evaluate whether or not a visualization of a feature is important and
understandable, quick qualitative interviews with user experts from Falcon Social
have been conducted. The interviews also discussed the viability of each feature as
part of the overall solution.
To make the process of evaluating the prototype more user focused, part of the
assessment and interviews have been focusing on how a real client of Falcon Social,
such as Carlsberg1, would beneﬁt from the solution, as well as the added value of
each feature to the solution as a whole.
To limit the amount of data, only information provided from the Twitter API 2 has
been explored. Twitter was chosen because it is free of charge to access its information,
it is one of the largest social networks and, also, it is based on connections between
users. The prototype uses Twitter, but is made so that other networks like Facebook,
Instagram and Google+ can be integrated later on. For this iteration, other networks
will not be discussed further.

5.4
Results
After analysing the Twitter API with focus on the individual user and what informa-
tion can be obtained about him from the his network, a list of features was created
in cooperation with a product manager from Falcon Social. The features were chosen
based on the hypothesis that they would give Carlsberg and similar customers value
by getting insight about speciﬁc users. The features were:
1. Who is the user? (user name, user photo, tweets, followers/following and where
the user is located.)
2. The most recent tweets of the user
3. How active the user is
4. What topics the user talks about

Network score algorithm
To give Carlsberg an idea of how important a user is compared to all other users, a
simpliﬁed version of a PageRank algorithm has been implemented. It is based on the
original PageRank proposed in [Pag+99] But instead of web pages linking to each
other, it operates with friends on Twitter, i.e. an outgoing (friend) link from one
Twitter user to another is equal to a link from one webpage linking to another.
The algorithm described in [Seg07] has been used as a reference of how to imple-
ment it and what to look for.

the PageRank algorithm does not necessarily provide a better result in the value for a
given node. For this prototype, 20 iterations suﬃced to obtain a qualiﬁed estimate of
the rank, an idea based on [Seg07, page 70]. In the ﬁnal product, a daemon would be
running the TwitterRank algorithm constantly to update the score, especially if new
people or relations are added. Figure 6.2 shows the diﬀerence in the value compared

as well. The name is changed from “connections” to “occurrence”
Network score is nearly identical, but instead of looking at the relation between
users, the TwitterRank algorithm is modiﬁed to check the relation between hashtags.

but the hashtag. Finally, the last score, deals with how many times the hashtag has
occurred divided by the days between the ﬁrst occurrence and today. This is done
to diﬀer between old and new hashtags. An old hashtag might have a really high
occurrence, while a new one has a relatively low occurrence. Yet, it might be that
one hashtag has only been in existence for ﬁve days, while another has been used for
over a year. Also, the name is changed to “relative occurrence”.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This study consisted of four iterations, each aiming at exploring diﬀerent aspects re-
lated to how to visualize social big data and using the ﬂexibility and the possibility
of fast responses on queries by using a graph database.
In iteration one, it is shown what features are important to visualize so as to un-
derstand a user on Twitter better. The iteration explores the range of information
that can be obtained about a user on Twitter, in particular using a graph approach.
In iteration two, a graph database, Neo4J, is implemented and used to store data
about Twitter users, their tweets and the tags used in them. Based on the stored
data, it is possible to use a type of eigenvector, a TwitterRank, that is based on the
PageRank algorithm, to give a value of importance to each user. Based on that value,
a sub-cluster approach was implemented, to calculate a TwitterRank with priors,
based on a user and his friends. In the end of the iteration, the database prototype
and the electronic prototype were connected to test if the speed was fast enough to
show live data.
In iteration three a major interview was conducted, conﬁrming the viability of the
prototype, and giving new ideas. The TwitterRank with priors was used to a higher
degree, stating how important it is to look at sub-clusters to understand the whole
cluster and, thus, understanding the network.
Finally, in iteration four, the work of the previous iterations culminated, to apply
the models to a new domain, hashtags. It exempliﬁes the possibilities of rapid proto-
typing and the ﬂexibility of the proposed prototype. Additionally, due to the already
existing data in the graph database, it demonstrated the extent of information that
can be gained by looking at the network.
In the four iterations, the three sub-questions of the research objective were answered.
Based on the functionality and speed of the prototype and interviews with various
participants, like experts from Falcon Social, it can be concluded that a graph ap-
proach in storing and visualizing of social network data can help giving users like
Carlsberg value and take evaluated decisions for a social network in real-time. It was,
however, also found, that some preprocessing is needed for calculating a TwitterRank,
in order to faciliate the scoring.
The project also conﬁrms that it is possible to get additional information, by
looking at a users network.

Visualization and data
Finding the right way to visualize social big data, and big data in general may not be
not an easy task, but an important one. This paper explores some possibilities and
demonstrates that it is possible to convey a lot of information with simple charts and
tables by having focus on what information is shown and how the data is processed.
In future iterations, it would be beneﬁcial to users like Carlsberg, to improve
the visualization using maps and graphs, as there are a lot of possibilities in that
ﬁeld, given the data supports it. Social media applications using location data are
increasingly gaining momentum, as smartphones become an integrated part of our
daily lives. Instagram is a great example of a social media platform, only working on
mobile devices, that forces the user to provide a location when posting an image. If
other social media applications like Google+, Facebook and Twitter force the user to
include some kind of location data when using the mobile phone, it would give a lot
of possibilities to target users based on this location. In turn, this could entail major
privacy issues, as discussed below. For companies like Carlsberg, it would be a great
opportunity to target users from a speciﬁc area, or ﬁnd areas where users are writing
negative comments about the company, enabling them to be proactive.
