Abstract
This work investigates the spatially resolved photocurrent response of Sb2S3-based thin-
film solar cells fabricated in a substrate configuration using RF sputter-deposited Sb2S3
absorber layers. The study focuses on devices incorporating a thin MoS2 interfacial layer,
which has been shown to enhance open-circuit voltage and promote favorable crystal
orientation, thereby improving overall device performance. To assess local variations in
photocurrent generation and identify performance-limiting factors, spatial photocurrent
mapping was performed using a laser-scanning microscope system equipped with a Source
Measurement Unit (SMU). The laser scanned predefined regions of the solar cells point
by point, while the corresponding photocurrent was measured and compiled into high-
resolution two-dimensional maps. These maps enabled the visualization and analysis of
grain-level photocurrent behavior, revealing correlations between crystallographic features,
charge collection eﬀiciency, and spatial non-uniformities. Additional analysis using varying
laser polarization revealed performance differences between regions of the sample.
To
support this analysis, antimony selenide (Sb2Se3) samples were studied under identical
conditions to serve as a comparative reference and aid in the contextual interpretation
of Sb2S3 results.
The results highlight the critical role of both absorber morphology
and interfacial engineering in the performance of Sb2S3 solar cells and demonstrate the
effectiveness as well as the limitations of photocurrent mapping as a diagnostic tool for
optimizing thin-film photovoltaic materials.

Introduction
1.1
Background on solar cells and the energy context
The global population is rapidly increasing, driving a steady rise in energy demand across
the world[1]. To meet this growing need in a sustainable manner, the shift from fossil
fuels[2] to renewable energy sources has become both urgent and essential. Among renew-
able energy technologies, solar power is one of the most abundant and scalable, offering a
clean, sustainable solution to future energy challenges. In this context, photovoltaic (PV)
technology, which converts sunlight directly into electricity, has played a central role.
The most mature and widely adopted PV technology is based on crystalline silicon (c-
Si). With a global market share of over 90%, silicon solar cells have demonstrated high
power conversion eﬀiciencies (up to 27.3%[3]) and strong operational stability. Silicon’s
dominance stems from decades of optimization and industrial investment. However, it also
comes with drawbacks. The indirect bandgap (1.12 eV) of silicon leads to relatively poor
absorption of high-energy photons[4], necessitating thicker absorber layers to achieve high
eﬀiciency. Moreover, the production of high-purity silicon wafers is energy-intensive and
expensive, requiring high-temperature processes and complex purification steps.
To overcome the cost and manufacturing limitations of silicon, thin-film solar cells have
emerged as a promising alternative. These devices use much thinner absorber layers -
often 10 to 100 times thinner than silicon - which reduces material usage and enables flex-
ible or lightweight designs[5]. Thin-film PV technologies also allow for low-temperature,
scalable deposition techniques that are potentially more compatible with industrial mass
production.
Several materials have been explored in this field, including cadmium telluride (CdTe),
copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS), copper zinc tin sulfide (CZTS), and more recently,
halide[6] perovskites. These materials possess direct bandgaps in the optimal range for
solar absorption and high absorption coeﬀicients (>105 cm−1), making them excellent
candidates for eﬀicient thin-film solar devices. Many of these technologies have achieved
impressive power conversion eﬀiciencies - for example, 22.6% for CdTe, 23.6% for CIGS,
and 26.7% for perovskite solar cells[7][8][9].

1.2
Motivation for studying Sb2S3
Antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) is a binary metal chalcogenide that has emerged as a highly
promising material for next-generation thin-film solar cells. As part of the broader chalco-
genide family[13] - compounds formed between chalcogen elements (S, Se, Te) and metals
- Sb2S3 shares several defining characteristics with its sister materials, antimony selenide
(Sb2Se3) and the alloyed compound Sb2(SxSe1−x)3. These semiconductors exhibit strong
optical absorption (coeﬀicients >105 cm−1), tunable bandgaps (from 1.1 eV for Sb2Se3
to 1.7 eV for Sb2S3), and crystallize in a quasi-one-dimensional orthorhombic structure
composed of (Sb4X6)￿ribbons (X = S, Se). This unique crystal motif results in highly an-
isotropic charge transport properties that, when properly oriented, enable eﬀicient carrier
collection and low recombination losses.
The ability to tune the bandgap via sulfur-
selenium alloying makes Sb2(SxSe1−x)￿particularly adaptable for optoelectronic design,
including tandem solar cell architectures.
Compared to established photovoltaic technologies such as cadmium telluride (CdTe), cop-
per indium gallium selenide (CIGS), and lead-based perovskites, antimony chalcogenides
offer several critical advantages. They are composed of earth-abundant, non-toxic, and en-
vironmentally benign elements, avoiding the toxicity of cadmium and lead or the scarcity
of indium, gallium, and tellurium. Additionally, their simple binary composition reduces
the complexity of defect chemistry and facilitates stoichiometric control during synthesis.
These attributes make Sb2S3 and related antimony chalcogenides attractive not only from
a sustainability perspective but also for scalable, low-cost photovoltaic manufacturing
From a photovoltaic materials standpoint, antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) exhibits a set of phys-
ical and electronic properties that make it particularly appealing for thin-film solar cell
applications. It is a binary chalcogenide semiconductor with a direct bandgap of approx-
imately 1.7 eV, well-aligned with the visible region of the spectrum, allowing it to absorb
solar radiation eﬀiciently. Its high absorption coeﬀicient, exceeding 105 cm−1, enables
effective light harvesting even in films only a few hundred nanometers thick. These qual-
ities make Sb2S3 suitable for lightweight and flexible device architectures, and position it
as a promising top-cell material in tandem configurations with silicon, where theoretical
power conversion eﬀiciencies can approach 46%[14][15][16][17][18]. Additionally, Sb2S3 has
a relatively high melting point (550°C) and can be crystallized at moderate temperatures
below 350°C, making it compatible with a variety of substrates and scalable processing
techniques. It crystallizes in an orthorhombic structure and belongs to the Pnma space
group (#62)[19], providing a stable framework for achieving good structural order in thin-
film form.
Despite growing interest in Sb2S3, there is still limited research on solar cells made in
the substrate configuration, which could offer advantages in interface control and integ-
ration into advanced device architectures. At the same time, various methods have been
explored for depositing Sb2S3 absorber layers, including hydrothermal growth[20], chem-
ical bath deposition (CBD)[6][21], vapor transport deposition[22], thermal evaporation[23],
rapid thermal evaporation[14], and closed-space sublimation[24]. Among these, sputter-

1.3
The Photovoltaic Effect in Thin-Film Semiconductors
The generation of electric current in thin-film semiconductors under illumination is primar-
ily governed by the structure and behavior of the p-n junction[27]. This junction is formed
by adjoining two regions of a semiconductor that have been selectively doped to possess
differing majority carriers. The p-type region is doped to have an abundance of holes,
while the n-type region contains an excess of electrons. The doping process introduces im-
mobile ionized donor and acceptor atoms that become crucial in establishing an internal
electric field.
When the p-type and n-type materials are joined, electrons diffuse from the n-side to the
p-side, and holes move in the opposite direction. This diffusion results in recombination
near the junction and leaves behind charged, immobile ions on both sides. These exposed
charges generate an electric field across a region known as the depletion zone - so named
because it lacks mobile charge carriers. This built-in field forms a potential barrier that
prevents further carrier diffusion, establishing equilibrium, as shwon in figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: PN Junction equilibrium
Upon illumination, photons with energy equal to or greater than the bandgap of the semi-
conductor can excite electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, creating
1.3. THE PHOTOVOLTAIC EFFECT IN THIN-FILM SEMICONDUCTORS
5
==PAGE:12==
electron-hole pairs. When these photogenerated carriers are generated within or near the
depletion region, the built-in electric field separates them before they can recombine. Elec-
trons are swept toward the n-side and holes toward the p-side, generating a photocurrent
when an external circuit allows for carrier collection.
This process is especially critical in thin-film devices, where the absorber layer is designed
to be only a few hundred nanometers thick. Thin films allow for eﬀicient light absorption
with minimal material use, but also impose constraints on carrier lifetime and mobility.
Thus, maximizing the electric field region and minimizing recombination losses are vi-
tal. Advanced designs, such as heterojunctions and passivated interfaces, often augment
traditional p-n junctions in thin-film technologies to enhance carrier collection eﬀiciency.
In Sb2S3 thin-film solar cells, the photovoltaic effect arises when photons are absorbed
by the Sb2S3 absorber layer, which is typically p-type. This layer is often paired with a
CdS buffer layer that functions as the n-type semiconductor, forming a heterojunction.
Upon illumination, light penetrates through the transparent front contact and CdS layer,
generating electron-hole pairs in the Sb2S3. The built-in electric field at the p–n hetero-
junction interface drives the separation of these carriers - electrons are swept into the
CdS and holes into the Sb2S3 - resulting in a photocurrent. Eﬀicient charge separation
and collection depend on the quality of this interface, the crystallinity of the absorber,
and the minimization of recombination losses, which are particularly critical in thin-film
configurations due to their limited thickness.
1.4
Introduction to photocurrent mapping as a diagnostic
method
Photocurrent mapping techniques, such as laser-beam-induced current (LBIC) scanning[28],
have become indispensable tools in the characterization and diagnostics of photovoltaic
(PV) devices. These methods provide spatially resolved information about the genera-
tion and collection of photogenerated carriers, thereby offering insights into the electrical
uniformity and local performance of solar cells.
At the heart of photocurrent mapping lies the principle of locally illuminating the solar
cell with a focused light source - typically a laser - while measuring the resulting short-
circuit current (Isc) at each position[29]. This localized excitation allows for the detection
of variations in carrier collection eﬀiciency that arise from material inhomogeneities, grain
boundaries, shunt paths, cracks, or interfacial defects. By raster-scanning the laser across
the device surface, a two-dimensional map of the induced current is constructed, revealing
spatial fluctuations in electrical activity.

Figure 1.4: LBIC schematic
The strength of photocurrent mapping lies in its non-destructive nature and its sensitivity
to defects not visible through conventional visual inspection[30].
In polycrystalline or
defect-prone materials, regions with high recombination activity - such as grain boundaries
or delaminated zones - manifest as local depressions in the photocurrent signal. These
deviations from uniformity directly correlate with performance losses in the cell, making
LBIC and similar techniques powerful tools for quality control, failure analysis, and process
optimization.
Photocurrent mapping can be applied across a range of PV technologies, including crys-
talline silicon, amorphous silicon, and emerging thin-film materials. It also complements
other characterization techniques such as electron-beam-induced current (EBIC) and elec-
troluminescence (EL) imaging.
Additionally, enhancements like reduced spot size, im-
proved signal acquisition via lock-in amplifiers, and temperature-controlled measurements
have pushed the resolution and accuracy of these methods, enabling detection of increas-
ingly subtle defects.
In research and manufacturing contexts alike, the ability to visualize and quantify local
photocurrent variations underpins efforts to understand material limitations, refine fab-
rication protocols, and improve overall device eﬀiciency. As such, photocurrent mapping
has become a critical diagnostic instrument in the modern photovoltaics toolbox.
1.5
Aims and objectives
This project is centered on the study of Sb2S3-based thin-film solar cells fabricated in
a substrate configuration. The absorber layer was deposited via radio frequency (RF)
sputtering, and a thin MoS2 interfacial layer was introduced between the back contact
and the absorber in selected samples to explore its influence on device performance. The
materials and fabrication approach were chosen for their relevance to scalable, low-toxicity
photovoltaic technologies.
The methodology involves spatially resolved photocurrent mapping using a laser-beam-
induced current (LBIC) system, which enables the detection of local variations in car-
rier collection across the solar cell surface. These measurements were complemented by
current–voltage (I–V) characterization to assess global electrical behavior. Devices were
examined under varying illumination conditions and laser polarizations to probe aniso-
tropies in light absorption and charge transport.

As part of the broader effort to evaluate the performance of Sb2S3-based devices, a com-
parative analysis was also conducted using antimony selenide (Sb2Se3) solar cells. Sb2Se3
shares several chemical and structural characteristics with Sb2S3, but also presents distinct
differences in optical and morphological behavior. Notably, Sb2Se3 features a quasi-direct
bandgap of approximately 1.21 eV and a unique vertically aligned, needle-like crystalline
structure, in contrast to the compact, cobblestone-like texture typical of Sb2S3. Its best
reported power conversion eﬀiciencies currently reach around 5.34%, significantly higher
than the 1.28% observed in the Sb2S3 samples studied here.
Including Sb2Se3 in this
investigation not only offers a useful benchmark for interpreting the results obtained from
Sb2S3, but also highlights the importance of interfacial and structural engineering in op-
timizing device behavior. This comparison enriches the experimental findings and provides
valuable context for identifying promising directions for future research.
The central aim of this study is to investigate how microstructural and interfacial charac-
teristics influence the local photocurrent response in Sb2S3-based thin-film solar cells using
polarization-dependent Laser Beam Induced Current (LBIC) mapping. The primary hy-
pothesis is that polarization-resolved LBIC can reveal anisotropic photoresponse arising
from crystallographic orientation, particularly due to the alignment of grains along the
quasi-one-dimensional [001] axis of Sb4S6 ribbon chains.
The study further explores
whether grain structure and surface morphology, such as roughness, defects, or misalign-
ments, manifest in the spatial distribution of current generation, and whether polarization-
induced photocurrent modulation can serve as an indicator of anisotropy or interfacial
effects.
In parallel, the integration of the Source Measurement Unit (SMU) enables a
more comprehensive analysis of the sample’s behavior during scanning - providing not only
visual insight through spatial photocurrent maps, but also detailed electrical measurements
that help reveal local variations in carrier collection, resistive pathways, and recombination
activity. Finally, the investigation seeks to evaluate the effectiveness and limitations of
LBIC as a diagnostic tool in resolving subtle spatial and polarization-dependent behaviors
in thin-film photovoltaic devices.

Chapter 2
Experimental Setup
2.1
System Overview
The experimental measurements in this study were performed using the Xper-PC system, a
laser-beam-induced current (LBIC) platform designed for high-resolution spatial mapping
of photocurrent in thin-film photovoltaic devices. This setup enables the investigation of
localized electrical response and defect-related non-uniformities across the active area of
solar cells under controlled illumination, as shown in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Experimantal Setup
The system is built around a 532 nm continuous-wave laser, selected for its compatibility
with the absorption characteristics of the studied Sb2S3-based devices. Polarization control
is implemented using interchangeable waveplates: a quarter wave plate is used in the first
configuration and a half wave plate in the second and third. This arrangement enables the
investigation of polarization-dependent photoresponse behavior across the device surface.
To prepare for measurement, the sample is carefully mounted on a mechanical X–Y–Z
9
==PAGE:16==
stage, as shown in figure 2.2, that allows for precise manual positioning. Electrical probing
is conducted using fine-manipulator units with magnetic bases and LM lead guides, offering
stable and repeatable contact with the device electrodes. A vacuum chuck is used to hold
the sample securely in place, maintaining consistent contact and planarity during scanning.
Figure 2.2: Sample Mounting Setup
The laser beam is then directed through a dual-axis scanning mirror system that raster-
scans the focused spot across the sample area. A 150× long-working-distance microscope
objective focuses the beam to a tight spot, enabling high-resolution mapping. For align-
ment and visual inspection, the setup includes a 6 MP camera and a bright-field LED
illuminator, which aid in sample positioning and focus prior to data acquisition.
Photocurrent signals generated during scanning are collected using a Keithley 2420 sourcemeter,
as shown in figure 2.3, which provides both current and voltage readout from the device
under test. This is complemented by global I–V characterization, enabling correlation
between local and overall device performance.
Figure 2.3: Source Measurement Unit (SMU)
All scanning, control, and data acquisition tasks are managed through the NanoSpectrum
software suite, which provides real-time visualization and exports quantitative photocur-
rent maps in standard formats (e.g., .csv). These maps are used to assess spatial photocur-
rent variations and identify performance-limiting regions within the solar cell structure.

Solar Cell Samples (Antimony Sulfide Sb2S3)
The devices investigated in this study are thin-film antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) solar cells
fabricated in a substrate configuration on soda-lime glass (SLG) coated with molybdenum

(Mo). A key feature of these devices is the incorporation of a thin MoS2 interfacial layer
formed by sulfurizing the Mo back contact prior to absorber deposition. This interfa-
cial layer is intended to improve band alignment and reduce recombination at the back
contact[31].
The Sb2S3 absorber layer was deposited by RF magnetron sputtering using a stoichiometric
Sb2S3 target.
The deposition was carried out in an argon atmosphere, with the RF
power set to 35 W and a working pressure of 5 × 10−3 mbar. A tilt angle of 35° was
applied to the target to promote uniform film growth, and substrates were continuously
rotated. Absorber thicknesses were controlled by adjusting deposition time, with 100 nm
layers used for the final working devices, as thicker films tended to develop morphological
defects such as pinholes and double-layer structures. Post-deposition, the Sb2S3 films were
annealed at 350 °C in a sulfur-rich atmosphere to enhance crystallinity. The annealing
was conducted in a tube furnace using a graphite box containing elemental sulfur, and
optimal sulfurization was found with 100 mg of sulfur.
Following annealing, a CdS buffer layer (50 nm) was deposited on top of the Sb2S3 absorber
by chemical bath deposition (CBD). Finally, a bilayer transparent conductive contact was
added, consisting of a 50 nm resistive aluminum-doped ZnO (R:AZO) layer followed by a
250 nm AZO top electrode, both deposited via RF sputtering. The full device stack for
the main sample structure is therefore, as shown in figure 2.4, SLG / Mo / MoS2 / Sb2S3
/ CdS / R:AZO / AZO.
Figure 2.4: Sb2S3 Sample Layer Structure
The use of this substrate configuration is intended to offer better control over the formation
of the junction interface, improved spectral matching, and easier integration into tandem
or flexible solar cells. The inclusion of a MoS2 interfacial layer, produced by sulfurizing the
Mo back contact, is expected to influence the crystal orientation of the Sb2S3 absorber,
favoring vertical growth directions such as (001) or (hk1), as shown in figure 2.5. These
orientations can support more eﬀicient charge transport by aligning with the direction
of the quasi-one-dimensional Sb4S6 ribbons. Since charge carriers in Sb2S3 move more
easily along the [001] axis-parallel to these ribbon chains-vertical alignment ensures that
transport is directed toward the top and bottom contacts, reducing recombination losses
and enhancing collection eﬀiciency. The fabricated Sb2S3 devices used in this study, with
the aforementioned structural and interfacial engineering, achieved a power conversion
eﬀiciency of 1.28%. The current density–voltage (J–V) characteristics of these devices,

Antimony selenide (Sb2Se3) is included in this work mainly to provide a direct point-
of-reference for interpreting the antimony-sulfide (Sb2S3) results obtained with the same
optical and electrical test conditions. Sb2Se3 couples a quasi-direct bandgap of 1.21[32]
eV with a very high absorption coeﬀicient (>105cm−1), moderate carrier mobilities of
roughly 1–10 cm2 V −1 s−1, and a quasi-one-dimensional crystal lattice that gives rise to
pronounced in-plane anisotropy. Together with its low toxicity, earth-abundant composi-
tion, and excellent chemical stability, these features underpin the material’s rapid progress
in thin-film photovoltaics and photodetectors. The specific Sb2Se3 devices characterized
here deliver a certified power-conversion eﬀiciency of 5.34 %, and their behavior therefore
serves as an internal performance benchmark against which the Sb2S3 observations can
be more critically evaluated.

2.4
Measurement Procedure
Before each measurement, the Sb2S3 solar cell sample is carefully mounted on the vacuum
chuck to ensure firm and even contact during scanning. The chuck is part of the system’s
probe station and allows stable positioning of the sample throughout the measurement.
Once mounted, electrical contacts are established using two fine-point manipulators with
magnetic bases, which are adjusted manually using fine-knob controls. One probe is placed
on the top transparent electrode (typically AZO), and the other on the Mo contact on the
side, ensuring reliable connection to both terminals of the device.
The laser source used is a 532 nm continuous-wave laser, directed through a series of
optical elements designed to define the beam shape and polarization, as shown in figure
2.10. In phase one, the beam is initially circularly polarized and passes through a quarter
wave plate followed by a linear polarizer, resulting in a linearly polarized beam before it
enters the scanning optics. In phase two and three, the beam is first passed through a
Glan polarizer, producing a clean linearly polarized beam, and then through a half wave
plate, which rotates the polarization direction without altering its linear nature. After
polarization control, the beam is directed through the scanning mirror system, which
raster-scans the focused spot across the surface of the solar cell.

The laser is focused onto the device using a 150× long-working-distance objective lens,
providing a tightly confined beam for high-resolution photocurrent mapping. An integ-
rated LED illuminator and 6 MP camera are used to visually align the probe tips and
sample under the microscope before starting the scan.
During measurement, the scanning mirrors move the laser beam across a pre-defined area of
the device surface, typically in a raster pattern. At each position, the local photocurrent
generated by the focused illumination is measured using a Keithley 2420 sourcemeter,
connected to the top and side electrodes. The data is collected by the NanoSpectrum
software, which records the current values and generates a spatial map of the photocurrent
distribution. These maps are saved as .csv files and used for further analysis.
This process is repeated under different light intensities and polarization conditions, de-
pending on the specific experiment, to investigate spatial variations in photoresponse and
their relationship to device microstructure or fabrication conditions.

4.1
Experimental Setup Limitations and Resulting Data In-
accuracies
Throughout the various stages of the experimental process, a number of limitations and
instabilities within the equipment became evident, significantly affecting the reliability and
consistency of the results. These challenges were a recurring source of error, often lead-
ing to distorted images, disconnected readings, or incorrectly recorded values - rendering
several attempts unusable despite careful preparation and execution.
A major contributing factor to this unreliability was the need to break the process into
multiple sequential steps, each requiring strict timing and precise handling.
The high
sensitivity of the system’s sensors made the setup particularly vulnerable to external dis-
turbances such as subtle movements, ambient light fluctuations, or floor vibrations. This
meant that even slight interference during any part of the measurement cycle could impact
the data, making the recorded photocurrent values inconsistent or misleading. The time
lost to rerunning measurements and discarding invalid datasets was substantial.
Among these issues, the most prominent and impactful was the Z-axis drift of the mech-
anical stage holding the solar cell. Even when all experimental parameters were fixed
and no manual adjustments were made, the platform was observed to gradually sink over
time. This subtle change in focus altered the laser spot size and intensity distribution
on the sample surface, which in turn caused a gradual but unavoidable drop in measured
current.
Attempts were made to characterize and correct this drift - figure 4.1 shows
several experimental trials tracking the average current decay - but the trend proved too
inconsistent to apply a universal correction. In some cases, the decline was linear and
correctable; in others, it fluctuated unpredictably. This added a layer of uncertainty to
every measurement, both in visual map clarity and in the quantitative current data.

Other limitations, while secondary, also contributed to the overall unreliability. The laser
power stability, particularly when using the quarter wave plate configuration, was diﬀicult
to control, and small adjustments in polarization angle often led to unexpected intensity

it hard to maintain a constant intensity profile. In addition, making electrical contact with

presented in this work, all maps, current values, and conclusions must be viewed within
the context of potential measurement error and instrumental limitations.
4.2
Surface Morphology Effects and Focusing Uncertainty
This part of the discussion focuses on the behavior of the samples themselves - specifically,
how the antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) cells respond during the LBIC process. The particular
samples used in this project follow a substrate configuration, where the laser beam enters
from above and passes through all layers until it reaches the absorber, typically at the
Sb2S3/CdS interface. Depending on the parameters used, a wide range of outcomes can
be observed.
As mentioned in earlier sections, the surface morphology of the Sb2S3 samples is far from
uniform. On the contrary, it is characterized by a range of surface features - small or large
defects, voids, roughness, or height imbalances - naturally introduced during fabrication.
These features interfere with the measurement process, which relies on high sensitivity and
precision. As demonstrated, attempting to focus directly and sharply on the surface often
leads to noisy, inconsistent results that make it diﬀicult to extract useful information about
spatial current distribution or any signs of anisotropic response or grain-related structure.
To improve the clarity of the results, the best working solution was to slightly raise the
laser focus - effectively defocusing the beam. This increases the beam spot size, allowing
the system to average out smaller features by including a wider area, often spanning

above 15 µW, often resulted in two recurring complications. First, in several instances,

parameters.

4.4
Assessment of Anisotropy in Polarization-Dependent Pho-
tocurrent Response
As stated at the beginning, one of the central aims of this study was to investigate whether
Sb2S3 thin-film solar cells exhibit anisotropic photocurrent behavior under polarized illu-
mination. This assessment was based on measurements conducted under the configuration
established during Phase 2, utilizing Sb2S3 samples, a half wave plate optical setup, and
laser intensity Profile B, delivering an average power of approximately 2 µW.
The spatial distribution current maps generated in this phase, as shown in figure 3.21,
exhibit localized domains with similar dimensions and contrast boundaries, indicating
possible alignment with underlying grain structures. These domains appear consistently
across different polarization states and show variation in photocurrent generation that
may correspond to differences in local crystallinity, orientation, or interfacial quality.
However, despite these visual patterns, and as illustrated in earlier figures, the overall
spatial current distribution remains stable across all polarization angles, with only modest
relative changes. This assessment is further supported by the quantitative data presented
in Tables 3.3 and 3.4. Specifically, the average standard deviation between maximum and
minimum current values within individual maps is approximately 16 nA, while the av-
erage current variation between polarization angles, computed as the standard deviation
of the differential maps and divided by two to reflect the net directional shift, is 2.3 nA.
This represents a 14% relative modulation compared to the underlying spatial variability.
Moreover, given that the average photocurrent measured across the maps is around 348
nA, the polarization-induced change constitutes less than 1% of the mean signal. Together,
these observations reinforce the conclusion that the apparent features in the maps do not
provide suﬀicient evidence of a pronounced anisotropic photoresponse.
In principle [14], the quasi-one-dimensional crystal structure of Sb2S3, composed of (Sb4S6)￿
ribbon-like chains aligned along the [001] axis, should give rise to polarization-dependent
photoresponse, particularly when grains are vertically oriented. Linearly polarized light
interacts with anisotropic materials differently depending on the alignment of the light’s
electric field vector relative to the crystallographic axes. When the polarization is aligned
parallel to the [001] direction, absorption and carrier excitation are expected to be more
eﬀicient, enhancing charge separation and transport along the ribbon axis and resulting
in increased photocurrent. Conversely, polarization perpendicular to this axis should lead
to reduced absorption and collection eﬀiciency. Vertical grain alignment, with the [001]
axis perpendicular to the substrate, should therefore enhance this polarization-dependent
behavior by aligning the internal anisotropic features with the optical path.
Nonetheless, the largely isotropic character of the photocurrent maps observed in this
study is likely the result of a combination of experimental and material-related limitations
that obscure any underlying anisotropic response. One important factor is the potential

randomness in the in-plane orientation of the grains, which would average out directional
effects when measured over a broad area. Additionally, the grain sizes may be too small

carrier diffusion lengths, which restrict the lateral transport of charge carriers and reduce
the sensitivity of the measurement to grain-specific behavior.
Finally, the intentional
defocusing of the laser beam, introduced to mitigate surface height artifacts, may also in-
troduce optical averaging, further smoothing out local variations. Together, these factors
diminish the contrast and resolution necessary to clearly resolve polarization-dependent
effects, making it diﬀicult to draw firm conclusions about intrinsic anisotropy based solely
on the spatial photocurrent maps.
4.5
Crystallographic Grain Structure and Absorber Mor-
phology
As discussed in the previous section, this study did not find conclusive evidence of an-
isotropic photoresponse in the Sb2S3 samples. Nevertheless, the Phase 2 configuration,
featuring Sb2S3 absorbers, a half wave plate, and low-intensity laser illumination under
Profile B, yielded spatial photocurrent maps that exhibited features suggestive of under-
lying microstructural organization. Specifically, the maps revealed distinct micro-regions
with consistent or correlated photocurrent generation behavior, even in the absence of
clear polarization dependence.
These recurring features bear a notable visual resemblance to the surface morphologies
previously reported in the literature [31]. In particular, SEM micrographs highlight the
polycrystalline nature of Sb2S3 thin films. The grains observed range from sub-micron
sizes up to 4 µm² and display compact, irregular geometries with sharply defined bound-
aries - attributes that contribute to the material’s known anisotropic optoelectronic char-
acteristics.
The spatial photocurrent maps generated in this study, presented in figure 4.3, exhibit
domain-like features whose dimensions and distribution resemble the grain patterns repor-
ted in SEM micrographs. These regions remain visible across different polarization angles
and are accompanied by localized variations in photocurrent, implying a potential correla-
tion with underlying differences in crystallinity or interface structure. Similar modulations
also appear in the polarization-dependent differential maps, where specific areas undergo
recurring intensity shifts as the polarization angle is varied. Although these visual cues
fall short of confirming grain boundaries, they point to the possibility that, under stable
and well-calibrated conditions, LBIC mapping can qualitatively reflect microstructural
characteristics within the absorber layer.

variation relative to its mean current. In contrast, Sb2S3 exhibited a smaller absolute
standard deviation of 0.0074 µA, but a proportionally higher fluctuation of 2.1% relative
to its mean. This indicates that Sb2S3 is more sensitive to changes in polarization angle,
despite generating less current overall.
Figure 4.7: Polarization-Dependent Isc Trends at Selected Spots for Sb2S3 (left) and
Sb2Se3 (right) Samples.
This behavior is visually reinforced in figure 4.7, which displays the polarization-dependent
short-circuit current (Isc) curves for three selected spots on each sample. Sb2S3 exhibits
sharper, more abrupt sinusoidal fluctuations with more pronounced peak-to-valley vari-
ations.
In contrast, Sb2Se3 displays smoother, more stable curves, suggesting that its
photocurrent response is less influenced by polarization and potentially more isotropic in
character.
A possible explanation for the differences in performance between antimony selenide
(Sb2Se3) and antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) lies in the distinct crystallographic morphology
of their absorber layers. The vertically aligned, columnar crystal structure observed in
Sb2Se3 promotes more eﬀicient carrier transport along the [001] direction - directly from
the absorber to the electrodes - minimizing recombination losses and supporting higher
photocurrent generation and overall eﬀiciency. This vertically structured transport path-
way may also explain the larger gaps observed between maximum and minimum current
values across the scanned regions: when local defects or morphological disruptions occur,
they can effectively sever the vertical transport channels, leading to sharp local drops in
current collection. In contrast, the more compact and irregular grain structure of Sb2S3
results in lower average current but also a more uniform current distribution, with smaller
deviations between high and low regions. Finally, the smoother and more complex surface
texture of Sb2Se3 - as opposed to the more random and less ordered morphology of Sb2S3
- may contribute to its relatively diminished sensitivity to polarization.
The inherent
symmetry and optical uniformity in Sb2Se3 likely reduce the degree to which polarization
angle influences light absorption and charge generation, resulting in a less pronounced
anisotropic response across the polarization spectrum, as visually reinforced in figure 4.5.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
This work presents a systematic study of the spatially resolved photocurrent response in
thin-film solar cells based on antimony chalcogenides - specifically Sb2S3 and Sb2Se3 -
using laser beam induced current (LBIC) mapping. The goal was to evaluate local vari-
ations in short-circuit current under linearly polarized illumination, investigate potential
anisotropic photoresponse, and explore how absorber morphology and interfacial structure
affect device behavior. The study was divided into three experimental phases, each defined
by a different configuration of polarization optics, laser intensity profile, and sample ma-
terial.
Phase 1: Sb2S3 - Quarter wave plate - Laser Profile A (average: 12.07 µW,
peak-to-valley variation: 0.99%)
The device exhibited predominantly isotropic behavior with minimal spatial or angular
dependence. After applying correction factors for laser intensity fluctuations and Z-axis
drift, the average recorded current was 1.71 µA, with an average coeﬀicient of variation
of 5.4%. The relative difference in generated current across the polarization spectrum
reached 5.26% of the average current. Sinusoidal modulation of the short-circuit current
at selected locations mirrored the intensity fluctuations of the laser, suggesting no intrinsic
polarization-dependent photoresponse.
Phase 2: Sb2S3 - Half wave plate - Laser Profile B (average: 2.05 µW, peak-
to-valley variation: 2.93%)
At lower laser power, the spatial maps revealed finer grain-like structures with greater
visual clarity. After applying the necessary correction factors, the average recorded cur-
rent decreased to 0.348 µA. The coeﬀicient of variation dropped to 1.6%, and the current
difference across the polarization sweep was limited to 2.13% of the average current. While
the overall photocurrent trends closely followed the expected sinusoidal modulation im-
posed by laser intensity fluctuations, small but consistent deviations were observed at
specific locations, suggesting localized, subtle polarization-dependent behavior that may
point to weak anisotropic effects at the microscale.
Phase 3: Sb2Se3 - Half wave plate - Laser Profile B (average: 2.05 µW, peak-
to-valley variation: 2.93%)
Sb2Se3 devices demonstrated a higher average photocurrent of 2.26 µA after corrections,
reflecting their improved crystallinity and higher power conversion eﬀiciency (5.34%) com-
pared to Sb2S3 (1.28%). Although grain-like spatial features were observed, they were
46
==PAGE:53==
broader and less distinct than those seen in Phase 2. The coeﬀicient of variation increased
to 7%, but the relative photocurrent fluctuation across polarization angles remained small
at 1.95%. These findings align with the device’s superior short-circuit current density (Jsc
= 30 mA/cm²) relative to Sb2S3 (Jsc = 10 mA/cm²), and suggest a more stable but less
structured polarization response. Once again, sinusoidal trends matched the laser power
pattern rather than revealing intrinsic anisotropy.
Final Remarks
The use of Light Beam Induced Current (LBIC) mapping as a diagnostic tool for analyzing
the spatial performance of thin-film solar cells - particularly within the framework of the
configuration and equipment used in this study - proved to be both insightful and chal-
lenging. While the technique offers potential for high-resolution spatial characterization,
the current experimental setup introduced several limitations that ultimately hindered the
extraction of definitive conclusions.
Mechanical instabilities, such as the gradual Z-axis drift of the sample stage, and issues
with sensor sensitivity, connection reliability, and laser power control significantly im-
pacted the accuracy and consistency of the results. These factors introduced variability
into the current measurements and map visualizations, making it necessary to interpret
all data within a margin of uncertainty. Particularly at higher laser intensities, systematic
distortions and imaging artifacts further reduced the dependability of the outputs.
Despite these limitations, certain expected features associated with the antimony sulfide
(Sb2S3) absorber layer - based on prior literature and theoretical predictions - did emerge
during the experimental process. Specifically, spatial maps revealed grain-like formations
in both current generation and polarization-dependent differential data, which bore re-
semblance to grain boundaries observed in SEM imagery. However, due to the resolution
limitations and the influence of experimental noise, it was not possible to reliably match
these observed current features to specific crystallographic grains or defects. This lack
of certainty restricts further application of the method for tasks such as morphological
analysis, defect localization, or targeted optimization.
Moreover, the observed sinusoidal trends in short-circuit current (Isc) across polarization
angles suggested a recurring, though non-causal, correlation with laser intensity fluctu-
ations. While this reinforces the sensitivity of the device to polarization-dependent con-
ditions, it also highlights the influence of external variables that may obscure intrinsic
device behavior.
In its current form, the system provides only limited diagnostic value for advanced solar
cell evaluation. More robust instrumentation, automated focusing mechanisms, improved
vibration isolation, and more homogeneous sample fabrication would be essential in future
work to unlock the full potential of LBIC-based spatial mapping. Until such improvements
are implemented, the technique remains a valuable but imprecise tool for qualitative insight
rather than quantitative analysis.
