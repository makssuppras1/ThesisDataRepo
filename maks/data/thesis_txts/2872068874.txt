Abstract
The tramp shipping industry plays an important role in global trade, particularly in the
transportation of bulk commodities. Tramp ship operators face complex decision­making
challenges due to factors such as fluctuating fuel costs and various route specific restric­
tions. This thesis develops a mixed­integer programming model to determine the optimal
route of a tramp ship transporting a single type of cargo between predetermined origin
and destination ports. The model incorporates cargo intake planning, bunker (fuel) pur­
chase decisions, and compliance with draft restrictions which determine how deep a ship
can reach. Additionally, a two­stage stochastic model is developed to account for uncer­
tainty in bunker prices and a Benders decomposition algorithm is developed as an alter­
native solution method. A priori information is used to strengthen the model formulations
to improve the efficiency of the models. In addition to addressing the core optimization
problem, this thesis presents an extensive review of contributions to tramp ship routing
and scheduling problems (TSRSP) from 2020 to April 2025, complimenting the existing
state of the art literature review from 2019. The results of the thesis offer insights for
tramp shipping operators into route selection, cargo intake planning and bunker purchase
decisions, and the models developed can serve as decision­support tools.

1
Introduction
The international maritime shipping industry plays a critical role in global trade, handling
approximately 80% of the world’s cargo transportation in terms of weight. In 2023, mar­
itime trade volumes reached over 12 billion tons (UNCTAD, 2024). Maritime shipping
services are generally divided into three categories: liner, tramp and industrial. Liner ser­
vices operate on fixed schedules and routes, primarily transporting containers and gen­
eral cargo. Tramp shipping is more flexible, serving spot cargo demand and handling bulk
and tanker cargo under contracts of affreightment, which specify that a certain quantity of
cargo must be transported between designated ports within a set time frame. Industrial
operators are companies that transport their own goods, rather than providing shipping
services for others.
Tramp shipping is mainly responsible for the transportation of essential raw materials
such as iron ore, coal and grain. These commodities are typically moved in large volumes,
making tramp shipping an important link in global supply chains for industries such as steel
production, agriculture, and energy. They are carried in specialized vessels known as bulk
carriers, which are designed to maximize cargo capacity and operational efficiency. Bulk
carriers can vary in size and shape and have different characteristics. Due to efficiency
and scale, tramp shipping accounts for almost 50% of the world’s seaborne trade (J. Gao
et al., 2023).
The tramp shipping industry faces several operational challenges, particularly fluctuating
fuel prices and complex routing logistics, both of which impact profitability. Maritime fuel,
also known as bunker, is one of the most significant operating costs for bulk carriers,
sometimes accounting for more than 50% of total voyage expenses (Wang et al., 2024).
Bunker prices are volatile, influenced by market dynamics and geopolitical events, and
add uncertainty to voyage planning. Routing logistics are further complicated by multiple
factors such as draft (how deep a ship can reach) and weight restrictions, canal tolls, and
taxes on CO2 emission. Cargo intake planning, involving decisions about which cargo to
load, where, and in what quantities, add another layer of complexity to voyage planning
in tramp shipping.
Bunker can be purchased either at spot prices, which reflect the current market rate at the
time of bunkering, or through forward contracts, which lock in a predetermined price for fu­
ture delivery. The spot market offers flexibility and can be advantageous when short­term
bunker prices are expected to decline or remain stable. However, it exposes operators
to price volatility, which may be problematic on long voyages or when sailing via ports
with unpredictable supply conditions or high demand. In contrast, forward contracts en­
able operators to lock in a fixed bunker price in advance, reducing exposure to market
fluctuations. However, forward contracts typically come at a premium over spot prices.
1.1
Research objectives
Tramp shipping operates in a complex environment where bunker fuel optimization and
cargo intake planning must be carefully balanced to maximize profits and adhere to reg­
ulatory restrictions. This thesis aims to address these challenges by developing models
and a methodology to solve a tramp ship routing problem with bunker optimization and
load line zones (geographical regions that impose draft restrictions). The following re­
search question is addressed:

==PAGE:9==
What is the most profitable route between predetermined origin and destina­
tion ports for a given type of bulk carrier transporting a single type of cargo at
a fixed freight rate; how much cargo should be carried; at which ports should
bunkering take place and how much bunker should be purchased, taking into
account operational bunker constraints, and draft and weight restrictions at
ports, canals and load line zones?
1.2
Contribution
This thesis contributes with a mixed­integer programming (MIP) model, designed to de­
termine the optimal route, cargo intake and bunkering decisions, subject to bunker, draft
and weight constraints. An extension is presented where bunker purchase quantities are
modeled as discrete stepwise values. In addition, a two­stage stochastic model is devel­
oped to address uncertain bunker prices. It is modeled such that routing and cargo intake
decisions are made before bunker prices are revealed and bunkering decisions are made
after prices are known. The model is presented with two extensions: bunker purchase
quantities as discrete stepwise values, and considering bunker purchase at the origin
port as decisions that are made before bunker prices are known. A Benders decomposi­
tion algorithm is developed as an alternative solution method to the two­stage stochastic
model and constraints derived from a priori information are proposed to improve solution
efficiency. The novel modeling features of this work include:
• The inclusion of draft and weight restrictions at load line zones and canals.
• Modeling cargo load as a continuous decision variable, which is not commonly ap­
plied in tramp ship routing models.
The thesis also contributes with an extensive literature review on contributions to the tramp
ship routing and scheduling problem between 2020 and April 2025, complimenting the
existing state of the art literature review from 2019.
1.3
Motivation
The results of this thesis are expected to be of value to shipping companies, such as Navi
Merchants A/S, which provided data for model development. The insights are expected to
be integrated in the company to support decision­making. Currently, shipping routes are
evaluated by manually calculating expected profit from different sailing routes and bunker
purchase quantities, which can be time consuming and may not result in the most prof­
itable option. Therefore, the optimization models developed in the thesis can be valuable
tools to support the decision­making process and enhance its efficiency.
1.4
Thesis outline
The thesis is organized as follows: A literature review is conducted in Chapter 2. A prob­
lem description and a MIP model is proposed in Chapter 3. Theory on stochastic optimiza­
tion is explained and a two­stage stochastic model, a Benders decomposition algorithm
and constraints based on a priori information are proposed in Chapter 4. The test in­
stance used for analysis and the implementation of models and algorithms is described
in Chapter 5. The results and analysis are presented in Chapter 6. Finally, the results are
discussed and concluding remarks are presented in Chapter 7.

2
Literature review
The purpose of this literature review is to provide context for the research conducted in
this thesis. A comprehensive summary of recent advancements in the tramp ship rout­
ing and scheduling problem (TSRSP) is presented. In most of the literature, tramp ship
routing is considered jointly with scheduling aspects so this gives a complete overview
of the relevant research, even though scheduling aspects are not studied in this thesis.
Additionally, a review of existing literature on bunker optimization with stochastic bunker
prices is conducted to provide context for the two­stage stochastic model developed.
Academic contributions to routing and scheduling problems in maritime transportation
have increased in recent years and are summarized in the review papers by Christiansen
et al. (2013) and Pache et al. (2019). Ksciuk et al. (2023) summarize literature in maritime
routing and scheduling problems with uncertainty. The state and trends in tramp ship
routing and scheduling from 2013 to 2019 are discussed in the review paper by Pache
et al. (2019).
The remainder of this literature review is structured as follows: In Section 2.1 literature
on the TSRSP from 2020 to April 2025 is summarized, continuing the review conducted
by Pache et al. (2019) on literature from 2013 to 2019. This summary follows the same
structure as the earlier review and provides an insight into the current state and trends of
the TSRSP. In Section 2.2 literature on bunker optimization with stochastic bunker prices
is reviewed.
2.1
Recent contributions to the TSRSP
Essentially, the TSRSP involves the planning of routes and schedules for a heteroge­
neous fleet of ships, ensuring that cargo demand is delivered at the lowest operational
cost while revenue is maximized. This typically includes assigning ships to cargoes, de­
termining the optimal order of port visits, and scheduling each ship’s arrival and departure
times for a fleet of tramp vessels. In addition to mandatory contracted cargoes, which are
cargoes that must be transported, some approaches include the option of transporting
spot cargoes, which are optional cargoes. Furthermore, the treatment of shiploads differ
between approaches. Some consider full­shiploads, where the transportation of a single
cargo must be carried out before moving on to the next cargo assignment. Other allow
the option of mixing cargoes, i.e. ships transporting multiple cargoes at a time. Some
approaches even consider dividing cargo demand between the same ports on different
ships.
Recent contributions to the TSRSP, published between 2020 and April 2025 were identi­
fied using DTU Findit and Google Scholar, based on the keywords: ‘routing’, ‘scheduling’,
and ‘tramp ship’ or ‘dry bulk’. Figure 2.1 shows a histogram of the number of publications
per year which indicates that research in this area remains active.
In the following subsections, literature on the TSRSP, published between 2020 and April
2025, is reviewed and categorized based on extensions of the TSRSP. Some studies
research multiple extensions and are placed in the category deemed most applicable.

In the approach by J. Gao et al. (2023), a speed variable is included in the TSRSP and
cargo demands for the planning period are known beforehand, with time window require­
ments given for cargo loading and unloading. They use an extended space­time network
to capture operational decisions over the planning horizon and formulate a MIP model to
optimize them. The model is tested on data from a grain shipping company’s dry bulk
shipping fleet and results are analyzed for different cargo demands and penalties for not
meeting demand. The speed variables and the discrete time windows are fixed in the test
instances. J. Gao et al. (2023) evaluate the computational performance of their MIP and
its linear relaxation. They conclude that the model is very tight and can be solved within
a reasonable amount of time.
Louzada et al. (2021) also take a variable speed approach to the TSRSP. They consider
three speed profiles. In their approach, all cargoes are considered contracted cargoes
and the proposed model includes time window requirements for loading and unloading,
and the option to split cargo onto multiple ship. A MIP formulation is presented and tested
on six real­life instances from a tramp shipping company. The operational viability and
cost calculation of the results from the MIP model were validated by three shipping plan­
ners and compared with the company’s solutions. The MIP formulation reduced the total
operating cost in all instances up to 7%. The instances took up to 16 hours computational
time to reach optimality.
L. Li et al. (2022) consider the influence of both the speed and payload on fuel consump­
tion in the TSRSP. The speed is modeled with a discretized speed variable. A mixed
integer non­linear programming model (MINLP) and a set partitioning model is estab­
lished. They propose a Branch­and­Price approach to solve the model. The efficiency of
the Branch­and­Price approach is significantly improved compared with solving the model
directly with the CPLEX solver. Test instances are randomly generated and the results
show that considering speed and payload reduces the fuel consumption by 2.42% on
average compared with only considering speed.

2.1.2
TSRSP with bunker optimization
Although less frequently explored, bunkering decisions can have a big impact on tramp
ship routing and scheduling due to their influence on operational costs. Bunker consump­
tion is the main variable cost of tramp ship voyages and therefore an important aspect to
consider.
He et al. (2024) develop optimization models for determining the optimal route, sailing
speed and bunkering strategy for a single LNG­fueled tramp ship operating on a prede­
termined shipping line with full shipload and time windows for loading and unloading. They
present both arc­based and leg­based MIP models to solve test instances. The models
are formulated under assumptions specific to LNG operating conditions which may limit
their applicability to the broader TSRSP research area.
Omholt­Jensen et al. (2023) study the tramp ship routing and scheduling problem with
bunker optimization (TSRSPBO), considering time windows for loading and unloading
and both contracted and spot cargoes, while assuming constant sailing speed. A novelty
in their model is the inclusion of flexible cargo quantities, where cargo loads are treated
as decision variables. They propose a solution method based on a MIP path flow formu­
lation, using Dantzig­Wolfe decomposition with pre­generated routes. The results show
that flexible cargo quantities can have a significant impact on profitability, as they enable
transporting larger quantities of the same cargo or taking on more spot cargoes. The
solution method can solve instances with up to 30 cargoes, 10 vessels, and 10 bunker
ports within 30 minutes, indicating that larger instances would require improved solution
techniques.
Wang et al. (2024) present a simplified version of the TSRSPBO that focuses on the core
problem, optimizing route, speed, and bunkering decisions, while excluding additional
complexities present in other models. They present a MIP model and test its implementa­
tion on a small example instance. Compared to the more advanced approach by Omholt­
Jensen et al. (2023), this study offers a more foundational contribution to the TSRSPBO
literature.
2.1.3
TSRSP with environmental regulations
Growing awareness of environmental issues puts tramp ship companies under increasing
pressure. New regulations, such as the International Maritime Organization’s (IMO) Car­
bon Intensity Indicator (CII), place pressure on shipping companies to reduce emissions,
influencing routing, speed, and fuel decisions.
In the publication by Huang et al. (2024), a time efficiency and carbon dioxide emission
multi­objective optimization algorithm (MOOA) is proposed to solve the TSRSP. The ap­
proach considers full shiploads, time windows for loading and unloading and integrates
ballast and laden sailing speed as decision parameters. The validity of the MOOA and

approach. They propose an adaptive genetic algorithm to solve the problem, which is
tested on operational data from a coastal shipping company. Their results demonstrate
its ability to improve the CII rating while also reducing operational costs.
2.1.4
TSRSP with uncertainties
Many elements in tramp ship routing and scheduling are subjected to uncertainty. In
recent studies, uncertainty of various elements such as waiting time, cargo demand or
voyaging times have been included in the TSRSP to improve the robustness and realism
of decision models.
M. Li et al. (2020) analyze the impact of the Northern Sea Route on CO2 emissions. Their
TSRSP assumes full shiploads and is extended to include uncertain availability of spot
cargo. They propose a two­stage stochastic programming model. In the first stage they
decide which deterministic cargoes to accept, their pickup time and assign ships to cargo.
At the second stage they decide which spot cargoes to accept as well as making routing
and scheduling decisions. The TSRSP with uncertainties is extended in the study by M. Li
et al. (2022) to include carbon tax and speed optimization, using a linear approximation to
model the relationship between speed and fuel consumption. They solve randomly gen­
erated instances by sample average approximation (SAA) and propose pre­processing
procedures and inequalities to speed up the solution process. The authors conclude that
the Northern Sea Route improves gross margins and can reduce the CO2 emissions for
tramp operators. Another paper, by Liu et al. (2024), examines the Northern Sea Route
and explores an optimal ship scheduling scheme of tramp ships between Asia and Europe
considering the opening of the Arctic route. They introduce a decision variable related to
whether to build vessels with ice­breaking ability when year­round navigation becomes
possible via the Arctic route. The results demonstrate the economic viability of investing
in ice­breaking vessels for year­round Arctic navigation.
Fuentes et al. (2024) propose a TSRSP with bunker optimization where waiting times and
bunker prices are stochastic. They consider a single ship and include variable speed and
draft restrictions at ports. Uncertainties are represented by scenarios and they formulate
a four­stage model to solve the problem. Their findings indicate that bunker price and its
variability are the primary drivers of port selection. They also analyze an extension of the
model that incorporates risk aversion and conclude that under risk averse preferences,
ports with longer waiting times may become less attractive, even if they offer lower fuel
prices.
J. Gao and Wang (2021) consider the TSRSP with navigation risk as a stochastic vari­
able. They consider an environment where each ship has a pre­determined origin and
destination and no decisions on cargo are included. They develop a robust optimization

model and propose a matheuristic based on adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS)
for column generation. The method can solve instances with up to 120 cargoes, 30 ves­
sels, and 10 bunker ports within one hour, significantly improving the performance of the
solution method proposed in Omholt­Jensen et al. (2023).
2.1.5
Integrated TSRSP models
Much of the prior research on TSRSP is somewhat fragmented, considering various con­
straints and additional elements. A recent approach has integrated many of these ele­
ments into one model.
Tamburini et al. (2025) introduce a unified TSRSP model that incorporates contract and
spot cargoes, speed optimization, chartering costs, bunker contracts, and hull cleaning.
They propose a dynamic column generation approach to solve large instances. By com­
paring their results with historical results from a case company, they find that their ap­
proach performs 7% better, assuming only first­day information is available at the start of
the planning horizon.
2.1.6
Summary of recent TSRSP contributions
To summarize Subsections 2.1.1­2.1.5, various elements have been researched in the
TSRSP area, with an increasing number of extensions introduced in recent years. Speed
optimization is becoming increasingly integrated in TSRSP approaches and is included in
many studies. Bunker optimization has been addressed in a few studies, with the models
proposed by Omholt­Jensen et al. (2023, 2025), Fuentes et al. (2024) and Tamburini et
al. (2025) providing the most comprehensive ones to date. Recent years have seen an
increase in studies taking into account environmental aspects, either optimizing carbon
dioxide emissions or addressing new environmental regulations. The addition of various
uncertainties has become more prevalent in recent years, with studies accounting for
uncertainty in parameters such as cargo demand, waiting and voyaging times, bunker
costs, or navigation risk, making recent formulations of the TSRSP more comprehensive
and realistic.
Many approaches rely on solvers to solve MIPs directly but column generation and Branch
and Price algorithms are also widely used. In the models where uncertainties are present,a
wide range of solution methods is applied, such as multi­stage programming models and
various algorithms or heuristics.

2.2
Bunker optimization with stochastic bunker prices
Bunker optimization with stochastic bunker prices has been addressed in some papers,
with most applications focusing on liner shipping problems, where shipping routes are
predetermined and routing decisions are not considered.
Beyond the study by Fuentes et al. (2024) presented in Subsection 2.1.4, only a few
papers, those by Besbes and Savin (2009) and Lashgari et al. (2021), consider routing and
bunkering decisions with stochastic bunker prices. Besbes and Savin (2009) proposed
heuristics of stochastic dynamic programming to solve a bunker optimization problem
where bunker prices are generated by a combination of crude oil price forecasts and
correction factors modeled with a Markovian structure. Lashgari et al. (2021) introduce
a scenario­based two­stage stochastic model where bunker prices are represented by
scenarios.
Stefanakos and Schinas (2014) and Gu et al. (2018) incorporate risk preferences in their
models with stochastic bunker prices by including Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) con­
straints. Stefanakos and Schinas (2014) develop a mixed integer second order cone
programming (MISOCP) model using a distribution free method to model bunker price
uncertainty, only requiring their lower and upper bounds, means and covariances. Gu
et al. (2018) formulate a two­stage stochastic programming model where bunker prices
are represented by scenarios.
Chew et al. (2014) formulate a multistage model for a bunker optimization problem with
stochastic bunker consumption and prices. The evolution of bunker prices is modeled as
a Markovian process and prices are represented by scenarios. They adopt a modified
rolling horizon method to tackle the problem.
Overall, the most common approach for modeling stochastic bunker prices is to represent
them by scenarios, which is also applied in this thesis. The models are typically formulated
and solved as two­stage or multistage stochastic models, which is also the approach taken
in this thesis.

Description and mathematical modeling
This chapter provides an explanation of the tramp ship routing problem with bunker opti­
mization and load line zones addressed in this thesis and presents the MIP model devel­
oped to solve it. A detailed problem description is given in Section 3.1. Assumptions and
notation used in the MIP model is listed in Section 3.2 and the full MIP model is presented
in Section 3.3.
3.1
Problem description
Compared to most TSRSP approaches where problems are typically considered on tac­
tical level, this thesis focuses on an operational level variant referred to as the tramp ship
routing problem with bunker optimization and load line zones. It examines the routing,
bunkering, and cargo loading decisions for a single ship tasked with transporting a single
type of bulk cargo between predetermined origin and destination ports, aiming to maxi­
mize profits.
The ship can carry multiple bunker grades, each with its own tank. Each tank has an
upper capacity limit determined by physical capacity and a lower capacity limit to ensure
safe operations. Bunkering of different grades cannot occur simultaneously because the
bunker tanks are often accessed through the same piping system and must therefore be
carried out sequentially.
As the ship sails between ports, it passes through various load line zones, which are geo­
graphical areas that impose restrictions on the maximum allowable draft. The restrictions
reflect the potential hazards present in different zones. A ship’s draft is defined as the
distance between the waterline and the ship’s keel as shown in Figure 3.1 and is directly
impacted by the weight of the ship. The deadweight tonnage (DWT) of a ship is defined
as the sum of cargo and bunker weight. Consequently, draft restrictions can be inter­
preted as limits on deadweight tonnage. Figure 3.2a shows the load line markings on a
ship’s hull that indicate how the maximum allowable draft changes across different load
line zones and Figure 3.2b illustrates load line zones on a map. In addition to draft restric­
tions, some load line zones impose carbon tax regulations and require the use of specific
bunker grades.

All ports impose draft restrictions by specifying a maximum allowable draft, and each port

The decisions that need to be made when addressing the tramp ship routing problem with
bunker optimization and load line zones are: (1) the cargo quantity to be transported, (2)
the route between origin and destination ports, i.e. selecting which bunker ports to stop
at, if any, and (3) the quantity of each bunker grade to purchase at each port. Bunker
constraints, as well as load line, weight and draft constraints, must be respected. The
overall objective of the problem is to maximize profit which is defined as the revenue from
cargo transport minus operational costs.

4
Modelling under uncertainty
In Model 1 bunker prices were assumed to be deterministic but historical data show that
they vary significantly over time and across regions. This chapter presents a two­stage
stochastic model to address the tramp ship routing problem with bunker optimization and
load line zones with uncertain bunker prices. A Benders decomposition algorithm is de­
veloped as an alternative solution method and a priori information is used to strengthen
the model formulations to improve the efficiency of the models. In Section 4.1 theory on
stochastic optimization is introduced. In Section 4.2 the two­stage stochastic model is
presented and in Section 4.3 the Benders decomposition algorithm is presented. In Sec­
tion 4.4 additional constraints are presented to strengthen the model formulations based
on a priori information.
4.1
Stochastic optimization theory
Ruszczyński and Shapiro (2003) discuss stochastic optimization in detail. Uncertainty is
often given as discrete scenarios, each with a certain probability of being realized. Let
Ωbe the finite set of all scenarios and π(ω) be the probability of scenario ω ∈Ωbeing
realized. Assume there exists a set of feasible decisions X and an objective function
F(x, ξ(ω)) of decision vector x ⊂X and a finite dimensional vector ξ(ω) containing the
uncertain parameters. The realization of the uncertain parameters is obtained in a sce­
nario ω. The maximization of F(x, ξ(ω)) subject to x ∈X depends on ω, so for different
realizations of the uncertain parameters different optimal solutions are obtained.
If full information about the uncertain vector ξ is known in advance, the optimization prob­
lem reduces to a deterministic problem. Solving the deterministic problem for all scenar­
ios results in multiple solutions, each of which is optimal for a specific scenario but may
not perform well across all scenarios. The average optimal value across all scenarios is
known as the expected value optimal­in­hindsight solution (OiH). The OiH solution rep­
resents the expected value of choosing the best possible decision after uncertainty has
been revealed. In practice this is not possible since some decisions need to be made
before uncertainty is resolved.
Stochastic optimization can also be solved by a two­stage stochastic model, which aims to
find a decision that performs well across all scenarios. In this framework, some decisions
are made before the uncertainty is realized. These are called first­stage (here­and­now)
decisions. Other decisions, known as second­stage (recourse) decisions, can be adjusted
after the uncertainty is revealed. In a two­stage model, decision variables are separated
into first­stage variables, which remain fixed across all scenarios, and second­stage vari­
ables, which adapt to each scenario after uncertainty is revealed. In the case of discrete
scenarios, a two stage stochastic model can be written as:

4.3
Benders decomposition algorithm
A tailored Benders decomposition algorithm is developed to solve Model 2. In Benders
decomposition the model is decomposed into a master problem and scenario­based sub­
problems. The master problem represents the first­stage decisions and the scenario­
based second­stage decisions are analyzed in the subproblems. The overall problem is
solved iteratively. At each iteration the optimal solution of the master problem provides
first­stage decisions which are fixed in the subproblems. Cuts are added to the master
problem based on the results of the subproblem to reduce the feasible region and to guide
the solution process towards optimality. The next iteration begins after cuts are added to
the master problem. Assuming a feasible solution exists, the process repeats until the
optimal solution is obtained. The master problem, subproblems, cuts, and an explanation
of the algorithm are presented as follows:

Deriving constraints from a priori information
Additional constraints can be derived from a priori information to improve the solution
efficiency of Model 2 and the Benders decomposition algorithm by tightening the model
formulations. Both approaches consider all permutations of routes that start at the origin
port and end at the destination port, including voyages with multiple bunker port visits and
unnecessarily long voyages.
Based on a priori information, route configurations with more than a certain number of
bunker port visits may be unrealistic and not worth considering. This information can be
used to strengthen the formulations of both Model 2 and the master problem in the Ben­
ders decomposition by introducing the following constraint (4.32) which limits the number

7
Discussion and conclusion
In this thesis a tramp ship routing problem with bunker optimization and load line zones
was studied. A mixed­integer programming (MIP) model was developed to optimize rout­
ing, bunkering and cargo load decisions for a single tramp ship transporting a single type
of cargo between predetermined origin and destination ports. In addition, a two­stage
stochastic model was developed to account for uncertain bunker prices, along with a Ben­
ders decomposition algorithm. Constraints based on a priori information were proposed to
improve solution efficiency. Furthermore, an extensive literature review on contributions
to the tramp ship routing and scheduling problem from 2020 to April 2025 was conducted,
complimenting the existing state of the art literature review from 2019.
In the test instance, the optimal route includes a stop at one bunker port, and analysis
of each individual scenario shows that none has more than one bunker stop in the op­
timal solution. The analysis of the test instance shows that hire costs and bunker costs
constitute the two largest operational expenses of tramp shipping voyages, while agency
fees and fixed routing costs are negligible in comparison. Sensitivity analysis suggests
that variation in freight rates, hire cost and minimum bunker purchase quantities at ports
have a limited impact on expected profit and the optimal solution. However, the analysis
shows that high agency fees may lead to fewer bunker stops in the optimal solution. The
initial and ending bunker loads also have an impact on the results of the model. Keeping
these loads as low as possible can lead to a profit increase because it allows for increased
cargo load.
Computational analysis shows that the two­stage stochastic model can solve instances
with up to 36 ports within 11 minutes.
The models developed can serve as decision­support tools to evaluate whether to hedge
bunker prices via forward contracts or to wait for spot prices to be revealed. Comparing
results from the two­stage stochastic model (Model 2) with spot price predictions, with
results from the MIP model (Model 1) with forward contract bunker prices, can provide an
insight into whether waiting for spot prices yields a higher expected profit. The quality of
this comparison, however, depends on representative spot price predictions, represented
as scenarios, which can be difficult to generate. Ideally price scenario generation should
incorporate time­dependent fluctuations, as ports scheduled later in voyages are subject
to greater price uncertainty due to the longer time horizon.
A limitation of this thesis is that the proposed models have only been tested on one in­
stance, so the results may not be reflective of other instances. Further testing should
be made to verify the representativeness of the results and analysis. Additionally, as­
sumptions made in the model such as fixed speed and deterministic sailing times may
not reflect actual operational conditions.
Future work could investigate how the initial and ending bunker loads can be optimized
across cargo assignments to maximize profit considering a longer time horizon. This
could be achieved by integrating load line zone constraints and other features from the
proposed models into existing routing and scheduling problems. Another area which could
be explored further is how to improve the feasibility cuts in the Benders decomposition
algorithm to improve its efficiency in instances with many ports and routes. Future work
could also develop a method to solve the two­stage stochastic model with discrete bunker
purchase quantities considering many scenarios.
