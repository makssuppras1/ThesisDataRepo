Abstract
Power-to-X plants and district heating offer interesting synergies as they can utilise excess
heat to produce heat at a lower cost and reduce emissions to align with the net zero
emissions scenario. The work presents two models to identify, analyse and calculate the
synergies between excess heat from Power-to-X plants and District heating.
The first model is based on an electrochemical model of an Alkaline cell to evaluate the
energy and mass balance of a green hydrogen plant consistent with experimental designs
and provides the ability to scale and calculate excess heat from water electrolysis. The
model is based on a Matlab Simulink model, and it can be configured to operate at different
working conditions and scaled to other power loads.
The second model is based on a cooling system that separates the thermal and electro-
chemical models. The second model consists of two parts. Firstly a geographical mapping
in GIS is performed to find information about local heat production, energy prices and
fuel types and existing district heating networks. Secondly, an optimisation model is
programmed in Python to evaluate the feasibility of utilising surplus heat from Power-to-X
to produce heat for the surrounding areas. A sensitivity analysis of different working
conditions at the Hydrogen plant is included to measure its impact on the heating cost.
The results show that recovering excess heat from water electrolysis accounts for 15-30
EUR/MWh, considerably cheaper than the actual heating price. Thus, some of the nearest
and most expensive district heating plants would replace their primary fuel to reduce the
total cost and CO2 emissions.

Introduction
Producing energy through fossil fuels has been the main engine of the development of
modern economies. On the other hand, the increasing energy demand and the commitment
to reduce greenhouse gas emissions (GHGs) are speeding up the energy transition to an
affordable, reliable and sustainable energy system, which is crucial to achieving many
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) formulated in 2015 by the United Nations General
Assembly.
1.1
World Energy Outlook
The World is facing its first global energy crisis of unprecedented complexity, and Europe
is in the middle of the problem. The situation in the 70s was about reducing dependence
on oil. However, today’s energy crisis has different dimensions: natural gas, but also
electricity, coal, climate change and food.
Market pressures are dated before the War in Ukraine, but Russia´s invasion has turned a
rapid economic rebound from COVID-19 into a full-blown energy turmoil. After falling
by 4,5 % in 2020 due to COVID-19, global energy consumption rebounded by five % in
2021, up to 14,000 Mtoe (Mega tonnes oil equivalent). According to [26], fossil fuel’s share
accounts for 80 % of total energy demand while electricity and biomass only account for
20 %.

the situation due to Europe’s dependency on Russian gas. Monthly spot prices of oil, coal

to heat their homes [21]. Therefore, this problematic situation has led Europe to look for

To shield consumers from the impacts of this complex crisis, advanced economies have
committed well over 500 billion USD [45]. In addition to diversifying Europe gas imports
from other countries, other short-term actions have included increasing power generation
through other high-carbon energy fuels like oil and coal, extending the nuclear lifecycle
and accelerating new renewables projects.
Looking back, renewables have been blamed for contributing to the run-up of energy prices.
However, as a result of the existing crisis, it can be seen that those countries with a higher
share of renewables in their energy mix are contributing to decrease electricity prices.
Electrified heat and better building efficiencies are also correlated.
According to the Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS), new policies in significant energy
markets propel annual clean energy investment to reach more than 2 trillion USD by 2030.
In that sense, renewables are expected to become the largest source of electricity by 2027,
growing almost 2400 GW, equal to the existing power capacity in China [44]. This record
will be primarily led by wind and PV deployment: Solar PV will account for more than 60
% of the expansion, and wind energy is expected to expand from 74 GW in 2021 to 109
GW in 2027.

These data show a clear commitment to developing low-carbon energy sources, but not
sufficient to meet the targets set in the Paris Agreement [63]. From 1850 to 2019, historical
cumulative CO2 emissions raised to 2400 GtCO2. Of these, around 58 % occurred between
1850 and 1989, while the exceed amount was emitted between 1990 and 2019. About
17 % of the historical amount was cast between 2010 and 2019 [65]. As a result, the
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) [57] report confirms that net emissions
of GHGs need to be reduced by 43 % by 2030 compared to emissions in 2019. Regarding
CO2 emissions, it is required to reach net zero emissions by 2050. This scenario concords
with the Paris Agreement of limiting warming to 1.5ºC.
This increasing focus on cutting emissions by 2050 has highlighted the challenge of tackling
those emissions sources harder to reduce. These emissions coincide in those sectors where
electricity is not the end-user resource. Around four-fifths of total end-user final demand
is produced by high carbon-containing fuels. On the other hand, most majority of raw
materials for chemical products are carbon-based and generate significant amounts of
CO2. Emission sources that are difficult to eliminate include long-distance transportation
(i.e. aviation and shipping), steel production, chemical manufacturing or heat supply for
off-grid applications [13].
1.2
The future of hydrogen
In 2021 Global hydrogen demand reached 94 Mt. NG accounts for approximately three-
quarters of the annual global dedicated hydrogen production, which means around 205
bcm or 6 % of total natural gas use [47]. As a result, GHG emissions due to hydrogen
production accounted for more than 900 Mt of CO2.

Fortunately, thanks to the rapid technological developments of the last few years, hydrogen
can be produced in many different ways. In that sense, it can be classified as attending to
other sources of hydrogen production as shown in fig. 6.
Figure 6: Colours of hydrogen [2]
”Blue” and ”Green” hydrogen are considered low-emissions. The term ”Blue hydrogen”
is commonly used for producing hydrogen from NG with the implementation of carbon
capture and storage (CCS). On the other hand, ”Green Hydrogen” refers to hydrogen
production from renewable energy where no carbon dioxide is emitted during production.
Over the last few years, governments have adopted new policies favouring hydrogen as
a clean energy vector in the future energy system. Even so, today’s hydrogen capacity
produced by low-emission sources is too low. However, this is expected to change in the
next few years according to the project pipeline announced by 2030 and the IEA net zero
emissions scenario (NZE) as seen in fig. 7.

Hydrogen demand remains concentrated in refining and the chemical sector as seen in
fig. 8. Even so, the emergence of new hydrogen applications in heavy transport and
high-temperature heat in Industry and power can be highlighted, with a grow up of 60 %
in 2021, but still 0.04 % of global hydrogen demand. As stated before, this growing de-
mand also coincides with the massive deployment of electrolyser plants for green hydrogen
production and other low-emission hydrogen sources.
In refining, hydrogen is used to upgrade heavy oil fractions into lighter products. In 2018,
it reached its maximum historic at 40 Mt, resulting in more than 200 Mt CO2. During
the pandemic, it dropped to around 38 Mt [46]. Even so, it is expected to recover the
same or even higher values in the following years. The highest consumers of hydrogen
demand in refining are North America and China at ten and more than 9 Mt, respec-
tively. STEPS scenario suggests that hydrogen demand in refining will reach 47Mt by 2030.
In industry, hydrogen is mainly used for ammonia production (34 Mt of hydrogen demand),
methanol (15 Mt) and DRI in steel production (5 Mt), resulting in 630 Mt of CO2 emissions.
During the pandemic, hydrogen consumption in the industrial sector remained almost
constant. Methanol production dropped by 4 % in 2020 due to transport reductions during
COVID-19 (One-third of global methanol is used in fuel applications). By 2030, hydrogen
demand in the industry is expected to increase by 11 Mt relative to 2021 levels.
In transport, Hydrogen demand will reach 30 kt by 2021. Hydrogen, ammonia and

District heating and Excess heat utilization
District heating is a system that generates and distributes heat energy to multiple buildings
within a defined area from a central heat source. The heat energy is typically sourced
from one or multiple thermal power plants, waste to energy plants, geothermal plants or
other energy sources fig. 18.
Figure 18: District Heating sample scheme
DH has become popular worldwide, with growing adoption across Europe, Asia, and
North America. In Europe, DH accounts for over half of the heat energy used in existing
buildings in many countries like Denmark (65 %) and Sweden (above 45 %) [49]. Modern
district heating systems have improved efficiency while reducing emissions. The primary
drivers for DH adoption are cost savings, energy efficiency, and improving the energy mix.
Aligning with NZE, DH will have to significantly reduce emissions and improve the energy
efficiency of existing district heating networks (DHN), especially in China and other parts
of the world.

DH networks today correspond to the second or third generation, according to Lund
et al. [53]. DH generations represent the network temperature evolution over time as
seen in fig. 19, where a decrease in temperate has marked this evolution. Lower network
temperatures cause lower losses in the system and allow the integration of low-carbon

One of the critical potential developments in DH is incorporating renewable electricity
and waste heat to generate and store heat. The electricity can be converted to heat via
heat pumps using low-temperature waste heat. Low-temperature waste heat is found as a
final product of many primary energy conversion processes in Industry or even the service
sector, such as data centres.
An important limitation for integrating industrial waste heat in DHN is the seasonal
mismatch and the competition with other renewable heat sources in the summertime
when heat demand is considerably reduced and primarily accounts for DHW. As in the
electricity market case, those heat sources with the lowest marginal cost are the first
to cover the demand. Therefore, waste heat recovery for DH must be cheaper than the
marginal production costs of the existing supply units to make this source attractive [72].

when no interruption is possible, such as solar thermal.
Excess heat (EH) during lower-demand periods could be potentially stored to substitute
fossil fuel-based heat during peak-demand periods. Different seasonal storage types are
available today: aquifers, boreholes, pits, and other tank storages [74]. In that sense, some

2.2.2
Definitions and basic principles of excess heat utilization
Waste heat recovery systems are equipment or devices that are used to capture the heat
emitted by process industries and convert it into thermal energy that can be reused [9].
The quality and quantity of heat in waste heat recovery systems are critical parameters to
consider.
The quality of heat determines the range of temperatures at which the waste heat can
be effectively harnessed. Waste heat recovery systems are designed to capture heat at
different temperature levels, ranging from low-grade to high-grade heat. The temperature,
heat transfer rate, and the waste heat stream’s chemical composition influence the heat
quality. Therefore, the higher the temperature, the higher the quality of that waste heat.
On the other hand, the quantity of heat is the amount of thermal energy generated by the
waste heat source. The heat quantity depends on the volume or flow rate of the waste
heat released and the specific heat capacity of the medium. The amount of heat energy
generated by the waste heat source determines the size and efficiency of the waste heat
recovery system needed to harness and convert the heat energy effectively.
Some of the components that will appear in our waste heat recovery system are briefly
presented below:
Heat Pump
When low-temperature waste heat is considered, HPs are typically used to boost the
temperature at a certain level according to the heat sink requirements. The HPs principle
is the same as in-house refrigerators or air conditioners. It extracts heat from a heat source
and amplifies and transfers the heat. Surrounding air, geothermal energy, water or waste
heat are typically used as the heat source for a heat pump.
As stated before, heat is transferred rather than generated, making the process more
efficient than electric boilers or heaters. The output heat usually is several times greater
than the power needed to run the HP, which uses electricity as an input. The performance
of a heat pump can be measured with the Coefficient of Performance (COP). The ideal
COP is defined as the Carnot efficiency as follows in eq. (35), where TH is the output

3.1
Hydrogen plant modelling
In this chapter, a Simulink model of an AEL plant is proposed to calculate the valuable
heat released by the system at different operating conditions.
Several mathematical models have been proposed to simulate the performance of an AEL.
The most widely used model is the one offered by Ulleberg [68], which determines the
polarisation curve of an AEL through some parameters that consider the overpotentials in
the stack. However, the Ulleberg model only takes temperature as an input parameter.
Thus, several models have been proposed recently, taking into account other parameters
such as species concentration, pressure or electrode/diaphragm distance [10],[23],[3].
Empirical correlations have also modelled the Faraday efficiency, where Ulleberg´s equation
is again the most widely used. This equation calculates the Faraday efficiency through
four parameters (experimentally obtained) at different currents and temperatures.
Therefore, developing a mathematical model through empirical correlations will represent
a precious tool to scale the performance of a single cell up to higher stack capacities
composed of hundreds of cells. This case study will use parameters from Monica´s model
[23] based on an experimental electrolyser.

Cooling system
The cooling system is essential in maintaining the nominal conditions of electrolysers and
protecting the stack. The temperature control of the Stack and, therefore, the electrolyte
is achieved by controlling the electrolyte mass flow rate through a combination of heat
exchangers placed in each stacked module. The mass flow rate negatively depends on
the gradient temperature between the inlet and the outlet temperature of the electrolyte,
which should be kept constant and at a minimum value, to ensure the correct operation of
the stack (the stack performance is temperature dependent as seen in section 3.1.1). The
large temperature gradient across the cell increase degradation and ageing rate according
to [71]. For further simplifications, the gradient temperature in the stack is fixed by ∆T,
which should not exceed 10ºC.
There are more cooling positions in the hydrogen production process. Alternating current
electricity from the wind farm is converted into DC, which releases heat in the power
converter system. The end product is also cooled down before further purification. Finally,
the hydrogen is usually compressed downstream, which requires compression interstage

Pressure effect
The pressure variation in hydrogen production affects the plant’s performance differently.
A system simulation at different pressure levels is obtained to identify these effects. Firstly,
the pressure effect on the polarisation curve can be seen in fig. 38.
Figure 38: Polarization Curve at different pressure levels (Matlab model)
The graph shows the pressure effect on the ohmic losses, which increase the voltage in the
cell along with the current density. The main consequence is that the reversible voltage
increase along with the pressure. In addition, the size of the bubble decreases along with
the pressure, which increases its density, leading to lower mobility. This effect is countered
by reducing the electrolyte resistance when bubbles get smaller due to higher pressures.
Therefore, the pressure effect on the polarisation curve is low.
The faradaic efficiency is temperature dependent, so hydrogen production remains constant
at different pressure levels at a given current density. Regarding the Voltage efficiency, it
decreases along with the pressure, as seen in the dotted curve. Finally, stack efficiency is
defined by the continuous line.

Discussion
5
Discussion
As stated by Butler et al.[18], Leading manufacturers of alkaline electrolysers typically have
Stack efficiencies of 65-70 % on an LHV basis, resulting in energy consumptions of between
44.5-52.3 kWh/kgH2. If energy consumption from auxiliary components is considered,
it will vary between 55.6 and 62 kWh/kgH2. The model set in this project estimates a
nominal energy consumption, including auxiliary consumption, of 55 kWh/kgH2, so we
can verify that the model can be regarded as valid.
On the other hand, the optimisation model shows that the levelised cost of Heat from
GreenLab excess heat ranges between 15-30 EUR/MWh, depending on each case. As
stated at the beginning of this project, little attention has been paid to feeding the DHN
with excess heat from Electrolyser plants. Even so, looking into other examples of large
heat pump projects in district heating, it is stated that prices range between 42 to 49
EUR/MWh, but considering half the COP, [31].
5.1
Future works
According to the 4th DH Generation, it would be interesting to carry out future studies to
analyse the impact of lowering the supply temperature on the cost of heat production from
surplus heat from water electrolysis with other heat sources such as seawater, geothermal
heat, data centres, etc.
The quality of the heat source mainly drives the heating cost in these cases and the
quantity, so it would be interesting to make a proper comparison to different scenarios.
In addition, heat selling revenues could considerably decrease Hydrogen’s levelised cost,
making green hydrogen a competitive solution compared to lower energy-intensive solutions
based on fossil fuels, as seen in [17].

Conclusions
6
Conclusions
The study presented has demonstrated the potential of connecting excess heat from the
GreenLab plant to District heating. The first model was used to calculate the yearly excess
heat at the Stack under different working conditions. The output of this model was then
used as an input for the optimisation model.
The results of the optimisation model, along with the GIS mapping, were suitable to
identify those cases where excess heat from GreenLab can be utilised as the primary heating
source. The model was tested for 64 scenarios, but only four were considered feasible. The
heating cost at GreenLab (including investment and operating and maintenance costs) is
considerably cheaper than the actual price in the surrounding areas. Even so, the cost of
piping makes a difference in supplying heat to those areas furthest away from the heat
source. So, although the heat produced by GreenLab is cheap, it could become more
expensive than the actual heat produced at the existing District heating plants.
The quality of the excess heat when the stack operates at a higher temperature shows a
cost reduction in energy consumption since the COP increase, and the Heat exchanger
makes it possible to exchange most of the supplied heat. The electrochemical model
works in a temperature range of 80-70ºC while the district heating network operates
between 70-90ºC. As a result, the optimisation model shows that energy consumption is
considerably reduced during summer.
In conclusion, The synergies between P2X and district heating are clear:
• Optimize energy use: P2X technology can optimise energy use by capturing waste
heat from industrial processes and converting it into useful energy. The generated
power can be supplied to the district heating system, which distributes it to various
areas. This process ensures that there is no wastage of energy and that all energy is
used efficiently.
• Reduce carbon emissions: Combining P2X with district heating can help reduce
carbon emissions significantly. By capturing waste heat and converting it into
energy, less energy is required from primary sources, such as fossil fuels, to meet the
energy demands of the areas selected. This reduces the amount of carbon emissions
generated from energy production, leading to a cleaner and more sustainable energy
future.
• Increase energy efficiency: P2X and district heating systems increase energy
efficiency by converting waste heat into valuable energy and distributing it to a DHA.
This would reduce the need for separate energy generation systems, which can be
less efficient. Combining the two technologies would also reduce transmission energy
losses, increasing energy efficiency.
Page 89 of 118
==PAGE:91==
6
Conclusions
• Stabilize energy supply: P2X and district heating systems can work together
to stabilise the energy supply. The district heating system can use waste heat as a
supplementary energy source to ensure a stable and reliable building energy supply.
This is particularly useful in areas with high energy demand, and the energy grid
may experience fluctuations.
• Promote circular economy: P2X technology promotes a circular economy by
harnessing waste heat from industrial processes and converting it into sound energy.
Combining it with district heating further strengthens this circular economy by
returning the generated power to society. This promotes sustainability, reduces
waste, and helps achieve a more sustainable energy future.
