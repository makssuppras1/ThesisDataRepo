ABSTRACT 
Background: Treatment of neurological disorders is highly challenged, because the blood brain barrier 
exerts efficient impediments limiting delivery of drugs to the brain. The blood brain barrier represents 
a vast array of complex structures, among which include endothelial cells. The endothelial protein C 
receptor (EPCR) is a well characterized membrane protein abundant on endothelial cells. The role of 
the EPCR is shown to be highly pertinent in many aspects. Some few examples are such as being 
involved in activation of protein C, hemostasis, cell signaling and cytoprotective pathways. Interaction 
between the EPCR and its ligands at the cell surface is assumed to impact cellular trafficking. On this 
basis, there is rationale in suggesting the use of this receptor as a target for carrying payloads across 
the blood brain barrier. 
Purpose: The aim with this project was to study i) whether murine brain microcapillary express the 
EPCR; ii) if the EPCR could be used as a receptor-mediated transport system for delivering payloads 
across the capillary walls of the BBB and iii) whether tissue engineered spinach could be proposed as 
a scaffold for studying receptor-mediated transport.  
Method: To investigate these questions primary murine brain and retinal endothelial cells were 
cultured on 2D monolayer under various conditions. The expression of the EPCR was assessed by 
employing methods such as siRNA transfection and blocking of the EPCR. The expression of the EPCR 
was quantitatively identified and characterized d via flow cytometry. In vivo biodistribution studies 
were included to explore if the brain EPCR could be targeted with specific antibodies. Assessment of 
data was conducted with the use of immuno-dot blotting. Furthermore, spinach was tissue engineered 
as to offer a 3D scaffold replicating ‘in vivo’ conditions for culturing of primary mammalian cells. 
Results: With flow cytometry it was possible to quantitatively characterize the expression of EPCR 
present on primary murine endothelial cells. In vivo studies successfully provided tissue lysates for the 
analysis of antibody accumulation in the brain and heart. Detection of antibodies was verified, but 
immuno-dot blotting did not provide reliable evidence of specific antibody binding to organs of 
interest.    
Conclusion: Based on experiments, demonstrating the presence of EPCR on murine endothelial cells 
is not sufficient of indicating its potential for receptor-mediated molecule transport, which would 
require further studies. Likewise, targeting of the EPCR in the murine brain is not sufficiently 
determined with immuno-dot blotting and requires application of more quantitatively methods. 
Culturing mammalian cells on engineered plant-based tissue scaffolds appeared feasible to some 
extent, however, it requires optimization of culture conditions to provide more viable cell cultures.

1 | INTRODUCTION 
The brain is the most complex organ of the human organism. This is why neurological disorders, 
treatments, and studies related to the brain and the central nervous system (CNS) also hold a high 
degree of complexity. In the past decades technological advances within several scientific fields have 
led to remarkable findings and progress within neuroscience 1–4.  
 
Examples of some of the main progressions include merging single-cell sequencing strategies and 
genetic tools within biotechnology. This has revealed the existence of likely hundred different 
neuronal and non-neuronal cell types. Furthermore, combining functional genomics and genome-
editing now allows genetic analysis in human brain tissue models 3. Increasingly higher resolution has 
improved imaging techniques in e.g. magnetic resonance imaging. On this basis a comprehensive 
cellular atlas of the mammalian brain has been mapped out 2,5. One of the greatest innovations is the 
fusion of functional neuroscience with cell biology introducing optogenetics. It represents a tool for 
controlling brain circuits with light enabling to turn neurons on or off 2–4,6. By joining computational 
and machine learning approaches 3  the interdisciplinary progress has enabled artificial intelligence to 
expose biomarker combinations, which improved diagnostics in e.g. Alzheimer’s Disease 4,7 . Molecular 
biology has shown that dopamine and serotonin, besides being cell-to-cell receptor mediators of 
intracellular signaling, attach to chromatin to regulate gene transcription 3. Within therapeutics, 
NMDA receptors are now target candidates for disorders like depression and schizophrenia, based on 
the identification of the NMDA receptor antagonists, ketamine and phencyclidineb 3. 
 
The above-mentioned findings are examples reflecting recent neuroscientific progression. However, 
successful treatment of neurological disorders remains highly challenged. A main reason for this is the 
impediments in delivering drugs to the brain. Because of that the field of drug delivery to the brain 
continues to be extensively researched 8–17. 
 
1.1 | Facts and Numbers 
As of 2018, more than 500 drug formulations were in development either in clinical trials or 
undertaking regulatory review (ref). Of these medicines, most were targeted towards brain tumors or 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD), while others aimed at treating other diseases such as chronic pain, 
Parkinsons’ disease (PD), epilepsy, genetic disorders, multiple sclerosis (MS) and amyotrophic lateral 
sclerosis (ALS)18. 
 
Examples among the medicines in progress include utilization of antibody-drug conjugates (ADC) to 
deliver chemotherapy drugs. ADC target epidermal growth factor receptors (EGFR) typically 
overexpressed in relation to brain tumors (glioblastoma). The medicine has reached phase II and is 
being tested in patients with EGFR-amplified glioblastoma 14. Tau protein tangles and beta-amyloid 
plaques are found to cause the disease progression involved with AD. Therefore, these protein 
structures have become targets for medicines in development 12. 
 
A monoclonal antibody that blocks the activity of Semaphorin 4D (SEMA4D) is in the preclinical stage. 
The protein SEMA4D plays a key role in neuroinflammatory processes associated with Huntington’s 
disease (HD) 17. Immunoglobin-like domain-containing protein 1 (LINGO1) appears to inhibit the

myelin repair associated with MS. With the intention of blocking LINGO1 production, a monoclonal 
antibody targeting this protein is in development 16. Currently, a cell therapy for ALS is in clinical trial 
phase II. The therapy is based on mesenchymal stromal cells (MCSs), customized to secrete 
neurotrophic factors to support neuron survival 15. 
 
In May 2020, WHO released a report with specific focus on neurological diseases, emphasizing the 
demand for increased attention in this field 19. The report is the first of its kind representing an 
initiative towards helping people with neurological conditions worldwide. On a global scale, 
neurological disorders are the second leading cause of death after cardiovascular diseases. This is an 
increase of 39% in the absolute number of deaths over the last three decades. Accounting for a 
combined 15.2 million deaths in 2016 stroke, attributing with more than 6 million deaths annually, is 
together with ischaemic heart disease one the world’s biggest killers. Claiming 2 million deaths in 
2016, dementia has become the fifth leading cause of death. With an estimated contribution of 60-
70%, AD is the most common cause of dementia among the 47.5 million people suffering from this 
condition. These numbers only issue mortality rates and outshine the equally important focus on 
morbidity and disability leaving the influenced with remarkably affected disability-adjusted life-years, 
and not least important the socioeconomic impacts 19–21,2223.  
 
1.2 | Redirecting Focus  
The numbers stated above speak for themselves and emphasize that contribution to studies of 
neurological diseases is highly relevant. Based on current knowledge and approaches there is a 
demand for reevaluating existing methods to take treatment to new levels beyond the borders of the 
brain. A particular hallmark is the blood-brain barrier (BBB), which has been subject to numerous 
studies that explore current approaches to and treatments of neurological conditions 24–30. 
 
Often chronic diseases, e.g. MS, AD, PD, HD, epilepsy, and chronic inflammation related to the CNS 
are associated with aberrant endothelium function 27,31–33. Interestingly, the endothelium function is 
less elucidated compared to the aforementioned neuroscientific focus areas. Active protein C (APC) 
and the endothelium protein C receptor (EPCR) are two important proteins involved in the 
endothelium functions. If impaired they have been shown to be severely implicated in diseases such 
as sepsis 34–36, ischemic stroke 24,28,37 and thrombosis 38–40. Based on the endothelium being one of the 
main components of the BBB, this work will pivot on the study of the EPCR.   
 
 
1.3 | Aim of Study and Hypothesis 
The project is divided into two parts with distinct scopes. Part I covers in vitro and in vivo identification 
and characterization of the EPCR. Part II covers identification of the EPCR in primary cells cultured on 
engineered tissue scaffolds to explore if these can be utilized for 3D cultivation.  
Current literature indicates that the EPCR might play an essential role with regard to driving 
transportation mechanisms in the brain endothelium. However, and to our knowledge, the concept 
of using the receptor as target to enable transport across the BBB has not yet been explored. This has 
led to the hypothesis that the EPCR can be exploited as a trafficking molecule.  
An emerging field within tissue engineering is using plants as 3D scaffolds for cell culturing to provide 
‘in vivo-like’ conditions. Recent studies have demonstrated this feasibility. Some examples include 
cultivation of human endothelial cells and mesenchymal stem cells. This could potentially lead to

increased understanding of underlying mechanisms related to e.g. protein expression. This current 
project replicates these studies with a modified protocol aiming for shorter ‘production time’. 
 
Main questions raised in project part I and II: 
 
(i) 
Does the brain microcapillary in mice express the EPCR? 
(ii) 
Can the EPCR be used as a receptor-mediated transport system for delivering payloads 
across the capillary wall and beyond the BBB? 
(iii) 
Can spinach be proposed as a cell scaffold to study transport mechanisms? 
 
If these questions can be successfully answered, 
 
(i) 
the EPCR could be proposed as a potential target for brain drug delivery and initiate 
further exploration of its property as trafficking molecule. 
(ii) 
it presents a novel application of the receptor in designing therapeutics targeting CNS-
related conditions. 
(iii) 
it might provide a prospect to derive information about transportation mechanisms 
involving the EPCR in a more ‘in vivo’-like setting than a monoculture can offer. 
 
Three goals and strategies are employed to test the overall addressed questions.  
 
Firstly, through in vitro studies the expression of the EPCR will be investigated by culturing primary 
murine retinal endothelial cells (mRECs) and primary murine brain endothelial cells (mBECs) on a 2D 
monolayer under different conditions. A successful outcome will provide the opportunity to conduct 
and evaluate an in vivo biodistribution study. The expression of the EPCR in the primary cells will be 
validated by various methods such as siRNA transfection and blocking of the EPCR. These applications 
will also serve to validate antibodies used in the study. The expression of the EPCR will be 
quantitatively determined via flow cytometry by use of EPCR-specific antibodies. (Part I). 
 
Secondly, using in vivo studies it will be explored whether the brain EPCR can be targeted and used 
for transportation of payloads across the BBB. A pilot study including a total of n = 6 mice (3 per group) 
will be established. In the study, EPCR-specific antibodies, and transferrin-specific antibodies (control) 
will be injected intravenously (i.v.) via the tail vein to assess if brain endothelial cells can be targeted. 
To compare if the antibodies accumulate in other organs, the heart will be retrieved. Validation and 
subsequent analyses are carried out using immuno-dot blotting. (Part I). 
 
Thirdly, spinach will be used as an engineered plant tissue scaffold to explore whether the surface 
structure as well as the microvasculature of a plant leaf can be used as a scaffold for 3D-cultivation of 
mammalian cells. It will be attempted by culturing mRECs and mBECs on decellularized spinach leaves 
to obtain a 3D culture mimicking the native environment of these cells. The subsequent analyses are 
carried out using spinning disk confocal microscopy and scanning electron microscopy. (Part II). 
 
The primary aim of the study is to establish some proof of concepts. Successful in vitro identification 
of the EPCR in primary mouse cells, in vivo targeting of the brain EPCR using antibodies as a biomarker, 
as well as 3D-culturing of the cells in a native-like spinach model might potentially open new doors

2 | BACKGROUND 
PART I: INVESTIGATING THE ENDOTHELIAL PROTEIN C RECEPTOR 
A Receptor Propelling into the Future 
For the past two decades, the EPCR has evoked increased interest within research. In the mid-1990s, 
when the receptor was first identified and characterized41,42, it was believed to reside only in large 
vessels, i.e. veins and arteries43,44. Soon it appeared to have anticoagulant properties and came to play 
a role in understanding hemostasis45. New findings demonstrated that it was highly involved in both 
the anticoagulation PC pathway 24,46–49 and the cytoprotective PC pathway 37,48,50. Studies of the 
receptor propelled in to including its role in thrombosis 38,39,51–53 and sepsis 51,54,55 . Researchers realized 
that the properties of the EPCR spanned much further to be involved in anti-inflammatory, anti-
apoptotic and growth signaling46,56–59. Likewise, the detailed EPCR binding to ligands is well studied 
42,57,60–62. 
 
Over the years, the EPCR has been extensively investigated with respect to its role in the immune 
system62–64; CNS-related conditions 24,25,65–68 and the retina69. Only to a less extent has the association 
with trafficking of ligands via the EPCR been described60,66,70–74. Promoting the potential of exploiting 
the EPCR as a trafficking molecule is practically non-existing. Hence, despite substantial understanding 
of the EPCR has been gained, there are naturally still territories that remain elusive or unexplored. 
 
A Potential Transport Molecule? 
The brain contains ~ 644 km of blood vessels, among which capillaries account for 85 % of cerebral 
vessel length representing a major site of the BBB. The vessels provide ~ 12 m2 of endothelial cell 
surface area available for transport of solutes from the blood to the brain, and vice versa31,75,76. A 
recent study indicated the presence of the EPCR in endothelial cells, for example in mRECs77. Given its 
fundamental role in the environment described above, this finding spurs the assumptions that mBECs 
express the EPCR to the same extent or maybe even to a higher degree. If this is the case, the role of 
the EPCR as a potential transport carrier remains unexplored and is subject to be scrutinized. However, 
proposing this concept should instigate caution. In particular, considering that the purpose of this 
project suggests an alternative route of targeting drug delivery to the brain. As of today, the BBB has 
perpetually represented the absolute major hurdle, which entails consideration of many parameters.

2.1 | Endothelial Protein C Receptor  
2.1.1 | Structure, Size and Localization 
The endothelial protein C receptor (EPCR) is a transmembrane glycoprotein partly homologous to the 
major histocompatibility class 1/CD1 family of proteins related to immune and inflammation 
responses 39,41,45,58,60,62,70. The mass of EPCR is 49-kDa 42. The predicted cDNA for human EPCR codes 
for a protein of 238 amino acids including a 15-amino acid signal sequence at the NH2-terminal end. 
This makes the mature protein contain 223 amino acids, while the COOH-terminal end in the 
transmembrane region contains 25 amino acids41.   
 
The EPCR is mainly localized in caveolin-1 (a scaffolding protein) positive caveolae. These are 
microdomains in the cell membrane, also referred to as lipid rafts. A minor part of EPCR is localized 
intracellularly in the recycling compartment58,62,71. In the antigen-presenting groove, EPCR is tightly 
bound to phospholipid phosphatidylcholine, which enables binding to protein C (PC)60 in a Ca2+-
dependent manner41. The binding site of PC is outside the groove distal from the membrane spanning 
domain, which through a series of events leads to the activated protein C (APC). PC is a vitamin K-
dependent plasma glycoprotein circulating in plasma as an inactive zymogen39,78 and plays a pivotal 
role in regulating blood coagulation. Binding between EPCR and PC involves several ligands and 
initiates multiple signaling pathways39,41,78.  
 
EPCR is expressed primarily on the endothelium of large vessels like arteries and veins 43,44.  However, 
expression of EPCR has also been reported on numerous other cells such as monocytes, neutrophils 
and placental trophoblasts 47,79,80, smooth muscle cells81 and keratinocytes82. EPCR-expression has 
moreover been observed in gastric epithelial cells83, cardiomyocytes78, neurons and brain capillary 
endothelial cells46,78,84, as well as on hematopoietic stem cells58,85, epithelial progenitor cells and breast 
cancer stem cells62. The tissue distribution of the EPCR and the endothelial cell receptor 
thrombomodulin (TM) is notably distinct and different, with lower concentration of TM on larger 
versus smaller vessels, while the opposite pattern applies for EPCR43,46. When Laszik et al investigated 
and compared the distribution of EPCR and TM back in 1997, they proposed that activation of PC 
occurred mainly in the microcirculation. This proposal led to the question whether particular 
processes might exist that selectively stimulate activation of PC within larger vessels43.  
 
2.1.2 | Ligands and Function 
The functional selectivity of the EPCR gives it multiple properties depending on which ligands bind to 
the receptor and which signaling pathways are activated upon this binding. One central function of 
EPCR is within hemostasis, where it exerts both procoagulant and anticoagulant activities depending 
on ligand-binding61,62,70.  In addition to PC, other significant ligands of the EPCR are thrombin (Thr) and 
TM. Thr is an enzyme of the coagulation system, while TM is an endothelial cell surface protein. PC is 
activated upon binding to the EPCR which then presents it to a complex of Thr and TM (Thr:TM) 
promoting the activation39,41,43,57,70. The procoagulant cofactors Factor (F) Va and VIIIa are also crucial 
key players interacting with EPCR39,56,57,70. FVII and FVIIa bind to EPCR with similar affinity as of PC (~40 
nM)62 (Rao, 2014).  Upon formation of the APC-EPCR, FVa and FVIIIa inactivated by down-regulation 
of Thr formation, mediated by the APC39,70. Another important ligand is the protease activated 
receptor-1 (PAR-1). PAR-1 belongs to a family of G protein-coupled cell surface receptors widely 
distributed on endothelial and blood cells, where it too plays a vital role in hemostasis59,86.

Additionally, EPCR binds to the T-cell receptor (TCR) present on a subset of Vδ2−γδ T cells, as well as 
Plasmodium falciparum erythrocyte membrane protein 1 (pfEMP1), when erythrocytes are infected 
by the malarial parasite58,62.  
 
2.1.3 | Signaling Pathways 
Two primary signaling pathways for which the EPCR plays an essential role are i) the anticoagulant 
protein C pathway and ii) the cytoprotective protein C pathway68,87. In the anticoagulant protein C 
pathway, the anticoagulant properties of APC are activated through a complex sequence of events. In 
brief, when PC binds to EPCR, the membrane surface-bound co-factors FVa and FVIIIa are inactivated 
due to vitamin K-dependent proteolysis43,78. The inactivation of the co-factors gives APC anticoagulant 
properties24,87,88. In the cytoprotective PC pathway, APC acts specifically on cells to exert multiple 
cytoprotective effects in reactions mediated by cell signaling between EPCR and the PAR-148,58,61,62,86,89. 
These effects include (i) anti-inflammatory activities; (ii) antiapoptotic activity; (iii) protection of 
endothelial barrier function; and (iv) alteration of gene expression profiles70. In 2003 Cheng and his 
group demonstrated some of these anti-inflammatory and anti-coagulant effects by preventing 
apoptosis through inhibition of p53, thereby reducing organ damage in animal models of sepsis, 
ischemic injury, and stroke24. 
 
These two pathways are very distinct with respect to biological responses including the involved 
substrates and co-factors39,70. As might be apparent from above, the assembly of the involved 
components functions in a ‘clock-work-manner’. If ‘mis-alignment’ occurs this assembly reacts to even 
miniscule changes, where the consequences can lead to conditions such as thrombosis, sepsis, 
ischemic injury, and stroke24,39. 
 
2.1.4 | Pioneers on the Trail of New Discoveries 
Does the EPCR have potential to be exploited as a transport molecule? Indications and findings 
throughout the years point in that direction. The proposed putative property of the receptor was 
introduced in early 200070. Since then researchers have made discoveries about the EPCR supporting 
this notion. The following section highlights selected pertinent literature reinforcing the concept of 
the EPCR as a trafficking molecule.  
 
In the early years of 2000, it was proposed that the EPCR hold the property of translocating itself from 
the membrane surface to the nucleus and internalizing APC into vascular cells60,70. Nayak and his group 
studied transcytosis and endocytosis involving the EPCR. In a transwell study, they used CHO-EPCR 
cells to determine EPCR-specific transcytosis, by testing transport of the radioactive tracer 125I-FVIIa 
from the apical to the basal side. The cells were treated with antibodies and bovine serum albumin 
(BSA) or 125I-FVIIa. Their results showed that > 4-fold FVIIa relative to BSA ended up on the basal side71. 
They also demonstrated that endocytosis and maybe recycling is promoted by the EPCR upon binding 
to APC and FVIIa. Their data suggested possible internalization of APC and FVII via endocytosis with 
recycling back to the surface. Rab11 and transferrin (Tf) were used to confirm intracellular 
colocalization with EPCR in the recycling endosomal compartment (ReC). Tf is known to enter the ReC 
upon internalization. Rab11, specific for ReC, is one of several Rab GTPases serving as important 
regulators of vesicular membrane trafficking of cell surface proteins in endocytic and exocytic 
pathways72. The result showed accumulation of the receptor-ligand complex in the intracellular ReC 
==PAGE:16==
 
7 
 
indicating it was endocytosed, before reappearing to the cell surface71. These findings led to 
investigating the mechanisms involved in internalization of molecules via the plasma membrane. In 
2013 they showed that Rab GTPases regulate the endocytosis and that trafficking of FVIIa is mediated 
by the EPCR74.  
 
In 2009, Deane and his group explored whether the EPCR and PAR-1 are required for APC transport 
from cerebrovascular circulation into the brain. Using antibodies specifically blocking APC-binding site 
on EPCR inhibited uptake of 125I-APC into the brain by > 85%. In comparison, no effect on 125I-APC 
transport was observed when using antibodies blocking the activation of PAR-1. To confirm that EPCR 
was required for BBB transport of APC and not PAR-1, they studied brain uptake of APC in severely 
deficient EPCR mice and PAR-1 null mice. By introducing 125I-APC to severely EPCR-deficient mice, the 
brain uptake of 125I-APC was reduced by 64%, while 125I-APC brain uptake was unchanged in PAR-1 null 
mice. These findings indicated that the EPCR mediated the transport of APC across the BBB66. Clark et 
al. found evidence that a recombinant variant of FVIIa (rFVIIa) is transcytosed via the EPCR and 
distributed into extravascular tissues. In the study, the rFVIIa remained functionally active in the 
extravascular tissues for a long time compared to i.v. administration of the protein which resulted in 
rapid clearance73.  
 
Despite the association between EPCR and cell signaling activities, lack of insight remains in 
understanding the receptor as a trafficking molecule. As to the best of the author’s knowledge, 
exploring this specific property of the EPCR appears limitedly supported by later studies. Thus, detailed 
knowledge about this transportation across the brain capillary endothelial is driven by the goal to 
investigate these mechanisms.  
 
2.2 | Blood-Brain Barrier 
2.2.1 | Features and Function 
Neuronal signaling within the CNS requires a highly controlled microenvironment. The CNS is 
separated from the circulatory system by cells at mainly these three boundaries: the blood-brain 
barrier (BBB), blood-CSF (cerebrospinal fluid barrier) and the arachnoid barrier. At the level of brain 
microvessel endothelium, the BBB represents the major site of blood-CNS exchange. The key role of 
the BBB is to prevent access of pathogens and neurotoxins from the blood circulation to the CNS, in 
addition to allowing influx of nutrients and efflux of waste metabolites90. 
 
2.2.2 | The Neurovascular Unit  
The endothelium, neurons, and non-neuronal cells form a functional unit referred to as the 
neurovascular unit (NVU). Pericytes, astrocytes, and microglia are nonneuronal cells. Neurons and 
non-neuronal cells are tightly positioned allowing effective paracrine regulations of normal CNS 
functioning and pathological processes. These critical regulations include control of angiogenic, 
neurogenic and neurotrophic coupling; matrix interactions; neurotransmitter inactivation; 
microvascular permeability and hemodynamic neurovascular coupling31. 
 
Stanimirovic et al (2012) raised an interesting discussion about the role of the NVU as an active 
participant in pathogenesis of brain pathologies. The review considers, whether primary vascular 
events cause neurological disease, rather than contribute in disease pathogenesis recruited by a 
==PAGE:17==
 
8 
 
principal neuronal origin. If CNS diseases originating from primary NVU pathology trigger neuronal 
dysfunction, then, refocusing strategies are encouraged to target vascular components. In turn, it 
could prevent current challenges of drug delivery across the BBB and adverse effects associated with 
targeting neuronal compartment(s)91. 
 
2.2.3 | The Basement Membrane 
Brain endothelial cells (BECs) are separated from their neighboring cells, astrocytes and pericytes, by 
the basement membrane. This membrane is composed of various extracellular matrix (ECM) structural 
proteins such as, laminin, collagen type IV, fibronectin and heparan sulfate proteoglycan92. The BBB 
barrier properties and basement membrane structure and function are maintained by the BECs, 
astrocytes and pericytes in collaboration. An example of a group of proteins essential for the BBB 
integrity are matrix adhesion receptors (MAR). MARs are expressed in neurons, vascular cells, as well 
as in supporting glial cells, such as oligodendroglia, microglia and astrocyte end-feet31. Alterations in 
the barrier in disease states are often promoted by modulation of MARs. As an example, growth 
factors bound to ECM proteins, like vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), can be activated by 
matrix metalloproteinases31, which can lead to regulation of postischemic angiogenic and neurogenic 
repair responses32. Their functional importance in the normal state is corroborated, when observing 
how significant alterations in cellular adhesion receptors and their matrix ligands are involved in 
multiple sclerosis (MS), encephalomyelitis (EAE) and certain tumors of the CNS31. 
 
2.2.4 | Molecular Structure and Transport Mechanisms 
The nervous tissue requires many important water-soluble nutrients and metabolites, to which the 
BBB has low passive permeability. To ensure adequate supply of these substances, specific transport 
systems are expressed in the BBB. The BBB is structured by physical and transport barriers consisting 
of endothelial junctions and membrane transporters, respectively (Abbott, 2010). 
 
Physical barriers 
The physical barrier is formed by tight junctions (TJs) and adherens junctions (AJs) that connect the 
BECs. TJs exist between the cells of the arachnoid epithelium, choroid plexus epithelial cells and 
cerebral endothelial cells90. TJs form a continuous belt (zonula occludens) around each endothelial 
cell75. These cells function to reduce permeation of polar solutes from blood plasma to the brain 
extracellular fluid90 (Abbott, 2010). Some of the important proteins in TJs involve occludin, zonula 
occludens, and claudins, which are linked to regulatory proteins like ZO-1, ZO-2 and ZO-376,90. The 
endothelium is enclosed by the basement membrane75. Between the endothelial cells are the 
junctional complexes which besides TJs include the AJs. AJs are essential when TJs are formed and 
their function is to support tissue structure by holding cells together. When AJs are disrupted it can 
lead to disruption of the barrier90. Some of the important proteins in AJs involve cadherins, platelet 
endothelial cell adhesion molecule (PECAM-1), and the junctional adhesion molecules (JAMs)76,90. 
 
Transport barriers 
Permeation across the BBB is mediated through different transport barriers, such as passive diffusion, 
ATP-binding cassette transporters (ABC transporters), solute carriers (SCs) and transcytosis (receptor-
mediated or adsorptive mediated)90.  
 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
 
Passive diffusion 
Oxygen supply to the brain and removal of carbon dioxide occur via passive diffusion, where the 
dissolved gasses move down their concentration gradients when moving across the BBB90. Other 
solutes, such as small lipid-soluble molecules and compounds with a molecular weight < 400 Da or 
containing <8 hydrogen bonds may also cross the endothelium and passively diffuse through the 
membrane. This process is favored by higher lipid solubility and several other physicochemical 
factors76,90.  
 
ATP-binding cassette transporter  
ABC transporters are expressed at the luminal side of the BBB to carry out vital neuroprotective and 
detoxifying functions76. They act as active efflux pumps which consume ATP. ABC transporters carry a 
diverse range of lipid-soluble compounds out of the brain capillary endothelium and CNS. This removal 
prevents the accumulation of potentially neurotoxic endogenous molecules, such as drugs and 
xenobiotic agents. Some of the most important ABC transporters for efflux transport are the P-
glycoprotein and the multidrug resistance-associated proteins90. 
 
 
Solute carriers  
The barrier to paracellular diffusion potentially isolates the brain from many essential polar nutrients 
necessary for metabolism. Therefore, the BBB endothelium must contain some specific SCs90. The SCs 
are uni- or bi-directional, driven by ion gradients or involved in substrate-exchange90. To supply the 
CNS with nutrients SC-mediated transport facilitates the transport of glucose, fatty acids, amino acids, 
nucleotides, hormones, and vitamins76. Among several others, the most important SC, is the glucose 
transporter type 1 (GLUT1)90,93,94. 
 
Transcytosis 
Large macromolecules such as proteins and peptides enter the CNS via endocytosis and subsequent 
transcytosis. The endocytotic uptake is based on internalization of macromolecules into the 
endothelial cell. The mechanisms can be either receptor-mediated transcytosis (RMT) or absorptive-
mediated transcytosis (AMT). Upon endocytosis they are transcytosed across the cytoplasm to be 
exocytosed at the opposite pole of the cell. Both RMT and AMT appear to be vesicular-based 
mechanisms which carry their macromolecular content across the BBB90. Where AMT seems to be 
induced in a non-specific fashion by positively charged macromolecules90, RMT requires receptor 
binding of ligands enabling trans-endothelial transport of proteins and peptides90. The delivery across 
the cerebral endothelium is bi-directional, where e.g. transferrin and insulin are carried from blood to 
brain and apolipoproteins from brain to blood76.  
 
2.3 | Coupling EPCR, BBB and Neuropathology 
Since the beginning of the 2000s, the physiological role of the EPCR has gained momentum in relation 
to CNS pathophysiology. The following section highlights selected important findings associating the 
EPCR with the BBB. 
 
EPCR Required for Mediating Neuroprotective and Cytoprotective Effects 
During ischemic brain damage, endothelial dysfunction plays a central role. Hypoxia and stress signals 
cause cellular injury partially through the action of the tumor suppressor protein p53. Research related

to ischemic injury showed that APC required the EPCR and PAR-1 in order to mediate its 
neuroprotective and cytoprotective effects. The results were based on both a model of hypoxic BECs 
and a severe EPCR-deficient mouse stroke model. This was initial compelling evidence showing that 
APC directly inhibited p53-mediated apoptosis, reduced caspase-3 signaling and normalized the pro-
apoptotic Bax/Bcl-2 ratio24,46. 
 
EPCR-APC binding Regulates Calcium Flux in the Brain 
PAR-1 receptors expressed in brain endothelium mediate intracellular calcium (Ca2+) concentration 
signaling. Based on the previous findings that APC elicits EPCR-dependent neuroprotective effects, it 
was found that APC regulates intracellular Ca2+ flux in human BECs by binding to EPCR and signaling 
via PAR-184. 
 
EPCR Related to the NVU and Alzheimer’s Disease  
It was suggested that neuronal degeneration was affected by vascular-derived effects. Examples of 
such effects are deficits in the NVU, e.g. hypoxia, reduction in cerebral blood flow and accumulation 
of neurotoxic and vasculotoxic molecules within the brain parenchyma. Based on this, mechanisms of 
BBB dysfunction in neurodegenerative disorders, particularly AD, were investigated highlighting the 
therapeutic opportunities95. 
 
Endothelial Cell Interaction and Activation in CNS Inflammation 
Recent studies indicate that the pathogenesis of CNS inflammatory diseases is related to cerebral 
endothelial cells, such as leukocyte migration across the endothelial barrier25. A process requiring 
complex endothelial adhesion molecules assembled at the junctions of endothelial cells. The 
mechanisms underlying the leukocyte endothelial cell interactions have been discussed together with 
the properties of different inflammatory mediators of leukocyte migration on cerebral endothelial 
cells. In addition, the crosstalk between glial and endothelial cells have been elucidated25. With regard 
to these highlighted aspects it could be speculated as to whether the EPCR plays a significant role in 
relation to how endothelial cell interactions are activated in CNS inflammation. 
 
EPCR is a Key Feature in Cerebral Malaria Pathogenesis 
Cerebral malaria (CM) is caused by infection with Plasmodium falciparum (P. falciparum) parasites. 
CM is the most lethal infection, resulting in more than half a million deaths annually96. 
Numerous studies on patients in Asia and Africa have shown that disruption of the BBB in CM causes 
severe neurological complications, ultimately leading to CNS dysfunction and death97.  
Recent findings highlight that the EPCR plays a main role in CM pathogenesis. It has been 
demonstrated that P. falciparum exert increased binding to brain endothelial cells. This occurs due to 
binding of PfEMP1 via the EPCR and the intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1). In turn it leads to 
acquired functional PC system deficiency96,98 . The disruption of the BBB as a response to Plasmodium-
infected red blood cells manifests due to failure of maintaining an intact barrier at the NVU, where 
BEC junctions are controlled  by e.g. astrocytes and pericytes99. 
 
EPCR Involved in Multiple Sclerosis Pathogenesis 
PC pathway components are highly involved in the neurodegenerative process in MS, where 
pathogenesis is characterized by e.g. increased BBB permeability. Clinical findings in a study including

11 
 
MS patients indicated an association between PC plasma concentrations and neurodegeneration 
related to MS. Due to several differences amongst protein plasma levels, dysregulation of PC pathway 
elements was proposed. Examples of correlating values involving PC, EPCR and thrombomodulin were 
detected in MS patients, while PC and PAR-1 correlations were present in healthy individuals only. The 
results implied that proteins inhibiting thrombin formation with anti-inflammatory properties could 
play a protective role in MS progression100. 
 
EPCR Plays an Essential Role in Neural Plasticity  
In a study related to synaptic neural plasticity regulated by blood coagulation factors, it was discovered 
that EPCR played an important role49. In brief, neural plasticity is based on decreased or increased 
activity in synapses, and thus the capacity of the nervous system to change101–103. The ability of the 
brain to adapt new memory, cognitive or motor skills is related to long-term potentiation (LTP) and 
long-term depression (LTD). LTP and LTD are two key terms related to synaptic plasticity, usually 
involving the hippocampus104. To the researchers’ surprise in the study and in contrast to their 
previous studies49, it was discovered, that by blocking EPCR signaling, PAR-1 was able to evoke LTP 
and APC failed to facilitate LTP24. This indicated that APC operates through EPCR and demonstrated 
that signaling pathways involving the EPCR plays an essential role in neural plasticity. Based on the 
ability of the EPCR to promote neuronal survival49 it was speculated whether the neuroprotective 
effects of the EPCR are mediated by PAR-166. Though it was shown by Deane et al that the transport 
of APC across the BBB did not rely on PAR-1105. 
 
Concluding Comment  
Based on this summary covering findings from the past 20 years, the endothelial barrier stabilization, 
angiogenesis and neurogenesis appear to be favored by the anti-inflammatory, anti-apoptotic and 
cytoprotective effects produced by the EPCR-bound APC.  However, given that the EPCR is not a ‘new 
kid in town’ and despite substantial results, this receptor is attracting proportionally less attention 
compared to other potential targets for drug delivery to the brain. This should support the evident 
demand for increased focus on the intriguing role of the EPCR and its potential use in drug delivery.

2.4 | Tissue Engineering 
2.4.1 | Purpose and Principles 
Due to shortage of organs or organ tissue for tissue transplantation, creating substitutes for living 
tissue, gave rise to the emergence of tissue engineering in the 1980s105. As of 2014, the donor waiting 
list in the U.S. had 100.000 patients anticipating an available donor organ or tissue and each day more 
than 20 people died while waiting107,105. What drove Gershlak and his group is the impeding issue of 
translating practical solutions for patients waiting for a donor organ or tissue. The lack of a functional 
vascular network is one of the main factors that limits clinical applicability of tissue engineered 
solutions108.   
 
Numerous production methods have been applied to regenerate diverse tissues and body organs 
based on various scaffold materials created from a selection of biomaterials. Regardless of the tissue 
type, factors like scaffold architecture, mechanical properties, biocompatibility, biodegradability, and 
manufacturing technology are critical in the design of a scaffold for use in tissue engineering106. With 
advanced approaches in manufacturing synthetic grafts for tissue substitutes, e.g. 3D printing has 
produced scaffolds with exceptional physical properties. However, it is still highly challenged to 
manufacture scaffolds with a distinct range of attainable physical and biological properties. The 
challenge of absent functional vascular network, can be evaded by using scaffolds that substitute 
tissue, based on animal-derived or autologous grafts. Yet, with the drawbacks of being expensive, 
limited in availability, having batch-to-batch inconsistency, and poor biocompatibility105. Currently, 
bioengineering approaches are faced by the limitation that basic research in different organisms is 
isolated in terms of their biological kingdoms. Exploiting cross-kingdom contributions within the same 
bioengineered platform can overcome this challenge108.  
 
Derived from green and sustainable sources, plant-based natural polymers convene the demands for 
innovative biomaterials for tissue engineering. In addition, they require minimal chemical processing 
for industrial applications. The considerable advantage of plant scaffolds is the simplicity with which 
they can be prepared, controlled and fashioned to form various shapes and sizes106,108. Moreover, 
plant tissues are inexpensive, easily accessible and offer a broad range of additional beneficial 
characteristics making them exceptionally appropriate for use as scaffolds. Examples include 
interconnected porosity, good water transport, preexisting vascular networks, high surface area, and 
a wide array of mechanical properties109. 
 
Plants and animals utilize fundamentally distinct methods for the transportation of macromolecules, 
chemicals and fluids, but their vascular network structures are surprisingly similar. The physiological 
law describing the branching network design of the human cardiovascular system, referred to as 
Murray's Law110, is also followed by plant vasculature111. Like human tissue, structures within plant 
tissue105 display different mechanical properties, allowing different functions. Plant cell walls are 
comprised of a range of polysaccharides, of which the most important are cellulose, pectin, and 
hemicellulose. These polysaccharides have been studied for e.g. bone tissue engineering and been 
demonstrated to promote wound healing112. Additionally, cellulosic tissue engineering scaffolds 
==PAGE:22==
 
13 
 
derived from decellularized apple slices have indicated the ability for mammalian cell attachment and 
proliferation105. The inherent similarities and obvious biocompatibility of plant extracellular matrix 
(ECM) compelled Gershlak et al to explore whether plants and their intrinsic vasculatures could serve 
as perfusable scaffolds for engineering human tissue113. 
 
2.4.2 | Methods Used in Tissue Engineering 
Often incorporated in complex scaffolds, tissue engineering and regenerative medicine involve several 
distinct artificial and biologic materials, which can be repopulated with different cell types. On this 
basis a promising scaffold is the allogeneic ECM114. As an alternative effort to engineer a vascular 
network, bio-inspired approaches now focus on the introduction of perfusion-based techniques for 
decellularization115,116. Decellularization eliminates cellular material from a tissue or organ resulting in 
an acellular scaffold comprising of an ECM, while maintaining an intact vascular network105. By 
eliminating the cellular material of a tissue, a decellularized graft becomes nonimmunogenic while 
preserving overall organ structure115. In turn decellularized tissues and organs can be recellularized 
with e.g. stem cells or a patient's own cells creating an autologous graft113. Basically, all human 
tissues/organs have been decellularized for tissue engineering purposes. In vitro recellularizations 
have been reported with various cells and procedures like perfusion, injection, and seeding113. The 
nonimmunogenic ECM can then be provided with specific differentiating actions due to its 
architecture and bioactive factors. This method offers fully personalized clinical approaches to be 
developed117.  
 
A scaffold that maintains growth, cell adhesion, alignment and differentiation is required to obtain 
structure and function in engineered tissues. Introducing decellularization as an approach for 
preserving the biological architecture has been used for fabricating 3D scaffolds like e.g. skeletal 
muscle tissue. As opposed to many decellularization methods focusing on using animal muscle as the 
starting material, decellularized plants are a prospective source of highly structured cellulose-rich 
scaffolds117. As an example, Cheng et al (2020) evaluated the ability of decellularized plant scaffolds 
to promote mouse and human muscle cell alignment and differentiation. Various fruits and vegetables 
were decellularized, followed by recellularization with C2C12 mouse cells and human skeletal muscle 
cells. Their results showed that decellularized onion cellulose scaffolds offer a plain substrate to 
engineer human skeletal muscle106. In terms of plant decellularization, two protocols have been 
described targeting tissue engineering applications106,108. Both approaches leave behind the ECM of 
the plant tissues altered, which can then be applied as scaffolds. These approaches present grafts with 
equivalent mechanical characteristics and low cellular metabolic impact. One uses detergent baths to 
eliminate cellular matter, while the other is a detergent-free approach using a salt soak and a hot 
bleach to isolate leaf vasculature to free the leaves and stems108.   
 
2.4.3 | Current Status  
Documentation as e.g. from Gershlak et al confirms the feasibility of exploiting the vascular structure 
resemblances in plant and animal tissues. However, despite significant progress in manufacturing 
bioengineered scaffolds, delivery of nutrients in intricate engineered human tissues remains an 
imperative challenge113. Studies concerning in vivo behavior are naturally poorly represented, 
considering the recent emergence of this area within tissue engineering. The upcoming challenge is 
to develop human grafts that can be implanted fully restored in all their functional and structural

aspects105. The future perspective of this field holds great potential, by offering a relatively novel 
approach to cost-efficient and ‘green’ technology for regenerating large quantity vascularized tissue 
mass105105.  
 
2.4.4 | Crossing Kingdoms: Decellularized Plants as Scaffolds  
In context of the elaboration of tissue engineering, the following sections present a short summary of 
the study conducted by Gershlak and his group and in brief, how the current study has adapted 
accordingly. 
 
For the different scaffold geometries, the researchers used spinach, parsley, Artemisia annua leaves 
and peanut hairy roots. In their further experiments spinach leaves were selected due to their density 
and vascular network pattern, their wide diameter petiole, and their availability114. The group applied

was detected. Contraction was observed 5 days after initial seeding. Ca-handling capability was also

3 | MATERIALS AND METHODS 
PART I 
3.1 | In vitro (Culturing of mREC)       
Primary murine retinal endothelial cells, mRECs, were acquired at The Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU). At initiation, the mRECs used were at passage 4, cryopreserved June 2018. To ensure optimal 
growing conditions and proper cell proliferation endothelial cells require an adhesion surface. Hence, 
prior to seeding mRECs, a T25 flask (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was precoated with a solution containing 
fibronectin (FN) bovine plasma (10 μL/mL; Sigma-Aldrich) in phosphate buffered saline (PBS; Sigma-
Aldrich) and incubated at room temperature (RT) for 20 min.  mRECs were thawed and transferred to 
warm Endothelial Cell Medium (ECM; Innoprot) supplemented with 1 % penicillin-streptomycin (P/S) 
solution (Innoprot), 1 % Endothelial Cell Growth Supplement (ECGS; Innoprot), and 5 % fetal bovine 
serum (FBS; Innoprot). Next, the cells were centrifuged for 5 min at 2100 rpm and 23 °C, the 
supernatant was removed, and the pellet resuspended in supplemented ECM. mRECs were then 
cultured in the precoated T25 flask and incubated at 37 °C in a humidified 5 % CO2 incubator. When 
mRECs reached 80-90 % confluency, the cells were passaged (~ 1 x 10 6 cells/T75 flask) to provide 
samples for the various experiments. At the end of the study, mRECs had reached passage 8. Note, for 
all protocol steps including centrifugation and incubation at 37 °C the above given conditions applied, 
unless otherwise specified. Likewise, PBS, 10 % FBS (Sigma-Aldrich) in PBS and 0.05 % trypsin-EDTA 
were used for all washing and passaging steps. All experiments including mRECs were carried out in 
duplicates. 
 
3.2 | Transfection of EPCR siRNA in mRECs 
To validate the antibodies used throughout the current study, transient siRNA transfection was carried 
out using EPCR small interfering RNA (siRNA) to inhibit the assumed EPCR expression in mRECs.  
 
3.2.1 | Establishing Culture Conditions for Transfection 
3 x 24-well tissue culture plates (Thermo Fisher Scientific) were precoated with 1 % FN in PBS and 
incubated for 20 min at RT. mRECs were seeded (~ 0.5 x 10 5 cells/well) in growth medium containing 
0.5 mL warm ECM, 1 % ECGS, 5 % FBS, and without antibiotics, followed by incubation at 37 °C.  
Successful knock down of EPCR gene expression required healthy and subconfluent cells. Thus, cell 
viability was assessed one day prior to transfection, when mRECs had reached ~ 60-80 % confluency 
(~ 18-24 h culture).  
 
3.2.2 | Preparation of Transfection Solutions   
Solutions for the transfection were prepared according to protocol from Santa Cruz Biotechnology. In 
brief, lyophilized siRNA duplex (EPCR siRNA plasmid, corresponding to 0.25-1 µg or 20-80 pmols siRNA) 
(cat.39933; Santa Cruz) was prepared in RNAse-free water. The siRNA duplex corresponded to 3.3 
nmol in 10 µM buffered solution of 10 µM Tris-HCl, 20 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, pH 8. siRNA duplex was 
==PAGE:25==
 
16 
 
then diluted in siRNA Transfection Medium (TM), (cat.36868; Santa Cruz), while a second solution with 
siRNA Transfection Reagent (TR), (cat.29528; Santa Cruz) was diluted in TM. TR is a reagent used to 
enhance delivery of siRNA. For both solutions dilution corresponded to ~ 40 µL in 0.5 mL. Immediately 
after, the two solutions were gently mixed at a total volume of ~ 1 mL and incubated for ~ 30 min at 
RT as to allow complexes to form.  
 
3.2.3 | Transfection of siRNA  
Old medium was aspirated from mRECs, followed by wash with TM. A master mix containing TM and

3.5 | In vitro (Culturing of mBEC)  
3.5.1 | An in vitro Model Resembling BBB with in vivo Conditions 
Primary murine brain endothelial cells (mBECs) were kindly gifted by Assistant Professor Maj 
Schneider Thomsen (MST), Aalborg University, Denmark, who had isolated the mBECs from C57BL/6 
mice. The current study followed a protocol described by MST105118.   Initially 12-well plates were 
coated with fibronectin bovine plasma and collagen type IV (1 mg/mL; Sigma-Aldrich) and incubated 
at 37 °C for minimum 1 h. This was conducted as to provide optimal culture conditions and obtain 
morphology resembling in vivo conditions. To induce protein expression in the brain cells and mimic 
the properties generally present in the BBB, basic mBEC medium was supplemented with basic human 
fibroblast growth factor (bFGF) (Sigma-Aldrich) and BBB-inducing factors (BBB-IFs). bFGF was added 
at every medium change, while BBB-IFs were only added 1-2 days prior to analysis. BBB-IFs contained 
the following: 17.5 μM Ro 20-1724 (Sigma-Aldrich), 250 μM 8-(4-Chlorophenylthio)-adenosine 30,50-
cyclicmonophosphate sodium salt (pCPT-cAMP) (Sigma-Aldrich) and 550 nM hydrocortisone (Sigma-
Aldrich). pCPT-cAMP is involved in cellular increase of cAMP and Ro 20-1720 is a phosphodiesterase 
inhibitor that prolongs the effects of pCPT-cAMP114,115. 
 
3.5.2 | Isolating mBECs 
mBECs were of passage 1, cryopreserved June 2018. Upon thawing, the cells were transferred to warm 
basic mBEC medium, containing Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium-F12 (DMEM-F12; Sigma-Aldrich) 
supplemented with 10 % plasma derived serum (PDS) (First Link), gentamicin sulphate (10 μg/mL; 
BioWhittaker, Lonza) and insulin-transferrin sodium selenite (0.1 mg/mL; ITS-X (100x), Thermo Fisher 
Scientific). Next, the cells were centrifuged for 7 min at 700 g, resuspended in basic mBEC medium, 
supplemented with) bFGF (1 ng/mL; Sigma-Aldrich) and puromycin (4 μg/mL; Sigma-Aldrich). To 
obtain a purer culture and isolate mBECs from pericytes, puromycin was supplemented in the first 
addition of basic mBEC medium. Subsequently, mBECs were seeded on the fibronectin/collagen IV 
precoated 12-well plates. The cells were incubated for 4 days at 37 °C without medium change. Cell 
growth was then assessed on day 4. Depending on the confluency (below/above 80 %) the protocol 
prescribed two different steps. If below 80 %, step 1 included wash with PBS, addition of fresh basic 
mBEC medium supplemented with bFGF and observe cell growth until >80-90 % confluent. If above 
80 %, step 2 included wash with PBS, addition of fresh basic mBEC medium supplemented with bFGF 
and BBB-IFs. mBECs would then be ready for subsequent analysis 1-2 days following addition of BBB-
Ifs. All experiments were conducted as duplicates.

3.7 | Immuno-Dot Blotting Assay              
3.7.1 | Homogenization and Preparation of Tissue Lysates  
After retrieval of the brains and hearts from storage at −80 °C, tissue samples were dissected, 
individually weighed and transferred to new Eppendorf tubes kept on ice. Next, samples were 
processed by manual homogenization using a 2 mL Kimble™ Kontes™ Dounce Tissue Grinder. In brief, 
tissue samples were immersed into the glass homogenizer with RIPA lysis buffer containing 150 mM 
NaCl, 50 mM Tris-HCl (Thermo Scientific), 0.5 % sodium-deoxycholate (SDS) (Sigma-Aldrich), 0.1 % 
sodium dodecyl sulphate (Sigma-Aldrich), 0.1 % Triton X-100 (Sigma-Aldrich), adjusted to pH 8. Prior 
to use protease inhibitors (complete Mini, EDTA-free, Roche) were added. To obtain a high 
concentration of protein extract, the concentration of tissue/lysis buffer (w/v) corresponded to 10 
mg/100 uL. The tissues were individually homogenized by 5-10 grinding motions with a glass pestle 
with subsequent transfer of the homogenates to new Eppendorf tubes kept on ice. Next, the 
homogenates were maintained at agitation for 2 h at 4 °C. Using a Thermo Scientific SL 16R Centrifuge, 
the samples were centrifuged for 20 min at 12000 rpm at 4 °C to pellet tissue fragments. The 
supernatant was aspirated and transferred to new Eppendorf tubes and the pellet was discarded. 
Tissue lysates were stored at -20 °C till further processing.

3.8 | Decellularization of Spinach Tissue Scaffolds 
Spinach was acquired from the local supermarket. Decellularization was adapted partly from Gershlak 
et al119 and partly from techniques used for whole organ perfusion decellularization114,115. The protocol 
below describes a modified version suited for the current project. In brief, the spinach leaves (SLs) 
were initially washed by vigorously swirling, alternating between ~ 30 sec in n-Heptane (Merck 
Millipore) and PBS. This treatment removed cuticles from the plant surface120. The SLs were then 
cannulated through the petiole with a 25G-syringe needle attached with waterproof adhesive tape 
and parafilm. A reservoir was set up to obtain gravity driven flow through rubber tubes allowing 
perfusion through the needles.  Throughout the whole decellularization process, SLs were submerged 
in MilliQ (MQ) water to keep them hydrated. A solution of 1 % sodium dodecylsulfate (SDS; Sigma-
Aldrich) (w/v) in MQ (pH ~ 6.7) was perfused through the cannulas for minimum 48 h. This step 
ensured removal of remaining proteins and chlorophyll from the plant material. Next, the SLs were 
perfused with 0.1 % TritonX-100 in 0. 5 % citric acid (Sigma-Aldrich)  (w/v) in MQ (pH ~ 2.4) for 
minimum 24 h, followed by perfusion with 0.1 % TritonX-100 in 10 % sodium hypochlorite (ClNaO) 
(Sigma-Aldrich)  in MQ (pH ~ 12.1) for another 24 h. PBS and MQ was then perfused through the 
==PAGE:30==
 
21 
 
cannulas for additionally ~ 24 h each. The SLs could be stored for at least 2 weeks in MQ at 4 °C until 
further processing.

3.9 | Recellularization of Spinach Surface and Microvasculature     
To investigate the possibility of using the decellularized spinach tissue scaffolds for culturing 
mammalian primary cells, recellularizing the SLs with mRECs was attempted. Before cultivation of 
cells, the SLs were treated with UV-B for ~ 45 min. This step was done partly to sterilize the leaves 
before seeding cells and partly to eliminate DNA in the plant material to reduce background 
fluorescence when imaging the samples. Autofluorescence is a natural and common feature of nearly 
all plant material121. Then, samples were cut in ~ 3 x 3 cm and placed in 6 well plates, where the 
surfaces of the SLs were coated with FN. For cannulated samples ~ 1-2 mL FN was injected into the 
petiole as to coat the inner microvasculature of the leaf, which was kept in a petri dish and not cut 
until right before use. All experiments involving decellularized SLs were executed with a minimum of 
duplicates. Upon incubation at 37 °C for ~ 24 h, mRECs were tested for good viability and seeded (~ 
300.000 cells/sample) either on the surface or via injection into the microvasculature of the SLs. The 
recellularized SLs were maintained in regular growth medium (supplemented ECM) and incubated at 
37 °C to allow adherence until use.

5 | DISCUSSION 
PART I 
5.1 | In vitro (mREC)       
Despite the unfortunate event of mycoplasma detection, mRECs were able to provide samples for 
execution of several of the experiments involving cell supply. The fact that the first batch was tested 
positive for mycoplasma, did not seem to affect the study of antibody validation nor blocking the 
expression of EPCR. The following batch of mRECs used was tested negative and was likewise able to 
provide samples for studying whether spinach tissue could serve as scaffolds for cell cultivation. It can 
be speculated, whether the altered morphology of the mRECs can be attributed to infection with 
mycoplasma or the high passage number given it is a primary cell type. Though tempting to consider 
the first, it is likely more due to higher passage number, since the second batch showed a similar 
pattern in morphological changes. 
 
5.2 | In vitro (mBEC)  
Using mBECs in this study plays a profound role, since it forms the basis of studying the ability of 
exploiting the EPCR in endothelial brain cells to investigate molecular transport targeting the BBB. For 
==PAGE:46==
 
37 
 
obvious reasons this is not explored further. Despite the unfortunate circumstances leading to 
experiment failure, some aspects still need to be highlighted. Two batches of mBECs were provided 
by MST. The intention was to seed the first batch and replicate the receptor blocking-experiment 
carried out for mRECs. Partly to determine and characterize EPCR-expression in mBECs and partly to 
compare the two cell types. The second batch was intended for conducting transportation studies 
using antibody functionalized gold nanoparticles. 
It is speculated what led to the irregular event of poor cell growth for both batches. Primary cells are 
highly sensitive and despite providing them optimal growth conditions, they respond to even minor 
changes122. Firstly, it is conceivable that low quality of cells could be ascribed the event, since both 
batches exhibited similar low to no growth. Given that primary cells are derived from varying donors 
there are batch-to-batch differences that account of varying quality. Based on a recent study 
conducted with mBECs (non-published), the cultures used here behaved differently. With reference 
to the previous study, the mBECs used had been cryopreserved October 2019, while the batches used 
in this current project were cryopreserved June 2018. Whether time for primary cells in 
cryopreservation is a matter of importance is speculated, though cryopreservation is meant for 
longtime storage of samples and should therefore not influence quality of stored samples. The fact 
that mycoplasma was present during cultivation of mBECs should in this case be irrelevant, due to the 
fact that the mBEC medium was added puromycin, which is also used to eliminate mycoplasma118. 
 
5.3 | Evaluation of EPCR Transfection and Flow Cytometry 
By silencing the EPCR-gene with the method applied shows lowest expression levels of the receptor 
within the first 24 h. To some extent this validates that the antibodies used throughout this work are 
of reliable quality. However, this notion is not heavily supported by data. Since histograms 
representing non-transfected cells show steady EPCR-expression at all 3 time points, it is expected 
that the MFI reflects a similar pattern. In contrast, the MFI at 48 h and 72 h shows a ~ 3-fold decrease. 
 
Furthermore, the gene-silencing is anticipated to decrease over the time period, but expression of 
EPCR appears to increase after 48 h, though still being lower than that of non-transfected cells. The 
gated live-cell populations in the density plots also display inconsistencies worth of notice. A higher 
population of transfected cells 48 h post-transfection is in contrast to both the population of non-
transfected cells for the same day and transfected cells at 24 h. Fluctuations in data can be explained 
by the fact that mRECs were very adherent, in particular the transfected, and challenging to release. 
To detach the cells longer trypsinization than usual was attempted (8-10 min). This might have 
resulted in ruining membrane bound proteins such as the EPCR, while also affecting diverging number 
of cells obtained for analysis. The similar challenge applied for the duplicate experiment, where cells 
were attempted to be gently scraped off to avoid the long trypsinization. It is speculated whether 
transfected cells excrete compounds to their surroundings allowing them to adhere stronger to the 
surface as a protection against the stress they are exposed to during transfection.  
 
Perhaps the increased expression of EPCR in transfected cells can be explained by the fact that the 
degree of transfection depends on the concentration of transfection reagents, which optimally should 
be tested prior an experiment. Why transfected cells express higher levels of EPCR over the time span 
might also be caused by the fact that transfection is transient. It is not a gene knock-out but has 
potential to slowly recover and re-express EPCR.

5.4 | Evaluation of Blocking of EPCR and Flow Cytometry 
Based on data from the transfection study it is hypothesized whether the knockdown of EPCR reflects 
a higher knockdown efficiency. It can appear as such if EPCR i.e. is expressed at lower levels and needs 
some days before receptor expression is facilitated. Speculating that the degree of EPCR expression is 
associated with time and confluency post seeding formed the basis of the blocking study. Under the 
assumption that by blocking expression of the receptor, it might be possible to determine if EPCR-
expression is ‘provoked’ by eg signaling molecules depending on decreased/increased vicinity to 
neighboring cells. However, flow cytometric data from blocking EPCR expression shows no significant 
difference in expression of the receptor over the 3 days (4 days post seeding), indicating that time 
does not seem to influence expression of EPCR. This is also supported by the fact that EPCR is actually 
expressed at almost similar levels at 24 h, 48 h and 72 h in the transfection study, even taking the 
decreasing MFI into account.  
 
Interestingly, data shows that blocking EPCR expression is not complete by staining with 1° EPCR first 
followed by staining with 1° EPCR PE. This can be attributed to various factors such as either the 
availability of binding sites, antibody dilution prior flow cytometric analysis or maybe even that there 
is a presence of soluble EPCR able to bind some of the PE-labeled EPCR antibodies. In terms of blocking 
the receptor expression as a mean to offer an alternative method for evaluation of the antibodies 
used, it supports flowcytometric data from the transfection study. Based on two experiments testing 
the antibody validation of the EPCR antibody is consistent. 
 
5.5 | In vivo 
When handling the animals and after i.v. injection with antibodies they were monitored for signs of 
distress or discomfort. Animals administered with the EPCR-specific antibody showed no signs of 
discomfort. 15-20 min upon administration with RI7-specific antibodies, this group of animals became 
lethargic. This was expected and indicated that RI7-antibody binding had occurred.  
 
Conducting the in vivo experiment is based on a pilot study to investigate if specific antibodies can be 
used for targeting the EPCR in the brain. Depending on data retrieved from the experiment, it can 
form the basis of which parameters need to be optimized for future experiments that include carrying 
more specific payloads across the BBB. As a short note, no negative control is included in the 
experiment. With no previous data publishing the application of the EPCR-specific antibody in vivo, it 
is unknown whether the antibodies could be applied and accumulate in a measurable range. Given 
the trial format and with the availability of animals, it was prioritized to ensure a sample size big 
enough for measurements. 
 
5.6 | Evaluation of Dot Blotting Assay  
5.6.1 | Limitations 
It is important to highlight that this experimental setup is limited in many parameters if used for 
studying specific targeting of the EPCR at the BBB. Administration of EPCR- and RI7-specific antibodies 
is measured 30 min after distribution throughout the whole organism, where target-binding occurs in 
multiple areas. Hence, accumulation of antibodies presumably occurs at other organs and tissues, 
besides the brain and heart, such as in the blood, liver, lung, spleen etc. Furthermore, it cannot be 
==PAGE:48==
 
39 
 
ruled out that some of the EPCR-accumulation observed in data could be ascribed to binding to soluble 
EPCR. Additionally, using a semi-quantitative method as dot blotting does not display the proportion 
of antibody delivered and antibody measured. Therefore, the experiment only gives a rough estimate 
of antibody accumulation in the brain or heart.  
 
5.6.2 | Miscalculations 
The calculated concentration of antibody ng/mL should preferably correspond to what is determined 
for the tissue lysates, both in terms of dilution of lysates and antibodies, in addition to measurement 
of integrated density from the nitrocellulose membrane. Currently, the antibody concentration 
determined for EPCR appears ~ 1.5-fold higher than for RI7. That does not correlate with what is seen 
in the dot blot, where signal intensities for both RI7-lysates and the RI7-antibody are much stronger 
than for EPCR. This indicates either erroneous dilutions or miscalculation. However, based on the 
standard curves determined for EPCR and RI7 dilutions appear to have been executed relatively 
correctly. Despite several attempts it has not been possible to correct the miscalculation. 
 
The much stronger signal from RI7 compared to EPCR seen in the dot blot, can be due to several 
reasons, such as RI7 dotted on the membrane is not from the same batch than the one injected in the 
animals. As it is used from an in-house made stock, the exact concentration is unknown, and therefore 
needs to be determined in order to make a correct calculation. Also, the dilutions are disproportioned.  
The initial dot for EPCR [1 mg/mL] represents a 10-fold dilution and the subsequent dots are 2-fold 
diluted from the initial. The initial dot for RI7 [5 mg/mL] represents a 30-fold dilution followed by 2-
fold dilutions. The 30-fold dilution for RI7 is based on the strong signal it already produces, maybe 
indicating a higher concentration than [5 mg/mL]. The different signal intensities can be due to 
difference in binding abilities. The stronger signal can be caused by the physical properties of RI7, such 
as antibody specificity and avidity and it can be speculated whether RI7 represents a higher number 
of binding sites compared to EPCR. To avoid skewed results, aspects like aiming for more ‘equal’ 
starting points need to be considered, such as using antibodies from same batches and maybe of equal 
concentration, so data becomes more comparable. 
 
5.6.3 | Cross-reactivity 
Another aspect that need emphasis, is the data from binding of goat anti-mouse IgG antibody. 
Interestingly, the signal for the EPCR and RI7 antibodies dotted to generate standard curves are 
observed. The two 2° antibodies in use are goat anti-mouse IgG and goat anti-rat IgG, while both 1° 
antibodies are produced in rat, which indicates indicate inadvertent cross-reactivity. 
Statistics 
 
5.6.4 | Concluding the Use of Dot Blotting 
Notable mentioning, in order to explore the EPCR and its ability of carrying payloads across the BBB, 
will require a functional payload, e.g. antibody functionalized gold nanoparticles (Au-NPs). Second, a 
larger amount of sample size is required, i.e. more animals or inclusion of blood and other organs like 
the liver, lungs, spleen etc. Third, to measure binding of e.g. antibody to the tissue of interest it is 
imperative to exsanguinate or perfuse123,124  the animal to avoid that antibodies are measured in blood 
fractions. Fourth, it requires application of other methods to analyze the samples. If using Au-NPs 
inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) offers an excellent opportunity for 
==PAGE:49==
 
40 
 
quantitative measurements. ICP-MS is particularly useful due to presenting a wide dynamic linear 
range, which allows to include samples with no need to be diluted prior analyses119. Last, but not least, 
when applying statistics to life sciences, it is essential for the interpretation of results that the 
appropriate statistical test is applied to the test. Unpaired-t-test was applied for these analyses, 
however using a one-way ANOVA-test might provide other more useful information for evaluation of 
data. 
 
PART II 
5.7 | Recellularized Spinach Tissue Scaffolds 
Culturing the mRECs on and in SLs is interesting to investigate in further detail, if viable to e.g. analyze 
if expression of EPCR may be altered compared to cultures in 2D monolayer. If possible, it can be useful 
to apply engineered tissue scaffolds for studying receptor-mediated transport mechanisms. Culturing 
mammalian cells on plant tissue scaffolds has proven feasible105,117,125, however, data presented from 
SEM implies that mRECs can be cultured under more optimal conditions. 
 
A consideration for optimization could be coating SLs alternatively. Only FN was used, but maybe 
adding other proteins such as collagen can allow better growing conditions for the cells. Perhaps, by 
following the protocol described by Gershlak et al could provide improved leaf samples for cultivation 
of cells. However, different timeframes and experimental setups, such as e.g. providing cannulated 
leaves with continuous fresh medium105, are required to comply with their protocol. Another issue to 
keep in mind for optimizing culture conditions, could be residual detergents in the decellularized 
spinach. Despite washing SLs with PBS and MQ and storage in MQ in up to 14 days, it cannot be 
excluded that cells do not proliferate properly due to detergent-induced cytotoxicity126.  
 
A challenge with decellularizing plants is the intricacies in eliminating plant DNA. Additionally, reducing 
scattering and autofluorescence from pigments with complex absorption in plant material complicate 
imaging with confocal microscopy127. Requirements exist determining when tissues are classified as 
decellularized and released from residual DNA. Complying with such requirements are not the project 
scope, but interest in removing DNA is. The clearing solutions and decellularization protocols followed 
by Gershlak et al were incapable of completely removing all plant cellular material105, as was the case 
in this current project. Hence, it was attempted to improve DNA-removal by including treatment with 
UV-light128. Various time frames with UV-A (320–390 nm) and UV-B (280–320 nm) were applied. 
Stomates in the SLs are paired guard cells forming pores that regulate gas exchange in plant 
epidermis129,130 and they are easily observed in the initial images processed from SDCM. In the 
proceeding images they are seen to a less extent, suggesting that 45 min with UV-B might have had 
an effect. However, the high presence of autofluorescence in the images is causing noise that might 
cover the visuals of stomates. 
 
5.8 | Spinning Disk Confocal Microscopy (SDCM) 
Noteworthy mentioning is when performing live cell imaging, cultured cells only behave normally if 
offered an optimal physiological environment. This is of particular importance if studying e.g. cell-cell 
interactions131. During image acquisition providing optimal conditions for the cells by setting 
temperature and CO2 were not applied, since the primary purpose was to observe if detection of 
==PAGE:50==
 
41 
 
cannulated mRECs was feasible.  Based on data it appears as if this is possible, but to support this 
notion using fluorescent polymer beads might better confirm the presence of seeded cells in the 
microvasculature. Whether mRECs on spinach leaves can be detected with SDCM is associated with 
uncertainty based on data from this project. It might appear as such, however when analyzing the 
images in detail it is not always clearly determined from which level in the z-stack the various elements 
originate. 
 
Acquiring useful information with SDCM can be limited by the sample thickness due to the vertical 
distance between the objective and sample, which was an initial issue. Conditions were optimized by 
placing the leaf samples ‘bottom-up’ in the Ibidi-chambers and remove excess PBS by pipette. The 
underpressure created by suction made samples ‘attach’ to the bottom surface prior imaging in SDCM. 
This improved the acquisition of images with the inverted microscope, enhancing the chance to detect 
cells on the leaf surface, as they were brought closer to the objective. Only 4x and 20x objective lenses 
were used, but more useful information is high likely gained using e.g. 60× oil immersion objective 
lenses. The challenge with autofluorescence from the SLs can be circumvented by e.g. UV-treatment 
as described above. Adjusting parameter settings such as exposure time and laser power also play a 
role. A trade-off needs consideration though, since samples risk getting ruined due to 
photobleaching132. These changes need to be accounted for in future experiments, also to reduce the 
risk of observing artifacts as what might suggest the presence of EPCR. In addition, which stains are 
chosen is significant. To avoid crosstalk Hoechst, Cell Mask and AF488 were chosen based on the 
separation between their emission and excitation spectra. Also, the concentrations of antibodies can 
influence output. In these experiments various concentrations were attempted. Higher 
concentrations such as 1:500 appeared to produce better data, though these do not unambiguously 
support observation of mRECs.

6 | CONCLUSION 
With this project it was desired to investigate whether the brain microcapillary in mice express EPCR. 
This could not be confirmed, since mBECs were discarded. However, using flow cytometry it was 
possible to identify and characterize EPCR expression in mRECs. Under the assumptions stated in the 
introduction, it is conceivable that similar data could display analogous properties for mBECs.  
However, as a pilot study it did serve as a framework of what should be included in a future 
experiment. Examples are such as including blood perfusion, maybe use antibody functionalized 
nanoparticles and other methods for analysis such as applying the use of ICP-MS. 
 
Whether EPCR can be used as a receptor-mediated transport system for delivering payloads across 
the capillary wall and beyond the BBB cannot be answered with studies conducted in this work. It 
would require different applications of methods to investigate this matter. Data suggested that EPCR- 
and transferrin-specific antibodies could be detected in the brain and heart of mice. However, 
accumulation of antibodies in the brain and heart of the animals did not provide information, whether 
antibodies were bound to tissue, blood or maybe even soluble EPCR. Thus, the semiquantitative data 
from immuno-dot blotting is insufficient of suggesting that antibodies were carried across the capillary 
wall of the BBB. 
 
If spinach can be proposed as a cell scaffold to study receptor-mediated transport mechanisms in 
primary mammalian cells is still subject for exploration. Displayed by SEM spinach showed the ability 
to function as a scaffold for culturing cells, however it was not shown to an extent, where healthy cell 
growth could be observed. 
Based on SDCM, indications that the microvasculature could be used for culturing cells certainly raises 
speculation in the possibilities of how transport mechanisms could be studied. It would require 
inclusion of other applications than those used in this study. Perhaps altered coating strategies could 
establish improved culture conditions. An example could be using combinations of proteins 
represented at e.g. the microvasculature of the BBB. This would raise new implications though. 
Despite, culturing cells in conditions resembling the in vivo environment in more detail, cells in vivo 
function in concert with many other parameters such as neighboring cells, signaling molecules, 
nutrient supply, metabolic waste disposal etc. Some of these conditions could to some extent be 
provided by establishing provision of medium, which has been attempted in other studies with gravity 
driven reservoirs. 
 
As might appear evident from this background, orchestrating studies as presented here applies 
considerations within several fields spanning from molecular biology, chemistry, material science, and 
imaging techniques. However, to produce the results in this thesis relied on working across science 
fields. For the observant reader, the substantial results that neuroscience achieved within a timeframe 
of 20 years showed that advances within science indeed have increased with exponential pace. In 
particular given that when results really began to unfold, was when science fields started engaging.    
One scientific field cannot do without the other, for real progression to occur. Hence, undoubtedly, it 
is truly at the interdisciplinary crossroads that the real magic starts to happen.
