Abstract 
As the building sector shifts toward ambitious decarbonization goals, there remains a 
lack of comprehensive studies that combine Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) with occupant-
centered evaluations of lighting installations in real office environments. This study 
assessed the environmental performance and user perception of upcycled pendant 
luminaires installed in an office in Denmark by following an approach that integrated 
cradle-to-gate LCA, DIALux lighting simulations, on-site illuminance measurements, and 
subjective occupant surveys. 
The study compared an upcycled luminaire scenario with a standard industry 
alternative, defining a functional unit based on recommended office illuminance of 500 
lux and a uniformity index of at least 0.6 on work plane (0,8m) over a 20-year and 60-
year lifetime with 2500 annual operating hours. Results showed that the operational 
electricity use stage remains the dominant contributor to total Global Warming Potential. 
Reusing luminaire housings significantly reduces the impacts linked to the product stage, 
especially in a 60-year use period with component replacements, compared to the 
replacement of the total installation. Survey responses from occupants revealed 
generally high satisfaction with visual comfort regarding light level perception, 
preference, and tolerance. However, it highlighted a need for individual light control to 
accommodate the varied brightness preferences. 
The study showed that higher luminous output and a more uniform light distribution 
in luminaires can reduce the number of luminaires required to cover office lighting 
recommendations. The lower number of lamps needed and a lower production impact 
through the reuse of luminaire housing and easy repairability can significantly lower the 
GWP contribution of light installations while providing adequate visual comfort for the 
occupants.

Introduction 
Driven by increasingly worrying issues such as resource depletion, biodiversity loss, 
and climate change, global attention is shifting toward sustainability and 
decarbonization. To minimize the environmental impact of our society, many nations 
agreed to limit global warming well below 2 °C and pursue a scenario closer to 1,5 °C, 
which would require a 45% global reduction of greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 and 
reach net zero by 2050 (1). 
The building and construction sector is a major greenhouse gas emitter, accounting 
for 37% of emissions globally (2). In Europe alone, it accounts for more than 30% of the 
total environmental footprint, making it a crucial sector to decarbonize and optimize for 
minimum resource depletion. According to a report of the EEA (European Environmental 
Agency), this sector accounts for one-third of the total material consumption and about 
42% of the total energy consumption in the EU, with 35% of the total greenhouse gas 
emissions coming from constructing, operating, and dismantling buildings (3). 
Although lighting represents a small fraction of a building´s material consumption, it 
has a large operational impact. According to UNEP, lighting accounts for around 15% of 
global electricity consumption and 4.6% of greenhouse gas emissions (4). While the 
transition to LED has substantially increased the energy efficiency of luminaires and 
hence reduced their environmental impact, the operational energy use remains the 
primary contributor to their footprint (5). 
While there have been several studies on the Life Cycle Assessment of luminaires, 
there is a lack of comprehensive and updated assessments, especially for eco-design 
purposes. Those studies often differ in methodology, functional units, and system 
boundaries, and don´t consider the visual perception and comfort of the lighting for 
occupants. That is why this study aims to comprehensively study and analyze the 
environmental and perceptual impact of upcycled and easily repairable lighting 
installations through LCA analysis, measurements, light simulations, and surveys. 
In collaboration with Fischer Lighting, this project assesses an office building that has 
implemented low-carbon lighting solutions. Specifically, the study aims to:

- 
Assess the environmental impact of the lighting solutions through LCA 
simulations and compare them with standard luminaires. 
- 
Evaluate the performance of the lighting solutions through lighting simulations 
and measurements.

comfort and satisfaction.

1. Background   
This section presents a comprehensive overview of sustainability practices and LCA in 
lighting solutions, light properties, occupant behavior, and comfort. 
 
1.1. 
Sustainability in lighting 
Although a lighting installation may not take a big part of the mass of a building, its 
operational energy use represents a significant share of the global electricity demand, 
with about 15% of the global electricity consumption (4). Electricity use in the commercial 
sector accounts for 11% of the global consumption, with the remaining 4% being used 
in residential buildings (6).  
Lighting installations have the most impact on the use phase, which is dependent on 
the national electricity mix. As countries shift towards a 100% renewable energy scenario, 
these impacts will be considerably minimized. Denmark is targeting a scenario of fully 
renewable electricity by 2050 (7). 
According to the IEA, electricity consumption increased in 2022, with more efficient 
lighting not offsetting the increased use of lighting. There is considerable progress in 
LED technologies and their lighting efficiency. Some countries are already adopting 
strategies, such as the United Kingdom, which proposed gradually increasing the 
minimum energy performance for lighting to 140lm/W in 2027 (8).  Best technologies 
can now achieve over 200 lm/W, and, despite being more expensive, they are expected 
to be more affordable and widely available over time (9). 
Phasing out older and less efficient technologies, such as the incandescent and 
fluorescent lamps, is crucial to align with the Net Zero Emissions by 2050 scenario, as 
well as a continued decarbonization of electricity generation through renewable sources. 
By 2030, emissions must fall by two-thirds by prioritizing more efficient technologies and 
longer lifetimes (10).

1.2. 
Circularity of lighting 
Earth is going through a resource depletion crisis, which makes not only the global 
warming potential of lighting products important, but also the circularity of their 
components. According to the Circularity Gap Report, the global circularity metric was 
7,2% in 2023. Despite Denmark´s effort to lead in sustainability, only 4% of materials 
made it back into the economy in the shape of recycled materials (11).  
Circularity in lighting installations is a sustainable approach that aims to minimize 
waste and optimize resource use throughout the lifecycle of lighting products and 
systems (12). It represents a shift from the traditional "linear model" of extract, 
manufacture, use, and discard, towards a system where resources are continually used 
and reused (13). This concept is crucial for reducing energy consumption, achieving 
sustainability targets, and future-proofing premises.  
Regarding material recovery, it has been argued that recovering rare earth elements 
and critical minerals from lighting products is not only better for the environment but 
also commercially viable, both for fluorescent and LED-based luminaires (14). 
Moreover, making sure lighting installations are designed for easy disassembly makes 
it possible to improve their lifecycle by allowing replacements of components. Design 
for disassembly is linked to numerous circularity and sustainability goals over the lifetime 
of a luminaire and can have a positive impact on the carbon footprint of the product (15). 
In Denmark, companies such as Fischer Lighting are leading the development of 
sustainable lighting design by upcycling old luminaire housings and other construction 
elements into highly efficient luminaires. Thanks to the implementation of clever magnet 
attachments instead of permanent fixing of the components, easy disassembly is 
achieved. This makes it possible to replace its components when needed, extending the 
lifetime of their luminaires (16). 
 
1.3. 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) in lighting 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a process used to evaluate the environmental impacts 
of a product during its entire lifecycle, which contributes to resource-use efficiency and 
sustainability evaluations (17). To quantify the environmental impact of lighting 
==PAGE:12==
 
5 
installations, it is crucial to analyze their impact from production to disposal, accounting 
for every step in between, such as transport and the use phase. In a context of buildings 
such as offices, an LCA study captures both the impacts from the production of the 
installed luminaires and operational impacts over a defined operational use. This 
approach can help identify hotspots and improvement opportunities to make more 
sustainable lighting solutions.  
According to ISO 14040 (18) and ISO 14044 (19) standards, an LCA must have a well-
defined Functional Unit (FU) and System Boundary. The Functional Unit is a quantified 
reference to which all inputs and all outputs are related, which allows fair comparison 
between alternatives. It defines the function of the product or system being evaluated. 
Meanwhile, system boundaries determine which life-cycle stages, processes, and flows 
to include in the scope of the study. In the case of construction products like luminaires, 
the European standard EN 15804:2012+A2:2019 (20) provides a modular structure: the 
production stage (A1-A3), construction stage (A4-A5), use stage (B1-B7), end-of-life (C1-
C4), and the additional D module, which establishes the benefits and loads beyond the 
system boundary. These definitions are essential to ensure conformity and comparability 
for LCA studies. 
 
1.3.1. 
Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) 
As sustainability becomes a global priority for lighting design, manufacturers are 
increasingly publishing Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) to document LCA 
results of their luminaires under standardized rules. In the EU, EN 15804:2012+A2:2019 
establishes core Product Category Rules (PCR) for the product category of construction 
products, including luminaires, to ensure consistency, comparability, and transparency 
of EPDs (20).  
 
1.3.2. 
Tools for LCA   
As an LCA requires extensive data and calculations, several tools and databases are 
available, which make this process more efficient and accurate. Tools such as SimaPro 
(21) and One Click LCA (22) are industry standard, combined with databases such as 
==PAGE:13==
 
6 
EcoInvent (23), which provides generic background data such as materials, processes, 
and transportation.  
 
1.3.3. 
Environmental impact metrics 
Environmental impact metrics quantify various specific types of damage inflicted on 
the environment throughout the life cycle of a product. Global Warming Potential (GWP) 
evaluates climate change contribution by comparing all greenhouse gas emissions to an 
equivalent amount of carbon dioxide (kg CO₂-eq). Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP) 
measures the effect of emissions that impact the ozone layer breakdown. Acidification 
Potential (AP) evaluates the potential to acidify the environment, primarily because of 
emissions such as sulfur dioxide or nitrogen oxides. Eutrophication Potential (EP) 
measures the potential for enrichment of water or soil with nutrients to lead to harmful 
algal blooms. Photochemical Ozone Creation Potential (POCP) measures the potential 
of ground-level ozone or smog formation. Among the environmental impact metrics 
described, GWP is usually highlighted due to its direct connection with climate change. 
 
1.3.4. 
Ongoing research on LCA and the environmental 
impacts of lighting 
Several studies analyzed focus on individual luminaires, assessing their environmental 
impacts based on defined functional units. For instance, a study conducted by Tähkämö 
et al. conducted a detailed lifecycle assessment of a downlight LED luminaire, setting 
the functional unit (FU) as a one luminaire used for 50.000h (24). The study concluded 
that the use phase dominates the GWP impacts, followed by the manufacturing stage, 
with approximately 23% of the impacts on a French low-carbon electricity grid scenario. 
The LED board, driver, and aluminum parts were identified as hotspots. The use-phase 
GWP impacts depended on the average electricity mix of the country, with higher 
impacts on the European grid (over 90%) compared to the French one (76%). Moreover, 
the study highlighted that luminous efficacy is a relatively appropriate environmental 
indicator of a luminaire. 
==PAGE:14==
 
7 
Another study conducted in Hong Kong compared high-power LEDs with Compact 
Fluorescent Lamps (CFL) for indoor lighting (25). The study set 40 million lumen-hours 
(lm-h) over an operative time of 20.000h and concluded that the use phase was the 
predominant contributor of GWP impacts for both lamps. Despite the CFL containing 
hazardous materials such as mercury, their contribution to environmental impacts 
remained lower than the use phase, especially on the GWP. In the LED lamp, the LED 
package showed a dominant environmental impact, followed by the driver. Moreover, a 
scenario analysis on electricity production sources showed that prospective sources 
lowered the impacts compared to the current electricity mix of the country by 61%. 
A LCA study of an industrial LED lighting product extended the analysis to include 
social lifecycle impacts, setting one unit of a 100W luminaire during 40.000h of operating 
time as the FU (5). The study concluded with recommendations to lower its 
environmental impacts by energy efficiency improvements, a lifetime prolongation, and 
a reduction of housing material. 
A study conducted by Principi and Fioretti presented an LCA of two lighting 
technologies, LED and CFL, used for general office lighting (26). The study defined two 
FUs: 1 lm per 50.000h and 1 lux per 50.000h. Setting the illuminance (lux) as the 
functional unit not only evaluated the efficiency of the luminaires but also considered the 
position and characteristics of the room. On the other hand, lumen-hours were 
recommended for use in simple LCA evaluations that are not context-specific.  
A comparative LCA of LED products by Casamayor et al. (27) made a comparison 
between a new eco-lighting product and an existing LED product to assess and compare 
eco-design in lighting. It used multiple scenarios of lifetimes ranging from 1.000 to 
40.000h and different end-of-life options, demonstrating a 60% reduction of 
environmental impacts on the eco-designed version.  
Albu et al. conducted a study that combined LCA of an LED suspended luminaire in 
Romania with lighting simulations in DIALux Evo (28). The simulations served to 
determine the correct number and position of the studied luminaires, taking into 
consideration the solution of implementing daylight system control. The study 
concluded that about 96% of the environmental impacts on total GWP fossil were 
generated in the production phase. Moreover, it highlighted that the environmental 
==PAGE:15==
 
8 
impact at the manufacturing stage was mainly determined by the aluminum, mostly 
present in the luminaire body, fixing clips, LED module, and driver. 
The definition of a Functional Unit (FU) is critical when conducting and evaluating LCA 
studies, especially when several products are compared in a case study. Studies vary 
between lamp-based units, such as one luminaire per a defined number of operational 
hours, and lumen/flux-based units. In a 2017 case study on LCA of lighting technologies, 
Tähkämö and Dillon emphasized how lumen and illuminance-based functional units 
better capture the performance of lighting systems in real-world settings, especially 
when lighting installation layout and room characteristics are relevant (29)  
Regarding system boundary definition, most studies adopt either a “cradle-to-grave” 
or “cradle-to-gate” approach, which covers raw material extraction, transportation, 
manufacturing, use, and end-of-life stages.  
Overall, all studies conclude that the use-phase is by far the most contributor of 
environmental impacts and a clear hotspot for major sustainability improvements. The 
choice of the FU should align with the goal and scope of the study, especially when 
investigating real-scenario case studies. 
 
1.4. 
Lighting quality in indoor workspaces 
Light quality is a crucial aspect of sustainable building design of indoor workspaces, 
where specific technical criteria ensure visual comfort, performance, and well-being. This 
section explores lighting principles, requirements, and standards that support the well-
being of occupants.  
 
1.4.1. 
Lighting principles 
The Correlated Color Temperature (CCT) describes the color appearance of white 
light emitted by a light source, measured in Kelvin (K). It quantifies how warm the light 
looks. Warm CCTs are perceived as cozy and relaxing, being ideal for social and lounge 
areas, especially in the evenings. Cooler CCTS (4000-6500K) are associated with 
alertness and concentration and are often used in task areas (30). 
==PAGE:16==
 
9 
Illuminance refers to the amount of light that falls on a surface and is measured in lux, 
which measures the number of lumens that impact a square meter. Horizontal 
illuminance refers to the amount of light that falls on a horizontal surface, such as a desk, 
while vertical illuminance refers to vertical surfaces, such as walls and windows. Sufficient 
horizontal illuminance is crucial when performing tasks such as reading. 
Uniformity (U0) indicates the evenness of the light distribution on a defined space and 
is determined by the light distribution of the luminaire and the number of luminaires 
placed in an area.  
Glare is a term that describes the reduction of visual performance or the disturbance 
of perception. It is caused by high contrast or brightness interference within a visual 
environment and can reduce visual comfort and performance (31). The discomfort from 
glare is quantified through a unitless Unified Glare Rating (UGR).  
 
1.4.2. 
Lighting requirements for office spaces 
The international standard EN 12464-1:2021 (32) sets lighting requirements to ensure 
an optimal visual comfort and performance in indoor workspaces for people with normal 
vision. For example, around a horizontal illuminance of 500 lux is required in the task 
area for performance and visual comfort, and 750 lux when performing detailed tasks 
such as technical drawings. Tasks that have a longer duration or require higher visual 
demand may also require higher illuminance levels. When it comes to uniformity, the 
standard recommends a Uₒ equal to or higher than 0.60.  
For offices, the standard recommendations should be minimized, with a Unified Glare 
Rating (UGR) of less than 19 in office spaces with screens. It is recommended to have a 
minimum color rendering index (CRI) of 80. To avoid visual discomfort, light should be 
free of perceptual flicker. 
Lighting must support the well-being of its occupants, maintain a healthy work 
environment by reducing fatigue, and maintain a high alertness. As it influences the 
circadian rhythm and mood, it must be carefully planned. 
When it comes to energy, efficient lighting design that incorporates daylight and low-
power solutions is promoted. Having the possibility of dimming lighting systems is ideal 
==PAGE:17==
 
10 
to adapt to the changing needs of occupants, depending on their tasks, and reduce 
unnecessary electricity consumption through lower light levels when needed. 
When it comes to daylight availability, using natural daylight is a key practice to 
improve energy efficiency and occupant well-being.  
 
1.5. 
User perception and visual comfort 
A subjective evaluation of user perception and visual comfort is crucial to understand 
the perception and experience of occupants when it comes to the lighting environment 
beyond technical objective measurements. Despite meeting objective illuminance and 
uniformity recommendations, individual preferences and comfort can vary from occupant 
to occupant.  
 
1.5.1. 
Subjective judgement scales (ISO 10551) 
The standard ISO 10551:2019 provides examples and principles for creating and 
making use of subjective judgment scales to assess how individuals perceive and 
evaluate physical environments (33). It is particularly useful for studies that explore the 
link between environmental conditions and the human perception of a space. While the 
standard was originally developed for thermal environments, some visual scale examples 
can be found in Annex C of the official document. These questions revolve around key 
visual comfort subjects such as glare and brightness. The key subjective scales consist 
of: 
- 
Perceptual scales capture direct sensations such as the level of brightness and 
dimness of an environment.  
- 
Preference scales indicate the desired change in preference for an 
environment. In the case of lighting, they serve to evaluate whether individuals 
prefer the environment to be brighter or darker.  
- 
Evaluative scales evaluate the level of comfort and discomfort of an 
environment.  
==PAGE:18==
 
11 
- 
Tolerance scales serve to assess whether individuals accept the lighting 
conditions of their environment. 
To ensure unbiased and valid questions, some principles must be followed. Questions 
should make use of clear and neutral wording. Instead of leading questions with terms 
such as too dark or too bright, neutral words such as brightness level should be used. 
Moreover, they must be balanced, with equal options on either side from the neutral 
point and have a consistent context.  
Those scales can be numeric or consist of wordings such as bright and dark, which 
can have several levels of intensity. Questions may be polar or bipolar, and have 3, 5, or 
7 intensity levels. Some may require even two, such as acceptable and unacceptable, 
while others, such as the brightness level, may go up to 7. An example of this regarding 
brightness perception would be: “very dark, dark, slightly dark, adequate, slightly bright, 
bright, very bright”.  
The standard can then be complemented with quantitative environmental 
measurements such as illuminance levels, making a holistic study on visual comfort. 
Although subjective scales described above provide insights into how occupants 
perceive the lighting of their visual environment, combining these subjective responses 
with objective measurements offers valuable insights into visual comfort. The compliance 
with the EN 12464-1:2021 standard recommendations does not guarantee that 
occupants feel satisfied or comfortable. 
 
1.6. 
Research gap 
While several research studies have compared the environmental performance of 
lighting products through LCA, none have directly accounted for upcycled luminaires 
and included user perception and visual comfort, which are vital factors in lighting design 
for the workplace. In addition, studies differ in methodology, functional units, and system 
boundaries, which limits the comparability and practical application in a real case 
scenario.

2. Methodology 
The study followed an evaluation of pendant luminaires installed in an office in 
Denmark by integrating a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) with quantitative illuminance 
measurements and subjective surveys, backed with light simulations.  
 
2.1. 
Case Study 
The case study took place in IKANO Bolig’s office in Glostrup, Denmark, which has 
integrated low-carbon lighting solutions from Fischer Lighting (34). The office consists of 
two open-plan workspaces, a flexible-use common area, three meeting rooms, and three 
meeting pods. Although several luminaires from Fisher Lighting´s catalog were 
implemented throughout the different spaces of the office, only the two open-plan 
workspaces were selected for the study (see Figure 1). This served as a practical example 
of how the implementation of low-carbon solutions performs in a real office space 
setting. 
Figure 1: IKANO Bolig´s office - open-plan workspaces (north and south)

These two open-plan workspaces have a total capacity of 36 desks: the larger northern 
workspace accommodates 24 desks, while the southern workspace accommodates 12 
(see Figure 2 and Figure 3). At the time of the case study, the office was being occupied 
by 17 employees, leaving more than half of the workspace empty. As the sustainability 
responsible stated, the extra capacity of the office contemplated the future expansion 
to accommodate new employees, as the previous one had been near full capacity. 
 
 
 
2.1.1. 
Current light installation  
In the current light installation, the northern workspace consists of two rows of 7 Judd 
lamps installed perpendicular to the desks, totaling 14 luminaires. On the other hand, 
the southern workspace consists of two rows of 4 lamps installed in parallel to the desks, 
adding up to 8 luminaires. 
The Judd luminaires are made from upcycled light fixtures from a grocery chain (35). 
The fixtures consist of a steel housing with a long-lasting quality and architecture (see 
Figure 4). These pendant linear luminaires are installed at a height of 2,6m. The shape 
suits the modern, industrial, and minimalist aesthetic often used in architectural contexts. 
The proportions and dimensions of 1529 x 231 x 85 mm make the fixture an iconic yet

discreet object in the space. Moreover, the metallic finish makes it possible to admire 
the history of the lamps, as they keep their original look.  
Each Judd luminaire emits 9663 lm of luminous flux at a CCT of 4000 K, delivering a

2.1.2. 
Alternative low-carbon lighting scenario 
Due to limited data on the Judd lamp for a lifecycle assessment, a similar luminaire 
from Fisher Lighting with an available EPD was selected: Sunrise (36). Despite being 
about 30cm shorter and having nearly half of the lumen output as Judd, both have a 
similar architecture and efficiency. Its luminous flux of 4931 lumens is delivered with a 
luminous efficacy of 127,3 lm/W. As Judd, Sunrise is made from the reuse of an old 
luminaire fixture that has been turned into an efficient LED lamp with a new diffuser. 
 
2.1.3. 
Alternative lighting scenario for comparison 
A new luminaire with an available EPD and DIALux file was found to make a 
comparison. The luminaire had to have a similar architecture to Sunrise, and similar 
specifications regarding light output and distribution. Several luminaires were 
considered, but one lamp stood out: Fagerhult Notor 65 Delta (37). Like the Judd 
luminaire, this pendant lamp is made from a rectangularly shaped metallic housing and

2.3. 
Life Cycle Assessment 
The study was carried out by comparing two similar luminaires: Sunrise, from Fischer 
Lighting, and Notor 65 from Fagerhult. Both are pendant downlights for general lighting, 
ideal for office spaces in a grid distribution. The lifecycle assessment was conducted 
according to the standard EN 15804_2012+A2_2019, using SimaPro 10.2.0.0 with the 
“EcoInvent 3 – allocation, cut-off by classification – unit” library. With this method, unit 
processes are handled transparently, with recycled materials entering the system having 
zero burden for their previous lifecycle and “starting fresh” with no upstream impacts.   
 
2.3.1. 
Goal and Scope 
An LCA analysis was carried out to investigate the benefits of low-carbon lighting 
installation in an office space in Denmark compared to conventional new luminaire 
installation. As mentioned above, due to limited data on the Judd lamps, the Sunrise 
lamp was selected and compared to Notor 65 from Fagerhult. 
Both lamps have their respective Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs). Despite 
Sunrise’s EPD not being public by the time of the study, Fisher Lighting shared it 
confidentially. Notor 65´s EPD is publicly available (38), but does not dive deep into 
lifecycle stages and inventory analysis. Moreover, it is not product-specific but represents 
the worst-case scenario out of several products in the Notor 65 family. Both make use of 
“EcoInvent – allocation, cut-off by classification – unit”. 
 
2.3.2. 
Functional Unit 
The next crucial step was to define a functional unit that could fairly assess and 
compare the selected lamps in the case study. Despite EPDs differing in their functional 
unit, both had a functional unit that assessed the environmental impacts per luminaire 
for a given Reference Service Life (RSL). Fisher Lighting´s EPD sets an RSL of 60 years, 
accounting for the replacement of components such as the driver and LED board. 
According to Fischer Lighting, LEDs have an operating life of 100.000h, while drivers 
50.000h. On the other hand, Notor 65´s EPD defines an RSL of 20 years with no 
==PAGE:26==
 
19 
component replacements needed for that period, with a technical lifetime of their lamps 
set to 100.000h, meaning that the lamp could potentially be used over 40 years before 
having to replace it with new lamps. 
The two major distinctions that impact their environmental impacts are the electricity 
mix of the country, with Notor 65 being operated in Sweden by mostly renewable 
sources, and their RSL. 
For this study, a lifetime of 20 years was defined, with an annual operation of 2500h. 
As 2500 hours per 20 years equals 50.000h, neither of the components must be replaced 
during that period for any of the lamps, ignoring potential early failures of the most 
critical components.  
As the light simulations would provide the number of lamps needed for each scenario, 
the functional unit was set to “Provide a maintained horizontal illuminance of 500 lux on 
the work plane with a minimum uniformity of 0.6, operating 2,500 hours per year for 20 
years”. The FU does not account for any daylight contribution.

2.3.4.4. 
End-of-life of Sunrise (C1-C4) 
After 20 years of RSL, the luminaire is assumed to be removed in an office 
refurbishment. The EPD assumes that the luminaires are manually dismantled with hand 
tools, with no energy use and material losses. That means that the disassembly stage 
(C1) is a zero-input process.  
As for the waste transport stage (C2), all components are sorted out and transported 
by EURO 6 trucks. Fischer Lighting assumes that the distance from the usage site to the 
local recycling station is 20 km for drivers and LED boards. However, the transport for 
recycling plastics is set to be 1000 km, incineration of plastic 84,5km, recycling metals to 
356 km, and metal sent to landfill to 1000 km.  
Regarding the waste processing stage (C3), 3,166 kg of metal components are sent 
to recycling centers (97%). Moreover, 0,145 kg of plastics are sent for recycling (27%) 
while 0,392 kg for incineration (73%). Electronics are also sent to recycling facilities, such 
as the LED board and driver, where they are manually dismantled to separate metals and 
plastics that can be further recycled. 
Finally, a small portion of the materials end up going through the disposal stage (C4). 
A conservative 3% of metal components is assumed to be lost to landfill.

2.3.4.5. 
End-of-life of Notor 65 (C1-C4) 
After its 20-year RSL, Notor 65 luminaires are manually dismantled and sorted as 
electronic waste by a certified professional, with no mechanical deconstruction required. 
This means that there is no energy input assumed for the deconstruction stage (C1), the 
same as Sunrise´s case. 
Regarding the waste transport stage (C2), waste is transported to municipal waste 
handling facilities by truck. The average assumed distance is around 400 km.  
Moving on to the waste processing stage (C3), materials are manually sorted, 
separated, and sent for recycling whenever possible. As it takes place in Sweden, 
average municipality rates are applied: metals have high recycling rates, while plastics 
have lower recycling efficiency. Electronics are processed to recover metals, and their 
residual fractions are treated as residual waste. 
Finally, as for the final disposal stage (C4), no figures for fractions per material are 
specified, but the EPD states that the impacts were calculated by using the Swedish 
municipal average disposal routes. 
 
2.3.4.6. 
Module D of Sunrise and Notor 65 
Lastly, the module D describes the potential of reuse, recovery, and/or recycling of 
the materials by the end of their lifecycle. Fischer Lighting applies the EASETECH 2015 
substitution factors in their EPD, where they state that: 1kg of secondary aluminum 
replaces 0,97 kg of primary aluminum; 1kg of secondary steel replaces 0,87 kg of primary 
steel; 1kg of plastic sent to recycling can replace 0,37 kg of virgin material. 
The plastic that is sent to incineration can substitute energy in the form of heat and 
electricity. Moreover, it is assumed that the pallets used during the transportation can 
be reused 25 times. 
Regarding Notor 65, no recycling rates are shown in the EPD. It is only mentioned 
that, if recycled according to average municipal recycling in Sweden, the production of 
raw materials will be potentially avoided.

2.4. 
Field study 
The purpose of the field study was to document real-world lighting conditions at the 
office through measurements and surveys. The study took place in the open-plan work 
areas, and measured objective light data was linked with occupants´ subjective 
responses. 
 
2.4.1. 
Field study instruments 
The field study was conducted between Wednesday, 14 May 2025, and Friday, 16 
May 2025. Wednesday was chosen because, due to a bi-weekly general meeting, most 
of the 17 employees would be present. The following devices were used to measure the 
necessary parameters: 
- 
HOBO U12-012 data loggers (42) to measure horizontal illuminance on the work 
plane, together with the HOBOware software (43). 
- 
Spectrometer, to measure eye-level illuminances towards the screen, desk, and 
window from the occupant’s position. 
- 
Google Forms survey to capture subjective responses on the occupant’s visual 
environment. 
HOBO sensors’ accuracy was tested with a comparison with the spectrometer, which 
had higher measurement accuracy, in a uniformly and artificially lit room at DTU. This 
was crucial to make sure that the measurements taken during the field study were 
reliable. The Absolute Percentage Error (APE) was calculated for each sensor to assess 
their deviation from the spectrometer’s measurement. These calculations are found in 
Appendix C – Measurements. 
The day before the study, HOBO sensors were programmed using the HOBOware 
Software to record illuminance measurements from 8:30 am the next morning, every five 
minutes, until turned off. The sensors were secured with adhesive Velcro to easily stick 
them to the desks in a fixed position. Moreover, it was ensured that all sensors had 
sufficient battery, replacing the drained ones to avoid losing data.

2.4.2. 
Field study preparation 
The field study started at 8:30 am on 14 May 2025, where a short meeting took place 
with Lone, the employee responsible for sustainability and the direct contact of the case 
study company. Before the general meeting started, the thesis topic and agenda were 
introduced to the occupants, and Lone sent the link to the Google Forms survey via 
Outlook. It was clearly explained that they should not cover or move the hobo sensors 
until deactivated on Friday. 
While the occupants were in the general meeting, occupied desks were identified and

The decorative lamps were turned off as they were not relevant for the study and

analyze the eye-level illuminance to which occupants are exposed in their workspace. 
The occupants were asked to switch off their monitors right before the measurements 
were taken. This approach minimized the influence of screen luminance and glare, 
isolating the effect of the installed luminaires and daylight.

scenarios can be found in Appendix A – DIALux Simulations. 
Overall, the simulation showed that, despite a high-power consumption of the Judd 
luminaires, major electricity savings could be achieved by dimming the luminaires by 
about half of their total lumen output. Moreover, the current lighting installation achieves 
the minimum light requirements for office spaces.

3.3.1. 
Survey participants 
The survey gathered the answers from 14 of the 17 occupants of the two workspaces,

with an average age of 49 years, most in their forties and fifties. The majority usually

and visual comfort of the occupants in both workspaces. All responses can be found in

3.3.2. 
Survey responses 
All Survey answers on visual perception, preference, comfort, tolerance, and glare 
disturbance on north and south workspaces are represented in Figure 21. 
In the North workspace, most occupants perceived their light levels as bright or very 
bright. However, most would not change the current light levels, with only two occupants 
preferring darker light levels and two brighter. The majority felt comfortable with the 
lighting, while 2 felt slightly uncomfortable, and 1 felt uncomfortable. All occupants 
except one found the lighting acceptable for long working hours. Glare was not a 
significant issue overall, as 3 never experienced disturbing glare, while a few experienced 
it occasionally. One occupant stood out, seated next to the window with the curtains on 
and a structural pillar blocking most of the daylight from the desk. She mentioned she 
would always dim down the general lighting of the north office as she felt comfortable 
when the visual environment was too bright, altering the illuminance levels of the whole 
workspace. She would always use the desk light whenever task lighting was required. 
This highlighted the need for individual light control systems. 
In the South workspace, all 7 occupants described their lighting as adequate to bright, 
suggesting lower overall brightness perception compared with the north workspace. 
Most of them would not change the light levels, except 2 occupants who preferred 
slightly brighter conditions. All respondents felt comfortable and found the lighting

Discussion 
DIALux simulations demonstrated the importance of high luminous flux and uniform 
light distribution in meeting office lighting standards efficiently. The current workspace 
installation with Judd luminaires complied with the EN 12464-1:2021 recommendations 
for visual comfort (≥500 lux, U₀ ≥ 0.6), although excessive brightness was observed in 
both workspaces. This suggests that under non-daylight conditions, these upcycled 
luminaires could be dimmed to around 50–55% to achieve the target horizontal 
illuminance on the work plane, reducing operational energy consumption. These energy 
savings and the associated environmental impacts could be further reduced by including 
daylight contribution, which was not modeled in this study. 
Simulations of alternative lighting scenarios showed that the upcycled Sunrise 
luminaire scenario required fewer lamps (24 units) to meet the same lighting targets 
compared to 30 units for Notor 65. The Judd installation required even fewer (22 units), 
emphasizing how luminous efficacy and light distribution directly affect the number of 
luminaires needed to meet minimum office lighting requirements. Better uniformity and 
higher output enable more efficient installations. 
The Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) confirmed well-established findings in lighting LCA 
research, showing that the operational electricity-use stage (B6) remains the dominant 
contributor to Global Warming Potential (GWP) impacts. In the Sunrise scenario, the use 
stage accounted for 97,3% of total GWP; in the Notor 65 scenario, it represented 79%. 
Although Sunrise consumed 15% more electricity per luminaire to meet lighting targets, 
its significantly lower product stage (A1–A3) impacts, of around 3% of total GWP, 
resulted in a total GWP 6% lower than Notor 65 over a 20-year operational period. 
Despite its lower electricity use, Notor 65 required more luminaires, which raised its 
product stage emissions to 1740 kg CO₂-eq, about seven times higher than Sunrise. The 
raw material stage alone accounted for 18% of Notor 65’s total GWP, compared to only 
2,7% for Sunrise. This highlights the environmental benefit of reusing luminaire housings, 
which nearly eliminates the raw material burden and greatly reduces embodied 
emissions. Other stages barely contributed on the GWP, especially in the case of the 
upcycled luminaires. 
==PAGE:59==
 
52 
When extending the analysis to a 60-year operational use, the Sunrise scenario still 
scored 0,7% lower in total GWP despite its higher cumulative electricity use, which 
accounted for the 98,5% of GWP impacts. This is explained by Sunrise’s modular design, 
which allowed for only two driver and one LED board replacements per luminaire instead 
of full luminaire replacements. In contrast, the Notor 65 luminaires had to be fully 
replaced, doubling the product and end-of-life stage impacts. For Sunrise, the 
replacement stage (B4) represented only 0,55% of total GWP, considerably less than 
what full luminaire replacements would have incurred. On the other hand, the Notor 65 
scenario´s raw material stage alone represented 12,6% of the total GWP impacts with 
3480 kg CO₂-eq, almost 14 times higher than Sunrise. 
Regarding other environmental impacts, Sunrise had a lower impact overall, which 
was mostly attributed to the electricity-use phase. On the other hand, Notor 65 showed 
to have a more varied distribution of impacts on each lifecycle, with higher shares on the 
raw materials and transport stages. The sunrise scenario showed a 20% lower Water 
Depletion Potential (WDP) on both scenarios, as well as considerably lower 
Eutrophication (EP) and Acidification Potential (AP). 
These findings highlight the growing importance of lowering product stage 
environmental impacts, especially as electricity grids transition to renewable energy. As 
highlighted in the literature, a cleaner grid mix reduces the relative contribution of the 
B6 stage, shifting attention to other lifecycle stage impacts. Therefore, minimizing 
material use, optimizing light distribution and layout, and promoting design for 
disassembly and component-level repairability are essential for reducing environmental 
impacts in future lighting systems. 
Survey results from the Judd luminaire installation showed that most occupants found 
their workspace lighting to be adequate or bright and comfortable. Glare was rarely 
reported, and visual discomfort was minimal. Occupants in the north workspace 
perceived their environment as brighter, while some in the south workspace desired 
slightly brighter conditions, consistent with measured daylight and artificial lighting 
levels. One occupant in the north consistently adjusted the general lighting to suit 
personal preferences. The occupant preferred lower environment brightness levels and 
used a personal desk lamp when performing desk tasks, suggesting that standardized 
lighting levels may not meet all individual needs. This underscores the importance of 
personalized lighting control. 
==PAGE:60==
 
53 
Overall, most occupants perceived the current desk lighting as better than their 
previous office and were aware of the upcycled nature of the luminaires, showing positive 
attitudes toward sustainable lighting design. There was no agreement on the aesthetics 
of the luminaires. 
During the field study, conducted on a sunny and long spring day, most occupants 
were seated at desks closest to the windows. As a result, horizontal illuminance during 
working hours was notably higher than at night. In the north workspace, average desk-
level illuminance was above 500 lux, with some desks peaking above 1000 lux. Night 
measurements still reached around 500 lux in many cases. In contrast, the south 
workspace showed greater variation, with several desks being insufficiently lit with 
average illuminance values below 300 lux.  
Daylight contribution was measured by switching off the luminaires during the 
afternoon. In the north, daylight added 400–800 lux, while in the south, the contribution 
dropped to 100 lux or lower. This meant that luminaires were likely considerably dimmed 
down, as artificial lighting did not provide much light overall, especially considering the 
high luminous output of the Judd luminaires. This could substantially affect the LCA 
results if daylight were modeled in the study, reducing the environmental impacts 
embodied to the B6 stage. In some south desks, night measurements were paradoxically 
higher than daytime averages, likely due to occupant shading, partial sensor obstruction, 
or luminaire dimming during the field study.  
Eye-level measurements using a spectrometer revealed that North workspace desks 
averaged 400–500 lux, compared to 150–270 lux in the south. Illuminance levels toward 
screens were generally slightly higher, and window-facing measurements peaked above 
3000 lux in some desks. These results align with subjective feedback: those exposed to 
higher eye-level illuminance often reported the lighting as “very bright” and preferred 
dimmer settings. In contrast, south workspace users with lower eye-level illuminance 
expressed a need for brighter desk conditions. 
Overall, eye-level illuminance measurements correlated well with survey responses, 
although variation in perception was noticeable even among desks with similar measured 
values. This highlights, once again, that standard light levels do not ensure universal 
comfort, reinforcing the value of individual light control systems in open-plan offices. 
==PAGE:61==
 
54 
Limitations 
This study was subject to various limitations, which are crucial to acknowledge for an 
accurate interpretation of the findings and to provide a foundation for future research 
on lifecycle assessment of lighting in real case scenarios with a subjective assessment: 
- 
The selected luminaires for the LCA study were not the same as the ones installed 
in the real case scenario. For this reason, a similar luminaire from the same 
company was used as a proxy, which may not represent the real impact of the 
originally installed luminaires. 
- 
There was a lack of detailed and product-specific EPDs with available light files, 
which limited the extent and depth of the LCA study and the interpretation of the 
results. As the EPD of the compared luminaire was not product-specific, the 
choice of the simulated luminaire may have considerably altered the results. 
- 
There was a limited sample size, with only 14 occupants surveyed, which limited 
the quantification of the response data and correlation with measurements. 
- 
The study evaluation was based on one office location in Denmark with a specific 
type of luminaire, which may not reflect other office lighting setups and user 
behaviors in other contexts. 
- 
The field study was conducted during long spring sunny days, where daylight 
played a big role. This may not represent lighting conditions during darker winter 
months, where artificial light is more in demand.  
- 
Survey responses relied on self-reported perceptions of the occupants and may 
be influenced by external factors such as mood. Moreover, although avoided, bias 
could have possibly influenced their responses. 
- 
Horizontal illuminance measurements recorded by the HOBO data loggers may 
not be accurate enough, and the occupants' behavior may have altered the real 
values through total or partial covering and shadows, as well as the adjustment of 
the desk heights. 
 
 
==PAGE:62==

Conclusion 
This thesis aimed to evaluate the environmental performance and user perception of 
low-carbon lighting solutions through a real case study that combined a Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA), lighting simulations, on-site measurement, and surveys. The results 
indicate that upcycled and easily repairable lamps can fulfill standard office illumination 
requirements while substantially lowering environmental impacts, especially in the 
product stage of their lifecycle.  
The LCA results validated that the use phase of operational electricity continues to be 
the dominant contributor to total Global Warming Potential (GWP). Reusing luminaire 
housings and a design for disassembly with easy replacements significantly reduces the 
embodied impacts of raw material manufacturing and extraction. While the Sunrise 
luminaires consumed more electricity due to their light distribution and lumen output, 
their reduced production and replacement effects produced a 6 percent lower total GWP 
over 20 years and still outperformed over a 60-year operational life. Other environmental 
impacts were further reduced through such low-carbon solutions. 
Lighting simulation showed high luminous efficacy and improved light distribution, 
reducing the number of luminaires to satisfy workplace demand, resulting in lifecycle 
impact savings. Survey and measurement data indicated that most occupants were 
satisfied with lighting levels, but individual preference was uneven, favoring the 
requirement for more individualized and flexible lighting control systems. 
As electric grids go towards complete decarbonization, the relative significance of the 
luminaire product stage will increase. This highlights the necessity of circular lighting by 
reusing components and materials, easy repairability, and optimizing light installations 
through planning and higher efficiency.  
This study's findings aim to provide recommendations for more sustainable light 
installations and a framework for a holistic lifecycle assessment combined with visual 
comfort. The study combined several methodologies for a complete assessment of the 
lighting installation, which had not previously been carried out. One highlighted 
challenge was the lack of product-specific lighting EPDs with available light files. A more 
==PAGE:63==
 
56 
standardized database would positively impact the quality and availability of more 
research on sustainable lighting.  
The study could extend to multiple office spaces with several lighting solutions in 
future work, making the study more reliable and complete. Daylight contribution could 
be considered to explore the further benefits of smarter lighting installations.
