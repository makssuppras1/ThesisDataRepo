Abstract
In this thesis, a spar cap of a wind turbine blade is modelled as a simple cracked config­
uration, which contains an initial defect. The steady­state energy release rate of such a
specimen is derived using the J contour integral and two intermediary cracked configura­
tions. Theoretical solutions and numerical results are given for stress concentrations and
failure indices in isotropic and orthotropic plates with circular openings. Lastly, numeri­
cal results, obtained with finite element modeling, are presented to establish the effects
of various model parameters on the transient zone leading to the theoretically obtained
steady­state results.

1
Introduction
The use of carbon pultrusions in wind turbine blades has become increasingly common
due to their high stiffness and strength. However, these materials are susceptible to inter­
nal defects such as fiber misalignment, voids or foreign objects, which can cause splitting
cracks along the length of the carbon fibers when subjected to axial loading. This can
lead to delamination of the pultrusions and pose a high risk of blade failure. In this study,
we aim to investigate the effect of splitting cracks on the performance of carbon pultrusion
spar caps when subjected to static loading. Our analysis will focus on the energy release
rate at the crack tip of splitting cracks and will be conducted using finite element mod­
eling, based on theoretical considerations. By understanding the robustness of carbon
pultrusion design against this type of crack, we can improve the reliability and safety of
wind turbine blades.
In our investigation, the spar cap has been simplified into a simple pultrusion, which con­
tains an initial defect, as illustrated in Figure 1.1. The defect is modelled as a circular
hole in the later parts of this study, which is described by its radius R. The pultrusion has
a thickness of b and is loaded in uniaxial tension of magnitude σ. The splitting cracks,
initiating from the defect have a length a and their distance from the symmetry plane of
the pultrusion is h. Their distance from the edge of the plate is H. The length of the
pultrusion, L is assumed to be infinite.

Finite Element Model
This chapter very briefly presents the most important considerations about the finite ele­
ment models created to obtain the numerical results of Chapter 4. The simulation models
are built using the Abaqus finite element software.
3.1
Plate geometry, loading and boundary conditions
For the cracked specimens investigated in Chapter 2 three distinct finite element models
are created. In order to increase the efficiency of the simulations, the symmetry conditions
of the models are exploited in all cases. Therefore, Figures 2.1b, 2.3b and 2.5b can be
used to illustrate the geometry, the boundary conditions and the loading for the three
structures. Since the derivations of Chapter 2 assumed that the cracked plate is infinitely
long, this condition has to be reflected in the finite element model in some way. Therefore,
in order to eliminate finite effects, the length of the plate is established to be at least 10
times larger than the length of the crack in the case of all investigated crack lengths.
Figure 3.1a and Figure 3.1b show how the delamination and H­cracked specimens are
modelled in the finite element software. In Figure 3.1a it can be seen that the model has an
additional boundary condition, defined at the top left vertex, compared to Figure 2.1b. This
boundary condition does not influence the value of the energy release rate, however, it is
necessary to include it in the model to avoid the unwanted rotation of the whole structure.
Furthermore, it can be seen that there are some additional geometric regions defined in
the model. The purpose of these is solely to increase mesh efficiency.

3.3
Crack and interaction
In this thesis, crack initiation and crack propagation are not modelled. Therefore, the crack
is predefined in every simulation. However, the length of the crack and its distance from
the top of the domain can be easily adjusted between simulations, making it possible to
carry out an extensive parameter study. The crack is defined using the Contour integral
option in the software. This requires the definition of the crack tip, the crack front and
the crack propagation direction. Additionally, the crack seam also has to be defined.
Figure 3.3 illustrates, how a Contour integral crack can be formulated in the software.
Figure 3.3: Sketch of the crack definition in the finite element software
When the H­cracked specimen and the specimen with the initial circular defect are mod­
elled, it is necessary to define a surface­to­surface contact between the crack faces. If
this interaction is excluded from the model the crack faces are allowed to close into each
other, which leads to incorrect results as it will be shown in Chapter 4. Therefore, in the
simulations, a frictional contact, described by the friction coefficient µ must be defined
between the crack interfaces.
3.4
Mesh
Although the geometries investigated are rather simple, there are some regions in the
model where the application of a refined mesh is necessary. These regions are in the
close vicinity of the crack tip and crack seam and if there is a hole present in the model
the edge of it should also be finely meshed in order to obtain accurate results. However,
far away from these regions, a coarser mesh can be used. For the investigated cracked
configurations quadratic plane strain elements are used for the purpose of increased accu­
racy. Figure 3.4 illustrates a meshed model when the H­cracked specimen is investigated.

Conclusion
Based on the results presented in Chapter 4 it can be concluded that the geometric prop­
erties (η = h/H) of the investigated specimens and the properties of the materials do
not influence the steady­state energy release rate, they only affect the length of the tran­
sient zone. It was found, that the non­dimensional elastic parameter, ρ, in the case of the
carbon/vinyl­ester model used in this paper falls out of the practical range. As a result, the
transient zone of the energy release rate for this material is significantly prolonged and
the steady­state energy release rate is only reached if the effective crack length is large.
The effect of geometric and material properties is relevant to the mode mixity values in
case of the delamination specimen, however, their effects are negligible in the case of
the other specimens and effective crack lengths larger than 0.5. Furthermore, it can be
concluded that the shear stress values along the crack interfaces are influenced by the
material properties and the length of the crack.
From the investigations concerning the friction coefficient, it can be deduced that their
effect is significant as it influences the specimen’s susceptibility to unstable crack growth.
From the investigations of the specimen with an initial defect, it can be inferred that the
location of the crack relative to the center of the hole, θ, can influence the fracture char­
acteristics and crack growth. From the numerical results presented for this specimen,
it can be inferred, that the built finite element model has some shortcomings when the
combination of large θ values and small crack lengths is considered.

Future work
This chapter presents the possible improvements and extensions that can be made to the
model.
First of all, it was seen in Chapter 4 that there were some parameter regions that could
not be mapped properly, as the built finite element model produced unphysical results.
Therefore, the underlying cause of these results has to be found and a model should be
built accordingly.
Once this model was built and tested, it could be extended to three dimensions. The
basis of the calculations presented in this paper, the J integral, can be extended to three
dimensions, however in that case it becomes a surface integral. Extending the model
would make it possible to model not only a single pultrusion, but a pultrusion stack as
well, which would be a more accurate representation of the wind turbine blade’s spar cap.
However, before moving on to the modeling of the pultrusion stack, the 3D model of a
single pultrusion should be tested and validated. In this case, the delamination between
the damaged and surrounding pultrusions should be investigated as well.
Furthermore, the effects of cyclic loading should also be investigated for both a single
pultrusion and a pultrusion stack. With everything taken into account, a very robust model
could be created, which then could be used to create real­life applications.
