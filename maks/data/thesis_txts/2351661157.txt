Summary
Hospitals have the highest energy consumption per unit ﬂoor area in the buildings sector. A
great proportion of it is intended for maintaining a good indoor environment (ASHRAE, 2008).
Nowadays, ventilation systems used are mostly total volume ventilation systems, e.g. mixing
or displacement ventilation, requiring a considerable amount of energy and not ensuring to be
always an eﬀective way to provide high air quality to the space.
The requirements for ventilation in health care facilities by the present standards are high, with
values from 4 to 6 air change rates for hospital patient rooms (Melikov, 2016). Thus, a lot of
energy is used. One solution is to use less air and exhaust locally the generated pollutants
where and when is needed, aiming to improve the air quality, provide local thermal comfort
and reduce the exposure to airborne pollutants. An advanced air distribution (AAD) system
that makes this possible is the novel bed integrated ventilation method, so-called ’ventilated
mattress’ (VM). This method eﬃciently achieves thermal comfort and controls the exposure of
room occupants to contaminants in order to provide high air quality while reducing energy use.
This study was focused on evaluating the energy-saving potential of the VM when applied in
hospital rooms. Energy simulations were carried out with the IDA ICE software. Three hospital
patient room settings, namely single, double and 6-bed room, were simulated for three diﬀerent
locations: Copenhagen, Abu Dhabi and Singapore. Besides, four air-conditioning scenarios were
applied: 1) Only background mixing ventilation (MV) at the recommended air ﬂow rates by
the present standards (4 to 6 air change rates), 2) Reduced background MV combined with
the use of VM, 3) Reduced background MV combined with radiant ceiling panels, 4) Reduced
background MV combined with radiant ceiling panels and the use of VM.
In addition, initial values of several parameters such as VM heat removal, VM power consumption,
occupancy schedule, room facade orientation and upper operative temperature limit were varied
in order to investigate their inﬂuence on the percentage of energy savings.
The study showed that the VM combined with mixing ventilation at 3 ACH, for all the studied
scenarios, not only maintained the required standard thermal comfort conditions for hospital
patient rooms but also achieved energy savings compared to the reference case with only MV.
Furthermore, in hot and humid climates such as Singapore it was found that the VM in operation
with MV at only 1.8 ACH and radiant ceiling panels provided the highest percentage, i.e. 58 %
energy savings compared to the other studied scenarios.
Among all the studied parameters, increasing the operative temperature upper limit from 24 to
26 C had the greatest positive impact on the percentage of energy savings, leading to 67 % as
maximum percentage of energy savings for single room located in Copenhagen.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The main goal of ventilation systems in health care facilities is to provide high air quality and
thermal comfort to the occupants. The two main groups of occupants in these facilities are
patients and medical staﬀand they spend most of the time indoors. There is strong evidence
that the indoor environment quality can largely aﬀect their comfort and health as well as the
work performance of the staﬀ(Molhave et al., 1997; EN15251, 2007; Kehayova, 2015).
1.1
Indoor air quality
Requirements for acceptable indoor air quality have been long debated. Historically, the main
considerations have included the amount of supply outdoor air required to control moisture,
carbon dioxide, odors or tobacco smoke generated by occupants, and this has led to requirements
for minimum rates of outdoor air supply per occupant (ASHRAE, 2013).
Humans are able to perceive air quality by two senses: olfactory sense, which is located in the
nasal cavity, and general chemical sense, that can be found throughout the mucous membranes
both in the nose and in the eyes. The ﬁrst is responsible of perceiving a wide range of odors in the
air and the last one is responsible for reacting against some irritants in the air. The combination
of both reactions determines the air quality perceived by a person (Communautes Europeennes
and Bienfait, 1992).
The World Health Organization (WHO) estimated that more than 30 % of all new or renovated
buildings worldwide had indoor air quality (IAQ) problems (WHO, 2006). These problems
can be caused by indoor contamination sources such as occupants, furnishing, outdoor air,
dirty ventilation system ducts and ﬁlters, building materials, soil adjacent to the building, etc.
Because pollutant source strengths are highly variable, maintaining minimum ventilation rates
does not ensure acceptable indoor air quality in all situations (ASHRAE, 2013).
Health eﬀects due to indoor air pollutants may be short as well as long-term. Short-term
problems include symptoms such as eye irritation, allergies, coughing, sneezing, nausea, skin
irritation or headaches. Long-term health problems have a longer latency period and they can
be even chronic. Factors such as the time of exposure, concentration, presence of a preexisting
unhealthy condition and age, strongly inﬂuence the magnitude and duration of these health
eﬀects (Spengler, Samet, and McCarthy, 2001). The World Health Organization has listed the
maximum permissible concentration and corresponding exposure times for most common air
pollutants in order to oﬀer guidance in reducing the health impacts of air pollution (WHO,
2006).

Temperature and humidity extremes can aﬀect the emission rates of some pollutants as well as
the perceptions of building occupants. High humidity and high temperature makes people feel
lethargic. Low humidity induces coughing, dry throats, and dry eyes. An additional problem
with low humidity is that it accentuates the sense of smell. Besides, noise from mechanical
systems or glare from lights can cause headaches and fatigue. These are all symptoms of sick
building syndrome and they are usually blamed on poor air quality. (Hays, Gobbell, and
Ganick, 1995). Sick building syndrome is a type of building-related illness that has received
public attention since 1970. American Society of Heating, Refrigerating, and Air-Conditioning
Engineers (ASHRAE) deﬁned this term as building in which a signiﬁcant number of building
occupants report illness perceived as being building related (ASHRAE, 1987).
There are several causes of poor indoor air quality. Inadequate ventilation and the release of
contaminants from indoor and outdoor sources are the primary reasons for indoor air quality
problems.
Other factors such as temperature, humidity and microbial contamination can
aggravate the situation. ASHRAE deﬁnes a contaminant as an unwanted airborne constituent
that may reduce human acceptability of air. Contaminants can include gases, vapors, smoke
and other particulate matter. In the indoor air quality program of the WHO, the IAQ concept
includes physical, chemical and biological pollutants, and relates them to human health
(ASHRAE, 1989). The air is mixed with many kinds of contaminants coming from outdoors,
indoor surfaces and also from occupants (ASHRAE, 2013). Therefore, it is important to classify
and analyze them and their interaction with the surroundings from diﬀerent points of view such
as health, energy eﬃciency, comfort, etc.
1.2
Indoor air pollutants
There are many indoor air contaminants such as by-products of combustion such as CO, CO2
and NOx, environmental tobacco smoke, formaldehyde or other volatile organic compounds,
radon, pesticides, asbestos, heavy metals (paints, soil, dust) and bio aerosols.
Their concentrations in the air depend on the strength of the sources and the total rate of
pollutant removal (ASHRAE, 2013). Generally, they can be classiﬁed as either particles or gases,
both released from building components and from occupants. Pollutants released from building
materials include formaldehyde, asbestos, and to a lesser extent radon. Pollutants released from
humans include bio-eﬄuents and bio aerosols. Humidiﬁers, air conditioning systems and air
distribution ducts among others, also help bio aerosols to get indoors.

1.2.1
Indoor air pollutants generated by occupants
Occupants are one of the main sources of contaminants in health care facilities. Indoor air in
these premises contains a wide range of human particles and gaseous contaminants such as total
volatile organic compounds, skin scales and biological contaminants or bio aerosols (Daisey,
Angell, and Apte, 2003).
1.2.1.1
Bio-eﬄuents
Gaseous contaminants generated from the human body are called bio-eﬄuents. They can be
subdivided in gases, with lower building point than ambient temperature and vapours which
have a higher boiling point than ambient temperature. They include both, volatile organic
compounds (VOCs) and inorganic air contaminants.
VOCs are deﬁned as organic chemicals which have a high vapor pressure, resulting from a low
boiling point (less than or equal to 250 C measured at a standard atmospheric pressure of 101.3
kPa). This low boiling point causes molecules to evaporate and enter the surrounding air, which
may have an eﬀect on human health depending on their concentration (Jahren and Sui, 2013).
VOCs can contain nitrogen, oxygen, CO2, H2O and inert gases, and the rate of each component
varies considerably from person to person.
Human body also generates inorganic air contaminants such as ammonia gas, nitrogen oxides
and organic nitrogen compounds, ozone, sulfur dioxide and sulphides (ASHRAE, 2013). Usually
these contaminants are expressed in parts per million (ppm) and milligrams per cubic meter
(mg/m3).
Several factors such as race, metabolism level, health, occupational exposure and habits,
determine human bio-eﬄuents emission. Armpits, hands or feet are the parts from the body
more likely to emit bio-eﬄuents and therefore they are largely responsible for the body odor
(Wang, Long, and Zhang, 2014). Multiple studies have deeply analyzed and characterized the
VOCs. Gallagher et al. (2008) analyzed the VOCs originated from the skin and reported that
they certainly contribute to a person’s total body odor. James, Hyliands, and Johnston (2004)
provided information about the biochemical origins of the Volatile Fatty Acids (VFA) on the
axillary skin. The results conﬁrmed that two main organisms, namely cutaneous Staphylococci
and Corynebacterium (A) were able to carry out the biochemical transformations of odorless
natural secretions into volatile odorous products that constitute the body odor. Dormont,
Bessiere, and Cohuet (2013) used diﬀerent methods in order to analyze skin volatiles. One of
them consisted of sampling human skin volatiles by solvent extraction. Another method involved
absorbing skin compounds with cotton pads and then, by means of an air ﬂow process, release
volatiles onto adsorbent traps. A more recent method, SPME (solid-phase micro-extraction)
enabled trapping volatiles on adsorbent-coated ﬁbers (Dormont, Bessiere, and Cohuet, 2013).

Tsushima et al. (2016) measured and compared the bio-eﬄuents emitted by occupants, both
through the skin and via exhaled breath. Diﬀerent scenarios were analyzed. First, ﬁve subjects
inside a chamber exhaled through breathing masks which were connected at the same time

air quality. They stated that the air quality in the chamber with only dermal bio-eﬄuents was
lower than the one containing only orally exhaled bio-eﬄuents. Moreover, air quality levels were
similar for the chamber with only dermal bio-eﬄuents and the one containing both dermal and
orally exhaled eﬄuents. This study reveals that dermal pollutants have higher inﬂuence on
indoor air quality compared to orally exhaled bio-eﬄuents (Tsushima et al., 2016).
Odors that human skin emits are extremely important as diagnostic tools, design of deodorants,
etc. They can be divided in three categories: perspiration odor, exhaled breath odor and human
excreta odor.
Perspiration odor comes from sweating. Human sweat is emitted through three diﬀerent skin
glands distributed around the body: apocrine, eccrine and sebaceous. These gland secretions
and their interactions with bacteria from the skin are responsible for the production of the VOCs
responsible for perspiration odor. Eccrine glands are the most abundant and they are especially
concentrated on hands and feet. These glands account for body’s temperature regulation and
produce odorless sweat. Apocrine glands are mainly found in the axillae, pubic area and areolas.
When they ﬁrstly emanate from the skin, apocrine secretions are odorless. The large diversity of
microbial ﬂora, which multiply on the skin and chemically alter the sweat when interacting with
these secretions, contribute to the development of the typical strong mostly axillary body odor
within several hours. This is commonly called ’body odor’ even though it comes mostly from
the axillae region (Inaba and Inaba, 1992). Sebaceous glands, on the contrary, are distributed
all over the body but they are more concentrated on the upper part of the body such as back,
scalp, face and forehead.
According to Pandey and Kim (2001), exhaled breath odor is generated approximately 90 %
by the oral cavity and the remaining 10 % by the digestive and respiratory systems. The
composition of VOCs responsible for exhaled breath odor varies widely (quantitatively and
qualitatively) among people. Generally, CO2, O2, H2, N2 and inert gasses are present in this
group (Pandey and Kim, 2011).
The volatile substances responsible from human excreta odor are acids, ammonia, sulfur and
nitrogen compounds. Ammonia gas (NH3) has an oﬀensive and disagreeable odor, and it may
induce human skin problems and microbial infections when concentrated.

1.2.1.2
Particles
In comparison to gases, particles are heavier, larger and fewer in number. There are many ways
of classifying the particles. They can be divided by the state of matter, being solid (dusts, fumes
and bio aerosols), liquid (mists and fogs) and complex (smoke, environmental tobacco smoke
and fog). They can also be classiﬁed by size, biotic content or origin (e.g. human respiratory
activities) (ASHRAE, 2013).
Regarding the size, particles are commonly divided in three general groups: coarse, ﬁne and
ultra-ﬁne particles (Figure 1). Coarse particles or PM10 which may aﬀect the human health are
in the size range of 2.5 - 10 µm. Examples of such coarse particles (also known as bio aerosol
particles) are mold spore, pollen and animal dander. These particles are inhalable and can be
deposited in the thoracic region. Fine particles or PM2.5 are smaller than coarse particles with
a maximum size range of 2.5 µm and a minimum of 0.1 µm. They are the result of chemical
reactions or condensing gases and, when inhaled by a person, they can reach the lungs. Ultra
ﬁne particles or PM0.1 have a maximum diameter of 0.1 µm. Because of their diminutive size,
they can pass without any problem through the body’s natural defense mechanisms and arrive
to the circulatory system (Liu et al., 2015; ASHRAE, 2013).
Figure 1: Particle size terminology (Source: Technologies for particulate emission control, 2016)
Biological aerosols, known as bio aerosols, are airborne biological particles in the air containing
living organisms or released from living organisms. Therefore, the particulate matter is from
bio-origin. They are derived from viruses, bacteria, fungi, protozoa, algae, mites, plants, insects,
and their by-products and cell mass components. Bio aerosols contribute to about 2-34 % of
indoor pollution. They can be present in both indoor and outdoor environments (ASHRAE,
2013).
Bio aerosols released by people, usually in the form of droplets, are formed by means of speech,
coughing, sneezing, vomiting, etc., or as a result of hair breakage and shedding of epidermal

cells (a release of dead skin cells) from all over the body. After coming to equilibrium with
ambient humidity, they can remain suspended in the air for long periods and may penetrate the
human lungs. In addition, physical characteristics of the indoor environment and ventilation
systems have a huge inﬂuence on the possibility for these microorganisms to survive (Spendlove
and Fannin, 1983).
Their diﬀusion and their fast deposition on surfaces depend mostly on their droplet size. Bio
aerosols have a wide size range from 0.003 to 100 µm in diameter. Viruses are in size 0.003-0.06
µm. Normally they aggregate and can be dispersed in sputum or saliva. Individual bacteria
with size 0.4-5 µm appear individually or aggregated. Intact spores are normally 2-10 µm and
pollen grain range in size from 10 to 100 µm (ASHRAE, 2013).
Bio aerosols are the responsible for diseases such as Legionellosis, Pneumonia, Tuberculosis,
asthma, etc. Moreover, some bio aerosols may also cause other problems such as eye irritation,
headaches, sneezing or fatigue (ASHRAE, 2013).
Another type of bio aerosols generated from the human body, included in the category of coarse
particles, are skin scales. They can carry with them microorganisms that, in case of exposure,
may be responsible to some extent for contamination and infection in hospitals. Their size goes
from 10 to 25 µm. Skin scales are constantly being shed from the body by the general movement
of the skin surface and by the grazing action of clothes (Clark, 1974). Through convective
ﬂows established around the human body due to the temperature diﬀerence between the skin
and surroundings, particles up to 80 µm in equivalent diameter can be dispersed and inhaled,
causing infections (Clark and Calcina-Goﬀ, 2009).
1.3
Thermal comfort
ISO-7730 Standard (2005) deﬁnes thermal comfort as a ’condition of the mind which expresses
satisfaction with the thermal environment’. Thermal comfort is also deﬁned as the absence of
discomfort or as the state where the subject cannot decide whether he would like the temperature
raised or lowered. It is highly subjective and it widely varies from person to person.
Thermal comfort depends on the upward free convection movement existing around the human
body due to the temperature diﬀerence between the surrounding air and the surface of the body.
The skin temperature of the human body will change due to convective heat transfer caused by
the air movement around the body and thus will aﬀect thermal sensation (Melikov, Kaczmarczyk,
and Cygan, 2000). It also depends on persons’ behaviours that are initiated consciously or
unconsciously and guided by thermal and moisture sensations to reduce discomfort. Some
examples are: altering clothing, altering activity, changing posture or location, changing the

thermostat setting, opening a window, etc. (ASHRAE, 2013).
Thermal discomfort is caused in the ﬁrst place by an unsuitable combination of the six factors
determining the heat balance of the human body. Two personal factors: clothing thermal
insulation (clo) and metabolic rate, and four environmental factors: air temperature, mean
radiant temperature, air velocity and relative humidity. These factors will be discussed in the
following sections. It should be noted that personal factors have a remarkable importance when
determining the feeling of thermal comfort.
1.3.1
Relative humidity
Humidity aﬀects the rate at which the body can release moisture into the air, either by
evaporation from the skin (sweating) or through breathing. Often, relative humidity (RH) is
evaluated rather than absolute humidity, because it aﬀects the rate of evaporation since it refers
to the amount of water vapor that is contained in the air, related to the amount of water vapor
contained in a saturated air. Although the human body is generally tolerant to a wide variation
of RH (typically between 35 % and 75 %), there are serious concerns at the extremes of dryness
(low RH) and dampness (high RH). Either of these can occur at high or low temperatures.
1.3.2
Operative temperature
In order to provide an acceptable thermal conditions there are two main factors that have
to be taken into account: the indoor air temperature and the mean radiant temperature or
average temperature of surrounding surfaces. The combined inﬂuence of these two parameters is
expressed as the operative temperature. For low air velocities and small temperature diﬀerence
between them, the operative temperature can be approximated with the simple average of air
and mean radiant temperature (Babiak, Olesen, and Petras, 2009).
1.3.3
Clothing thermal insulation
Clothing thermal insulation can be deﬁned as the thermal protection against the surroundings
provided by clothing. Clothing insulation is expressed in clo units (1 clothing unit = 1 clo =
0.155 m2K/W). The thermal comfort of an occupant is aﬀected by the level of clothing insulation.
Figure 2 illustrates several clothing insulation levels: the minimum level of zero clo expresses
the clothing insulation level of naked person and a maximum value of three clo refers to the
clothing insulation level corresponding to a person wearing winter clothes (ISO-7730, 2005).
Hipler and Elsner (2016) stated that clothing insulation operates as a resistance to moisture and

1.3.5
Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) and Predicted Percentage Dissatisﬁed (PPD)
Povl Ole Fanger was an expert in the ﬁeld of thermal comfort and perception of indoor
environments. Fanger was the ﬁrst who developed an extensive model for thermal comfort using
heat balance equations and empirical studies about skin temperature to deﬁne comfort (Dallo,
2013).
In the process of developing the method, Fanger used a seven-point thermal sensation scale
alongside with several experiments involving human subjects in diﬀerent environments. Then,
he built a correlation between the subjects and their response to the variables, which inﬂuences
the condition of thermal comfort. The model proposes the use of both Predicted Mean Vote
(PMV) and Predicted Percentage Dissatisﬁed (PPD) indexes in order to determine thermal
comfort evaluation. Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) is an index that predicts the thermal sensation
for the body as a whole and refers to a 7-point thermal sensation scale that runs from Cold (-3)
to Hot (+3) (Figure 4) (ISO-7730, 2005).

1.3.6
Local thermal discomfort
According to the ISO Standard 7730 (2005), the PMV and PPD express warm and cold
discomfort for the body as a whole. However, unwanted local cooling or heating of the body can
also cause thermal discomfort. This is known as local thermal discomfort. The most common
local thermal discomfort factors are cold or warm ﬂoors, vertical air temperature diﬀerence,
radiant temperature asymmetry and draught (local cooling of the body caused by air movement).
People at sedentary activity are more sensitive to local thermal discomfort. The risk of thermal
discomfort is lower with higher levels of activity (ISO-7730, 2005).

1.3.6.1
Cold or warm ﬂoor
The ﬂoor temperature has a direct eﬀect on comfort of the feet. Too warm or too cold ﬂoors
may cause thermal discomfort because of the thermal sensation of the feet. Floor temperature
in the range from 19 C to 29 C is recommended in the occupied zone for rooms with standing
occupants wearing normal shoes (ISO-7730, 2005).
1.3.6.2
Vertical air temperature diﬀerence
Vertical air temperature diﬀerence is the thermal stratiﬁcation that results in the air temperature
at the head level being higher than at the ankle level, causing warm discomfort at the head
and cold discomfort at the feet. It may occur in rooms provided with displacement ventilation
systems (ISO-7730, 2005).
1.3.6.3
Radiant temperature asymmetry
Radiant temperature asymmetry is caused by large diﬀerences in the thermal radiation of the
surfaces surrounding a person. Radiant asymmetry in modern buildings constitutes a problem
and can create local thermal discomfort. People are more sensitive to radiant asymmetry caused
by warm ceiling or cool walls-windows (ISO-7730, 2005).
1.3.6.4
Draught and air velocity
While the air movement can be pleasant and provide comfort in warm environment, it may
also cause discomfort when the room temperature is in the comfortable range or lower. People
are most likely to feel draught on uncovered body parts such as their head, neck, shoulders,
ankles, feel and legs. This sensation also depends on the clothing, air temperature, activity and
air speed. Rapid temperature ﬂuctuations around a constant mean air velocity might result
in draught complaints. Annoying air movements in a room may be caused by air entering the
room through leaky external walls or to some extent, by the surface temperature being low or
high in relation with the air temperature.
At air temperature lower than 24 C, up to 0.15 m/s air velocity is accepted. If air velocity
is higher than 0.15 m/s, this is due to an incorrect adjustment of ventilation or to surface
temperatures in the room being too high or too low. With temperatures above 24 C, higher air
velocities are acceptable (ISO-7730, 2005).

1.4
Ventilation systems
Ventilation systems supply clean and cool or warm air to remove the polluted room air, and
thus to improve the indoor air quality and thermal comfort of occupants. These systems also
control the spread of infections by diluting the concentration of pollutants, which are found
indoors, and removing them to the outside.
Ventilation systems can be classiﬁed according to their driving forces (natural, hybrid and
mechanical ventilation), supply or exhaust (supply-only mechanical ventilation, exhaust-only
ventilation or balanced mechanical ventilation), integration with air-conditioning systems (fan
coil and induction systems, constant air volume systems, variable air volume systems, single air
duct systems, and dual air duct systems) and air distribution strategies (total ventilation and
local ventilation). Section 1.4.1 and section 1.4.2 present the mechanical ventilation principles
subdivided in total volume ventilation and advanced air-distribution ventilation.
1.4.1
Total Volume Ventilation (TVV)
Total Volume Ventilation (TVV) is the most commonly used ventilation method today. This
method provides the entire space in a building with conditioned clean air in order to maintain
uniform temperature and pollution level in the whole environment. There are two main air
distribution strategies commonly used in practice: mixing and displacement air distribution.
They diﬀer in the methods used to supply and exhaust the air.

1.4.1.1
Mixing ventilation
Mixing room air distribution or mixing ventilation (MV) is one of the traditional ventilation
strategies that supplies air in a way that the entire room air volume is fully mixed. The outdoor
air is supplied into the room through diﬀusers, grilles and nozzles at high speed, generating
a turbulent ﬂow. Thus, pollution is diluted and its concentration and temperature are kept
at acceptable level one and the same in the entire space. In order to reduce the pollution
concentration to acceptable levels, high amounts of air need to be delivered in the space.
Therefore, the operation costs are relatively high (Bivolarova et al., 2014a). Figure 7 illustrates
the mixing ventilation principle in a room.
Figure 7: Mixing ventilation principle (Source: Porges, 2014)
1.4.1.2
Displacement ventilation
Displacement ventilation (DV) is another air distribution strategy that relies on buoyancy ﬂows.
It supplies cool air directly into the space at relatively low speed through outlets that are located
close to the ﬂoor, which causes minimal induction and mixing. Buoyancy forces ensure that the
supply air is pooled up from the ﬂoor level, entrained into the thermal plumes that are formed
by heat sources such as occupants, lighting, computers, etc. and transported to the ceiling. It is
then exhausted from the space through air exhaust diﬀusers placed on the walls near the ceiling,
while removing contaminants and heat from the occupied zone. Resulted air quality is normally
better than the one achieved with mixing ventilation.

Ventilation systems in health care facilities must be carefully designed to be installed, operated

1.4.1.3
Pressurization and total ventilation rate
ASHRAE Standard 170 states pressurization requirements and minimum total air changes
per hour (ACH) for diﬀerent kinds of hospital rooms in order to have a better control of the
pollutants inside the room (ASHRAE, 2008). Air changes per hour describe the amount of air
in a room or building that is designed to be exchanged in one hour.
Airborne Infection Isolation (AII) rooms are areas which accommodate patients with airborne
diseases caused by organisms spread via airborne droplet nuclei smaller than 5 µm in diameter.

Minimum ventilation rates equivalent to 12 ACH as well as a negative pressure diﬀerence are
required in these areas. The minimum speciﬁed pressure diﬀerence between these rooms and
the adjacent spaces should have a minimum value of 2.5 Pa. The air in an AII room must be
exhausted to the outside. It may be recirculated only if the return air is ﬁltered through a high
eﬃciency particulate air (HEPA) ﬁlter.
Protective Environment (PE) rooms are specialized patient-care areas, with a required positive
pressure relative to the corridor. A minimum ventilation rate of 12 total ACH and a pressure
diﬀerence of 2.5 Pa between these areas and the adjacent spaces are also required in these
rooms. By contrast, general patient rooms are not required to maintain a diﬀerential pressure
relationship with adjacent areas and a total volume ventilation rate from 4 to 6 total ACH is
required for this kind of rooms. The minimum outdoor air change rate required is 2 ACH for all
kind of hospital rooms.
1.4.1.4
Operative temperature
According to the ASHRAE Standard 170, the operative temperature in hospitals should be
maintained within a range of 21-24 C (ASHRAE, 2008). Higher indoor air temperatures may
cause discomfort and they oﬀer more favorable growing conditions for bacteria or their mitigation
from and to the patient (Balaras, Dascalaki, and Gaglia, 2007). However, temperatures outside
this range might be also needed depending on individual circumstances during patient care. For
example, for delicate procedures, such as cardiac surgery, the indoor air temperature may be set
at 17 C; on the other hand, pediatric surgeries usually demand a higher indoor air temperature
because children are more sensitive to lower temperatures (Balaras, Dascalaki, and Gaglia,
2007).
1.4.1.5
Relative humidity
The ASHRAE Standard 170 recommends 30-60 % of relative humidity for most areas in
health-care facilities (ASHRAE, 2008). A maximum level of 60 % generally provides acceptable
comfort conditions for human occupancy and minimizes the risk of mold growth and dust mites
and the speed at which chemical interactions occur. It has also a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the control
of airborne infection. In particular, at 50 % RH, the mortality rate of certain organisms is
the highest (ASHRAE, 2013). RH levels higher than 60 %, in addition to being perceived as
uncomfortable, increase microbial growth, that leads to poor indoor air quality. Low RH levels
increase evaporation from the membranes of the nose and throat, drying the mucous membranes
in the respiratory system. Besides, they increase the risk of colds, ﬂu and other infections.
This happens especially in winter conditions, due to the heating season when the air is dryer
(ASHRAE, 2013).

1.4.1.6
Heating, ventilating and air-conditioning systems in health care facilities
Heating, ventilating and air-conditioning (HVAC) installations in health care facilities contribute
to reduce concentration of microorganisms, harmful gases and annoying odors. They also
improve thermal comfort taking control of thermal loads and compensate unfavorable external
and internal conditions (FprCEN-TR16244, 2011).
The air handler or air-handling unit (AHU), is a device used to regulate and circulate air as
part of the HVAC system. An AHU system simultaneously performs several functions in order
to provide thermal comfort and ventilation. These functions include the intake of outdoor air to
meet ventilation air requirements, thermal mixing of this air with recirculate air from occupied
zones, thermal conditioning, moisture control, ﬁltration to protect equipment and to remove
contaminants and attenuation of fan-generated noise to control ambient noise levels in occupied
spaces. AHU systems in health care facilities include more components than AHU systems
serving other types of buildings (Figure 9).

(2008) recommends the use of steam injection type humidiﬁers for health care facility applications
since steam is sterile and, therefore, it eliminates the risk of introducing viable microorganisms,
such as Legionella, into the building airstream. Only dry steam should be supplied to the steam
injector, and this may be generated centrally (using a boiler or heat exchanger) or locally at the
humidiﬁer by a separate electrical generator (ASHRAE, 2008).
Air ﬁltration should always be present in order to provide clean air to the building occupants.
The type and design of the ﬁlters determine the eﬃciency at removing particles of a given size
and the amount of energy needed to pull or push air through the ﬁlter.
Primary ﬁlters in the AHU system are cheap and disposable with relatively low dust-holding
capacity. They are generally used as preﬁlters to eliminate large particles that could cause
damage to the fan and heating and cooling coils. Secondary ﬁlters in the AHU system, with a
high eﬃciency, act as ﬁnal ﬁlters.
In health care facilities, medium eﬃciency preﬁlters and a high eﬃciency extended surface ﬁnal
ﬁlter are recommended (Rolloos, 1993).
Heating and cooling coils regulate the temperature of the air delivered to the space. During
the cooling mode, the cooling coil provide dehumidiﬁcation as water condenses from the
air-stream. Dehumidiﬁcation can only take place if the chilled ﬂuid is maintained at a cold
enough temperature (Rolloos, 1993).
After passing through the coil section where heat is either added or extracted, air moves to the
supply fan chamber and the distribution system. Fan performance is expressed as the ability to
move a given quantity of air at a given resistance or static pressure (Rolloos, 1993).
Dampers are used as controls to restrict airﬂow. Damper positions may be ﬁxed or may change
in response to signals from the control system (Rolloos, 1993).
1.4.2
Advanced air-distribution methods
As stated in section 1.4.1.3 on page 14, requirements for total volume ventilation in hospitals
are high, since there is a need to supply huge amounts of air to the space in order to dilute the
concentration of contaminants and improve perceived air quality. Consequently, a great amount
of energy is used and, in addition, this results in an increase of air velocity in the occupied zone
may create thermal discomfort due to draught, as well as high cost for ventilation itself.
Despite of the great amount of energy used to remove indoor air pollutants, existing total volume
air-distribution principles are not always eﬃcient to provide high air quality and satisfy every
occupant. There is a need for new air distribution systems that are able to remove pollutants at
17
==PAGE:36==
1. INTRODUCTION
the source before they are mixed with the rest of the room air.
Recently, several innovative local ventilation systems have been designed based on more advanced
air distribution (AAD). Several studies have shown that integrating AAD systems can help to
provide occupants with high inhaled air quality, thermal comfort and lead to energy savings
(Bivolarova et al., 2016; Melikov, 2016; Kehayova, 2015). TVV systems can be complemented
(in some cases replaced) by AAD systems. Two examples of AAD applications in practice are
discussed below.
1.4.2.1
Advanced air distribution for bed environment
The novel ventilation method, so-called Hospital Bed Integrated Ventilation and Cleaning Units
(HBIVCU) is an AAD system designed for hospital ventilation and it was ﬁrst reported in 2011
(Melikov, Bolashikov, and Georgiev, 2011).
It consists of two units that are shaped as boxes, attachable to both sides of a hospital bed
(Figure 10) that improve the protection of occupants against airborne cross infections. The
ﬁrst unit exhausts room polluted air and, after cleaning it from pathogens (via UVC light or
other cleaning techniques), it discharges this clean air horizontally over the patient to guide the
exhaled/coughed contaminated air from the patient towards the exhaust opening of the second
unit. This second unit again cleans this polluted air and discharges it through an horizontal slot
upward to the ceiling exhaust as an air curtain between the patient and the person standing
next to the bed, as illustrated in Figure 10.
This system acts as an eﬃcient means to provide better indoor air quality and it helps to
decrease the risk of airborne cross infection by employing a more eﬀective air distribution
method. However, the method might not be eﬃcient for capturing polluted air released from the
body bio-eﬄuents while the person is lying in bed (Melikov, Bolashikov, and Georgiev, 2011).
1.4.2.2
Novel bed integrated ventilation method
A novel method for advanced ventilation in hospital rooms by pollution source has been presented
and studied by Bivolarova et al. (2016). This new method, so called Ventilated Mattress
(VM) aims to improve air quality and reduce total ventilation rates by locally exhausting the
bio-eﬄuents generated from the human body directly from the source, before they have the
chance to disperse into the room. To do so, the VM is provided with suction openings that are
responsible for exhausting the bio-eﬄuents emitted from the human body (Figure 11). There is
a mesh inside the ventilated mattress, which provides support and allows the exhaust air to
move through the whole mattress. The method is especially suitable for patients that hardly
ever move from their beds, unable to refresh themselves.

was maximized with the increase of the ventilation ﬂow through the mattress and minimized
with the increase of the room temperature. This could have a potential for energy savings in

et al., 2014b).

1.5
Motivation of present research
The main goal of ventilation systems in health care facilities is to achieve a better IAQ by
reducing the concentration of pollutants within a space. Conventional mechanical ventilation
principles supply signiﬁcant amounts of air in order to achieve this. The ASHRAE standard for
ventilation of health care facilities recommends ventilation rates for general patient rooms to be
20

from 4 to 6 ACH. High ventilation rates will lead to high energy consumption and investment
costs (larger ducts, more powerful fans, more space to accommodate the HVAC unit, etc). This
will also result in high air velocity in the occupied zone, which can create draught and decrease
patient’ thermal comfort (Bivolarova et al., 2016). Besides, high background ventilation rates
under MV could cause the spread of airborne pollutants indoors if the exposed person is close
to the pollution source (Melikov, Bolashikov, and Georgiev, 2011).
The local exhaust bed ventilation, i.e. the ventilated mattress, removes bio-eﬄuents released
from the human body. Therefore, this new method has a potential to avoid the spread of human
generated body pollutants and improve the air quality in the whole space. Less air will be
needed to ventilate the space compared to the hospital requirements in the current standards.
Thus the VM has also a potential for decreasing the energy consumption by reducing the total
volume ventilation in the standards. The reduction of the energy consumption in hospital rooms
by using this method of local exhaust ventilation was investigated in the current study. The
results are presented in this report.

3.1
Simulation software

interfaces and customized models.

3.7
Radiant heating and cooling panels
Radiant heating and cooling panels are room air conditioning systems that provide heating
and cooling by radiation. They handle the sensible portion of a building load and must be
paired with an air ventilation system that provides fresh air into the room and removes latent
loads (Lipczynska, Kaczmarczyk, and Melikov, 2014). These systems cool and heat the room
by a combination of natural convection and radiation. As radiation is the main heat transfer
mechanism for the panels, these systems are known as radiant ceiling panels.
The ceiling panels have the advantage of providing heating and cooling, improving thermal
comfort thanks to a privileged transfer by radiation and limitation of air speeds. Exceeding
allowable air velocities is not possible with these systems. Thus, draft which could arise with a
usual air conditioning system, does not occurs with the use of radiant ceiling panels (Hui and
Leung, 2012). Besides, contrary to classic air conditioning systems, they cannot be the cause of
disease transmission (Miriel, Serres, and Trombe, 2002).
In this project, integrated heating and cooling panels were installed, with top insulation to avoid
heat losses from the construction above. Their exposed building surface includes a water piping
conﬁguration installed for heat exchange within the conditioned space (Figure 21). The radiant
surface is cooled or heated by cool or warm water supplied into the pipes. Insulation over the
panels is used to avoid heat losses to the construction above.
Radiant systems are becoming a popular solution as they help to remove a great part of the
sensible gains in the space by radiation. Thermal energy is exchanged between the room and
the heated or cooled surface. The maximum surface for the installation of the panels depends
35

on the criteria for radiant asymmetry or direct contact with the surface. It was assumed in this
study a 70 % of the total ceiling area to be covered with radiant panels.
In practice, the temperature diﬀerence of the ceiling panels surface and supplied air is controlled

Plant
The plant is an object that must be always present in a model, unlike the air handling unit.
The installed plant in the simulation models was a standard IDA ICE plant that consisted of a
boiler and a chiller.
The boiler converts the purchased energy (e.g. gas and electricity) to warm water with a given
temperature and pressure for circulation through water based heat exchangers in the building.
Boiler eﬃciency was by default constant and set to 90 %. The boiler had an unlimited capacity
and a hot water supply temperature set point of 35 C, that comes from a special controller
component connected to the boiler. The controller provides a graph showing the set point as a
function of the ambient temperature. The unlimited capacity was based on the assumption that
the boiler was able to meet the heating demand in all conditions.
The chiller and its circulation circuit operate in a similar way, but diﬀer in some aspects from
the boiler. The chiller used electrical power to produce chilled water at two diﬀerent constant
temperatures but with the same pressure. The colder water, at 5 C, supplied the AHU system.
The warmer temperature, at 15 C, supplied the radiant panels instead.

3.10
Internal gains
The total building load consists of heat transferred through building envelope (external load)
and heat generated by the presence of occupants, equipment and lights (internal gains). Internal
sources existing in the room during operational hours make a remarkable contribution to the
total heat gain. Each internal heat gain has its own type of properties that should be deﬁned
and a corresponding schedule for when the given heat gain occurs. As a result, the number and
speciﬁcations of the mentioned units become a determinant factor of thermal comfort. Heat
gain from occupants, lights and medical equipment were simulated in all cases.
3.10.1
Medical equipment
The heat gain from the medical equipment was considered to be 114 W per occupant, which
corresponds to the average heat gain from a blood warmer (ASHRAE, 2003). The equipment
was set to be turned on when patients were inside the room. It has to be noted, however, that
in reality the heat load generated by the hospital equipment may vary signiﬁcantly due to the
fact that patients with particular illnesses might require the use of diﬀerent medical equipment
for their treatment.
39
==PAGE:58==
3. METHODS
3.10.2
Occupants
Internal gains from the occupants are the most varying parameter due to several reasons such
as their mobility, level of activity and preference of clothing. These factors are presumable
depending on the type of work in a closed space. Depending on the simulated case, one, two
or even six occupants were contributing to the sensible and latent heat load in the room. The
software calculated the balance between the sensible and latent heat loads in the room.
Each occupant in the model was set to have, as a reference value, a metabolic rate of 0.8 MET,
which corresponds to 46 W/m2 total heat produced per occupant and per square meter and
transferred to the surroundings, because it was assumed that most of the time, occupants are
resting in bed, totally relaxed (ISO-7730, 2005).
A second value of 0.7 MET for the occupants’ metabolic rate was set afterwards in order to
simulate a the local cooling eﬀect of the VM, caused by the action of exhausting air close to
the occupants, and to investigate the possible energy savings. Therefore, the initial value of 46
W/m2 was lowered to 40.6 W/m2 corresponding to 0.7 MET. In order to calculate the total
heat produced by the occupants, it was assumed an average body surface of 1.6 m2. Therefore,
it was concluded that 73.6 W and 65 W of heat were produced by one occupant when applying
0.8 MET and 0.7 MET, respectively.
Clothing insulation was calculated as a combination of the bedding and the sleepwear used by
occupants during sleep. The clothing insulation value was set to 2 clo. This is the maximum
value the program allows to set. This value was chosen based on a previous study taking into
account that the patients had 67 % of their bodies covered with a summer quilt and they were
wearing a full-slip sleepwear. The correspondent clothing insulation value for these characteristics
and considering a conventional mattress was 2.41 clo (Lin and Deng, 2008).
3.10.3
Lights
For this study, light-emitting diode lamps were installed. In particular, 6, 8 and 20 ceiling lights
were installed for the single, double and 6-bed hospital rooms respectively, having each one 7 W,
a luminous eﬃcacy of 60 lm/W and a convective fraction of 0.3.

Air-conditioning scenarios
Four air-conditioning scenarios were simulated in order to evaluate the yearly energy savings.
They were subdivided in two groups depending on whether the VM was used or not.
A CAV mixing ventilation system was used for all scenarios. The desired room operative
temperature range of 21-24 C was maintained by adjusting in every simulation the supply air
temperature of the AHU system. However, the lowest permissible supply air temperature was
limited by thermal comfort and condensation issues to minimum value of 15 C, that corresponds
to 9 C below the desired room operative temperature.
In addition, if the minimum supply air temperature of 15 C was not enough to lower the
operative temperature to 24 C inside the room, then the air ﬂow rate of the AHU system was
increased. The four scenarios, divided in two groups, are presented below:
The ﬁrst group of scenarios focused only on the room air conditioning systems without including
the use of the VM. Hence, the schedule used for this device in the software was set as ”always
oﬀ”.

4. RESULTS
4
Results
Hospitals have the highest energy consumption per unit ﬂoor area in the buildings sector. This is
due to the high space heating, cooling and ventilation loads, high number of medical equipment
and continuous 24 hours of the facilities operation. From an energy audit campaign of Hellenic
health care buildings in the 1990s, it was reported an average annual total energy consumption
of 407 kWh/m2. More recently other data from ten hospitals in Athens also reported an average
annual energy consumption of 426 kWh/m2 (Balaras, Dascalaki, and Gaglia, 2007).
According to the Standard EN 15651 (2007), the energy need is the sum of the energy for heating
(AHU Heating) and cooling (AHU Cooling) in order to reach the desired supply temperature
added to the energy needed for heating (Room Heating) and cooling (Room Cooling) of the
conditioned space in order to maintain the indoor operative temperature within the required
range during the period of occupancy (EN15251, 2007). The energy need is measured in kWh/m2
and it is used to evaluate how much energy has to be delivered to the systems in order to create
the desired thermal indoor environment.
The energy delivered is, according to the same Standard EN 15615 (2007), the total energy that
has to be supplied to the building in order to satisfy all the users’ needs and it includes heating,
cooling, ventilation, domestic hot water, lighting, fans, pumps, etc. (EN15251, 2007).
In this study, the energy saving potential of the VM was evaluated by investigating the total
annual energy need in four diﬀerent scenarios comprising four room air-conditioning strategies
and by evaluating the percentage of energy savings reached in the scenarios including the use of
the VM (MVVM and MVRPVM) compared to the reference case (MV supplying air at 4 ACH).
The energy need is reported in this study instead of the energy delivered, in order to make
the results independent of the speciﬁc characteristics of the HVAC system. However, in reality
energy consumption of the room will also depend on the energy eﬃciency of the selected HVAC
system.
Results for the ﬁrst 45 energy simulations are presented as follows. For each climatic region, the
resulting annual energy need from applying four air-conditioning scenarios and three diﬀerent
hospital patient rooms, is depicted. It was initially assumed a constant operative temperature
range of 21-24 C and also that the patients were present in the room 24 h/day. This means that
the heat loads by occupants and equipment were continuously generated. Regarding the lights,
they followed a schedule, as indicated in Figure 24 on page 42. These initial assumptions do not
take into account the heat removal from the VM because of its local cooling eﬀect. Furthermore,
a South orientation of the facades and a constant VM power consumption of 5 W were set in all
cases.

5
Discussion
Considering the large amount of fresh air that must be drawn into a hospital continuously,
turning ventilation systems into more eﬃcient in terms of energy saving should be a priority.
However, creating comfortable conditions in spaces while using lower energy use becomes an
important challenge and it requires the development of new solutions.
This project is a theoretical work that aims to study the energy-saving potential of the VM
when combined with reduced mixing ventilation and also when combined with radiant room
air conditioning systems. First energy simulations were carried out comprising three climatic
regions, three hospital patient rooms and four room air-conditioning scenarios. Furthermore,
several parameters were varied to study their inﬂuence on the energy consumption compared to
the initial case. Assumptions and simpliﬁcations were made in order to be able to isolate the
inﬂuence of the investigated parameters from other factors.
In this section, results obtained in the energy simulations will be discussed. For this project,
only the energy-saving potential of the VM has been studied. It is important to note that
the beneﬁts that result from the use of this method might be much higher than the energy
savings achieved by its use. Important beneﬁts of the VM include the improvement of occupants’
health and comfort by removing certain amount of pollutants, as well as protection against
cross-infection, smaller and fewer HVAC modules, less space for accommodating the ducting
and HVAC units, reduce maintenance costs and increased ﬂexibility. Increased ﬂexibility means
that the recirculation mode of the VM avoid making additional changes in already existing
background ventilation room systems. This will increase the ﬂexibility of the patient’s bed
location, allowing the bed to be moved around and being always functional.
Deﬁned occupancy proﬁles have been implemented in this study, with a ﬁxed daily occupancy
schedule, automatic control for shading and a constant operative temperature range between 21
and 24 C (as initial assumption).
One of the assumptions made for simpliﬁcation of the results was keeping constant the
temperature of the supplied air by the ventilation system when occupants were not present in
the room. This assumption still allows achieving the desired operative temperature range of
21-24 C in the room. However, it is expected that decreasing the temperature of the supplied
air in case of Copenhagen when the occupants are not present in the room would lead to energy
savings and thus, this could be considered for further works as energy saving strategy.
Another assumption was keeping a constant value of 100 kWh/m2 as minimum criteria for
activating the solar shading device for all facade orientations. But in reality the frequency of
drawning the shading device may change according to the occupants preferences or circumstances
inside the hospital patient room.

Occupants were assumed to have a ﬁxed daily occupancy schedule and windows were set to be
closed during the whole day for better control of the pollutants inside the room. However, in
reality the presence of occupants in the room is unpredictable and the frequency of opening the
windows is uncertain and driven by other concerns than just the concentration of pollutants
inside the room, such as the desire of natural ventilation or the presence of noise. Besides,
personal tolerance of the occupants to high or low temperatures will also aﬀect the energy
consumption results by making increase or reduce the need for heating and cooling depending
on the circumstances.
The focus of this study was on design parameters to be adjusted to the present standards’
speciﬁcations. The occupants behaviour and comfort were not taken into account for the study.
From the results, several conclusions can be extracted. Overall, the use of the VM combined
with mixing ventilation supplying air at 3 ACH, less than the minimum air ﬂow rate required
by AHSRAE Standard 170 (2008) for normal patient rooms in health care facilities, is an
eﬀective energy saving strategy for single, double and 6-bed patient hospital room, simulated in
Copenhagen, Singapore and Abu Dhabi. Thus, this study proved that in all cases, a minimum
air ﬂow rate of 3 ACH in combination with the VM is enough for maintaining the required
thermal comfort conditions while providing energy savings.
This ﬁnding suggests that the minimum air ﬂow rates required by the present standards for
normal patient rooms in health care facilities might be too high.
The highest percentage of energy savings was obtained for a single room simulated in Singapore
in MVRPVM scenario. The energy consumption was reduced by 58 % (Table 14) compared to
the reference case MV scenario supplying air at 4 ACH, the minimum required air ﬂow rate
by ASHRAE Standard 170 (2008). In contrast, the simulation of a 6-bed hospital room in
Copenhagen in MVRPVM scenario not only did not lead to energy savings but it also increased
the energy consumption by 8 % compared to the reference case, as indicated in Table 14.
Nowadays, HVAC systems in operation are mostly all-air systems. They use air not only for
ventilation but also as a heat transfer medium in order to cool or heat the room and reach
the desired indoor environment. However these systems can create discomfort caused by draft
and air temperature diﬀerences between the head and feet of the occupants. Besides, due to
limitation of the supply air temperatures or air ﬂow rates, sometimes it is not possible to remove
all the heat loads generated in the space. In such cases, additional cooling systems are needed.
Water-based radiant cooling systems are gaining popularity as an energy eﬃcient approach for
conditioning buildings. The interest for these systems have increased in recent years since there
is strong evidence of their eﬃciency in comparison to all-air distribution systems (Feng, Bauman,
and Schiavon, 2014).
70

They are sized to handle a great part of the sensible heat loads and they become a very eﬀective
strategy in order to reduce the energy consumption. This is explained since, as part of sensible
heat loads are removed by radiant ceiling panels, the volume of the supplied air by the ventilation
system can be reduced and the desired operative temperature inside the room will be still reached.
However, these systems need to be combined with ventilation systems to maintain adequate
indoor air quality levels.
Based on the results, it can be concluded that the inclusion of radiant ceiling panels for partially
removing the heat loads generated in the room combined with reduced mixing ventilation rate
is a very eﬀective strategy in order to reduce the energy consumption in hot climates such as
Singapore and Abu Dhabi.
However, the results showed that radiant ceiling panels combined with reduced background
mixing ventilation brings to more energy consumption in cold climates like Copenhagen and
therefore, it is not a good strategy for saving energy.
Three hospital patient room models and four scenarios implying four diﬀerent air-conditioning
strategies were simulated in this study for Copenhagen, Singapore and Abu Dhabi. In each
scenario, a combination of air-conditioning systems was used to adapt the room to the desired
comfort conditions. Regarding the hospital patient room models, it can be observed that the
percentage of energy savings in MVRPM scenario becomes lower when increasing the number
of occupants. This can be explained because the total ﬂoor area in a double room was only
1.3 times bigger than the total ﬂoor area in a single room. The same occurs with the 6-bed
room that was only 3.3 times bigger than the single room. For that reason, more Room Cooling
demand per square meter will be required by the radiant ceiling panels in order to remove all
the heat loads generated in the room.
In contrast, increasing the number of occupants did not have almost any eﬀect on MVVM
scenario and approximately the same percentage of energy savings was reached for hospital
rooms with one, two and six occupants.

The justiﬁcation for the high amounts of energy consumed by Singapore would be that in hot
and humid climates like Singapore, extreme conditions of the outdoor air (high temperature
and relative humidity) need to be adapted to the desired indoor environment, which involves
processes such as cooling and dehumidiﬁcation (Licina and Sekhar, 2012).
Singapore has an average of 27.5 C outdoor temperature, almost steady throughout the year, and
an average RH of almost 84 % (section 3.2 on page 25). Prevailing tropical weather conditions
cause more than the 50 % of the total load to be latent (Licina and Sekhar, 2012). Therefore,
a signiﬁcant amount of AHU Cooling demand is needed for dehumidifying air to the required
level. This is done to compensate latent loads and to avoid condensation on the piping or heat
transfer surfaces of the ventilation system. To do so, cooling coils in the AHU system need
to over-cool the incoming air below its dew point in order to cause water vapor to condense
out of the air and achieve a suﬃciently low sensible heat factor. Once this is achieved, as the
temperature is far lower than the required operative temperature inside the room, this forces
the use of the heating coil to reheat the air and reach the required comfort conditions.
In conclusion, for hot and humid climates as Singapore, a signiﬁcant amount of mechanical
energy is needed. This energy will be used for over-cooling the air to a lower temperature and
for re-heating it afterwards in order to reduce the outdoor relative humidity from over 84 % to
50 % (Katili, Boukhanouf, and Wilson, 2015).

In contrast, Abu Dhabi, with an average outdoor relative humidity of 60.6 % (closer to the 50 %
RH recommended in hospital patient rooms) needs less energy in order to condition the outdoor
air to the desired indoor environment. In particular, among the four scenarios Abu Dhabi spent
on average 40 % less energy than Singapore.
In general, a huge percentage of energy savings is achieved when including radiant ceiling panels
in the simulations for Singapore and Abu Dhabi. Radiant ceiling panels account for most of
the sensible loads. Thus, the energy consumed in air dehumidiﬁcation and reheating by the
ventilation system is thus drastically reduced when including radiant ceiling panels.
On the other hand, Copenhagen, with an average outdoor temperature of 8.3 C and a relative
humidity of 77.4 % (section 3.2 on page 25) needs less energy in order to adapt the outdoor
air to the desired comfort conditions. Even if the average RH is relatively high, it goes down
when heating the outdoor air to reach the desired 24 C inside the room. Thus, in this case the
cold outdoor air used for ventilating is eﬀective for saving energy compared to the other two
climatic regions since it is not necessary to over-cool the air to and re-heat it again to reach
the desired conditions of temperature and relative humidity. Around 90 % less energy is used
in Copenhagen compared to Singapore, and 85 % less energy compared to Abu Dhabi, as an
average for the four scenarios applied.
In this study, heat removal of the VM was tested in some energy simulations by reducing the
metabolic rate from occupants from 0.8 to 0.7 MET. This local body cooling of the VM due
to air movement through the mattress was already tested in a recent study (Bivolarova et al.,
2014a). It involved a thermal manikin lying on the VM. The results indicated that the VM
provided cooling to the body parts in contact with the surface of the mattress at 23, 26 and
29 C room air temperature. This eﬀect could be beneﬁcial for people exposed to elevated air
temperatures since it would increase the thermal comfort.
The results showed that decreasing the occupants’ metabolic rate from 0.8 MET to 0.7 MET
did not cause a real impact in terms of energy consumption results because of the small rate
of change compared to the initial case. However, it would be interesting for further works to
investigate to what extent the local cooling eﬀect of the VM aﬀects the occupants’ thermal
comfort and if this eﬀect could create local thermal discomfort. Besides, three VM power
consumptions of the VM were also simulated to investigate the impact of this parameter on the
resulting energy consumption. The results indicated that increasing the VM power consumption
is not a good strategy for saving energy in cold climates like Copenhagen and it does not have a
real impact on the other two climatic regions Singapore and Abu Dhabi because of their high
energy consumption compared to Copenhagen.

For hot climates like Abu Dhabi and Singapore, it is necessary a dehumidiﬁcation process
to adapt the relative humidity to the required values. As commented above, air needs to be
over-cooled and afterwards reheated. Thus, a reduction of internal gains will make the ventilation
system reheat even more the air in order to adapt it to the desired internal temperature range.
Therefore, this leads to the conclusion that the reduction of the internal gains caused by this new
occupancy schedule proﬁle, is only beneﬁcial in terms of saving energy in MVRP and MVRPVM
scenario where radiant ceiling panels are used for removing the sensible heat loads generated in
the room. This is due to the fact that less Room Cooling demand will be needed because of the
lower amount of internal gains inside the room.
Building orientation is a factor that strongly aﬀects energy consumption of a building. Selecting
the most optimal building orientation is one of the critical energy eﬃcient design decisions.
In Singapore, the sun is almost directly overhead throughout the year since Singapore is located
only 1o north of the equator. East and West orientations receive the greatest amount of solar
exposure and therefore have the highest potential for solar heat gains. The Sun Path diagram
for Singapore indicates that both North and South orientations also receive solar exposure for a

portion of the year.
To avoid direct entrance of sunlight into the facades, buildings are usually designed to have
minimal windows at the eastern and western facades. In contrast, northern and southern facades
are usually equipped with windows to allow maximum penetration of natural light into the
building. This natural light is deﬁned as diﬀused light which a lesser thermal impact compared
to direct sunlight. This strategy allows to increase thermal comfort in this climate (Building and
authority, 2010). Therefore, the optimised orientation for Singapore is the one that minimises
solar heat gains with minimal direct East-West facing facade. As it could be expected, identical
results for energy consumption are found for a single room with North and South oriented facade
in Singapore because of its low latitude.
In Abu Dhabi, latitude and high insulation levels lead to the highest intensity of solar radiation
being on the West and East facing walls. This promotes a strong preference for the North-South
orientation of main facades and glazing. The South, being the most solar exposed orientation in
the northern hemisphere, requires solar access and glare to be carefully controlled.
South and North orientation were investigated in this study and a constant value of 100
kWh/m2 was kept as minimum criteria for activating the solar shading device in all cases. In
case of Copenhagen, when selecting North oriented room facades, the solar shading device
did not have any eﬀect on the energy results since facades are were receiving direct sun light
anymore (CLair, 2009).
Results for a single room located in Copenhagen and Abu Dhabi follow the same pattern. North
orientation decrease the solar radiation that enters the room compared to South orientation.
This means that the total amount of internal heat gains decreases and this has diﬀerent
consequences depending on the scenario applied. For MV and MVVM scenario, a reduction of
the internal heat gains leads to an increase of AHU Heating in order to maintain the same
operative temperature range in the room. In contrast, in MVRP and MVRPVM scenarios,
less Room Cooling demand will be necessary to remove the reduced amount of internal heat gains.
Therefore, it could be concluded that North oriented room facades are positive in terms of saving
energy in scenarios where radiant ceiling panels are combined with mixing ventilation system.
Finally, the impact of changing the operative temperature upper limit was studied by extending
the temperature range from 24 to 26 C. It is expected that the local cooling eﬀect of the VM
will help to maintain the same level of thermal comfort. However, this local cooling eﬀect of
the VM was not considered for the energy simulations when studying the impact of this last
parameter because it was already proved that decreasing the occupants’ metabolic rate from 0.8
to 0.7 MET does not have a real impact on the energy results.

5. DISCUSSION
From the results it can be seen that the obtained energy savings in MVRPVM scenario are
greater than the obtained energy savings when considering the initial assumption of 21-24 C
as operative temperature range. Thus, it could be concluded that increasing the operative
temperature range from 24 to 26 C has the greatest positive impact in terms of energy savings
in MVRPVM scenario, compared to the percentage of energy savings obtained when varying
other parameters in this study.
In particular, the results indicated that increasing the operative temperature range from 24 to
26 C implies a signiﬁcant decrease of energy need in MVRP and MVRPVM scenarios, due to
decrease of Room Cooling demand since there is no need to cool the room until 24 C anymore.
On the other hand, increasing the operative temperature range does not aﬀect the energy need
in MV and MVVM scenarios since the AHU Cooling demand for Singapore and Abu Dhabi
and the AHU Heating demand for Copenhagen stay both constant. These ﬁndings agree with
conclusions in previous studies focused on cold climates (Schiavon and Melikov, 2009) and hot
and humid climates (Schiavon, Melikov, and Sekhar, 2010; Sekhar et al., 2005).

6. CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
The performance of a VM used as a bed-integrated local exhaust ventilation system was studied.
Energy simulations were carried out in order to test the energy-saving potential of this device
when combined with reduced background ventilation and also with radiant ceiling panels. The
main conclusions of this study are as follows.
• The use of the VM combined with reduced background mixing ventilation at 3 ACH is an
eﬀective energy-saving strategy in a single, double and 6-bed room for all the climatic regions
and scenarios investigated in this study.
• The highest percentage of energy savings considering initial assumptions was obtained for a
single room simulated in Singapore when combining the use of the VM with reduced mixing
ventilation and radiant ceiling panels in MVRPVM scenario. The energy consumption was
reduced by 58 % compared to the reference case: MV scenario with only mixing ventilation
supplying air at 4 ACH. In contrast, for a 6-bed room simulated in Copenhagen, not only no
energy savings were reached in MVRPVM scenario but energy consumption increased by 8 %
compared to this reference case.
• Depending on climate, room type and system conﬁguration, the combination of radiant ceiling
panels and MV systems can bring to a higher or lower energy consumption compared to only
MV. In hot climates such as Singapore and Abu Dhabi the use of the VM in combination
with radiant ceiling panels and reduced MV was a very eﬀective strategy in order to reduce
the energy consumption of the room. In cold climates like Copenhagen it was proved that the
use of the VM combined with only reduced MV resulted in more energy savings than using
the VM in combination with reduced MV and radiant ceiling panels.
• Increasing the number of occupants decreases the energy savings in MVRPVM scenario, since
more Room Cooling demand per square meter is required to remove the heat loads generated
inside the room. Therefore, the use of the VM in combination with reduced MV and radiant
ceiling panels in hospital patient rooms with 6 or more patients is not an eﬃcient strategy for
energy saving.
• For Copenhagen climate, an equal or higher percentage of energy savings was achieved in
MVVM scenario compared to MVRPVM scenario for a single, double and 6-bed room. It can
be concluded that in cold climates like Copenhagen reducing the air ﬂow rate does not always
lead to energy savings because of the free cooling eﬀect of the outdoor air.
On the other hand, reducing the air ﬂow rate in hot climates like Singapore and Abu Dhabi
is an eﬀective strategy for saving energy
79

6. CONCLUSION
• Local cooling eﬀect of the human body by the VM, simulated in this study as the reduction
of the occupants’ metabolic rate from 0.8 to 0.7 MET, does not have a real impact on the
energy consumption results.
• Increasing the VM power consumption in Copenhagen leads to more energy consumption and
therefore it is not an eﬀective way of saving energy. Increasing the VM power consumption in
Singapore and Abu Dhabi does not have an eﬀect on the energy consumption results.
• The application of the 20 h/day occupancy schedule proﬁle implies an increase of the energy
consumption in MVVM scenario, compared to the energy consumption when the initial
occupancy schedule proﬁle of 24 h/day is applied. It can be concluded that the reduction of
the internal heat gains caused by the 20 h/day occupancy schedule proﬁle is only beneﬁcial in
scenarios where radiant ceiling panels are combined with mixing ventilation system.
• North orientation causes a reduction of solar heat gains entering the room in Abu Dhabi and
Copenhagen, compared to South orientation. As a consequence, more energy is consumed in
MVVM scenario. In contrast, this results in more energy savings in MVRPVM scenario. In
conclusion, North oriented room facades are positive in terms of saving energy in scenarios
where radiant ceiling panels are combined with MV systems.
Regarding Singapore climate, changing from South to North orientation has no impact on the
energy consumption since approximately the same amount of solar heat gain enters the room
for both orientations.
• Increasing the operative temperature upper limit from 24 to 26 C has the greatest positive
impact on the energy consumption since it results in the highest percentage of energy savings:
67 %, 65 % and 64 % for Copenhagen, Abu Dhabi and Singapore, respectively, for a single
room in MVRPVM scenario. In contrast, extending the operative temperature range in
MVVM scenario does not have any impact on the energy consumption results.
