Abstract
Steel monopile foundations applied for support of offshore wind turbines are subject to
significant dynamic loading during the installation and service phase. The dynamic loading
leads to cyclic stresses in the foundation which cause crack propagation within the material,
where the cracks propagates until the structure fails. The current conventional design proce-
dures ensure that the monopile foundations has sufficient fatigue capacity to withstand the
crack propagation with agreed design life involve the use of the S-N curve approach. The S-N
curve approach provides the option to standardize the fatigue design and to determine the
design driving failure mode, provided that the structural detail of consideration is covered
by this approach.
The scope of the following thesis entails fatigue analysis of offshore wind monopile foun-
dations with welded attachments with a focus on current design and optimization opportu-
nities challenged by the uncertainty of the design driving failure mode. Industry guidelines
state that cracking through the weld toe must be the driving fatigue failure mode. As the
information about the failure mode estimations is very limited, a fracture mechanics based
Engineering Critical Assessment will be carried out. The analysis is based on cruciform
joints with load-carrying attachments. The failure modes are estimated with the use of the
Generalized Stress Parameter approach, that utilizes Crack Propagation integrals to predict
the driving failure mode based on the weld geometry alone. The approach will be used with
geometry expressions presented in the standards BS7910, FKM, and DNV as well as a study
conducted by S. J. Maddox. An FE analysis will validate the methods along with a range
of actual experimental test results. The last scope of this thesis is to investigate the driving
failure mode for a T-joint in a monopile foundation with non-load carrying attachment plates.
The results of the ECA was 8 different combinations of Crack propagation integrals that
can be chosen based on the need for either accuracy, conservatism towards weld root failure
and conservatism towards weld toe failure. The experimental test results and the FE anal-
ysis showed that the combinations are provide a useful method for the fatigue failure mode
prediction of load-carrying cruciform joints based on the geometry alone. The generalized
stress parameters can be used to see the difference in fatigue strength between the weld root
and weld toe, which allows the designer to optimize the joint without excessive material.
The T-joint analysis showed that the weld toe was the driving failure mode, and that no
weld root penetration is needed. Furthermore, the results showed that weld root grinding
cause elevated SIFs when the crack depth reaches a=0.5mm.

Resumé
Stål monopæl fundamenter, der bærer vindturbiner, udsættes for dynamisk- og udmattelses-
belastning under installations- og servicefaserne af konstruktionens levetid. Den dynamiske
last giver cykliske spændeinger i monopælen, hvilket giver anledning til revnedannelse og ud-
vikling af denne revne indtil konstruktionen kollapser. De nuværende konventionelle design
procedurer skal sørge for, at monopælene har tilstrækkelig kapacitet i forhold til udmat-
telse ved de respektive udmattelseslaster. Levetiden bestemmes ved brug af S-N kurver.
S-N kurver standardiserer udmattelses analysen, og den dominerende failure mode kan
bestemmes, hvis den givne samling eksisterer i databasen.
Følgende kandidatafhandling omfatter udmattelsesanalyse af monopæl fundamenter med
påsvejste plader, med fokus på den aktuelle viden om design og optimeringsmuligheder med
fokus på usikkerheden omkring den dominerende failure mode.
Branchens retningslinjer
beskriver, at revner gennem svejsetåen skal være den dominerende failure mode. Da infor-
mation omkring failure modes er meget begrænset, vil en Engineering Critical Assessment
baseret på brudmekanisk teori blive udført. Analysen er baseret på Cruciform samlinger
med lastbærende påsvejste plader. Failure modes vil blive undersøgt ved brug af Gener-
alized Stress Parameter, der gør brug af Crack propagation integrals til at forudsige hvad
den dominerende failure mode er, baseret på svejsegeometrien alene. Fremgangsmåden vil
blive brugt med geometri-udtryk præsenteret i standarderne BS7910, FKM og DNV samt en
udgivelse af S. J. Maddox. En FE-analyse vil validere metoderne sammen med en række af
faktiske eksperimentelle test-resultater. Det sidste formål med dette speciale er at undersøge
den dominerende failure mode for en T-samling i et monopælfundament med ikke-bærende
påsvejste plader.
Resultaterne af Engineering Critical Assessment var 8 forskellige kombinationer af Crack
propagation integrals, der kan vælges ud fra behovet for enten nøjagtighed, konservatisme
over for svejserods failure og konservatisme over for svejsetå failure.
De eksperimentelle
testresultater og FE analysen viste, at kombinationerne er en brugbar metode til at bestemme
udmattelses failure modes i cruciform joints, udelukkende baseret på geometrien af samlinen.
Generalized stress parameter kan bruges til at analysere forskellen i udmattelses styrke for
svejse roden og svejsetåen, hvilket gør det muligt for designeren at skabe en samling, hvor
der ikke bliver brugt for meget svejsemateriale. T-samling analysen viste, at svejsetåen var
den dominerende failure mode, og at der ikke er behov for gennemtrængning af svejserod.
Desuden viste resultaterne, at weld toe grinding øger spændings intensitets faktorerne, når
revnedybden når a=0.5mm.

The increased demand for the development and construction of offshore wind farms drives
the offshore wind industry to investigate the limits and optimization possibilities of current
fatigue design procedures for primary load-carrying foundation members. In this chapter, a
brief overview of the historical development of the offshore wind market and the currently
most widely applied offshore wind foundation solution is provided.
This is followed by an introduction of the current offshore wind industry design methods
and identified challenges in relation to fatigue design of load-carrying members at the posi-
tion of welded attachments, which form the objectives that this thesis addresses.
The project is initiated in cooperation with the offshore wind engineering consultancy com-
pany Wood Thilsted (WT).
1.1
Background and motivation
The increasing demand of green energy production has caused rapid development of the size
of the wind turbines to produce more electricity. Furthermore, the offshore wind sites in
general continue to progress towards deeper waters, which requires that the next generation
of offshore wind turbine foundations shall be able to accommodate larger forces at an in-
creased lever arm in order to transfer the forces in the seabed.
The increased capacity requirements of the offshore wind turbine foundations result in con-
siderations towards if the most commonly applied bottom fixed foundation solutions can
continue to develop in size and at what point foundation solutions such as floating founda-
tions are required or are more cost-efficient.
According to the Global Wind Energy Council’s (GWEC) report from 2021, the develop-
ment of total wind turbine installations has increased from 24 GW to 743 GW between 2001
and 2020 [2]. The contribution of offshore wind turbines has increased significantly from less
than 1 GW to 35 GW as seen on Figure 1.1[2]. According to the same report, there is an
anticipated annual growth rate of 4%, resulting in an average of 94 GW of new installations
per year until 2025. In total, more than 70 GW of these installations are expected to be
offshore.

The monopile foundation is exposed to aerodynamic and hydrodynamic loads which require
that the main load carrying members transfer the resulting bending moments and shear
forces to the supporting soil at the embedded section[6]. The additional loads are axial loads
from the self-weight of the wind turbine and the foundation, and torsion induced by the

The main load carrying members of the monopile is assembled from several steel plates,

obtain the required length, which ensures that the required monopile diameter is obtained
during the subsequent cold rolling process. The free ends of the rolled plate are then welded
together to form a can section. As a result of the size of the monopile foundation, each
foundation is composed of a large number of individual can sections. The individual can
sections are welded together in a repetitive process in order to obtain the required total
length of the monopile.

1.1.2
Monopile fatigue design at welded attachments
Monopile foundations will during the in-service phase be subjected to significant cyclic fa-
tigue loads from the wind turbine generator (WTG) in combination with site-specific envi-
ronmental loads that in general cover wind, wave, and current as illustrated in Figure 1.7.
Figure 1.7: Depiction of bending forces in monopile structures and their origin.
The cyclic loads result in the generation of fluctuating stresses in the main load-carrying
steel members of the monopile foundation and lead to the development of fatigue cracks. As
a result, the monopile foundations are designed to have sufficient fatigue capacity to ensure
that the structural integrity is maintained throughout the project-agreed design life of the
foundations.
The monopile foundations have several areas that are sensitive to crack initiation and propa-
gation which leads to this structural failure mode, which can be decisive for the determination
of the required foundation dimensions.
One region of the monopile foundations that is sensitive to fatigue are areas with welded
attachments. The foundations are sensitive to fatigue in these regions because the local
geometrical discontinuity of the welds in combination with the additional stiffness from the

during the lifetime.
Presently, there are three conventional fatigue analysis design procedures[9] that in gen-
eral are applied in the offshore wind industry, and all are dependent on the application of
design code and guideline S-N curves[8]. The guideline S-N curves are based on experimental
joint detail specific fatigue tests and relate the nominal stress range in the detail (S) with

the number of cycles to failure (N).
The three conventional procedures are defined as:
1. Nominal stress approach
2. Hot spot stress approach
3. Notch stress approach
The nominal stress approach relies on the availability of an experimental S-N curve in the
design standards for the specific welded attachments required for the foundation. The avail-
ability of the exact structural detail and cyclic load direction means that all potential fatigue
crack failure modes are considered by this approach.
The Hot spot approach can be applied if an attachment-specific S-N curve is not avail-
able in the design standards. This approach relies on the application of an experimental S-N
curve for a full penetration butt weld (see circumferential weld in Figure 1.7). The local
stress raising effects induced by the stiffness of the attachment is calculated by the designer
and added to the nominal cyclic stresses in the monopile can, where the attachment is posi-
tioned. This approach is limited to checking the fatigue capacity for the weld toe positioned
at the external surface, and as a result, is limited to checking the fatigue capacity for weld
toe fatigue crack growth. Hence, this procedure assumes that fatigue crack growth from the
weld toe and into the base material will be the design driving failure mode.
The Notch stress approach is the most recent addition to the conventional procedures and
requires increased calculation complexity. The notch stress approach assesses the fatigue
capacity assuming the design driving failure mode is fatigue crack growth from the weld toe
and weld root. The present available procedure does not capture stress components parallel
to the weld and is sensitive to as-built variations in the weld geometry.
The selection of a conventional fatigue design procedure for the foundations at the posi-
tion of welded attachments, therefore, depends on the availability of an S-N curve for the
specific attachment detail. Otherwise, it must be ensured that the design driving fatigue
crack failure mode is known to the foundation designer.
The design guidelines furthermore require that the design should be performed such that
fatigue cracking from the weld root is less likely than from the weld toe region[8]. This is
required because a fatigue crack at the weld toe can be identified at inspections during the
operational period of the foundations, whereas fatigue cracking from the weld root is less
likely to be captured before it has propagated through the weld or the section under the weld.
The monopile fatigue design at welded attachments is, therefore, to be performed such that
it is ensured that fatigue crack growth from the weld toe is the design driving failure mode.
The dependency on detail-specific experimental S-N curves for the conventional design pro-
cedures provides the opportunity that this easily can be ensured, provided a suitable S-N

1.1.3
Motivation of work
The offshore wind industry investigates areas where the weight of the monopile foundations
can be optimized. One identified area that may locally drive the required foundation dimen-
sions is the fatigue design at areas with welded attachments.
The DNV design guideline[8] requires that the joint geometry is to be designed such that the
critical failure mode is weld toe cracking. Conventional design procedures can be applied to
ensure this if experimental S-N curve results are available for the attachment detail in con-
sideration and otherwise an uncertainty is in general introduced towards the design driving
failure mode.
In the industry, an opportunity furthermore exists for optimizing the fatigue design at
welded attachments by application of weld toe grinding to increase the weld toe fatigue
capacity. However, the application of weld toe grinding exceeds the available S-N curves and
introduces an uncertainty towards the improved fatigue capacity. The uncertainty is about
whether the increase in capacity of the weld toe will result in crack propagation from the
weld toe remaining the critical failure mode, or if the crack propagation from the weld root
becomes design driving.
Presently, the design norm and standards do not directly treat this uncertainty. The DNV
design guideline[8] provides a procedure for the selection of weld geometry that ensures
that the weld toe crack growth is the critical failure mode, as illustrated in Figure 1.9. The
results present the probability of root failure equal to toe failure as a function of joint dimen-
sions but are restricted to a non-grinded transverse load carrying partial penetration welded
(cruciform) joint which is the only joint type where this additional guidance is provided in
DNV.

Theory and conventional procedures
This section covers the theory and methods that are often utilized when the fatigue design
of welded joints are carried out.
2.1
Welds in offshore structures
When having to connect metallic components together in order to make them act as one
structure, welding is an excellent method. However, it does pose challenges that must be
considered, such as a change in the microstructure of the material, the risk of weld flaws,
and amplified stress concentrations near notches. These effects are of great importance when
designing the joint, especially when fatigue is considered.
2.1.1
Change in micro-structure
The welding process involves exposing the parent material to extremely high temperatures
that exceed the melting point. The large magnitude and rapid change of the temperature,
results in changes the microstructure of the parent material. The region in close proximity
to the weld is known as the heat affected zone (HAZ). The size of the HAZ depends on
material properties as well as the heat input itself and the time of exposure. This zone can
be split into several sub-zones, which are characterised by the distance from the weld. In
Figure 2.1, the HAZ along with its sub-zones are presented.
Figure 2.1: The heat affected zone when welding.
The weld zone is the liquid region where the welding process occurs.
Adjacent to this
region is the fusion boundary or solid-liquid boundary, where the liquid and solid metal
interface. This boundary is also where the HAZ begins, as the HAZ does not include the

weld zone. Adjacent to the boundary layer lies the grain growth zone, which is the area
that experiences the most elevated temperatures aside from the weld zone. This results in
coarser grains within this zone, which consequently increases the hardness and lowers the
fracture toughness of the material, resulting in a more brittle behavior. The rest of the zones
have not experienced the same high temperatures and are therefore less affected in terms of
structural behavior [10].
2.1.2
Residual stresses
Residual stresses are defined as built-in stresses that exist in a component after a given
procedure, despite the component is not subjected to external loading [11]. The residual
stresses can be both tensile and compressive, which means the stresses can be favorable and
unfavorable for different cases. Compressive residual stresses are favorable, as these will
cancel out a certain amount of tensile stresses acting on the weld. Compressive residual
stresses can be obtained with weld improvements such as hammerpeening, which force the
weld to be in compression, just as the process where glass is tempered.
For welded structures, the residual stresses are a product of the fusion of the material,
as welding produces very high temperatures[12]. The welding process causes variations in
the thermal expansion of the material, which means that stresses inside the component arise.
Following the welding process the heated material cools down, and due to the variations
in heat expansion, the resulting residual stresses vary along the section. The melted ma-
terial can not expand due to the adjacent parent material, and this expansion restriction
introduces compression stresses in that area. The opposite process occurs when the melted
material cools, as the parent material will restrict the retraction. This causes residual tensile
stresses to arise. This concept is sketched in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Concept sketch of how the residual stresses develop during the welding process.
The residual stresses in and near the weld can in some cases reach the yield strength of the
material[13]. Thus, the component can be subjected to constant tensile stress, which will

thereby reduce the lifetime of the component.
2.1.3
Flaws
Both manual and automatic welding methods have risks of flaws and imperfections in the
weld. These flaws vary in terms of size, location, and type as well as significance to the
structural integrity. Depending on the type of flaw or defect, there are measures to remediate
these. There are many types of welding flaws, which include lack of weld root fusion, lack
of fusion, weld cracks, porosity, undercutting, and slag inclusion. These types of flaws are
illustrated in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Types of flaws.
Lack of root fusion covers the cases where the weld beads fail to penetrate the desired
areas. This type of flaw can be both an external and internal defect and is often present due
to an insufficient heat input[14].
Lack of fusion is primarily an internal defect, where there is no fusion between the parent
metal and the weld metal. This flaw also often occurs due to insufficient heat input[14].
Weld cracks covers flaw-types such as weld toe cracks, longitudinal cracks, centerline cracks
an more that occur because of the welding process. Often the cracks develop due to the
shrinkage of the weld metal as it cools while being restrained by the surrounding cold metal.
Careful planning of the welding process can reduce the risks of weld cracks.
Porosity flaws appear as clusters of gas pores in the weld beads after solidification. The
Page 14 of 85
==PAGE:37==

usual reason for porosity flaws is atmospheric contamination, where the gases in the sur-
rounding air react with the weld metal.
Undercutting appears as groves in the parent material at the toe of the weld. This type
of flaw often occurs because the weld metal near the edges of the weld is drawn towards
the middle due to surface tension. Undercutting may also occur when welding is done too
quickly, causing the parent material to melt without being replenished with filler material.
Slag inclusion is the imperfection where slag particles are left inside the weld. This can
happen if the slag on a weld bead is not properly removed before the next weld bead is
placed. The slag will not melt under the next weld bead, and will therefore be trapped
inside the weld as a defect.
While welding defects are flaws that should be avoided at all times, the nature and in-
fluence of defects vary for different cases. Defects can initiate cracks and accelerate crack
propagation, which will reduce the fatigue strength and thereby the lifetime of the compo-
nent. However, defects do not necessarily have consequences for the strength of the weld
[15][16]. Defects such as slag intrusions and porosity are classified as volumetric flaws and
might not be critical due to the lack of sharp edges. Defects such as lack of fusion and weld
toe undercuts are classified as planar flaws and they often have sharp edges i.e. being the
crack initiators[17][18].

When a material undergoes alterations, such as welding or cutting, there is inevitably a
local magnification of stresses in the vicinity. This stress magnification leads to an increase
in stress at the weld toe, which under sufficient stress levels, can cause local failures due to
crack initiation, as illustrated in Figure 2.4, where it is compared to a regular plate without
a weld.

In a regular plate without welding, the stress will be constant through the thickness and
equivalent to the nominal stress. However, the presence of a weld also alters the stiffness
of the plate, elevating the local stress concentration at these points. The maximum stress
concentration is located at the weld notch and stresses subsequently converge to nominal
levels further away from the weld. Consequently, stress concentration factors arising from
welds are crucial considerations in the design process of joints[19].
2.2
Fatigue
Fatigue in general is the propagation of a crack through the material. Fatigue in metals is
a failure mechanism, where the structural capacity of a detail is gradually decreasing due
to cyclic loading [20]. Fatigue failure can occur at relatively low stresses, below the yield
strength of the material, when the number of load cycles is large enough as illustrated in
Figure 2.5. Each load cycle damages the structure further until the capacity is exceeded by
the stresses.

2.2.1
Initiation
Crack initiation is the first phase of the fatigue process, during which a microscopic crack
forms due to repeated local plastic deformation[21].
This phase cannot be described by
conventional crack laws and equations, and the crack may propagate at a 45◦angle along
crystallographic slip planes. In this phase, the crack is not yet visible to the naked eye, but
it gradually increases in size until it reaches a critical length and becomes a macroscopic
crack. For welded joints, the initiation phase typically accounts for 10 - 30% of the total
life-time. Therefore, understanding the factors that contribute to crack initiation is crucial
for developing effective inspection and maintenance strategies to prevent structural failure.
2.2.2
Propagation
Crack propagation is the second phase of the fatigue process, where the microscopic crack
from the initiation has developed to a size that can be detected by non-destructive testing
methods. The propagation of the crack happens in the direction perpendicular to the max-
imum principal stress[22]. In this region, the propagation happens in a log-linear relation
with the applied crack driving force. This is often referred to as the stress range, which is
the difference between max and min principal stresses. This phase covers 70 - 90% of the
total lifetime depending on how easily the crack initiated.
2.2.3
Structural failure
Structural failure is the phase where the propagation transitions from the log-linear path
and accelerates towards a vertical tangent. This is also the shortest of the three phases,
which ends with failure in the structural member. Some expressions of the propagation in
this phase have been developed[23], but that is out of the scope of this analysis.

2.2.4
Fatigue in monopile structures
Monopile structures are subjected to bending loads due to prevailing wind, wave, and cur-
rent loads acting on the turbine[24], which can result in the development of cracks. This
phenomenon was illustrated in Figure 1.7. The cracks can potentially originate due to irreg-
ularities in the base material, stress concentration at the weld toe, or surface irregularities
near and on the weld bead combined with transverse residual stresses. Some designs might
require attachments welded onto the monopile, which introduces more local stress concen-
trations at the location of the attachment. The weld notch stress concentrations positioned
in the HAZ, combined with the bending loads and residual stresses cause cracks to prop-
agate parallel to the circumferential weld as well as the potential fatigue cracking failure
modes for welded attachments as illustrated in Figure 1.8. The progressive deterioration of
the structure through crack propagation is characterized by a slow crack growth rate in the
beginning, which gradually accelerates until reaching complete fracture. In particular, this is
a problem for inspection, because the cracks are detected relatively late in the development,
sometimes close to the point of fracture.
2.3
S-N Curve Approach
The fatigue life of a structure is by conventional design procedures considered by the ap-
plication of a simplified analysis model based on S-N curves that are available for specific
structural details. An S-N curve for a specific structural detail is developed based on a larger
number of experiments that have been performed on identical fabricated versions of one de-
tail type. The specific structural detail is subjected to a specific environmental condition and
a specific constant amplitude loading magnitude, which result in a constant amplitude nom-
inal stress range in the detail. The experiments yield a number of load cycles until failure,
which then describes the fatigue lifetime of the specific structural detail for a specific stress
range[25]. When several experimental results have been collected, a regression analysis can
provide a curve showing the log-linear relation between the stress range (S and the number
of cycles (N) until failure for the corresponding stress range. These graphs are referred to as
S-N curves. An example of a stress history from the experiments is provided in Figure 2.6a
along with an example of an S-N curve in Figure 2.6b.

The log-linear relation illustrated on Figure 2.6b is the best-fit curve to the scatter of the
data. However, it represents a mean curve, since each point has the same weight in the
regression analysis. Therefore, this approach lacks conservatism and safety, since there will
be a 50% probability of failure (PoF)[26]. To increase the conservatism in the prediction of
the fatigue life, and thereby lower the PoF significantly, the mean curve can be shifted into
a design curve by subtracting two standard deviations. This causes a reduction to the mean
curve of approximately 25% while reducing the PoF to only 2.3%[8]. This is illustrated in
Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: Shift from mean to characteristic S-N curve.
Although the S-N curve method is based on constant amplitude loading, offshore structures
will be subjected to variable amplitude loading. To address this variation in loading, the
concept of cumulative damage is introduced. Different methods are available to estimate
cumulative damage using S-N curves, but the Palmgren Miner’s summation method is widely
accepted as the preferred approach[26].

Theory and fracture mechanical procedures
This section covers the fracture mechanical approach to the fatigue design of offshore struc-
tures, which differentiates from the conventional theory and procedures.
3.1
Fracture Mechanics
To verify the strength of a structural detail, the material strength must be sufficient for the
stresses that the structure will experience. However, this design method does not account
for the fatigue damage that occurs when the structure is subjected to cyclic loading. Using
the theory from fracture mechanics, this can also be accounted for.
3.1.1
Nature of cracks and fracture
The concepts of fracture mechanics are based on the linear elastic fracture mechanics theory[23].
The first theories, which were only applicable to materials that obey Hooke’s law, have been
extended to also cover nonlinear material behavior.
A fracture occurs in a material when the stress reaches a point where the atomic bonds
in the material are exceeded. The theoretical strength of a material will in most cases be
larger than the actual strength due to flaws. Flaws reduce the material strength, which
means that the detection and estimation of flaws are very important in order to assess a
given structural detail. Thus, the local increase in stresses will affect the global structural
integrity. This effect is referred to as the local stress concentration.
Cracks can propagate as a result of three different crack opening modes, referred to as
Mode I (opening), Mode II (sliding), and Mode III (tearing). These three modes are sketched
in figure 3.1[27][28].

For the analysis of monopile foundations, Mode I and Mode II will be the ones investigated,
as these is the mode that corresponds to the tensile stresses in the regions around the weld

The crack propagation rate depends on several parameters. These include material strength,
the geometry of the component, surface conditions, and load spectrum as well as the crack
propagation constants mentioned in the previous lines.
Material strength
For most materials, the static strength properties such as the yield strength and ultimate
tensile strength are widely known. However, for cyclic loading, the material will have a
different behavior compared to a standard static load.
Geometry of the weld detail
Usually in fatigue tests, larger specimens have a lower fatigue strength. This is due to the
fact that the larger the specimen is, the more likely is it that a critical flaw or defect will
be present[30]. These flaws can be from the material itself or a defect due to the welding
process.
Surface conditions
There are several surface conditions that can influence the fatigue strength of the material[30].
There are macroscopic effects such as tool marks, machining grooves, and weld flaws, which
can reduce the cross-section as well as cause local stress hot spots. These flaws and hot spots
are often the location where the fatigue cracks will initiate from[31]. Generally, the rougher
the surface, the lower the fatigue strength
Environmental conditions
The environment in which the structure is located significantly influences the fatigue strength.
If the component is in a corrosive environment, the fatigue strength will decrease[32]. The
corrosive environment causes edging in the material surface, where degradation occurs on
the surface. This increases the surface roughness. Thus, the stress intensity increases in this
location and cracks can initiate and propagate from here. Furthermore, the cyclic loading
can break the potential oxide layer that is formed on the surface, which will remove the pro-
tection against further corrosion. Therefore corrosion accelerates both the crack initiation-
and crack propagation rate.
Temperature also influences the fatigue strength of the material. When the temperature
is increased, the crack propagation rate increases, while also enhancing the negative effects
of corrosion. Thus, the fatigue strength decreases with increasing temperatures[32].
Load history
The load history, magnitude, and type of load also influence the fatigue life of a component.
Tensile stresses decrease the fatigue life, whereas crack closing forces can increase it[32].
For tensile stresses, the crack propagation rate increase when the magnitude of the stresses
increases.

Background investigation of cruciform joint
4.1
Introduction and scope
The DNV guidelines require that the driving fatigue failure mode of a cruciform joint must
be weld toe failure[8]. To assist with the design, a graph is provided in the guidelines to
show the geometries that will lead to weld root and weld toe failure (Figure 2.11). However,
the information about the background for this graph and how it is derived is limited.
A background study of cruciform joints is carried out with the scope of providing a basis
for the prediction of driving fatigue failure modes. An overview of cruciform joint geometry
is presented with the dimensions that are used to determine the driving fatigue failure mode.
Afterwards, a study by S. J. Maddox on fatigue failure in the root and toe of cruciform
joints is presented. The study introduces the Generalized Stress Parameter (GSP) that pro-
vides an approach to compare different weld details and predict the driving failure modes.
The GSP approach make use of Crack Propagation Integrals (CPI), that describe the fatigue
strength of a joint based on geometry alone.

Cruciform joints consist of a main plate with a plate attached perpendicular to the surface
of each side of the main plate. These attachment plates can be welded with different welding
methods and can be both load-carrying and non-load carrying.

subjected to axial fatigue loads are reviewed. The two failure modes will be weld root failure
and weld toe failure. The geometry of cruciform joints and the initial flaw sizes for the weld

Engineering Critical Assessment of cruciform joint
5.1
Introduction and scope
The Generalized stress parameter approach presented in the study by S. J. Maddox provided
a method to compare the fatigue strength of the weld toe and root, based on geometry and
initial flaws. This method is based on expressions for the geometry correction factor.
An engineering critical assessment is carried out in this chapter, where expressions related
to fatigue failure in cruciform joints are presented. The scope of this ECA is to provide an
overview of the different approaches that are available in the industry standards and guide-
lines. The guidelines that will be included in this analysis are BS7910, FKM Richtlinie, and
DNV-RP-C210.
The guidelines contain expressions for the SIFs and the stress magnification factors, that
include geometry parameters for the cruciform joints. Thus, the geometry correction factor,
Y, from Equation 4.2 is represented in the expressions and can be used to create CPIs. The
FPD presented in DNV (Figure 2.11) is replicated with a selected number of CPIs to show
the process behind this type of graph.
5.2
Assumptions
In order to predict the fatigue failure mode of the cruciform joints, the initial flaws sizes
must be defined. The root flaw will be defined as the lack of penetration (the 2a parameter
defined in Figure 4.1). The toe flaws are defined as surface cracks that adopt a semi-elliptical
crack shape. The initial crack length of the toe flaws will be assumed to be 0.5mm, which is
recommended in DNV-RP-C203[8] when the actual initial crack length is not known.
With the non-destructive testing (NDT) methods, cracks smaller than 0.5mm in depth are
very difficult to identify[35]. Appendix B contains information about NDTs and requirements
that are stated in the standard DNV-RP-C401. This standard describes the requirements
for the fabrication and testing of offshore structures. The exact NDT ranges are stated in
the standard FKM Richlinie[36] and are described in Appendix C.
With the assumption of a 0.5mm initial crack length, the crack aspect ratio a/2c (illus-
trated in Figure 4.2) will converge towards a value of 0.4, regardless of the initial value of
this ratio[37]. This covers surface cracks with a semi-elliptical shape. Assuming a low crack
aspect ratio adds conservatism to the design, as a lower crack aspect ratio will increase the
stress magnification at the deepest point of the crack[38]. The extent of this effect decreases
as the crack propagates further into the material. Based on this information, the a/c ratio
will be assumed to be 0.1 in this analysis, as this ratio was assumed in the GSP approach[31].
The Wöhlers coefficient, m, normally lies in the range between 2.4 and 3.6 for structural
steels. For analyses of welded joints, the assumption of m = 3 is often used[39], which is also
Page 39 of 85
==PAGE:62==

BS7910
in agreement with experimental data [31]. In DNV-RP-C203, m = 3 is widely acknowledged
to a point of 107 load cycles, and then a kink is introduced where m changes value[8].

FE analysis of cruciform joints
The failure mode determination for cruciform joints has been described with expressions
from literature and industry standards. As these methods yield different results, a Finite
Element (FE) analysis of cruciform joints is carried out to model the driving failure mode.
This is done for a selected number of geometries. The FE analysis provides another perspec-
tive on the driving failure mode of cruciform joints.
The FE analysis is carried out with the use of two different software programs:
• ABAQUS CAE
• Franc3D
First, the two FE programs will be presented, followed by a benchmark example of a plate
with a buried crack. The benchmark example will be compared to hand calculations.
Afterwards, five different cruciform joints will be modelled, and the driving failure mode
for each model will be presented.
6.1
ABAQUS
6.1.1
Geometry and material
The geometry of the joints in this analysis is established in ABAQUS as a solid 3D model. A
coarse mesh is added to the model, which will be refined once it is imported to FRANC3D.
The initial flaws and lack of penetration will not be modeled in ABAQUS. The material
parameters will be added as elastic mechanical steel parameters:
E = 210 GPa
ν = 0.3

Loads and boundary conditions
The boundary conditions in ABAQUS allow the user to fix different nodes, edges, and sur-
faces in the desired directions. The boundary conditions can also be applied as symmetry

The loads can be applied to nodes, edges, and surfaces of the model.
In this analysis,
pressure loads will be used both as static loads and dynamic loads on the monopile.

FRANC3D is an FE software that will be used to insert and propagate cracks in models

SIFs and crack depth per calculation step in the simulations.

7
Comparison and discussion for cruciform joint
To estimate the driving fatigue failure mode of cruciform joints, the GSP approach with CPIs
from different literature and standards has been introduced. These have been supplemented
by an FE analysis for five different cruciform joint geometries.
The CPIs from the different guidelines provide varying results, which means that the fa-
tigue design of welded joints will be influenced by the choice of CPI source. To compare
the different approaches for failure mode prediction, the different CPIs will be evaluated
by applying the expressions on results from actual experimental fatigue tests of cruciform
joints. Two different sets of test results will be used for the evaluation.
The two sets of experimental tests contain cruciform joints with varying geometry, which
are subjected to an axial fatigue load in the attachment plates. When the cruciform joints
fail, it is noted whether the failure was due to a crack in the weld root or weld toe.
The different CPIs will be applied in different combinations with the aim of predicting
the same failure mode as the experiments resulted in. This will estimate the accuracy and
conservatism of each CPI combination. The applicability of the GSP will also be illustrated
with the test results.
The CPIs that have been introduced in this analysis are the weld root CPIs from Mad-
dox and BS7910 and the weld toe CPIs from Maddox, BS7910, FKM, and DNV. This yields
8 different combinations of CPIs that all have been used to predict the failure type with the
aim of finding the optimal combination for the failure type prediction.
The initial crack length at the weld toe of the test specimen is not measured in the two
ranges of tests. This parameter influences the fatigue behavior of the weld, which means
that the accuracy of the prediction depends on the correct estimation of this crack length.
As DNV states that a defect of a=0.5mm can be assumed[8], which also was the crack length
used to calculate the CPIs, this initial crack length will be assumed.
The prediction of the failure type will be based on the expressions

7.3
Discussion of the ECA results on cruciform joints
7.3.1
Evaluation of the CPI and GSP approach
The GSP approach, which is based on weld toe- and weld root CPIs, provide a method to
predict the driving fatigue failure mode of cruciform joints. Furthermore, the GSP is supe-
rior to conventional S-N curves, as it can be modelled for any given crack depth, whereas
the standard S-N curves only estimates the total fatigue lifetime for a given stress range.
This means that the GSP approach can applied to welded joints at a certain point in the
fatigue lifetime, if a crack in the weld toe or weld root is observed. However, while the
GSP approach has a wider application compared to S-N curves, it is also necessary that the
geometry can be described sufficiently in the expressions.
In addition to predict the driving fatigue failure modes and estimation of fatigue lifetime,
The GSP approach can also illustrate whether a toe- or root failure is dominant, or if the
two failure modes are almost equally likely. If two methods predict the same failure type,
the GSP approach can give insights to how close to equal the two predictions are.

Loads on the monopile
This section will cover the loads that the structure will be subjected to as well as an analysis
of the different possible sequences the loads can occur within. This includes a description
of how the different loads will be modelled in the FEA. The loads will be divided into two
different phases, which are the installation process and the fatigue service loads.
8.2.1
Loads from installation process
The structure will be subjected to a dynamic impact load during the installation process,
before it is in service. During the installation process, the monopile will be hammered on
top of the structure to drive it into the seabed, and this is a dynamic load that also causes
vibration-induced stresses in the joint.
The installation process, where the monopile is hammered into the seabed, is simulated
in ABAQUS in order to describe the displacements caused by this.
Franc3D is used to
model the crack propagation that happens during the installation process. This simulation
is simplified, which means that the results will be approximate. However, the analysis indi-
cates the order of magnitude of the stresses. The joint is subjected to two different types of
stresses during the installation process.
The first is the compression stress from the impact load that is transferred from the top
of the monopile and down to the bottom of the seabed. This impact is repeated 2000 times
with an interval of 2-3 seconds. The stresses will be modeled as 2000 separate static loads
with a magnitude of σ = 123MPa[45].
The second type of stress is present due to the vibrations of the transverse attachment
plate after each impact load. This effect is visualized in Figure 8.2.

8.4
Results
The crack growth analysis is divided into three different stages. First, the crack growth from
the vibrations is modeled, followed by the static loads from the hammer, and afterward, the
service fatigue loads are applied.
8.4.1
Installation process
The hammer-induced vibrations in the attachment plate are the first load that is applied to
the model. During this simulation, the crack growth for all three fatigue crack types has a
magnitude too small for Franc3D to extend the crack front. This is due to the two crack
fronts overlapping in the mesh of the model. This is the case for all of the models. See
Figure L.5 to Figure L.8 for the resulting SIFS.
During the 2000 static compression loads, the same results are observed. The crack propa-
gation is so insignificant, that the new crack fronts can not be modeled.
8.4.2
Service loads
During the service fatigue loads, the joints show no significant crack propagation from the
initial flaw in the weld root. The fillet welds are modeled both with full root penetration
(2a/T=0) and without any root penetration (2a/T=1), where both cases show insignificant
crack propagation.
The two different weld toe flaws in the weld toe both propagate as the crack growth analysis
progresses, until fatigue failure is reached when the crack has propagated through the ma-
terial. The crack from the weld toe into the base material (no. 1 in Figure 8.8) propagates
with a significantly higher rate compared to the crack from the weld root into the section
under the weld (no. 3 in Figure 8.8). The SIFs computed for the two cracks showed that

crack type no. 1 are 5-6 times larger than the SIFs for crack type no. 3 at the initial stage
before the first crack growth step.
To see whether the presence of both of the types of toe cracks at the same time influ-

8.4.3
Model with weld toe grinding
Weld toe grinding enhance the fatigue strength of the weld toe, which means that fatigue
failure due to cracking from the weld toe requires a larger number of cycles.
To investigate the effect of weld toe grinding on the fatigue strength, a model with and
a model without weld toe grinding are analyzed in Franc3D. Both models have a weld toe
crack as the initial flaw.
8.5
Discussion of the results
8.5.1
Loads
The installation process does not yield any significant crack growth. The hammer- and vibra-
tion loads are applied separately, which is a simplification of how the loads will actually be
applied. Furthermore, a realistic simulation of the dynamic response require that a complex
model of the whole structure is created with very detailed modelling of the impact energy
from the hammer and the energy dissipation in the seabed.
The fatigue service loads applied with the DEL method propagate the toe cracks, but when
this load is applied with the reference number of cycles, Nref = 107, the joint is not close to
fatigue failure. While the DEL method provides a conservative load case due to a constant
Wöhlers exponent, this load is not critical for the T-joint in this analysis. This might be
influenced by the simplification of the model and loads.
8.5.2
Crack propagation
The results for the weld root crack indicate that initial flaws in the weld root will not be
critical and determining for the fatigue design of these joins in the monopile. The simplifica-
tion of the loads, where the stresses are added parallel to the weld root crack, is decreasing
the SIF at the weld root. During the actual installation and service time of the monopile,
the crack propagation at the weld root might therefore be more critical than the results of
this analysis indicates. Local increases in section forces due to the global bending of the
monopile have been neglected, which also influence the results. Based on the results of the
crack propagation in the weld root no weld penetration is needed, which means that K-butt
welds are excessive in terms of welding time- and material.
Page 79 of 85
==PAGE:102==
8.5
Discussion of the results
The results from the analysis indicate that failure due to an initial flaw from the weld toe
into the base material will be the driving failure mode for a T-joint with a non load-carrying
transverse stiffener, when subjected to the loads presented in section 8.2. The crack from
the weld root through the base material also propagated during the analysis, but with a
significantly slower rate compared to the crack initiating from the weld toe.
The comparison between the models with and without weld toe grinding showed that the
SIFs (presented in Appendix M) are larger for the model with weld toe grinding improve-
ment. This contradicts the results from an FE study by Tsutsumi et al., which show that
weld toe grinding improves the fatigue life of welded joint[49]. In this study, an initial crack
with a depth of a=0.05mm was inserted. A study by Lieurade et al. described that weld
toe grinding prolongs the crack initiation phase rather than the crack propagation phase[50].
This indicates that the initial crack length of a=0.5mm that was modelled in Franc3D in
the FE analysis was too large. Therefore the initial crack length of 0.5mm might be too
large to take the weld toe grinding into account, due to the SIFs having a higher initial value
compared to an initial crack length of 0.05mm. With an initial flaw of 0.5mm, the crack
initiation phase is exceeded, and the crack propagation phase is in progress. Therefore these
results indicate that weld toe grinding does not improve the fatigue strength of welded joints
with non-load carrying transverse attachments, when the crack depth at the weld toe has
reached 0.5mm.
8.5.3
Modelling process
The crack growth analysis is carried out with a large number of approximations and as-
sumptions. These assumptions will impact the precision of the calculations and results of
the analysis, which therefore should be used as an approximation that describes the ten-
dency of the crack propagation. The following parameters can be optimized in order to yield
a more precise crack growth analysis:
• ABAQUS model complexity
• Dynamic response analysis
• Load sequences
• Crack growth parameters
• Crack growth model in Franc3d
• Adding more steps in the Franc3d analysis

Conclusion
This chapter encompasses the conclusions related to the investigations, results and discus-
sions. The conclusions for the cruciform join analysis is presented, followed by the conclusions
for the non-load carrying joint. Lastly, a proposal for future work related to this thesis will
be presented.
9.1
Conclusion on cruciform joints
• The driving fatigue failure modes for cruciform joints with load-carrying attachments
can be due to cracks initiating in the weld root or weld toe. The failure mode can be
predicted based on geometry alone, if the geometry correction factor can be estimated
accurately.
• The generalized stress parameter approach provides information about how close the
fatigue strength of the weld root and weld toe are.
• The crack propagation integrals provided by S. J. Maddox, BS7910, FKM and DNV
yield 8 combinations that can predict the driving failure mode of cruciform joints,
where each combination has a different degree of accuracy and conservatism.
The
most accurate combination was root CPI from Maddox combined with toe CPI from
BS7910, based on the number of correct predictions as well as the deviation percentage
compared to the actual test results.
• The Finite Element analysis and experimental test results confirms that the Gener-
alized stress parameter and crack propagation integrals can be combined to form an
accurate failure mode prediction tool.
• It is concluded that the GSP and CPI expressions was found only to fit cruciform
joints with load-carrying attachments, which is why an FE analysis with Franc3D is
necessary for T-joints with non-load carrying attachments.
9.2
Conclusion on failure prediction for T-joint
• The welded joint with non-load carrying attachment plate on the monopile foundation
will not have cracks from the weld root as the driving failure mode for the installation
process and service fatigue lifetime for the investigated dimensions and load ranges.
• Fillet welds provide sufficient fatigue capacity for the loads, and K-butt welded joints
are therefore excessive, as weld root penetration is not needed.
• Weld toe grinding weakens the fatigue strength of the joint, when the crack length
reaches a depth of a=0.5mm
• The modelling of the loads during the installation phase was simplified to an extend,
where the results of the analysis are only approximate. A more complex and realistic

Further use of Generalized Stress Parameter
When the CPI for the weld toe and weld root is known, the failure location can be predicted
as these integrals include the parameters that vary throughout different weld details. The
optimal weld design can be chosen based on the geometrical dimensions that yield an equal
probability of failure for the weld root and weld toe, as this would ensure that no part of
the weld detail has excessive strength that is not needed[31]. This also provides a method
to replicate the graph from DNV-RP-C203[8] (also shown in Figure 2.11, where the failure
mode can be predicted).
S.J. Maddox created a version of the graph in Figure 2.11 using the GSP approach. To
do this, the SIFs for the weld toe and root were calculated, and the CPIs could then be
estimated using the GSP. For the weld root, the material parameter, C, was based on exper-
imental data that was converted to an S-N curve with GSPs. This was then compared with
a simple S-N curve for weld toe failure, which yielded a ratio of 5.3. The reasoning behind
the use of S-N curves for weld toe failure was that test results from plates will correspond to
this type of failure. This means that the C-value for the weld toe was 5.3 times larger than
for the weld root. The GSP for the weld root includes the CPI, but the S-N curve for the
weld toe does not. This is illustrated with the equation below, where the 5.3 ratio is also
