Abstract
Geological carbon storage has been recognized as one of the most viable tech-
nologies to reduce carbon emissions in the atmosphere. However, salt precipitation 
arising from CO2 flush is of great hazard in permeability and injectivity. This study 
applied CMG-GEM to simulate the salt blockage phenomena based on the 
literature at both laboratory and field scales. The models were validated by 
history match and were utilized to investigate further the contributions of a series 
of physical processes and governing parameters on the dry-out effects.
The results give a comprehensive understanding of how governing parameters 
control various physical processes and how the coupling mechanisms would exert their 
influences on halite precipitation.
The study suggests that water backflow is a deterministic factor controlling the 
magnitude of the halite precipitation process. But the adverse flow is not only de-
termined by the capillary pressure but also by other governing parameters including 
temperature, resistance factor exponent, permeability and injection rate. In the diffu-
sive regime, salt diffusion could either boost or prevent halite precipitation depending 
on different stages. The brine salinity mainly plays a role in the physical processes of 
immiscible water displacement, CO2 dissolution and water evaporation. The rock-fluid 
reactions are barely observed in the lab scale simulations but the effects are obvious 
in the field scale.
Keywords: Carbon storage, 
Halite precipitation, 
Simulations, 
History 
match, 
Coupling mechanisms.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Global warming, resulting from huge amounts of greenhouse gas emissions, has
been identified as one of the severest issues for human beings over the last several
decades [1][2]. Research has shown that the global annual temperature has increased
at an average rate of 0.08◦C per decade since 1880 and over twice that rate of 0.18◦C
since 1981 [3]. The rising temperature has been attributed to the frequent disasters
and extreme events during the years such as drought, flooding, bad harvest, and
biodiversity loss, which severely threaten the ecosystem and human beings’ welfare
[4][5].
Recently, the issues of global warming have attained more attention from all over
the world and have been set as the priority to be urgently resolved [6]. The United
Nations’ Global Roadmap has set out the milestone that the world needs to decrease
emissions by 45% by 2030 and achieve net-zero emission by 2050, in order to refrain
from warming to less than 1.5◦C as called in the Paris Agreement in 2015 [7][8].
CO2 is the major emission of greenhouse gas with an average of 35.85 billion
tonnes exhausted into the atmosphere annually [9]. The contribution of CO2 to global
warming is over 60% [10][11].
Therefore, the controlling of carbon content in the
atmosphere is critical and highly demanded to build resilience for climate and bring the
net-zero goal within reach. Substantial relevant researches and field trials have been
conducted so far, among which carbon Geo-storage has been recognized as one of the
most effective techniques to reduce anthropogenic carbon emission in the atmosphere
[12][13]. Researchers estimated that up to 10,000 Gigatons of CO2, which is 20-500% of
predicted emissions in 2050, could be geologically stored in deep saline aquifer [14][11].
1
==PAGE:13==
As presented in Figure 1.1 1.2, there are four main storage mechanisms of CO2 in saline
aquifers including: 1) structural trapping, 2) residual trapping, 3) solubility trapping
and 4) mineral trapping [15]. As the injection of CO2, the gas can dissolve into the
brine water and can react with the rocks to form stable minerals. They are recognized
as the most secure storage mechanisms since the seal of CO2 in either pore water or
rocks has low risks of leakage into the surface. By contrast, the structural trapping has
the lowest security because, with the quantities of gas accumulating around the tight
caprocks, there would be high risks of fracture and gas leakage. Besides, considerable
CO2 can also be stored in the small pores by residual trapping.

However, the places of geological CO2 storage is not limited to the aquifers and
can also be deployed in other formations such as depleted oil and gas reservoirs and
coal seams, depending on a combination of economic, political, and technical factors
[18][19]. But no matter where the CO2 is stored, damaging injectivity over time is
always a great concern for the formations’ substantial and continuous carbon storage.
That is because, during CO2 flooding, there is a high risk of salt precipitation from
brine water near injection [20][21], which will clog CO2 flow pathways and induce ex-
tra pressure build-up [22][23]. Although the jeopardizing effects are well recognized,
the mechanisms of salt formation are rather intricate. Research showed that the salt
precipitation could be mainly attributed to the complexity of the chemical-physical
process including water displacement, water evaporation, capillary backflow, salt dif-
fusion and self-enhancing effects[24][21]. The schematic diagram is shown in Figure

These physical processes are highly coupled with each other and codetermine
macroscopic halite distribution and pore-scale arrangement of fluid phases. However,
modeling these physical phenomena is notoriously challenging. Current research has
the trend to explore the drying out and salt out mechanisms by simulation since current
experimental works of salt clogging are at a much smaller scale. Nevertheless, due to
the lack of sound physical models, uncertain and conflicting results were ubiquitous
in various works.
Therefore, despite several experimental and simulation studies conducted in this
area [21], unanimous conclusions and robust modeling efforts are still lacking for salt
precipitation in the context of CO2 storage.
1.2
Literature review
This section generalizes the most relevant experimental and simulation studies
during the last two decades. It is aiming at attaining a more comprehensive under-
standing of halite precipitation near wellbore during geological carbon storage.
1.2.1
Experimental review
The pioneering experimental work on dry-out and salt precipitation within the
context of CO2 geological sequestration could be retrieved to the study of Muller et
al. for the CO2SINK, a European Union project assessing the CO2 storage potential
near Ketzin, Germany [25].
Core flooding experiments at the reservoir conditions
were conducted through dry clean Berea cores fully saturated with the 25% salinity
NaCl brine.
After flushing supercritical (SC) CO2 for 32 hours, the permeability
decreased by approx. 60%. The environmental scanning electron microscope (ESEM)
and scanning electron microscope (SEM) images and elemental analysis allocated the
permeability impairment to the halite precipitation on the grains and pore throats.
And the halite crystals were estimated to occupy up to 16% of the total pore volume.
Later experiments works have tented to assess the formation damage due to halite
precipitation by relationships between permeability alteration and porosity reduction.
Bacci et al. experimentally calibrated the verma-pruess ’tube-in-series’ model by con-
ducting core flooding experiments on the St. Bees sandstones [26][27]. The cores
with porosity of 22.59% and permeability of 7.78 mD were subjected to four consec-
utive cycles of brine saturation and dry SC CO2 flooding. The ancillary results of
atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES) in the experiments diminished the impacts
of fluid-rock geochemical reactions on the permeability and thus could only focus the
4
==PAGE:16==
impairment on the salt precipitation. The results showed that salt precipitation near
the injection point has decreased porosity by 2.83% but has impaired permeability
significantly by 75%, which indicated the small reduction in porosity can induce sig-
nificant impairments in permeability. The same trend was observed along Guiting
limestones with an initial permeability of 1.04 mD and porosity of 30.38% in the
experiments of Bacci et al. where the porosity decline ranged from 3 to 5% and per-
meability impairments ranged from 13% to 75% [28]. Peysson et al. further quantified
the permeability damage with the effects of salt distribution. To eliminate the fluid-
rock reactions, the rocks of Vosges sandstones and Lavoux limestones saturated with
three types of salt were flushed by N2 in the experiments. Based on that, a simple
volume balance model describing permeability alteration only due to salt clogging was
established. The comparisons between the model and the results of the experiment
indicated that the permeability alteration was highly dependent on the length scale
of salt distribution [29].
Many works have reported the local salt precipitation phenomena near cores’
inlet. Wang et al. assessed the local halite precipitation on the Berea sandstones by
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and ESEM. The brine saturation profile mapped
by MRI images showed that the brine saturation was much lower at inlet end and
increased gradually towards outlet end, which was consistent with observation under
ESEM where the halite accumulated near the inlet end [30]. The conceptualized model
in this study described, as SC CO2 flushed, in addition to the brine displacement by
CO2 and CO2 dissolution into brine, the dry SC CO2 evaporated water and carried
it downstream. During the process, the brine could flow reversely towards the inlet
because of capillary pressure. Due to the contributions of the physical processes, salt
precipitated and the permeability near inlet was reduced from 152 mD to 15 mD.
However, the permeability of the middle section of the core increased from 138mD to
174 mD.
The local salt precipitation along the Berea sandstones was also observed by the
experiments of ott et al. where the spatial and temporal evolution of salt saturation
were dynamically monitored by means of micro computer tomography (µCT) under
the reservoir conditions [31].
However, the amount of local salt precipitation was
higher than the original salt contents present in the brine, which was explained by the
capillary-driven backflow. Due to the local salt precipitation, the absolute permeabil-
ity decreased by a factor of four. However, there was not any injectivity impairment
and the effective CO2 permeability increased by a factor of five after the flooding
experiments. That is mainly because salt is mostly distributed into pores that were

5ml/min at the reservoir conditions [32]. Moreover, studies also showed that homoge-
neous salt precipitation could have less harm to permeability. In the experiments of
peysson, the decrease of homogeneous precipitation in Moliere cores, 60% was less than
the local precipitation permeability impairment, 70% in the Vosges sandstones [33].
Besides, the homogeneous salt precipitation in the Berea sandstones of experiments
of Ott et al. increased the effective permeability [34].
Studies explained that the salt distribution was pertinent to pelect number (Pe),
representing the ratio between the capillary convective backflow and diffusive flow [35]
[36][37]. If Pe ≫1, the capillary force dominated the regime and the salt accumulated
near the injection area while if Pe ≪1, salt diffusion would counter the capillary flow
and distribute the salt more homogeneously. The schematic illustration of various
regimes is shown in Figure 1.4.
Besides, water evaporation was demonstrated to play a paramount role in drying
out and salt precipitation. Tang et al. investigated the influences of complete water
evaporation on the rocks with various initial permeability and saturated brine [38].
The results showed that the higher the salinity of brine, the greater the drop in per-
meability was observed. The lower the initial reservoir permeability was, the greater
6
==PAGE:18==
the decrease in permeability. Peysson et al. further measured the evaporation rate in
convection and diffusion-driven conditions on a shaly sandstone utilizing nuclear mag-
netic resonance (NMR) [39]. The results indicated that both the drying mechanisms
were subjected to two drying periods where the drying rate firstly witnessed a slowly
decreasing trend until Sw=0.2 and then the rate slumped to zero. The measurements
under different experimental conditions also concluded that higher temperature would
always boost the evaporation in the diffusive regime while the injection rate could lin-
early correlate to the drying rate within the convective regime. However, the effect
of salt was surprising because the higher salinity could lead to a higher evaporation
rate in the experiments, which was explained by the perturbing boundary conditions
(efflorescence) [40].
Further experiments investigating the effects of injection rates and brine salinity
on the drying out mechanism were conducted by Jeddizahed et al [41]. The study
implied that a higher injection rate would boost water displacement but decrease
water evaporation. But higher salinity brine could weaken both displacement and
evaporation because of higher brine viscosity and lower water activity.
In recent years, a new insight into the physical mechanism of salt precipitation
gradually emerged. Kim et al. firstly observed the polycrystalline aggregated in the
liquid phase via the microfluidic chip experiments [42]. Inspired by the observation,
Miri et al. recognized the self-enhancing salt nucleation and precipitation [24] through
further experiments. The high-resolution imaging of microfluidics showed that the
salt aggregate protruded into the gas phases near the evaporation front. Due to the
surface energy effects and hydrophilic nature of the salt, the brine was observed to
be drawn from the deep water zone to the salt growing in the gas phase through the
continuous water film attached to the grains. The brine transported to the crystals was
subsequently evaporated by the CO2 flooding and the precipitated salt could provide
spaces for more crystallization.
1.2.2
Simulation review
The uncertainties in experiments could originate from the scales [43]. The ef-
fects including Geochemical reactions, heterogeneity and heat loss are neglectable in
laboratory experiments but play paramount roles in the fields [44][45][23]. Therefore,
the physical processes at the field scale were recognized to be more sophisticated and
elusive [46]. Simulation works were thus indispensably utilized to investigate the dry-
ing out mechanisms further since current experimental works of salt clogging are at a
much smaller scale.
7
==PAGE:19==
The evaluation of flow mobility over time and space is vitally critical to validate
the ability of models to reproduce salt blockage behavior. In the simulation of Hurtel
et al, the mobility of flow impairment due to salt precipitation was implemented by
allocating arbitrary values to the mobility multiplier [47]. The results showed a 10m
dry-out zone in 2 years. In an attempt to improve the prediction of halite precipitation,
simulation works have tended to explore the optimal k −ϕ relationship to assess the
impairment.
Giorgis et al.
developed an extended Verma and Pruess model and
integrated it into the TOUGH2 to simulate the dry-out effects during CO2 injection in
a depleted gas reservoir. The application of the permeability relationship in simulation
has shown that injection rate was significant in avoiding or allowing salt clogging. The
study also pointed out that experiment-verified data on the pore size distribution and
k −ϕ relationship were demanded for the validity of future simulations [48]. The
new experiment-calibrated Verma and Pruess model was utilized in the simulation of
Bacci et al. where the 2D field-scale simulation by TOUGH2 showed a permeability
impairment up to 50% due to only a few meters of salt precipitation near the injection
well [28]. Kim et al. reported the permeability reduction in the dry out zone and dry
out front was 25% and 90% respectively in 10 wt% brine [49]. In addition to Verma and
Pruess model, the Kozeny-Carman model was also widely used in recent simulation
works. The resistance factor exponent in the model controlled the response of the gas
resistance factor to the halite volume. The values ranging from three to five, which
means severe damage of permeability due to halite precipitant, were normally chosen
in the literature. However, the validity of values should be subject to the calibration
of core flooding experiments. In studies of Tang et al., the 3D field-scale simulation
in 30 years by compositional simulator CMG-GEM showed a maximum reduction of
permeability by 6.75% with applying the gas resistance factor of three [50]. Nourouzi
showed the gas resistance factor reached around 1.26 at the year 30 in his 2D field
simulation by employing a gas resistance factor of five [51].
Besides, one of the other challenges in simulating dry-out effects during CO2 in-
jection is to model a series of physical processes that contributed to salt precipitation
effects. One of the main driven mechanisms is capillary backflow. To simulate the
process, Van Genuchten capillary pressure function was widely used in previous stud-
ies and the results showed that the presence of capillary pressure could exacerbate the
salt blockage near the wellbore. Hurter et al. showed that the halite precipitation
hardly appeared without the capillary forces [47]. Besides, based on the comparisons
with no capillary effects, Nourouzi et al. concluded capillary backflow was the most
dominant process controlling the salt out effects [51]. The study also illustrated vari-
ous governing parameters could affect halite precipitation because they can influence
8
==PAGE:20==
the backflow profile and its magnitude and extent. The conclusion was supported by
the simulation of Andre et al. where the negative effects of capillary backflow near
the wellbore were suppressed by a high gas injection rate [52]. Sensitivity analysis in
the work made it possible to determine a threshold gas flow rate (Qr) where if the
gas injection rate is higher, halite barely precipitated near the injection well. Besides,
modeling the H2O-CO2 composition partition between gas-liquid phases over time and
space is critical but also challenging. In the recent simulation works such as Cui et
al. and Norouzi et al., the processes of water evaporation and CO2 solubility were
simplified to be in thermodynamic equilibrium, among which the CO2 solubility was
mainly modeled by Henry’s law [53][51]. However, the hypothesis was contradicted
to the experimental results where water evaporation rates fluctuated before reaching
equilibrium [39][54][41]. In the simulation of Andre et al., raoult law has been used
to model the water vapor fraction in the gas phase, calculated with the IAPWS95
equation of state [52]. Ott et al. considered the evaporation kinetics in their simula-
tion by modifying the gradient of water saturation in the attraction zone. The results
showed the vapor fraction in CO2 rich phase would increase with a lower injection
rate and farther distance from the injection well [34]. As for a series of geochemical
reactions contributing to salt precipitation or dissolution, the aqueous reactions with
the existence of only H+ and OH−were presented as chemical equilibrium reactions
while the mineral reactions and salt precipitations were considered to be the chemical
kinetic reactions in the simulation of CMG-GEM. The results showed mass diffusion is
another significant process co-determining macroscopic halite precipitation. Cui et al.
showed that the CO2 diffusion would extend its distribution in brine and modify the
fluid properties [53]. Nourouzi et al. stated that CO2 diffusion could boost geochem-
ical reactions by increasing its contact area with rocks [51]. However, although CO2
diffusion has been well modeled in various simulation works by fick’s law, the modeling
of ions diffusion was usually neglected. There are few simulation studies investigating
the effects of salt diffusion on halite precipitation although it is equivalently important
as other physical mechanisms.

The effects of injection rate
One of the most conspicuous influences of injection rate is on the physical pro-
cess of immiscible water displacement. As per Darcy’s law, a higher injection rate is
sustained by a higher pressure difference shown in Figure 3.15(b). This will lead to
a higher viscous force against the negative capillary backflow. However, as shown in
Figure 3.15(a), a faster breakthrough time is present in a case with higher injection
rate. Normally, a shorter breakthrough time in reality means a lower water displace-
ment factor and a higher quantity of remaining water. It indicates a greater number
of ions are still reserved in the core and are more likely to cause salt clogging. The
difference of breakthrough is not obvious in our simulation cases since they are 1D
isotropic models as presented in Figure 3.15(a). But its effects on halite precipitation
and porosity reduction are more distinct as per Figure 3.15(e)(f).
In reality, a lower injection rate could boost water evaporation during the immis-
cible water displacement since CO2 would have more contact time with water during
the stage [41]. However, it is not observed in our current study because the model
assumes the CO2 and water could equilibrate instantaneously no matter how long the
gas could contact with water.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This study investigates the coupling mechanisms of various physical processes and
governing parameters on the dry-out effects by simulation in CMG-GEM at both lab
and field scales. The key conclusions can be summarised as follows:
There are two stages during the CO2 flooding in a saline aquifer. The first stage
is dominated by immiscible water displacement where almost 90% of water can be
displaced out in a very short time. After the breakthrough, the water evaporation will
take over the regime, in which the water can continue to be taken out in the form of
vapor at a much lower rate.
Water backflow controls the magnitude of halite precipitation. The phenomenon
yields more salt at the injection point than the salt amount the location originally has.
There are many factors that could control the process including capillary pressure,
resistance factor and temperature.
The capillary pressure acts as a driving force
for water backflow and can exert its influence on halite precipitation by coupling
with the physical process of immiscible water displacement and water evaporation.
The resistance factor shows the impairment of absolute permeability due to halite
precipitation. And the water backflow is active in a condition with low resistance
factor (high absolute permeability). High temperature could significantly boost the
water backflow.
The effects of salt diffusion vary between different stages. Before the halite starts
to precipitate, the ions diffuse from the injection cell to the nearby region to alleviate
the concentration increment caused by the water evaporation.
This behavior can
diminish the halite precipitation. However, after the salt starts to precipitate, the
ions diffuse reversely towards the evaporation front from the nearby zone. This will
intensify the halite precipitation. But the presence of salt diffusion will not extend
56
==PAGE:68==
the evaporation zone because the water would not move with ions in the regime.
As for the geochemical reactions, their effects are not obvious in the laboratory
scale simulations but they are more pronounced in the field scale with extended years.
The governing parameters have influenced salt precipitation by controlling phys-
ical processes. The high injection rate will lead to an earlier breakthrough time and
a longer water evaporation process. It means more water can be evaporated and a
wider evaporation area can be witnessed. In reality, a low injection rate can promote
water evaporation during the first stage because the gas has more time to contact
with the water during the displacement. However, it is not observed in the current
simulations since CO2 and water are assumed to be instantly equilibrated and CO2
will be saturated with vapor no matter how high the injection rate or how short its
contact time with water is.
The salinity can affect the immiscible water displacement by the viscosity. The
higher the salinity is, the more viscous the brine will be. But the effects are minor
compared with its impacts on the water evaporation process. The evaporation rate
decreases in a highly saline formation due to the decline of water activity. Besides,
the presence of highly saline brine will hamper the CO2 dissolution because of the
salt-out effects. Moreover, The higher salinity would lead to higher salt precipitation
and severer porosity and permeability reduction near the injection point.
The vertical permeability has impacts on the halite precipitation mainly by chang-
ing temperature and water backflow at the injection well.

Recommendations for future research
In this study, the contributions of a series of physical processes and governing
parameters to dry-out effects are elucidated. However, new issues are detected simul-
taneously.
The first and foremost is the assessment of permeability impairment. Because
there is always residual water left in the core after flooding tests, the experiments
have to evaluate the impairment by the effective permeability.
However, the per-
meability decline takes into account the resistance of both residual water and halite
precipitation. And the impact of residual water on effective permeability decline is
significant because of the low relative permeability of CO2 at residual water satura-
tion, 0.5 in the experiments of Jeddizahed et al. and 0.6 in the experiments of Bacci
et al. This dramatically distorts the effects of the halite precipitation and makes the
impairment more pronounced.
Although current experiments tend to evaluate the permeability alteration in-
directly by the porosity reduction. However, the sensitivity of the permeability to
porosity is full of uncertainties depending on the pore morphology, mineralogy, spe-
cific locations of mineral growth, etc [66][73][74].
Besides, water evaporation at different stages is still elusive. The experiments
display a much higher water evaporation compared with the calculated maximum
vapor production.
Experiments
In future studies, the core flooding experiments could be further optimized.
For example, control experiments with brine and with deionized water could be
conducted simultaneously.
Ideally, both of the experiments would leave the same
amount of water in the cores but only the experiments with brine will have halite
58
==PAGE:70==
precipitated. The comparison of the effective permeability after flooding tests could
tell the impairment extent due to halite precipitation.
As per the simulation results in the current study, a lower range of injection
rate is suggested to prevent water evaporation in the brine trap. Besides, a shorter
experimental time could also be considered since the evaporation in the brine trap is
more acute in the later stage of the experiment.
Besides, CT scanning, a micro visualization tool could be utilized as an ancillary
to verify the effects of the salt precipitation near the injection point. The reconstructed
3D model based on CT images could distinguish the location of halite precipitation.
This would help to develop a more robust K −ϕ model for simulation.
Simulations
The models and parameters used in the simulator are suggested to be more rele-
vant to the experiments. For example, the resistance exponent and the water evapo-
ration model should be validated by the experiments.
This study has indicated that the kernel of simulating halite precipitation is to
model a variety of composition and phase alterations in the porous media. However,
the current simulator is prone to assume these thermodynamic processes are initially
in equilibrium for the sake of simplicity. This may cause little discrepancy at the
lab scale but the impacts could be augmented as the scale enlarges. Therefore, it is
inevitable to create experiment-verified models and integrate them into the simulator.
