Abstract
The purpose of this paper is to investigate whether the changes in shape of a new bio­
polymer, which has uses in the 3D printing of buildings, can be learned by a machine­
learning model. The inputs used by the model are the shapes of 3D printed objects and
the time differences between different stages of curing. The data used in this project was
captured by the researchers at C.I.T.A using sensors and it is stored in point cloud format.
For use in this project it was cleaned and fed to a PointNet architecture based on the one
developed by Charles R. Qi et al. 2017a. CPAB velocity fields and transformations were
used to shape the input point cloud into the desired form. The Chamfer Distance will be
used as a loss metric, and the approach is to minimize this loss by gradient optimiza­
tion. Due to the limited number of data points the model is trained using leave­one­out
cross­validation and thus 20 models were eventually trained and evaluated. The origi­
nal PointNet was adapted and developed upon in order to address some key challenges,
and this helped increase the overall performance. The model is able to predict the cor­
rect direction of the deformations, but this comes with a caveat. The time difference input
still presents a challenge and thus the magnitude of the transformation is not always well
calibrated.
Keywords: machine learning, deep learning, point clouds, PointNet, velocity fields, CPAB
transformations, sustainability, architecture, bio­polymer, 3D printing, additive manufac­
turing

Introduction
Climate change and actions to mitigate it have been in the attention of states such as
Denmark, but also super­national entities such as the European Union and the United
Nations for a long time now. Several countries, including Denmark, have pledged to be­
come carbon­neutral by 2050. The construction industry is responsible for 39% or carbon
emissions globally (New report: the building and construction sector can reach net zero
carbon emissions by 2050 2022). That number is split into 28% energy consumption and
11% construction materials (Climate change explained: a guide for construction special­
ists 2022).
Fortunately, there is innovation happening in this space and the one innovation addressed
here is that of additive manufacturing, commonly known as 3D printing. A newly discov­
ered bio­polymer is by Chiujdea and Nicholas 2019 written by the Royal Danish Academy
of Fine Arts. Mette Rasmussen and her colleagues are studying this material and it’s
possible applications. This thesis is an effort to help their project.
1.1
Building sector CO2 emissions
With 39% of global CO2 emissions, the construction sector remains one of the leading
industries in term of world­wide pollution. This is exacerbated by the fact that construc­
tion of new real estate doesn’t seem to slow down. Architecture 2030, a NGO in the
climate space, states that the global building floor area is expected to double by 2060
(Why the building sector? (2022)). This will release enormous amounts of CO2 into the
atmosphere. It seems critical at this point to construct new buildings with CO2­neutrality
in mind, because houses and other buildings will be needed.
1.1.1
3D­Printing houses
One solution to this seems to be constructing houses through additive manufacturing,
better known as 3D­printing. Besides being cheaper and faster to build, these homes
might emit less CO2 into the atmosphere than their manually­built counterparts. A study by
Weng et al. 2020 studied a prefabricated bathroom unit and it’s manufacturing process. In
the first case, a precast approach was used and in the second one, a 3D concrete printing
approach. They found that the printed version achieved a reduction of CO2 emissions by
85.9%. It also achieved a reduction of 87.1% in energy consumption and a 25.4% in overall
cost. Even thought the study is centered around a particular unit, namely the bathroom,
those numbers are looking promising.
One reason why additive manufacturing manages to save time, money and CO2 is a
technique called dematerialization (Towards a dematerialization of object thanks to 3D
printing 2022), and refers to the process of creating more efficient geometries that use
much less material than traditional manufacturing. Another is by changing the materials
all together, for example replacing metals with polymers (Why 3D Printing Is a Critical Part
of the Battle Against Climate Change 2022). Others have tried 3D­printing homes using
clay and soil (3D printed houses to lower CO2 emissions 2022).
1.1.2
C.I.T.A., the bio­polymer and predictions
This brings us to the problem at hand. An next step solution after 3D­printing concrete is
3D­printing something that is 100% organic and has very little CO2 impact. The Center for
Information Technology and Architecure, or C.I.T.A. in short, is an innovative organization

inside the Royal Danish Academy. They have developed a cellulose­based slurry suited
for robotic 3D printing. The material, once seen unfit for architecture, is now embraced

The reason why it was not deemed fit is that during the process of drying, also called

serve as the basis for this thesis as well. However, the approach will differ.
1.2
Thesis Goals
This thesis sets out to explore the material deformation of these 3D­printed blocks using
a deep­learning model. The data will be received in point cloud format from members of
C.I.T.A. and the author sets to explore the possibility of using these point clouds as direct
input to a deep neural network. In essence, we want to learn straight from the shape. The
hope is that by knowing how the material will deform, the architects will be able to use it
better and print it in the proper shape according to their needs.

Thesis organization

2.1
Overview of deep learning on PointClouds
With the advent of 3D acquisition technologies, the amount of 3D data is growing by the
day. The applications of 3D data are many, including self­driving cars, robotics, medical
treatment, etc. There are several formats in which this data can be represented.
The point­cloud is an important data structure. A point cloud is essentially a series of 3D
points that describes the outer shell of an object. Note the absence of vertices, edges or
triangles. Essentially a point cloud is a n × 3 array. Compared to more regular 3D objects
like voxels or triangle meshes, the point­cloud is less voluminous and therefore faster to
run operations on, like training a neural network. Point clouds do come, however, with
the problem of irregularity. Two point clouds created from the same object can be very
different in terms of the actual positions of their respective points. Those points can be in a
different order in the matrix describing the point cloud. Also, the two point clouds can have
a different number of points all­together. Further, a collection of points does not exactly
describe a surface as well as a mesh would. Because of their irregularity, researchers
typically transform point clouds into 3D voxelized grids or a collection of 2D views, as we
will see later in this section. This transformation makes the data more voluminous than it
needs to be and adds complexities and other irregularities (Guo et al. 2021).
Deep learning on point clouds has become a more active field in the last five years. New
datasets have been released such as ModelNet (Z. Wu et al. 2015), ShapeNet (Leng et al.
2018), Semantic3D (Hackel et al. 2017), etc. They have significantly helped research on
point clouds evolve. The paper by Guo et al. 2021 focuses on a comprehensive study of
deep learning methods for point cloud understanding. Of course, this paper will focus on
the methods relevant for the purposes of our project.
There are three main tasks that the paper categorizes models by are classification, de­
tection and tracking, and segmentation. Since our task does not involve tracking the point
cloud in an environment or segmenting parts of it, we will only look at the classification sec­
tion. Usually methods for this task learn the embedding of each point first and then extract
the overall object embedding, using an aggregation method. Classification is achieved by
feeding the global embedding into a fully­connected neural network, like we will see later
in the next section. Depending on which data format is passed to the neural network, the
classification methods can be divided as such: multi­view methods, volumetric methods
and point­based methods.
2.1.1
Multi­view based methods
These methods first project the 3D shape onto separate 2D views, and then fuse these
views. Aggregating multiple views into a global representation is the key challenge here.
Through projection in 2D views, some information is lost most of the time. MVCNN (Su
et al. 2015) is one of these methods, which uses max­pooling to aggregate these multi­
view features into a global descriptor. However, due to max­pooling only retaining the
maximum elements, some information is necessarily lost. MHBN (Yu, Meng, and Yuan
2018) integrates local convolutional features using harmonizer bilinear pooling to produce
a global descriptor.

2.1.2
Volumetric based methods
These methods usually voxelize point clouds, transforming them into three­dimensional
grids of voxels. Then a three­dimensional Convolutional Neural Network is applied on
the volumetric representation for classification. Maturana (Maturana and Scherer 2015)
introduced a volumetric occupancy network that they called the VoxNet and managed to
obtain a good object recognition performance.
2.1.3
Point­based methods
Point­based methods are those which take the point cloud as direct input, and these are
going to be the focus of this report, as will be justified later. They can be divided based on
the network architecture used for feature learning. The resulting categories are: pointwise
MLP, convolution­based, graph­based and hierarchical data structure­based.
Pontwise MLP Methods
In these models each point is processed independently using several shared Multi­Layer
Perceptrons, reffered to as MLPs, and then combine the global features using a symmetric
aggregation function. Normal 2D deep learning methods that work on images don’t work
on 3D point clouds due to their irregularity, like mentioned before.
The PointNet (Charles R. Qi et al. 2017a) was a pioneering work that accepts raw point
clouds as input and achieves the permutation invariance with the help of a symmetric
function, max­pooling. A simplified PointNet can be seen in Figure 2.1.
PointNet++ was proposed by (Charles R Qi et al. 2017b) to address the fact that PointNet
learns features for each point independently and thus the local structural information be­
tween those points cannot be learnt. PointNet++ is a hierarchical network that captures
the fine geometries from the neighborhood of each point. The core of it is made by three
layers: the sampling layer, the grouping layer and the PointNet­based learning layer. By
stacking these set abstraction levels, PointNet++ can learn from a local geomtetry and
abstract its local features, layer by layer.
Convolution­based Methods
These methods are also harder to implement than with a 2D image as input, because
of the high irregularity of point clouds. They can be divided into continuous and discrete
convolutional networks, as can be seen in Figure 2.2.
3D Continuous Convolution Methods. They define convolutional kernels on a contin­
uous space, where the weights on the model for neighboring points are related to their

Graph­based Methods in the Spatial Domain. They define operations like convolutions
and pooling in the spatial domain. Here, convolution is usually implemented using an MLP
over spatial neighbors and pooling is used to produce a new graph by aggregating infor­

coordinates or colors, and features at each edge are assigned using geometric attributes
between two connected nodes.
Simonovsky et al. (Simonovsky et al. n.d.) regarded each point as a node in the graph
and each node is connected to its neighbors by a directed edge. After that, an Edge­

MLP. Max­pooling aggregates the neighborhood information and simplifies (coarsens)
the graph. In DGCNN (Wang et al. 2019), a graph is made in the feature space and
updated after each layer of the network.
Graph­based Methods in the Spectral Domain. These methods define convolutions as
spectral filtering, implemented as the multiplication of signals on a graph with eigenvectors
of that graph’s Laplacian matrix, (Laplacian Matrix (2022)). An example is the RGCNN
(Te n.d.), that makes a graph by connecting each point in the input with all other points and

adjacent vertices, a graph­signal smoothness prior is added to the loss function. While
this method works on full graphs, there are methods that work on localized graphs of
neighborhoods.
Hierarchical data structure­based Methods
These networks are constructed on different hierarchical data structures, like an octree
(Octree data structure (2022)) or a kd­tree (K Dimensional Tree (2022)). Point features
are learned hierarchically from leaf nodes to the root node. Kd­Net (Klokov and Lempit­
sky n.d.) is built using kd­trees with different splitting directions at each iteration. Using
a bottom­up approach the value of a non­leaf node is calculated from the values of its
children using an MLP. The feature that describes the whole point cloud, that of the root
node, is feed to an MLP to predict classification scores. Kd­Net shares parameters at
each level according to what splitting type a node is.
2.2
PointNet
2.2.1
General concept
As we will see in the Chapter 4, our data is quite complex and irregular, having organic
shapes and many small details. Because of this a multi­view based model will not work, as
the 2D views will neccesarily loose important details along the way. Also, the voxelization
process will not work for the same considerations, we do not wish to tamper with the
data more than absolutely necessarily. Besides that, the speed of computation is also
an important factor to keep in mind, and this is where working with point clouds directly
excels. Therefore, this project will focus on the original PointNet by Charles R. Qi et al.
2017a as our starting neural network. This will be later adapted, but let’s take a deep dive
into it.
The PointNet is a unified architecture for point­cloud applications. It has originally been
developed for classification, object segmentation and scene segmentation, as seen in
Figure 2.4. Even though it is a fairly simple architecture, the model is highly efficient,

being on par with the previous state­of­the­art ,Charles R. Qi et al. 2017a. In the following

The basic architecture of the point cloud is quite simple. This project follows the architec­
ture found at Griffiths 2020 and is inspired by the properties of point sets. Its three main
properties are:
1. Points are unordered, unlike in an image or a voxel grid. So the network has to be
invariant to any permutations of the points inside the cloud.
2. Invariance under transformation: As a geometric object, the representation should
be invariant to rigid transformations like rotation or translation. This property is not
something necessarily useful to us, as we want to know which way is up, but some
measures will be taken to alleviate this.
3. Interaction amongst points. Neighboring points form meaningful subsets, like for
example distinctive shapes. The model needs the ability to capture local structures
from neighborhoods of points.
2.2.2
Transform
This part refers to every element of the PointNet before the max pool, shown in Figure
2.5. As we can see, there is a input transform module, a MLP, a second transform module
this time called feature transform, and finally another MLP, before passing the data to max
pooling layer. We will look at them one­by­one and explain what each do.
T­Net: Joint alignment network
The two transform modules are two instances of the same class, called a T­Net. This
type of network resembles the big network and it’s primary function is to make the input
invariant to rigid transformations. The first T­Net takes the raw input. This block has its
input layer as a convolutional layer, together with the next two hidden layers. All these
convolutional layers have a kernel of 1, so they don’t pay attention to the point’s neigh­
bors, essentially not acting like a convolution. The authors of PointNet Charles R. Qi
et al. 2017a describe these layers as an shared­weight MLP. The author believes that
the implementation by Griffiths 2020 implemented the first layers as CNNs because they

are invariant to input size, and as mentioned earlier, point clouds can have very different
numbers of points. The first convolutional layer has 64 channels, the next 128 and the
next 1024. Then a three fully connected layers follow, with 1024, 512 and 256 neurons
respectively. The output of these is a 3×3 matrix. That matrix is then applied on the input.
After the first T­Net, the data goes through another convolutional layer, with 64 channels
and kernel 1. This is again, described as an MLP by Charles R. Qi et al. 2017a, but the
code source ,Griffiths 2020, implements it as an CNN. After this, our data goes through
a second T­Net, this time with size n × 64. The architecture is identical to the first T­Net,
it just happens in higher dimensions. Again the input is multiplied by a transform matrix,
generated by the second T­Net. After this, two more convolutional layers of 128 and
1024 channels respectively. Then the input goes to the max pooling layer. The authors
discovered that these T­nets helped significantly in training.
2.2.3
Symmetric Function: Max Pooling layer
This layer ensures that the model is invariant to point permutations in the input, i.e. the
order of the points. The solution found by the authors is using a symmetric function, a
max pooling layer. This layer is used to aggregate information from each point. It takes
n vectors of size 1024 as input and outputs a new single vector of the same size, that
is invariant to the input order. Max­pooling is symmetric in that the maximum of a set of
numbers is the same, regardless of the order they’re in. This layer essentially aggregates
the maximum­value features from each point. The network learns a set of optimization
functions that select informative points and encode the reason for their selection. This
solution to the aforementioned variance of the input is very useful in making the model
more robust.
The final fully­connected layers aggregate these learnt values into a label for the entire
point­cloud, as classification, or as labels per points, as segmentation. Segmentation will
be shortly explained in the next part.
2.2.4
Local and Global Information Aggregation
The above section outputs a vector of size 1 × 1024, which represents the global features
for the input point cloud. From here we can train a simple MLP classifier on the global
features vector, in order to classify the point cloud.
Point segmentation on the other hand requires both global and local features. This sub­
model can be observed on the bottom­right of Figure 2.5. We do this by feeding the global
feature vector back to the per point features. We concatenate the global vector with each
of the point features. This will have the shape n × 64 + n × 1024 = n × 1088. Then
we extract per point features based on the combined vectors. Now the per point feature
takes into account both local and global features. Thus, the network can predict per point
quantities or classes from both local shapes and global semantics. In this project, we do
not look to segment our blocks so this part of the model will not be implemented.
Even though there are multiple ways of solving the issue of processing point clouds, it
seems like PointNet has a good performance and a fairly simple architecture. These two
qualities are desired, because the model will need to be changed in order to fit the needs
of the project, fx. output a transformation instead of a class. Thus, a simple yet robust
model was chosen to begin with.

2.4
Chamfer distance
The task at hand is to compare two point clouds and find their degree of similarity, so we
will need a reliable metric to measure said similarity. This might be challenging consid­
ering the unordered and irregular form of the point clouds. For this, a broadly adopted
metric is the Chamfer Distance (T. Wu et al. 2021). First we need to establish what a ’dis­
tance’ between two point clouds is. ’Distance’ refers to the value of a non­negative func­
tion, which measures the dissimilarity between two point sets. Since point clouds are an
unordered structure, the shape­level distance usually comes from aggregating pair­wise
point­level distances, based on a certain assignment strategy. The assignment strategy
used here is nearest neighbor, in 3D space. The Chamfer Distance (CD) between two
point sets S1 and S2 is defined by the following formula:

Each point x ∈S1 finds its nearest neighbour y ∈S2 and their difference is calculated.
Then, the L2 Norm calculates the distance of the vector from the origin of its vector space.
It is also known as the Euclidean norm because it calculated the Euclidean distance to
the origin (L2 Norm 2021). These values are summed for each x ∈S1 and divided by
the number of elements in S1, thus becoming an average value. The same procedure
is carried for points y ∈S1. Therefore the CD is an average of the L2 Norms of the
differences between all the points and their nearest neighbors. Equation 2.5 can also be
called a Bidirectional Chamfer Distance, because it takes into account nearest neighbors
of points from both S1 and S2.

The CD has an advantage in the fact that it’s relatively fast to calculate and this allows
for training in a decent amount of time. Its issue, however, is the fact that it is not a
comprehensive metric for evaluating the visual quality of the generated point cloud. In
other words, a lower CD does not always correlate with a more similar point cloud to
the target. One reason is that the square operation in the L2 Norm is heavily influenced
by outliers. A second reason is that the nearest point operation makes the metric less
sensitive to mismatched density functions and thus less discriminative in terms of visual
evaluation. A third reason would be that CD does not return any information with respect
to the actual position of any points, being a sum of all distances. That being said, the
library developed by Murthy 2020 will be the implementation used for this project.

Aligning data and normalization
The previously mentioned the LibCPAB library, developed by Skafte 2018, that handles
the transformations, has one requirement that is crucial when we talk about data normal­
ization. The library works in the interval [0, 1], so all data will need to be in this space. Even
further, the data should have the middle point of axes x and y in the point (0.5, 0.5) and it’s
lowest point on the z axis should be at 0. The first condition is for obtaining a somewhat
balanced tessellation of the input point cloud, i.e. approximately the same ’volume’ of
object in every sub­division. The latter condition refers to using an option within LibCPAB
that makes the points lying on the edges, i.e. close to either 0 or 1, to not move. The
options is called through a flag called zero­boundary and essentially it would ’freeze’ the
bottom part of the point cloud in space. This will make sure that the bottom doesn’t ’fly’
up or move in another direction.

point clouds have different number of points, but the Data Loader object can only handle
same­size inputs. Batching also requires having inputs of the same size. Because of
this, the data is padded with zeros to the longest length. Thus, one data point will be a list
containing the following elements: The two point clouds for input and label, the original
lengths of the input and label and the time difference, ∆time, in minutes.
When loaded into the training loop, the data points are cut back to their original lengths,
and after that they are randomly permuted using PyTorch’s randperm function, and cut to
the length of the shortest element from that batch. In this way, we can train in batches
even if the input has varying length. The downside, of course, is that we loose some of
our data at every batch. This should be offset by a relatively large number of epochs, in
which case the random permutations even out as a uniform sampling.
One thing to mention is that ∆time can be a large number, so two variations of the dataset
were created, one where ∆time has its original value and one where ∆time has a normal­
ized value across the dataset. Models will be trained using each of them, in order to find

5.3
The custom PointNet
The overall flow of the pipeline can be seen in Figure 2.9, and the equivalent equation is
Equation 2.6. The input, containing a point cloud and the time difference, ∆time, is pased
through the PointNet f and the result is the velocity field θ. This field will be then ap­
plied to the output, using LibCPAB’s transform grid function, Skafte 2018. This deformed
point cloud will then be compared to the label using the loss function Chamfer Distance,
described in Section 2.4.
The architecture of the PointNet, presented in Figure 2.5, has been slightly changed, to
accomodate our specific needs. First off, the output layer of the model has been changed
to 78 neurons, the required size for a LibCPAB transformation that uses the following pa­
rameters: tessellation [3,3,3], zero_boundary = False, perserve_volume = False. These
our are initial parameters used during learning. The second change is that the 1024­
element feature vector resulting after the Max Pooling operation is being added a 1025th
element, that is the time difference between the input and the label, in minutes.

5.4
Training Setup
A few initialization techniques were used. The first one was randomly initializing the whole
model, which had some very undesirable consequences like completely distorted outputs.
After that, the whole model has been initialized with the value zero, in both the weights and
biases. This also has some consequences that were not desirable, that will be presented
the Section 6. Finally, the model has been initialized in the following way: all the weights
and biases are random, except those in the final three layers that are initialized with zero
in both weights and biases. The results will be presented as well in the following section.
A few learning rates have been tried, in the interval [0.001, 0.0001, 0.00001]. The best
learning rate is 0.0001. The usual number of epochs for training is 100. As mentioned
before, the leave­one­out cross­validation will be employed, as described in Subsection
5.2.3.
5.4.1
Force field: zero boundary and stretching the z­axis
Another variation of the experiment is enabling the zero_boundary function of LibCPAB to
True. This will essentially trap the points closest to the edges of the [0, 1] interval in which
LibCPAB operates. In our case, this will trap the points that are closest to the bottom
of the point clouds, essentially creating a force field. Remember that the bottom of the
z­axis sits on the point 0 for every point cloud. This option will essentially fix those points
in place, putting an extra constraint on the transformation. It needs to be said that with
this option enabled, the size of the PointNet’s output­layer will need to be 96 neurons, as
per LibCPAB’s implementation.
After that, another modification has been done. Each point cloud has been stretched along
the z­axis to occupy more of the interval [0, 1]. This would allow the model to use more
parameters on the input point cloud as opposed to on empty space, and thus achieve
greater expressivity and hopefully better results. It must be said that the stretching on the
z­axis happens only before the velocity field transformation, and the input and label are
reduced again to their original size before being passed into the loss function. The results
of this approach are discussed in Section 6.1.2 of the following chapter.

7
Discussion
From the previous chapter it can be said that the model architecture presented in Section
2.2 can perform quite well on the given data and is expressive enough to quite accurately
move the points of a cloud to match a label. The model is able to encode the input into
a (1 × 1024) feature vector and then concatenate it with the time difference ∆time. In this
way the model is able to have an underlying representation of the point cloud shape and
time.
The technique presented in Section 6.1.2 where the z­dimension of the point cloud is
stretched in order to use more of the model’s parameters added to the expressive poten­
tial. The vanilla model can barely perform on the early examples with a low time difference,
in the case of the first example it performs even worse than the identity. The model that
employs this technique obtains much better losses than the identity on all the examples.
It must be said that this difference in performance shrinks as ∆time increases. The time
difference is that part where the model struggles most. It seems that there is a break­
ing point of sorts, around ∆time = 6700 minutes, roughly 4­5 days, after which the power
of the transformation power increases quite abruptly and the loss decreases. Visual in­
spection confirms this. The introduction of the SimpleNet sub­model helped with this, as
the magnitude of the transformation became more linear and thus more correlated with
the time difference. The model was able to more gradually shrink the points along the
x­axis. The weights of SimpleNet were initialized randomly as well, at first without much
consideration and then it was discovered that this seems to work well. It might be that the
extra numerical parameters help the model interpret time. It has to be said though that the
model can not yet mimic the perfect shape of an object and struggles with details. This
was discovered through visual evaluation. The reason for this is unclear, and it might be
the loss function’s fault. Chamfer Distance does not return any meaningful information
about the shape of an object, especially its details.
This performance is achieved even though the set of data points available for training is
quite small, approximately 600 examples. This was partly offset by the transformation
itself not being very substantial, in essence the input and the label being quite similar.
Therefore it is sensible that even a simple model can perform the transformation. The
model also does not seem to over­fit the training data, as the performance on the test data
is also good compared to the training data. The test losses of Section 6.2 are actually
lower than their analogue training losses. This is unexpected at first glance, but one
reason could be the sheer difference of the number of examples in the two datasets. Like
mentioned before, any of the 20 test sets has only approximately 8 elements. Due to
the design of the train and test datasets, presented in Sections 5.2.2 and 5.2.3 the train
dataset has more examples with a smaller time difference. As discussed earlier, these
examples tend to have a higher loss which explains the apparent problem. One thing that
is harder to explain is the train loss along epochs, presented in Figure 6.4. It does not
decrease steadily, as it is expected.
Another thing to be mentioned is that the model works well on unsampled data, which is
20 times denser than the training data. It is quite easy to explain this, as the CPAB trans­
formations fold the space in a certain area together with all its elements. This provides
a neat property of the pipeline, considering that the original data points are quite heavy.
One can train the model on sampled data using batching and train much faster, and then
evaluate the model on the unsampled data.

The loss function has to be talked about as well. In some machine learning tasks there
are loss functions or metrics that are objective and definitive. One example are the Accu­
racy/Precision/Recall metrics. They sum up the model’s performance in a few numbers.
Our Chamfer Distance loss metric can not function in the same way. We can not say what
percentage of accuracy a certain point cloud is compared to another, we can only com­
pare results and inspect them visually. Like mentioned earlier the Chamfer Disntace also
does not return any information regarding the structure of the point clouds. That is why
the identity loss is taken as reference, and it must be said that compared to this metric
our models performed quite well, obtaining an overall reduction of loss by 47.43%.
However, our PointNet still has an issue in interpreting time. The abrupt change in the
magnitude of the transformation indicates this. One interesting finding is that this break­
ing point does not coincide with the apparent breaking point of Figure 4.5, that presents
the relative sizes of each block during curing. In that figure, the volume and height are
shrinking most between examples 0 and 1, or times t1 and t2. The block before curing is
considered t0. While the data seems to shrink most in the first day of curing, our model
applied more powerful transformations after a time difference of 4 or 5 days. This mis­
match further highlights the challenges that the time variable presents to the model. A
more refined approach is needed in the future.
Cross­validation shows us that the above­mentioned results hold to a certain extent. How­
ever, some of the models struggle more than others with interpreting the time difference.
The graphs of the losses in many of these models closely follow the graph of their identity
losses, which is something that was not observed in Model 1. The inherent variance in
point clouds and the noise in the readings make it difficult for the model to accurately pre­
dict some of the blocks. Also, the magnitudes of the transformations seems to generally
increase with the increase of the time difference, but do not follow a easily­observable
pattern. It can be said that Model 1 is one of the best performing models.
With respect to the research question introduced in Chapter 3, if one wishes to predict
the final shape of a block after the curing process has completed, it seems that the model
is close to performing that task well. Accuracy in details is not very high, and there are
stil issues with the height but the overall shape can be reproduced. I presume that most
applications of the bio­polymer are more interested in the final shape, as to know how to
3D­print the object in order for it to fit into a larger whole. For this task, it can be said
that a foundation work has been done which can be taken forward and integrated into a
Computer Assisted Drawing program in order to help architects all over the world. Further
improvements will be needed in order to make the scripts reach a degree of reliability
appropriate for industry applications.
