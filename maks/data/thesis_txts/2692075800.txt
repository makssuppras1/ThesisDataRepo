Abstract
The purpose of this thesis project was to evaluate the performance of supercapacitors fabricated from
polymer-2D material composites and develop a transparent composite supercapacitor. Graphene and
ﬁve selectively oxidised graphene (SOG) derivatives were synthesised and supercapacitors using them as
active materials were tested by cyclic voltammetry, galvanostatic charge-discharge and electrochemical
impedance spectroscopy (EIS). Two best-performing materials were selected for the composite prepa-
ration: graphene (2.9 mF cm−2) and SOG-EG (4.9 mF cm−2). Supercapacitors from ﬁve polymers, 4
previously and 1 newly synthesised (PNDI-TT), were tested. Two polymers, PNDI-TT (0.26 mF cm−2)
and PIDTT-TBzT (0.34 mF cm−2) were selected for the composite preparation. Supercapacitors made
from SOG-EG composites in 1, 1.5 and 2% concentrations, as well graphene 2% with both PNDI-TT
and PIDTT-TBzT were tested. The addition of 2% SOG-EG was found to improve the performance
of PNDI-TT as an active material by 60% in terms of speciﬁc capacitance at 5 mV s−1 scan rate.
Graphene 2% composites also improved the performance of the polymers: by 25% for PNDI-TT and
21% for PIDTT-TBzT. Finally, a 9 cm2 transparent supercapacitor was fabricated with PVA hydrogel
electrolyte and PIDTT-TBzT SOG-EG 2% spray-coated onto ITO/PET, with measured capacitance of
0.60 mF cm−2.
.

1
Introduction
1.1
Project formulation
With rapid development of renewable energy technologies, electrical vehicles and smart grids comes the need
to provide eﬃcient and reliable energy storage solutions. In many applications, batteries have been utilised to
meet this need. However, their limited power density and relatively short cycle lives make them insuﬃcient
in many cases. Supercapacitors oﬀer a solution, with very high power densities, short charge and discharge
times and cycling stability for up to million cycles with minimal losses of capacitance. The technology still
faces challenges that need to be addressed, such as improved energy density and higher operating voltage.
Of many types of investigated materials, conjugated polymers oﬀer the advantages of low costs, ease of
processing and inherent elasticity. In this study, conjugated polymers will be used to prepare composites
with 2D materials, with the goal of preparing a transparent, polymer-2D material composite supercapacitor.
1.2
Literature study
1.2.1
Supercapacitors
Supercapacitors are energy storage devices exhibiting very high power densities. This is due to the fact, that
the storage mechanism is based on the reactions at the surface of the electrodes, with no need for the ions
to diﬀuse into the bulk material. As such, they provide a complementary solution for batteries, which have
much higher energy densities, but lack the power output and responsiveness exhibited by supercapacitors [1].
Supercapacitors can be divided into electrochemical double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudocapacitors,
based on the storage mechanism. In EDLCs, ions are adsorbed and desorbed at the electrode/electrolyte in-
terface, i.e. capacitive behaviour. In pseudocapacitors, faradaic surface redox reactions take place. Typically,
carbon materials are used for EDLCs, whereas transition metals and polymers dominate in pseudocapacitors
and typically display higher capacitance [1].
1.2.2
Applications
In many applications, supercapacitors oﬀer an economically attractive solution. Energy storage systems can
be divided into three types: direct current (DC), pulse and bidirectional. The purpose of the DC system
is exclusively to deliver power. When in use, a device will only be discharged, though the rate of energy
depletion can change. A ﬂashlight or a phone battery can be used as examples of this system. Pulse systems
deliver power in isolated or repetitive pulses, often at high rates. An example of this type of system is
1
==PAGE:10==
starting-lighting-ignition battery in combustion-engine vehicles, which delivers energy to start the engine in
a single, less than 1 second lasting pulse. In both DC and pulse systems the energy only decreases during
exploitation. In contrast, bidirectional energy storage systems can both deliver and absorb power during
device operation. An example could be a diesel-electric hybrid bus [2]. A paper by Miller [2] illustrates this by
analysing regenerative braking energy storage systems in a hybrid city bus. By calculating the energy, which
could be regained during breaking, the costs of supercapacitor and Li-ion battery storage are estimated.
Even though the supercapacitors are more expensive per kWh stored, they do not require supersizing of
the system, due to very high power densities. The supersizing is necessary in batteries to prevent too high
state-of-charge increase in short time. Taking this into consideration results in virtually equal costs for both
solutions. However, cycle life is longer for supercapacitors, making them more cost-eﬀective long-term [2].
An example of a hybrid, heavy-duty application of supercapacitors are rock crushers. During the operation of
those machines, power peaks are encountered. Combining a combustion engine, which supplies power steadily,
with a supercapacitor to provide additional power pulses at the peaks allowed for 30% fuel use reduction
and 43% increase in crushing throughput compared to conventional models, where much larger engines are
required [2]. Another example in the mining industry is mining shovel. The application of bidirectional energy
storage systems allows for the recovery of energy during swing deceleration and downwards movements,
leading to 25% fuel use reduction [2].
1.2.3
Materials
Carbons are the most commonly used and investigated materials for porous electrodes. The reason for this
is a variety of structures available and the ease of tuning of the physical parameters, such as speciﬁc surface
are, volume and size distribution of pores, dimensionality, particle size and texture. Amongst the utilised
materials are activated carbons, carbide-derived carbons, templated carbons and graphene [3]. Activated
carbons are the most widely implemented carbon material commercially. They are produced by chemical
or physical activation of charcoal, wood, nutshells, synthetic polymers or other precursor. In the chemical
activation process, the precursor is mixed with acids, alkali, carbonates or chlorides at high temperatures.
In the physical activation high temperature is used in mixed inert/oxidising atmosphere to activate the
precursor with steam or CO2.
Disordered structure is obtained in both processes, with wide pore sizes
distribution in the range of 0.4-4 nm.
The chemical activation provides greater control over pore size
distribution. Capacitance values achieved by activated carbon-based devices reach as high as 200 F g−1 and
60 F cm−3 [3][4]. Redondo et al. fabricated a supercapacitor with gravimetric capacitance of 180 F g−1
and volumetric capacitance of 60 F cm−3 using activated carbon produced from biomass [5]. However, the
activation process is hardly sustainable, as the high temperatures used consume a lot of energy. Additionally,
chemical activation results in waste streams and requires safety measures due to the chemicals used, making
2
==PAGE:11==
the process more costly and complex. The development of easily-processable alternative materials would
clearly improve supercapacitor technology.
Another type of carbon-based materials are carbide-derived carbons. They are produced by the etching of
metal carbides (e.g. TiC, SiC) at high temperature under chlorine atmosphere or by vacuum deposition.
The resulting structure is rich in micro-pores with narrow size distribution. Capacitance values up to 220 F
g−1 and 126 F cm−3 are achieved in KOH aqueous electrolyte. Carbide-derived carbons exhibit very high
energy densities [3]. Power densities can be improved by the introduction of meso-pores, to provide space for
fast ion transport. A study by Korenblit et al. introduced meso-pores to SiC-derived carbon improving the
ion transport and leading to 85% capacitance retention at currents up to 20 A/g [6]. Still carbide-derived
carbons are more expansive to produce than activated carbons, which hinders their commercial application
[3].
Templated carbons are used to obtain materials with very high speciﬁc areas and narrow pore size distribu-
tion. This is achieved by utilising zeolites as porous structure directing agent, which is dissolved at the end of
the process. Itoi et al. obtained zeolite-templated carbon material, which exhibited high energy and power
densities, due to the interconnected 1.2 nm pores, as well as up to 190 F g−1 and 83 F cm−3 capacitance
values, with good retention even at 20 A/g current density [7]. However, maintaining material uniformity
and the use of HF for template removal limit the scale-up of this method [3].
Graphene was also employed for use in electrodes in supercapacitors. The mayor problem of the material is
the re-stacking of graphene layer, which leads to huge losses in surface area and in consequence, capacitance.
Another problem is low density of graphene, which leads to poor values of volumetric capacitance [3]. It
is possible to activate graphene using KOH, which leads to broad pore size distribution (0.6-5 nm), high
volumetric capacitance (60 F cm−3) and high energy density (70 Wh/kg) [8]. Graphene-based electrode
was obtained by Tao et al. by drying of graphene hydrogel, combining high density (1.58 g cm−3) and
microporous structure, resulting in high volumetric capacitance of 376 F cm−3 in an aqueous electrolyte [9].
Li et al. developed a diﬀerent method to create dense, porous graphene structures using zinc chloride as
a sacriﬁcial pore former. Resulting materials had tunable density of 0.6-1.6 g cm−3, reaching volumetric
capacitance of 150 F cm−3 in ionic liquid electrolyte and energy density of 65 Wh l−1 [10]. However, in most
cases the low density of graphene is the limiting factor for the device energy density [3].
1.2.4
Electrolytes
Since the energy storage mechanism in supercapacitors relies on the adsorption of the electrolyte ions on
the electrode surface, electrolyte plays a crucial role in the performance of a EDLC device. Stability of
the electrolyte determines the operating voltage of a device and since both energy and power densities are
3
==PAGE:12==
proportional to the square of the operating voltage, the choice of an appropriate solvent can greatly improve
a supercapacitor. Another important metric of a supercapacitor electrolyte is viscosity. Low viscosity is
correlated with high ion mobility and therefore crucial for fast device response. High conductivity (propor-
tional to the ion mobility and concentration) is also required in operating conditions. High chemical and
electrochemical stability of the electrolyte has to be ensured in order for the device to have a long cycle
life. Operating temperatures should be in the range of -60 to 100◦C to be attractive for many diﬀerent
applications. This puts another requirements on the electrolyte: low melting point, high boiling point and
high ﬂash point. In addition the use of non-toxic and low-cost salts and solvents is preferable [3]. All those
considerations make the development of good electrolytes for EDLC devices a challenging process.
Commercially available supercapacitors are based on aqueous or organic electrolytes. Since aqueous sys-
tems require low operating voltages, state-of-the-art electrolytes are currently 1M tetraethylammonium
tetraﬂuoroborate (Et4NBF4) solutions in acetonitrile or propylene carbonate. They can operate in broad
range of temperatures, under 2.8 V. However, the cycle life of devices based on those electrolytes reduces
greatly when higher voltage is used [3]. A study by Ruch et al. determined 51% capacitance loss in 1M
Et4NBF4/acetonitrile cell and 34% in 1M Et4NBF4/propylene carbonate cell after 100 hours of aging at 3.5
V ﬂoat voltage [11]. As increasing the operating voltage would be hugely beneﬁcial for the power and energy
densities of supercapacitors, alternative electrolytes are investigated. Substitution of acetonitrile with several
other nitrile-based solvents was proposed. Examples include glutaronitrile, adiponitrile, butyronitrile and
2-methylglutaronitrile [3]. In a study by Brandt et al. 0.7 M Et4NBF4 solution in adiponitrile was tested
for 35000 cycles at 3.5 V operating voltage and exhibited high cycling stability [12]. Sulfones have also been
considered, as exempliﬁed in a paper by Chiba et al., where ethyl isopropyl sulfone could operate in 3.3-3.7
V due to its high stability [13]. In another paper from the same research group, six cyclic carbonates were
investigated for the use in supercapacitors. 2,3-butylene carbonate was found to be a promising candidate,
with the ability to operate in 3.5V potential window, ensured by high oxidative stability of the compound
[14]. The substitution of Et4NBF4 with diﬀerent salts has also been investigated. Quarternary onium salts
- triethylmethylammonium, ethylmethylpyrrolidinium, and tetramethylenepyrrolidinium tetraﬂuoroborates
were better soluble than state-of-the-art tetraethylammonium salt, resulting in higher electrolytic conductiv-
ity [15]. N-methyl-N-butylpyrrolidinium tetraﬂuoroborate has been proposed by Pohlmann et al., displaying
good conductivity, low viscosity and higher solubility in propylene carbonate than Et4NBF4 [16].
Ionic liquids in solvent-free systems can also be used as electrolytes in supercapacitors. They oﬀer higher
operating voltages than aqueous and organic solvents, but due to higher viscosity their conductivity is
lower.
Ionic liquids such as 1-ethyl-3-methyl-imidazolium-bis(triﬂuoromethanesulfonyl)imide, N-butyl-N-
methylpyrrolidinium-bis(triﬂuoromethanesulfonyl)imide, allowing the operation in high voltage and achiev-
ing high energy densities [17]. However, high costs limit their commercial application. Pohlmann et al.
investigated the performance of an ionic liquid based on azepanium, a waste product from polyamide in-

Dicyanamide anions were used to develop low-viscosity ionic liquids with high power density, but their

Methods
In this chapter, methods implemented in the thesis project are described. Section 2.1 provides theoretical
background for the evaluation of supercapacitors’ performance. Project plan and detailed descriptions of the
methods are presented in Section 2.2.
2.1
Evaluation of supercapacitors’ performance - background
The evaluation of supercapacitors is a complex matter and many parameters are needed to provide a suﬃ-
cient description of a material or device. In case of commercially available devices, cell (total) capacitance
C, operating voltage Vo and equivalent series resistance RES are enough to assess the power and energy per-
formance. However, their materials, fabrication method and cell design are ﬁxed, thus allowing for a simpler
description. When a new material, manufacturing process or cell design is tested, a range of parameters is
required to give a full overview of the performance. Multiple reviews have been published, expressing the
need to establish standards for the reporting of studies on supercapacitors’ performance and emphasizing
the prevalence of misleading data reported [20][21][22][23]. Attempts have been made to standardize the
evaluation methods by diﬀerent bodies, such as US Department of Defence, International Electrochemical
Commission or Society of Automotive Engineers, but the resulting documents are heavily oriented towards
speciﬁc applications [22]. The most common causes of inconsistencies are diﬀerent instrumental methods as
base for calculations, diﬀerent experimental set-up, diﬀerent electrode dimensions and packing (thickness,
mass loading, density), diﬀerent base for speciﬁc values calculation (e.g. mass of the entire device, active
material only, active material and electrolyte) and diﬀerent measurement conditions (rate dependency).
Most commonly used tests to characterize the electrochemical performance of supercapacitors are cyclic
voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic charge discharge (GCD) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy
(EIS). All three techniques can be used to measure fundamental parameters, but each has its focus, ap-
plications and limits [22].

2.1.2
Equivalent series resistance
Supercapacitors exhibit internal resistance, which leads to the dissipation of the stored energy. To reﬂect
this, a device can be treated as resistor and capacitor in series arrangement, where the resistance of the
resistor is called equivalent series resistance RES. Smaller values of RES are beneﬁcial for the performance
of supercapacitors and the devices can be compared with RES normalised to the area of the device (one
electrode) expressed in Ωcm−2 [22]. The evaluation of RES can be conducted by applying Ohm’s law to the
IR drop in the discharge curve of galvanostatic charge-discharge measurement:
RES = ∆V
∆I ,
(9)
where ∆V - voltage drop, ∆I - current drop of the IR drop. Two major factors aﬀecting the precision
of this method are dwelling time and the size of the device. If there is a dwelling period included in the
measurement procedure, the current drop ∆I = I, where I is the applied constant current. In case of no
dwelling period ∆I = 2I, as the current changes from charge to discharge immediately. The values of ∆V
will be diﬀerent as well, but including dwelling period will result in lower RES values [22]. Experimental
data suggests that the IR drop calculation works well only for small devices. Larger supercapacitors should

be characterised using steady-state voltage drop for more accurate evaluation of power performance instead
[22]. Figure 2 shows IR drop and steady-state voltage drop on a GCD curve.

Equivalent series resistance can also be evaluated from electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, where at
least 2 methods appear in literature. The ﬁrst method takes real component of the complex impedance at
1 kHz. The second method uses linear interpolation of the low-frequency region in the Nyquist plot. RES
is the value of the real component, when the imaginary component reaches 0 in the interpolation. However,
the values of RES obtained from GCD discharge curves tend to be higher than those derived from EIS [22].

2.1.7

SOG
Selectively oxidized graphene was synthesized using modiﬁed Hummers’ method. In the ﬁrst step, 0.148 g of
KNO3 in 15 ml of concentrated H2SO4 was added to 0.25 g of graphite nanoparticles. The mixture was then
placed in a water bath at 14◦C and 0.15 g of KMNO4 was added. The reaction was kept for 30 minutes and
subsequently quenched with 50 ml of deionized water and 3 ml of hydrogen peroxide [30%]. The mixture
was then transferred into 2 vials and centrifuged at 4500 rpm for 15 minutes. Once the supernatant was
discarded, the precipitate was washed with 30 ml of HCl [10%] at 4500 rpm for 20 minutes and washed three
times with 30 ml deionized water with the same parameters. The obtained precipitate was dispersed in 25
ml of deionized water using ultrasonic bath for 1 hour. Three batches were prepared.
SOG-F
SOG was functionalized using heptadecan-9-amine (HD9A). Solution of 623.0 mg of HD9A in 50 ml of
ethanol was prepared and added to 50 ml of the SOG in water (6.23 mg ml−1). The mixture was stirred
at 450 rpm for 5 minutes. Subsequently 100 ml of toluene (1:1 volume ratio) were added and the mixture
was mixed at the same rate for another 5 minutes to promote the phase transfer. The mixture was then
transferred into a separatory funnel, shaken and left for 30 minutes to allow the phases to separate. The
aqueous phase was discarded and SOG-F dispersion in toluene was obtained.
rSOG-F

and stirred at 450 rpm for 5 minutes.
rSOG-EG

Results and discussion
3.1
Characterization of 2D materials
Graphene, SOG, SOG-F and rSOG-F were characterised using XPS. High resolution C1s signal of graphene
and graphite nanoparticles, which were the starting material in all 2D materials synthesis, were superimposed
in Figure 6. Both spectra are practically identical, suggesting there are only C-C bonds in the structure.
Figure 7 shows the C1s signal of XPS spectrum of SOG. The C:O ratio of 2.4 proves, that signiﬁcant levels
of oxidation took place. Spectrum of SOG-F in Figure 8 and the calculated C:(O,N) ration of 4.3 show that
the addition of long alkyl chains at the surface of the 2D material was successful, as the percentage of carbon
in the structure increased. Figure 9 shows the spectrum of rSOG-F. C:N ratio equals 3.6 and C:O ratio 13.5,
clearly showing that a lot of oxygen was removed from the structure, while the nitrogen-ended groups were
still attached to the surface of the 2D material.
Raman spectrum of graphene was used to determine the quality of the material. The G band at 1580 cm−1 is
higher than the 2D band at 2700 cm−1, which is asymmetrical at the top. Considering those results and the
comparison of band ratio, it was estimated based on literature that graphene with 3-7 layers was obtained
[25][26].

3.2.1
Electrolyte selection
With the ﬁnal goal of preparing a transparent supercapacitor, three electrolytes have been evaluated - 1M
1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium tetraﬂuoroborate (EMI) in propylene carbonate, 2M aqueous Li2SO4 and PVA
hydrogel. Devices from graphene and rSOG-F were prepared for each electrolyte and their performance
was measured, as described in Section 2.2.3. Table 1 shows the capacitance of the devices calculated from
cyclic voltammetry measurement at scan rate 5 mV s−1. The hydrogel and Li2SO4 resulted in similar values
of capacitance, with Li2SO4 being slightly better with graphene and the hydrogel with rSOG-F. This is
encouraging, as it shows that PVA hydrogel can perform at least as good as a standard aqueous electrolyte
and at the same time eliminate the need for an additional separator. The highest values of capacitance were
obtained for the devices with EMI electrolyte. One of the reasons is the fact that they could operate in
higher potential window than aqueous electrolytes (2 V in this measurement, compared to 0.8 V for aqueous
counterparts). An additional factor is the wettability of the 2D material surface. Graphene’s surface is
hydrophilic - therefore the change in capacitance is not very high. However, rSOG-F is highly hydrophobic,
which would hinder the diﬀusion of aqueous electrolytes into the electrode material. This is reﬂected in

Transparent device
Two prototype devices prepared from PNDI-TT and PIDTT-TBzT composites with 2% SOG-EG underwent
series of electrochemical measurements, as other devices. However, the resulting capacitance was minuscule
and unreasonable for the used area. Upon investigation it turned out, that the ITO electrode was removed
from PET substrate at the air/hydrogel interface, thus eﬀectively cutting oﬀthe supercapacitor from the
potentiometer.
To avoid this problem, the ﬁnal device from PIDTT-TBzT SOG-EG 2% composite was
fabricated with copper wires placed inside the hydrogel to collect the current from ITO inside the gel and
transport it outside. Immediately after the fabrication, cyclic voltammetry at scan rate of 5 mV s−1 was
55
==PAGE:64==
run. The resulting capacitance was 5.39 mF, which translates to 0.60 mF cm−2 or 3.60 F g−1. Test device
in Swagelok cell from the same composite achieved 0.35 mF cm−2 and 2.66 F g−1 in the same conditions.
However, the diﬀerence in mass loading was substantial, which is why the results diﬀer.
After the conditioning for 12 hours, all measurements were made, as described in Section 2.2.6. However, the
capacitance decreased dramatically, with the value of 0.02 mF cm−2 at 5 mV s−1. Green colour was observed,
again at the air/hydrogel interface in the surroundings of the copper wire. This suggest an electrochemical
reaction occurred, as in the previous cases. ITO is generally considered to be very stable, so more experiments
would have to be done to explain this unexpected behaviour.

4
Conclusions
The purpose of this project was to investigate whether the performance of supercapacitors based on conju-
gated polymers could be improved by utilising polymer composites with 2D materials. Another goal of the
project was to prepare a transparent, full-size device based on a polymer-2D material composite. Six 2D ma-
terials were synthesised and characterised and the performance of supercapacitors prepared from them was
evaluated. Graphene was found to exhibit the best capacitance retention at high scan rates and high current
densities amongst the chosen materials, as well as the highest energy density. The experiments showed that
the functionalisation of SOG with oxygen-rich side chain improved the capacitance, as well as capacitance
retention. The device prepared from SOG-EG had capacitance value of 4.9 mF cm−2 at the scan rate of 5
mV s−1. Subsequent reduction didn’t improve the performance any further. Quite opposite, the capacitance
decreased to 4.6 mF cm−2 at the scan rate of 5 mV s−1 and the capacitance retention lowered, as well. This
is due to the loss of redox activity, provided by oxygen functional groups. The functionalisation of SOG
with an alkyl chain deteriorated the performance with PVA hydrogel electrolyte in all aspects. Graphene
and SOG-EG were chosen for the composite preparation.
New polymer, PNDI-TT was synthesised with the goal of using it as active material in a transparent su-
percapacitor. It was tested with four previously synthesised polymers. PNDI-TT and PIDTT-TBzT were
chosen as polymers for the composite preparation. SOG-EG composites were prepared in 1, 1.5 and 2%
concentrations, with an increasing trend in capacitance for both polymers. PNDI-TT SOG-EG 2% compos-
ite exhibited 0.42 mF cm−2 areal and 3.15 F g−1 gravimetric capacitance at 5 mV s−1 scan rate, i.e. 60%
increase compared to pure polymer. For PIDTT-TBzT SOG-EG 2% composite those values were 0.35 mF
cm−2 and 2.66 F g−1, respectively. Graphene 2% composites increased the capacitance, as well, with 0.30
mF cm−2 and 2.26 F g−1 for PNDI-TT composite (25% increase) and 0.41 mF cm−2 and 3.07 F g−1 for
PIDTT-TBzT composite (21% increase).
Transparent supercapacitor with an active area of 9 cm2 was fabricated by spray-coating PIDTT-TBzT
SOG-EG 2% composite onto ITO electrode on PET substrate and using PVA hydrogel as electrolyte. The
values of capacitance obtained by testing the device directly after assembly resulted in 0.60 mF cm−2 areal
and 3.60 F g−1 gravimetric capacitance at 5 mV s−1 scan rate. However, ITO degraded quickly at the
air/hydrogel interface, resulting in the failure of the device. A solution would have to be developed to avoid
this problem, in order for the device to be fully functional.

==PAGE:66==
5
Future work
Performance evaluation of supercapacitors based on 2D materials has shown that the introduction of oxygen-
rich functional groups improved the capacitance of SOG. In the future, more similar compounds could be
investigated to further increase the performance. SOG-EG composites have shown increasing capacitance
trends with the 2D materials concentration, but were studied only in very low concentrations. This was
decided in order not to aﬀect the colour of the device. However, it would be reasonable to study more and
higher concentrations to see what level of improvement can be reached. Ideally, more than one device of
each type should be tested, to give an idea of repeatability and deviation between the results. However, due
to the testing taking up to 20 hours per device, it was outside the scope of this project. Another critical
point would be to explain ITO electrodes degradation and develop a solution to avoid it. With this problem
solved, a transparent supercapacitor would be achieved.
