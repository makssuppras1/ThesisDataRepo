Abstract
In this project the frequency-to-time conversion using a time is investigated. This is done
through considerations on the implementation of a time lens.
Through simulations the
conversion of a highly dispersed WDM signal to a OTDM signal is investigated. It is found
that if the signal is highly dispersed the duty cycle of the pump is less important. This
supports the idea that a spectral magniﬁcation can be done on a highly dispersed WDM
signal. In experiments it is found that the transmission length limits the performance of the
OTDM signal. In the experiments it is found that the temporal overlap of the pump and
signal is highly important. If the pump overlapped two neighbouring signal symbols it would
cause a bit error. If the system should be used as a part of a spectral magniﬁcation this
could be insigniﬁcant.

Introduction
All-optical signal processing shows promise for reducing energy usage in networks. If the signal
processing can be done without converting from and to the electrical domain energy usage could
be reduced [1]. Optical signal processing can be done in many ways but an interesting case is
the time lens. Time lenses can be used for a range of applications including OTDM-to-WDM
transformation and short pulse generation [2].
Time lenses can be used for all-optical regeneration of WDM signals as showed in [3]. An im-
portant part of this is the frequency-to-time optical Fourier Transform (OFT) and this will be
the focus of the thesis.
The thesis will focus on ideal simulations of time lenses and implementation of a time lens using
four-wave mixing(FWM).
The function of a time lens is very dependent on the temporal matching of the time lens and
the signal. Only where the time lens and signal overlaps the needed phase modulation would
be applied to the signal. It is recently shown that a time lens magniﬁcation system can work
properly on a dispersed WDM signal [4]. The idea is that the dispersion will broaden the pulses
to a degree that they would overlap with several time lenses
The goal of the thesis is to study frequency-to-time OFT on a highly dispersed signal. This
will be done by a study of relevant litterature, developing a simulation model in matlab and
experimental veriﬁcation of performance of the time lens in an optical communication system.

The Time Lens
The term time lens originates from the space-time duality and the similarities of temporal and
spatial imaging. The full derivation of the space-time duality and equations for a spatial lens
can be found in [5].
A time lens is in simplicity a quadratic phase modulation of the electric ﬁeld. Similarly a thin
lens is a spatial phase modulation. A quadratic phase modulation is also called a linear chirp,
since chirp is the derivative of the phase modulation.
A spatial lens makes an optical Fourier Transform (OFT) at the focal length[6].
Generally
speaking the full OFT would be after a certain amount of diﬀraction. Similarly a time lens
makes a Fourier transform after a certain amount of dispersion.
Two simple OFT systems can be made using a time lens and a single dispersive element. These
are shown in ﬁgures 2.1 and 2.2.

2.5
Implementation of Time Lens
There are several ways which a quadratic phase modulation could be applied to a signal[2]. A
simple approach could be to use an electro-optic modulator. There are several limitations of this
including limited bandwidth and phase modulation. Another approach is to utilize nonlinear
optical eﬀects. If the time lens is based on optical (glass) ﬁbres it would be third-order nonlinear
eﬀect, such as the Kerr eﬀect. In this section an overview of the nonlinear eﬀects will be given.

A modulation format is the way that data is applied to the electric ﬁeld.
The modulation of the electric ﬁeld can be done on the intensity, phase
or frequency. Throughout this thesis only intensity modulated signals are
considered. On-oﬀkeying (OOK) is an intensity modulation format. Here

or nonreturn-to-zero (NRZ). The diﬀerence between these are sketched in

whether the amplitude goes to zero between succeeding one-bits.

To modulate an optical signal a Mach-Zender Interferometer (MZI) is often used.

3.2
Multiplexing
To increase the amount of data through a communication system multiplexing can be used to
enhance the number of data streams in the system. Two of the major forms of multiplexing
channels in optical communication systems are wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) and
optical time-division multiplexing (OTDM). This thesis deals with the conversion of a WDM
signal to a OTDM signal through a time lens. So with that in mind only relevant concepts are
discussed. A WDM signal consists of several channels whereas they are called tributaries in the
OTDM signal.

3.2.1
WDM multiplexing
A way to make a WDM signal is to have a number of cw lasers with diﬀerent wavelengths. These
are then combined into a single ﬁber. The modulation of the signals can be done either before
or after the multiplexing of the channels. In the experiments the modulation is done on the
combined channels so only a single modulator is needed. To minimize the eﬀect of interference
between channels the channels should be decorrelated. This could be done by demultiplexing the
channels and propagate them through diﬀerent lengths of ﬁber and then multiplex them again.
3.2.2
OTDM demultiplexing
After the time lens the signal is now an OTDM signal. To demultiplex an OTDM signal several
approaches exists. It can be done with MZI’s to suppress all but one channel. It could also be
done with a nonlinear optical-loop mirror (NOLM). These methods have diﬀerent drawbacks,
but an important consideration is the repetition rate of the OTDM signal. MZI’s is limited by
the electrical signals and properties. So for high-speed signals a NOLM would be preferred.

3.3
System characterization
A communication system can be characterised by several parameters. The ones that are used
later on are shortly explained here.
OSNR
The optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR) is the measure of the signal power divided by the ”noise
power”. In optical systems the noise is often very broadband related to the signals bandwidth.
Therefore the OSNR is often given in a reference bandwidth (often 0.1 nm) and the OSNR
given is independent of modulation format, receiver and frequency of the signal. This allows for
comparison of diﬀerent systems.
3.3.1
Bit Error Rate and System Performance
The bit error rate (BER) is given as the number of wrongly received bits divided by the total
number of received bits.
BER is often plotted against the average received power.
Several
factors can inﬂuence the power that is needed to achieve a certain BER. The power penalty is
the increase in received power that is necessary to get a certain BER.
3.3.2
Eye diagrams

The vertical arrow marks the

Eye diagrams are a way of visually representing the received signal. All
symbols are ”overlapped” and the result is a diagram as shown in ﬁgure 3.4.
The more open the eye is the better quality the received signal is - i.e. it
is easier to distinguish 1’s and 0’s. If the signal is not stable in time it will
close the eye horizontally - this is called timing jitter.
PRBS
To test optical communication systems a pseudo random bit sequence
(PRBSx) is often used. The pattern is generated using a shift register of
length x. Sending 2x −1 bits through the system gives all possible x-bit
combinations. Using a PRBS sequence makes it possible ﬁnd the errors in
the received signal without synchronizing to the send signal.

The WDM-to-OTDM system is simulated in Matlab using an ideal time lens. The f-t conversion
consists of a perfect linear chirp and second-order dispersion. To measure BER many symbols
need to be transmitted through the system. These simulations are done using a Monte Carlo
method where Gaussian noise is applied to the WDM signal as they are generated. The OTDM
signal is ”received” and the BER is calculated. To make sure that the BER is statistically signiﬁ-
cant a large number of symbols is simulated and as a rule of thumb only results where at least 10
errors are detected is used. The number of errors to be detected to ensure statistical signiﬁcance
could be explored, but was out of the scope of this thesis.
The time lens and the dispersive elements are assumed ideal. A more realistic simulation could
be done by e.g. using a ﬁber model to model the phase modulation. Other parameters that
could be considered is higher order dispersion and dispersion slope.
As BER measurements are computationally heavy and require many(!) symbols it would be very
diﬃcult to combine this with a ﬁber model.
The simulations are used as a tool to test how certain parameters aﬀect the conversion. The
results of the simulation is not directly comparable with the experimental results, but are used
as a tool to test how the BER changes in diﬀerent situations.
A WDM-to-OTDM conversion is made in the lab. Here the time lens is implemented using
four-wave mixing (FWM). The system has a repetition rate a 10 GHz WDM channels. This is
chosen in combination with 4 WDM channels so that the OTDM signal would have a 40 GHz

repetition rate (each tributary has a 10 GHz repetition rate). This repetition rate can be demul-

to get a true OTDM signal. One could imagine that if the WDM signal is extremely dispersed
the pump would at all times overlap with the WDM channels. To transmit the signal through
many km of ﬁbre the power must be managed as to not introduce nonlinearities or too much
loss. This requires several ampliﬁers that all would add noise to the signal. In the experiments
50 km and 100 km transmission is done but the power in the resulting OTDM signal was very
low and induced a limit on the number of measurements. This could be improved by amplifying
the idler before demultiplexing.

5.1
Simulation setup
The simulations of the system is done in Matlab. These simulations is implemented using object
oriented programming and a link to the full code can be found in appendix A. The two functions
that are of most importance are the ones that constitutes the time lens system: dispersion
and phase modulation. As discussed earlier the phase modulation is ideal. The dispersion is
implemented as
Efft = f f t ( Ein )∗exp(1 i ∗0.5∗(2∗pi∗f ∗10^−3).^2∗Dacc ) ;
Eout = i f f t ( Efft ) ;
The ”E” corresponds to the envelope and the dispersion is implemented according to equation
(2.3). The phase modulation is applied in the time domain according to equation (2.2). The
signal is divided into sections corresponding to the overlap of the pump and a linear chirp
is multiplied onto the signal together with a window function (in most of the simulation the
window is rectangular with a variable duty cycle). The phase modulation is
Eout = Ein .∗exp(1 i ∗0.5∗K∗( t r e l ) . ^ 2 ) ;
Where ”trel” is a vector with the relative time scale of each chunk of the E vector.
These two functions deﬁne the core of the time lens system. The envelope is made by using the
function opticalPulseSource which creates a bit sequence with a speciﬁed pulse shape. In the
simulations a Gaussian pulse is typically used. The bit sequence is a pseudo random sequence
as described in section 3.3.2. Since all simulations are done ideally the frequency of the signal is
centered around 0 - So all frequencies are relative to each other. A WDM signal is constructed of
a number of electrical ﬁelds that are shifted in frequency and then added together. Each channel
gets applied a random phase to ensure that the channels are decoherent.
5.1.1
Noise
Noise is added to the signal with the function addAWGN that adds Gaussian distributed white
noise to the signal by a speciﬁed OSNR.
5.1.2
Receiver
To measure BER a receiver is implemented. The receiver consists of a ”optical” band pass ﬁlter,
a photo diode (squaring the signal), a demultiplexer and an error counter. The band pass ﬁlter
is a Gaussian ﬁlter with a tunable bandwidth. The signal is then squared to obtain the intensity
and then it is demultiplexed.
The demultiplexing of OTDM signals are using time gates of
appropriate width and repetition rates. The error counter goes through each bit and ﬁnd the
optimal sampling point, sampling time and also shifts through the bit pattern. The lowest amount
of errors are saved and used to ﬁnd the BER. To make sure the BER is statistically signiﬁcant

expensive and has set the limit on the best achievable BER.

5.5
Summary
An ideal time lens system is implemented and used for a WDM-to-OTDM conversion.
The
system is used to analyze the eﬀect a dispersed WDM signal at the input. It is showed in ﬁgure
5.3 that a OTDM signal can be generated from a dispersed WDM signal. The performance of the
WDM-to-OTDM system is evaluated for diﬀerent parameters. It was found that the bandwidth
of the ﬁlter at the receiver should be fairly broad for the best performance for both dispersed
and non-dispersed signals. It is found that the duty cycle of the pump should be over 50% for
a constant performance. It is found that the channels should have a spacing of 50-100 GHz for
the best performance.
At last the BER as a function of the OSNR is found.
Here it is found that the amount of
dispersion on the WDM-signal determines the error ﬂoor. As the pump is held constant and
the signal shifts due to dispersion a decrease in performance is seen for the channels that are
shifted in the modulation window. The conclusions are similar for both 4-channel and 8-channel
systems. The worse performance of the 8 channel system compared to the 4 channel system
stems from the time lens parameters needed to make a WDM-to-OTDM conversion.

Experimental Demonstration
In this chapter it is investigated how well a WDM signal is converted into an OTDM signal. This
is done both by examining eye diagrams, spectra and by BER measurements. It is investigated
how the time lens system for WDM-to-OTDM conversion performs if the WDM signal has been
transmitted through standard single mode ﬁber(SMF) before entering the time lens.
As mentioned earlier the time lens is a highly nonlinear ﬁbre (HNLF) used for four wave mixing
(FWM). The generated idler will have a quadratic phase modulation relative to the signal. For
a OFT the idler has to be dispersed according to K = 1/D. In order to quantitatively analyze
OTDM signal a BER measurement is made for diﬀerent scenarios.
First a overview of the experimental setup is given and afterwards the diﬀerent parts are examined
in greater detail.
To see how such a system could perform in real life, the WDM signal is
propagated through 50 km and 100 km SMF. The impact of this initial dispersion on the output
OTDM signal is discussed. Lastly the BER curves are shown and discussed.
6.1
Time Lens system
To achieve the ”ideal” setup shown in ﬁgure 4.1 the setup in ﬁgure 6.1 is build in the lab. In
short it consists of a signal generator producing a WDM signal with a PRBS sequence that is
amplitude modulated to a NRZ format. The WDM signal has a repetition rate of 10 GHz and
four channels. The resulting OTDM signal will be a 40 GHz signal. This is chosen so that the
demultiplexing could be done with an MZI. The pump is generated to have a 80% duty cycle
and a square spectrum and ideally a square pulse shape. The pump and signal are combined
in a dispersion ﬂattened HNLF to achieve FWM and the idler will then have a quadratic phase
shift relative to the signal. This will make a WDM-to-OTDM transformation after propagation
through a SMF. The receiver demultiplexes the OTDM signal to measure the BER for a single
tributary.

OFT

(the idler is placed in the L-band) and a broad band pass ﬁlter. The ﬁlter is placed so the lower
cut-oﬀis between the idler and the pump to remove the remains of the pump.

6.4
Summary
A time lens system was demonstrated for WDM-to-OTDM conversion. The time lens was im-
plemented by FWM. The pump was made by band pass ﬁltering a super continuum spectrum
to the desired width. The pump was not completely ﬂat and this could have had an eﬀect on
the conversion. The FWM process was done in a dispersion ﬂattened HNLF that increased the
spectrum of the FWM. This can be seen as several higher order products in ﬁgure 6.5. If the
signal was dispersed before the time lens the OTDM signal was signiﬁcantly worse. This was
seen in BER measurements of the system. Especially for the channel that was closest to the
pump. The channel with the best performance was not the one furthest from the pump. Instead
it was the second channel(B). A guees on why this channel had a signiﬁcantly better performance
could be that it is the one in time domain that is furthest away from the ”bad” channel (D).
This is only seen in the time domain. The spectrum of the OTDM signal was not changed when
propagation was added before the time lens.

Conclusion
The frequency-to-time conversion by a time lens was described theoretically. These results were
used to deﬁne the WDM-to-OTDM system that was simulated and experimentally tested. In
the simulations the system is assumed to be ideal so that the BER could be evaluated. In the
experiments the time lens was implemented through FWM in a HNLF. A time lens could be
implemented in diﬀerent ways for example by utilizing nonlinear eﬀects in other materials. There
is no discussion on the dispersion which also plays a vital role for the OFT and it is assumed that
only second order dispersion inﬂuences the signal. In the simulations a WDM-to-OTDM system
was demonstrated. To test the performance the BER was evaluated.It was found that the there
is no variation in performance then the duty cycle of the pump is above 50% and even smaller
for the dispersed WDM signal. This support the idea that an un-compensated signal could be
spectrally magniﬁed without synchronizing with the pump. The BER as a function of OSNR
shows the same results as those that are obtained experimentally. By increasing the transmission
length a power penalty is added. When only a single channel is transmitted through the system
it is found experimentally that the BER is similar independent of the transmission length until
the error ﬂoor is reached. This eﬀect is also seen in the simulation but to a smaller degree.
The simulations are done without simulating wavelength conversion and can therefore not be
used to investigate the performance of the channels in the experiments. Here the channel closest
to the pump has the worst performance.
It is showed that even for a highly dispersed input signal a WDM-OTDM conversion can be made.
The performance could (maybe) be improved by amplifying the idler before the demultiplexing.
It is seen in simulations that for even a very dispersed WDM signal a OTDM signal can be
generated.
In the experiments the overlap of the pump and signal is very important (and very unstable). If
the pump overlapped two neighbouring signal symbols it would cause a bit error. If the system
should be used as a part of a spectral magniﬁcation this could be be less inﬂuential on the
performance of the system.
