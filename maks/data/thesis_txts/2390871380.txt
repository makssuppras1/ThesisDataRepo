Abstract 
Venture capitalist’s success is to a large degree determined by their ability to invest in the right ventures. In 
deciding which ones to invest in, ventures are filtered through an investment process where they are assessed 
based on investment criteria, and due diligences are conducted on them. Numerous factors such as the 
market, product, exit-potential and technology, will influence the decision-making. One factor is however 
given more weight than the others: The management team of the new venture. Yet, little is understood of why 
the team is so important, what influences that, and how it can be assessed. 
 
This study investigated venture capitalist decision-making with emphasis on the assessment of the 
management team of a potential investment. This was done by interviewing nine venture capitalists, 
representing 11 different venture firms in Denmark. All participants had decision-making power and invested 
in technology ventures. Given literature scarcity and the complexity of the topic, the research was conducted 
using a qualitative exploratory method, aiming at guiding more generalizable quantitative and qualitative 
studies in the future.  
 
The author suggests updating the current investment process used in literature to: i) Origination, ii) screening, 
iii) pre-analysis, iv) negotiation, v) due diligence and vi) deal closure. In addition, updating the categorization 
of investment criteria to: Team, product, market, business, exit and venture capitalist specifics. This research 
found that the management team is the most important factor in assessing a new venture because: i) All other 
factors are hygiene factors, that just needs to pass a certain threshold, ii) the team influence all other factors, 
that can be considered static, iii) the future is unpredictable and the team is the one adopting the venture to 
it, and iv) because of the intangibility and complexity of people, it is the most difficult to assess. This research 
also found that there has been a recent shift in the industry towards giving more weight to the team. That can 
be explained by its origination from private equity, investor assessment preferences and a change in historical 
successes. 
 
It was found that venture capitalists look at numerous human capital attributes, but that six was of significance 
importance: i) motivated, ii) entrepreneurial, iii) selling personality), iv) stamina, v) coachable and vi) integrity. 
Given that it is contingent of each venture case, it is not possible to prioritize attributes. In relation to how the 
management team can be assessed, two holistic ways of thinking was proposed; static or moving methods. 
An elaboration of specific methods is given in the report. Three factors were found to influence the resources 
spent and the weight given to the team: i) Investment strategy, ii) investment phase and iii) industry.  
 
Given the sample size of n = 9, and the exploratory nature of this research, the findings are limited in their 
generalizability. Especially in relation to the management team, findings are considered less robust than those 
of investment process, criteria and due diligence. This research has however discovered several interesting 
topics for future studies and provided a foundation for designing them. This includes homogenizing human 
capital attributes, modelling influences on management team assessment to guide better utilization of venture 
capital resources, and distinguishing between moving and static methods.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Background and Motivation 
Venture Capital (VC) is an industry built on making risky investments with the potential upside of large 
returns. Some of the most successful companies existing today, is here thanks to VCs willingness to take 
risk. For example: Apple, Google, Amazon, LinkedIn, Tesla and eBay. These historical successes and an 
increase in capital under management, has turned the public eye towards VC and increased the interest 
of academic scholars. One area of research focus on a fundamental element for VCs to be successful; 
how they are making investment decisions. Why invest in some new ventures and not others? This 
involves understanding the VCs investment process, investment criteria and the due diligence made on 
potential portfolio ventures.  
 
The management team is considered to be the most important factor when assessing a potential 
portfolio company (Macmillan, Siegel, & Narasimha, 1985). Arthur Rock, often considered the 
grandfather of VC, stated that “Nearly every mistake I have ever made has been in picking the wrong 
people, not the wrong idea” (W.D. Bygrave & Timmons, 1994). Even with the focus on VC and the 
importance of the management team, little progress has been made in this field. The literature is not 
clear on why the team is so important and what factors influence that importance. 
 
As confirmed by a well-recognised figure in the industry, Don Valentine, the management team is also 
one of the most difficult factors to assess: “I think choosing great people is much more difficult than 
picking great markets because we have always understood the technology and understood the markets. 
Picking great people is a less than 50 percent proposition, and if you are right 52% of the time, they 
ought to build a statue to you” (Stanford Graduate School of Business, 2017). Despite this challenge, 
how venture capitalists can assess a management team is under-researched: “Venture capitalists can 
use theories from marketing and strategy to guide them on product and market factors in the due 
diligence. They have theories from economics to guide on the financial aspect. However, there are no 
theories or models to guide them in assessment of human capital” (Smart, 1999). 
 
This exploratory study aims at taking a first step in filling the literature gaps, and provide future 
researchers with topics to study and a foundation for doing so. Ultimately, this research could lead to 
better decision-making in VC. This in return would mean that the scarce risk-capital can be allocated to 
those ventures with the greatest likelihood of survival and biggest potential for societal value creation.

1.2. Research Problem and Objectives 
In understanding what led to a decision, it is not enough to know only one aspect of it (Fried & Hisrich, 
1994). The assessment of the management team should be seen in the context of decision-making. Prior 
to investment, a new venture will go through the VCs investment process. Here the new venture will be 
assessed, and possibly rejected, based on the VCs investment criteria. If the new venture gets far 
enough in the process, it will be subjected to a due diligence conducted by the VC. 
 
Several researchers has investigating the steps in the VCs investment process, but they are not 
consistent in their findings; different researchers have found different steps (for example: (Albers, 2006) 
versus (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984)). There is a significant body of literature on investment criteria as VCs 
success in selecting ventures has been credited to them (Hall & Hofer, 1993). However, the research  
might be outdated, with the newest being 14 years old. In addition, there is differences between the 
investment criteria used by VCs in the US, Canada, Asia Pacific and Europe (Knight, 1994), yet the 
majority has been conducted in the US. 
 
Given the limitations of the current research, and the importance of understanding the VCs decision-
making, this research will first investigate the investment process, investment criteria and due diligence. 
The goal is to compare literature with data on the sample population, and establish models that 
accurately represent them. 
 
(1) What is the investment process, the criteria and the due diligences used by venture capitalists? 
 
Before going into the specifics of the management team assessment, this research will investigate its 
importance as a factor in the decision-making. This by exploring the literature as well as by analysing 
primary data. The goal being to put the management team assessment into the context of the overall 
decision-making process. 
 
(2a) Why is the team perceived to be the most important factor in the assessment of a new venture? 
 
As stated in the introduction, current research provides little insight into how VCs can assess the 
management team of a potential investment. Only a single paper (Smart, 1999) has been published 
looking specifically into it; that is now 18 years old. Moreover, it is unclear if it accurately represents the 
==PAGE:12==

methods used in the VC industry as the author of the 1999 paper is a psychologist, and only applied 
methods from his own field. Hence, the following research question: 
 
(2b) How can venture capitalists evaluate the management team of a potential investment? 
 
Not only exploring how, this study will also investigate what VCs are looking for. The what is in the 
literature most-often referred to as human capital; the personal attributes leading to behaviour valued 
by an organization (Smart, 1999).  
 
(2c) What human capital attributes are venture capitalists looking for in the management team? 
 
Lastly, this thesis will investigate what factors influence the management team assessment. Why does 
some VCs only assess the team based on a few meetings, while others spend hours together with them

of this author, no such investigation has been conducted before. 
 
(2d) What influences the management team assessment? 
 
It is hoped that the findings from this research will have implications for both VCs and entrepreneurs 
alike. For VCs, this report could provide an overview of the different methods, and give insights to other 
VCs in Denmark. For entrepreneurs, this report could lower information asymmetries and thereby 
increase the chances of getting funded, and decrease the VCs cost that otherwise will be discounted in 
their funding.

2.1. Sources of Capital for New Ventures 
External financing of new ventures can happen through equity financing, debt financing or grants. In 
equity financing, equity is given in return for capital. In debt financing, the equity owners will have to 
pay back the capital within a limited timeframe. In grant financing, private organizations and

Different sources offer different levels of capital, and is therefore suited for different stages in the new

2.2. Fundamentals of Venture Capital 
2.2.1. Venture Capital as a Type of Investment 
VC is an alternative investment form, as compared to the traditional ones; investing in stocks and bonds. 
VC is also a form of private equity, but unlike other types of private equity, such as leveraged buyout 
funds, mezzanine funds, distress funds and growth capital funds, VC funds invest in new ventures with 
little or no proven market and product. Given the uncertainty of investing in new ventures, VC is 
considered a very risky type of investment (Yasuda, 2009). Capital provided through VC is also often 
referred to as risk-capital. 
 
Figure 1 VC as an alternative investment and a private equity fund 
That VC originates from private equity, is also evident in the definitions of VC: “Venture capital is an 
independently managed, dedicated pool of capital that focuses on equity or equity-like investments in 
privately held, high-growth companies” (Hudson & Evans, 2005). Note here the emphasises on high-
growth companies. A growth venture is defined as a business with revenues that growths significantly 
higher than the overall economy. This is predominantly seen in technology ventures (“Growth Company 
- Investopedia,” 2017). The focus on growth is a consequence of investing in new, risky,  ventures.  
 
Given the uncertainty of new ventures, there is a high risk of failure. VCs mitigate this risk by creating 
portfolios of multiple companies, with the goal of having the mega-successes offset the losses from the 
failed ones. This requires that the new venture has the potential for high-growth; or in order words that 
it is highly scalable. In addition to the uncertainty, VCs require high-growth potential because their funds 
are temporary. A VC is under time pressure and must within, what is a typically 10-year lifespan, invest 
all the money in the fund and exit again. (Bob Zider, 1998). 
==PAGE:18==
 
  9 
Another definition of VC is given by the National Venture Association in the US. This definition highlights 
the VCs role as an enabler to build companies, by offering risk-capital: “Venture capital funds build 
companies from the simplest form – perhaps just the entrepreneur and an idea expressed as a business 
plan – to freestanding, mature organizations. Venture capital firms are professional, institutional 
managers of risk capital that enable support for the most innovative and promising companies” 
(National_Venture_Capital_Association, 2016). Note from this definition that unlike other sources of 
capital, such as business angels, VCs are institutional. 
 
VCs act as a financial intermediary between investors and the new ventures in their portfolio. A typical 
VC company is structured so that the investor, called a Limited Partner (LP), invest in the fund managed 
by a General Partner (GP). It is then the GPs job to invest the capital (investment period) in new ventures 
and exit those same new ventures again (harvest period) through either an initial public offering (IPO), 
selling their equity to another private company, other investors or the founders. (Yasuda, 2009). 
 
2.2.2. Stages and Rounds of Funding 
As new venture growths, it goes through several stages. As a general terminology, four broad types of 
stages is used (National_Venture_Capital_Association, 2016). Note that it is no always clear-cut in 
practice when a venture is in one stage or another, and what is done in each stage; it depends on the 
specific circumstances. Here is the scope of each stage as given by the National Venture Capital 
Association in the US: 
 
Seed/Startup Financing 
Small amount of capital to prove the concept and prior any marketing. Often this is the step involving 
angle investors. 
 
Early Stage Financing 
At this stage, the product is in its final development phase and typically only needs final testing and pilot 
production. The company is younger than three years and will typically have conducted marketing 
studies, have the key management in place, developed a business plan and are ready to or already 
started doing business. The networking capabilities of the VC is more important here than the later 
stages. 
 
==PAGE:19==

Expansion (mid) Stage Financing 
Here the company has generated revenue and proven their product on the market, but it does not have 
to be profitable yet. The financing can be used for plant expansion, marketing development or 
improving existing products. The VCs role switch from being supportive to focusing more on helping 
with the strategy of the company. 
 
Later Stage 
The company has reached a somewhat stable growth-rate, and are more likely to be profitable than the 
earlier stages. At this stage, the company starts to consider its exit opportunities. 
 
Given that raising capital can be both expensive and time consuming, the investments happens in 
financing rounds; the first round is called Series A, the next funding round Series B, then Series C and so 
on. Each round should provide enough capital to reach the next big milestone, such as buying a 
competitor or releasing a new product to the market. (National_Venture_Capital_Association, 2016). A 
clear distinction should be made between funding stages and funding rounds.  
 
2.2.3. Multiple-Agent Setting and its Compensation Structure 
There are three agents in the VC set-up; the investors (LPs), the fund managers (GPs) and the 
entrepreneurs. This can cause frictions because of information asymmetries, different timeframes and 
because LPs are not allowed to interfere with how the GPs invest the capital (hence limited partner). It 
is a typical situation where an agent (entrepreneur and VC) has control over the asset by a principal (the 
VC and LP) (Steven N. Kaplan and Per Strömberg, 2001). The VC is an underwriter, as they are an agent 
with multiple relationships. (Younge, 2016). It is imperative to have a compensation structure where 
each stakeholder will not only do what is best for them, but do what would benefit all three 
stakeholders. 
 
The GPs are paid an annual management fee to cover the costs of managing the fund. The industry 
standard is 2,5% but can vary and be as low as 1,5%. The fee can be calculated on the total committed 
capital (most common), net assets value or invested capital. Often the management fees decline in the 
later years of the fund as the investments are being realized (harvested). By keeping the management 
fees relatively low, and only enough to what can be expected to run the fund, the GPs are incentivised 
to make their earnings through the profits of the fund. The GPs gets the profit from the funds through 
so called carried interest, in what is typically an 80/20 structure; the GPs get 20% and the LPs 80%.  
==PAGE:20==
 
  11 
The carried interest is normally calculated by one of three methods; i) deal by deal, ii) aggregated or iii) 
by hurdle rates (also known as priority return). By having the GPs making the bulk of their money 
through carried interest they are incentivised to increase the returns of the fund; aligning the interests 
with the LPs. The GPs often co-contribute to the funds capital with 1% of the total capital. This way the 
GPs have an incentive to as a minimum get their investment in return. (Kaplan, 2000), (Fahlenbraach, 
2016). 
 
2.2.4. Asymmetric Information 
A source of friction from the multiple-agent setting is asymmetric information, or as it is also called; 
information asymmetry. It is a fundamental concept in economics that awarded its authors a Nobel-
prize. In contrast to a world with perfect information were all agents knows the same, information 
asymmetry is a theory on what happens when agents have different information. This leads to several 
problems, including adverse selection and moral hazard. (Quy-Toan Do, 2003). Two problems there are 
immensely important to understand in the context of VC and entrepreneurial finance in general.  
 
In adverse selection, one agent has more information than the other one. Given this, a market will 
rationally undervalue some good ideas and overvalue some bad ideas. This is leading to what is most 
often called a market for lemons, from the monumental 1970 “The Market for Lemons: Quality 
Uncertainty and the Market Mechanism” paper by George Akerlof. This paper investigated the second-
hand car market, where the buyers don’t know the real quality of the cars, and therefor are willing to 
pay the average of all the cars. At the same time, the sellers know the quality and sell both bad and 
good quality cars. They don’t want to sell the good cars at an average price, leading them to withdraw 
the higher quality cars from the market. This in return lowers the average price of the cars in the market. 
In the end, one is left with only lemons; bad-quality cars. (Younge, 2016). This same problem has been 
observed in the market for new ventures (Cumming, 2006).  
 
If a VC only finances using common equity (so not one of the different preferred stocks types or other 
downside protection methods (Yasuda, 2009)), new ventures with lower expected returns have lower 
opportunity costs from giving up equity, than those a new venture with high expected returns. Since 
the new venture better knows the quality of the venture, then those the buyer, the VC, it can lead to a 
market for lemons (Cumming, 2006).

The moral hazard problem occurs when one agent will take additional risk, if someone else is paying for 
that risk. Here the information asymmetry arises from one agent knowing more about their future 
actions, than those the other one. When a new venture is getting funded by a VC, that VC then takes on 
some of the risk of the new venture. This might lead the new venture to take on additional risk they 
would not have done otherwise; they have gotten an incentive to act opportunistically. (Wang & Zhou, 
2003) 
 
Numerous solutions has been given and investigated in the literature to the problems from information 
asymmetries. Examples include guarantees/warranties, industry standards, consumer protection laws, 
using information intermediates and signalling (Younge, 2016). In the context of VC, the use of term 
sheets (or the binding agreements in the end), syndication and multi-stage financing are all proven ways 
to deal with adverse selection and moral hazard. Staged financing controls the risk and mitigate moral 
hazard (Wang & Zhou, 2003). Syndication is shown to align the agency costs and positively affect venture 
performance (Kamuriwo, Bellavitis, & Hommel, 2015). The term sheet is a contract that can be used to 
align interest, by for example having stated the compensation agreements (Younge, 2016). A deeper 
explanation of the different solutions to information asymmetry is given, starting below.

at different times” (Yasuda, 2009). This means that the entrepreneur will not be given a lump-sum but 
instead multiple payments in milestones. About 19% of investments in the US in the period July 2007 to

ventures and concentrate resources on those ventures with the greatest likelihood of success.

Term Sheets 
A fundamental concept in VC is the use of term sheets. They are used by VCs to signal to the 
entrepreneur that they have the intention to invest and will be used as a starting point for all future 
discussions. The document can contain a great number of different things, including offering terms, 
capitalization tables, the charter (certificate of Incorporation) and all the specific details on the different 
types of shares, liquidation preferences and voting preferences. The term sheet forms the basis of the 
final legal agreement between the VC and the entrepreneur. In some ways, the term sheet is a summary 
for the longer legal document that will be signed when the deal is being made. (Yasuda, 2009).  
 
Having a contract between the VC and the entrepreneur decreases the entrepreneur’s incentive to act 
opportunistically, and thereby decreases the moral hazard problem. For example, if the contract 
dictates the next milestone for the investment trench, it ensures that the entrepreneur will not spent 
the VCs capital going in other directions. 
 
Syndication in VC 
Syndication is when several VCs go together in a funding round. The syndication has a number 
implications for the individual VC: i) It increases the amount of information on any one investment as it 
pools it together (Florida & Smith, 1990), ii) can reduce the cost of doing due diligence , iii) another VC 
will have different knowledge and experience, and thereby increase the diversity of the information 
gathered (Subrahmanyam, 1999), iv) it minimizes biases in the interpretation of the information found 
(Sah, 1986). 
 
In general, syndication reduces risks while keeping the same level of returns; a VC can make better 
investment decisions while still being able to invest the same amount (William D. Bygrave, 1987). 
Furthermore, the actual investments made are superior to those only done by a single VC (Sah, 1986). 
Some researchers also state that the VC selection process becomes more effective the larger the 
number of participants in the investment is (Lockett & Wright, 2001).

2.3. The Venture Investment Scene in Denmark 
In Denmark, there are private VCs, but also public sources of equity financing in the form of the earlier 
mentioned innovation environments. Specifically, there are four innovation environments: Borean 
Innovation, Capnova, Syddansk Innovation and Pre-seed Innovation. They provide the capital necessary 
to prove the venture and move it on to private investors (Preeseesinnovation, 2017). The capital 
includes equity financing and loan guarantees (DVCA_Danish_Venture_Capital, 2015). 
 
The past two years in Denmark, there has been 107 completed venture deals with total investments of 
1,2 Billion DKK. Of these, the biggest industries were: IT 41%, B2B 33% and healthcare 18%. The most 
active investors in Denmark are: i) SEED Capital Denmark, ii) Vaekstfonden, iii) Sunstone Capital, iv) Novo 
and v) NorthCap Partners. (PitchBookNews, 2017) 
 
The VC scene is very different in Denmark as compared to the US. In 2016, the institutional funds in 
Denmark managed 15 billion DKK. In contrast, in 2015 in the US there was 798 funds with circa 1200 
billion DKK under management. (National_Venture_Capital_Association, 2016) (Vaekstfonden, 2016). 
Not only in the size of funding differs Denmark from to the US, but also how the VCs operate.  
 
A qualitative study investigated what the differences between VC investing in California and Scandinavia 
are. They found that. i) Deal flow is of a higher quality in US; especially in terms of the management 
team, ii) Scandinavian VCs has less technical expertise, iii) Scandinavian VCs tend to outsource parts of 
the due diligence, iv) US VCs experimented with the deals and worked closely with management, v) US 
VCs had more operational experience from running technology ventures, vi) US VCs prioritized a good 
working relationship with the venture higher, vii) USs VCs met more frequently with the team, viii) 
Scandinavian VCs focused heavily on exploring exit strategies early on, ix) US VCs had close relationship 
with potential industrial buyers whereas Scandinavian VCs relied more on investment or overseas 
bankers. (Berglund, 2011).

3. Methodology 
3.1. Research Design and Strategy 
In general, two broad sets of research strategies exist; qualitative and quantitative. Quantitative 
research is to answer the research question through numerical data. The data is analysed using statistics 
and the results can often be generalized to a larger population. Qualitative research is most often used 
to create theories and emphasizes how individuals interpret their social world. Furthermore, qualitative 
research view social reality as continuously shifting emergent property of individuals creation. (Bryman 
& Bell, 2015). 
 
Given that management assessment in VC is under-researched and complex, an exploratory qualitative 
research methodology was chosen. This methodology is used when little or nothing is known about the 
phenomenon in focus (Singleton & Straits, 2010). In exploratory research, a researcher describes the 
phenomenon through observations and interviews. Once the phenomenon is better comprehended, it 
is possible to design better quantitative studies. 
 
Another reason for using qualitative exploratory research is the authors limited knowledge within the 
subject, while at the same time the current literature is lacking and/or not consistent. Therefore, it 
would not have been fruitful to try and design hypothesis and test them with data. Furthermore, 
research question (2b) is a how question, which qualitative research is well-suited at addressing (Pratt, 
2009). Finally, obtaining quantitative data on VC and their investments is extremely hard (Norton, 
Tenenbaum, & Bernard H., 1992). 
 
Testing and confirming hypotheses is used in deductive research, and are often quantitative. Instead, 
the exploratory research in this paper, use an inductive process to describe a phenomenon (Pope, 
Ziebland, & Mays, 2000). This research will, based on observations, find patterns and then form the 
basis for hypothesis building that can lead to general theories.

3.2. Data Collection and Analysis 
The sample population was chosen non-random because of the hard-to-reach participants. As is typical 
for studies on VC, the sampling was instead done at convenience: “A convenience sample has been the 
most common sampling strategy in the field of venture capital because of the challenge of accessing the 
population” (Smart, 1999). Using a probability sampling would also have been infeasible due to the low 
number in the population in general. 
 
The sampling population was chosen based on the following criteria: i) Institutionalised investors, ii) 
providing risk-capital in exchange for equity in the firm, iii) investing in tech companies, and iv) people 
with decision-making power. In addition, to decrease the resource constrains on the individual VCs, only 
one person from each VC was interviewed. Note that for one VC, two persons was interviewed, as they 
both changed job during the time of the research. Given the low number of actual VCs in Denmark, the 
study also expanded to the innovation environments. 
 
Reaching the population was done strategically because of their seniority, and because they are in an 
industry with extreme time scarcity. The strategy was to first reach participants through personal 
networks or friends-of-friends. Next, these first participants were used when cold-contacting other VCs. 
The assumption here being, that if other respected persons in the industry has participated in this study, 
the likelihood of others participating, is higher. As an example, a managing director in a well-known VC 
was reached by having someone knowing him closely in own personal network. This person was then 
always mentioned in-emails sent to others as having been a participant. 
 
Another strategy, also used by other researchers of VC, was the snowball-effect. Having researched 
both the company and the interviewee thoroughly prior to the interview, it was possible to ask for 
contacts in VCs with whom they had done syndications. Finally, importance was put on writing longer 
individual and personal mails when contacting the VCs to increase the likelihood of them responding. 
 
13 VCs was contacted and of those nine decided to participate (note that one more could have 
participated, but responded to late in the process to be included). Three persons in the sample group 
changed company during the research period, resulting in an even larger number of different VCs 
covered in the research. Having a ≈77% response rate is high for studies in VC. A total number n=9 in 
the research can be considered to be normal in the research on VC: “Many studies with actual venture 
capital firms in their sample have between 5 and 20 firms in the sample” (Smart, 1999).

3.3. Reliability and Validity 
Bryman and Bell define three criteria to judge the quality of research: i) Reliability, ii) replication and iii) 
validity (Bryman & Bell, 2015). Reliability defines whether results are repeatable and thereby assess the 
consistency in the measures used. Replication, is the replication of the study by other researchers. 
Validity is the integrity of the results of the research. 
 
Bryman and Bell, divide reliability into external and internal reliability. The internal reliability is the 
consistency of observations between researchers. That is not a problem in this research, conducted by 
only a single person. The external reliability is the degree to which it can be replicated. As also in 
accordance with Bryman and Bell, this research is a qualitative study and thereby external reliability is 
challenging. (Bryman & Bell, 2015). 
 
Like reliability, validity can also be divided into the sub-concepts of external and internal. With one 
researcher, the internal validity of the match between theoretical ideas can be ignored. The external 
validity of this study, and thereby the generalizability of findings across settings, is problematic; the 
research is done on specific Danish VCs and there are in general large differences between VCs. 
 
Another critique of the validity of this research, is in its subjectivity. Given the exploratory nature of the 
research and the small population, it was not possible to make meaningful frequency analysis or other 
more quantifiable analysis on the qualitative data. The findings are there for influenced by the biases 
and world views of the author. It is something to be aware of and will be further discussed in the 
limitations section.

4. Literature Review 
4.1. Venture Capital Decision-Making 
One stream of venture capital decision-making literature is focused on the investment process with 
heavy emphasis on investment criteria (A. Zacharakis, 2010). The investment process refers to the 
different steps the VC goes through; from hearing about the venture up until deciding to invest or not. 
The investment criteria are those the VC use to judge whether he will invest in the new venture or not. 
Both will be further discussed later in this section.  
 
In understanding VC decision-making, it is also important to recognise that VCs use mental models, 
which affect the investment process and criteria. As decision-makers become more experienced, they 
learn which factors to look for in assessing whether a new venture will be successful or not (Shepherd, 
Zacharakis, & Baron, 2003). The VC creates mental models that can be adapted accordingly to the new 
venture in focus. If the investor cannot directly use experience, he or she uses an evaluation strategy to 
construct a new mental model. From the perspective of information processing theory (see next sub-
section), the investor determines what information that will be attended to (investigated) and what 
information that only will be interpreted based on experience (A. L. Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2001). The 
result of which is, that there will be differences between the things investors will investigate, as there 
are differences between their experiences. 
 
Prior moving into the specifics of VC decision-making, a general comment on VC decision-making made 
by several scholars is that the process is very unscientific (Cope, Cave, & Eccles, 2004). Because VCs 
operate in an information-rich environment with high uncertainty and extreme time constrains, they 
have a highly constrained information processing capacity (Shepherd et al., 2003). The result of which 
is that they make their decisions in a more automatic manner as appose to a more conscious, step by 
step systematic process (Logan, 1990). Making decisions in this unstructured way leads to the VCs not 
always understanding their own decision-making process (Hudson & Evans, 2005). This also makes 
research into this field extremely difficult, as getting unbiased objective data is almost impossible. This 
also applies for this research and its implications will be further discussed in 6.3. Limitations.

4.1.1. The Investment Process 
In general, the investment process is designed to reduce the risk of adverse selection (Fried & Hisrich, 
1994). Through the process ventures are filtered out, but for those there are not, more and more data 
is gathered and information asymmetries reduced.  The investment process is sometimes in the 
literature connected with information processing theory (IPT). In IPT a decision is based on what attracts 
the managers attention, how the information is interpreted and what actions that follows based on that 
decision (Barr et al., 1992). This theory can be applied to the VCs investments process (Shepherd et al., 
2003). 
 
What the exact steps in the investment process is, differs in the literature. Some of these are: i) 
Screening, preliminary due diligence, term sheet, final due diligence and closing (Yasuda, 2009), ii) 
origination, VC firm-specific screening, generic screening, first-phase evaluation, second-phase 
evaluation and closing (Fried & Hisrich, 1994), iii) deal origination, screening, evaluation and structuring 
(Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984), iv) quick review, longer review, due diligence, negotiations (Albers, 2006), v) 
origination, screening, evaluation and negotiation (A. Zacharakis, 2010). It is unclear from the literature 
which process most accurately represent actuality, and as a result, assuming the newest is the most up-
to-date, the one from Andrew Zacharakis is chosen for this study. This process is coupled with the IPT 
and graphically illustrated in figure 5. A deepening of each step is presented afterwards.

Origination 
Origination, or as it is also referred to as; the deal flow, is the potential ventures that VCs screens as 
they look for the best investment opportunities (Manigart et al., 2006). Origination is critical for being 
a successful VC, as more higher-quality deals increases the likelihood of higher-return deals 
(Fahlenbraach, 2016). The source of a potential venture affect resource allocation, and new ventures 
referred from respected sources will result in the VC spending additional resources in assessment 
(Shane & Cable, 2002). The sources can be investment bankers, managers from the ventures already in 
the VCs portfolio and consultants that worked with the VC in the past (Fried & Hisrich, 1994). As referred 
deals are more likely to go through the first screening, and the ones referring better understand the VC 
preferences in investing, the VC spend significant resources on developing a personal network (Fried & 
Hisrich, 1994). 
 
Screening 
At this stage, the VC decides if it is worth spending additional time on evaluating the company and move 
it to the next stage. This decision is often made quickly, as proven by one paper finding that VCs on 
average spent less than 21 min per case in the screening stage (Hall & Hofer, 1993). As shown earlier, 
there are different proposals in the literature on the exact steps in the investment process. Some 
distinguish between VC firm-specific screening (what the firm-specific criteria are, is shown in the later 
chapter on criteria) and generic screening. The first screening filters the ventures that clearly does not 
fit the VC, and in the next screening, more time will be allocated to assess the ventures potential based 
on the generic criteria (Fried & Hisrich, 1994). 
 
Evaluation (Due Diligence) 
In this stage, VCs thoroughly investigates the company they are considering to invest in. This includes 
collecting information from a multitude of sources, and in an iterative process, interpret information 
and take new investigative actions based on it. (A. Zacharakis, 2010). More on the due diligence stage 
can be found in section 4.1.3 Investment Due Diligence. 
 
Negotiation 
In the final stage, the VC and the entrepreneur enters negotiations. Here the specific terms are outlined 
with considerations of: Company valuation, contract provisions against agency risks, staging of future 
rounds, and board representation (Sahlman, 1990).

4.1.2. Investment Criteria 
When investors (not only VCs) make decisions to invest in a venture or not they use criteria to evaluate 
the venture. What the investment criteria are, depends on who the investor is (see chapter 1. 
Fundamentals). For example friends and family will make investments based on personal relationships 
and trust criteria, banks will do so based on credit ratings and collateral criteria, public funding based 
on the number of jobs created criteria and business angels based on market knowledge on the 
entrepreneur criteria (Mason & Stark, 2004). VCs also use their own investment criteria based on 
financial returns, market, technology etc. 
 
The importance of understanding the VCs investment criteria should not be understated, as VCs success 
in selecting ventures has been credited to them (Hall & Hofer, 1993) (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984). 
Therefore, most research on VCs decision-making process has also been on what the investment criteria 
are, and what the relative importance of each one is. The research started in the early days of VC, with 
some being as old as from 1974, and has continued up until today, with some of the newest papers 
from 2003. These studies have been conducted by either personal interviews, questionnaires or a 
combination of both. The sample sizes have been anywhere from one person and up to a hundred. 
These low sample numbers is a general problem in the current research on VC, as it leads to the problem 
of “believing in small numbers”; the chances of statistical outliers is much greater (Amos Tversky & 
Daniel Kahneman, 1974). This problem also holds true for this research and will be discussed later in 
chapter 6.3 Limitations.

An important dimension when discussing criteria, is that research suggest that VCs use different criteria

are compensatory and a low score can be offset by another high score. (Hudson & Evans, 2005). 
 
4.1.3. Investment Due Diligence 
When the venture in scope reaches the due diligence phase, they have already gone through initial 
screening and the VC believe in its potential. The focus is then to validate the claims made by the 
entrepreneur and ensure it is worth investing in (Steven N. Kaplan and Per Strömberg, 2001). Using the 
idea of asymmetric information, the VC tries to reduce the information gap; consequently the level of 
due diligence conducted depends on the cost of reducing information asymmetries (Harvey & Lusch, 
1995). The due diligence becomes a cost/benefit trade-off between resources invested and reducing 
adverse selection (Sah, 1986).

Another, yet linked, way to perceive the due diligence is the idea of risk. Just as VCs are trying to 
decrease information asymmetry, they are trying to decrease risk. Several scholars has taken this idea 
and proposed the specific types of risks. (Steven N. Kaplan and Per Strömberg, 2001) categorized risks 
in terms of i) internal risks (entrepreneur’s human capital), ii) external risks (market acceptance, 
competitive reaction, etc.), and iii) execution risks (development and implementation of strategy). In 
two different studies conducted by Macmillian et al. (1985 & 1987), they also categorized their findings 
in terms of risks. In the study (Macmillan et al., 1987) they found the following risks that needs to be 
managed: i) Management risk, ii) investment risk, iii) inexperience risk, iv) cash out risk, v) viability risk. 
In their other study (Macmillan et al., 1987) they categorized risks as: i) Risk of losing the entire 
investment, ii) risk of being unable to bail if necessary, iii) risk of failure to implement the venture idea, 
iv) competitive risk, v) risk of management failure, vi) risk of leadership failure. 
 
Whatever lens in which to see due diligence through, in general, a due diligence is both looking a 
tangible assets such as machines and patens, and intangible assets such as culture and leadership 
(Harvey & Lusch, 1995). VCs conducts due diligences on new ventures, meaning that there are more 
intangible assets than tangible ones. The intangible ones are much harder to asses and thereby also 
more costly to do so (Norton et al., 1992). One type of intangible asset is the management team, that 
will be discussed in later chapters. The academic literature provides little insights to what the other

4.2. Management Team Assessment 
The literature on evaluating the new venture team has not yet been homogenised in its terminology, 
and it is referred to as: “Human capital valuation”, “senior management assessment”, “founder due 
diligence”, “entrepreneurial capital” or different variations of these. However, they all focus on the VCs 
assessment of top managers of the new venture. For this thesis, the terminology will be defined in the 
following way:  
 
Human capital; defined as being “The propensity of a person or group to perform behaviours that are 
valued by an organization” (Smart, 1999). It is the behaviour of the team seen as optimal by the VC firm 
to create a successful new venture. The concept will be explained further in the next section. 
 
Management team; defined here as the top management with decision-making power in the new 
venture. In order words, it is the C-level team (CEO, CTO, COO, etc.). This could be the original founders, 
but it could also be any later hires. However, given that this research mostly is done on earlier stages of 
investments, it will most likely be the founders. 
 
Management due diligence; defined as the assessment or valuation that the VC makes on the new 
venture in scope. A distinction will be made between management team due diligence and management 
team assessment. When using the term due diligence, it is referred to what happens specifically in the 
due diligence phase of the investment process. Management team assessment refers to evaluation of 
the team happening from day 1, and until an investment decision has been made. This includes(!) the 
due diligence of the team. In this paper, management team assessment is considered the same as 
human capital valuation. 
 
4.2.1. Importance of the Management Team 
The most important factor to look for is also the factor that best predicts the success of a new venture 
in the future. Researchers have tried to answer this question in several ways: indirectly by investigating 
criteria (Macmillan et al., 1985), others have tried directly measuring the effects of different factors 
(Cooper, Gimeno-Gascon, & Woo, 1994), others again have recorded retrospective ratings of the factors 
used by VCs (Macmillan et al., 1987). Whatever the method, it is generally the believe that the 
entrepreneur or team that is the most important factor.

4.2.2. Human Capital Attributes 
The concept of human capital originates from the economist Gary Becker that earned a Nobel prize for 
his work (Smart, 1999). The concept, is in its core that “…the idea that people possess potentials that 
manifest themselves in behaviours that have economic value to organizations”. Gary Becker states that 
there are two dimensions of human capital: knowledge and skills. This being the specific information 
that can be applied to perform the tasks, and the competencies needed to do so. In adapting this 
concept to VC, Georffrey H. Smart added the dimension of motivation, to better categorize findings by 
other papers. For example, the study by Macmillan et al. found that the most important factor was “the 
entrepreneur’s capability of sustaining intense effort”. This is neither knowledge or skills, and hence the 
need for the motivation dimension. These dimensions are also used by other scholars under the 
concepts of intellectual or entrepreneurial capital. 
 
The author of an article in the Sloan Management Review argues that intellectual capital is the 
multiplication of competence and commitment. This implies that both is needed to have a high 
intellectual capital (Dave, 1998). This concept has been directly transferred to entrepreneurial capital 
by Truls Erikson (Erikson, 2002). Truls defines entrepreneurial competence as “combined capacity to 
identify and pursue opportunities, and to obtain and coordinate resource”. Entrepreneurial commitment 
is defined as “reflects the capacity to see ventures through to fruition”. The competence dimension is 
comparable with the skills dimension proposed by Gary Becker in human capital. 
 
A final way to distinguish human capital used by some scholars, is generic and specific human capital. 
The generic human capital is the general knowledge acquired through education and experience. 
Specific human capital is the skills that can be directly deployed to the new venture, such as industry-
specific knowledge and how to manage it (entrepreneur-specific knowledge) (Colombo & Grilli, 2005).  
Within human capital, there exist numerous characteristics or attributes. These are the specifics of the 
human capital and includes both knowledge and skills. There has been research specifically trying to 
identify the attributes that VCs are looking for, but also indirectly there is evidence from the literature. 
The attributes have however not been homogenised in their terminology, and it is possible to create a 
list with hundreds of specific attributes. In the table below, the author of this paper provides an 
overview of some of the better-known studies and the attributes they have found:

The immense number of specific attributes makes the research challenging and hard to compare across 
studies. This could also help to explain why an important question is still under-researched by the 
literature; of all these different human capital attributes, which ones are the most important? There are 
indications in the literature from for example Ian C. Macmillan el al (1985), where some criteria can be 
interpreted as attributes. However, only one study was found to have looked at it specifically (Colombo 
& Grilli, 2005). Besides the number of attributes, another explanation can be that each new venture 
case is different. That makes it unfeasible, both theoretically and empirically, to identify a generic set of 
attributes that can define the successful against unsuccessful entrepreneurs (Sandberg & Hofer, 1987).  
 
The author of this paper argues that parallels can be drawn to contingency theory, which state that 
there is no best way to organize a corporation (Wikipedia (English), 2017). The optimal thing to do is 
depended (contingent) on the internal and external situation; hence, the frequent use of “It depends on 
the situation”. The same is true about what the most important human capital attributes in the venture 
is; it depends on the specific situation, which is influenced by a multitude of factors (market, products, 
people etc.). 
 
As stated before, some research provides insights to the importance of different attributes. One paper, 
found that in general, young companies are having a greater need for specific knowledge than general 
managerial talent (Kaplan et al., 2012). Furthermore, they found that the personal attribute of being 
proactive is positively associated with performance, whereas teamwork is negatively correlated. The 
related attributes of being able to communicate and coordinate was found in another study to be of 
little importance in VC-backed companies (Bolton, Brunnermeier, & Veldkamp, 2008). The explanation 
offered being that these companies is so small that they require less coordination. Another study, 
mentioned before, specifically tried to empirically find the most important aspects of human capital. 
The study was conducted by Massimo G. Colombo and Luca Grilli on a sample of 506 young Italian 
companies in the high-tech industry and analysed the effects of human capital on the growth of the 
companies. They found that “Similar prior work experience in the same industry of the new firm is 
positively associated with growth, while prior experience in other industries are not” (Colombo & Grilli, 
2005). In addition to this, they found that it was specifically prior technical work experience of founders 
as opposed to their commercial work experience that predicted growth. Lastly, they found that high-
growth companies also had founding team members with prior entrepreneurial experience.

4.2.3. Methods for Management Team Assessment 
The only empirical study specifically on management assessment methods in VC that exists, to the best 
of the knowledge of this author, is the one by Geoffrey H Smart published in 1998. The research was on 
86 cases and used theories from both psychology and economics. In general, he found that: “There are 
vast differences in the way which venture capitalists conduct human capital evaluations”. This was also 
evident from the data’s high standard deviations; for example, work sample ranged from 4 to 290 hours. 
 
Geoffrey H Smart used five different methods of assessing management in his research, taken from the 
field of psychology; i) job analysis, ii) document analysis, iii) past-oriented interviews, iv) reference 
interviews and v) work sample discussions. Underneath is a further description of the different methods 
where other relevant literature has been included. 
 
Job Analysis 
The job analysis is figuring out what human capital is needed to succeed and is conducted prior to 
interacting with the candidate (Pulakos, Whitney, & Smith, 1996). In the context of VC, the VC would 
consider what human capital the management team should have for their specific venture to become 
successful. 
 
Documentation Analysis 
This is the collection and review of written material on the management team members such as legal 
documents, tax forms, publication and resumes (Smart, 1999). In another study they commented the 
following on documentation analysis: “Perhaps documentation analysis is more useful as a discriminator 
in the earlier phases of venture screening processes.” (Erikson & Nerdrum, 2001). 
 
An important concept to understand in relation to the usefulness of documentation analysis is signalling 
theory from the field of economics. Fundamentally, the entrepreneur (agent) can try to decrease the 
information asymmetries using signals to the VC (principal) (Younge, 2016). A signal shows the VC some 
specific attributes about the person, that the VC sees as beneficial (positively shifts the conditional 
probability distribution for the VC). A signal could be having a technical education from a prestigious 
university. This, from the perspective of the VC, lowers the information asymmetry and the probability 
that, that person can successfully develop the technical product in the new venture increases. 
 
==PAGE:52==
 
  43 
Past-oriented Interviews 
In past-oriented interviews, VCs interview members of the management team about their past, such as 
prior jobs or ventures. The motivation for this type of interview is that past performance can be a 
predictor of future performance (Smart, 1999). In doing past-oriented interviews, one should clearly 
distinguish between two interview formats; structured and unstructured interviews. The research 
consistently finds that structured interviews are much better predictors than are unstructured, as 
proven by a meta-study by Weisner and Cronshaw that found the mean validity coefficients for 
structured interviews where over three times as high as unstructured interviews (Wiesner & Cronshaw, 
1988). 
 
Reference Interviews 
Reference interviews is interviews with people whom have seen the persons on-the-job. References 
could be; co-workers, personal references, supervisors and managers, lawyers, bankers, other investors 
etc. (Smart, 1999) 
 
Work Sample Discussion 
In the context of VC, one describe work sample discussions as “…sessions in which venture capitalists 
quizzes the target managers on various aspects of the business” (Smart, 1999). In the work sample 
discussion, the VC tries to assess what actions a manager would take in different scenarios.

5.1. Innovation Environments 
This section expands on the desk-top research conducted in 2.3. The Venture Investment Scene in 
Denmark with primary data from two interviews, to determine if it will significantly influence the 
findings. 
 
The innovation environments invest pre-seed and are therefore the earliest investors of all VCs 
participating in this research. One investment manager put it as “We are basically investing in a power-
point deck”. They are overlapping the space of other early investors and positioned to bridge the gap 
between the earliest funding methods and VC. Because of the immaturity of the ventures they are 
investing in, emphasis is on development over commercial competencies. That innovation 
environments are early investors is also evident from their aim of taking a high equity stake of 25%. 
 
A major difference between innovation environments and a normal VC, is that the capital is raised on a 
year-by-year basis instead of in a 10-year fund. The capital is allocated in the Danish governments annual 
budgets, and the innovation environments are given a mandate to invest it on their behalf. The capital 
amount of given to each of the four innovation environments is determined by past performance 
through an allocation key. The innovation environments are measured on both soft and hard 
parameters, with the most important ones being: i) Number of projects invested in, ii) how much 
external capital is attracted and iii) financial returns. 
 
Another major difference, is that the goal is not purely financial returns, but also serving society through 
job creation and by offering high-risk-capital. One investment manager elaborated: “We bring projects 
to a place where it is interesting for others”. If a project has VC potential, they will get in contact with 
potential VCs early on and determine the steps needed to get it there. For example, if a VC wants better 
proof-of-business, the innovation environment will push for it.  Exits to VCs is however the rarest form 
of exit. Second most is exit to industry and the most common type of exit is to the entrepreneur 
themselves.  
 
The differences highlighted in this section are not perceived to significantly distort the findings. 
Innovation environments still have the same set-up towards the entrepreneurs as normal VCs, and still 
goes through an assessment of the management team.

5.1. Venture Capital Decision-Making 
5.1.1. The Investment Process 
From the literature, the investment process best representing reality, was unclear and the four-step 
process by Andrew Zacharakis (2010) was chosen. There is essentially two ways the data differs: i) An 
extra initial due diligence step and ii) the main due diligence after negotiation. 
 
The initial screening was found to involve, corresponding with Vance H. Fried et al. (1994), first 
conducting a firm-specific screening and then a generic screening. Then the extra due diligence was 
conducted and was by some called a pre-analysis or simply analysis. From the literature, Ayoka Yasuda 
called it preliminary due diligence (Yasuda, 2009) and Vance H. Fried et al. first-phase evaluation (Fried 
& Hisrich, 1994). In this paper, it will be called a pre-analysis. It can encompass initial meetings and pitch 
sessions. If the venture is then believed attractive, it will not move on to the evaluation (due diligence) 
as found in the literature review, but instead to an initial negotiation. 
 
Because the due diligence entitles a cost in internal labour hours and to external partners, a term sheet 
is signed beforehand. The new venture will pay for the due diligence expenses as part of their 
investment and must therefore agree to it. Moreover, having to sign the term sheet also opens the 
valuation discussion. If the VC and the new venture is miles away from one another, it does not make 
sense to spend resources on due diligence. 
 
Given the differences between the literature and the empirical data, a new generic investment process 
is proposed with the extra step of a pre-analysis and where negotiation is conducted prior to the due 
diligence.  
 
Figure 7 Proposed Updated Model for the Investment Process 
 
Having proposed an updated model, one should note that there still are differences between 
individual VCs. For example, one of the interviewed followed the process proposed by Andrew 
Zacharakis (2010) and had the due diligence before negotiation: “It is rare that a term sheet is signed 
before the due diligence begins… …it allows us to stay more agile”. 
==PAGE:56==
 
  47 
Another finding on investment analysis, is that the percentage of that ends up getting funding is higher 
than the 1-2% found in the literature. This research found that anything from 2-8% ends up getting 
funding. One hypothesis for this difference is the difference between American VCs and Danish VCs, but 
nothing can be confirmed from this research. Another difference to the percentages of filtering was 
that: “Once a startup has gone through the screening and analysis phase, it almost always gets to 
something”. Where the literature found that only 1/3 got through due diligence, reality is closer to 3/4 
or even higher. The due diligence could however still influence the deals terms, including valuation, the 
investment milestones and tranches. 
 
5.1.2. Investment Criteria 
Primary research on the investment criteria showed a reasonable fit with what had been found in the 
literature. All VCs used a multi-phase evaluation based on several overarching categories. The specific 
categories were however found to be slightly different from the five synthesized in the literature review 
(team, product/service, market, financial and VC-specific). 
 
First, not a single VC in the research had financials as an explicit category. Instead, almost all VCs had 
exit options or exit possibilities as a category. Second, it was found that a category was lacking. Several 
VCs had criteria that could not be categorized as being either the market or the product, as it is 
somewhat both. One partner elaborated the following on their reasons for this criterion: “It can be that 
the market is there, the customers wants it, the product is there and it is good, but if you do not have a 
cost-effective, fast and scalable way to reach the market, it does not work”. Other interviewees referred 
to this as the money-making part, go-to-market strategy or simply scalability. Consequently, this author 
proposes a new category called business model, business plan or simply business.

5.1.3. Investment Due Diligence  
The VCs in this study looked, in alignment with the literature, at both tangible and intangible assets. 
Checklists was used by all interviewed when conducting due diligence on tangible assets. For example, 
a list item in the financial due diligence was to go through all past yearly reports. In contrast, only two 
of the VCs in this study had checklists for the due diligence of the management team. 
 
In the literature review, Steven N. Kaplan et al (2001) and several others described the due diligence as 
decreasing risk. The findings confirm this, as evident from an internal document shared by one of the 
interviewees. It highlights three reasons for conducting the due diligence: i) “Verify the most important 
information that forms the basis for investing in the company”, ii) “Ensure that there are not internal or 
external factors that to a large degree makes the investment less attractive” and iii) “Get insights to the 
most important areas for creating value in the new venture in the future.”. The second reason is in 
alignment with Steven N. Kaplans et al (2001) categorization of external and internal risk. The third 
reason highlights an important feature of the due diligence, mentioned by several of the interviewed; 
the future value of the due diligence. The due diligence is not only about verification, but also about 
guiding the time post deal signing. 
 
==PAGE:58==
 
  49 
The type of due diligence conducted by the participants is in alignment with the findings from the 
literature. How many different types varied, but it always includes: the team, legal, market/commercial, 
technical and financial 
 
Unintentional, the data from the research provided insights to the outcome possibilities of the due 
diligence: i) Investment can go through, ii) Investment can go through given that there are changes to 
the agreement, iii) Investment can go through given that certain circumstances changes, iv) Investment 
cannot go through. As described in the findings on the investment process, only in a minority of cases 
does the investment not go through. 
 
5.2. Management Team Assessment 
5.2.1. Importance of the Management Team 
This study finds, in alignment with the literature, that the management team assessment is unique 
relative to the other types of assessment, in two ways: “The team pops up as being the most important 
thing. Both because it is the most important, but also because it is the hardest thing to assess”. Another 
finding from this study was that the importance given to the team in the decision-making has increased: 
“It is said that the team gets more and more important, which I totally agree with. It is also the most 
difficult point of all”. This section will present the findings of why this change might have occurred, in 
addition to the findings of why the team is important and hard to assess. 
 
Change in Management Team Due Diligence 
In the past, the team has not been weighted as importantly as many believe it should today. One 
interviewee stated that “In the beginning of when I started in the venture fund, we properly tended to 
underestimate the importance of the team” and another “In the next fund we raise, the team is 
something we will look much more for in the investments”. 
 
That the team historically has been given less weight than it deserves, can partly be explained by VCs 
origin as an industry. Referring to section 2.2.1. Venture Capital as a Type of Investment, VC comes from 
private equity, and hence, has its roots from that. One interviewee commented: “Venture is coming 
from private equity, and that is why they look at teams the way they do. They have taken the due 
diligence framework from private equity”. As the VC industry matured, they started to specialize and 
distinguish themselves further from private equity. One such specialization was adapting methods used 
for evaluating ventures to reflect the special situation of investing in immature, new ventures. 
==PAGE:59==
Findings 
 
 50    
Another, somewhat related explanation, could be that an investor would prefer investing in things they 
can measure. One interviewee commented: “The problem is that just looking at the team is not very 
fact-based, which is the way that most investors assess a new venture”. It is difficult to quantify the 
team, making it more difficult to make investment decisions. Findings from this research is however 
that some players in the industry are trying to combat this problem, and are working on ways to quantify 
the team. It is still in its early-stage, but another proof that the industry is adapting their methods, away 
from private equity, and into becoming more self-standing.  
 
A final reason for the shift, can also be the change in what ventures becomes mega-successes. One 
interviewee stated that “Back then, the success stories was very technology-based. Whereas today, a 
lot of business models where the technology is not great, but it is content, marketplace etc.”. VCs 
invest in ventures that can bring the greatest returns when exited; in the beginning of the industry, 
this was heavy high-tech companies, but with success stories like Facebook, Uber and Snapchat, that 
has changed. A VC commented “One could see that it was not necessarily the technology that 
generated the exit value”. In shifting away from technology, more weight has been given to the team. 
 
Importance 
All interviewees fully, or at least to a large degree, stated that the team is the most important aspect in 
deciding to invest or not. For example, one commented: “I would rather have a good team then a good 
project, than a not-so-good team and a good project”. In this finding, it is important to acknowledge the 
sample group; all invested in software and technology ventures, and almost all were early investors. 
Influencers on the team assessment, is discussed in 5.2.3. Influences on Management Team Assessment. 
 
The research offers several insights to why the team is so important. One explanation is that all other 
factors than the team is, what one interviewee called hygiene factors. A concept that comes from 
Frederick Herzbers motivation-hygiene theory, or as it is also know; two-factor theory. It is a theory of 
what makes people satisfied on their jobs, and in this theory, motivators increase satisfaction and 
hygiene factors dissatisfies. More precisely “Hygiene, in the sense that they are considered maintenance 
factors that are necessary to avoid dissatisfaction, but that by themselves do not provide satisfaction” 
(NetMBA.com, 2017). This theory of course having no relation to VC, it stills gives the concept that the 
hygiene factors are simply necessary for an investment to be made, but by themselves are not 
interesting. 
==PAGE:60==
 
  51 
Considering all but the management team assessment as hygiene factors, can also be induced from the 
research. For example, as one put it: “Of course we look at the team throughout the process, but once 
we get to the point of making a decision we only look at the team. The other things are gates, or entry 
criteria, that have already been fore filled”. The market, competition, product etc. are hygiene factors 
that needs to get over a certain threshold where it gets interesting enough, and then the rest is the 
team. The same interviewee also stated that: “The due diligence besides the management is boring to 
do and just facts, and we do not believe that one should make an investment based on it”. 
 
Another way to explain the importance of the team, is through the idea that all but the team is static 
factors. For example, the market is of a certain size and there are these and these distribution channels. 
What the static values are, depends on the team: “Static variables are depended on the strategy 
decisions of the team members”. The VC went on by giving an example with Uber. In the beginning the 
market  was a high-end limousine service and if one only looked at static values, that market would had 
been too small for most investors. However, if weight was given to the team and how they worked 
together, one might instead see a team that could adapt and find a larger market; a change in the static 
values. 
 
An explanation of the importance of the team, which is linked with the explanation before, comes from 
the unpredictability of the future: “You cannot predict what is going to happen. You cannot predict if 
they will have the competencies for were they need to be in 3-5 years”. The new venture will evolve over 
time, and it is not possible to hire a person only to do one job specifically, as that might change over 
time. The hygiene/static factors might change and the market today can be something completely 
different in 5 years. The team however is constant; they are the ones adapting the new venture to best 
cope with the changes. Hence, the importance of team over other factors. 
 
Finally, it was clear that VCs are struggling with assessing the management team and that this is a reason 
why it is so important. One interviewee commented: “I could argue for that the most important thing is 
a market, but that is easier to assess. It is possible to find out and make a judgement whether it is big 
enough or not”. Because the team cannot be easily assessed from just doing desk-top research, it 
becomes important.

Difficulty in Assessment 
As highlighted earlier, the team is difficult to assess because it is hard to quantify: “All except the team, 
you can basically put into a spreadsheet and measure”. People is “soft values” and for all other 
information than the management team, it is possible to somewhat look at it factual and objectively: 
“The market, competition and so on is something that I can look at today and do so via desk research. 
The information is available and factual, and it can give me a correct current insight”. From this last 
statement, it also becomes evident that the team is difficult to assess because information is not readily 
available; the VC themselves must go out in to the field to collect it. 
 
Referring to 4.2.1 Human Capital Attributes, it is difficult to put an unambiguous label on specific human 
attributes, and even harder to then measure them. One VC put it the following way: “The human psyche 
is very complex”. Also, referring to that previous section, difficulties arises from contingency theory, 
were an ideal team depends on the case. What the specific attributes of the team then is, is difficult to 
determine and depends on the VC, making the assessment more complicated. In contrast, it is easier to 
for example have a minimum requirement for the market size.

5.2.3. Methods for Management Team Assessment 
The literature on the methods used by VCs is lacking and does not represent reality. Specifically, several 
methods found in the literature was not found in the research, whereas other important ones were not 
covered; both will be discussed under their individual headlines. 
 
From the research, it was found to be useful to make a distinction between two different types of 
methods; static or moving. The usability emerges from the different schools of thoughts between VCs, 
and because a new way of looking at methods was needed, as the team assessment did not happen 
through structured interviews as suggested by the literature: “The conversations with the entrepreneurs 
was not so structured, but was more based on all their interaction throughout the process, from they 
come and are pitching, to term sheet negotiations to the due diligence”. Referring to 4.1. Venture Capital 
Decision-Making, there is evidence in the data of mental models as an evaluation strategy. The VC might 
not assess the team in a conscious step-by-step approach, but in a more automatic way. 
==PAGE:63==
Findings

Moving vs Static Methods 
The static methods are those looking at the past or that makes a snapshot of today. The moving methods 
are those assessing how the person and team are doing over a period. Where static methods have no 
time dimension, the moving method does. Some investors believe that there almost only is value from 
the moving methods, while others believe that there is just as much value from the static methods. Why 
some investors believe the moving methods are so important, is an interesting discussion that have not 
existed in the literature so far. 
 
One interviewees viewpoint was that: “What you find out by intuition from working with start-ups is 
that all the past information does not mean anything. What means something, is how the team is 
working together and how good they are at solving problems together”. This VCs emphasis was also 
greatly on the team as a whole and how these people solved problems together: “We have realised that 
as soon as a team works well enough, they will solve problems and change the business. What was a 
problem before, will not be a problem afterwards”. It is not possible to assess the whole team working 
together using static methods, that can only be done through moving methods. It might be possible to 
get an understanding of different person’s competencies, but not how they work together. 
 
Another argument for the importance of the moving variable, goes back to the difficulties in assessing 
people: “You cannot judge the team on a power-point slide. All the team dynamic, how much do they 
believe in it and how far are they willing to go?”. Another interviewee also stated that “You cannot 
interview your way to this, it can only be observed. There is simply no other way to collect that data than 
through observation”. The logic here being that the things you are assessing in the management team, 
are things happening now and excludes looking at past work experience and education. 
 
For the above discussion, it is central to again consider the industry and the stages that the sample 
group invest in. For later stages or more high-tech products, static methods might weight higher than 
they do in early stage software companies.

did not show the characteristics associated with a leader, and they then had a discussion on possible 
alternative roles in the team before making an investment.

Observation 
A core moving method, is that of observation. These observations could happen during internal strategic 
meetings in the new venture, customer meetings and everyday settings in the new ventures office. The 
purpose of the observations was to get a better feel of the team and how they behave on an everyday 
basis, and not only how they say they behave. For example, one interviewee gave the following reason

Interacting with the management team was mentioned by all VCs in the research as a method the assess 
the management team. This interaction served two purposes: i) Assessing the quality of the 
management team and ii) seeing if there is personal chemistry with the investor. The second purpose 
has not been highlighted in the literature, but is an important reason for interacting with the team: 
“Simply spent time with them to see if there is a match, like personally”, “A lot of it was also to see if we 
could see ourselves working together with this team”.

level, all the interactions from day one was used to assess the team. This includes how they respond in

also get to know people. For example, how are they reacting to the negotiation pressure”. These 
interactions could happen over a long period, and for example, one VC typically interacted with a new 
venture over a year. Even though there are many interactions at this lower level, the cumulative time is 
still limited. The latter mentioned VC, spent no more of 10 hours with the new venture even though it 
was over a year. 
 
On a higher level, two ventures in this research interacted with the new venture intensely over several 
months. One VC had an accelerator connected to their venture fund, in which the investment managers 
and partners were part. This allowed them to intensely interact with the management team and use 
the information in their decision to invest or not. Another had a similar period that was not an 
accelerator explicitly, but had strong similarities: “We found that it is difficult to get to know people on 
a few meetings. We get in contact with those we believe in, and then help them with the challenges that 
they have today”. They for example helped them with their strategy or getting a bank loan. Interacting

allows the VC to both observe and interact with the team. 
 
5.2.4. Influences on Management Team Assessment 
In the literature, there was little or no attention given to what effects the management team 
assessment. Effect here understood as what changes the resources allocated to it and its weight relative 
to the other factors. Not understanding these influencing factors could help to explain some of the 
conflicting views in the literature about the importance of the team.  
 
Based on the research and analysis, three different factors that affect VCs assessment of the 
management team has been synthesized: i) Their investment strategy, ii) the investment phase and iii) 
the industry. Note that these things will also effect the other factors in the decisions-making, but no 
research has been conducted as to identify to what degree. 
 
Investment strategy 
Simplified, some investors use the spray-and-pray strategy and invest in numerous companies, while 
others use targeted strategies and only investment in those they truly believe can succeed. In the spray-
and-pray strategy, less resources are used in the assessment as compared to the targeted strategies, 
because the VCs resources are stretched out. The differences in strategies is evident from the number 
of investments made; some made 20 new investments every year and others had made, and was not 
planning on making more, than 10 investments throughout the 10-year lifetime of the fund. Of course, 
these number are greatly depended on the fund size and number of partners in the VC, but still indicates 
the differences in strategy. 
 
Another strategic decision made by one VC influencing the management team assessment, was to 
spend more time with the team to improve the valuation (as seen from the perspective of the VC). The 
VC spend up to three months closely together with the venture in scope, partly for this reason: “Some 
==PAGE:69==
Findings

asked about the period if we are afraid that the evaluation goes up? Instead what we see is that the 
evaluation goes down. All the things they said was true, was not always the full truth”. For example, that 
the one big customer was further out in the future than what they initially thought. This VC used more 
resources on assessing individual ventures, but was rewarded with better valuations. 
 
Investment Phase 
From the research, the author would hypothesize that the biggest influence is the phase being invested 
in, or in other words, the maturity of the company. As this study is still exploratory, this hypothesize was 
not explicitly tested.  
 
In the beginning, a venture is nothing more than the people in it, and consequently they must build 
everything from the ground up. One interviewee gave the following insight: “In the beginning, it is 
mainly a team risk. Why? Because there is nothing else. Everything needs to build. However, as things 
are being build the team risks decreases and to a point where you can change the management team 
without big problems”. When the new venture has become more mature, internal systems has been 
build and people hired to take care of it. The importance of the team in the beginning influences the 
resources spent on it, as the following VC commented: “As all the risk is in the team, then that is also 
where the due diligence should be made”. 
 
There is however a complication between the importance of the team and the resources that can be 
allocated to it: “Given that we only invest early, it is limited how much due diligence that you can make. 
For example, you cannot invest a million if you only invest 4 million in total”. Because the early 
investments are smaller, it limits the resources an investor can put into it. 
 
Based on the above findings it would be possible to model the different relationships. One would be 
that the importance of the venture is inverse correlated with the maturity of the venture; the more 
mature the company gets, the less important is the team going to be.

Validating the models would mean quantifying the different axis and finding ways to then measure it. 
Venture maturity could be post-evaluation of the venture or the investment round. VC resources could 
either be labour hours or financial cost. A difficult thing to measure is the importance of the team, as 
that is going to be a subjective judgement made by the VC. One way to quantify the importance could 
be as a percentage of the total importance of the factors influencing the investment. For example, in a 
seed round the team would be 60%, market 10%, product 10% etc. If one are to avoid the judgement 
of the VC, studies could try and establish the relative importance of the team at different stages of 
venture, taking in to account different markets, customers, industries etc. That could then in return be 
fed in to the models. 
 
Validating the proposed models might not yield results that could dictate how VCs should make 
decisions about resource allocation, because of the uniqueness of each case. The author of this paper 
considers it infeasible to have a general model were variables are plot in, and it gives a reliable result to 
act on. However, these models could help explain phenomenon’s that would give VCs a better 
understanding of how the variables influence each other. This in return would again help to improve 
investment decisions. 
 
The investment stage was found to not only influence the resources allocated for management team 
assessment, but also influencing the specific skills required. One interviewee that invested in the pre-
seed or seed stage commented: “Because we are investing in the development phase, we are looking 
for people that can actually develop. Later is then commercial competencies”. They are investing in 
companies there are so early that they are yet to have a product to sell on the market. Having 
commercial competencies this early would simply be an expensive detriment resource. The maturity of 
the new venture not only influences the types of skills needed, but their depth: “The more the company 
grows, the more specialized the people in it also becomes”. 
 
==PAGE:72==
 
  63 
The importance of different team skills at different stages of the venture would be another thing to try 
and model. Here it could be relevant to use theories such as Larry Greiners model of an organizational 
life cycle with its five cycles: i) Existence, ii) survival, iii) maturity, iv) renewal and v) decline (Greiner, 
1998) and combine that with theories from entrepreneurship. That is however out of scope for this 
project. 
 
Industry 
Referring to 5.2.1 Importance of the Management Team, it was found that the change in the importance 
of the team had changed due to a shift in focus. That shift was a change in industry. 
 
The industry affects where the risk is and thereby how many resources are spent on the management 
team assessment. For example, in the software industry it was more important that the market was 
there: “In IT software we do not invest before there is some market-proof, there needs to be a product 
and there needs to be customers. The investment is then often for scaling the sales”. The risk for medical 
technologies is very different. “The risk-profile in medtech, is that if it works we do not doubt that they 
have a strong value proposition and there is a market. However, the problem is that it is not clear if it 
works”. In the software industry, the technical risk is also lower: “In the software industry, the technical 
risk is not very high. It can be made, might be delayed and some problems with quality, but it can be 
made”. 
 
In industries were the proprietary technology or having access to unique distribution channels is more 
important, the importance of the team decreases. It is still important, but less so than compared to 
software ventures, were technical risk is lower.

6. Discussion 
Research on VC is challenging because of data inaccessibility and complexity. VCs are reluctant to share 
internal data and reaching a significant large population is cumbersome. Complexity arises from VCs 
operating in an information-rich environment with extreme time constraints, leading them to not 
always understanding their own decision-making. Add then that decisions are made on intangibles that, 
by their nature, is difficult to measure.  As an often-cited paper stated: “In each case the major difference 
between a winner and loser cluster is some difficult-to-define venture team characteristic, which is 
probably much easier to identify post hoc than a priori. Thus, it is not surprising that venture evaluation 
remains an art, a long way from becoming a science” (Macmillan et al., 1987). Add again the element 
of uncertainty and luck - or simply randomness. Making decisions in VC is, in summary, difficult. One 
interviewee framed it as: “If we really could do evidence-based decisions, we would not have 80% that 
failed”. It is just an accepted nature of the game, and as one interviewee said about the responses they 
get when presenting a potential venture to the industry: “Then there are some industry people saying 
that most likely that is not going to be anything, and then we simply say “Yes - that we fully agree on”, 
but we judge there is an above 15% chance that it actually is going to work”. 
 
Stating all that, research is still of significant importance and should not be disregarded. The better the 
data gathered and the better interpretation of that data, the greater is the likelihood of identifying the 
successful ventures. One interviewee commented: “Looking at information asymmetries, a big part of 
the success of an investor comes from the quality of the information available on the underlying asset – 
the startup.”. Research is also important for VCs in their competition against their peers: “How much 
data is necessary is very difficult to say. For sure there are diminishing returns on the data that you get. 
In reality, we just need better data then other investors”.

Discussion 
 
 66    
6.1. Theoretical Contribution 
This research contributes to the current literature by better understanding the current gaps and 
confirming some of the current theories. 
 
The newest investment process used in the literature, was found to be flawed in several ways. Even 
though some literature did use an investment process more like the findings, they had not been 
recognised as a “standard”. The proposed updated model could be used as a standard. 
 
The investment criteria used in research was found to not represent the current reality. Based on the 
primary data, a new categorization of investment criteria was proposed with the replacement of one 
category. This could be used in the future as a new standard categorization and for the future research 
investigating the specific criteria under each category. 
 
Even though it is well-acknowledged in the literature that the management team in a new venture is 
the most important aspect, little was understood of exactly why. This research offered an explanation 
by drawing parallels to motivation-hygiene theory. Furthermore, the shift in the importance of the 
management team had not been recognised. There is offered explanations to why it had happened. 
 
Some of the most important contributions from this research is in offering a better understanding of 
the methods used for management team assessment, and by suggesting two ways of categorizing them. 
Prior, there was only one study by Geoffrey H. Smart, and based on this research, that study was 
imperfect. It was based on five possible methods coming from another field (psychology), and “forced” 
participants to only allocate their time to one of these methods. For example, if they had a pitch session, 
it is not “work sample” on the definition given in the study, but could wrongly had been categorized as 
such. VCs use methods unique for their needs, and this highlights the importance of doing exploratory 
research prior to designing experiments. This research also provides two fundamental ways of thinking 
about assessment methods: Static or moving. 
 
Another important contribution is a better understanding of the factors that influence the management 
team assessment. Prior, research did not take these nuances into account, that otherwise can have a 
dramatic effect on the results. This research identified and described three key factors influencing the 
assessment (investment strategy, investment phase and industry).  
==PAGE:76==
 
  67 
6.2. Practical Contribution 
For VCs, this research provides an overview of the current literature. Given that all the research was 
conducted in Denmark, it might provide insights to their peers. This research could also provide VCs 
with new concepts, such as thinking about either static or moving methods. 
 
For entrepreneurs seeking VC funding, this research can potentially help them decrease the asymmetric 
information by now knowing what the VCs are looking for. This both generally, in the form of investment 
criteria and due diligences, and more specifically on them as persons. In addition, this report has shown 
the process they can expect to go through. 
 
6.3. Limitations  
Several possible limitations exist in this study. First, given that this study is qualitative and with a 
relatively low sample size of n=9, the generalizability of the results is limited. As has been highlighted in 
this report, there exists large individual differences between VCs and draing general conclusions can be 
misleading. This is also evident from the quantitative studies used as references in this report, where 
the standard deviation often is very high. This research is also exceedingly narrative in nature with 
numerous citations, and therefor highly biased by the author. 
 
In discussing the generalizability of the results, a distinction should be made between the different 
research questions. Some of the findings are more standardized in the industry than others. For 
example, the types of due diligence conducted is very similar between VCs, whereas there are significant 
variations in the methods for assessing the management team. 
 
On research question (1) the data is more objective and can thereby be more easily compared. The data 
is also more readily available and for example, the investment process is often available on the individual 
VCs webpage. For research question (2a) there existed quantitative data as well as qualitative data in 
the literature, almost all of which found the management team to be the most important aspect. This 
was additionally confirmed by all participants in the study. Hence, the generalizability and validity of this 
finding is considered very high.

For research question (2b), there only existed one prior study on the subject and this study suggested 
new methods. The new findings are only supported by the relatively low sample size of this study. There 
existed more prior studies on (2c), but here there is a somewhat different issue.  
 
Because of the multiple ways to describe a human capital attribute, it is difficult to compare the results. 
For example, one study found an important attribute to be “the entrepreneur’s capability of sustaining 
intense effort” and another might simply describe that as “stamina”. Given this issue, these research 
results are also biased by the interpretation of the author. As there was not time in the interviews to go 
into depth with what was meant in more details with each human capital attribute mentioned, 
important nuances and exact interpretations might have gotten lost. This is especially true when they 
were aggregated to create categories. Given the problem of heterogeneousness in the terminology, the 
incomplete data collection and personal biases of the author, the results of (2c) is not seen as being 
generalizable before additional research. 
 
Research question (2d) investigated something new and provided the ground work for future studies. 
The factors identified is based on the synthesis of the author, and is therefore influenced by the biases 
of the author.

6.4. Recommendations for Future Research 
Future researchers should focus on making the results more generalizable and less narrative. This could 
be achieved by doing more rigours content analysis on qualitative data, by fully coding it and do 
frequency analysis. Additionally, based on these research findings, a quantitative study could be 
conducted. 
 
The research on the investment process, criteria and types of due diligences is already covered in the 
literature and readily available from non-academic sources. Hence, it is not recommended to spend 
resources here. It would be possible to try and validate the proposed updated models, but given 
individual differences between VCs and it not being useful for the industry, it is still recommended not 
to pursue it. 
 
It is also recommended not to further validate that the team is the most important aspect. Instead, it 
would be more interesting to better understand what factors influence the importance. 
 
This research proposed a whole new set of methods used by VCs to assess the management team. 
Future research could try and confirm this with a larger sample size. 
 
It is important for future researches to homogenize the terminology used for human capital attributes. 
Definitions should be made for each in the context of VC to decrease the ambiguity and subjectivisms 
of each attribute.    
 
The synthesis of the factors influencing the management team assessment should be validated with 
more data, to decrease the biases of the author of this paper.

Conclusion

7. Conclusion 
(1) What is the investment process, the criteria and the due diligences used by venture capitalists? 
From the literature review the following investment process was synthesized: i) Origination, ii) 
screening, iii) evaluation (due diligence), iv) negotiation and v) deal. Empirical findings are different in 
two ways: i) An initial due diligence, and ii) main due diligence after signing the term sheet. An updated 
model with two extra steps was proposed. 
 
The research confirmed the literature that VCs use a multi-phase evaluation based on several 
overarching categories. The synthesized list of categories from the literature review was modified in the 
following way: i) No financial category as no VC used this, ii) a new category of exit options was proposed, 
and iii) a new category called business was added. An updated table was created with the new 
categorizes and with examples of specific investment criteria. 
 
(2a) Why is the team perceived to be the most important factor in the assessment of a new venture? 
Majority of the literature found the team to be the most important factor in the VCs decision to invest 
or not. This was confirmed from the research where all interviewed stated the significance of the team.  
 
Using the concept from motivation-hygiene theory, all but the team is considered hygiene factors. They 
are simply necessary for an investment to be made, but by themselves are not interesting. All but the 
team is a static factor, and it is the team that influences what these static values are. Because the future 
is unpredictable, the team becomes important, as they are the ones adapting the venture to it.  
 
Several of the VCs also stated that the management team is the most difficult thing to assess in the new 
venture. People are intangibles and governed by soft values. Assessing the management team cannot 
be done as desk research, less quantifiable and more subjective than the other factors. 
 
The weight given to the management team has increased over several years before reaching the point 
where it is at today. This can be explained by VCs origin in private equity and a change in those ventures 
that becomes very successful.  
 
==PAGE:80==
 
  71 
(2b) How can venture capitalists evaluate the management team of a potential investment? 
From the literature, it was found that VCs use five different methods for assessing the management (job 
analysis, document analysis, past-oriented interviews, reference interviews and work sample 
discussions). The primary data however revealed that this does not accurately represent the real world. 
 
A categorization was proposed to distinguish two different types of methods: moving and static 
methods. Static methods do not require a time dimension to assess, whereas moving methods do. The 
proposed methods in the literature were all static. However, most VCs put much greater weight to the 
moving methods. Beneath is the possible methods VCs can use to assess the management team:

The research revealed that the static method of psychological testing was, even though not highlighted 
in the literature, of much importance to the VCs. It could provide valuable insights to the team and 
provided the basis for important discussions. 
 
Pitch sessions was used by most VCs in this study and gave the senior team in the VC a chance to both 
observe the potential team and interact with them. 
 
Interacting with the team was found to be an important method for assessing the new team.  
It served two purposes: i) assessing the quality of the team, and ii) personal chemistry with investor. 
As making an investment is like a “marriage”, interacting before the investment is made is the “dating 
period”. Through interaction the VC could asses if they had the human capital attributes they deemed 
necessary for that specific venture.

Conclusion 
 
 72    
(2c) What human capital attributes are venture capitalists looking for in the management team? 
The research showed that there is not an ideal list of human capital attributes that the VCs are looking 
for. Instead, drawing parallels to contingency theory, ideal attributes depends on the situation. Variables 
such as the customer, venture stage, the product and the team influenced the attributes.  
 
However, there were some human capital attributes that almost all VCs in the study mentioned: 
motivation, entrepreneurial, selling, stamina, coachable and integrity. These are in addition to the 
specific skills and knowledge required in the venture case. 
 
(2d) What influences the management team assessment? 
From the research, it was found that three different things influence how many resources is used and 
the weight given to the management team: i) Investment strategy, ii) investment phase and iii) industry. 
 
A VC leaning more to the strategy of spray-and-pray, use less resources on assessment. Vice versa for 
VCs using a targeted strategy. Another strategy was to spend more time with the team to improve the 
valuation in favour of the VC. 
 
The stage at which the VC invests was found to have a significant influence on the resources used on it. 
The earlier the investment, the less resources could be used, due to its relative cost. However, the 
earlier the investment, the more important the team is. Several different models were proposed to 
model these relationships together with suggestions to how they could be quantified. It was also found 
that the phase of the investment influence the specific skills that the management team should possess. 
 
Different industries have different types of risk, and that influences the team assessment. In industries 
were the technology is less important, the team becomes more important.
