Identifying needs for grid reinforcements in the European transmission
system
by Riccardo Zanetti
In 2030 the electricity generation mix will be diﬀerent than today comprising of a strongly
increased proportion of large, remote and decentralized power production plans from re-
newables energy sources (RES). The actual design of the grid is not capable of transmitting
this additional amount of electricity. This scenario, together with the diﬃculties of obtain-
ing new right-of-ways (ROWs) for the construction of new lines, calls for a novel approach
for the usage of the existing grid.
The present study analyzes two AC line upgrade technologies chosen for their outstand-
ing technical potential, namely high-temperature low-sag (HTLS) conductors and voltage
upgrade of the existing AC lines. The analysis focus mainly on the ﬁrst technology due to
issues that interfered on the analysis of the application of voltage upgrade on the existing
branches. A theoretical analysis of both the upgrade technologies is oﬀered to evaluate
their advantages and disadvantages. Moreover, a mathematical formulation of a DC opti-
mal power ﬂow (DC-OPF) is presented. This is inserted in the proposed algorithm for the
expansion of the grid in order to estimate which corridors need to be upgraded. Further-
more four key performance indicators (KPIs) are introduced, namely RES penetration,
Reduced energy curtailment from RES, Extended asset lifetime and Continuity of supply.
They are used for evaluating the extended performances of the grid after the upgrade. The
algorithm is then tested on the 7754-bus system that models the European transmission
grid in the 2030 load and generation scenario created.
The application of HTLS conductors is evaluated on two diﬀerent grid scenarios: a fully
constrained grid, and the same grid with all the HVDC connection unconstrained. From
the ﬁrst scenario it is found that the Business As Usual has a potential RES penetration
equal to 56%. The application of HTLS conductors on existing AC corridors can increase
this penetration of 3% and reduce at the same time the energy not served with a proﬁtable
investment. The second scenario, where the capacity of the HVDC lines is unconstrained,
shows the higher potential of the AC grid, able to reach more than 60% of RES penetra-
tion, but also the limits of the upgrade with the HTLS conductors where the existing lines
needs a capacity increase higher than 100%. By combining the results obtained from both
the scenarios, it is possible to state that the corridors more in need of upgrade are the
corridors connecting Germany with the neighbouring states, most of all with Denmark.

1 Introduction
1.1
Introduction and Problem Statement
The European Union has set the goal of reducing, by 2050, the greenhouse gas emissions
by 80-95% compared to the levels registered in 1990 [15]. The role played by European
climate and energy policy is increasingly important. The Member States are developing
power generation from renewable energy sources with the aim of cutting greenhouse gas
emissions. Looking at the data, the share of energy from renewable sources in gross ﬁnal
consumption of energy raised up to 17 % in the European Union in 2016. In around ten
years it doubled the share seen in 2004, that was just the 8.5%. Now new strategies,
like Europe 2020, aim to reach 20% of energy in gross ﬁnal consumption of energy from
renewable sources by 2020 and the minimum target for 2030 is set to 27% [16].
In many regions across the globe, electricity power production from renewable energy
sources is taking over conventional non-renewable sources like fossil fuels and nuclear
power. This shift in power production will be faster and faster in the near future.
Renewable energy sources across the European continent are integrated rapidly into the
energy mix. Most of these sources, like wind energy, are located far from the the places
where the load is installed. Unlike conventional power generation, for which the fuel (like
coal and natural gas) can be transported, many of these new sources must be installed
where the natural ”fuel” is available. Recent studies [9] show that the North sea region
with higher and more constant wind speeds compared to the rest of Europe oﬀers huge
potential for wind farms. This is due to the fact that oﬀshore wind energy is now a mature
technology and it is becoming cheaper than most of the non-renewable energy source.
Other technologies, like photovoltaics, are spread and dislocated in several areas. They
are mainly installed on a small scale (for example rooftop PV) and connected to the dis-
tribution level. While the single installation may produce a negligible amount of energy,
the combined generation of all the devices installed can not be neglected.
The decarbonization process will also lead to a fuel switch in many strategic sectors like
transport and heating [3] [13].
Many car manufacturers are introducing in the market several models of Electric Vehicles
(EV) for private and public transport. Due to the large scale of production the cost of EV
will decrease signiﬁcantly and their use will most probably increase exponentially in the
next years.
Due to the strong will of the European Union to reduce the green house emission, other
sectors, like the heating sector, will face a fuel switch, moving from oil and gas towards
electric heating systems.

There are two main takeaways from this investigation.
• The energy mix is changing and the production is being dislocated far away from
the centers were the load is located;
• The electric power consumption will grow due to the fuel switch of strategic sectors
as well as the increase of population.
This growing and changing process can be divided in several steps, one for each decade:
2020/2030/2040/2050. The 2030 horizon is the closest and, hence, the most predictable.
Several companies started already projects to install new wind farms as well as to re-power
existing ones in the whole continent. It is then of interest to focus on the development of
the grid in the next ten years.
Due to these reasons, the actual design of the European transmission grid is limited and it
would not be able to transfer the bulk energy generated nor support the increase of load
in the nearest future.
This integration of large, remote and decentralized power production calls for the ex-
pansion of the grid to transfer the produced electricity to the consumer. The collective
European eﬀort to bring our continent’s energy mix into the age of renewable energy re-
quires power transmission lines to connect supply and demand.
Finding new corridors and upgrading existing lines is of high interest because the elec-
tric lines are already saturated. The saturation leads to critical values of ampacity and
sag. The congestion of lines leads also to the increase of generation costs and a lower
penetration level of Renewable Energy Sources (RES).
Due to the integration of ﬂuctuating generation, the grid needs a new design that enhances
the power system ﬂexibility.

high power DC links within AC meshed network. This master thesis is part of the project’s
scalability assessment, which analyzes the impact of the demonstrated technologies when
deployed on a large scale in the pan-European transmission system.

Research Questions
Based on this central problem the following research questions arise. The present thesis
aims to answer them through an accurate research approach.
1. Which technologies and which method can be applied for the AC scalability of the
European transmission grid?
2. Which are the potentials, not explored yet, and which are the boundaries of the actual
European transmission grid in the future generation mix scenario?
3. Which are the costs and which are the economical and technical beneﬁts of the grid
expansion?

1.3
Thesis Layout: Research Approach and Limitations
The methodology of this project is based on ﬁve steps. They allow to achieve a complete
assessment of the impact that the repowering of the existing corridors would cause.
The ﬁrst step is the literature review that creates a general understanding of recent de-
velopment within this ﬁeld. Speciﬁcally it analyzes two distinct areas of the problem:
upgrade methods of existing lines and tools to analyze the Transmission Expansion Plan
(TEP). The evaluation of upsides and downsides of diﬀerent line upgrade technologies
helps to evaluate which of them is (or are) the most suitable for the purpose of this mas-
ter’s thesis.
The other part of the literature review helps to deﬁne a tool that can perform the aimed
analysis. The chosen tool for this analysis is an extended version of the DC Optimal Power
Flow (DC-OPF) including HVDC lines. The beneﬁt of this linear OPF is the reliable and
unique solutions with relatively simple methods. In fact the injections and the resulting
ﬂows are optimized eﬃciently. The DC-OPF does not take into account losses, as well as
the inﬂuence of the reactive power on the voltage magnitudes of the AC busses. However
the reactive power production has strong eﬀect only on a local basis, while this master’s
thesis aims to analyze the whole European transmission grid without focusing on one spe-
ciﬁc area.
The third step is the mathematical formulation and the creation of a code of the DC-OPF.
This is then tested on grids of diﬀerent scale in order to verify the accuracy of the solution
as well as the robustness of the written code.
It follows the fourth step, where it is performed the deﬁnition of the Key Performance
Indicators (KPIs) based on the outputs of the DC-OPF created. The KPIs are used in
the analysis of the BAU grid and in its comparison with the Best Paths grids proposed.
The ﬁfth steps is the evaluation of the case study with the model deﬁned above. the case
study is preceded by the deﬁnition of the pan-European grid model. The aim is to use
a model as complete as possible, containing most of the busses of the continental grid.
However the model has also to take a not excessive calculation burden. For this reason the
chosen model is the one created for the Best Paths project by the Fundaci´on CIRCE [5]. It
contains most of the elements of the transmission European grid and includes also the new
AC and HVDC lines designed in the projects e-Highway 2050 and TYNDP 2016. The grid
chosen is composed by 7754 network nodes (7474 AC and 280 DC) and 8923 lines (8783
AC and 140 HVDC). In the case study, the upgrade methods deﬁned from the literature
review are applied and the new model created are compared with the Business As Usual
(BAU) model through the help of the KPIs. Due to the complexity of the study, this
master’s thesis can not cover all the aspects of the grid expansion. several other scenarios
and technologies could be applied.
Yet the application of HTLS conductors and the voltage upgrade of existing lines are most
representable because of their high capacity increase without the need of new ROWs. The
thesis ends answering the research questions proposed in the previous section and provid-
ing recommendations for further research. Limitations of the empirical application and
the insights gained from it will be discussed in the end.

2.1
Methods for Transmission Expansion Planning
The process of identiﬁcation of place and size of existing and new corridors that bring
an economic and eﬃciency improvement to an existing grid is known as Transmission
Expansion Planning (TEP) [17]. There are several publications about this topic in the
literature. The authors in [20] analyze and classify many of the TEP methods, tools and
models published.
the models can be classiﬁed in two diﬀerent categories.
The mathematical optimization models express the problem through a mathematical for-
mulation that comprises an objective function, as well as the constraints of the process.
It ﬁnds the optimum point solving an optimization problem.
On the other side, heuristic models follow an iterative algorithm that selects expansion
options and that evaluates their impact after every iteration. Some of the criteria consid-
ered in this category are investment and operation costs, as well as spilled and not served
power.
in [17], the authors propose a long-term TEP model to increase transmission capacity
through, among other technologies, HTLS reconductoring. Its purpose is to increase the
capacity of the existing corridors in order to limit the need of new right-of-ways (ROWs).
The TEP model presented in [17] is a mixed integer programming (MIP) problem that
contains also a direct current optimal power ﬂow (DC-OPF). The possibility of this last
tool, i.e the DC-OPF, to be used for the analysis of large scale networks makes this method
interesting for this master’s thesis.
Barrios, Schrief and Schnettler [10] use, in the solution proposed, line upgrade methods like
HTLS reconductoring, voltage upgrades and the addition of parallel lines to the corridor.
In their work it is discussed an approach considering an around-the-year analysis. This
approach, that opposes to the study of the critical scenarios, analyses all the snapshot of
the grid over one year with a certain time step. Furthermore, it is necessary when volatile
generation is connected to the grid. In fact, the analysis of the critical scenarios is not
suﬃcient due to the high number of diﬀerent cases that the generation from RES involves.
In fact, in the circumstances described, it is diﬃcult to determine which are the critical
scenarios.
It is also of interest to review the publications of previous projects of expansion of the
European grid. The ENTSO-E project TYNDP [13] and the project e-Highway 2050 [14]
focused on creating new electric generation/load future scenarios (2030 TYNDP 14/16,
2040 TYNDP 18, 2050 e-Highway 2050) for the European Union and analyze the grid
in order to ﬁnd new corridors that allows to optimize the transmission of power between
generation and demand.

2.2.2
Voltage Upgrade
The second transmission line upgrade method analyzed in this master’s thesis is the voltage
upgrade of the existing lines. This technology involves more steps and necessitates of the
availability of suitable voltage transformers in the substation to which the line is connected
or the creation of a new substation(s) dedicated to the candidate lines for the upgrade.
This lead to a high investment cost for the application of this method. However, this
technology allows to obtain less I2R losses, compared to every current upgrade method.
This is due to the fact that, increasing the voltage level of the line, the current ﬂowing
through the line decreases when the same power is injected. This method can be suitably
used where the power transfer capability is inﬂuenced by voltage regulation or stability
considerations [11].
Suﬃcient electrical clearance is a necessary condition to achieve in order to increase the
voltage rating of overhead lines. two aspects must be considered for this purpose. The ﬁrst
is the identiﬁcation of the availability of the required air clearances for a higher voltage
in an existing structure. The second aspect is the consideration of a suﬃcient insulation
level required to withstand overvoltages due to power frequency, lightning impulse and
switching surge.

2.4. Optimal Power Flow

2.4
Optimal Power Flow
In this chapter it is described the mathematical formulation of the Optimal Power Flow
used for the network analysis. The purpose of the optimal power ﬂow is to minimize one
parameter of the grid such as costs or losses, taking into consideration all the physical
constraints of the network.
Since the aim of this study is the upgrade of the existing lines, as well as the integration of
the new generation from RES, the optimal power ﬂow is the tool chosen for the analysis.
there are two formulation of the optimal power ﬂow problem: AC- and DC- OPF. The
AC-OPF is the complete and non-linear version. It takes into account the losses of the
system. For this reason it is the most complete and accurate method but it has a high
computational burden. It follows that the computational time increases.
On the contrary, the DC-OPF is a linear and simpliﬁed version of the AC optimal power
ﬂow. In this formulation the losses are not taken into account (hence the non linear part
is removed) and several simplifying hypothesis are made. For this reason the DC-OPF is
not as accurate as the AC-OPF, but it is faster and it can be used for bigger systems,
since it can ﬁnd a good approximation of the results.
The authors in [21] show the beneﬁts of the DC-OPF and they analyze the diﬀerences in
the results of the two methods. Due to the goal of ﬁnding a tool for the analysis of large
scale system, this master’s thesis will focus only on the DC-OPF.
The AC-OPF allows to calculate active and reactive power ﬂows. On a local basis the
eﬀect of the reactive power is signiﬁcant. On the contrary, big systems, like large scale
energy markets, focus mainly on the active power ﬂows [24].
The DC optimal power ﬂow method denotes the linearized method in order to solve the
OPF problem. The classic formulation of the DC-OPF does not include the DC grid. Due
to the fast spread of the HVDC technology, it is considered necessary to formulate the
OPF taking into account the modelling of AC/DC converters and of the DC branches, as
well as the constraints of the DC grid.
In [24], the authors present a version of the DC-OPF for a mixed AC-HVDC grid. They
test the proposed algorithm on the IEEE 14-bus system with a 5 node DC overlay grid.
In the results they show the increased calculation performances of the DC-OPF and they
compare the results of the proposed algorithm with the results computed through the full
AC-OPF.
The section will proceed in the following way: ﬁrst the general mathematical formulation
of the optimal power ﬂow is presented. This is followed by the description of both the
vectors of the variables of the AC and DC OPF. The third and fourth paragraphs explain
respectively the equality and inequality constraints taken into account in the formulation
of the DC-OPF. The sections ends with presenting two extensions of the DC-OPF:the
DC-OPF with additional slack variables and the DC-OPF including load shedding.
The code of the DC-OPF written for this thesis is presented in Appendix A.

2.6
Grid Development Methodology
This section aims to connect all the elements described above in an algorithm that will be
applied in the case study of the European transmission grid.
First of all the Business As Usual (BAU) scenario has to be deﬁned. This comprises the
base case model of the grid and the generation al load scenario in which the grid wants to
be tested. Every grid expansion is compared to its BAU scenario grid, without changing
the input data of load and generation.
The iterative procedure of the analysis of every grid is done as follow:
• An around-the-year analysis (with time step of 1h) of the deterministic scenario is
carried out through the DC-OPF;
• The KPIs of the grid are analyzed;
• The KPIs of the upgraded grid are compared with the ones of the BAU grid and
with the results of the previous step of the model (iterative process).
• The congested AC lines and transformers are analyzed and divided in groups. Each
group is formed based on the congestion level of the elements: this process allows to
ﬁnd the candidate lines for the upgrade;
• All the lines with a congestion level higher than 30% are analyzed and, if possible,
they are upgraded with the speciﬁc technology. The threshold of 30% is chosen em-
pirically. It has been noticed, during initial tests, that when the congestion is released
form the branches with the highest congestion level, the lines already overloaded of-
ten becomes the candidate for the upgrade during the next step. Upgrading more
lines per step reduces the total time of the analysis since it decreases the number of
recursive steps;
• The process is repeated until there are no more candidate lines to upgrade.

2.7
Validity of the Method and Limitations
In order to complete the theoretical model used for this master’s thesis, it is necessary
to discuss the objective limitations of the method and the validity of the results obtained
through it.
The linear version of the optimal power ﬂow, used because of the big scale of the system,
does not consider the eﬀect of the reactive power ﬂowing into the system, simplifying
all the voltage magnitudes at one per unit.
Furthermore the model of the grid used
for the studies does not consider the shunt elements nor the resistances of the AC lines.
Hence, the results of the DC-OPF can be considered only a ﬁrst approximation of the real
results. Nonetheless it is possible to consider this level of approximation accurate enough
to evaluate the congestion of the lines of the system.
The model of the storage, due to the size of the system for which this method is considered,
have some limitation too. In order to optimize the storage in a system, a time coupling
between the hours is necessary. However, it is impossible, with the computational power
available, to create a time coupled model of the variables of a large scale system for an
around-the-year study. Since the ﬁrst goal of this project is not the optimization of the
storage of the grid, the results of the load ﬂow can be considered correct.
Another limitation of the model is the fact that it neglects the losses.
The DC-OPF
neglects the transmission losses and an AC-OPF is not a feasible solution because of
the size of the system. Furthermore all the elements of the grid, like AC/DC converters
and transformers are ideal elements.
Due to the high eﬃciency of this elements, this
approximation can be considered correct and the results can be still considered accurate.
Further studies that wants to focus on a smaller scale starting from the results obtained
with this method, should increase the accuracy of the method based on the scale of the
system under analysis.
Lastly, the grid expansion resulting from this project do not

element). It would be necessary, when a component is designed for a grid, to analyze
not only its steady state performance but also to make more security tests like a N-1
contingency analysis.

3.1.2
Data File Format
The ﬁrst necessary step to start the study is to create a data format easy to use that
contains all the information needed and suitable for the DC-OPF coded. The reference
format used is Matpower. The data are structured into a set of struct variables, scalar
or matrices, each of which contains the information on speciﬁc network elements (i.e.,
generator data, bus data, branch data, etc.). Matpower does not include a structure for
==PAGE:50==
32
Chapter 3. Case Study: the European Transmission Grid
DC lines and Converters. For this reason the format is extended creating the matrices
that contains data on DC lines, DC buses and AC/DC converters.
two diﬀerent matrix structures are used for these three elements.
Due to their similarities the DC buses are structured as the AC ones, while AC/DC
converters and DC lines have the same format of the AC branch matrix.
The reason
behind this choice is the similarity between the modeling of the elements that is described
in 2.3.1 and 2.3.2. All matrices are saved to a Matlab structure called mpc (Matpower case)
along with a scalar value denoting the base power (Sbase).
The elements of the Matlab structure mpc are:
• baseMVA (scalar)
• version
• busAC(matrix)
• busDC (matrix)
• branch (matrix)
• branchDC (matrix)
• conv (matrix)
• gen (matrix)
• gencost (matrix)
Each row in the matrices corresponds to a single bus, branch, converter, generator, etc..
Note that transformers are modeled as AC lines and included in the branch matrix.
The extended data ﬁle format is presented in Appendix B.
Furthermore it is also of interest to explain the format of generation, load and storage
contained in mpc.gen and mpc.bus.
Generation
the generators contained in the vector Pg can be of three diﬀerent types:
• Conventional: All the type of conventional generators connected to the grid are
presented in Table 3.3. Their maximum and minimum power is constant all over the
year;
• RES: RES generators include PV and Wind generators. The proﬁles for PMAX
for this generators are contained in the matrix Pmax RES. Even if Hydro generation
is considered a RES in the grid expansion analysis, it is included in the conventional
generators due to the fact that its maximum power is assumed to be constant all
over the year. Volatyle res generators, i.e. wind and PV, have a marginal price equal
to zero;
• Pumped Hydro Storage: Pumped hydro Storage are described in detail in 3.1.2.
Load
Since the full model of the grid is reduced from ≈17000 to ≈8000 bus, all the generators
and load connected to the distribution grid are included in the load. The generators are
considered negative load and they contribute to decrease the magnitude of the load. all the
busses connected to a load are related to a load proﬁle. in fact the load is not considered
constant but is varies according to the proﬁle. Due to the fact that the model requires a
value of the load connected to each bus, all the other busses have a constant load proﬁle
equal to zero.

Storage
The only storage system that appears in the data set is the pumped hydro storage. It
is modeled as a generator that can deliver power only for 1100 h 1. As for RES, their
maximum power output is not ﬁxed but the proﬁle is a binary variable: when they are
allowed to produce they can produce at their maximum power, in all the other hours their
capacity is equal to zero. The hours in which they are allowed to produce are the hours
with the highest demand and lower RES generation. The minimum power produced by
all the storages in the data set is equal to zero. hence any of them is allowed to absorb
power, but only to generate. This limits their application, but due to the fact that the
number of storages in the grid is low, this does not inﬂuence signiﬁcantly the results.
3.1.3
Data Conversion and Test
The process of data conversion is an important process that is carried out in diﬀerent
steps. Its purpose is to verify the accuracy of the data format as well as of the data set
used, since the data set was created alongside with this project.
During the ﬁrst step of the process only the original Matpower matrices are created,
hence the converters and the DC grid are not taken into account. This allows to test,
using the Matpower code, portions of the continental AC grid in order to ﬁnd mistakes
and anomalies in the data set.
Every single country is tested alone for one single hour and, if the code converges to a
result, the adjacent country is connected and the test is repeated. In order not to have
convergence problems in the simulation of the single country, a fake generator is connected
to the end of each tie line. This generators can create or absorb inﬁnite power with a high
penalty factor associated to it in the objective function. The penalty factor helps not
to generate or absorb more power than the one needed to reach the convergence of the
DC-OPF.
During this step of the process it was discovered that the grid of the ﬁrst data set was
split in several islands and this violated the power balance equations at the nodes.
With the help of internal Matpower functions it was possible to see that the grid under
investigation was split in islands and there were some isolated elements. The problem,
created during the reduction of the model from the full grid to the transmission grid, was
solved by CIRCE creating the missing connections.
During the second step, the new data set, based on the complete data format, was created.
This was tested with the DC-OPF created for this master’s thesis.
During this step the main problem was still the convergence of the DC-OPF. Before using
the DC-OPF coded, this was tested on the IEEE 14-bus system presented in [24]. The
results of this test are presented in Appendix E.
The focus of the analysis moved from the search of the islands to the constraints of the
DC-OPF, mainly on the line’s capacity. For this reason the grid was tested using the
DC-OPF with additional slack variables described in 2.4.6. The method allowed to obtain
signiﬁcant results and solve the problem.

3.1.4
Calculation of the Slack Variables
The ﬁrst version of the mpc ﬁle contains the initial line capacities taken from the ﬁle
provided by CIRCE. Since the DC OPF is hard constrained by the line capacities, a nec-
essary condition for the convergence of the problem is the availability of suﬃcient line
capacities. As explained in the previous section, the lack of this necessary capacities led
to a problem of convergence. This obstacle can be solved by creating a DC-OPF with soft
penalty factors for the line capacities in order to ﬁnd the minimum increase to allow the
convergence of the BAU case. The values of the slack variables are obtained and they are
added to the value of the maximum possible power ﬂowing through those lines in a new
mpc ﬁle. The list of all the lines involved in this process can be found in Appendix D.
Figure 3.1 shows the graphical comparison between the old and the new capacities
The minimum capacity increase is obtained simulating the optimal power ﬂow of the
grid for the whole year (step of 1 hour) using the function deﬁned in 2.4.6. It is then
extrapolated the maximum factor of each AC branch. Lastly the slack variables are tested
with the classic formulation of the DC-OPF and the BAU grid is created.

3.6
Method 1: HTLS conductors
The ﬁrst line upgrade method analyzed is the line reconductoring with HTLS conductors.
It is worth resuming here the advantages of this technology. As explained more in details
in 2.2.1 the line upgrade with HTLS conductors have the following upsides:
• No need of new ROW;
• No need of new transformers (unless the transformers installed are overloaded);
• No need of structural reinforcements;
• No need of new insulation methods;
• The capacity of the line can be doubled;
==PAGE:67==
3.6. Method 1: HTLS conductors
49
and downsides:
• Cost of the conductor from 2 to 6.5 times higher that the cost of conventional
conductors;
• Method not suitable over long distances;
• Higher I2R losses due to the higher current ﬂowing in the line.
Furthermore it is also of interest to explain how the studies are performed since both the
cases follow the same methodology.
After the analysis of the BAU scenario, the overloaded lines are grouped in diﬀerent groups.
All the lines with a congestion level above 30% are candidated for the upgrade and the
BAU capacity of these lines is doubled. it is worth noticing that several lines present
a capacity higher than the maximum capacity that one single AC branch can carry at
voltage levels equal to 220/380/400 (kV). Since the data set does not speciﬁed the number
of parallel lines of which every corridor is composed, it is assumed that each line is formed
by a certain number of parallel lines. It is also supposed that after the line upgrade none of
the lines hit its maximum capacity for the given voltage level. It follows that the capacity
upgrade is always considered feasible.
Since the process is recursive, every line that was already reinforced in one of the previous
steps cannot be upgraded anymore in case it appears again among the candidate lines.
Another assumption made is that it is always possible to add one or more transformers
in parallel in the substations if necessary. Like the line upgrade, also the transformers are
analyzed based on their congestion level. Every transformer with a congestion level equal
or higher than 30 % is reinforced. This upgrade is made adding a second transformer,
equal to the one installed, in parallel. In this way the original capacity is doubled while
the original reactance is halved.
Once the expansion process is over, the second part of the analysis is the economical
comparison between the cost of the reinforcement and the diﬀerential cost of generation
between the BAU and the Best Paths scenario.
The expansion costs on which the analysis is based are taken from [10] and resumed in
Table 3.13. The lines are divided in 380 (kV) (that includes also the cost of all the lines
with a higher voltage level) and 220 (kV). The cost of the transformer station is given for
a transformer station of 800 (MVA). The total cost of installation of the transformers n
this project is calculated as follow:
• The total new capacity installed is calculated.
This is equal to the sum of the
capacities of all the transformers with a congestion level above 30%;
• This capacity is then divided by the nominal capacity of the transformer station
provided, 800 (MVA);
• The result of the ratio times the cost of one transformer gives the total cost of
installation of the transformers.

3.8
Limitation of the Case Study
For diﬀerent reasons the case study under investigation has encountered several limitations.
The aim of this chapter is to resume and explain the main obstacles.
Two key factors outline the framework for the analysis of such a big system in the context
of a master’s thesis:
• the required accuracy of the model of the grid described by the data set;
• the limited amount of time given to complete the analysis.
The Data set under analysis has never been used for other studies before this project.
Such a big and young data set necessitated some months of work in order to ﬁt it to the
model without any mistake and in order to obtain meaningful results and proceed with
all the steps of the analysis.
The main issues that arose during the process can be resumed under the following points:
• The reduction of the grid model from a ≈15000 bus system (transmission and
distribution grid) to a ≈8000 bus system (only transmission grid) divided the grid
in several islands leading to convergence problem of the OPF;
• The export of the data from Powerfactory to Excel caused the loss of several rows of
data. More than 3000 lines were doubled and lacking the correct values of reactance
and active power capacity. The ﬁnal version of the lines tables was delivered only in
the second half of February;
• Spelling mistakes as well as errors in the data structure slowed down signiﬁcantly
the data conversion process due to the impossibility to check the whole data set
with a secure and fast method. The spelling mistakes changed signiﬁcantly the grid
introducing not existing elements, hiding existing ones and allocating them in zones
that diﬀers from reality. During the creation of the model, the test and the analysis,
all the mistakes found were corrected, the data set recreated every time and all the
work on the wrong model made again from the beginning.
• A new problem of convergence arose due to a non-suﬃcient capacity of the lines
when the load ﬂow is a hard constraint (like in the DC-OPF). The problem was
solved with the introduction of slack variables discussed previously.
• The need of a meaningful 2030 projection of the RES generation and load. The data
set distinguishes the generation at transmission level (modeled as positive genera-
tion) and the one connected at distribution level. In the literature about the actual
and future projection of the load this distinction is not clearly expressed. This lead
to a misalignment between the data available and the projections.
The solution
adopted is to consider all the capacity available found in the literature connected at
transmission level.This solution is due to the high time necessary to create a model

4.1
Conclusion
In 2030 the electricity generation mix will be very diﬀerent from today’s, including a large
share of renewables. The integration of large, remote and decentralized power generation
calls for an expansion of the grid, to allow the transmission of the generated power to
consumers. Indeed, the current grid is not capable of transmitting this additional amount
of power, and political as well as environmental limitations hamper the construction of
new lines. This calls for new approaches for using the existing grid in an optimal way. In
the light of this fact, the following research questions were asked:
1. Which technologies and which method can be applied for the AC scalability of the
European transmission grid?
2. Which are the potentials, not explored yet, and which are the boundaries of the actual
European transmission grid in the future generation mix scenario?
3. Which are the costs and which are the economical and technical beneﬁts of the grid
expansion?
The present thesis contributes to answer this call, from a theoretical point of view, by
proposing a suitable method for the analysis of large grids, and from a practical point of
view, by applying the method proposed on a real model of the European transmission grid
analyzed in a realistic scenario created for this project.
Two technologies for repowering the existing corridors are investigated in this master’s
thesis: the reconductoring of the lines using high-temperature low-sag conductors and
voltage upgrade of the existing corridors. The technical features of both this technologies
were investigated, as well as their theoretical advantages and disadvantages. The recon-
ductoring method with HTLS conductors was explored extensively, testing its potential
in the case study proposed. On the contrary, the voltage upgrade of the existing corridors
could only be studied theoretically due to several obstacles that arose during the whole
project. The main interferences encountered were:
• the lack of an accurate data set that had to be adjusted in order to be analyzed.
• the extra time spent on the study of the ﬁrst scenario due to convergence issues not
known a priori.
For these reasons, this conclusion is drown mainly from the results obtained in the case
study analyzed: the AC upgrade of the corridors with HLTS conductors.
Aiming to
==PAGE:82==
64
Chapter 4. Resume
perform the AC scalability analysis of a real model of the European transmission grid, an
iterative method for the analysis of large systems has been designed and implemented, as
well as the deﬁnition of suitable key performance indicators needed to to acquire useful
values from the output of the DC-OPF algorithm to evaluate the enhancements of the
new grid topology.
The method involves the analysis of the results of 8760 snapshots of the grid through the
DC-OPF. All this results are highlighted in the KPIs namely: RES penetration, reduced
energy curtailment from RES, extended asset lifetime, continuity of supply. It is possible
to state that in general the KPIs were useful to analyze the grid. However the KPI deﬁning
the extended asset lifetime was not used as much as the others during the process and
could be redeﬁned or substituted. The values obtained with this KPI were diﬀerent from
what expected and it was hard to conclude whether there was an improvement or not in
the grid. For this reason their usage was limited.
Two diﬀerent scenarios were built, applying the same input parameters of generation and
load but changing the constraints of the grid. The aim of the ﬁrst scenario, in which the
grid was analyzed with all its HVDC lines constrained, was to explore the potential of the
existing European AC transmission grid.
On the other side, the second scenario was more focused on ﬁnding the boundaries of
the existing AC grid. The analysis of each single scenario and its relative expansion has
lead to interesting outcomes. From the results obtained in the scenario where the HVDC
connection are constrained, it was found that it is possible to obtain an increase of the
RES penetration level of about 3 % with an investment that would be payed back in
one year due to the saving in the cost of generation. Furthermore, it was observed that
the existing grid, with the proposed upgrade, can allow a RES penetration of 59% at
most.
Instead, in the second scenario where the HVDC lines were unconstrained, the
penetration of RES was about 60% and there were still candidate lines to upgrade. to
achieve an higher penetration of RES, a higher investment is necessary due to the cost of
the HTLS conductors and also due the necessity of increasing the capacity of the existing
transformers, which are not ready for this expansion.
To conclude, the comparison of all the scenarios showed that the reconductoring with
HTLS conductor can increase the potential of the grid, but there are limitations that
should be considered. In fact some lines, located mainly between Germany and Denmark,
can not be further upgraded with the usage of HTLS conductors, even though they would
need it to allow an higher penetration of RES. One solution, that could not be tested,
might be the voltage upgrade of those lines.
4.2
Further Research
For the purpose of further testing the grid, it would be beneﬁcial to increase the level
of accuracy of the RES projection, including in a more accurate way the PV production
and the wind production not connected at transmission level. It would also be interesting
to investigate other line upgrade methods and technologies like the voltage upgrade that
could not be tested in this master’s thesis.
Furthermore, a higher accuracy in the transformers’ upgrade might be needed, in order
==PAGE:83==

not to terminate with unnecessary over-investments. A possible solution might be the
inclusion of a slack variable in the OPF in order to know how much extra power is needed.
Lastly, it is worth noticing that the research is moving now towards the installation of
HVDC lines to transfer the bulk power in focal points of the continent due to the fact
that they can cover longer distances. It would be then of interest to perform again an AC
upgrade analysis of the transmission grid considering also these new connections in the
BAU grid.

The network buses are divided into two categories: AC and DC buses. The two matrices

positive integer value ID Number is assigned to each bus (starting from 1) and represents
the numerical ID in the bus matrix. Unique positive integer values are also assigned to the
string IDs of areas and zones. We use conversion tables to translate between numerical

zones and areas in the Excel ﬁle) - and vice versa:
• BusConversionAC/DC.mat:

• ZoneConversion.mat:
zone ID to ID Num conversion;
• AreaConversion.mat:
area ID to ID Num conversion.
Every conversion table relates the elements’ string IDs provided in the Excel ﬁle to its
numerical ID.

contains the annual load proﬁle (i.e., the time series) for all nodes and its row indices
are aligned with the bus indices in the bus matrix. The third column of the bus matrix
referring to the nodal active power demand needs to be updated with the values of the
considered hour.

B.2.6
General Comments
The ﬁrst version of the mpc ﬁle contains the initial line capacities provided in the Excel ﬁle,
which are not suﬃciently high to allow for feasible solutions of DC Optimal Power Flow
(DC-OPF) simulations, where the line ﬂow constraints are considered as hard constraints
as opposed to standard power ﬂow analyses.
If the data set is used for DC Optimal
Power Flow (DC-OPF) simulations, either the line capacities need to be adjusted, the
load decreased or the optimization has to be modiﬁed to allow for load shedding.
We determine the minimum required line increase to allow for feasible solutions during all
hours of the year without the need to shed to load in an optimization using positive slack
variables in the line capacity constraints, which are penalized in the objective function.
The optimal slack variables represent the minimum required percentage increase in line
capacities and are provided in the ﬁle slackvariables.mat. The ﬁnal adjusted line capacities
are provided in UpdatedCapacities.mat. Note that we determine the minimum required
line increase for AC lines and transformers only but not DC lines.
