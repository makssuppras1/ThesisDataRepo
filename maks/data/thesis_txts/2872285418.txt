Abstract
Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia), the thermonuclear explosions of white dwarfs in binary systems,
play a key role in observational cosmology due to their use as standardizable candles. They
have been studied extensively, but there are still systematic e!ects not yet entirely accounted
for. Host galaxy dust extinction introduces signiﬁcant uncertainties in the luminosity estimates
leading to scatter in the Hubble diagram. Furthermore, SNe Ia show appreciable diversity in the
UV emission that has not been fully modeled. This thesis investigates the viability of analyzing
SNe Ia using photometric data from the BlackGEM telescope array in the u, q and i ﬁlters, with
a focus on u-band light curves. We compile a sample of →90 SNe Ia observed by BlackGEM and
validate the quality of the u-band photometry against SDSS data. We combine the BlackGEM
photometry with data from other telescopes, primarily ATLAS and ZTF. We ﬁt the light curves
using SALT2 (Guy et al., 2007) and models in SNooPy (Burns et al., 2010)), with SALT2 showing
more consistent results.
The distribution of the SALT2 model parameters suggests that our
sample of SNe Ia is representative of typical SNe Ia. We use SN 2025cy as an example case,
and ﬁnd that due to its fast-declining light curve (”m15(B) = 1.62 ± 0.05), it cannot be ﬁt well
by standard models despite being a normal type Ia SN. We also model the early light curve
rise as a power law for a subset of the SNe and ﬁnd average rise indices of ωq = 2.2 ± 0.4 and
ωu = 2.6 ± 0.7.
We constrain host galaxy reddening by deriving relations between the decline rate in the q-band
and q ↑X color at peak, allowing us to estimate the color excess for di!erent ﬁlters. We apply
this method to the reddened supernova SN 2024xal, estimating E(B ↑V )host = 0.39 ± 0.02 and
RV = 2.28±0.68. RV is lower than the average value for the Milky Way, a result that is consistent
with the values of other SNe Ia. We also estimate E(B ↑V )host using EBV model2 in SNooPy
for the 49 SNe in the sample that converge. For SN 2024xal we get E(B ↑V ) = 0.58 ± 0.08, not
fully consistent with the intrinsic color relation method. The distribution of E(B ↑V )host for
the sample seems reasonable and is centered around E(B ↑V )host = 0.1.
We predict that over the next three years, BlackGEM will yield a more statistically valuable
sample of SNe Ia by more than doubling the number of observed SNe Ia.
We also discuss
methodological limitations and propose future improvements, including the use of alternative
light curve models to better separate intrinsic color from host galaxy extinction e!ects as well
as improving the quality of the photometry included in the analyses.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A type Ia supernova (SN Ia) happens when a white dwarf in a close binary system undergoes
an extremely energetic thermonuclear explosion. Due to the established relation between light
curve width and luminosity for SNe Ia (Phillips, 1993), the accelerating expansion of the universe
was discovered (Riess et al., 1998, Perlmutter et al., 1999). The importance of SNe Ia in the
ﬁeld of cosmology cannot be understated. SNe Ia are the most common type of supernova to
be detected and numerous properties of theirs have been studied in depth. However, there are
still important systematic errors associated with the luminosity estimation of SNe Ia, leading
to scatter on the Hubble diagram.
The dominant systematic error is the e!ect of the host
galaxy extinction on the light from the supernovae (Knop et al., 2003). Accounting for the host
extinction is a complicated matter, and requires a way to extract the intrinsic color of supernovae
(e.g. Phillips et al., 1999, Folatelli et al., 2009, Wang et al., 2008). SNe Ia also show a diversity
in the UV (Ellis et al., 2008, Brown et al., 2010) that is still not fully explained.
In this thesis we investigate what analyses of SNe Ia are viable for photometric data from the
BlackGEM telescope array (Groot et al., 2024). BlackGEM is an array of three telescopes in La
Silla, Chile. The telescopes each have a large ﬁeld of view, allowing them to monitor signiﬁcant
parts of the southern sky every night. Along with the u-band, BlackGEM mainly observes in a
broad visual ﬁlter, q, and in the i-band. The u-band is atypical for a wide-ﬁeld survey. We recover
every SN Ia observed by BlackGEM. We pay special attention to the light curves in the u-band
to probe the observed UV diversity in SNe Ia. BlackGEM is ideal for this, especially at early
times. The goal of the thesis is to test and assess di!erent analysis methods and predict what a
future, larger sample may comprise. We also validate the u-band photometry by comparing it
to the results of another survey with a similar UV ﬁlter, the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS)
(York et al., 2000), to ensure that we can trust the results. With the advent of telescopes such as
the Vera C. Rubin Observatory, SNe Ia at higher redshifts will be detected more frequently. The
light received in visual ﬁlters will probe the UV in the rest frame of a high redshift supernova,
so gaining a deeper understanding of the UV emission of SNe Ia is an important endeavor.
The thesis is structured as follows: In chapter 2, we start by introducing BlackGEM. We then
provide an overview of type Ia supernovae and how to model their light curves, with a focus
on the aspects that are still under investigation as of 2025. One example of such an aspect is
the aforementioned diversity in the UV, what causes it and how to model it. Another is how to
account for extinction of the supernova light by dust in its host galaxy, as we also have mentioned.
We therefore also describe astronomical extinction and how it is modeled in the Milky Way and
other galaxies. We speciﬁcally relay e!orts to estimate the host galaxy extinction of other type
Ia supernovae.
We then describe the data we work with in chapter 3. We use photometry from the BlackGEM
transient database as well as forced BlackGEM photometry for select targets.
Our sample
contains 89 type Ia supernovae.
Along with BlackGEM we also have photometry from the

In chapter 4 we go over the di!erent analyses we perform on the data set. We use di!erent light

Chapter 2
Theory
In this chapter we outline the theoretical background needed for the analysis of the u-band light
curves of type Ia supernovae observed by BlackGEM. First, we describe the BlackGEM telescope
array and some of its technical speciﬁcations to get a better understanding of the data we have
from it. We then turn our attention to type Ia supernovae and explain how they are formed, how
they can be modeled and which aspects are still uncertain. We also underline their importance
in the ﬁeld of cosmology. Finally, we dedicate a section to extinction by interstellar dust. The
light from supernovae is attenuated by dust in both the Milky Way as well as the host galaxy
of a supernova, and accounting for this is a very signiﬁcant part of the light curve analysis.
2.1
BlackGEM
BlackGEM (Groot et al., 2024) is a wide-ﬁeld array of optical telescopes currently comprising
three telescopes at the European Southern Observatory in La Silla, Chile along with a prototype
telescope named MeerLICHT (Bloemen et al., 2016) at the Sutherland site of the South African
Astronomical Observatory (SAAO). Each telescope has a ﬁeld of view of 2.7 square degrees and
six ﬁlters, encompassing an optimized Sloan set (u, g, r, i and z) as well as a broad visual ﬁlter
(q). Of these, the u, q and i-bands are mainly used, with observations in the q-band dominating.
We show the ﬁlter transmission proﬁles of these three bands in Figure 2.1.
3
==PAGE:11==
Figure 2.1: The transmission proﬁles (Groot et al., 2024) of the u, q and i-bands of BlackGEM.
The telescopes are sensitive down to a magnitude of →22. Science operations for BlackGEM
commenced in April 2023 and are scheduled to run for ﬁve years.
The main scientiﬁc goal of BlackGEM is to detect the optical counterparts of gravitational waves
(GW) formed by compact object mergers. Due to the large ﬁeld of view as well as the multiple
ﬁlters, BlackGEM is also very well-suited for other areas of time-domain astronomy. The array
therefore also runs di!erent surveys, for example the Local Transient Survey (LTS), where the
telescopes point to ﬁelds with areas of mass over-densities less than 100 Mpc away every night
from twilight and every three hours afterwards. The LTS uses the u, q and i-bands. 50 square
degrees of the night sky is monitored per night. Another survey is the BlackGEM q-scan survey.
In an e!ort to monitor slowly evolving transients (in comparison to GW counterparts) such as
supernovae, BlackGEM will chart > 1000 square degrees of the sky in the q-band every two days
in this survey.
With wide-ﬁeld surveys like the ones performed by BlackGEM, large amounts of data are gath-
ered every single night. Transients are identiﬁed through the procedure for proper image sub-
traction deﬁned in Zackay et al., 2016. A detection is ingested into the transient database if the
signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) exceeds six, meaning SNR > 6. BlackGEM also has a forced pho-
tometry routine available which allows for the retrieval of detections below the SNR threshold.
2.2
Type Ia supernovae
A type Ia supernova (SN Ia) is the thermonuclear explosion of a degenerate white dwarf accreting
matter from a companion in a close, interacting binary system. This distinguishes SNe Ia from
other supernovae where the explosion mechanism is the core collapse of massive > 8M→stars
(CC SNe). SNe Ia are identiﬁed spectrally by the absence of helium and hydrogen and the
presence of strong Si II absorption lines at 6150, 5800 and 4000 ˚A (Filippenko, 1997). They
are the most common type of supernova and are found in all types of galaxies. The lifetime
of a white dwarf is long, making it possible for the binary system to be o!set far from its host
galaxy before reaching the supernova stage. A SN Ia is an extremely energetic event, typically
4
==PAGE:12==
reaching an absolute B-band magnitude of →↑19 (Hamuy et al., 1996), many billion times
brighter than the absolute magnitude of the Sun. SNe Ia, like CC SNe, play a major part in the
nucleosynthesis of iron-group elements and approximately half the iron present in the universe
can be attributed to SNe Ia (Maoz et al., 2017).
The light curves of SNe Ia can be standardized, leading to the Phillip’s relation (see subsec-
tion 2.2.2) between absolute magnitude and light curve shape (Phillips, 1993).
SNe Ia can
therefore be used as extragalactic distance indicators, making them extremely important within
astronomy in general, but especially cosmology. The acceleration of the expansion of the uni-
verse was discovered via a sample of SNe Ia used to measure cosmic distances (Riess et al.,
1998), (Perlmutter et al., 1999). These results eventually led to the 2011 Nobel prize in physics.
Further work with SNe Ia has sought to decrease the uncertainty on the Hubble diagram and is
still ongoing. Gaining a ﬁner understanding of type Ia supernovae is therefore a step towards
understanding the universe itself better.
2.2.1
Companion and explosion mechanism
Despite the abundance of type Ia supernovae observed, the nature of the progenitor system
and the explosion mechanism remains poorly understood. In most cases the progenitors cannot
be observed, meaning knowledge about the companion must be inferred from the supernova
explosion itself. There are two possible conﬁgurations of the binary system (Liu et al., 2023).
One is a white dwarf with a non-degenerate companion, meaning a normal star where fusion still
takes place. This is called a single-degenerate system. The other option is a double-degenerate
system where the mass donor is another white dwarf. As for the explosion itself, the current
models can also be divided into two categories. Explosions happening at masses approaching
the Chandrasekhar limit (↓1.4M→) and explosions at masses signiﬁcantly below the limit.
In the single-degenerate scenario, the white dwarf accretes hydrogen / helium-rich through Roche
Lobe overﬂow. This process continues until the white dwarf approaches the Chandrasekhar limit
and a thermonuclear explosion is ignited (Whelan et al., 1973). The supernova ejecta and the
companion can interact in the early epochs, leading to excess emission in the light curves (Kasen,
2009). We elaborate on this e!ect in subsection 2.2.2. The Chandrasekhar-mass explosion in
the single-degenerate conﬁguration can be modeled in di!erent ways.
In the favored model
for normal SNe Ia, the delayed detonation model (Khokhlov, 1989), the burning starts as a
deﬂagration. This aligns with the detected presence of intermediate-mass elements in SNe Ia,
as a prompt supersonic detonation would instead only produce iron-group elements (Liu et al.,
2023). After a period of expansion, the white dwarf then detonates. In this model, the predicted
mass of 56Ni produced ﬁts well with what has been observed for supernovae.
For a double-degenerate binary system, there are multiple paths to a SN Ia explosion. The
ﬁrst studies investigating this scenario predict a SN Ia if the merger of two C+O white dwarfs
produces an object with a mass exceeding the Chandrasekhar limit (Webbink, 1984). Another
possibility is the ”violent merger model” (Pakmor et al., 2012), where carbon detonation can
be set o! directly through the interaction between the debris of the companion white dwarf
with the primary white dwarf. A third path towards explosion can occur if the companion is a
pure He white dwarf. By accumulating a He shell on top of the primary white dwarf, an initial
detonation of He can be triggered. This can in turn trigger a detonation of the C core near the
center of the white dwarf, leading to a SN Ia. Such an explosion will happen at masses below
the Chandrasekhar limit and is therefore called the sub-Chandrasekhar-mass double detonation
model (Fink et al., 2007).
2.2.2
Light curve properties
While a thermonuclear disruption ignites a type Ia supernova, the continued emission over the
course of a few months is mainly powered by the di!usion of the energy produced by radioactive

decay of nickel synthesized in the explosion (Truran et al., 1967 and Colgate et al., 1969). The
decay chain is as follows
56Ni ↔56Co + e+ + εe
56Co ↔56Fe + e+ + εe
(2.1)
Here we see how the iron production occurs, as 56Fe is a stable isotope. The decay of 56Ni
(with a half-life of six days) powers the light curve around the time of peak, while the tail of the
light curve is mainly driven by 56Co decay, which has a half-life of 77 days. The luminosity of
the supernova can be directly related to the nickel mass, with brighter SNe Ia producing larger
amounts of nickel. In Figure 2.2 we show a comparison between observed light curves of SNe Ia
and model light curves for di!erent values of 56Ni mass.

di!erent amounts of nickel (0.4, 0.6 and 0.8M→) compared to UVOIR light curves of three SNe

Phillips’ relation
In Phillips, 1993, a quantitative relation between the absolute B-band magnitude and light curve

Early rise
The time before the peak of a supernova Ia is of great interest. Observations around the time of
ﬁrst light can provide insights into the nature of the progenitor system. In Miller et al., 2020b,
they model the early rise of a SN Ia as a power law using a sample of 127 SNe observed by ZTF
in the g and r bands. The early ﬂux is given by
fX(t) = C + H[tfl]AX(t ↑tfl)ωX

by →2.5 mag in the three days post-discovery, followed by a slower, gradual rise and fall as
expected from a typical SN. They also detect the ﬂash in the optical. They do not deﬁnitively
attribute the ﬂash to interaction with a non-degenerate companion, but they highlight it as a
likely explanation.

2.2.4
SNe Ia in the UV
In this thesis, we are particularly interested in investigating the u-band observations of type Ia
supernovae that probe the UV region of the emission. Many studies have revealed a considerable
diversity in the rest frame UV spectra of SNe Ia that is not fully understood. The UV region is
dominated by a large number of metal absorption lines from the iron-group elements produced
in the explosion, leading to a blanketing e!ect of the radiation (Walker et al., 2012).
The
metallicity of the progenitor inﬂuences the absorption lines and thus the appearance of the UV
spectra. The UV is also a!ected more severely by interstellar extinction in the Milky Way and
the SN host galaxy. In Ellis et al., 2008, a set of optical spectra of 36 intermediate redshift
(z ↗0.5) SNe Ia probing the UV is studied. They ﬁnd that even when using metal-dependent
models, the diversity in the UV colors is larger than what the models can account for. In Brown
et al., 2010, the absolute magnitudes of 14 nearby SNe Ia (z = 0.004 ↑0.027) observed by Swift
in the UV are examined. They ﬁnd signiﬁcant di!erences between the photometric bands. The
ﬁlter covering the mid-UV shows a higher degree of scatter than the near-UV ﬁlters do. For
near-UV they ﬁnd that the peak absolute magnitudes are correlated with the optical decay rate
(”m15(B)) with a scatter of →0.4 mag, similar to but slightly higher than the typical scatter for
optical bands (→0.2 mag). The near-UV ﬁlters can therefore be used for measuring luminosity
distances when combined with optical data. For mid-UV the scatter is →1 mag, much higher.

2010 is very small, so the conclusions from this paper may change with a larger set of SNe Ia.
As mentioned in subsection 2.2.2, it is also in the UV where the excess emission caused by
interaction between the SN ejecta and the companion is predicted to be the most prominent.
This is another motivational factor for understanding the typical UV emission better.

2.4
Dust extinction
As the light from a supernova travels through its host galaxy and the Milky Way, it will interact
with interstellar dust grains. The light is scattered or absorbed by the dust. The combination
of these e!ects is called extinction. The level of extinction is dependent on the wavelength of
the light as well as the optical properties of the dust along a given line of sight. For the use of
type Ia supernovae in cosmology, the e!ects of host galaxy extinction remains the main system-
atic contribution to the error budget (Knop et al., 2003, Amanullah et al., 2015). Interstellar
extinction was ﬁrst observed for open stellar clusters by Trumpler, 1930. Shorter wavelengths
of light are extinguished to a higher degree than longer wavelengths, leading to the term ”red-
dening” for extinction. The reddening of an object is typically quantiﬁed by the color excess
E(B ↑V ) = (B ↑V )observed ↑(B ↑V )intrinsic deﬁned with respect to the B and V ﬁlters.
Measuring the color excess therefore requires knowledge of the intrinsic color of an object. The
extinction along a line of sight can be estimated with the so-called pair method (Draine, 2003).
Comparing the spectrophotometry of two stars of the same spectral class allows for the determi-
nation of extinction, Aε, as a function of wavelength, ϑ, provided one of the stars has negligible
foreground dust.
2.4.1

on data from the ultraviolet to the near-infrared. The law depends on only one parameter, the
total-to-selective absorption, RV = A(V )/E(B ↑V ). The actual functional expression of the
extinction law comprises di!erent segments depending on the wavelength considered. The mean
law takes the form

2.4.2
Interstellar extinction in other galaxies
While the extinction of light in the Milky Way can generally be parameterized with RV = 3.1,
this is not true for every galaxy. In Draine, 2003, the optical properties of the dust in the Small
Magellanic Cloud (SMC) and the Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) have been investigated. As
for the Milky Way, RV also depends on the speciﬁc line of sight for the LMC and SMC. Average
values for the LMC and SMC have been derived in Wang et al., 2023. For the LMC they get
RV ↓3.4 and for the SMC RV ↓2.5.
However, in Gordon et al., 2003 they ﬁnd that for
many lines of sight, the Cardelli et al., 1989 law does not ﬁt the observed extinction in both
the LMC and SMC. This result should be kept in mind when modeling the extinction of more
distant galaxies, where the Cardelli et al., 1989 and Fitzpatrick, 1999 laws naturally are the
most common choices.
For this project we are interested in determining the extinction from the host galaxies of SNe
Ia. The host galaxies are far away, so individual stars are hard to resolve. Therefore, di!erent
methods than for the Milky Way must be employed to estimate the extinction. We describe and
apply a number of methods in chapter 4 and chapter 5. Many studies have aimed to constrain
the extinction laws governing the host galaxies of SNe Ia with conﬂicting results. In general, the
Cardelli et al., 1989 and Fitzpatrick, 1999 laws have been used with success. There are many
cases where the host galaxies of SNe Ia favor lower RV values, implying a steeper extinction law.
An example is Nobili et al., 2008, where the average is RV = 1.7 for a sample of supernovae.
When only considering the SNe with moderate to low reddening (E(B ↑V )host < 0.25), they
recover an even lower average of RV = 1.01. These results are in clear contrast to what Burns
et al., 2014 ﬁnd. They determine an average RV ↓3 when only including low extinction SNe,

4.2.1
Polynomials
To get an initial sense of the properties of the supernova sample we work with, we use third
or fourth order polynomials to ﬁt the light curve data points for each ﬁlter around the time of
maximum light. The order depends on the speciﬁc light curve. The ﬁts help in carving out a path
to more sophisticated analyses. They enable us to get quick estimates of ”m15 for a given ﬁlter
as well as the color between ﬁlters around the time of peak brightness. The polynomial models
are not motivated by any physical background, but merely by the typical shape of the supernova
light curve. We ﬁt the following two polynomial models with NumPy’s function polyfit
m = at3 + bt2 + ct + d, where t < tpeak + 25
m = at4 + bt3 + ct2 + dt + e, where t < tpeak + 25
(4.1)
Where m is the apparent magnitude and t is the time in days/MJD. We use the polynomial
models alongside Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling (see section 4.4) to determine the absolute
magnitude at peak luminosity for a selection of the SNe. For the ﬁlter X, it is given by
MX = mX ↑5 log10(dpc) + 5 ↑AX
(4.2)

BlackGEM bands.

4.2.3
SNooPy
We also ﬁt the light curves using models in the Python package SNooPy (Burns et al., 2010).
As for sncosmo, the transmission proﬁles for the BlackGEM ﬁlters are not included in the

In SNooPy, the input data to their light curve ﬁtter also needs to follow a speciﬁc
tabular format. The ﬁrst row of the table has to contain the name of the supernova, the redshift
and the coordinates. In SNooPy, K-corrections, time dilation and Milky Way extinction are
automatically accounted for.
As described in section 2.3, there are multiple available light curve models in SNooPy, and we
use EBV model and EBV model2. We mainly use EBV model2 parameterized by the color-stretch
sBV rather than by ”m15(B), as this produces the best results. More SNe converge and the
ﬁts generally look better. EBV model2 ﬁts four parameters. Tmax, the time of peak B-band
brightness, sBV , the distance modulus DM and the host galaxy reddening E(B ↑V )host. The
total-to-selective absorption RX for the ﬁlter X is not free, but is a member variable instead. We
ﬁnd that the combination of three broad bands q, o and c is detrimental to the ﬁts. An example
of how the ATLAS data produces unnatural ﬁt results is shown in Figure C.3. Including the
ATLAS data also caused the estimations of E(B ↑V )host to skew more blue. In some cases
E(B↑V )host is signiﬁcantly below 0, which cannot be interpreted physically in a way that makes
sense. We therefore discard the ATLAS data in the ﬁts.
We use the SNooPy function fit() to produce the results. It uses the Levenberg-Marquardt
algorithm (Marquardt, 1963) to minimize the variance-weighted residuals between the data and
the model. It is a non-linear least-squares algorithm.

Rise

4.3
Host galaxy dust estimation
In section 2.4, we describe the challenges associated with accounting for the presence of host
galaxy reddening in the light curves of type Ia supernova. In this thesis, we test multiple methods
to estimate this reddening. We try methods that only use the light curve data itself as well as
a method where additional information about the environment of each supernova is included.
4.3.1
SALT2
In sncosmo, a dust component in the rest frame of the supernova can be set, exactly in the same
manner as for Milky Way reddening in the observer frame. Di!erent extinction laws are possible,
and the reddening can technically be included as a free parameter in the ﬁtting procedure. We
ﬁnd, however, that the sncosmo fit lc function does not constrain the host galaxy reddening
very well for the cases we have tested. There is no obvious minimum in the reduced ς2 values of
the ﬁt when varying E(B ↑V ) and RV . Adding a dust component to SALT2, however, is a quick
way to gauge by eye what approximate level of reddening a SN light curve might be subject to.
4.3.2
SNooPy
As described in section 4.2 the models EBV model and EBV model2 in SNooPy include the host
color excess as a parameter to be ﬁt. It is therefore enough to ﬁt these models to the light
curves of the SN sample to get a distribution of E(B ↑V )host values. Whether the values are
reliable is not easy to answer. We therefore also test out a more manual approach described in
the following subsection.
4.3.3
Intrinsic color
We also attempt to use a more elaborate method to estimate the host galaxy extinction as
described in Folatelli et al., 2009. In this paper, they select a subset of SNe Ia that they deem to
su!er from little to no reddening in the host galaxy. These are supernovae that have large o!sets
from their host galaxy or that reside in elliptical or S0 galaxies which are thought to contain
negligible dust (Folatelli et al., 2009). While these requirements are quite straightforward to
implement, for our sample we end up using more of an iterative approach. For each supernova,
we have identiﬁed the host galaxy and noted its morphology (when available) and o!set from the
SN. With this information, we make an initial partition of the supernovae into three categories.
A supernova is either assigned a ”Yes”, ”Maybe” or ”No”, answering whether it is added to the
22
==PAGE:30==
low-reddening sample. We then include every ”Yes” and ”Maybe” supernova in the analysis,
and if we see a SN behaving very di!erently from the others in the sample, we check its light
curve and remove it from the subset if it looks reddened. As described in chapter 2, the presence
of circumstellar material can also give rise to reddening. This is a challenge associated with
the method that we will expand on in chapter 6. We ﬁt the light curves of the SNe in the
low reddening sample using SALT2 and use the results to determine ”m15(q) and the q ↑X at
maximum brightness for both bands. We then use MCMC sampling to estimate the uncertainties
on ”m15(q) and q ↑X. We set Gaussian priors on light curve shape parameters, as detailed in
section 4.4.
With our ﬁnal sample of SNe with low host galaxy reddening, we continue to follow Folatelli
et al., 2009 by deriving linear relations between ”m15(q) and the q ↑X colors at maximum
for each ﬁlter X with detections for the given supernova. We do this using orthogonal distance
regression (ODR) (Boggs et al., 1990) as there are uncertainties associated with both the decline
rate and the colors. A major deviation from Folatelli et al., 2009 is that we use the q-band as the
reference band, while they use the V -band. We choose the q-band as this is the only ﬁlter that
is guaranteed to have detections for every supernova. The wavelength coverage of the V -band
is also contained in q. The linear relations we obtain are then a measure of the intrinsic color of
a type Ia supernova as a function of the shape of the q-band light curve. We account for Milky
Way extinction and redshift e!ects as described in section 4.2.

6.1
Photometry
All of the analyses done in this thesis depend on having data that is reliable in quality. However,
we encounter di!erent challenges in working with the data set, so we want to discuss the merit
of the choices we have made as well as what can be improved.
6.1.1
BlackGEM
As for the BlackGEM data, we ﬁnd it to be generally of a satisfactory quality. We do notice
one phenomenon that is slightly worrying. For many supernova light curves there are three
detections per night. Around the time of maximum light, the detections in the same night show
signiﬁcant scatter.
An example of this is the SALT2 ﬁt for SN 2023xqm (Figure 5.2).
This
behavior is peculiar, since it occurs at a time where the SNR is at its highest. It is yet unclear
what the exact cause of this is, but it is likely an e!ect of a processing step in the photometry
pipeline. The scatter does not negatively impact the light curve ﬁt parameters, but we assume
that it worsens the goodness of ﬁt. A way to mitigate this is to bin the data from the same
night. This would be ﬁne to do for global light curve analyses, such as determining the shape
parameters. If we want to study the early rise, however, it is beneﬁcial to have multiple data
points per night as there may be e!ects in the rise that occur on short timescales. However, as
mentioned, the scatter seems to be worse for higher ﬂux, meaning it will not be as prominent
for the early rise.
Towards the end of the project we added forced BlackGEM photometry to a number of the SNe.
This was pivotal for analyzing the early rise, as it enables us to establish a baseline ﬂux by going
below the SNR limit of six that is imposed on the detections in the transient database. It is also
useful for getting a better temporal coverage in the u-band speciﬁcally, as the limiting magnitude
for the u-band is much worse than for the other bands. At the moment, producing BlackGEM
forced photometry is not as fast a process as it is for ATLAS, e.g. Running the procedure for
50 targets on a virtual machine takes approximately seven hours. Doing it locally would be so
much slower that it would not have been feasible within the time frame of the project. We also
include the forced photometry in the other analyses. We want to know whether it will make
sense in the future to run the forced photometry routine on every target by default. If it does
not improve the results signiﬁcantly, then the cost in terms of computing time is possibly too
high. We therefore calculate the SALT2 ﬁts both with and without the forced photometry to
investigate the e!ects on the resulting model parameters x1 and c. The comparison is shown in

6.2
Light curve models
We make use of two main light curve models in this thesis, SALT2 (Guy et al., 2007) and
EBV model2 in SNooPy (Burns et al., 2010). While we can ﬁt the light curves of most SNe in
our sample, there is a signiﬁcant portion that fail. We want to discuss whether the number of
converging supernovae can possibly be increased. For many of the SNe that cannot be ﬁt, the
issue is clear. There is not enough data from the epochs around the peak of the light curve.
Naturally, the shape of the light curve cannot be constrained from only the tail. This is not the
case for every SN that fails, however. We will also discuss the ﬁt results in general.
6.2.1
Fitting procedure
For both SALT2 and EBV model2 we ﬁt the light curves through minimization of ς2, as this is
the default in the standard ﬁtting functions in sncosmo and SNooPy. However, both packages
include ﬁtting functions that use MCMC methods to estimate the model parameters. We did
not test this in this project, but it would certainly be of interest to do so. ς2 minimizers can have
multiple local minimum solutions. The uncertainties associated with the solution parameters
are also based solely on the goodness-of-ﬁt and the ς2 distribution (Aster et al., 2012). These
problems are avoided by using MCMC methods to sample the model space. It is not guaranteed
that using MCMC methods will increase the number of converging SNe. But by improving the
uncertainty estimation on the model parameters and exploring the entire model space, it is likely
to yield better results in any case.
6.2.2
SALT2 vs. SNooPy
SALT2 has become the predominant light curve model of SNe Ia due to its ﬂexibility. We ﬁnd that
SALT2 successfully ﬁts a larger number of SNe in the sample than EBV model2. For EBV model2
there are less SNe that produce good ﬁts. In many cases, the model light curves display strange
behavior. This is generally the case when including the ATLAS photometry (see Figure C.3),
and so we removed this data for all SNe. For some SNe EBV model2 also struggles when there
are multiple q-band data points per night, producing nonsensical ﬁts. We therefore exercise a
higher degree of caution when using SNooPy and thus we want to evaluate the results from the
ﬁts that we do include in the results. In Figure 5.5 we compare ”m15(B) from EBV model2 with
”m15(q) from SALT2, and see a positive correlation. We want to compare those results with
synthetically generated ”m15 examples. We generate ”m15 for the q and B-band by randomly
selecting combinations of x1 and c and using them to produce synthetic light curves in q and B
with SALT2 with z = 0. We mimic the distributions of x1 and c in Rigault et al., 2025 to get
combinations that are likely to have been observed for real SNe. The comparison is shown in

6.4
Host galaxy dust estimation
We test two main methods of estimating the reddening caused by the host galaxy of the SNe in
our sample. They each have merits and drawbacks which we will discuss in this section. There
is no clear superior technique, as it is a complicated endeavor requiring many assumptions.
6.4.1
Intrinsic color relations
The procedure described in Folatelli et al., 2009 that we copy for the BlackGEM sample relies
on a number of assumptions that can be challenged. We ﬁrst want to mention the selection
of a low reddening subsample. We select the SNe that either reside in elliptical or lenticular
galaxies or that are clearly o!set from the host. As has been detected for SN 2006X (Patat
et al., 2007), circumstellar material can be present in the progenitor system of the SN and can
cause signiﬁcant reddening. This material is likely to have been accreted from the companion
star. Such a scenario is also possible for a SN that otherwise fulﬁlls the low reddening criteria.
This is a risk in the method. We also do not impose a threshold on the o!set, however, making
the selection process more lax than desired. We could be more rigorous by choosing a minimum
o!set, e.g.
10 kpc.
In Uddin et al., 2020, they ﬁnd a 50% ↑60% reduction in the Hubble
diagram dispersion when only studying SNe o!set by at least 10 kpc from the host, suggesting
a lower level of host galaxy reddening for this sample. There is therefore some value in choosing
a low reddening sample. In Figure 5.11, for the bands with the most SNe, g, c, o, r, many of
the SNe outside of the low reddening sample are clearly redder, while the distribution of the
low reddening sample is fairly tight. This brings us to the next assumption we want to discuss.
Second, expecting a linear relation between ”m15(q) and the colors at maximum, q ↑X is not
very well supported by the results of this thesis. One could argue that the ﬁlters g, c, o, r show
a linear dependence on ”m15(q). This is not especially surprising, as the q-band is so broad
that these four ﬁlters are either fully engulfed by it (c, g, r) or the majority of the ﬁlter proﬁle
overlaps with it (o). They therefore probe the same parts of the SN spectrum. The slopes of
the relations for these ﬁlters are also very close to 0, with o having the highest slope, as seen
in Table 5.4. When we consider the ﬁlters that do not share a signiﬁcant wavelength interval
with q, the u and i-bands, the linear relations are very dubious. For the u-band there are only
four data points and the supernovae outside the low reddening showing a high degree of scatter.
While we expect this result, it makes it challenging to derive a defensible linear relation. Another
curious feature of the q ↑u plot is that there are more SNe that are above the linear relation
than below, meaning they are bluer than the low reddening sample. As for the i-band, there is
also signiﬁcant scatter in general and within the low reddening sample. The i-band also su!ers
from a small sample size. We expect the slope of the linear ﬁt to be positive and higher than
for the q ↑o relation. We get a slightly negative slope, however, along with several SNe that are
bluer than the low reddening sample. This is a surprising result as the i-band is not known to
show as much diversity as the u-band. It would be of great interest to repeat this analysis for a
much larger sample size. We nonetheless move forward by using the linear relations to estimate
the color excess for a reddened supernova, SN 2024xal. The large uncertainties on the linear
ﬁt parameters in turn give large errors on the E(q ↑X) estimates. To ﬁt an extinction law to
the color excess we have to make a choice for the e!ective wavelength of each ﬁlter included
47
==PAGE:55==
in the analysis to use for the x-axis value. To do this properly, the ﬁlter response needs to
be integrated over the SED of the observed object. We do not do this, instead we make the
simple choice of using the pivot wavelength as stated on the SVO Filter Proﬁle Service (Rodrigo
et al., 2020) for ATLAS and ZTF, and the middle wavelength of the transmission proﬁles of the
BlackGEM ﬁlters. For the q-band this is almost identical to the canonical e!ective wavelength
of the V -band (0.55 µm), which is the reference band used in Folatelli et al., 2009. This choice
might not reﬂect reality, and changing the value of the e!ective wavelength actually impacts the
results signiﬁcantly, especially for the reference band q. A future analysis might use a di!erent
ﬁlter as the reference to see how the results are a!ected. For SN 2024xal we recover a lower
value of RV = 2.28 ± 0.68 than the average Milky Way value of 3.1. This result falls in line with
the results for other type Ia SNe (Wang et al., 2008, Hicken et al., 2009) where values of RV < 2
are found to generally be favored.
6.4.2
SNooPy
We also estimate E(B ↑V )host in SNooPy with the EBV model2, as shown in Figure 5.13. For
the model to estimate the host galaxy reddening, it needs a way to determine the intrinsic color
of a SN Ia to extract the host contribution to the light curves. By looking at the source code
for EBV model2, we ﬁnd that the intrinsic color relations used are in fact the ones derived in
Folatelli et al., 2009 that we used for reproducing the SN 2005A and SN 2006X color excess
plots (Figure 4.1). This means that using EBV model2 also relies on a selection of low reddening
supernovae, albeit from an entirely di!erent sample. The relations in Folatelli et al., 2009 are
not based on a particularly large sample of supernovae (< 15 for every ﬁlter combination), and
this thus calls into question the validity of using the model. Another aspect of EBV model2 is
that the total-to-selective absorption is a member variable, and is therefore not included in the
ﬁt. Instead, there are six di!erent calibrations available. We have used the default, which is
RV = 1.01 ± 0.31. This is a very low value which may a!ect the resulting E(B ↑V )host, as the
two parameters are highly correlated. It may explain the discrepancy in the estimation of the
host reddening of SN 2024xal. It would be worthwhile to change the calibration and consider
the di!erences.
6.4.3
Other methods
We describe other SN Ia light curve models in section 2.3. Both MLCS2k2 (Jha et al., 2007)
and BayeSN (Mandel et al., 2021) allow for estimations of the host galaxy reddening. Especially
BayeSN is interesting since it uses a Bayesian framework which can be a powerful tool. If we
can use these models on BlackGEM data, they could provide results that we can use instead or
compare to our current results. Currently, the model is only valid from B through NIR bands,
excluding the UV from being included. Testing MLCS2k2 on our sample would be a fairly simple
task, as it is integrated in sncosmo.
An interesting scenario would be if there were BlackGEM observations of so-called sibling su-
pernovae. This is when at least two supernovae occur in the same galaxy. It has been recorded
multiple times for SNe Ia, e.g. in Gallego-Cano et al., 2022 for SN 1974G and SN 2021J, in
Biswas et al., 2022 for AT 2019lcj and SN 2020aewj, and in Burns et al., 2020 for SN 2013aa and
SN 2017cbv. In Biswas et al., 2022 the two supernovae occurred within 200 days of each other.
When two supernovae are observed in the same galaxy, many systematic e!ects due to distance
and velocity are eliminated and the host galaxy dust content can be investigated more directly.
In Biswas et al., 2022 they ﬁnd a 2 mag di!erence in the peak B-band luminosity. The stretch
values are similar for the two, while one has a signiﬁcantly red c-value suggesting that dust in the
host is causing the di!erence in brightness. This result highlights that the distribution of dust
in a galaxy naturally is not uniform. The likelihood of a set of sibling supernovae being observed
during the operational period of BlackGEM is small, but we will keep our ﬁngers crossed.

6.5
Future work
Aside from the improvement suggestions that have already been discussed in the sections pre-
ceding this one, there are several other areas within the project that could be expanded upon
or investigated more thoroughly in the future. We will discuss some of them in the following
subsections.
6.5.1
Type Ia SNe
Based on the conservative predictions in section 5.2, the total sample size of SNe Ia will more
than double in the next three years. This will allow for more statistically sound analyses, e.g.
in estimating the host galaxy extinction as discussed in section 6.4. As for the u-band, the total
sample with at least ﬁve detections, will comprise approximately 50 SNe. This is not exactly
a large sample to be able to draw conclusions about the diversity in the u-band from, but it
is not insigniﬁcant either. Additionally, in combination with other SNe Ia observed in the UV,
however, it may be a valuable addition. A key advantage of the BlackGEM u-band is that it
can cover very early epochs of the SNe. Phenomena occurring during the rise of the SNe, such
as the UV ﬂashes (Miller et al., 2020a) mentioned earlier, are more likely to be discovered with
the advent of BlackGEM.
6.5.2
Hosts
For each SN in the sample we also identify its host galaxy when possible. We only use the o!set
and galaxy morphology to select the low reddening subset. Other properties of a SN Ia are
related to host galaxy observables, however. It is well known, e.g. that the host galaxy type
is correlated with the luminosity and thus light curve width of a SN Ia (Lampeitl et al., 2010).
Faint SNe Ia favor passive host galaxies, while brighter SNe Ia are more likely to be found in
star-forming galaxies. We could re-conﬁrm this trend using the BlackGEM sample. However, to
utilize the host galaxy information better, it is also necessary to develop an automatized method
for identifying the host. In this project, we pick out the host galaxies completely manually,
a time-consuming process. It is most likely possible to extract search results from NED using
scripts, but we did not look into this during the project.
An identiﬁcation procedure could
then be set up based on matching the redshifts and/or minimizing the angular separation to the
potential host. Great care would have to be taken to construct this procedure, as crowded ﬁelds
or SNe with large o!sets complicate the selection.
6.5.3
Core collapse SNe
This project has focused on SNe Ia. BlackGEM naturally also detects core collapse SNe, and it
would therefore be a logical progression to explore analysis methods of these. They are detected
less frequently than SNe Ia, so the sample size would be smaller. The light curves of CC SNe
are also much more diverse than for SNe Ia.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this thesis we explore the light curves of type Ia supernovae observed by BlackGEM with
a focus on the u-band. We validate the quality of the u-band photometry by comparing the
magnitude of the calibration stars used by BlackGEM with the SDSS u-band magnitude. We
ﬁnd increasing scatter for fainter sources, but still at an acceptable level.
We successfully extract the current sample of SNe Ia from the BlackGEM transient database. As
of spring 2025, there are →90 SNe Ia with 5+ detections above SNR = 6. Based on the numbers
for the ﬁrst six months of 2025 only, where operations in BlackGEM have largely followed the
same procedure as planned for the next three years, we predict that the sample will grow by 130
SNe in three years. Out of these, 30 will have 5+ u-band detections, 40 will have good coverage
in q-band before the time of maximum light and 15 will be highly reddened. Analyses with the
future sample will produce results that are statistically more signiﬁcant.
For our sample we also add photometry from other telescopes when available, mainly from
ATLAS and ZTF, but also from SWIFT and LCO for SN 2025cy speciﬁcally.
We test several analysis methods for the sample of supernovae, focusing on ﬁtting the light
curves using di!erent models and estimating the host galaxy dust extinction. For the light curve
models, we mainly use SALT2 as implemented in sncosmo and the EBV model2 in SNooPy. We
ﬁnd that BlackGEM data, despite having less typical photometric ﬁlters, can be ﬁt nicely with
both models, with SALT2 being the more reliable model. The combination of the BlackGEM q-
band and the ATLAS bands, all three quite broad ﬁlters, seems to throw o! the SNooPy models.
We ﬁnd that the distribution of SALT2 parameters aligns nicely with the much larger sample of
Rigault et al., 2025 of SNe Ia, suggesting that the current sample is fairly representative of the
typical population of SNe Ia.
We also model the early rise of a subset of the SNe in the q and u-bands and ﬁnd considerable
diversity in the results.
However, the average values of the rise indices ωq = 2.2 ± 0.4 and
ωu = 2.6±0.7 are consistent with a value of →2 as expected from models of the early expansion
of a type Ia SNe.
For each supernova in the sample we estimate the ”m15(q) parameter and the colors at peak
magnitude to derive linear relations between the two parameters as a way to estimate the intrinsic
colors of the SNe. We use these relations to determine the color excess for a highly reddened
supernova, SN 2024xal. We recover a host galaxy reddening value of E(B ↑V )host = 0.39±0.02
by ﬁtting the Fitzpatrick et al., 2019 extinction law to the color excess and total-to-selective
absorption of RV = 2.28 ± 0.68. We compare this result to the estimate from the EBV model2
in SNooPy where we get E(B ↑V )host = 0.58 ± 0.08. While not consistent, both methods agree
that there is a signiﬁcant amount of reddening.
We also validate most of the analyses using the third CSP data release of 134 type Ia supernovae
for which extensive studies have already been undertaken.

We discuss the limitations of the analyses and ﬁnd that there are several areas of potential
improvement.
The data ingested into the light curve models can be improved by removing
errant data points and binning over the nights. Our methods for estimating the host galaxy
extinction are also rife with dubious assumptions.
We wish to try other light curve models
that disentangle the intrinsic color and host galaxy reddening such as MLCS2k2 and BayeSN to
compare our results.
