Abstract
This thesis documents the development, fabrication and measurement of a capacitive micro-
machined ultrasonic transducers (CMUTs) that exploits the good bonding capability of the pho-
tosensitive BenzoCycloButene (BCB 4022-25) resin from Dow Chemical. A large part of this work
rests upon the characterization of the photosensitive BCB 4022-25 resin for the special purpose
of CMUT fabrications. Using this resin, one can expect to reduce the manufacturing time to
a week for a fully functional device, as opposed to several weeks with the LOCOS based pro-
cess used currently to design CMUTs in the DTU MEMS Applied Sensors group. In addition, all
fabrication steps required equipment commonly available in the micro-electromechanical sys-
tems (MEMS) industries. The characterization of the resin has shown that the suggested curing
time by Dow Chemical was not long enough to obtain a fully cured resin. Low breakdown ﬁeld
could be measured when using standard proposed annealing time suggesting a low cross-link
of the polymer. The suggested time must be increased from 60 minutes to 420 minutes to ob-
tain a full cross-link of the polymer chains, hence allowing a larger breakdown. The capability of
the device to vibrate at a frequency of 6.9 MHz is demonstrated. Initial results suggest that the
introduced fabrication based on BCB 4022-25 is an appealing method for transducers manufac-
turing. In addition, the fabrication method reduces signiﬁcantly the buckling effect observed
on the LOCOS process, thus allowing a larger fabrication yield.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The understanding of human anatomy and its functioning is a fundamental science that has
never ceased to intrigued human curiosity. Early researches on this subject involving human
body were already reported in 1600 BC through a papyrus, today called the Edwin Smith Pa-
pyrus. This document describes, with a large degree of medical science; wounds, fractures or
tumors in the ancient Egypt [1]. Since this early work, sacriﬁcial human victims and animal
have been used through the ages to improve the understanding of human anatomy and dis-
eases, and it is commonly known today as medical science . Nowadays, the mission of medical
imaging is to provide a view of the internal body to observe and measure the complex dynamic
and static system of a living body, without the need of surgery. Several techniques are used
to reach this goal, and ultrasonic imaging is one of them. Online historical researches, based
on the number of publication per year treating the subjects "Ultrasonic Imaging" or "Capac-
itive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer (CMUT)" 1, shows that non-destructive diagnosis
tools for ultrasonic images, primarily based on piezoelectric transducer technology, have al-
ready started in the middle of the past century (Figure 1.1). It is only in the late 90’s and early
2000 that the number of publications involving Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Trans-
ducer have shown a sharp increase. Igal Ladabaum et al. [2] reported in 1998, that many of the
current piezoelectric based transducer problems could be overcome by the use of CMUT. This
latter shows larger bandwidth, higher frequency range and better resolution than piezoelectric
transducers. These abilities lead to a promising future in the ﬁeld of sensing application and
medical imaging, and today, the use of CMUT devices to create 3D imaging or 4D imaging (time
resolving) has become a reality.
This chapter introduces a state of the art in the ﬁeld of ultrasonic medical imaging systems.
An explanation of the basic functioning of piezoelectric and CMUT device is explained and the
project motivation and objectives are described. Finally, the scientiﬁc approach and the struc-
ture of the thesis is outlined.

1.1
Medical Ultrasonic Imaging
Over the last decades, different medical imaging equipments have been developed with their
own advantages and drawbacks, but with the same aim to achieve non invasive internal body
imaging. Equipments exist to perform either 2-D or 3-D imaging and recent devices have in-
troduced the 4th dimension allowing to resolve a volumetric images real-time. Today’s massive
medical equipments such as MRI, CT scan or X-Rays Radiography can produce images with
a level of resolution that an ultrasonic (US) based equipment would not be able to rival with.
However, the inoffensive ultra-sound and the portability of the US equipments, together with
the possibility to integrated real-time images, makes the ultrasonics imaging a valuable tech-
nique.
1.1.1
Physics principles
Sound corresponds to a mechanical disturbance caused by a pressure wave traveling through a
medium. When the frequency at which the wave propagates through the medium is between
20 Hz to 20 000 Hz, the sounds is perceptible for human ear. Below and above these limits, we
enter respectively in the infrasonic and ultrasonic inaudible sound wave regime. In medical
imaging, frequencies in the ultrasonic range of 1MHz to 20MHz are commonly used. The prop-
agation velocity of a wave in a medium will depend of the density and stiffness of this latter.
For very low density media, the wave will travel relatively slow due to the lack of neighbour-
ing molecules required to propagate the sounds wave. In solid media, the opposite effect takes
place and the wave travels faster. Liquids exhibit propagation speeds between low and high
density, and most of the biologic tissues can be assimilate to liquid. The exception are bones,
exhibiting a very large density as opposed to lungs showing a low density due to the air content

1.1.2
Image formation
When an ultrasound wave travels across a medium, it may encounter obstacles. At the bound-
ary of these obstacles, the wave will be partially reﬂected and transmitted, depending on the
difference between acoustic impedance on the interface between the object and the medium
in which the wave travels. To reduce the mismatch of acoustic impedance between the air and
the human tissue, a coupling medium gel is often used, thus allowing the source wave to be
fully transmitted in the body. The part of the wave that is reﬂected is usually described as the
reﬂected echo and will travel back from the obstacle to the transducer, as illustrated in Figure
1.2a. When a wave encounters an obstacle two phenomenons occur; if the obstacle has a size
comparable to, or smaller than the wavelength, the incident energy wave will undergo a scatter-
ing effect leading to a succession of constructive and destructive interferences that will be seen
on the ﬁnal image as a complex structure called "speckle" in the literature. These reﬂections are
used to deﬁne biological tissues such as liver and capillaries. On the other hand, if the obstacle

Two-dimensional imaging
To produce a 2-D image, a series of transducers are combined into individual transducer ele-
ment forming a 1-D linear array as illustrated in Figure 1.2b. This combination of transducers
is often refereed as a probe, and it allows the generation and the detection of ultrasounds. The
sounds beam is focused electronically by applying an electric signal with phase delay to each in-
dividual element, from the outward to the inward of the array. The signal phase delays between
each element will allow the pulses to reach the focal point at the same time. The focus point
can be dynamically changed by changing the delay. This technique allows to create a 2-D cross-
section images based on the change of intensity of each reﬂected signal and the position of the
reﬂected boundary. This will give rise to an illustration with a gray scale known as sonogram
image as seen in Figure 1.2c.

Commonly used ultrasonic transducers in the medical imaging ﬁeld are based on piezoelectric

1.2.1
Piezoelectric transducer
Background
The discovery of the piezoelectric effect dates back to 1880, when Pierre and Jacques Currie
observed an electric potential when applying a mechanical stress on quartz and Rochelle salt
connected with to electrodes. The year after, Gabriel Lippman, a Franco-Luxembourgian physi-
cist, demonstrated that this effect could be reversed when applying potential to the material.
This way he shown that a potential difference could induce a mechanical deformation [7]. It is
only 30 years later that the use of piezoelectric effect was used by Paul Langevin to create ul-
trasound device for the detection of submarines during World War I, known as sonar [8]. After
the war, the researches on piezoelectricity and ultrasound transducer rose and it is nowadays a
widely used method to create non-invasive medical imaging devices.

Operating principle
When an electrical potential is applied to a piezoelectric material encapsulated in two elec-
trodes, a deformation of the material arises. The change is either negative (compression) or
positive (extension) and will depend of the polarity of the applied voltage. This cyclic change
in compression and extension will generate an ultrasonic wave that will propagate through the
biological tissue. Similarly, the reverse effect will occur; an ultrasonic pressure wave will induce
an electric signal proportional to the amplitude of the wave. Figure 1.4 shows the two working
conﬁguration of a piezoelement.
To obtain a good signal, the piezoelement must operate at its resonance frequency which is
deﬁned, for a conventional ultrasonic transducer, by the thickness, t, of the material in the di-
rection of the wave propagation and the velocity, c, of ultrasound in the piezoelectric material.
Lead zirconate titanate (PZT) is often used for the fabrication piezoelectric transducer.

Limitation
With conventional ultrasonic piezoelectric transducers, the acoustic impedance mismatch be-

mission through the anatomical layers, even when a coupling matching layer, such as gel, is
placed between the element and the tissue. In addition, the high requirement of precision on
the thickness for high frequency, induces a complex fabrication [6]. However, before stating
that the piezoelectricity is no longer the future of medical imaging one must mention the cur-
rent state of the art in the research ﬁeld of Piezoelectric MUT (PMUT). Unlike the conventional
piezoelectric transducer, the PMUT, illustrated in Figure 1.3c, utilizes the piezoelectric effect to

1.2.2
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonics Transducer
Background
One of the ﬁrst reported Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer dates back to 1996,
where Matthew Haller et al. reported the fabrication of a capacitive membrane on a thermal
grown oxide [10]. In their work, they constructed an electrostatic transducer able to operate
at 4.6MHz. Although condenser transmitters devices were reported before [11], [12], Haller et
al. seem to be the ﬁrst to have used advance fabrication technology to produce a dimension
controlled circular membrane. Since the late 90’s, the CMUT devices has never ceased to be a
large part of the ultrasonic imaging research.
Operating principle
A CMUT device is a small capacitor consisting in a metalized membrane bonded over a cavity
where in the bottom of it, lies another metalized layer acting as the bottom electrode. If a DC
voltage is applied between the two metalized layers, the plate will deﬂect due to the polarity
difference, inducing an electrostatic force between the top and bottom electrode. Because the
plate has an inherent stiffness, due to material and geometry, a mechanical restoring force, de-
scribed later as the spring force, will force the membrane to resist to the deformation and go
back to its equilibrium position. Applying a AC voltage will force the membrane to harmon-
ically move to the frequency of the applied AC voltage, thus inducing an ultrasound from the
oscillation. A cross-section schematic of a CMUT is illustrated in Figure 1.5.

Limitation
Looking at the number of research made on MUT devices, CMUT gathers a large part, if not the
largest. Its ability to perform within a large bandwidth, together with its good electromechan-
ical coupling, and acoustic performances [6], [2], [13] makes the CMUT a promising device for
ultrasonic imaging. However, CMUTs operate near what is commonly called the pull-in voltage.
This voltage corresponds to the maximum applied voltage before the top and bottom electrode
collapse together due to the attraction force. Hence, this increases the risk of collapsing ele-
ments which would limit the general functioning of the device. In addition, the CMUT devices
produced at DTU Nanotech present a very low yield due to the buckling of the top plate, mainly
observe on the 2-D array. Although, the reason why this buckling occurs is not fully understood,
it is an important limitation in for the fabrication process of those devices.
1.3
Motivation and objectives
Literature in the ﬁeld of ultrasonic transducer does not indicated obvious reason to choose
PMUT over CMUT. Both methods are emergent researches with their own beneﬁt and draw-
back. However the large internal know-how on the design, fabrication and measurement of
CMUT has lead the MEMS-Applied Sensor group at DTU Nanotech, led by Erik V. Thomsen, to
perform their research in this direction.
1.3.1
Previous work at DTU Nanotech
The fabrication process currently used in the group have been developed by Thomas L. Chris-
tiansen during his PhD thesis [4]. In his work, he created a process based on a LOCOS (Local
Oxidation of Silicon) to create the transducer cavities for a 2-D row-column array of a CMUT
device. This latter is capable of triggering each row and column separately in order to gener-
ate a ﬁnal 3-D image. Successful and promising results indicate the viability of this technique
to create 3-D imaging device based on capacitives micromachined ultrasonic transducer. This
method has been entitle as the LOCOS process. The work of Thomas L. Christiansen was taken
over by a current PhD student in the group, Mathias Engholm, who has been working on the
LOCOS process for the past two years. Although the solution based on this process has shown
viable results, some fabrication problems, such as buckling effect of the top plate remain. In ad-
dition, the LOCOS process required tedious fabrication step in addition to be time consuming
manufacturing steps. This will inherently increase the price of the ﬁnal device. A large descrip-
tion of the state of the art of silicon dioxide based CMUT devices, together with manufacturing
problem is described in Mathias Engholm’s Master Thesis [14].

Approach
To meet the objectives of the project, the approach is ﬁrst to simulate the behavior of the deﬂec-

velopment times will be tested in order to ﬁnd the most appropriate one. Finally, the bonding
between the silicon top plate and the BCB will be tested to verify the bond quality. The inﬂu-
ence of defects, such as particles and inhomogeneities will be investigated. Once the estimate
fabrication parameters are obtained, the fabrication of the ﬁnal device will take place and the
characterization will be performed to prove the viability of the use of BCB for CMUT.

2.3.5
Effective radius
The soft boundary induces a change in the effective radius (aeff) of the plate. When zooming
on the outer bound, one can see the shape of the plate changing when the intermediate mate-
rial layer is either made of SiO2 or BCB. This is seen in Figure 2.7 where the deﬂection shape is
plotted as function of the relative distance across plate radius from 1.22 to 1.50. The less rigid
BCB creates a wave-like change in the deﬂection near the anchor point, whereas the stiff sili-
con dioxide induces a slow exponential change in the deﬂection. Because of this difference in
shape between the two material, the two deﬂections cannot be directly compared. Therefore
two distinct effective radii have been calculated. One being taken before the wave-like shape of
the BCB begins and a second one where the bending slope reaches a value close to zero.
The ﬁrst effective radius, before the wave-like shape, has been deﬁned where the deﬂection is
equivalent to an arbitrarily chosen value of 0.1% of the total normalized deﬂection. This value
of 0.1 has been estimated sufﬁciently small to be considered as an anchor point. The value of
this effective radius can be quantitatively extract from the COMSOL Multiphysic simulation and
has been entitle has "effective radius at 0.1% deﬂection". The second effective radius, entitled as
"mechanical effective radius", has been deﬁned where the slope of the deﬂection shape reaches
a value close to zero. Because the slope of the shape never actually reaches the exact value of
zero, there is an inﬁnite number of possible mechanical effective radius. Therefore, 0.0002 as
value for the normalized deﬂection slope has been chosen to deﬁned the mechanical effective
radius in this study. The results of the relative increase of the effective radii for different cavity
depth for a deﬂection of "0.1% deﬂection" or for "zero slope" are illustrated in Figure 2.8 a and
b.
Depending on which of the method is used to estimate the effective radii, a signiﬁcant differ-

mechanical effective radius, the increase can be up to 73% of the initial radius on a BCB, and up
to 50% for SiO2. This means that if these numbers are applied to a standard CMUT 1D array of
30µm radius membranes, with a cavity depth of 370nm, the mechanical effective radius would
then be 51µm for BCB and 45µm for SiO2 respectively. This would induced a cross-talk of ap-
proximately 1/3 over the neighbouring cavity placed 7.5µm further as foreseen by the mask de-

Chapter conclusion
The dimensions of the transducer for the BCB method used in this project is based on the cur-
rent fabrication process using the LOCOS method. All the dimensions have been kept identical
for the cavities and electrodes. However, due to the signiﬁcantly more ﬂexible BCB over the SiO2
currently used, some differences in the deﬂection shape, resonance frequency and pull-in volt-
age are observed. The simulations made in COMSOL Multiphysic have shown for a BCB, that
the effective radius taken at 0.1% of the plate deﬂection was giving a relative increase of 22% of
the initial radius. This value goes up to 72% if one sets the effective radius at a distance where
the deﬂection slope approaches zero. When using a stiffer intermediate layer, here simulated
with SiO2, these two effective radii show a relative increase of 14% and 51% respectively. Based
==PAGE:39==

31
solely on the FEM simulation it seems rather tedious to estimate an exact effective radius for a
soft intermediate layer such as BCB. A development of a more accurate analytical model taking
into account the soft boundary of the clamping is recommended for further accurate analytical
studies. This change of behavior between the two different material also affects the resonance
frequency. A another FEM simulation shows a difference of 10% in the resonance frequency
between the two different material, yet having an identical thickness of 370nm. Simulations
have also shown that to keep the resonance frequency identical between a SiO2 and a BCB in-
termediate layer, the membrane radius should be designed 1.3µm smaller. An analytical model
of the pull-in voltage based on the plate energy has been derived and compared to the Mat-

simulate realistic deﬂection. A difference of 17% between the analytical model of a fully clamped
plate and the realistic FEM simulation on a BCB is observed. This deviation is constant for the
membrane diameters tested.

3.2.1
Thickness investigation
The thickness of the ﬁnal polymer layer is believed to be inﬂuenced by different factors such as
the spinning method, resist age, resist temperature, spinning speed or development time. The
age and temperature will inﬂuence the thickness due to the change in viscosity. Although it is
important to control the age and temperature of the resist, it is not the most suitable method
to ﬁne control the ﬁnal thickness, as it is not a controllable technique and therefore a less re-
producible method. Long development time may also inﬂuence the thickness, but it is not an
==PAGE:42==
CHAPTER 3. CHARACTERIZATION AND OPTIMIZATION OF CYCLOTENETM BCB4022-25
34
adequate method to customize the ﬁnal thickness either, as the resolution of the pattern will be
inﬂuenced by the development time.
Therefore, the spinning velocity has been chosen in order to investigate the change in thickness
depending on the different spinning velocity. The spinning method has been kept the same dur-
ing the entire period of the project; the resist was poured manually onto the wafer surface and
the spinning time was set to 30 seconds. No obvious change in thickness could be observe for
longer spinning time.
Thickness measurement method
To study the inﬂuence of the spinning velocity on the ﬁnal thickness, ﬁve different rotation
speeds have been run in order to deﬁne a trend. The measurement of the thickness have been
performed with ellipsomety on 9 points across the wafer and the ﬁnal reported thickness is the
average of these points. The ellipsometer is a technique that compares the polarization change
of the light between the reﬂected and transmitted wave with a pre-deﬁned model, conﬁgured in
the equipment software. Because the ellipsometer did not have any pre-deﬁned model for BCB

3.3
Planarization
Prior bonding of the top plate, the intermediate BCB layer should be as plan as possible to allow
a large bonding surface. It is expected that the spun BCB should smooth the height variation
and create an even surface if the resist layer is thicker than the metal pattern. However, it has
been reported by Avago Technologies, a company active in the semiconductor technology, that
the planarization is not perfect over the region where high feature density exist [28]. To verify
this planarity, a layer of aluminium of 400 nm has been patterned on a silicon wafer substrate
with various feature sizes. A quality control mask used for lithography tools at the DTU Danchip
facility has been used for this test. It contains an array of line with different line widths from
1µm to 10µm. The BCB resist has been spun upon the structure at 8000 rpm, leading to a thick-
ness of 740 nm. Confocal measurements shown in Figure 3.4 reveal a varying height across the
metallic features, thus verifying what has been observed by Avago Technologies. Issues cause by
this height difference could lead to a change in the features during bonding. Indeed, the force
applied on the top wafer during the bonding may induce an overﬂow of the resin toward the free
of BCB areas. To limit this problem, the mask for the bottom electrode has been ﬁlled up with
aluminium all over the wafer with a minimum distance of 2.5 µm from the electrode pattern.
The change in height can also induce issue during the bonding, such as preventing the bonding
to occur in between features.

3.4.1
Wafer preparation
Substrate wafer
Two tests have been performed to study the impact of thickness inhomogeneity and dust on the

the inﬂuence of dust and particles lying on the BCB surface and how these defects could inﬂu-

400nm, were spun with BCB stored at 20◦for several hour. No signiﬁcant defects such as dust,
particles or holes were observed on the surface. The second wafer is a unit that received some
miss-handling and suffered from dust exposure after the BCB was spun. It therefore presents
some interesting criteria to analyse in order to understand the behavior and sensitivity of the
bonding. Several inhomogeneities on the layer thickness can also be observed. In this test, the
silicon substrate had no aluminium layer and had a polished surface.
Top wafer for bonding
To test the inﬂuence of the patterned aluminium layer below the BCB, a double side top silicon
wafer was used for the bonding. This was in order to reduce the charging effect during SEM
imaging and to make the cleavage easier. However, to facilitate the observation of the inﬂuence
of dust, cavities and inhomogeneity of the BCB on the ﬁnal bonding, a transparent boroﬂoat
glass wafer of 350µm with double-side polish was used.
For both top wafers, the adhesion promoter was spun onto it by the same mean that of the
substrate wafer described previously.
3.4.2
Bonding method
The bonding took place in an bonder tool type EV Group 520 Hot Embosser. The substrate
wafer is manually aligned with the top wafer and place in the chamber on the equipment chuck.
Vacuum is made in the chamber and a load of 5kN is applied onto the wafers. The chamber is
previously ﬂush with a ﬂow of nitrogen while vacuum is created. The bonding occurs when the
resin cures in contact with the glass. The whole process takes place at 250◦C for 1 hour.

any way, as it can be seen in Figure 3.5. The transition between the top Si layer and the BCB is

Breakdown test
3.5.1
Breakdown voltage
The breakdown voltage deﬁnes the maximum potential difference that a dielectric material can
withstand before it becomes conductive. The breakdown voltage is not a single value but more
a statistical value based on a large number of sample. For the BCB4022-25 used in this project, a
value of 0.3 V/nm is often reported in articles and literature when referring to BCB. However, the
breakdown voltage reported by Dow Chemical is supposed to be 0.53 V/nm. Therefore, for an
expected thickness of 400nm of the resin, the driving voltage can be maximum 212 V. This values
would allow the driving of the CMUT device at the DC voltage currently used in the LOCOS
process, that is to say 190V. The value reported in Table 3.5 have been carried out on the test
structures placed on the CMUT mask set that were designed for that purpose. They are placed
around the wafer to evaluate the breakdown of the resin. Three different sizes of electrode have
been tested; 4x4mm, 5x5mm and 6x6mm square electrodes. The hypothesis is that smaller the
electrode lower the number of possible impurities or defects in the polymer which can gives
rise to breakdown. This trend can be seen in Table 3.5 where the smallest test structure gives
a relative breakdown voltage of 0.02 V/nm. The thickness of the BCB in this device has been
measured after development of the resin. The measurement returned a thickness of 549nm,
which has been assumed unvarying after bonding. The breakdown values measured are several
orders of magnitude smaller than the theoretical values of 0.53V/nm reported by Dow Chemical.
The reason of this poor electrical strength may be the result of a lack of cross-linked polymer
chains, leaving behind, dangling bonds allowing current to go through. A thorough study of the
breakdown voltage as function of the curing has therefore been carried out.

3.5.2
Inﬂuence of the curing on the breakdown ﬁeld
Cyclotene BCB belongs to the so-called thermoset polymer family. Unlike thermoplastic, ther-
mosets undergo a chemical reaction when transforming from a liquid state to a solid state and
will not retrieve their initial shape when heated up. The chemical change is irreversible and
additional high heat can give rise to degradation of the polymer. The polymerization occurs
spontaneously at room temperature with additive material acting as catalyst (i.e. epoxy based
resin, silicone) or by heat causing the chemical reaction (i.e. benzocyclobutene (BCB)). Figure
3.9 shows a Divinyl Siloxane bis-Benzocyclobutene (DVS-BCB) monomere with two benzocy-
clobutene on its extremities. It corresponds to the basic compound used in the creation of the
dry etch and photosensitive Cyclotene BCB resins. The monomers present in the solvent is the
chemical stage often refereed as the A-stage in the literature. In this stage, the material is in its
liquid phase [29]. When the polymerization reaction starts upon heat, the cyclobutene rings
opens up to create a highly reactive o-quinodimethane which will induce an organic chemical
reaction leading to the formation of cross-linked polymer chains, also know as the B-stage [30].
The Cyclotene BCB 4000 series is supplied by Dow Chemical in a partial B-stage [20], meaning
that it has undergone a partial curing prior shipping to the end user.
The recommendation from Dow Chemical suggests a curing time of 60 minutes at a temper-
ature of 250◦C to obtain a full curing (> 95%) of the polymer. This combination of time and
temperature have been widely reported in papers using BCB. The suggested values for differ-
ent percentage of curing of BCB as function of time and temperature are illustrated in Figure
3.10 and Table 3.6. It is these temperatures and time values that have been used to fabricate the

3.5.3
Inﬂuence of pumping, process time and temperature
To narrow down the main factors as function of their inﬂuence on the breakdown ﬁeld, 10 sili-
con wafers were spun for the screening experiment, with a spinning velocity of 3000 rpm. The
curing process took place in a covered vacuum sealed curing oven connected to a pumping sys-
tem and a nitrogen ﬂow. After curing, 400 nm thick aluminium top electrodes size 5x3mm were
thermally evaporated onto the surface by an e-beam evaporation process. A total of 40 elec-
trodes were deﬁned using a silicon shadow mask placed on top of the wafer before evaporation
of the aluminium.
The curing method follows the steps indicated in the proﬁle curve in Figure 3.11. Step 0, corre-
sponds to the nitrogen ﬂushing+pumping. It is an additional step based on the recommenda-
tion of the equipment responsible at the DTU Danchip facility. This additional step is a pump
down of the chamber, alternated with a 1 minute high ﬂow of nitrogen ﬂushing. This alternate
ﬂushing/pumping is run 5 times, thus leading to a 10 minutes pumping+ﬂushing step. This step
has the objective to reduce the content of oxygen in the chamber and ﬂush away other gas com-
pound that may remain in the chamber prior curing. To estimate the inﬂuence of this step on the
breakdown, it has been programmed to either be tested with the alternate pumping+ﬂushing for
2x5 minutes or with a ﬂushing+pumping combination corresponding to a pumping of 5 min-

3.5.4
Inﬂuence of the curing time
The investigation on the time inﬂuence of the BCB curing process was performed at a temper-
ature of 290◦C, which corresponds to the best output result of the aforementioned study. The
electrical measurement were performed maximum 7 days after the curing, and the sampled
were stored at a constant temperature of 20◦C ± 1◦C with a relative humidity of 42% ± 2%. The
effect of storage, meaning a continuous slow polymerization of the partial cured resist and time
degradation, have not been investigated, but may inﬂuence the results on more or less long
term.
The time inﬂuence study shows that the breakdown voltage increases with the annealing time
thus leading toward a breakdown ﬁeld closer to the theoretical value. This is illustrated in Fig-

is calculated being 0.007 V/nm per minute. This rate reduces to 0.0005 V/nm per minutes be-
yond this time. The change in rate can be explained by the polymer becoming more and more
stiff as the cross-link occurs, thus reducing the ability of the monomers to move freely around.

Recommended further study
The test performed to characterized the photosensitive polymer have shown some disparities
in both the resist thickness and homogeneity after development. These results combined with
some previous experiences and literature reviews, indicate that the change in thickness and
homogeneity of the ﬁnal layer may be inﬂuenced by several factors, such as the resist, develop-
ment method, storage environment, operator and equipment. Figure 3.15 indicated the factors
that are believed to inﬂuence the ﬁnal thickness and homogeneity of the resist. The highlighted
factors are the ones that would be recommended to further study. In addition, a study on the
required minimum force during the bonding would informed on the inﬂuence of the force on
the ﬁnal cavity height. This has not been investigated in this project, but may inﬂuenced signif-
icantly the ﬁnal thickness. It is highly recommended to perform a complete study of the resist
in order to guarantee the best reproducibility of the ﬁnal device.
Based on the breakdown ﬁeld, one can estimate a curing time of 420 minutes in order to ob-
tained the theoretical breakdown value of 0.53 V/nm. However this recommended time and
curing temperature are optimized for the BCB oven available at the Danchip facility and for a
silicon substrate. These values may differ from the curing process performed in the EVG bonder
equipment and with a quartz wafer substrate or with a different pressure. It is therefore rec-
ommended to pursue the curing investigations using the bonder equipment and a fused silica
substrate.

This chapter summarizes the tests performed to characterize the Dow Chemical Cyclotene BCB
4022-25 for the purpose of this project. The tests are done with an emphasize on the exposure,

development time, curing procedure and breakdown ﬁeld. The samples were spun at 3000 rpm
which has given the most suited ﬁnal thickness for the CMUT device. Exposure time calculation
and experimental exposure tests are in agreement with each other and lead to a total exposure
dose of 41,3 mJ/cm2 with a i-line (365nm) light. A development of 30 seconds in a DS3000 so-

loading effect - believed to be due the mask design - induces an inhomogeneity in the BCB layer
after development. Nevertheless, no signiﬁcant issue seems observed when the BCB is bonded
to top. Impurities and particles at the interface between the resin and the top silicon wafer intro-
duce weaknesses in the bonding. The fabrication of the sample has shown that it is difﬁcult to
remove the particles from the surface of the polymer with air gun. It is therefore recommended
to proceed to the bonding shortly after development of the resin in order to avoid particle to
stick to the uncured BCB. Several effects, such as the inhomogeneity and the change of the re-
sist viscosity over time may have impact on other type of devices using smaller structures. These
effects have not been investigated in this project as the dimensions in the mask used in this work
are above 5 µm and did not revealed any dimensional issue. However, smaller features may re-
acts differently. Further studies about the viscosity change over time and temperature is highly
recommended to understand the behaviour of the BCB thickness thus allowing a more accurate
control of the thickness. The breakdown voltage of the BCB has shown an increasing value as
the curing time was increased, therefore leading to the conclusion that the recommended cur-
ing time proposed by Dow Chemical is not suitable for the equipment used, and a longer curing
time must be applied to obtained the expected breakdown ﬁeld.

4.2
Fabrication details
The bottom wafer consists in a quartz wafer onto which aluminium electrodes are patterned
and combined with the BCB cavities as illustrated in Figure 4.1 c. In order to reduce parasitic
capacitance, the bottom wafer is a 350µm thick fused silica glass, also deﬁned in this work as
quartz bottom wafer.
4.2.1
Cleaning
Prior any fabrication, the quartz substrate undergone a standard clean (SC), also commonly
known in the literature as RCA 1, where the wafers are immersed successively in two cleaning
baths: SC-1 and SC2. The ﬁrst chemical bath, SC-1, contains an aqueous mixed solution of
NH4OH (ammonium hydroxide), H2O2 (hydrogen peroxide) and H2O (deionized water) with
1Method developed by Werner Kern for his work at Radio Corporation of America (RCA) [34]
==PAGE:65==

a ratio 1:1:5. The wafers are dip into it for 10 minutes. This ﬁrst cleaning step is designed to
removed organic contaminants and metal elements such as Ni, Co, Cr or Cu, Au and Ag (IIB
and IB group) [34]. The second cleaning step, SC-2, is composed of an aqueous solution of
Hydrochloric acid (HCL), H2O2 and H2O with a ratio of 1:1:5. The wafers are dip in it for 10 min.
The SC-2 is intended to remove from the surface the residual traces of metal. The two cleaning
solutions were kept at constant temperature of 80◦C and a rinse in DI water for 3 minutes was
performed between each steps.
4.2.2
Bottom electrodes
The bottom electrode consists in a patterned aluminium layer. The minimum resist height to
perform a lift-off patterning is deﬁned by a general rule of thumb, stating that the minimum
resist thickness must be at least 3x the thickness of the deposed metal. For this fabrication, the
bottom electrode is 400 nm, therefore, the resist must be at least 1.2 µm. The pre-deﬁned recipe
available in the equipment (SVG 88 series track system from Rite Track) carries out the deposi-
tion of the adhesion layer, hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS), followed by the spinning of 1.8µm of
resist with 0.5% uniformity. It also includes the soft bake. No addition manual handling of the
wafers is done during these steps.
The exposure of the resist is accomplished with the i-line (365nm) of the mask aligner Karl Süss
MA6. The mask used corresponds to the BOT mask described in appendix C. The resist has been
exposed 8.6 seconds at an intensity of 13W/cm2 corresponding to a total dose of 112 mJ/cm2.
The puddle development is performed in TMAH for 60 seconds after a post-exposure bake at
110◦C for 60 seconds. The aluminium deposition is performed by physical vapor deposition
(PVD) using an e-beam evaporation system where the wafers are placed approximately 50 cm
above the metal source. This latter is exposed to a magnetically focused electron beam which
heats the metal up, inducing its evaporation onto the sample. A layer of 10 nm of titanium is
deposed by the same method prior the aluminium in order to enhanced the adhesion. The
evaporation rates are ﬁxed to 5 Å/s for the titanium and 10 Å/s for the aluminium. The full pro-
cess is performed under a pressure in the order of 4 × 10−6mBar to allow the electrons to move
to the metal sources. This low pressure also induces a very directional deposition of the metal
onto the substrate as no atoms can have their path disturbed through the working distance. This
makes this technique ideal for lift-off process.
4.2.3

The cavities in this project are manufactured into the photosensitive resin BCB4022-25. Prior
the spinning of the resist, the adhesion promoter AP3000 from Dow Chemical is spun onto the
bottom substrate to enhance the adhesion. To accommodate to the current fabrication process,
the ﬁnal cavity height must be approximately 400nm. Therefore, the resin is spun at a spin-
ning velocity of 3000 rpm for 30 seconds. According to the characterization tests performed in
Chapter 3, this velocity provides a thickness of 370nm ± 15nm after development. However, this
thickness has been measured up to 527nm ±38 on some of the tests performed in this project.
This change in thickness is believed to be due to the change of the resin temperature when
spinning. The bottle is stored at 5◦C and is allowed to warm up at room temperature, for 1 hour

Removal of handling layer and buried oxide
The deﬁnition of the top electrodes takes place after the reduction of the handling layer (<100>
orientation) and buried oxide layers.
The bonded stack is placed in a potassium hydroxide (KOH) mixed with water in a ratio of 0.5:1.
The mixture is heated up at 80◦C. The KOH will etch away the silicon handling layer. The stack
was left in the solution for 6 hours when it reached the oxide layer acting has an etch-stop, thus
giving an etch rate of 0.94 nm/min. The 1 µm buried oxide layer was removed in a solution of
buffered hydroﬂuoric acid (BHF) for 14 minutes. The etch rate was approximately 70 nm/min.
The removal of the handling layer in the KOH has been successful, yet some defects on the edges
of the wafer remain seen. In addition, some structures peeled off. However, the overall result
seem to indicate that the remaining stress in the membrane after etching does not lead to the
buckling effect observed on the current LOCOS process. Figure 4.4 shows the overview of the
wafer device after KOH etching.
To reduce the etch damage on the outer ring of the wafer, a test has been run with a dry etch
process based on ﬂuorine gas. The advantageous of the dry etch process is that it is a faster
etch (approximately 6 µm/min). In addition, the physical etch occurs on the top of the sample
mainly, thus reducing the gas to attack the side of the stack, hence reducing the edge damages.
However, due to the poor heat transfer ability of the quartz substrate, it is difﬁcult to keep the
substrate at a temperature below the glass transition of the BCB during the etch. On the test
performed with dry etch, the BCB has been observed to ﬂow over areas which were supposed to
be free of resin. This is the result of a overheated resin which has reached the melting point and
ﬂowed around. A combination of lower power and alternated plasma on and off, may reduce
the heat and allow to keep the resin to a temperature below melting point. However, this may
lead to process time as long as a KOH etch, thereby removing the advantageous of being a faster
etch. Therefore, it is recommended to ﬁnd a suitable back side protection for a KOH etch to
protect the edges. In addition, the chemical etch has the ability to be performed in batch of
several wafers which would encourage a large scale manufacturing.

Impedance measurement
The main purpose of a transducer in the case of ultrasonic imaging, is the conversion of an
electrical signal into a mechanical signal such as a pressure wave and vice versa. The simpliﬁed
equivalent circuit of CMUT device can be schematized as in Figure 4.5. Here, 3 components
have an impedance changing as function of the frequency; the two capacitors C0 and C1 and the
inductor L. The resistance, R, corresponds to an internal loss in the circuit and will be constant
for all frequencies. In practise, the mechanical domain can be pictured as the combination of
a spring like membrane which can be interpreted as C1. The potential energy coming from the
movable mass can be illustrated as the inductance, L. The mechanical losses corresponds to
==PAGE:71==
CHAPTER 4. CMUT FABRICATION AND MEASUREMENT
63

combination.
the resistance R. Two frequencies can be pointed out from this schematic. First, the resonance
frequency, ωres occurs when the impedances of the inductor L and the capacitor C1, connected
in series, equal each other. In an ideal case, the total impedance of the system would reach the
value of R and the phase would shift from -90◦to 90◦.
The second resonance peak, called the anti-resonance frequency, will appear when the impedance
of the inductor and the electrical impedance C0, connected in parallel, are equal. In this case,
the impedance of the system shifts back to -90◦and the impedance of the system becomes in-
ﬁnite. It is within this range of frequencies that one should drive the transducer to create a
pressure wave use.

for a sweep of the bias voltage from 0 V to 90 V. The device behaves like a capacitor until it reaches
the breakdown limit at 55 V and acts as a resistance. This low limit is likely to be due to the lack

Plate quality
The current manufacturing process using LOCOS induces the bonded top plate to systemati-

rication. The understanding of this buckling effect is not fully understood yet in the group, and

the BCB process.
4.4
Chapter conclusion
This chapter summarized the fabrication process of the CMUT device. The overall fabrication
did not present signiﬁcant fabrication issues. However the KOH etch required to removed the
handle layer of the SOI wafer has created considerable damages on the edges of the wafer. These
damages may induced problems in future devices fabrication if the alignment marks get etched
away during this steps. Therefore, it is recommended to place the alignment mark further in-
side the mask design, or to use a backside protection appropriate for the thickness of the stack.
Nevertheless, unlike the current LOCOS process, no buckling of the top plate could be observed
after the bonding, leading to promising fabrication yield. The characterization of the ﬁnal de-
vice was performed on an electrode column of 288 membranes from a 1-D array. An AC voltage
of 50mV with a frequency range from 1MHz to 20MHz was tested. The bias voltage was set to
50V corresponding to the highest value before breakdown of the BCB occurs. Impedance mea-
surements reported a small resonance peak at 6.9MHz. This frequency is 15% smaller than what
the FEM simulations returned in Figure 2.5.2c in Chapter 2. Although some investigations re-
main necessary, one can expect this difference being due to the small degree of cross-linked
resist in the device, as opposed to the theoretical fully cross-linked resin used in the FEM. It is
believed that a non fully cross-linked BCB would shown an higher elasticity, thereby increasing
the membrane radius and lowering its resonance frequency. It has also been observed that the

prior the bonding returned a broken top plate on the 2-D arrays.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary of the experiment
The process described in this work is inﬂuenced by the recent study performed by Zenhao Li et
al. [15] at the department of System Design Engineering of the university of Waterloo in Canada.
In their work they fabricated a CMUT device using a photosensitive benzocyclobutene resin (Cy-
clotene BCB 4022-35). They produced an array of 360 circular membranes able to resonate at
a frequency of 5.5 ± 0.1 MHz. Their work proves the capability of the technique to create and
receive ultrasound signal.
This master thesis had for goal to use Zenhao Li et al.’s approach, and developed a CMUT device
with an insulating layer using Cyclotene BCB4022-25. This latter is a photosensitive resin from
the same series used by Zenhao Li et al. but showing a lower viscosity. The substrate was made
of fused silica in order to reduce the parasitic capacitance in the ﬁnal device. The bottom elec-
trodes were patterned in a 400 nm aluminium layer. The top plate was made of the 2 µm device
layer of a SOI wafer, and the top electrodes were patterned in an evaporated layer of aluminium.
The dimension of the cavities were kept as close as possible to the dimensions of the current
process used in the MEMS-applied group for the commonly called LOCOS process. This was in
order to allow, for later researches, a comparison side by side between the two techniques.
The little knowledge available in the literature about the Cyclotene resin BCB4022-25 has forced
the investigations of its behavior regarding its thickness as function of spinning velocity, ex-
posure doses and breakdown voltage. The resist spinning tests returned an optimum rotation
speed of 3000 rpm for 30 second. This combined velocity and time, induced the most homo-
geneous layer and created a ﬁnal thickness of 370nm after curing. As one could expect, the
temperature of the resist before spinning, inﬂuences the viscosity and thus the ﬁnal thickness.
Therefore, it is recommended to let the resist reaches room temperature before spinning. A
minimum of 4 hours in a 75ml bottle is suggested to obtain the right temperature leading to
reproducible thickness. An exposure dose of 41,6 mJ/cm combined with a development time
of 30 seconds in a developer DS3000 solution has shown the optimum parameters to obtain a
sharp and fully developed pattern for the speciﬁc mask design.
The impedance measurement performed on an electrodes row covering 288 membranes of 60
µm diameter each, has returned a resonance frequency of 6.9 MHz corresponding to a differ-
ence of 15% from the predicted FEM simulation. This difference is believed to be due to the lack
of cross-linked polymer chains inducing an lower Young modulus and creating a larger effec-
68
==PAGE:77==

69
tive radius of the membrane, thus lowering the resonance frequency of the plate. However, this
hypothesis remain to be veriﬁed. Nevertheless, the device prepared in this work has shown the
ability of a bonded membrane on top of BCB4022-25 to generate ultrasound waves in air. This
lead to a new simple and attractive way for CMUT manufacturing. The tests were performed
with a bias voltage of 50V corresponding to the maximum applied voltage before breakdown of
the resin occurs. The additional characterization tests of the BCB4022-25, after fabrication of
the ﬁnal device, have led to the conclusion that the resin was not fully cured. This had for con-
sequences to leave behind dangling bonds thus enhancing the ability of the electrons to move
through the polymer, hence lowering the general dielectric strength. A curing time of 420 min-
utes is expected to give the breakdown voltage of 0.53 V/nm.
The proposed fabrication steps for a fully functional device can be realized in approximately
3 to 4 days. Depending on the availability of the equipments, this can be extended to several
weeks. However, this technique has the advantages of using standard lithography process and
wet chemistry easily available in the MEMS industry. Although, some more investigations need
to be performed to obtain a device working with higher breakdown ﬁeld, the study described in
this thesis suggests that the use of BCB4022-25, as alternative to the LOCOS process, is a viable
solution that worth to investigate further.
5.2
Recommendation for further work
To obtain a fully functional CMUT device, several optimization remain needed. Tests have
shown a breakdown ﬁeld 70% inferior to the expected value provided by Dow Chemical for the
same annealing time and temperature. It has been shown in this work that the curing was most
likely not completed during the recommended annealing procedure most likely due to curing
pressure and actual temperature around the sample. Increasing the curing time and temper-
ature allowed to reach a breakdown up to 80% of its anticipated value, and it is expected that
increasing the curing time to 420 minutes would give the attended breakdown ﬁeld. The ﬁrst
further work recommended would be to performed a similar curing experience in the bonder
equipment. The change of equipment may inﬂuence the heat distribution and induces a differ-
ent curing rate.
In addition, aging of the resist has not been investigated in this project. Literature and datasheet
often referred the BCB resins as being highly resistant to chemical attacks and water absorption,
however, no obvious results regarding the tightness of the bonding over time has been investi-
gated. The change in pressure in the cavity over time will inﬂuence the damping of the vibrating
membrane thereby inducing a signal change on long or short term, it is therefore important to
be able to control it. It has been seen in this work that directly after bonding under vacuum,
the membranes were bended toward the inside, thus revealing a lower pressure in the cavities.
This has not been investigated in detail, but after one month, one could see that most of the
membranes on the ﬁnal device were ﬂattened, suggesting that air had ﬂown into the cavities,
hence reestablishing the pressure to equilibrium. One could imagine that the lack of curing on
this device may have inﬂuenced the bonding and the tightness, however investigations should
be planned.
Finally, simulations and analytical models have shown quite some differences with the ﬁnal de-
vice resonance frequency obtained. The softer BCB, as opposed to the currently used stiff SiO2,
==PAGE:78==
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
70
induces a larger effective radius which is rather difﬁcult to estimate from the FEM simulations.
Theoretical development of a model taking into account an effective radius would provide more
accurate analytical results. However, this study might induce cumbersome and tedious calcula-
tion which may require some time to evaluate.
The overall outcome of this project surely proves the ability of BCB4022-25 to be used in CMUT
devices. The results obtain so far, together with the relative simplicity of the process fabrication,
make this technique a solution that I recommend to investigate further.

gentle mixing of the contents is desirable to

is to remove the product from the dry ice and 
store it at -30°C to -40°C, as we have found 
that it is the transition from -78°C to -15°C that 
tends to initiate crystallization. 
 
1.2  Storage 
As photosensitive CYCLOTENE resin ages, the 
spun-on thickness will gradually increase.  The 
lifetime is based on the criterion of less than 5% 
change in thickness.  Resins should be allowed 
to equilibrate to room temperature before use.

2.  PROCESSING 
Several process options are available, and are 
shown in Figure 2.  Process A uses a hot plate 
soft bake and includes a develop end point 
monitor with each lot.  Process B uses a hot 
plate soft bake and a pre-develop bake to 
stabilize the develop end point.  (See below for 
further description of these process options.)  
An oven soft bake is also possible.  The process 
that you choose is dependent on tool availability 
and manufacturability requirements.  
 
Step 1.  Surface Preparation 
Substrates to be coated with CYCLOTENE resin 
should be free of all organic impurities and other 
contaminations prior to coating.  A clean surface 
is important to ensure good adhesion.  An 
example of a cleaning procedure is an oxygen 
plasma clean, followed by dump rinse and spin-
rinse dry.

the resin is to be adhered to any exposed metal 
or inorganic (silicon oxide, silicon nitride, 
alumina) 
surfaces.

Step 3.  BCB Coating  
3.1 Equipment 
It is recommended that coaters be equipped 
with two dispense heads (CYCLOTENE resin and 
adhesion promoter) backside rinse and EBR 
capability, hot plates and bowl exhaust. 
 
3.2 Coating Process 
Photo BCB films are spun onto the substrate 
directly after the adhesion promoter application 
and spin dry. The precise conditions used to 
deposit the resins (e.g. spin speed) will vary 
according to the final film thickness desired and 
which formulation of resin is being used. Table 6 
shows thickness vs spin speed for CYCLOTENE 
4022-35, 4024-40, and 4026-46 resins after soft 
bake (see section 4) and final thickness after 
exposure, development, and cure.  Most of the 
loss in film thickness in the final, cured film 
occurs during the develop step.  The loss in film 
thickness during the cure step (other than 
removal of residual developer solvent) is less 
than 5%.  The thicknesses in Table 6 were 
determined using an open spin bowl.  If a 
covered or closed cup coater is used, the 
thicknesses will differ and will depend on spin 
time as well as spin speed.  Figure 3 shows a 
comparison of film thickness using open and 
closed bowl configurations.

Final hard cured film thickness is also a function 
of subsequent processing steps, including soft 
bake conditions, exposure dose and develop-

3.3 Dispense  Resin 
Dispense a puddle of resin of 1-5 ml (depending 
on 
topography, 
substrate

and 
resin 
viscosity) on the wafer.  The dispense can be 
static or dynamic (10 - 100 rpm). 
 
3.4 Spread 
Increase the substrate speed to 500 rpm for 
approx. 5 – 10 seconds to spread the resin out 
from the center of the substrate. 
 
3.5 Spin 
Increase the substrate speed to a rate that will 
achieve the desired pre-exposure thickness (see 
Tables 6, 7). Backside rinse during spin of 
CYCLOTENE 4026-46 resin during the spin 
process will help suppress polymer filament 
(“cotton candy”) formation.

3.6 Edge bead removal and backside rinse 
Decrease the substrate speed to 600-1000 rpm 
and dispense the backside solvent (T1100 Rinse 
Solvent) for 5-10 seconds to remove any 
contamination from the back side of the 
substrate and remove the “bead” that has 
formed on the front side edge.  Increase the 
speed and spin for 10 seconds to dry (do not 
exceed the original spin speed).  Top side edge 
bead removal can also be used, either from a 
dispense head on a track or manually with a 
syringe. 
 
Step 4.  Soft Bake 
After spin coating, the films should be heated 
for a short period of time to drive out residual 
solvent.  The specific time and temperature are 
dependent on the composition of the substrate 
as well as the thickness of the film. This can be 
done on a hot plate in conjunction with a 
develop end point monitor (Step 4A), on a hot 
plate in conjunction with a pre-develop bake 
(Step 4B).  Either Step 4A or Step 4B is used 
and correspond to Process A and B in Figure 2, 
respectively.   The soft bake is normally carried 
out immediately after spin coating. 
 
If rework is needed after coat and soft bake, the 
film can be stripped with T1100 Rinse Solvent.  
Either a puddle process on a track or immersion 
in a tank can be used.  DS2100 developer can 
also be used to remove an unexposed film. 
 
Step 4A.  Hotplate soft bake; develop end 
point monitor (Process A) 
The recommended hot plate bake temperature 
depends on the thickness of the film after coat 
and bake.  Recommended bake temperatures 
when using the end point monitor process are 
shown in Table 8.  The end point monitor 
process is explained in more detail in Step 6.  
These are suggested guidelines; with the

and temperature will, however, have an effect 
on the subsequent processing.  A higher soft

After the soft bake, the substrates should be 
allowed to cool to room temperature before 
photolithography.  The photo-BCB films should 
be given an exposure dose appropriate for the 
thickness of the film.  Typical exposure doses 
for photo BCB films are given in Table 10.  For 
example, a film of CYCLOTENE 4024-40 resin 
spun at 2500 rpm will have a thickness after soft 
bake of 7.4µm, thus, the recommended dose 
will be 25 mJ/cm2/µm x 7.4µm = 185 mJ/cm2. 
 
These doses were based on intensity measured 
at 
I-line 
and 
were 
determined 
on 
a 
proximity/contact 
aligner 
with 
broad-band 
exposure.  Exposure dose and focus (gap 
setting for proximity printers, focal offset for  
steppers or projection printers) will have an 
effect on film quality, resolution, and sidewall 
slope.  If exposure tools with only I-line or only 
G-line radiation are used (e.g., stepper), a 
higher exposure dose will be needed.  Note that 
when the coating thickness varies due to 
topography on the wafer, the exposure dose 
should be based on the thickness of the thickest 
regions.  Note also that these recommended 
doses were determined on silicon substrates. 
Re-optimization of the dose may be necessary 
based on substrate roughness and reflectivity 
(e.g., ceramic substrates, varying topology). 
 
Exposure 
can 
be 
performed 
essentially 
immediately after soft bake, as soon as the 
wafer has cooled to room temperature.  The 
delay time between soft bake and exposure can 
be at least 24 hours with no adverse effects.  
Slight film thickness drift, and CD loss, may be 
seen at longer delay times. 
 
When fabricating multilayer devices, BCB is 
deposited on top of BCB.  In these cases, higher 
exposure doses are often needed for the second 
and subsequent BCB layers, because of absorp-
tion of light by the underlying BCB and loss of 
reflected light.  Insufficient exposure can lead to 
wrinkling of the film during the develop step.

Step 7.  Pre-Develop Bake (Process B) 
Before solvent development, an oven bake step 
can be added to stabilize the development end 
point time. Without this bake, the development 
end point time will increase as the film sits at 
room temperature, and is thus dependent on 
the time delay between process steps (see Fig. 
3).  The pre-develop bake will re-set the develop 
end point to the same time, regardless of the 
time delay between soft bake and develop.  The 
pre-develop 
bake 
must 
be 
carried 
out 
immediately 
before 
developing 
the wafer, 
otherwise the end point will again drift toward 
longer times.  For films with a thickness of 3-
10µm after soft bake, a 5 minute pre-develop 
bake of 55°C - 65°C in an oven is suggested.  
For thicknesses outside this range, please 
contact a Dow representative for suggested 
bake conditions.  In addition to the time delay, 
the actual end point will be a function of film 
thickness, soft bake time and temperature, and 
developer temperature. For this reason a 
develop end point cannot be precisely defined 
here; each user will have to determine the end 
point at their facility on their tool set by 
developing at least one monitor substrate.  A 
pre-develop bake will eliminate develop end 
point variation due to time delays.  The user 
should realize that, in addition, the variables 
listed above need to be stable and controlled to 
achieve a uniform develop end point. 
 
Step 8.  Develop 
Pattern development after exposure can be 
accomplished by puddle, immersion, or spray 
techniques.  This processing guide is based on 
an immersion develop process.  Please refer to 
“Processing Procedures for CYCLOTENE 4000 
Series Photosensitive Resins (Puddle Develop)” 
for puddle development processing guidelines. 
 
Immersion development uses DS3000 develop-
er; puddle development uses DS2100 developer.  
These developers cannot be interchanged. 
 
Develop can follow immediately after exposure; 
no wait time is needed.  The delay time 
between exposure and develop can be at least 
48 hours with no adverse effects.  Some slight 
thickness drift, and CD loss, may be seen at 
longer delay times. 
 
8.1 Safety and Handling 
Advanced Developer DS3000 is composed of 
1,3,5-triisopropylbenzene (CAS# 717-74-8).  Its 
boiling point is 233°C and it has a flash point of 
95°C and an autoignition temperature of 402°C.  
The Advanced Developer DS3000 should be 
stored 
at 
room 
temperature 
in 
sealed 
containers.  Although DS3000 has a high flash 
point, preventive measures should be taken to 
avoid electrical discharges and ignition sources.  
Non-metallic develop tanks should be grounded.  
Refer to the Material Safety Data Sheet for 
further information on DS3000. 
 
8.2  Process Equipment 
The DS3000 develop process consists of three 
steps:  
1) Develop in heated DS3000  
2) Rinse in DS3000 at room temperature  
3) Spin dry  
The 
develop 
step 
should 
be 
run 
in 
a 
temperature-controlled 
tank 
with 
±0.5°C 
temperature accuracy in the 30-40°C operating 
range. The develop tank should be installed 
inside a solvent wet bench with adequate 
ventilation. Additional developer circulation and 
filtration units are suggested for production 
processes. Ideal wet bench building material is 
stainless steel. A second bath with DS3000 at 
room temperature is recommended for stopping 
the develop process and rinsing the wafers. A 
suitable spin-rinse dryer (SRD) with heated 
nitrogen blow is needed to clean and dry the 
wafers and boat. 
 
8.3  Suggested Develop Procedure 
For the chosen set of process parameters (film 
thickness, soft bake time and temperature, 
delay time between soft bake and develop), the 
develop time in DS3000 will depend on the bath 
temperature.  At room temperature, develop 
time may be an hour or more, whereas, in the 
region of 30 to 35°C, the develop time is in the 
range of several minutes.   
 
The process wafers are immersed in the heated 
DS3000 bath for a pre-determined length of 
time.  If an end point monitor (Step 6) is 
included with the lot, this is used to determine 
the develop time.  The total develop time should

8.4  Rework 
Once the film is exposed, it is insoluble in most 
solvents.  Exposed and developed films can be 
reworked by stripping in Primary Stripper A. The 
wafer is immersed in the stripper bath for 30 
minutes at room temperature or for 5 minutes 
at 80°C.  This is followed by a rinse in IPA and a 
water rinse.  The stripper absorbs atmospheric 
moisture at room temperature, which inactivates 
the bath and makes it corrosive to metals.  Use 
at 80°C is recommended because the bath 
remains dry at this temperature.  If the stripper 
is to be used at room temperature, it is 
recommended that only a freshly poured bath 
be used, and that the chemical not be re-used.  
See “Rework Procedures for CYCLOTENE 3000 
Series and 4000 Series Resins” for more details. 
 
Step 9.  Post-Develop Bake 
For optical inspection after development, a one 
minute bake at 80°C – 100°C is suggested. 
 
Step 10.  Cure 
After photolithographic processing is complete, 
the film is cured.  A variety of equipment can be 
used for curing CYCLOTENE resins, such as a 
box oven, belt furnace, tube furnace, and hot 
plate.  Except for early out-gassing of residual 
solvent, CYCLOTENE resins do not evolve 
volatiles during cure, and, thus, there are no 
constraints on the heating rate.  The only 
requirement is that, since films of CYCLOTENE 
resin are susceptible to oxidation at elevated 
temperatures, the film must be under an inert 
atmosphere 
at 
high 
temperature 
(recom-
mended: <100 ppm of O2 at >150°C).  Please 
refer to “Cure and Oxidation Measurements for 
CYCLOTENE Advanced Electronic Resins”.  Thus, 
the maximum oven ramp rate depends on how 
rapidly the oven can be purged of oxygen.  The 
extent of cure is a function of time and 
temperature, as shown in Figure 5.
